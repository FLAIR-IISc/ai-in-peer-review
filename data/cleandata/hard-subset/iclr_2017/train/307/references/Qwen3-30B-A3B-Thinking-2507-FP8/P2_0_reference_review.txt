

# Review for ICLR Submission

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contribution

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of dialog systems by introducing a new benchmark dataset and task set for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. The authors address a critical gap in the literature: the lack of reproducible, well-defined benchmarks for evaluating end-to-end models in goal-directed conversations, which are common in real-world applications like restaurant reservation systems. 

The key contribution is a set of five well-designed synthetic tasks within a restaurant reservation domain that test specific capabilities of dialog systems: issuing API calls (T1), updating API calls (T2), displaying options (T3), providing extra information (T4), and conducting full dialogs (T5). The authors also include two real-world datasets (DSTC2 and Concierge) to validate that their synthetic benchmark correlates with real-world performance. 

The paper demonstrates that their benchmark can effectively evaluate models, with Memory Networks (with match type features) emerging as the strongest baseline. The authors show that while current models can handle some aspects of goal-oriented dialog (like issuing API calls), they still struggle with interpreting knowledge base results to display options to users. Crucially, they demonstrate that the patterns observed in their synthetic tasks correlate well with performance on real-world data, suggesting the benchmark can serve as a useful proxy for real-world evaluation.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

**Key reasons for acceptance:** (1) The paper addresses a significant gap in the dialog systems literature with a well-motivated, reproducible benchmark, and (2) the benchmark is rigorously evaluated and shown to correlate with real-world performance.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

### Specific Question/Problem Tackled (Question 1)
The paper clearly identifies and addresses a critical problem in dialog systems research: the lack of standardized, reproducible benchmarks for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. While end-to-end models have shown promise in chit-chat settings, their performance in goal-oriented domains (where clear objectives like restaurant reservations must be completed) remains poorly understood due to the absence of appropriate evaluation frameworks. The authors provide a compelling argument that existing datasets are either too noisy, too domain-specific, or not designed for end-to-end learning, making it difficult to fairly compare different approaches.

### Approach Well Motivated (Question 2)
The approach is exceptionally well-motivated. The authors provide a thorough literature review of existing datasets and their limitations, positioning their work within the context of the bAbI tasks (Weston et al., 2015b), which have been successful in providing standardized benchmarks for question answering. They clearly articulate why their approach is necessary: "It is unclear if end-to-end models are in a position to replace traditional dialog methods in a goal-directed setting." The task design is thoughtful, with each task targeting a specific capability (dialog management, KB querying, interpretation of results), and the inclusion of OOV (out-of-vocabulary) tests to evaluate generalization capability is particularly strong.

### Claims Supported by Evidence (Question 3)
The paper provides robust empirical evidence to support its claims. The experimental design is rigorous:
- They create a synthetic dataset with controlled variables (including OOV testing)
- They compare multiple model architectures (rule-based, IR methods, supervised embeddings, Memory Networks)
- They evaluate on both synthetic and real-world datasets (DSTC2 and Concierge)
- They use appropriate metrics (per-response and per-dialog accuracy)
- They provide detailed error analysis (e.g., Memory Networks can issue API calls but struggle with displaying options)

The results are scientifically rigorous and well-presented. The key finding that Memory Networks with match type features outperform other methods, and that the synthetic benchmark correlates well with real-world performance, is supported by multiple lines of evidence. The paper also thoughtfully acknowledges limitations (e.g., "the per-dialog accuracy remains low" for the best models), which strengthens the credibility of their claims.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

The paper is strong overall, but I have a few suggestions to make it even better:

1. **Clarify the "Why Synthetic" Justification**: While the authors explain the benefits of synthetic data for reproducibility, they could more explicitly contrast their approach with existing synthetic data generation methods (like user simulators for POMDPs) and explain why their specific synthetic approach is better suited for end-to-end learning.

2. **Expand on the "Match Type Features"**: The match type features are a crucial innovation that enables better OOV performance. A brief theoretical explanation of why these features work (beyond just stating they help) would strengthen the paper. For example, why do exact matches with type information work better than embeddings for entities?

3. **Discuss Limitations More Thoroughly**: The authors note that their benchmark is for restaurant reservation, but they don't discuss how generalizable their findings are to other goal-oriented domains (e.g., flight booking, hotel reservations). A brief discussion of potential limitations in domain transfer would be valuable.

4. **Clarify the Concierge Dataset**: The paper mentions the Concierge dataset has "higher variability of the language" but doesn't provide concrete examples or statistics to support this claim. Adding a small example of the variability or a simple metric (e.g., vocabulary size, dialog length) would strengthen this point.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper states that "the pattern of results observed in our tasks is indeed a good proxy for what would be observed on real data." Could you provide more specific evidence of this correlation beyond the similar relative performance patterns? For example, what is the correlation coefficient between performance on your synthetic tasks and performance on the real datasets?

2. In Table 2, Memory Networks with match type features perform worse on Task 2 (updating API calls) compared to without match type features. Could you provide more insight into why this might be the case, given that the match type features are designed to help with OOV words?

3. The paper mentions that "the per-dialog accuracy is still close to 0 on T3 and T4" even for the best models. Could you provide a qualitative analysis of the specific errors made by the Memory Networks on these tasks? What are the most common types of errors, and what might be the root causes?

4. Given that the authors state "the goal here is explicitly not to improve the state of the art in the narrow domain of restaurant booking," could they clarify how their benchmark might be extended to other domains (beyond restaurant reservation) without requiring significant re-engineering of the task definitions?

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of dialog systems by providing a well-designed, reproducible benchmark for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog models. The work is well-motivated, rigorously evaluated, and provides important insights into the current capabilities and limitations of end-to-end approaches. I believe it will be a useful resource for the community and should be accepted for publication at ICLR.
