

# Peer Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper addresses a critical gap in the field of dialog systems by proposing a systematic, reproducible framework for evaluating end-to-end neural models in goal-oriented dialog settings. The authors introduce a novel set of 5 synthetic tasks (Task 1-5) within the restaurant reservation domain, designed to test specific capabilities of dialog systems: issuing API calls (T1), updating API calls (T2), displaying options (T3), providing extra information (T4), and conducting full dialogs (T5). These tasks are grounded in an underlying knowledge base (KB) with structured entities (restaurants, locations, cuisine types, etc.) and are designed to be systematically broken down into testable subtasks. The authors also include two real-world datasets: Dialog State Tracking Challenge (DSTC2) as Task 6 and an online concierge service dataset (Concierge). 

The paper's key contribution is not to improve the state-of-the-art in restaurant booking but to provide a standardized evaluation framework that enables rigorous comparison of different end-to-end approaches. The authors evaluate multiple baselines including rule-based systems, classical information retrieval methods, supervised embeddings, and Memory Networks. Their results reveal that while end-to-end models can handle certain aspects of goal-oriented dialog (e.g., issuing API calls), they struggle with interpreting KB results to display options (T3) and updating information (T2), suggesting significant room for improvement in current end-to-end approaches for goal-oriented tasks.

## 2. Decision

**Accept with minor revisions.**

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field by providing a well-designed, reproducible benchmark for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. The systematic breakdown of the dialog process into testable subtasks is particularly valuable and addresses a critical need in the community.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Acceptance

This paper excels in several key areas:

First, the motivation is exceptionally strong. The authors clearly articulate the gap in the literature: while end-to-end models have shown promise in chit-chat settings, there's been no systematic way to evaluate them in goal-oriented settings where traditional slot-filling systems excel. The comparison to bAbI tasks for question answering is particularly apt and well-justified.

Second, the experimental design is rigorous and well-structured. The synthetic tasks are carefully constructed to test specific capabilities, and the evaluation uses both per-response and per-dialog accuracy metrics, which is crucial for goal-oriented dialog where a single incorrect response can derail the entire conversation. The inclusion of out-of-vocabulary (OOV) test sets is particularly insightful for evaluating generalization capabilities.

Third, the results provide valuable insights into the current limitations of end-to-end models. The finding that Memory Networks with match type features can solve T1-2 but still fail on T3-4 (displaying options) is particularly informative and will guide future research. The authors also convincingly demonstrate that the patterns observed on synthetic data generalize to real-world datasets (DSTC2 and Concierge), strengthening the validity of their benchmark.

Finally, the paper provides an open resource (http://fb.ai/babi) that will undoubtedly benefit the community by enabling consistent, reproducible comparisons of future approaches.

## 4. Additional Feedback

While the paper is strong overall, I would recommend the following minor improvements:

1. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the synthetic dataset. While the authors acknowledge it's artificial, they could elaborate on how well the specific restaurant reservation domain generalizes to other domains (e.g., hotel booking, travel planning) and what modifications might be needed for those domains.

2. The computational complexity of the proposed methods (particularly Memory Networks with multiple hops) isn't discussed. For practical deployment considerations, it would be valuable to include a brief comparison of inference time and memory requirements across methods.

3. The paper mentions that "the pattern of results observed in our tasks is indeed a good proxy for what would be observed on real data," but this claim could be strengthened with a brief analysis of which specific aspects of the synthetic tasks most closely match the real-world data (e.g., which task features are most predictive of real-world performance).

4. The authors could briefly discuss how their benchmark might be extended to handle more complex dialog structures, such as multi-domain conversations or dialogues requiring multiple API calls.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper states that Memory Networks with match type features significantly improve performance on OOV tasks. Could the authors elaborate on how the match type features work in practice, and whether this approach could be generalized to other types of entities beyond those in their KB (e.g., names of people, organizations, or other domain-specific entities)?

2. The authors mention that their synthetic tasks are designed to be completely predictable for rule-based systems. How would they adapt their benchmark to handle more ambiguous or open-ended user requests that don't fit neatly into their predefined structure?

3. Given that the Concierge dataset has more diverse language and structure, how might the authors modify their benchmark to better capture the challenges of this more realistic setting while maintaining the reproducibility that makes their benchmark valuable?

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. The proposed benchmark will be valuable for future research on end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems, and the insights into where current models fail will guide the community toward more effective approaches. I strongly recommend acceptance with the minor revisions noted above.
