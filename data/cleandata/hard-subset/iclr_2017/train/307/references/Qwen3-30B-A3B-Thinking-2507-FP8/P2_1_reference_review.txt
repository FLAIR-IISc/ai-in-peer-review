

# ICLR Review

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contribution

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of dialog systems by addressing a critical gap: the lack of a standardized, reproducible benchmark for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. The authors propose a new open resource consisting of five well-defined tasks within a restaurant reservation domain, designed to test specific capabilities required for successful goal-oriented dialog (dialog management, KB querying, interpreting results, and handling new entities). Unlike previous datasets that are either too domain-specific or designed for slot-filling systems, their tasks are specifically designed to evaluate end-to-end models. The authors also validate their synthetic tasks with two real-world datasets (DSTC2 and an online concierge service), demonstrating that the pattern of results observed on their synthetic tasks is a good proxy for real-world performance. The contribution is not to improve the state of the art in restaurant booking, but to provide a standardized testbed that will enable more rigorous comparisons of end-to-end dialog models in goal-oriented settings.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**. This paper makes a clear, well-motivated contribution to the field by providing a much-needed standardized evaluation framework for end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. The paper is scientifically rigorous, well-placed in the literature, and provides a valuable resource that will benefit the entire community.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper successfully addresses a critical and well-identified gap in the literature. As the authors correctly note, while end-to-end dialog systems have shown promise for chit-chat settings, their performance in goal-oriented dialog settings is unclear, and there is no standardized benchmark to evaluate them. The motivation is strong because:

1. The authors clearly identify the limitations of existing approaches (chit-chat datasets don't translate well to goal-oriented tasks, and existing goal-oriented datasets are not suitable for end-to-end learning).

2. The task design is thoughtful and well-executed, breaking down the complex goal-oriented dialog process into specific, testable components. The OOV testing is particularly valuable for evaluating generalization capabilities.

3. The experimental design is scientifically rigorous, with multiple baselines (rule-based, IR methods, supervised embeddings, Memory Networks) evaluated across both synthetic tasks and real-world datasets.

4. The validation with real-world data (DSTC2 and Concierge) is crucial, demonstrating that their synthetic tasks are indeed a good proxy for real-world performance, which is a significant contribution.

5. The paper provides an open resource (dataset and tasks) that will benefit the community, with the dataset made publicly available at http://fb.ai/babi.

The paper is well-written, the figures and tables are clear, and the results support their claims that current end-to-end models have specific weaknesses in goal-oriented dialog (particularly in interpreting KB results to display options), while their synthetic tasks provide a good proxy for real-world performance.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

These suggestions are intended to help strengthen the paper further, not as requirements for acceptance:

1. **Rule-based system details**: The paper mentions a rule-based system for T6 with 40.7% per-response accuracy but doesn't provide details about how the rules were constructed (e.g., how many rules were needed, how they were prioritized). A brief paragraph on this would help readers understand why rule-based approaches are less effective on real-world data.

2. **Failure examples**: The paper states that "the per-dialog accuracy is still close to 0 on T3 and T4" for Memory Networks. Providing a few specific examples of where the models fail would help readers better understand the specific weaknesses of current end-to-end models.

3. **Match type features explanation**: The paper mentions match type features but doesn't explain them in sufficient detail. A brief explanation of how these features are integrated into the model would help readers understand why they work.

4. **Limitations of synthetic tasks**: While the paper validates with real-world data, it would be helpful to have a more explicit discussion of what aspects of real-world dialog are not captured by their synthetic tasks. This would help readers understand the boundaries of their testbed.

5. **Quantifying correlation**: The paper claims that "the pattern of relative performances of methods is the same on Concierge and on our series of tasks." Providing a quantitative measure of this similarity (e.g., correlation coefficients) would strengthen this claim.

## Questions for the Authors

1. The paper states that "Memory Networks with match type features give two performance gains: (i) T4 becomes solvable because matches can be made to the results of the API call; and (ii) out-of-vocabulary results are significantly improved." Could you provide more details on how the match type features specifically enable these improvements, particularly for T4?

2. The paper mentions that "the per-dialog accuracy is still close to 0 on T3 and T4" for Memory Networks. Can you provide examples of where the models fail on these tasks, and what specific aspects of the task they struggle with?

3. The paper states that "the pattern of relative performances of methods is the same on Concierge and on our series of tasks." Could you provide a quantitative measure of this similarity (e.g., correlation between performance on synthetic tasks and real-world data)?

4. The paper mentions that "the set of user requests is much wider" in the Concierge dataset. Could you elaborate on how this wider range of requests affects the model performance, and whether there are specific request types that are particularly challenging?

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by providing a standardized evaluation framework for end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. The work is well-motivated, scientifically rigorous, and will undoubtedly benefit future research in this area. I strongly recommend acceptance.
