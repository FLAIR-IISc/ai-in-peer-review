

# Peer Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of goal-oriented dialog systems by introducing a systematic, reproducible testbed for evaluating end-to-end dialog models. The authors identify a critical gap in the literature: the lack of standardized, open resources for evaluating end-to-end models in goal-oriented settings, where current evaluation methods either lack reproducibility (e.g., Mechanical Turk-based evaluations) or don't correlate well with human judgments. 

The paper's main contributions are:
1. A set of five synthetic goal-oriented dialog tasks based on restaurant reservation, designed to test specific capabilities (issuing API calls, updating requests, displaying options, providing information, and full dialog management)
2. A careful design of the tasks to enable error analysis by isolating specific dialog capabilities
3. Two additional datasets based on real human-machine interactions (DSTC2 and an online concierge service) to validate that the synthetic tasks are good proxies for real-world performance
4. Comprehensive evaluation of multiple model classes (rule-based, classical IR, supervised embeddings, and Memory Networks) across these tasks
5. An open dataset and code repository to facilitate reproducibility and future research

The paper successfully demonstrates that while end-to-end models can learn to perform some aspects of goal-oriented dialog (like issuing API calls), they still struggle with key capabilities like interpreting and presenting information from knowledge bases. The authors provide compelling evidence that the synthetic tasks are good proxies for real-world performance, as the relative performance patterns of models are consistent across synthetic and real datasets.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by addressing a critical gap in evaluation methodology for goal-oriented dialog systems. The paper is well-motivated, well-executed, and provides a valuable resource for the community. The key reasons for acceptance are:
1. The paper addresses a genuine and important problem in the field: the lack of systematic, reproducible evaluation methods for end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems
2. The proposed testbed is well-designed, with clear task definitions that enable error analysis, and the authors provide strong evidence that it's a good proxy for real-world performance

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper's contributions are timely and address a critical need in the field. The authors demonstrate a deep understanding of the literature on dialog systems, effectively positioning their work within the context of both traditional slot-filling approaches and recent end-to-end neural methods. The related work section is comprehensive and well-structured, clearly articulating the limitations of current evaluation methods.

The methodology is sound and rigorously executed. The synthetic task design is thoughtful, with careful attention to isolating specific dialog capabilities (Tasks 1-4) and combining them in Task 5. The inclusion of OOV testing is particularly valuable for understanding generalization capabilities. The validation on real-world datasets (DSTC2 and Concierge) is a strong point, as it demonstrates that the synthetic tasks are indeed good proxies for real-world performance.

The results are scientifically rigorous, with appropriate baselines, proper train/validation/test splits, and multiple metrics (per-response and per-dialog accuracy). The analysis of failure modes is insightful, particularly the observation that models can learn to issue API calls but struggle with interpreting and presenting information from knowledge bases.

The paper's contribution is significant because it provides a standardized testbed that can be used by the community to fairly compare different approaches to end-to-end goal-oriented dialog. This will help focus research and development efforts on the specific capabilities that need improvement.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

While the paper is strong, the following suggestions would further enhance its contribution:

1. **Clarify the relationship between the synthetic tasks and real-world dialog complexity**: The paper mentions that the synthetic tasks are "artificial" but doesn't adequately address how much the simplified structure of these tasks might limit their applicability to more complex real-world scenarios. A brief discussion of the limitations of the synthetic approach would strengthen the paper.

2. **Expand the discussion of model limitations**: While the paper identifies that Memory Networks struggle with interpreting and presenting information, it would be valuable to provide more specific examples of the types of errors made (beyond the examples in Appendix B). This would help the community better understand the specific challenges that need to be addressed.

3. **Address the scalability of the approach**: The paper mentions that the goal is "not to improve the state of the art in the narrow domain of restaurant booking" but rather to evaluate end-to-end models. However, it would be helpful to briefly discuss how the proposed testbed would scale to more complex domains beyond restaurant reservation.

4. **Include more details on the Concierge dataset**: The paper mentions that the Concierge dataset is more diverse and variable than the synthetic tasks, but doesn't provide specific examples of the types of challenges it presents. Adding a few representative examples would help readers better understand the nature of the real-world data.

5. **Discuss the implications for future work**: The paper concludes by stating that "future work on breaking down, analysing and developing models over the simulated tasks should help in the real tasks." It would be valuable to provide more specific suggestions for what future work might look like, such as architectural modifications to address the specific limitations identified.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper claims that the synthetic tasks are "good proxies" for real-world performance, as evidenced by consistent relative performance patterns across datasets. Could you provide more specific analysis of the conditions under which the synthetic tasks might fail to be good proxies? For example, would the same patterns hold for domains with much more complex user requests or more diverse conversation flows?

2. The Memory Networks architecture used in the paper is based on the MemN2N model from 2015. Given that more recent end-to-end dialog models (e.g., Transformer-based models, sequence-to-sequence models with attention) have been proposed, why was this particular architecture chosen? Would the results change significantly if more modern architectures were used?

3. The paper states that "the set of user requests is much wider" in the Concierge dataset. Could you elaborate on how this wider set of requests impacts the evaluation, particularly in terms of the challenges faced by the models? Are there specific types of requests that prove particularly difficult for the end-to-end models?

4. The OOV evaluation is an important contribution, but the paper notes that "performance drops slightly on T2 compared to not using match type features." Could you elaborate on why match type features would have this negative effect on T2 (updating API calls) but positive effects on other tasks? This seems counterintuitive and warrants further explanation.

5. The paper mentions that "the per-dialog accuracy is still close to 0 on T3 and T4" even for the best models. Given that the authors state that "the goal here is explicitly not to improve the state of the art in the narrow domain," could you elaborate on what specific capabilities would need to be improved for a model to solve T3 and T4 completely? This would help the community understand the specific research directions needed.
