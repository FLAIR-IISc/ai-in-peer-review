

# Review

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper introduces a comprehensive benchmark for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. The authors propose a set of five synthetic tasks within a restaurant reservation domain that systematically test crucial dialog capabilities: issuing API calls, updating API calls, displaying options, providing extra information, and conducting full dialogs. Each task isolates specific dialog skills while maintaining a coherent scenario. The authors also provide two real-world datasets (DSTC2 and an online concierge service) to validate that their synthetic tasks serve as a good proxy for real-world performance. The key contribution is creating a reproducible, lightweight, and well-defined testbed for goal-oriented dialog systems, addressing a critical gap in the literature where existing datasets are either noisy, domain-specific, or designed for different components (like dialog state tracking) rather than end-to-end learning.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper makes a strong contribution by providing a well-motivated, rigorously designed benchmark for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. The proposed tasks are scientifically sound, the experimental validation across synthetic and real data is thorough, and the benchmark has clear potential to advance the field.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper addresses a well-defined problem: evaluating end-to-end dialog systems in goal-oriented settings where traditional slot-filling approaches excel. The motivation is clear and well-placed in the literature, as the authors correctly identify the lack of reproducible, task-focused benchmarks for this specific evaluation scenario. They demonstrate the limitations of existing datasets (which are often noisy, designed for state tracking, or require proprietary access) and position their work as filling this critical gap.

The paper strongly supports its claims through a comprehensive experimental evaluation. The authors design five tasks that systematically test specific dialog capabilities, with careful attention to OOV generalization by creating disjoint KBs. The evaluation includes multiple baselines (rule-based systems, classical IR methods, supervised embeddings, and Memory Networks) across both synthetic and real-world data. The results demonstrate that the synthetic tasks serve as a valid proxy for real-world performance (as evidenced by consistent relative performance patterns across datasets), and they provide valuable error analysis by task.

The experimental design is scientifically rigorous. The authors clearly distinguish between in-vocabulary and out-of-vocabulary test sets to evaluate generalization, and they provide detailed ablation studies on the impact of match type features. The paper demonstrates that Memory Networks with match type features can outperform other baselines on most tasks, particularly on OOV generalization, and the results are consistent across synthetic and real data.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To further strengthen the paper, the authors could:
- Provide more detailed discussion about the limitations of their benchmark (e.g., how well it generalizes beyond restaurant reservation to other domains with different dialog structures).
- Include a more thorough analysis of why Memory Networks struggle with Task 3 (displaying options) even with match type features, which appears to be a key limitation in their approach.
- Discuss the practical implications of their findings for real-world deployment of end-to-end dialog systems, particularly regarding the gap between per-response accuracy and per-dialog success.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper demonstrates that the synthetic tasks serve as a good proxy for real-world performance, but how would you expect the benchmark to scale to more complex domains (e.g., multi-domain dialog systems or domains with more complex reasoning requirements)?
2. Task 3 (displaying options) remains challenging for all models, even with match type features. Could you provide more insight into why this specific task is particularly difficult for end-to-end models, and what architectural or training modifications might help overcome this limitation?
3. The Concierge dataset has more diverse language and variable dialog structures compared to the synthetic tasks. How do you think these factors specifically impact model performance, and what modifications to the benchmark would better capture these real-world challenges?
