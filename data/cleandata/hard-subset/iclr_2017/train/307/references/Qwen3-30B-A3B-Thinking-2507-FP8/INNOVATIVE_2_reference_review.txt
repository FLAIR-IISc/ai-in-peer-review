

# Review

## Summary
This paper introduces a novel evaluation framework for end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems, addressing a critical gap in the field. The authors propose a set of five synthetic tasks grounded in restaurant reservation scenarios that test crucial capabilities of dialog systems (issuing API calls, updating requests, displaying options, providing information, and full dialog management). They create a reproducible, lightweight testbed with controlled variables to systematically evaluate end-to-end models, contrasting with current evaluation methods that rely on noisy, non-reproducible human interactions. The authors validate their framework against real-world datasets (DSTC2 and a concierge service), demonstrating that performance patterns on their synthetic tasks correlate with real-world performance. This contribution is significant as it provides the community with a standardized, interpretable benchmark to assess end-to-end dialog models' strengths and weaknesses in goal-oriented settings.

## Decision
Accept

## Key Reasons
1. The paper's core contribution - a novel evaluation framework for goal-oriented dialog systems - is highly valuable and addresses a fundamental gap in the field. It shifts the focus from "how to build better dialog systems" to "how to better evaluate them," which is a bold meta-contribution that could significantly impact future research direction.

2. The framework is well-motivated, well-structured, and validated against real-world data. The authors demonstrate that their synthetic tasks provide a reliable proxy for real-world performance, making their contribution both theoretically sound and practically useful.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper excels in novelty and boldness, which are highly valued in ICLR. While many papers focus on improving model architectures or training methods for goal-oriented dialog, this paper tackles the foundational problem of how to properly evaluate these models. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of current evaluation practices (reliance on Mechanical Turk, lack of reproducibility, poor correlation with human judgments) and provide a compelling alternative.

Their approach is methodologically sound and well-executed. The five tasks are carefully designed to test specific capabilities, the synthetic data generation process is transparent, and the validation against real-world datasets is thorough. The paper demonstrates that memory networks, while not perfect, can learn to handle key aspects of goal-oriented dialog (like issuing API calls and dealing with OOV entities) - a significant insight into current model capabilities.

The authors also make a strong case for the importance of this work: goal-oriented dialog has clear real-world utility, and without proper evaluation frameworks, progress in the field will be hampered. This is not just another incremental improvement to existing models but a foundational contribution that could reshape how the entire field evaluates progress.

## Additional Feedback
1. The paper could benefit from more explicit discussion of how this testbed might be extended to other domains beyond restaurant reservation. While the authors mention this as a future direction, a brief discussion of potential domain adaptation challenges would strengthen the paper.

2. The description of the match type features (Section 4.5) is somewhat technical. A simpler explanation of why this approach works better than standard embeddings for entity handling would make the contribution more accessible to a broader audience.

3. The authors should clarify the exact relationship between their tasks and the bAbI tasks. While they draw the analogy, a more detailed comparison would help position their work within the broader context of evaluation frameworks.

## Questions for Authors
1. How do you envision the community adopting this framework? What specific steps could the authors take to facilitate wider adoption beyond their own experiments?

2. The paper mentions that "the pattern of results observed in our tasks is indeed a good proxy for what would be observed on real data." Could the authors provide more specific evidence about which aspects of the real data correlate best with their synthetic tasks?

3. In the evaluation, Memory Networks with match type features performed better on T4 (providing information) but worse on T2 (updating API calls) compared to without match type features. Could the authors share their insights on why this specific trade-off occurs?
