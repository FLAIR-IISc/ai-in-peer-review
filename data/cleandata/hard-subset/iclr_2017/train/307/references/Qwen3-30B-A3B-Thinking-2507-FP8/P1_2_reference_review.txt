

# Peer Review

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Contribution

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of dialog systems by proposing a systematic, reproducible, and lightweight benchmark for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. The authors recognize a critical gap in the current literature: while end-to-end neural models have shown promise for chit-chat dialog, there is a lack of standardized, reproducible evaluation methods for goal-oriented dialog systems that can properly assess their capabilities in structured, task-oriented scenarios. 

The authors design a set of five well-structured tasks within the restaurant reservation domain that test specific capabilities of dialog systems (issuing API calls, updating requests, displaying options, providing information, and conducting full dialogs). These tasks are grounded in an underlying knowledge base and are designed to be easily reproducible. To validate the relevance of their synthetic tasks to real-world scenarios, they also evaluate on two additional datasets from real human-machine interactions (DSTC2 and an online concierge service).

The paper thoroughly evaluates multiple model architectures (rule-based, classical IR, supervised embeddings, and memory networks) on these tasks, providing insightful analysis of what capabilities end-to-end models have and where they fall short. The introduction of "match type features" to handle entities and out-of-vocabulary words is a particularly valuable contribution. The authors convincingly demonstrate that their synthetic tasks provide a good proxy for real-world performance, which is crucial for future research in this area.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

The key reasons for acceptance are: (1) the paper addresses a clear and important gap in the field with a well-motivated and well-designed benchmark, and (2) the experimental evaluation is comprehensive, scientifically rigorous, and provides valuable insights into the current capabilities and limitations of end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper makes a strong case for the importance of their contribution. The problem statement is well-justified by the current literature, which shows that existing evaluation methods for goal-oriented dialog systems are either not reproducible (e.g., using Mechanical Turk) or do not correlate well with human judgments. The authors' proposed benchmark is directly inspired by the successful bAbI tasks and is designed to be lightweight, easy to use, and focused on specific capabilities of dialog systems.

The experimental design is particularly strong. The authors not only evaluate on their synthetic tasks but also validate their findings on two real-world datasets, demonstrating that the patterns observed in their synthetic tasks generalize to more realistic scenarios. Their ablation studies (with and without match type features) provide valuable insights into what aspects of the model design matter most.

The paper's findings are scientifically rigorous. They use appropriate metrics (per-response and per-dialog accuracy), carefully designed training/validation/test splits, and multiple model architectures to demonstrate the relative strengths and weaknesses of different approaches. The observation that memory networks with match type features perform best, but still struggle with interpreting API results to display options, is both insightful and well-supported by the data.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

While the paper is strong overall, there are a few areas where the authors could improve clarity and impact:

1. **Clarify the relationship between per-response and per-dialog accuracy**: The paper mentions that per-dialog accuracy is crucial because "if only one response is incorrect this could result in a failed dialog," but doesn't provide specific examples of how this failure manifests. Adding a concrete example from the results (e.g., "In Task 3, a model might correctly identify the top-rated restaurant but fail to present it properly, causing the dialog to fail") would strengthen the argument for why per-dialog accuracy is important.

2. **Expand on the limitations of the proposed benchmark**: The authors note that their tasks are synthetic and may not capture all aspects of real-world dialog, but could provide a more thorough discussion of what aspects of goal-oriented dialog might be underrepresented in their benchmark. For example, how might the benchmark handle unexpected user behavior, handling of errors, or multi-turn negotiation?

3. **Enhance the discussion of related work**: While the related work section is good, it could be strengthened by more explicitly contrasting the authors' approach with other proposed benchmarks. For instance, how does their work differ from the tasks in Dodge et al. (2016), which the authors mention as a similar but not quite goal-oriented resource?

4. **Provide more details on the real-world datasets**: The paper mentions that the Concierge dataset has additional challenges (e.g., users not staying focused on the request, typos), but doesn't elaborate on how these challenges specifically impact model performance. Adding a brief analysis of how the model's performance on Concierge differs from the synthetic tasks due to these challenges would be valuable.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper states that the OOV test sets are designed to test if models can generalize to new entities (restaurants, locations, cuisine types) unseen in training. However, the paper doesn't clarify whether the models have access to the KB during testing. Could you please clarify this? If models don't have access to the KB during testing, how does the OOV challenge differ from standard OOV evaluation?

2. In Table 2, the Memory Networks with match type features show slightly worse performance on Task 2 (Updating API calls) compared to without match type features. Could you provide some insight into why this might be the case? Is there a specific aspect of the updating process that the match type features might be interfering with?

3. The paper mentions that the Concierge dataset is "more diverse and so is the candidate set" compared to the synthetic tasks. How did the authors handle the larger candidate set in the evaluation, and did this impact the relative performance of the models compared to the synthetic tasks?

4. The authors state that "our best models can learn to track implicit dialog states and manipulate OOV words and symbols (T1-T2) to issue API calls and progress in conversations, but they are still unable to perfectly handle interpreting knowledge about entities (from returned API calls) to present results to the user, e.g. displaying options in T3." Could you elaborate on what specific challenges the models face in interpreting API results to display options, and whether you believe this is a fundamental limitation of current end-to-end approaches or something that could be addressed with additional training data or architectural changes?
