

# Review

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contribution

The paper proposes a new open resource consisting of five synthetic tasks within the goal-oriented restaurant reservation domain to systematically evaluate end-to-end dialog systems. The authors aim to address the lack of reproducible, lightweight resources for testing end-to-end models in goal-oriented settings, contrasting with existing resources that are domain-specific, noisy, or proprietary. They introduce a simulation-based framework with a knowledge base, generate dialog data covering various dialog stages (dialog management, KB querying, option presentation, etc.), and evaluate multiple models (rule-based, IR, supervised embeddings, Memory Networks) on these tasks. The paper claims their synthetic tasks serve as a good proxy for real-world performance, with Memory Networks showing promising results relative to baselines.

## 2. Decision: Reject

**Key reasons for rejection:**
1. Critical disconnect between claimed capabilities and reported results (per-dialog accuracy remains near zero despite claims of "non-trivial operations").
2. Inconsistent and poorly defined evaluation metrics that undermine the paper's core contribution.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

**Critical disconnect between claims and results:** The paper repeatedly claims Memory Networks "can be trained to perform non-trivial operations such as issuing API calls to KBs and manipulating entities unseen in training." However, Table 2 shows per-dialog accuracy for all tasks (including T1-T2 where they claim success) is "close to 0" (e.g., 0.3% for T1, 0.0% for T3). Per-dialog accuracy is the key metric for goal achievement (booking a table), and the near-zero values contradict the paper's claims. The paper acknowledges this in the abstract ("per-dialog one remains low"), yet still makes strong claims about the model's capabilities. This is a fundamental misalignment between evidence and conclusion.

**Poorly defined evaluation metric:** The paper introduces "per-dialog accuracy" as a critical metric for goal achievement but fails to clearly define it in the main text. The abstract states it "tracks completion of the actual goal," but the paper never specifies the exact calculation method (e.g., is it the percentage of dialogs where *all* responses are correct, or where *at least one* response is correct?). This ambiguity undermines the paper's core contribution, as the proposed tasks are designed to evaluate goal completion. The definition appears only in a parenthetical in the abstract, not in the main text or methodology.

**Additional methodological inconsistencies:**
- The paper claims their synthetic tasks are "a good proxy for what would be observed on real data" based on similar performance patterns, but Table 2 shows significant performance differences between synthetic tasks (T1-T5) and real data (T6, Concierge). The paper fails to adequately explain why these differences exist or how they impact the proxy claim.
- The paper states "Memory Networks can outperform a dedicated slot-filling rule-based baseline" on DSTC2 (T6), but the rule-based system achieves 40.7% per-response accuracy while Memory Networks achieve 55.2% per-response accuracy. However, the per-dialog accuracy for Memory Networks is only 15.2% (Table 2), which is still very low. The paper does not clarify why a 15.2% per-dialog success rate constitutes "outperforming" the rule-based system (which presumably has a similarly low per-dialog accuracy).
- The paper claims to use a "lightweight and easy to use" resource, but the implementation details (Appendix A) and task design (3.1.1) are complex and require significant domain knowledge to implement correctly.

**Formatting inconsistencies:**
- Inconsistent citation formats throughout (e.g., "Dodge et al\.,? 2016" vs. "Dodge et al., 2016").
- Inconsistent capitalization of "bAbI" (sometimes "bAbI", sometimes "babi").
- Inconsistent hyphenation of "end-to-end" (sometimes "end-to-end", sometimes "end to end").
- The reference list contains formatting errors (e.g., "Dodge, Dodge,? \Q2016\E, Dodge," in the middle of the references).

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

1. **Clarify and define evaluation metrics:** Provide a formal definition of "per-dialog accuracy" in the methodology section, including the exact calculation method. This is essential for the paper's core contribution.

2. **Address the disconnect between claims and results:** Either revise claims to match the reported results (e.g., "Memory Networks can correctly respond to individual requests but fail to complete dialogs"), or provide a more detailed analysis of why per-dialog accuracy remains low despite good per-response accuracy.

3. **Justify the ranking vs. generation approach:** Explain why the paper chose to evaluate in a ranking setting rather than generation, and how this impacts the interpretability of results.

4. **Improve consistency:** Standardize citation formats, hyphenation, and capitalization throughout the paper. Fix reference list formatting errors.

5. **Clarify the proxy claim:** Provide a more detailed analysis of why the performance patterns across synthetic and real data are similar despite differences in absolute performance. Explain how the synthetic tasks can reliably predict real-world performance.

6. **Add a discussion of limitations:** Explicitly state the limitations of the synthetic tasks as a proxy for real-world performance, including specific scenarios where the synthetic tasks may not generalize well.

The paper addresses an important gap in the literature, but the fundamental disconnect between claimed capabilities and reported results, combined with poorly defined metrics and numerous formatting inconsistencies, prevent it from making a scientifically rigorous contribution to the field. The authors should address these issues before resubmission.
