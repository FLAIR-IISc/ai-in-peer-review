

# Review

## Summary
This paper proposes a new benchmark for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems, consisting of five synthetic tasks within a restaurant reservation domain. The authors create a controlled environment with a structured knowledge base to test dialog systems' capabilities in handling dialog management, KB querying, and entity manipulation. They present a dataset with both standard and out-of-vocabulary test sets, and evaluate several baseline models including rule-based systems, classical information retrieval methods, supervised embeddings, and Memory Networks. The authors claim that their synthetic tasks provide a reproducible, lightweight resource that can effectively proxy real-world performance, with evidence from two additional datasets (DSTC2 and a concierge service dataset).

## Decision
Reject. The paper's main claim that their synthetic tasks provide a valid proxy for real-world goal-oriented dialog performance is not sufficiently supported by the evidence provided.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper's contribution is methodologically sound in creating a new benchmark dataset, but the key claim about the synthetic tasks being a good proxy for real-world performance lacks rigorous validation. While the authors show that relative model performance patterns are similar across synthetic and real datasets, they fail to demonstrate that absolute performance on the synthetic tasks correlates with real-world success. This is a critical gap, as the paper's entire motivation rests on the assumption that the synthetic tasks capture the essential challenges of goal-oriented dialog.

The evidence presented is insufficient for several reasons:

1. The synthetic tasks are highly constrained and simplified compared to real-world dialog. For instance, in Task 3, "users have a 25% chance of accepting" options, which is an artificial probability not found in real conversations. The paper acknowledges the Concierge dataset is "much more variable" than their synthetic tasks, yet they don't quantify how much this variability impacts the correlation between synthetic and real performance.

2. The authors show that Memory Networks with match type features outperform other methods on their tasks, but they don't demonstrate that this translates to actual utility in real applications. The per-dialog accuracy remains "close to 0" on the most important tasks (T3 and T4), suggesting the models still fail to achieve the core goal of completing restaurant bookings. The paper doesn't provide evidence that the models would actually be useful in real-world deployment.

3. The comparison with real data (DSTC2 and Concierge) is limited and not rigorously analyzed. The DSTC2 dataset was converted to the task format, but the original dataset was designed for dialog state tracking, not end-to-end learning. The Concierge dataset is described as "much more variable" with "no structured facts about restaurants," yet the authors don't provide a statistical analysis of how this variability affects the correlation between synthetic and real performance.

4. The paper's main claim—that their synthetic tasks can serve as a "good proxy" for real-world performance—relies on a correlation of relative model performance, not absolute performance. This is insufficient evidence for a claim that has significant practical implications. The authors acknowledge that "good performance would carry over from such synthetic to more realistic conditions," but provide no evidence that this is true.

The paper demonstrates a standard benchmarking approach, but the authors make a bold claim about the utility of their synthetic data that isn't adequately supported. This is particularly problematic given the reviewer's skepticism toward new approaches without extensive validation. The authors acknowledge that their end-to-end models still have significant limitations ("the per-dialog accuracy is still close to 0"), yet they claim their benchmark can help "foster progress" without demonstrating how their benchmark will actually help overcome these limitations.

## Additional Feedback
1. The paper would be significantly strengthened by providing a statistical analysis of how the variability in real-world data (as seen in the Concierge dataset) affects the correlation between synthetic and real performance. A simple linear regression showing the relationship between synthetic task performance and real-world performance would be more convincing than just showing similar relative rankings.

2. The authors should explicitly acknowledge the limitations of their synthetic tasks in capturing real-world dialog complexity. For example, they should quantify how much the artificial probability in Task 3 (25% acceptance rate) differs from real-world acceptance rates.

3. The paper should provide more concrete evidence about how this benchmark would help improve real end-to-end dialog systems. For instance, what specific improvements would the authors expect to see in the model architectures or training procedures that would be guided by this benchmark?

4. The paper claims their approach is "lightweight and easy to use," but the authors don't provide evidence that their benchmark is actually adopted by the community. They should include a plan for how they will encourage adoption and use the benchmark to drive progress.

## Questions for Authors
1. Can you provide a quantitative analysis showing that the correlation between synthetic task performance and real-world performance is strong enough to be useful for model development? What statistical significance tests would you perform to support this claim?

2. Given that the per-dialog accuracy remains "close to 0" on the most important tasks, how do you believe this benchmark will help overcome the fundamental limitations of current end-to-end dialog systems in goal-oriented settings?

3. How would you modify your benchmark to better capture the variability and complexity observed in the Concierge dataset, and what evidence do you have that these modifications would improve the correlation with real-world performance?
