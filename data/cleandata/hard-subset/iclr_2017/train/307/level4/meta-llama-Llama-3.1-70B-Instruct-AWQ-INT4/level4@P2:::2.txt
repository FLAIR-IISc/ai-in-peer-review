This paper represents a serious reality check on the capabilities of current chatbots in goal-oriented dialogs, making it highly significant for both Dialog/Natural Language and Machine Learning venues. The authors tackle the challenge of evaluating end-to-end dialog systems in a goal-oriented setting, proposing a testbed to break down the strengths and shortcomings of these systems. They introduce an open dataset and task set for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog learning methods in a systematic and controlled way.

The paper's clarity is excellent, making it accessible to a readership beyond ML and dialog researchers. The experimental protocol is also well-designed, and the use of both synthetic and natural data is a strong aspect of the paper. The authors' effort to match the synthetic and natural conditions is notable, and their results suggest that relative performance on synthetic data can be a good predictor of performance on natural data.

The paper's original algorithmic contribution, the addition of match type features to memory networks, is simple yet effective. The authors demonstrate the benefits of this approach in various tasks, including goal-oriented dialogs. The paper also provides a thorough evaluation of different models, including rule-based systems, classical information retrieval models, supervised embedding models, and memory networks.

One of the paper's strengths is its ability to identify the limitations of current chatbots in goal-oriented dialogs. The authors show that while end-to-end ML approaches can perform well in certain tasks, they still struggle with more complex tasks that require interpreting knowledge about entities. The paper's results highlight the need for more realistic validation of ML applied to dialogs.

However, the paper also raises some questions about the metrics used to evaluate dialog systems. The authors use per-response accuracy and per-dialog accuracy, but these metrics have their limitations. For example, per-response accuracy can be overly pessimistic, as shown in Table 3, where a correct API call and a reasonably short dialog would only result in a 1/7 accuracy. Per-dialog accuracy is also sensitive to the experimental protocol, as shown in Table 2.

Furthermore, the paper does not address the issue of representation learning when the vocabulary used by the user does not match exactly the vocabulary in the knowledge base. The match type algorithm requires exact word matches, which may not always be possible in real-world scenarios. The authors could have discussed this limitation and potential ways to address it, such as learning associations between words and entities.

Overall, this paper is a valuable contribution to the field of dialog systems, highlighting the challenges and limitations of current chatbots in goal-oriented dialogs. While it raises some questions about evaluation metrics and representation learning, it provides a solid foundation for future research in this area.

Decision: Accept

Reasons:

* The paper tackles a significant challenge in the field of dialog systems, providing a thorough evaluation of end-to-end dialog systems in a goal-oriented setting.
* The experimental protocol is well-designed, and the use of both synthetic and natural data is a strong aspect of the paper.
* The paper's clarity is excellent, making it accessible to a readership beyond ML and dialog researchers.

Additional feedback:

* The authors could have discussed the limitations of the match type algorithm and potential ways to address the issue of representation learning when the vocabulary used by the user does not match exactly the vocabulary in the knowledge base.
* The paper could have benefited from a more detailed discussion of the implications of the results, particularly in terms of the limitations of current chatbots in goal-oriented dialogs.
* The authors may want to consider exploring alternative evaluation metrics that take into account the complexity of goal-oriented dialogs, such as the weighted sum of errors and turns proposed in the review.
