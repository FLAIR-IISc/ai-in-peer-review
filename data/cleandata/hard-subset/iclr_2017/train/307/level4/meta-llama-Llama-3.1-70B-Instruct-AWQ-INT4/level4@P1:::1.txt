This paper presents a new, public dataset and tasks for goal-oriented dialogue applications. The dataset and tasks are constructed artificially using rule-based programs, allowing for the evaluation of different aspects of dialogue system performance, ranging from issuing API calls to displaying options, as well as full-fledged dialogue. This is a welcome contribution to the dialogue literature, which will help facilitate future research into developing and understanding dialogue systems.

I am inclined to accept this paper, with two key reasons for this choice. Firstly, the paper introduces a well-structured and systematic approach to evaluating goal-oriented dialogue systems, which is a significant contribution to the field. Secondly, the authors provide a thorough analysis of the strengths and weaknesses of different models on the proposed tasks, including Memory Networks, Supervised Embeddings, and classical Information Retrieval models.

However, I also have some concerns regarding the suitability of Deep Learning models for these tasks compared to traditional methods. The tasks 1-5 are completely deterministic, which means evaluating performance on these tasks won't measure the ability of the models to handle noisy and ambiguous interactions, a crucial aspect in dialogue applications. Furthermore, the paper lacks a baseline model with word order information, which makes the neural networks appear unreasonably strong. I strongly recommend that the authors experiment with an additional non-neural network benchmark model that takes into account word order information, such as a logistic regression model with word embeddings, bi-gram features, and match-type features.

To improve the paper, I suggest the authors address the following points:

* Provide a more detailed comparison of the proposed tasks with existing dialogue datasets, highlighting the unique aspects of the proposed tasks.
* Include a discussion on the limitations of the deterministic nature of tasks 1-5 and how they can be extended to handle noisy and ambiguous interactions.
* Experiment with additional baseline models, including non-neural network models with word order information, to provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed models.
* Clarify the statement in the conclusion regarding the lack of well-defined performance measures for end-to-end trainable models for task-oriented dialogue, as this is not entirely accurate.

Questions for the authors:

* Can you provide more details on how the tasks were designed to evaluate different aspects of dialogue system performance?
* How do you plan to extend the tasks to handle noisy and ambiguous interactions?
* Can you discuss the potential applications of the proposed tasks and models in real-world dialogue systems?

Overall, this paper presents a significant contribution to the dialogue literature, and with some revisions, it has the potential to become a valuable resource for researchers in the field.
