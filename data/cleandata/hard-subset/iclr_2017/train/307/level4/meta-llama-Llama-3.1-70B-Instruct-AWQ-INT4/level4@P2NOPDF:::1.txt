This paper presents a novel, publicly available dataset and tasks for goal-oriented dialogue applications, constructed artificially using rule-based programs. The dataset and tasks are designed to evaluate various aspects of dialogue system performance, ranging from issuing API calls to displaying options and full-fledged dialogue. This contribution is a welcome addition to the dialogue literature, facilitating future research into developing and understanding dialogue systems.

However, there are potential drawbacks to this approach. The suitability of deep learning models for these tasks, compared to traditional methods (rule-based systems or shallow models), is unclear. Deep learning models typically require numerous training examples, so performance differences between neural networks may simply be attributed to regularization techniques. Furthermore, tasks 1-5 are completely deterministic, meaning that evaluating performance on these tasks does not measure the ability of models to handle noisy and ambiguous interactions, such as inferring a distribution over user goals or executing dialogue repair strategies. These aspects are crucial in dialogue applications.

Despite these concerns, I believe this is an interesting direction to explore.

One notable weakness of the paper is the absence of a baseline model incorporating word order information. I think this omission makes the neural networks appear unreasonably strong, as simpler baselines could potentially be competitive or even outperform the proposed neural networks. To ensure a fair evaluation and accurately assess the power of representation learning for this task, I recommend that the authors experiment with an additional non-neural network benchmark model that takes into account word order information. For instance, they could experiment with a logistic regression model that uses word embeddings, bi-gram features, and match-type features as inputs. Including such a baseline would more convincingly demonstrate the utility of deep learning models for this task. If this is done, I would increase my rating to 8.

Finally, I would like to point out a minor inaccuracy in the conclusion. The statement "the existing work has no well-defined measures of performances" is not entirely accurate. End-to-end trainable models for task-oriented dialogue do have well-defined performance measures, as seen in "A Network-based End-to-End Trainable Task-oriented Dialogue System" by Wen et al. While non-goal-oriented dialogue systems can be more challenging to evaluate, they can be assessed using human subjects, as demonstrated by Liu et al. (2016) for Twitter and "Strategy and Policy Learning for Non-Task-Oriented Conversational Systems" by Yu et al.
