This paper presents a new, public dataset and tasks for goal-oriented dialogue applications. The dataset and tasks are constructed artificially using rule-based programs, in such a way that different aspects of dialogue system performance can be evaluated, ranging from issuing API calls to displaying options, as well as full-fledged dialogue. This is a welcome contribution to the dialogue literature, which will help facilitate future research into developing and understanding dialogue systems.

I decide to accept this paper with one key reason being the introduction of a new, public dataset and tasks for goal-oriented dialogue applications, which will facilitate future research in this area. However, I also have some concerns regarding the suitability of Deep Learning models for these tasks and the lack of a baseline model with word order information.

The approach of constructing the dataset and tasks artificially using rule-based programs is a double-edged sword. On the one hand, it allows for a systematic evaluation of different aspects of dialogue system performance. On the other hand, it may not accurately reflect real-world dialogue scenarios, which can be noisy and ambiguous. The tasks 1-5 are also completely deterministic, which means evaluating performance on these tasks won't measure the ability of the models to handle noisy and ambiguous interactions.

The paper also lacks a baseline model with word order information. I think this is a strong weakness of the paper, because it makes the neural networks appear unreasonably strong, yet simpler baselines could very likely be competitive (or better) than the proposed neural networks. To maintain a fair evaluation and correctly assess the power of representation learning for this task, I think it's essential that the authors experiment with one additional non-neural network benchmark model which takes into account word order information.

Some minor comments: in the conclusion, the paper states "the existing work has no well-defined measures of performances". This is not really true. End-to-end trainable models for task-oriented dialogue have well-defined performance measures. See, for example "A Network-based End-to-End Trainable Task-oriented Dialogue System" by Wen et al. On the other hand, non-goal-oriented dialogue is generally harder to evaluate, but given human subjects these can also be evaluated.

To improve the paper, I would like the authors to address the following points:

1. Include a baseline model with word order information to maintain a fair evaluation and correctly assess the power of representation learning for this task.
2. Discuss the limitations of the artificially constructed dataset and tasks, and how they may not accurately reflect real-world dialogue scenarios.
3. Clarify the statement in the conclusion regarding the existing work on well-defined measures of performances.

Questions to the authors:

1. How do you plan to address the limitations of the artificially constructed dataset and tasks, and make them more representative of real-world dialogue scenarios?
2. Can you provide more details on the baseline model with word order information that you plan to include in the future work?
3. How do you think the results of the paper would change if the tasks 1-5 were not completely deterministic?
