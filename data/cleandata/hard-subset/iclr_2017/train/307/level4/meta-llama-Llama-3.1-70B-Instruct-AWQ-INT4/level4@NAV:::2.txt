This paper serves as a crucial reality check on the trend of utilizing chatbots for various human-computer interactions, which gained significant attention in 2016. It presents a timely and significant contribution, particularly relevant to both Dialog/Natural Language and Machine Learning venues, as it highlights the limitations of current chatbots and the need for more realistic validation of machine learning applied to dialogs.

Two underlying conjectures in this paper, as mentioned by Antoine Bordes during a NIPS workshop presentation, are likely to spark further research. These conjectures, supported by the chosen metrics, suggest that:

1) The performance of end-to-end machine learning approaches is still insufficient for goal-oriented dialogs.
2) Relative performance on synthetic data can be a reliable predictor of performance on natural data, provided that the conditions are carefully matched.

The paper's original algorithmic contribution, a simple yet effective addition to memory networks (match type), is notable for being the first to be deployed and tested on a goal-oriented dialog. The experimental protocol is well-designed, and the overall paper clarity is excellent, making it accessible to a broad readership.

The authors' concise summary of memory networks in the appendix, accompanied by informative tables explaining the influence of the number of hops, is particularly impressive.

While this paper represents the state-of-the-art in exploring more rigorous metrics for dialog modeling, it also underscores the brittleness and arbitrariness of these metrics. This observation is more a recommendation for future research than a suggestion for revision.

The use of per-response accuracy as a metric is problematic, as it can lead to misleading results. For instance, table 3 demonstrates that a correct API call and a reasonably short dialog are often the primary concerns, even if this would only yield a 1/7 accuracy. The per-dialog accuracy, where all responses must be correct, may be a better alternative, but table 2 shows its sensitivity to the experimental protocol.

The authors' choice of metrics, while standard in the field, may not be the most effective. A more meaningful approach might be to represent dialogs in a way that maximizes revenue, such as a weighted sum of errors in the API call, the number of turns to reach that API call, and the number of rejected options by the user. However, this would require a real human user or a realistic simulator, rather than canned dialogs.

Another issue that this paper does not adequately address is the problem of vocabulary mismatch between the user and the knowledge base. The match type algorithm, for instance, relies on exact vocabulary matching, which may not always be the case in real-world scenarios. This limitation highlights the need for more robust representation learning methods that can learn associations between words and concepts, rather than relying on manual annotation.
