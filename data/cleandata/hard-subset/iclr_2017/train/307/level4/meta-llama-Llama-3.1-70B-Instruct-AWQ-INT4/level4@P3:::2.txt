This paper presents a serious reality check on the capabilities of current chatbots in handling goal-oriented dialogs. The authors propose a testbed to evaluate end-to-end dialog systems in a goal-oriented setting, specifically in the context of restaurant reservation. The testbed consists of five tasks that require manipulating sentences and symbols to conduct conversations, issue API calls, and use the outputs of such calls. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach by comparing an end-to-end dialog system based on Memory Networks to a hand-crafted slot-filling baseline on data from the second Dialog State Tracking Challenge.

The paper's significance lies in its ability to educate software engineers and researchers about the limitations of current chatbots and the need for more realistic validation of machine learning applied to dialogs. The authors' experimental protocol is excellent, and the overall paper clarity is excellent and accessible to a readership beyond machine learning and dialog researchers.

The paper's original algorithmic contribution is the addition of match type features to Memory Networks, which is tested on a goal-oriented dialog. The results show that Memory Networks with match type features outperform other baselines, including classical information retrieval methods and supervised embedding models. However, the authors also highlight the limitations of their approach, including the brittleness and arbitrariness of the metrics used to evaluate dialog modeling.

One of the key takeaways from the paper is that the performance of end-to-end machine learning approaches is still insufficient for goal-oriented dialogs. The authors also suggest that relative performance on synthetic data is a good predictor of performance on natural data, which is a departure from previous observations.

The paper raises several important questions and issues that are relevant to the field of dialog modeling. For example, the authors note that the per-response accuracy metric can be absurd in practice, as it requires exact matches for all responses, including those that are not critical to the success of the dialog. The authors also suggest that the per-dialog accuracy metric is sensitive to the experimental protocol and may not always reflect the true performance of the dialog system.

Furthermore, the paper highlights the importance of considering the user's perspective in evaluating dialog systems. The authors suggest that a more meaningful metric for goal-oriented dialogs might be one that maximizes revenue, such as a weighted sum of the number of errors in the API call, the number of turns to reach that API call, and the number of rejected options by the user.

However, the paper also raises some questions and issues that are not fully addressed. For example, the authors do not fully explain how their approach handles situations where the vocabulary used by the user does not match exactly the vocabulary in the knowledge base. This is an important issue, as it can affect the performance of the dialog system and the accuracy of the match type features.

Overall, I would recommend accepting this paper, as it presents a significant contribution to the field of dialog modeling and raises important questions and issues that are relevant to the field. The paper's clarity, accessibility, and experimental protocol are all strengths, and the authors' willingness to acknowledge the limitations of their approach is a positive aspect of the paper.
