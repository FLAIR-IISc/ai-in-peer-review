This paper presents a new, publicly available dataset and tasks for goal-oriented dialogue applications. The dataset and tasks are artificially constructed using rule-based programs, allowing for the evaluation of various aspects of dialogue system performance, ranging from issuing API calls to displaying options, as well as full-fledged dialogue. This contribution to the dialogue literature is welcome, as it will facilitate future research into developing and understanding dialogue systems.

However, there are potential pitfalls to this approach. Firstly, it is unclear how suitable Deep Learning models are for these tasks compared to traditional methods (such as rule-based systems or shallow models), as Deep Learning models are known to require a large number of training examples. Consequently, performance differences between different neural networks may simply be due to regularization techniques. Furthermore, tasks 1-5 are completely deterministic, which means that evaluating performance on these tasks does not assess the ability of models to handle noisy and ambiguous interactions (e.g., inferring a distribution over user goals or executing dialogue repair strategies). This is a crucial aspect of dialogue applications. Overall, I still believe that this is an interesting direction to explore.

As discussed in the comments below, the paper lacks a baseline model that incorporates word order information. I consider this a significant weakness, as it makes the neural networks appear unreasonably strong, while simpler baselines could potentially be competitive or even outperform the proposed neural networks. To ensure a fair evaluation and accurately assess the power of representation learning for this task, I think it is essential that the authors experiment with an additional non-neural network benchmark model that takes into account word order information. This would more convincingly demonstrate the utility of Deep Learning models for this task. For example, one could experiment with a logistic regression model that takes as input 1) word embeddings (similar to the Supervised Embeddings model), 2) bi-gram features, and 3) match-type features. If such a baseline is included, I will increase my rating to 8.

As a minor comment, the conclusion states that "the existing work has no well-defined measures of performances." This is not entirely accurate. End-to-end trainable models for task-oriented dialogue do have well-defined performance measures. For instance, see "A Network-based End-to-End Trainable Task-oriented Dialogue System" by Wen et al. While non-goal-oriented dialogue systems are generally harder to evaluate, they can also be evaluated using human subjects, as demonstrated by Liu et al. (2016) for Twitter, and "Strategy and Policy Learning for Non-Task-Oriented Conversational Systems" by Yu et al.
