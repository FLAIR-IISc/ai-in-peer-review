This paper presents a significant reality check on the current state of chatbots and their ability to handle goal-oriented dialogs. The authors propose a testbed to evaluate end-to-end dialog systems in a goal-oriented setting, specifically in the context of restaurant reservation. The testbed consists of five tasks that require manipulating sentences and symbols to conduct conversations, issue API calls, and use the outputs of such calls. The authors evaluate several models, including rule-based systems, classical information retrieval methods, supervised embeddings, and end-to-end Memory Networks, on these tasks.

The paper's significance lies in its ability to educate software engineers about the limitations of current chatbots and researchers about the need for more realistic validation of machine learning applied to dialogs. The authors' experimental protocol is excellent, and the overall paper clarity is excellent and accessible to a readership beyond machine learning and dialog researchers.

The paper's original algorithmic contribution consists of a simple addition to memory networks, which is the first time these are deployed and tested on a goal-oriented dialog. The authors' use of the per-response accuracy and per-dialog accuracy metrics is standard, but they also highlight the limitations of these metrics. For instance, the per-response accuracy can be misleading, as it only considers the correct API call and ignores the rest of the dialog. The per-dialog accuracy is more sensitive to the experimental protocol and can be affected by the task definitions.

The authors' results show that while end-to-end Memory Networks outperform classical IR and supervised embeddings across all tasks, they still lack in some key areas, such as interpreting knowledge about entities from returned API calls to present results to the user. The authors also evaluate their models on a real-world dataset extracted from an online concierge service, which confirms the pattern of relative performances of methods.

One potential issue with the paper is its reliance on standard dialog metrics, which may not be the most meaningful way to evaluate goal-oriented dialogs. The authors could consider alternative metrics, such as a weighted sum of the number of errors in the API call, the number of turns to reach that API call, and the number of rejected options by the user. However, such a metric would require a real human user or a realistic simulator.

Another issue closely related to representation learning that this paper fails to address is what happens if the vocabulary used by the user does not match exactly the vocabulary in the knowledge base. The authors' match type algorithm requires the word to occur exactly in the KB, which may not always be the case. The authors could consider alternative approaches that can learn associations between words and KB entities, rather than relying on exact matches.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the authors' experimental protocol is excellent. The paper's significance lies in its ability to educate software engineers and researchers about the limitations of current chatbots and the need for more realistic validation of machine learning applied to dialogs. However, the authors could consider alternative metrics and approaches to address the limitations of their current evaluation methodology.

Additional feedback:

* The authors could consider alternative metrics, such as a weighted sum of the number of errors in the API call, the number of turns to reach that API call, and the number of rejected options by the user.
* The authors could consider alternative approaches that can learn associations between words and KB entities, rather than relying on exact matches.
* The authors could provide more details on the match type algorithm and how it handles out-of-vocabulary words.

Questions to the authors:

* How do the authors plan to address the limitations of their current evaluation methodology, such as the reliance on standard dialog metrics?
* How do the authors plan to extend their work to handle more complex goal-oriented dialogs, such as those that require multiple API calls or more nuanced user interactions?
* How do the authors plan to evaluate their models on more realistic data, such as data from real human users or more realistic simulators?
