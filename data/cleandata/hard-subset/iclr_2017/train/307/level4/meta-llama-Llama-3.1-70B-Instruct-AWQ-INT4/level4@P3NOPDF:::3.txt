**Synopsis**

This paper introduces a new dataset for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems, specifically designed for the restaurant setting. The primary objective is to find availability and eventually book a table based on parameters provided by the user to the bot as part of a dialog. The dataset is generated using a simulation that leverages an underlying knowledge base to produce samples for various parameters, such as cuisine and price range, and applies rule-based transformations to create natural language descriptions. The evaluation metric focuses on ranking a set of candidate responses for each next turn of the dialog, with results reported in terms of per-response accuracy and per-dialog accuracy. The authors demonstrate that Memory Networks can improve upon basic bag-of-words baselines.

**Thoughts**

I appreciate the authors' contribution to the field, and I acknowledge the effort invested in creating this new dataset. However, I have reservations about the utility of end-to-end trained systems in narrow-domain settings. In open-domain environments, the argument for end-to-end trained methods is compelling, as hand-coding all states and responses would be impractical. Nevertheless, in narrow-domain settings, where the domain is well-understood, and the primary objective is to achieve high user satisfaction, it seems more sensible to utilize domain knowledge to engineer an optimal system.

Given the restricted domain, I am somewhat disappointed that the primary goal is to rank responses rather than generate them. Although I understand that this approach simplifies evaluation, I am uncertain about how these candidate responses would be obtained in practice. The paper suggests that the models rank the set of all responses in the train, validation, and test sets. This raises concerns about the scalability of the end-to-end training approach, as a key advantage is its ability to adapt to new domains without manual re-engineering. In a real-world scenario, where is this information obtained for a new domain? Generating responses would enable better generalization to new domains, rather than merely ranking a list of generic, hand-collected responses. In my opinion, this is the most significant limitation of this work.

Furthermore, as the data is generated using a simulation that expands tuples (e.g., cuisine, price) using NL-generation rules, it inherently restricts the variability in the training responses. While this allows for the generation of unlimited data, I was unable to find the list of rules used in the simulation. Publishing this information would be beneficial for future research.

Despite my reservations, I believe this paper is an interesting contribution worthy of publication at the conference. The updated score reflects the clarifications and new results provided by the authors.
