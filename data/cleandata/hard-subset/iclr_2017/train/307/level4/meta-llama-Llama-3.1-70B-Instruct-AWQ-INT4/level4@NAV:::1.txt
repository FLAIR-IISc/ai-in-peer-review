This paper, recently posted on arXiv, introduces a novel public dataset and tasks tailored for goal-oriented dialogue applications. The dataset and tasks are artificially constructed using rule-based programs, allowing for a comprehensive evaluation of dialogue system performance across various aspects, including API calls, option display, and full-fledged dialogue interactions.

This contribution is a valuable addition to the dialogue literature, facilitating future research into the development and understanding of dialogue systems. However, this approach also has its limitations. Firstly, the suitability of Deep Learning models for these tasks, compared to traditional methods such as rule-based systems or shallow models, is unclear, particularly given the known requirement of Deep Learning models for extensive training data. Furthermore, the deterministic nature of tasks 1-5 means that model performance evaluations will not capture their ability to handle noisy and ambiguous interactions, a crucial aspect of dialogue applications.

Overall, this direction is still considered interesting to explore.

As noted in the comments below, the paper lacks a baseline model incorporating word order information. This omission is seen as a significant weakness, as it may make neural networks appear disproportionately strong, while simpler baselines could potentially be competitive or even outperform the proposed neural networks. To ensure a fair evaluation and accurately assess the power of representation learning for this task, it is recommended that the authors experiment with an additional non-neural network benchmark model that considers word order information. For instance, a logistic regression model incorporating word embeddings, bi-gram features, and match-type features could be explored. The inclusion of such a baseline would provide more convincing evidence of the utility of Deep Learning models for this task. If this is addressed, the rating would be increased to 8.

A minor comment is that the paper's conclusion stating "the existing work has no well-defined measures of performances" is not entirely accurate. End-to-end trainable models for task-oriented dialogue do have well-defined performance measures, as seen in Wen et al.'s "A Network-based End-to-End Trainable Task-oriented Dialogue System". While non-goal-oriented dialogue evaluations can be more challenging, human subjects can be employed for assessment, as demonstrated by Liu et al. (2016) for Twitter, and Yu et al.'s "Strategy and Policy Learning for Non-Task-Oriented Conversational Systems".
