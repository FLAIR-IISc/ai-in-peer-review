This paper presents a new, publicly available dataset and tasks for goal-oriented dialogue applications, constructed artificially using rule-based programs. The dataset and tasks are designed to evaluate various aspects of dialogue system performance, ranging from issuing API calls to displaying options, as well as full-fledged dialogue. This contribution is welcome in the dialogue literature, as it will facilitate future research in developing and understanding dialogue systems.

However, there are potential drawbacks to this approach. Firstly, it is unclear how suitable deep learning models are for these tasks compared to traditional methods, such as rule-based systems or shallow models. Deep learning models are known to require large amounts of training data, and performance differences between neural networks may be attributed to regularization techniques rather than the models themselves. Furthermore, tasks 1-5 are completely deterministic, which means that evaluating performance on these tasks will not assess the models' ability to handle noisy and ambiguous interactions, such as inferring a distribution over user goals or executing dialogue repair strategies. This is a crucial aspect of dialogue applications that is not adequately captured by the proposed tasks.

Despite these limitations, I believe that this is an interesting direction to explore. However, I have several concerns regarding the paper's methodology and conclusions. As discussed in the comments below, the paper lacks a baseline model that incorporates word order information. I consider this a significant weakness, as it makes the neural networks appear unreasonably strong. Simpler baselines that take into account word order information could potentially be competitive with or even outperform the proposed neural networks. To ensure a fair evaluation and accurately assess the power of representation learning for this task, I strongly recommend that the authors experiment with an additional non-neural network benchmark model that incorporates word order information. For example, a logistic regression model that uses word embeddings, bi-gram features, and match-type features as input could provide a more comprehensive comparison. If such a baseline is included, I would be willing to increase my rating to 8.

Finally, I would like to point out that the paper's conclusion stating that "existing work has no well-defined measures of performance" is not entirely accurate. End-to-end trainable models for task-oriented dialogue do have well-defined performance measures, as demonstrated in "A Network-based End-to-End Trainable Task-oriented Dialogue System" by Wen et al. While non-goal-oriented dialogue systems are indeed more challenging to evaluate, human subjects can be used to assess their performance, as shown in Liu et al. (2016) and "Strategy and Policy Learning for Non-Task-Oriented Conversational Systems" by Yu et al.
