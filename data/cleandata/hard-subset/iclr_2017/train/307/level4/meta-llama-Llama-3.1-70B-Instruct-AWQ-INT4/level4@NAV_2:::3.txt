This paper proposes a testbed to evaluate end-to-end dialog systems in goal-oriented applications, specifically in the context of restaurant reservation. The authors aim to break down the strengths and shortcomings of these systems by designing a set of tasks that test various abilities, such as dialog management, querying knowledge bases, and interpreting output. The paper presents a comprehensive evaluation of several learning methods, including rule-based systems, classical information retrieval models, supervised embeddings, and end-to-end Memory Networks.

The paper claims to contribute to the field by providing a systematic and controlled evaluation of end-to-end goal-oriented dialog learning methods. The authors argue that existing measures of performance either prevent reproducibility or do not correlate well with human judgments. They also highlight the utility of goal-oriented dialog in real applications.

I accept this paper, with two key reasons for this choice. Firstly, the paper provides a well-motivated and well-placed contribution to the literature, addressing a significant gap in the evaluation of end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. Secondly, the paper presents a thorough evaluation of several learning methods, providing a comprehensive understanding of the strengths and weaknesses of each approach.

My supporting arguments for the reasons for this decision are as follows. The paper's contribution is well-motivated, as it addresses a significant gap in the evaluation of end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. The authors provide a clear and compelling argument for the need for a systematic and controlled evaluation of these systems. The paper is also well-placed in the literature, building on existing work in the field and providing a comprehensive evaluation of several learning methods.

The paper's evaluation of several learning methods is thorough and comprehensive, providing a detailed understanding of the strengths and weaknesses of each approach. The authors present a clear and concise discussion of the results, highlighting the key findings and implications for future research.

However, I do have some reservations about the paper's focus on ranking responses rather than generating them. While I understand that this makes evaluation easier, I am unsure how these candidate responses would actually be obtained in practice. I also have some concerns about the variability in the training responses, which are generated using a simulation by expanding (cuisine, price, ...) tuples using NL-generation rules.

To improve the paper, I would suggest that the authors provide more information about the rules used to generate the training responses. I would also like to see a more detailed discussion of how the candidate responses would be obtained in practice, and how the system would generalize to new domains.

Some questions I would like the authors to answer are: How do you plan to obtain the candidate responses in practice, and how would the system generalize to new domains? Can you provide more information about the rules used to generate the training responses? How do you plan to address the variability in the training responses, and what are the implications for the evaluation of the system?
