This paper presents a new, publicly available dataset and tasks for goal-oriented dialogue applications. The dataset and tasks are artificially constructed using rule-based programs, allowing for the evaluation of different aspects of dialogue system performance, ranging from issuing API calls to displaying options, as well as full-fledged dialogue. This contribution is a welcome addition to the dialogue literature, as it will facilitate future research into developing and understanding dialogue systems.

However, there are potential pitfalls in this approach. Firstly, it is unclear how suitable deep learning models are for these tasks compared to traditional methods, such as rule-based systems or shallow models. Deep learning models are known to require a large number of training examples, and performance differences between different neural networks may simply be attributed to regularization techniques. Furthermore, tasks 1-5 are completely deterministic, which means that evaluating performance on these tasks will not measure the ability of the models to handle noisy and ambiguous interactions. This is a crucial aspect in dialogue applications, such as inferring a distribution over user goals or executing dialogue repair strategies.

Overall, I still believe this is an interesting direction to explore. Nevertheless, I have some concerns regarding the evaluation methodology. As discussed in the comments below, the paper lacks a baseline model that takes into account word order information. I consider this a significant weakness, as it makes the neural networks appear unreasonably strong. Simpler baselines could likely be competitive with, or even outperform, the proposed neural networks. To ensure a fair evaluation and accurately assess the power of representation learning for this task, I strongly recommend that the authors experiment with an additional non-neural network benchmark model that incorporates word order information. For instance, they could experiment with a logistic regression model that takes as input word embeddings, bi-gram features, and match-type features. If such a baseline is included, I would be willing to increase my rating to 8.

As a minor comment, I would like to correct a statement made in the conclusion. The paper claims that "the existing work has no well-defined measures of performance." However, this is not entirely accurate. End-to-end trainable models for task-oriented dialogue do have well-defined performance measures, as demonstrated in "A Network-based End-to-End Trainable Task-oriented Dialogue System" by Wen et al. While non-goal-oriented dialogue systems are generally harder to evaluate, they can still be evaluated using human subjects, as shown in the work of Liu et al. (2016) on Twitter, and "Strategy and Policy Learning for Non-Task-Oriented Conversational Systems" by Yu et al.

I have updated my score following the new results added to the paper.
