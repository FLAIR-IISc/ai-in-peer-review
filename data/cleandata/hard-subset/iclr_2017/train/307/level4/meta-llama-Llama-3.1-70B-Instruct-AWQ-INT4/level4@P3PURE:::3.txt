SYNOPSIS:
This paper introduces a new dataset for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems, specifically in the restaurant setting. The goal of these systems is to find availability and eventually book a table based on parameters provided by the user to the bot as part of a dialog. The data is generated by running a simulation using an underlying knowledge base to generate samples for various parameters (such as cuisine and price range), followed by the application of rule-based transformations to render natural language descriptions. The objective is to rank a set of candidate responses for each next turn of the dialog, with evaluation reported in terms of per-response accuracy and per-dialog accuracy. The authors demonstrate that Memory Networks can improve upon basic bag-of-words baselines.

THOUGHTS:
I would like to thank the authors for their interesting contribution. However, I remain skeptical about the utility of end-to-end trained systems in narrow-domain settings. In open-domain settings, there is a strong argument to be made that hand-coding all states and responses is not scalable, making end-to-end trained methods a more sensible approach. In contrast, narrow-domain settings typically involve a well-understood domain, where the primary goal is to achieve high user satisfaction. In such cases, it seems more practical to leverage domain knowledge to engineer the most effective system possible.

Given the restricted domain, I am somewhat disappointed that the objective is to rank responses rather than generate them, although I understand that this simplifies evaluation. Furthermore, I am uncertain about how these candidate responses would be obtained in practice. It appears that the models rank the set of all responses in the train, validation, and test sets (as mentioned in the last sentence before Section 3.2). A key argument in favor of the end-to-end training approach is its ease of scaling to new domains without requiring manual re-engineering of the system. However, it is unclear where this information would be obtained for a new domain in practice. Generating responses would enable better generalization to new domains, as opposed to simply ranking a list of hand-collected generic responses. In my opinion, this is the weakest aspect of this work.

Additionally, the data generation process using a simulation by expanding tuples (such as cuisine and price) with NL-generation rules inherently constrains the variability in the training responses. Although this is a trade-off for the ability to generate unlimited data using the simulator, I was unable to find the list of rules used in the process. It would be beneficial to publish this information as well.

Overall, despite my reservations, I believe this is an interesting contribution worthy of publication at the conference.
