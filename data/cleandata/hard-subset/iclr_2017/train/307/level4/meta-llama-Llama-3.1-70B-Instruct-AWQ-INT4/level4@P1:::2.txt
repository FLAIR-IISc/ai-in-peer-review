This paper presents a much-needed reality check on the capabilities of end-to-end machine learning (ML) approaches for goal-oriented dialogs. While the trend of using chatbots for human-computer interaction has gained significant attention, this work highlights the limitations of current chatbots in handling more complex dialogs beyond simple chit-chat.

The paper is highly relevant for both Dialog/Natural Language and Machine Learning venues, as it educates software engineers about the limitations of current chatbots and researchers about the need for more realistic validation of ML applied to dialogs. The work is of high significance, and two underlying conjectures are likely to open up more research in this area.

The first conjecture is that the performance of end-to-end ML approaches is still insufficient for goal-oriented dialogs. This is evident from the results presented in the paper, which show that even the best-performing models struggle to achieve high accuracy in tasks that require more complex reasoning and decision-making.

The second conjecture is that when comparing algorithms, relative performance on synthetic data is a good predictor of performance on natural data. This is a departure from previous observations, but the authors have made a strong effort to match the synthetic and natural conditions, making this conjecture more plausible.

The paper's original algorithmic contribution is the addition of match type features to memory networks, which is a simple yet effective modification. This is the first time these features have been deployed and tested on a goal-oriented dialog, and the experimental protocol is excellent. The overall paper clarity is excellent, making it accessible to a readership beyond ML and dialog researchers.

One of the strengths of the paper is its use of a systematic and controlled evaluation protocol, which allows for a fair comparison of different models and algorithms. The authors have also provided a clear and concise summary of the memory networks architecture and its modifications, making it easy to follow for readers who may not be familiar with the details of the model.

However, there are some limitations and potential issues with the paper that are worth discussing. One of the main concerns is the choice of evaluation metrics. The authors use per-response accuracy and per-dialog accuracy, which may not be the most informative metrics for goal-oriented dialogs. For example, the per-response accuracy can be misleading, as it only considers the correctness of individual responses without taking into account the overall flow of the dialog.

The per-dialog accuracy is also sensitive to the experimental protocol, as shown in Table 2. The accuracy for subtask T3 is much lower than the accuracy for the full dialog T5, which may be due to the differences in the task definitions. Additionally, the concierge data evaluation metric only considers the top-5 responses, which may not be sufficient to capture the nuances of human language.

A potential solution to these issues is to use more informative metrics that take into account the overall flow of the dialog and the user's goals. For example, a metric that maximizes revenue for a virtual assistant service could be a weighted sum of the number of errors in the API call, the number of turns to reach that API call, and the number of rejected options by the user. However, such a metric would require a more realistic simulator or real human user interactions.

Another issue that the paper fails to address properly is the representation learning aspect, particularly when the vocabulary used by the user does not match exactly the vocabulary in the knowledge base. The match type algorithm requires the word 'Indian' to occur exactly in the KB to be coded as 'type of cuisine'. However, in real-world scenarios, the KB may use obfuscated terminology, and it would be desirable for ML to learn the associations rather than relying on human hand-annotation.

Overall, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of goal-oriented dialogs, highlighting the limitations of current chatbots and the need for more realistic validation of ML approaches. While there are some limitations and potential issues with the paper, it provides a solid foundation for future research in this area.
