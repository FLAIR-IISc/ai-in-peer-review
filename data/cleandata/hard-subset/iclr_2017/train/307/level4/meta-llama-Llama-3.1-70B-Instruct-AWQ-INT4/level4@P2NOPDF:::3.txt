This paper introduces a novel dataset for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems, specifically designed for the restaurant setting. The dataset aims to facilitate the development of systems that can find availability and book a table based on user-provided parameters. To generate the data, the authors employed a simulation using an underlying knowledge base to produce samples for various parameters, such as cuisine and price range. These samples were then transformed into natural language descriptions using rule-based methods.

The primary objective of this work is to rank a set of candidate responses for each turn in the dialog, with evaluation metrics focused on per-response accuracy and per-dialog accuracy. The authors demonstrate that Memory Networks can improve upon basic bag-of-words baselines.

I appreciate the authors' contribution to the field, but I have some reservations regarding the utility of end-to-end trained systems in narrow-domain settings. In open-domain environments, it is often impractical to hand-code all possible states and responses, making end-to-end trained methods a more viable option. However, in narrow-domain settings, where the domain is well-understood and the primary goal is to achieve high user satisfaction, it may be more effective to leverage domain knowledge to engineer a high-performing system.

Given the restricted domain, I was slightly disappointed to see that the primary goal is to rank responses rather than generate them. Although I understand that this approach simplifies evaluation, I am uncertain about how these candidate responses would be obtained in practice. The text suggests that the models rank a set of responses from the training, validation, and testing datasets. A key argument in favor of end-to-end training is its ability to scale to new domains without requiring manual re-engineering. However, it is unclear how this information would be obtained for a new domain in practice. Generating responses would allow for better generalization to new domains, rather than simply ranking a list of hand-collected generic responses.

Furthermore, the data generation process, which relies on a simulation that expands tuples using natural language generation rules, inherently limits the variability in training responses. Although this approach allows for the generation of unlimited data, it would be beneficial to publish the list of rules used in the simulation.

Despite my reservations, I believe that this work is an interesting contribution worthy of publication at the conference. I have updated my score following the clarifications and new results provided by the authors.
