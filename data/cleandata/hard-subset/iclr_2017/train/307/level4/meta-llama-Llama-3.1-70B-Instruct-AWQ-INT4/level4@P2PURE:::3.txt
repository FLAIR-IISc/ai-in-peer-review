SYNOPSIS:
This paper introduces a novel dataset for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems, specifically designed for the restaurant setting. The primary objective is to find table availability and eventually book a table based on user-provided parameters. The dataset is generated using a simulation that leverages an underlying knowledge base to produce samples for various parameters, such as cuisine and price range, which are then transformed into natural language descriptions using rule-based methods. The evaluation metric is based on ranking a set of candidate responses for each dialog turn, with performance reported in terms of per-response accuracy and per-dialog accuracy. The authors demonstrate that Memory Networks can improve upon basic bag-of-words baselines.

THOUGHTS:
I appreciate the authors' interesting contribution to the field. However, I have reservations about the utility of end-to-end trained systems in narrow-domain settings. In open-domain settings, it is often impractical to hand-code all states and responses, making end-to-end trained methods a more viable option. In contrast, narrow-domain settings typically involve well-understood domains, where the primary goal is to achieve high user satisfaction. In such cases, it seems more sensible to utilize domain knowledge to engineer the optimal system.

Given the restricted domain, I am somewhat disappointed that the focus is on ranking rather than generating responses, although I acknowledge that this simplifies evaluation. I am also uncertain about how candidate responses would be obtained in practice. It appears that the models rank the set of all responses in the train, validation, and test sets (as mentioned in the last sentence before Section 3.2). A key argument in favor of end-to-end training is its ability to scale to new domains without requiring manual re-engineering. However, it is unclear how this information would be obtained for a new domain in practice. Generating responses would enable better generalization to new domains, as opposed to simply ranking a list of hand-collected generic responses, which I believe is the weakest aspect of this work.

Furthermore, the use of a simulation to generate data by expanding tuples (e.g., cuisine, price) using NL-generation rules inherently limits the variability in training responses. Although this allows for unlimited data generation, I was unable to find the list of rules used in the simulation. It would be beneficial to make this information publicly available.

Despite my reservations, I believe this is an interesting contribution worthy of publication at the conference.
