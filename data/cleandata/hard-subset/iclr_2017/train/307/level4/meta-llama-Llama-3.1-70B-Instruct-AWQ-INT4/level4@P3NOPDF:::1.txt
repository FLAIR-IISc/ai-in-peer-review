This paper proposes a novel, publicly available dataset and tasks for goal-oriented dialogue applications. The dataset and tasks are artificially constructed using rule-based programs, allowing for the evaluation of various aspects of dialogue system performance, including API calls, displaying options, and full-fledged dialogue.

This contribution is a welcome addition to the dialogue literature, as it will facilitate future research into developing and understanding dialogue systems. However, there are potential pitfalls in this approach. Firstly, it is unclear how suitable Deep Learning models are for these tasks compared to traditional methods, such as rule-based systems or shallow models. Deep Learning models are known to require large amounts of training data, and performance differences between neural networks may be attributed to regularization techniques rather than the models themselves.

Furthermore, tasks 1-5 are completely deterministic, which means that evaluating performance on these tasks does not assess the models' ability to handle noisy and ambiguous interactions. This is a crucial aspect of dialogue applications, as models need to be able to infer a distribution over user goals and execute dialogue repair strategies.

Despite these limitations, I believe that this is an interesting direction to explore. However, I do have some concerns regarding the evaluation methodology. As discussed in the comments, the paper lacks a baseline model that takes into account word order information. I consider this a significant weakness, as it makes the neural networks appear unreasonably strong. Simpler baselines that incorporate word order information could potentially be competitive with, or even outperform, the proposed neural networks.

To maintain a fair evaluation and accurately assess the power of representation learning for this task, I strongly recommend that the authors experiment with an additional non-neural network benchmark model that incorporates word order information. For example, they could consider a logistic regression model that takes as input word embeddings, bi-gram features, and match-type features. Including such a baseline would more convincingly demonstrate the utility of Deep Learning models for this task.

On a minor note, the conclusion states that "the existing work has no well-defined measures of performance." This statement is not entirely accurate. End-to-end trainable models for task-oriented dialogue do have well-defined performance measures, as seen in "A Network-based End-to-End Trainable Task-oriented Dialogue System" by Wen et al. While non-goal-oriented dialogue systems can be more challenging to evaluate, they can still be assessed using human subjects, as demonstrated by Liu et al. (2016) and "Strategy and Policy Learning for Non-Task-Oriented Conversational Systems" by Yu et al.
