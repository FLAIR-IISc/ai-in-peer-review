**Revised Review**

**Synopsis:**
This paper presents a novel dataset for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems, specifically designed for the restaurant domain. The dataset is generated through simulation, leveraging an underlying knowledge base and rule-based transformations to create natural language descriptions. The primary objective is to rank candidate responses for each dialog turn, with evaluation metrics focusing on per-response and per-dialog accuracy. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of Memory Networks in improving upon basic bag-of-words baselines.

**Thoughts:**
I appreciate the authors' contribution to the field, and I acknowledge the value of their work. However, I have some reservations regarding the utility of end-to-end trained systems in narrow-domain settings. In such scenarios, where domain knowledge is well-established and the primary goal is to achieve high user satisfaction, it may be more effective to engineer the system using domain-specific knowledge rather than relying on end-to-end training methods. This approach is particularly relevant in narrow domains, where scalability is less of a concern.

Furthermore, I am slightly disappointed that the authors focus on ranking responses rather than generating them, although I understand the evaluation challenges associated with response generation. I am also uncertain about the practicality of obtaining candidate responses in real-world scenarios. The authors' approach relies on ranking a set of responses from the training, validation, and testing datasets. However, this raises questions about the scalability of their method to new domains, where such information may not be readily available. Response generation would likely offer better generalization capabilities to new domains, rather than simply ranking pre-collected generic responses. In my opinion, this is the most significant limitation of this work.

Additionally, the use of simulation to generate data, while allowing for unlimited data creation, inherently restricts the variability in training responses. Although the authors provide some insight into their methodology, I would have liked to see the specific rules used for NL-generation. Publishing these rules would be beneficial for future research.

Despite my reservations, I believe this paper makes an interesting contribution to the field and is worthy of publication at the conference.
