

This paper introduces a novel, publicly available dataset and associated tasks for goal-oriented dialogue applications, constructed via rule-based programs to evaluate diverse aspects of dialogue system performance—from issuing API calls and displaying options to full dialogue management. This contribution is valuable to the dialogue research community, enabling more standardized evaluation of dialogue systems. However, several critical limitations warrant attention. First, the paper does not adequately address whether deep learning models offer advantages over traditional methods (e.g., rule-based systems or shallow models), as deep learning typically requires extensive training data, and observed performance differences may merely reflect regularization techniques rather than inherent model superiority. Second, Tasks 1–5 are entirely deterministic, precluding evaluation of models’ ability to handle noisy, ambiguous interactions—such as inferring user goal distributions or executing dialogue repair strategies—which are essential in real-world applications. 

Crucially, the paper lacks a baseline model incorporating word order information, a significant weakness that overstates the capabilities of neural networks. Simpler baselines (e.g., logistic regression with word embeddings, bi-gram features, and match-type features) could likely outperform or match the proposed neural models, undermining the assessment of representation learning’s utility. To ensure a fair evaluation and robustly demonstrate deep learning’s value, the authors must include such a non-neural benchmark. If implemented, I would raise my rating to 8. 

Minor correction: The conclusion states "the existing work has no well-defined measures of performance," which is inaccurate. End-to-end trainable task-oriented dialogue systems (e.g., Wen et al., "A Network-based End-to-End Trainable Task-oriented Dialogue System") employ well-defined metrics. While non-goal-oriented dialogue evaluation is challenging, human-annotated frameworks (e.g., Liu et al., 2016; Yu et al., "Strategy and Policy Learning for Non-Task-Oriented Conversational Systems") provide valid evaluation approaches.
