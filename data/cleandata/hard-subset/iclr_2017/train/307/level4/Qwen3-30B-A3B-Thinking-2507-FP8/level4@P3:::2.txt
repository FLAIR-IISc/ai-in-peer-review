

# Review for ICLR 2017

## Summary
This paper makes a significant contribution by introducing a well-designed testbed to systematically evaluate end-to-end dialog systems in goal-oriented applications. The authors propose a set of five tasks within the restaurant reservation domain that require models to perform non-trivial operations such as issuing API calls, interpreting query results, and managing dialog states. The testbed is designed to promote reproducibility and comparison while providing error analysis by design. The authors demonstrate the utility of their framework by evaluating multiple models (including Memory Networks with match type features) against a rule-based baseline on both synthetic and real-world datasets (DSTC2 and an online concierge service). The paper is exceptionally clear, well-structured, and accessible to a broad audience beyond dialog researchers.

## Decision
Accept. The paper makes a strong contribution to the field by providing a rigorous evaluation framework for end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems, addressing a critical gap in the literature.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper successfully addresses three key questions:

1. **Specific question/problem**: The paper clearly identifies the problem of evaluating end-to-end dialog systems in goal-oriented settings, where current approaches have shown promise in chit-chat but may not generalize to more structured, goal-directed conversations. The authors demonstrate that traditional slot-filling systems remain competitive in narrow domains, while end-to-end systems still struggle with certain aspects of goal-oriented dialog.

2. **Well-motivated approach**: The paper is exceptionally well-motivated, placing itself within the literature by highlighting the limitations of existing datasets and evaluation methods. The authors provide a compelling case for why current metrics are insufficient for goal-oriented dialog evaluation and why a systematic testbed is needed. The design of the tasks (testing specific capabilities like API querying, result interpretation, and entity handling) is particularly well-justified.

3. **Support for claims**: The paper provides strong empirical evidence supporting its claims. The experimental protocol is rigorous, with careful attention to OOV generalization and comparison across synthetic and real-world data. The results consistently show that while end-to-end models can learn to perform certain tasks (issuing API calls), they still struggle with others (interpreting query results to display options), which aligns with the authors' central claim. The comparison between synthetic and real-world data (DSTC2 and concierge service) is particularly convincing, showing that relative performance patterns hold across domains.

## Additional Feedback
To further strengthen the paper, I suggest the following:

1. The paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of the limitations of the per-response accuracy metric, particularly as noted in the draft review. While this metric is standard, the paper could acknowledge its limitations in capturing the true utility of dialog systems in goal-oriented settings.

2. The authors might consider discussing how their framework could be extended to other domains beyond restaurant reservation, which would help emphasize the broader applicability of their testbed.

3. The discussion of match type features could be expanded to better explain how they work beyond just adding special tokens to the vocabulary.

## Questions for Authors
1. The paper notes that per-dialog accuracy is much lower for Task 3 (displaying options) than for Task 5 (full dialogs), which requires fewer options to be displayed. Could you clarify whether the difference in accuracy is due to the requirement of displaying multiple options (which increases the chance of error) or whether the task design itself creates different learning challenges?

2. Regarding the match type features, how would the approach handle cases where the KB uses different terminology than the user (e.g., "Indian" cuisine in user input versus "Indian" as a type in the KB)? Would the system need to learn these mappings automatically, or is this handled through the match type mechanism?

3. The paper states that the concierge dataset has "more diverse vocabulary" and "more variable dialog structure" than the synthetic tasks. How would you expect the performance gap between the synthetic and real-world datasets to change if the synthetic tasks were designed to better mimic this diversity and variability?

This paper represents a major step forward in the evaluation of goal-oriented dialog systems. The testbed is well-designed, the evaluation is rigorous, and the results provide valuable insights into the strengths and limitations of current end-to-end approaches. The paper is well-written, clear, and accessible, making it an excellent contribution to the field. I believe this work will serve as a valuable resource for future research in dialog systems.
