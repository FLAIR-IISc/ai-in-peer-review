- Introduces a novel, public dataset and task framework for end-to-end goal-oriented dialogue in the restaurant domain.  
- Proposes five synthetic tasks (API call generation, query updating, option display, info provision, full dialogue management) grounded in a knowledge base.  
- Tasks aim to test both language understanding and symbolic manipulation capabilities.  
- Demonstrates Memory Networks achieving promising results and outperforming rule-based baselines on DSTC2 and Concierge datasets.  
- Provides a systematic breakdown of dialogue capabilities to analyze model performance, useful for future research.  
- Recommends rejection primarily because the synthetic tasks disconnect from real-world dialogue challenges.  
- Main criticism: tasks are deterministic and fully predictable, enabling perfect rule-based solutions and lacking realism (noise, ambiguity, probabilistic reasoning).  
- Inclusion of DSTC2 and Concierge does not sufficiently mitigate the synthetic framework’s lack of real-world relevance.  
- Major methodological omission: no baseline that incorporates word order information (e.g., n-grams), making neural results appear artificially strong.  
- Comparing against TF-IDF is misleading because it ignores word order, a fundamental language feature.  
- Without a word-order baseline, claimed gains from Memory Networks with match type features are hard to interpret.  
- Suggested fixes: add a word-order baseline (n-grams or logistic regression with embeddings), expand discussion of synthetic task limitations, and justify synthetic tasks’ appropriateness.  
- Note that the claim about lack of well-defined performance measures is inaccurate; existing metrics for goal-oriented dialogues should be acknowledged.  
- Questions for authors: how to bridge deterministic tasks and noisy real dialogues, why omit word-order baselines, and evidence that synthetic results generalize beyond DSTC2/Concierge.
