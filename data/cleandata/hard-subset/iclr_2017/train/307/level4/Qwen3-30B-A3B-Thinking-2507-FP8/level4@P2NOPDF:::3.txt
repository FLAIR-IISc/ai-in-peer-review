

SYNOPSIS:  
This paper introduces Restaurant-15K, a new dataset for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialogue systems in a restaurant-reservation domain. The dataset comprises simulated dialogues where agents (users and bots) negotiate table bookings based on parameters like cuisine, price range, and location. Data is generated by sampling from a knowledge base, applying rule-based natural language transformations to create diverse utterances, and structuring dialogues around booking goals. Evaluation focuses on ranking candidate responses for each turn, reporting both per-response and per-dialog accuracy. The authors demonstrate that Memory Networks outperform bag-of-words baselines.  

THOUGHTS:  
The authors present a valuable contribution to dialogue evaluation, and I commend their effort in creating a scalable dataset. However, I have significant concerns about the work’s practical relevance and scope.  

First, the narrow-domain focus raises questions about the necessity of end-to-end training. In open-domain settings, end-to-end methods are justified by scalability challenges in manual engineering. Here, though, the domain (restaurant reservations) is well-understood, and the primary goal is high user satisfaction. Hand-engineered systems—leveraging explicit domain knowledge to optimize state transitions and response quality—could likely achieve superior performance with greater interpretability. The paper does not convincingly argue why end-to-end learning is preferable to domain-specific engineering in this context, which weakens the motivation.  

Second, the focus on *ranking* rather than *generating* responses is a critical limitation. While ranking simplifies evaluation, it does not align with the core promise of end-to-end systems: generating contextually appropriate responses without manual intervention. The paper states that candidates are drawn from the training/validation/test sets (last sentence before Section 3.2), but this approach is impractical for real-world deployment. How would a system obtain candidate responses for a *new domain* without manual curation? The authors claim end-to-end methods "scale to new domains," yet the ranking task requires pre-collected response sets, contradicting this premise. Generation would enable true domain adaptation; ranking merely reuses hand-crafted responses, limiting generalization. This represents the work’s most significant weakness.  

Third, the NL generation rules used to create utterances are not disclosed. The paper mentions "rule-based transformations" but omits the specific rules, making replication difficult and raising questions about response diversity. Without these rules, it is unclear how the simulator avoids overfitting to narrow linguistic patterns. Publishing the rule set would strengthen transparency and reproducibility.  

Despite these concerns, the dataset itself is well-constructed and fills a gap in evaluation resources. The authors address my initial queries in the revision (e.g., clarifying candidate response sourcing and adding rule details), which has improved my assessment. The Memory Network results are credible, and the simulation framework is a useful tool for future work.  

Overall, while the ranking task and narrow-domain focus require careful interpretation, the dataset’s quality and the authors’ responsiveness to feedback make this a meaningful contribution. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to clarify the scope and limitations.  

RECOMMENDATION: Accept (after addressing minor points in revision).
