

This paper presents a novel open-source dataset and task suite designed to systematically evaluate end-to-end dialogue systems in goal-oriented applications. The authors construct a synthetic restaurant reservation environment where dialogue capabilities can be broken down into specific subtasks, including API call issuance, knowledge base querying, option display, and full conversational flow, enabling precise error analysis.

I recommend acceptance with the condition that the authors incorporate a baseline model incorporating word order information. The current evaluation framework is valuable for controlled testing, but the absence of this baseline creates an uneven comparison that may overstate the advantages of neural network approaches.

The proposed dataset is a welcome contribution that will facilitate future research in goal-oriented dialogue systems. However, the synthetic nature of the tasks, while enabling controlled evaluation, limits the assessment of models' ability to handle noisy, ambiguous user interactions—a critical aspect of real-world dialogue applications. Most significantly, the lack of a baseline model that incorporates word order information (such as a logistic regression model with bi-gram features and match-type features) weakens the comparative analysis. Without this baseline, the performance gap between neural models and traditional methods appears larger than it might be with a more complete evaluation framework.

Additional feedback: To strengthen the paper, please add a baseline model incorporating word order information. Additionally, the conclusion's claim that "existing work has no well-defined measures of performance" is inaccurate; several end-to-end task-oriented dialogue systems have well-defined evaluation metrics (e.g., Wen et al., 2015).

Questions for authors:
1. Could you elaborate on why a baseline incorporating word order information was not included in your evaluation, and how this might affect the interpretation of your results?
2. The conclusion states "the existing work has no well-defined measures of performance" - could you clarify this statement in light of existing literature on evaluation metrics for task-oriented dialogue systems?
