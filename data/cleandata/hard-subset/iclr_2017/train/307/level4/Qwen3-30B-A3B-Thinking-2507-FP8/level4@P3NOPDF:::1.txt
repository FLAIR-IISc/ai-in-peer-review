

This paper introduces a new public dataset and suite of tasks for goal-oriented dialogue systems, constructed via rule-based programs to enable evaluation across multiple dialogue capabilities—from API invocation to full conversational flow. This contribution is valuable for the dialogue research community, providing standardized benchmarks to advance the field. However, the paper contains significant limitations that undermine its core claims and require substantial revision.

The primary concern lies in the evaluation methodology. The tasks (1-5) are fully deterministic, offering no opportunity to assess models on critical real-world challenges such as handling noisy user inputs, resolving ambiguity, inferring user intent distributions, or implementing dialogue repair strategies. These are not merely "pitfalls" but fundamental omissions, as goal-oriented dialogue systems must inherently manage uncertainty. The absence of such evaluation criteria misrepresents the dataset’s utility for practical deployment.

More critically, the paper lacks a strong non-neural baseline incorporating word-order information—a severe methodological flaw. The authors report neural models (e.g., LSTM, Transformer) achieving high performance, but without a comparable baseline that leverages linguistic structure (e.g., n-grams, feature-based models), the claimed superiority of deep learning is unconvincing. As stated in the draft, simpler models (e.g., logistic regression with word embeddings, bi-gram features, and match-type features) could outperform or match neural approaches, potentially attributing results to regularization rather than representation learning. The current evaluation artificially inflates neural performance, making it impossible to isolate the true value of deep learning for this task. **I strongly recommend including this baseline**; its absence is a critical weakness that must be addressed to validate the paper’s core thesis. Should the authors add such a baseline, I would raise my score to 8.

Regarding the conclusion’s claim that "existing work has no well-defined measures of performance," this is factually incorrect. End-to-end task-oriented dialogue systems (e.g., Wen et al., *A Network-based End-to-End Trainable Task-oriented Dialogue System*, 2015) employ well-established metrics like slot-filling accuracy and success rate. While non-goal-oriented dialogue (e.g., chit-chat) presents unique evaluation challenges, these are addressed via human evaluation (e.g., Liu et al., 2016; Yu et al., *Strategy and Policy Learning for Non-Task-Oriented Conversational Systems*, 2016). The paper’s assertion mischaracterizes the literature and should be corrected.

Minor issues include inconsistent terminology (e.g., "rule-based programs" vs. "rule-based systems") and a lack of clarity on how the dataset’s artificial construction affects generalizability. The authors should explicitly discuss whether the deterministic nature of the tasks limits the dataset’s applicability to real-world scenarios where user queries are inherently ambiguous.

In summary, while the dataset itself is a useful resource, the paper’s evaluation framework is flawed due to the absence of a meaningful non-neural baseline and the omission of uncertainty-handling tasks. The current results do not substantiate the claim that deep learning is uniquely suited for this domain. Addressing the baseline requirement is non-negotiable for a fair assessment. If revised to include a robust feature-based baseline (e.g., logistic regression with embeddings and n-grams), this work could make a meaningful contribution. Without it, the paper’s conclusions are unsupported.

**Recommendation**: Major Revision. The paper’s core contribution is promising but requires critical methodological corrections to be credible. The absence of a baseline incorporating word-order features is the most urgent issue. I am willing to recommend acceptance upon incorporation of this baseline and clarification of the evaluation scope.
