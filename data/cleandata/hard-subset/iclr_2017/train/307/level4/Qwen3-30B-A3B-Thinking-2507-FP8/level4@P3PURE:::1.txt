

This paper introduces a new public dataset and associated tasks for goal-oriented dialogue systems, constructed via rule-based programs to enable evaluation across multiple dimensions—including API invocation, option display, and full dialogue. This constitutes a valuable contribution to the dialogue literature, accelerating future research on dialogue system development and evaluation. However, the approach presents notable limitations. First, the paper does not clarify how deep learning models compare to traditional methods (e.g., rule-based or shallow models), as deep learning typically requires substantial training data; performance differences between neural architectures may thus primarily reflect regularization choices rather than model capacity. Second, Tasks 1–5 are entirely deterministic, precluding assessment of models’ ability to handle real-world noise and ambiguity (e.g., inferring user goal distributions or executing dialogue repair strategies), which are critical for practical dialogue applications.  

Crucially, the paper lacks a baseline model incorporating word-order information, a significant methodological flaw. This omission artificially inflates the perceived strength of neural networks, as simpler baselines (e.g., logistic regression with word embeddings, bi-gram features, and match-type features) could likely match or exceed the proposed neural models. To fairly evaluate the utility of deep learning for this task, the authors must include such a non-neural baseline. If added, I would increase my rating to 8.  

Minor correction: The conclusion erroneously claims "existing work has no well-defined measures of performance." This is inaccurate; end-to-end trainable task-oriented dialogue systems (e.g., Wen et al., *A Network-based End-to-End Trainable Task-oriented Dialogue System*, 2016) employ well-defined metrics. While non-goal-oriented dialogue evaluation is more challenging, it is feasible via human evaluation (e.g., Liu et al., 2016; Yu et al., *Strategy and Policy Learning for Non-Task-Oriented Conversational Systems*).
