- Introduces a well-designed testbed with five restaurant-reservation tasks to systematically evaluate end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems.  
- Tests require non-trivial operations (API calls, interpreting query results, dialog-state management) and promote reproducibility and error analysis.  
- Evaluates multiple models (including Memory Networks with match-type features) against a rule-based baseline on synthetic and real datasets (DSTC2, concierge).  
- Results show end-to-end models can learn some behaviors (issuing API calls) but struggle with others (interpreting query results/displaying options).  
- Paper is exceptionally clear, well-structured, and accessible beyond dialog researchers.  
- Decision: Accept — strong contribution filling a gap in evaluation methodology for goal-oriented dialog.  
- Clearly identifies the evaluation problem and shows slot-filling systems remain competitive in narrow domains while end-to-end models have limitations.  
- Well-motivated approach that highlights limitations of existing datasets/metrics and justifies task design to test specific capabilities.  
- Strong empirical support with rigorous protocol, OOV attention, and consistent patterns across synthetic and real data.  
- Suggest discussing limitations of per-response accuracy metric and its inadequacy for true goal-oriented utility.  
- Recommend discussing extension of the framework to domains beyond restaurant reservation to emphasize broader applicability.  
- Ask authors to clarify (1) whether Task 3’s low per-dialog accuracy stems from displaying multiple options or task design, (2) how match-type features handle KB/user terminology mismatches, and (3) how synthetic-vs-real performance gaps would change if synthetic tasks had more diverse/variable structure.
