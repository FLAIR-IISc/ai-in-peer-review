

This paper presents a valuable contribution to dialogue research: a new public dataset and set of tasks for goal-oriented dialogue applications, constructed via rule-based programs to evaluate diverse aspects of dialogue system performance—from API calls and option display to full dialogue management. While this dataset will undoubtedly facilitate future research, several important considerations warrant attention.

First, the paper does not adequately address how deep learning models compare to traditional approaches (e.g., rule-based or shallow methods) for these tasks. Given that deep learning typically requires substantial training data, performance differences between neural architectures may primarily reflect variations in regularization rather than inherent model superiority. More critically, Tasks 1–5 are entirely deterministic, meaning evaluation on them fails to measure a critical capability of real-world dialogue systems: handling noisy, ambiguous interactions (e.g., inferring user goal distributions or executing dialogue repair). This gap significantly limits the dataset’s relevance to practical applications.

The most substantial weakness lies in the absence of a baseline model incorporating word order information. This omission overstates the strength of the proposed neural networks, as simpler baselines (e.g., logistic regression with appropriate features) could likely match or exceed their performance. To fairly assess the value of representation learning for this task, the authors should include a non-neural baseline that accounts for word order. For instance, a logistic regression model using (1) word embeddings (mirroring the Supervised Embeddings approach), (2) bi-gram features, and (3) match-type features would provide a meaningful comparison. Including such a baseline would strengthen the paper’s claims, and I would raise my score to 8 upon its addition.

Finally, the conclusion states that "existing work has no well-defined measures of performance," which is inaccurate. End-to-end trainable task-oriented dialogue systems (e.g., Wen et al.’s *A Network-based End-to-End Trainable Task-oriented Dialogue System*) employ well-defined metrics. While non-goal-oriented dialogue evaluation presents greater challenges, methods like human evaluation (as used by Liu et al., 2016, for Twitter dialogues) or policy-based frameworks (e.g., Yu et al.’s *Strategy and Policy Learning for Non-Task-Oriented Conversational Systems*) provide robust evaluation approaches. The paper should revise this claim to reflect current literature.
