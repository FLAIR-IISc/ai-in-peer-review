

SYNOPSIS:  
This paper introduces a new dataset for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems, focused exclusively on restaurant reservations. The dataset comprises simulated dialogues where the user specifies parameters (e.g., cuisine, price range) to a bot, which then seeks table availability and facilitates booking. Data is generated via a simulation leveraging an underlying knowledge base to sample parameter combinations, followed by rule-based natural language transformations to produce realistic utterances. The task involves ranking candidate responses for each dialog turn, with evaluation metrics reported as per-response accuracy and per-dialog accuracy. The authors demonstrate that Memory Networks achieve superior performance over basic bag-of-words baselines.  

THOUGHTS:  
I commend the authors for an interesting contribution. However, I remain skeptical about the utility of end-to-end trained systems in narrow-domain settings. While end-to-end approaches are compelling in open-domain scenarios—where manual state/response engineering is infeasible—the narrow-domain context typically involves well-understood domains where high user satisfaction is the primary goal. In such cases, leveraging explicit domain knowledge to engineer the optimal system appears more appropriate than relying on end-to-end learning.  

I am also somewhat disappointed that the work focuses on *ranking* rather than *generating* responses, despite acknowledging that ranking simplifies evaluation. I question how candidate responses would be obtained in practice: the paper states that candidates are drawn from the train/val/test sets (last sentence before Section 3.2). Given that a core motivation for end-to-end training is scalability to new domains without manual re-engineering, it remains unclear how candidate responses for a new domain would be sourced. Generating responses would enable stronger generalization across domains, whereas ranking a pre-collected list of generic responses represents a significant limitation. This is the weakest aspect of the work.  

Additionally, the simulation-based data generation (expanding parameter tuples via NL-generation rules) inherently restricts response variability. While the simulator enables unlimited data synthesis, the specific NL-generation rules were not provided. Publishing these rules would enhance reproducibility and transparency.  

Despite these concerns, I consider this an interesting contribution worthy of publication. After considering the authors’ clarifications and supplementary results, I have updated my score accordingly.
