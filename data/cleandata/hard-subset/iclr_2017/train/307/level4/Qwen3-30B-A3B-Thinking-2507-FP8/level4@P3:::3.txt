

# Review for ICLR 2017

## Summary
This paper introduces a novel testbed for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems in the restaurant reservation domain. The authors propose a set of five synthetic tasks designed to test crucial capabilities of dialog systems, including issuing API calls, updating requests, displaying options, providing additional information, and conducting full dialogs. The testbed is grounded in a knowledge base of restaurant properties and is designed to be reproducible and easy to use. The authors demonstrate that Memory Networks can achieve promising results on these tasks, particularly at the per-response level, while still falling short of perfect performance at the per-dialog level. They validate their findings by comparing against a rule-based baseline on the DSTC2 dataset and on data from a real online concierge service, showing consistent performance patterns across synthetic and real-world settings.

## Decision
Accept. The paper provides a well-motivated and rigorously designed testbed that addresses a significant gap in the evaluation of end-to-end dialog systems. The key reasons for acceptance are the paper's well-structured testbed that enables systematic analysis of model capabilities, and the compelling empirical results showing consistent patterns across both synthetic and real-world data.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper effectively addresses a critical problem in the field: the lack of standardized, reproducible benchmarks for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of existing approaches and justify their testbed design through careful task decomposition that isolates specific capabilities required for successful goal-oriented dialog. This design enables meaningful error analysis that would be difficult to achieve with existing datasets.

The experimental setup is scientifically rigorous. The authors not only provide results on their synthetic tasks but also validate them on two distinct real-world datasets (DSTC2 and a concierge service), demonstrating that the patterns observed in synthetic data generalize to real-world settings. Their evaluation metrics (per-response and per-dialog accuracy) are well-chosen to assess both component-level performance and overall task completion.

The comparison of multiple baselines (rule-based, IR methods, supervised embeddings, and Memory Networks) is comprehensive and appropriately contextualized. The authors' findings that Memory Networks outperform other methods, particularly when augmented with match type features, are well-supported by the data. The observation that even the best models fail to perfectly handle interpreting API call results (Tasks 3 and 5) provides valuable insights into the current limitations of end-to-end approaches.

## Additional Feedback
The paper would be strengthened by:
1. More explicit discussion of the limitations of the ranking approach versus generation, particularly regarding how the candidate set is constructed and whether this approach would scale to truly open domains where the set of possible responses is infinite.
2. Clarification of how the match type features specifically interact with the Memory Network architecture, as this appears to be a key innovation that significantly impacts performance on certain tasks.
3. A more detailed description of the natural language generation rules used to create the synthetic data, which would enhance reproducibility.

## Questions for Authors
1. The paper uses a ranking approach rather than generation. How would you expect the candidate set to be constructed for a new domain in practice? Given that the authors emphasize the scalability of end-to-end approaches, what mechanism would you propose for obtaining the candidate responses without manual engineering for each new domain?
2. The authors note that the rule-based system for the DSTC2 task achieved 40.7% per-response accuracy on the validation set. Given that traditional slot-filling approaches typically achieve much higher accuracy on DSTC2, could you clarify how the rule-based system was designed to handle the nuances of the real-world DSTC2 data, and why it doesn't outperform the Memory Networks on this task?
3. The OOV test sets present a significant challenge for embedding-based methods. How might the authors' approach be extended to handle truly open-domain entities (e.g., new restaurant names that don't appear in any training data) without requiring a predefined knowledge base?

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by providing a well-designed, reproducible testbed that enables systematic evaluation of end-to-end dialog systems in goal-oriented settings. The authors' findings provide important insights into the current strengths and limitations of end-to-end approaches, which will be valuable for guiding future research. The work is well-executed, rigorously evaluated, and makes a clear contribution to the field.
