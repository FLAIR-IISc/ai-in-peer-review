

This paper introduces a systematic testbed for evaluating end-to-end dialog systems in goal-oriented applications, specifically focusing on restaurant reservation. The authors propose a set of five well-defined tasks grounded in a restaurant knowledge base that systematically test crucial capabilities including API call handling, dialog management, knowledge base interaction, and response generation. They demonstrate that Memory Networks can achieve promising but imperfect performance on these tasks, and importantly validate their findings on real-world datasets from the Dialog State Tracking Challenge (DSTC2) and an online concierge service, confirming that their synthetic testbed serves as a good proxy for real-world performance.

I recommend acceptance with two key reasons: (1) The testbed design is exceptionally well-suited for systematically evaluating end-to-end dialog systems in goal-oriented contexts, addressing a critical gap in the field, and (2) The experimental protocol is rigorous with validation across multiple datasets, providing strong evidence for the claims about the limitations of current end-to-end approaches.

The paper makes a significant contribution by creating a reproducible framework that allows for controlled evaluation of end-to-end dialog systems in goal-oriented settings. The authors effectively demonstrate that while end-to-end models can learn to handle implicit dialog state tracking and perform basic operations like API calls, they still struggle with interpreting knowledge base results to present options to users. Their validation across synthetic tasks, DSTC2 data, and real concierge data provides compelling evidence that their testbed reliably measures capabilities relevant to real-world applications. The paper's clarity and accessibility make it valuable not only to dialog researchers but also to machine learning researchers interested in more rigorous evaluation methods for conversational systems.

For further improvement, the authors should clarify the relationship between per-response and per-dialog accuracy metrics in the main text, as this is crucial for understanding the results. The current explanation in Table 2's footnote is insufficient for readers who may not have read the full paper. A brief discussion of potential limitations in generalizing the testbed to other goal-oriented domains would also strengthen the paper.

I would like to ask the authors to clarify how the match type features specifically help with handling OOV entities, and whether this approach could be extended to other types of entities beyond those in the restaurant domain. Additionally, could you elaborate on how the testbed would need to be modified to evaluate systems on different goal-oriented domains (e.g., travel booking, customer support), and what challenges might arise in such generalization?
