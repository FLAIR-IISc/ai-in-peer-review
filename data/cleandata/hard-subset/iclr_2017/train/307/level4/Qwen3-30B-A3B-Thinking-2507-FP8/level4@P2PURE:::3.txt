

SYNOPSIS:  
This paper introduces a new dataset for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems, focused exclusively on restaurant reservations. The dataset simulates user-bot interactions where the goal is to identify available tables and complete bookings based on user-provided parameters (e.g., cuisine, price range). Data is generated via simulation: a knowledge base supplies parameter values, which are then transformed into natural language descriptions using rule-based methods. The task requires ranking candidate responses for each dialog turn, with evaluation reported via per-response and per-dialog accuracy. The authors demonstrate that Memory Networks achieve superior performance over basic bag-of-words baselines.  

THOUGHTS:  
I commend the authors for this interesting contribution. However, I remain skeptical about the utility of end-to-end trained systems in narrow-domain settings. While end-to-end approaches are compelling for open-domain dialog due to scalability concerns with hand-coded states and responses, narrow domains typically involve well-understood, constrained spaces where high user satisfaction is the primary goal. In such cases, leveraging explicit domain knowledge to engineer an optimal system seems more appropriate than relying on end-to-end training.  

I also find it disappointing that the work focuses on *ranking* rather than *generating* responses, despite acknowledging that ranking simplifies evaluation. Crucially, the paper does not clarify how candidate responses are sourced in practice. As stated in the last sentence before Section 3.2, the model ranks responses from the training/validation/test sets. Given that a core motivation for end-to-end training is scalability to new domains without manual re-engineering, the source of candidate responses for a new domain remains unclear. Generating responses would enable stronger generalization across domains compared to ranking pre-collected generic responses—a limitation I consider the weakest aspect of this work.  

Additionally, the rule-based natural language generation used to expand parameter tuples (e.g., cuisine, price) inherently limits response variability. While this trade-off enables unlimited synthetic data generation, the absence of published generation rules hinders reproducibility. Including these rules would strengthen the contribution.  

Despite these reservations, I consider this a valuable contribution worthy of publication at ICLR.
