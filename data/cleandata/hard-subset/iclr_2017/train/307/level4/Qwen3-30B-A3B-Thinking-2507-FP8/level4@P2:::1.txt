

This paper introduces a valuable new benchmark for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialogue systems. The authors propose a well-designed suite of five synthetic tasks within a restaurant reservation context, each targeting specific capabilities such as issuing API calls, updating requests, displaying options, and providing information. The tasks are grounded in a structured knowledge base, designed to be reproducible and facilitate error analysis. The authors validate their testbed by comparing multiple models (including Memory Networks) against baselines on both synthetic data and real-world datasets from the Dialog State Tracking Challenge (DSTC2) and an online concierge service.

The contribution is significant as it addresses a clear gap in the literature: the lack of standardized, reproducible benchmarks for goal-oriented dialogue systems. Previous datasets either lack proper structure for end-to-end learning or are noisy and domain-specific, making consistent comparisons difficult. The authors' synthetic approach provides a controlled environment to systematically test specific capabilities while maintaining reproducibility.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper has been significantly improved since the initial submission, particularly by addressing the key concern about the absence of a word-order aware baseline. The authors have now included a logistic regression model with bi-gram features, which provides a more balanced comparison to the neural models. This addition strengthens the paper's claims about the utility of deep learning for these tasks.

The paper's conclusion has also been revised to accurately state that well-defined performance measures exist for goal-oriented dialogue systems (e.g., as in Wen et al., 2015), rather than making the misleading claim that "existing work has no well-defined measures of performance."

Supporting arguments for acceptance include:
1. The testbed design effectively breaks down goal-oriented dialogue into manageable subtasks, enabling focused research on specific capabilities.
2. The authors demonstrate that the synthetic tasks serve as a good proxy for real-world performance, as evidenced by consistent relative performance patterns across synthetic and real datasets.
3. The Memory Networks approach with match-type features shows promising results, particularly in handling out-of-vocabulary entities.

Additional feedback to improve the paper:
The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of synthetic tasks in capturing real-world dialogue complexity. Specifically, the authors could elaborate on how the deterministic nature of the tasks differs from the ambiguity and noise present in human interactions, and what future work might bridge this gap.

I also recommend expanding the discussion of the rule-based system for DSTC2. While the authors note it achieved 40.7% validation accuracy, a brief description of the specific rules and failure modes would help readers better understand the relative strengths of rule-based versus neural approaches.

Questions for authors:
1. The paper states that the synthetic tasks provide a good proxy for real-world performance. Could the authors elaborate on which specific aspects of the synthetic tasks (e.g., the structure of dialog management, the types of errors) most closely mirror real-world challenges?
2. The Memory Networks with match-type features show improved performance on T4 (providing information), but the paper notes that OOV performance improvements are "all due to better performance on known words." Could the authors provide more insight into why the match-type features don't help with OOV words in this specific task?
3. The paper mentions that the rule-based system for DSTC2 was optimized using the validation set. Could the authors share more details about the specific rules and prioritization strategy to help readers understand the system's limitations?

Overall, this is a strong contribution that provides a valuable resource for the dialogue systems community. The authors have addressed key concerns raised in the initial review, and the paper now presents a compelling case for the utility of their testbed. The work is well-motivated, properly situated in the literature, and the claims are well-supported by empirical results. The paper will help accelerate research in end-to-end goal-oriented dialogue systems by providing a standardized benchmark for evaluation.
