

The paper introduces a new dataset for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems in a restaurant domain, where the agent must book tables based on user-provided parameters (e.g., cuisine, price range). Data is synthesized via simulation using a knowledge base and rule-based NL generation, enabling scalable data creation. The task focuses on ranking candidate responses per dialog turn, with evaluation metrics including per-response and per-dialog accuracy. The authors demonstrate Memory Networks outperform bag-of-words baselines.  

The dataset construction is well-motivated and addresses a genuine need for standardized evaluation in narrow-domain dialog. The simulation approach efficiently generates diverse training samples, and the evaluation framework is clearly defined. However, the core contribution is undermined by two critical limitations that warrant deeper scrutiny.  

First, the focus on *ranking* rather than *generating* responses fundamentally misaligns with the primary value proposition of end-to-end dialog systems: scalability to new domains without manual re-engineering. The authors acknowledge this trade-off (Section 3.2), noting candidates are drawn from the training set. Yet, for a system to generalize to a *new* domain (e.g., hotel booking), the candidate set would need to be manually curated *for that domain*, negating the end-to-end advantage. In contrast, generative models could leverage learned patterns to produce domain-specific responses *without* predefined candidate lists. This omission weakens the paper’s central claim that end-to-end methods offer a scalable solution. The evaluation methodology assumes candidates are pre-specified—a scenario rarely encountered in real-world deployment—making the results less relevant to the practical problem the authors aim to solve.  

Second, the dataset’s reliance on rule-based NL generation limits natural language diversity. While the simulation enables unlimited data, the absence of published generation rules (e.g., templates, transformation logic) hinders reproducibility and transparency. The authors state rules are applied "to render natural language descriptions" (Section 3.1), but without details, it is unclear how linguistic variation is achieved or whether responses remain constrained to the rule set. This is especially problematic given the paper’s emphasis on end-to-end learning, which typically thrives on diverse, human-like data. Including these rules would strengthen the work’s credibility and utility.  

The authors’ skepticism about end-to-end approaches in narrow domains is well-founded. In settings like restaurant booking—where domain knowledge is well-understood and user satisfaction hinges on precise, context-aware responses—hand-engineered systems (e.g., state-tracking with domain-specific rules) often achieve superior performance and interpretability. The paper does not compare against such systems, leaving open the question of whether end-to-end methods offer *any* tangible advantage over carefully designed rule-based agents in this context. The evaluation of "accuracy" also risks conflating task success with naturalness, as the ranking task prioritizes correctness over user experience.  

Despite these concerns, the dataset itself is a valuable contribution. Its structured design and clear evaluation protocol provide a useful benchmark for future work. The authors demonstrate a solid baseline comparison and provide sufficient detail on data generation to enable replication of the dataset. However, the work’s broader impact is constrained by the ranking framework and lack of generative capability.  

**Recommendation**: Accept (with minor revisions).  
**Justification**: The dataset is well-constructed and fills a gap in the literature. To strengthen the paper, the authors should:  
1. Explicitly address the ranking vs. generation limitation in the discussion, clarifying how the approach scales to *unseen* domains (e.g., by proposing a candidate generation strategy).  
2. Publish the NL generation rules in an appendix to ensure reproducibility.  
3. Add a brief comparison to a strong rule-based baseline (e.g., using slot-filling with template responses) to contextualize the end-to-end advantage in narrow domains.  

The paper’s core contribution—providing a scalable, well-defined dataset for end-to-end dialog evaluation—is significant. While the evaluation methodology has inherent limitations, the dataset’s utility for benchmarking remains high. With the suggested clarifications, this work will serve as a valuable resource for the community. The authors’ response to these points would further solidify the paper’s impact.
