

This paper was submitted to arXiv last week and introduces a new public dataset and associated tasks for goal-oriented dialogue applications. The dataset and tasks are artificially constructed via rule-based programs, enabling evaluation across diverse dialogue system capabilities—from issuing API calls and displaying options to full dialogue management.  

This represents a valuable contribution to dialogue research, facilitating future work on dialogue system development and analysis. However, significant concerns exist regarding the methodology. First, the paper does not clarify how Deep Learning models compare to traditional approaches (e.g., rule-based systems or shallow models), given that neural networks typically require extensive training data; performance differences may merely reflect regularization rather than inherent model superiority. Second, Tasks 1–5 are entirely deterministic, failing to assess models’ capacity for handling noisy, ambiguous interactions (e.g., inferring user goal distributions or executing dialogue repair), which are critical in real-world applications.  

Critically, the paper lacks any baseline incorporating word-order information, a substantial weakness that artificially inflates the perceived strength of the neural models. Simpler baselines (e.g., logistic regression using word embeddings, bi-gram features, and match-type features) could outperform the proposed neural models, undermining a fair evaluation of representation learning’s utility. Including such a baseline would strengthen the claims significantly. If added, I will raise my score to 8.  

Minor correction: The conclusion states "the existing work has no well-defined measures of performance," which mischaracterizes the field. End-to-end trainable task-oriented dialogue systems (e.g., Wen et al., "A Network-based End-to-End Trainable Task-oriented Dialogue System") employ well-defined metrics. While non-goal-oriented dialogue evaluation is challenging, methods like Liu et al. (2016) and Yu et al. ("Strategy and Policy Learning for Non-Task-Oriented Conversational Systems") demonstrate viable human-subject-based evaluation.  

I have updated my score following the new results included in the revised paper.
