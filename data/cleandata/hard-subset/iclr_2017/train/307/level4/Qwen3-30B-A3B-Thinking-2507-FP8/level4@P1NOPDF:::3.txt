

SYNOPSIS:  
This paper introduces a new dataset for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialogue systems, focused exclusively on restaurant reservations. The dataset is generated via simulation: a knowledge base defines parameters (e.g., cuisine, price range), which are expanded into dialogue samples, then converted to natural language using rule-based transformations. The task is to rank candidate responses for each dialog turn, with evaluation metrics including per-response and per-dialog accuracy. The authors demonstrate that Memory Networks outperform basic bag-of-words baselines.  

THOUGHTS:  
I appreciate the authors’ contribution and the effort in creating this dataset. However, I remain skeptical about the practical utility of end-to-end approaches in narrow-domain settings like restaurant booking. While end-to-end methods are compelling for open-domain dialogue due to scalability—where hand-coding states and responses is infeasible—the narrow-domain scenario presents a different case. Here, the domain is well-understood, and the primary goal is high user satisfaction. Given this, wouldn’t it be more effective to leverage domain knowledge to engineer a tailored system rather than relying on end-to-end training?  

I also find it somewhat disappointing that the work focuses on *ranking* candidate responses rather than *generating* them. While ranking simplifies evaluation, it raises practical questions: How would candidate responses be sourced in real deployments? The paper implies ranking all responses in the train/val/test sets (as noted before Section 3.2). Yet a core advantage of end-to-end training is its ability to scale to new domains without manual re-engineering. Where would these candidate responses come from for a new domain? Generation, by contrast, would enable better generalization across domains—simply ranking pre-collected responses seems to miss this opportunity. This limitation weakens the paper’s broader contribution.  

Additionally, the dataset’s reliance on rule-based natural language generation constrains response variability. While simulation enables unlimited data generation, the fixed rules limit natural linguistic diversity. The paper does not provide the full set of generation rules, which hinders reproducibility. Including these rules would strengthen the work’s transparency and utility for future research.  

Despite these concerns, I find the dataset valuable for benchmarking. The empirical results are clear, and the focus on a well-defined restaurant domain is a reasonable starting point. The work merits publication, though I recommend addressing the ranking/generation trade-off and sharing the rule set in future iterations.  

Overall, while the paper’s narrow focus and ranking approach present valid challenges, its contribution to structured dialogue evaluation is meaningful and aligns with ICLR’s scope. I have updated my score following the authors’ clarifications and supplementary results.
