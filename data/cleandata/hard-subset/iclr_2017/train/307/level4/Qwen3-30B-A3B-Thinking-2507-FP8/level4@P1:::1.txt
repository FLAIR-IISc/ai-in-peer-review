

This paper introduces a novel, publicly available dataset and task framework for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialogue systems. The authors propose a set of five synthetic tasks within the restaurant reservation domain, designed to test specific capabilities including API call generation, query updating, option display, information provision, and full dialogue management. The tasks are grounded in a knowledge base and designed to test both language understanding and symbolic manipulation abilities. The authors demonstrate that Memory Networks can achieve promising results on these tasks, outperforming rule-based baselines on real-world data from the Dialog State Tracking Challenge (DSTC2) and an online concierge service. Crucially, the paper provides a systematic way to break down dialogue capabilities and analyze model performance, which will be valuable for future research.

I recommend rejection for two key reasons:
1) The synthetic nature of the tasks creates a significant disconnect between the evaluation framework and real-world dialogue challenges.
2) The paper fails to include a critical baseline that considers word order information, making the neural network results appear artificially strong.

The primary issue with the paper is that the proposed tasks are entirely deterministic, which limits their relevance to real-world dialogue systems. The tasks are designed to be completely predictable, allowing for perfect rule-based solutions, but this control comes at the cost of realism. Real dialogue systems must handle noisy input, ambiguous user intentions, and context requiring probabilistic reasoning—none of which are captured in these tasks. While the authors acknowledge this limitation by including real-world datasets (DSTC2 and Concierge), the main contribution of the paper is the synthetic framework, which doesn't adequately prepare models for real-world deployment. The paper's own claim that "the goal here is explicitly not to improve the state of the art in the narrow domain of restaurant booking" makes the synthetic tasks even more problematic as a benchmark.

The second major issue is the omission of a critical baseline that incorporates word order information. The paper compares neural models against rule-based systems, classical information retrieval, and supervised embeddings, but fails to include a straightforward baseline that would use word order features. The authors argue that "TF-IDF Match performs the worst of all methods," but this is misleading because TF-IDF Match doesn't consider word order, which is a fundamental aspect of language. The paper states that "TF-IDF Match outperforms Nearest Neighbor, as general conversations on a given subject typically share many words," but this is precisely where word order information would be valuable. Without this baseline, it's impossible to determine whether the neural models' success stems from meaningful representation learning or simply from the use of more sophisticated features.

The authors claim that "Memory Networks with match type features give two performance gains," but without a proper baseline that considers word order, it's impossible to assess whether these gains are meaningful. The paper's assertion that "matching words on its own is not enough" is undermined by the absence of a baseline that demonstrates this. This omission significantly weakens the paper's contribution and makes the evaluation framework misleading.

Additional feedback for the authors:
- The paper would be substantially strengthened by including a baseline that incorporates word order information (e.g., n-gram features or a logistic regression model with word embeddings and n-grams).
- The discussion of the limitations of the synthetic tasks should be expanded to better address the gap between the controlled evaluation framework and real-world dialogue systems.
- The claim that "existing measures of performance either prevent reproducibility or do not correlate well with human judgements" is inaccurate. Well-defined metrics exist for goal-oriented dialogue systems (e.g., Wen et al.'s "A Network-based End-to-End Trainable Task-oriented Dialogue System"). The authors should revise this claim to acknowledge existing performance measures.
- The paper should clarify why the synthetic tasks are appropriate for evaluating end-to-end dialogue systems despite their deterministic nature.

Questions for the authors:
1. How would you address the disconnect between the deterministic synthetic tasks and the noisy, ambiguous nature of real-world dialogue interactions?
2. Why did you omit a baseline that incorporates word order information, given that this is a fundamental aspect of language processing?
3. Can you provide evidence that the pattern of results observed on your synthetic tasks would generalize to more complex, real-world dialogue scenarios beyond the specific datasets tested (DSTC2 and Concierge)?

While the paper has a clear motivation and provides a useful dataset, the critical omissions in the baseline evaluation and the synthetic nature of the tasks prevent it from making a meaningful contribution to the field. The authors should address these issues before the work can be considered for publication.
