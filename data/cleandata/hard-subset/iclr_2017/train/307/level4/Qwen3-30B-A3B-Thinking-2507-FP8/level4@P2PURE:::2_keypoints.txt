- Paper offers a timely, necessary reality check on overhyped chatbot capabilities, highly relevant to Dialog/NLP and also important for ML venues.  
- Two unstated but important conjectures: (1) end-to-end ML is insufficient for goal-oriented dialogs, and (2) relative performance on synthetic data predicts natural-data performance.  
- Main algorithmic contribution is a simple extension to memory networks ("match type"), representing the first rigorous deployment of such networks on goal-oriented dialog.  
- Experimental protocol and rigor are exemplary; clarity is outstanding with a concise appendix and informative tables (e.g., impact of number of hops).  
- Paper establishes state-of-the-art rigor in dialog evaluation while highlighting the brittleness and arbitrariness of current metrics.  
- Use of per-response accuracy (next-utterance classification) is problematic/absurd in practice because a single correct API call can still yield low per-response accuracy due to preceding required exact matches.  
- Raises the question whether per-dialog accuracy (all responses correct) would be a superior metric; shows extreme sensitivity of results to experimental protocol.  
- Clarifies apparent contradictions (e.g., T3 0.0% vs T5 19.7%) as due to task definitions (T3 requires showing three options, T5 requires one).  
- For concierge data, questions whether "correct" should mean best among all options rather than just top 5 and how that would affect results.  
- While standard metrics are defensible and proposed pessimistic metrics useful, a more meaningful service-oriented metric (e.g., revenue-maximizing loss combining API errors, dialog length, user rejections) is suggested but requires real users or a realistic simulator.  
- Unaddressed representation-learning issue: the paper does not explain how vocabulary mismatches between users and the KB are handled; "match type" requires verbatim KB presence.  
- Concern that relying on verbatim KB tokens is problematic (e.g., obfuscated KB terminology); ML should learn KB–user vocabulary associations rather than depend on manual specs.
