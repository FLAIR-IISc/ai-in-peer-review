

This paper introduces a novel public dataset and associated tasks for goal-oriented dialogue systems, constructed via rule-based programs to enable evaluation across multiple dimensions—including API invocation, option presentation, and full dialogue management. This is a valuable contribution to the dialogue research community, facilitating more structured future work. However, significant methodological concerns undermine the paper's claims.

First, the paper fails to address whether deep learning (DL) models offer meaningful advantages over traditional approaches (e.g., rule-based systems or shallow models) for these tasks. Given DL's typical data hunger, observed performance differences might merely reflect regularization techniques rather than inherent model superiority. More critically, tasks 1–5 are entirely deterministic, neglecting real-world dialogue challenges like handling noisy user inputs, ambiguous intent resolution, or dialogue repair strategies. This omission prevents evaluation of models’ robustness—a core requirement for practical dialogue systems.

The most severe weakness is the absence of a baseline model incorporating word-order information. The proposed neural networks are compared only against simpler, non-sequential baselines (e.g., bag-of-words), artificially inflating their perceived performance. A fair assessment of DL’s utility requires a non-neural benchmark that respects linguistic structure. I recommend adding a logistic regression baseline using: (1) word embeddings (mirroring the Supervised Embeddings model), (2) bi-gram features, and (3) match-type features. Including this would decisively demonstrate whether DL’s gains stem from representation learning or merely its capacity to process sequential data. Without it, the paper’s claims about DL’s efficacy remain unsubstantiated. If implemented, I would raise my score to 8.

Minorly, the conclusion erroneously states that "existing work has no well-defined performance measures." This is incorrect. End-to-end task-oriented dialogue systems (e.g., Wen et al., 2015) employ clear metrics like success rate and slot accuracy. While non-goal-oriented dialogue evaluation is more challenging, human-annotated benchmarks exist (e.g., Liu et al., 2016; Yu et al., 2016). The authors should revise this statement to avoid misleading readers.

In summary: The dataset is a useful resource, but the evaluation framework lacks rigor. The missing word-order baseline and deterministic tasks prevent valid conclusions about DL’s role in dialogue systems. Addressing these points would substantially strengthen the paper. I have updated my score following the authors’ revisions to the experimental section.
