

This paper provides a timely and necessary reality check regarding the overhyped potential of chatbots for diverse human-computer interactions, a major trend in 2016 claiming they could resolve complex dialogues beyond simple chit-chat. While primarily relevant for Dialog/Natural Language Processing venues (to inform software engineers about current chatbot limitations), its significance extends to Machine Learning venues (to emphasize the need for more realistic validation of ML in dialog systems), warranting high consideration. Two key, though unstated, conjectures underpin the work and promise to stimulate further research, as clearly articulated by Antoine Bordes during a NIPS workshop presentation on this paper: (1) end-to-end ML approaches remain insufficient for goal-oriented dialogs, and (2) relative performance on synthetic data strongly predicts performance on natural data—a departure from prior observations, robustly supported by the authors' careful alignment of synthetic and natural conditions. Although the core algorithmic contribution is a relatively simple extension to memory networks (introducing a 'match type'), this represents the first deployment and rigorous testing of such networks on a goal-oriented dialog, with an exemplary experimental protocol. The paper's clarity is outstanding and accessible to audiences beyond ML and dialog specialists; the concise appendix effectively summarizes memory networks, and the tables lucidly illustrate the impact of the number of hops. While establishing the state-of-the-art in rigorously evaluating dialog modeling metrics, the paper also underscores the inherent brittleness and arbitrariness of current metrics—this constitutes a recommendation for future research rather than a revision request. The paper employs per-response accuracy (next-utterance classification from a fixed response list). Table 3 reveals its absurdity in practice: success hinges solely on a correct API call and a short dialog, yet achieving this yields only 1/7 accuracy (since all six bot responses preceding the API call must also be exact). Would per-dialog accuracy (requiring all responses correct) be superior? Table 2 demonstrates its extreme sensitivity to the experimental protocol. Initial confusion regarding the stark contrast between subtask T3 (0.0% accuracy) and full-dialog T5 (19.7% accuracy) was resolved by the task definitions (3.1.1), clarifying T3 demands displaying three options while T5 requires only one. For the concierge data, how would results change if 'correct' meant being the best among all options, not just the top 5? While the authors' use of standard metrics is defensible, and new metrics proposed are appropriately pessimistic, a more meaningful metric for goal-oriented dialogs could be derived from a service-oriented perspective: if selling virtual assistants based on successful dialog completion, the optimal metric would maximize revenue. For the restaurant task, this loss might be a weighted sum of API call errors, dialog turns, and user-rejected options—though such a metric cannot be measured on canned dialogs and would require real human users or a realistic simulator. A closely related, unaddressed issue concerns representation learning: the paper fails to explain what occurs when user vocabulary mismatches the knowledge base (KB) vocabulary. Specifically, for the 'match type' algorithm to map 'Indian' to 'type of cuisine', 'Indian' must appear verbatim in the KB. This is problematic when the KB uses obfuscated terminology; ML should ideally learn these associations rather than relying on manual human specification.
