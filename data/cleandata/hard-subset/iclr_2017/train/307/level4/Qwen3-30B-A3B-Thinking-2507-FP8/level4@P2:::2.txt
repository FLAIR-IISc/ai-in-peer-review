

# Review for ICLR 2017

## Summary
This paper proposes a systematic testbed to evaluate end-to-end dialog systems in goal-oriented applications, specifically addressing the gap between promising results in chit-chat dialogs and the more complex requirements of task-oriented conversations. The authors introduce a set of five restaurant reservation tasks grounded in a knowledge base, designed to test specific capabilities like dialog management, API querying, and entity manipulation. They demonstrate that while Memory Networks can achieve promising per-response accuracy on these tasks, per-dialog accuracy remains low, indicating significant room for improvement. The paper also validates these findings on two real-world datasets (DSTC2 and an online concierge service), showing consistent performance patterns across synthetic and natural data.

## Decision
Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by providing a well-designed, reproducible testbed for goal-oriented dialog systems, and the results provide valuable insights into the current limitations of end-to-end approaches.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper directly addresses a critical gap in the literature: the lack of standardized evaluation frameworks for end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. While previous work has focused on chit-chat or narrow-domain slot-filling, this paper provides a controlled environment to systematically assess where end-to-end models succeed and fail.

The approach is well motivated by the need to move beyond simplistic chit-chat metrics and understand how end-to-end systems perform in practical, task-oriented settings. The authors effectively position their work within the literature by acknowledging the limitations of both traditional slot-filling approaches and existing end-to-end methods, and by clearly articulating the gap their testbed fills.

The claims are well-supported through rigorous experiments. The authors compare multiple models (rule-based, classical IR, supervised embeddings, Memory Networks) across both synthetic tasks and real-world datasets, using appropriate metrics (per-response and per-dialog accuracy). The results consistently show that while Memory Networks can learn to perform non-trivial operations (like issuing API calls), they struggle with higher-level dialog management tasks (like interpreting API results to present options to users), which is precisely the gap the paper aims to identify. The validation on real-world datasets (DSTC2 and concierge service) strengthens the findings by showing the synthetic testbed serves as a good proxy for real-world performance.

## Additional Feedback
The paper would benefit from clarifying the relationship between per-response and per-dialog accuracy. The authors note that per-dialog accuracy is low even when per-response accuracy is moderately high (e.g., 0.0% vs 19.7% for Tasks 3 vs 5), but this difference stems from the requirement to generate multiple correct responses in sequence (Task 3 requires displaying 3 options, while Task 5 requires only 1). A more detailed explanation of how these metrics interact would help readers better understand the performance limitations.

The authors could strengthen the discussion of the "match type" feature by providing more concrete examples of how it improves performance on specific tasks (e.g., explaining why it helps on Task 4 but not on Task 2). Additionally, a brief discussion of how the testbed could be extended to other domains would enhance the paper's broader impact.

## Questions for Authors
1. The paper notes that per-dialog accuracy is sensitive to the specific task requirements (e.g., Task 3 vs Task 5). How might the authors design a more consistent metric that better reflects real-world success in goal-oriented dialog systems, rather than just requiring all responses to be perfect?

2. The authors state that the testbed serves as a good proxy for real-world performance. Given the challenges with real-world data (e.g., Concierge's diverse language and variable dialog structures), what additional modifications might be needed to extend this testbed to other domains beyond restaurant reservation?

3. The paper discusses the limitations of "match type" features for handling OOV entities. How might the authors integrate these features with more advanced techniques (e.g., entity linking or knowledge graph embeddings) to further improve performance on out-of-vocabulary entities?
