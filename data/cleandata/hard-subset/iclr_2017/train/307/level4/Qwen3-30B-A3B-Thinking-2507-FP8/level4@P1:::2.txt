

I am pleased to provide a review of this paper, which introduces a valuable testbed for evaluating end-to-end dialog systems in goal-oriented applications. The authors address a critical gap in the literature by proposing a systematic framework to assess how well end-to-end models handle the specific challenges of goal-directed conversations, particularly in restaurant reservation scenarios. Their approach breaks down the complex task into five well-defined subtasks that test key capabilities such as dialog management, KB querying, and handling new entities. The paper demonstrates that while Memory Networks can achieve promising results on these tasks, they still fall short in critical areas like interpreting and presenting API query results to users. The authors further validate their findings using real-world datasets from the Dialog State Tracking Challenge and an online concierge service, showing consistent patterns across synthetic and natural data.

I recommend acceptance for this paper. The primary reason for acceptance is that the authors successfully create a well-motivated, reproducible testbed that addresses a significant gap in dialog system evaluation. The second key reason is that the paper provides rigorous empirical evidence supporting its claims through multiple datasets and evaluation metrics, demonstrating that end-to-end systems still struggle with core goal-oriented dialog capabilities despite promising results in other areas.

The paper excels in its experimental design and thorough evaluation. The authors clearly articulate the specific challenges of goal-oriented dialog compared to chit-chat settings, and they provide a compelling argument for why current evaluation methods are inadequate. Their synthetic testbed is carefully constructed to isolate specific capabilities, and the conversion of real-world datasets (DSTC2 and concierge service) into this framework demonstrates strong methodological rigor. The results consistently show that Memory Networks outperform classical IR and supervised embedding baselines across all tasks, with the addition of match type features providing significant improvements on out-of-vocabulary entities. The paper's conclusion that end-to-end models still need to improve before reliably handling goal-oriented dialog is well-supported by the data.

The paper's clarity and accessibility are particularly noteworthy. The authors provide an excellent explanation of their Memory Networks implementation in the appendix, and the task definitions are clear and well-structured. The writing is precise and technical without being unnecessarily complex, making the paper accessible to a broad audience beyond dialog specialists.

For additional feedback to help improve the paper, I suggest the authors provide a more detailed discussion of the practical implications of their findings. While the paper focuses on technical evaluation metrics, it would be valuable to elaborate on how these findings might influence the development of real-world applications. For instance, the paper mentions that "the per-dialog accuracy remains low" - it would be helpful to clarify what specific dialog failures are most common and whether they could be mitigated through additional training or architectural modifications.

I also suggest the authors consider addressing the following questions to further strengthen their evaluation framework:
1) How would the results change if the evaluation focused on the minimum number of turns to successfully complete a reservation, rather than requiring all responses to be correct?
2) Could the match type features be extended to handle semantic variations in entity names (e.g., recognizing "Indian" as cuisine type even when the KB uses "Indian food") without requiring exact matches?
3) How might the testbed be adapted to evaluate more complex goal-oriented dialog scenarios beyond restaurant reservation?

Finally, the paper's conclusion that "the pattern of relative performances of methods is the same on Concierge and on our series of tasks" is particularly compelling. I would encourage the authors to expand on this finding in the conclusion, as it suggests their testbed could serve as a valuable proxy for evaluating future end-to-end dialog systems in real-world settings.

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field by providing a much-needed rigorous evaluation framework for goal-oriented dialog systems. The authors' approach is well-motivated, their methodology is sound, and their findings are clearly presented. The testbed they propose has clear potential to become a standard evaluation tool for future work in this area, making this paper highly relevant to the ICLR community.
