

# Review for ICLR 2017

## Summary of Contributions
This paper makes a valuable contribution to the dialogue systems research community by proposing a well-structured testbed for evaluating end-to-end dialogue systems in goal-oriented applications. The authors design a set of five tasks within a restaurant reservation domain, grounded in a knowledge base, that systematically test crucial capabilities such as issuing API calls, updating requests, displaying options, providing extra information, and conducting full dialogues. The testbed is designed for reproducibility and comparison, with both synthetic data and real-world datasets (DSTC2 and a concierge service) to validate results. The paper demonstrates that Memory Networks can achieve promising performance on these tasks, showing the ability to learn non-trivial operations like KB interactions, though per-dialog accuracy remains limited. This work provides an important resource for systematically analyzing end-to-end dialogue models.

## Decision
Reject. While the paper makes a useful contribution to the dialogue systems literature, I cannot recommend acceptance due to two key issues: 1) insufficient baseline comparisons that undermine the claimed advantages of neural approaches, and 2) the synthetic nature of the testbed which limits generalizability to real-world dialogue challenges.

## Supporting Arguments
The primary weakness is the lack of appropriate baselines that would allow a fair assessment of neural approaches. The authors compare against rule-based systems (which are trivial for their synthetic tasks), classical IR methods, and supervised embeddings, but omit a critical baseline: models that incorporate word order information. Given that the tasks require understanding of sentence structure (e.g., distinguishing between "I want to book a table for 4 people" and "I want to book a table for 2 people"), a logistic regression model using word embeddings with bigram features would provide a much more meaningful comparison. Without this, the paper overstates the advantage of neural approaches. The claim that "Memory Networks can reach promising, yet imperfect, performance" is not sufficiently supported, as the neural models appear superior primarily because they're compared against weak baselines.

The second major issue is the synthetic nature of the testbed. While the authors validate their tasks against real-world data (DSTC2 and concierge service), the core tasks (1-5) are completely deterministic and artificially constructed. This fails to capture important aspects of real dialogue systems, such as handling ambiguous user requests, noisy speech recognition, or dialogue repair strategies. The paper claims their tasks "break down the strengths and shortcomings of end-to-end dialog systems," but the deterministic nature of the tasks means they don't actually measure the ability to handle the core challenges of real-world dialogue. The paper states "the pattern of results observed in our tasks is indeed a good proxy for what would be observed on real data," but this claim isn't sufficiently validated given the synthetic construction of the core tasks.

## Additional Feedback
To strengthen the paper, I recommend the following improvements:
1. Add a logistic regression baseline incorporating word order information (bigrams) to provide a fairer comparison with neural approaches.
2. Expand the discussion of the synthetic vs. real-world limitations, particularly addressing how the deterministic nature of the tasks affects the generalizability of the results.
3. Clarify the relationship between the proposed testbed and existing evaluation methodologies, particularly addressing the reviewer's comment about "well-defined measures of performance" (the paper's claim that existing work lacks such measures is inaccurate, as demonstrated by Wen et al. and others).

## Clarifying Questions
To help me better assess the paper, I'd appreciate answers to the following questions:
1. Why was the logistic regression baseline with bigram features not included, given that the tasks require understanding sentence structure? How would this baseline's performance compare to the current baselines?
2. Given that the core tasks (1-5) are completely deterministic, how do you expect these results to generalize to real-world dialogue systems that must handle ambiguity, noise, and incomplete information?
3. The paper states that "Memory Networks can outperform a dedicated slot-filling rule-based baseline" on DSTC2. However, the rule-based system described is not the standard slot-filling approach used in the DSTC2 challenge. How does this compare to the standard slot-filling baselines used in previous DSTC2 work?
