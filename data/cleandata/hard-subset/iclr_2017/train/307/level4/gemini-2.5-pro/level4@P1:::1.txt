**Summary**

This paper introduces a new public dataset and a suite of tasks for evaluating end-to-end, goal-oriented dialogue systems. The work provides a valuable testbed for the community, composed of five synthetic tasks that systematically break down a restaurant-booking dialogue into distinct challenges (e.g., issuing API calls, displaying options) and two additional tasks based on real-world data (DSTC2 and a concierge service). The goal is to provide a reproducible and interpretable framework for analyzing the strengths and weaknesses of different dialogue models. This is a welcome contribution that will help facilitate future research into developing and understanding such systems.

**Decision and Key Reasons**

Decision: Weak Accept

My primary reservation stems from the choice of baseline models, which makes it difficult to fairly assess the contribution of the proposed neural network architecture. The paper's evaluation would be significantly strengthened by including a more competitive baseline that incorporates word order information. Without this, it is difficult to ascertain whether the strong performance of the Memory Network is due to its sophisticated architecture or simply its ability to learn from data in a way that a simpler, word-order-aware model might also achieve.

**Supporting Arguments**

The main strength of this work is its well-designed, public dataset that allows for the controlled evaluation of dialogue systems. This is a valuable resource, as it enables reproducible comparisons and targeted error analysis, which is often difficult with existing corpora.

However, the experimental evaluation has a significant weakness: all baseline models (TF-IDF, Nearest Neighbor, Supervised Embeddings) operate on bag-of-words representations and thus ignore word order. This makes the Memory Network appear stronger than it might be in a more competitive comparison. It is plausible that a simpler machine learning model incorporating word order (e.g., through n-gram features) could perform competitively with the neural models. To properly assess the power of representation learning and complex architectures like Memory Networks for these tasks, it is crucial to compare against a stronger non-neural baseline. I suggest adding a model such as logistic regression using a combination of features: 1) summed word embeddings (as in the Supervised Embeddings model), 2) n-gram features, and 3) the proposed match-type features. Including such a baseline would provide a more convincing demonstration of the benefits of deep learning for this problem.

Furthermore, two aspects of the task design are worth noting as limitations. First, the synthetic tasks (1-5) are deterministic. While useful for controlled analysis, this means the evaluation does not measure a model's ability to handle the noise and ambiguity inherent in real user interactions, such as inferring a distribution over user goals or executing dialogue repair strategies. Second, the training sets are relatively small, which may not be ideal for data-hungry deep learning models; performance differences between complex models could be more attributable to effective regularization than to fundamental architectural advantages.

**Additional Feedback**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not central to my decision:

*   The conclusion claims that for existing work, "measures of performance either prevent reproducibility... or do not correlate well with human judgements." This statement seems overly strong. Well-defined performance measures do exist for task-oriented dialogue. For instance, Wen et al. (2016, "A Network-based End-to-End Trainable Task-oriented Dialogue System") use metrics like success rate and efficiency. While evaluating open-domain dialogue is more challenging, human evaluation is a standard approach, as demonstrated by Liu et al. (2016) and Yu et al. ("Strategy and Policy Learning for Non-Task-Oriented Conversational Systems"). It would be more accurate to contextualize the paper's contribution within these existing evaluation paradigms.
