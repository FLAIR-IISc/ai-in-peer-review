This paper presents a new, public dataset and a series of tasks for goal-oriented dialogue systems. The dataset is synthetically generated using rule-based programs, which allows for the isolated evaluation of different dialogue system components, ranging from issuing API calls and displaying options to conducting complete, end-to-end dialogues.

This is a welcome contribution to the dialogue literature that should facilitate future research into developing and understanding dialogue systems. However, this synthetic approach has several potential limitations. First, it is unclear whether deep learning models are the most appropriate for these tasks compared to traditional methods, such as rule-based systems or other shallow models. Given that deep learning models typically require vast amounts of training data, performance differences observed between various neural network architectures on this dataset may simply reflect differences in regularization rather than fundamental architectural advantages. Furthermore, the first five tasks are entirely deterministic. Consequently, performance on these tasks does not measure a model's ability to handle the noisy and ambiguous interactions common in real-world dialogue applications, such as inferring a distribution over user goals or executing dialogue repair strategies. Despite these concerns, I believe this is an interesting and valuable direction for the community to explore.

A major weakness of the paper is the absence of any baseline that incorporates word order information. This omission makes the neural network models appear stronger than they might actually be, as simpler baselines that leverage word order could potentially be competitive or even superior. To ensure a fair evaluation and accurately assess the power of representation learning for these tasks, the authors should include an additional non-neural benchmark that incorporates word order. This would more convincingly demonstrate the utility of deep learning models for this task. For example, the authors could implement a logistic regression model with features such as word embeddings (similar to their Supervised Embeddings model), n-grams (e.g., bi-grams), and match-type features. If such a baseline is included, I will increase my rating to 8.

Finally, a minor comment on the conclusion: the paper states that "existing work has no well defined measures of performances." This claim is not entirely accurate. End-to-end trainable models for task-oriented dialogue have well-defined performance measures (e.g., Wen et al., "A Network-based End-to-End Trainable Task-oriented Dialogue System," 2016). While non-goal-oriented dialogue is generally harder to evaluate, performance can still be measured, often through human evaluation, as demonstrated by Liu et al. (2016) for Twitter and Yu et al. ("Strategy and Policy Learning for Non-Task-Oriented Conversational Systems," 2016).
