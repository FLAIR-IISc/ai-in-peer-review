- Introduces a new public dataset and five synthetic, diagnostic tasks for end-to-end, goal-oriented dialogue in the restaurant reservation domain, including an OOV split.  
- Provides evaluations on two real-world datasets (DSTC2 and a new concierge dataset) to complement the synthetic tasks.  
- Evaluates multiple baselines (IR, supervised embeddings, Memory Networks) and shows end-to-end models are promising but struggle on per-dialogue success.  
- Decision: Accept (Weak Accept) based on the dataset contribution and identified limitations.  
- Positive: The testbed fills a community need for standardized, diagnostic evaluation of goal-oriented dialogue systems.  
- Negative: Lacks a strong baseline that captures word order (e.g., n-grams), so gains by neural models may be overstated.  
- Main strength: The five sub-tasks and OOV test enable fine-grained analysis of specific model capabilities.  
- Main weakness: Bag-of-words baselines make it unclear whether Memory Networks’ advantage is due to architecture or implicit word-order modeling.  
- Suggestion: Add a stronger baseline (e.g., logistic regression/SVM with bi-grams, embeddings, and match-type features) to better benchmark neural models.  
- Concern: The deterministic synthetic tasks do not capture human-like ambiguity, noise, or dialogue repair, limiting ecological validity.  
- Request for clarification: Explain data efficiency given small training sets (1,000 examples) and how that influences model comparisons.  
- Suggestion to revise claim: Tone down the broad statement that existing performance measures prevent reproducibility; be more precise and cite relevant task-oriented evaluation work.
