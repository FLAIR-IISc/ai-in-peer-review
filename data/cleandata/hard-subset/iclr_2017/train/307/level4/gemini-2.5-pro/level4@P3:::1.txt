### Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces a valuable new public dataset and a suite of tasks designed to systematically evaluate end-to-end, goal-oriented dialogue systems. The work is framed in the context of restaurant reservations and is inspired by the diagnostic nature of the bAbI tasks. The authors provide a set of five synthetically generated tasks that test distinct capabilities, such as issuing API calls, handling updates, and using knowledge base (KB) information. This controlled environment, which also includes an out-of-vocabulary (OOV) split, allows for a fine-grained analysis of model strengths and weaknesses. To ground the synthetic tasks in reality, the authors also provide results on two datasets derived from real user interactions: the DSTC2 dataset and a new dataset from an online concierge service. The paper provides a thorough evaluation of several baselines, including classical IR methods, supervised embeddings, and Memory Networks, demonstrating that while current end-to-end models show promise, significant challenges remain, particularly in per-dialogue success rates. This is a welcome contribution that will help facilitate reproducible research and a deeper understanding of goal-oriented dialogue models.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept (Weak Accept)

My decision is based on two key factors:

1.  **Positive:** The paper introduces a well-designed, public testbed that addresses a clear need in the community for standardized and diagnostic evaluation of goal-oriented dialogue systems. This is a significant service contribution.
2.  **Negative:** The experimental evaluation, while thorough in the models it covers, lacks a strong baseline that incorporates word order information (e.g., n-grams). The current bag-of-words baselines may present an overly optimistic view of the performance gains achieved by the neural network models. Addressing this would substantially strengthen the paper's conclusions.

### Supporting Arguments

The primary strength of this paper is the creation and release of a valuable research resource. The breakdown into five sub-tasks is insightful, allowing researchers to isolate and probe specific model capabilities. The inclusion of an OOV test set is particularly important for assessing a model's ability to generalize to new entities, a crucial requirement for real-world deployment. The paper's empirical analysis is extensive and provides a clear picture of how different classes of models perform, highlighting that while per-response accuracy can be high, achieving multi-turn conversational success (per-dialogue accuracy) remains a major hurdle.

However, the main weakness lies in the choice of baseline models. The classical IR and Supervised Embedding models are all based on bag-of-words representations. This makes it difficult to ascertain whether the superior performance of the Memory Network is due to its sophisticated reasoning and memory architecture or simply its ability to implicitly capture word order and local context, a feature the baselines lack. To make a convincing case for the utility of deep learning models on these tasks, it is essential to compare them against stronger, yet still relatively simple, models. A standard approach would be to use a linear model (e.g., logistic regression or SVM) with a richer feature set that includes n-grams (specifically bi-grams) in addition to the bag-of-words and match-type features. Such a baseline is often surprisingly competitive and would provide a much more rigorous benchmark to properly assess the contribution of the neural architecture. Without this comparison, the claims about the relative strengths of the models are not as well-supported as they could be.

A secondary concern is that the deterministic nature of the simulated tasks (1-5) does not capture the ambiguity and noise inherent in real human-computer interactions. Important challenges in dialogue, such as inferring a distribution over user goals or executing dialogue repair strategies, cannot be measured. The authors acknowledge this by including the DSTC2 and Concierge datasets, which is a commendable step. However, the core analysis is built upon the synthetic tasks, and the paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of these limitations.

### Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not central to my decision, with the exception of the first point about baselines, which I strongly encourage the authors to address.

*   **Improving Baselines:** As detailed above, I believe the paper would be significantly strengthened by including a baseline that incorporates word order. For example, a logistic regression model trained on features combining word embeddings, bi-grams, and the proposed match-type features would be a fair and powerful competitor. If such a baseline were included and the Memory Network still demonstrated a clear advantage, I would be happy to raise my score.
*   **Clarification on Data Size:** The training sets are relatively small (1,000 examples) to "create realistic learning conditions." While this is understandable for avoiding the need for massive datasets, deep learning models are notoriously data-hungry. Could you comment on the data efficiency of the models? It's possible that with this limited data, performance differences are more influenced by regularization and optimization than by architectural superiority.
*   **Correction in Conclusion:** In the conclusion, the paper states that "existing measures of performance either prevent reproducibility... or do not correlate well with human judgements." This statement is too strong and potentially misleading. End-to-end trainable models for *task-oriented* dialogue have well-defined performance measures such as goal completion rate and dialogue length (e.g., Wen et al., "A Network-based End-to-End Trainable Task-oriented Dialogue System", 2016). While evaluation is certainly a major challenge for *non-goal-oriented* chit-chat systems, even there, standardized human evaluation protocols exist (e.g., Liu et al., 2016). I suggest rephrasing this to be more precise about the specific evaluation challenges your work is addressing.
