This paper presents a new public dataset and a suite of tasks for developing and evaluating goal-oriented dialogue systems. The dataset is generated synthetically via rule-based programs, enabling the isolated evaluation of specific system capabilities, such as issuing API calls, presenting options to a user, and conducting a full dialogue.

This work is a welcome contribution to the dialogue research community. The availability of a new, well-structured public dataset with clearly defined tasks will help facilitate future research and accelerate progress in the field.

However, the paper has several weaknesses in its current form. The most significant is the lack of a sufficiently strong non-neural baseline that considers word order. This absence makes the proposed neural network models appear artificially strong, as they are primarily compared against simpler bag-of-words models. To fairly assess the contribution of representation learning and contextualize the performance of the neural architectures, it is essential to include a more competitive benchmark. A logistic regression model using features such as n-grams (e.g., bi-grams), pre-trained word embeddings, and match-type features would serve as a much more robust baseline. Including such a model would convincingly demonstrate the utility of deep learning for these tasks. If the authors were to include this baseline and its results, I would be prepared to raise my recommendation.

Furthermore, the synthetic and deterministic nature of the dataset introduces certain limitations. First, it is not clear how well-suited these tasks are for deep learning models, which typically require vast amounts of diverse training data. The performance differences observed between various neural networks may stem from differences in regularization rather than a deeper capacity for language understanding. It is plausible that traditional methods, such as rule-based systems or shallow statistical models, could perform competitively. Second, the deterministic nature of tasks 1-5 means that the evaluation cannot measure a model's ability to handle the noise and ambiguity inherent in real-world interactions. Crucial dialogue capabilities, such as inferring a distribution over user goals or executing dialogue repair strategies, are not addressed by the current task suite. While this is an interesting direction, the authors should acknowledge these limitations more explicitly.

Finally, a minor point of correction: the conclusion states that "the existing work has no well defined measures of performances." This claim is an overstatement. End-to-end trainable models for task-oriented dialogue have well-defined performance measures (e.g., Wen et al., "A Network-based End-to-End Trainable Task-oriented Dialogue System," 2016). While evaluating non-goal-oriented dialogue is more challenging, established methods exist, particularly those involving human subjects (e.g., Liu et al., 2016; Yu et al., "Strategy and Policy Learning for Non-Task-Oriented Conversational Systems," 2016). The authors should revise this statement to more accurately reflect the state of the art.

Overall, this paper introduces a valuable resource, but the experimental evaluation needs to be strengthened to fully support its claims.

*Review Update:* I have raised my score following the inclusion of new experimental results in the revised paper, which have addressed my primary concern regarding baseline comparisons.
