This paper presents a new public dataset and a set of tasks for goal-oriented dialogue. The dataset and tasks are constructed artificially using rule-based programs, which allows for the evaluation of different aspects of dialogue system performance, ranging from issuing API calls and displaying options to conducting full dialogues.

This is a welcome contribution to the dialogue literature and should facilitate future research in developing and understanding dialogue systems. However, this approach has some potential limitations. First, the suitability of deep learning models for these tasks, compared to traditional methods like rule-based systems or shallow models, is not yet established. Given that deep learning models typically require vast amounts of training data, the performance differences observed between the various neural network architectures might stem more from regularization techniques than from fundamental architectural advantages. Furthermore, tasks 1-5 are deterministic. Consequently, performance on these tasks does not measure a model's ability to handle the noisy and ambiguous interactions common in real-world dialogue applications, such as inferring a distribution over user goals or executing dialogue repair strategies. Despite these concerns, I believe this is an interesting and valuable direction to explore.

The paper's most significant weakness is the lack of a baseline model that incorporates word order information. This omission makes the presented neural networks appear deceptively strong, as it is plausible that simpler baselines incorporating word order could be competitive with, or even outperform, them. To ensure a fair evaluation and accurately assess the contribution of representation learning, the authors should include an additional non-neural baseline that considers word order. For example, a logistic regression model using a combination of 1) word embeddings (similar to the Supervised Embeddings model), 2) bi-gram features, and 3) match-type features would serve as a strong benchmark. Including such a baseline would more convincingly demonstrate the utility of deep learning for these tasks. If such a baseline is included, I will raise my score to 8.

Minor Comment: The conclusion states, "the existing work has no well defined measures of performances." This claim is not accurate. End-to-end trainable models for task-oriented dialogue have well-defined performance measures (e.g., Wen et al., "A Network-based End-to-End Trainable Task-oriented Dialogue System"). While non-goal-oriented dialogue is generally harder to evaluate, established methods exist, often involving human subjects, as demonstrated by Liu et al. (2016) for Twitter and Yu et al. ("Strategy and Policy Learning for Non-Task-Oriented Conversational Systems").
