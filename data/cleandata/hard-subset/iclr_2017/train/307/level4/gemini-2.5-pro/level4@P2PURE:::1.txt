This paper introduces a new, publicly available dataset and a suite of tasks for goal-oriented dialogue systems. The dataset and tasks are synthetically generated via rule-based programs, enabling the isolated evaluation of different dialogue system capabilities, ranging from issuing API calls and displaying options to conducting full-fledged dialogues.

This is a welcome contribution to the dialogue literature and should facilitate future research in the development and understanding of dialogue systems. However, this approach has potential limitations. First, it is not clear whether deep learning models are inherently more suitable for these tasks than traditional methods (e.g., rule-based or shallow models). Given that deep learning models typically require extensive training data, performance differences between neural networks may stem from regularization techniques rather than architectural advantages. Second, the deterministic nature of Tasks 1-5 means they do not evaluate a model's ability to handle the noise and ambiguity inherent in real-world interactions, such as inferring distributions over user goals or executing dialogue repair strategies—both critical aspects of practical dialogue applications. Overall, despite these concerns, I believe this is an interesting and valuable direction for research.

A significant weakness of the evaluation is the lack of a baseline model that incorporates word order. This omission makes the reported performance of the neural networks appear stronger than it may actually be, as simpler baselines that leverage word order could be highly competitive or even superior. To provide a fair comparison and accurately assess the contribution of representation learning, the authors should benchmark against a non-neural model that considers word order. For example, a logistic regression model using a combination of word embeddings (similar to the Supervised Embeddings model), bi-gram features, and match-type features would be an appropriate choice. The inclusion of such a baseline would more convincingly demonstrate the value of deep learning for these tasks, and I would raise my score to 8 in that event.

Finally, a minor comment: the conclusion's claim that "the existing work has no well defined measures of performances" is inaccurate. Well-defined performance measures exist for end-to-end, task-oriented dialogue systems (e.g., Wen et al., "A Network-based End-to-End Trainable Task-oriented Dialogue System"). Furthermore, while non-goal-oriented dialogue is more challenging to assess, established evaluation methods, often involving human subjects, are used in the literature (e.g., Liu et al., 2016; Yu et al., "Strategy and Policy Learning for Non-Task-Oriented Conversational Systems").
