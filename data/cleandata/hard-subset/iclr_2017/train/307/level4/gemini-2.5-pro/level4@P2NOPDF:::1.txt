This paper introduces a new, publicly available dataset and a set of tasks for developing and evaluating goal-oriented dialogue systems. The dataset is synthetically generated using a rule-based system, which allows for the systematic evaluation of various dialogue system components, from issuing API calls and displaying options to conducting full end-to-end dialogues.

This is a valuable contribution to the dialogue research community that will likely facilitate future work in developing and understanding dialogue systems. However, the synthetic nature of the dataset presents some important limitations. First, it is not clear how suitable deep learning models are for these tasks compared to traditional methods, such as rule-based systems or shallow models. Given that deep learning models typically require vast amounts of training data, performance differences between various neural architectures on this dataset might be attributable more to effective regularization than to fundamental architectural advantages. Second, tasks 1-5 are entirely deterministic. Consequently, evaluating models on these tasks does not measure their ability to handle the noise and ambiguity inherent in real-world user interactions, such as inferring a distribution over user goals or executing dialogue repair strategies. These are critical aspects of practical dialogue applications. Overall, despite these concerns, I believe the work represents an interesting and worthwhile direction for the field.

A significant weakness of the current evaluation is the absence of any baseline model that incorporates word order information. This omission makes the reported performance of the neural network models appear stronger than it might be, as simpler, traditional baselines could potentially be very competitive or even superior. To provide a fair and rigorous evaluation, and to more accurately assess the contribution of representation learning for this problem, it is crucial for the authors to include at least one non-neural benchmark that accounts for word order. This would more convincingly demonstrate the utility of deep learning models for these tasks. For instance, the authors could experiment with a logistic regression model that uses a combination of features, such as 1) averaged word embeddings (akin to the Supervised Embeddings model), 2) n-gram features (e.g., bi-grams), and 3) task-specific match-type features. If such a baseline is included, I will increase my rating to 8.

Finally, a minor point regarding a statement in the conclusion. The paper asserts that "the existing work has no well defined measures of performances." This statement is not entirely accurate. Well-defined performance measures exist for end-to-end trainable, task-oriented dialogue systems (e.g., "A Network-based End-to-End Trainable Task-oriented Dialogue System" by Wen et al.). Furthermore, while non-goal-oriented dialogue is more challenging to evaluate, established methods using human evaluation exist, as demonstrated by Liu et al. (2016) for Twitter data and by Yu et al. in "Strategy and Policy Learning for Non-Task-Oriented Conversational Systems."
