This paper introduces a valuable new public resource for research in goal-oriented dialog. The authors present a testbed comprising a suite of synthetically generated tasks in the restaurant reservation domain. These tasks are designed to systematically evaluate key capabilities of conversational agents, from issuing API calls and displaying options to conducting a full dialog. By breaking down the overall objective into distinct subtasks, this work provides a framework for fine-grained analysis of model strengths and weaknesses, which is a welcome contribution to the field.

I am recommending a conditional accept. My decision hinges on the inclusion of a stronger baseline in the experimental evaluation. My primary concern is that the current set of baselines does not include any models that incorporate word order information, making it difficult to fairly assess the contribution of the more complex neural network architectures.

The supporting arguments for this decision are as follows. The current baselines, namely TF-IDF and supervised bag-of-embeddings models, are insensitive to word order. Consequently, the performance gains demonstrated by the Memory Network, while promising, may not be solely due to its sophisticated reasoning capabilities but could partially stem from simply capturing sequential information that the simpler models inherently ignore. To properly contextualize the results and make a more convincing case for the utility of deep learning on these tasks, a more competitive non-neural baseline is essential. For example, a strong baseline could be a logistic regression model that uses a combination of n-gram features, the supervised embeddings, and the proposed match-type features. Including such a model would help disentangle the benefits of representation learning from the benefits of capturing basic sequence information.

Additionally, while the deterministic and synthetic nature of Tasks 1-5 is useful for controlled analysis, it is a limitation. It means the evaluation does not measure a model's ability to handle the noise, ambiguity, and variability of real user interactions, such as inferring a distribution over user goals or executing dialog repair strategies. The inclusion of the DSTC2 and Concierge datasets is a good step to address this, but the core focus remains on the synthetic tasks.

The following points are intended as constructive feedback to help improve the paper:
The claim in the conclusion that "existing measures of performance either prevent reproducibility... or do not correlate well with human judgements" is stated too strongly. Well-defined performance measures do exist for end-to-end, task-oriented dialog systems (e.g., success rate and task completion time in work by Wen et al.). Furthermore, while challenging, evaluation methods for non-goal-oriented dialog have been explored using human judgments (e.g., Liu et al., 2016). I suggest nuancing this claim to more accurately reflect the existing literature on dialog evaluation.
