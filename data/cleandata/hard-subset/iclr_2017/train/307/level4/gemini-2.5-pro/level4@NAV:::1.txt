This paper introduces a novel, publicly available dataset and a set of tasks for developing goal-oriented dialogue systems. The resources were generated synthetically using rule-based programs, enabling the structured evaluation of various system capabilities, from handling API calls and displaying information to conducting a full dialogue.

This work represents a valuable contribution to the dialogue research community and is poised to support future investigations into the development and analysis of dialogue systems. Nevertheless, this synthetic approach has certain limitations. First, it is unclear how well-suited Deep Learning models are for these tasks in comparison to traditional methods like rule-based systems or shallow models. Given that deep models typically require extensive training data, performance differences among neural networks could merely reflect the efficacy of regularization techniques rather than fundamental architectural advantages. Second, the deterministic nature of tasks 1-5 prevents the assessment of a model's ability to handle noisy or ambiguous user interactions—such as inferring a distribution over potential user goals or executing dialogue repair strategies—which are crucial for real-world applications. Overall, despite these concerns, I believe this is a promising research direction.

A significant weakness of the paper is the exclusion of any baseline model that accounts for word order. This makes the presented neural networks appear stronger than they might be, as simpler baselines that leverage sequential information could very likely be competitive with, or even outperform, the proposed models. To provide a fair evaluation and accurately assess the power of representation learning for this problem, the authors should experiment with at least one additional non-neural benchmark that incorporates word order. For example, a logistic regression model using 1) word embeddings (similar to the Supervised Embeddings model), 2) bi-gram features, and 3) match-type features would be a convincing addition. If such a baseline were included, I would increase my rating to 8.

As a final minor comment, the conclusion claims that "the existing work has no well defined measures of performances." This statement is inaccurate. End-to-end trainable models for task-oriented dialogue have established, well-defined performance measures (see, for instance, "A Network-based End-to-End Trainable Task-oriented Dialogue System" by Wen et al.). Furthermore, while non-goal-oriented dialogues are more difficult to evaluate, they can also be assessed, typically with human subjects, as demonstrated by Liu et al. (2016) and Yu et al. ("Strategy and Policy Learning for Non-Task-Oriented Conversational Systems").
