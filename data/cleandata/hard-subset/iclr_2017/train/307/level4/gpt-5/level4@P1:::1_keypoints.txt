- Introduces an open, reproducible testbed for end-to-end, goal-oriented dialog in a restaurant reservation scenario with five synthetic tasks and two real-data evaluations (DSTC2, Concierge).
- Synthetic tasks isolate capabilities: issuing/updating API calls, reading/using KB outputs, sorting options, handling OOV entities.
- Evaluates baselines: TF–IDF, nearest-neighbor IR, supervised embeddings, and Memory Networks (with/without match-type features).
- Results: Memory Networks show promising per-response accuracy, learn some non-trivial operations and OOV handling with match types, beat a rule-based baseline on DSTC2, but have low per-dialog accuracy.
- Decision: Reject (as-is).
- Primary criticism 1: Missing a strong non-neural, word-order-aware baseline in the main evaluation, threatening fairness and possibly overstating neural model advantages.
- Primary criticism 2: Synthetic tasks are fully deterministic and do not probe uncertainty, ambiguity, noise, or repair strategies found in real dialogs.
- Baselines fairness: Simple well-regularized non-neural classifiers with bigrams and entity typing could be competitive given small training sets.
- Determinism & scope: Tasks solvable by rules don’t assess robustness to ASR/SLU errors, paraphrases, or user variability, so transfer to realistic settings is unclear.
- Claims/positioning: Paper overstates the lack of existing performance measures; prior task-oriented and human-evaluation studies exist and should be acknowledged.
- Recommendation: Add a word-order-aware non-neural baseline (e.g., logistic regression/SVM with averaged embeddings, bigrams, match-type features).
- Robustness check: Simulate noise (paraphrases, typos, slot confusions) to quantify brittleness and generalization beyond determinism.
- Error analysis: Provide deeper qualitative analysis of per-dialog failures and which error types cause goal completion failures.
- Data scale: Discuss sensitivity of models (especially neural ones) to training size and regularization and whether gains persist as data changes.
- Evaluation wording: Rephrase claims about “no well-defined measures” to acknowledge existing work and clarify how the testbed complements it.
- Question (baselines): Did you evaluate a linear model with bigrams and match-type features, and does adding n-grams help supervised embeddings?
- Question (OOV): How sensitive are results to the OOV split; were alternative partitions or larger KBs tried; can match-types handle misspellings/canonicalization errors?
- Question (robustness): How do models perform with injected noise (paraphrases, slot confusions, ASR-like errors)?
- Question (reporting): For per-dialog failures, what fraction are due to single critical mistakes vs cumulative minor errors, and how does candidate set size affect ranking performance?
- Post-rebuttal note: Reviewer raised score after authors added a strong non-neural, word-order-aware baseline, which materially strengthened the empirical case.
