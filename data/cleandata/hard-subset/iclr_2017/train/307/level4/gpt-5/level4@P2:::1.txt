1. Summary of contributions
The paper introduces an open, reproducible testbed for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialogue systems. It decomposes the restaurant-booking task into five synthetic subtasks (API call formulation, updates, option ranking, KB grounding, and full dialogues), and supplements these with two datasets derived from real interactions (DSTC2 and a concierge service). The framework enables per-turn and per-dialog evaluation and supports systematic error analysis. The authors benchmark rule-based systems, IR baselines, supervised embeddings, and Memory Networks (with and without match-type features), and report that Memory Networks achieve promising but imperfect performance, especially on KB use and OOV entities. Overall, the work provides a valuable shared resource and clear methodology for comparing models and diagnosing their weaknesses.

2. Decision
Accept. The main reasons are:
- The dataset and task suite fill a clear gap in the community: a controlled, reproducible benchmark targeted at goal-oriented dialogue, with both synthetic decomposition and real-data validation.
- The empirical study is carefully executed, revealing strengths and limits of end-to-end methods (e.g., handling OOV entities via match types, difficulty with option ranking and KB interpretation), which will guide subsequent research.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper addresses the lack of standardized, reproducible evaluation resources for end-to-end goal-oriented dialogue. The breakdown into subtasks is well motivated and well placed in the literature (e.g., bAbI, DSTC). The inclusion of per-dialog accuracy is appropriate for measuring task completion.
- Claims and evidence: The authors support their claims with comprehensive baselines and ablations (IR, supervised embeddings, MemNNs, match types) across both synthetic and real datasets. The results clearly show where models succeed (state tracking, issuing/adjusting API calls) and where they fall short (ranking/displaying options, robust KB grounding), and they corroborate patterns on DSTC2 and concierge data.
- Limitations: The synthetic tasks are deterministic and thus do not measure robustness to noisy or ambiguous interactions, nor do they stress strategies for repair and clarification. This limits conclusions about deployment in real-world, stochastic settings. In addition, at submission time the baselines did not include a simple non-neural model with word-order information, making it harder to judge how much of the benefit comes from representation learning versus memorization or regularization. The authors’ later addition of word-order features helps, and I have updated my score accordingly.

4. Suggestions for improvement (to help the authors; not all affect the recommendation)
- Baselines with word order: To fairly assess the value of deep architectures, include a non-neural benchmark that incorporates word order and exact matching. For example, a multinomial logistic regression or linear ranking model that uses: (i) averaged word embeddings for utterance/response; (ii) bigram features; and (iii) match-type features. This will clarify whether the gains over IR stem from learned representations or from exploiting structure already available to simpler models.
- Clarify evaluation claims: The conclusion states that existing work lacks well-defined performance measures. Please soften this, acknowledging established metrics for task-oriented dialogue (e.g., Wen et al., 2017) and human-in-the-loop evaluations for open-domain systems (e.g., Liu et al., 2016; Yu et al.). Your contribution is strongest as a reproducible, decomposed, and widely accessible benchmark, not as the first to define metrics.
- Learning curves and data efficiency: Since deep models often need substantial data, report performance as a function of training size. This would illuminate whether simpler models are more data-efficient on these tasks.
- Noise and non-determinism: Consider adding stochastic variants of the synthetic tasks (ASR-like noise, paraphrasing, variable question order, ambiguous goals) or a user simulator with probabilistic behavior to better approximate real-world conditions and test robustness.
- Generation vs. ranking: Discuss the implications of ranking from a fixed candidate set. It would be useful to report results with constrained decoding or copy/pointer mechanisms in a generative setting, especially for OOV entities.
- Reproducibility details: Release the simulator and conversion scripts for DSTC2; document candidate-set construction across tasks; and provide ablations on match-type features (e.g., types vs. exact string matches).

Questions for the authors
- Candidate set construction: You rank over a pooled set of all bot utterances and API calls across tasks and splits. How does this choice affect realism and difficulty? Did you evaluate task-specific candidate pools, and if so, how do results differ?
- Template sensitivity: The synthetic data use a limited set of patterns for users and bots. How sensitive are the models to expanding or diversifying these templates? Have you tried paraphrase augmentation?
- OOV handling: Match-type features require access to the KB at test time. How would your approach generalize when KB coverage is incomplete or when entities appear in user text but not in the KB?
- Option ranking (Task 3): Do errors stem more from sorting by rating, from tracking previously proposed options, or from the accept/reject state? Any ablations disentangling these?
- DSTC2 conversion: The converted dataset introduces ratings to make option selection deterministic. Could this inadvertently alter the original task semantics? How often do converted labels disagree with the original system’s behavior?
- Data efficiency: What are the learning curves for MemNNs versus supervised embeddings on Tasks 1–5 and DSTC2? Are there regimes where simpler models dominate?

I appreciate the authors’ additional experiments and have updated my score to reflect the new results. The resource is likely to have significant impact and should stimulate careful, comparable progress on goal-oriented dialogue.
