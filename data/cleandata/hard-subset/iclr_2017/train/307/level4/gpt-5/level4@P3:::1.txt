Summary
The paper introduces an open, reproducible testbed for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. Centered on restaurant reservation, it decomposes the overall task into five synthetic subtasks (API call issuance and updates, option ranking, and KB-based information provision) plus a combined full-dialog task, and supplements these with evaluations on converted DSTC2 dialogs and a real-world concierge dataset. The benchmark targets capabilities critical to transactional dialog: state tracking without explicit annotations, interaction with a KB, handling unseen entities (OOV), and dialog completion. The authors compare several baselines (IR, supervised embeddings, rule-based) with an end-to-end Memory Network model, and introduce match-type features to better handle entities. Results show promising—but incomplete—performance, especially highlighting difficulties in KB result interpretation and full-dialog success, with encouraging consistency between synthetic and real datasets. The data, tasks, and methodology are clearly described and valuable to the community.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- The paper provides a well-motivated, public benchmark with clear task decomposition and OOV protocols, filling a gap for reproducible evaluation of goal-oriented dialog.
- The empirical study is thorough and illuminates both strengths and current limitations of end-to-end models for transactional dialogs, with evidence carried over to real datasets.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper squarely addresses the lack of standardized, reproducible resources to evaluate end-to-end methods for goal-oriented dialog—a setting where off-the-shelf chit-chat benchmarks (e.g., Twitter/Reddit) are ill-suited and where slot-filling pipelines rely on domain-specific engineering. The decomposition into subtasks is well thought out and enables targeted error analysis (dialog management vs. KB interaction vs. OOV handling). This framing is well placed in the literature (POMDPs, DSTC, bAbI, chit-chat datasets).
- Method and baselines: The study compares rule-based, IR, supervised embeddings, and Memory Networks, with and without match-type features. The deterministic synthetic tasks are intentionally solvable by rules, emphasizing the goal of diagnosing ML methods; the inclusion of DSTC2 and a concierge dataset complements this with more realistic variability. The addition of match-type features is a sensible and effective design to mitigate entity/OOV issues in embedding-based models.
- Evidence for claims: Results are presented with both per-response and per-dialog measures, aligning with the end goal of successful task completion. They convincingly show that MemNNs learn API issuance/updates and benefit from multi-hop attention; match-type features markedly improve OOV and KB-answering (Task 4). Crucially, the same ranking of methods holds on DSTC2 and the concierge data, supporting the claim that the synthetic tasks are a useful proxy. The reported failures on option ranking (Task 3) and full dialogs (Task 5) are frank and instructive.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions, not central to the accept decision)
- Strengthen non-neural baselines with word order: While TF-IDF and supervised embeddings are reasonable, a competitive non-neural baseline that leverages word order could better calibrate the impact of representation learning. For example, a multinomial/logistic classifier with (a) averaged word embeddings, (b) bigram features, and (c) match-type features would help assess how far simple discriminative models go. The new bigram and match-type variants reported in the appendix are a welcome step; presenting their results more prominently in the main text would clarify the comparison.
- Noise and robustness: The synthetic tasks are deterministic and linguistically constrained. Consider a noisy variant of the simulator (paraphrases, slot retractions, ASR-like perturbations, off-topic turns) to probe dialog repair, uncertainty handling, and robustness—essential in deployed systems.
- Generation vs. ranking: The current evaluation is next-utterance classification over a fixed candidate set. A complementary generation-based assessment (even on a subset) would test generalization beyond seen responses and avoid candidate pool artifacts.
- Error analysis and ablations: Provide finer-grained error breakdowns for Tasks 3 and 5 (e.g., sorting failures vs. stopping conditions vs. entity mismatches), ablate match-type features by entity type, and report sensitivity to the number of hops and candidate set size.
- Clarify evaluation claims: The conclusion should soften the statement about “no well-defined measures.” Task-oriented dialog has established metrics (task success, slot accuracy, BLEU/NLG for certain subtasks), and recent end-to-end systems (e.g., Wen et al.) define clear evaluation protocols. For non-task-oriented dialog, human-in-the-loop evaluations (e.g., Liu et al.; Yu et al.) exist despite known difficulties.
- Reproducibility details: Please confirm the license for data/code, provide exact candidate set sizes per task, and ensure no leakage across tasks/splits (especially with shared candidates).

Questions for the authors
- Candidate sets: What are the exact candidate set sizes per task (including OOV), and what is the chance performance? How sensitive are results to enlarging the candidate pool with paraphrases?
- OOV protocol: During training, models see both KBs but only dialogs from one. Can you quantify how often OOV entity tokens are matched purely via type features versus memorized embeddings of seen entities?
- Match-type features: Do they rely on exact string matching only? How would they extend to noisy inputs (typos, morphology, ASR) or multi-token entity variants?
- Sorting and options (Task 3): Where do MemNNs fail most—reading ratings, sorting stability, or decision to stop offering options? Any attempts at explicit differentiable sorting or pointer mechanisms?
- Generalization: How do results scale with larger KBs, longer dialogs, or more slot types? Have you tested sensitivity to party-size distributions or non-deterministic bot policies?
- Significance and variance: Could you report variability over multiple runs and statistical significance of key comparisons (e.g., MemNN with/without match-type features)?
- Alternative architectures: Did you try sequence models (GRU/LSTM with attention) or key–value memory variants for KB interactions? If so, how do they compare?

Overall, this is a timely, well-motivated benchmark with clear utility. The paper is thoughtfully executed, candid about limitations, and likely to catalyze progress on goal-oriented dialog. I recommend acceptance.
