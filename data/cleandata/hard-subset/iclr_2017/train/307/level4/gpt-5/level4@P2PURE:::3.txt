SYNOPSIS:
This paper presents a new dataset for evaluating end-to-end, goal-oriented dialog systems in a restaurant-booking domain. The task is to determine availability and ultimately book a table based on user-provided constraints during the dialog. Data are generated by simulating interactions grounded in a knowledge base that supplies samples for parameters (cuisine, price range, etc.), followed by rule-based transformations to produce natural-language utterances. The objective is to rank a set of candidate responses at each turn, with evaluation reported as per-response accuracy and per-dialog accuracy. The authors show that Memory Networks improve over simple bag-of-words baselines.

THOUGHTS:
Thank you for an interesting contribution. That said, I am skeptical about the utility of end-to-end training in narrow domains. In open-domain settings, hand-coding all states and responses does not scale, making end-to-end methods compelling. In narrow domains, however, we generally understand the space well and aim for high user satisfaction; in such cases, it may be more effective to leverage domain knowledge to engineer the best system.

Given the restricted domain, I am also disappointed that the task is to RANK rather than GENERATE responses, although I recognize this simplifies evaluation. I am unsure how candidate responses would be obtained in practice. As noted (last sentence before Section 3.2), the models appear to rank the set of all responses in train/val/test. If a key motivation for end-to-end approaches is ease of scaling to new domains without manual re-engineering, where would this candidate set come from in a new domain? Generation would likely generalize better than ranking a hand-collected list of generic responses; in my view, this is the weakest aspect of the work.

Finally, because the data are produced by simulating (cuisine, price, ...) tuples and applying rule-based NL generation, the variability of responses is inherently limited. While this provides the ability to generate unlimited data, I could not find the list of rules used; it would be valuable to publish them as well.

Overall, despite my reservations, I believe this is an interesting contribution worthy of publication at the conference.
