Note: The paper was posted on arXiv last week.

This paper introduces a new public dataset and a suite of tasks for goal-oriented dialogue applications. The dataset and tasks are constructed artificially using rule-based programs to isolate and evaluate different aspects of dialogue system performance, ranging from issuing API calls and displaying options to handling full dialogues. This is a welcome contribution that can facilitate future research on developing and understanding dialogue systems.

That said, there are pitfalls to this approach. First, it is not clear how suitable deep learning models are for these tasks compared with traditional methods (rule-based systems or shallow models). Deep learning models typically require many training examples, and performance differences between neural architectures may largely reflect regularization choices rather than fundamental modeling advances. In addition, Tasks 1–5 are completely deterministic, so performance on these tasks will not measure a model’s ability to handle noisy and ambiguous interactions (e.g., inferring a distribution over user goals or executing dialogue repair strategies), which is a critical aspect of real-world dialogue applications. Overall, however, I still believe this is an interesting direction to explore.

As discussed in the comments, the paper does not include any baseline model that incorporates word order information. I see this as a significant weakness, because it can make the neural models appear unreasonably strong, whereas simpler baselines could very likely be competitive or even outperform the proposed neural networks. To ensure a fair evaluation and to correctly assess the benefits of representation learning for this task, it is important to include at least one additional non-neural benchmark that accounts for word order. For example, one could evaluate a logistic regression model with inputs comprising: (1) word embeddings (analogous to the Supervised Embeddings model), (2) bigram features, and (3) match-type features. If such a baseline is included, I will increase my rating to 8.

Final minor comment: the conclusion states that “the existing work has no well defined measures of performances.” This is not accurate. End-to-end trainable models for task-oriented dialogue do have well-defined performance measures; see, for example, “A Network-based End-to-End Trainable Task-oriented Dialogue System” by Wen et al. While evaluation of non-goal-oriented dialogue is generally harder, it is still feasible with human subjects. For instance, Liu et al. (2016) conduct human evaluations for Twitter data. See also “Strategy and Policy Learning for Non-Task-Oriented Conversational Systems” by Yu et al.
