This paper was posted to arXiv last week.

It introduces a new public dataset and a set of tasks for goal-oriented dialogue applications. The dataset and tasks are artificially constructed using rule-based programs to isolate and evaluate different aspects of dialogue system performance, from issuing API calls and presenting options to conducting full dialogues.

This is a valuable contribution that can facilitate future research on building and understanding dialogue systems. However, there are caveats to this approach. First, it is unclear how well Deep Learning models suit these tasks relative to traditional methods (e.g., rule-based systems or shallow models), given that Deep Learning typically requires many training examples; as a result, performance differences across neural architectures may largely reflect regularization choices. In addition, Tasks 1–5 are entirely deterministic, so performance on these tasks does not assess the ability to handle noisy or ambiguous interactions (e.g., inferring a distribution over user goals or executing dialogue repair strategies), which are crucial in real-world dialogue applications. Overall, I still view this as an interesting direction to explore.

As noted in the comments below, the paper does not include any baseline that incorporates word order information. This is a significant weakness because it makes the neural networks appear unreasonably strong, whereas simpler baselines could plausibly be competitive with—or even outperform—the proposed neural models. To ensure a fair evaluation and properly assess the benefits of representation learning for this task, the authors should include at least one additional non-neural benchmark that accounts for word order. This would more convincingly demonstrate the utility of Deep Learning for the proposed tasks. For example, the authors could evaluate a logistic regression model that uses: 1) word embeddings (as in the Supervised Embeddings model), 2) bigram features, and 3) match-type features. If such a baseline is added, I will raise my rating to 8.

Final minor comment: the conclusion states that “the existing work has no well defined measures of performances.” This is not strictly true. End-to-end trainable, task-oriented dialogue models do have well-defined performance measures; see, for example, “A Network-based End-to-End Trainable Task-oriented Dialogue System” by Wen et al. Non-goal-oriented dialogue is generally harder to evaluate, but with human subjects it can be assessed; for instance, Liu et al. (2016) evaluate Twitter conversations, and see also “Strategy and Policy Learning for Non-Task-Oriented Conversational Systems” by Yu et al.
