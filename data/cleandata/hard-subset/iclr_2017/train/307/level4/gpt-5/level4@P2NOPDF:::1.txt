Note: the paper appeared on arXiv last week.

This paper introduces a new public dataset and a suite of tasks for goal-oriented dialogue. The tasks are generated by rule-based programs to isolate and evaluate specific capabilities, from issuing API calls and presenting options to executing full multi-turn dialogues. This is a valuable contribution that can help standardize evaluation and spur further research on task-oriented dialogue systems.

That said, there are important caveats to this approach. It remains unclear how suitable deep learning models are for these tasks relative to traditional approaches (rule-based systems or shallow models). Neural methods typically require large training sets, and observed performance differences across architectures may largely reflect differences in regularization rather than superior representations. In addition, Tasks 1–5 are fully deterministic, so they do not probe the ability to handle noisy or ambiguous interactions—such as inferring distributions over user goals or performing dialogue repair—which are central to real-world dialogue. Despite these limitations, the direction is promising.

A major weakness is the absence of any baseline that incorporates word order. This omission likely inflates the apparent strength of the neural models, whereas simpler baselines might be competitive or even superior. To ensure a fair assessment of representation learning for these tasks, the paper should include at least one non-neural benchmark that uses word order. For example, a logistic regression model with inputs comprising (1) word embeddings (as in the Supervised Embeddings model), (2) bigram features, and (3) match-type features would be an appropriate comparator. Including such a baseline would more convincingly demonstrate the value of deep learning on this benchmark. If this baseline is added, I would raise my rating to 8.

Minor comment: the conclusion claims that “the existing work has no well defined measures of performances.” This is not accurate. End-to-end trainable task-oriented dialogue systems do have well-defined evaluation metrics; see, for example, Wen et al., “A Network-based End-to-End Trainable Task-oriented Dialogue System.” While non-task-oriented dialogue is harder to assess, it can still be evaluated with human studies; see Liu et al. (2016) for Twitter conversations and Yu et al., “Strategy and Policy Learning for Non-Task-Oriented Conversational Systems.”

I have updated my score in light of the new results added to the paper.
