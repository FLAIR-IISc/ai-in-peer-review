SYNOPSIS:
This paper presents a new dataset for evaluating end-to-end, goal-oriented dialog systems. All dialogs take place in a restaurant reservation setting, where the system must find availability and ultimately book a table based on user-provided constraints (e.g., cuisine, price range). Data is generated via a simulator that queries an underlying knowledge base to sample parameter combinations (cuisine, price, etc.), followed by rule-based transformations to produce natural language utterances. The task is framed as ranking candidate responses at each turn, with evaluation reported as per-response accuracy and per-dialog accuracy. The authors show that Memory Networks outperform simple bag-of-words baselines.

THOUGHTS:
Thank you to the authors for an interesting and timely contribution. That said, I remain somewhat skeptical about the practical benefits of end-to-end learning in narrow domains. For open-domain dialog, where enumerating states and responses is infeasible, end-to-end methods are compelling. In contrast, for well-understood, narrow domains where user satisfaction is paramount, leveraging domain knowledge to engineer high-quality systems may be more effective and reliable.

Given the restricted domain, I am also disappointed that the task is limited to ranking rather than generation, though I appreciate the evaluation simplicity this affords. I am unclear how the candidate set is obtained in practice. The paper appears to rank from the set of all responses observed in train/validation/test (see the sentence before Section 3.2). If one motivation for end-to-end methods is easy portability to new domains without extensive re-engineering, where do these candidate responses come from when switching domains? A generation-based approach would better support transfer to new domains than ranking from a curated list of generic responses. In my view, this reliance on pre-collected candidates is the weakest aspect of the work.

Additionally, because the dataset is produced by simulating (cuisine, price, etc.) tuples and applying natural-language generation rules, the variability of the language is necessarily constrained. While the simulator enables unlimited data, the induced linguistic diversity is limited by the rule set. I could not find the list of generation rules used. Publishing these rules would improve transparency, enable reproducibility, and help the community assess linguistic coverage and potential biases.

Despite these reservations, I find the dataset and results valuable. The setup is clearly defined, the evaluation metrics are appropriate for the ranking task, and the empirical results show meaningful gains from Memory Networks over strong baselines. I encourage the authors to:
- Clarify how candidate responses are constructed and how this would scale to unseen domains.
- Consider adding a response generation track and corresponding metrics, which could test generalization more directly.
- Release the natural-language generation rules and any templates used by the simulator.

Overall, despite my skepticism about end-to-end learning for constrained domains, I believe this is a worthwhile contribution suitable for presentation at the conference.
