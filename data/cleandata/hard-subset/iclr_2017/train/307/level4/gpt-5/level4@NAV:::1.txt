Review- This paper was posted to arXiv last week:
The paper introduces a new publicly available dataset and a set of tasks for goal-directed dialogue applications. Both the dataset and tasks are synthetically generated via rule-based programs, enabling evaluation of different facets of dialogue system performance—from issuing API calls and presenting options to conducting full dialogues.

This is a valuable contribution to the dialogue literature that should facilitate future work on developing and analyzing dialogue systems. Nevertheless, this approach has pitfalls. First, it is unclear how well Deep Learning models suit these tasks relative to traditional approaches (rule-based systems or shallow models), since Deep Learning typically requires large training sets; hence performance gaps among neural models may largely reflect differences in regularization. In addition, Tasks 1–5 are entirely deterministic, so results on these tasks will not assess models’ ability to handle noise and ambiguity (e.g., maintaining a distribution over user goals or performing dialogue repair), which is crucial in practical dialogue settings. Overall, I still consider this a promising direction.

As noted in the comments below, the paper lacks any baseline that incorporates word order. I view this as a significant weakness because it can make the neural methods look disproportionately strong, whereas simpler baselines might plausibly be competitive (or superior). To ensure a fair comparison and properly assess the value of representation learning for this problem, the authors should include one additional non-neural benchmark that accounts for word order. This would more convincingly demonstrate the utility of Deep Learning here. For example, one could try a logistic regression model that uses as input: 1) word embeddings (as in the Supervised Embeddings model), 2) bi-gram features, and 3) match-type features. If such a baseline is added, I will raise my rating to 8.

Final minor comment: the conclusion states “the existing work has no well defined measures of performances.” This is not quite accurate. End-to-end trainable task-oriented dialogue systems do have well-defined evaluation metrics; see, for instance, “A Network-based End-to-End Trainable Task-oriented Dialogue System” by Wen et al. By contrast, non-goal-oriented dialogue is generally harder to evaluate, but with human subjects it can be assessed; indeed, Liu et al. (2016) do this for Twitter. See also “Strategy and Policy Learning for Non-Task-Oriented Conversational Systems” by Yu et al.
