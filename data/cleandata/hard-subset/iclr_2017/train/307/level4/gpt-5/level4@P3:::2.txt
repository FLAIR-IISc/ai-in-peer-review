Summary and contributions
The paper introduces an open, lightweight testbed for evaluating end-to-end dialog systems in goal-oriented settings. Focusing on restaurant reservation, it decomposes the overall task into five controlled subtasks (API call issuance and update, option display with ranking, returning factual attributes, and full dialogs), and adds two real-world evaluations: a conversion of DSTC2 to the same format and an “online concierge” dataset. The authors benchmark rule-based systems, IR baselines, supervised embeddings, and Memory Networks (with a simple but important “match type” addition) using per-response and per-dialog accuracy. The study shows that end-to-end models can learn non-trivial behaviors (slot elicitation, API calls) but still fall short on managing knowledge grounding (e.g., ranking and presenting options) and achieving high per-dialog success, especially in OOV conditions. A strong, well-motivated experimental protocol, clear write-up, and public data make this a valuable contribution that should catalyze further research.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
1) The paper provides a much-needed, reproducible benchmark and careful error decomposition for goal-oriented dialog—exactly what the community needs to move beyond chit-chat metrics.
2) The empirical analysis is credible and informative: it both highlights current end-to-end limitations and offers evidence that relative performance on synthetic tasks predicts behavior on real data.

Supporting arguments
- Problem significance and placement: The work addresses a core gap—scalable evaluation for goal-directed dialog systems. It is well positioned relative to POMDP/slot-filling pipelines, non-goal dialog work, and the bAbI QA tasks, and it makes a convincing case for decomposed evaluation in a controlled domain before scaling to real data.
- Methodology: The task design is principled, with clear capabilities tested (state tracking without explicit labels, API call formation and revision, KB grounding, sorting, OOV handling). The OOV split is thoughtful and directly probes entity generalization.
- Claims and evidence: The paper’s main claims are supported. Memory Networks outperform IR and supervised embeddings, match type features meaningfully help on KB grounding and OOV, and yet per-dialog success remains low on the harder tasks. The same relative ordering of methods is observed on DSTC2 and Concierge, supporting the conjecture that synthetic tasks are predictive of real-world trends.
- Clarity and accessibility: The paper is exceptionally clear. The appendix offers a crisp summary of MemNNs and useful ablations (hops), making it accessible beyond specialists.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions, not central to my decision)
- Metrics and task success: Per-response accuracy can obscure end-goal success, while per-dialog accuracy can be overly brittle for long dialogs. Consider reporting:
  - Task Success Rate (booking achieved) with a tolerance for semantically equivalent responses.
  - API Call Exactness (slot-wise accuracy) and a weighted slot error metric that reflects real cost (wrong cuisine vs wrong party size).
  - Efficiency metrics (turns to success) and cumulative penalty for presenting low-utility options.
  - Top-k response accuracy (e.g., Hits@k), especially on Concierge, to separate retrieval quality from template rigidities.
- T3 vs T5 discrepancy: The definition difference (3 options vs ≥1) materially affects per-dialog accuracy. Consider a normalized option-presentation metric (e.g., mean reciprocal rank of accepted option, or success within N offers) to make cross-task comparisons more interpretable.
- OOV and entity linking: Match type features require exact surface matches against the KB. In realistic deployments, user and KB vocabularies often diverge (synonyms, abbreviations, brand vs canonical names). Discuss or experiment with:
  - Entity linking/canonicalization or lexicon/ontology mapping prior to typing.
  - Character or subword features (to better handle unseen entities, typos).
  - Paraphrase augmentation for user mentions of KB entities.
- Statistical validation of “synthetic-to-real” claim: Strengthen the transfer conjecture by reporting correlations (e.g., Spearman) between methods’ rankings on synthetic tasks and on DSTC2/Concierge, with confidence intervals.
- Generality beyond restaurants: Briefly discuss how easily the simulator and task suite can be ported to other domains (e.g., travel, retail) and which components (API schema, option ranking) need reconfiguration.
- Robustness and variance: Include variance across seeds and sensitivity to hyperparameters (especially number of hops) to gauge stability; consider a small data ablation to assess sample efficiency.
- Human evaluation: A small-scale human judgment on Concierge (task success and appropriateness) would complement automatic metrics and calibrate their practical relevance.

Questions for the authors
- Candidate set and top-k metrics: What is the size of the candidate set for each dataset (especially Concierge)? Can you report Hits@k (k∈{5,10}) to mitigate brittleness of exact template matching?
- Success-oriented metrics: Do you have results for API call slot-wise accuracy and turns-to-success (or any proxy for dialog efficiency)? If not, could the simulator be extended to measure these?
- Synthetic-to-real correlation: Can you quantify the correlation between method rankings on T1–T5 and on DSTC2/Concierge, and test its statistical significance?
- OOV handling: How does performance change if entity surface forms differ slightly from the KB (synonyms, spelling variants)? Have you tried subword models or entity linking?
- DSTC2 conversion: Since bots in DSTC2 are non-deterministic and noisy, do you have an analysis of failure modes specific to ASR noise vs policy inconsistency?
- Reproducibility: Will you release code for data generation, task conversion (DSTC2), and training scripts alongside the datasets to facilitate standardized comparisons?

Overall, this paper sets a high bar for rigor and transparency in goal-oriented dialog evaluation. Its open benchmark, careful experiments, and honest assessment of limitations will be valuable to both the dialog and ML communities. I recommend acceptance.
