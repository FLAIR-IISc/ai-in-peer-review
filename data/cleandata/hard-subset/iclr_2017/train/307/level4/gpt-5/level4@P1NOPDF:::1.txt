This paper was posted to arXiv last week. It presents a new public dataset and a set of tasks for goal-oriented dialogue applications. The dataset and tasks are constructed artificially using rule-based programs to isolate and evaluate different aspects of dialogue system performance, from issuing API calls to displaying options, as well as full dialogues.

This is a welcome contribution to the dialogue literature that should facilitate future research on developing and understanding dialogue systems. That said, there are pitfalls to this approach. First, it is not clear how suitable deep learning models are for these tasks compared with traditional methods (rule-based systems or shallow models). Deep learning models typically require many training examples, and observed performance differences between neural networks may simply reflect different regularization choices. In addition, Tasks 1–5 are completely deterministic, so performance on these tasks does not measure a model’s ability to handle noisy or ambiguous interactions (e.g., inferring a distribution over user goals or executing dialogue repair strategies), which is a crucial aspect of real-world dialogue applications. Overall, I still believe this is an interesting direction to explore.

As discussed in the comments below, the paper lacks a baseline model that incorporates word order. I consider this a significant weakness because it makes the neural networks appear unreasonably strong, whereas simpler baselines could very likely be competitive—or even outperform—the proposed neural models. To maintain a fair evaluation and properly assess the value of representation learning for this task, it is important to include one additional non-neural benchmark model that accounts for word order. For example, one could use a logistic regression model with the following inputs: (1) word embeddings (similar to the Supervised Embeddings model), (2) bigram features, and (3) match-type features. If such a baseline is included, I will increase my rating to 8.

Final minor comment: in the conclusion, the paper states that “the existing work has no well defined measures of performances.” This is not accurate. End-to-end trainable models for task-oriented dialogue do have well-defined performance measures; see, for example, “A Network-based End-to-End Trainable Task-oriented Dialogue System” by Wen et al. While non-goal-oriented dialogue is generally harder to evaluate, it can still be assessed with human subjects. This is what Liu et al. (2016) do for Twitter. See also “Strategy and Policy Learning for Non-Task-Oriented Conversational Systems” by Yu et al.
