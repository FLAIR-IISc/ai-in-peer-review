This paper provides a much-needed reality check amid the 2016 trend of using chatbots for nearly every kind of human–computer interaction, accompanied by claims that they could handle dialogs far beyond simple chit-chat. While it is primarily relevant to dialog/NLP venues (to inform software engineers about the limitations of current chatbots), it also belongs in machine learning venues (to encourage more realistic validation of ML methods applied to dialog). I consider the work to be of high significance.

Two important conjectures underlie this paper and are likely to spur further research. Although they are not explicitly written, they were clearly stated by Antoine Bordes during a NIPS workshop presentation covering this work. Given the metrics used here:
- The performance of end-to-end ML approaches remains insufficient for goal-oriented dialogs.
- When comparing algorithms, relative performance on synthetic data is a good predictor of performance on natural data. This departs from prior observations, but the authors make a strong effort to align the synthetic and natural conditions.

The original algorithmic contribution is a relatively simple addition to memory networks (match type), but this is the first time they are deployed and tested on a goal-oriented dialog, and the experimental protocol is excellent. The paper is very clear and accessible to audiences beyond ML and dialog specialists. I was particularly impressed by the concise appendix summarizing memory networks and by the tables that illuminate the influence of the number of hops.

While this paper represents the state of the art in exploring more rigorous metrics for dialog modeling, it also underscores how brittle and somewhat arbitrary these metrics remain. This is more a recommendation for future research than a request for revision.

Evaluation metrics:
- The paper uses per-response accuracy (next-utterance classification among a fixed list of responses). Table 3 highlights how problematic (arguably absurd) this can be in practice: what mostly matters is a correct API call and a reasonably short dialog, yet per-response scoring would yield only 1/7 accuracy because the six preceding bot responses must also be exactly correct to reach that API call.
- Would per-dialog accuracy (all responses must be correct) be better? Table 2 shows extreme sensitivity to the experimental protocol. I was initially puzzled that accuracy for subtask T3 (0.0) was much lower than for the full dialog T5 (19.7), until the task definitions (3.1.1) clarified that T3 requires displaying three options whereas T5 requires displaying only one.
- For the concierge data, what would happen if “correct” meant returning the single best option, rather than being within the top five?

I do not fault the authors for using standard dialog metrics and proposing new ones that may even be pessimistic. Still, I suggest a more outcome-driven representation of goal-oriented dialogs that could yield more meaningful metrics. Consider a setting in which a virtual assistant service is paid upon successful completion of a dialog. What metric would maximize revenue? For the restaurant scenario, the loss might be a weighted sum of the number of errors in the API call, the number of turns required to reach that call, and the number of user-rejected options. However, such a loss cannot be measured on canned dialogs and would require either real human users or a realistic simulator.

Another issue, closely related to representation learning and not fully addressed here, concerns vocabulary mismatch between users and the knowledge base. For the match-type method to code “Indian” as a cuisine type, that exact token must appear in the KB. In cases where the KB uses obfuscated or domain-specific terminology, we would prefer ML to learn such associations rather than rely on humans to hand-specify them.

Overall, this is a strong and timely paper with a clean experimental design, a clear exposition, and a valuable contribution to the dialog community and the broader ML audience. Its findings, conjectures, and careful alignment of synthetic and natural data provide an important benchmark and a sober assessment of current end-to-end methods for goal-oriented dialog. My main concerns are about evaluation metrics and the handling of vocabulary mismatch; I view these primarily as directions for future work rather than reasons to reject.
