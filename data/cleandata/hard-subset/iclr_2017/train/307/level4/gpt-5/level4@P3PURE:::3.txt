SYNOPSIS:
This paper introduces a new dataset for evaluating end-to-end, goal-oriented dialog systems. All data is generated in a restaurant domain, where the goal is to find availability and ultimately book a table based on parameters provided by the user (e.g., cuisine, price range). Data is produced via simulation using an underlying knowledge base to sample parameter values, followed by rule-based transformations to render natural-language utterances. The task is to rank a set of candidate responses for each dialog turn, with evaluation reported as per-response accuracy and per-dialog accuracy. The authors show that Memory Networks outperform basic bag-of-words baselines.

THOUGHTS:
I thank the authors for an interesting contribution. That said, I remain skeptical about the utility of fully end-to-end trained systems in narrow domains. In open-domain settings, hand-coding all states and responses clearly does not scale, making end-to-end methods attractive. However, in constrained domains—where we understand the space well and the objective is high user satisfaction—it often seems preferable to leverage domain knowledge to engineer the best system possible.

Given the restricted domain, I am also disappointed that the task is to RANK rather than GENERATE responses, although I understand this simplifies evaluation. I am unclear how the candidate responses would be obtained in practice. The paper suggests that models rank the set of all responses in train/val/test (last sentence before Sec. 3.2). Since a key argument for end-to-end training is ease of scaling to new domains without manual re-engineering, where would this candidate set come from in a new domain? Generating responses would allow better generalization across domains than ranking a list of hand-collected generic responses; in my view, this is the weakest aspect of the work.

Finally, because data is generated via simulation by expanding (cuisine, price, …) tuples with NL-generation rules, the variability of training responses is necessarily constrained. This is traded off against the ability to generate unlimited data, but I could not find the list of rules used. Publishing these rules would be very helpful.

Overall, despite my skepticism, I believe this is an interesting contribution worthy of publication at the conference.

I have updated my score following the clarifications and new results.
