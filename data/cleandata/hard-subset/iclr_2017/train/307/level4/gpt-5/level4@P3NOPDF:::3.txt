SYNOPSIS
This paper introduces a new dataset for evaluating end-to-end, goal-oriented dialogue systems in a restaurant reservation setting. Dialogues are simulated using an underlying knowledge base (KB) to sample slot values (e.g., cuisine, location, price range), and rule-based transformations are then applied to produce natural-language utterances. The task is response selection: for each turn, the model ranks a fixed set of candidate responses, and performance is reported via per-response and per-dialog accuracy. The paper shows that Memory Networks outperform simple bag-of-words baselines on these metrics.

STRENGTHS
- A focused benchmark for goal-oriented dialogue: The dataset targets a canonical task (restaurant booking) and provides a controlled environment to compare methods.
- Clear evaluation protocol: Per-response and per-dialog accuracies are straightforward and reproducible.
- Scalable data generation: The simulator enables the creation of large training sets with controllable complexity.
- Empirical results: Memory Networks provide measurable improvements over basic baselines, suggesting that maintaining dialogue context and KB interactions is valuable.

CONCERNS AND QUESTIONS
- Value of end-to-end in narrow domains: In restricted domains, domain knowledge and explicit state design often yield high reliability and user satisfaction. The paper motivates end-to-end approaches by scalability, but the narrow setting weakens that argument. A discussion or comparison to a strong modular or rule-based system would help clarify the tradeoffs (engineering cost vs. performance, adaptability, maintenance).
- Response ranking vs. generation: The task formulation focuses on ranking from a finite candidate set rather than generating responses. While this simplifies evaluation, it limits the model’s ability to generalize to new domains and handle linguistic variability. For end-to-end approaches that purportedly scale with minimal manual engineering, generation is a more natural target.
- Candidate response set realism: It appears that the candidate list includes all responses from train/validation/test (as implied before Section 3.2). In practice, where would this candidate set come from in a new domain? If the list is curated by hand, the approach still relies on manual effort and may not scale. If it is mined from logs, how is coverage ensured for unseen intents or phrasing? Clarifying this pipeline and its feasibility in deployment is important.
- Limited linguistic variability: Because utterances are produced via rule-based transformations of slot tuples, the dataset may not capture the range of paraphrases, disfluencies, or pragmatic phenomena seen in real use. This could inflate performance for models that exploit surface regularities. Publishing the full set of generation rules (and releasing them with the dataset) would allow the community to assess coverage and introduce more variability.
- Generalization to unseen slots/entities: It is not clear how well models handle out-of-vocabulary entities or slot combinations that were unseen during training. Results on OOV splits or KB expansions would better support the claims about scalability.

SUGGESTIONS FOR IMPROVEMENT
- Provide the rule set and simulator: Release the natural-language generation rules alongside the dataset to increase transparency, encourage extensions, and enable more realistic paraphrase augmentation.
- Evaluate generation-based models: Include, at minimum, a sequence-to-sequence or copy/pointing-based model as a generative baseline, and report both automatic metrics and goal-completion or human preference where feasible.
- Clarify candidate selection: Describe in detail how candidate responses are constructed at train and test time, and propose a realistic mechanism for new domains (e.g., retrieval from a large unlabeled corpus, templated semantic realizations from predicted dialogue acts, or constrained generation).
- Stronger baselines: Add a modular, rule-based, or dialog-state-tracking baseline that leverages domain knowledge, to directly address the cost–benefit question in narrow domains.
- Robustness tests: Include OOV entity evaluations, paraphrase stress tests, or human paraphrase augmentations to measure resilience beyond the simulation distribution.
- Report per-dialog outcomes: Beyond accuracy per turn, provide success rates for end-to-end goal completion and error analyses that identify common failure modes (e.g., slot carryover, KB lookup errors, contradiction handling).

OVERALL ASSESSMENT
This is a valuable contribution that provides a clear, reproducible benchmark for end-to-end goal-oriented dialogue and demonstrates the potential of memory-augmented models. The main weakness lies in the reliance on response ranking from a fixed candidate set and the limited linguistic variability introduced by rule-based generation, both of which reduce the practical significance of the results and weaken the scalability argument. Addressing candidate construction in realistic settings, releasing the generation rules, and adding generation-based and modular baselines would substantially strengthen the paper.

Despite these concerns, I find the dataset and empirical analysis useful to the community and believe the work is worthy of publication. I have updated my score following the authors’ clarifications and new results.
