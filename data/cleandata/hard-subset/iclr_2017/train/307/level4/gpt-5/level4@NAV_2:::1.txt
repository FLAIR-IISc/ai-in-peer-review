1. Summary of contributions
The paper (recently posted to arXiv) introduces a public benchmark for goal-oriented dialogue, built via rule-based simulation in the restaurant-booking domain. It decomposes the problem into several tasks spanning issuing and updating API calls, presenting ranked options, and using KB results within full multi-turn dialogues. The testbed is designed for reproducibility and targeted error analysis, and is complemented by evaluations on DSTC2 and an online concierge dataset. The work also benchmarks a range of methods—including Memory Networks—highlighting both promising capabilities and current limitations of end-to-end models in transactional settings.

2. Decision
Weak accept. Primary reasons: (i) the benchmark is a timely, useful resource that will catalyze research on goal-oriented dialogue with clear, reproducible tasks; (ii) with the newly added results, the empirical study is more convincing, though further strengthening of non-neural baselines with word-order features would make the case even stronger.

3. Supporting arguments
- What problem is tackled? The paper seeks to rigorously evaluate end-to-end dialogue systems in goal-oriented contexts, where manipulating symbols, interfacing with KBs, and completing tasks within few turns are essential.
- Is the approach well motivated and well placed in the literature? Yes. Conventional slot-filling/POMDP frameworks work but are hard to scale; end-to-end neural approaches are promising yet under-tested for task-oriented requirements. The proposed controlled, decomposed tasks offer reproducibility and interpretable failure modes, and the related work discussion is appropriate.
- Do the results support the claims, and are they rigorous? Largely yes. The study compares rule-based, IR, supervised embeddings, and Memory Networks, including OOV splits and real-world data (DSTC2, concierge). Findings that MemNNs outperform IR and simple embedding baselines (but still struggle on option presentation and full-dialog success) are credible and consistent with the task design. That said, because Tasks 1–5 are fully deterministic and initially lacked a word-order-aware non-neural baseline, it was difficult to judge how much gains derive from representation learning vs. missing features. With the added results, the picture improves, but a stronger, explicitly word-order-aware non-neural baseline would further solidify conclusions.

4. Feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions, not all central to the decision)
- Strengthen non-neural baselines with word order. A competitive logistic regression or linear SVM baseline using: (a) averaged word embeddings (as in the Supervised Embeddings setup), (b) bi-gram (and possibly tri-gram) features, and (c) match-type features, would better calibrate the value of representation learning on these deterministic tasks.
- Clarify limitations of determinism. Since Tasks 1–5 prescribe a fixed dialogue management order, they cannot assess handling of noisy inputs, ambiguity, or repair strategies. It would help to explicitly position these tasks as necessary but not sufficient, and to discuss plans for non-deterministic variants (e.g., varied slot-question order, ASR-style noise).
- Refine claims around evaluation metrics. The conclusion currently suggests that prior work lacks well-defined performance measures. For task-oriented dialogue, end-to-end trainable systems have been evaluated with clear metrics (e.g., Wen et al., “A Network-based End-to-End Trainable Task-oriented Dialogue System”). For non-task-oriented settings, while automated metrics are imperfect, human evaluations (e.g., Liu et al., 2016 for Twitter; see also Yu et al., “Strategy and Policy Learning for Non-Task-Oriented Conversational Systems”) are established. Please revise to reflect this nuance.
- Report sensitivity and ablations. Briefly analyze data-size sensitivity, the impact of candidate-set construction, and ablations for match-type features (e.g., types only vs. exact matches) to clarify where gains arise.
- Bridging synthetic and real data. The inclusion of DSTC2 and concierge data is valuable. Consider adding a short analysis of error types unique to real data (e.g., ASR noise, user drift, non-standard entities) to guide future task design.

Questions for the authors
- Candidate set construction: Since candidates are pooled across tasks (including OOV), how do you prevent leakage of task-specific patterns and ensure this setup doesn’t advantage models via global frequency cues? Could you report results with per-task candidate sets as a control?
- Deterministic dialogue flow: How sensitive are results to the fixed order in which the bot solicits missing slots? Have you tried randomized slot-question orders or stochastic user behaviors, and how do models degrade under such variations?
- OOV handling and KB access: At training time, systems have access to both KBs. At test time on OOV splits, do models still see the OOV KB for type matching? Please clarify what information is available at inference and how you ensure no dialogue-text leakage.
- Word-order baselines: With the newly added experiments, can you provide a stronger non-neural baseline explicitly combining n-grams and match-type features (e.g., logistic regression or linear SVM) across all tasks, including OOV, and report per-dialog as well as per-response accuracy?
- Mapping DSTC2 to tasks: How do ASR errors and non-deterministic bot behaviors in DSTC2 affect label construction and evaluation? Could you share inter-annotator or alignment checks to validate the converted API calls and KB outputs?
- Robustness to noise: Have you experimented with synthetic noise (e.g., paraphrases, typos) or alternative word orders to test robustness of MemNNs and baselines?
- Entity ambiguity: How do match-type features behave when surface forms overlap across entity types (e.g., ambiguous tokens)? Any heuristics to disambiguate, and how does ambiguity affect performance?
