- Proposal: An open, reproducible testbed for end-to-end goal-oriented dialog, decomposing restaurant reservation into five synthetic tasks plus two real-data evaluations (DSTC2 reformulation and Concierge).
- Task design: Tasks probe state tracking, API issuing/updating, option sorting/presentation, and KB-grounded fact retrieval, including an OOV setup to test generalization.
- Evaluation: Uses next-utterance ranking with per-response and per-dialog accuracy as primary metrics.
- Baselines: Benchmarks several models (rule-based, IR, supervised embeddings, Memory Networks with/without match-type features).
- Empirical findings: MemN2N shows promising per-response performance and learns behaviors like API calls and OOV handling with type features, but per-dialog success is limited.
- Transfer claim: Synthetic tasks provide diagnostic signals that correlate with behavior on real datasets (DSTC2 and Concierge).
- Decision: Weak accept — paper is a valuable, reproducible benchmark with clear empirical insights.
- Key reason for accept: Fills a needed gap for controlled, KB-grounded, goal-oriented evaluation and highlights strengths/weaknesses of current end-to-end architectures.
- Problem importance: Timely and under-resourced question of whether end-to-end models can replace or complement slot-filling systems.
- Motivation/placement: Well-motivated relative to chit-chat and DST benchmarks; provides controlled, task-decomposed evaluation with OOV stress tests.
- Evidence: Results support claims—MemN2N outperforms simpler baselines and consistent rankings across synthetic and real data validate the suite’s usefulness.
- Limitations highlighted: Notable gap between per-response and per-dialog performance, especially for option ranking and multi-step reasoning.
- Rigor: OOV setup and ablations (match-type, memory hops) are thoughtful; ranking formulation trades realism for reproducibility.
- Ranking vs. generation feedback: Recommend adding sequence-generation baselines (seq2seq, pointer/copy, KB-augmented decoders) for realism and domain scaling.
- Candidate set design feedback: Ranking over train/val/test candidates may leak test information and reduce deployment realism; suggest restricted or turn-specific candidate sets and sensitivity analyses.
- Language variability feedback: Simulator’s limited templates and deterministic order risk overestimating robustness; publish templates and add paraphrased/noisy variants.
- Metrics feedback: Complement per-dialog accuracy with task success under noise, slot accuracy, API-argument F1, and option-order correctness for finer diagnostics.
- Error analysis feedback: Expand qualitative analysis for Tasks 3 and 5 (sorting, multi-hop KB aggregation, acceptance/decline loops) to guide architectural improvements.
- Domain generality feedback: Discuss porting to other transactional domains (travel, retail) and whether match-type features generalize across ontologies.
- Reproducibility feedback: Urge release of simulator, KBs, NL templates, rule-based DSTC2 system, and candidate-generation scripts.
- Q1 (candidate responses): Justify ranking over train/val/test candidates and report results when candidates are restricted to training-only or turn-constrained sets.
- Q2 (generation baselines): Report whether sequence generation models (seq2seq, pointer-generator, copy with KB linking) were attempted and their per-dialog performance.
- Q3 (template and KB release): Confirm releasing simulator templates/rules and full KBs (including OOV split) or at least enumerate templates and dialog policies.
- Q4 (determinism and ordering): Report sensitivity to deterministic slot-question order and option acceptance process, and results when randomized.
- Q5 (OOV mechanism): Clarify assumed KB access for match-type features, entity matching method (exact/normalized), and robustness to surface variation (misspellings, synonyms).
- Q6 (DSTC2 conversion): Assess whether the DSTC2 reformulation introduces artifacts favoring certain models and quantify disagreements with original system behavior.
- Q7 (data efficiency): Provide learning curves per task/model (including match-type ablations) to show sample efficiency and saturation points.
- Q8 (human evaluation): State whether human evaluations (next-utterance preference or task success) were performed for Concierge and DSTC2 to complement ranking metrics.
