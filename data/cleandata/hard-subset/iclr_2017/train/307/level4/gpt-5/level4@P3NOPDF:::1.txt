Summary
This paper introduces a new public dataset and a suite of tasks for goal-oriented dialogue. The tasks are generated with rule-based programs to target specific capabilities, from issuing API calls and presenting options to conducting full dialogues. A version of the paper appeared on arXiv last week. The contribution is timely and likely to facilitate research on building and analyzing task-oriented dialogue systems.

Strengths
- A clearly defined benchmark that decomposes dialogue capabilities into targeted tasks, enabling more systematic evaluation.
- Public release of data and tasks, which can standardize comparisons and accelerate progress in the community.
- The focus on goal-oriented dialogue is valuable, complementing existing work on open-domain chat.

Main concerns
1) Suitability and interpretability of deep learning performance on synthetic tasks:
- It is unclear how advantageous deep models are for these tasks relative to simpler rule-based or shallow models. Given that deep models often require large datasets and are sensitive to regularization, observed differences between neural architectures may reflect training and tuning choices rather than representational power.
- Tasks 1–5 are fully deterministic. As a result, they do not assess a model’s ability to handle noise, ambiguity, or user variability, such as inferring distributions over user goals, performing clarification and repair strategies, or recovering from errors. These abilities are central to practical dialogue systems.

2) Missing baseline with word order:
- The paper lacks a non-neural baseline that captures word order. This omission is a significant weakness because it can make the neural models appear unreasonably strong when, in fact, simpler order-aware baselines might be competitive or superior.
- To fairly assess the value of learned representations for these tasks, the authors should include at least one strong non-neural benchmark that models word order. For example, a logistic regression or linear classifier with:
  - Word embeddings as features (as in the Supervised Embeddings model),
  - Bi-gram features to capture local order,
  - Match-type features to encode slot and entity matches.
- Including such a baseline would substantially improve the evaluation’s credibility. If this baseline is added and reported thoroughly, I would raise my rating to 8.

Additional suggestions
- Consider augmenting the tasks with stochastic user behaviors or noise (e.g., paraphrases, typos, ellipses, missing or conflicting information). This would better test robustness and generalization, bringing the benchmark closer to real-world conditions.
- Include analyses that disentangle data size effects and regularization choices from architectural benefits (e.g., learning curves, parameter sweeps, and ablations).

Minor comments
- The conclusion states that “the existing work has no well defined measures of performances.” This is not accurate. End-to-end trainable task-oriented dialogue systems do have clear evaluation metrics (e.g., task success, slot accuracy, API call correctness). See, for example, “A Network-based End-to-End Trainable Task-oriented Dialogue System” by Wen et al. For non-goal-oriented dialogue, evaluation is harder but still possible via human studies and task proxies; see Liu et al. (2016) for Twitter-based evaluations and “Strategy and Policy Learning for Non-Task-Oriented Conversational Systems” by Yu et al.
- Please proofread for minor typographical errors and consistency (e.g., “more convincingly,” avoid duplicated words like “be be,” consistent use of “performance” vs. “performances”).

Overall evaluation
This is a valuable and well-motivated contribution: a public, targeted benchmark for goal-directed dialogue that can catalyze research. However, the current evaluation setup limits the strength of the conclusions. The deterministic nature of several tasks and the absence of an order-sensitive non-neural baseline make it difficult to attribute performance gains to deep architectures rather than to dataset properties or implementation details. Addressing these concerns—especially by adding a competitive, word-order-aware baseline and, if possible, incorporating noisy or ambiguous phenomena—would significantly strengthen the paper’s empirical claims and practical relevance.

I have updated my score based on the new results added in the revision.
