Attempts to deploy chatbots across all forms of human-computer interaction were a major trend in 2016, accompanied by claims that they could handle dialogs far beyond simple chit-chat. This paper provides a valuable and sobering reality check. While most directly relevant to dialog and natural language processing venues – educating software engineers about the limitations of current chatbots – it also merits publication at machine learning conferences to inform researchers about the need for more rigorous validation of ML models applied to dialog systems. I therefore consider this work to be of high significance.

Two important conjectures underpin this paper and are likely to stimulate further research. Though not explicitly stated, Antoine Bordes clearly articulated them during a NIPS workshop presentation on this work. These conjectures, given the metrics employed in the paper, are: 1) the performance of end-to-end machine learning approaches remains insufficient for goal-oriented dialogs, and 2) relative performance on synthetic data is a surprisingly good predictor of performance on natural data. This would represent a significant departure from previous findings, but the authors have made a substantial effort to align the synthetic and natural conditions. 

While the original algorithmic contribution consists of a relatively simple addition to memory networks – a new match type – this is the first time this approach has been deployed and tested on a goal-oriented dialog system, and the experimental protocol is excellent. The paper is exceptionally clear and accessible to readers from both machine learning and dialog research backgrounds. I was particularly impressed by the concise and informative appendix on memory networks, which effectively summarized the underlying architecture, followed by tables clearly illustrating the influence of the number of hops.

This paper represents the state-of-the-art in exploring more rigorous metrics for dialog modeling, but also highlights how fragile and somewhat arbitrary these metrics remain. This is more a suggestion for future research than a request for revision.

The authors utilize per-response accuracy, essentially classifying the next utterance from a fixed list of responses. Table 3 clearly demonstrates the limitations of this metric in practice: success is largely determined by a correct API call and a reasonably short dialog length, which would only yield an accuracy of 1/7 given that all six preceding bot responses must also be exact.

Would per-dialog accuracy, requiring all responses to be correct, be a better alternative? Table 2 reveals its sensitivity to the experimental protocol. I was initially puzzled by the significantly lower accuracy for subtask T3 (0.0) compared to the full dialog task T5 (19.7), until the authors clarified the task definitions (3.1.1). T3 requires displaying three options, whereas T5 only requires displaying one.

Regarding the concierge data, what would happen if ‘correct’ were defined as ‘best’ rather than simply being within the top 5 responses?

While I find no fault with the authors’ use of standard dialog metrics, and acknowledge the potential for overly pessimistic alternatives, I envision one way to represent dialogs that could lead to more meaningful metrics in goal-oriented settings. If I were to offer Virtual Assistants as a service, paid upon successful dialog completion, the appropriate metric would maximize my revenue. In this restaurant reservation problem, the loss function might be a weighted sum of errors in the API call, the number of turns required to reach that call, and the number of rejected options by the user. However, such a loss function cannot be measured on pre-defined dialogs and would necessitate either a real human user or a realistic simulator.

Another issue, closely related to representation learning, that this paper does not fully address is the potential mismatch between the vocabulary used by the user and the vocabulary present in the knowledge base. Specifically, for the match type algorithm to correctly categorize ‘Indian’ as ‘type of cuisine,’ that word must appear exactly in the knowledge base. I can readily imagine scenarios where the knowledge base employs obfuscated terminology, and we would want machine learning to *learn* these associations rather than relying on explicit, human-defined mappings.
