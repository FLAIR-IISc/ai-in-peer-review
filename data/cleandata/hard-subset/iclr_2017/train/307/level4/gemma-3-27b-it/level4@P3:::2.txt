This paper represents a valuable and timely reality check on the progress of end-to-end dialog systems, moving beyond the successes in chit-chat to tackle the more challenging domain of goal-oriented conversations. While the algorithmic contribution – a simple addition of match-type features to Memory Networks – is incremental, the paper's strength lies in its carefully designed testbed, thorough experimentation, and insightful analysis. The work is of high significance, potentially impacting both the Natural Language Processing and Machine Learning communities by highlighting the need for more realistic validation of ML applied to dialog.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper addresses a critical question in the field, presents a well-motivated approach, and provides convincing evidence supporting its claims. The experimental protocol is excellent, and the paper is clearly written and accessible.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper convincingly demonstrates that current end-to-end models struggle with goal-oriented dialog, even in a relatively narrow domain like restaurant reservation. The authors identify two key conjectures – the insufficiency of current performance and the predictive power of synthetic data compared to natural data – and structure their work to investigate these. The introduction of a testbed comprising five tasks, along with evaluation on both synthetic and real-world datasets (DSTC2 and an online concierge service), is a significant contribution. The consistent pattern of results across these datasets strongly suggests that performance on the synthetic tasks is a reliable proxy for real-world performance. The detailed ablation studies, particularly the inclusion of match-type features, provide valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the Memory Network architecture.  The analysis of per-response versus per-dialog accuracy is particularly insightful, highlighting the limitations of standard evaluation metrics and prompting a discussion on more meaningful alternatives.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong, several points could be addressed to further improve its impact:

*   **Metric Limitations:** The authors rightly point out the limitations of per-response and per-dialog accuracy. Exploring alternative metrics that better reflect real-world value (e.g., a cost-based metric representing revenue maximization for a virtual assistant service) would be valuable. While acknowledging the difficulty of applying such metrics to canned dialogs, outlining potential avenues for future research in this area would be beneficial.
*   **Vocabulary Mismatch:** The paper briefly touches upon the issue of vocabulary mismatch between user input and the knowledge base. Expanding on this limitation and exploring potential solutions (e.g., learning entity mappings) could strengthen the work. Investigating how the model handles synonyms or paraphrases would also be useful.
*   **Error Analysis:** While the paper provides some insights into the types of errors the model makes, a more detailed error analysis could be helpful. Identifying specific scenarios where the model consistently fails (e.g., complex reasoning, ambiguous user requests) would inform future research directions.
*   **Match Type Feature Detail:** Could the authors clarify the process of determining which words are 'typed' beyond simply stating that they appear as KB entities? A clearer description of the matching process would be useful.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Regarding the observation that per-dialog accuracy is highly sensitive to experimental protocol (e.g., Task 3 vs. Task 5), could you elaborate on the factors that contribute to this sensitivity? Are there ways to design tasks that are less susceptible to these variations?
2.  For the concierge data, how would the results change if 'correct' was defined as being among the top-N predicted responses (e.g., top-5) rather than requiring an exact match to the best response?
3.  You mention the potential for a cost-based metric to better represent real-world value. Could you provide a more concrete example of how such a metric might be formulated for the restaurant reservation task, including specific weighting factors for different types of errors?
4.  What strategies could be employed to address the vocabulary mismatch issue, allowing the model to learn associations between user terms and KB entities even when exact matches are not available?
