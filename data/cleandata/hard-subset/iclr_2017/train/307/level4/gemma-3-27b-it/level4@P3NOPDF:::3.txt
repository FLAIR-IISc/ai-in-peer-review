This paper introduces a new dataset for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems, focused on the restaurant reservation domain. The dataset consists of dialogs generated through a simulation that leverages an underlying knowledge base to produce variations in parameters such as cuisine and price range, which are then rendered into natural language using rule-based transformations. The task is framed as ranking a set of candidate responses for each turn in the dialog, with evaluation metrics reported as per-response and per-dialog accuracy. The authors demonstrate that Memory Networks outperform basic bag-of-words baselines on this task.

I thank the authors for this interesting contribution. However, I harbor some skepticism regarding the utility of fully end-to-end trained systems within narrow, well-defined domains. The strongest justification for end-to-end approaches lies in the open-domain setting, where the complexity and scale preclude exhaustive hand-coding of states and responses. In contrast, narrow domains typically benefit from thorough domain understanding, allowing for the engineering of highly optimized systems specifically designed for user satisfaction. Therefore, it’s worth questioning whether an end-to-end approach is the most effective strategy when explicit domain knowledge is readily available.

My concerns are compounded by the choice to *rank* candidate responses rather than *generate* them, although I appreciate the practical benefits this brings to evaluation. A significant practical question arises: how are these candidate responses obtained in a real-world deployment? The paper suggests that the models rank from the set of responses seen during training, validation, or testing (last sentence before Section 3.2). While this approach is suitable for evaluation, it seems to undermine the core argument for end-to-end training – namely, the ability to easily scale to new domains without extensive manual re-engineering. For a new domain, where would this set of candidate responses originate? A generative model would offer considerably improved generalization capabilities, moving beyond simply ranking pre-defined responses to formulating novel utterances, and this represents a critical limitation of the current work in my opinion. 

Furthermore, the dataset’s generation process, reliant on a simulation and rule-based natural language generation, inherently restricts the variability of the training data. This is a necessary trade-off for the ability to generate a large volume of data. However, the specific rules used to expand parameter tuples into natural language descriptions are not detailed in the paper. Publishing these rules would significantly enhance reproducibility and allow for a better understanding of the data’s inherent biases and limitations.

Despite these concerns, I believe this work represents a valuable contribution to the field and is worthy of publication at the conference. The dataset itself is a useful resource, and the initial results demonstrating the effectiveness of Memory Networks provide a solid foundation for future research.
