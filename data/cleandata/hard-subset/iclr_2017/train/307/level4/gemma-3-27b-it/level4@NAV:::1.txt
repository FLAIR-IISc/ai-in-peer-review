This paper introduces a novel, publicly available dataset and associated tasks designed for the evaluation of goal-oriented dialogue systems. The dataset and tasks are generated through rule-based programs, enabling controlled assessment of dialogue system capabilities – from API call execution and option presentation to complete dialogue management. 

This contribution is valuable to the dialogue research community and offers a useful resource for future work. However, the artificial construction raises concerns. Specifically, the potential advantage of Deep Learning models over traditional approaches (rule-based or shallow learning methods) is unclear, given the known data requirements of deep learning and the possibility that performance differences may primarily reflect variations in regularization. Furthermore, the deterministic nature of tasks 1-5 limits the evaluation of crucial dialogue skills like handling noisy input, resolving ambiguity, inferring user intent distributions, and employing dialogue repair mechanisms. Nevertheless, the overall direction explored in this work remains interesting.

A key weakness lies in the absence of a baseline model incorporating word order information. This omission inflates the apparent performance of the neural networks, while simpler models leveraging word order may prove competitive or superior. To ensure a fair evaluation and accurately gauge the benefits of representation learning in this context, the inclusion of an additional non-neural baseline that accounts for word order is essential. For instance, a logistic regression model utilizing word embeddings, bi-gram features, and match-type features could serve this purpose. Incorporating such a baseline would strengthen the paper’s conclusions and justify the use of Deep Learning, leading to an increased rating of 8. 

Finally, the claim in the conclusion that existing work lacks well-defined performance measures is inaccurate. End-to-end trainable task-oriented dialogue systems, such as that presented by Wen et al., already employ established metrics. While evaluating non-task-oriented dialogue is more challenging, methods exist – as demonstrated by Liu et al. (2016) and Yu et al. – that utilize human evaluation.
