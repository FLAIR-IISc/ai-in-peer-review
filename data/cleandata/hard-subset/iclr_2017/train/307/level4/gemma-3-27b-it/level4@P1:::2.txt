Attempts to use chatbots for every form of human-computer interaction has been a major trend, with claims that they could solve many dialogs beyond simple chit-chat. This paper represents a serious reality check. While it is most relevant for Dialog/Natural Language venues (to educate software engineers about the limitations of current chatbots), it can also be published at Machine Learning venues (to educate researchers about the need for more realistic validation of ML applied to dialogs), so I consider this work of high significance.

Two important conjectures underlie this paper and are likely to open avenues for further research. While not explicitly stated, Antoine Bordes articulated them during a NIPS workshop presentation on this work: 1) the performance of end-to-end ML approaches remains insufficient for goal-oriented dialogs; and 2) relative performance on synthetic data is a good predictor of performance on natural data. This would be a notable departure from previous observations, but the authors have made a strong effort to align synthetic and natural conditions.

The paper’s original algorithmic contribution—a relatively simple addition to Memory Networks (match type features)—is notable as the first deployment and testing of these features on a goal-oriented dialog task. The experimental protocol is excellent, and the overall paper is clear and accessible to a readership beyond ML and dialog researchers. I was particularly impressed by how concisely the appendix summarized Memory Networks and then explained the influence of the number of hops through clear tables. 

While this paper represents the state-of-the-art in exploring more rigorous metrics for dialog modeling, it also reminds us how brittle and somewhat arbitrary these metrics remain. This is more a recommendation for future research than a revision request. 

The use of per-response accuracy (essentially next utterance classification among a fixed list of responses) feels limited. Table 3 illustrates how this can be misleading in practice: a correct API call and a reasonably short dialog might only yield 1/7 accuracy, given the need for exact matches in the preceding bot responses. 

Would per-dialog accuracy, requiring all responses to be correct, be a better metric? Table 2 shows its sensitivity to the experimental protocol. I initially found the lower accuracy for subtask T3 (0.0) compared to the full dialog T5 (19.7) puzzling, until the authors clarified in the task definitions (3.1.1) that T3 requires displaying three options while T5 requires only one.

Regarding the concierge data, how would results change if ‘correct’ meant being among the top five responses, rather than being the absolute best?

While I cannot fault the authors for using standard dialog metrics – nor for recognizing the difficulty of devising new, less pessimistic ones – I can envision a representation of dialogs that might yield more meaningful metrics for goal-oriented tasks. If one were selling Virtual Assistants as a service, paid upon successful completion of a dialog, the relevant metric would maximize revenue. In this restaurant problem, the loss function might be a weighted sum of errors in the API call, the number of turns to reach that call, and the number of rejected options by the user. However, such a loss function cannot be measured on canned dialogs and would require either a real human user or a realistic simulator.

Another issue, closely related to representation learning, that this paper does not fully address is the problem of vocabulary mismatch between the user and the knowledge base. Specifically, for the match type algorithm to recognize ‘Indian’ as a ‘type of cuisine’, that word must appear exactly in the KB. I can imagine scenarios where the KB uses specialized terminology, and we would want ML to *learn* the associations rather than requiring humans to hand-define them.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper tackles a crucial problem – evaluating the capabilities of end-to-end dialog systems in goal-oriented settings – and provides a valuable resource in the form of the proposed testbed. The experimental results are well-presented and insightful, clearly demonstrating the limitations of current approaches.

**Supporting Arguments:**

*   **Problem Addressed:** The paper directly addresses the key question of whether end-to-end models can compete with traditional methods in goal-oriented dialog and identifies specific areas where they fall short.
*   **Approach & Motivation:** The proposed testbed is well-motivated and fills a critical gap in the evaluation of dialog systems. The focus on reproducibility and controlled experimentation is commendable.
*   **Results & Rigor:** The experimental results are thorough and supported by a clear description of the methodology. The comparison with a rule-based baseline and evaluation on multiple datasets strengthens the findings. The analysis of per-response vs. per-dialog accuracy highlights the nuances of evaluation.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   Consider discussing the potential for using reinforcement learning to address the per-dialog accuracy issue, as a reward function incorporating the factors mentioned above (API call errors, turn count, rejected options) could be a natural fit.
*   Exploring methods to bridge the vocabulary gap between users and knowledge bases would be a valuable future direction.
*   The discussion of evaluation metrics is excellent. Expanding on the challenges of creating meaningful metrics for goal-oriented dialogs could be beneficial.
