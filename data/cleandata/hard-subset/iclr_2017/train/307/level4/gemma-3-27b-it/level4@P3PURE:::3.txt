SYNOPSIS:
This paper introduces a new dataset designed for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. The data focuses on the restaurant reservation domain, where the objective is to determine availability and ultimately book a table based on user-provided parameters within a dialog. Data generation utilizes a simulation driven by an underlying knowledge base to produce samples for various parameters (cuisine, price range, etc.), followed by rule-based transformations to render natural language descriptions. The task is framed as ranking a set of candidate responses for each dialog turn, with evaluation reported using per-response and per-dialog accuracy. The authors demonstrate that Memory Networks outperform basic bag-of-words baselines.

THOUGHTS:
I thank the authors for this interesting contribution. However, I remain skeptical regarding the utility of end-to-end trained systems in narrow-domain settings. While end-to-end training is compelling in open-domain scenarios—where hand-coding states and responses would not scale—narrow domains typically benefit from leveraging well-understood domain knowledge to engineer systems optimized for high user satisfaction. Doesn’t this suggest that explicitly incorporating domain expertise is preferable in these cases?

Given the domain’s inherent restrictions, I am also somewhat disappointed that the task focuses on *ranking* rather than *generating* responses, although I recognize this simplifies evaluation. Furthermore, it is unclear how these candidate responses would be obtained in a practical deployment. The models appear to rank the complete set of responses present in the training, validation, and test sets (as noted in the last sentence before Section 3.2). Considering that a primary justification for end-to-end training is its potential for scaling to new domains without extensive manual re-engineering, it is unclear where this information would come from in a novel domain. Generating responses would facilitate better generalization to new domains, rather than simply ranking a pre-defined list of hand-collected responses; this aspect represents, in my view, the weakest point of this work. 

Finally, the data generation process, relying on a simulation and expanding tuples (e.g., cuisine, price) using NL-generation rules, inherently limits the variability of the training responses. While this is a trade-off for the ability to generate an unlimited dataset, the specific rules used were not provided. Publishing this list of rules would be beneficial.

Overall, despite my reservations, I believe this is an interesting contribution worthy of publication at the conference.
