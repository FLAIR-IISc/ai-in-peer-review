This paper presents a new, publicly available dataset and associated tasks designed for goal-oriented dialogue applications. The dataset and tasks are constructed artificially using rule-based programs, enabling evaluation of a wide range of dialogue system performance aspects, from API calls and option display to full dialogue management. 

This is a welcome contribution to the dialogue research community and has the potential to facilitate future work in developing and understanding dialogue systems. However, the artificial construction of the dataset introduces potential limitations. It is unclear whether deep learning models are particularly well-suited to these tasks compared to traditional approaches, such as rule-based or shallow models. Deep learning methods typically require large amounts of training data, and observed performance differences between neural networks may be attributable to regularization techniques rather than fundamental representational advantages. Furthermore, tasks 1-5 are entirely deterministic, which limits the evaluation of a model’s ability to handle the noisy and ambiguous interactions inherent in real-world dialogue – for example, inferring user goals from incomplete information or employing dialogue repair strategies. Despite these concerns, I believe this represents an interesting research direction.

A significant weakness of the current evaluation is the absence of baseline models incorporating word order information. This omission makes the neural network performance appear stronger than it may be, as simpler baselines leveraging word order could potentially be competitive or even outperform the proposed models. To ensure a fair evaluation and accurately assess the benefits of representation learning in this context, I strongly recommend the authors experiment with an additional non-neural network benchmark. For example, a logistic regression model utilizing 1) word embeddings (similar to the Supervised Embeddings model), 2) bi-gram features, and 3) match-type features would be a valuable addition. Inclusion of such a baseline would strengthen the paper’s conclusions and justify the use of deep learning, and would lead me to increase my rating to 8. 

Finally, the paper’s claim in the conclusion that “the existing work has no well-defined measures of performance” is inaccurate. End-to-end trainable task-oriented dialogue systems *do* have established performance metrics (e.g., Wen et al., “A Network-based End-to-End Trainable Task-oriented Dialogue System”). While non-task-oriented dialogue evaluation presents greater challenges, it is possible with human subject evaluation, as demonstrated by Liu et al. (2016) for Twitter data and Yu et al. ("Strategy and Policy Learning for Non-Task-Oriented Conversational Systems").
