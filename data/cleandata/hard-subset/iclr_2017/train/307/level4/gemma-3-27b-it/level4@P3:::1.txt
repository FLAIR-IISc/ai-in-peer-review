This paper presents a new, publicly available dataset and set of tasks designed for evaluating goal-oriented dialogue systems. The core idea is to create a controlled environment – built using rule-based programs – to assess specific capabilities like API calling, information presentation, and complete dialogue management. This is a valuable contribution, as it addresses the need for reproducible and interpretable evaluation methods in this field. The authors demonstrate the utility of their testbed by applying a Memory Network model and comparing it to several baselines.

**Reject.** While the paper identifies an important problem and proposes a potentially useful resource, the results do not yet demonstrate a compelling advantage for end-to-end neural models over simpler, more traditional methods. The core issue is that the Memory Network’s performance, while promising, is not consistently or significantly superior to the baselines, given the computational complexity and data requirements of neural networks. This raises questions about whether the tasks are sufficiently challenging to highlight the strengths of deep learning approaches.

The paper’s primary strength lies in its identification of the need for a standardized evaluation benchmark. The construction of the dataset and tasks is well-motivated, providing a breakdown of goal-oriented dialogue into testable sub-components. The comparison to a rule-based baseline and classical information retrieval methods is also a positive aspect, offering a relative performance perspective. The inclusion of data from the Dialog State Tracking Challenge and an online concierge service further strengthens the argument for the generalizability of the testbed. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of existing evaluation metrics and the benefits of their approach in terms of reproducibility and interpretability.

However, the experimental results are not convincing enough to warrant acceptance. The Memory Network achieves improvements over some baselines, but the per-dialog accuracy remains low, indicating limited success in handling complete dialogues. Furthermore, the authors acknowledge that simpler models can perform competitively on some tasks. The lack of a strong baseline incorporating word order information (as noted in the original review and addressed in later revisions) initially weakened the assessment of the Memory Network's advantages. While the inclusion of match-type features improves performance, it doesn’t fundamentally alter the conclusion that the tasks, as currently defined, may not be sufficiently discriminatory. The initial claim that existing work lacks well-defined performance measures was also inaccurate and has been addressed by the authors.

Here are some suggestions for improving the paper:

*   **Increase Task Complexity:** Consider adding variations to the tasks to increase their difficulty and require more sophisticated reasoning abilities. For example, introduce ambiguity in user requests, require handling of implicit confirmations, or incorporate multi-turn reasoning.
*   **Explore More Robust Baselines:** Experiment with more advanced non-neural network baselines that capture word order information, such as those incorporating n-grams or syntactic features. Logistic regression with combined features (embeddings, bi-grams, match types) as suggested in the initial review would be a good starting point.
*   **Error Analysis:** Conduct a more detailed error analysis to identify specific types of errors made by the Memory Network. This would provide valuable insights into the model's weaknesses and guide future research.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Perform ablation studies to assess the contribution of different components of the Memory Network, such as the match-type features or the number of hops.
*   **Clarify Evaluation Metrics:** While the paper defines per-response and per-dialog accuracy, it might be helpful to explore other relevant metrics, such as task completion rate or user satisfaction (even if simulated).

I have questions for the authors:

*   Given the limited improvement of the Memory Network over the baselines, do you believe the current tasks are sufficiently complex to effectively evaluate end-to-end dialogue systems? If not, what modifications could be made to increase the task difficulty?
*   The paper highlights the importance of handling out-of-vocabulary entities. How do you envision this testbed being used to evaluate more sophisticated techniques for handling OOV words, such as subword modeling or entity linking?
*   Could you provide more detail on the specific types of errors the Memory Network makes? Are there any recurring patterns in the errors that suggest specific areas for improvement?
*   How sensitive are the results to the specific hyperparameters of the Memory Network? Have you performed a thorough hyperparameter search?
