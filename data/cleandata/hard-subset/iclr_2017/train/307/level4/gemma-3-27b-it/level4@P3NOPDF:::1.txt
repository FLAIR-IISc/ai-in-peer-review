This paper introduces a novel, publicly available dataset and a suite of tasks designed for evaluating goal-oriented dialogue systems. The dataset and tasks are artificially constructed using rule-based programs, enabling controlled assessment of system performance across a range of capabilities – from API call execution and option presentation to complete dialogue management.

This contribution is a welcome addition to the dialogue research community. The availability of a controlled environment for evaluation has the potential to accelerate progress in developing and understanding dialogue systems, particularly in the task-oriented domain. However, the chosen methodology presents certain limitations. Specifically, the suitability of deep learning models for these tasks, compared to traditional rule-based or shallow learning approaches, is not fully established. Deep learning methods generally require substantial training data, and observed performance differences between neural network architectures may largely reflect variations in regularization rather than fundamental representational advantages. Furthermore, Tasks 1-5 are entirely deterministic. This precludes evaluation of a dialogue system’s ability to handle the inherent noise and ambiguity of real-world interactions, such as inferring user intent from imperfect input or employing dialogue repair mechanisms – crucial components of successful dialogue applications. Despite these concerns, I believe the work represents an interesting and potentially valuable direction for the field.

A significant weakness of the current evaluation is the absence of a baseline model incorporating word order information. This omission leads to an inflated perception of the neural network’s performance, as simpler, yet potentially competitive (or superior) baselines have not been explored. To provide a fairer assessment and more convincingly demonstrate the benefits of representation learning, I strongly recommend that the authors include at least one non-neural network baseline that accounts for word order. For example, a logistic regression model utilizing 1) word embeddings (akin to the “Supervised Embeddings” model presented in the paper), 2) bi-gram features, and 3) match-type features would be a relevant and informative comparison. Inclusion of such a baseline would substantially strengthen the paper’s conclusions and justify the application of more complex deep learning architectures. I would revise my rating to an 8 if this baseline is incorporated.

Finally, the claim in the conclusion that “the existing work has no well-defined measures of performance” is inaccurate. Existing end-to-end trainable task-oriented dialogue systems *do* employ well-defined performance metrics. For instance, Wen et al. (“A Network-based End-to-End Trainable Task-oriented Dialogue System”) provides a relevant example. While evaluation of non-task-oriented dialogue remains challenging, it is also achievable, often through human subject evaluation as demonstrated by Liu et al. (2016) for Twitter conversations and Yu et al. (“Strategy and Policy Learning for Non-Task-Oriented Conversational Systems”).
