This paper introduces a new, publicly available dataset and accompanying tasks designed for goal-oriented dialogue applications. The dataset and tasks are synthetically generated using rule-based programs, allowing for evaluation of various dialogue system capabilities, from API calls and option presentation to complete dialogue interactions.

This contribution is valuable to the dialogue research community and has the potential to facilitate progress in the development and understanding of dialogue systems. However, the synthetic nature of the data raises some concerns. It is unclear whether deep learning models are demonstrably superior to traditional methods—such as rule-based or shallow models—for these tasks, given the large amounts of training data typically required by deep learning and the possibility that performance differences may primarily reflect variations in regularization techniques. Furthermore, the deterministic nature of tasks 1-5 limits the evaluation of a model’s ability to handle the noise and ambiguity inherent in real-world dialogue, including inferring user intent distributions and implementing dialogue repair mechanisms – crucial aspects of practical dialogue systems. Despite these limitations, I believe this is a worthwhile research direction.

A significant weakness of the paper, as discussed in the comments, is the absence of a baseline model incorporating word order information. This omission potentially overstates the performance of the neural networks, as simpler baselines considering word order could be competitive or even outperform them. To ensure a fair evaluation and accurately assess the benefits of representation learning in this context, I recommend the authors include an additional, non-neural network benchmark. For example, they could experiment with a logistic regression model utilizing 1) word embeddings (similar to their Supervised Embeddings model), 2) bi-gram features, and 3) match-type features. Inclusion of such a baseline would strengthen the conclusions and I would revise my rating to an 8.

Finally, the claim in the conclusion that “existing work has no well-defined measures of performance” is inaccurate. End-to-end trainable task-oriented dialogue systems already possess established performance metrics, as demonstrated by work such as Wen et al. ("A Network-based End-to-End Trainable Task-oriented Dialogue System"). While evaluating non-task-oriented dialogue remains challenging, it is also possible with human subjects, as shown in the work of Liu et al. (2016) on Twitter data and Yu et al. (“Strategy and Policy Learning for Non-Task-Oriented Conversational Systems”).
