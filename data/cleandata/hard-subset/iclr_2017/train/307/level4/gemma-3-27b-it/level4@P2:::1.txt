This paper introduces a new, publicly available dataset and a set of tasks designed for evaluating goal-oriented dialogue systems. The resource focuses on a restaurant reservation scenario and breaks down the problem into several subtasks, ranging from API call generation to complete dialogue management. This is a valuable contribution to the field, as it provides a controlled environment for researching and understanding the capabilities of dialogue systems. However, several aspects warrant consideration.

**Decision:** Reject. While the dataset and tasks are well-motivated and represent a useful step forward, the current experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate the advantage of end-to-end neural models over simpler, traditional approaches. The performance gap isn't substantial enough to justify the complexity of the proposed neural network architecture.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper highlights a critical issue: the difficulty of scaling traditional, handcrafted dialogue systems to new domains. The proposed end-to-end approach aims to address this limitation. However, the experiments reveal that even with Memory Networks, the per-dialog accuracy remains low, indicating challenges in maintaining coherent and goal-oriented conversations. 

The initial lack of a baseline model incorporating word order information was a significant concern. The authors have now addressed this by including logistic regression models with bi-gram features, demonstrating that these simpler models achieve competitive performance, particularly when match-type features are added. This casts doubt on the necessity of complex neural architectures for achieving reasonable performance on these tasks and suggests the benefit comes from careful feature engineering rather than deep learning. 

Furthermore, the deterministic nature of tasks 1-5 limits the generalizability of the findings. Real-world dialogue is inherently noisy and ambiguous; a system’s ability to handle such variability is crucial. The synthetic nature of the tasks, while facilitating controlled evaluation, doesn’t fully capture the complexities of real user interactions. 

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Error Analysis:** A more detailed analysis of the errors made by the Memory Networks, particularly concerning the failure to interpret API call results (Task 3 & 4), would be beneficial. Identifying specific patterns in these errors could guide future research.
*   **KB Complexity:** The current Knowledge Base (KB) is relatively simple. Exploring more complex KBs with richer relationships and larger entity sets could provide a more challenging and realistic evaluation environment.
*   **Dataset Diversity:** While the inclusion of the DSTC2 and Concierge datasets is valuable, the authors might consider expanding the dataset with data from diverse sources and dialogue domains.
*   **Evaluation Metrics:** While per-response and per-dialog accuracy are useful metrics, exploring other evaluation measures, such as task completion rate or dialogue efficiency (number of turns), could provide a more comprehensive assessment of system performance.
*   **Clarification on 'Match Type' Features:** The description of the “match type” features could be clarified. It is not immediately clear how these features are incorporated into the model’s architecture and how they contribute to improved performance.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Given the competitive performance of the logistic regression baseline, what specific architectural modifications or training techniques could be employed to further improve the Memory Network’s performance and justify its increased complexity?
2.  The paper mentions that tasks were designed to break down goal-directed objectives into subtasks for error analysis. Can you provide a more detailed breakdown of the types of errors observed in each task and how these insights could inform future model development?
3.  How robust are the results to variations in the training data size and distribution? Would performance improve significantly with larger and more diverse datasets?
4.   You mention the dataset as an open resource. Has it been released, and if so, how can others access it?
