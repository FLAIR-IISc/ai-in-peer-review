This paper introduces a novel, publicly available dataset and accompanying tasks designed to facilitate research in goal-oriented dialogue systems. The dataset and tasks are synthetically generated using rule-based programs, enabling evaluation of various dialogue system capabilities, from API calls and option presentation to complete dialogue interactions. 

This contribution is a welcome addition to the field. The availability of a controlled, synthetic environment promises to accelerate progress in developing and understanding dialogue systems. However, the chosen methodology presents certain limitations. Specifically, it is unclear whether deep learning models are demonstrably superior to traditional approaches – such as rule-based systems or simpler statistical models – given the substantial data requirements of deep learning and the potential for performance differences to be attributable primarily to regularization strategies. Furthermore, tasks 1-5 are entirely deterministic, and thus fail to assess a crucial aspect of real-world dialogue: the ability to handle ambiguity, noise, and the complexities of human interaction, including inferring user intent and employing dialogue repair mechanisms. Despite these concerns, the overall direction of this work remains interesting and potentially valuable.

A significant weakness of the current paper lies in the lack of a baseline model incorporating word order information. The absence of such a baseline artificially inflates the apparent strength of the neural network models presented, as simpler models that consider word order could very well be competitive, or even outperform, the proposed architectures. To ensure a fair and accurate evaluation and to convincingly demonstrate the benefits of representation learning in this context, I strongly recommend that the authors include a non-neural network benchmark. For example, a logistic regression model incorporating 1) word embeddings (akin to the ‘Supervised Embeddings’ model presented), 2) bi-gram features, and 3) match-type features would provide a valuable point of comparison. Including such a baseline would lead me to increase my rating to 8.

Finally, the paper's claim in the conclusion – that “existing work has no well-defined measures of performance” – is inaccurate. End-to-end trainable task-oriented dialogue systems already utilize established performance metrics (e.g., Wen et al., 2016). While evaluating non-task-oriented dialogue remains challenging, robust evaluation methodologies exist, often involving human subjects (e.g., Liu et al., 2016; Yu et al., 2017).
