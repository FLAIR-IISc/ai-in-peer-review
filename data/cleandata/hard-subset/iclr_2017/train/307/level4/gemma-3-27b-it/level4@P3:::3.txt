This paper introduces a new dataset and benchmark for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems within the restaurant reservation domain. The authors address the challenge of assessing the strengths and weaknesses of these systems, which, while promising in open-domain chit-chat, haven’t clearly demonstrated comparable performance in goal-oriented tasks. The dataset consists of five tasks, generated through a simulation leveraging a knowledge base, designed to test specific dialog capabilities like API querying and entity manipulation. The paper demonstrates that Memory Networks can achieve promising, though imperfect, performance on these tasks, surpassing baseline methods like TF-IDF and supervised embeddings. The findings are further validated on real-world datasets from the Dialog State Tracking Challenge and an online concierge service.

**Reject.** While the paper presents a valuable contribution in terms of a new benchmark and insightful analysis, the limitations surrounding the ranking-based evaluation and the synthetic nature of much of the data outweigh the benefits at this time. The core argument for end-to-end systems – scalability to new domains – is not convincingly addressed by a ranking task reliant on pre-defined candidate responses.

The paper's strength lies in its systematic approach to dissecting the capabilities required for goal-oriented dialog. The proposed tasks are well-defined and the comparison between Memory Networks and baseline methods offers useful insights. The validation on real-world datasets strengthens the argument that the synthetic tasks capture relevant aspects of real-world dialog. The provided ablation studies and hyperparameter tuning details are commendable.

However, the reliance on a ranking task, rather than generation, significantly limits the applicability of the findings. The method for obtaining candidate responses – drawing from the entire training, validation, and test set – feels contrived and doesn’t reflect a realistic scenario for a new domain where such a curated list wouldn’t exist. The potential for scaling to new domains, a key motivation for end-to-end systems, is not adequately addressed. Furthermore, the synthetic nature of the primary dataset, while enabling controlled experimentation, raises concerns about the generalizability of the results to more diverse and noisy real-world conversations. While the inclusion of real-world datasets mitigates this concern, the synthetic data forms the core of the analysis.  More details on the natural language generation rules used in the simulation would also be beneficial for assessing the diversity and realism of the generated data.

To improve the paper, the authors could consider the following:

*   **Explore generative models:** Investigating generative models alongside ranking models would provide a more complete picture of the capabilities of end-to-end systems.
*   **Address candidate response sourcing:** Discuss strategies for generating candidate responses in a new domain without relying on a pre-existing dataset. Perhaps exploring techniques like back-translation or paraphrasing could be beneficial.
*   **Expand real-world data analysis:** A more in-depth analysis of the real-world datasets, including error analysis, could provide valuable insights.
*   **Provide details on NLG rules:** Publishing the natural language generation rules used to create the synthetic data would improve reproducibility and allow for a better understanding of the dataset's limitations.
*   **Discuss limitations more explicitly:** A more thorough discussion of the limitations of the ranking-based evaluation and the synthetic data would strengthen the paper.

I have the following questions for the authors:

*   What is the computational cost of generating the synthetic dataset?
*   How sensitive are the results to the specific NLG rules used? Have you explored different rule sets?
*   Could you provide examples of specific failure cases observed with the Memory Networks, particularly related to interpreting KB results?
*   What is the rationale for choosing Memory Networks as the primary end-to-end model? Were other architectures considered?
*   Regarding the real-world datasets, how were the API calls and KB outputs reconstructed for the DSTC2 data?
