This paper introduces a new dataset for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. All data is generated in the restaurant setting, where the goal is to find availability and eventually book a table based on parameters provided by the user to the bot as part of a dialog. Data is generated by running a simulation using an underlying knowledge base to generate samples for the different parameters (cuisine, price range, etc.), and then applying rule-based transformations to render natural language descriptions. The objective is to rank a set of candidate responses for each next turn of the dialog, and evaluation is reported in terms of per-response accuracy and per-dialog accuracy. The authors show that Memory Networks are able to improve over basic bag-of-words baselines.

I want to thank the authors for an interesting contribution. Having said that, I am skeptical about the utility of end-to-end trained systems in narrow-domain settings. In the open-domain setting, there is a strong argument to be made that hand-coding all states and responses would not scale, and hence end-to-end trained methods make a lot of sense. However, in the narrow-domain setting, we usually know and understand the domain quite well, and the goal is to obtain high user satisfaction. Doesn't it then make sense in these cases to use the domain knowledge to engineer the best system possible? 

Given that the domain is already restricted, I am also somewhat disappointed that the goal is to *rank* instead of *generate* responses, although I understand that this simplifies evaluation. I am unsure how these candidate responses would actually be obtained in practice. It seems that the models rank the set of all responses in train/val/test (last sentence before Section 3.2). Since a key argument for the end-to-end training approach is ease of scaling to new domains without having to manually re-engineer the system, where would this information come from for a new domain in practice? Generating responses would allow much better generalization to new domains, as opposed to simply ranking a list of hand-collected generic responses, and in my mind this is the weakest part of this work.

Finally, as data is generated using a simulation by expanding (cuisine, price, etc.) tuples using natural language generation rules, it necessarily constrains the variability in the training responses. Of course, this is a trade-off with the ability to generate unlimited data using the simulator. However, I was unable to find the list of rules used. It would be good to publish this as well.

Overall, despite my skepticism, I think this is an interesting contribution worthy of publication at the conference.

**Decision:** Accept. While I have reservations about the ranking approach and the reliance on a simulation, the paper addresses an important problem – understanding the capabilities and limitations of end-to-end dialog systems in goal-oriented tasks – and provides a valuable resource for the research community. The results demonstrate that end-to-end models can achieve reasonable performance in this setting, and the comparison to a rule-based baseline is insightful.

**Supporting Arguments:**

*   **Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the need for a standardized testbed to evaluate end-to-end dialog systems in goal-oriented tasks, bridging the gap between successful open-domain chatbots and the challenges of real-world applications.
*   **Well-Motivated Approach:** The authors convincingly argue that end-to-end models require careful evaluation in goal-oriented settings, as success in chit-chat does not guarantee success in tasks requiring reasoning and interaction with knowledge bases.
*   **Supported Claims:** The experimental results, while not state-of-the-art, demonstrate that Memory Networks can learn to perform non-trivial operations such as API calls and entity manipulation. The comparison to the rule-based baseline and the evaluation on real-world data (DSTC2 and the concierge dataset) provide evidence for the generalizability of the findings. The observation that per-response accuracy is encouraging but per-dialog accuracy remains low is a valuable insight.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Candidate Response Generation:** The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of how candidate responses would be generated in a real-world deployment. Exploring potential methods for generating diverse and relevant candidates would strengthen the work.
*   **Natural Language Generation Rules:** Publishing the natural language generation rules used in the simulator would improve reproducibility and allow other researchers to analyze the impact of these rules on the dataset.
*   **Error Analysis:** A more in-depth error analysis, focusing on the types of errors made by the Memory Networks, would provide valuable insights for future research. What specific KB interactions or reasoning steps are the models struggling with?
*   **Comparison to More Recent Models:** While Memory Networks are a reasonable baseline, comparing to more recent and powerful sequence-to-sequence models with attention mechanisms could provide a more comprehensive assessment of the current state-of-the-art.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you elaborate on how the candidate responses would be obtained in a new domain, assuming no pre-existing response library? Would you envision a mechanism for dynamically generating candidates, or would some degree of manual effort be required?
*   Would you be willing to release the natural language generation rules used to create the simulated dialogs?
*   Have you performed a more detailed error analysis to identify the specific challenges that the Memory Networks face in this task? What types of errors are most common?
*   Have you experimented with other sequence-to-sequence models with attention, and how do they compare to the Memory Networks on this dataset?
