- Paper is highly significant and provides a valuable, realistic assessment of current chatbot capabilities for both dialog/NLP and ML communities.  
- Builds on two conjectures: end-to-end ML is inadequate for goal-oriented dialogs, and synthetic-data performance can predict real-world performance.  
- Authors make substantial effort to align synthetic and natural conditions to test cross-domain generalization.  
- Algorithmic contribution (adding a match type to memory networks) is incremental but is the first evaluation of this approach in goal-oriented dialog.  
- Experimental protocol is exceptionally well-designed; paper is clearly written and accessible to non-specialists.  
- Appendix and tables (e.g., impact of number of hops) are particularly useful and well-presented.  
- Work advances state-of-the-art metrics for dialog modeling while showing those metrics are fragile and arbitrary.  
- Per-response accuracy (next-utterance classification) is problematic and can mislead because it requires exact matches across multiple responses (Table 3).  
- A stricter per-dialog accuracy (requiring correctness across all responses) may be preferable; metrics are sensitive to task definitions (e.g., T3 vs T5).  
- For concierge data, evaluation should consider achieving the best outcome rather than being among the top-five candidates.  
- Proposes considering revenue-maximization or loss functions (errors in API call, turn count, rejected options) for goal-oriented dialog evaluation, but notes this needs real users or realistic simulators.  
- Paper fails to adequately address vocabulary mismatch between user input and knowledge base; the match-type method requires exact lexical overlap.  
- In real systems KBs may use obfuscated terms, so models must infer associations rather than rely on human-defined lexical matches.
