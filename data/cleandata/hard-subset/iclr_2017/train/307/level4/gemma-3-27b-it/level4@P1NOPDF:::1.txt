This paper introduces a new, publicly available dataset and associated tasks designed for goal-oriented dialogue applications. The dataset and tasks are artificially constructed using rule-based programs to allow for the evaluation of diverse dialogue system capabilities, ranging from API call execution and option presentation to complete dialogue management.

This is a valuable contribution to the dialogue research community and will likely facilitate future work in both the development and understanding of dialogue systems. However, the chosen approach also presents potential drawbacks. It’s unclear whether deep learning models are particularly well-suited to these tasks compared to traditional methods – such as rule-based or shallow models – given the large amounts of training data typically required by deep learning, and the possibility that performance differences between neural networks simply reflect variations in regularization techniques. Furthermore, tasks 1-5 are entirely deterministic, which limits the ability to evaluate a model's robustness to the noisy and ambiguous interactions common in real-world dialogue (e.g., inferring user goals from incomplete input, or employing dialogue repair strategies). Despite these concerns, I believe this is an interesting and worthwhile research direction.

As discussed in the comments, the paper currently lacks a baseline model incorporating word order information. I consider this a significant weakness, as it leads to an inflated perception of the neural networks’ performance, while simpler baselines could potentially achieve competitive – or even superior – results. To ensure a fair evaluation and accurately assess the benefits of representation learning in this context, I recommend the authors include an additional, non-neural network benchmark. For example, they could experiment with a logistic regression model that utilizes 1) word embeddings (similar to the Supervised Embeddings model), 2) bi-gram features, and 3) match-type features. The inclusion of such a baseline would provide a more convincing demonstration of the value proposition of deep learning for this task, and would prompt me to increase my rating to 8.

Finally, a minor point regarding the conclusion: the statement that “existing work has no well-defined measures of performance” is inaccurate. End-to-end trainable models for task-oriented dialogue *do* have established performance metrics. For example, see "A Network-based End-to-End Trainable Task-oriented Dialogue System" by Wen et al. While evaluating non-task-oriented dialogue can be more challenging, it is also possible with human subjects, as demonstrated by Liu et al. (2016) on Twitter data, and Yu et al. in “Strategy and Policy Learning for Non-Task-Oriented Conversational Systems”.
