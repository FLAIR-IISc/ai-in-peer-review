This paper represents a valuable and timely reality check on the recent enthusiasm for deploying chatbots in goal-oriented dialogs. While most relevant to Dialog/Natural Language Processing venues, its insights are significant for the Machine Learning community, highlighting the need for more realistic validation of ML approaches applied to dialog systems. The work’s high significance stems from two key conjectures – that current end-to-end ML performance remains insufficient for goal-oriented dialogs, and that relative performance on synthetic data accurately predicts performance on natural data – both of which warrant further research.

The paper’s primary contribution lies in systematically evaluating end-to-end dialog systems, specifically Memory Networks with a simple but effective addition (match type features), within a goal-oriented restaurant reservation setting. The experimental protocol is exceptionally well-designed and the paper is written with excellent clarity, making it accessible to a broad audience beyond ML and dialog specialists. The concise appendix summarizing Memory Networks and the tables detailing the influence of the number of hops are particularly commendable.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The strength of the evaluation framework and the insightful analysis of results outweigh the limitations in algorithmic novelty. The work provides a crucial benchmark and a clear roadmap for future research in this important area. 

The paper convincingly demonstrates the challenges of applying end-to-end learning to goal-oriented dialog, revealing weaknesses even in tasks where traditional, handcrafted systems excel. The authors meticulously compare several methods, including rule-based systems, information retrieval techniques, supervised embeddings, and Memory Networks, offering a comprehensive performance analysis. The validation of results across both synthetic and real-world datasets (DSTC2 and a concierge service) lends strong support to their claims.  The observation that relative performance trends are consistent across these datasets is particularly compelling and suggests the synthetic tasks are a useful proxy for real-world performance.

While the paper is strong, several points could be addressed to further enhance its impact.  The reliance on standard dialog metrics like per-response accuracy is acknowledged as a limitation. The paper correctly points out that this metric can be misleading, focusing on individual utterance correctness rather than overall dialog success. The per-dialog accuracy is more informative, but sensitive to task design, as demonstrated by the disparity between subtask T3 and the full dialog T5. 

Exploring alternative metrics that better reflect real-world goals – for instance, a weighted sum of API call errors, dialog turns, and user rejections – could provide a more nuanced evaluation. However, the authors appropriately note the difficulty of applying such metrics to canned dialogs without real user interaction or a sophisticated simulator.

Another important consideration is vocabulary mismatch between user input and the knowledge base. The current "match type" feature requires exact matches between words in the user’s request and the KB, potentially limiting generalization to situations where different terminology is used. Investigating methods for learning associations between user vocabulary and KB concepts, rather than relying on explicit hand-coding, would be a valuable direction for future research.

Specifically, I have the following questions for the authors:

*   Regarding the concierge dataset, how would the results change if 'correct' was defined as being among the top-5 suggested options, rather than requiring the absolute best suggestion?
*   Could you elaborate on the potential benefits and challenges of incorporating reinforcement learning techniques into this framework, particularly in defining a reward function that aligns with real-world business goals (e.g., maximizing successful dialog completion)?
*   What modifications to the Memory Network architecture or training procedure might improve performance on tasks requiring more complex reasoning and KB interaction, such as Task 3 (displaying options)?
*   Given the vocabulary mismatch issue, are there plans to explore methods for learning to map user utterances to KB entities, even when there isn't an exact lexical match?
