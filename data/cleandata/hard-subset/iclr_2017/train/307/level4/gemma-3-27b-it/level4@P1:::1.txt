This paper presents a new, public dataset and tasks for goal-oriented dialogue applications. The dataset and tasks are constructed artificially using rule-based programs, designed to evaluate different aspects of dialogue system performance, ranging from issuing API calls to displaying options, and facilitating full-fledged dialogues.

This is a welcome contribution to the dialogue literature, which will help facilitate future research into developing and understanding dialogue systems. However, there are potential limitations to this approach. First, it is not immediately clear how well-suited Deep Learning models are for these tasks compared to traditional methods (rule-based systems or shallow models), given that Deep Learning models typically require large amounts of training data, and performance differences between neural networks may be attributable to regularization techniques rather than inherent model capabilities. Second, the tasks 1-5 are entirely deterministic, meaning that evaluating performance on these tasks may not fully capture the ability of models to handle the noise and ambiguity inherent in real-world interactions – such as inferring distributions over user goals or employing dialogue repair strategies – which are crucial aspects of dialogue applications. Overall, I still believe this is an interesting and valuable direction to explore.

As discussed below, the initial paper lacked a baseline model incorporating word order information. I believe this was a significant weakness, as it potentially overstated the advantage of neural networks compared to simpler, yet potentially competitive, models. To ensure a fairer evaluation and more accurately assess the benefits of representation learning, it's important for the authors to experiment with an additional non-neural network benchmark model that accounts for word order. For instance, they could consider a logistic regression model incorporating 1) word embeddings (similar to their Supervised Embeddings model), 2) bigram features, and 3) match-type features. Inclusion of such a baseline would strengthen the paper's conclusions.

Minor comment: In the conclusion, the claim that “existing work has no well-defined measures of performance” is not entirely accurate. End-to-end trainable models for task-oriented dialogue already have established performance measures (e.g., Wen et al.). While evaluating non-goal-oriented dialogue is more challenging, human subject evaluations are often employed (e.g., Liu et al., 2016), and research exists in this area (e.g., Yu et al.).

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:** The paper addresses an important need in the dialogue community – a standardized testbed for evaluating goal-oriented dialogue systems. The proposed tasks and dataset offer a valuable resource for researchers, and the initial experiments demonstrate the potential of the setup for analyzing strengths and weaknesses of different approaches. The authors have responded positively to feedback and addressed my main concerns regarding the inclusion of a more competitive baseline.

**Supporting Arguments:**

*   **Novelty and Importance:** The creation of a controlled environment for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialogue systems is a significant contribution. Existing datasets are often noisy or difficult to use for systematic comparisons.
*   **Rigorous Evaluation:** The paper provides a thorough experimental evaluation, comparing several methods and analyzing performance across different tasks.
*   **Clear Problem Definition:** The motivation for the work is well-articulated, and the paper clearly defines the research questions being addressed.
*   **Responsiveness to Feedback:** The authors have thoughtfully addressed the concerns raised in the review, adding a crucial baseline model to strengthen the analysis.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement, not part of the decision):**

*   **Error Analysis:** While the paper provides quantitative results, a more detailed qualitative error analysis would be beneficial. Examining specific examples of failures could offer deeper insights into the limitations of current models.
*   **Generalization to Real-World Data:** The authors acknowledge the gap between synthetic and real-world data. Further investigation into how well models trained on this dataset generalize to more noisy and complex scenarios would be valuable.
*   **KB Complexity:** The current KB is relatively simple. Exploring the impact of a more complex and realistic KB on model performance could be an interesting avenue for future work.



**Questions for the Authors:**

*   What are the computational costs associated with running experiments on this dataset, particularly for larger models?
*   Do you have plans to release additional datasets or expand the current dataset with more complex scenarios or dialogue variations?
*   Have you considered incorporating user simulators alongside the rule-based simulator to provide a more realistic evaluation environment?
