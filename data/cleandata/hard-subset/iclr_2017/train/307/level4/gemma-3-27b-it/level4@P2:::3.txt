This paper introduces a new dataset and evaluation framework for goal-oriented, end-to-end dialog systems, specifically within the restaurant reservation domain. The authors address the challenge of evaluating these systems, which aim to learn dialog policies directly from data without hand-engineered components, in a setting where traditional, knowledge-based systems perform well. The dataset is generated through a simulation leveraging a knowledge base, allowing for controlled experimentation and large-scale data creation. The task is framed as ranking candidate responses, and the authors demonstrate that Memory Networks outperform simpler baselines, including information retrieval methods and supervised embedding models.

I recommend accepting this paper. The primary strength lies in its systematic approach to evaluating end-to-end dialog systems in a goal-oriented setting. The creation of a controlled testbed is a valuable contribution, allowing for focused analysis of model capabilities and limitations.

The paper effectively motivates the need for a dedicated evaluation framework. While end-to-end approaches excel in open-domain scenarios due to scalability, their performance in well-defined, narrow domains remains an open question. This work directly tackles that question by providing a benchmark for comparison. The authors clearly articulate this motivation and position their work within the existing literature on both traditional and end-to-end dialog systems. 

The experimental results are well-presented and demonstrate the potential of Memory Networks, while also highlighting their current limitations. The inclusion of both synthetic and real-world (DSTC2, online concierge) datasets strengthens the findings, suggesting the synthetic data effectively proxies for real-world performance patterns. The ablation studies, particularly those exploring the impact of match-type features, provide valuable insights into model behavior. The analysis of per-response vs. per-dialog accuracy is crucial, revealing that while individual responses can be accurate, maintaining coherence throughout a dialog remains a challenge.

However, several points could improve the paper. The ranking-based evaluation, while simplifying assessment, feels restrictive. While the authors acknowledge this, a more detailed discussion of its practical implications would be beneficial. Specifically, how would candidate responses be generated in a truly novel domain? The dependence on a predefined candidate set limits the potential for generalization. Publishing the natural language generation rules used in the simulation would enhance reproducibility and allow for more detailed analysis of the dataset's characteristics. Finally, a discussion of error analysis – *what* specific types of errors are the Memory Networks making? – would be insightful. Are they failing to correctly interpret API results, manage dialog state, or handle unseen entities despite the match-type features? 

I have the following questions for the authors:

*   Could you elaborate on the challenges of extending the evaluation to a generation task? What specific technical hurdles prevent this, and what modifications to the framework would be necessary?
*   The paper mentions that the simulation is designed to be deterministic. However, real-world user behavior is inherently unpredictable. How might the framework be extended to incorporate more realistic user modeling?
*   Could you provide more specific examples of the types of errors the Memory Networks make, particularly in the more challenging tasks (e.g., Task 3 – Displaying Options)?
*   What is the computational cost of training the Memory Networks compared to the simpler baseline models? Is this a significant barrier to adoption?
*   Regarding the concierge dataset, how was the anonymization process performed, and what potential biases might have been introduced by this process?
