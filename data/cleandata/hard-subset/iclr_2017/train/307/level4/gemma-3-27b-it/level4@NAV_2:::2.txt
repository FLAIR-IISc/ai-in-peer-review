This paper represents a serious reality check for the recent trend of applying chatbots to goal-oriented dialog, a domain where claims of solving tasks beyond simple chit-chat have been prevalent. While most relevant for Dialog/Natural Language venues, its potential impact extends to Machine Learning communities, highlighting the need for more realistic validation of ML models applied to dialog systems. I consider this work of high significance.

Two key conjectures underlie this research, explicitly articulated during a NIPS workshop presentation: 1) Current end-to-end ML approaches are insufficient for goal-oriented dialog; and 2) Relative performance on synthetic data can predict performance on natural data – a potentially significant finding.

The algorithmic contribution, a simple addition of match type features to Memory Networks, is the first application of this architecture to a goal-oriented dialog setting. The experimental protocol is particularly strong, and the paper is written with excellent clarity, accessible to a broad audience. I was impressed by the concise and informative appendix detailing Memory Networks and the illustrative tables explaining the impact of hop count.

While the paper advances rigorous dialog modeling, it also subtly highlights the inherent brittleness and arbitrariness of current evaluation metrics. This serves as a recommendation for future research rather than a necessary revision.

The use of per-response accuracy (next utterance classification from a fixed list) presents challenges. As Table 3 demonstrates, focusing solely on correct API calls and short dialogs can be misleading, potentially resulting in low accuracy despite functional completion. Similarly, the per-dialog accuracy is sensitive to experimental protocol, with the difference between subtask T3 (0.0 accuracy) and the full dialog T5 (19.7% accuracy) stemming from the differing number of options presented (3 vs. 1).

Regarding the Concierge data, I’m curious about the results if 'correct' was defined as being among the top responses, rather than strictly the best. 

While the authors appropriately employ standard dialog metrics, exploring alternative, revenue-driven metrics relevant to real-world applications (e.g., a weighted sum of API call errors, dialog turns, and rejected options) could offer more meaningful evaluation. However, such metrics would necessitate real human users or a highly realistic simulator.

Another crucial aspect, and a potential limitation, concerns vocabulary mismatch between user input and the knowledge base. The ‘match type’ algorithm relies on exact term overlap, potentially failing when the KB uses obfuscated or non-standard terminology. Enabling ML models to learn associations rather than requiring explicit human-defined mappings deserves further investigation.

I **accept** this paper, with a strong recommendation for publication. The rigorous methodology, insightful analysis, and clear presentation significantly contribute to the field of goal-oriented dialog systems.

To further strengthen the work, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:

1.  Regarding the Concierge dataset, what results are observed if correctness is determined by being *among* the top 5 responses, rather than strictly the best?
2.  Could the authors elaborate on strategies to address vocabulary mismatch between user input and the knowledge base, potentially exploring methods that learn associations rather than relying on exact term matches?
3.  Are there plans to explore alternative, application-specific evaluation metrics that move beyond standard measures and reflect real-world business goals, such as revenue optimization in a virtual assistant context?
4.  The paper briefly touches on the sensitivity of per-dialog accuracy to task definitions (specifically, the number of options presented). Could the authors discuss whether normalizing for task complexity (e.g., weighting dialogs based on the number of options) might provide a more robust evaluation metric?
