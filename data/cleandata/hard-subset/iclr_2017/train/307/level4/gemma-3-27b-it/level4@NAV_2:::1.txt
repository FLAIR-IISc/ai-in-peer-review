This paper presents a novel dataset and set of tasks designed to evaluate goal-oriented dialogue systems. The work focuses on dissecting the capabilities required for end-to-end models in this domain, moving beyond the successes observed in open-domain chit-chat. The proposed tasks, centered around restaurant reservation, systematically test components like API calling, knowledge base interpretation, and multi-turn dialogue management. This is a valuable contribution to the field, providing a controlled environment for research and comparison of different approaches.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The core idea of creating a targeted testbed to understand the strengths and weaknesses of end-to-end dialogue systems is sound and well-executed. The paper demonstrates a clear understanding of the challenges in applying these models to goal-oriented tasks and provides a useful resource for the community.

The paper is well-motivated, recognizing the limitations of scaling traditional, handcrafted dialogue systems and the potential—but unproven—transferability of end-to-end approaches from open-domain settings. The authors carefully delineate the specific capabilities being evaluated through their task design, grounding the work in a practical scenario. The experimental setup, including comparisons to rule-based and embedding-based baselines, as well as evaluation on real-world datasets (DSTC2 and a concierge service dataset), provides sufficient evidence to support the claims made. The observed pattern of results—encouraging per-response accuracy but limited per-dialog success—highlights the remaining challenges in building robust goal-oriented dialogue systems. The inclusion of out-of-vocabulary tests further strengthens the analysis.

Several avenues for future work could enhance the paper. It would be beneficial to explore the impact of incorporating word order information into the baseline models, potentially through bi-gram features or similar techniques. This would provide a more comprehensive comparison against the neural network models and clarify the specific benefits of representation learning. A more detailed analysis of the types of errors made by the Memory Networks, beyond the general observation of low per-dialog accuracy, would also be valuable. Finally, while the paper acknowledges related work on dialogue evaluation, expanding on the existing metrics for task-oriented dialogue and clarifying the advantages of this new testbed in relation to them could be helpful. 

I have several questions for the authors:

1.  The paper mentions that tasks 1-5 are deterministic. Could you elaborate on the potential limitations of this determinism and discuss how the results might generalize to more realistic, non-deterministic environments?
2.  Regarding the match type features, did you consider adding these features to the classical IR models (TF-IDF Match and Nearest Neighbor) as well? If not, why?
3.  The Concierge dataset includes challenges related to noisy language and variable dialogue structure. How do you see this dataset complementing the simulated tasks in terms of providing a more comprehensive evaluation?
4.  Could you provide some specific examples of the types of errors the Memory Networks made in tasks T3 and T5 that led to low per-dialog accuracy? This would help understand where the models are struggling and guide future research.
