This paper presents a novel dataset designed for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems, specifically within the restaurant booking domain. The dataset is generated through a simulation that leverages an underlying knowledge base to create diverse parameter combinations (cuisine, price range, etc.). Natural language descriptions are then rendered using rule-based transformations, resulting in a collection of dialog examples. The task is framed as ranking a set of candidate responses for each turn in a dialog, and the authors report performance using both per-response and per-dialog accuracy metrics. Their experiments demonstrate that Memory Networks outperform simpler bag-of-words baselines.

I appreciate the authors’ contribution; however, I have some reservations regarding the practical utility of end-to-end trained systems in narrow-domain scenarios. While end-to-end approaches are compelling for open-domain dialog, where hand-crafting states and responses becomes intractable, narrow domains typically benefit from leveraging well-understood domain knowledge to engineer highly optimized systems with a focus on user satisfaction. It’s unclear whether the complexity of end-to-end training is justified when domain expertise could lead to superior results.

Furthermore, I find the choice to focus on *ranking* candidate responses, rather than *generating* them, somewhat limiting, although I acknowledge the simplification this provides for evaluation. A crucial question arises regarding the practical sourcing of these candidate responses. The paper suggests the models rank responses observed during training, validation, and testing (as stated in the last sentence before Section 3.2). However, a key motivation for end-to-end training is the ability to adapt to new domains without extensive manual re-engineering. If a new domain is considered, where do these candidate responses originate? Generating responses would facilitate better generalization, allowing the system to move beyond ranking a predefined set of responses, which appears to be the core limitation of this work. 

Finally, the simulated data generation process, employing rules to expand parameter tuples into natural language, inherently restricts the variability of the training data. This trade-off is understandable, given the benefit of unlimited data generation through simulation. However, the specific rules used in this process were not disclosed. Publishing these rules would significantly enhance the reproducibility and understanding of the dataset’s characteristics.

Despite these concerns, I believe this is an interesting and valuable contribution, worthy of publication at the conference.
