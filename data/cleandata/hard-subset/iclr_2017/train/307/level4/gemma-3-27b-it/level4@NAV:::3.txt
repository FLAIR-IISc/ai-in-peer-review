SYNOPSIS:
This paper presents a novel dataset designed for the evaluation of end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. The dataset focuses on the restaurant reservation domain, where the task involves determining availability and completing a booking based on user-specified criteria communicated through dialog. Data generation employs a simulation driven by an underlying knowledge base to produce variations in parameters like cuisine and price range, followed by rule-based natural language generation to create realistic dialog turns. The evaluation framework centers on ranking candidate responses for each dialog turn, with performance measured by per-response and per-dialog accuracy. The authors demonstrate that Memory Networks outperform simpler bag-of-words baselines.

THOUGHTS:
I commend the authors for this valuable contribution. However, I harbor reservations regarding the practicality of end-to-end training within narrowly defined domains. While end-to-end methods offer clear advantages in open-domain scenarios where manual state and response coding is unsustainable, narrow domains typically benefit from well-understood domain knowledge. In such cases, leveraging this knowledge to engineer an optimized system specifically tailored for user satisfaction seems a more sensible approach.

The restriction of the task to ranking, rather than generating responses, is somewhat limiting given the constrained domain. While I appreciate the simplification this affords for evaluation, it raises questions regarding real-world applicability: how would these candidate responses be sourced in a practical system? The methodology appears to rely on ranking all responses present in the training, validation, and testing sets (as noted before Section 3.2). A central justification for end-to-end training is its potential for scalability to new domains without extensive manual re-engineering. However, the origin of comparable candidate responses for novel domains remains unclear. Response generation would facilitate better generalization, contrasting with the current approach of ranking a pre-defined set of generic responses; this aspect represents a key weakness of the work.

Finally, the simulation-based data generation, utilizing NL generation rules to expand parameter tuples (e.g., cuisine, price), inherently limits the diversity of training responses, although this is acknowledged as a trade-off for the ability to create a large dataset.  I would appreciate the inclusion of the specific rules used in this process, potentially as supplementary material, to allow for a more complete understanding of the data generation procedure.

Overall, despite my concerns, I believe this is an interesting and worthwhile contribution suitable for presentation at the conference.
