This paper introduces a novel, publicly available dataset and associated tasks designed for the evaluation of goal-oriented dialogue systems. The dataset and tasks are synthetically generated using rule-based programs, enabling controlled assessment of various dialogue system capabilities – from API interaction and option presentation to complete dialogue management. 

This contribution is valuable to the dialogue research community and has the potential to advance both the development and understanding of dialogue systems. However, the artificial construction of the dataset presents certain challenges. Specifically, the suitability of Deep Learning models for these tasks, compared to more traditional approaches like rule-based or shallow models, is unclear. Given the data requirements of Deep Learning, observed performance differences between neural networks may be attributable to regularization rather than inherent model superiority. Furthermore, the deterministic nature of tasks 1-5 limits the evaluation of crucial dialogue skills such as handling noisy input, resolving ambiguity (e.g., inferring user intent distributions), and employing dialogue repair mechanisms. Despite these limitations, the work represents a promising research direction.

A significant concern, as detailed in the comments, is the absence of a baseline model incorporating word order information. This omission potentially overstates the performance of the neural network models, as simpler baselines leveraging word order could prove competitive or even superior. To ensure a fair evaluation and accurately assess the benefits of representation learning in this context, the authors should include an additional, non-neural network benchmark that accounts for word order. A logistic regression model utilizing word embeddings, bi-gram features, and match-type features would be a suitable choice. Inclusion of such a baseline would warrant an increased rating of 8.

Finally, the claim in the conclusion that existing work lacks well-defined performance measures is inaccurate. End-to-end trainable task-oriented dialogue systems, such as that presented by Wen et al., already employ established evaluation metrics. While non-task-oriented dialogue evaluation remains challenging, it is addressable through human subject evaluation, as demonstrated by Liu et al. and Yu et al.
