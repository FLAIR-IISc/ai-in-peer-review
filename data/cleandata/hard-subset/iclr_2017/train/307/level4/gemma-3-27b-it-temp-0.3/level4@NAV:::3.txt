## Review

SYNOPSIS:
This paper presents a novel dataset designed for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. The dataset focuses on the restaurant reservation domain, tasking systems with determining availability and booking tables based on user-provided criteria within a conversational context. Data generation employs a simulation driven by an underlying knowledge base to produce variations in parameters like cuisine and price range, subsequently transformed into natural language utterances via rule-based methods. The task is framed as ranking candidate responses for each dialog turn, with performance measured using per-response and per-dialog accuracy. The authors demonstrate that Memory Networks outperform simpler bag-of-words baselines.

THOUGHTS:
I would like to thank the authors for this interesting contribution. However, I harbor some reservations regarding the benefits of end-to-end training in narrowly defined domains. While end-to-end approaches are compelling in open-domain scenarios where manual state and response engineering become impractical, narrow domains typically benefit from leveraging explicit domain knowledge to maximize user satisfaction. It is unclear whether the complexities of end-to-end training are justified when a well-understood domain allows for the deliberate engineering of optimal system performance.

Furthermore, the choice to focus on *ranking* candidate responses, rather than *generating* them, is somewhat disappointing given the domain’s constraints, although I recognize the simplification this brings to evaluation. The practical sourcing of these candidate responses also raises questions. The models appear to rank the complete set of training, validation, and test responses (as noted before Section 3.2). Considering the purported advantage of end-to-end training – scalability to new domains without manual re-engineering – it is unclear how this candidate set would be established for a novel domain. Response generation would facilitate better generalization to new domains compared to simply ranking a pre-defined set of responses, and this aspect represents a key limitation of the work.

Finally, the simulation-based data generation process, utilizing NL-generation rules to expand parameter tuples (e.g., cuisine, price), inherently limits the diversity of training responses. While this is a necessary trade-off for the ability to generate large-scale data, the specific rules employed were not detailed in the paper. Publishing these rules would be beneficial for reproducibility and further research. 

Overall, despite these concerns, I believe this is a valuable contribution and recommend its acceptance to the conference.
