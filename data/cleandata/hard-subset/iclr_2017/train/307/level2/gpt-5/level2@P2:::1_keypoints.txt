- Paper introduces a reproducible, lightweight testbed and five synthetic tasks plus two real-data tasks for goal-oriented dialog in a restaurant reservation domain.  
- Benchmarks IR, supervised embeddings, and MemN2N, and proposes match type features for entity/OOV handling.  
- MemN2N substantially outperforms baselines, learns API issuing/updating and some memory reasoning, but struggles on KB interpretation and option offering (low per-dialog accuracy).  
- Decision: Accept.  

- Key reason 1: Public benchmark isolates critical subskills for goal-oriented dialog and enables reproducible, interpretable comparisons.  
- Key reason 2: Careful empirical analysis exposes strengths and limits of end-to-end models (OOV handling, KB use) and is corroborated on real datasets.  

- Problem/significance: Scaling goal-oriented dialog beyond handcrafted slot-filling is important; current corpora are limited or not reproducible.  
- Approach/motivation: Clear, KB-grounded task design with incremental difficulty, OOV splits, and real-data evaluations; sensible per-turn and per-dialog metrics.  
- Evidence/correctness: Results support claims—MemN2N + match types gives clear gains; ablations and hyperparameters are documented; oracle/rule baselines provide useful contrast.  
- Context/originality: Builds on bAbI QA and prior dialog work; benchmark and diagnostic analysis are meaningful contributions despite MemN2N not being novel.  

- Concern: Synthetic design choices (deterministic slot order, fixed patterns, constrained KB) may over-structure tasks and limit realism.  
- Concern: Candidate set includes utterances across splits which can inflate accuracy and hinder cross-paper comparability.  
- Concern: DSTC2 conversion adds ratings that may bias option ranking and limit direct comparison to original DSTC2 baselines.  
- Concern: Missing stronger baselines (seq2seq/HRED, pointer/copy, RNN trackers, RL approaches) that could close gaps on harder tasks.  
- Concern: OOV/type-matching assumes access to KB entity lists at training time, which may not generalize to open settings.  
- Concern: Low per-dialog accuracy on key tasks shows end-to-end approaches remain brittle for full goal completion.  

- Feedback: Report sensitivity to candidate set size/composition and release standardized candidate pools.  
- Feedback: Compare to strong neural baselines with copy/pointer and seq2seq attention; include minimal generative evaluation.  
- Feedback: Relax synthetic constraints (variable slot order, paraphrases, multiple APIs, off-goal turns) to better mimic real dialogs.  
- Feedback: Provide detailed error analysis for tasks 3–5 (sorting, grounding, long-range dependencies, entity carryover).  
- Feedback: Try reinforcement learning on the simulator to study sample efficiency and robustness (e.g., ASR noise).  
- Feedback: Clarify statistical significance and add confidence intervals for per-dialog accuracy.  
- Feedback: For DSTC2 conversion, detail rating assignment, quantify introduced biases, and report parallel evaluations without enforced sorting.  

- Question: Do training candidate sets include all candidates from test and OOV; how does performance change if restricted to seen or task-specific candidates?  
- Question: How were DSTC2 ratings assigned and how often do they contradict original bot choices; can you evaluate without rating-based sorting?  
- Question: For match type features at test time with OOVs, do you assume perfect KB gazetteers; how does noisy/incomplete entity lists affect performance?  
- Question: In MemN2N ablations, what are the contributions of time features and speaker markers versus hops and type features; any position encoding/layer tying results?  
- Question: How robust are results to increased linguistic variability (more templates/paraphrases) and larger training sets?  
- Question: Can you compare against stronger DSTC2 baselines (e.g., RNN/LSTM state trackers) within your converted scoring framework?  
- Question: For failures in T3/T5, are errors mainly due to sorting, state carryover, or grounding; do attention visualizations point to correct KB facts when answers are wrong?
