Summary
The paper introduces a reproducible, lightweight testbed to evaluate end-to-end models for goal-oriented dialog. Within a restaurant reservation domain, the authors define five synthetic tasks that decompose key capabilities: (1) asking for missing slots and issuing API calls, (2) updating API calls, (3) ranking/displaying options using KB outputs, (4) retrieving extra information from KB facts, and (5) conducting full dialogs. They further provide two evaluations on real data: a conversion of DSTC2 transcripts into the same format (Task 6) and a separate “Concierge” human-human dataset. The paper benchmarks IR baselines, supervised embedding models, and Memory Networks (MemN2N), and proposes “match type” features to better handle entities and OOVs. Results show MemN2N substantially outperforms other baselines, learns non-trivial behavior (issuing/updating API calls, some reasoning over memory), and benefits from match types, but still has low per-dialog accuracy on harder tasks involving KB result interpretation and option offering.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) The paper provides a well-motivated, publicly released benchmark that isolates critical subskills for goal-oriented dialog, enabling reproducible and interpretable comparisons. (2) The empirical analysis is careful, revealing strengths and limitations of current end-to-end models (incl. OOV handling and KB use), with corroboration on real datasets.

Supporting arguments
- Problem/Significance: Scaling goal-oriented dialog beyond slot-filling and heavy handcrafting is important. Existing corpora are either not public, not end-to-end suitable, or not reproducible. Decomposing the goal into subskills is valuable for diagnosis and progress tracking. This is well-placed vis-à-vis POMDP literature and recent end-to-end dialog work.
- Approach/Motivation: The task design is clear, grounded in a KB, and incrementally challenging. The inclusion of both OOV splits and real data (DSTC2, Concierge) addresses concerns about synthetic-only evaluation. The choice of per-turn and per-dialog metrics is appropriate for goal completion.
- Evidence/Correctness: Claims are largely supported. MemN2N outperforms IR and supervised embeddings, with match types giving clear gains on entity use and OOVs; tasks 1–2 are learned, 3–5 remain hard—consistent across synthetic and real data. The rule-based oracle solves deterministic synthetic tasks, but loses to MemN2N on DSTC2, underscoring the value of learning on noisy real dialogs. Ablations (with/without match types, hops) are informative and hyperparameters are documented.
- Context/Originality: The work builds on bAbI QA tasks and prior non-goal-oriented dialog evaluations, extending them meaningfully into goal-oriented, KB-grounded settings. While MemN2N is not novel, the benchmark and the diagnostic analysis are valuable contributions.

Concerns and limitations (non-blocking)
- Synthetic design choices (deterministic slot order, fixed patterns, ranking candidate responses, constrained KB schema) may over-structure the problem. The candidate set includes utterances from train/val/test across all tasks, which can inflate absolute accuracy and complicate cross-paper comparability if others vary this detail.
- The conversion of DSTC2 introduces ratings to enforce consistent option ranking; this could bias the task and limits direct comparison to DSTC2 baselines. The rule-based baseline on DSTC2 is relatively simple; stronger pipeline systems (e.g., word-level RNN trackers) would strengthen comparisons.
- Missing baselines: modern seq2seq/HRED and pointer/copy mechanisms (particularly relevant for entities) are absent; these might reduce the gap on tasks 3–5. Also, no RL-based turn-level optimization, even though the simulator could support it.
- The OOV setup assumes access to KB entities at training time for type matching, which partly sidesteps the unknown-token problem via exact-type features; the generality of this mechanism in truly open settings is unclear.
- Low per-dialog accuracy on key tasks highlights that the end-to-end approach remains brittle for full goal completion.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Report sensitivity to candidate set size/composition; consider standardized candidate pools per task and release them with the data.
- Provide comparisons to strong neural baselines with copy/pointer networks and seq2seq with attention; include a minimal generative evaluation (even if primary eval remains ranking).
- Relax synthetic constraints to better approximate real dialogs: variable slot ordering, paraphrase diversity for the bot, multi-criteria ranking beyond a single rating, multiple APIs, corrections, and off-goal turns.
- Include more detailed error analysis for tasks 3–5 (e.g., failures in sorting, grounding, long-range dependencies, entity carryover).
- Consider a reinforcement learning experiment on the simulator to bridge to deployment settings; report sample efficiency and robustness to ASR-like noise.
- Clarify statistical significance of key improvements; add confidence intervals for per-dialog accuracy.
- For DSTC2 conversion, detail potential biases introduced by adding ratings and quantify inconsistency/noise in the original system responses.

Questions for the authors
- Candidate set construction: Do you include all candidates from test and OOV sets at training time? How does performance change if the candidate set is restricted to seen training responses or to task-specific candidates?
- DSTC2 conversion: How were ratings assigned, and how often do added ratings contradict the bot’s original choices? Can you provide a parallel evaluation without enforcing rating-based sorting?
- Match type features: At test time for OOV, do you assume perfect entity lexicons from the KB (e.g., full gazetteers)? How would performance change with noisy or incomplete entity lists?
- MemN2N ablations: What is the contribution of time features and speaker markers relative to hops and type features? Any results with position encoding or layer tying variants?
- Generalization: If you increase linguistic variability (e.g., more bot templates, paraphrases), how do results change? Are the findings robust to larger training sets?
- Real-data baselines: Can you compare against stronger DSTC2 baselines (e.g., RNN/LSTM-based state trackers producing actions) within your converted scoring framework?
- Per-dialog performance: Where do most failures in T3/T5 occur (sorting vs. state carryover vs. grounding)? Do attention visualizations consistently highlight the correct KB facts when answers are wrong?
