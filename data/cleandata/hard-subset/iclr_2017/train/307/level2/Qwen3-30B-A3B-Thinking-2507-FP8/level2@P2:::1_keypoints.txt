- Introduces a five-task restaurant reservation testbed and shows Memory Networks can perform non-trivial operations; testbed correlates with DSTC2 and a concierge dataset.
- Recommendation: reject due to insufficient novelty and impact for ICLR.
- Testbed resemblance to existing datasets (e.g., DSTC) without clear articulation of advantages or significant advancement.
- Evaluation limited to a single model architecture (Memory Networks) and basic baselines, lacking comparisons to recent end-to-end methods.
- Results are not convincing: marginal improvement over rule-based system on DSTC2 (45.1% vs 40.7%) does not demonstrate clear superiority.
- Insufficient error analysis; claims about strengths/shortcomings (e.g., Task 3 failures) are not substantiated or explained.
- Does not thoroughly explore limitations of the "match type features" approach for entity handling.
- Evaluation metrics problematic: per-dialog accuracy is near zero across tasks despite decent per-response accuracy, and this disconnect is unexplained.
- Paper should more clearly differentiate and justify how the testbed improves upon existing datasets with concrete examples.
- Needs comprehensive error analysis to identify specific failure modes in end-to-end systems.
- Should compare performance against more recent and diverse end-to-end architectures beyond Memory Networks.
- Must better justify the significance of marginal improvements over rule-based systems and consider stronger baselines.
- Should discuss how the testbed could be extended to domains beyond restaurant reservation.
- Key questions for authors: how the testbed addresses existing dataset limitations; confidence in the rule-based baseline comparison; detailed analysis of Task 3 failures; clarification of how Memory Networks differ from traditional slot-filling; and explanation for low per-dialog accuracy despite decent per-response scores.
