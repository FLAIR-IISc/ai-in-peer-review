

This paper introduces a testbed for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems, consisting of five restaurant reservation tasks that require manipulating natural language and symbols to conduct conversations, issue API calls, and interpret results. The authors demonstrate that Memory Networks can learn to perform non-trivial operations in this setting, and show that their testbed correlates with performance on two real-world datasets (DSTC2 and a concierge service dataset).

I recommend rejection. The primary reason is that the contribution is not sufficiently novel or impactful for ICLR. While creating a testbed is valuable, the paper fails to establish how this testbed significantly advances the field beyond existing datasets or provides a meaningful framework for driving progress in end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems.

The paper's main claims are not strongly supported. The testbed resembles existing datasets (e.g., DSTC) but doesn't clearly articulate its advantages. The evaluation is limited to a single model architecture (Memory Networks) compared to basic baselines, and the results don't convincingly demonstrate that end-to-end systems can compete with traditional methods. For instance, on DSTC2, Memory Networks achieve only 45.1% per-response accuracy versus 40.7% for a rule-based system, which is a marginal improvement that doesn't establish clear superiority.

The paper claims to "break down strengths and shortcomings" but provides insufficient error analysis to support this. For example, the authors note that Memory Networks fail on Task 3 (Displaying options), but don't analyze why this happens or how the testbed could help researchers address this specific issue. The paper also doesn't thoroughly explore the limitations of their "match type features" approach for handling entities.

The evaluation metrics are problematic. Per-dialog accuracy remains near 0 across most tasks, indicating that even when per-response accuracy is decent, the systems fail to complete the goal. The paper doesn't sufficiently explain why this disconnect exists or how the testbed could help address it.

The paper would benefit from:
1) A clearer articulation of how this testbed differs from and improves upon existing datasets, with specific examples of limitations in previous resources
2) More comprehensive error analysis to identify specific failure modes in end-to-end systems
3) Comparison against more recent end-to-end architectures beyond Memory Networks
4) Better justification for why the marginal improvements over rule-based systems on DSTC2 are meaningful
5) Discussion of how the testbed could be extended to other domains beyond restaurant reservation

Key questions for the authors:
1) How does your testbed specifically address the limitations of existing datasets for goal-oriented dialog evaluation? What concrete advantages does it offer over DSTC datasets, which are widely used in the field?
2) Given that your rule-based system achieves only 40.7% accuracy on DSTC2, how confident are you that this is a meaningful comparison against end-to-end models? Could a more sophisticated rule-based system close this gap?
3) Can you provide detailed error analysis for Task 3 failures? What specific aspects of the task are causing these failures, and how could the testbed help researchers address them?
4) The paper claims Memory Networks "learn to perform non-trivial operations," but doesn't clearly distinguish this from what traditional slot-filling systems can do. Can you clarify this distinction?
5) Why is per-dialog accuracy so low (close to 0) when per-response accuracy is decent on most tasks? What does this imply about the challenges of end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems?

While the paper identifies a relevant problem in the field, it doesn't provide sufficiently novel or impactful contributions to warrant acceptance at ICLR. The testbed is presented as a resource, but without clear evidence that it will significantly advance the field or that the evaluation framework provides unique insights beyond what's already available. The limited scope of the evaluation (only one model architecture) and lack of detailed error analysis make it difficult to assess the true value of the proposed testbed.
