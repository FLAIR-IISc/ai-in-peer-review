

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of goal-oriented dialog systems by introducing a well-designed testbed to systematically evaluate end-to-end approaches. The authors identify a critical gap in the literature: while end-to-end models have shown promise in chit-chat dialog, their performance in goal-oriented settings remains unclear due to the lack of suitable evaluation frameworks. The paper proposes five synthetic tasks within a restaurant reservation context that break down the key capabilities needed for successful goal-oriented dialog (issuing API calls, updating requests, displaying options, providing information, and conducting full dialogs), along with two real-world datasets for validation. The authors demonstrate that Memory Networks can achieve promising performance on these tasks, particularly when augmented with match type features for entity handling, while also highlighting persistent challenges in interpreting API results to guide user interactions.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The primary reasons are: (1) The paper addresses a significant gap in the literature with a well-motivated and clearly articulated problem statement, and (2) The proposed testbed is scientifically rigorous, well-designed, and provides a valuable resource for the community to systematically evaluate end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems.

The paper's strengths lie in its thoughtful design of the testbed tasks, which systematically isolate different capabilities required for goal-oriented dialog. The authors carefully consider the need for reproducibility and interpretability, addressing key limitations of existing datasets. The experimental setup is thorough, with ablation studies on match type features, OOV performance analysis, and validation on two real-world datasets (DSTC2 and Concierge). The results are scientifically sound, showing that while Memory Networks can learn to handle implicit dialog state and perform non-trivial operations like issuing API calls, they still struggle with interpreting API results to display options to users (Task 3), which is a crucial capability for goal-oriented dialog. This nuanced analysis provides valuable insights for future research.

To further improve the paper, I recommend the following specific suggestions:

1. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of why Task 3 (displaying options) proves particularly challenging for the models. The authors note that the models fail to interpret API results to display options, but it would be helpful to understand whether this is due to limitations in the model architecture, insufficient training data, or fundamental challenges in the task design. A brief analysis of specific failure cases would strengthen the paper's contribution.

2. The paper mentions that "Memory Networks with match type features give two performance gains" but doesn't clearly articulate the magnitude of these improvements. A more precise quantitative comparison (e.g., "Task 4 accuracy improved from X% to Y% with match type features") would help readers better understand the practical impact of this technique.

3. The discussion of the rule-based baseline for DSTC2 (T6) is somewhat brief. Given that the rule-based system achieved 40.7% per-response accuracy, it would be valuable to include a sentence or two explaining why this is considered a reasonable baseline for real-world data rather than a perfect solution.

4. The paper could clarify the relationship between the proposed testbed and the broader field of dialog state tracking. While the authors mention that DSTC data was converted to their format, it would help readers understand how this testbed complements rather than replaces existing dialog state tracking benchmarks.

I have a few questions to clarify my understanding:

1. The paper states that the model's failure on Task 3 is "typically" due to errors in interpreting API results. Could the authors provide one or two specific examples where the model correctly generated API calls but failed to interpret the results appropriately? This would help clarify the nature of the failure.

2. The paper mentions that the OOV performance improvements with match type features are "all due to better performance on known words." Could the authors elaborate on why this is the case, and whether they believe there's potential for further improvement in handling truly novel entities?

3. The authors note that the pattern of relative performance across methods is similar on both their synthetic tasks and the real-world Concierge dataset. Could they provide a brief explanation for why this consistency is significant for the field, beyond simply confirming that their synthetic data is a good proxy for real-world scenarios?
