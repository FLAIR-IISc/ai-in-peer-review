

The paper proposes a comprehensive testbed to evaluate end-to-end dialog systems in goal-oriented applications, specifically focusing on restaurant reservation. It introduces five well-designed tasks that break down the dialog process into subtasks testing dialog management (Task 1-2), KB querying and result interpretation (Task 3-4), and full dialog execution (Task 5). The testbed is designed to be lightweight, reproducible, and grounded in a knowledge base of restaurants. The authors demonstrate that Memory Networks can achieve promising but imperfect performance on these tasks, with per-dialog accuracy remaining low despite reasonable per-response accuracy. They validate their findings using two real-world datasets (DSTC2 and a concierge service dataset), showing that the synthetic tasks serve as a good proxy for real-world performance patterns.

The paper should be accepted. The primary reasons are that it addresses a significant gap in the literature (lack of standardized evaluation for end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems) and provides a well-designed, reproducible testbed that enables systematic analysis of model capabilities. The experimental setup is thorough, with careful consideration of OOV challenges and comparison across multiple baselines.

The work is well-motivated by the limitations of traditional slot-filling systems (hard to scale) and the question of whether end-to-end approaches successful in chit-chat can generalize to goal-oriented settings. The authors clearly position their work within the literature, acknowledging the lack of appropriate evaluation resources for end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. The testbed design is thoughtful, with each task targeting specific capabilities required for goal-oriented dialog that go beyond simple language modeling.

The paper provides strong empirical support for its claims through comprehensive experiments across synthetic and real-world data. The evaluation metrics (per-response and per-dialog accuracy) are appropriate for goal-oriented dialog, and the comparison against multiple baselines (rule-based, IR methods, supervised embeddings, Memory Networks) is thorough. The authors demonstrate that Memory Networks can learn to perform non-trivial operations (issuing API calls, handling OOV entities) while highlighting persistent challenges (interpreting KB results to display options). The correlation between performance patterns on synthetic and real-world data is particularly compelling evidence that the testbed serves as a valid evaluation proxy.

The paper could benefit from more detailed analysis of why certain tasks (particularly Task 3: displaying options) remain challenging for end-to-end models despite improvements with Memory Networks. While the paper notes that interpreting KB results is difficult, it would be valuable to identify specific aspects of this task that pose the greatest challenges to current models. A more concrete discussion of the error patterns for Task 3 would help guide future research.

The relationship between the proposed testbed and existing datasets (particularly DSTC) could be clarified. While the authors converted DSTC2 data into their task format, a more explicit comparison of how their testbed complements existing evaluation frameworks would strengthen the contribution. It would also be helpful to discuss how the testbed might be adapted for other goal-oriented domains beyond restaurant reservation.

The paper's discussion of match type features could be expanded to provide more specific examples of how these features help with OOV entities. The current explanation is somewhat abstract, and concrete examples would better illustrate the mechanism by which these features improve performance on OOV test sets.

The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed analysis of the differences between the synthetic tasks and the real-world concierge dataset. While the paper notes the concierge dataset is more diverse and variable, it would be valuable to identify which specific aspects of this variability (beyond vocabulary diversity) most significantly impact model performance.

The authors should clarify why the per-dialog accuracy remains low despite reasonable per-response accuracy. The paper mentions that "one incorrect response could result in a failed dialog," but a more detailed analysis of how small per-response errors compound into dialog failures would be helpful.

The paper provides a valuable resource for the community by making the testbed and datasets publicly available. This contribution has significant potential to accelerate research in end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems by enabling standardized evaluation and comparison. The work demonstrates that while end-to-end approaches show promise for goal-oriented dialog, they still face challenges in certain critical areas, providing clear directions for future work.
