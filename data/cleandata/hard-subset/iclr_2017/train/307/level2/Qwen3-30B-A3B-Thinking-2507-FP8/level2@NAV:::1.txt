

This paper proposes a valuable testbed for evaluating end-to-end dialog systems in goal-oriented applications, specifically designed to break down strengths and shortcomings through five carefully constructed restaurant reservation tasks. The authors demonstrate that Memory Networks can achieve promising performance on these tasks while revealing specific limitations in interpreting API results and handling OOV entities. They validate their findings across synthetic data, the DSTC2 dataset, and a real concierge service dataset, showing consistent patterns across evaluation settings.

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a significant contribution by providing the first systematic testbed for goal-oriented end-to-end dialog systems, addressing a clear gap in the field. The well-designed tasks effectively isolate key capabilities required for goal-directed conversations, and the comparison across multiple models (including a rule-based baseline) provides strong evidence for the testbed's utility.

The paper's main strength is its methodological rigor: the testbed is reproducible, the tasks are well-motivated, and the evaluation spans synthetic and real-world data. The authors convincingly show that end-to-end models can handle dialog management and API calls but struggle with interpreting results from knowledge bases (e.g., displaying restaurant options). The inclusion of match type features to address OOV challenges is particularly insightful.

To further strengthen the paper, I recommend:
1) More detailed discussion of why Memory Networks with match type features underperform on Task 2 (updating API calls) compared to without them.
2) Clarification on the training/validation split for the concierge dataset, as it's unclear how the authors handled the temporal nature of the data.
3) A brief discussion of how the testbed could be extended to other domains beyond restaurant reservation.

I would like to ask: How did you handle the potential distribution shift between the synthetic data used for training and the real-world DSTC2/concierge data? Specifically, did you observe any performance degradation when applying models trained on synthetic data to real-world datasets beyond the reported results?
