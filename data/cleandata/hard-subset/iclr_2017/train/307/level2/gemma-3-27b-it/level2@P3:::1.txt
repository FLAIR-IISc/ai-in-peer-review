This paper introduces a testbed for evaluating end-to-end dialog systems in a goal-oriented setting, specifically restaurant reservation. The work addresses the challenge that success in chit-chat does not necessarily translate to goal-oriented tasks, and aims to provide a reproducible and lightweight framework for analyzing the strengths and weaknesses of end-to-end models. The paper proposes five synthetic tasks, along with evaluations on two real-world datasets (DSTC2 and a concierge service dataset). The authors demonstrate the performance of Memory Networks, comparing them to a rule-based baseline and other methods.

The decision is to **accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a valuable contribution by creating a focused testbed for evaluating goal-oriented dialog systems. The synthetic tasks seem well-designed to isolate key capabilities, and the comparison to a rule-based baseline and the inclusion of real-world data strengthens the analysis. The experimental results clearly demonstrate the challenges faced by end-to-end models in this setting, highlighting areas for future research.

The paper’s strengths lie in its clear motivation, careful experimental design, and thorough analysis. The synthetic tasks offer a controlled environment for understanding model behavior, and the use of multiple datasets adds robustness to the findings. The inclusion of match type features to handle OOV words is a particularly insightful addition. However, there are several areas that could be improved. 

Firstly, while the paper mentions the hyperparameters used, a more detailed explanation of the hyperparameter tuning process would be beneficial. It's not entirely clear how the optimal settings were determined for each task. Secondly, the descriptions of the tasks, while generally clear, could be further enhanced with more illustrative examples. Specifically, demonstrating the differences between the tasks with concrete dialog turns would aid understanding. Thirdly, the impact of the match type features could be analyzed more thoroughly. The paper states that it improves performance on OOV tasks, but a more detailed ablation study showing the effect on each individual task would be informative. Finally, while the paper mentions limitations in the conclusion, expanding on potential avenues for future work – beyond simply improving the models – would be valuable. For instance, discussing alternative task formulations or architectural improvements.

Specifically, some questions for the authors are: 
* Could the authors provide more detail on the hyperparameter tuning process? Were grid search, random search, or other methods used?
* Can the authors elaborate on the rationale behind the choice of 3 or 4 hops for the Memory Networks? Is there a clear trade-off between the number of hops and performance?
* In addition to the OOV performance, how do the match type features affect the performance on in-vocabulary entities? Is there any degradation in performance for known entities?
* The paper shows Memory Networks outperform other models on DSTC2. What are the specific error modes of Memory Networks on this dataset? Can the error analysis inform future model development?
*  The rule-based system is only implemented for the DSTC2 dataset. Was there a reason why it wasn't implemented for the other datasets, especially the synthetic tasks? Would a rule-based system for the synthetic tasks offer a stronger baseline comparison?
