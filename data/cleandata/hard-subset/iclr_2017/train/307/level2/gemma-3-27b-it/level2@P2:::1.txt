This paper presents a valuable testbed for evaluating end-to-end dialog systems in a goal-oriented setting, specifically restaurant reservation. The authors identify the gap between the success of end-to-end models in chit-chat and their applicability to goal-oriented tasks, and propose a series of tasks with associated datasets designed to dissect the strengths and weaknesses of these models. The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and provides a thorough experimental evaluation of Memory Networks against several baselines.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper addresses an important problem in dialog systems research, proposes a well-designed testbed, and provides compelling evidence supporting its claims. The experimental setup is comprehensive, and the analysis is insightful.

**Supporting Arguments:**

* **Well-defined Problem:** The paper clearly articulates the challenge of transferring success from chit-chat to goal-oriented dialog. It convincingly argues that existing evaluation methods are insufficient and motivates the need for a more granular assessment of end-to-end models.
* **Strong Methodology:** The proposed tasks (T1-T5) are thoughtfully designed to isolate key capabilities required for goal-oriented dialog, such as API calling, KB interaction, and state tracking. The inclusion of out-of-vocabulary (OOV) tests adds another layer of complexity and realism. Supplementing synthetic data with real data from DSTC2 and an online concierge service further strengthens the evaluation.
* **Compelling Results:** The experimental results demonstrate that while Memory Networks show promise, they still fall short of achieving human-level performance, particularly in completing full dialogs. The consistent pattern of results across different datasets (synthetic and real) validates the testbed's effectiveness. The ablation study with match type features provides valuable insights into the importance of handling entities correctly. The systematic comparison with rule-based, IR, and supervised embedding baselines establishes a clear benchmark.
* **Reproducibility:** The authors openly release the dataset and tasks (http://fb.ai/babi), promoting reproducibility and facilitating future research.

**Additional Feedback:**

* **Task Difficulty:** While the breakdown into tasks is a strength, it might be useful to discuss the inherent difficulty of each task. For example, are certain tasks disproportionately challenging for current models? A more detailed error analysis for each task could be beneficial.
* **Match Type Features:** The improvements gained with match type features are substantial. It would be helpful to explore the limitations of this approach. Does it rely on a comprehensive KB? Are there cases where it fails to generalize?
* **Model Capacity:** The paper focuses on Memory Networks. While a reasonable choice, it would be interesting to briefly discuss the potential of other end-to-end architectures (e.g., Transformers) within this testbed.
* **Concierge Dataset Details:** Providing a bit more detail about the online concierge service data—e.g., the nature of the tasks users presented—would be helpful. Also, how the data was anonymized beyond the stated steps would be good to know.

**Questions for the Authors:**

* Could you elaborate on the design choices behind the specific patterns used to generate user and bot utterances in the simulation? Were any experiments conducted to assess the sensitivity of the results to these patterns?
* In the OOV tests, how are the new entities represented to the model beyond just being "unknown" words? Are any embedding techniques used to leverage information from similar entities?
* You mention that match type features improve performance on out-of-vocabulary tasks. How does the performance of match type features degrade as the size of the KB increases (i.e., with more potential entity types)?
* What were the primary reasons for choosing Memory Networks as the main end-to-end model? Were other architectures considered, and if so, why were they not explored in this study?
