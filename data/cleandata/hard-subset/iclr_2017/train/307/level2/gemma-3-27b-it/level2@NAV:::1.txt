## Review of "Breaking Down the Challenge of End-to-End Dialog Systems"

This paper presents a valuable contribution by introducing a testbed for evaluating end-to-end dialog systems in a goal-oriented setting, specifically restaurant reservation. The authors rightly point out the limitations of directly applying successes from chit-chat models to goal-oriented tasks and propose a series of tasks designed to isolate key capabilities like API calling and knowledge base manipulation. The inclusion of both synthetic and real-world datasets (DSTC2 and a concierge service) is a significant strength, allowing for both controlled experimentation and evaluation of generalizability.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The work addresses a critical gap in the evaluation of end-to-end dialog systems – a structured way to understand *where* they fall short in goal-oriented contexts. The proposed tasks are well-motivated and the experimental results clearly demonstrate the challenges remaining, even with a relatively powerful model like Memory Networks. 

The paper is well-structured and provides clear explanations of the tasks, models, and results. The analysis comparing Memory Networks to simpler baselines (rule-based, IR, supervised embeddings) is insightful and highlights the value of the proposed testbed. The inclusion of an OOV setting is particularly clever, forcing the model to generalize beyond memorization. The detailed appendices providing hyperparameters and example predictions further strengthen the paper.

However, there are a few points that could be improved. The discussion of the match type features feels slightly disconnected from the overall narrative and could be better integrated. Further analysis of *why* the Memory Networks struggle with tasks 3 and 5 would be beneficial. While the paper acknowledges limitations, a more detailed discussion of the potential biases introduced by the synthetic data would be helpful. 

Specifically, I would like to know: 

*  What is the impact of the specific language patterns used to generate utterances in the simulation? Could variations in these patterns affect the results?
*  How sensitive are the results to the size of the training dataset? Would increasing the training data lead to significant performance gains?
*  Could the authors provide more examples of failure cases for the Memory Networks in tasks 3 and 5, illustrating the specific errors being made?



Overall, this is a well-executed and insightful paper that provides a valuable resource for the community. Addressing the minor points above would further enhance its impact.
