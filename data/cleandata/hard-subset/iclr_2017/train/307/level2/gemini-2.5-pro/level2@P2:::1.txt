### Summary

This paper introduces a valuable new resource for the community: a testbed for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. The authors argue that while end-to-end systems have shown promise in chit-chat settings, their capabilities in goal-oriented scenarios remain unclear, partly due to a lack of suitable, reproducible, and analyzable benchmarks. To address this, they propose a set of five diagnostic tasks in the domain of restaurant reservations, generated by a simulator grounded in a knowledge base (KB). These tasks are designed to isolate and test specific abilities required for goal completion, such as issuing API calls, handling updates, and using information retrieved from the KB. The paper provides a thorough evaluation of several baseline methods, from classical IR to supervised embeddings and Memory Networks (MemNNs). A key finding is that while MemNNs show promising per-response accuracy, they still struggle to achieve high per-dialog completion rates, highlighting specific areas for improvement. Crucially, the authors validate their synthetic benchmark by showing that the relative performance of different models on their tasks mirrors the performance on two real-world datasets: the Dialog State Tracking Challenge 2 (DSTC2) and a proprietary dataset from an online concierge service.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Addresses a critical need:** The paper tackles the important and timely problem of benchmarking goal-oriented dialog systems. The lack of standardized, analyzable, and reproducible evaluation frameworks has been a significant bottleneck for progress in this area. This work provides a high-quality public resource that directly addresses this gap.
2.  **Strong methodology and execution:** The proposed task set is well-conceived, drawing inspiration from the successful bAbI framework to create diagnostic subtasks. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, comparing a strong end-to-end model against multiple relevant baselines. The validation of the synthetic tasks against real-world data is a major strength, providing confidence that improvements on this testbed will be meaningful for practical applications.

### Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The main contribution of this paper—the public dataset and task suite—is a significant service to the research community. Traditional dialog systems are difficult to scale, while the end-to-end systems that promise scalability have been difficult to evaluate rigorously in goal-oriented settings. This work provides the tools to do so in a controlled manner.

The design of the tasks is thoughtful. By breaking down the complex process of a goal-oriented conversation into sub-tasks (T1: API calls, T2: Updates, T3: Displaying options, etc.), the authors enable fine-grained analysis of model capabilities. This is far more insightful than a single, monolithic task. The inclusion of an Out-Of-Vocabulary (OOV) test set is particularly important, as it directly tests a model's ability to generalize to new entities, a crucial requirement for real-world deployment.

The experimental section is thorough and provides convincing evidence for the paper's claims. The baselines are well-chosen, ranging from simple IR models to a strong rule-based system and a state-of-the-art end-to-end model (Memory Networks). The results clearly demonstrate the limitations of current models; for instance, the large gap between per-response and per-dialog accuracy highlights how a single error can derail an entire conversation, a critical insight for future work. The introduction of "match type features" is a simple but effective idea that demonstrates how to inject domain knowledge (from the KB) to help models generalize to OOV entities.

Finally, the paper’s effort to ground its synthetic benchmark in reality is commendable. By converting the DSTC2 dataset into their format and also testing on the Concierge dataset, the authors show that the performance patterns observed on their synthetic tasks carry over to real, noisy, human-generated dialog. This validation step significantly increases the credibility and likely impact of the proposed testbed.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are intended to be constructive suggestions and do not detract from the paper's overall strength.

1.  **Ranking vs. Generation:** The evaluation is framed as a ranking task from a fixed candidate set. While this is a standard and practical approach, it is a simplification of the full dialog problem. It would strengthen the paper to include a brief discussion of this limitation. For example, acknowledging that generation is a harder problem and that this ranking setup is a first step towards evaluating generative models would provide useful context for the reader.
2.  **Simplicity of Simulated Language:** The user and bot utterances in the simulation are generated from a limited number of templates. While this is necessary for a controlled environment, it is a key difference from real-world language. The paper could be more explicit about this limitation in the main text (e.g., in Section 3.1) and perhaps suggest that future work on this testbed could involve augmenting the simulator with paraphrasing models to increase linguistic diversity.
3.  **Model Choice Justification:** The choice of Memory Networks is well-justified by their strong performance on related tasks. However, a single sentence explicitly contrasting them with other popular architectures like sequence-to-sequence models (e.g., mentioning that prior work found MemNNs to be more effective for QA-like tasks that require reasoning over context) would further strengthen the motivation in Section 4.4.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  The candidate set for ranking is constructed from all utterances across all splits and tasks. Could you comment on the size of this set? Furthermore, is there significant overlap between the correct responses in the test set and the candidate responses seen during training? Understanding this could help clarify the difficulty of the ranking task.
2.  The "match type features" are an effective way to handle OOV entities by leveraging the KB. Have you considered alternative approaches that don't rely on an explicit KB lookup for typing, such as using character-level or subword-level models (e.g., BPE) to generate representations for unseen entity names? A brief discussion on the potential of such methods could be insightful.
