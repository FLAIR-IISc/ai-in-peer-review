This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of goal-oriented dialog systems by introducing a new public testbed designed to systematically evaluate and analyze the capabilities of end-to-end models. The authors propose a suite of five synthetic tasks in the restaurant reservation domain, each targeting a specific sub-skill such as issuing API calls, handling updates, and using knowledge base results. The work is further grounded by including experiments on two datasets derived from real user interactions (DSTC2 and an online concierge service), demonstrating that the synthetic tasks serve as a useful proxy for real-world challenges.

I strongly recommend **Accepting** this paper. The key reasons are its creation of a much-needed, well-designed, and reproducible benchmark for a critical problem, and its thorough experimental analysis that provides both strong baselines and clear insights into the current limitations of end-to-end approaches.

The paper’s strengths are numerous. The decomposition of the goal-oriented dialog problem into discrete, testable subtasks is a significant strength, enabling fine-grained error analysis that is often lacking in holistic evaluations. The inclusion of an Out-Of-Vocabulary (OOV) evaluation setting directly addresses the crucial challenge of generalization to unseen entities. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, comparing a strong end-to-end model (Memory Networks) against a thoughtful set of baselines, including IR methods and a rule-based system. The finding that performance patterns on the synthetic tasks correlate with those on real data effectively validates the benchmark’s utility for the research community.

For potential improvement, I would suggest the following:
*   The paper focuses on a ranking-based evaluation. A brief discussion on the challenges of extending this testbed to generative models would be insightful for future work.
*   While the analysis of per-dialog accuracy highlights model failures, a qualitative error analysis with a few illustrative examples of where the best model fails on Task 3 (Displaying options) or Task 5 (Conducting full dialogs) could provide deeper intuition.

I have a few questions for the authors to clarify my understanding:
*   The Memory Network with match-type features performed slightly worse on Task 2 (Updating API calls) than the version without. Do you have an intuition for why this feature might hinder performance on this specific task?
*   The results on the DSTC2 data show that the Memory Network outperforms the rule-based baseline. Could you elaborate on the types of errors the rule-based system made that the neural model was able to handle correctly?
