### 1. Summary of the paper's claims/contributions

This paper addresses the important and timely problem of evaluating end-to-end, goal-oriented dialog systems. While end-to-end models have shown promise in open-domain chit-chat, their capabilities in task-oriented settings remain poorly understood. The authors argue that a lack of suitable, reproducible, and analyzable benchmarks hinders progress.

The main contribution is the introduction of a new open-source testbed designed to systematically evaluate the strengths and weaknesses of such systems. Inspired by the bAbI tasks, the authors propose a suite of five synthetic tasks in the restaurant reservation domain. These tasks are carefully designed to isolate and test specific abilities, such as issuing API calls (Task 1), updating queries (Task 2), presenting options from a knowledge base (Task 3), providing details about an entity (Task 4), and conducting a full dialog (Task 5). The testbed also includes an out-of-vocabulary (OOV) setting to test generalization to unseen entities.

To ground their synthetic tasks in reality, the authors also provide results on two datasets derived from real user interactions: a converted version of the DSTC2 dataset and a new dataset from an online concierge service. The paper provides a thorough empirical evaluation of several models, including classical IR methods, supervised embeddings, and Memory Networks. Their results show that Memory Networks, especially when augmented with "match type features" to handle entities, are a promising approach but still fall short of solving the more complex tasks, highlighted by the gap between per-response and per-dialog accuracy. The consistency in relative model performance across synthetic and real datasets suggests the proposed testbed is a valuable proxy for real-world challenges.

### 2. Decision and key reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Significant Community Contribution:** The paper introduces a well-designed, open, and reproducible benchmark for a critical research area. The lack of such resources has been a major bottleneck for progress in goal-oriented dialog systems. This work provides a valuable tool for future research, enabling standardized comparisons and targeted model development.
2.  **Insightful Task Design and Analysis:** The decomposition of the goal-oriented dialog problem into a series of subtasks is highly effective. It allows for a fine-grained analysis of model capabilities, revealing specific failure modes (e.g., interpreting and using KB results) that are obscured in end-to-end evaluations on monolithic tasks. The inclusion of both synthetic and real-world data provides a strong argument for the benchmark's validity and relevance.

### 3. Supporting arguments for the decision

The paper's primary strength lies in its contribution of a new public resource that directly addresses a clear need in the community. Traditional dialog datasets are often small, proprietary, or not well-suited for training end-to-end models from scratch. This work provides a clean, controlled environment that lowers the barrier to entry for research in this area.

The "bAbI-style" design of the tasks is a major advantage. By isolating capabilities like KB querying, state updates, and result presentation, the authors enable a much deeper form of error analysis than is typically possible. The finding that current end-to-end models can learn to issue API calls but struggle to correctly use the returned information is a crucial insight that can guide future architectural innovations. The distinction between per-response and per-dialog accuracy is a simple but powerful way to highlight the difficulty of maintaining conversational coherence and correctness over multiple turns, which is the ultimate goal.

The experimental evaluation is thorough and provides strong baselines for future work. The comparison spans from simple IR methods to a strong neural baseline (Memory Networks). The introduction of "match type features" is a simple yet effective technique for dealing with named entities and OOV words, a common challenge in dialog systems that must interface with knowledge bases. The empirical results are compelling: they demonstrate that while end-to-end models are learning non-trivial behaviors, they are far from achieving the reliability of traditional, hand-crafted systems, even in a narrow domain. This is a sobering but important conclusion. Finally, showing that the performance patterns on the synthetic tasks mirror those on the noisier, real-world DSTC2 and Concierge datasets validates the use of the synthetic benchmark as a proxy for research and development.

### 4. Additional feedback and questions for the authors

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   **Generative Models:** The evaluation is framed as a ranking task (Next-Utterance-Classification). This is a practical and common evaluation paradigm. However, a brief discussion on the challenges of adapting this testbed for generative models (e.g., seq2seq) would be valuable. For instance, how would correctness be evaluated for generated API calls or when presenting restaurant options?
*   **Simulator Language Complexity:** The user and bot utterances are generated from a relatively small set of patterns. This might lead models to overfit to specific phrasings rather than learning more general semantic understanding. It would be useful to discuss this limitation and how the consistent results on the DSTC2 and Concierge datasets (which feature more linguistic diversity) help to mitigate this concern.
*   **Error Analysis on Task 3:** The paper notes that models fail on Task 3 (Displaying options), which involves sorting restaurants by rating. It would be very insightful to include a more detailed error analysis here. Is the model failing to understand the sorting requirement, or is it failing at something more fundamental, like identifying the correct restaurant names from the KB results in the dialog history?
*   **Rule-Based System Details:** The paper mentions that the rule-based system for Task 6 was optimized on the validation set. Providing a few more details on this process (e.g., was it manual tuning of rule priority, and roughly how much effort was involved?) would help contextualize its performance relative to the learning-based systems.

**Questions for Clarification:**
*   In Table 2, the Memory Network with match type features performs slightly worse than the one without on Task 2 (per-response accuracy drops from 99.6% to 96.1%). This is somewhat counter-intuitive, as the features are designed to help. Do you have a hypothesis for why this might be the case?
*   For Task 1, the bot asks for missing information in a fixed, deterministic order. Have you experimented with randomizing this order? It seems this would make the task more challenging by requiring more robust implicit state tracking, and I would be interested to see how the models cope.
*   The choice of Memory Networks as the primary end-to-end model is well-justified. Have you considered other contemporary end-to-end architectures, such as a standard sequence-to-sequence model with attention, as an additional point of comparison? It would be interesting to see if different architectures exhibit different strengths and weaknesses across your suite of tasks.
