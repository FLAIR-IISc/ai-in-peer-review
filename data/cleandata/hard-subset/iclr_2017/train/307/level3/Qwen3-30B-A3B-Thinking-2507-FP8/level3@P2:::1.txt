

# Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary
This paper introduces a valuable synthetic testbed for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. The authors propose a set of five well-defined tasks within the restaurant reservation domain that require systems to perform non-trivial operations such as issuing API calls, updating requests, displaying options, and providing information. The paper makes a significant contribution by providing a standardized, reproducible evaluation framework that fills an important gap in the literature. The authors demonstrate that Memory Networks can achieve promising performance on these tasks, with results that generalize to real-world datasets (DSTC2 and an online concierge service), suggesting their testbed is a useful proxy for real-world performance.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field with a well-designed testbed that addresses a real need. The key reason for acceptance is that the proposed testbed provides a much-needed standard for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems, which will facilitate future research and comparisons.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper is well-motivated and addresses a genuine gap in the literature. The authors correctly identify that while end-to-end approaches have shown promise in chit-chat settings, their performance in goal-oriented settings is unclear due to the lack of standardized evaluation frameworks. Their synthetic testbed is designed to systematically break down the capabilities required for successful goal-oriented dialog, making it an excellent resource for the community.

The experimental results are scientifically rigorous, with careful attention to the OOV setting and validation across multiple datasets (including real-world data from DSTC2 and an online concierge service). The paper demonstrates that Memory Networks can learn to perform non-trivial operations in goal-oriented dialog, and that the patterns observed on the synthetic data generalize to real-world data, which is an important finding.

However, the paper has a few weaknesses that should be addressed in revision. The most significant is the lack of a non-neural baseline that incorporates word order (e.g., logistic regression with word embeddings, bigrams, and match-type features), which makes the neural models appear unnaturally strong. The authors acknowledge that "classical IR methods with no machine learning are standard baselines" but fail to implement a non-neural model that incorporates the same features as their neural baselines (e.g., match-type features). This omission makes the comparison less fair and potentially misleading.

Additionally, the claim that "existing work has no well-defined measures of performance" is inaccurate, as prior work (Wen et al., Liu et al., Yu et al.) has established evaluation metrics for end-to-end task-oriented dialog systems. This claim should be revised to acknowledge prior evaluation work.

## 4. Additional Feedback
To further strengthen the paper, I recommend adding a non-neural benchmark that incorporates word order information (e.g., logistic regression with word embeddings, bigrams, and match-type features). This would provide a more balanced comparison and help clarify whether the observed performance gaps are due to model architecture or simply the lack of appropriate baseline comparisons. The current baselines (TF-IDF Match, Nearest Neighbor) are too simplistic and don't represent the state of the art in non-neural approaches.

Also, while the synthetic tasks are useful for controlled evaluation, the authors should acknowledge that the deterministic nature of the tasks doesn't fully capture the noise and ambiguity present in real-world dialog systems (e.g., users making mistakes, needing dialogue repair). This limitation should be discussed in the paper.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. Why was a non-neural baseline that incorporates word order (e.g., logistic regression with bigrams and match-type features) not included in the comparison? How would the results change with such a baseline?
2. How do you respond to the claim that existing work (Wen et al., Liu et al., Yu et al.) has already established well-defined evaluation metrics for end-to-end task-oriented dialog systems? Should this claim be revised in the paper?
3. Given that the tasks are deterministic, how do you expect the performance of these models to degrade on more realistic, noisy dialog data, and what additional techniques might be needed to address this challenge?
