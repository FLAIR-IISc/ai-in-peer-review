

# Review for ICLR 2017 Paper

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces a novel, publicly available synthetic dataset and set of tasks for evaluating goal-oriented dialogue systems. The authors propose five rule-based tasks within a restaurant reservation domain that test specific capabilities including issuing API calls, updating API calls, displaying options, providing extra information, and conducting full dialogues. The dataset is designed to be deterministic and reproducible, with an underlying knowledge base to support the dialogue scenarios. The authors demonstrate that an end-to-end Memory Network model can achieve promising performance on these tasks, outperforming a hand-crafted slot-filling baseline on DSTC2 data and data from an online concierge service. This contribution is valuable as it provides a well-defined testbed for evaluating end-to-end dialogue systems in a controlled, reproducible manner.

## 2. Decision

I recommend acceptance with major revisions. The paper makes a useful and welcome contribution to the dialogue systems field by providing a well-designed testbed for evaluating end-to-end systems. However, the lack of a proper baseline comparison and the deterministic nature of the tasks require significant revisions before acceptance.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper addresses a genuine need in the field by providing a reproducible benchmark for goal-oriented dialogue systems. The synthetic dataset design allows for controlled experiments that are not possible with real-world data, which is valuable for isolating specific capabilities of dialogue systems. The authors' claim that existing work lacks well-defined performance measures is inaccurate—prior work by Wen et al., Liu et al., and Yu et al. has established evaluation metrics for dialogue systems.

The major weakness in the paper is the absence of a non-neural benchmark that incorporates word order (e.g., logistic regression with word embeddings, bi-gram features). Without such a baseline, the paper's claim that neural models outperform traditional approaches is misleading, as the comparison is biased toward neural methods. The authors' claim that "existing work has no well defined measures of performances" is inaccurate—prior work has established evaluation metrics for dialogue systems.

Additionally, the tasks are entirely deterministic, which limits their ability to evaluate how well models handle noisy or ambiguous interactions. Real-world dialogue systems must deal with uncertain user inputs, partial information, and dialogue repair, which these tasks do not test. This is a significant limitation for a paper claiming to evaluate goal-oriented dialogue systems.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To improve the paper, I recommend:

1. Adding a non-neural benchmark (e.g., logistic regression with word embeddings, bi-gram and match-type features) to provide a fair comparison with neural methods. This would likely increase the paper's score by providing a more balanced evaluation.

2. Clarifying the claim about evaluation metrics. The paper should acknowledge prior work on dialogue evaluation (Wen et al., Liu et al., Yu et al.) and explain how their evaluation approach differs.

3. Addressing the deterministic nature of the tasks. The authors should discuss how they would extend the tasks to include realistic noise and ambiguity, and how this would impact model performance.

4. Including a more thorough analysis of the Memory Networks' error patterns to better understand where the model fails, particularly on Tasks 3 and 5.

## 5. Clarification Questions

To better assess the paper's claims, I would like the authors to clarify:

1. Why the authors believe that traditional models would not be competitive with neural models on these tasks, given the deterministic nature of the tasks and the small training sets (1,000 examples)?

2. How the authors would modify the tasks to incorporate realistic noise and ambiguity, and what specific challenges such modifications would introduce for model evaluation?

3. Why the authors chose to exclude the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) test sets from the baseline comparison in Table 2 (the rule-based system results are only reported for the in-vocabulary test sets), and whether the rule-based system could be extended to handle OOV entities?

4. The authors' justification for claiming that "existing work has no well defined measures of performances" given the prior work cited in the references (Wen et al., Liu et al., Yu et al.).

The paper makes a valuable contribution by providing a new testbed for goal-oriented dialogue research, but addressing these weaknesses would significantly strengthen the paper and make it a more compelling contribution to the field.
