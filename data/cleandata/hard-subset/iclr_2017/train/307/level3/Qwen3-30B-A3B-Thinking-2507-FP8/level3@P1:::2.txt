

This paper introduces a valuable testbed for evaluating end-to-end dialog systems in goal-oriented applications, specifically for restaurant reservation. The authors create a controlled synthetic environment with five well-defined tasks that systematically break down the complex requirements of goal-oriented dialog into manageable subtasks. The paper demonstrates that current end-to-end models, while promising, still fall short in key areas like interpreting knowledge base results to present options to users. Crucially, the authors show that the relative performance of models on their synthetic testbed correlates strongly with performance on real-world data from the Dialog State Tracking Challenge and an online concierge service, making it a useful evaluation proxy. The paper's contribution is significant for both the dialog systems and machine learning communities, as it provides a much-needed standardized framework for assessing end-to-end approaches in goal-directed settings.

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a clear, important contribution to the field by addressing a significant gap in the evaluation of end-to-end dialog systems. The authors demonstrate that while end-to-end approaches can learn to track implicit dialog states and handle OOV entities, they still struggle with critical aspects of goal-oriented dialog, such as interpreting KB query results to display options to users. This finding is important because it challenges the assumption that success in chit-chat dialog settings automatically translates to success in goal-oriented applications.

The experimental protocol is excellent, with careful design of the synthetic tasks, clear evaluation metrics, and meaningful comparisons across multiple model types. The paper's strength lies in its systematic approach to breaking down the problem and providing error analysis by design. The results on the synthetic tasks correlate well with results on real-world data, which is compelling evidence for the testbed's validity as an evaluation proxy. The authors provide thorough ablation studies (e.g., the impact of "match type" features and number of hops in Memory Networks) that offer valuable insights into model behavior.

The paper's clarity is exceptional, with well-organized sections, informative tables, and a helpful appendix. The task definitions are precise and grounded in a realistic KB, making the testbed both practical and theoretically sound. The authors acknowledge the limitations of current end-to-end approaches without overstating their contribution, which is refreshing.

For improvement, I suggest clarifying the evaluation metrics. The paper's use of per-response accuracy can be misleading since it over-penalizes intermediate responses, while per-dialog accuracy is highly sensitive to task definitions (e.g., the counterintuitive T3 vs T5 results). Consider adding a discussion of how these metrics relate to real-world success, such as booking completion rate. Additionally, the "match type" feature, while effective for handling entities, doesn't fully solve vocabulary mismatch between user language and KB terms. The authors should elaborate on how their approach would scale to domains with more complex semantic relationships.

I have two specific questions for the authors to clarify my understanding:
1. The paper states that "match type features" improve OOV performance but slightly decrease performance on Task 2. Can you explain why this occurs, given that Task 2 involves updating API calls with new information?
2. For the concierge dataset, you evaluate using per-response accuracy among all candidate responses. Given that in real-world systems, the "correct" response might be the single best among many plausible options, why not use a metric like "is the correct response within the top 5 candidates" rather than requiring exact match?

The paper does not adequately address how to handle vocabulary mismatch beyond the "match type" feature. While the feature works well for exact KB matches, it doesn't learn semantic associations automatically, which could limit scalability to domains with more diverse user language. A discussion of how to extend this approach to handle semantic matching of entities would strengthen the paper.

The authors suggest that more meaningful loss functions tied to real-world success metrics would be valuable. I agree, and would encourage future work to explore application-driven evaluation metrics, such as weighted sums of API errors, dialog turns, and user satisfaction, which would require human users or realistic simulators. This would move the field beyond current brittle metrics that often don't correlate well with human judgment.

Overall, this is a strong contribution that provides a much-needed framework for evaluating end-to-end dialog systems in goal-oriented settings. The testbed is well-designed, the experiments are thorough, and the insights are valuable for both researchers and practitioners. The paper's contribution to the field is significant, and I believe it would be a valuable addition to ICLR 2017.
