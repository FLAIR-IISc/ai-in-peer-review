

This paper introduces a novel testbed for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems using a restaurant reservation domain with a structured knowledge base (KB). The authors propose five specific tasks that break down the dialog process into subtasks requiring manipulation of both natural language and KB symbols, including issuing API calls, updating requests, displaying options, providing information, and conducting full dialogs. The key contribution is a well-designed synthetic dataset that enables reproducible evaluation of end-to-end models, with evaluation using both per-response and per-dialog accuracy metrics. The authors demonstrate that Memory Networks outperform classical IR and supervised embedding baselines, particularly when augmented with match type features for handling entities.

I recommend acceptance. The paper addresses a critical gap in evaluating end-to-end dialog systems for goal-oriented applications, where prior work has focused on chit-chat settings that don't translate well to practical applications. The authors clearly position their work within the literature, showing how their testbed differs from existing datasets (which are either noisy, domain-specific, or not designed for end-to-end learning) and how their tasks systematically break down the capabilities needed for goal-oriented dialog.

The paper supports its claims through rigorous experiments across multiple datasets (simulated tasks, DSTC2, and a real concierge service), showing consistent patterns of results. The Memory Networks approach demonstrates promising performance on the synthetic tasks, particularly for tasks requiring implicit state tracking (Tasks 1-2), and the results generalize to real-world data. The ablation studies on match type features and OOV performance provide valuable insights into model capabilities and limitations. The results are scientifically rigorous, with careful attention to data splits, evaluation metrics, and baselines.

The primary concern I have is the ranking-based evaluation approach. While the paper justifies this as a practical approach for evaluation, the ranking task depends on pre-collected candidate responses, which creates a significant practical limitation. In a new domain, where would these candidate response lists come from if not hand-collected? This undermines the claim that end-to-end systems can escape the need for domain-specific handcrafting. The paper would be stronger if it demonstrated generation capabilities or provided a clearer path to obtaining candidate responses in new domains.

Another concern is the rule-based NL generation for creating the synthetic dialog data. While this ensures reproducibility, it limits the variability of training data compared to human-generated conversations. I request that the authors publish the full list of NL generation patterns to enable full reproducibility and to allow readers to assess the potential biases introduced by the rule-based approach.

The authors make a compelling case that their synthetic data serves as a good proxy for real-world performance, as evidenced by the consistent relative performance patterns across the synthetic tasks and real datasets. However, I'm skeptical about the practical value of end-to-end training in narrow domains where hand-crafted systems are known to work well, as the paper acknowledges. The authors should address this tension more directly, perhaps by discussing when end-to-end approaches might be preferable to hand-engineered systems in practice.

To improve the paper, I recommend:
1. Clarifying the practical path for obtaining candidate responses in new domains
2. Providing the full NL generation rules as supplementary material
3. Adding a more nuanced discussion of the trade-offs between end-to-end and hand-crafted approaches in narrow domains

I have two key questions for the authors:
1. How would you propose obtaining candidate response lists for a new domain without handcrafting, given that the ranking approach depends on these candidates?
2. Given that the rule-based NL generation creates a limited vocabulary of possible utterances, how would you expect the model to generalize to more diverse human language in real-world applications?

The paper makes an important contribution to the field by providing a well-designed testbed for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. While the ranking approach presents some practical limitations, the overall contribution is valuable and addresses a significant gap in the literature. The systematic evaluation approach, combined with the demonstration of model capabilities and limitations, makes this an important resource for future research in end-to-end dialog systems.
