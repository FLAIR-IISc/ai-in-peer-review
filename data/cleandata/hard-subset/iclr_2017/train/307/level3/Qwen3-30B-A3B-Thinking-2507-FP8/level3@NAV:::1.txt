

**Review**

This paper introduces a valuable new synthetic dataset and set of tasks for evaluating goal-oriented dialogue systems, specifically designed around restaurant reservation scenarios. The authors propose five well-defined tasks covering API calls, option display, and full dialogues, along with real-world datasets for validation. This contribution is highly useful and addresses a genuine need in the field for standardized, reproducible evaluation of end-to-end dialogue systems, particularly in goal-oriented settings.

**Decision:** Accept (with minor revisions)

**Reasons for Acceptance:** The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by providing a well-structured testbed for evaluating dialogue systems. The synthetic dataset is clearly designed to isolate specific capabilities of dialogue systems, and the authors demonstrate that their Memory Network approach shows promise while highlighting clear limitations. The comparison with real-world datasets (DSTC2 and Concierge) strengthens the paper's relevance.

**Supporting Arguments:** The paper successfully addresses the need for a controlled environment to test end-to-end dialogue systems, and the tasks are well-motivated by the limitations of traditional slot-filling approaches. The results show that neural models can handle some aspects of goal-oriented dialogue (like API calls) but struggle with others (like interpreting and displaying results from API calls), providing clear insights for future research.

**Additional Feedback:** The paper would be significantly strengthened by adding a non-neural benchmark (e.g., logistic regression with word embeddings, bigram features, and match-type features) that properly accounts for word order. The current baselines (TF-IDF Match, Nearest Neighbor) are inadequate for comparison, making neural models appear unnaturally strong. The claim that "existing work has no well-defined measures of performance" is inaccurate - prior work by Wen et al., Liu et al., and Yu et al. has established evaluation metrics for dialogue systems. Including these references would improve the paper's scholarly rigor.

**Questions for Authors:** 
1. Could you provide the non-neural baseline results (logistic regression with word order features) to better contextualize your neural model performance?
2. How would the inclusion of match-type features for non-neural models affect the relative performance comparison between neural and non-neural approaches?
