

This paper introduces a valuable new testbed for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialogue systems, addressing a significant gap in the literature. The authors propose a set of five synthetic tasks within a restaurant reservation domain that systematically break down the capabilities required for goal-oriented dialog (issuing API calls, updating requests, displaying options, providing information, and conducting full dialogs). These tasks are designed to be reproducible and facilitate precise error analysis by isolating specific capabilities. The authors also provide two additional datasets from real-world interactions (DSTC2 and a concierge service) to validate that their synthetic testbed correlates well with real performance. Their experiments demonstrate that Memory Networks can learn to perform non-trivial operations like issuing API calls and manipulating entities, but struggle with interpreting KB results to display options or provide information, offering clear insights into current end-to-end models' limitations.

Decision: Accept (Weak Accept)

Key reasons: The paper makes a significant contribution by providing a well-structured, reproducible testbed for goal-oriented dialogue systems that addresses a clear gap in the literature. The experiments are well-designed and provide valuable insights into the strengths and limitations of current end-to-end approaches.

Supporting arguments: The synthetic nature of the tasks enables precise error analysis, which is difficult to achieve with real-world datasets. The authors demonstrate that their testbed correlates well with real-world data (DSTC2 and concierge service), validating its usefulness as an evaluation proxy. Their findings that Memory Networks can learn to issue API calls but fail at interpreting results to display options provide clear guidance for future work. The paper makes all data and code publicly available, which will facilitate reproducibility and further research.

Additional feedback:
1) The claim that "existing work has no well defined measures of performance" is inaccurate. Prior work by Wen et al. (2015), Liu et al. (2016), and Yu et al. has established evaluation protocols for end-to-end dialogue systems. Please revise this statement to be more precise.

2) The evaluation of neural models is missing a crucial comparison to non-neural baselines that incorporate word order information (e.g., logistic regression with bi-grams and match-type features). The current baseline (TF-IDF) is too simplistic and makes neural models appear stronger than they might be relative to more sophisticated non-neural approaches. Adding such a baseline would strengthen the paper.

3) The tasks are completely deterministic, which is a limitation for evaluating dialogue systems in real-world settings where interactions are noisy and ambiguous. Consider adding a section discussing how the synthetic nature of the tasks might limit the generalizability of the findings.

4) The paper states that "end-to-end models are not competitive with traditional methods" but then shows Memory Networks outperform a rule-based baseline on DSTC2. This is confusing and should be clarified.

Questions for authors:
1) How do you justify the claim that "existing work has no well defined measures of performance" given the extensive prior work on evaluation metrics for dialogue systems?

2) What would be the impact on the results if a non-neural baseline that incorporates word order information (e.g., logistic regression with bi-grams) were included?

3) The paper states that the synthetic tasks are "lightweight and easy to use" - how would you recommend researchers adapt this testbed to other domains beyond restaurant reservation?

4) You show that Memory Networks with match-type features improve OOV performance, but do you see any limitations to this approach when applied to domains with more complex entity types?
