

# Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary
This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by introducing a well-designed testbed for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. The authors propose a set of five tasks within the restaurant reservation domain that require manipulating natural language and symbols to conduct conversations, issue API calls, and use the outputs of such calls. Grounded in a knowledge base of restaurants, these tasks test crucial capabilities such as dialog management, KB querying, and interpretation of query results. The authors create a simulated dataset with rule-based natural language generation, and demonstrate that Memory Networks can learn to perform non-trivial operations on these tasks, showing promising but imperfect performance. They validate their findings on real-world datasets from the Dialog State Tracking Challenge and an online concierge service, confirming that their synthetic tasks serve as a good proxy for real-world performance. The paper provides a useful resource for the community to systematically evaluate and improve end-to-end dialog systems.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by providing a well-structured, reproducible testbed for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems, addressing an important gap in the literature. The experimental results provide valuable insights into the capabilities and limitations of current end-to-end approaches.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper successfully addresses three key questions that are critical for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems:

1. The specific question tackled is: "How well do end-to-end dialog models perform in goal-oriented settings compared to traditional hand-crafted approaches, and where do they fall short?"

2. The approach is well motivated and placed in the literature. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of current evaluation methods (reliance on Mechanical Turk, lack of reproducibility) and the gap in available testbeds for goal-oriented dialog. They correctly note that success in chit-chat settings does not necessarily translate to goal-oriented applications, and their testbed is designed to systematically break down these capabilities.

3. The paper strongly supports its claims through rigorous experiments across multiple datasets (simulated tasks, DSTC2, and real concierge data). The results consistently show that Memory Networks outperform classical IR and supervised embedding baselines on the proposed tasks, and the pattern of results generalizes to real-world data. The authors provide detailed error analysis, demonstrating that the models can learn to track implicit dialog states and manipulate OOV entities but struggle with interpreting KB results to present options to users (Task 3).

## 4. Additional Feedback and Questions
While the paper is strong, I have a few suggestions to improve it:

1. The ranking-based approach (selecting from pre-defined candidates) limits the generalization potential of the models. The authors should discuss how candidate response lists would be generated in new domains without hand-crafting. In practice, how would this system scale to new domains without extensive human annotation of candidate responses?

2. The rule-based NL generation for the simulator creates a very constrained training environment. To improve reproducibility and allow for more rigorous comparison, the authors should publish the complete list of NL generation rules used in the simulator. This would help other researchers understand the limitations of the synthetic data.

3. The paper could benefit from more discussion on the practical implications of these findings. While the authors state their goal isn't to improve state-of-the-art for restaurant booking, they could better address why end-to-end approaches might be preferable to traditional slot-filling in practice, given that hand-crafted systems may yield better user satisfaction in narrow domains.

4. The authors should clarify how their testbed differs from the bAbI tasks. While they mention bAbI as inspiration, the tasks here are dialog-based rather than question-answering, which is an important distinction.

5. The paper could include a brief discussion of how the proposed testbed might be extended to other domains beyond restaurant reservation, and what modifications would be needed for different application scenarios.

These points are meant to help strengthen the paper and make it even more valuable to the community, rather than being a reason for rejection. The paper is well-written, the experiments are thorough, and the contribution is significant for the field of conversational AI.
