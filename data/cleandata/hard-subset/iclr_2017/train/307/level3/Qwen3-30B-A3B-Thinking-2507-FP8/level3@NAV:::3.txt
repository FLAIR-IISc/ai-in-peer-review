

# Review

The paper introduces a valuable resource for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems through a novel simulated restaurant reservation dataset. The authors create a controlled testbed with five specific tasks that systematically break down the capabilities required for successful goal-oriented dialog, including API call generation, state updates, option display, and information provision. By grounding the simulation in a knowledge base and using rule-based NL generation, they provide a reproducible framework to analyze system strengths and weaknesses. The paper demonstrates that Memory Networks can learn non-trivial operations in this domain, outperforming simpler baselines in per-response accuracy, though per-dialog performance remains limited. The evaluation on real-world datasets (DSTC2 and a concierge service) confirms the synthetic data serves as a reasonable proxy for real-world performance patterns.

I recommend acceptance. The paper addresses a significant gap in the literature by providing a well-structured, reproducible testbed specifically designed for goal-oriented end-to-end dialog evaluation, which is lacking in current resources. The systematic decomposition of tasks enables meaningful error analysis that would be difficult to achieve with existing datasets. The authors' demonstration that Memory Networks can learn to manipulate symbols and KB entities in a goal-oriented context is a meaningful contribution to the field.

To further strengthen the paper, I suggest:
1) Publishing the complete list of NL-generation rules to ensure full reproducibility
2) Exploring the potential of generation rather than ranking to better reflect real-world deployment scenarios
3) Addressing the practical concerns about candidate response lists in new domains by discussing potential collection methods

I would appreciate clarification on how the authors envision the candidate response lists being generated for new domains beyond the restaurant reservation setting, as this is a critical practical consideration for real-world adoption of the approach. The current ranking setup, while useful for controlled evaluation, may limit generalizability to domains where candidate response generation would be required.
