

# Review of "A Testbed for End-to-End Goal-Oriented Dialog Systems"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contribution

This paper introduces a valuable new simulated restaurant-domain dataset designed to systematically evaluate end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. The authors create a testbed consisting of five specific tasks within the restaurant reservation domain, grounded in an underlying knowledge base (KB) with restaurant properties. The dataset is generated using rule-based natural language generation, allowing for controlled evaluation of key dialog capabilities: issuing API calls, updating API calls, displaying options, providing extra information, and conducting full dialogs. The authors evaluate multiple models (rule-based systems, classical IR methods, supervised embeddings, and Memory Networks) using both per-response and per-dialog accuracy metrics. Their main contribution is demonstrating that Memory Networks can outperform bag-of-words baselines and learn to perform non-trivial operations like manipulating KB entities and issuing API calls, while also providing a reproducible testbed for future research. The paper's value lies in its systematic approach to evaluating end-to-end systems in goal-oriented settings, a critical gap in the literature.

## 2. Decision

Accept

Key reasons: The paper provides a much-needed reproducible testbed for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems, and the results demonstrate meaningful insights about the capabilities and limitations of current approaches. The experimental design and evaluation metrics are well-suited for the problem domain.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper is well-motivated, clearly identifying the gap in the literature regarding systematic evaluation of end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. Traditional slot-filling approaches are known to perform well in narrow domains but are hard to scale, while end-to-end approaches have shown promise in chit-chat settings but their applicability to goal-oriented tasks remains unclear. The authors correctly identify that existing datasets are either too noisy (DSTC), proprietary, or designed for different purposes (e.g., state tracking), making consistent comparisons difficult.

The methodology is scientifically rigorous. The authors designed five specific tasks that isolate different capabilities of dialog systems, allowing for detailed error analysis. The use of both synthetic data (with controlled variations like OOV entities) and real-world data (DSTC2 and concierge service) strengthens the validity of their findings. Their evaluation metrics (per-response and per-dialog accuracy) are well-chosen for goal-oriented dialog, with per-dialog accuracy being particularly important for measuring success in achieving the actual task.

The results support the paper's claims: Memory Networks consistently outperform classical IR and supervised embedding baselines, demonstrating the value of end-to-end learning for certain aspects of goal-oriented dialog. The authors also show that their synthetic data is a good proxy for real-world performance, with similar relative performance patterns across datasets. The OOV experiments further highlight the limitations of current approaches in handling new entities, which is critical for real-world deployment.

## 4. Additional Feedback and Questions

The paper would benefit from addressing the following points to strengthen its contribution:

1. The reviewer's skepticism about the value of end-to-end training in narrow domains is valid. The authors could better address why end-to-end approaches might still be valuable despite potential user satisfaction benefits of hand-crafted systems, perhaps by discussing the long-term maintenance costs or scalability advantages.

2. The ranking-based evaluation approach (selecting from pre-defined candidates) is a limitation that the reviewer rightly identifies. The authors could better justify why this was chosen over generation, and discuss how their testbed might be adapted for generation-based evaluation.

3. The rule-based NL generation used in the simulator restricts variability in training data. The authors should publish the list of NL-generation rules as supplementary material to ensure full reproducibility, as requested by the reviewer.

4. The paper could better address the practical question of where candidate response lists would come from in a new domain. The authors should clarify how their testbed would scale to new domains without requiring pre-collected candidates.

Questions for the authors:
1. How would you expect the dataset to be adapted for evaluating systems in new domains (e.g., hotel booking) where candidate response lists would need to be generated rather than selected from a pre-defined set?
2. Can you provide more detail on the specific advantages of end-to-end learning that might outweigh the potential user satisfaction benefits of hand-crafted systems in narrow domains?
3. How would you address the concern that ranking-based evaluation might not generalize to settings where candidate responses must be generated rather than selected?
4. Would the authors consider extending their testbed to include a generation-based evaluation option in future work?

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field by providing a reproducible testbed for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. While there are limitations in the evaluation approach, the authors provide valuable insights into the current capabilities and shortcomings of end-to-end models, and the testbed itself will likely become a standard resource for future research in this area.
