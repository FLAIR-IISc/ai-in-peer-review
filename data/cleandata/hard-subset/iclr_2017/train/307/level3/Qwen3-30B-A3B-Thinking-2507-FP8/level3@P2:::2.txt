

# Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary
This paper makes a significant contribution by introducing a well-designed testbed to systematically evaluate end-to-end dialog systems in goal-oriented settings. The authors address a critical gap in the literature by proposing five specific tasks within a restaurant reservation domain that test crucial capabilities such as dialog management, KB querying, result interpretation, and handling of OOV entities. The paper provides synthetic data for these tasks, creates OOV test sets to evaluate generalization, and validates findings on real-world data from DSTC2 and an online concierge service. The authors demonstrate that while current end-to-end approaches (particularly Memory Networks with "match type" features) show promise, they still fall short of rule-based systems in this well-defined narrow domain, providing valuable insights into the limitations of end-to-end methods for goal-oriented dialog.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by providing the first comprehensive testbed for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems, with strong experimental validation across multiple datasets. The work is significant for both the Dialog/NLP and ML communities.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper excels in several key areas that justify acceptance:

First, the paper successfully addresses a critical gap in the literature. While end-to-end dialog systems have shown promise in chit-chat settings, their performance in goal-oriented applications has been unclear. The authors' well-designed testbed provides a much-needed standardized framework for evaluation, which has been lacking in the field.

Second, the two key conjectures made by the authors are well-supported by their experiments: (1) end-to-end ML performance is still inadequate for goal-oriented dialogs, and (2) relative performance on well-matched synthetic data predicts performance on natural data. The paper provides convincing evidence through multiple datasets and careful analysis of error patterns.

Third, the experimental protocol is excellent. The authors pay careful attention to reproducibility, create meaningful OOV test sets, and provide detailed ablation studies (e.g., the impact of different numbers of hops in Memory Networks). The paper is exceptionally clear with helpful appendices and informative tables.

Fourth, the work represents the first thorough deployment and test of end-to-end methods on goal-oriented dialog tasks. While the main algorithmic contribution is the "match type" addition to Memory Networks, the real value lies in the systematic evaluation framework and the insights it provides about current end-to-end approaches.

## 4. Additional Feedback
While the paper is strong, there are several areas where it could be improved:

- The discussion of evaluation metrics would benefit from more depth. The authors rightly note that per-response accuracy can be misleading, but they could explore how to better align metrics with real-world success criteria. For instance, a booking success rate metric (focusing on the API call correctness rather than all intermediate responses) might be more meaningful than the current per-dialog accuracy.

- The authors raise an important point about the counterintuitive T3 vs T5 results driven by task definitions. They could provide more detailed analysis of why Task 3 (displaying options) proves particularly challenging despite the model's ability to handle other aspects of the dialog.

- The paper could be strengthened by discussing how to handle vocabulary mismatch between user language and KB terms more thoroughly. The "match type" approach requires exact KB occurrences and doesn't automatically learn semantic associations, which is a limitation in real-world applications where user language varies.

- The authors might consider suggesting more application-driven loss functions (e.g., weighted sum of API errors, dialog turns, user rejections) tied to real-world business metrics, which would require human users or realistic simulators for proper evaluation.

## Questions for Authors
1. How would you modify the evaluation metrics to better align with real-world success criteria (e.g., booking success rate, user satisfaction) rather than focusing solely on response correctness?

2. Could you provide more insight into why Task 3 (Displaying options) is particularly challenging for end-to-end models, despite the model's ability to handle other aspects of the dialog? What specific aspects of the task make it difficult to learn?

3. How would you extend this testbed to handle more complex dialog domains with richer semantic structures, and what additional challenges would arise in such extensions?

The paper provides a valuable reality check for the field, demonstrating that current end-to-end ML approaches are insufficient for goal-oriented dialog tasks, while also showing promising directions for future work. The authors have created an important resource for the community that will help guide future research in this critical area.
