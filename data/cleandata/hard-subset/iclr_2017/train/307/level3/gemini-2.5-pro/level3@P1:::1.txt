### Review

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a new public dataset and a set of tasks for evaluating end-to-end, goal-oriented dialog systems. The work is framed in the context of restaurant reservations and is designed to break down the problem into five distinct, progressively more complex sub-tasks, such as issuing API calls, displaying options, and conducting a full conversation. The tasks are generated via a simulator, which allows for controlled experiments, including an out-of-vocabulary (OOV) setting to test generalization to unseen entities. The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation of several baseline models, including classical IR methods, supervised embeddings, and Memory Networks (MemNNs). Their experiments show that MemNNs, particularly when augmented with "match type" features, outperform other baselines and can learn non-trivial behaviors like issuing API calls and handling OOV entities. However, performance on full dialogs remains low, highlighting current limitations. The authors validate these findings on two real-world datasets (DSTC2 and a proprietary concierge service), showing that the performance patterns on their synthetic tasks are a good proxy for real-world performance. The release of this task suite is a welcome contribution that can facilitate reproducible research and systematic analysis of goal-oriented dialog agents.

**Decision: Reject (with encouragement to resubmit)**

The primary reasons for this decision are:
1.  The paper's central claim about the strength of end-to-end neural models is not fully supported due to the absence of a strong, non-neural baseline that incorporates word order information.
2.  The deterministic and template-based nature of the synthetic tasks limits the scope of the evaluation, as it does not test the models' ability to handle the noise, ambiguity, and error-recovery scenarios that are central to real-world dialog challenges.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The main weakness of this paper lies in its experimental comparison. The baselines chosen—TF-IDF, Nearest Neighbor, and bag-of-embeddings—all fundamentally ignore word order. This is a significant omission, as it creates an unfair comparison that makes the attention-based Memory Network appear stronger than it might be against a more competitive baseline. On tasks with relatively structured and templated language, a simple but powerful model like logistic regression or an SVM equipped with bag-of-word, n-gram (e.g., bigram), and the authors' own match-type features could perform very well. Without this comparison, it is difficult to conclude that the MemNN's performance advantage stems from its sophisticated architecture rather than its implicit ability to capture some sequence information that the other baselines cannot.

Furthermore, the five synthetic tasks are generated by a deterministic simulator. This means the bot's behavior is entirely predictable, and user utterances are constructed from a small set of patterns. While this controlled environment is valuable for analyzing specific capabilities, it sidesteps many core challenges of dialog systems. Real-world dialog involves understanding ambiguous requests, tracking distributions over user goals (belief tracking), and engaging in dialogue repair when misunderstandings occur. The current tasks do not evaluate a model's robustness to these phenomena. While the inclusion of the DSTC2 and Concierge datasets is a good step towards validation on real data, the primary contribution is the new synthetic task suite, and its limitations should be a key consideration.

Finally, the training sets are quite small (1000 dialogs). In such low-data regimes, complex deep learning models are prone to overfitting, and their success can be highly dependent on regularization. It is plausible that a simpler, well-regularized model could match or exceed the performance of the MemNN. The paper does not provide analysis on how performance scales with data size, which would help clarify whether the MemNN is truly a more suitable architecture for this problem.

**Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors:**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper. The dataset you have created is a valuable resource, and strengthening the experimental section would make this a very strong contribution.

*   **Crucial Baseline:** I strongly recommend adding a non-neural but strong baseline to your experiments. A logistic regression classifier with features including unigrams, bigrams, and your proposed match-type features would be an excellent candidate. This would provide a much more compelling point of comparison and help isolate the true benefits of the MemNN architecture. The inclusion of such a baseline would very likely increase my assessment of the paper.
*   **Clarification of Claims:** The introduction states that "no good resources exist to train and test end-to-end models in goal-oriented scenarios," and the conclusion suggests that existing measures of performance "prevent reproducibility... or do not correlate well with human judgements." These claims are slightly overstated. There has been prior work on end-to-end evaluation in task-oriented dialog (e.g., Wen et al., 2015) and on developing better automatic evaluation metrics for open-domain dialog (e.g., Liu et al., 2016). Please consider softening this language to more accurately position your contribution within the existing literature.

I would appreciate it if you could address the following questions in a rebuttal:

1.  What was the reasoning for not including a baseline that uses n-gram features to capture word order, such as a logistic regression model or even a simple RNN/LSTM?
2.  Given the small dataset size, how can you be confident that the MemNN's superior performance is not primarily an effect of its specific regularization or optimization, compared to the other models?
3.  Could you elaborate on the design choice to make the simulator fully deterministic? While this aids interpretability, it moves the task away from key dialog challenges like handling ambiguity. Do you have plans to introduce stochasticity or more linguistic variation in future versions?
