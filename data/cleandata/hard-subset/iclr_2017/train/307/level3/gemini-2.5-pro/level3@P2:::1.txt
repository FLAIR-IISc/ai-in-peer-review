### Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces a valuable and much-needed open-source testbed for evaluating end-to-end, goal-oriented dialog systems. The authors propose a set of five synthetically generated tasks in the restaurant reservation domain, designed to systematically probe the capabilities of models in areas such as dialog management, issuing API calls, and using the results from a knowledge base. A key contribution is the thoughtful breakdown of a full dialog into subtasks, which facilitates detailed error analysis. The authors also test generalization to out-of-vocabulary (OOV) entities by providing a distinct test set. To ground their synthetic benchmark in reality, they demonstrate that performance patterns on their tasks correlate with those on two real-world datasets (DSTC2 and a proprietary concierge service). The paper provides a comprehensive set of baselines, including IR methods, supervised embeddings, and Memory Networks, showing that while current end-to-end models are promising, they still fall short of solving these goal-oriented tasks completely. This work represents a significant contribution that will likely foster reproducible research and accelerate progress in goal-oriented dialog systems.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Weak Accept

This paper is a borderline case, but I am leaning toward acceptance for two primary reasons:

1.  **Positive:** The main contribution is a well-designed, publicly available dataset and benchmark that addresses a clear need in the community for reproducible and analytical evaluation of goal-oriented dialog systems. This is a strong and welcome contribution.
2.  **Negative:** The experimental evaluation is undermined by the absence of a strong, non-neural baseline that incorporates word order information (e.g., n-grams). This makes it difficult to ascertain whether the superior performance of the Memory Network is due to its sophisticated reasoning capabilities or simply because the simpler baselines are artificially weak.

Addressing the weakness in the baselines would significantly strengthen the paper and would likely raise my score to a clear accept.

### Supporting Arguments

The primary strength of this paper is the creation of a public resource for the systematic evaluation of goal-oriented dialog systems. By breaking down the problem into five distinct tasks, the authors provide a framework that not only measures overall performance but also helps diagnose specific model failures. The inclusion of an OOV test set is particularly important for assessing a model's ability to generalize to new entities, a crucial requirement for real-world deployment. The validation of these synthetic tasks against real-world datasets (DSTC2 and Concierge) adds credibility, suggesting that improvements on this testbed are likely to translate to practical applications.

However, the paper's central weakness lies in its choice of baselines. The current baselines—TF-IDF, Nearest Neighbor, and supervised bag-of-embeddings—all disregard word order. This is a critical omission, as many dialog phenomena are sequence-dependent. Consequently, the Memory Network, which can implicitly model some sequential information through its attention mechanism, appears stronger than it might be against a more competitive baseline. A simple yet often powerful baseline, such as logistic regression with features including word n-grams (bigrams) and the authors' proposed match-type features, is missing. Without this comparison, the claims about the unique advantages of end-to-end neural models are not fully substantiated.

Furthermore, the training sets are quite small (1,000 dialogs), which is a challenging setting for data-hungry deep learning models. In such low-data regimes, simpler, well-regularized models (like logistic regression) can often outperform more complex neural architectures. Including such a baseline would provide a more realistic assessment of what is truly gained by using a Memory Network.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are intended as constructive feedback to help strengthen the paper and are not the primary basis for my decision.

*   **Deterministic Nature of Tasks:** The simulated tasks (1-5) are deterministic. While this is useful for controlled analysis, real-world dialog is fraught with ambiguity, noise, and user errors that require clarification and repair strategies. The paper would be more complete if it explicitly acknowledged this limitation and perhaps briefly discussed how the framework could be extended in the future to model such stochasticity.
*   **Inaccurate Claim about Prior Work:** The introduction states that "existing measures of performance either prevent reproducibility ... or do not correlate well with human judgements". This is a strong claim that overlooks relevant prior work. For example, the PARADISE framework has long been used for task-oriented evaluation, and recent work by Wen et al. (2015) and others has also proposed metrics for end-to-end task-oriented systems. Similarly, Liu et al. (2016), cited by the authors, extensively reviews and proposes metrics for non-goal-oriented dialog. The authors should soften this claim and more accurately situate their contribution within the existing evaluation literature.

### Questions for the Authors

Your response to the following question will be crucial in my final assessment of the paper.

1.  The most significant concern is the lack of a strong baseline that incorporates word order. Would you be willing and able to add experiments with a model like logistic regression using a combination of TF-IDF weighted bag-of-words, bag-of-bigrams, and your proposed match-type features? Such a baseline is standard in text classification and would provide a much more compelling point of comparison for the Memory Network.
2.  The simulated user is deterministic. Have you considered any extensions to the simulator to introduce more realistic user behaviors, such as providing partial information, changing their mind in less structured ways, or introducing linguistic noise? While likely beyond the scope of this paper, I would be interested to hear your perspective on the challenges involved.
