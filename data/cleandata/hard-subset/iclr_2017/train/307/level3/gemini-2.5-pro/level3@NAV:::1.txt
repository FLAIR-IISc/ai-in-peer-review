### Conference Review

**Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces a valuable new resource for the community: a publicly available, rule-based synthetic dataset and a corresponding set of tasks for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialogue systems. The tasks are well-designed to break down the complexities of a full dialogue into distinct capabilities, such as issuing API calls, displaying options, and conducting a complete conversation. This testbed is a welcome contribution that provides a controlled, reproducible environment to systematically analyze the strengths and weaknesses of different models, which should facilitate future research in this area.

**Decision: Weak Accept**

My recommendation is a weak accept. The primary reason for this is the significant value of the dataset itself, which addresses a clear need in the community. However, this strength is offset by a major weakness in the experimental evaluation: the lack of a sufficiently strong non-neural baseline makes it difficult to properly assess the reported performance of the neural models.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper's main contribution—the dataset and tasks—is well-motivated and thoughtfully constructed. By providing a simulated environment, the authors enable researchers to perform controlled experiments and detailed error analyses that are often difficult with noisy, real-world corpora.

The central weakness, however, lies in the choice of baselines. The provided non-neural baselines (e.g., TF-IDF, supervised bag-of-embeddings) do not incorporate word order. On the deterministic, rule-based tasks presented (Tasks 1–5), it is highly plausible that a relatively simple model like logistic regression, augmented with n-gram features and word embeddings, could perform very competitively. Without this comparison, the reported strength of the Memory Network model may be inflated, as it is unclear if its performance gains stem from complex reasoning or simply from capturing sequential information that the baselines ignore. Furthermore, the deterministic nature of the tasks means they do not evaluate a model's ability to handle ambiguity or noise, which are critical challenges in real-world dialogue.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

To significantly strengthen the paper, I strongly recommend adding a more competitive non-neural baseline. A logistic regression model incorporating word embeddings, bi-gram features, and match-type features would provide a much more rigorous benchmark and help to truly isolate the benefits of the end-to-end Memory Network architecture. Inclusion of such a baseline would likely increase my score.

As a minor point, the claim that "existing work has no well defined measures of performances" is inaccurate. There is prior work on evaluating both task-oriented and non-goal-oriented dialogue systems that should be acknowledged (e.g., Wen et al., Liu et al.).

**Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you provide results for a stronger non-neural baseline that incorporates word order, such as the logistic regression model suggested above, to better contextualize the performance of the Memory Network?
2.  Given that Tasks 1–5 are deterministic, have you considered how these tasks might be extended in the future to evaluate model robustness to phenomena like noisy user input or ambiguous requests?
