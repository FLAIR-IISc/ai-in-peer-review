### Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces a valuable new resource for the community: a set of tasks and datasets designed to benchmark end-to-end, goal-oriented dialog systems. The authors correctly identify a critical gap in current research—while end-to-end systems have shown promise in chit-chat settings, their applicability to goal-oriented tasks is less understood, partly due to a lack of standardized testbeds. To address this, the paper presents a series of five simulated tasks in the restaurant reservation domain, which are generated using a knowledge base (KB) and are designed to test specific capabilities like issuing API calls, handling updates, and using KB information. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of several baselines, including classical IR methods, supervised embeddings, and Memory Networks, demonstrating that while modern neural architectures show promise, they still fall short of solving these tasks reliably, especially on a per-dialog level. A key strength of the work is the inclusion of an out-of-vocabulary (OOV) test set to measure generalization to unseen entities, as well as experiments on real-world datasets (DSTC2 and a proprietary concierge service) to validate that the patterns observed in simulation hold on more naturalistic data.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision: Accept**

1.  **Valuable Community Resource:** The paper's primary contribution is a well-designed, open-source benchmark for a crucial and under-explored area. The lack of reproducible and analytical testbeds for goal-oriented dialog has been a significant bottleneck for progress. This work provides a clear, lightweight, and extensible framework that will enable standardized comparison and systematic analysis of end-to-end models.
2.  **Rigorous Analysis and Insights:** The experimental design is thoughtful. By breaking down a full dialog into sub-tasks (Tasks 1-5), the authors provide a methodology for fine-grained error analysis, allowing researchers to pinpoint where models succeed or fail. The comparison against multiple baselines is thorough, and the finding that Memory Networks outperform simpler models but still struggle with full dialog completion is an important, sobering result for the field.

### Supporting Arguments

The motivation for this work is compelling. Scaling traditional, hand-crafted, slot-filling dialog systems to new domains is a labor-intensive process. End-to-end systems offer a promising alternative, but their capabilities in structured, goal-driven conversations are not well-established. This paper provides the tools to start answering these questions systematically. The simulated tasks are cleverly constructed to isolate different challenges: dialog state tracking (T1), state updates (T2), KB query interpretation (T3), and information provision (T4). This structure is far more insightful than a single, monolithic task.

Furthermore, the inclusion of an OOV test set is a critical component that tests a core promise of end-to-end learning: generalization. The proposed "match type features" are a simple but effective method to help models handle unseen entities, and the experiments clearly demonstrate their benefit. The authors also show commendable diligence by validating their synthetic task findings on two real-world datasets. The fact that the relative performance of the models remains consistent between the synthetic and real data lends credibility to the proposed testbed as a useful proxy for real-world challenges. While the models do not achieve perfect performance, the paper successfully fulfills its primary goal: to provide a testbed that reveals the strengths and weaknesses of current end-to-end approaches.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

The following points are intended as constructive feedback to strengthen the paper and are not central to the decision to accept.

1.  **Ranking vs. Generation:** The choice to frame the task as ranking from a fixed set of candidate responses is a significant simplification. While this aids reproducibility, it sidesteps the much harder and more realistic problem of response generation. This framing limits the "end-to-end" nature of the system, as it presupposes the existence of a comprehensive candidate list. In a new domain, creating this list could be just as labor-intensive as engineering a slot-filling system, which seems to contradict the core motivation of the work. The paper would be stronger if it acknowledged this limitation more directly and perhaps discussed a potential path toward generative evaluation on these tasks.

2.  **Limited Linguistic Diversity:** The simulator uses a relatively small number of patterns to generate user and bot utterances (43 for the user, 20 for the bot). This risks creating a dataset where models can succeed by learning surface-level cues rather than deeper semantic understanding. While the combinatorial combination of entities creates many unique utterances, the underlying linguistic structure is limited.

3.  **Hand-Crafted vs. End-to-End in Narrow Domains:** The paper sets up a dichotomy between scalable end-to-end systems and brittle hand-crafted ones. However, for a narrow, high-value domain like restaurant reservations, a meticulously engineered system may provide a far superior and more reliable user experience than the imperfect end-to-end models shown here. The paper could benefit from a more nuanced discussion on where end-to-end models fit. Are they meant to replace hand-crafted systems in narrow domains, or are they better suited for long-tail, low-resource domains where engineering effort is prohibitive?

### Questions for the Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the rationale for choosing a ranking-based evaluation over a generative one? How do you envision the practical deployment of a system that relies on a pre-compiled list of all possible bot responses, especially when scaling to a new, unseen domain?
2.  The user simulator is a key component of this work. To improve reproducibility and allow the community to better understand the linguistic diversity of the dataset, would you be willing to publish the list of 43 user and 20 bot NL-generation patterns used by the simulator?
3.  Given the relatively low per-dialog accuracy of even the best model (MemNNs) on the full task (T5), what specific architectural or methodological improvements do you believe are most critical for closing this gap? For example, is the primary challenge in memory representation, multi-turn reasoning, or integrating symbolic KB operations?
