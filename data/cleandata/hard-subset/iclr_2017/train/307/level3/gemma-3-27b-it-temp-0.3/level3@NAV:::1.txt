## Review of "A Testbed for Goal-Oriented Dialog"

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of goal-oriented dialogue systems by introducing a new, publicly available, rule-based synthetic dataset and a set of tasks designed to evaluate key capabilities like API calls and option display. The motivation for creating this testbed – the difficulty of scaling traditional systems and the uncertain transfer of success from chit-chat to goal-oriented settings – is well articulated and timely. The breakdown of the goal-oriented task into smaller, more manageable sub-tasks is a clever approach for focused analysis.

I recommend **Accept** with minor revisions. While the paper demonstrates the utility of the dataset and provides initial results, the lack of a strong, non-neural baseline significantly weakens the conclusions drawn about the performance of Memory Networks. The authors correctly identify this as a major limitation.

The paper effectively demonstrates that Memory Networks can learn to perform non-trivial operations within this constrained environment. However, without a competitive baseline that doesn't rely on the strengths of neural networks (e.g., a logistic regression model with carefully engineered features like word embeddings, bi-grams, and match-type features), it’s difficult to assess whether the observed performance is truly indicative of the model’s capabilities or simply a result of its capacity to memorize patterns. The claim regarding the lack of well-defined performance measures in existing work is also inaccurate, as several end-to-end and non-goal dialogue evaluation methods already exist (Wen et al., Liu et al., Yu et al.).

To strengthen the paper, I strongly recommend including the suggested non-neural baseline. This would provide a more nuanced understanding of the strengths and weaknesses of the Memory Network approach. Additionally, correcting the statement about existing evaluation metrics is crucial. Finally, while the tasks are well-defined, they are deterministic and don’t evaluate handling of noisy or ambiguous interactions. It would be beneficial to acknowledge this limitation more explicitly.

**Questions for the authors:**

*   What considerations were given to introducing noise or ambiguity into the tasks to better reflect real-world dialogue scenarios?
*   Could you elaborate on the computational cost associated with training and evaluating the Memory Networks, particularly as the complexity of the tasks increases?
*   How do you envision this testbed being used by other researchers, and what types of extensions or modifications might be particularly valuable?
