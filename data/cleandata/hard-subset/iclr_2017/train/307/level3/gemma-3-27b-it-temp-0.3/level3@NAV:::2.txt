## Review of "Breaking Down the Goal-Oriented Dialog Challenge"

This paper presents a valuable and timely reality check on the current state of end-to-end dialog systems, convincingly arguing that they are not yet sufficient for goal-oriented tasks despite recent successes in chit-chat. The introduction of a new testbed, comprising five tasks within a restaurant reservation domain, is a significant contribution to both the Dialog/NLP and ML communities. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of existing evaluation metrics and highlight the importance of considering per-dialog accuracy rather than solely relying on per-response metrics.

I **accept** this paper. The work is well-motivated, the experimental setup is rigorous, and the findings are insightful. The paper effectively demonstrates the challenges faced by end-to-end models in goal-oriented dialog and provides a useful resource for future research. The thoroughness of the evaluation, including comparisons to a rule-based baseline and experiments on real-world data, strengthens the validity of the conclusions.

The paper’s strength lies in its systematic approach. The authors don’t simply claim end-to-end models are lacking; they *show* where they struggle through a carefully designed set of tasks. The analysis of the impact of the “match type” addition to memory networks is particularly compelling, demonstrating a practical improvement in handling out-of-vocabulary entities. The clear presentation of results, aided by informative tables (like the impact of the number of hops), makes the findings easily accessible. The authors also correctly identify the brittleness of common dialog metrics and advocate for more application-driven loss functions.

However, the paper could be further strengthened by addressing a few points. The vocabulary mismatch issue, while acknowledged, could be explored in more detail. While the “match type” feature is a good first step, it relies on exact KB occurrences and doesn't learn semantic associations. Furthermore, the evaluation on the concierge data raises a question: is “correct” defined as being among the 5-best responses, or strictly the single best? This choice significantly impacts the interpretation of results. 

I would like to ask the authors: 

1.  Could you elaborate on potential avenues for addressing the vocabulary mismatch problem beyond the “match type” feature? Perhaps exploring sub-word units or incorporating external knowledge resources?
2.  Regarding the concierge data, what justification was used for selecting the single best response as “correct”? How sensitive are the results to using a top-N approach?
3.  You mention the need for application-driven loss functions. Could you provide more concrete examples of how such loss functions, tied to real-world revenue or user satisfaction, might be formulated and implemented?
