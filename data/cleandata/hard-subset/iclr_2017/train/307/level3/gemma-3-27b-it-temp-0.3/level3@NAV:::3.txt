## Review of "Breaking Down the Strengths and Shortcomings of End-to-End Dialog Systems in Goal-Oriented Applications"

This paper presents a new, simulated restaurant-domain dataset designed for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. The dataset utilizes a knowledge base and rule-based natural language generation to create dialogs, and the proposed task focuses on ranking candidate next-turn responses. The authors demonstrate that Memory Networks outperform bag-of-words baselines on this task, and validate their findings on existing datasets. Overall, this is an interesting contribution with the potential to facilitate research in this area.

**Decision: Accept (with minor revisions)**

My primary reason for acceptance is the clear need for controlled environments to evaluate end-to-end dialog systems in goal-oriented settings, which this work addresses. The authors identify a gap in existing resources and provide a valuable tool for the community. Secondly, the systematic comparison of different models, including a rule-based baseline, provides useful insights into the strengths and weaknesses of each approach.

However, I initially had significant reservations regarding the task formulation and the reliance on a ranking-based evaluation. Ranking inherently depends on a pre-defined set of candidate responses, limiting the system’s ability to generalize to novel utterances. This raises the practical question of where these candidate lists would originate in a real-world deployment. Furthermore, the rule-based NLG within the simulator, while enabling controlled data generation, potentially restricts the variability of the training data and may not fully reflect the complexities of natural language. 

The authors’ clarification regarding the validation on real-world datasets (DSTC2 and the concierge service) and the observed consistency in performance patterns alleviated some of these concerns. The fact that the relative performance of the models is maintained across these datasets suggests the simulator is a reasonable proxy for real-world scenarios.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Rule List Publication:** To address concerns about the simulator’s limitations, I strongly recommend the authors publish the complete list of NLG rules used for generating user and bot utterances. This would allow for a more thorough understanding of the data generation process and facilitate reproducibility.
*   **KB Structure:** A more detailed description of the KB structure and the API query format would be beneficial.
*   **Error Analysis:** While the paper highlights areas where end-to-end models struggle, a more detailed error analysis, perhaps with specific examples of failure cases, would strengthen the conclusions.
*   **Discussion of Limitations:** A more explicit discussion of the limitations of the simulator and the ranking-based evaluation would be valuable.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the rationale behind choosing a ranking task over a generation task? Were there specific technical challenges that led to this decision?
2.  What considerations were made when designing the NLG rules to ensure sufficient variability in the generated utterances?
3.  How sensitive are the results to the size of the candidate response list used for ranking? Would performance significantly degrade with a smaller list?
