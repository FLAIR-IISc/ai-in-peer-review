Summary of contributions
The paper introduces a publicly available, rule-based synthetic dataset and a suite of tasks for evaluating end-to-end, goal-oriented dialogue systems in a restaurant-booking domain. The tasks decompose the problem into capabilities such as issuing and updating API calls, sorting and displaying options, and retrieving entity attributes, plus a full-dialog task. The resource is complemented with two more realistic evaluations: a converted version of DSTC2 into the same task framework and an operator–user Concierge dataset. The authors provide extensive baselines, including TF–IDF, nearest neighbor, supervised embeddings, and Memory Networks (with and without match-type features), and report per-response and per-dialog accuracies. Key findings are that MemNNs learn non-trivial behaviors (API calls, some reasoning over KB facts) but remain imperfect, especially in end-to-end success on full dialogues; match-type features help with OOV entities and attribute retrieval. The paper is well written, the dataset is welcome and likely to catalyze progress, and the cross-dataset evaluation pattern is informative.

Decision: Accept
Primary reasons: (1) The dataset and task suite are a timely, useful, and well-engineered contribution that will facilitate reproducible, fine-grained evaluation of goal-oriented dialogue models. (2) The empirical study is broad and carefully executed, and the triangulation on synthetic, DSTC2, and Concierge data provides a persuasive initial validation of the testbed’s relevance.

Supporting arguments
- Specific question/problem: Provide a reproducible, controlled testbed to probe strengths/weaknesses of end-to-end models for goal-oriented dialogues, including interaction with KBs and entity handling, and assess whether such models can rival traditional methods in narrow domains.
- Motivation and placement: Strong. The work fills an acknowledged gap: most available corpora are either not designed for end-to-end evaluation, are noisy/proprietary, or lack the decomposition needed for analysis. The connection to bAbI-style tasks is apt, and comparisons to DSTC2/Concierge ground the synthetic tasks in reality.
- Do results support claims: Largely yes. The experiments demonstrate that MemNNs outperform simple IR and supervised embedding baselines on most tasks and that match-type features are crucial for OOV and attribute lookup. The per-dialog metrics appropriately reflect end-to-end success and expose remaining gaps (especially Tasks 3 and 5). The conversion of DSTC2 and the Concierge results show similar relative method rankings, supporting the claim that the synthetic tasks are a useful proxy.

Caveats and weaknesses (do not preclude acceptance)
- Determinism and coverage: Tasks 1–5 are fully deterministic, so they do not evaluate robustness to ASR noise, ambiguity, user goal uncertainty, or repair. This limits conclusions about real deployment readiness or the ability to infer and manage distributions over user states.
- Suitability of deep models vs simpler models: The superiority of neural models on small training sets may partially reflect inductive biases/regularization rather than inherent suitability. The study does not clearly disentangle sample efficiency of deep vs shallow methods.
- Baselines and word order: There is no strong non-neural baseline that leverages word order or simple compositional features (e.g., logistic regression or linear SVM with uni/bi-grams, match types, and pre-trained embeddings). Given the deterministic language patterns, such baselines could be highly competitive and would recalibrate how “strong” the neural gains are.
- Claims about prior evaluation measures: The statement that existing work has “no well defined measures of performance” is too strong. There is relevant evaluation work in both task-oriented and open-domain settings (e.g., Wen et al., Liu et al., Yu et al.), even if imperfect.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Add a non-neural benchmark: Include logistic regression with pre-trained word embeddings, bi-grams, and match-type features; optionally a CRF/SVM slot tagger pipelined with retrieval for response selection. This would directly address whether simple models can solve these tasks with fewer parameters and clarify the contribution of word order.
- Report sample complexity: Learning curves for each model would illuminate data needs, especially as end-to-end systems are often data-hungry.
- Stress-test robustness: Add noisy/ambiguous variants of Tasks 1–5 (ASR errors, paraphrase, conflicting slot mentions, user corrections) and tasks that require clarification or confidence-based strategies.
- Expand evaluation: Consider a generation setting alongside ranking, and analyze calibration of per-response vs per-dialog metrics (e.g., how often a single error derails completion).
- Clarify candidate set: Since candidates are pooled across all tasks and splits, discuss potential leakage of template knowledge and its impact on difficulty.
- Minor correction: Soften the claim about the absence of well-defined metrics and cite prior evaluation efforts (e.g., Wen et al. 2015; Liu et al. 2016; Yu et al.).
- Reproducibility: Release the simulator, language templates, and conversion scripts for DSTC2, with licensing notes for Concierge. Document the 43 user and 20 bot patterns to facilitate extensions.

Questions for the authors
- How do results change with a strong linear baseline that includes bi-grams and match-type features? Could you report these in the final version?
- Can you provide learning curves to compare sample efficiency across models?
- How sensitive are MemNN results to the size of the candidate set and to cross-task pooling of candidates?
- Do match-type features leak KB structure in ways that make OOV generalization easier than in real deployments where gazetteers are incomplete?
- Could you add noisy/ambiguous variants (e.g., simulated ASR errors, paraphrase) to assess robustness and dialogue repair?
- For Task 3, have you tried architectures with explicit pointer/copy and sorting mechanisms (or constraints) to see if performance is limited by the model class?
- In DSTC2 conversion, how often do non-deterministic bot behaviors violate your assumptions (e.g., ordering of slot requests), and how does that affect error modes?
- Will you release the Concierge data or a comparable anonymized subset with clearer licensing?

Reviewer update
After the rebuttal and the addition of new results addressing some baseline and analysis gaps, I updated my score upward. The final recommendation remains accept, primarily on the strength of the dataset contribution and the careful, multi-dataset evaluation.
