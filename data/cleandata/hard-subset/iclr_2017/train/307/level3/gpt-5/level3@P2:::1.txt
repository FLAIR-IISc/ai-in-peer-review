Summary of contributions
- The paper introduces a publicly available, controlled testbed for end-to-end goal-oriented dialogue, centered on restaurant booking. It decomposes the problem into five synthetic but structured tasks (API call issuance and updates, displaying sorted options, providing KB-grounded information, and full dialogues), plus a conversion of DSTC2 to the same format and an additional evaluation on a real concierge dataset.
- It evaluates a range of methods—rule-based, classical IR (TF-IDF, nearest neighbor), supervised embeddings, and end-to-end Memory Networks—and proposes “match type” features to better handle entities and OOV terms. The empirical results clearly highlight capabilities and failure modes: MemNNs learn non-trivial behaviors (API calls, OOV handling with type features), but per-dialog success remains low on the harder tasks (e.g., sorting, KB result interpretation), and performance patterns carry over to DSTC2 and the concierge setting.
- The resource is well-motivated, easy to reproduce, and provides built-in error analysis via subtasks. This is a useful contribution that should help the community compare methods and make progress on goal-oriented dialogue.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
1) The dataset and task suite is a valuable, reusable community resource with clear evaluation protocols, filling a gap in end-to-end, goal-oriented dialogue benchmarking.
2) The empirical analysis is careful and honest, with results (including OOV and real-data evaluations) that substantively support the paper’s claims and illuminate where current end-to-end models succeed and fail.

Supporting arguments
- What question is tackled? The paper asks whether current end-to-end neural approaches—trained solely from dialogue data—can replace traditional slot-filling systems in narrow goal-oriented tasks, and if not, precisely where they fall short. It further asks how to evaluate these abilities in a reproducible, interpretable way.
- Is the approach well motivated and well placed in the literature? Yes. The motivation—scalability beyond hand-crafted slot-filling and the need for reproducible end-to-end evaluation—is clear. The paper connects to POMDP/slot-filling work, recent end-to-end dialogue modeling, and DSTC datasets. Minor caveat: the claim that prior work lacks well-defined measures is too strong given existing end-to-end task-oriented and non-goal dialogue evaluation efforts (e.g., Wen et al., Liu et al., and others cited).
- Do the results support the claims? Largely yes. The controlled tasks allow precise measurement of abilities like API call formation/updates and KB-grounded reasoning (including sorting), and the OOV split tests entity generalization. MemNNs with match-type features convincingly improve on baselines where expected (e.g., T1/T2 and OOV), and fail predictably on harder skills (T3/T5), aligning with per-dialog metrics. The similar performance patterns on DSTC2 and the concierge data strengthen external validity. The per-dialog accuracy gaps transparently demonstrate that the field has work to do.
- Baselines and fairness: The inclusion of rule-based, IR, supervised embeddings, and MemNNs is thoughtful. However, there remains a gap between the non-neural baselines and the neural ones: a strong linear/log-linear model that explicitly exploits word order and entity overlap (e.g., logistic regression or MaxEnt with uni/bi-grams and type/overlap features) would sharpen conclusions. The authors’ added bigram and match-type variants for supervised embeddings help, but a non-neural linear benchmark would further deconfound modeling capacity vs. regularization.

Additional feedback to help improve the paper (not all are decision-critical)
- Baselines:
  - Add a non-neural linear benchmark: e.g., a log-linear model scoring (context, candidate) with features including uni/bi-grams, context–candidate n-gram interactions, entity overlap/type matches, turn index, and simple heuristic KB consistency features. This could narrow the gap to MemNNs and clarify the incremental value of attention/memory vs. stronger lexical baselines.
  - Consider stronger retrieval baselines (BM25+RM3) augmented with type/overlap features.
- Word order and structure: While supervised embeddings with bigrams were explored, also consider comparisons to simple CNN/RNN encoders (still retrieval-based) to quantify the role of sequence modeling in these tasks, especially in handling option listing and updates.
- Determinism and noise: Tasks 1–5 are deterministic (e.g., fixed question order), which simplifies dialogue management. Adding stochastic variants (randomized prompt order, paraphrase variability, ASR-like noise, ambiguous user goals, and repair/clarification turns) would improve ecological validity and stress-test state tracking.
- Evaluation protocol:
  - The ranking formulation is practical but can be sensitive to candidate set design. Provide ablations showing robustness to candidate set size/composition.
  - Consider including generation-based results (even if imperfect) and human-in-the-loop evaluation for a subset to connect ranking accuracy with perceived quality.
- Clarify scope of claims: Soften statements implying the absence of well-defined evaluation in prior work; acknowledge complementary efforts (e.g., Wen et al. NLG/evaluation, Liu et al. on metrics) to better position the contribution.
- Analysis:
  - Deepen the error analysis for T3/T5: is sorting the primary failure, or is it entity grounding across KB returns? Provide per-skill breakdowns (sort accuracy, option stopping behavior, info retrieval accuracy).
  - Include sample-efficiency plots (accuracy vs. training size), given the small training sets and the motivation around scalability.

Questions for the authors
1) Candidate set sensitivity: How does per-response/dialog accuracy change as you vary the size and composition of the candidate pool? Have you tried “in-domain only” candidates vs. the union from all tasks?
2) Sorting behavior: Can you report explicit sorting accuracy for T3 and whether MemNNs learn a true ordering vs. rely on spurious cues (e.g., surface form, position in KB)?
3) OOV mechanics: For match-type features, how robust is performance when the KB is larger, noisier, or partially unavailable at test time? Does performance degrade gracefully?
4) Determinism: Have you tried a variant of T1/T2 with randomized question order and paraphrased system prompts? Do the relative gains persist?
5) Linear baseline: Would you be willing to add a log-linear model with uni/bi-grams and type/overlap features (non-neural) to further calibrate the gap to MemNNs?
6) DSTC2 conversion: Could you release the exact conversion scripts and splits to ensure reproducibility, and comment on how often the original system’s non-deterministic behavior breaks the ranking assumption?
7) Concierge dataset: Since it is proprietary, can you share an anonymized subset or synthetic proxy with similar statistics to facilitate independent validation?

Note on updated assessment
The additional results incorporating bigrams and match-type variants for the supervised embeddings addressed part of the baseline concern and improved my confidence in the empirical conclusions; accordingly, I updated my score to accept.
