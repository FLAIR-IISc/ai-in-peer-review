1. Summary
The paper introduces a clean, reproducible testbed for end-to-end goal-oriented dialog in a restaurant domain. It contributes (i) a simulated dataset grounded in a KB with rule-based natural language generation, decomposed into five subtasks, plus conversions of DSTC2 and a real concierge dataset; (ii) a ranking-based evaluation protocol with per-response and per-dialog accuracy; and (iii) empirical evidence that Memory Networks outperform TF-IDF/nearest-neighbor and supervised bag-of-embeddings, especially on tasks requiring multi-hop reasoning and KB use. The work is well positioned in the literature, explicitly targeting the gap between chit-chat progress and the demands of goal-directed dialog.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) The dataset and task decomposition provide a much-needed, reproducible benchmark to analyze capabilities (KB use, state tracking, OOV handling) with clear diagnostics. (2) The experimental study is careful and informative, showing consistent gains of Memory Networks across synthetic and real tasks and honest reporting of remaining gaps (low per-dialog accuracy).

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem: Can end-to-end models handle the symbolic and procedural aspects of goal-oriented dialog, including API calls and KB grounding, and how do they compare to hand-engineered systems?
- Motivation and placement: Strong. Prior datasets are either small, noisy, or not designed for end-to-end learning; this testbed mirrors bAbI-style decomposition while connecting to DSTC2 and a live concierge corpus.
- Evidence: The claims are supported by rigorous comparisons, OOV splits, and cross-dataset validation. Results correctly highlight strengths (API call issuance/updates) and weaknesses (option display/sorting, full-dialog success). That said, the reliance on ranking limits ecological validity.

I remain skeptical about the ultimate value of end-to-end training in narrow domains where well-engineered slot-filling systems may yield better user satisfaction; nevertheless, as a research probe, the contribution is substantial.

4. Feedback and questions (to improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Please release the full set of rule-based NL generation templates and coverage statistics; current linguistic variability is constrained and likely inflates performance.
- Ranking vs generation: why not include a generative baseline (e.g., seq2seq or pointer-generator) to assess portability beyond a fixed candidate set?
- Practicality: In a new domain, where do candidate response inventories come from if not hand-collected? How sensitive are results to candidate set size/quality?
- Can you provide error analyses for Tasks 3/5 (e.g., failures in sorting vs entity grounding vs discourse control)?
- Consider reporting human evaluation or task-completion/user-satisfaction proxies, and additional metrics (calibration, latency).
- Clarify OOV handling assumptions: models see the KB entries at train time—how does performance change when KB access is restricted or entities are partially unseen?

Addressing these points would strengthen the case and, if clarified, I would keep or even raise my score.
