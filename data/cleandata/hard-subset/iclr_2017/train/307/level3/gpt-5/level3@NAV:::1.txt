Summary of contributions
The paper introduces a useful, publicly available, rule-based synthetic dataset and a suite of goal-oriented dialogue tasks in the restaurant domain that decompose capabilities such as issuing and updating API calls, displaying ranked options, and end-to-end dialogues. The testbed is designed for reproducibility, controlled error analysis, and includes OOV variants, plus results on DSTC2 and a real-world concierge dataset. The authors benchmark IR baselines, supervised embeddings, and Memory Networks (with type-match features), providing a clear picture of where end-to-end models succeed and fail.

Decision: Accept (weak accept)
Key reasons: The dataset and task breakdown are a welcome, impactful contribution for the community, enabling standardized evaluation and analysis of goal-oriented dialogue. Empirical results across synthetic and real settings are comprehensive and informative, despite methodological gaps.

Supporting arguments
- Value and novelty: A clean, modular benchmark for goal-directed dialogue has been missing; this work plausibly becomes a reference testbed. The OOV setup and per-dialog accuracy are especially useful.
- Empirical thoroughness: Multiple model families are compared, with careful discussion of match-type features and OOV generalization; the consistent performance patterns across synthetic and real datasets increase external validity.
- However, the current comparisons blur the line between the advantages of deep architectures vs stronger shallow baselines. There is no non-neural baseline that meaningfully leverages word order and regularization (e.g., logistic regression with bag-of-embeddings, bigrams, and match-type features). This omission may overstate the apparent superiority of neural models, especially since tasks 1–5 are deterministic and largely compositional.
- The tasks do not test robustness to noise, ambiguity, or goal uncertainty (e.g., inferring distributions over user goals, dialogue repair), limiting conclusions for real-world deployment.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Add a competitive non-neural benchmark (logistic regression or linear SVM with unigram+bigram features, word-embedding averages, and match-type features). I would likely raise my score further if this is included and analyzed.
- Clarify DL vs shallow model suitability given the small data regime; discuss whether gains reflect capacity or regularization via type-matching.
- Minor correction: The claim that “existing work has no well defined measures of performance” is too strong; see prior end-to-end task-oriented and non-goal dialogue evaluation work (e.g., Wen et al., Liu et al., Yu et al.).
- Consider adding a noisy/ambiguous track (ASR noise, conflicting constraints, repair turns).

Questions for the authors
- Can you report results for a linear model with bigrams and match-type features? How do they compare on T1–T5 and OOV?
- How sensitive are MemNN results to training set size? Any learning curves to assess sample efficiency?
- Do type-match features alone explain most OOV gains, or do multiple hops add independent benefits?
- Could you release a variant with stochastic user policies and noisy KB returns to probe robustness?
