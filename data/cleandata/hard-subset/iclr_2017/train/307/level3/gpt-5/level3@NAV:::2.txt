Summary of contributions
This paper offers a valuable reality check for the field: end-to-end ML methods that excel in chit-chat remain inadequate for reliably completing goal-oriented dialogs. The authors contribute (i) a clean, reproducible task suite in the restaurant domain that decomposes key capabilities (dialog management, API calls, KB use, sorting, OOV entities), (ii) two important conjectures—E2E ML remains insufficient for goal-directed scenarios, and relative performance on well-matched synthetic tasks predicts performance on natural data—and (iii) the first thorough deployment of Memory Networks for goal-oriented dialog with a simple yet useful “match type” augmentation. The experimental design and paper clarity are excellent; the appendix and tables (e.g., hop ablations) are instructive.

Decision
Accept, primarily for significance and careful experimentation: the testbed and empirical findings will influence both Dialog/NLP and ML communities, despite modest algorithmic novelty.

Supporting arguments
- Specific problem: Can end-to-end models replace traditional systems in transactional dialogs, and what capabilities are lacking? The testbed isolates skills such as issuing/updating API calls, KB interpretation, and handling OOV entities.
- Motivation and placement: The work is well-situated relative to POMDP/slot-filling and recent E2E dialog; it fills a clear gap in resources for goal-oriented evaluation with reproducibility.
- Evidence and rigor: Results robustly support both conjectures. Per-dialog accuracy remains low even when per-response is encouraging, and the relative ranking of methods on synthetic tasks largely transfers to DSTC2 and Concierge. The protocol, ablations (hops), and OOV splits are sound.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Metrics: Common dialog metrics are brittle. Per-response accuracy over-penalizes intermediate turns when only API-call correctness determines task success; per-dialog accuracy is highly protocol-sensitive (cf. counterintuitive T3 vs T5). Please report API-call correctness and success-weighted costs (e.g., weighted sum of API errors, dialog turns, and user rejections).
- Concierge evaluation: Clarify whether “correct” should be a single best response vs top-5; justify your choice and report both top-1 and Recall@k to reduce ambiguity.
- Vocabulary mismatch: The match-type mechanism needs exact KB matches and does not learn semantic associations (synonyms/paraphrases). Consider soft typing, entity linking, subword/character models, schema linking, or dual-encoder retrieval to bridge user-KB lexical gaps.
- Protocol sensitivity: Since the candidate set spans tasks, provide sensitivity analyses for smaller, task-restricted candidate pools and report how definitions drive T3 vs T5 outcomes.
- Assumptions: You grant access to both KBs at training time for OOV tests; discuss realism and whether results change if the OOV KB is hidden until test.

Questions for authors
1) Can you report task success conditioned on API-call correctness and the cost-based metric you advocate? 2) On Concierge, please provide top-1 vs top-5 results and human judgments for a subset. 3) How does match-type perform with fuzzy matching or learned entity linking? 4) Could pointer or copy mechanisms improve T3 sorting/option presentation? 5) How robust are results to alternative candidate sets and to stochastic variants of bot behavior?
