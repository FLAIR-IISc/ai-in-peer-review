1. Summary of contributions
The paper introduces a carefully designed, open testbed for end-to-end, goal-oriented dialog in the restaurant reservation domain, decomposed into five synthetic tasks (issuing/updating API calls, displaying options with sorting, providing KB-derived information, and full dialogs), plus two evaluations on real data (a DSTC2 conversion and an online concierge dataset). It shows how to use these tasks to assess key capabilities such as implicit dialog state tracking, KB querying and reasoning over returned facts, sorting, and handling OOV entities. The authors benchmark several strong baselines (IR, supervised embeddings) and a modified Memory Network (MemN2N) with “match type” features, and analyze hops and OOV behavior. The main findings are that (i) end-to-end systems achieve encouraging per-response accuracy but remain far from reliable per-dialog success, and (ii) the relative ranking of methods on the synthetic tasks mirrors their ranking on real datasets, suggesting the synthetic suite is a useful proxy. The paper is very clear, reproducible, and includes helpful ablations and appendices.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- The benchmark and experiment design provide a much-needed, reproducible reality check for goal-oriented dialog, with clear insights that are valuable to both NLP/dialog and ML communities.
- The evidence that relative method performance transfers from synthetic to real settings is a significant and actionable result for researchers choosing modeling directions.

3. Supporting arguments
- What is the problem tackled? The paper asks whether current end-to-end neural systems can handle goal-directed dialogs (beyond chit-chat), and whether performance on well-matched synthetic tasks predicts performance on real interactions. This is a timely and important question given the limitations of slot-filling/POMDP pipelines and the lack of suitable end-to-end benchmarks.
- Is the approach well motivated and well placed in the literature? Yes. The authors situate their work against slot-filling/POMDP systems, chit-chat datasets, and prior MemNN work. The task design isolates key subskills (state tracking, KB access, reasoning, sorting, OOV handling) and complements them with DSTC2 and a Concierge dataset. The MemN2N baseline with “match type” features is a reasonable, transparent testbed model, and the baselines (TF-IDF, nearest neighbor IR, supervised embeddings) are well chosen.
- Do the results support the claims? Broadly yes. The paper shows:
  - MemN2N outperforms IR and supervised embeddings across tasks, especially on state-tracking style tasks (T1–T2), but struggles on reasoning over KB outputs (T3–T5), with low per-dialog success.
  - Match type features substantially improve OOV handling and make T4 solvable by enabling exact entity matching, confirming the entity-matching hypothesis.
  - Hops matter, indicating iterative reasoning is beneficial.
  - The relative ordering of methods (MemNN > supervised embeddings > IR) persists on DSTC2 and Concierge, supporting the proxy-testbed claim.
  These findings are accompanied by qualitative examples and ablations. At the same time, some evaluation choices reveal brittleness: per-response accuracy may not reflect task success, and per-dialog accuracy is highly sensitive to task design (e.g., differences between T3 and T5 largely reflect task definitions). Still, taken together, the evidence convincingly supports the central claims.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Evaluation metrics and protocol:
  - Per-response accuracy can be misleading in goal-oriented settings; consider metrics centered on task success and efficiency: success rate, number of turns, API-call correctness, user rejections, with a weighted objective aligned to application utility. Even a simple utility function would strengthen the conclusions, and a small user simulator or limited human-in-the-loop study could operationalize this.
  - For Concierge, many turns have multiple acceptable responses. Reporting top-k accuracy (e.g., top-5) and/or human judgments of appropriateness would be fairer than top-1 only; alternatively, measure recall@k or MRR.
  - Provide confidence intervals or multiple-seed variance and significance tests, especially for real datasets.
  - Clarify and justify the candidate set construction across train/val/test and tasks. Using a pooled candidate set (including test items) in ranking can change difficulty and potentially leak distributional information; a discussion would help.
- Task design:
  - The deterministic prompting order in T1 simplifies learning; consider a variant with varied slot request orders to avoid overfitting to a script.
  - For T3 (sorting options), disentangle sorting errors from retrieval/memory errors with targeted probes (e.g., evaluate pure sort given perfect retrieval).
- Modeling suggestions:
  - The match-type mechanism is simple and effective for exact matches, but vocabulary mismatch between user language and KB entries remains unaddressed. Consider entity linking or alias tables, subword/character modeling for names, synonym expansion via embeddings or paraphrase resources, and copy/pointer mechanisms to better bridge user mentions to KB entries.
  - Add stronger neural retrieval baselines (dual-encoder and cross-encoder with attention over context; pointer-generator Seq2Seq with KB attention; key-value memory networks; end-to-end differentiable KB readers).
  - Report calibration or confidence estimates to study abstention/clarification behavior.
- Generalization:
  - Test cross-domain portability (e.g., hotels or movies) to support the broader claim about synthetic-to-real transfer.
  - Examine error breakdowns by entity type and OOV category to sharpen insights.

Questions for the authors
- Concierge evaluation: Did you consider top-k success or human acceptability judgments to account for multiple valid responses? If so, how do conclusions change?
- Candidate set: Are candidate responses drawn from all splits/tasks at evaluation time? Could this inflate difficulty or leak entity forms into the candidate pool for OOV? Please detail safeguards.
- T3 vs T5 discrepancy: Can you quantify how much of the per-dialog gap is attributable to task-definition differences (e.g., acceptance policy, minimum options) versus model capability?
- OOV handling: Do match types rely strictly on exact surface forms in the KB? Have you tried alias dictionaries or character/subword models to handle spelling variants and unseen names?
- Sorting and KB reasoning: What fraction of T3/T5 errors are due to incorrect sorting versus failing to condition on returned facts? An error taxonomy would be helpful.
- Robustness: How sensitive are results to the deterministic request-order in T1 and to noise (ASR-like perturbations) in the synthetic tasks?
- Reproducibility: Could you release scripts that generate alternative task variants (e.g., varied slot orders, different acceptance policies) to encourage robustness testing?
