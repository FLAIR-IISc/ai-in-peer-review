Summary of contributions
The paper introduces a lightweight, publicly available testbed for goal-oriented dialog, centered on restaurant booking. It decomposes the problem into five synthetic, deterministic tasks that probe key capabilities (issuing and updating API calls, ranking options by KB attributes, retrieving KB fields, and conducting full dialogs), plus a converted version of DSTC2 and a realistic “Concierge” dataset. The authors evaluate a spectrum of methods—IR baselines, supervised embeddings, and end-to-end Memory Networks (with and without match-type features)—and report both per-response and per-dialog accuracies, including an OOV setting that stresses entity handling. The resource is valuable, the breakdown of abilities is insightful, and the empirical analysis identifies clear strengths and failure modes of current end-to-end models. The release will likely catalyze reproducible progress in goal-directed dialog.

Decision
Accept (weak). The main reasons are: (1) the testbed and datasets are a useful, well-motivated, and much-needed resource to benchmark end-to-end goal-oriented dialog; (2) the study provides a careful analysis across synthetic and real data that surfaces important limitations (e.g., per-dialog fragility, OOV entities), offering a roadmap for future work.

Supporting arguments
- Problem tackled: The paper asks whether end-to-end neural dialog models can reliably handle goal-oriented tasks (including KB access and symbolic reasoning) and offers a reproducible evaluation framework to assess that question.
- Motivation and placement: The work is well-motivated relative to slot-filling/POMDP systems and complements prior bAbI QA tasks by focusing on dialog control and KB usage. The inclusion of DSTC2 and Concierge helps bridge synthetic and real-world scenarios. One caveat: the claim that existing work lacks well-defined evaluation measures overstates the case; there is relevant work on evaluation for task-oriented and non-goal dialog (e.g., Wen et al., Liu et al., Yu et al.).
- Do the results support claims? Yes, broadly. The experiments show that Memory Networks (especially with match-type features) learn non-trivial behaviors (API calls, OOV entity handling) and outperform simpler baselines, yet still fall short on end-to-end success (low per-dialog accuracy on complex tasks). The OOV split and analysis of option ranking and KB extraction substantiate the identified weaknesses. That said, the comparison set could be stronger (see below), and the deterministic nature of synthetic tasks limits conclusions about robustness to noise and ambiguity.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not necessarily part of the accept decision)
- Strengthen non-neural/shallow baselines:
  - Add a linear classifier (logistic regression or linear SVM) over rich sparse features: bag-of-words + bigrams/trigrams over context and candidate, plus exact-match and match-type features, positional indicators, and simple slot-state features. Such models are competitive on retrieval-style tasks and would better calibrate the gains of end-to-end architectures.
  - Consider a simple learned finite-state policy that tracks slots and uses trained classifiers for action selection and API updates.
- Word order and structured features: The current baselines largely ignore word order; incorporating n-grams for IR and supervised embeddings, or simple composition (e.g., averaging with positional weights), would clarify how much MemNN gains stem from attention/memory vs. lack of order in baselines. I appreciate the added bigram results; please surface them in the main text with analysis.
- Robustness and realism:
  - Augment synthetic tasks with noise: paraphrases, typos, ASR-like errors, user corrections, and non-deterministic bot behavior. Add ambiguous user turns that require clarification to probe dialog repair.
  - Report sample complexity: learning curves contrasting neural vs. shallow baselines to quantify data efficiency and regularization effects.
- Evaluation:
  - In addition to per-response/dialog accuracy, report slot-level accuracy and API-call exact match; for option display, report accuracy of top-k ranking and sorting correctness.
  - Consider hits@k to account for mild surface-form variability in realistic datasets.
  - Clarify the implications of ranking over a candidate pool that includes utterances from test sets (plain and OOV). While this is a common setup for next-utterance classification, a discussion of fairness and practical relevance would help.
- Data and construction clarity:
  - Be explicit about tie-breaking in Task 3 sorting, the acceptance probability mechanism, and whether the deterministic question order in Task 1 could inadvertently encourage brittle policies.
  - Justify giving models access to both KBs during training when evaluating OOV generalization; discuss realism and alternatives (e.g., KB unseen at train but typed at test via schemas).
- Related work: Soften the statement about the absence of evaluation measures; cite and discuss prior evaluation protocols and metrics (e.g., Wen et al., Liu et al., and contemporaneous task-oriented resources).

Questions for the authors
1) Candidate set construction: You pool candidates from train/val/test (including OOV). Does this ever introduce test-only lexicalizations that make the task easier/harder in unintended ways? How would results change if the candidate pool were restricted to train/val only?
2) OOV setup: Models can access the OOV KB at training time to enable match-type features. How realistic is this assumption? Could you report results when the OOV KB is not available until test time?
3) Word order: Beyond bigrams in the appendix, did you try simple position-aware features (e.g., positional bins, dependency heads) for non-neural baselines? If so, how close do they come to MemNN performance?
4) DSTC2 conversion: How sensitive are results to your choices when constructing API calls and ratings (e.g., always selecting the highest-rated option)? Could inconsistencies in the original dialogs bias the evaluation?
5) Error analysis: For Tasks 3 and 5, what are the dominant failure types (sorting, stopping criterion, entity grounding, multi-hop retrieval)? Can you provide targeted ablations isolating each?
6) Generalization: Do results hold in other domains (e.g., hotel/flight booking)? Any preliminary evidence on cross-domain transfer using the same task templates?

I updated my score after the authors added additional results clarifying baseline choices; addressing the above points—especially a stronger shallow baseline and clearer treatment of candidate pools and OOV KB access—would further strengthen the paper.
