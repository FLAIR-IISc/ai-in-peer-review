Summary of contributions
The paper introduces a reproducible, lightweight testbed for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems in a controlled restaurant-reservation setting. It breaks the overall goal into five synthetic tasks (dialog management, updating requests, ranking options from a KB, using KB outputs, and full dialogs), and supplements them with two datasets derived from real interactions (DSTC2 and an online concierge service). The evaluation is cast as next-utterance ranking with per-response and per-dialog accuracy. The authors benchmark rule-based systems, IR baselines, supervised embedding models, and Memory Networks (with and without match-type features), showing that MemNNs outperform strong baselines, learn non-trivial operations like issuing API calls and manipulating OOV entities via type features, yet still struggle on end-to-end dialog completion. The testbed is positioned as an open, interpretable proxy for progress in goal-oriented dialog, with an explicit OOV split to probe entity generalization.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) The testbed fills an important gap for reproducible, fine-grained evaluation of end-to-end goal-oriented dialog, with clear tasks and OOV generalization built in. (2) The empirical study is thorough and well-situated in the literature, with multiple baselines, ablations (match types), and results on both synthetic and real datasets that convincingly support the paper’s claims.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper tackles whether end-to-end models can replace or complement traditional slot-filling/POMDP systems in narrow, goal-oriented domains. Existing chit-chat benchmarks don’t probe the skills needed for goal completion (state tracking, KB querying, using structured outputs). A synthetic-yet-structured suite with OOV entities and per-dialog metrics is well motivated and addresses a clear evaluation need.
- Positioning in literature: The work is framed relative to POMDP/slot-filling systems (Young et al., 2013; Henderson et al., 2014), chit-chat end-to-end models (Vinyals and Le, Sordoni et al., Serban et al.), and QA-style toy tasks (Weston et al.). The gap—a reproducible goal-oriented end-to-end benchmark with KB interaction—is accurately identified and addressed.
- Claims vs. evidence: The methodology and metrics are clear. The candidate-ranking formulation is consistent across tasks. The ablation on match-type features is informative, especially for OOV and KB lookup tasks. Performance patterns transfer from synthetic tasks to DSTC2/Concierge, supporting the claim that the testbed is a useful proxy. The per-dialog metric highlights remaining gaps, making the conclusions appropriately cautious. The inclusion of a (weaker) rule-based baseline on DSTC2 helps ground the MemNN gains in a realistic setting.
- Rigor and reproducibility: The dataset is publicly released; tasks and KB are described in detail; hyperparameters and ablations are provided. The OOV protocol is principled. One caveat is the construction of the candidate set from all splits/tasks, which may be unrealistic in deployment and could inflate ranking results. Still, the setup is consistent and transparent, enabling comparisons.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not all points affect my decision)
- Generation vs ranking: While ranking yields stable metrics and reproducibility, it sidesteps the practical challenge of generating responses in new domains. Please add a small generative baseline (e.g., a standard attention-based seq2seq or a model with copy/pointer) to quantify the gap and demonstrate how the testbed might inform end-to-end generation. At minimum, discuss how candidate lists would be obtained in a new domain without hand-crafting.
- Candidate set realism: Using a candidate pool aggregated from train/val/test and all tasks is unrealistic and may leak test-time knowledge. Please report sensitivity analyses where candidates are restricted to train-only utterances, vary candidate pool sizes, and/or use retrieval-based prefiltering to approximate deployment.
- Publish simulator templates: The simulator relies on a finite set of templates/patterns. To encourage replication and extension, please release the NL generation templates and rules (including deterministic question order) and the seeds used for sampling. Also report performance as the number/variety of user templates increases to quantify robustness to linguistic variability.
- DSTC2 conversion: Because ratings were added and the dataset was repurposed from state-tracking, please provide more detail on the conversion procedure, error sources (e.g., ASR noise), and quality checks. A brief error analysis on DSTC2 (and Concierge) would strengthen the paper.
- Stronger baselines: Consider adding (i) a seq2seq with attention and copy mechanism for ranking via re-scoring, (ii) a key–value memory read over KB facts, and (iii) a nearest-neighbor over dialog states, to better probe where MemNNs’ advantages arise.
- Success metrics: Per-dialog accuracy is informative; adding goal success rate under stochastic user acceptance in Task 3/5 (and possibly simulated user-ablation tests) could further illuminate practical impact.

Questions for the authors
1. Candidate pool: In a new domain, how would you operationalize candidate lists without handcraft? Have you tried retrieval-first pipelines (IR shortlist + neural reranker), and how does varying the candidate size affect results?
2. Leakage: What happens if the candidate set is restricted to utterances seen only in training (i.e., no test/val candidate texts)? Does performance or ranking stability change markedly?
3. Template diversity: How sensitive are results to the number of user templates and to relaxing the deterministic question order? Can you report performance as you progressively increase linguistic variability?
4. OOV handling: Match-type features help. Did you try explicit copy/pointer mechanisms or delexicalization–relexicalization? How do they compare on OOV-heavy tasks?
5. Task 3 failure modes: Is the bottleneck sorting, grounding ratings to options, or dialog control (knowing when to stop)? Please provide an error breakdown and attention visualizations.
6. DSTC2 conversion details: How were ratings assigned, and could this bias the option-selection policy? Do MemNNs learn to rely on injected ratings rather than language cues?
7. Generality: Have you tested the same framework in a non-restaurant domain (e.g., hotels, flights) to demonstrate portability of the simulator and the evaluation protocol?
8. Human evaluation: For Concierge or DSTC2, have you considered a small human assessment of usefulness/appropriateness of selected responses to complement per-response accuracy?

Overall, this is an interesting, useful contribution that should catalyze more rigorous study of end-to-end goal-oriented dialog and is suitable for publication.
