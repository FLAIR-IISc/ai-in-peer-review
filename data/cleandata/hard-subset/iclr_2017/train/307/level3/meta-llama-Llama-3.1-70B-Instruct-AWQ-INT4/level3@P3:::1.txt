This paper introduces a novel, publicly available, rule-based synthetic dataset and tasks for goal-oriented dialogue, covering API calls, option display, and full dialogues. This contribution is both useful and welcome, as it can facilitate future dialogue-system research. The authors propose a testbed to break down the strengths and shortcomings of end-to-end dialog systems in goal-oriented applications, set in the context of restaurant reservation.

The specific question tackled by the paper is how well end-to-end dialog models can perform in goal-oriented settings, and whether they can replace traditional dialog methods in narrow-domain tasks. The approach is well-motivated, as it addresses the limitations of traditional dialog systems, which require domain-specific handcrafting, and the need for a systematic evaluation of end-to-end dialog systems.

The paper supports its claims through a series of experiments on the proposed tasks, using various learning methods, including rule-based systems, classical information retrieval models, supervised embeddings, and end-to-end Memory Networks. The results show that Memory Networks outperform classical IR and supervised embeddings across all tasks, but still lack in some key areas, such as interpreting knowledge about entities to present results to the user.

I decide to accept this paper, with the key reason being that it introduces a valuable resource for the research community, and provides a systematic evaluation of end-to-end dialog systems in goal-oriented applications. The paper is well-written, and the experiments are thorough and well-designed.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. For example, the tasks 1-5 are deterministic, which means they do not evaluate the handling of noisy or ambiguous interactions. Additionally, the paper does not provide a baseline that incorporates word order, which makes neural models appear unnaturally strong. I recommend adding a non-neural benchmark, such as logistic regression with word embeddings, bi-gram, and match-type features, to increase the review score.

Furthermore, the paper claims that existing work has no well-defined measures of performance, which is inaccurate. Prior end-to-end task-oriented and non-goal dialogue evaluation work exists, such as Wen et al., Liu et al., and Yu et al.

To improve the paper, I suggest the authors provide more analysis on the results, particularly on the performance of Memory Networks with and without match type features. Additionally, the authors could provide more details on the implementation of the Memory Networks, and the hyperparameters used in the experiments.

Questions I would like the authors to answer:

* Can you provide more details on the implementation of the Memory Networks, particularly on how the match type features are used?
* How do you plan to address the limitations of the tasks 1-5, such as the deterministic nature of the interactions?
* Can you provide more analysis on the results, particularly on the performance of Memory Networks with and without match type features?
* How do you think the proposed testbed can be extended to more complex goal-oriented applications?

Overall, this paper is a valuable contribution to the research community, and provides a systematic evaluation of end-to-end dialog systems in goal-oriented applications. With some minor revisions, it has the potential to be a strong paper.
