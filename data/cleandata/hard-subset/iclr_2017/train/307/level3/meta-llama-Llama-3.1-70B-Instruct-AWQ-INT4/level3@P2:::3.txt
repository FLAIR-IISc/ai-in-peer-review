This paper proposes a new simulated restaurant-domain dataset for end-to-end goal-oriented dialog, using a knowledge base (KB) and rule-based natural language (NL) generation. The dataset task is to rank candidate next-turn responses, with evaluation by per-response and per-dialog accuracy. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of Memory Networks on this task, showing improvement over bag-of-words baselines.

I decide to accept this paper, with two key reasons for this choice: (1) the paper introduces a valuable new dataset and task for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems, and (2) the authors provide a thorough evaluation of various models on this task, including Memory Networks, which show promising results.

The paper's contribution is significant because it provides a systematic and controlled way to evaluate end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. The dataset is well-designed, with a clear goal-oriented objective, and the evaluation metrics are well-chosen. The authors also provide a thorough analysis of the results, highlighting the strengths and weaknesses of each model.

However, I do have some reservations about the value of end-to-end training in narrow domains where hand-engineered systems may yield better user satisfaction. The paper's focus on ranking rather than generation also limits the generalization of the results to new domains, as it depends on pre-collected candidates. I question the practicality of this approach, as it is unclear where candidate response lists would come from in a new domain if not hand-collected.

Furthermore, the simulator's rule-based NL generation restricts variability in training data, which may not be representative of real-world conversations. I would like the authors to publish the list of NL-generation rules used in the simulator to provide more transparency and allow for better reproducibility.

To improve the paper, I suggest the authors address the following points:

* Provide more discussion on the limitations of end-to-end training in narrow domains and how this approach can be generalized to broader domains.
* Consider evaluating generation-based models in addition to ranking-based models to provide a more comprehensive evaluation.
* Provide more details on the NL-generation rules used in the simulator and make them publicly available.
* Discuss the practicality of the approach and how candidate response lists can be obtained in new domains.

To clarify my understanding of the paper, I would like the authors to answer the following questions:

* Can you provide more details on the NL-generation rules used in the simulator and how they were designed?
* How do you plan to address the limitation of end-to-end training in narrow domains and generalize this approach to broader domains?
* Have you considered evaluating generation-based models in addition to ranking-based models, and if so, what were the results?

Overall, I consider this an interesting contribution worthy of publication, and I look forward to seeing the authors' responses to these questions and concerns.
