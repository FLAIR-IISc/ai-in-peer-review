This paper proposes a testbed to evaluate end-to-end dialog systems in goal-oriented applications, specifically in the context of restaurant reservation. The authors introduce a new publicly available dataset and tasks that cover various aspects of goal-oriented dialog, including API calls, option display, and full dialogues. The dataset is designed to be rule-based and synthetic, allowing for reproducibility and comparisons. The authors also provide results on two additional datasets extracted from real interactions with users, confirming that the pattern of results observed in their tasks is a good proxy for what would be observed on real data.

I decide to accept this paper with the following key reasons:

Firstly, the paper tackles a specific and well-defined problem in the field of end-to-end dialog systems, namely evaluating their performance in goal-oriented applications. The authors provide a clear and well-motivated approach to addressing this problem, including the introduction of a new dataset and tasks.

Secondly, the paper supports its claims with extensive experiments and results, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The authors compare multiple methods on their tasks, including rule-based systems, classical information retrieval methods, supervised embeddings, and end-to-end Memory networks. The results show that Memory Networks outperform classical IR and supervised embeddings across all tasks, but still lack in some key areas.

However, I have some concerns and suggestions for future work:

Firstly, it is unclear how suitable deep learning models are for these tasks versus traditional/rule-based or shallow models. The authors note that the tasks are deterministic, which may favor deep learning models. However, it would be interesting to see how these models perform on more complex and noisy tasks.

Secondly, the paper lacks a baseline that incorporates word order, making neural models appear unnaturally strong. The authors should consider adding a non-neural benchmark, such as logistic regression with word embeddings, bi-gram, and match-type features.

Lastly, the authors claim that existing work has no well-defined measures of performances, which is inaccurate. Prior end-to-end task-oriented and non-goal dialogue evaluation work exists, and the authors should acknowledge and build upon this work.

Additional feedback:

* The paper is well-written and easy to follow, but some sections could be improved. For example, the introduction could be more concise, and the related work section could be more comprehensive.
* The authors should consider providing more details about the dataset and tasks, such as the number of examples, the vocabulary size, and the evaluation metrics used.
* The results section could be improved by providing more visualizations and summaries of the results, making it easier to understand the performance of each method.
* The authors should consider providing more analysis and discussion of the results, such as the strengths and weaknesses of each method, and the implications of the results for future work.

Questions for the authors:

* Can you provide more details about the dataset and tasks, such as the number of examples, the vocabulary size, and the evaluation metrics used?
* How do you plan to address the limitations of the current tasks, such as the lack of noisy or ambiguous interactions?
* Can you provide more analysis and discussion of the results, such as the strengths and weaknesses of each method, and the implications of the results for future work?
* How do you plan to extend the current work to more complex and real-world tasks?
