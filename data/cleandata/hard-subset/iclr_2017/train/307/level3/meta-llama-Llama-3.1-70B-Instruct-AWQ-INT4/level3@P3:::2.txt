**Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions**

This paper proposes a testbed for evaluating end-to-end dialog systems in goal-oriented applications. The authors argue that current chatbots and end-to-end machine learning (ML) models are insufficient for goal-oriented dialogs and aim to provide a thorough evaluation of these models. They introduce a set of five tasks within the context of restaurant reservation, which require manipulating sentences and symbols to conduct conversations, issue API calls, and use the outputs of such calls. The authors also propose a simple "match type" addition to memory networks, which is the first thorough deployment and test on goal-oriented dialog tasks.

**Decision and Key Reasons**

I decide to **accept** this paper. The two key reasons for this choice are:

1. The paper provides a valuable reality check on the current state of end-to-end dialog systems in goal-oriented applications, highlighting the limitations of these models and the need for further research.
2. The authors propose a well-motivated and well-designed testbed for evaluating end-to-end dialog systems, which can facilitate future research and development in this area.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper's main contribution is the proposal of a testbed for evaluating end-to-end dialog systems in goal-oriented applications. The authors argue that current chatbots and end-to-end ML models are insufficient for goal-oriented dialogs, and their testbed is designed to break down the strengths and shortcomings of these models. The testbed consists of five tasks within the context of restaurant reservation, which require manipulating sentences and symbols to conduct conversations, issue API calls, and use the outputs of such calls.

The authors also propose a simple "match type" addition to memory networks, which is the first thorough deployment and test on goal-oriented dialog tasks. The results show that the proposed model can learn to perform non-trivial operations such as issuing API calls and manipulating entities unseen in training.

The paper's experimental protocol and clarity are excellent, with a helpful appendix and informative tables. The authors also provide a thorough evaluation of their model, including an analysis of the impact of the number of hops on performance.

**Additional Feedback**

One potential limitation of the paper is that it does not adequately address the vocabulary mismatch between user language and KB terms. The "match type" feature needs exact KB occurrences and does not learn semantic associations automatically. This limitation could be addressed by exploring more advanced techniques for handling vocabulary mismatches.

Another potential area for improvement is the evaluation metric used in the paper. The authors use per-response accuracy and per-dialog accuracy, which may not be the most informative metrics for evaluating goal-oriented dialog systems. The authors suggest using more meaningful, application-driven loss functions, such as a weighted sum of API errors, dialog turns, and user rejections, which could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the model's performance.

**Questions for the Authors**

1. How do you plan to address the vocabulary mismatch between user language and KB terms in future work?
2. Can you provide more details on the "match type" feature and how it is implemented in the memory network model?
3. How do you think the proposed testbed can be extended to other goal-oriented applications beyond restaurant reservation?
4. Can you provide more insights on the results of the evaluation, particularly on the performance of the model on the OOV test sets?

Overall, this paper provides a valuable contribution to the field of end-to-end dialog systems and goal-oriented applications. The proposed testbed and model can facilitate future research and development in this area, and the authors' suggestions for more meaningful evaluation metrics can provide a more comprehensive understanding of the model's performance.
