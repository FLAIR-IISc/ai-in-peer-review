This paper introduces a new simulated restaurant-domain dataset for end-to-end goal-oriented dialog, using a knowledge base and rule-based natural language generation. The dataset task is to rank candidate next-turn responses, with evaluation by per-response and per-dialog accuracy. The authors show that Memory Networks improve over bag-of-words baselines, and provide results on two additional datasets extracted from real interactions with users.

I decide to accept this paper, with the key reason being that it makes an interesting contribution to the field of goal-oriented dialog systems. The paper proposes a new dataset and task set for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog learning methods, which can help foster progress in the development of conversational agents.

One reason I was initially skeptical about this paper was the value of end-to-end training in narrow domains where hand-engineered systems may yield better user satisfaction. However, the authors provide results on real human-machine dialogs from the restaurant reservation dataset of the 2nd Dialog State Tracking Challenge, which shows that Memory Networks can outperform a dedicated slot-filling rule-based baseline.

I also appreciate the authors' efforts in providing a detailed analysis of the results, including the use of match type features to alleviate the problems of exact matches and out-of-vocabulary words. The paper provides a thorough evaluation of the proposed method, including a comparison with other baselines and an analysis of the results on different tasks.

One area for improvement is the task definition, which is currently focused on ranking candidate next-turn responses. I would like to see the authors explore generation tasks, which can provide more flexibility and generalization to new domains. Additionally, I would like to see more discussion on the practicality of the proposed method, including where the candidate response lists would come from in a new domain.

I have a few questions for the authors:

1. Can you provide more details on the rule-based natural language generation used in the simulation? Specifically, what are the 43 patterns for the user and 20 patterns for the bot, and how were they created?
2. How do you plan to address the issue of out-of-vocabulary words in the proposed method? While the use of match type features can alleviate some of the problems, it may not be sufficient for all cases.
3. Can you provide more details on the evaluation metrics used in the paper? Specifically, how do you define per-response and per-dialog accuracy, and how do you calculate them?

Overall, I think this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of goal-oriented dialog systems, and I look forward to seeing the authors' future work in this area.

Additional feedback:

* The paper could benefit from more discussion on the related work in the field of goal-oriented dialog systems. While the authors provide a brief overview of the existing work, it would be helpful to have a more detailed analysis of the strengths and weaknesses of different approaches.
* The paper could also benefit from more visualizations of the results, such as plots or tables that show the performance of different methods on different tasks.
* The authors may want to consider providing more details on the implementation of the Memory Networks, including the architecture and hyperparameters used in the experiments.
* The paper could benefit from more discussion on the potential applications of the proposed method, including its potential use in real-world dialog systems.
