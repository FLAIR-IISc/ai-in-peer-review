This paper proposes a novel testbed for evaluating end-to-end dialog systems in goal-oriented applications, specifically in the context of restaurant reservations. The authors introduce a synthetic dataset and tasks that cover various aspects of goal-oriented dialog, including API calls, option display, and full dialogues. The paper also presents an evaluation of several learning methods, including rule-based systems, classical information retrieval models, supervised embeddings, and end-to-end Memory Networks.

The paper's main contribution is the introduction of a publicly available dataset and tasks for goal-oriented dialogue, which can facilitate future research in this area. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their proposed testbed by evaluating various learning methods on the tasks and showing that end-to-end Memory Networks can outperform other baselines.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. Firstly, the tasks introduced are deterministic, which means they do not evaluate the ability of models to handle noisy or ambiguous interactions. This is an important aspect of goal-oriented dialogue, as real-world interactions often involve uncertainty and ambiguity. Secondly, the paper lacks a baseline that incorporates word order, which makes neural models appear unnaturally strong. Adding a non-neural benchmark, such as logistic regression with word embeddings, bi-gram and match-type features, would provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the models.

In terms of the specific question/problem tackled by the paper, it is clear that the authors aim to address the limitations of traditional dialog systems in goal-oriented applications. They propose a testbed that can evaluate the strengths and weaknesses of end-to-end dialog systems in a systematic and controlled way. The approach is well-motivated, as it is based on the need for a more effective way to evaluate goal-oriented dialog systems.

Regarding the support for the claims made in the paper, the authors provide a thorough evaluation of various learning methods on the proposed tasks. The results show that end-to-end Memory Networks can outperform other baselines, which supports the claim that this approach is effective for goal-oriented dialogue. However, the lack of a non-neural baseline and the deterministic nature of the tasks limit the generalizability of the results.

To improve the paper, I would recommend adding a non-neural benchmark to provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the models. Additionally, the authors could consider introducing tasks that evaluate the ability of models to handle noisy or ambiguous interactions. This would provide a more complete picture of the strengths and weaknesses of end-to-end dialog systems in goal-oriented applications.
