This paper proposes a novel simulated restaurant-domain dataset for end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems, leveraging a knowledge base (KB) and rule-based natural language (NL) generation. The dataset is designed to evaluate the performance of end-to-end models in a systematic and controlled way, breaking down the strengths and shortcomings of these models in goal-oriented applications. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach by comparing the performance of Memory Networks, a type of end-to-end model, to that of classical information retrieval models and supervised embedding models.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of natural language processing (NLP) by providing a well-structured and reproducible evaluation framework for end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. The proposed dataset and tasks are well-suited for analyzing the capabilities and limitations of these models, and the results provide valuable insights into their performance.

One of the strengths of the paper is its clarity and organization. The authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the problem, followed by a detailed description of the proposed dataset and tasks. The experimental setup and results are also well-presented, making it easy to follow and understand the paper.

However, I have some concerns regarding the practicality and generalizability of the proposed approach. Specifically, I am skeptical about the value of end-to-end training in narrow domains where hand-engineered systems may yield better user satisfaction. Additionally, I am disappointed that the task is ranking rather than generation, as ranking depends on pre-collected candidates and limits generalization to new domains. I also question the practicality of obtaining candidate response lists in a new domain, as this may require significant manual effort.

Furthermore, I believe that the simulator's rule-based NL generation restricts the variability in training data. I would like to request the list of NL-generation rules used in the simulator to be published, as this would provide more transparency and allow for better reproducibility.

Despite these concerns, I consider this paper an interesting contribution worthy of publication. The authors have made a significant effort to provide a well-structured evaluation framework for end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems, and their results provide valuable insights into the performance of these models.

Decision: Accept
