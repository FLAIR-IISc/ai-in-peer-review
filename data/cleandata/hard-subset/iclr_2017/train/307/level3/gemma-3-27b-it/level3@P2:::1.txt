This paper introduces a new dataset and set of tasks designed to evaluate end-to-end goal-oriented dialogue systems, specifically in the context of restaurant reservation. The authors motivate this work by highlighting the limitations of traditional, hand-crafted dialogue systems and the uncertainty surrounding the performance of end-to-end systems in goal-directed settings, where success in chit-chat does not necessarily translate. The provided tasks test key capabilities like API calling, knowledge base querying, and managing dialogue state. The paper also presents experiments with Memory Networks, comparing their performance to a rule-based baseline and evaluating generalization to out-of-vocabulary entities. This is a valuable contribution with the potential to facilitate research in this challenging area.

**Reject** with a strong encouragement to resubmit after addressing the key concerns detailed below. While the paper identifies an important problem and proposes a novel evaluation framework, the lack of strong, competitive baselines significantly weakens the conclusions drawn from the experimental results.

The paper successfully articulates the need for a dedicated testbed for evaluating end-to-end dialogue systems in goal-oriented scenarios. The tasks are well-defined and cover critical components of a restaurant reservation system, such as issuing API calls and interpreting results. The creation of both in-vocabulary and out-of-vocabulary test sets is a thoughtful approach to assess generalization capabilities. The experiments with Memory Networks provide a starting point for evaluating end-to-end models, and the inclusion of results on existing datasets (DSTC2 and a concierge service dataset) helps to validate the findings. The analysis revealing the weakness of end-to-end systems in achieving full dialogue success (low per-dialog accuracy) is insightful.

However, the primary weakness lies in the baseline comparisons. While a rule-based system is included for DSTC2, the presented comparison lacks a sufficiently competitive, non-neural baseline for the core synthetic tasks (T1-T5). The comparison to TF-IDF and supervised embeddings is underwhelming, and the underperformance of these methods feels primarily attributable to the lack of incorporation of word order or n-gram features, rather than an inherent limitation of non-neural approaches. Without a more robust baseline, it’s difficult to ascertain whether the observed performance of the Memory Networks is truly indicative of their strength or simply a result of the limitations of the comparison methods. The statement that existing work has no well-defined measures of performance is also inaccurate, overlooking relevant research by Wen et al., Liu et al., and Yu et al.

Furthermore, the deterministic nature of tasks 1-5 is a limitation. While this simplifies evaluation and allows for the creation of a controlled environment, it doesn’t capture the complexities of real-world interactions, which often involve ambiguity, noise, and the need for dialogue repair. The lack of evaluation of handling noisy or ambiguous interactions hinders the assessment of how well these models generalize to more realistic scenarios. 

**Additional Feedback (for improvement, not part of the decision):**

*   **Stronger Baselines:** Implementing a baseline that incorporates word order, such as logistic regression with bigram features and match-type features, is crucial. This would provide a more fair and informative comparison to the Memory Network model.
*   **Task Variation:** Consider adding tasks that introduce more realistic challenges, such as handling ambiguous user requests or correcting misinterpretations.
*   **Error Analysis:** A more detailed error analysis of the Memory Network’s failures would be beneficial. Identifying the specific types of errors the model makes could provide valuable insights for future research.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Discuss the sensitivity of the Memory Network's performance to hyperparameter tuning.
*   **Clarify Match Type Feature Impact:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the impact of match-type features, particularly on out-of-vocabulary performance. Does this largely address the OOV problem, or are there still limitations?
*   **Addressing the Claim about Evaluation Metrics:** Reword the claim about a lack of established evaluation metrics to acknowledge existing work in the field.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Why were more sophisticated non-neural baselines (e.g., logistic regression with n-grams) not included in the evaluation? Do you anticipate that incorporating such baselines would significantly alter the conclusions of the paper?
2.  Could you elaborate on the limitations of the deterministic nature of tasks 1-5? Do you have plans to incorporate more realistic, noisy interactions in future iterations of the dataset?
3.  What specific types of errors is the Memory Network struggling with? A detailed error analysis would be valuable for understanding the model's weaknesses.
4.  How sensitive is the Memory Network's performance to the choice of hyperparameters?
5.  Regarding the match-type features, how much of the improvement in OOV performance is attributable to these features, and are there specific types of OOV entities where the features are most effective?
