This paper introduces a new dataset and set of tasks for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems within the restaurant reservation domain. The authors motivate this work by highlighting the limitations of traditional, hand-crafted dialog systems and the potential, yet unproven, benefits of end-to-end approaches in goal-directed settings. They present a simulated environment with an underlying knowledge base, allowing for controlled experimentation and evaluation using per-response and per-dialog accuracy metrics. The core contribution is a testbed designed to break down the strengths and weaknesses of end-to-end systems, demonstrated with Memory Networks and comparisons to a rule-based baseline and other simpler methods.

**Decision: Accept**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of dialog systems, particularly regarding evaluation methodologies. While end-to-end dialog systems haven’t surpassed traditional methods in narrow domains, this work offers a clear and reproducible framework for studying their capabilities and limitations, which is a crucial step toward progress. The dataset and tasks are well-defined, and the experimental results, while not state-of-the-art in absolute performance, provide insightful comparisons between different approaches.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The motivation for this work is strong. Scaling traditional slot-filling approaches to new domains is a significant challenge, and understanding the potential and pitfalls of end-to-end models is vital. The authors appropriately acknowledge this and frame their work as an investigation, rather than a claim of achieving superior performance. The simulated environment is a clever way to address the data scarcity issues that plague research in this area. It allows for controlled experimentation and reproducibility, which are often lacking in dialog system studies that rely on real-user interactions. The comparison to a rule-based baseline, while expected to be outperformed, serves as an important benchmark. The results demonstrate that Memory Networks can learn to manipulate symbolic information and perform some of the necessary operations for goal-oriented dialog, even if they still struggle with completing full dialogs. The extension of the evaluation to existing datasets (DSTC2 and the online concierge service) strengthens the findings and suggests the synthetic data is a useful proxy for real-world scenarios. The inclusion of out-of-vocabulary tests adds another dimension to the evaluation, highlighting the limitations of current approaches in generalizing to unseen entities.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong overall, several points could be addressed to further enhance its impact. The choice of a ranking task, rather than generation, is a limitation. Although the authors acknowledge this, it's worth emphasizing the implications for real-world deployment. In a new domain, generating a candidate list of responses would be a significant challenge. Exploring methods for generating diverse and plausible responses, even if initially suboptimal, could be a fruitful avenue for future research. The use of a rule-based natural language generator in the simulator introduces a potential bias. The variability of natural language is a key characteristic of human-computer interaction, and a more stochastic generation process might reveal further weaknesses in the end-to-end models. Publishing the complete set of natural language generation rules used in the simulator would increase transparency and allow for more thorough analysis. Finally, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of error analysis. Identifying the specific types of errors made by the Memory Networks (e.g., incorrect API calls, misinterpretation of KB results) would provide valuable insights for future model development.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Regarding the choice of a ranking task, do you foresee any strategies for adapting this framework to a generation setting, even if it requires significant modifications to the evaluation metrics?
2.  Could you provide the full list of natural language generation rules used in the simulator? This would allow for a better understanding of the variability (or lack thereof) in the training data.
3.  What specific types of errors were most frequently observed in the Memory Networks’ performance, particularly in tasks 3 and 5? A more detailed error analysis would be helpful.
4.  You mention the importance of match type features for handling OOV entities. Have you explored other methods for addressing the OOV problem, such as subword embeddings or character-level models?
5.  The paper evaluates performance on several datasets. Do you have any insights into the transferability of models trained on the simulated data to the real-world datasets, and vice versa? What factors seem to be most important for successful transfer learning?
