This paper introduces a new, publicly available, rule-based synthetic dataset and associated tasks designed to evaluate goal-oriented dialogue systems, specifically focusing on API calls, option display, and full dialogue management within a restaurant reservation scenario. The authors motivate this work by highlighting the difficulty of scaling traditional, handcrafted dialogue systems and the uncertainty of whether recent advances in end-to-end neural dialogue models, successful in chit-chat, translate effectively to goal-oriented contexts. The paper provides a valuable resource for the research community and offers a comparative analysis using Memory Networks alongside several baselines.

**Accept** with reservations. The paper presents a significant and useful contribution to the field of dialogue systems research by providing a much-needed benchmark for evaluating goal-oriented dialogue capabilities. However, the lack of a strong, non-neural baseline significantly impacts the interpretation of the results, and some limitations in task design limit the assessment of more nuanced dialogue skills.

The primary strength of this paper lies in its creation of a controlled and reproducible testbed. The synthetic nature of the data allows for precise control over complexity and the isolation of specific dialogue skills. The breakdown of a goal-oriented task into smaller, manageable subtasks (Tasks 1-5) is a smart design choice facilitating error analysis and pinpointing weaknesses of different models. Furthermore, the inclusion of real-world data from the DSTC2 challenge and an online concierge service lends credibility and helps demonstrate the generalizability of the findings. The evaluation metrics – per-response and per-dialog accuracy – are appropriate for assessing performance on these tasks. The Memory Network implementation and comparison to other methods like TF-IDF and supervised embeddings provides a reasonable starting point for analysis. The observation that performance patterns are consistent across the synthetic and real datasets is encouraging.

However, a critical weakness is the absence of a robust non-neural baseline. While TF-IDF and supervised embeddings are included, they are relatively simple and do not adequately capture the potential of traditional or shallow models with well-engineered features. Specifically, the paper doesn’t include a logistic regression or similar model incorporating word order information (e.g., bigrams, position features), which could be surprisingly competitive. This omission artificially inflates the apparent strength of the neural Memory Network, as it’s difficult to determine if the observed improvements are due to the model’s inherent capabilities or simply its ability to leverage information that simpler baselines cannot. The authors acknowledge this limitation themselves.

Another area for consideration is the deterministic nature of Tasks 1-5. While useful for initial evaluation, these tasks do not assess the ability of models to handle noisy or ambiguous user interactions, infer user goals from incomplete information, or perform dialogue repair. Real-world dialogue is inherently uncertain, and a comprehensive evaluation should include tasks that explicitly challenge these capabilities.

Finally, the authors' claim that “existing work has no well-defined measures of performances” is inaccurate. Numerous prior works on end-to-end task-oriented dialogue (Wen et al., Liu et al., Yu et al.) *do* propose and utilize specific evaluation metrics. While these metrics may have limitations, dismissing the existing body of evaluation work is a disservice to the field.

**Additional Feedback & Questions:**

*   **Stronger Baseline:** Adding a logistic regression baseline with features incorporating word order (bigrams, trigrams) and potentially matching keywords would significantly strengthen the paper. This would provide a fairer comparison and a more realistic assessment of the benefits of using neural networks.
*   **Task Variation:** Consider adding tasks that introduce uncertainty or ambiguity. For example, tasks where the user’s request is underspecified or where multiple restaurants meet the criteria.
*   **Error Analysis:** A more detailed error analysis, especially focusing on the failure cases of the Memory Networks, would be beneficial. What types of errors are most frequent? What specific aspects of the tasks prove most challenging?
*   **KB Integration:** How sensitive are the results to the size and complexity of the knowledge base? Would performance degrade significantly with a larger and more diverse restaurant database?
*   **Match Type Features**: Was any ablation study conducted to determine the individual contribution of each of the seven match-type features?
