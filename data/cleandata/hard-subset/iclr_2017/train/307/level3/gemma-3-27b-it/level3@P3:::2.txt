This paper presents a valuable reality check for the field, demonstrating the significant gap between current end-to-end learning approaches and the requirements for robust, goal-oriented dialog systems. The work is particularly important given the hype surrounding end-to-end models in conversational AI and provides a much-needed, rigorous evaluation framework. The authors correctly identify that success in chit-chat does not readily translate to goal-oriented tasks.

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper offers a compelling analysis, a well-designed experimental setup, and raises crucial questions about evaluation metrics in dialog systems. The core contribution – a systematic evaluation of Memory Networks with a simple “match type” addition – is a solid step forward, even if the absolute performance remains limited.

The paper’s strengths lie in its thoroughness. The defined tasks, building upon a restaurant reservation scenario, provide a controlled environment for dissecting the challenges of goal-oriented dialog. The experimental protocol is excellent, with clear explanations and helpful supporting materials (e.g., the impact of hop number in the Appendix). The inclusion of results on both synthetic and real-world datasets (DSTC2 and a concierge service) significantly strengthens the conclusions. Furthermore, the authors’ insightful critique of common dialog metrics (per-response vs. per-dialog accuracy) and the sensitivity of per-dialog accuracy to task definition are particularly valuable contributions. The identification of vocabulary mismatch as a limitation is also crucial.

However, the paper could be improved with a more in-depth discussion of the vocabulary mismatch issue. While the “match type” feature is a good first step, it’s a relatively superficial fix. The system doesn’t *learn* semantic associations; it relies on exact KB occurrences. This limits its ability to handle paraphrases or variations in user language. Further exploration of techniques to address this, such as incorporating external knowledge bases or using more sophisticated embedding methods, would be beneficial. Additionally, while the paper acknowledges the limitations of current loss functions, it doesn’t delve into the practical difficulties of implementing more application-driven loss functions (e.g., weighted API errors, user rejections). A more detailed discussion of these challenges would strengthen the argument for a shift in evaluation paradigms.

**Additional Feedback & Questions for the Authors:**

*   **Vocabulary Mismatch:** Could you elaborate on why you chose an exact match approach for the “match type” feature? Were other methods (e.g., fuzzy matching, semantic similarity) considered and rejected? If so, what were the reasons?
*   **KB Integration:** Beyond the “match type” feature, have you explored more sophisticated methods of integrating the knowledge base into the Memory Network architecture? For example, could you represent KB entities as embeddings and use attention mechanisms to focus on relevant facts?
*   **Loss Function Implementation:** You rightly point out the need for application-driven loss functions. What are the biggest practical hurdles to implementing these? For instance, how would you accurately quantify the “cost” of a user rejection or the impact of an incorrect API call on revenue?
*   **Concierge Dataset:** The paper raises a valid point about whether "correct" in the concierge data should be a single best response or among the 5-best. Could you share the distribution of correct answers within the 5-best candidates? Does this distribution vary significantly across different dialog turns or user requests?
*   **Task 5 & Realism:** Task 5 aims to combine all previous tasks into a full dialog. While useful, does this combined task potentially introduce an unrealistic level of complexity? Does the performance drop in Task 5 disproportionately compared to the individual tasks?
*  **OOV performance:** The improvement in OOV performance with "match type" is mentioned. Can you provide examples of the types of OOV entities the system correctly handled after the addition of this feature?
