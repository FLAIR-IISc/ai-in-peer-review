This paper introduces a new dataset and task set for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems, grounded in a restaurant reservation domain. The core contribution is a simulated environment and a ranking-based evaluation protocol designed to isolate and assess the capabilities of these systems, specifically their ability to handle KB interaction and dialog management. The authors demonstrate that Memory Networks outperform bag-of-words baselines on these tasks and confirm these results on existing datasets (DSTC2 and a proprietary concierge dataset).

**Accept** – despite some limitations in the task design and evaluation metric, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field. The creation of a controlled environment for studying end-to-end dialog systems is a significant step towards understanding their strengths and weaknesses, and the results provide useful insights.

The paper effectively addresses the need for better evaluation methods for end-to-end dialog systems, especially in goal-oriented settings where simply predicting the next utterance (as in chit-chat domains) is insufficient. The proposed tasks, while simplified, force the models to demonstrate key capabilities like API querying and entity manipulation. The simulation framework allows for reproducible experiments and controlled comparisons, a major advantage over relying solely on real-world data which is often noisy and difficult to analyze. The comparison to a rule-based baseline and the validation on real-world datasets further strengthen the findings. The observation that Memory Networks show improvement over simpler methods, but still fall short of perfect performance, is a realistic and important finding.

However, the choice of a ranking task, rather than generation, is a significant limitation. While the authors acknowledge this, it fundamentally restricts the potential for generalization. The reliance on pre-collected candidate responses limits the system’s ability to produce novel utterances and truly understand the nuances of language.  Furthermore, the practicality of this approach in a real-world setting is questionable. Where would the candidate response lists come from if not hand-collected in a new domain? This raises concerns about the scalability of the proposed evaluation framework.

The rule-based NL generation within the simulator is also a potential bottleneck. The authors state that there are 43 patterns for user utterances and 20 for the bot. This constrained variability in the training data might limit the system’s ability to generalize to more diverse and natural language. Publishing the list of these NL generation rules would be crucial for understanding the scope of this limitation and allowing other researchers to build upon this work.

Finally, while the observation that per-dialog accuracy remains low is acknowledged, further analysis of *where* the system fails in completing the dialog would be valuable. What specific subtasks consistently cause errors? This level of granularity would provide more actionable insights for future research.

To clarify my understanding and potentially strengthen my assessment, I have the following questions for the authors:

1. **Candidate Response Generation:**  Given the limitations of relying on pre-collected candidates, have you explored any methods for automatically generating candidate responses (even if they are noisy) to mitigate this issue?
2. **NL Generation Rules:** Can you provide access to the list of NL generation rules used in the simulator? Understanding these rules is vital to assessing the realism and potential biases of the training data.
3. **Error Analysis:**  Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the types of errors made by the Memory Network, specifically identifying which subtasks (e.g., API querying, entity resolution, option presentation) contribute most to the low per-dialog accuracy?
4. **KB Interaction Complexity:** How does the performance of the Memory Network scale with the size and complexity of the KB? Would a larger, more realistic KB significantly impact the results?
5. **Beyond Ranking:** Have you considered exploring a hybrid approach that combines ranking with a limited generation component, perhaps by fine-tuning a pre-trained language model to re-rank or slightly modify the candidate responses?
