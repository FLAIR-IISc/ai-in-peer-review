## Review for ICLR 2017 Submission

This paper introduces a new testbed for evaluating end-to-end dialog systems in goal-oriented tasks, specifically restaurant reservation. The authors acknowledge the limitations of scaling traditional, handcrafted dialog systems and propose a set of tasks designed to break down the capabilities required for successful end-to-end learning in this domain.  The testbed includes tasks involving API calls, knowledge base querying, and multi-turn dialog management.  The paper presents results using Memory Networks, comparing them to a rule-based baseline and demonstrating performance on both synthetic and real-world datasets. This is a valuable contribution with the potential to facilitate research in end-to-end dialog systems.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper addresses an important open problem—evaluating end-to-end dialog systems—and provides a well-motivated and constructed testbed that should prove useful to the community. The empirical results, while not state-of-the-art, provide a good starting point and demonstrate the potential and limitations of current end-to-end approaches.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The motivation for this work is strong.  Scaling traditional slot-filling approaches is a significant challenge, and understanding the capabilities of end-to-end models is crucial for progress. The authors correctly identify that success in chit-chat does not necessarily translate to goal-oriented scenarios, which require more complex reasoning and interaction with external knowledge bases.  The proposed testbed is a logical and well-considered approach to tackling this problem.  Breaking down the goal-oriented objective into smaller, manageable tasks allows for a more granular analysis of model performance, facilitating error analysis and targeted improvement. 

The experimental setup is also well-defined.  The use of both synthetic and real-world datasets (DSTC2 and a proprietary concierge service) strengthens the validity of the findings.  Comparing the Memory Network to a hand-crafted rule-based baseline provides a crucial point of reference. The inclusion of out-of-vocabulary tests is particularly valuable, forcing the model to generalize beyond the training data. The observation that the patterns of results observed in the synthetic tasks translate to the real-world data further validates the testbed’s utility as a proxy for real-world performance.

Finally, the authors are careful to frame their work as an exploration of capabilities rather than a push for state-of-the-art performance.  This focus on understanding the strengths and weaknesses of end-to-end systems is commendable.  The reported results, even with their limitations, provide valuable insights into the current state of the field.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

While the paper is strong, there are a few areas where it could be improved.  The lack of a strong non-neural baseline is a notable omission.  While a rule-based system is presented, it's limited in scope.  The performance of simpler models like logistic regression with word embeddings, incorporating features like bi-grams and match-type features, would provide a more comprehensive comparison and a better understanding of whether the gains achieved by Memory Networks are truly substantial or simply a result of increased model complexity and regularization. The authors acknowledge this in their response.

The tasks themselves, while well-defined, are relatively deterministic. This limits the evaluation of models' ability to handle the ambiguity and noise inherent in real-world conversations.  Exploring variations in task formulation, such as introducing probabilities for user requests or allowing for dialog repair mechanisms, could lead to a more robust assessment of model performance.

The claim regarding the lack of “well-defined measures of performance” in existing work is slightly overstated.  While the field certainly benefits from more standardized evaluation metrics, prior work (e.g., Wen et al., Liu et al.) has established evaluation protocols for both task-oriented and non-goal dialogue systems. Acknowledging this existing work would strengthen the paper’s positioning within the broader research landscape.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  You mention that the tasks were designed to be deterministic. Could you elaborate on the reasoning behind this design choice? Do you plan to explore more stochastic task formulations in future work?
2.  The Memory Network performance on Tasks 3 and 5 remains limited. What specific aspects of these tasks appear to be the most challenging for the model?  Are there particular error patterns you’ve observed during analysis?
3.  The match-type features improve performance on out-of-vocabulary tasks.  Do you have any insights into *why* these features are particularly effective in this scenario? Are there limitations to their applicability?
4. The addition of bigrams to the TF-IDF baseline was found to have no impact. Does this suggest that word order is less important in these tasks than previously thought, or that the TF-IDF approach is simply not sensitive enough to capture the nuances of word order?
