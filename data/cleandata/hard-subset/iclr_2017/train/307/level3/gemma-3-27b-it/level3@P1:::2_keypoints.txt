- Paper evaluates end-to-end dialog systems on goal-oriented restaurant reservation, highlighting that chit-chat success doesn't imply task success.
- Introduces a well-designed testbed with multiple tasks/datasets to systematically evaluate goal-oriented dialog systems.
- Compares end-to-end Memory Network models to a traditional slot-filling (rule-based) baseline.
- Decision: Reject — improvements shown are not substantial enough for ICLR.
- Main claims: (1) end-to-end ML is inadequate for goal-oriented dialogs, (2) synthetic data performance predicts real data performance; experiments largely support both.
- Memory Network models consistently underperform compared to traditional methods, even with added ‘match type’ features.
- ‘Match type’ features improve handling of OOV entities but do not close the performance gap.
- Experimental protocol is strong: staged Tasks 1–5 enable detailed error analysis and pinpoint failures in KB interpretation and response generation.
- Use of both synthetic and real-world datasets (DSTC2, concierge) strengthens generalizability claims.
- Paper is clear and well-documented with helpful appendices and result tables.
- Improvements are incremental; per-dialog accuracy remains low and systems struggle with conversational flow and task completion.
- Current evaluation metrics (per-response, per-dialog accuracy) are brittle and may be misleading.
- Suggested improvements: explore application-driven loss functions tied to practical objectives (API errors, dialog turns, revenue).
- Suggested methods: address vocabulary mismatch via semantic learning, pre-trained embeddings, or entity linking.
- Experimental suggestions: evaluate concierge data with top-N correctness, perform finer-grained ablation studies of ‘match type’ features, and deeper error analysis.
- Open questions: top-N evaluation impact, alternative metrics to address metric brittleness, simulation limitations affecting generalizability, and scalability/costs of ‘match type’ features with large KBs.
