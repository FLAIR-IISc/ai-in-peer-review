## Review for ICLR 2017 Submission

This paper presents a valuable and timely investigation into the capabilities of end-to-end dialog systems within a goal-oriented setting, specifically restaurant reservation. The authors rightly point out that success in chit-chat doesn't automatically translate to more complex, task-driven interactions. They introduce a well-designed testbed, comprising a series of tasks and datasets, to systematically evaluate these systems and compare them to a traditional slot-filling baseline. The work is significant for both the Dialog/NLP and broader Machine Learning communities, providing a much-needed reality check on the current state of end-to-end dialog systems.

**Decision: Reject**

While the paper is well-written and provides a solid experimental analysis, the improvements demonstrated, even with the proposed ‘match type’ feature, are not substantial enough to warrant acceptance at ICLR. The gap between end-to-end systems and traditional methods remains significant, and the paper, while acknowledging this, doesn't present a breakthrough that significantly moves the field forward. The value lies primarily in the creation of a useful benchmark and a clear articulation of the challenges, rather than in a novel algorithmic advance.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The authors establish two key conjectures: (1) end-to-end ML performance is still inadequate for goal-oriented dialogs, and (2) relative performance on well-matched synthetic data predicts performance on natural data. The experiments largely support these conjectures. The Memory Network model, even with the added ‘match type’ features designed to handle out-of-vocabulary entities, consistently underperforms compared to the potential of traditional slot-filling systems (as evidenced by the rule-based baseline on DSTC2).  While the “match type” addition shows a notable improvement, particularly in handling OOV entities, it doesn't close the performance gap sufficiently. 

The experimental protocol is exceptionally well-designed. The gradual breakdown of the goal-oriented task into smaller, manageable components (Tasks 1-5) allows for a detailed error analysis and reveals the specific areas where the end-to-end system struggles – particularly in interpreting KB results and presenting them to the user.  The inclusion of both synthetic and real-world datasets (DSTC2 and the concierge data) adds credibility and strengthens the claim that the synthetic data effectively proxies for real-world performance. The paper's clarity is also commendable, with a helpful appendix and informative tables detailing the experimental setup and results.

However, the improvements achieved are incremental. The per-dialog accuracy remains stubbornly low across most tasks, indicating that the systems struggle with the overall conversational flow and achieving the ultimate goal. The paper highlights this limitation, but doesn't offer a clear path towards addressing it.  The authors correctly point out the limitations of common dialog metrics; per-response accuracy can be misleading, and per-dialog accuracy is highly sensitive to task definitions and experimental setups.  

**Additional Feedback:**

This is a strong paper with a clear presentation. To improve it, consider the following:

*   **Loss Function Exploration:** The current evaluation focuses heavily on response accuracy. Exploring more application-driven loss functions, such as a weighted sum of API errors, dialog turns, and user rejections, could provide a more nuanced and meaningful evaluation.  This would be particularly valuable if tied to a simulated or actual revenue model.
*   **Vocabulary Mismatch:** The paper acknowledges the limitations of the ‘match type’ feature in handling vocabulary mismatch. Investigating methods for learning semantic associations between user language and KB terms would be a valuable extension. Perhaps incorporating pre-trained word embeddings or exploring techniques for entity linking could improve performance.
*   **Evaluation on Concierge Data:** The evaluation on the concierge data raises a question: Should “correct” be defined as the single best response, or simply being among the top 5? This could influence the interpretation of the results.
*   **Ablation Studies:** A more detailed ablation study of the ‘match type’ features, isolating the impact of different components, could provide further insights into their effectiveness.
* **Error Analysis**: More in-depth error analysis of common failure modes beyond those already mentioned could guide future research.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Regarding the concierge dataset, did you consider evaluating based on whether the correct response was *within* the top N candidates, rather than requiring an exact match? How would that impact the reported results?
2.  The paper highlights the brittleness of current evaluation metrics.  What alternative metrics are you considering for future work, and how might they address the shortcomings of per-response and per-dialog accuracy?
3.  What are the most significant limitations of the current simulation environment that might hinder the generalizability of the findings to more complex real-world scenarios?
4. What are the computational costs associated with adding the 'match type' features, particularly as the KB grows? Is this scalable to larger, more realistic knowledge bases?
