This paper presents a valuable and timely investigation into the capabilities of end-to-end dialog systems in goal-oriented settings, specifically restaurant reservation. The authors rightly point out that success in chit-chat does not necessarily translate to tasks requiring manipulation of both natural language and symbolic knowledge bases. They introduce a well-designed testbed and demonstrate that while Memory Networks show promise, significant challenges remain before these systems can reliably handle goal-oriented conversations.

**1. Summary of Claims**

The paper’s central claim is that current end-to-end dialog systems, while improving, still fall short of reliably handling goal-oriented tasks compared to traditional, hand-crafted approaches. To support this, the authors propose a new testbed comprised of five tasks designed to isolate key capabilities needed for restaurant reservation (API calling, knowledge base interaction, dialog management). They demonstrate that a Memory Network architecture, augmented with “match type” features to handle out-of-vocabulary entities, achieves encouraging but imperfect performance on these tasks. Furthermore, they validate these findings by evaluating the system on two real-world datasets: the DSTC2 challenge and a proprietary concierge service dataset, showing consistent patterns of performance. The authors explicitly state that the goal is *not* to achieve state-of-the-art performance in restaurant booking, but to use this narrow domain to rigorously assess the strengths and weaknesses of end-to-end approaches.

**2. Decision**

I recommend **Accept** with minor revisions. The paper addresses an important question in the field of dialog systems, provides a solid experimental setup, and presents insightful results. The combination of synthetic and real-world evaluation is particularly valuable. 

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The paper is well-motivated. The limitations of traditional slot-filling systems and the potential of end-to-end learning are clearly articulated. The introduction effectively establishes the gap in knowledge that the paper aims to fill: how well do end-to-end systems generalize to goal-oriented tasks? 

The proposed testbed is a significant contribution. By breaking down the restaurant reservation task into smaller, manageable subtasks, the authors enable a more granular analysis of system performance. The inclusion of both in-vocabulary and out-of-vocabulary test sets is crucial for assessing generalization ability. The conversion of the DSTC2 data and the use of the concierge service data provide strong validation of the synthetic results, supporting the claim that the testbed accurately reflects real-world challenges.

The experiments are thorough and well-documented. The authors compare their Memory Network implementation against strong baselines (rule-based, information retrieval, supervised embeddings), and carefully analyze the results. The ablation study with the “match type” features demonstrates the value of incorporating knowledge base information. The detailed analysis of hyperparameters and the inclusion of additional results in the appendix enhance the reproducibility of the work. The clear presentation of per-response *and* per-dialog accuracies provides a more nuanced understanding of performance than relying on a single metric.

**4. Additional Feedback**

While the paper is strong, several minor points could be addressed to further improve its clarity and impact:

*   **Discussion of Per-Dialog Accuracy Sensitivity:** The paper rightly points out the sensitivity of per-dialog accuracy to task definitions. A more detailed discussion of *why* certain task configurations lead to lower per-dialog accuracy (e.g., the strict all-or-nothing nature of the metric) would be beneficial.
*   **Concierge Evaluation Metric:** The question raised regarding the Concierge dataset evaluation—whether “correct” should mean among the top 5 responses—is a valid one. Exploring this variation in the results might reveal additional insights.
*   **KB Vocabulary Mismatch:** The authors acknowledge the vocabulary mismatch issue but could expand on potential solutions beyond the “match type” features. Investigating techniques for learning semantic associations between user language and KB terms (e.g., using external knowledge bases or pre-trained word embeddings) would be a valuable direction for future work.
*  **Loss Function Exploration:** The suggestion of more application-driven loss functions (weighted sum of API errors, turns, rejections) is excellent. While a full implementation is beyond the scope of this paper, a more detailed discussion of the potential benefits and challenges of such approaches would strengthen the work.
*   **Error Analysis:** While the tasks allow for some error analysis, providing specific examples of common failure modes (e.g., misinterpreting user intent, incorrect API calls) would be helpful.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Regarding the concierge data, did you experiment with evaluating based on the top-5 responses instead of requiring an exact match? If so, how did the results change?
*   Could you elaborate on the limitations of the “match type” features? Specifically, how do they handle situations where the KB contains synonyms or related terms for entities?
*   What are the computational costs associated with adding the "match type" features, and did you observe any performance bottlenecks during training or inference?
*   You mention the limitations of the current evaluation metrics. Do you have any preliminary ideas on how to develop more application-driven loss functions that better align with real-world goals?
