## Review of “Breaking Down the Goal-Oriented Dialog Task”

This paper introduces a new testbed for evaluating end-to-end dialog systems in a goal-oriented setting, specifically restaurant reservation. The authors motivate this work by highlighting the limitations of scaling traditional, hand-crafted dialog systems and questioning whether recent successes in chit-chat dialog translate to more complex, goal-driven interactions. They present a set of five tasks, grounded in a knowledge base, designed to assess specific capabilities like API querying and entity manipulation.  The paper demonstrates that a Memory Network architecture can achieve promising, though imperfect, performance on these tasks, outperforming a rule-based slot-filling baseline on related datasets.

**Decision: Accept**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of dialog systems research. The proposed testbed offers a much-needed resource for systematically evaluating end-to-end models in a goal-oriented domain, and the initial results provide insightful observations about their strengths and weaknesses. The detailed task definitions and comparison to a strong baseline are particularly commendable.  While end-to-end systems still lag behind traditional methods in overall dialog success rate, this work provides a useful framework for driving future research and pinpointing areas for improvement.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The primary strength of this work lies in the creation of a well-defined and reproducible evaluation environment. The task breakdown—issuing API calls, updating requests, displaying options, and providing information—effectively isolates key components of a goal-oriented dialog system. This allows for a granular analysis of performance and helps identify specific challenges for end-to-end models.  

The comparison to a hand-crafted slot-filling baseline is also crucial.  While slot-filling remains a robust approach in narrow domains, the authors rightly point out its inherent scalability issues. Demonstrating that an end-to-end model can achieve competitive performance, even if imperfect, suggests potential for future progress and offers a pathway to more flexible and adaptable dialog systems.  The authors also wisely validate their findings on both a standard dataset (DSTC2) and a proprietary dataset from an online concierge service, increasing confidence in the generalizability of their results. 

Furthermore, the inclusion of out-of-vocabulary (OOV) tests highlights a critical challenge for these models – their ability to generalize to unseen entities. The experiments reveal that while Memory Networks show some promise in handling OOV entities, there is still considerable room for improvement. This line of investigation is particularly valuable.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong overall, a few points could be addressed to further enhance its impact. 

*   **Ranking vs. Generation:** The choice of a ranking-based evaluation limits the model's potential for genuine generalization.  As the authors acknowledge, creating a comprehensive candidate list in a new domain would be challenging. Exploring generation-based evaluation, perhaps with appropriate constraints or reward shaping, would be a valuable extension.
*   **Natural Language Generation Rules:**  The reliance on a fixed set of natural language generation patterns within the simulator introduces a potential bias. The variability in real human language is likely higher. Publishing the complete list of these patterns would allow for a better understanding of the simulator's limitations and facilitate comparisons with other research.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A more detailed discussion of hyperparameter tuning and sensitivity analysis could be beneficial. While the authors mention optimizing hyperparameters, providing more specific details about the search process and the impact of different settings would strengthen the reproducibility of the results.
*  **Error Analysis:** A more in-depth error analysis would be very helpful. What types of errors are most frequent? Where does the Memory Network struggle the most?  Providing examples of failed dialogs and a categorization of error types would offer valuable insights into the limitations of the current approach.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the rationale for choosing Memory Networks specifically? Were other architectures (e.g., seq2seq with attention) explored, and if so, why were they not included in the main experimental results?
2.  The paper mentions that the simulator uses 20 patterns for bot utterances. Could you share those patterns, or at least examples, to help understand the limitations imposed by this approach?
3.  You mention the challenges of dealing with OOV entities. Have you considered any techniques for dynamically incorporating new entities into the knowledge base or updating the model’s representations during dialog?
4.  Given the ranking evaluation metric, how confident are you that the observed performance differences truly reflect differences in the *quality* of the generated responses, or are they simply due to differences in the ranking of pre-defined candidates?
5. Could you discuss the computational cost of training and evaluating the Memory Network, particularly in relation to the size of the datasets used?
