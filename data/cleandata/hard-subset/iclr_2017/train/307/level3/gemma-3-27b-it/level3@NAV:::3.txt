## Review of “A Testbed for End-to-End Goal-Oriented Dialog”

This paper presents a new, simulated restaurant-domain dataset designed for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. The dataset leverages a knowledge base and rule-based natural language generation to create conversations, and proposes a ranking task for evaluating system performance – assessing the quality of candidate next-turn responses. The authors demonstrate that Memory Networks outperform bag-of-words baselines on this task, and validate their findings on existing datasets (DSTC2 and a proprietary concierge service). 

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper, with minor revisions. While the work doesn't represent a breakthrough in dialog *generation*, it provides a valuable and well-motivated contribution to the development of evaluation methodologies for end-to-end goal-oriented systems – a crucial area for progress. The creation of a controlled, reproducible environment is particularly commendable.

My primary reason for acceptance stems from the clear need for focused evaluation benchmarks in this area. The paper convincingly argues that existing resources are insufficient for assessing the strengths and weaknesses of end-to-end systems in goal-oriented settings. The synthetic dataset, while simplified, offers a level of control and reproducibility lacking in real-world data. The validation against real-world datasets adds confidence to the findings.

However, my initial skepticism regarding the ranking task remains. Ranking, by its nature, relies on a pre-defined set of candidate responses, which limits the system's ability to truly *generalize* and potentially hinders innovation in response generation.  Furthermore, the practicality of obtaining candidate response lists in novel domains is questionable – where would these come from without significant manual effort? The simulator’s rule-based NL generation is also a concern, as it restricts the diversity of the training data. I would appreciate seeing the list of rules used for NL generation to better understand this limitation.

To improve the paper, I suggest the authors:

*   **Discuss the limitations of the ranking task more explicitly.** Acknowledge how this approach might impact the evaluation of truly generative models.
*   **Address the practicality of candidate response lists.** Offer potential solutions or alternative evaluation methods for real-world deployment.
*   **Provide the complete list of NL generation rules** as supplementary material.
*   **Clarify the hyperparameter tuning process** for each model in greater detail.



I have a few clarifying questions:

*   How sensitive are the results to the size of the candidate response list used for ranking?
*   Were any attempts made to introduce more variability into the rule-based NL generation process, even if it meant sacrificing some control?
*   Could you elaborate on the reasoning behind choosing Memory Networks as the primary end-to-end model? Were other architectures considered and why were they not pursued?
