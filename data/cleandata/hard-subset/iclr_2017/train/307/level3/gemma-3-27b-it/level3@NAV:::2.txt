## Review of "Breaking Down the Goal-Oriented Dialog Problem"

This paper presents a valuable and much-needed reality check for the field, demonstrating the significant gap between current end-to-end models and the requirements for robust goal-oriented dialog systems. The authors convincingly argue that progress in chit-chat doesn’t automatically translate to task completion, and provide a well-designed testbed to investigate this discrepancy. The creation of these synthetic tasks, alongside evaluation on real-world data from DSTC2 and an online concierge service, is a significant contribution to both the Dialog/NLP and ML communities.

I **accept** this paper. The core arguments are well-supported by thorough experimentation and insightful analysis. The paper highlights the brittleness of common dialog metrics and demonstrates that per-dialog accuracy is highly sensitive to task definition, raising crucial points about evaluation protocols. The key contribution of the "match type" addition to memory networks, while simple, is effectively deployed and shows tangible benefits, particularly in handling out-of-vocabulary entities.

The strength of this work lies in its rigorous experimental protocol and clarity of presentation. The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and includes a helpful appendix with detailed results. The analysis of the synthetic tasks is particularly strong, and the confirmation of the observed trends on real-world data lends further credibility to the findings. The authors correctly identify vocabulary mismatch as a significant challenge, and the match type feature is a sensible first step towards addressing it. 

However, the vocabulary mismatch issue could be more thoroughly explored. While the match type helps with exact KB occurrences, a deeper dive into learning semantic associations would be beneficial. Furthermore, the paper briefly mentions the potential for more application-driven loss functions (weighted API errors, etc.) but doesn’t elaborate on the practical challenges of implementing such functions (e.g., requiring human feedback or realistic simulators).

I have a few questions for the authors:

1.  Regarding the concierge dataset, you mention evaluating whether a “correct” response is among the 5-best.  Have you experimented with different cutoff thresholds (e.g., top-3, top-1) and how does performance change? Would a stricter threshold reveal different weaknesses in the models?
2.  The "match type" feature appears beneficial, but relies on exact KB matches. Could you elaborate on the limitations of this approach, specifically how it handles synonyms or paraphrases of KB entities that aren't explicitly present in the KB?
3.  You briefly discuss application-driven loss functions. Could you provide a more concrete example of how such a loss function, tied to a real-world revenue metric, could be designed and trained, even if it requires a simplified simulation or limited human feedback?
