This paper introduces nonparametric neural networks, a framework for dynamically adjusting network size during training. The core idea is to optimize not only the weights but also the network’s dimensionality, guided by an l2 penalty to prevent unbounded growth. A novel optimization algorithm, AdaRad, is presented to facilitate this process. The paper provides theoretical justification for the approach and demonstrates promising results on several datasets.

**Reject** with a strong encouragement to revise and resubmit. While the idea is interesting and the theoretical grounding is a positive aspect, the experimental results are not consistently compelling and the presentation lacks sufficient clarity for a full assessment of the contributions. The main concern revolves around the limited novelty and the lack of a convincing advantage over existing techniques, particularly given the complexity of the proposed method.

The main arguments supporting this decision are:

1. **Incremental Novelty:** The core concept of dynamically growing/shrinking networks isn’t entirely novel. Prior work (referenced in the paper) has explored pruning and dynamic network expansion. The key contribution, AdaRad, seems to be an adaptation of existing optimization techniques (radial-angular gradients) tailored to this dynamic size setting. The degree to which AdaRad *necessitates* the nonparametric framework, or could be applied elsewhere, is not clearly articulated. The advantage of AdaRad over standard adaptive optimizers (Adam, RMSprop) in this context is not convincingly demonstrated.

2. **Inconsistent Empirical Performance:** The results are mixed. While the method shows promise on MNIST and rectangles, it performs poorly on the convex dataset. The claim that the method achieves performance “competitive with” or “better than” existing approaches is qualified and sometimes not supported by a substantial margin. Furthermore, the experimental setup appears somewhat limited; a more comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods, including more robust hyperparameter optimization techniques, is needed. The claim of “globally best performance” on rectangles dataset needs further justification.

Several points would significantly improve the paper:

*   **Clarify Novelty:** More explicitly delineate the contributions of this work relative to existing approaches like pruning, dynamic expansion, and network architecture search. A clearer explanation of *why* a nonparametric approach is advantageous over these alternatives is needed.
*   **Stronger Empirical Validation:** Perform a more thorough evaluation on a wider range of datasets, including larger and more complex ones. A more systematic comparison with strong baselines (e.g., exhaustive grid search, Bayesian optimization with existing neural network libraries) is crucial. Ablation studies to assess the contribution of each component of AdaRad (radial vs. angular gradient) would be valuable.
*   **Elaborate on Theoretical Justification:** While Theorem 1 is presented, the connection between the theorem and the practical implementation (e.g., the choice of the l2 penalty) is not fully explained. More intuition about the theorem's implications would be helpful.
*   **Improve Clarity and Organization:** The paper is dense and occasionally difficult to follow. Clarifying the notation and providing more intuitive explanations of the key concepts would improve readability.
*   **Explain Parameter Sensitivity:** The influence of hyperparameters like `αr`, `αφ`, `β`, and `ν` on performance is not discussed. A sensitivity analysis would offer insight into the robustness of the method.
*   **Address Limitations:** A discussion of the limitations of the approach (e.g., computational cost, potential for instability during dynamic resizing) would strengthen the paper.

Specific questions for the authors:

*   How does the performance of AdaRad compare to standard adaptive optimizers (Adam, RMSprop) when applied to *fixed* network architectures? This would help isolate the benefit of the dynamic resizing aspect.
*   What is the computational overhead of AdaRad compared to standard optimizers? The paper mentions scalability, but more quantitative data would be helpful.
*   Why does the nonparametric approach perform poorly on the convex dataset?
*   The `CapNorm` layer is presented as essential. How sensitive is the performance to the choice of using a capped vs. uncapped batch normalization layer?
*   Could the AdaRad algorithm be adapted to work with other regularization techniques beyond the l2 penalty (e.g., l1 regularization)?
*   What are the implications of the choice of self-similar nonlinearities? Would the performance be significantly affected by using other nonlinearities?
