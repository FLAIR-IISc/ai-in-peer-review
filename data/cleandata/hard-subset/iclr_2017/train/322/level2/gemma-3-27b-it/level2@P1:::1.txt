This paper introduces a novel framework for automatically determining neural network size during training, termed "nonparametric neural networks." The core idea is to dynamically grow and shrink the network while penalizing large weights, thereby simultaneously performing model selection and training. The authors propose a specific optimization algorithm, AdaRad, to navigate this non-standard optimization landscape. The paper provides theoretical justification for the approach and demonstrates promising results on several datasets.

**Reject.** While the idea is interesting and the technical development is substantial, the empirical results are not convincing enough to warrant acceptance. The performance gains are inconsistent across datasets, and the comparison to well-tuned parametric networks isn't always favorable. The claims of achieving higher accuracy than possible with black-box methods are not fully substantiated.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Inconsistent Performance:** The results in Section 4 show that nonparametric networks significantly outperform parametric networks on the MNIST dataset, perform comparably on the rectangles dataset (depending on the regularization parameter), but *underperform* on the convex dataset. This inconsistency raises concerns about the general applicability and robustness of the proposed method. The authors attribute the poor performance on convex to the tendency to converge to smaller network sizes, but this seems like a fundamental limitation rather than a fixable issue. A truly compelling method should exhibit consistent benefits across a range of problems.

2. **Limited Comparison to Strong Baselines:** While the authors compare to parametric networks, the comparison isn't consistently rigorous.  The random search baseline, a strong competitor for hyperparameter optimization, isn’t comprehensively shown to be surpassed. The statement that the nonparametric network achieves “globally best performance” on the rectangles dataset is questionable, given that random search could potentially find even better solutions with sufficient exploration. A more thorough comparison with optimized parametric networks across a wider range of datasets is needed.

3. **Complexity and Practicality:** The AdaRad algorithm, while theoretically sound, introduces additional complexity compared to standard training procedures. The tuning of parameters like αr and αφ, along with the management of unit addition and removal, adds overhead.  While the authors acknowledge this, the practical benefits don’t consistently outweigh the increased complexity, especially given the results on the convex dataset.



**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Ablation Studies:** Conduct ablation studies to isolate the impact of different components of the framework (e.g., the l2 penalty, AdaRad, CapNorm). This will help identify which aspects contribute most to the observed performance.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Perform a sensitivity analysis of the key hyperparameters (λ, αr, αφ).  Understanding how performance changes with different settings will provide valuable insights and guidance for practical usage.
* **Scaling to Larger Datasets:** While the poker dataset experiment is a good step, more extensive evaluation on larger, more complex datasets (e.g., ImageNet) is crucial to demonstrate scalability and real-world applicability.
* **Theoretical Discussion:** Expand the theoretical discussion. The proof of Theorem 1 is valuable, but further investigation into the properties of the loss landscape and the convergence behavior of AdaRad would strengthen the theoretical foundation.  What guarantees can be made about the optimality of the resulting network size?
* **Clarify CapNorm:** The justification for capping the standard deviation in CapNorm is a bit hand-wavy. Explain more clearly *why* this modification is necessary and how it avoids negating the regularizer.
* **Code Release:** Releasing the code would significantly enhance the reproducibility and impact of the work.

**Questions for the Authors:**

* On the convex dataset, what specific modifications were attempted to improve the performance of the nonparametric network? Was a different regularization scheme or optimization strategy explored?
*  Could you elaborate on the computational cost of AdaRad compared to standard optimization algorithms (e.g., Adam) in terms of memory usage and training time per epoch? Provide more detailed profiling results.
* How sensitive is the performance to the choice of the initial network size? Does starting with a smaller or larger network significantly impact the final result?
*  What is the rationale behind choosing ReLU as the nonlinearity? Would the framework perform differently with other activation functions?
*  How does the framework handle situations where the optimal network size is significantly larger than the initial size? Is there a mechanism to ensure that the network can adequately grow to accommodate complex relationships in the data?
