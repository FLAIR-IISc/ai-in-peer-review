

Summary: The paper introduces "nonparametric neural networks," a novel framework for automatically determining the optimal size of a neural network during a single training cycle without requiring multiple network training runs. The authors prove that with an appropriate fan-in or fan-out regularizer (specifically using p=2), the optimization problem achieves a global minimum at finite network size. They develop a novel training algorithm (AdaRad) and normalization layer (CapNorm) to implement their approach, and demonstrate promising empirical results across multiple datasets.

Decision: Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field with a theoretically sound approach that addresses an important practical problem in neural network training. The theoretical foundation (Theorem 1) is rigorous and the experimental results provide compelling evidence of the approach's effectiveness.

Supporting arguments: The paper successfully addresses a well-known challenge in deep learning: the need for expensive hyperparameter search to determine optimal network size. The authors provide a clear theoretical justification for their approach through Theorem 1, which establishes the soundness of their framework under reasonable assumptions. This is a notable contribution as most existing methods for network size selection require multiple training runs or a good starting point. The experimental results are well-designed and show that nonparametric networks can achieve competitive performance with parametric networks of the same size, and in some cases even outperform exhaustive search methods. The MNIST and rectangles images results are particularly convincing, showing that the approach can find smaller networks with comparable or better performance. The paper also provides valuable insights into the training dynamics of nonparametric networks through detailed analysis in Section 4.2.

Additional feedback: The paper would benefit from more discussion about practical implementation details. Specifically, the authors could provide more guidance on how to choose the initial network size, unit addition rate, and regularization parameter in practice. The observation that the approach underperforms on the convex dataset (Section 4.1) is interesting but could be expanded upon - a deeper analysis of why this happens and potential remedies would strengthen the paper. The authors also mention that AdaRad-M performs similarly to AdaRad, but don't provide sufficient explanation for why the momentum variant doesn't yield significant improvements. A more detailed comparison between AdaRad and AdaRad-M would be valuable.

Questions for authors: 
1. The convex dataset results show significantly worse performance compared to the other datasets. Could you provide more insight into why nonparametric networks struggle with this particular dataset, and whether there are specific modifications to the framework that could improve performance on such tasks?
2. In Figure 3, the nonparametric network takes longer to converge than parametric networks. How does the overall training time compare when considering the total number of units added during training, and what is the trade-off between the time spent adding units and the final network size/accuracy?
3. The paper states that the nonparametric network achieved near-zero training error in some cases (Figure 3B), while parametric networks reached much lower training errors. Could you elaborate on the relationship between training error and validation/test performance in your approach, and whether this difference in training error might indicate any potential overfitting concerns?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of neural network architecture search. The theoretical foundation is sound, the experimental results are convincing, and the approach has clear practical value. The paper is well-written and well-organized, with a clear explanation of the motivation, methods, and results. I believe this work would be valuable to the ICLR community and recommend acceptance.
