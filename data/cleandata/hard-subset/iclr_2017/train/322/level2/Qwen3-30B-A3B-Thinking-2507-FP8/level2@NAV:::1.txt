

This paper introduces a novel framework for automatically determining optimal neural network size during training through "nonparametric neural networks," which dynamically grow and shrink network units while maintaining performance. The authors prove theoretically that their framework achieves a global minimum under L2 regularization and introduce AdaRad, a novel optimization algorithm for training these networks. They demonstrate promising results on MNIST and rectangles datasets, showing nonparametric networks can match or exceed parametric networks of the same size, while using significantly fewer parameters. The theoretical foundation is strong, with a rigorous proof of Theorem 1 establishing the framework's soundness.

Decision: Accept

Key reasons: The paper addresses a meaningful problem in neural network architecture selection with a theoretically grounded solution. The experimental validation across multiple datasets demonstrates practical utility, and the framework's ability to find competitive network sizes without exhaustive search represents a significant contribution. The paper thoroughly places its work within the relevant literature on network pruning, model selection, and regularization.

Supporting arguments: The theoretical proof in Appendix 7.1 is comprehensive and addresses potential degeneration issues in nonparametric optimization. The experiments on MNIST show nonparametric networks achieving comparable accuracy to the best parametric networks while using 70% fewer parameters. The scalability experiments on the poker dataset demonstrate practical applicability to larger problems. The paper's focus on L2 regularization (p=2) is well-motivated for compatibility with modern hardware.

Additional feedback: While the results on MNIST and rectangles are compelling, the poor performance on the convex dataset requires more thorough analysis. Please clarify why the framework fails on this dataset and whether the issue relates to the specific architecture or data characteristics. Additionally, provide more detailed computational cost comparisons with standard approaches to better quantify the efficiency gains. The paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of the trade-offs between network size, training time, and final performance.

Questions for authors: How would the framework perform on more complex architectures (e.g., CNNs) or tasks with more challenging data distributions? Can the theoretical guarantees be extended to other regularization schemes beyond L2?
