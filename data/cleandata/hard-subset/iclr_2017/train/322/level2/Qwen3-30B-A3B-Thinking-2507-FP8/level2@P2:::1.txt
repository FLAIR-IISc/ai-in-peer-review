

**Review**

**Summary of Contributions**
This paper introduces "nonparametric neural networks," a framework for automatically determining optimal network size during a single training cycle without requiring prior information or extensive hyperparameter search. The authors theoretically prove that their approach achieves a global training error minimum at finite dimensionality under certain regularization conditions. They propose a novel training algorithm (AdaRad) with a specialized normalization layer (CapNorm) that enables continuous network growth and pruning during training, eliminating the need for expensive black-box optimization methods. The paper demonstrates promising empirical results on MNIST and rectangles images datasets, with nonparametric networks outperforming parametric networks of the same size and achieving competitive results with exhaustive search methods.

**Decision**
Reject. The paper makes an interesting theoretical contribution but fails to provide sufficient empirical evidence to support its claims, particularly regarding the general applicability of the framework. The failure on the convex dataset without adequate explanation undermines the main claim that the approach is broadly effective.

**Supporting Arguments**
The paper's theoretical foundation is solid, with a well-structured proof of Theorem 1 establishing the theoretical soundness of the framework. The introduction of AdaRad and CapNorm is novel and well-motivated for the specific problem. However, the empirical evaluation has several critical weaknesses:

First, the paper claims the approach "obtains promising results" but fails to provide sufficient evidence of statistical significance or consistent performance across datasets. The convex dataset results are particularly problematic - nonparametric networks perform "badly" compared to parametric networks, with a significantly higher error rate and less stable performance across random seeds. The paper doesn't provide any explanation for this failure, which is a major concern given the claim that the framework "works well" for tasks without prior information.

Second, the comparison to parametric networks is limited to networks of the same size, which doesn't fully validate the claim that nonparametric networks can achieve better performance than networks trained from scratch. The paper doesn't compare with more recent methods for automatic network sizing (e.g., neural architecture search approaches emerging in 2017) that could provide a more meaningful benchmark.

Third, the paper doesn't address computational efficiency, which is a critical factor for practical adoption. While the authors note that AdaRad has "more graceful" computational cost scaling than RMSprop, they don't provide actual training time comparisons or resource usage metrics that would help assess the practical trade-offs.

**Additional Feedback**
1. The authors should thoroughly investigate and explain the failure on the convex dataset. This is critical for establishing the framework's general applicability.

2. The paper should provide more detailed comparisons with state-of-the-art methods for automatic network sizing, including recent neural architecture search approaches, rather than limiting comparisons to parametric networks of fixed size.

3. The authors should include computational efficiency metrics (training time, memory usage) to help readers understand the practical trade-offs of their approach.

4. The paper should clarify how the approach differs from related work on network pruning (e.g., Alvarez & Salzmann, 2016; Molchanov et al., 2017) and why their framework is more effective without requiring a high-performing starting point.

**Questions for Authors**
1. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of why the convex dataset results are so different from the other two datasets, and whether you've identified specific characteristics of the convex dataset that make it challenging for your framework?

2. How does your framework compare to more recent neural architecture search methods (e.g., Zoph & Le, 2017) in terms of both performance and computational efficiency?

3. The paper claims nonparametric networks "achieve a higher accuracy than networks of the same size that are trained from scratch." Could you provide more specific quantitative comparisons to support this claim?

4. Have you explored whether the framework could be extended to automatically determine network depth, as mentioned in the conclusion, and what challenges might arise with such an extension?
