

The paper introduces nonparametric neural networks, a framework for automatically determining optimal network size during a single training cycle without requiring expensive global search. The approach dynamically adds and removes units via L2 regularization and introduces a novel training algorithm, AdaRad. The authors prove theoretical soundness under specific conditions (Theorem 1) and demonstrate promising results on MNIST and rectangles images datasets, showing the ability to achieve better accuracy than parametric networks of the same size. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the problem of automatic network size determination.

The paper should be accepted. The problem of automatically determining optimal network size is well-motivated and important. The theoretical contribution (Theorem 1) provides a sound foundation for the approach, and the experimental results are generally convincing for two of the three benchmark datasets.

The theoretical proof is thorough but complex, and the conditions under which the framework is sound (e.g., self-similar nonlinearities, specific regularization parameters) could be more clearly articulated in the main text. The paper's performance on the convex dataset is notably poor, with nonparametric networks substantially underperforming parametric networks of the same size. This represents a significant limitation that should be addressed in the revision. The authors should clarify why the convex dataset presents such a challenge and whether the framework can be adapted to handle such cases.

The AdaRad algorithm is well-motivated and addresses the unique challenges of training nonparametric networks, particularly the need to normalize gradients at the fan-in level rather than the weight level. The computational cost analysis compared to RMSprop is helpful, though a more comprehensive comparison with recent adaptive optimizers would strengthen the argument. The paper would benefit from a clearer explanation of how the framework compares to the most recent methods in neural architecture search, such as those using reinforcement learning (Zoph & Le, 2017), which have become increasingly popular for automatic network design.

The experimental setup for the convex dataset is problematic as the nonparametric networks performed significantly worse than parametric networks, and the paper does not adequately explain this failure mode. The authors should provide more detailed analysis of the training dynamics on this dataset to understand why the framework fails here but succeeds on other tasks.

The paper would benefit from a more comprehensive comparison to recent pruning methods that allow for dynamic network size adjustment during training, such as those by Wei et al. (2016) and Chen et al. (2016). While the authors mention these works, the specific relationship between the proposed framework and these approaches could be clarified.

The following questions would help clarify the paper's contribution:
1. How do the authors explain the poor performance on the convex dataset, and what modifications to the framework might address this issue?
2. What is the expected scalability of the approach to deeper networks and larger datasets beyond the poker dataset?
3. How does the framework compare to methods that use weight pruning after training, such as those by Molchanov et al. (2017), in terms of computational efficiency and final model size?

The theoretical contribution is strong, and the experimental results are generally positive. The poor performance on the convex dataset is concerning but could be addressed in a revision. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the problem of automatic network size determination and would be a significant addition to the ICLR program.
