## Review of "NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS"

This paper introduces a novel framework called Nonparametric Neural Networks (NNNs) for automatically determining the optimal size of a neural network during a single training cycle. The core idea is to dynamically grow and shrink the network by adding and removing units, guided by an L2 penalty and a new optimization algorithm, Adaptive Radial-Angular Gradient Descent (AdaRad). The authors provide theoretical justification for their approach and demonstrate promising results on several datasets.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The proposed approach addresses a significant challenge in deep learning – efficient network architecture search – and offers a compelling alternative to computationally expensive methods like grid search or Bayesian optimization. The theoretical grounding, particularly Theorem 1, is a strong point, establishing conditions for convergence and justifying the framework.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulating the limitations of existing model selection techniques. The authors correctly identify the high cost of training multiple networks and the lack of exploitation of opportunities to refine network size *during* training. The proposed solution directly tackles these issues. The experimental results, while not overwhelmingly superior to existing methods in all cases, demonstrate competitive performance, particularly on the MNIST and rectangles datasets, and showcase the potential of NNNs to achieve higher accuracy with fewer parameters. The ablation study and analysis of the training process (Section 4.2) provide valuable insights into the behavior of the framework.

However, the paper could benefit from further clarification and refinement. The explanation of AdaRad, while detailed, is somewhat complex and could be simplified. A more intuitive explanation of the radial and angular components would be helpful. The choice of hyperparameters, particularly the learning rates and regularization strength, feels somewhat arbitrary and could be better justified. The performance on the convex dataset is notably poor, and a more thorough discussion of the reasons for this failure is warranted. Additionally, the computational cost of AdaRad compared to other optimizers could be more rigorously analyzed. 

I encourage the authors to address these points in a revised version. Specifically, providing a more detailed comparison of the computational overhead of AdaRad, exploring the reasons for the poor performance on the convex dataset, and clarifying the hyperparameter selection process would significantly strengthen the paper. Despite these minor shortcomings, the NNN framework presents a novel and promising approach to neural network architecture search, and I believe it warrants acceptance to the conference.
