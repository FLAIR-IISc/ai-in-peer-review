### Review of "NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS"

**1. Summary**

This paper introduces a novel framework, termed "nonparametric neural networks," for automatically determining the width of a neural network's hidden layers during a single training process. The goal is to eliminate the need for expensive, iterative hyperparameter search procedures like grid or random search. The authors formulate model selection as an optimization problem over both network weights and dimensions.

The key contributions are:
1.  A theoretical result (Theorem 1) proving that the proposed optimization problem has a global minimum at a finite network size when using a fan-in or fan-out `p-norm regularizer. This provides a solid theoretical foundation for the approach.
2.  A practical training scheme that combines network growth and pruning. New, randomly initialized units are periodically added to each hidden layer, while an `2 fan-in regularizer encourages group sparsity, effectively shrinking the fan-in of redundant units to zero so they can be removed.
3.  Two novel algorithmic components designed specifically for this framework:
    *   **AdaRad (Adaptive Radial-Angular Gradient Descent):** A new optimization algorithm that decomposes the gradient for each unit's fan-in into radial (length-changing) and angular (direction-changing) components. It normalizes only the angular component, allowing new units to learn directions quickly without interfering with the regularizer's shrinking effect on the fan-in's magnitude.
    *   **CapNorm (Capped Batch Normalization):** A modification of batch normalization that prevents it from negating the effect of the fan-in regularizer.
4.  An empirical evaluation on three benchmark datasets and one larger dataset. The results show that the proposed method can find well-performing networks, in some cases outperforming both parametric networks of the same size and networks found via an exhaustive random search.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and well-motivated solution to the important problem of neural network architecture selection. The approach is elegant, principled, and supported by both theoretical justification and solid empirical results.

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper proposes a genuinely new way to think about architecture search, framing it as a continuous optimization process within a single training run. This is a significant departure from standard black-box optimization methods and has the potential to save substantial computational resources.
2.  **Technical Soundness:** The approach is carefully constructed. The theoretical guarantee of a finite optimal network size is a major strength. Furthermore, the development of the AdaRad optimizer and CapNorm demonstrates a deep understanding of the problem dynamics; these components are not ad-hoc but are well-justified solutions to specific problems that arise when combining dynamic resizing with regularization.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

**Strengths:**
*   **Principled Approach:** The core idea of using a group-sparsity-inducing regularizer to prune units while simultaneously adding new ones is compelling. The paper provides a clear and coherent narrative, from the high-level formulation in Section 2 to the low-level algorithmic details in Section 3.
*   **Strong Experimental Protocol:** The experiments are well-designed. Comparing the final nonparametric network to a parametric network of the same size is a fair baseline. The inclusion of a comparison against a random search over architectures provides a strong benchmark for the method's ability to find good models.
*   **Insightful Analysis:** Section 4.2 provides an excellent, detailed analysis of the training dynamics. This section greatly enhances the paper's contribution by helping the reader understand *how* the method works, showing the evolution of layer sizes, the lifespan of individual units, and the interplay between fan-in and fan-out norms.
*   **Practicality:** The method appears practical and scalable, as demonstrated by the successful application to the large 'poker' dataset. The custom optimizer, AdaRad, is also shown to be computationally efficient.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Mixed Performance:** The method's poor performance on the 'convex' dataset is a notable weakness. The paper acknowledges this but does not provide a satisfying explanation. This raises questions about the general applicability of the method and the conditions under which it might fail.
*   **New Hyperparameters:** While the method automates the selection of layer widths, it introduces its own set of new, potentially sensitive hyperparameters, such as the radial and angular step sizes (`αr`, `αφ`), and the unit addition rate (`ν`). The paper does not include a sensitivity analysis for these parameters, making it difficult to gauge how much tuning they require in practice. The coupling of `αr` to `λ` seems particularly important.

**4. Additional Feedback**

This is an excellent paper and the following points are intended to help improve it further.
*   The term "nonparametric" is used in a slightly non-standard way. In statistics, nonparametric models typically have a number of parameters that can grow with the size of the dataset. Here, the model complexity is adapted to the complexity of the dataset, but not necessarily its size. While this is a minor point of nomenclature, clarifying the connection to the standard definition or acknowledging the difference might be helpful.
*   The appendix proof of Theorem 1 is extensive and detailed, which is commendable. However, for broader accessibility, a short, high-level proof sketch in the main paper or at the beginning of the appendix could be beneficial to convey the main argument more directly.
*   The analysis of the failure on the 'convex' dataset could be strengthened. Even some speculation would be valuable. For instance, does this dataset require a much larger network than the regularization scheme is biased towards? Or is there an optimization challenge specific to that loss landscape?
*   The strategy for adding units (one per layer per epoch) is simple and effective for the experiments, but feels somewhat heuristic. It would be interesting to know if the authors explored more adaptive schedules, for example, linking the addition rate to the validation error's rate of improvement.

**5. Questions for Authors**

I would appreciate it if the authors could address the following points during the discussion period:
*   Could you provide more insight into the poor performance on the 'convex' dataset? What characteristics of this dataset might explain why your method converges to a small, suboptimal architecture?
*   The relationship `αr = 150λ` seems to be a crucial choice that determines the "default lifespan" of a new unit. How sensitive is the final model's performance and size to this ratio? Is this a new "meta-hyperparameter" that is difficult to set?
*   How does the total wall-clock training time of a single nonparametric run compare to the total time required for the random search protocol to find a model of comparable accuracy?
*   In AdaRad, you chose not to normalize the radial component of the gradient at all. Was this choice based on empirical results, or is there a stronger theoretical motivation? Did you experiment with alternatives, such as applying a much weaker normalization to the radial component compared to the angular one?
