### Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces a novel framework called "nonparametric neural networks" to address the problem of automatically determining the width of hidden layers in a neural network during a single training process. The core idea is to treat the network size as a variable to be optimized alongside the weights. This is achieved by combining a mechanism for continuously adding new hidden units with a group sparsity regularizer (an l2 penalty on the fan-in of each unit) that encourages and prunes redundant units.

The key contributions are threefold:
1.  **Theoretical Framework:** The paper provides a formal definition of nonparametric neural networks and proves a core theorem stating that, under this framework with a fan-in/fan-out regularizer, a global minimum of the training objective exists at a finite network size. This provides a solid theoretical justification for the approach.
2.  **Novel Training Algorithm:** To make the framework practical, the authors propose a new optimization algorithm, Adaptive Radial-Angular Gradient Descent (AdaRad). This algorithm cleverly decouples the gradient update for each unit's fan-in into a radial component (affecting its magnitude) and an angular component (affecting its direction). By normalizing only the angular component, AdaRad allows newly added units to learn useful features without being prematurely eliminated by the regularizer.
3.  **Empirical Validation:** The method is evaluated on several benchmark datasets. The results demonstrate that, in some cases, the proposed method can find well-performing, compact architectures that outperform parametrically-trained networks of the same size and are competitive with architectures found through an extensive random search. The paper includes a detailed analysis of the training dynamics and a successful scalability test on a large dataset.

### Decision and Justification

**Decision:** Accept

This paper presents a novel, elegant, and well-motivated solution to the important and challenging problem of neural architecture selection. The core reasons for my recommendation are:

1.  **Strong Combination of Theory and Practice:** The work is grounded by a significant theoretical result (Theorem 1) that guarantees the soundness of the optimization problem, a common concern in methods that grow networks. This theoretical contribution is complemented by the development of a novel and practical optimization algorithm (AdaRad) that is specifically designed to overcome the challenges inherent in the proposed framework.
2.  **Thorough and Honest Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are comprehensive, comparing the proposed method against strong baselines (fixed-size parametric networks and random architecture search). The detailed analysis of a single training run provides valuable insight into the method's internal dynamics. Crucially, the authors are transparent about the method's limitations, frankly discussing its poor performance on the 'convex' dataset, which strengthens the credibility of the overall evaluation.

### Supporting Arguments

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance:** The paper tackles a problem of high practical importance. Automating the search for an effective network size in a single training run could save enormous amounts of computational resources and manual effort compared to standard black-box hyperparameter optimization techniques.
*   **Novelty:** While network pruning and growth are not new concepts in isolation, their combination into a unified, theoretically-grounded optimization framework is novel. The AdaRad optimizer is a genuinely new contribution, cleverly designed to handle the interaction between the learning signal and the group sparsity regularizer. The introduction of "CapNorm" is another thoughtful detail that makes the system work.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The motivation for each component is clearly articulated. The proof of Theorem 1, detailed in the appendix, appears rigorous and provides a strong foundation for the work. The experimental design is sound and the results on the MNIST, rectangles images, and poker datasets convincingly demonstrate the potential of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Performance Inconsistency:** The method's failure on the 'convex' dataset indicates that its effectiveness may be dataset-dependent. The paper acknowledges this but does not provide a deep analysis of the potential causes, which limits our understanding of the method's failure modes.
*   **Introduction of New Hyperparameters:** While the framework automates the selection of layer widths, it introduces its own set of hyperparameters (e.g., unit addition rate ν, regularization strength λ, and the two step sizes αr and αφ for AdaRad). The relationship between the radial step size and λ (αr = 150λ) appears to be a crucial but heuristic choice. It is not clear how much tuning these new hyperparameters require in practice.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

The following points are intended as constructive suggestions to further strengthen the paper:

*   **Analysis of Failure Case:** It would significantly improve the paper if the authors could offer more speculation or preliminary analysis on why the method performed poorly on the 'convex' dataset. Is it related to the loss landscape, the nature of the required features, or an inability of the growth/pruning dynamic to find a good solution in that specific problem space?
*   **Computational Cost Comparison:** While Table 1 provides a theoretical comparison of optimizer costs per iteration, a practical comparison of total wall-clock time would be very impactful. For instance, showing the time taken for a single nonparametric run versus the total time for the random search experiment would provide a more concrete illustration of the method's efficiency benefits.
*   **Unit Initialization:** The paper uses a simple random initialization for the fan-in of new units. It might be interesting to discuss or experiment with alternative strategies. For example, could new units be initialized in a way that attempts to correct the current model's largest errors, akin to boosting? This could potentially accelerate convergence.
*   **Clarity on AdaRad's Scaling:** The claim that "many of its operations scale only with the number of units and not with the number of weights" could be slightly misleading. Operations like the vector decomposition and rotation in AdaRad are still dependent on the fan-in size (i.e., number of weights per unit). Rephrasing this to more precisely state that the memory overhead for the running averages scales with the number of units (unlike RMSProp/Adam) would be more accurate.

### Questions for the Authors

I would appreciate it if the authors could clarify the following points in their rebuttal:

1.  How sensitive is the final architecture and performance to the unit addition schedule (ν)? Have you experimented with adaptive schedules, such as adding units only when learning plateaus?
2.  Could you elaborate on the choice of the heuristic αr = 150λ? How critical is this ratio to the balance between learning and pruning, and how was it determined?
3.  Following up on the poor performance on the 'convex' dataset, do you have any intuition about whether the problem lies with the optimization (e.g., getting stuck in a poor local minimum corresponding to a small network) or with the final model itself (e.g., the group-l2 regularization imposes an unfavorable bias for this task)?
