This paper introduces "nonparametric neural networks," a novel and compelling framework for automatically determining a suitable network size during a single training run. The core contributions are threefold: 1) a formal framework for optimizing over network architectures of varying sizes, 2) a theoretical proof (Theorem 1) guaranteeing that a global minimum exists at a finite network size when using fan-in/fan-out regularization, and 3) a practical training methodology that combines unit addition with l2-based pruning, supported by a novel optimizer (AdaRad) and a modified batch normalization layer (CapNorm).

I recommend **Accepting** this paper. The work addresses the highly relevant and challenging problem of neural architecture search with a well-motivated and elegant approach. The claims are strongly supported by a combination of rigorous theoretical analysis and a thorough empirical evaluation.

The primary strength of this paper is its principled approach. Unlike many heuristic methods for network pruning or growth, the authors ground their framework in a clear optimization problem and provide a formal proof of its soundness. The introduction of AdaRad is a clever solution to the specific optimization challenges that arise from simultaneously training and regularizing newly added units. The experimental results are convincing; on two of three benchmark datasets, the proposed method finds compact, high-performing networks that are competitive with or superior to those found by extensive random search. The successful application to the large-scale Poker dataset demonstrates the method's scalability. The inclusion of a negative result on the 'convex' dataset is commendable and adds to the credibility of the study.

For improvement, the paper could benefit from further discussion on the sensitivity to the unit addition schedule (e.g., one unit per epoch). An ablation study on this hyperparameter would be valuable. While the focus on l2 regularization is justified by hardware efficiency, a brief discussion on how an l1 penalty might alter the resulting architectures would be insightful. Finally, a deeper analysis into why the method underperformed on the 'convex' dataset could provide valuable lessons about the framework's limitations.

I have two main questions for the authors:
1.  In your experiments, how does the performance of a parametrically trained network, initialized from scratch but with the *exact final architecture* found by your nonparametric method, compare to the nonparametrically trained model? This would help clarify whether the benefit comes from the final architecture itself or the dynamic training process.
2.  The radial step size `αr` is tied to the regularization parameter `λ`. Have you experimented with decoupling these two hyperparameters, or is this coupling fundamental to the balance between learning and pruning?
