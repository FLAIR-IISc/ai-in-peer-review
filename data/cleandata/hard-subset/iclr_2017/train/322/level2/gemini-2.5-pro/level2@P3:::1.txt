**Summary of the Paper's Claims**

This paper introduces a framework called "nonparametric neural networks" to address the challenge of automatically selecting an appropriate network size. Instead of performing an expensive search over different architectures, this work proposes to dynamically adjust the network's width during a single training run. The core idea is to frame model selection as an optimization problem over both the network weights and the number of hidden units. The framework is supported by a theoretical result (Theorem 1) proving that, under an `l_p` fan-in or fan-out regularization scheme, a global minimum exists at a finite network size.

To implement this, the paper proposes a training methodology where new units are periodically added, and a group `l_2` penalty on the fan-in of each unit encourages sparsity, leading to the removal of redundant units. The work introduces two novel components to support this process:
1.  **Capped Batch Normalization (CapNorm):** A modification of batch normalization that is compatible with the shrinkage-inducing regularizer.
2.  **Adaptive Radial-Angular Gradient Descent (AdaRad):** A new optimization algorithm that decomposes gradient updates into radial (magnitude-changing) and angular (direction-changing) components. It selectively normalizes only the angular component, allowing new units to learn effectively without being immediately eliminated by the regularizer.

Empirical results on several benchmark datasets demonstrate that this approach can discover well-performing networks, often outperforming fixed-size architectures and being competitive with models found via random search, sometimes with significantly fewer parameters.

**Decision and Key Reasons**

Decision: **Accept**

This paper presents a novel and well-motivated solution to the important and practical problem of neural network architecture selection. The decision to accept is based on two primary strengths:

1.  **Principled and Novel Approach:** The work frames dynamic network sizing as a coherent optimization problem and provides a strong theoretical justification for its well-posedness. The development of specialized components like the AdaRad optimizer and CapNorm demonstrates a thorough and insightful treatment of the problem's complexities.
2.  **Strong Empirical Support:** The claims are supported by a comprehensive set of experiments that not only show the method's effectiveness on multiple datasets but also include a detailed analysis of the training dynamics and a scalability test. The honest reporting of a failure case (the 'convex' dataset) further strengthens the paper's scientific contribution.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper's motivation is clear: to overcome the inefficiency of standard black-box methods for hyperparameter search. The proposed solution is elegant, combining a continuous growth mechanism with a principled pruning mechanism based on group sparsity.

The theoretical contribution in Theorem 1 is significant, as it provides confidence that the optimization objective will not degenerate by favoring infinitely large networks. The proof provided in the appendix appears rigorous and sound.

The technical contributions are well-conceived. The AdaRad optimizer is particularly noteworthy. By separating the radial and angular components of the gradient update, it cleverly resolves the tension between allowing new units to learn and the regularizer's role in shrinking useless units. This design is directly motivated by the core problem and is a non-trivial contribution. Similarly, CapNorm is a simple but crucial adaptation that makes batch normalization compatible with the proposed regularization scheme.

The experimental evaluation is thorough. The comparison to both fixed-size networks and architectures found via random search provides a solid baseline. The detailed analysis in Section 4.2, showing the lifecycle of individual units and the evolution of network size, offers valuable insight into how the method works. The inclusion of the 'poker' dataset demonstrates that the approach is not limited to small-scale problems.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The following points are suggestions aimed at further strengthening the paper and are not critical to the acceptance decision.

*   **Analysis of the Failure Case:** The paper notes poor performance on the 'convex' dataset. A brief discussion hypothesizing the reasons for this would be very valuable. For example, is it possible that this particular task requires a network structure (e.g., much wider layers) that the current growth and pruning dynamics are not well-suited to discover? Such analysis would better define the method's limitations.
*   **Clarity on AdaRad's Practical Cost:** Algorithm 1 is presented in its "most instructive (but not fastest) order." A brief note in the main text on the practical implementation and its efficiency would be helpful. While Table 1 gives a theoretical complexity comparison, a comment on observed wall-clock time overhead versus optimizers like Adam would provide a more complete picture for practitioners.
*   **Unit Addition Strategy:** The current strategy adds a fixed number of units at a fixed frequency (e.g., one per epoch). It would be interesting to learn if more adaptive strategies were considered, such as increasing the rate of unit addition when learning stagnates. A short discussion on this design choice could be beneficial.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The radial step size is coupled to the regularization strength (`αr = 150λ`), which is justified by giving new units a fixed "lifespan" to prove their utility. A comment on how sensitive the final performance is to this ratio would be useful for understanding the robustness of the method.

**Clarifying Questions**

Answering the following questions could help clarify specific aspects of the work:

*   Regarding the 'convex' dataset, does re-training the final architecture discovered by the nonparametric method from scratch as a standard parametric network improve its performance? This could help determine whether the final architecture is suboptimal or if the dynamic training process itself is less effective for this task.
*   The initialization of new units (random fan-in, zero fan-out) is a key design choice. Were other initialization strategies explored, for instance, strategies that use gradient information to place new units in promising regions of the function space?
