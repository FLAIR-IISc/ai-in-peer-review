Summary of claims and contributions
The paper proposes “nonparametric neural networks,” a framework to optimize model width during a single training run by dynamically adding zero units and pruning via group L2 (fan-in/fan-out) regularization. It proves a key soundness result: with an ℓp fan regularizer (1 ≤ p < ∞), the objective attains a global minimum at finite dimensionality. The authors introduce CapNorm (a capped batch-normalization variant compatible with shrinkage) and AdaRad, an optimizer that decomposes gradients into radial and angular components, normalizing only the angular part to enable new units to learn without being immediately shrunk. Experiments on MNIST, rectangles-images, convex, and a large Poker dataset show the approach can discover compact models and sometimes match or exceed parametric baselines and random search at lower parameter counts.

Decision: Reject (insufficient empirical support and analysis)
Key reasons: (1) The empirical evaluation and baselines do not convincingly isolate and support the central claims; (2) Limited ablations and potential fairness issues make it hard to attribute gains to the proposed components (nonparametric growth, CapNorm, AdaRad) versus training choices.

Supporting arguments
- Problem tackled: selecting network width automatically during training is timely and important; the theoretical result is nontrivial and reassuring. Novelty is credible (soundness theorem; orthogonal/parallel gradient normalization in AdaRad).
- Motivation and placement: Related work on pruning/growing and HPO is discussed; however, comparisons to strong modern HPO (e.g., early-stopping BO, Hyperband/ASHA) and structured sparsity methods are absent.
- Support for claims: While some datasets show advantages, results are inconsistent (notably poor on convex). The comparison protocol differs across NP and parametric settings (CapNorm vs BN; λ ≠ 0 vs λ = 0) and manual selection of “representative” runs introduces bias. No wall-clock or compute comparisons are reported, despite claims of reduced search cost. Ablations (NP without CapNorm, with other optimizers; AdaRad vs RMSprop/Adam under identical normalization constraints; p=1 vs p=2; fan-in vs fan-out) are missing, limiting causal conclusions. Reproducibility is hampered by heuristic schedules and manual choices.

Additional feedback to improve (constructive; not solely used for the decision)
- Provide rigorous, apples-to-apples baselines: same normalization scheme, same regularization, early-stopping, and identical compute budgets. Include time-to-accuracy and total FLOPs.
- Add ablations: (i) NP w/ standard optimizer; (ii) AdaRad on parametric nets; (iii) CapNorm vs BN-without-scale/shift vs LayerNorm/GroupNorm; (iv) growth/pruning alone vs combined; (v) effect of p, fan-in vs fan-out.
- Evaluate on modern architectures (CNNs) and tasks; consider depth selection; compare to Hyperband/ASHA/BO with early stopping.
- Clarify theory scope and assumptions; summarize which common activations/losses satisfy them; discuss behavior for p=1 in practice.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are results to λ, unit-addition rate, and αr/αφ? Can you provide robustness sweeps?
- What is the wall-clock/compute overhead of AdaRad and dynamic sizing vs parametric baselines achieving similar accuracy?
- Do gains persist under identical normalization/regularization across NP and parametric models?
- Can you report ablations isolating the contribution of CapNorm and AdaRad separately?
- Are there practical guidelines or adaptive schemes for λ (e.g., annealing) and addition/pruning schedules?
- Any empirical results on convolutional layers or depth selection to support generality?
