Summary and contributions
- The paper formulates “nonparametric neural networks,” a framework that selects layer widths during a single training run by dynamically adding zero-units and removing redundant units under a group-ℓ2 (fan-in or fan-out) penalty. The main theoretical result (Theorem 1) shows that, with such regularization and 1 ≤ p < ∞, the training objective attains a global minimum at a finite network size. A proposition shows that, with self-similar nonlinearities (e.g., ReLU), layerwise regularization strengths can be reduced to a single scalar via weight rescaling.
- A training procedure is proposed: periodically inject zero units, use group-ℓ2 shrinkage to prune entire fan-ins, and train with a new optimizer, AdaRad, which decomposes gradients for each fan-in into parallel (radial) and orthogonal (angular) components, applying distinct step sizes and normalizing only the angular component. A variant with momentum (AdaRad-M) is also described.
- A batch-normalization variant (CapNorm) is introduced to avoid nullifying the effect of the group-ℓ2 penalty by capping variance normalization below 1 and removing trainable scale/shift.
- Experiments on MNIST, rectangles-images, and convex show that nonparametric networks can converge to data-dependent sizes and, in some cases, outperform parametric networks of the same size; on a large OpenML poker dataset, results are competitive, with the best nonparametric model achieving very low error.

Decision: Reject (weak)
Primary reasons: (1) Empirical support is mixed and limited in scope; performance advantages are not consistent across datasets and are confined to small fully connected settings. (2) Comparisons and ablations do not isolate the contribution of each novel component (CapNorm, AdaRad, unit addition/removal), and several natural baselines are missing. (3) The theoretical result is valuable but does not connect strongly to the practical optimization scheme (no guarantees on learned size or training dynamics), and the framework’s computational advantages over black-box search are not convincingly demonstrated.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: Selecting width during training is important; the formulation of a single-run procedure that grows/prunes units is well motivated and connected to group sparsity and constructive/pruning literature. Positioning relative to pruning and dynamic growth methods is reasonable; however, related optimization techniques that separate weight direction and magnitude (e.g., methods akin to weight normalization or path-normalized updates) are not discussed, making it harder to assess the novelty of AdaRad.
- Theory: Theorem 1 establishes existence of a finite global minimizer with fan-in/fan-out group-ℓp regularization under broad conditions. This addresses a key concern (unbounded growth). The result is conceptually meaningful, though it does not provide guidance on algorithmic convergence or the learned size, and empirical sections do not test sensitivity to p or to fan-in vs fan-out beyond choosing p=2/fan-in.
- Method: The training loop is carefully engineered. AdaRad’s radial/orthogonal decomposition is intuitive under group-ℓ2 shrinkage and self-similar nonlinearities. CapNorm is a pragmatic modification to preserve the effect of the penalty, but its interaction with generalization is not fully justified. Overall, the design introduces several coupled mechanisms whose individual necessity is unclear.
- Empirics: 
  - The small-benchmark results are mixed: improvements over parametric networks of the same size on MNIST and rectangles-images, but clear underperformance on the convex dataset. The large-scale poker experiment is promising but still uses fully connected networks; no convolutional or modern architectures are included.
  - Fairness: parametric baselines receive substantial tuning; the nonparametric method also relies on multiple schedules (addition rate, step sizes, annealing) and long training. There is no runtime or sample-efficiency comparison demonstrating an advantage over black-box search in practice.
  - Ablations: no systematic ablations of CapNorm vs BatchNorm (with constrained scales), AdaRad vs standard optimizers under matched shrinkage, or unit addition schedules. AdaRad-M is briefly mentioned with similar performance, but no detailed analysis is provided.

Feedback aimed at improvement (not all essential)
Essential to strengthen the case:
- Provide ablation studies:
  - CapNorm: compare against BatchNorm variants that fix or restrict affine parameters or explicitly prevent counter-scaling, and report effects on pruning and accuracy.
  - AdaRad: compare to standard optimizers with careful handling of shrinkage (e.g., decoupled weight decay, group-lasso proximal steps) and to direction/magnitude-decoupled methods; quantify when angular normalization alone helps new units learn.
  - Unit addition/removal: test different addition rates and schedules; compare to a “prune-only” group-ℓ2 baseline starting from an overparameterized net trained with standard optimizers.
- Runtime and efficiency: report wall-clock and step counts needed to reach a given validation error versus tuned parametric baselines and versus a modest black-box search budget. This directly addresses the claimed practical advantage.
- Broader benchmarks: include at least one convolutional setting or a deeper MLP to demonstrate generality beyond small FNNs; clarify failure modes observed on the convex dataset.

Nice-to-have but optional:
- Explore p=1 (group-lasso vs group-ℓ2) and fan-out regularization; report sparsity/size and accuracy trade-offs.
- Sensitivity of results to self-similarity assumption (e.g., tanh) to validate the proposition’s practical importance.
- Clarify relationships to existing direction/length parameterizations and normalization methods, and discuss differences.

Questions for clarification
- How sensitive is performance to the unit addition rate and the choice of initializing fan-ins with “expected length 1”? Does adaptive addition (based on signal-to-noise or gradient statistics) improve stability or speed?
- CapNorm prevents scaling below variance 1. Does this create optimization plateaus or harm calibration? Is there a learned alternative (e.g., constrained affine parameters) that preserves the regularizer’s effect but recovers some of BatchNorm’s flexibility?
- AdaRad’s normalization uses per-fan-in statistics. How does it compare to weight-direction updates with explicit length parameters and separate learning rates? Are there cases where angular normalization hurts (e.g., near-zero fan-ins)?
- The theorem guarantees a finite minimizer for the penalized objective. In practice, λ is annealed to zero late in training. How does this affect learned size and the theorem’s relevance? Is the final architecture determined before λ→0?
- On the poker dataset, very low errors are achieved. Is this due to the nonparametric procedure or simply the capacity and data scale? Can similar parametric models, matched in training budget and regularization, close the gap?

Overall assessment
The paper presents an interesting and well-articulated framework with a solid existence theorem and thoughtful algorithmic design. However, empirical validation is not yet sufficient to establish clear practical advantages or isolate the contribution of each component. Strengthening the experimental section and clarifying connections to related optimization techniques would make the work considerably more compelling.
