- Proposes "nonparametric neural networks" that grow/prune units during a single training run using group-ℓp regularization and proves (Theorem 1) the penalized objective has a finite global minimizer.  
- Shows weight-rescaling reduces layerwise regularization to a single scalar for self-similar nonlinearities (e.g., ReLU).  
- Introduces a training scheme that periodically adds zero-units, prunes via group-ℓ2 shrinkage, and uses AdaRad (radial/orthogonal gradient decomposition) with a momentum variant AdaRad-M.  
- Proposes CapNorm, a batch-normalization variant that caps variance normalization below 1 and removes affine parameters to preserve group-ℓ2 effects.  
- Experiments on MNIST, rectangles-images, convex, and a large poker dataset show learned data-dependent sizes, mixed accuracy improvements, and a competitive result on the poker data.  
- Decision: Reject (weak) due to limited and mixed empirical support, incomplete ablations/baselines, and weak practical connection between theory and optimization.  
- Primary concern 1: Empirical advantages are inconsistent and limited to small fully-connected settings.  
- Primary concern 2: Missing comparisons/ablations mean individual contributions (CapNorm, AdaRad, unit add/remove) are not isolated.  
- Primary concern 3: Theorem is valuable but does not guarantee convergence or learned size under the practical algorithm; claimed computational benefits over black-box search are not demonstrated.  
- Problem/motivation: Selecting width during training is well-motivated and related to pruning/growth literature, but related direction/magnitude-separating optimizers are not discussed.  
- Theory: Theorem 1 ensures bounded minimizers under group-ℓp regularization, but gives no guidance on algorithmic convergence, sensitivity to p, or fan-in vs fan-out choices.  
- Method: AdaRad’s radial/orthogonal update is intuitive for group-ℓ2; CapNorm is pragmatic, but the necessity of the many coupled mechanisms is unclear.  
- Empirics (benchmarks): Small-benchmark results are mixed (wins on some tasks, loss on convex), and experiments are limited to fully-connected nets without modern architectures.  
- Empirics (fairness/tuning): Both parametric and nonparametric methods require heavy tuning; no runtime or sample-efficiency comparisons to tuned baselines or black-box search are provided.  
- Empirics (ablations): Lacks systematic ablations of CapNorm vs BatchNorm variants, AdaRad vs standard optimizers with matched shrinkage, and unit addition/removal schedules.  
- Essential improvements requested: ablations for CapNorm, AdaRad, and unit add/remove schedules; compare to prune-only baselines and direction/magnitude-decoupled optimizers.  
- Essential improvements requested: report wall-clock and step counts versus tuned parametric baselines and black-box search to validate claimed efficiency gains.  
- Essential improvements requested: broaden benchmarks to at least one convolutional setting or deeper MLP and analyze failure modes (e.g., convex dataset).  
- Nice-to-have: explore p=1 vs p=2 and fan-out regularization, sensitivity to self-similarity (non-ReLU), and clarify links to existing normalization/parameterizations.  
- Clarification questions: sensitivity to unit addition rate and initialization length; whether adaptive addition helps.  
- Clarification questions: does CapNorm create plateaus or harm calibration compared to constrained BatchNorm?  
- Clarification questions: how does AdaRad compare to explicit length/direction parameter updates and when might angular normalization hurt?  
- Clarification questions: how does annealing λ→0 affect learned size and the practical relevance of the theorem?  
- Clarification questions: on the poker dataset, is the low error due to the nonparametric method or simply capacity/data scale, and can matched parametric baselines match it?  
- Overall: Interesting framework with a solid existence theorem and thoughtful algorithmic design, but empirical validation and isolation of components need strengthening to be convincing.
