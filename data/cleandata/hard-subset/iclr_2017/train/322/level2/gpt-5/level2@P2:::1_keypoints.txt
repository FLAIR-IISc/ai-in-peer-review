Introduces “nonparametric neural networks” that jointly optimize weights and width via a global objective with fan-in/fan-out group-regularization and proves existence of a finite-dimensional global minimizer (Theorem 1).  
Proposes a practical training loop that alternates continuous weight updates with periodic unit addition (f-equivalent initialization) and unit removal via group shrinkage to adaptively grow and shrink networks.  
Introduces CapNorm, a capped batch-normalization variant designed to prevent normalization from nullifying group regularizer effects.  
Introduces AdaRad, an optimizer that splits gradients into radial and angular components and normalizes only the angular part so new units can learn without immediate elimination.  
Decision: Reject due to insufficient and uneven empirical validation and methodological concerns preventing strong conclusions about consistent superiority over parametric baselines.  
Theorem 1 is technically interesting but gives no guarantees about learned width’s proximity to optimal validation performance or optimization dynamics under the proposed scheme.  
Proposition 1 linking self-similar nonlinearities to a single λ is useful, but practical performance still depends on hyperparameters (λ, ν, αr, αφ).  
The combination of group sparsity-driven width optimization, CapNorm, and AdaRad is novel and well-motivated.  
Experimental comparisons are confounded: parametric baselines use BN without weight decay while nonparametric uses CapNorm, making it unclear which change drives results.  
Scope is limited to fully-connected networks; scalability and effectiveness on convolutional or modern architectures are not empirically evaluated.  
Selection protocol is biased: manually choosing a “typical representative” from runs and different training/annealing protocols for methods undermines fairness.  
No wall-clock, FLOPs, or compute-budget comparisons are reported despite the method’s acknowledged slowness when growing from scratch.  
Missing ablations isolating components (unit addition/removal, ℓ2 fan-in, CapNorm, AdaRad) and comparisons p=1 vs p=2, fan-in vs fan-out, and optimizer alternatives.  
Mixed and unstable results: underperformance and small-width bias on convex; striking poker result lacks diagnostics to rule out data leakage or metric artifacts.  
Paper lists related pruning/growth/structured-sparsity work but fails to empirically compare to strong width-selection baselines or discuss connections to WeightNorm and scale-invariant optimizers.  
Suggest matching baselines fairly (weight decay with BN, BN with/without affine), and directly compare CapNorm to BN/LayerNorm/WeightNorm under same regularization.  
Suggest ablations: disable unit addition, replace AdaRad with RMSprop/Adam with group-lasso, compare p=1 vs p=2, fan-in vs fan-out, and test CapNorm alone with group-lasso.  
Recommend reporting wall-clock time, FLOPs, parameter counts during training, and final test metrics with confidence intervals across multiple seeds.  
Advise evaluating on convolutional architectures (e.g., small CNNs on CIFAR) to demonstrate applicability beyond MLPs.  
Request clarification on CapNorm’s test-time behavior (running averages vs per-batch) and whether its cap induces distribution shift.  
Ask authors to justify annealing λ to zero late in training and show its effect on generalization versus keeping regularization.  
Recommend an objective selection protocol for representative runs (e.g., median over seeds) instead of manual selection.  
Theoretically suggest discussing whether existence results can be strengthened (bounds relating λ to width) or whether convergence of the proposed dynamics can be shown under assumptions.  
Questions for authors: how is CapNorm applied at inference and sensitivity to batch size?  
Questions for authors: provide ablations quantifying contributions of AdaRad, CapNorm, and unit addition/removal.  
Questions for authors: why omit weight decay in BN baselines and how conclusions change if included?  
Questions for authors: how was data leakage prevented on poker, and can you provide confusion matrices and tree/GBDT baselines?  
Questions for authors: is the small-width bias observed on other datasets and can tuning λ/ν/αr mitigate underfitting?  
Questions for authors: any plans or preliminary results for applying the method to convolutional layers (per-channel/filter group-lasso)?
