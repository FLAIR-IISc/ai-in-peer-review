- Tackles automatic selection of neural network width during a single training run via “nonparametric neural networks.”  
- Introduces per-unit group ℓp penalty on fan-in/fan-out to allow units to be added/removed during training.  
- Theorem 1: proves the training objective attains a global minimum at finite network size for 1 ≤ p < ∞ and λ > 0, avoiding unbounded growth.  
- Provides proposition on self-similar nonlinearities (e.g., ReLU) explaining why a single λ suffices across layers up to rescaling.  
- Proposes a practical grow-prune training scheme alternating gradient updates with periodic zero-unit addition and pruning.  
- Introduces CapNorm, a BatchNorm variant compatible with per-unit regularization.  
- Introduces AdaRad optimizer that splits gradients into radial and angular components, normalizing only the orthogonal component to help newborn units learn.  
- Experiments on MNIST, Rectangles-Images, Convex, and Poker show convergence to reasonable sizes and sometimes outperform comparable parametric networks.  
- Convex dataset shows negative/mixed results; authors analyze this as tendency to converge to smaller sizes.  
- Training-dynamics analysis highlights the roles of angular and radial steps and supports intended behavior of αr and αφ.  
- Decision: Weak accept — novel, well-motivated formulation with a meaningful theoretical guarantee and original algorithmic design.  
- Strength: clear motivation addressing hyperparameter search and limitations of pruning-only methods.  
- Strength: theorem meaningfully supports the modeling choice despite not proving algorithm convergence.  
- Strength: algorithmic novelty is practical and thoughtfully addresses conflicts between normalization and shrinkage.  
- Strength: empirical results are promising and include scalability demonstration (Poker), with transparent negative case discussion.  
- Strength: methodology clearly described with pseudocode and thorough appendix.  
- Limitation: experiments limited to fully-connected MLPs; no convolutional or sequence models tested.  
- Limitation: lack of ablations isolating contributions of AdaRad and CapNorm versus other optimizers/normalizations.  
- Limitation: reliance on hand-crafted schedules and heuristic hyperparameters (αr ≈ 150/λ, addition rates), potential result bias from selecting “typical” runs.  
- Limitation: Theorem 1 applies to training objective but gives no guarantee about validation/test generalization or learned size minimizing test error.  
- Limitation: practical details underexplored—frequency of unit add/removal, CapNorm vs BN effects, and computational overhead of AdaRad.  
- Suggestions: provide ablations comparing AdaRad vs Adam/RMSprop with CapNorm, CapNorm vs BN, and grow-only vs prune-only variants.  
- Suggestions: run sensitivity analyses for αr, αφ, unit addition rate/frequency, and λ; consider automatic scheduling for these knobs.  
- Suggestions: empirically compare p = 1 vs p = 2 and fan-in vs fan-out regularization on at least one dataset.  
- Suggestions: report wall-clock time and memory overhead versus parametric baselines and quantify AdaRad/unit management cost.  
- Suggestions: extend experiments to convolutional models (e.g., small CIFAR-10) and clarify mini-batch shrinkage/zeroing implementation; release code for reproducibility.  
- Questions for authors: sensitivity of αr and addition schedule; comparisons of Adam/RMSprop with CapNorm; effects of fan-out or p = 1; Convex failure causes and forced minimum-width test; frequency/effect of CapNorm cap; possibility of adapting depth as well as width.
