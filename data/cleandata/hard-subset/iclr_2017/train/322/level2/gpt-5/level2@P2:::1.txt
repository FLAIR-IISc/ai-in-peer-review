Summary and contributions
The paper introduces “nonparametric neural networks,” a framework for jointly optimizing network weights and width within a single training run. The key ideas are:
- A global objective over all widths coupled with either fan-in or fan-out group-regularization (ℓp, focusing on p=2) that provably admits a global minimum at finite dimensionality (Theorem 1), thereby preventing degenerate growth.
- A practical training mechanism that alternates continuous weight optimization with periodic unit addition (initialized to be f-equivalent) and unit removal via group shrinkage, enabling the network to grow and shrink adaptively.
- CapNorm, a capped batch-normalization variant designed to preserve the effect of the group regularizer (avoiding scaling that could nullify shrinkage pressure).
- AdaRad, an optimizer that decomposes the gradient into radial (parallel) and angular (orthogonal) components relative to each unit’s fan-in, normalizing only the angular component to allow new units to learn without being immediately eliminated.
Empirical studies on MNIST, rectangles-images, convex, and a large “poker” dataset show that the approach often finds competitive (sometimes better) accuracies with compact models, while selecting width automatically.

Decision: Reject
Primary reasons:
- Insufficient and uneven empirical validation and comparisons given the paper’s broad claims; key baselines and ablations are missing or confounded.
- Methodological concerns in the experimental protocol (fairness, selection, and reporting) make it difficult to conclude that the approach consistently outperforms strong parametric baselines.

Supporting arguments
- Soundness and theory: Theorem 1 is technically interesting and addresses a potential pathology of unbounded growth by showing existence of a finite-width global minimizer of the training objective under group regularization and mild conditions. However, the theorem does not provide guarantees about the learned width’s proximity to optimal validation performance, nor about the optimization dynamics under the proposed scheme. Proposition 1 (self-similar nonlinearities) is a useful observation linking layer-wise regularization to a single scalar λ, but the method’s practical performance still hinges on selecting λ, ν, αr, and αφ.
- Novelty and design: The combination of (i) continuous width optimization via group sparsity and unit addition/removal; (ii) CapNorm to prevent trivial rescalings; and (iii) AdaRad’s per-fan-in normalization of the orthogonal gradient is original and well-motivated. The fan-in vs. fan-out distinction and the choice of p=2 for dense matrices are sensible.
- Experimental limitations:
  - Comparators: Parametric baselines use vanilla BN while nonparametric uses CapNorm; regularization is set to zero for parametric nets “since ℓ2 is not compatible with BN,” which is not standard practice—weight decay is commonly used with BN. This mismatch confounds whether observed gains stem from nonparametric training vs. different normalization/regularization choices.
  - Model scope: Results are on fully-connected networks, with no convolutional or modern architectures where width selection is most impactful. The approach’s scalability to CNNs is only discussed, not evaluated.
  - Protocol concerns: Selecting a “typical representative” from 10 nonparametric runs by manual inspection of size and accuracy introduces bias. In addition, the nonparametric method involves extensive step-size annealing and even turning λ to zero late in training, while parametric baselines are searched under different protocols. No wall-clock or compute-budget comparison is provided, though the method’s growth-from-scratch procedure is acknowledged to be slow.
  - Missing ablations: The contribution is multi-component (unit addition/removal + ℓ2 fan-in + CapNorm + AdaRad). There are no systematic ablations isolating the impact of each component (e.g., AdaRad vs. RMSprop/Adam under group-lasso; CapNorm vs. alternatives; p=1 vs. p=2; fan-in vs. fan-out).
  - Mixed results and stability: On convex, the method underperforms and tends to converge to small widths, raising concerns about robustness. The poker result is striking (including 0% test error), but the paper provides little diagnostic analysis to rule out data leakage or metric artifacts and does not compare to strong tree/GBDT baselines commonly competitive on tabular data.
- Relation to prior work: The paper lists relevant pruning, growth, and structured sparsity literature, but does not empirically compare to strong width-selection baselines (e.g., group-lasso training with standard optimizers; dynamic network surgery; other grow/prune training). Also, connections to closely related normalization methods (e.g., Weight Normalization) and sphere-constrained/scale-invariant optimizers are not discussed.

Suggestions to improve (helpful, not all decision-critical)
- Strengthen empirical evaluation:
  - Provide matched and fair baselines: parametric models with weight decay, with and without BN; compare CapNorm directly to BN (with/without affine), and to other stabilizers (LayerNorm, WeightNorm) under the same regularization.
  - Add ablations: turn off unit addition (pure pruning), turn off AdaRad (use RMSprop/Adam with appropriate shrinkage), compare p=1 vs. p=2, fan-in vs. fan-out. Show the effect of CapNorm alone with group-lasso.
  - Report wall-clock time, FLOPs, and parameter counts over training, and final test metrics with confidence intervals across multiple seeds, to quantify the cost-benefit of growing from scratch.
  - Evaluate on convolutional architectures (e.g., small CNNs on CIFAR-10/100) where width is crucial. Even a preliminary study would help substantiate applicability beyond MLPs.
- Clarify methodology:
  - Explain test-time behavior of CapNorm (do you use running averages or per-batch stats?), and analyze whether CapNorm’s “cap at 1” induces distribution shift.
  - Justify switching λ to zero late in training, and show whether this materially affects generalization vs. continuing with regularization.
  - Provide a more objective selection protocol for “representative” runs (e.g., median over seeds).
- Theoretical angle:
  - Discuss whether the existence result can be strengthened (e.g., bounds relating λ and achievable width) or whether there are convergence properties of the proposed dynamics under mild assumptions.

Questions for the authors
1. How is CapNorm applied at inference? Do you maintain running estimates of mean/std per unit, and how sensitive are results to batch size at test time?
2. Could you provide ablations quantifying the individual contributions of AdaRad, CapNorm, and the unit addition/removal schedule?
3. Why not include weight decay in BN baselines (with decoupled weight decay if desired), and how do your conclusions change if you do?
4. On the poker dataset, how did you guard against data leakage (e.g., ordering, splits)? Can you share confusion matrices and alternative baselines (e.g., calibrated tree ensembles)?
5. Do you observe the “small-width bias” across other datasets, and can annealing λ or changing ν/αr mitigate underfitting on tasks like convex?
6. Any plans or preliminary results for convolutional layers where group-lasso per channel/filter could be used similarly?
