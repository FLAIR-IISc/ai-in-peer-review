Summary and contributions
The paper tackles the problem of selecting neural network width automatically during a single training run. It introduces “nonparametric neural networks,” which allow the number of hidden units to change during training through a per-unit group penalty on fan-in (or fan-out) weights. The main theoretical contribution is a proof that, under an ℓp per-unit penalty (1 ≤ p < ∞) with λ > 0, the training objective attains a global minimum at a finite network size (Theorem 1), establishing that the formulation avoids degenerate solutions that grow unboundedly. On the algorithmic side, the paper proposes: (i) a practical training scheme that alternates standard gradient-based updates with periodic addition of zero units and removal of pruned units; (ii) CapNorm, a variant of batch normalization designed to remain compatible with the per-unit penalty; and (iii) AdaRad, an optimizer that decomposes gradients into components parallel and orthogonal to a unit’s fan-in, normalizing only the orthogonal component to give new units a chance to learn while preserving the effect of shrinkage. Experiments on MNIST, Rectangles-Images, Convex, and a large Poker dataset show that the approach can converge to a good size and sometimes outperforms parametric networks of comparable size, with mixed results on Convex. The paper also analyzes the training dynamics and the role of the angular/radial steps.

Decision: Weak accept
Key reasons: (1) The paper presents a well-motivated and novel formulation—optimization over variable-width networks—with a nontrivial theoretical guarantee (existence of a finite minimizer) tightly coupled to the algorithmic design. (2) The training scheme (unit addition/removal + CapNorm + AdaRad) is original and practically oriented, and the empirical results, while mixed, are promising and provide insight.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The focus on width selection during training addresses a real pain point in hyperparameter search. The discussion of the drawbacks of black-box methods and pruning-only approaches is fair and accurate, and the proposed framework is well placed in that literature (Wei et al., dynamic growth; pruning with per-unit penalties; hyperparameter optimization).
- Theory: Theorem 1 is meaningful: it shows the objective is well-posed, i.e., the joint optimization over weights and size has a global minimizer at finite width when using per-unit fan-in/out regularization. The assumptions (left/right differentiability, bounded directional derivatives, standard losses) are reasonable for ReLU-like networks. The proposition on self-similar nonlinearities (ReLU) neatly explains why a single λ suffices across layers up to rescaling. While the theorem does not speak to the convergence of the proposed training algorithm, it convincingly supports the modeling choice.
- Algorithmic novelty: The grow-prune scheme is simple and leverages group ℓ2 shrinkage to create exact zeros, enabling discrete size changes without breaking locality (f-equivalence via zero units). AdaRad’s radial/orthogonal decomposition is a thoughtful response to the conflict between normalization and shrinkage for newborn units. CapNorm is a practical modification that avoids neutralizing the regularizer.
- Empirical support: On MNIST and Rectangles-Images, nonparametric networks outperform fixed-size baselines and are close to the best results found by broader searches, sometimes with fewer parameters. The Poker experiment shows scalability and strong absolute performance. The Convex dataset result is negative, but the paper analyzes why (tendency to smaller sizes) and is transparent about limitations. The training dynamics analysis is informative and shows the intended behavior of αr and αφ.
- Rigor and clarity: The methodology is clearly described, with explicit pseudocode and detailed experimental protocols. The theoretical appendix is long but appears careful.

Limitations and concerns
- Scope of experiments: All core experiments are on fully-connected MLPs; no convolutional or sequence models are considered. The negative result on Convex raises concerns about robustness of the automatic size selection.
- Comparisons and ablations: There is no ablation isolating the contributions of AdaRad and CapNorm (e.g., using Adam/RMSprop with CapNorm, or AdaRad with standard BN where applicable). It is difficult to disentangle whether gains come from the nonparametric framework, AdaRad, CapNorm, or the annealing schedule.
- Fairness and tuning: Nonparametric runs rely on hand-crafted schedules (unit addition rate, αr ≈ 150/λ, annealing of αφ) and manual selection of a “typical” run per λ for downstream comparisons. While detailed, this may bias results. Also, parametric baselines using BN are tuned by grid/random search, but no wall-clock or compute comparisons are reported.
- Generalization vs training objective: Theorem 1 pertains to the training objective; there is no guarantee that the learned size minimizes validation/test error. The empirical section partly addresses this with early stopping and annealing, but a more systematic study of generalization vs λ would help.
- Practicalities: CapNorm’s impact versus BN (with/without affine) deserves deeper analysis, and it would be useful to report how often units are removed/added per epoch and the overhead of AdaRad relative to RMSprop/Adam in wall-clock terms.

Suggestions for improvement (helpful, not all decision-critical)
- Provide ablations: (i) AdaRad vs Adam/RMSprop under CapNorm; (ii) CapNorm vs BN (affine/no-affine) under identical regularization; (iii) effect of removing unit addition (pure pruning) vs adding without pruning. Report test accuracy and convergence speed.
- Sensitivity analyses: Show how performance and final size vary with αr, αφ, unit addition rate/frequency, and λ. Consider an automatic schedule for unit addition/removal and for αr rather than fixed heuristics.
- Explore p = 1 vs p = 2 and fan-out vs fan-in empirically, at least on one dataset, to corroborate the design choices.
- Report wall-clock cost and memory against parametric baselines for comparable accuracy; quantify the overhead of AdaRad and unit management.
- Extend to convolutional layers to demonstrate broader applicability. Even a small CIFAR-10 experiment would be valuable.
- Clarify the practical implementation of shrinkage with mini-batches and stochasticity, and whether zeroing thresholds are used.
- Consider releasing code to facilitate reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
1) How sensitive are results to the heuristic αr = 150/λ and to the unit addition rate ν and frequency? Could you provide plots of performance vs these knobs?
2) Did you try Adam or RMSprop with CapNorm (and decoupled weight decay) for the nonparametric training? How do they compare to AdaRad?
3) What happens with a fan-out regularizer or with p = 1 (group lasso vs lasso)? Does sparsity at the weight level vs unit level change outcomes materially?
4) The Convex dataset underperforms. Is this primarily due to converging to too-small models? If you force a minimum width, does performance recover?
5) CapNorm scales only when σ > 1. Could you quantify how often this cap is active and its effect on gradient magnitudes for early vs late units?
6) Can the framework adapt depth as well as width (e.g., by adding/removing layers with zero mappings), and what obstacles do you anticipate?

Overall, this paper offers a compelling and carefully argued framework for variable-width networks, with sound theory and promising practice. Strengthening the empirical analysis and ablations would further solidify its impact.
