This paper introduces nonparametric neural networks, a framework for automatically adapting and choosing the size of a neural network during a single training run. The authors prove the theoretical soundness of the framework and demonstrate its effectiveness in achieving higher accuracy than parametric networks of the same size.

The paper tackles the problem of automatically finding a good network size during a single training cycle, which is a challenging task in deep learning. The authors' approach is well-motivated, and they provide a thorough review of the literature on model selection and neural network pruning.

The paper's main contribution is the introduction of nonparametric neural networks, which can dynamically and automatically shrink and expand the network as needed to select a good network size during a single training run. The authors also propose a novel optimization algorithm, Adaptive Radial-Angular Gradient Descent (AdaRad), which is specifically designed for training nonparametric neural networks.

The experimental results show that nonparametric neural networks can achieve higher accuracy than parametric networks of the same size, and in some cases, achieve a higher accuracy than is possible by black-box methods. The results are promising, especially for the MNIST and rectangles images datasets.

However, there are some concerns and suggestions for improvement:

* The paper could benefit from more intuition and discussion about Theorem 1, which is a key result in the paper. The proof is provided in the appendix, but a more detailed explanation of the theorem's implications and significance would be helpful.
* The comparison between nonparametric neural networks and fixed-size nets with the same final size would be useful to show the efficiency of the adaptive model in terms of performance vs. memory.
* Figure 2 is unclear and mixed; it would be helpful to have a clearer explanation of the results and why nonparametric nets sometimes outperform parametric ones even when the final net is known.
* The authors should provide more information about the choice of the regularizer parameter lambda and whether lambda mainly determines the final net structure.
* The sensitivity to initialization is an important aspect to consider, and the authors should provide more information about how different random starts yield different final network structures and how different they are.
* Combining fan-in and fan-out regularizers is an interesting direction to explore, and the authors should discuss whether the theoretical result still holds in this case.
* The claim that adding zero units changes the regularizer value is not clear, and the authors should provide more information about this aspect.
* The definition of "zero units" is not clear, and the authors should provide more information about what constitutes a removable unit in terms of fan-in and fan-out weights.
* The authors should provide more information about the unit addition schedule and how it affects the performance of the nonparametric neural networks.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a thorough review of the literature and a clear explanation of their approach. However, there are some aspects that could be improved, and the authors should provide more information about the experimental results and the implications of their findings.
