This paper introduces a novel framework for automatically adapting and choosing the size of a neural network during a single training run, termed nonparametric neural networks. The authors propose a method to dynamically add or remove units from the network while training, using a group-sparsity regularization technique to control the growth of the network. The paper also presents a new optimization algorithm, Adaptive Radial-Angular Gradient Descent (AdaRad), which is designed to work effectively with the nonparametric framework.

The paper claims to provide a solution to the problem of model selection in neural networks, which is a challenging task that typically requires an expensive global search and training many networks from scratch. The authors argue that their framework can adapt to different problems and datasets without requiring prior knowledge or manual tuning of hyperparameters.

The key contributions of the paper are the introduction of the nonparametric neural network framework, the AdaRad optimization algorithm, and the theoretical result showing that a finite-unit network can minimize the loss function. The authors also provide experimental results demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach on several benchmark datasets.

Strengths of the paper include its clear and concise writing style, making it easy to follow and understand the proposed framework and algorithms. The paper also provides a thorough analysis of the experimental results, discussing the strengths and weaknesses of the approach.

However, there are several limitations and potential issues with the paper. Firstly, the theoretical result does not guarantee the avoidance of over- or under-fitting, and the initial experiments are limited. While the authors suggest that their approach may be less prone to overfitting, this claim requires further investigation.

Another concern is that the framework does not remove the need for hyperparameter tuning. The authors acknowledge that hyperparameters, such as the add-frequency and decay rates, still implicitly control the final unit count and may be hard to tune. This limitation may reduce the practicality of the approach.

Furthermore, the authors do not compare their method against pruning or compression baselines, making it unclear how competitive their approach is with state-of-the-art size-reduction methods.

The paper also raises concerns about the scalability of the approach. The authors note that single hyperparameters control both model size and training time, which may lead to slower training and scalability problems for large networks.

In conclusion, while the paper presents an interesting and novel framework for adapting neural network size during training, it has several limitations and potential issues that need to be addressed. The experimental results are encouraging but not comprehensive, and further investigation is required to demonstrate the practicality and effectiveness of the approach.
