This paper proposes a novel framework for automatically adapting and choosing the size of a neural network during a single training run, called nonparametric neural networks. The key idea is to dynamically and automatically shrink and expand the network as needed to select a good network size during a single training run.

The paper claims to contribute to the field of neural networks by providing a simple and general framework for automatically adapting and choosing the size of a neural network during a single training run. The authors argue that this approach can improve the performance of the trained nets beyond what is achieved by regular parametric networks of the same size and obtain results competitive with those of an exhaustive random search.

I decide to accept this paper with one key reason being the novelty and potential of the proposed framework. The idea of automatically adapting and choosing the size of a neural network during a single training run is interesting and has the potential to improve the performance of neural networks.

The paper supports its claims through theoretical results and experimental evaluations. The authors prove the theoretical soundness of the framework and provide experimental results on several benchmark datasets. The results show that nonparametric neural networks can outperform parametric networks of the same size and achieve results competitive with those of an exhaustive random search.

However, I have some concerns and suggestions for the authors. Firstly, the paper could benefit from more intuition and discussion on the theoretical result, rather than just presenting the proof. The authors should provide more insight into why the error optimum uses a finite number of parameters and how this result relates to other regularization techniques such as Lasso and Elastic Net.

Secondly, the authors should consider adding an experiment that compares the adaptive (nonparametric) model to fixed-size nets with the same final size to show efficiency (performance vs memory). This would help to demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed framework.

Thirdly, Figure 2 is unclear and mixed, and the authors should consider revising it to make it easier to understand. Additionally, the authors should provide more explanation for why nonparametric nets sometimes outperform parametric ones even when the final net is known.

Fourthly, the authors should provide a clearer and more plain-English discussion of Theorem 1 and its key insight beyond the proof. This would help to make the paper more accessible to a wider audience.

Finally, the authors should consider addressing the following questions: Can the regularizer parameter lambda be predicted from data? Does lambda mainly determine the final net structure? How sensitive is the method to initialization, and do different random starts yield different final network structures? Can the fan-in and fan-out regularizers be combined, and does the theoretical result still hold? Does adding zero units change the regularizer value, and what is the definition of "zero units"?

To improve the paper, the authors could consider the following suggestions:

* Provide more intuition and discussion on the theoretical result, rather than just presenting the proof.
* Add an experiment that compares the adaptive (nonparametric) model to fixed-size nets with the same final size to show efficiency (performance vs memory).
* Revise Figure 2 to make it easier to understand and provide more explanation for why nonparametric nets sometimes outperform parametric ones even when the final net is known.
* Provide a clearer and more plain-English discussion of Theorem 1 and its key insight beyond the proof.
* Address the questions mentioned above, including the predictability of lambda, the sensitivity to initialization, and the combination of fan-in and fan-out regularizers.

I expect the authors to address these comments and suggestions to further improve the paper.
