- Proposes nonparametric neural networks that automatically determine optimal network size during training, avoiding costly global searches.  
- Introduces a non-probabilistic framework that optimizes over all network sizes using a sparse regularizer to zero out irrelevant weights.  
- Key method: randomly add zero units and use a sparse regularizer to eliminate useless units, performing a random search over discrete architectures.  
- Provides theoretical proof that nonparametric networks reach a global training-error minimum at some finite dimensionality with fan-in or fan-out regularizers.  
- Proposes a novel optimizer, Adaptive Radial-Angular Gradient Descent (AdaRad), with separate radial and angular step sizes for more flexible training control.  
- Experimental results show nonparametric networks outperform parametric counterparts of the same size on two of three datasets.  
- Demonstrates scalability by applying the approach to a large dataset.  
- Raises concern about additional computational complexity introduced by AdaRad relative to standard optimizers.  
- Requests quantification of extra operations and running-time experiments to validate computational claims.  
- Notes unexplained poor performance on the convex dataset where nonparametric models become very small and underperform.  
- Overall assessment: novel, well-motivated approach with solid theory and promising empirical results; recommended for acceptance.  
- Supporting points: tackles an important deep learning problem, clear presentation, and demonstrated scalability.  
- Suggested improvements: provide more detail on AdaRad’s complexity and comparisons to other optimizers.  
- Suggested investigations: explain the convex-dataset failure mode and explore applications in image classification and NLP.  
- Questions for authors: detail AdaRad’s computational complexity, plans to address convex-dataset performance, and intentions to extend to other domains.
