This paper proposes a novel approach to automatically adapting and choosing the size of a neural network during a single training run, which the authors term "nonparametric neural networks." The method dynamically adds and removes units from the network while training, using a group-sparsity regularizer to control the growth of the network. The authors provide a theoretical result showing that a finite-unit network can minimize the loss, and they demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on several benchmark datasets.

The paper is clearly written and conceptually elegant, and the authors' lifelong-learning goals are well-motivated. However, the results presented in the paper are limited, and several questions remain unanswered. For example, the authors do not compare their method against pruning or compression baselines, so it is unclear how competitive their approach is with existing size-reduction methods. Additionally, the authors' method does not remove the need for hyperparameter tuning, as hyperparameters such as the add-frequency and decay rates still implicitly control the final unit count and may be difficult to tune.

The authors' experiments are helpful and encouraging, but they are not comprehensive. Larger-scale tests are needed to demonstrate the practicality of the approach, particularly with respect to scalability. The authors' method uses a single hyperparameter to control both model size and training time, which may result in much slower training times and scalability problems for large networks.

Despite these limitations, the paper is a valuable contribution to the field, and the authors' approach has the potential to produce smaller networks that reduce test-time runtime, power, and memory. The authors' theoretical result is also an important contribution, as it provides a foundation for understanding the behavior of their method.

To further improve the paper, the authors could provide more comprehensive experiments, including comparisons against pruning and compression baselines. They could also explore the use of multiple hyperparameters to control model size and training time separately, which may improve the scalability of their approach.
