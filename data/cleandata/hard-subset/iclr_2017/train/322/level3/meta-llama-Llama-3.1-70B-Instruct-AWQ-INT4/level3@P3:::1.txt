This paper proposes a novel approach to automatically determining the optimal size of a neural network during training, eliminating the need for expensive global searches and training multiple networks from scratch. The authors introduce nonparametric neural networks, a non-probabilistic framework that optimizes over all possible network sizes using a sparse regularizer to null out irrelevant weights.

The key idea is to randomly add zero units and apply a sparse regularizer to eliminate useless units, effectively performing a random search over the discrete architecture space. This approach is well-motivated, as it tackles an important problem in deep learning and presents interesting empirical results. The paper also provides a theoretical justification for the proposed framework, proving that nonparametric neural networks achieve a global training error minimum at some finite dimensionality when using a fan-in or fan-out regularizer.

The authors also propose a novel optimization algorithm, Adaptive Radial-Angular Gradient Descent (AdaRad), which is specifically designed for training nonparametric networks. AdaRad has two step sizes, one for the radial and one for the angular shift, allowing for more flexible control over the training process.

The experimental results are promising, with nonparametric networks outperforming parametric networks of the same size on two out of three datasets. The authors also demonstrate the scalability of their approach by applying it to a large dataset.

However, there are some concerns regarding the additional computational complexity of the proposed approach. The authors should quantify the extra operations required by AdaRad compared to parametric networks and provide running-time experiments to support their claims.

One empirical note that requires further explanation is the poor performance of nonparametric networks on the convex dataset, where they yield very small networks and underperform parametric networks.

Overall, this paper presents a novel and interesting approach to automatically determining the optimal size of a neural network during training. While there are some concerns regarding computational complexity and empirical performance, the authors provide a solid theoretical foundation and promising experimental results.
