- Introduces "nonparametric neural networks" that dynamically adjust network size during training using group sparsity, pruning, and a new optimizer (AdaRad).
- Provides a theoretical guarantee that proper regularization yields a global training error minimum with finite network dimensionality.
- Empirically achieves comparable or better performance than parametric networks on small benchmarks while using fewer units.
- Presents a vision of adaptive, lifelong learning networks aimed at reducing hyperparameter tuning and resource use.
- Decision: Reject due to insufficient experimental validation and practical applicability concerns.
- Experiments are limited to small datasets (MNIST, rectangles, convex) and do not test scalability to large-scale tasks or deeper/convolutional architectures.
- Lacks direct comparisons to state-of-the-art pruning methods (e.g., Molchanov et al. 2017; Figurnov et al. 2016), leaving unclear relative benefits.
- Introduces new hyperparameters (radial/angular step sizes, unit addition rate) that undermine claims of reduced tuning burden.
- Dynamic unit adjustment slows training, raising scalability and practicality concerns.
- Suggest validating on larger, more complex datasets (ImageNet, CIFAR-100) and on convolutional or transformer architectures.
- Suggest including thorough comparisons to existing pruning and dynamic-architecture baselines for performance and efficiency.
- Suggest providing automated strategies or detailed guidance for tuning the new hyperparameters.
- Suggest performing runtime analysis to quantify trade-offs between reduced model size and increased training time.
- Asks authors to compare method to SOTA pruning in terms of performance, runtime, and memory efficiency.
- Asks whether the approach scales to deeper/convolutional architectures and what challenges are expected.
- Asks if hyperparameters can be optimized automatically and how sensitive results are to their values.
- Asks what steps can reduce the slower training caused by dynamic unit adjustment.
- Concludes the theoretical/methodological contribution is strong but needs stronger experimental validation and practical solutions to be acceptable.
