- Paper proposes nonparametric neural networks that dynamically adapt network size during training, removing need to preselect network size.
- The authors prove (Theorem 1) that with fan-in or fan-out regularization the error minimum uses a finite number of parameters, preventing unbounded growth.
- Introduces a new optimizer, Adaptive Radial-Angular Gradient Descent (AdaRad), and a modified normalization layer, CapNorm.
- Decision: Reject.
- Primary reason for rejection: insufficient discussion of theoretical results and unclear, insufficiently rigorous experimental validation.
- Theorem 1 is central but lacks intuition, practical implications, and deeper connections to sparse regularizers like Lasso or Elastic Net.
- Empirical results are mixed: nonparametric networks outperform in some tasks but underperform in others (e.g., convex dataset), with no adequate explanation.
- Claim that nonparametric networks outperform parametric networks of the same size is not convincingly validated; missing direct comparison to fixed-size networks with the same final structure.
- Definition and role of “zero units” and their effect on the regularizer value are unclear.
- Paper fails to address sensitivity of final network structure to initialization and the effects of combining fan-in and fan-out regularizers.
- Suggestion: expand theoretical discussion, provide intuition and comparisons to existing sparse regularization techniques.
- Suggestion: add experiments comparing dynamic models to fixed-size networks with identical final architectures.
- Suggestion: clarify “zero units,” initialization sensitivity, and interactions between fan-in and fan-out regularizers.
- Suggestion: investigate whether the regularization parameter λ can be predicted or tuned based on data properties.
- Suggestion: analyze when and why nonparametric networks outperform or underperform parametric counterparts to guide practitioners.
- Questions for authors include: why nonparametric sometimes outperforms parametric with known final structure; sensitivity to initialization; precise definition of zero units; effects of combining fan-in and fan-out; and whether λ can be dynamically adjusted or predicted.
