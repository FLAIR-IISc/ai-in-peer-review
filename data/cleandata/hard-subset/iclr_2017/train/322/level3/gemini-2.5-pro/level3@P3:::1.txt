### Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces a novel and interesting framework called "nonparametric neural networks" to address the challenging problem of automatically determining the optimal network size. Instead of relying on expensive black-box optimization over different architectures, the proposed method dynamically adapts the network size within a single training run. The core idea is to periodically add new, randomly initialized units (specifically, units with zero fan-out) and simultaneously apply a group-sparse regularizer (L2 norm on the fan-in of each neuron) to prune away units that do not prove useful. This process is framed as an optimization problem over a space of networks of all possible sizes, and the authors provide a theoretical justification (Theorem 1) showing that a global minimum at a finite network size is guaranteed under this regularization. To train these networks, the paper also proposes a new optimizer, Adaptive Radial-Angular Gradient Descent (AdaRad), which is specifically designed to handle the interaction between gradient updates and the group-sparse regularizer. The empirical results on the MNIST and rectangles-images datasets are promising, demonstrating that the method can discover compact and high-performing architectures that are competitive with, and sometimes superior to, those found by an extensive random search.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The paper tackles the highly significant problem of automated model selection for neural networks with a novel and creative approach. The concept of growing and pruning a network in a single training run as a form of architecture search is compelling and offers a promising alternative to computationally intensive hyperparameter optimization.
2.  The proposed method is well-motivated and supported by both theoretical analysis and encouraging empirical results on standard benchmarks, suggesting its potential for practical impact.

### Supporting Arguments

The primary strength of this work lies in its elegant formulation for learning network architecture. By starting with a small network and iteratively adding candidate units while a group-sparsity regularizer eliminates redundant ones, the method effectively performs a stochastic search over the discrete space of network widths. This approach is more efficient than training many separate networks from scratch. The empirical validation is a key strength; on two of the three datasets, the nonparametric networks achieve performance competitive with or better than parametric networks found via an exhaustive random search, often with a more compact final architecture. The theoretical proof that the `p-norm fan-in/fan-out regularizer guarantees a minimum at a finite network size provides a solid foundation for the framework.

Furthermore, the components developed to support this framework, such as the self-similar activation requirement, the CapNorm layer, and the AdaRad optimizer, are thoughtfully designed. AdaRad, in particular, is an interesting contribution. By decomposing the gradient into radial (length-altering) and angular (direction-altering) components and normalizing only the angular part, it cleverly navigates the tension between allowing new units to learn (requiring large updates) and the regularizer's pressure to shrink them to zero.

However, the paper has a few weaknesses that temper its impact. The most significant concern is the computational complexity of the AdaRad optimizer. The algorithm requires decomposing each fan-in gradient into parallel and orthogonal components and then performing updates via rotation in radial-angular coordinates. These steps appear to add substantial computational overhead compared to standard optimizers like Adam or RMSprop. The authors acknowledge this but do not adequately quantify the extra cost. Without empirical runtime comparisons or a detailed analysis of the additional floating-point operations, it is difficult to assess the practical trade-offs of the proposed method versus standard architecture search.

Additionally, the empirical results on the 'convex' dataset are a point of concern. The method converges to very small networks that significantly underperform their parametric counterparts. The paper notes this discrepancy but offers no explanation. This result suggests a potential failure mode where the regularization is too aggressive or the growth strategy is insufficient for certain problem types, which warrants further investigation.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

The following points are suggestions aimed at strengthening the paper and are not central to the acceptance decision.

*   **Computational Cost Analysis:** The paper would be significantly improved by a more thorough analysis of AdaRad's computational cost. I strongly recommend adding a table with wall-clock training time comparisons against the parametric baselines on the same hardware. A theoretical analysis comparing the number of floating-point operations per update for AdaRad versus RMSprop/Adam would also be highly valuable.
*   **Analysis of the Convex Dataset:** Providing a hypothesis or further analysis for the poor performance on the convex dataset would be very helpful. Is it possible that for this simpler problem, the regularization immediately prunes new units before they can learn useful features? Investigating the "lifetime" of added units on this dataset (similar to Figure 3D) might provide insight.
*   **Ablation Studies:** The contribution of CapNorm is interesting. An ablation study comparing it to standard batch normalization (and explaining why the latter fails) or other potential normalization schemes would help justify its design. Similarly, the unit addition strategy (one unit per layer per epoch) is fixed. Exploring or discussing more adaptive strategies (e.g., adding units when validation performance stalls) could be a valuable addition.

### Questions for the Authors

To help clarify my understanding, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:

1.  Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational overhead of AdaRad? Specifically, what is the cost of the vector decomposition and rotation operations per fan-in vector update compared to a standard Adam update?
2.  Can you share the approximate wall-clock training times for the nonparametric networks versus the parametric baselines in Section 4.1? This would provide a clearer picture of the practical efficiency of your approach.
3.  Regarding the convex dataset, do you have a hypothesis for why the method converges to small, suboptimal networks? Does this reveal a limitation where the balance between growth (unit addition) and pruning (regularization) is sensitive to the dataset's characteristics?
