### **Review of "NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS"**

#### **1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces a novel framework called "nonparametric neural networks" to automate the selection of network width during a single training process, thereby avoiding an expensive external search over different architectures. The core idea is to dynamically adjust the number of hidden units by periodically adding new units and simultaneously removing redundant ones. Unit removal is achieved by applying a group-sparsity regularizer (specifically, an L2 penalty on the fan-in or fan-out of each unit), which encourages the weights of unimportant units to become exactly zero. The authors provide a theoretical justification for this approach, proving that the regularized training objective is guaranteed to have a global minimum at a finite network size. To facilitate this dynamic training process, the paper also proposes a novel optimization algorithm, AdaRad, designed to separate the learning of a unit's direction from the shrinkage of its magnitude. The potential benefit of this method is the ability to produce smaller, more efficient networks in terms of test-time computation, memory, and power, without requiring a priori knowledge of the optimal architecture.

#### **2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **The central claim of automating model selection is undermined by the introduction of new, sensitive hyperparameters that implicitly control the final network size.** The method effectively replaces the discrete search for layer widths with a continuous, and likely equally difficult, search for parameters like regularization strength, unit addition frequency, and optimizer step sizes.
2.  **The experimental evaluation is insufficient to demonstrate the method's practical value, as it fails to compare against any established network pruning or compression baselines.** Without this crucial context, it is impossible to determine if this complex dynamic approach offers any tangible benefit over simpler, more conventional methods for reducing network size.

#### **3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The paper presents an elegant and theoretically grounded idea, but its practical utility is not convincingly demonstrated due to several weaknesses.

First, the claim of "automatically finding a good network size" is overstated. The final architecture is heavily dependent on the regularization hyperparameter `λ`, the unit addition rate `ν`, the addition frequency `νfreq`, and the radial step size `αr`. The paper itself shows that different values of `λ` result in networks of vastly different sizes and performance (Figure 2). Tuning these new, continuous hyperparameters seems no easier than tuning the discrete number of units directly. Furthermore, the explicit coupling of the radial step size to the regularization (`αr = 150λ`) intertwines control over the final model size with the training dynamics (i.e., how long a new unit survives). This is a risky design choice that could lead to prohibitively slow training for certain hyperparameter regimes and complicates tuning.

Second, the paper's experiments lack critical baselines from the well-established field of network pruning and compression. The primary comparison is made against parametric networks of the *same final size*. While this shows the resulting architecture is effective, it does not answer the more important question: is this dynamic grow-and-prune process better than simply training an over-parameterized network and pruning it? Many existing methods apply similar group-sparsity regularizers to a fixed, large network to remove units. A compelling evaluation would require comparing against such techniques to demonstrate that the proposed dynamic method finds better architectures or is more efficient. The absence of these comparisons makes the results difficult to interpret in the context of prior work on model size reduction.

Finally, the empirical validation is performed on relatively small-scale datasets (MNIST, etc.). While the poker dataset is large in the number of samples, it has very few features and is not a standard benchmark for testing the architectural limits of deep neural networks. To prove the method's practicality and scalability, experiments on more challenging and widely-used benchmarks like CIFAR-10/100 are necessary. The poor performance on the "convex" dataset also highlights a potential failure mode where the method converges to a sub-optimally small network, a risk that is not fully addressed.

#### **4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

Despite the recommendation, the paper has several strengths. It is exceptionally well-written, the core concept is creative, and the connection to lifelong learning is motivating. The theoretical result, while limited to the training objective, provides a solid foundation for the approach. The following suggestions are intended to help strengthen the work for a future submission:

*   **Strengthen Experimental Comparisons:** The most critical addition would be a direct comparison to established pruning methods. For example, train a large network and prune it using a group-lasso regularizer, comparing the final accuracy-size trade-off against your dynamic method.
*   **Ablation Studies:** A detailed analysis of the new hyperparameters (`λ`, `ν`, `αr`) would be invaluable. Showing their sensitivity and providing guidance on how to set them would substantially improve the paper's practical utility. It would be particularly interesting to see an experiment decoupling `αr` from `λ`.
*   **Broader Benchmarking:** Evaluating the method on standard vision datasets like CIFAR-10 would provide a much stronger signal of its effectiveness and scalability in a more competitive setting.
*   **Discuss Theoretical Limitations:** It would be beneficial to explicitly discuss the gap between the theoretical guarantee (minimizing regularized training loss) and the practical goal (good generalization). The theory does not preclude under- or over-fitting, and acknowledging this would frame the contribution more accurately.

#### **5. Questions for the Authors**

*   Could you clarify the rationale for omitting comparisons to network pruning techniques? How do you expect your method to perform against an alternative approach of starting with an oversized network and pruning units using a similar group-sparsity regularizer?
*   The new hyperparameters `λ`, `ν`, and `αr` appear to replace the original hyperparameter of network width. Could you comment on the difficulty of tuning these new parameters compared to a standard random or grid search over widths?
*   The choice to couple the radial step size with the regularization (`αr = 150λ`) means `λ` controls both the final size and how long a new unit has to "prove its worth." Did you experiment with decoupling these to see if it allows for more flexible control over the training dynamics?
*   In Section 4.2, it is noted that the nonparametric network did not achieve near-zero training error but generalized better than a parametric one that did. Could this be primarily an effect of the strong group-L2 regularization? Would a standard parametric network trained with a similarly tuned group-L2 regularizer exhibit the same generalization benefit?
