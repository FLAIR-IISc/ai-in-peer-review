This paper introduces "nonparametric neural networks," a framework designed to automatically determine an appropriate network size during a single training run. The core idea is to dynamically add new, randomly initialized units while simultaneously removing redundant ones using a group-sparsity regularizer (an l2 penalty on the fan-in weights of each unit). The authors provide a theoretical proof showing that under this regularization, a global minimum of the training loss can be achieved by a network with a finite number of units. The method is supported by a novel optimization algorithm, AdaRad, designed to handle the specific dynamics of this growing and shrinking process.

**Decision:** Reject

My primary reasons for this decision are: 1) The empirical evaluation is insufficient to demonstrate the method's practical value due to the complete absence of comparisons to established network pruning and compression techniques. 2) The method replaces the single hyperparameter of network size with a new set of sensitive hyperparameters that appear difficult to tune and implicitly control the final architecture.

**Supporting Arguments:**
The central claim of this work is that it provides a way to find a "good network size." However, this claim is not adequately supported without benchmarking against the extensive literature on network pruning and compression. State-of-the-art pruning methods are highly effective at producing small, efficient networks, and it is unclear whether this more complex dynamic approach offers any tangible benefit over a standard prune-then-finetune pipeline. Without these crucial baselines, the reported results are difficult to interpret and the method’s competitiveness remains speculative.

Furthermore, the paper's goal to automate model selection is undermined by the introduction of new, influential hyperparameters. The unit addition frequency, regularization strength (λ), and the radial step size (αr) in the custom optimizer now collectively determine the final network size and performance. The interplay between these parameters seems complex; for instance, αr and λ jointly control how long a new unit has to "prove its worth" before being eliminated. This risks trading one difficult hyperparameter search problem for another, potentially more complicated one. The coupling of model size and training time via these hyperparameters also raises concerns about the method's scalability and training efficiency.

**Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper:**
While the paper is not ready for acceptance, the core concept is elegant and well-presented. To strengthen a future submission, I suggest the following:
*   **Include Pruning Baselines:** The most critical addition would be a thorough comparison against several well-known pruning methods (e.g., iterative magnitude pruning, structured pruning techniques).
*   **Hyperparameter Analysis:** An ablation study on the key new hyperparameters (λ, αr, addition frequency) would be invaluable for understanding their sensitivity and providing practitioners with guidance on how to set them.
*   **Broader Empirical Evaluation:** Demonstrating the method's effectiveness on larger and more standard benchmarks, such as CIFAR-10/100, would significantly bolster claims of its practicality.
*   **Training Cost Analysis:** A comparison of the total wall-clock time or FLOPs required against fixed-architecture training and standard pruning methods would clarify the computational trade-offs involved.

**Questions for the Authors:**
*   Could you provide your reasoning for omitting comparisons to network pruning baselines and speculate on how your method might compare in terms of final accuracy and model size?
*   What is the sensitivity of the final network architecture to the initial random seed and the schedule of adding units?
*   Is the custom AdaRad optimizer essential? How does the framework perform if a standard optimizer like Adam is used instead, perhaps with modifications to handle the dynamic architecture?
