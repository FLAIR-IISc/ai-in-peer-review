- Proposes "nonparametric neural networks" that adapt layer widths during a single training run.  
- Adds "zero units" periodically and uses group-sparse (l2 fan-in) regularization to prune redundant units.  
- Provides a theoretical guarantee (Theorem 1) that training reaches a global minimum at a finite network size.  
- Introduces AdaRad optimizer that decomposes gradients into radial and angular components to protect new units.  
- Introduces CapNorm, a modified normalization layer, to work with growth and pruning.  
- Empirical results on MNIST and rectangles images show compact, competitive architectures found in one run.  
- Recommendation: Accept, contingent on addressing concerns about optimizer overhead and inconsistent empirical results.  
- Strength: Cohesive framework that integrates architecture search into training, avoiding many full training runs.  
- Strength: Theoretical soundness and a novel optimizer addressing interplay between regularization and learning.  
- Weakness: No quantitative analysis of AdaRad’s computational overhead or wall-clock time comparisons to standard optimizers.  
- Weakness: Inconsistent empirical performance—notably poor results on the convex dataset with no explanation.  
- Suggestion: Consider adaptive unit-addition strategies (e.g., add more when loss plateaus or targeted layer growth).  
- Suggestion: Explore smarter unit initialization (e.g., copy-and-perturb existing units like Net2Net).  
- Questions for authors: quantify AdaRad slowdown vs Adam, hypothesize why convex dataset fails, and analyze sensitivity of αr = 150λ coupling.
