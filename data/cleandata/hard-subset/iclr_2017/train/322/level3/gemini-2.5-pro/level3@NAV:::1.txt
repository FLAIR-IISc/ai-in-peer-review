**Review of "NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS"**

**1. Summary of Contributions**
This paper addresses the important and challenging problem of automatically determining neural network model size, aiming to avoid expensive, conventional hyperparameter search. The authors introduce "nonparametric neural networks," a novel framework where the network size is learned dynamically within a single training run. The core idea is to periodically add new "zero units" and employ a group-sparse fan-in regularizer to prune away units that do not prove useful. This approach effectively performs a search over network architectures, guided by sparse regularization. The paper supports this framework with a theoretical proof of soundness and presents promising empirical results on several benchmark datasets, demonstrating that the method can find compact, high-performing models.

**2. Decision: Reject**
I recommend rejecting this paper in its current form, though I believe the core idea is valuable and encourage a resubmission. My decision is based on two primary concerns: (1) The new training algorithm, AdaRad, introduces significant and unquantified computational complexity, undermining the paper's core motivation of efficiency. (2) The method's empirical performance is inconsistent, with a notable failure case that is not adequately explained.

**3. Supporting Arguments**
The primary motivation for this work is to offer a more efficient alternative to training multiple networks. However, the proposed AdaRad optimizer involves complex operations for each fan-in at every step, including decomposing gradients into parallel and orthogonal components and performing updates in radial-angular coordinates. The authors claim this scales well but fail to provide crucial wall-clock time experiments comparing their method against standard parametric networks trained with optimizers like Adam or RMSprop. Without this, it is impossible to assess if the proposed single, complex training run is truly more efficient than multiple, simpler ones.

Furthermore, the empirical results raise concerns about the method's robustness. On the convex dataset, the framework converges to very small networks that significantly underperform standard parametric baselines. This failure mode is critical but is mentioned only in passing. An analysis of why the method fails on this particular dataset is necessary to understand the limitations of the approach and build confidence in its general applicability.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**
The concept of dynamically growing and shrinking a network is compelling, and the theoretical justification is a strong point of this paper. To improve the work, I strongly suggest quantifying the computational overhead of AdaRad with direct runtime comparisons. The analysis would also benefit greatly from an investigation into the failure on the convex dataset—is this a limitation of the regularization scheme or the optimizer? Exploring alternative unit initialization strategies or a more adaptive schedule for adding units could also strengthen the framework.

**5. Questions for Authors**
*   Could you provide wall-clock training time experiments comparing a nonparametric network trained with AdaRad to a parametric network of the same final size trained with Adam on identical hardware?
*   What is your hypothesis for why the method converges to small, poorly-performing networks on the convex dataset? Does this suggest a fundamental weakness in the interaction between the regularizer and the dataset's properties?
*   Could you provide more intuition on why the radial-angular decomposition in AdaRad is necessary, as opposed to applying a standard adaptive optimizer like Adam and scaling the regularization term for new units?
