### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces a novel framework called "nonparametric neural networks" to address the challenging problem of automatically determining the optimal width of network layers. Instead of relying on expensive black-box optimization techniques that require training numerous networks, the proposed method dynamically adapts the network size within a single training run. The core idea is to periodically add new "zero units" that do not initially alter the network's function, while simultaneously applying a group-sparse (l2 fan-in) regularizer to eliminate redundant units. The paper provides a theoretical justification for this framework, proving that under this regularization scheme, the training objective achieves a global minimum at a finite network size. To implement this, the authors develop a custom optimization algorithm, Adaptive Radial-Angular Gradient Descent (AdaRad), and a modified normalization layer, CapNorm, which are designed to work in synergy with the growth and pruning mechanism. The empirical results on the MNIST and rectangles images datasets are promising, showing that the method can find compact, high-performing architectures that are competitive with or superior to those found by an exhaustive search.

### **Decision: Accept**

I recommend accepting this paper. The primary reasons are:
1.  It proposes an elegant and well-motivated solution to the important and practical problem of neural architecture search, moving beyond expensive, decoupled search procedures.
2.  The work is technically sound, supported by a novel optimization algorithm (AdaRad) and a theoretical proof of convergence to a finite-sized model, which lends significant credibility to the framework.

However, this recommendation is contingent on the authors addressing significant concerns regarding the computational overhead of the proposed optimizer and the method's inconsistent empirical performance.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The main strength of this paper lies in its formulation of a cohesive framework that integrates architecture search directly into the training process. The concept of growing the network by adding zero-impact units and then relying on a group-sparsity regularizer to prune unnecessary ones is intuitive and powerful. This approach elegantly sidesteps the primary drawback of methods like random search or Bayesian optimization: the need for many independent, full training runs.

The theoretical contribution in Theorem 1 is significant. It provides a formal guarantee that the network will not grow indefinitely, which is a critical soundness check for any dynamic growth method. The development of the AdaRad optimizer is another key strength. The authors correctly identify that standard optimizers would interfere with the delicate balance between learning useful features and shrinking useless units. By decomposing the gradient into radial and angular components and only normalizing the angular (direction-changing) part, AdaRad allows new units a chance to learn without being prematurely eliminated by the regularizer. This is a clever and non-trivial insight.

The experiments on MNIST and the rectangles images dataset successfully demonstrate the potential of this framework. On these tasks, the nonparametric networks converge to reasonably sized models and, in several cases, outperform both parametric networks of the same size and the best models found by a random search. This supports the paper's central claim that dynamically shaping the architecture during training can lead to better solutions.

Despite these strengths, there are two major weaknesses. First, the paper lacks a quantitative analysis of the computational cost of AdaRad. The algorithm requires decomposing each unit's fan-in gradient into parallel and orthogonal components and performing updates in radial-angular coordinates. These operations appear to add non-trivial overhead compared to standard optimizers like Adam or RMSprop. Without empirical evidence (e.g., wall-clock time comparisons), the claim of being more efficient than black-box search is not fully substantiated.

Second, the empirical results are inconsistent. The method performs poorly on the convex dataset, converging to a small network that is substantially outperformed by fixed-architecture parametric networks. The paper notes this failure but offers no analysis or hypothesis for it. This raises questions about the robustness and generalizability of the approach. A successful architecture search method should be reliable across different tasks, and this result highlights a significant limitation that needs to be understood.

### **Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not central to my decision.
-   The strategy for adding units is fixed (one unit per layer per epoch). Have you considered an adaptive strategy, where units are added more aggressively when the training loss plateaus, or added to specific layers that might benefit most?
-   The initialization of new units is random. It might be interesting to explore more informed initialization schemes, such as copying the weights of an existing, successful unit and adding a small amount of noise, similar to the ideas in Net2Net.
-   The analysis of the training dynamics in Section 4.2 is insightful. The discussion of fan-in/fan-out lengths and the role of CapNorm is particularly helpful for understanding the mechanics of the method.

I would appreciate it if the authors could address the following questions in their rebuttal:
1.  Could you please quantify the computational overhead of AdaRad? Specifically, how much slower is a training iteration using AdaRad compared to a standard optimizer like Adam for the networks used in your experiments? A comparison of wall-clock training times would be highly valuable.
2.  What is your hypothesis for the poor performance on the convex dataset? Does this task's loss landscape or data structure pose a specific challenge to the proposed growth-and-pruning mechanism, causing it to prematurely converge to a small, suboptimal model?
3.  The radial step size `αr` is coupled to the regularization parameter `λ` (`αr = 150λ`). This seems to imply that a stronger regularization (larger `λ`) also leads to faster shrinkage from the optimizer's radial step. Could you elaborate on the sensitivity of the final model's performance and size to this heuristic?
