### Summary of Claims/Contributions

This paper introduces a novel and elegant framework called "nonparametric neural networks" to address the challenging problem of automatically determining a suitable network size. Instead of performing an expensive search over different architectures, the proposed method dynamically adapts the network size within a single training run. The core idea is to continuously add new, randomly initialized units to the network while simultaneously removing redundant units via a group-sparsity (`l2`) fan-in regularizer.

The key contributions are threefold:
1.  A theoretical result (Theorem 1) which proves that, under this framework, the training objective is guaranteed to have a global minimum at a finite network size, providing a sound foundation for the approach.
2.  A novel optimization algorithm, Adaptive Radial-Angular Gradient Descent (AdaRad), specifically designed to handle the challenges of training a dynamically growing and shrinking network, separating the learning of a unit's direction (angular) from its magnitude (radial).
3.  Empirical results on several benchmark datasets demonstrating that this method can find compact networks that are competitive with, and sometimes superior to, architectures found via extensive random search, potentially reducing the need for manual architecture tuning.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Weak Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The paper presents a conceptually novel and well-motivated framework for dynamic network architecture adaptation, supported by a solid theoretical justification. This is a valuable contribution towards more automated machine learning.
2.  However, the claim of "automatically" determining network size is undermined by the introduction of a new set of sensitive hyperparameters (e.g., regularization strength, unit addition rate) that themselves require careful tuning and directly control the final architecture and training dynamics. The practical utility over standard hyperparameter search is therefore not yet fully established.

### Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The primary strength of this work is its principled approach to a long-standing problem. The formulation of network size selection as an optimization problem over an infinite-dimensional space and the proof that a finite solution exists (Theorem 1) are significant. This provides a formal grounding that is often absent in more heuristic methods for network growth or pruning. The proposed AdaRad optimizer is also a clever piece of engineering, thoughtfully designed to decouple the learning of feature direction from the magnitude-shrinking effect of the regularizer, which is critical for giving new units a chance to become useful.

Despite these strengths, the practical implementation of the framework raises concerns. The method effectively trades one form of hyperparameter tuning (explicitly setting layer widths) for another (tuning `λ`, the unit addition rate `ν`, and the radial step size `αr`). The experiments show that the final network size is highly dependent on `λ`. This suggests that a user must still perform a search over `λ` to find a network of the desired size or performance, which partially defeats the purpose of avoiding a global search. The interplay between these new hyperparameters appears complex, and it is not clear if tuning them is any easier than tuning the architecture directly.

Furthermore, the experimental evaluation, while encouraging, is not comprehensive enough to fully support the paper's claims of practicality. A crucial missing component is a comparison against modern network pruning and compression baselines. While the authors argue that pruning methods require a good, large network to start with, many contemporary techniques perform pruning *during* training, which is a more direct competitor to the proposed method. Without such comparisons, it is difficult to assess whether the resulting networks offer a superior trade-off between size and accuracy compared to the state-of-the-art. Finally, the scalability of adding units every epoch could be a bottleneck for very large datasets and models, potentially leading to significantly longer training times compared to training a fixed-size network. The single experiment on the `poker` dataset is a good start, but more evidence is needed on complex, large-scale problems to demonstrate wider applicability.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are intended as constructive suggestions and are not central to my decision.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity Analysis:** The paper would be significantly strengthened by an ablation study showing how the final network size, performance, and training time are affected by the key hyperparameters (`λ`, `ν`, `αr`). A visualization of this relationship would provide valuable insight into the method's behavior and tuning difficulty.
*   **Training Efficiency:** It would be very useful to report the wall-clock training time for the nonparametric method versus the total time required for the random search baseline (i.e., time per run multiplied by the number of runs). This would provide a clearer picture of the practical efficiency gains.
*   **Comparison with Pruning:** I strongly encourage the authors to compare their method against at least one strong pruning baseline, for example, starting with an oversized network and applying gradual magnitude-based pruning during training. This would provide a much-needed reference point for the quality of the discovered architectures.

### Questions for the Authors

*   Could you provide more intuition on the process for tuning the new hyperparameters (`λ`, `ν`, `αr`, `αφ`)? In your experience, is there a principled way to set them, or does it require extensive trial and error similar to a grid search for network width?
*   Your theory guarantees a minimum for the training loss. Do you have any insights, theoretical or empirical, into the generalization properties of the final networks? For example, does the dynamic add/remove process itself act as a regularizer that helps prevent overfitting beyond the explicit `l2` penalty?
*   In Section 4.2, you observe that the nonparametric network did not achieve near-zero training error but generalized better than parametric networks that did. Is this a consistent property of the method, or could it be an artifact of the specific hyperparameter settings that balanced the training loss against the regularization term?
