### **Summary**

This paper introduces a compelling framework called "nonparametric neural networks" to automatically determine an appropriate network size during a single training run. Instead of relying on expensive hyperparameter searches, the proposed method dynamically adds new units to increase model capacity while simultaneously using a group-sparsity-inducing regularizer to prune redundant ones. The core contributions are: 1) a novel `p fan-in or fan-out regularization scheme, 2) a key theoretical result (Theorem 1) proving that this regularization guarantees the existence of a global training error minimum at a finite network size, and 3) a custom optimization algorithm, AdaRad, designed to work with this dynamic architecture. The experimental results on several datasets show that this approach can find compact, high-performing networks, sometimes outperforming both fixed-size networks of the same final architecture and models found via random search.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept (7)**

This is a strong paper with a novel and well-motivated approach to a fundamental problem in deep learning: architecture selection. My decision to accept is based on two key reasons:

1.  **Novelty and Practicality of the Approach:** The concept of allowing a network to grow and shrink its capacity in response to the data during a single training pass is an elegant and practical alternative to computationally expensive black-box optimization methods. This "nonparametric" view is a valuable contribution to the field.
2.  **Sound Theoretical Foundation:** The paper is not merely a collection of heuristics. It provides a solid theoretical justification for the method's stability. Theorem 1 is a significant result, as it guarantees that the network will not grow indefinitely, addressing a critical potential failure mode of any network-growing algorithm.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The primary strength of this work lies in its principled approach to dynamic network sizing. By framing the problem as an optimization over both weights and network dimensions, the authors move beyond standard pruning techniques (which require a well-performing oversized network to start) and architecture search (which is often prohibitively expensive).

The theoretical result in Theorem 1 provides confidence in the framework. Proving that an optimal solution exists with a finite number of parameters under fan-in/fan-out regularization is crucial. This connects the work to the well-understood principles of group sparsity (akin to Group Lasso) and provides a rigorous underpinning that many other dynamic architecture methods lack.

The experimental results, while mixed on the `convex` dataset, are promising on `mnist` and `rectangles images`. The finding that the nonparametric network can sometimes outperform a parametric network of the exact same final size is particularly intriguing. It suggests that the adaptive training process itself—growing from a small base and continuously adding capacity—acts as a form of curriculum learning or regularization that helps the optimizer find better solutions.

### **Additional Feedback and Questions for Authors**

The following points are intended to help improve the clarity and impact of the paper. Addressing them would strengthen the work considerably.

1.  **Intuition behind Theorem 1:** While the proof is provided in the appendix, the main text would benefit greatly from a more intuitive, plain-English discussion of Theorem 1. A brief comparison to how Lasso and Group Lasso induce sparsity could help readers grasp the core mechanism. Why does penalizing the norm of entire fan-in/fan-out groups ensure that the optimal number of groups (units) is finite? This high-level insight is arguably more important for the reader than an extended review of prior work.

2.  **Analysis of Performance Gains:** The results in Figure 2 are fascinating, particularly where the nonparametric model outperforms its fixed-size counterpart. This is a powerful result that deserves more discussion. Why does this happen? Does the gradual addition of units help escape poor local minima? Does it provide a regularization effect that improves generalization? A deeper analysis here could be a major highlight of the paper.

3.  **Clarification on "Zero Units" and Regularization:**
    *   **Removability:** A unit can only be removed without altering the network's function if it has no influence on the output. This typically requires that *both* its fan-in and fan-out weights are zero (assuming a bias term or a subsequent layer can compensate for a zero-input unit). The paper states a removable "zero unit" has "either the fan-in or fan-out or both" as the zero vector. Please clarify this definition. If a unit with zero fan-in but non-zero fan-out is removed, the network's function changes.
    *   **Regularizer Value:** The paper claims that "adding and removing zero units... does change the value of the fan-in and fan-out regularizers." This seems incorrect for a unit that is truly "zero." If a new unit is added with a zero-norm fan-in, it contributes nothing to the fan-in regularizer, leaving its value unchanged. Please clarify this statement.

4.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity and Initialization:**
    *   **Role of λ:** The hyperparameter λ appears to be the main lever controlling the trade-off between model size and fit, thus largely determining the final architecture. Can you discuss the sensitivity of the final network structure to λ? Is it possible to anneal or adapt λ during training?
    *   **Initialization:** How stable is the final architecture to different random initializations? Do different runs converge to similarly sized and structured networks, or is there high variance? Understanding this is important for the method's reproducibility.

5.  **Further Questions:**
    *   **Combined Regularizers:** As a theoretical point, would Theorem 1 still hold if the regularizer was a combination of the fan-in and fan-out penalties?
    *   **Experimental Comparison:** To better highlight the efficiency of the adaptive process, an experiment comparing the final nonparametric model against a series of optimally trained fixed-size networks (of varying sizes) on a performance-vs-parameter plot would be very illustrative.

Overall, this is a very interesting and well-executed piece of research. I believe that by addressing the points above, particularly regarding the clarity of the core concepts and the analysis of the results, the authors can significantly enhance the paper's contribution.
