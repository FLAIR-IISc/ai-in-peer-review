## Review of “NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS”

This paper introduces a novel framework for automatically determining the size of a neural network during training, termed “nonparametric neural networks.” The core idea – dynamically growing and shrinking the network based on data rather than pre-defined hyperparameters – is a compelling and potentially significant contribution. The authors provide a theoretical justification for this approach, demonstrating that with appropriate regularization (fan-in or fan-out), the error optimum will utilize a finite number of parameters, preventing indefinite growth.

I **recommend accepting** this paper, albeit with a substantial revision request. The core concept is innovative and the theoretical grounding is interesting. However, significant clarity and further experimentation are required to fully assess the practical impact of the proposed method.

The strength of the paper lies in its theoretical result regarding the finite dimensionality of the optimal network under the specified regularization. This is a valuable addition to the literature, drawing parallels to Lasso/Elastic Net in terms of inducing sparsity. However, the explanation of *why* this finiteness holds beyond the proof itself is currently lacking.  The authors need to provide more intuitive explanations for this key finding.  Furthermore, the claim that adding zero units alters the regularizer value (specifically concerning the L2 norm) requires careful justification; a unit must have *both* fan-in and fan-out weights zero to not contribute to the network output.

A crucial experiment would be to compare the performance of the adaptive (nonparametric) model to fixed-size networks, matched in final size. This would convincingly demonstrate the efficiency gains (performance vs. memory usage) achieved by the adaptive approach. Currently, Figure 2 is somewhat unclear – why do nonparametric nets sometimes outperform parametric ones *even when the final net size is known*?  This suggests advantages beyond simply finding an optimal size.

Several questions remain. Can the regularization parameter λ be predicted from the data?  Does λ primarily dictate the final network structure?  How sensitive is the final network structure to different random initializations? Do different starting points lead to drastically different network topologies? Finally, what happens when combining fan-in and fan-out regularizers – does the theoretical guarantee of finite dimensionality still hold?

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Clarity:** The writing could be more accessible. Explanations of key concepts, like “CapNorm” and “AdaRad,” should be simplified for a broader audience.
*   **Algorithm 1:** The explanation of AdaRad is somewhat dense. A clearer walkthrough of the algorithm’s steps would be beneficial.
*   **Experimental Setup:** Provide a comprehensive table detailing all hyperparameters used in all experiments (as hinted at in Section 7.4).
*   **Robustness:** The experiments primarily focus on a limited number of datasets. Testing on a wider range of problems would strengthen the conclusions.



I believe addressing these points will significantly enhance the paper's clarity, rigor, and impact.
