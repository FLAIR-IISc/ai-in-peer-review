This paper proposes a novel approach to neural network design – nonparametric neural networks – that dynamically adjusts network size during training. The core idea of randomly adding zero units and employing a sparse regularizer to prune irrelevant weights is elegant and addresses a significant challenge: automatic model size selection. The resemblance to random search aided by sparse regularization provides an intuitive understanding of the method’s function. The empirical results, while not universally superior, demonstrate promising performance, particularly on the MNIST and rectangles datasets.

**Reject.** While the concept is interesting and the approach potentially valuable, the significant computational complexity introduced by the AdaRad algorithm, coupled with the unexplained poor performance on the convex dataset, currently outweigh the demonstrated benefits.

The primary concern lies with the computational overhead. The decomposition of fan-in weights into parallel/orthogonal components and subsequent conversion to radial-angular coordinates, while theoretically sound, likely incurs substantial computational costs. The paper acknowledges this but lacks a quantitative analysis. Providing a detailed breakdown of the additional operations compared to standard parametric networks, alongside running-time experiments demonstrating the practical impact of AdaRad, is crucial. Without this, it’s difficult to assess the method’s viability for larger, more complex datasets. The claim of graceful scaling needs empirical support.

Furthermore, the underperformance on the convex dataset is puzzling. Parametric networks significantly outperform the nonparametric approach in this setting, despite the latter’s ability to adjust size. The authors offer no explanation for this discrepancy. The convex dataset is well-suited for neural networks, so the failure to achieve competitive results raises concerns about the method’s robustness or suitability for certain problem types. A thorough investigation into why nonparametric networks struggle in this scenario is essential. Is it related to the regularization strength, the learning rate, or an inherent limitation of the approach when dealing with simpler functions?

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Clarify the connection to existing pruning techniques:** The paper mentions pruning but doesn’t fully articulate how this work differs. A more detailed comparison would be beneficial.
*   **Explore different regularization parameters:** The choice of λ seems critical. A more systematic exploration of its influence on network size and performance is warranted.
*   **Investigate alternative unit addition strategies:** The current strategy of adding one unit per layer per epoch appears somewhat arbitrary. Could more sophisticated schedules or criteria improve performance?
*   **Provide a more detailed ablation study:** Disentangling the effects of the zero-unit addition, sparse regularization, and AdaRad algorithm would help understand the contribution of each component.
*   **Expand the experimental evaluation:** Testing on a wider range of datasets, particularly those with varying degrees of complexity, would strengthen the conclusions.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a detailed breakdown of the computational cost of the AdaRad algorithm compared to standard backpropagation with SGD or Adam? Specifically, how do the decomposition and coordinate transformation steps impact the overall training time?
2.  What is the theoretical justification for the choice of adding *zero* units specifically? Would adding units with small random weights have a similar effect?
3.  Why do nonparametric networks perform so poorly on the convex dataset? What modifications to the approach might improve performance in this setting?
4.  How sensitive is the performance of the method to the choice of the angular step size (αφ)? Is there a principled way to adaptively tune this parameter during training?
5.  Could you elaborate on the implications of using different nonlinearities beyond those satisfying σ(0) = 0? What challenges would arise, and how might they be addressed?
6.  In Algorithm 2 (AdaRad-M), why is the orthogonal component of the fan-in explicitly re-orthogonalized after a unit is deleted? What happens if this step is omitted?
7.  The paper references scaling the regularization parameters in Proposition 1. What is the rationale for choosing the geometric mean as the scaling factor? Would other means (e.g., arithmetic mean) yield comparable results?
