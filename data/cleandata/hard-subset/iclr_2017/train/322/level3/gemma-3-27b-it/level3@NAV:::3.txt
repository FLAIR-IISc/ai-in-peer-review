## Review of "NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS"

This paper introduces a novel framework, Nonparametric Neural Networks, designed to automatically determine an appropriate network size during training. The core idea – dynamically adding and removing units based on input/output weight magnitude, coupled with group sparsity regularization – is conceptually elegant and addresses a significant challenge in deep learning: efficient model sizing. The theoretical result demonstrating that a finite-unit network can minimize loss under certain conditions is a valuable contribution. The proposed AdaRad algorithm, specifically designed for this dynamic network structure, shows promising initial results.

I recommend **Reject** with encouragement to revise and resubmit. While the core idea is strong, the paper lacks sufficient empirical evidence to demonstrate its practical advantages and robustness, and several critical limitations remain unaddressed. 

The primary weakness lies in the limited experimental validation. The results presented, while encouraging, are confined to a relatively small set of datasets and lack comparison to established network compression/pruning techniques. Without benchmarking against methods like pruning, knowledge distillation, or lottery ticket hypothesis approaches, it's impossible to assess whether the proposed method offers a genuine improvement. Furthermore, the hyperparameter dependence – specifically the interplay between the `add-frequency` and decay rates – which implicitly controls the final unit count, is acknowledged as a potential bottleneck. This effectively shifts the model selection problem rather than solving it, and the scalability of tuning these parameters for larger networks is concerning. The single set of experiments, while providing initial insights, isn’t sufficient to convincingly demonstrate the method's practicality and generalizability. A key issue is that controlling model size *also* impacts training time, creating a trade-off that isn’t thoroughly explored.

The theoretical work is a positive aspect, but the caveat that it doesn’t guarantee avoidance of overfitting or underfitting is significant.  The authors acknowledge this, but the experiments don't offer strong evidence that the method is inherently robust in this regard. The authors do a good job of clearly outlining their approach and motivation, which fits well within the goals of lifelong learning and dynamic model adaptation.

**Additional Feedback (for revision):**

*   **Comparative Analysis:**  A comprehensive comparison against state-of-the-art pruning, quantization, and knowledge distillation methods is crucial.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Investigate the sensitivity of the approach to the addition frequency and decay rates. Can a more principled way to set these parameters be developed?
*   **Scalability:** Conduct experiments on larger, more complex datasets (e.g., ImageNet) to assess scalability.
*   **Ablation Studies:**  Perform ablation studies to isolate the contribution of different components (e.g., AdaRad, the specific regularization scheme, the dynamic addition/removal).
*   **Theoretical Justification:** Can the theory be extended to provide some guarantees regarding overfitting or underfitting?




**Questions for the Authors:**

*   How does the computational cost of AdaRad compare to other adaptive optimization algorithms (Adam, RMSprop) as network size grows?
*   What strategies could be employed to automate the selection of the `add-frequency` and decay rates, mitigating the hyperparameter tuning challenge?
*   Could you elaborate on the specific constraints that would necessitate the use of your nonparametric approach over simpler pruning/compression techniques? What problem is this *specifically* solving that cannot be solved otherwise?
*   What is the memory footprint impact of dynamically adding and removing units during training? Does this present any practical limitations for very large models?
