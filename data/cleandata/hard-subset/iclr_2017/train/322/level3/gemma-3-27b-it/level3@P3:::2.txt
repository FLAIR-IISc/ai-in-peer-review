This paper introduces nonparametric neural networks, a framework for automatically determining network size during training. The core idea – dynamically growing and shrinking the network based on data rather than predefined priors – is a compelling one, and the theoretical result demonstrating finite parameter usage with appropriate regularization is significant. The authors present a novel optimization algorithm, AdaRad, to facilitate this process.

**Decision:** Accept (with major revisions). The core idea is strong and the theoretical grounding is valuable. However, several key aspects require clarification and further elaboration to fully justify the claims and demonstrate the practical benefits of the approach.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The primary strength lies in the theoretical result (Theorem 1) regarding the finiteness of the optimal network size under fan-in or fan-out regularization. This addresses a crucial concern with dynamic network architectures – the potential for unbounded growth. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing black-box model selection methods and offer a potentially more efficient alternative. The proposed AdaRad algorithm appears well-motivated and designed to address the challenges of training networks with a variable number of units. The empirical results, while promising, are not fully convincing and require further scrutiny.

However, several points require substantial improvement. The discussion surrounding Theorem 1 is insufficient. While the proof is provided in the appendix, a more intuitive, high-level explanation of *why* the error optimum uses a finite number of parameters is needed. The authors agree with Reviewer 2 regarding the appealing nature of automatic unit addition/removal, but don't sufficiently expand on the implications beyond stating it avoids manual hyperparameter tuning.  The comparison to Lasso/Elastic Net is apt, but doesn't go far enough – a more detailed analogy would strengthen the understanding of the regularization's effect.

The experiments need further work.  A critical experiment missing is a comparison of the adaptive nonparametric model to fixed-size nets with the *same final size*. This would directly demonstrate the efficiency gains (performance vs. memory) of the nonparametric approach.  Figure 2 is unclear and exhibits mixed results, particularly the observation that nonparametric nets sometimes outperform parametric ones even when the final net size is known. This raises a fundamental question: what is the benefit of the nonparametric approach if a well-sized fixed network can achieve comparable or better performance?

The definition of a “zero unit” is imprecise. A removable unit *must* have both fan-in and fan-out weights equal to zero to preserve the network's output; otherwise, the network's function changes. The paper’s claim that adding zero units changes the regularizer value (e.g., L2 norm with zeros) needs justification. While technically true, it's a subtle point and could be misleading.

Several questions remain unanswered: Can the regularization parameter λ be predicted from the data? Does λ primarily determine the final network structure? How sensitive is the approach to initialization? Do different random starts yield significantly different final network structures, and how different are they in terms of performance? What happens when combining fan-in and fan-out regularizers – does the theoretical result still hold?

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Theorem 1 Explanation:** Expand the discussion of Theorem 1. Provide an intuitive understanding of why finite dimensionality is guaranteed, going beyond the mathematical proof.
*   **Experimental Design:** Implement the experiment comparing nonparametric nets to fixed-size nets of equivalent final size.
*   **Figure 2 Clarity:** Redesign Figure 2 to clearly present the results and address the inconsistency of nonparametric nets outperforming parametric nets with the same size.
*   **Zero Unit Definition:** Clarify the definition of a “zero unit” to emphasize the requirement of zero fan-in *and* zero fan-out.
*   **Regularization & Zero Units:** Provide a more nuanced discussion of how adding zero units affects the regularizer value.
*   **λ Prediction:** Investigate the possibility of predicting λ from the data.
*   **Initialization Sensitivity:** Conduct experiments to assess the sensitivity of the approach to initialization.
*   **Combined Regularizers:** Explore the combination of fan-in and fan-out regularizers and their theoretical implications.
*   **AdaRad-M:** While AdaRad-M shows potential, justify the inclusion of momentum in the context of the fan-in regularizer and explain any observed performance differences.



**Specific Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Can you provide more intuition behind Theorem 1? Why does the error optimum necessarily use a finite number of parameters with the given regularization scheme?
2.  Why do nonparametric networks sometimes outperform fixed-size networks with the same number of parameters (as observed in Figure 2)? What inherent advantage does the dynamic adaptation provide?
3.  How sensitive is the final network structure and performance to different random initializations?
4.  Is there any inherent reason to prefer fan-in regularization over fan-out regularization, or vice versa? What happens if both are combined?
5.  Is it possible to predict the optimal value of λ from the data, and if so, how?
6.   How does the computational cost of AdaRad scale with network size compared to traditional optimization algorithms like Adam or RMSprop, particularly in terms of memory usage?
7.   Could you elaborate on the practical implications of the claim that the regularization prevents the network from growing indefinitely, and how this impacts the potential application of this framework to very large datasets?
