## Review of NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS

This paper proposes a novel framework for automatically determining the size of a neural network during a single training cycle, termed nonparametric neural networks. The authors address the challenging problem of model selection without prior knowledge, avoiding the computationally expensive global search typically required. The core idea involves dynamically adding and removing units during training, guided by an L2 penalty to eliminate redundant units, and a novel optimization algorithm called Adaptive Radial-Angular Gradient Descent (AdaRad). This is a promising approach to a difficult problem, and the empirical results presented are encouraging.

**Decision: Reject**

While the paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable approach, the lack of a clear justification for the computational overhead introduced by AdaRad, combined with the disappointing performance on one of the datasets, outweighs the positive aspects. The benefits of automatic network sizing are not convincingly demonstrated to justify the added complexity.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The primary concern centers around the computational complexity of the proposed AdaRad algorithm. The decomposition of fan-in weights into parallel and orthogonal components and the conversion to radial-angular coordinates, while conceptually intriguing, appear to introduce significant overhead. The paper does mention that the cost scales more gracefully than RMSprop, but it lacks a thorough quantitative analysis of this overhead.  A comparison of the actual running time for training networks of equivalent performance with AdaRad versus standard methods (e.g., Adam with a learning rate schedule) is crucial. Without this, it’s difficult to assess the practical utility of the approach. 

Furthermore, the results on the convex dataset are concerning. The nonparametric networks underperform parametric networks of the same size, and the authors acknowledge that this suggests a tendency to converge to relatively small sizes, hindering performance. A more in-depth analysis of *why* the method fails on this dataset is needed. Is it a limitation of the L2 regularization? Is it a characteristic of the dataset itself?  The discussion feels somewhat dismissive of this negative result.

The theoretical justification for the framework (Theorem 1) is also somewhat abstract and requires further clarification. The proof is relegated to the appendix, and a more intuitive explanation of its implications would be beneficial.  

**Additional Feedback:**

These points are offered as suggestions for improvement and do not necessarily dictate the final decision:

*   **Computational Analysis:** Provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of AdaRad compared to standard optimization algorithms (SGD, Adam, RMSprop). Include running time experiments on various datasets and network sizes.
*   **Convex Dataset Analysis:** Investigate the reasons for the poor performance on the convex dataset. Consider experimenting with different regularization schemes or network architectures specifically tailored to this dataset.
*   **Theorem 1 Explanation:** Offer a more intuitive explanation of Theorem 1 and its significance in the main body of the paper.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Explore the sensitivity of the results to the choice of hyperparameters, particularly the radial and angular step sizes (αr and αφ).
*   **Unit Initialization:** Describe the random initialization of added units in more detail. Could different initialization strategies impact performance?
* **Ablation study:** An ablation study of the various components of the proposed framework – the CapNorm, the radial-angular gradient descent, the unit addition/removal scheme – would help to understand the contribution of each component to the overall performance.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a quantitative comparison of the training time for nonparametric networks trained with AdaRad versus parametric networks trained with Adam (or another standard optimizer) for equivalent performance on the MNIST and Rectangles datasets?
2.  What specific characteristics of the convex dataset do you believe contribute to the poor performance of the nonparametric networks? Have you considered alternative regularization strategies or network architectures for this dataset?
3.  Can you provide a more intuitive explanation of Theorem 1 and why the p-norm restriction (1 ≤ p < ∞) is crucial for ensuring a global training error minimum?
4.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of αr and αφ? Did you perform any hyperparameter optimization, and if so, what were the optimal values found?
5.   Did you try training the network without the removal of units? Would the performance be comparable to standard networks? If not, what is the specific benefit of the removal step?
