This paper introduces nonparametric neural networks, a framework for dynamically adjusting network size during training. The core idea – automatically growing and pruning a network based on data, rather than relying on prior knowledge or exhaustive search – is compelling and represents an interesting direction for neural network research. The theoretical result demonstrating that a finite network size optimizes the objective function under certain regularization conditions is a significant contribution. Overall, I believe this work has merit and assign it a rating of 7/10, contingent on addressing the concerns outlined below.

**Decision:** Accept with minor revisions. The core idea and theoretical grounding are strong, but the experimental results require further clarification and the theoretical contributions need to be presented with more intuition.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The novelty of the approach and the theoretical guarantee of finite dimensionality are strong points. The authors demonstrate promising results on several datasets, suggesting the framework is viable and can achieve competitive performance. The introduction of AdaRad, a training algorithm tailored to this dynamic network structure, is a positive contribution. The thorough experimental analysis, including ablation studies and comparisons with standard methods, adds weight to the findings.

However, several aspects require clarification and further investigation. The presentation of the theorem (Theorem 1) feels overly formal and lacks intuitive explanation. While the proof is included in the appendix, the core *insight* – what makes this formulation different and advantageous – isn't clearly articulated in plain English. It’s not immediately obvious why this regularization scheme guarantees a finite solution, and a more accessible discussion would be beneficial. The paper dedicates significant space to related work, some of which feels tangential and could be streamlined to allow for more detailed discussion of the core theoretical result.

The experimental results, particularly Figure 2, are somewhat difficult to interpret. The mixed presentation of results (different markers, colors) makes it hard to directly compare the performance of nonparametric and parametric networks. More importantly, the paper states that nonparametric networks sometimes outperform parametric ones even when the final network size is known. This seems counterintuitive and requires a more detailed explanation. Why does dynamically adjusting the network structure lead to better performance than simply training a fixed-size network with the same final number of parameters? Is it due to the specific training dynamics induced by the addition/removal of units?

I recommend an experiment that directly addresses this question: train both the nonparametric network and a fixed-size parametric network (with the *same final size* as the nonparametric network) and compare their performance, training time, and memory usage. This would clearly demonstrate the efficiency gains (or lack thereof) achieved by the nonparametric approach.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Regularizer Parameter (λ):** Can the optimal value of λ be predicted from the data or does it require a separate hyperparameter search? Does λ primarily determine the final network structure (size and connectivity)? A discussion of this would be valuable.
*   **Initialization Sensitivity:** How sensitive is the approach to the initial random seed? Do different random initializations lead to significantly different final network structures? Reporting the variance in network size and performance across multiple random seeds would strengthen the findings.
*   **Fan-in and Fan-out Combination:** Does the theoretical result (Theorem 1) still hold if both fan-in and fan-out regularizers are used simultaneously? Exploring this combination could potentially lead to even better performance.
*   **Zero Units and Regularization:** The claim that adding zero units changes the value of the regularizer (e.g., L2 norm) seems incorrect. Adding a zero vector to a weight matrix does not affect the L2 norm. Please clarify this point.
*   **Definition of Zero Units:** The paper should explicitly state that a removable "zero unit" requires *both* its fan-in and fan-out weights to be zero to preserve the network's output. This is a crucial clarification.
*   **Figure 2 Clarity:** Redesign Figure 2 for better clarity. Consider using separate plots for each dataset and clearly labeling each data point with the corresponding hyperparameter settings.
*   **Theorem 1 Discussion:** Expand the discussion of Theorem 1. Focus on the intuition behind the result rather than solely on the mathematical proof. Explain why the regularization scheme prevents indefinite growth and ensures a meaningful solution.



I look forward to seeing the authors address these comments in a revised version of the paper. Addressing these points will significantly strengthen the paper and make the contribution more impactful.
