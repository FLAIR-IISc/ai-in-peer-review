## Review for ICLR 2017 – Nonparametric Neural Networks

This paper introduces a novel framework for dynamically adjusting the size of a neural network during training, termed “nonparametric neural networks”. The core idea – to simultaneously learn network weights *and* network structure – is appealing and addresses a significant challenge in deep learning: automatically determining an appropriate network size without extensive hyperparameter search. The authors provide a theoretical grounding for their approach, demonstrating that under certain regularization conditions, a finite network size will achieve optimal performance, preventing unbounded growth. The experimental results, while not universally superior, show promising performance on several datasets.

**Decision: Reject**

Despite the originality and theoretical contributions of this work, I recommend rejection at this time. While the approach is compelling, the experimental evidence isn’t consistently strong enough to demonstrate a clear advantage over well-established techniques, and several crucial aspects of the method require further clarification and justification. The key issue is a lack of convincing evidence that the dynamic adaptation process consistently leads to better models than can be obtained with a targeted search over fixed-size architectures.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Lack of Consistent Improvement:** The results presented in Figure 2 are mixed. While nonparametric networks outperform parametric networks of the *same size* in some cases, the comparison isn’t entirely fair. The experiments don't clearly demonstrate that the nonparametric approach can find architectures that outperform the best fixed-size networks discovered through a reasonable hyperparameter search. The claim that nonparametric nets can achieve “higher accuracy than is possible by black-box methods” isn’t convincingly supported.

2. **Insufficient Clarification of Theoretical Contribution:** Theorem 1 is a central contribution, yet its practical implications are not clearly articulated beyond stating that a finite network size is optimal. The intuition behind *why* this holds – why the combination of optimization and the specified regularization prevents runaway growth – isn’t adequately explained in a way accessible to a broad audience. More discussion on the interplay between the optimization process and the regularizer is needed. The paper would benefit from focusing more on elucidating the core theoretical insight rather than expanding the related work section.

**Additional Feedback (For Improvement):**

The following points are offered to help strengthen the paper and are not necessarily grounds for rejection in themselves.

* **Efficiency Comparison:** Conduct an experiment comparing the nonparametric approach to fixed-size networks *trained to the same final size* as the nonparametric network. This would directly demonstrate whether the dynamic adaptation offers any efficiency gains in terms of performance or memory usage.

* **Clarify Figure 2:** Figure 2 is difficult to interpret due to the overlapping ranges and the mix of data points. A clearer presentation, perhaps separating the different methods and providing error bars, would be beneficial. Why do nonparametric networks sometimes outperform parametric networks *even when the final network size is known*? This is a crucial question that requires deeper investigation.

* **Elaborate on Theorem 1:** Provide a plain-English explanation of the key insight behind Theorem 1. What does it mean in practice? How does the choice of `p` in the regularization term influence the network’s growth and structure?

* **Regularization Parameter (λ):** Can the optimal value of the regularization parameter (λ) be predicted from the data or dataset characteristics? Does λ primarily determine the final network size, or does it have a more complex influence on network structure?

* **Sensitivity to Initialization:** Investigate the sensitivity of the results to the initial random seed. Do different random initializations consistently lead to different final network architectures? How different are these architectures in terms of performance and size?

* **Combined Regularization:** Explore the potential benefits of combining fan-in and fan-out regularization. Does the theoretical result still hold under this combined approach?

* **Zero Units & Regularization:** Clarify the impact of adding “zero units” on the regularization term. The claim that adding zero units changes the regularizer value (e.g., in the case of the L2 norm) seems counterintuitive. A more detailed explanation is needed.

* **Definition of Zero Units:**  To ensure clarity, explicitly state that a removable “zero unit” requires *both* its fan-in and fan-out weights to be zero to maintain the network’s output function.  This prevents ambiguity.

* **Algorithm 2 (AdaRad-M):**  The explanation of why momentum is applied differently to weights and the orthogonal component of the gradient is not entirely clear. More detailed justification is warranted.



I believe that addressing these concerns would significantly strengthen the paper and make its contributions more compelling. While the concept is promising, further refinement and more convincing experimental evidence are needed before it can be considered suitable for publication in ICLR.
