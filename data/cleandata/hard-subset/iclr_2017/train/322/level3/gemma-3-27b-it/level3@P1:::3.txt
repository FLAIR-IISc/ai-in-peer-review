## Review of “NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS”

This paper introduces a novel framework called nonparametric neural networks, aiming to automatically determine an appropriate network size during a single training cycle. The core idea is to dynamically add and remove units by driving weights to zero via an L2 penalty, coupled with a novel optimization algorithm, AdaRad. The authors provide theoretical justification for the approach, proving that a finite network size can minimize the loss function under certain conditions.

**Decision: Reject**

While the paper presents an interesting and conceptually elegant approach, the evidence presented is not sufficiently compelling to warrant acceptance at this time. Specifically, the experimental results, though promising, are limited in scope and do not demonstrate a clear advantage over existing model selection or network compression techniques. Furthermore, the lack of scalability discussion raises concerns about the practical applicability of the method to large-scale datasets and networks.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The primary weakness lies in the limited experimental validation. While the results on MNIST and a rectangles dataset show comparable or even better performance than statically sized networks, the experiments lack a thorough comparison to established techniques for model selection (e.g., Bayesian Optimization) and network compression (e.g., pruning methods). A direct comparison would clarify whether the proposed approach offers a significant benefit beyond existing solutions. The positive results on the rectangles dataset are tempered by the fact that the method *underperforms* parametric networks of the same size when the regularization parameter is large. This suggests sensitivity to hyperparameter tuning, a concern that the authors acknowledge but don't adequately address.

The experiment on the convex dataset demonstrates a clear failure of the nonparametric approach.  The authors acknowledge this result but don’t provide a compelling explanation for it. This raises doubts about the robustness and generality of the proposed method.  Further investigation into why the method fails on this dataset is needed.

Scalability is another critical concern. The authors mention that growing nets gradually avoids the computational cost of full training runs required by black-box methods. However, the reliance on adding units iteratively, combined with the need to tune hyperparameters controlling the addition rate and decay, could potentially lead to significantly longer training times, especially for larger networks. The interplay between model size and training time is not fully explored.  The authors do mention experiments with a large dataset (Poker), but the details are limited and don't sufficiently address the scalability concern.

Finally, the method introduces a new set of hyperparameters—addition frequency, decay rates, and the radial/angular step sizes—that require careful tuning. While the authors provide some guidance, it’s unclear how sensitive the results are to these parameters, and a systematic hyperparameter optimization study is lacking. The authors’ claim that these parameters “implicitly control final unit count” highlights a potential complexity; effective tuning could prove challenging in practice.



**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Comparison to Baselines:** Rigorously compare the performance of nonparametric networks to state-of-the-art methods for model selection (e.g., random search, Bayesian optimization) and network compression (e.g., pruning, knowledge distillation).
*   **Scalability Analysis:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost and scalability of the approach, particularly for large-scale datasets and networks. Include timings for different network sizes and datasets.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Conduct a systematic hyperparameter optimization study to assess the sensitivity of the results to the various hyperparameters introduced by the method.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** Explore the conditions under which the theoretical result holds and how they relate to practical performance. Investigate the potential for overfitting or underfitting, despite the theoretical guarantees.
*   **Explain Convex Dataset Failure:** Investigate and explain why the nonparametric network performs poorly on the convex dataset.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Perform ablation studies to evaluate the contributions of the different components of the framework (e.g., the L2 penalty, the AdaRad optimizer, the CapNorm normalization).
*   **Expand Experiments:** Evaluate the method on a wider range of datasets and network architectures. Consider more complex datasets commonly used in the literature.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  How does the computational cost of training a nonparametric network scale with the size of the dataset and the complexity of the network, compared to training a statically sized network with a similar level of performance?
2.  Could you elaborate on the sensitivity of the results to the hyperparameters controlling the unit addition frequency and decay rates? How do you recommend tuning these parameters in practice?
3.  What is the intuition behind the failure of the nonparametric network on the convex dataset? Are there specific characteristics of this dataset that make it particularly challenging for this approach?
4.  How does AdaRad compare to other adaptive optimization algorithms (e.g., Adam, RMSprop) in terms of convergence speed and generalization performance for nonparametric networks?
5.  Have you explored alternative regularization techniques beyond the L2 penalty, such as L1 regularization or group sparsity? If so, what were the results?
