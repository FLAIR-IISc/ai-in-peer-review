

This paper introduces "nonparametric neural networks," a framework for automatically determining optimal network size during training rather than through expensive hyperparameter search. The authors propose dynamically adding and removing units via zeroing input/output weights with group-sparsity regularization (specifically, an l2 norm on fan-in/fan-out weights), proving that a finite network size minimizes training error under these conditions. They introduce a novel training algorithm called AdaRad and demonstrate promising results on small datasets.

Reject. The paper lacks sufficient empirical validation against relevant baselines and the theoretical guarantee does not address generalization performance.

The key limitation is that while Theorem 1 guarantees a finite network size minimizes training error, it provides no assurance about generalization performance. The authors do not demonstrate that their method avoids overfitting or underfitting compared to standard approaches. Their experiments on MNIST, rectangles images, and convex datasets show promising results, but these are limited to small-scale problems and do not compare against state-of-the-art pruning or compression methods (e.g., Molchanov et al. 2017, Guo et al. 2016). Without such comparisons, it's unclear whether the approach is competitive with existing size-reduction techniques, which is the core contribution claimed.

Additionally, the method introduces significant hyperparameter complexity that controls both model size and training time through single parameters (e.g., unit addition rate, decay rates). This creates a challenging trade-off: the method requires careful tuning to balance model size and training time, which contradicts the paper's goal of automating network size selection. The paper does not provide guidance on how to set these hyperparameters for optimal performance, making the method impractical for real-world applications.

The experiments are also insufficiently comprehensive. While the MNIST results are promising, the convex dataset results are disappointing, with parametric networks outperforming nonparametric ones. The poker dataset experiment on a large-scale problem is encouraging but lacks comparison to standard methods. The paper would benefit from more thorough analysis of how hyperparameters affect final network size and performance, as well as a clear discussion of the training time overhead compared to standard approaches.

The theoretical contribution is elegant and conceptually sound, but the lack of comprehensive empirical validation against relevant baselines and the unresolved hyperparameter tuning challenges undermine the practical significance of the work. The paper's claim that "nonparametric networks achieve higher accuracy than parametric networks of the same size" is only partially supported by the MNIST results, with the convex dataset showing the opposite pattern.

Additional feedback: The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1) Direct comparison with pruning/compression baselines on standard benchmarks
2) Analysis of the trade-off between model size and training time
3) More detailed discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity
4) Experiments on larger-scale problems to assess scalability

Questions for authors:
1) How does your method compare to existing pruning approaches (e.g., Molchanov et al. 2017) in terms of final test accuracy for the same number of parameters?
2) What is the expected training time overhead compared to standard parametric networks, and how does it scale with dataset size?
3) Can you provide more concrete guidance on setting hyperparameters like unit addition frequency and decay rates for optimal performance?
