

**Review**

This paper proposes a novel framework for automatically determining optimal neural network size during training, termed "nonparametric neural networks." The authors introduce a method that dynamically adds and removes units through a combination of random initialization of zero units and sparse regularization (specifically `2 fan-in regularizer). The approach leverages the theoretical foundation that network growth can be constrained via regularization, proving that a global minimum exists for the training error at finite dimensionality. The authors present a novel optimization algorithm, AdaRad, which handles the radial and angular components of weight updates separately, and demonstrate promising empirical results on MNIST and rectangles images datasets, achieving competitive performance with smaller network sizes compared to parametric alternatives.

**Decision:** Accept (with major revisions)

**Key reasons for acceptance:** The paper tackles an important problem in deep learning with a theoretically sound and conceptually elegant approach. The authors provide a solid theoretical foundation (Theorem 1) and demonstrate the method's effectiveness on multiple datasets, showing it can find good network sizes during a single training cycle without requiring expensive global search.

**Supporting arguments:** The paper makes a significant contribution by addressing the challenge of automatic network size selection. The theoretical proof establishes a sound foundation, and the empirical results on MNIST and rectangles images are compelling. The authors' approach to dynamically grow and shrink networks during training is novel and well-motivated within the context of sparse regularization and pruning literature.

**Additional feedback:** The authors should address two critical concerns. First, they must quantify the additional computational complexity of the radial-angular decomposition and provide running-time experiments comparing their method to standard parametric networks. Second, they need to explain why the method underperforms on the convex dataset, particularly why it consistently yields very small networks that underperform parametric networks of similar size. The authors should also clarify whether the convex dataset underperformance is due to the specific problem structure or limitations of their approach.

**Questions for authors:**
1. Can you provide a detailed computational complexity analysis of AdaRad compared to standard optimization algorithms (SGD, Adam, etc.)?
2. What is the specific reason for the poor performance on the convex dataset, and how might the method be adapted to handle such problems better?
