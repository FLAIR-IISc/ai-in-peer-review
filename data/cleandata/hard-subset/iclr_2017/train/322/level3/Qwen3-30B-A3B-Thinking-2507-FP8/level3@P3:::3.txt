

# Review of "Nonparametric Neural Networks" (ICLR 2017)

## 1. Summary
The paper introduces "nonparametric neural networks," a framework for automatically determining optimal network size during a single training cycle without requiring expensive global search. The key innovation is dynamically adding and removing units through zeroing input/output weights with group-sparsity regularization (specifically L2 regularization on fan-in/fan-out weights). The authors prove Theorem 1, showing that under certain conditions, a finite-unit network can minimize the loss. They propose a novel training algorithm (AdaRad) and demonstrate promising results on MNIST and rectangles datasets, suggesting their approach can produce smaller networks with comparable or better performance than parametric networks of the same size.

## 2. Decision
Reject. While the theoretical contribution is elegant and the initial results are promising, the paper lacks sufficient empirical validation to support its claims about practical utility and competitiveness with existing methods.

## 3. Supporting arguments
The paper's theoretical contribution (Theorem 1) is well-proven and conceptually sound, but the empirical results fail to adequately support the paper's claims about practical advantages. The key issues are:

- **No comparison to pruning/compression baselines**: The authors don't compare against state-of-the-art methods for network compression (e.g., Han et al., 2015; Molchanov et al., 2017), making it impossible to assess whether their approach is competitive with existing size-reduction techniques.

- **Limited experimental scope**: The experiments are confined to small datasets (MNIST, rectangles, convex) and shallow networks. The scalability analysis on the poker dataset is promising but still insufficient to establish practical viability for real-world applications.

- **Unaddressed hyperparameter challenges**: The paper acknowledges that hyperparameters (add-frequency, decay rates) implicitly control final unit count and may be hard to tune, but provides no guidance on how to effectively select them.

- **No guarantee against over/under-fitting**: The theoretical results don't guarantee avoidance of over- or under-fitting, and the limited experiments don't fully validate this claim.

- **Scalability concerns**: The approach risks much slower training for large networks due to the single hyperparameters controlling both model size and training time.

## 4. Additional feedback
- Include comprehensive comparisons against state-of-the-art pruning and compression methods to establish the approach's competitiveness.
- Conduct experiments on larger datasets and deeper network architectures to better assess scalability.
- Provide detailed analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity and practical guidelines for tuning.
- Clarify the theoretical limitations regarding over/under-fitting and provide more experimental evidence addressing this concern.
- Discuss the practical trade-offs between training time and final network size more thoroughly.

## 5. Questions for authors
- How does your approach compare to state-of-the-art pruning/compression techniques (e.g., Han et al., 2015; Molchanov et al., 2017) in terms of final network size and test accuracy on standard benchmarks?
- Could you provide more detailed analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity, particularly how the unit addition rate and regularization strength interact to determine the final network size?
- For the convex dataset where your method underperformed, what specific characteristics of this dataset caused the poor performance, and how might the approach be modified to handle such cases?
- What is the computational overhead of AdaRad compared to standard training algorithms like Adam on larger networks (e.g., ResNet-50)?
- How do you envision this approach scaling to very large networks (e.g., 100+ layers) and large-scale vision tasks?
