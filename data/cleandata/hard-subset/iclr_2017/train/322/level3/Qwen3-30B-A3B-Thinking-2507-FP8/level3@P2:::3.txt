

# Review for "NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS"

## Summary
This paper presents a novel framework called "nonparametric neural networks" for automatically determining the optimal network size during a single training cycle. The authors introduce a method that dynamically adds and removes units through an `2 penalty, proving theoretically that such networks achieve a global training error minimum at finite dimensionality. The approach uses a new normalization layer (CapNorm) and a novel optimization algorithm (AdaRad) to train these networks. The paper demonstrates promising results on MNIST and rectangles images datasets, producing smaller networks that achieve performance competitive with or better than parametric networks of the same size, and even outperforming exhaustive search on the rectangles dataset.

## Decision
Reject. The paper makes an interesting theoretical contribution but lacks sufficient empirical validation to justify acceptance. The theoretical soundness is strong, but the empirical results are limited in scope and fail to demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper's core theoretical contribution (Theorem 1) is well-motivated and rigorously proven. The approach of using group-sparsity regularization to control network size is conceptually elegant and addresses a genuine limitation in current model selection methods. However, the empirical evaluation has significant limitations that undermine the paper's claims:

1. The experiments only cover three small datasets (MNIST, rectangles images, convex), with the convex dataset showing poor performance. The paper does not demonstrate the method's effectiveness on larger-scale or more challenging datasets, which is crucial for claiming practical utility.

2. The paper does not compare against state-of-the-art pruning or network compression methods. Without these comparisons, it's impossible to determine if the proposed method offers advantages over existing techniques for reducing model size.

3. The hyperparameter sensitivity is not thoroughly explored. The authors note that "hyperparameters (e.g., add-frequency, decay rates) still implicitly control final unit count and may be hard to tune," yet they don't provide guidance on how to select these parameters effectively.

4. The paper claims to reduce test-time runtime, power, and memory through smaller networks, but fails to provide concrete measurements of these benefits in practice.

## Additional Feedback
To strengthen the paper, I recommend:

1. Extending the experimental evaluation to include larger-scale datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10/100, ImageNet) and comparing against established pruning/compression methods (e.g., pruning, knowledge distillation, weight sharing).

2. Providing a more thorough analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity and potentially offering guidelines for setting these parameters effectively.

3. Including concrete measurements of the practical benefits (inference speed, memory usage, energy consumption) of the resulting smaller networks.

4. Addressing the poor performance on the convex dataset to understand the method's limitations.

5. Exploring the relationship between the proposed method and existing approaches to network size reduction to better situate the contribution.

## Clarifying Questions
1. The paper claims nonparametric networks "achieve a higher accuracy than networks of the same size that are trained from scratch and, in some cases, achieve a higher accuracy than is possible by black-box methods." However, the results for MNIST show nonparametric networks outperforming parametric networks of the same size, but the parametric networks were trained with batch normalization (which the paper states is incompatible with the nonparametric framework). Can you clarify whether the comparison fairly accounts for the use of batch normalization in the parametric networks?

2. The paper states "parametric networks of the same size perform substantially better" on the convex dataset. Given the theoretical foundation, why does the nonparametric approach fail on this dataset? Is this related to the specific dataset characteristics or a limitation of the method?

3. How does the training time of the nonparametric approach scale with network size? The paper mentions that "the downside of increased training time as shown in Figure 3A incurred when networks are built gradually was ameliorated" for the poker dataset, but provides no quantitative analysis of this trade-off. Can you provide specific training time measurements for the nonparametric approach compared to standard parametric training?
