

# Review for "Nonparametric Neural Networks"

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contributions

The paper introduces a novel framework for automatically determining the optimal size of a neural network during a single training run, addressing a significant problem in deep learning model selection. The authors propose "nonparametric neural networks" that dynamically grow and shrink by adding zero-initialized units and applying an L2 fan-in regularizer to eliminate redundant units. They prove that this approach achieves a global minimum at finite dimensionality (Theorem 1) and introduce a novel optimization algorithm, AdaRad, which handles the radial and angular components of weight updates separately. The method is evaluated on three datasets (MNIST, rectangles images, and convex) showing promising results on two, though inconsistent performance on the third.

## 2. Decision

I recommend rejection. While the problem is important and the theoretical foundation is sound, the paper has significant concerns regarding computational complexity and inconsistent empirical results that undermine the practical viability of the approach.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's core contribution is well-motivated and theoretically grounded. The authors clearly position their work against existing black-box model selection methods (grid search, random search, Bayesian optimization) and recent network pruning approaches, identifying a key limitation: current methods require multiple full training runs. The theoretical proof (Theorem 1) is rigorous and addresses a critical concern about unbounded network growth.

However, the paper has two major concerns that prevent acceptance:

First, the computational complexity of the proposed AdaRad algorithm is not adequately justified. The algorithm requires decomposing each weight vector into parallel and orthogonal components and converting to radial-angular coordinates. This adds substantial computational overhead that the authors don't quantify or justify. The paper mentions that "AdaRad normalizes at the granularity of fan-ins instead of the granularity of individual weights" but fails to provide any runtime comparisons or computational complexity analysis showing this overhead is acceptable compared to standard optimizers like Adam or RMSprop. Without this, the practical value of the method remains unclear.

Second, the empirical results are inconsistent. While the method substantially outperforms parametric networks of the same size on MNIST and shows competitive performance on rectangles images, it performs poorly on the convex dataset, yielding networks that are too small and underperforming. The paper states "nonparametric networks performed badly on the convex dataset" but doesn't provide a clear explanation for this failure mode. This inconsistency raises questions about the robustness of the approach across different problem domains.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen this work, the authors should:

- Provide detailed computational complexity analysis and runtime experiments comparing AdaRad to standard optimizers (SGD, Adam, RMSprop) on multiple datasets.
- Investigate and explain the poor performance on the convex dataset, which is a critical weakness in the empirical evaluation.
- Include more details on how the regularization parameter λ affects network size and performance, particularly for the convex dataset.
- Consider addressing the issue of network size too small on convex by exploring alternative unit addition/removal strategies.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. Could you provide quantitative analysis of the computational overhead of AdaRad compared to standard optimizers? Specifically, what percentage increase in training time does AdaRad incur on MNIST and how does this scale to larger datasets?

2. Why does the method perform so poorly on the convex dataset compared to the other datasets? Is this due to specific characteristics of the convex dataset (e.g., linearity, feature distribution) or a fundamental limitation of the approach? The paper states "nonparametric networks performed badly on the convex dataset" but doesn't explain why.

3. The paper mentions that "the best nonparametric network had the globally best performance" for rectangles images, but for MNIST it "is close in performance to the best parametric network while being substantially smaller." Could you clarify the exact performance metrics for these comparisons (e.g., test error percentages) and how they compare to the best parametric networks found through exhaustive search?
