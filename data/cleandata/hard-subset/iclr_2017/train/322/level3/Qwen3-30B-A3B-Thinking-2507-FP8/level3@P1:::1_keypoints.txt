- Introduces "nonparametric neural networks" that grow/prune units during training using AdaRad and fan-in^2 regularization.
- Claims adding zero-initialized units plus sparse regularization lets a single training run search architectures and remove redundancies.
- Provides a theoretical proof (Theorem 1) guaranteeing a global minimum at finite network size under their regularization.
- Recommendation: reject.
- Major practical concern: paper fails to show significant advantages over existing methods.
- Computational overhead of AdaRad (radial-angular decomposition) is substantial and not justified.
- Missing experiments quantifying AdaRad's runtime and overhead versus standard optimizers.
- Empirical results are mixed: competitive on MNIST/rectangles but underperforming on the convex dataset.
- Convergence to unnecessarily small networks on the convex dataset needs explanation and analysis.
- Theoretical contribution is limited: proof applies only to self-similar nonlinearities (e.g., ReLU) and specific regularizers.
- Poor positioning relative to prior pruning and architecture-search work; connections to related work are superficial.
- AdaRad's claimed learning-dynamics benefits are unsubstantiated versus adaptive methods like Adam/RMSprop.
- Complexity analysis (Table 1) is incomplete—addresses memory but omits per-iteration computational cost of radial-angular updates.
- Suggested improvements: runtime comparisons of AdaRad, analysis of convex dataset failure, clearer positioning vs. prior work, extension to complex architectures, and ablation studies.
- Key open questions: AdaRad's scaling and actual time cost; reasons for small-network convergence on the convex dataset; potential combination with existing architecture-search methods; performance on larger vision tasks (CIFAR-10, ImageNet).
