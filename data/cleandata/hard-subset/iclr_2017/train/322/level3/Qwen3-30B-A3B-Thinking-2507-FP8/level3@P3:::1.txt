

# Review of "Nonparametric Neural Networks" (ICLR 2017)

## 1. Summary
The paper introduces "nonparametric neural networks," a novel framework for automatically determining optimal network size during a single training cycle without requiring expensive global search over multiple network architectures. The key innovation is a method that dynamically grows and shrinks networks by randomly adding zero units and applying a sparse L2 fan-in regularizer to eliminate irrelevant weights. The authors prove that this approach achieves a global minimum at finite network size, and develop a novel training algorithm (AdaRad) to implement their framework. The empirical results demonstrate promising performance on MNIST and rectangles images datasets, with nonparametric networks substantially outperforming parametric networks of the same size on MNIST while using significantly fewer parameters.

## 2. Decision
Accept with minor revisions.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper addresses a significant problem in deep learning: the computational expense of model selection through global search over network sizes. The theoretical foundation (Theorem 1) is sound and well-motivated, providing justification for why the nonparametric approach can converge to a finite-size solution. The empirical results are compelling on two of the three datasets tested (MNIST and rectangles images), showing that nonparametric networks can achieve performance comparable to or better than parametric networks while using significantly fewer parameters.

The authors' approach is well-placed in the literature, building upon recent work in network pruning and dynamic network growth while making a clear theoretical contribution. The AdaRad algorithm is a well-designed extension of standard optimization techniques that properly handles the unique challenges of nonparametric training.

The main concerns are the additional computational complexity of AdaRad and the poor performance on the convex dataset. However, these represent opportunities for improvement rather than fundamental flaws in the core idea.

## 4. Additional Feedback
To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Quantify the computational overhead of AdaRad compared to standard optimizers (SGD, Adam, RMSprop). The paper mentions that AdaRad has a better scaling behavior than RMSprop, but provides no concrete numbers on the additional operations or time required per epoch.

2. Provide running-time experiments showing how the training time of nonparametric networks compares to parametric networks of similar performance on the same datasets. This is critical for assessing the practical utility of the approach.

3. Include a more detailed analysis of the poor performance on the convex dataset. The paper notes that nonparametric networks yielded very small networks that underperformed parametric networks of the same size, but does not explain why this occurs. This is particularly important as the convex dataset is the simplest of the three tested.

4. Clarify the relationship between the regularization parameter λ and the network size. The paper states that λ determines how long units have to learn before being eliminated, but a more precise explanation would help readers understand how to tune this parameter.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. Could you provide specific numbers on the additional computational cost of AdaRad compared to standard optimizers? How many additional operations per epoch does AdaRad require, and how does this scale with network size?

2. On the convex dataset, the nonparametric networks converged to very small network sizes that underperformed parametric networks of the same size. Why does the method fail to find a sufficiently large network for this dataset, and how might this limitation be addressed?

3. The paper states that "the best nonparametric network had the globally best performance" on the rectangles images dataset, but the performance was achieved with a substantially smaller network. How does the performance of these small nonparametric networks compare to parametric networks trained for the same number of epochs (not just the same size)?
