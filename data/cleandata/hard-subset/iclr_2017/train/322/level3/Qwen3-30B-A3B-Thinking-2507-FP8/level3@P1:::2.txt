

The paper introduces a compelling framework for automatically determining neural network size during training, eliminating the need for expensive hyperparameter search. The authors propose "nonparametric neural networks" that dynamically grow and shrink by adding and removing units during training, using a novel fan-in or fan-out regularization with L2 penalty to ensure theoretical convergence to a finite network size. The core contribution is a proof that with proper regularization, the optimization problem achieves a global minimum at finite dimensionality (Theorem 1), which is theoretically sound and practically significant. The authors also introduce AdaRad, a novel training algorithm that handles the dynamic network structure by normalizing gradients in radial-angular coordinates, and CapNorm, a variant of batch normalization that works with their regularization scheme.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution with Theorem 1, which is well-motivated and proven. The experimental results demonstrate clear advantages over parametric networks on MNIST and rectangles datasets, with nonparametric networks achieving better performance with significantly fewer parameters. The approach effectively addresses the key limitation of black-box model selection methods (which require multiple full training runs) by finding optimal network size during a single training cycle.

The main concerns for improvement relate to clarity and depth of discussion. The theoretical contribution is strong but the explanation of Theorem 1 could be more intuitive for readers without deep mathematical backgrounds. The paper should provide a plain-English explanation of the key insight beyond the formal proof, particularly why the L2 fan-in/fan-out regularization prevents infinite growth while still allowing network adaptation. The paper also needs clearer discussion of why nonparametric networks sometimes outperform parametric networks of the same final size (as seen in Figure 2), which is a critical finding that needs better explanation.

I also request clarification on several implementation details: First, the claim that adding zero units changes the regularizer value needs justification, as adding a zero vector to an L2 norm should not change the value. Second, the paper should clarify that a "zero unit" requires both fan-in and fan-out to be zero to preserve network output, not just one. Third, the relationship between lambda and the final network structure needs more discussion - can lambda be predicted from data, or does it primarily determine the network's final architecture? Finally, the paper should investigate sensitivity to initialization: do different random starts yield different final network structures, and how different are these structures?

The experimental analysis in Section 4.2 is particularly valuable, showing the dynamic network growth process, but the paper would benefit from a controlled experiment comparing nonparametric networks to parametric networks of the exact same final size (not just size at convergence) to demonstrate the efficiency of the adaptive approach. This would better isolate the contribution of the dynamic growth mechanism from the final network size.

I have a few specific questions for the authors: 
1. How does the choice of lambda relate to the final network structure, and can it be determined without hyperparameter search? 
2. Is there a theoretical reason why the fan-in regularization works better than fan-out for the networks tested? 
3. Would combining both fan-in and fan-out regularizers preserve the theoretical guarantee of Theorem 1? 
4. How sensitive is the approach to the choice of self-similar nonlinearity (ReLU vs. others)?

The paper makes a significant contribution to the problem of automatic neural network architecture design, with strong theoretical grounding and promising empirical results. The authors have demonstrated that network size can be optimized during training without requiring multiple full training runs, which has important implications for efficiency. The theoretical result is particularly valuable as it provides a mathematical foundation for the approach, addressing a key concern about unbounded network growth. With the requested clarifications and additional discussion, this paper would make an excellent contribution to the field.
