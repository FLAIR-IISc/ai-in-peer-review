

This paper introduces a novel framework for automatically determining optimal neural network size during training, termed "nonparametric neural networks." The authors propose a method that dynamically grows and prunes network units during training via a novel optimization algorithm (AdaRad) and a specialized regularization scheme (fan-in `2 regularization). The key insight is that by adding zero-initialized units and applying sparse regularization, the network can effectively search the space of possible architectures during a single training process, eliminating redundant units while retaining useful ones. The framework is theoretically grounded with a proof that guarantees the existence of a global minimum for the training error at finite network sizes under their regularization scheme.

I recommend rejection. The core problem addressed is highly relevant and important, but the paper fails to convincingly demonstrate that the proposed approach offers significant practical advantages over existing methods. The computational overhead of the AdaRad algorithm, which requires decomposing weight vectors into parallel and orthogonal components and converting to radial-angular coordinates, is substantial and not adequately justified. The paper lacks concrete experiments quantifying this overhead compared to standard parametric networks, which is critical for assessing practical utility.

The empirical results are mixed and require deeper analysis. On MNIST and rectangles images datasets, nonparametric networks achieve competitive performance with parametric networks of comparable size, sometimes outperforming them. However, on the convex dataset, the method consistently underperforms parametric networks and converges to unnecessarily small network sizes. This negative result needs explanation, especially since the convex dataset is a standard benchmark for model selection methods. The authors should investigate why the framework fails on this dataset and whether the failure is due to the problem's nature or limitations in their approach.

The theoretical contribution is interesting but limited in scope. The proof of Theorem 1 is mathematically sound but only applies to networks with self-similar nonlinearities (like ReLU) and specific regularization schemes. The paper does not discuss how their framework would extend to more complex architectures or different types of networks. The connection to existing work on network pruning and architecture search (e.g., Molchanov et al. 2017, Wei et al. 2016) is superficial, and the paper does not convincingly position itself as a meaningful advancement over these approaches.

The AdaRad algorithm is the most innovative aspect of the paper, but its claimed benefits are not substantiated. The authors argue that radial-angular coordinate updates enable better learning dynamics, but they fail to provide compelling evidence that this is superior to standard adaptive gradient methods like Adam or RMSprop. The computational complexity analysis in Table 1 is incomplete, as it only considers memory requirements without addressing the per-iteration computational cost of the radial-angular decomposition.

To improve the paper, the authors should:
1. Conduct thorough runtime experiments comparing AdaRad with standard optimizers, including per-iteration computational cost
2. Provide a detailed analysis of the convex dataset failure, investigating whether it's related to the dataset characteristics or the framework's limitations
3. Better position the work against existing architecture search and pruning methods, clearly articulating the advantages over these approaches
4. Explore whether their framework can be extended to more complex architectures (e.g., convolutional networks)
5. Include ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component (unit addition, pruning, AdaRad)

I have several questions to clarify the paper's contributions:
1. How does the computational overhead of AdaRad scale with network size, and what is the actual time cost compared to standard optimizers on comparable hardware?
2. Why does the framework consistently converge to small networks on the convex dataset, and how does this relate to the dataset's properties?
3. Could the framework be combined with existing architecture search methods to further improve performance, and if so, what would be the benefits?
4. How would the framework perform on larger-scale vision tasks like CIFAR-10 or ImageNet, where network size and efficiency are particularly important?
