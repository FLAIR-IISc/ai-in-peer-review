

# Review

The paper introduces "nonparametric neural networks," a framework for automatically determining the optimal network size during a single training cycle. The authors propose dynamically adding and removing units via zeroing input/output weights with group-sparsity regularization, proving theoretically that a finite-unit network can minimize the loss under this framework. They introduce AdaRad, a novel optimization algorithm, and report promising results on small datasets where their approach produces smaller networks with competitive or better accuracy than parametric networks of the same size.

I recommend rejection. The key reasons are: (1) the theoretical results do not guarantee avoidance of over- or under-fitting, and (2) the experimental evaluation lacks comparison against established pruning/compression baselines, making it unclear how competitive the method is with state-of-the-art size-reduction techniques.

The theoretical contribution is conceptually elegant but incomplete. Theorem 1 shows that a finite-unit network can minimize the loss, but doesn't address whether the algorithm reliably converges to the optimal size without overfitting or underfitting. The experiments are limited to small datasets and don't compare against pruning methods like those in Molchanov et al. (2017) or Wen et al. (2016), which is critical for establishing the method's practical value. The paper also fails to address scalability concerns for large networks, where the unit addition/removal process could significantly slow training.

Additional feedback: The authors should conduct comprehensive experiments comparing against established pruning/compression methods, provide more detailed analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity (particularly the trade-off between model size and training time), and include larger-scale experiments to demonstrate practical utility. The paper would benefit from a clearer discussion of how this approach differs from and improves upon existing dynamic network size methods like those in Wei et al. (2016) and Chen et al. (2016).

To strengthen the paper, the authors should address these gaps before considering acceptance.
