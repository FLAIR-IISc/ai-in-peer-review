## Review of "NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS"

This paper introduces a novel framework for dynamically adjusting neural network size during training, termed “nonparametric neural networks.” The core idea – allowing the network to grow and shrink based on data rather than pre-defined hyperparameters – is a compelling one, and I agree with Reviewer 2 that this is an interesting direction. The theoretical result demonstrating that, with appropriate regularization (fan-in or fan-out), the error optimum utilizes a finite number of parameters is significant and provides a strong foundation for the approach.

I recommend **accepting** this paper with a request for major revisions addressing the points below. The work presents a valuable contribution to the field, but several aspects require clarification and further investigation to fully appreciate its potential.

The paper’s strength lies in its theoretical grounding. However, the discussion surrounding Theorem 1 feels somewhat terse. While the proof is provided, a more intuitive explanation of *why* this finiteness property holds would be beneficial. The connection to Lasso/Elastic Net, mentioned briefly, could be expanded upon to provide a clearer analogy for readers familiar with those techniques. I request the authors to elaborate on the key insight of the theorem beyond the formal proof.

An important empirical question arises: how does the performance of these adaptive networks compare to fixed-size networks with the *same final size*? Currently, the comparison is to networks of varying sizes. A dedicated experiment demonstrating the efficiency gains (performance vs. memory) of the adaptive approach would strengthen the paper considerably. 

Figure 2 is currently unclear and presents mixed results. The authors should clarify the presentation and discuss why nonparametric networks sometimes outperform parametric ones even when the final network size is known. This seems counterintuitive and warrants a detailed explanation.

Several technical questions remain. First, the claim that adding zero units changes the regularizer value (e.g., L2 norm with zeros) needs justification. My understanding is that adding a unit with all zero weights should not affect the regularization term. Second, the definition of a “zero unit” needs clarification. A removable unit should have *both* fan-in and fan-out weights equal to zero to preserve the network output, and this should be explicitly stated. 

Finally, I have questions regarding the practical aspects of the method. Is the regularization parameter λ predictable from the data? Does λ primarily determine the final network structure? How sensitive is the approach to initialization? Do different random starts yield significantly different final network structures? What happens if fan-in and fan-out regularizers are combined – does the theoretical result still hold?

Addressing these points will significantly improve the clarity, rigor, and impact of this promising work. I look forward to seeing a revised version of this paper.
