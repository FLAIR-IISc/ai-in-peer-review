## Review of "Nonparametric Neural Networks"

This paper tackles the important problem of automatically determining neural network size during training, avoiding the costly global search typically required. The core idea – randomly adding zero units and applying a sparse regularizer – is well-motivated, drawing parallels to random search within a discrete architecture space, but aided by regularization to prune irrelevant units. The empirical results are promising, demonstrating competitive performance on benchmark datasets, particularly MNIST and rectangles images.

**Decision: Reject**

While the approach is interesting and the paper presents valuable insights, I recommend rejection due to concerns regarding computational complexity and a puzzling underperformance on the convex dataset. The authors introduce a significant overhead with the decomposition of fan-in weights into radial/orthogonal components and the subsequent coordinate transformation. Without a clear quantification of this additional computational burden compared to standard parametric networks, and without running-time experiments, it’s difficult to assess the practical viability of the proposed method.

The underperformance on the convex dataset is particularly concerning. The authors acknowledge this, but a more thorough explanation is needed. Convex datasets are often used as a “sanity check” for optimization algorithms, and the observed result suggests a potential issue with the method's ability to navigate simpler landscapes effectively. Is this a limitation of the regularization scheme, the optimization algorithm, or a specific interaction with the dataset's properties?

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper’s strength lies in its novel approach to dynamic network sizing. The theoretical justification, establishing conditions for achieving a global minimum, is a positive contribution. However, the practical impact of this theory is unclear without a detailed analysis of computational cost. The empirical evaluation, while encouraging, lacks a comprehensive comparison to established methods in terms of training time and resource utilization. The analysis of the training process (Section 4.2) is insightful, but doesn’t fully address the computational concerns.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Computational Complexity:** Please provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of AdaRad compared to standard gradient descent and other adaptive optimizers (e.g., Adam, RMSprop). Include a table summarizing the number of operations per iteration. Running-time experiments on different datasets and network sizes would be highly valuable.
*   **Convex Dataset:** Investigate the reasons for the underperformance on the convex dataset. Experiment with different regularization strengths, learning rates, and network architectures. Consider whether the method is overly aggressive in pruning units on this dataset.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Analyze the sensitivity of the method to the choice of hyperparameters (αr, αφ, β, ν).
*   **Scalability:** While the poker dataset experiment is a good start, further investigation of scalability to larger, more complex datasets would be beneficial.
*   **Algorithm 2 (AdaRad-M):** The paper mentions AdaRad-M but doesn’t present comparative results. Was there a reason for not including these results?



I believe addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its potential impact.
