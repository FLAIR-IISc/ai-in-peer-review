## Review of "NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS"

This paper introduces a novel framework, Nonparametric Neural Networks (NNNs), for dynamically adjusting network size during training. The core idea – continuously adding and removing units based on weight magnitudes, guided by group sparsity regularization – is conceptually elegant and addresses the challenging problem of automatic network architecture search. The theoretical result demonstrating that a finite-unit network can minimize loss under certain conditions is a valuable contribution. The proposed AdaRad optimization algorithm appears well-suited to this dynamic environment.

**Decision: Reject.** While the paper presents an interesting and potentially impactful idea, the empirical validation is currently insufficient to support the claims of improved performance and practicality. The lack of comparison to established network compression/pruning techniques is a significant weakness.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The primary concern is the limited scope of the experiments. While the results on MNIST and the poker dataset are encouraging, they are not comprehensive enough to demonstrate the generalizability and competitiveness of NNNs. The poker dataset experiment is a positive step towards scalability, but more extensive testing on diverse datasets is crucial. 

Furthermore, the reliance on hyperparameters controlling both network size and training speed (add-frequency, decay rates) introduces a potential bottleneck. Optimizing these parameters appears non-trivial and could negate the benefits of automatic network sizing. The paper acknowledges this, but doesn’t offer concrete solutions. 

The theoretical guarantee of a finite-unit solution doesn’t preclude overfitting or underfitting, and the experiments don't convincingly demonstrate robust performance in avoiding these issues. The absence of comparisons to standard pruning or compression methods leaves the reader wondering how NNNs stack up against existing, well-optimized techniques.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Baseline Comparisons:** A thorough comparison with state-of-the-art pruning (e.g., magnitude pruning, lottery ticket hypothesis) and compression techniques (e.g., knowledge distillation, quantization) is essential.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A more detailed analysis of the sensitivity of the results to the key hyperparameters (add-frequency, decay rates) would be valuable. Perhaps an automated hyperparameter optimization strategy could be explored.
*   **Scalability Analysis:** Investigate the computational cost of training NNNs on larger, more complex datasets. The current experiments don't fully address scalability concerns.
*   **Theoretical Discussion:** Expand the discussion of the limitations of the theoretical result. Under what conditions might the network still overfit or underfit?
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct ablation studies to assess the contribution of different components of the framework (e.g., the CapNorm layer, the AdaRad algorithm).

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   What is the computational overhead of dynamically adding and removing units during training compared to training a fixed-size network?
*   How does the performance of NNNs change as the regularization strength (λ) is varied? Is there a clear relationship between λ and the final network size?
*   Have you explored alternative methods for controlling the addition/removal of units, such as reinforcement learning or evolutionary algorithms?
*   What strategies could be employed to automate the tuning of the hyperparameters that control network size and training speed?
*   Could you elaborate on the potential challenges of applying this framework to more complex architectures, such as convolutional neural networks?
