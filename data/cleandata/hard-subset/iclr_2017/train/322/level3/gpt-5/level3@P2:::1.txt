Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes “nonparametric neural networks,” a framework that learns the width of a feed-forward network during a single training run. It does so by continuously adding zero units (f-equivalent expansions) and eliminating units via group sparsity (fan-in or fan-out ℓp penalties, with a focus on p=2), avoiding expensive black-box architecture searches.
- A core theoretical result (Theorem 1) shows that, under a fan-in or fan-out ℓp regularizer (1 ≤ p < ∞, λ > 0), the nonparametric objective attains a global minimum at finite dimensionality, establishing well-posedness and preventing unbounded growth.
- The paper introduces two training components: (i) CapNorm, a modified batch normalization compatible with weight shrinkage that avoids neutralizing the regularizer; and (ii) AdaRad, an optimization method that decomposes gradients into radial (length-changing) and angular (direction-changing) components per fan-in, normalizing only the angular component so new units can learn without being prematurely zeroed by the regularizer. A momentum variant (AdaRad-M) is also discussed.
- Empirically, on MNIST and Rectangles-Images, nonparametric training often matches or exceeds parametric networks of the same final size, and can approach the best results found by extensive random search while using fewer parameters. On a large-scale dataset (Poker), the method scales and performs competitively, even achieving zero test errors in one setting. A detailed analysis of the growth–prune dynamics is provided.

Decision: Weak accept
- Reason 1: The paper tackles an important problem (automatic width selection) with a principled framework and a nontrivial theoretical guarantee; the training recipe (CapNorm + AdaRad) is innovative and addresses a real optimization tension.
- Reason 2: Experiments show the approach can find compact, accurate models and sometimes outperform matched-size parametric baselines; the large-scale experiment demonstrates feasibility beyond toy datasets.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: Automatically selecting width during training is a long-standing challenge typically addressed by costly outer-loop searches or pruning from a strong seed model. The proposed approach is well-motivated and well-positioned relative to pruning, hyperparameter optimization, and network-growth literature. The construct of f-equivalence via zero units is clean and allows discrete topology changes without disrupting function values.
- Theory: Theorem 1 addresses a key concern (degeneracy to infinite size) and, together with the detailed lemmas, appears technically sound. Proposition 1 clarifies why self-similar nonlinearities (e.g., ReLU) are especially convenient, reducing per-layer regularization tuning to a single parameter via rescaling.
- Method: CapNorm is a sensible and minimal modification to preserve the effect of regularization. AdaRad’s radial–angular decomposition directly targets the incompatibility between gradient normalization and group shrinkage; the idea of normalizing only the orthogonal component is elegant.
- Empirics: The protocol is careful (validation-driven hyperparameter selection, matched-size baselines, and random-search comparisons). The analysis section gives insight into how and when units survive or are eliminated, which increases confidence that the mechanism behaves as intended. The Poker result supports scalability.

Main concerns (tempering the recommendation)
- Computational overhead: AdaRad requires per-unit decompositions into parallel/orthogonal components and angular updates in radial–angular coordinates. While the paper provides an operation-count comparison and claims better scaling than RMSprop in some respects, the lack of wall-clock runtime and memory measurements (vs. standard optimizers and batchnorm baselines) makes it hard to gauge practical cost–benefit.
- Robustness/underperformance: On the Convex dataset the method converges to small networks and underperforms matched-size parametrics. This suggests a sensitivity to the trade-off between growth and shrinkage (λ, αr, αφ, unit-addition rate) and possibly a bias toward overly compact models in some regimes. The paper would be stronger with an explanation and mitigation strategies (e.g., adaptive λ, different schedules, or diagnostics to detect premature shrinkage).

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not necessarily decisive)
- Report wall-clock time, throughput, and peak memory on all datasets, and break down the cost attributed to AdaRad vs. SGD/RMSprop/Adam. Include results with equivalent compute budgets for parametric vs. nonparametric runs.
- Provide ablations: CapNorm vs. batchnorm vs. none; AdaRad vs. RMSprop/Adam (with regularizer-aware normalization if applicable); p=1 vs. p=2; fan-in vs. fan-out penalties; unit addition frequency and initialization scale. Some partial comparisons are in the text; a consolidated ablation table would clarify what drives gains.
- Fairness of baselines: Ensure the parametric random-search baselines are limited to similar total compute as the nonparametric procedure, or report performance as a function of compute.
- Clarify sensitivity and guidance for αr, αφ, λ, and unit-addition rate. The heuristic αr ≈ 150/λ is interesting; formalize or justify it empirically.
- Discuss applicability beyond MLPs: how to extend to convolutional layers and depth selection (briefly mentioned in conclusions). Even preliminary conv experiments would broaden impact.
- Terminology: “Nonparametric” may be confusing since the approach remains finite-parametric during training. A short note aligning the terminology with classical nonparametrics would help.
- Theory scope: Theorem 1’s assumptions are broad; give concrete examples of admissible nonlinearities and losses (beyond ReLU/softmax) and note any corner cases where CapNorm might affect the assumptions (e.g., distributional shifts in pre-activation scales).
- Visualization: Add plots of unit birth/death rates vs. training progress and distribution of fan-in norms to summarize the growth–prune dynamics across runs.

Questions for the authors
- Runtime/memory: What is the wall-clock overhead of AdaRad + CapNorm vs. RMSprop/Adam + batchnorm for the same model and dataset? Please provide numbers for MNIST, Rectangles, Convex, and Poker, including GPU utilization and peak memory.
- Sensitivity: How sensitive are final size and accuracy to λ, αr, αφ, unit addition rate/frequency, and initial widths? Can you provide response surfaces or at least 1D sweeps and recommendations?
- Convex dataset: Why does the method shrink too aggressively there? Did adaptive λ schedules, different unit-addition rates, or fan-out regularization improve results?
- CapNorm: Can you empirically show that CapNorm preserves the intended effect of the group penalty (e.g., compare distributions of fan-in norms under CapNorm vs. batchnorm)? Are there cases where σ < 1 frequently, weakening the intended separation of radial vs. angular updates?
- Comparisons: If you run nonparametric training to select the size and then retrain a parametric network of that size with the best parametric optimizer and batchnorm, how close is the performance? This would isolate the value of architecture adaptation from optimizer-specific effects.
- Extensions: Any preliminary results for convolutional layers or adaptive depth? Does the radial–angular decomposition generalize cleanly to convolutional kernels (fan-in per output channel)?
