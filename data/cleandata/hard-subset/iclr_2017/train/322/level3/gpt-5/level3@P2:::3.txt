Summary and contributions
- The paper tackles automatic network sizing during a single training run. It proposes “nonparametric neural networks” that can grow and shrink width on the fly by adding zero-output units and removing units whose fan-in (or fan-out) is driven to zero by a group-regularizer.
- A main theoretical result shows that, with fan-in or fan-out ℓp penalties (1 ≤ p < ∞), the global training objective attains its minimum at a finite dimensionality, ensuring the formulation is well-posed (no incentive to grow unboundedly).
- The paper introduces two training components tailored to this setting: (i) CapNorm, a variant of batch normalization that avoids neutralizing the effect of the penalty; and (ii) AdaRad, an optimizer that decomposes gradients into radial vs. angular components to balance learning of new units against shrinkage pressure.
- Empirically, the method is demonstrated on MNIST, rectangles-images, convex, and a larger Poker dataset. On two datasets, it can match or outperform parametric counterparts and sometimes produce smaller models; on convex it underperforms. The analysis includes insightful diagnostics of unit dynamics.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) Empirical validation is not yet convincing: comparisons omit strong pruning/compression/growing baselines and results are mixed across datasets; scalability and wall-clock efficiency are not demonstrated clearly. 2) Despite the “nonparametric” framing, several new hyperparameters (e.g., addition rate/frequency, radial/angular step sizes) implicitly control the final width and training dynamics, and their tuning burden and sensitivity are not resolved.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The problem (choosing network size online rather than via black-box search) is important. Framing width selection as optimization over architectures with a principled regularizer is appealing and well-motivated relative to prior pruning and model-selection work. The theoretical result that the objective admits a finite optimum under fan-in/out group regularization is neat and addresses a central concern.
- Positioning: The paper discusses related pruning, growing, and hyperparameter optimization literature. However, experimental baselines do not include the most relevant structured sparsity/pruning methods (e.g., group-lasso sparsity for neurons, recent pruning with regrowth, dynamic network surgery) or knowledge-distillation/compression pipelines, so practical competitiveness remains unclear.
- Claims vs. evidence:
  - “Single-run architecture selection”: In practice, the method still requires multiple hyperparameters that significantly affect final width and speed (λ, αr, αφ, addition rate/frequency), and the paper provides limited sensitivity analysis. Thus the burden of search is shifted rather than removed.
  - “Improved accuracy and compactness”: On MNIST and rectangles-images, there are encouraging results (sometimes smaller with similar or better accuracy). On convex, the method under-sizes and underperforms; this highlights a risk that the mechanism converges to too-small networks. Without stronger baselines or more diverse tasks (e.g., CIFAR, ImageNet-scale conv nets), it’s hard to conclude general utility.
  - “Efficiency”: Growing from scratch incurs extra training time (units must be added then pruned). While the Poker experiment suggests the growth overhead becomes a smaller fraction of total training on large datasets, the paper does not report wall-clock time-to-target-accuracy or memory/compute profiles vs. strong parametric and pruning baselines. The optimizer’s per-iteration cost is analyzed qualitatively, but practical runtime comparisons are missing.
- Methodology details: CapNorm and AdaRad are thoughtful contributions tailored to the setting, and the analysis of unit life-cycles is insightful. However, it is difficult to separate the benefits of the nonparametric framework from the optimizer/normalization choices (no ablations isolate AdaRad vs. RMSProp/Adam under the same regularizer).

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Include strong, targeted baselines: neuron-level group-lasso (fan-in/out) with fixed width; modern pruning with potential regrowth; Net2Net-style growth; dynamic network surgery; and post-training compression/distillation pipelines. Report both accuracy and compression/latency/energy.
- Provide wall-clock and compute metrics: time-to-target-accuracy, total FLOPs, memory footprint during training and at inference, and hardware-efficiency considerations (dense vs. sparse).
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: Systematically study how λ, αr, αφ, addition rate, and addition schedule affect final size, accuracy, and training time. If possible, propose heuristics or controllers to adapt these during training (e.g., validation-driven growth/shrink schedules).
- Broaden benchmarks: Include convolutional architectures on CIFAR-10/100 or ImageNet-scale subsets, where width selection is practically relevant. Evaluate stability across random seeds and datasets (variance of final widths).
- Ablations: Isolate the contributions of AdaRad and CapNorm. Compare with standard optimizers under the same regularizer and with/without CapNorm. Examine p = 1 vs. p = 2 group penalties and fan-in vs. fan-out choices.
- Clarify theory–practice link: The existence theorem is reassuring but does not guide the dynamics of growth/shrink under stochastic training. Any partial guarantees (e.g., monotonicity of objective relative to add/remove moves) or diagnostics to detect under/over-shrinking would help.

Questions for the authors
1) Hyperparameter control: How sensitive are final width and accuracy to λ, αr, αφ, addition rate/frequency? Can you provide sensitivity plots and recommended defaults? Could λ be adapted online (e.g., via validation) to mitigate under/over-fitting?
2) Addition policy: Have you tried data- or gradient-driven criteria for adding units (beyond fixed-rate addition), and do they help on datasets like convex where under-sizing occurs?
3) Baselines: How does your method compare against neuron-level group-lasso in a fixed-width net (same penalty, no growth) and against modern pruning with regrowth? Please include accuracy, final width, and training/inference time.
4) Efficiency: What is the wall-clock time-to-target-accuracy vs. a well-tuned parametric model and vs. pruning pipelines that start from an overparameterized model? How does AdaRad’s runtime compare to Adam/RMSProp in practice?
5) CapNorm: Can you show ablations quantifying CapNorm’s effect vs. BN (with/without affine params) under your penalty? Does CapNorm ever harm accuracy on deeper or convolutional networks?
6) Conv/deeper nets: Have you experimented with convolutional layers and deeper architectures? Does the finite-minimum theorem and training recipe carry over without modification?
7) Stability: How variable is the final unit count across seeds for the same λ and schedule? Can you quantify variance and provide diagnostics to detect premature shrinkage?

Overall, the paper presents an elegant idea with a solid theoretical underpinning and interesting training machinery, but the empirical evidence and baselines are not yet sufficient to establish practical impact.
