Summary and contributions
This paper proposes “nonparametric neural networks,” a framework that optimizes over network width during a single training run by dynamically adding units and removing them when their fan-in or fan-out becomes zero under an lp (primarily l2) group penalty. The authors prove a core existence result: with a fan-in or fan-out lp penalty (λ > 0, 1 ≤ p < ∞), the training objective attains a global minimum at finite width. They also introduce two training components tailored to this setting: (i) CapNorm, a variant of batch normalization that avoids negating the shrinkage effect of the regularizer by capping scaling when the minibatch std < 1 and removing affine re-scaling; and (ii) AdaRad, an optimization method that decomposes the gradient into radial and angular components per unit’s fan-in, normalizing only the angular component to let new units learn without overwhelming shrinkage. With self-similar nonlinearities (e.g., ReLU), they further show that per-layer regularization strengths can be consolidated into one effective λ up to rescaling. Experiments on MNIST, rectangles-images, convex, and a large OpenML poker dataset show that the method can converge to a compact final architecture and, on some tasks, outperform parametric networks of the same size; it sometimes even approaches the best models found by black-box searches while using far fewer parameters. The paper is well written, conceptually elegant, and connects naturally to lifelong-learning and resource-adaptive training.

Decision: Reject
Primary reasons:
- Empirical evidence is not yet sufficient: key comparisons to strong pruning/compression/growing baselines are missing, and results are mixed (notably weak on convex).
- Practicality is unclear: the method introduces several new hyperparameters that effectively control model size and training dynamics; scalability and training-time overheads are not convincingly resolved.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: Automatically choosing width in one training run is timely and well-motivated. The proposed add/remove mechanism, coupled with a group penalty, is coherent and better founded than ad-hoc pruning. The theorem establishing existence of a finite-width minimizer under lp fan-in/out regularization is a meaningful theoretical contribution for the training objective.
- Soundness vs. generalization: The theorem pertains to the training objective, not generalization. It does not prevent under- or over-fitting; the method may converge to small models that sacrifice accuracy (as seen on convex). The paper acknowledges this but does not provide strategies to balance size and accuracy beyond tuning λ and the add/remove schedule.
- Novelty of training components: AdaRad’s per-fan-in radial/angular decomposition is interesting and practically motivated by the incompatibility of standard adaptive methods with group shrinkage for newborn units. CapNorm is a reasonable tweak to BatchNorm for this setting. However, ablations are limited: it remains unclear how much gain comes from the nonparametric mechanism versus AdaRad/CapNorm, and whether standard optimizers carefully configured could match performance.
- Empirical support: Results are encouraging on MNIST and rectangles-images, and strong on poker, but poor on convex. Comparisons are primarily to parametric networks of the same size and to black-box searches over parametric models. Missing are comparisons against:
  - Training with group-lasso or structured sparsity from scratch and pruning after training (e.g., Molchanov et al. 2017; Alvarez & Salzmann 2016; Dynamic Network Surgery).
  - Growing methods (e.g., Wei et al. 2016) or Net2Net growth for fair speed/accuracy tradeoffs.
  - Modern compression/pruning pipelines that target small, accurate models.
  Without these, it is hard to gauge competitiveness as a practical approach to model-size selection and compression.
- Practicality and hyperparameters: The approach introduces new controls (radial/angular step sizes, add rate and frequency, moving-average rates, thresholds), many of which implicitly determine final size and training time. The paper offers heuristics (e.g., αr tied to λ), but sensitivity analyses are limited, and automated tuning is left as future work. The gradual growth strategy can delay convergence substantially; while the poker experiment suggests the relative overhead diminishes on very large datasets, end-to-end wall-clock comparisons (for comparable validation accuracy) are missing.
- Scope: The work focuses on fully connected nets with ReLU; convolutional/deeper nets are deferred. That limits evidence for broad applicability.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Add strong baselines: Compare against magnitude pruning, group-lasso/structured sparsity training, Dynamic Network Surgery, and growth-based methods (e.g., Net2Net, Wei et al.). Include end-to-end compute (GPU-hours), wall-clock, and final model size.
- Provide ablations: (i) AdaRad vs RMSprop/Adam within the same nonparametric framework; (ii) CapNorm vs BatchNorm-without-affine vs no normalization; (iii) fan-in vs fan-out penalties; (iv) p=1 vs p=2; (v) effect of the unit-addition rate/frequency and αr/αφ. Clarify how much each component contributes.
- Scalability: Report training time and memory across datasets, along with the overhead of add/remove operations and AdaRad. Include convergence speed vs final accuracy curves compared to training a fixed, larger net with early stopping.
- Robustness and tuning: Provide guidance or an adaptive controller for ν, νfreq, αr, αφ, and λ (e.g., validation-driven schedules). Explore automated λ annealing and per-layer λ despite the self-similarity proposition.
- Generalization: Investigate whether the method tends to under-size on challenging tasks (as on convex), and whether relaxing regularization late or adapting αr mitigates this.
- Extension to convnets: Even a small-scale CNN experiment would strengthen claims of generality.

Questions for the authors
1) How sensitive is final model size and accuracy to αr, αφ, ν, and νfreq? Can you provide quantitative sensitivity plots and a recommended tuning protocol?
2) Can you report wall-clock time and compute for nonparametric training vs best parametric baselines at matched validation accuracy, including the cost of black-box search when applicable?
3) How does your method compare empirically to structured sparsity training and pruning baselines (e.g., group-lasso, Molchanov et al., Dynamic Network Surgery) in terms of accuracy, size, and time?
4) What thresholding strategy do you use to detect “zero units” in finite precision? Does the result depend on an arbitrary tolerance, and how does that affect reproducibility?
5) Do AdaRad and CapNorm independently improve performance in the nonparametric setting, or could standard optimizers and (capped) BatchNorm variants suffice?
6) On convex, why does the approach converge to small underperforming models? Can adaptive λ or late unfreezing (λ→0) reliably recover accuracy?
7) What are the prospects and concrete modifications needed to handle convolutional layers and deeper networks? Can per-channel group penalties and fan-in/out decomposition be applied efficiently?
8) Have you explored p=1 vs p=2 empirically for model size vs accuracy vs training speed tradeoffs?
