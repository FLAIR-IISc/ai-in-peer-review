1. Summary of contributions
The paper proposes nonparametric neural networks that dynamically grow and shrink layer width during a single training run by adding zero-units and removing units whose entire fan-in (or fan-out) is driven to zero via group-sparsity (`2 over unit groups). A core theorem establishes that, under fan-in/out regularization, a global minimum is attained at finite width. The authors introduce two practical components: CapNorm (a batch-norm variant that preserves the effect of shrinkage) and AdaRad, an optimizer that decomposes gradients into radial and angular components to let new units learn while preserving the effect of group shrinkage. Initial experiments show encouraging behavior and suggest the method can yield smaller networks with similar accuracy, promising runtime/power/memory benefits. The paper is clearly written and conceptually elegant, with a nice fit to lifelong-learning/continual-capacity goals.

2. Decision: Reject
Primary reasons: (i) empirical support is limited and omits comparisons to strong pruning/compression or NAS baselines, so practical competitiveness is unclear; (ii) key hyperparameters implicitly control both final model size and training dynamics, leading to potential training-time blowups and difficult tuning.

3. Supporting arguments
- What problem is tackled? Learning network size during training instead of via expensive outer-loop search. This is important and timely.
- Is the approach well-motivated/placed? Yes; it connects group sparsity, functional equivalence via zero-units, and scale-aware training. However, placement w.r.t. modern structured pruning, network slimming, and resource-aware compression is underdeveloped.
- Do the results support the claims? The theory guarantees existence of a finite-width minimizer but does not address generalization or avoidance of over/under-fitting. Experiments, while helpful, are small-scale, lack hardware/resource metrics, and do not compare against strong pruning/compression/NAS methods. Moreover, hyperparameters (add frequency, λ, radial/angular steps) jointly govern size and cost, risking poor scalability on large models.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Include head-to-head comparisons with strong baselines (e.g., network slimming, structured L1/group-lasso, MorphNet/NetAdapt/AMC, modern magnitude/pruning-with-retraining, and lightweight NAS). Report accuracy, parameters, FLOPs, latency, energy, and memory on real hardware.
- Decouple hyperparameters that control capacity from those that control optimization speed; consider adaptive λ scheduling or bilevel tuning.
- Provide ablations on add/remove frequency, unit initialization, and p=1 vs p=2 group penalties.
- Demonstrate on convolutional networks and at least one large-scale task; quantify training-time overhead of AdaRad vs Adam/RMSprop.
- Discuss generalization (e.g., Rademacher/flatness proxies) and when capacity adaptation helps vs hurts.

Questions for the authors
- How do you recommend setting add-frequency, λ, and αr/αφ to avoid collapse or uncontrolled growth? Any adaptive controller?
- What is the wall-clock and memory overhead of AdaRad vs Adam/RMSprop at scale?
- Why not compare against structured pruning/compression and resource-aware methods? Do your networks remain competitive in latency/energy?
- Can you decouple hyperparameters that control size from those governing optimization to improve scalability?
- How sensitive are results to the unit initialization scheme and to choosing fan-in vs fan-out penalties?
- Can the method handle convolutional layers and learn depth (layer-level add/remove)?
