This paper proposes “nonparametric neural networks,” a framework that learns the width of each layer during a single training run by (i) optimizing over weights under a group sparsity regularizer (fan-in or fan-out, with p-norm), (ii) dynamically adding units and removing redundant ones, and (iii) introducing two practical ingredients: CapNorm (a modification of batch norm compatible with group shrinkage) and a new optimizer (AdaRad) that decouples angular and radial updates so new units can learn before being shrunk away. Theoretically, the authors prove that with a fan-in or fan-out regularizer the global training objective attains its minimum at a finite network size (Theorem 1), ensuring the model cannot grow without bound. Empirically, on MNIST, rectangles images, and a 1M-example Poker dataset, the approach often matches or exceeds strong parametric baselines; on convex, performance lags. The work is ambitious, well-motivated, and the central idea is novel and timely.

Decision: Accept
- Key reasons: (1) The finite-minimizer theorem is an elegant and useful result that grounds the formulation and distinguishes it from heuristic grow/prune methods. (2) The training scheme (AdaRad + CapNorm + add/remove units) is practical and shows promising results on both small and large datasets, advancing the “learn the model size during training” agenda.

Supporting arguments
- Problem significance: Selecting architecture size typically requires expensive black-box searches; a method that learns width during training can reduce tuning cost and potentially improve final quality by following better optimization paths.
- Motivation and literature placement: The paper is well-situated relative to pruning, dynamic growth, and hyperparameter optimization work. Key distinctions: unlike pruning-from-a-strong-seed, this method grows from scratch; unlike black-box search, it adapts in-run; unlike standard lasso-like sparsity, it focuses on unit-wise group shrinkage tied to dynamic addition/removal.
- Theoretical support: Theorem 1 (for 1 ≤ p < ∞, λ > 0) establishes existence of a global minimum at finite width for either fan-in or fan-out regularization. This provides a compelling guarantee that the nonparametric search space is well-posed. The assumptions cover common activations (σ(0)=0) and losses. The proposition on self-similar nonlinearities (e.g., ReLU) further clarifies why layer-wise λ’s can be consolidated, simplifying tuning.
- Empirical support: Results are mixed but generally positive. On MNIST and rectangles images, nonparametric nets are competitive or superior, sometimes even outperforming parametric models of the same final size, consistent with the claim that growing/shrinking during training can find better minima. On Poker, the method scales and achieves excellent accuracy. The failure on convex is a useful counterexample and suggests boundary conditions for success. Comparisons to parametric nets of the same size and to search over sizes are appreciated.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Plain-English intuition for Theorem 1: Please add a short, high-level explanation. For example: the group penalty enforces a budget on the sum of fan norms, which bounds the magnitude of effective features and, via backprop sensitivity bounds, precludes arbitrarily large layers from providing additional benefit at fixed objective value; thus a finite width suffices at the global optimum. A couple of sentences mapping main proof steps to this intuition would greatly improve accessibility.
- Clarify “zero units” and regularizer changes: A removable unit must have either zero fan-in or zero fan-out (or both) to preserve f. With Ωin, adding a unit with zero fan-in (and arbitrary fan-out) does not change Ωin, but adding with zero fan-out and random fan-in does. The paper sometimes states “adding zero units changes the regularizer” without conditioning on which regularizer is used. Please clarify this point explicitly to avoid confusion.
- Why nonparametric > parametric at same final size? Provide deeper analysis: is it due to a curriculum effect (progressive capacity), different implicit regularization, or better conditioning from CapNorm/AdaRad? An ablation that retrains the final discovered architecture from scratch under the same normalization/optimizer (as close as possible, e.g., CapNorm-compatible training) would isolate the benefit of dynamic growth vs path dependence.
- Figure 2 clarity: The takeaways are important but the figure is busy. Consider clearer legends, consistent color/marker schemes, and concise captions summarizing the main conclusions per dataset.
- Efficiency evidence: Report wall-clock, FLOPs, and peak memory for nonparametric vs best parametric runs, including overhead from unit bookkeeping and AdaRad’s caches. The discussion of scaling is good; concrete numbers would strengthen it.
- CapNorm ablation: Compare BN (with and without affine parameters), CapNorm, and perhaps LayerNorm/GroupNorm variants within your framework to quantify the necessity of capping and the effect on shrinkage dynamics.
- AdaRad ablation: Show nonparametric training with RMSprop/Adam + carefully scaled shrinkage to demonstrate why AdaRad’s radial/angular split is essential. Briefly discuss hyperparameter sensitivity (αr, αφ, β) and defaults.

Questions for the authors
1) Lambda and final structure: Empirically, how strongly does λ determine final width and performance? Can λ be predicted from data (e.g., via a rule-of-thumb based on dataset size/label noise), or adaptively tuned during training (e.g., annealing or gradient-based hyperparameter tuning)?
2) Initialization sensitivity: Across seeds, how variable are the final widths and unit-level structures? Please report dispersion (mean±std of widths, error) to assess robustness.
3) Combining fan-in and fan-out: Does Theorem 1 extend to Ω = Ωin + Ωout (or max of the two)? If not, is there a straightforward modification of the proof or additional conditions that would make it go through?
4) Zero-unit mechanism: When using Ωin, you add units with zero fan-out and random fan-in so they can learn before group shrinkage removes them. Did you test the symmetric choice (zero fan-in, random fan-out) with Ωout, and does it behave symmetrically?
5) Self-similar nonlinearities: Beyond ReLU, did you try leaky/parametric ReLU or ELU? Do they preserve the λ-equivalence proposition in practice and improve stability/accuracy?
6) Convex dataset gap: Can you diagnose the failure mode (e.g., under-capacity bias from shrinkage, optimization path, step-size interplay, CapNorm behavior in deeper layers)? Would a less aggressive λ, different unit addition schedule, or delayed shrinkage help?
7) Fairness of parametric baselines: Parametric models used (uncapped) BN with λ=0 for compatibility. Could you include variants with CapNorm or with weight decay but no BN to match the regularization dynamics more closely?

Overall, this is a well-motivated paper that blends a solid theoretical foundation with a practical training recipe. With improved clarity around the theorem’s intuition, zero-unit/regularizer interactions, and a couple of targeted ablations, it would be a strong contribution.
