Summary of contributions
- The paper tackles the important problem of selecting network size during training, aiming to avoid expensive black-box hyperparameter searches. It proposes a nonprobabilistic framework for nonparametric neural networks that grows and prunes units in a single training run.
- The core theoretical contribution is Theorem 1: with fan-in or fan-out group-regularization (Lp with 1 ≤ p < ∞), the training objective attains a global minimum at a finite network size, establishing the soundness of optimizing over unbounded architectures.
- Methodologically, the paper introduces two key ingredients to make growth/pruning work in practice: CapNorm (a capped batch normalization variant compatible with norm-based shrinkage) and AdaRad (Adaptive Radial–Angular Gradient Descent), which decomposes gradients into radial and angular components and normalizes only the angular component to prevent premature elimination of new units.
- Empirically, the approach automatically converges to reasonable sizes, sometimes outperforming parametric networks of the same size and, in one benchmark, competing with stronger black-box searches while using fewer parameters. The poker experiment demonstrates scalability to a large dataset.

Decision: Weak accept
Key reasons:
- Novel and well-motivated framework with a nontrivial theoretical guarantee and practical algorithmic tools (CapNorm, AdaRad).
- Promising empirical results and compelling analysis of the training dynamics, despite some mixed outcomes and missing wall-clock evaluations.

Supporting arguments
- Problem significance and positioning: Architecture selection is costly and widely relevant. The paper situates itself well with respect to pruning, model selection, and dynamic network growth, and proposes a principled alternative to black-box search. The f-equivalence notion and zero-unit add/remove mechanism are intuitive and connect discrete architecture changes with continuous optimization.
- Motivation and literature: The move from L1 to L2 group penalties to prune entire fan-ins/fan-outs is appropriate given current hardware and aligns with group-lasso literature. The emphasis on self-similar nonlinearities (ReLU) is well motivated and formalized (Proposition 1).
- Technical validity: Theorem 1 is carefully stated with explicit regularity conditions; the proof strategy (bounding activations/gradients and ruling out good minima for arbitrarily large widths) is sound. CapNorm’s design avoids undermining the regularizer, and AdaRad’s radial/angular decomposition is coherent with the geometry induced by group shrinkage.
- Empirical support: On MNIST and rectangles images, nonparametric models outperform comparable parametric models of the same size and can match larger models with fewer parameters. The detailed training-run analysis convincingly shows how growth and shrinkage interplay. The poker result demonstrates applicability at scale.
- Limitations: On convex, the method converges to small networks and underperforms; the paper acknowledges this but does not sufficiently explain why or how to detect/remedy such cases. Also, while a complexity table is referenced, there are no wall-clock comparisons or GPU utilization analyses; the added cost of per-fan-in projection and radial–angular updates needs empirical quantification. Finally, ablations are limited: it would help to isolate the contribution of CapNorm vs BatchNorm (without trainable params), AdaRad vs standard optimizers with appropriate handling of shrinkage, and p=2 fan-in vs fan-out.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Provide wall-clock and throughput comparisons (images/sec or examples/sec) across SGD, RMSprop/Adam, and AdaRad, on both CPU and GPU, for representative widths. Include memory overhead and scaling with width/depth. The current complexity discussion is useful but insufficient for practitioners.
- Add ablations:
  - CapNorm vs standard BatchNorm (with and without affine parameters) under group L2 shrinkage.
  - AdaRad vs Adam/RMSprop baselines adapted to preserve shrinkage (e.g., normalizing only orthogonal components, if feasible).
  - p=1 vs p=2, fan-in vs fan-out penalties, and sensitivity to λ.
  - Unit addition rate/frequency and initialization scale.
- Analyze the convex dataset failure: does the objective or CapNorm bias against wider solutions? Are there optimization barriers (e.g., angular updates too conservative) or validation-criterion interactions that favor smaller models? Include diagnostics (layerwise activation scales, fraction of eliminated units, gradient norms, and λ schedules).
- Report early-phase performance: growing nets can delay convergence; quantify the “overhead” (extra epochs to match a fixed-width baseline) and whether annealing αφ/αr, or temporarily pausing pruning, mitigates this.
- Clarify the relationship to pruning-from-scratch vs starting from large nets. Include experiments starting from a moderately large network to test whether your framework also prunes effectively in that regime.
- Discuss applicability to CNNs and residual architectures: which pieces (CapNorm, AdaRad, group penalties) transfer cleanly? A small convolutional experiment would broaden impact.

Questions for the authors
- Complexity/runtime: What is the measured wall-clock overhead of AdaRad relative to SGD and RMSprop/Adam for typical hidden sizes (e.g., 256–1024) on GPU? Please report per-iteration time, memory usage, and total time-to-target accuracy for your main benchmarks.
- Gradient decomposition: How sensitive is performance to the accuracy/stability of the parallel/orthogonal decomposition for very small fan-in norms? Do you clip, and how does ε affect results?
- CapNorm: Could you provide a controlled comparison showing that CapNorm is necessary (and sufficient) to avoid the “cancel the regularizer” failure mode? How does performance change if you allow affine BN parameters?
- Hyperparameters: How robust are αφ, αr, λ, and ν across datasets? Can you provide simple default rules (e.g., αr tied to λ) and show performance bands?
- Failure case on convex: What concrete diagnostics explain the tendency to settle on small models? Would a temporary relaxation of shrinkage, a schedule on ν, or a lower λ during growth prevent underfitting?
- Generality: Have you tried fan-out penalties, deeper MLPs, or convolutional layers? Any obstacles with AdaRad on convolutional kernels?
- Comparisons: Can you include a “prune-from-large” baseline using your L2 group penalty (no growth) to separate the benefits of dynamic growth from those of group pruning alone?
- Reproducibility: Will you release code and scripts to reproduce unit addition/removal schedules, CapNorm, and AdaRad, including the analysis plots?
