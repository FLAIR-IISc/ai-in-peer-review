Summary and contributions
- The paper tackles the important problem of selecting neural network size (number of hidden units per layer) within a single training run, avoiding costly black-box hyperparameter search over architectures.
- It introduces “nonparametric neural networks”: optimize over both weights and layer widths by (i) adding units during training and (ii) pruning units via group Lp (fan-in or fan-out) penalties that induce exact zeros. A central theoretical result (Theorem 1) shows that with fan-in or fan-out regularization (λ > 0, 1 ≤ p < ∞), the training objective attains a global minimum at a finite network size—i.e., the network cannot grow unboundedly at optimum.
- The paper proposes two practical components to make this work: (a) CapNorm (capped batch norm) to remain compatible with weight shrinkage and (b) AdaRad, an optimizer that decomposes gradients into radial (length-changing) and angular (direction-changing) components; it normalizes only the angular component to allow new units to learn while preserving the intended effect of group shrinkage on radial length.
- Empirically, on MNIST and rectangles-images the approach often matches or exceeds parametric baselines, sometimes using fewer parameters; on convex the method underperforms. A large-scale experiment on OpenML poker shows the approach scales and can achieve strong accuracy.

Decision: Accept (rating: 7)
Key reasons:
1) Novel and well-motivated framework with a clean theoretical guarantee that a finite-size optimum exists under group regularization; 2) A practical training recipe (CapNorm + AdaRad) that makes the nonparametric idea operational and shows promising results on multiple datasets, with clear analysis of dynamics.

Supporting arguments
- Problem significance: Choosing architecture size is central yet expensive; doing it in one training trajectory is impactful.
- Placement: The work bridges pruning/sparsity (Lasso/group-Lasso), model selection by black-box search, and constructive network growth. It clearly positions itself relative to pruning methods that require a good starting model, and to hyperparameter optimization.
- Theory: Theorem 1 is a strong, reassuring result. While the proof is long, assumptions are reasonable, and the proposition on self-similar nonlinearities provides a useful practical guideline (single λ per model).
- Empirics: The methodology can outperform fixed-size parametric training at the same final width, and in some settings rivals best black-box search while using fewer parameters. The detailed training analysis (e.g., survival dynamics of units) is informative. Results are mixed on convex, which is honestly reported and discussed.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not all decision-critical)
- Provide intuition for Theorem 1 in plain English. A concise explanation of why group penalties bound activations/gradients and thereby forbid unbounded dimensionality would greatly aid accessibility. A small schematic proof sketch would help.
- Clarify zero units and regularizer behavior. As written: adding/removing zero units leaves f unchanged but may change Ω. Yet under a fan-in regularizer, adding a unit with zero fan-in contributes zero to Ωin. Please reconcile: is the statement intended for the case where the nonzero fan (e.g., nonzero fan-in with zero fan-out) is used? A small table of cases (fan-in vs fan-out penalty; which vector set to zero) would remove ambiguity.
- Compare more directly to fixed-size networks with the same final architecture. You already compare to parametric nets of the same size; please also report compute/efficiency: wall-clock, number of updates to reach the same validation error, and peak parameter count during training (since the nonparametric model temporarily over-allocates units). This would quantify the practical efficiency trade-off relative to training the final size from scratch.
- Figure 2 presentation. The figure is hard to parse. Please improve labels, explicitly mark training recipe per baseline, and distinguish median/variance across seeds. A small table with final sizes, test error medians, and interquartile ranges would help.
- Why can nonparametric sometimes beat parametric even at the same final size? Please add commentary: e.g., curriculum-like growth, smoother optimization due to angular normalization, implicit regularization from unit turnover. If possible, include an ablation that retrains the final discovered architecture from scratch and compares both the final error and the speed to reach it.
- Sensitivity/robustness. You trained 10 seeds; consider reporting dispersion in final widths per layer, overlap in surviving units (e.g., cosine similarity of learned subspaces), and sensitivity to ν (unit addition rate), αr/αφ, and λ.
- Hyperparameter guidance for λ. Since λ appears to largely govern final width, provide practical heuristics (e.g., target sparsity vs λ) or an adaptive schedule. You mention future work on tuning λ online; even a simple annealing strategy baseline would be useful.
- Fan-in vs fan-out vs both. You focus on fan-in with p=2. Please discuss empirically the fan-out case and whether combining both (sum of fan-in and fan-out group penalties) helps. Does the finiteness theorem extend to the combined penalty?
- CapNorm vs BatchNorm variants. Provide an ablation comparing CapNorm to BN with frozen scale/shift (γ/β) or partial normalization, to strengthen the claim that standard BN undermines the group penalty.
- AdaRad ablations. Include comparisons to RMSprop/Adam with careful handling of the regularizer (e.g., no normalization of the shrinkage component). You mention AdaRad-M; brief quantitative results or a pointer to the appendix would complete the story.

Questions for the authors
- Theorem intuition: Can you provide a concise, high-level explanation of why the group penalty forces a finite optimal width? In particular, what is the key bottleneck that prevents “leaking” performance into infinitely many tiny units?
- Zero units and Ω: Under Ωin, adding a unit with zero fan-in contributes zero to Ωin; under Ωout, adding a unit with zero fan-out contributes zero to Ωout. In which scenarios does adding a zero unit change Ω as claimed? Please clarify the intended operational choice when adding units and its effect on Ω.
- λ selection and structure control: How predictive is λ of final width across datasets? Can you propose a rule-of-thumb to choose λ to target a desired parameter budget? Did you attempt online λ tuning (e.g., to hit a width constraint)?
- Sensitivity to initialization: How variable are the final layer widths and test errors across seeds? Do different runs converge to similar subspaces/features? Any evidence of mode collapse or brittle outcomes?
- Fan-in + fan-out: If one penalizes both (sum of group L2 across fan-ins and fan-outs), does Theorem 1 still hold? If not, what part of the proof breaks?
- CapNorm specifics: Could you report the fraction of units per layer for which σ < 1 (thus not rescaled) over training? Does CapNorm ever hinder learning for units that need larger pre-activation scales?
- Efficiency: What are the end-to-end compute and memory costs versus (i) training the final fixed-size net from scratch and (ii) a small grid/random search over widths? Please report wall-clock and number of forward/backward passes to a target validation error.
- Conv layers and depth: Do you foresee obstacles to extending the theory and AdaRad to convolutional layers and to nonparametric depth? Any preliminary results?

Overall, this is a thoughtful, well-grounded paper that opens a promising path for single-run architecture selection. With clarifications and a few additional comparisons, it will be a strong contribution.
