Summary and contributions
- The paper introduces “nonparametric neural networks,” a framework that allows the width of each hidden layer to be adapted during a single training run by adding and removing units. I agree with Reviewer 2 that automatic addition/removal is an interesting and impactful direction, reducing reliance on user priors and expensive outer-loop searches.
- Theoretical contribution: with fan-in or fan-out group-norm regularization (λ>0, 1≤p<∞), the objective achieves a global minimum at finite dimensionality (Theorem 1). This is a key result: the network cannot grow indefinitely in pursuit of lower training error.
- Practical contribution: a training recipe (CapNorm + AdaRad/AdaRad-M) that supports growth/shrinkage, plus empirical studies on MNIST, rectangles images, convex, and a large OpenML dataset.

Decision: Accept (7)
- Reason 1: Solid and novel formulation with a meaningful theoretical guarantee that addresses the core concern of unbounded growth.
- Reason 2: A practical algorithm with promising results and clear potential to save compute/memory compared to black-box model selection.

Supporting arguments
- Theory: The theorem aligns the method with group sparsity (akin to group lasso): fan-in/fan-out penalties encourage entire units to go to zero and be pruned. The “finite minimizer” result is both nontrivial and central for soundness.
- Method: The AdaRad decomposition into radial vs angular updates is a thoughtful response to the tension between normalization and regularization in nonparametric growth. CapNorm is a reasonable tweak to avoid defeating the regularizer.
- Empirics: Results are mixed across datasets but include instances where nonparametric nets match or outperform best parametric baselines at smaller sizes, supporting the promise of the approach.

Additional feedback and questions for authors (to improve the paper; not all affect the accept decision)
1) Theorem 1: intuition and placement in the literature
- Please provide a plain-English summary: What is the key mechanism by which a fan-in or fan-out group penalty guarantees a finite-width optimum? An analogy to group lasso/Elastic Net would help: the penalty bounds the “budget” per unit, and very wide networks cannot maintain nontrivial activations/gradients without incurring linear penalty cost, so beyond some width, adding units cannot reduce the objective further.
- Trade space: devote less text to sweeping prior-work lists, and more to intuition for Lemmas 2–4 (e.g., the role of bounded activations/gradients, how the box principle is used, why group penalties translate width into objective cost).
- Does a combination of fan-in and fan-out penalties (sum of both) preserve the finite-minimizer guarantee? If yes, can you state and sketch conditions; if not, provide a counterexample or discuss where the proof breaks.

2) “Zero units” and the objective
- Clarify definitions and claims to avoid confusion. A unit is functionally removable if either its fan-in is zero (activation is always zero) or its fan-out is zero (output contribution is zero), assuming σ(0)=0. However:
  - Under a fan-in penalty, adding a unit with zero fan-in and arbitrary fan-out is function-preserving and does not change the penalty (||0||=0).
  - Under a fan-out penalty, adding a unit with zero fan-out and arbitrary fan-in likewise does not change the penalty.
- The paper states that adding/removing “zero units” changes the regularizer value. This seems false for true zero units (both sides zero), and depends on which side is penalized otherwise. Please correct and make precise which case you mean. Also, when you initialize a new unit with nonzero fan-in but zero fan-out under a fan-in penalty, that is not a “zero unit” under your own definition; it changes the objective.

3) Why/when nonparametric > parametric at same final size?
- Figure 2 shows nonparametric models sometimes outperform fixed-size models even when the final net size is known. Please analyze hypotheses: better optimization path (curriculum over width), implicit regularization from shrinkage dynamics, CapNorm vs BN mismatch, different step-size schedules, or better conditioning due to angular/radial separation.
- Suggest a targeted experiment: for each trained nonparametric model, freeze the final architecture, then train the same architecture from scratch with the same normalization and optimizer (CapNorm + AdaRad), same training time/budget. Report performance vs memory and time-to-accuracy. This directly tests efficiency of the adaptive trajectory vs static training.

4) Clarity of Figure 2
- The figure is hard to parse and the story is mixed. Please standardize axes, add error bars (or 25–75% IQR) over seeds for both nonparametric and parametric models, and make the comparison pairs explicit (same final size, same optimizer/normalizer, similar training budget). A table summarizing median ± MAD over seeds would help.

5) Hyperparameter λ: role and predictability
- Empirically λ appears to largely control final structure/width (you even tie αr to λ to set a “lifetime” for new units). Could you quantify how much of the width variance across seeds is explained by λ? Provide plots of final widths vs λ with variance bands.
- Can λ be estimated from data characteristics (n, d, SNR) or adaptively tuned during training? Since Proposition 1 shows layer-wise λ equivalence for self-similar nonlinearities, a single scalar λ is attractive. Could you explore λ schedules or gradient-based hyperparameter tuning (e.g., Luketina et al.) and whether the finite-minimizer guarantee still holds under time-varying λ?

6) Sensitivity to initialization and structural variability
- You run multiple seeds but primarily show a “typical representative.” Please report across seeds: distribution of final widths per layer, test error, and the variability in the actual pruned structures (e.g., overlap statistics, correlation between early-added units and survivors). Do different seeds converge to materially different structures with similar accuracy?

7) Combining penalties and alternative norms
- What happens with an Elastic Net–style mixture across groups (e.g., α||·||2 + (1−α)||·||1 on fan-ins)? Does the finite-minimizer result extend to mixed/group-sparse penalties?
- Any observations comparing p=1 vs p=2 beyond hardware density trade-offs (e.g., accuracy/size curves, training stability)?

8) Experimental controls and fairness
- For fairness, align normalizers where possible (CapNorm vs BN-without-affine is close; ensure identical settings for parametric baselines).
- Consider reporting memory/computation over time: peak parameter count during growth vs final count, and wall-clock/epoch cost for AdaRad vs RMSprop with similar convergence.

9) Minor clarifications
- Self-similar nonlinearities: clearly state that ReLU satisfies σ(cs)=cσ(s) for c≥0 and why this eliminates the need for per-layer λ’s (great insight; elevate to the main text).
- Notation and algorithm listings: provide a brief geometric intuition for the “angular” rotation step in AdaRad.

Overall, this is a timely, well-motivated paper with a valuable theoretical guarantee and a promising practical recipe. I’m happy to recommend acceptance at 7, contingent on clarifying “zero units” and the penalty, expanding intuition for Theorem 1, and strengthening the fixed-size comparison experiment.
