Summary of contributions
- The paper introduces nonparametric neural networks: models whose width is learned during a single training run by interleaving gradient updates with the random addition of zero units and the removal of units driven to zero by a sparse group regularizer on fan-ins/fan-outs (primarily fan-in, p=2). A core theorem shows that, under this regularization, the objective attains a global minimum at a finite dimensionality, justifying the formulation.
- Two training components are proposed: (i) CapNorm, a variant of batch normalization that avoids scale-compensation that would nullify the regularizer, and (ii) AdaRad, an optimizer that decomposes each fan-in gradient into radial (parallel) and angular (orthogonal) parts, applying shrinkage and unnormalized radial updates while normalizing angular updates; the update is expressed in radial–angular coordinates.
- Empirically, the method often converges to compact architectures and can outperform parametric networks of the same size on MNIST and rectangles images; however performance degrades on the convex dataset. A large-scale poker experiment demonstrates feasibility and competitive accuracy.
- The approach addresses an important problem (learning model capacity online) and offers an alternative to expensive black-box architecture search.

Decision
Reject (borderline). Key reasons: (1) Computational complexity and wall-clock overhead of AdaRad’s radial–angular decomposition and rotations are not quantified or validated empirically; (2) Empirical support is mixed, with clear underperformance on the convex dataset accompanied by very small learned networks and no convincing explanation or mitigation.

Supporting arguments
1) Problem clarity: The paper targets automatic width selection during training, replacing outer-loop discrete model selection with a single training process. This is a timely, important question with practical impact.

2) Motivation and placement: The formulation is well motivated in relation to pruning/growing methods, hyperparameter search, and group sparsity. The f-equivalence trick (adding/removing zero units) is simple and elegant. The theoretical result that a finite optimum exists under group regularization is valuable. However, the approach to growth—periodically adding randomly initialized units with zero fan-out—resembles a random search over discrete architectures with a learned filter provided by the sparse regularizer. The paper would be stronger with direct comparisons to closely related structured sparsity and grow/prune baselines that do not require AdaRad or CapNorm (e.g., group-lasso with Adam/RMSprop, iterative magnitude pruning, or grow-and-prune methods).

3) Do the results support the claims?
- Accuracy and size: On MNIST and rectangles images, results are promising, including improved accuracy vs parametric networks of the same size. On convex, the method consistently underperforms and converges to small models; this undermines the claim of robust capacity discovery and calls for analysis.
- Ablations: It is unclear how much of the gain comes from (a) dynamic architecture, (b) AdaRad, or (c) CapNorm. The paper lacks ablations such as: fixed-width networks trained with AdaRad; nonparametric training with standard optimizers; CapNorm vs BN or weight normalization under compatible regularization.
- Efficiency: The core claim that AdaRad scales “more gracefully” than RMSprop is made qualitatively; but the algorithm requires, per fan-in, a parallel/orthogonal decomposition (inner products and vector norms), per-fan-in rotations, and per-unit caches. These constant factors can dominate in practice and may be unfriendly to GPU kernels. No wall-clock, throughput, or energy comparisons are provided.
- Experimental protocol: Selection of “representative” runs and heavy use of manual annealing/rewinding introduce room for bias. Median and variance over all runs should be reported systematically; random search comparisons should include model-size-normalized accuracy and time-to-accuracy.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Quantify computational and memory overhead precisely. Break down per-iteration FLOPs and memory for SGD, RMSprop/Adam, and AdaRad on a standard MLP. Provide GPU/CPU runtimes and throughput vs batch size across datasets and widths. Include time-to-target-accuracy comparisons and scaling with width/depth.
- Provide ablation studies:
  - Fixed-width networks with AdaRad vs Adam/RMSprop to isolate the benefit of radial–angular updates.
  - Nonparametric training with standard optimizers and group-lasso shrinkage (no radial–angular decomposition).
  - CapNorm vs BN-compatible regularization (e.g., apply regularization to pre-BN weight norm or use weight normalization) to ensure the comparison is not confounded by normalization choice.
  - Growth schedule: unit addition rate/frequency, initialization scale, and λ schedules.
- Analyze the convex dataset failure. Hypotheses to test: (i) the group penalty over-shrinks in regimes where many features are needed; (ii) CapNorm’s cap at σ<1 alters effective capacity; (iii) AdaRad’s radial shrinkage eliminates units before they learn (αr, αφ imbalance). Provide diagnostics: learned layer widths over time, gradient norms, effective margin, training dynamics with reduced λ or with λ annealing, and retraining the final width with a strong parametric baseline to decouple optimization from capacity.
- Strengthen fairness and statistical reporting: report medians with interquartile ranges across all runs; avoid manual “representative” selection. Include compute budgets and early stopping criteria matched across methods.
- Relate more directly to NAS/pruning literature: compare to structured sparsity (group-lasso) baselines trained with standard optimizers; include a prune-after-train baseline of comparable compute; position your method relative to Net2Net and modern grow/prune strategies.
- Discuss implementation practicality: how to implement per-fan-in rotations efficiently on GPUs; numerical stability of radial–angular updates when fan-in norms are near zero; effect of self-similar activations beyond ReLU.

Questions for the authors
- What is the exact per-iteration cost of AdaRad relative to SGD and RMSprop in terms of additional dot products, norms, and rotations per fan-in? How does this translate into GPU wall-clock time for your reported experiments?
- Can you provide timing curves (epochs and wall-clock) to reach a fixed validation error for NP vs parametric baselines on MNIST/rectangles/poker?
- How sensitive is performance to αr, αφ, λ, and the unit addition rate? Do learned widths meaningfully change under different seeds or schedules?
- Why does the method converge to very small networks on convex? Does annealing λ or delaying shrinkage (e.g., warm-start without regularization) fix it?
- How much of the observed gain over same-size parametric nets is due to AdaRad vs dynamic width vs CapNorm? Please provide the ablations listed above.
- Could BN be made compatible by regularizing a scale-invariant parameterization (e.g., weight normalization) or by penalizing norms prior to normalization? If not, why?
- Do you have results on convolutional architectures or deeper MLPs to test scalability and practical overhead? Is the per-unit normalization/rotation still efficient there?

Overall, the paper proposes a creative and principled framework with interesting theory and some encouraging results, but the lack of computational analysis and the mixed empirical picture make it hard to judge practical value. With the suggested evaluations and explanations, this could become a strong contribution.
