Summary of contributions
The paper tackles the important problem of selecting neural network size within a single training run. It proposes “nonparametric neural networks” that grow by randomly adding zero units and shrink via a sparse fan-in (group-ℓ2) penalty, with a theoretical guarantee that a finite optimal size exists. Training uses a novel optimizer, AdaRad, which decomposes fan-in gradients into parallel/orthogonal components and performs radial–angular updates, plus a modified normalization layer (CapNorm) to preserve the effect of the regularizer. Empirically, the approach often finds compact models that are competitive with or better than fixed-size parametric baselines and exhaustive search on MNIST and rectangles; it also scales to a large dataset (poker).

Decision
Reject (borderline). Primary reasons: (1) the paper does not quantify the additional computational and memory costs introduced by radial–angular updates and fan-in decompositions relative to standard optimizers; (2) inconsistent empirical support, notably clear underperformance and overly small networks on the convex dataset without sufficient analysis.

Supporting arguments
- Novelty and motivation: The formulation is clear and well-motivated, connecting unit addition/removal with sparse regularization and f-equivalence. The theoretical result ensuring a finite optimum under fan-in/out regularization is valuable.
- Relation to literature: The work sits between pruning, group-lasso sparsification, and NAS/black-box search. However, the paper should more directly position AdaRad/growth against simpler baselines that combine standard optimizers with group-ℓ2 penalties and occasional unit injection.
- Claims versus evidence: While accuracy results are promising on two datasets and the poker scale study is encouraging, there is no wall-clock or FLOP-based comparison to parametric training. AdaRad introduces per-fan-in orthogonal projections and rotations; without runtime profiling, it is unclear whether the purported benefits outweigh overheads. The convex dataset result suggests a bias toward overly small models; the paper does not explain why the method converges to small width there or how to mitigate it.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper)
- Provide precise per-iteration computational and memory complexity, and report measured training time to reach a target validation error versus SGD/RMSprop/Adam (with and without group-ℓ2).
- Add ablations: (a) AdaRad vs RMSprop/Adam with group-ℓ2 and the same add/prune scheme; (b) CapNorm vs BatchNorm-without-learnable-params; (c) unit addition rate/schedule; (d) p=1 vs p=2 penalties.
- Analyze the convex dataset: learning curves of width, validation error vs width, and whether early stopping or relaxed λ/αr prevents premature shrinkage.
- Clarify whether the approach resembles random search over discrete architectures or a guided local search; quantify how often added units survive and contribute.

Questions for the authors
- What is the exact extra cost (FLOPs and memory) per layer/unit for AdaRad versus RMSprop/Adam? How does wall-clock time compare to reach the same validation error?
- Does AdaRad alone (without growth) improve over standard optimizers under group-ℓ2?
- Why does the method yield very small networks on the convex dataset? Is this due to CapNorm behavior, λ choice, αr/αφ balance, or data geometry?
- How sensitive are results to initializing new units with zero fan-out vs small nonzero fan-out?
- Can you report the survival rate and usefulness of added units over time, and the effect of alternative addition schedules?
