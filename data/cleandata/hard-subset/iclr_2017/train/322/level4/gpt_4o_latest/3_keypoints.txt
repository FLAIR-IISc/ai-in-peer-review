- Method: dynamically add/remove units with zero input/output weights and use a group-sparsity norm to push unit weights to zero.  
- Theoretical contribution: shows that with proper regularization the loss is minimized by a network with a finite number of units.  
- Theory limitation: this guarantee does not ensure avoidance of overfitting or underfitting in practice; preliminary experiments suggest these issues did not appear in tested cases.  
- Potential benefit: could reduce hyperparameter tuning by adapting unit count automatically.  
- Remaining tuning: several hyperparameters (e.g., unit-add frequency, weight decay) still indirectly control unit count and their tuning difficulty is unclear.  
- Authors’ stance: they do not claim simplified training, and it is disappointing that reduced tuning is not demonstrated.  
- Claimed practical advantage: can train smaller networks with comparable performance, which may lower runtime, power, and memory for devices.  
- Missing comparison: no experiments against pruning or other size-reduction methods, so competitiveness is unclear.  
- Training-time coupling: same hyperparameters affect unit count and training time, potentially causing much slower training than fixed-parametric approaches.  
- Scalability concern: authors do not address how training time and practicality scale to much larger networks.  
- Experimental scope: experiments are encouraging but limited; larger-scale evaluations are needed to establish broader applicability.  
- Overall assessment: interesting, well-written step toward lifelong adaptive networks, but current findings remain largely speculative.
