This paper proposes a nonparametric neural network model capable of automatically learning its size during the training process. The core idea is to stochastically introduce new units with zero-initialized weights and then leverage a sparse regularizer to prune units that prove to be irrelevant to the task. Conceptually, this approach can be viewed as a form of random search over the discrete space of network architectures, guided by sparse regularization to eliminate superfluous components. The problem of automatic model selection is highly relevant, and the paper presents an interesting approach with promising results.

My main comments and questions for the authors are listed below:

**1. Computational Complexity and Scalability**

A primary concern is the computational overhead introduced by the proposed method. The decomposition of each unit's fan-in weights into parallel and orthogonal components, followed by the transformation into radial-angular coordinates, appears to be computationally intensive. This could potentially increase the training time significantly compared to a standard parametric neural network, especially as the network grows.

The paper would be substantially strengthened if the authors could:
*   Provide a theoretical analysis of the additional computational complexity. It would be helpful to quantify the extra floating-point operations (FLOPs) required per update step relative to a baseline feedforward network.
*   Present empirical results comparing the wall-clock training time of the proposed model against the parametric baselines used in the experiments. This would give readers a practical understanding of the trade-offs between architectural flexibility and computational cost.

**2. Performance on Simpler Datasets**

It is noted in the results that the nonparametric network converges to smaller models and, as a consequence, performs worse than fixed-size parametric networks on the convex dataset. This is an interesting and somewhat counterintuitive finding.

Could the authors offer more insight into this behavior? Is it possible that the sparse regularization is overly aggressive for this type of data distribution, prematurely pruning units that could otherwise be beneficial? Alternatively, does this observation point to a potential limitation of the method when applied to datasets with simpler underlying structures, where a denser representation might be more optimal? A more detailed discussion on this point would be valuable for understanding the model's scope and limitations.

Overall, this is a well-motivated paper that tackles an important problem. Addressing the concerns regarding computational overhead and the model's behavior on simpler datasets would significantly improve the contribution.
