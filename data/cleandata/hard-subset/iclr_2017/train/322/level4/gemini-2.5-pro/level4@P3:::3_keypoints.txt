- Introduces nonparametric neural networks that grow/shrink during a single training run using zeroed units and l2 group-sparsity (fan-in/fan-out) regularization.
- Theorem 1 proves the regularized objective has a global minimum at finite network size.
- Proposes two practical components: Capped Batch Normalization (CapNorm) and a novel optimizer AdaRad to protect newly added units.
- Experiments show the method can find compact architectures competitive with fixed-size networks from extensive search.
- Decision: Weak Accept — recommend acceptance.
- Main positive reason: novel, theoretically grounded, and conceptually appealing framework with thoughtful solutions (e.g., AdaRad).
- Main concern: practical advantages not fully established due to potentially sensitive new hyperparameters and missing key baselines.
- Strength: elegant unification of growth and pruning via simple zero-unit mechanism.
- Strength: formal proof giving theoretical soundness lacking in many architecture-search methods.
- Strength: technical contributions addressing practical challenges—AdaRad decouples direction/magnitude updates; CapNorm makes batch-norm compatible with sparsity regularizer.
- Weakness: hyperparameter burden remains (λ, ν, αr, αφ) and unclear if tuning is easier than traditional width search.
- Weakness: missing comparisons to established model-compression/post-hoc pruning baselines.
- Weakness: training time and scalability concerns—dynamic training appears slower and may be prohibitive for large/deep models.
- Suggestion: use adaptive unit-addition schedules rather than fixed per-epoch additions.
- Suggestion: explore smarter initialization for new units (e.g., Net2Net-style) to speed usefulness.
- Suggestion: investigate and analyze the failure on the 'convex' dataset more deeply.
- Question: how sensitive are final architecture and performance to λ and ν, and how does tuning effort compare to standard width searches?
- Question: how would nonparametric networks compare to pruning a large trained parametric network to the same size?
- Question: why did the method struggle on the 'convex' dataset, and does that indicate limitations in discoverable function complexity?
