This paper addresses the problem of allowing a network to dynamically adjust its number of hidden units during training. The proposed method is simple, elegant, and well-motivated: units are added or removed when their corresponding input or output weights become zero, and a group sparsity regularizer is used to encourage this condition. A key theoretical contribution is the proof that, with proper regularization, the loss function is minimized by a network with a finite number of units. While this theoretical result does not guarantee that the final network will avoid over- or under-fitting, the authors' initial experiments suggest this is not a practical concern.

One potential advantage of learning the network architecture is to reduce the burden of hyperparameter tuning. A disadvantage of this approach, however, is that it does not fully achieve this goal. Instead, it introduces new hyperparameters that implicitly control the final network size, such as parameters governing the frequency of unit addition and the rate of weight decay. It remains unclear whether tuning these new hyperparameters is any easier than tuning the network size directly in a standard fixed-architecture approach. To be fair, the authors do not claim to simplify the training process. Nevertheless, the introduction of a new set of sensitive hyperparameters is a notable drawback.

The authors emphasize their ability to train networks that achieve comparable performance with fewer units than their fixed-architecture counterparts. This is an important contribution, as smaller networks reduce inference time, power consumption, and memory footprint, which is particularly critical for mobile and embedded devices. However, the paper lacks experimental comparisons to other established methods for network compression, such as pruning. Without such a comparison, it is difficult to assess whether the proposed method is competitive with state-of-the-art techniques for creating compact models.

A further disadvantage is that the same hyperparameters appear to control both the final network size and the overall training time. Consequently, training could be significantly slower with this method than for a fixed-architecture network. While fixed-architecture networks can require a costly hyperparameter search (e.g., grid search), practitioners can often leverage experience from similar problems to select a suitable architecture quickly. In contrast, the training time of the proposed method appears to be inherently tied to the dynamic growth and pruning process, which may be unavoidable. The paper does not address how this method scales to larger datasets and deeper architectures, raising concerns about its practicality for large-scale problems where training efficiency is crucial.

In general, while the experiments presented are encouraging, they are not comprehensive enough to be fully convincing. To demonstrate the practical viability and general utility of this approach, experiments on substantially larger-scale problems are needed.

In summary, this is an interesting and clearly written paper that introduces a potentially valuable technique. The vision of creating networks that can grow and adapt, possibly enabling life-long learning, is compelling, and this work represents a step in that direction. However, based on the current results, the approach remains speculative.
