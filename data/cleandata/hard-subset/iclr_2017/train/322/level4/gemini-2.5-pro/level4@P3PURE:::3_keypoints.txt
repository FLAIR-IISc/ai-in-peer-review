- Method: add/remove units when their input/output weights become zero, encouraged by a group-sparsity regularizer, with a proof that proper regularization yields a loss-minimizing network with finite units.
- Theoretical guarantee does not ensure avoidance of over- or under-fitting, though initial experiments suggest this is not a practical issue.
- Introduces new hyperparameters (e.g., unit-addition frequency, weight-decay rate) that implicitly control final network size and may be as difficult to tune as choosing a fixed architecture.
- Claims comparable performance with fewer units (beneficial for inference, power, memory), but lacks comparisons to established compression methods like pruning to assess competitiveness.
- The same hyperparameters appear to govern both final size and training time, potentially making training slower and less predictable than fixed-architecture approaches.
- Scalability is unclear: the paper does not address behavior on larger datasets or deeper architectures, raising practicality concerns for large-scale problems.
- Experiments are encouraging but limited; substantially larger-scale evaluations are needed to demonstrate practical viability.
- Overall assessment: an interesting, well-written step toward adaptive/lifelong networks, but the approach remains speculative given current results.
