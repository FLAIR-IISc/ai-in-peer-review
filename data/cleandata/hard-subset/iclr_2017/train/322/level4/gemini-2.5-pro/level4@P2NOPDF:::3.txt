This paper presents a method for dynamically adjusting the number of hidden units in a neural network during training. The proposed approach is simple yet elegant: it adds or removes units based on whether their corresponding input and output weights are zero, using a group sparsity regularizer to encourage weights to decay. The primary theoretical contribution is a proof that, under appropriate regularization, the loss function is minimized by a network with a finite number of units. While this theoretical result does not guarantee good generalization, the preliminary experiments suggest that the learned network architectures perform reasonably well on the training data.

A key motivation for methods that learn network architecture is to reduce the burden of hyperparameter tuning, specifically the need to manually set the number of units. However, a potential weakness of this method is that it does not fully eliminate this burden. Instead, it introduces new hyperparameters that implicitly control the final network size, such as those governing the frequency of unit addition and the strength of the sparsity regularization. It remains an open question whether tuning these new hyperparameters is any less difficult than directly selecting the number of units in a fixed-architecture network. To be fair, the authors do not explicitly claim to simplify the training process, but the lack of a clear advantage in this regard is a notable limitation.

The authors emphasize that their method can produce networks that achieve comparable performance to their fixed-architecture counterparts but with fewer units. This is a significant claim, as smaller networks offer benefits in terms of inference speed, power consumption, and memory footprint, which are critical for deployment on resource-constrained devices. However, the experiments do not include comparisons to other well-established techniques for network compression, such as pruning. Without such a comparison, it is difficult to assess whether the proposed method is competitive with the state of the art in producing compact, efficient models.

A further concern is that the hyperparameters governing network growth also directly influence the total training time. Consequently, training a network with this dynamic approach could be substantially slower than training a fixed-architecture network. While standard training often involves a costly hyperparameter search (e.g., grid search), practitioners can often leverage experience from similar problems to select a suitable architecture, thereby avoiding this overhead. In contrast, the potential for slow training seems inherent to the proposed grow-and-prune mechanism. The paper does not address the scalability of this procedure, raising significant concerns that the method may not be practical for the large-scale networks common in modern deep learning due to prohibitive training times.

The empirical results presented are helpful and encouraging, but they are not comprehensive enough to be fully convincing. To demonstrate the practical utility and scalability of this approach, experiments on more complex, large-scale datasets would be necessary.

In conclusion, this is an interesting and well-written paper that introduces a promising idea for dynamically adapting network architectures. The work aligns with the inspiring vision of creating adaptive networks capable of lifelong learning, and methods of this nature are a valuable step in that direction. Despite its strengths, the practicality of the approach is not yet fully established, as the current empirical evidence remains preliminary.
