**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a framework for "nonparametric neural networks," designed to automatically determine an appropriate network size during a single training run. The core idea involves dynamically adding new, zero-initialized units to the network while simultaneously using a group-sparse regularizer (an l2 penalty on fan-in vectors) to prune redundant ones. This approach is presented as an efficient alternative to expensive, black-box hyperparameter search methods. The authors provide a theoretical justification for their framework, proving that a global minimum exists at a finite network size, and introduce a novel optimization algorithm, Adaptive Radial-Angular Gradient Descent (AdaRad), to facilitate this dynamic training process. The experimental results on several datasets are interesting, showing that the method can find well-performing, compact architectures.

**Decision and Key Reasons**

My decision is currently borderline, and I am prepared to be convinced by the authors' response. My recommendation hinges on clarification of two key points:

1.  **Computational Complexity:** The practical utility of the proposed method is unclear due to the potential computational overhead of the AdaRad optimizer, which is not analyzed in the paper.
2.  **Robustness and Performance Limitations:** The method's failure to find a high-performing model on the convex dataset raises concerns about its general applicability and robustness.

**Supporting Arguments**

1.  **Computational Overhead of AdaRad:** A primary concern is the computational complexity of the proposed training algorithm. For each training step and for every unit's fan-in vector, AdaRad requires decomposing the gradient into parallel and orthogonal components and performing an update in radial-angular coordinates. This appears significantly more computationally intensive than standard optimizers. The paper would be substantially strengthened by a detailed analysis of this additional computational cost, both theoretically (in terms of floating-point operations relative to standard training) and empirically (e.g., by providing wall-clock training time comparisons against the parametric baselines). Without this information, it is difficult to assess whether the method provides a practical advantage over conventional hyperparameter search.

2.  **Underperformance on the Convex Dataset:** As the authors honestly report, nonparametric networks converge to smaller and significantly less accurate models on the convex dataset compared to their parametric counterparts. This is a crucial finding that may highlight a limitation of the framework. An explanation for this behavior is needed. Is there a fundamental reason why the proposed growth and pruning dynamic fails on this type of problem? Understanding this failure case is essential for determining the scope and reliability of the proposed method.

**Questions for the Authors**

The following questions are intended to help me clarify my understanding of the paper and finalize my assessment.

1.  Could you please provide an analysis of the computational complexity of AdaRad compared to standard optimizers like RMSprop or Adam? It would be particularly helpful to see a comparison of wall-clock training times for the experiments presented.
2.  Could you offer further insight into the poor performance on the convex dataset? What characteristics of this dataset or task might cause the nonparametric approach to converge to a suboptimal, smaller network? Does this suggest a limitation in the ability of the fan-in regularizer or the AdaRad optimizer to explore the architecture space effectively for certain problem types?
