### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces a novel and theoretically grounded framework called "nonparametric neural networks" to address the challenging problem of model selection, specifically for determining network size. Instead of relying on expensive black-box optimization methods that require training multiple networks, the proposed approach dynamically adjusts the network architecture during a single training run. The core idea is to continuously add new units while simultaneously using a group-sparse (l2) fan-in regularizer to identify and prune redundant units. To facilitate this process, the authors develop a specialized optimization algorithm, Adaptive Radial-Angular Gradient Descent (AdaRad), which is designed to work in concert with the regularizer without negating its effect. The paper provides a formal proof that, under this regularization scheme, a global minimum of the training objective exists at a finite network size. Experimental results on several benchmark datasets demonstrate that this method can automatically find well-performing network architectures, in some cases outperforming both fixed-size networks and architectures found via exhaustive search.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Novel and Principled Approach:** The paper tackles the important problem of architecture search with a creative and well-motivated method that operates within a single training process. The formulation of dynamically growing and shrinking the network, supported by a theoretical guarantee of convergence to a finite size, is a significant and valuable contribution.
2.  **Thoughtful Algorithm Design:** The development of the AdaRad optimizer and the CapNorm layer demonstrates a deep understanding of the challenges involved. AdaRad's separation of radial and angular updates is a clever solution to the problem of reconciling gradient normalization with l2 shrinkage, which is crucial for the framework's success.

### **Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The primary strength of this work lies in its elegant reframing of the model selection problem from a discrete, expensive search into a continuous optimization process over an expanding parameter space. This is a powerful idea that could significantly reduce the computational burden of finding effective network architectures. The theoretical justification provided in Theorem 1, which guarantees the existence of a global minimum at a finite dimensionality, lends substantial credibility to the framework.

The proposed training machinery is also well-designed. Standard optimizers like RMSprop or Adam, which normalize gradient components, would interfere with the l2 regularizer's ability to shrink fan-ins to zero. AdaRad's decomposition of the gradient into radial and angular components is an insightful way to allow for adaptive learning rates on the direction of weight vectors while leaving their magnitudes to be controlled by the loss gradient and the regularizer. This shows a careful consideration of the interplay between optimization and regularization.

The empirical evaluation is generally supportive of the paper's claims. On the MNIST and Rectangles-Images datasets, the nonparametric networks achieve performance that is competitive with or superior to parametric networks found through extensive grid and random searches, often with a more compact architecture. This validates the core concept that a good network size can be discovered "on the fly." However, the results are not uniformly positive; the method's poor performance on the Convex dataset reveals a potential failure mode, where the framework might converge to a suboptimal, overly small network. While this is a weakness, the overall positive results and the novelty of the framework outweigh this concern for an acceptance recommendation.

### **Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not central to the decision to accept.

*   **Computational Complexity of AdaRad:** The draft review correctly raises the question of computational overhead. While Table 1 provides a helpful theoretical analysis of the asymptotic costs, it would strengthen the paper to include empirical measurements of wall-clock training time. How much slower is a single training iteration of AdaRad compared to a standard optimizer like RMSprop or Adam in practice? This would give readers a better sense of the practical trade-offs involved.

*   **Failure Case on the Convex Dataset:** The paper is transparent about the poor performance on the Convex dataset, noting that the nonparametric network converges to a much smaller size than optimal. Could you provide more insight or speculation as to why this occurs? Does this dataset's loss landscape particularly penalize the addition of new, randomly initialized units, causing the regularizer to dominate too early? Understanding this failure mode is crucial for assessing the general applicability of the method.

*   **Unit Addition Schedule:** The strategy of adding one new unit to each layer per epoch seems fixed. Have you explored more adaptive schedules? For example, one could envision adding new units only when the validation error plateaus, or varying the number of units added based on the layer size or the magnitude of gradients. An adaptive schedule might improve efficiency and potentially help avoid premature convergence to small models, as seen with the Convex dataset.

*   **Initialization of New Units:** New units are initialized with a random fan-in. Has the effect of different initialization strategies been investigated? For instance, initializing a new unit to compute a function related to the residual error, or in a way that minimally perturbs the network's current output, might allow it to become useful more quickly.
