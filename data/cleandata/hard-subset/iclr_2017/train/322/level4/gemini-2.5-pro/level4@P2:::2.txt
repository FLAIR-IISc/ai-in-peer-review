### Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces an interesting and promising framework called "nonparametric neural networks," which aims to automatically determine a suitable network size during a single training process. The core idea is to dynamically grow and shrink the network by adding new units and pruning redundant ones. The authors propose a novel optimization algorithm, Adaptive Radial-Angular Gradient Descent (AdaRad), to train these dynamic networks. Crucially, the paper provides a theoretical justification for this approach, proving that under the proposed fan-in or fan-out regularization scheme, the training objective is guaranteed to have a global minimum at a finite network size, preventing uncontrolled growth. The empirical results on several datasets demonstrate that this method can find compact, competitive models without the need for an expensive hyperparameter search over network architectures.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision: Accept**

My decision is based on two key strengths of this work:

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper tackles the important and challenging problem of neural architecture selection. The proposed method of dynamically adapting the network size within a single training run is an elegant and practical alternative to standard black-box optimization methods, which are computationally prohibitive. This is a valuable research direction that could significantly simplify the process of applying neural networks.
2.  **Theoretical Grounding:** A major contribution is Theorem 1, which provides theoretical guarantees for the proposed framework. By proving that the network size will not grow indefinitely, the authors add significant rigor to their method, distinguishing it from more heuristic approaches to network growth.

### Supporting Arguments

The primary appeal of this paper is its attempt to move beyond the standard "guess-and-check" paradigm of architecture design. The framework for adding and removing units, governed by a group-sparsity-inducing regularizer, is well-motivated. The experiments in Figure 2, while showing mixed results, are encouraging. In some cases, the nonparametric networks outperform parametric networks of the same final size, and in others, they achieve competitive performance with significantly smaller models than those found by random search. Even in cases where it underperforms, the ability of the method to automatically discover a reasonable architecture is a valuable contribution.

However, the paper's most significant theoretical result, Theorem 1, feels underdeveloped in the main text. While the proof is provided in the appendix, the paper would benefit greatly from a more intuitive discussion of the theorem's implications. The result feels somewhat expected—that a penalty on network complexity will limit that complexity—but a deeper discussion could reveal more powerful insights and better situate the contribution. This lack of discussion is a missed opportunity to strengthen the paper, but the merit of providing the formal proof is a clear positive.

### Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not central to my decision to accept. I would appreciate the authors' response to the questions to clarify my understanding.

**1. Discussion of Theorem 1:**
This theorem is a cornerstone of the paper, yet it is stated without much discussion of its broader implications or intuition.
*   **Suggestion:** Could you dedicate more space in the main paper to discuss the key insights from Theorem 1 in plain English? For example, drawing an analogy to how L1 regularization induces sparsity in linear models (like Lasso) might help build intuition for why the group-norm regularizer here guarantees a finite size. This could be achieved by slightly condensing the related work section.
*   **Question:** Beyond the formal proof, what do you see as the most powerful or surprising insight from this theoretical result?

**2. Understanding the Performance Advantage:**
The results show that nonparametric networks can sometimes outperform parametric networks of the same final size trained from scratch. This is a fascinating result.
*   **Question:** Could you provide more insight into why this might happen? Is it because the gradual growth process finds a better optimization path or a more robust local minimum than a fixed-size network initialized randomly?

**3. The Role of Hyperparameters and Initialization:**
*   **Question:** The regularization parameter `λ` seems critical for determining the final network structure. Is this intuition correct? Does this parameter have a predictable relationship with dataset characteristics?
*   **Question:** How sensitive is the final architecture to the random initialization of weights? If you start with different random seeds, do you converge to similarly sized and performing networks?

**4. Combination of Regularizers:**
*   **Question:** What would happen if the fan-in and fan-out regularizers were combined? Would the theoretical guarantees of Theorem 1 still hold?

**5. Technical Clarifications:**
*   **On the Regularizer's Value:** You state, "adding and removing zero units... does change the value of the fan-in and fan-out regularizers." Could you clarify this? A unit with a zero fan-in and zero fan-out would add a term of zero to the regularization sum. However, the paper's strategy is to initialize new units with a random fan-in and zero fan-out. In this case, the fan-in regularizer's value does increase. A brief clarification on this point would be helpful.
*   **On Defining "Zero Units":** A "zero unit" is defined as having either zero fan-in or zero fan-out. It seems that if the activation function `σ` satisfies `σ(0) = 0`, either of these conditions is sufficient to ensure that removing the unit does not change the network's overall function `f`. Is this understanding correct? Clarifying this in the text could prevent potential reader confusion.

**6. Presentation:**
*   **Suggestion:** I found Figure 2 to be quite dense and difficult to interpret at first glance. Improving its clarity, perhaps by using separate plots or better labeling, would strengthen the experimental section.
