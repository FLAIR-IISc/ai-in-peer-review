This paper introduces a simple yet elegant method for dynamically adjusting the number of units in a neural network during training. The approach involves adding or removing units with zero-valued input or output weights, guided by a group sparsity regularization norm that encourages weights to approach zero. The main theoretical contribution is a proof that with appropriate regularization, the loss function is minimized by a network with a finite number of units. While this result does not theoretically guarantee that the final network will avoid over- or under-fitting, the authors' initial experiments suggest this is not an issue in practice.

A potential advantage of learning the network architecture is to reduce the burden of hyperparameter tuning. However, a notable disadvantage of this approach is that it does not fully solve this problem. Instead, it introduces new hyperparameters that implicitly control the final number of units, such as parameters governing the frequency of unit addition and the rate of weight decay. It remains unclear whether tuning these new hyperparameters is any easier than selecting the network size directly in a standard fixed-architecture approach. To be fair, the authors do not claim to have simplified the training process, but the lack of this potential benefit is somewhat disappointing.

The authors emphasize their method's ability to produce networks that use fewer units to achieve performance comparable to their larger, fixed-size counterparts. This is a significant potential benefit, as smaller networks can reduce inference time, power consumption, and memory footprint, which is especially critical for deployment on mobile devices. However, the paper lacks an experimental comparison to existing network compression techniques, such as post-training pruning. Without this comparison, it is difficult to assess whether the proposed method is truly competitive with state-of-the-art approaches for reducing network size.

Another potential drawback is that the same hyperparameters govern both the final network size and the overall training duration. This coupling could make the proposed method significantly slower than training a fixed-architecture network. While standard approaches often require time-consuming hyperparameter optimization like grid search, this cost is not always incurred, as experience with similar tasks can often guide the choice of a suitable architecture. In contrast, the slower training of the proposed method may be an inherent characteristic. The authors do not discuss how this training time scales to much larger networks, raising concerns that the approach may be impractical for large-scale problems due to prohibitive training costs.

In general, the experiments are encouraging but not comprehensive enough to be fully convincing. To be persuaded of the approach's practicality and broad utility, I would need to see it evaluated on substantially larger-scale problems.

Overall, this is an interesting and well-written paper presenting a potentially valuable contribution. The overarching goal of building networks that can grow and adapt through lifelong learning is inspiring, and work of this nature may be a necessary step toward that vision. Nevertheless, the results presented in this paper remain speculative at this stage.
