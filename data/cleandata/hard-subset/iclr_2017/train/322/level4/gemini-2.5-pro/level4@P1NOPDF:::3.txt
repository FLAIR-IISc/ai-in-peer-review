This paper presents a method for dynamically adjusting the number of units in a neural network during training. The approach is simple, elegant, and well-motivated: units are added or removed during training based on whether their corresponding input and output weights are zero. To achieve this, a group sparsity regularizer is employed to encourage the weights associated with entire units to decay to zero. The primary theoretical contribution is a proof that, with appropriate regularization, the loss function is minimized by a network with a finite number of hidden units. While this result does not theoretically guarantee that the final network will avoid over- or under-fitting, the preliminary experiments suggest this is not an issue in practice.

A key motivation for methods that learn network architecture is to reduce the burden of hyperparameter tuning. However, a weakness of the proposed method—and perhaps any similar approach—is that it does not fully solve this problem but rather reframes it. The model introduces new hyperparameters that implicitly control the final network size, such as the rate at which new units are added and the strength of the sparsity-inducing regularization. It remains unclear whether tuning these new hyperparameters is any less difficult than tuning the network size directly in a standard fixed-architecture model. To be fair, the authors do not claim to have simplified the training process, but the lack of progress on this front is nonetheless a limitation.

The authors emphasize that their method can produce smaller networks that achieve performance comparable to that of larger, fixed-size networks. This is an important claim, as smaller networks reduce inference time, power consumption, and memory footprint—all critical factors for deployment on resource-constrained devices. However, the paper lacks an experimental comparison to other established techniques for network compression, such as post-hoc pruning. Without this comparison, it is difficult to assess whether the proposed method is competitive with the state-of-the-art in creating efficient models.

Another potential drawback is that the hyperparameters seem to jointly control both the final network size and the total training time. This entanglement suggests that achieving a desired network size may come at the cost of significantly longer training times compared to training a fixed-architecture network. While fixed-architecture models often require a costly hyperparameter search (e.g., grid search), an experienced practitioner can frequently select a suitable architecture without extensive tuning. In contrast, the slower training pace of the proposed method appears to be an intrinsic part of the algorithm, a cost that must be paid in every run. The paper does not address how this training overhead scales to much larger networks, raising concerns about the practicality of this approach for large-scale problems.

The experiments are encouraging but not comprehensive enough to be fully convincing. To demonstrate the practical utility and scalability of this method, experiments on more complex, larger-scale problems are needed.

In summary, this is an interesting and clearly written paper presenting a novel idea. The long-term vision of building networks that can grow and adapt, possibly enabling life-long learning, is compelling, and this work represents a step in that direction. However, in its current form, the practical benefits of the proposed method remain speculative, and further work is required to validate its effectiveness.
