This paper presents the interesting idea of dynamically adding or removing units, allowing a model's capacity to grow or shrink based on the demands of the problem and the data, rather than being fixed by user priors. I agree with Reviewer 2 that this is a promising direction.

The authors provide a valuable theoretical result (Theorem 1) which proves that under the proposed fan-in or fan-out regularization, the error function's optimum is achieved with a finite number of parameters. This guarantees that the network does not grow indefinitely until it perfectly overfits the training data. This result is reminiscent of more traditional approaches, such as Lasso or the Elastic Net, where regularization induces sparse weights.

While Theorem 1 is a nice result, the paper would be significantly improved by providing more intuition and discussion surrounding it. For me, the conclusion feels somewhat intuitive, and I would have liked the paper to explore and articulate this intuition. For example, some space from the otherwise good discussion of prior work could be reallocated to a deeper analysis of the paper's main theoretical contribution.

My main comments are as follows:

1.  **Experimental Comparison and Architectural Discovery:**
    A crucial experiment would be to demonstrate that a model whose units are added or removed automatically can outperform a network with an equivalent number of final units, but where the architecture is fixed from the start. This would prove the efficacy of the proposed dynamic procedure. The interesting question here is: does the method effectively prune unneeded nodes and replace them with more useful ones, thereby optimizing a performance-vs-memory trade-off?

    I understand that the experiments in Figure 2 attempt to address this, but the results are mixed and the figure itself is not immediately clear, though it can be interpreted upon careful inspection. In some cases, the non-parametric nets perform better, and in others, they perform worse than their parametric counterparts. Even in cases where performance is not superior, the method's utility in discovering network structure is evident. However, I do not fully understand why the dynamically trained models can sometimes perform *better* than the final, fixed network trained from scratch. When the final, optimal architecture is known, why would the non-parametric learning process be superior to the standard parametric one? More insight on this point would be very helpful.

2.  **Discussion and Implications of Theorem 1:**
    I feel that Theorem 1 is presented without adequate discussion of its meaning and implications. Beyond the appendix proofs, what is the key insight of this theorem in plain English? As mentioned, the conclusion seems almost natural; is there a deeper, more powerful insight that is not immediately obvious?

    This theoretical result deserves more space in the paper, potentially with more experiments to support it. For instance:
    *   Can the regularization parameter `lambda` be predicted from properties of the data? My impression is that `lambda` is the key factor determining the final network structure; is this correct?
    *   How sensitive is the final network structure to the initial random weights? Do different initializations lead to significantly different final architectures?
    *   What happens when the fan-in and fan-out regularizers are combined? Does the theoretical result still hold?

I also have a few additional questions for clarification:

1.  Why do you state that adding a "zero unit" changes the regularizer's value? For a standard L2 norm, for instance, adding zero-valued parameters does not change the norm's value.
2.  "Zero units" are defined as having either all fan-in or all fan-out weights equal to zero. To remove a unit without changing the network's output function *f*, it seems necessary for *both* the fan-in and fan-out weights to be zero. Could you please clarify this definition?

I have changed my rating to 7 in the hope that the authors will address these comments, which I believe would substantially strengthen the paper.
