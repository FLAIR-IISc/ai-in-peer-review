Echoing the sentiments of Reviewer 2, I find the core idea of this paper to be highly compelling. The proposed method for dynamically adding or removing units allows a model's architecture to adapt based on the problem and data, moving beyond reliance on a user's prior assumptions. This is a promising research direction.

The paper presents a significant theoretical result, demonstrating that the proposed fan-in or fan-out regularization ensures the error function's optimum is achieved with a finite set of parameters. This effectively prevents unbounded network growth and subsequent overfitting. This concept brings to mind established methods like Lasso or Elastic Net, which induce sparsity in weights. However, while the theorem is a valuable contribution, the paper would be strengthened by a more in-depth discussion of its intuition. The result feels somewhat expected, and elaborating on the underlying principles would be more impactful than the extensive review of prior work. Sacrificing some of that background discussion could create the necessary space to explore and contextualize the paper's core theoretical claims (see also point 2 below).

I have several other comments:

1.  A key experiment to demonstrate the method's efficacy would involve a direct comparison between your dynamically structured model and a static network designed from the outset with the same final architecture (i.e., the same number of nodes per layer as your model converges to). Such a comparison would directly validate the efficiency of the proposed approach. Does the dynamic process identify and prune superfluous nodes, thereby optimizing the performance-versus-memory trade-off? While Figure 2 seems to present experiments in this vein, its results are mixed and the figure itself is difficult to interpret. Crucially, the paper does not fully explain why the non-parametric training process can, in some cases, yield better results than training the equivalent static network from scratch. Further insight into how the learning trajectory influences the final performance would be a valuable addition.

2.  Theorem 1 and its implications warrant a much more thorough discussion. Currently, the theorem is presented without sufficient context or interpretation. Beyond the appendix's formal proof, what is the central, practical insight of this result conveyed in plain language? As the conclusion that regularization prevents infinite growth may seem intuitive, it is important to clarify if there are deeper, more powerful implications. This theoretical contribution is significant enough to merit additional space, including targeted experiments to substantiate it. Specifically, several questions arise:
    *   Is there a relationship between data properties and the optimal regularization parameter, `lambda`? Can `lambda` be predicted?
    *   How sensitive is the final network architecture to the choice of `lambda`? Is it the primary determinant of the resulting structure?
    *   To what extent does the final architecture depend on weight initialization? Do different random starts converge to substantially different networks?
    *   Does the theoretical guarantee still hold when fan-in and fan-out regularizers are combined?

I have two additional points requiring clarification:

1.  The paper states that adding zero units alters the regularizer's value. Could you clarify this? For many standard regularizers, such as the L2 norm, adding zero-valued parameters would have no effect.
2.  A "zero unit" is defined as having either its fan-in or fan-out weights equal to zero. However, for a unit's removal to not alter the network's output function `f`, it seems necessary for *both* the fan-in and fan-out weights to be zero. Please clarify this definition.

I have increased my score to 7, reflecting the paper's potential. I am hopeful that the authors will be able to address the aforementioned points in their revision.
