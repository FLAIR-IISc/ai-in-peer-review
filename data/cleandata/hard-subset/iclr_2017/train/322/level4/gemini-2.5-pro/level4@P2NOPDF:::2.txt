This paper presents a compelling approach for dynamically adapting neural network architecture by adding or removing units during training. The core idea of allowing a model to grow or shrink based on data and task complexity, rather than relying on a priori architectural choices, is a valuable research direction.

A key strength of the paper is the theoretical justification provided in Theorem 1. The theorem proves that with fan-in or fan-out regularization, the optimal solution is achieved with a finite number of parameters. This provides a solid formal grounding for the proposed method, ensuring the network does not grow indefinitely and simply memorize the training data.

While the paper's direction is promising, there are several areas where clarity and analysis could be significantly improved. My main comments are as follows:

**1. Deeper Discussion and Intuition for Theoretical Results**

While Theorem 1 is a valuable result, its presentation and discussion could be significantly strengthened. The connection to sparsity-inducing regularizers like Lasso or Elastic Net is clear, which also makes the result feel somewhat intuitive. The paper would benefit from a more detailed, plain-language explanation of the theorem's core insight and implications. Currently, the theorem is presented without sufficient discussion, leaving the reader to infer its deeper meaning. I would suggest dedicating more space to this analysis, perhaps by condensing the related work section. Specifically, I encourage the authors to address the following:

*   **Insight:** Beyond the mathematical proof, what is the key insight of Theorem 1? As the conclusion that regularization prevents infinite growth seems natural, is there a more powerful or subtle implication that is being missed?
*   **The Role of Lambda:** The regularization parameter lambda seems critical in determining the final network structure. Can the authors provide more insight into its role? Is there a property in the data that could help guide the selection of an appropriate lambda?
*   **Initialization:** How sensitive is the final network structure to the initial random weights? Do different initializations lead to significantly different final architectures?
*   **Combined Regularizers:** What are the theoretical implications of combining fan-in and fan-out regularizers? Does Theorem 1 still hold in this scenario?

**2. Clarity and Interpretation of Experimental Results**

The experimental section could be strengthened with more direct comparisons and deeper analysis to demonstrate the method's efficiency.

*   **Baseline Comparison:** A critical experiment would be to compare the final, learned network against a fixed-architecture network of the *same size*, trained from scratch for the same number of epochs. This would directly test the efficacy of the dynamic growing/pruning process itself, answering questions like: Does the method save unneeded nodes and replace them with more useful ones? Does it optimize the performance-vs-memory trade-off?
*   **Analysis of Mixed Results:** Figure 2 appears to address the comparison above, but the results are mixed and the figure itself is difficult to interpret. In some cases, the non-parametric nets perform better, and in others, they perform worse. While discovering the network structure is useful in itself, the paper should provide more insight into the intriguing cases where the non-parametric model outperforms its fixed-size counterpart. Why would the dynamic growing process find a better solution than training the final, optimal architecture from the beginning?

**3. Points for Clarification**

Finally, there are two technical points in the paper that require clarification:

*   **Regularizer Value of Zero Units:** The paper states that adding zero-weight units changes the regularizer's value. This is counter-intuitive. For standard norms like L2, adding zero-valued components to a vector does not change the norm. Please clarify why this is the case for the proposed regularizer.
*   **Definition of a "Zero Unit":** A zero unit is defined as having either its fan-in or fan-out weights equal to zero. However, to remove a unit without changing the network's output function `f`, it seems that *both* the fan-in and fan-out weights would need to be zero. This point should be clarified.

In summary, the paper introduces a very interesting and well-motivated idea. My enthusiasm is primarily tempered by a need for a deeper discussion of the theoretical results and a clearer, more insightful experimental evaluation. I believe that addressing the points above would significantly strengthen the paper.
