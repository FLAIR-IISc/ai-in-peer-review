This paper presents a novel and interesting framework, termed nonparametric neural networks, for automatically determining the size of a network's hidden layers within a single training run. The core contribution is a principled approach that avoids expensive, black-box hyperparameter optimization by dynamically growing the network (by adding new units) and shrinking it (by eliminating redundant units via an L2 fan-in regularizer). To facilitate this process, the authors introduce a specialized optimization algorithm, Adaptive Radial-Angular Gradient Descent (AdaRad), and a compatible normalization scheme, CapNorm. The paper provides a theoretical proof for the soundness of the framework, demonstrating that the optimization objective achieves a global minimum at a finite network size, and validates the approach empirically on several benchmark datasets.

I recommend accepting this paper. The primary reasons are the novelty of addressing the model selection problem within a single training cycle and the promising empirical results that suggest the method can find compact, high-performing models. However, my enthusiasm is tempered by questions regarding the practical computational overhead and the method's robustness across different types of datasets.

Supporting arguments:
The main strength of this paper is its elegant formulation for tackling the challenging and practically important problem of neural network architecture selection. Instead of treating network size as a hyperparameter to be tuned via expensive outer-loop searches, the proposed framework integrates size selection directly into the training optimization. This is a significant conceptual contribution. The theoretical analysis, culminating in Theorem 1, provides a solid foundation for the method, ensuring that the network's growth is appropriately constrained. The empirical validation on the MNIST and rectangles images datasets is compelling; the nonparametric networks not only find good architectures automatically but also achieve performance competitive with or even superior to networks found through an exhaustive random search.

However, the paper's claims would be stronger if two key concerns were addressed. First, the performance on the convex dataset highlights a potential limitation. The nonparametric approach converges to significantly smaller and less accurate models than parametric networks of an appropriate size. This suggests the method may have failure modes that are not yet fully understood. Second, while the paper provides a theoretical complexity analysis for the AdaRad optimizer in Table 1, the true practical overhead remains unclear. The per-iteration cost of decomposing gradients into radial and angular components for every fan-in could be substantial, potentially offsetting the gains from avoiding multiple training runs.

The following points are intended as constructive feedback to help improve the paper and are not central to my decision. I would also appreciate the authors' responses to the questions below to clarify my understanding.

*   The analysis of AdaRad's computational cost in Table 1 is helpful. To provide a more complete picture of the practical trade-offs, could you supplement this with an empirical comparison of wall-clock training time? For instance, showing the time to convergence for a nonparametric network versus a standard parametric network (trained with Adam/RMSprop) on one of the datasets would help quantify the real-world overhead introduced by the radial-angular decomposition.
*   The poor performance on the convex dataset, where the method converges to a suboptimal, small network, is a very interesting result. Do you have any further insights into why this might be happening? Could this behavior be related to the specific characteristics of the dataset interacting with the L2 regularization, or perhaps an artifact of the unit addition schedule or the dynamics of the AdaRad optimizer? Understanding this failure case would significantly strengthen the paper and clarify the scope of the proposed method's applicability.
