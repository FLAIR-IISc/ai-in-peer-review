This paper pursues an interesting and promising direction. The core idea of dynamically adding or removing units allows a model's architecture to adapt to the complexity of the problem and data, rather than relying solely on a user's prior specification.

The authors provide a valuable theoretical result (Theorem 1) demonstrating that under fan-in or fan-out regularization, the error function's optimum is achieved with a finite number of parameters. This guarantees that the network does not grow indefinitely and simply memorize the training data. This result is reminiscent of traditional methods like Lasso or Elastic Net, where regularization induces sparsity in the weights.

While Theorem 1 is a welcome finding, its presentation would be significantly strengthened by a more in-depth discussion of the underlying intuition. The result feels somewhat expected, and the paper would benefit from exploring *why* it holds and what its deeper implications are. This analysis is more critical than the extensive literature review; I suggest dedicating more space to exploring the paper's main theoretical contribution, perhaps by shortening the discussion of prior work. My suggestions in point 2 below elaborate on this.

I have a few other comments:

1.  A compelling experiment would be to compare the final, learned model against a conventional network whose architecture is fixed from the outset to match the final size of your model. This would directly test the efficacy of the proposed dynamic approach. The key question is whether dynamically adapting the architecture offers benefits beyond just finding a suitable size. For instance, does the method save unneeded units and replace them with more useful ones during training, leading to better performance or memory efficiency?

    I understand that Figure 2 presents experiments along these lines, but the results are mixed and the figure itself is difficult to interpret upon first glance. In some cases, the non-parametric nets perform better, while in others they do worse. Even in cases where performance is not superior, the method's value in automatic structure discovery is clear. However, I do not fully understand why the non-parametric model would ever outperform a fixed model of the same final architecture that is trained from scratch. What aspect of the dynamic training process leads to a better solution than starting with the "correct" architecture from the beginning? More insight here would be valuable.

2.  Could you please expand on the meaning and implications of Theorem 1? The theorem is presented without sufficient discussion. Beyond the formal proof in the appendix, what is the key insight in plain language? As mentioned, the conclusion that the network size remains finite seems intuitive, so I am looking for a deeper insight the authors may have. This theoretical result is central to the paper and warrants more dedicated space, potentially including supporting experiments. For example:
    *   Is it possible to predict an appropriate value for the regularizer hyperparameter λ based on properties of the data? My intuition is that λ is the primary factor determining the final network structure. Is this correct?
    *   How sensitive is the final architecture to the weight initialization? Do different random starts lead to significantly different final networks?
    *   Does the theoretical result of Theorem 1 still hold when fan-in and fan-out regularizers are combined?

I have a few additional clarification questions:

1.  Why is it stated that adding zero units changes the regularizer's value? For standard regularizers like the L2 norm, adding zero-valued parameters would have no effect.
2.  The paper defines zero units as having either zero fan-in or zero-fan-out weights. However, to remove a unit without altering the network's output function *f*, it seems necessary for *both* the fan-in and fan-out weights to be zero. This point requires clarification.

I have raised my score to 7 in anticipation that the authors will address these comments in their revision.
