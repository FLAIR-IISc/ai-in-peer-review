This paper proposes a method for dynamically adjusting the number of hidden units in a network during training. The approach is straightforward yet elegant and is founded on solid motivation: units are added or removed when their corresponding input or output weights become zero, a condition encouraged through the use of a group sparsity regularization norm. The primary theoretical result demonstrates that, given appropriate regularization, the loss function is minimized by a network of finite size. While this theoretical finding does not practically guarantee that the final network will be free from over- or under-fitting, the preliminary experimental results suggest this is not a significant issue.

A key motivation for methods that learn network architecture is the potential to simplify hyperparameter tuning. However, a notable drawback of this work is that it falls short of this goal. The proposed method introduces new hyperparameters that indirectly determine the final number of units, such as those governing the frequency of unit addition and the rate of weight decay. It remains an open question whether tuning these new parameters is any less challenging than selecting the network size directly in standard models. To be fair, the authors do not explicitly claim to have simplified the training process, yet this outcome is somewhat disappointing.

The paper highlights its ability to produce networks with fewer units that achieve performance comparable to their larger, parametrically-trained counterparts. This is a significant claim, as smaller networks offer benefits in inference speed, power efficiency, and memory usage, which are critical for applications on mobile devices. A key limitation, however, is the absence of experimental comparisons to established techniques for reducing network size, such as post-training network pruning. Consequently, it is difficult to assess whether the proposed method is competitive with the current state-of-the-art in model compression.

A further potential weakness is that the training duration and the final network size are coupled through the same set of hyperparameters. This suggests that training could be considerably slower compared to a parametric approach with a fixed architecture. While it is true that conventional methods often rely on time-consuming hyperparameter searches like grid search, this cost is not always incurred, especially when domain expertise can guide the choice of architecture. In contrast, the slower training pace of the proposed method may be an inherent characteristic. The paper lacks a discussion on how this training time scales to larger models, raising concerns about the practicality of the approach for large-scale deep learning, where training efficiency is paramount.

Overall, the provided experiments are informative and show promise, but they are not sufficiently comprehensive to be fully convincing. To establish the method's practical viability and broad utility, evaluations on significantly larger and more complex problems are necessary.

In conclusion, this is an interesting and well-written paper that introduces a potentially valuable concept. The broader ambition of creating networks capable of growth and adaptation, in the spirit of lifelong learning, is compelling, and this line of research represents a potential step toward that goal. Nevertheless, the results presented in this work remain preliminary and speculative at this stage.
