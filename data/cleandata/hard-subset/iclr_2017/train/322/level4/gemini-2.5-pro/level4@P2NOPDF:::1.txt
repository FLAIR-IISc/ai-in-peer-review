This paper introduces a nonparametric neural network model capable of automatically determining its architecture during training. The core mechanism involves incrementally adding new units and employing a sparse regularizer to prune those that are found to be redundant. This method can be viewed as a form of stochastic search over the discrete space of network architectures, guided by sparse regularization to eliminate superfluous connections. The problem of automatic model selection is highly significant, and the paper presents a novel approach with compelling results. My main comments are listed below:

1.  **Computational Complexity:** The paper would be significantly strengthened by a detailed analysis of the algorithm's computational complexity. The proposed method introduces several operations not found in standard network training, such as the decomposition of fan-in weights into parallel and orthogonal components and the transformation into radial-angular coordinates. These steps seem likely to introduce substantial computational overhead. I recommend that the authors provide a theoretical analysis of the additional operations (e.g., in Big-O notation) relative to a standard parametric network. Furthermore, empirical validation through a comparison of wall-clock training times would be highly valuable to quantify the practical cost of this flexibility.

2.  **Performance on Simpler Datasets:** The paper notes an interesting result on the convex dataset where the nonparametric approach yields a smaller, but less accurate, network compared to a fixed-size parametric model. Could the authors provide further insight into this phenomenon? For instance, is it possible that the regularization, which is designed to promote sparsity, is overly aggressive on simpler problem landscapes where a larger number of weak features might collectively be beneficial? A discussion on the interplay between the model's complexity, the dataset's intrinsic difficulty, and the regularization strength would be a valuable addition to the paper.
