This paper presents a novel and promising framework, termed "nonparametric neural networks," for automatically determining a suitable network size during a single training process. The core contribution is a method that dynamically adds new units to expand the network's capacity while simultaneously using an `l2` group-sparsity regularizer (on fan-in or fan-out) to prune redundant units. The authors provide an important theoretical result (Theorem 1) which guarantees that this process converges to a network of finite size. To facilitate this dynamic training, the paper also introduces a specialized optimization algorithm, AdaRad, and a modified normalization layer, CapNorm.

I recommend accepting this paper. The primary reasons for this decision are (1) the novelty and practical relevance of addressing the model selection problem within a single training run, moving beyond expensive black-box optimization, and (2) the provision of a solid theoretical foundation that proves the stability of the proposed framework, ensuring the network does not grow indefinitely.

The main strength of this work lies in its principled approach to dynamic network architecture search. Theorem 1 is a significant result; it formally proves that under fan-in or fan-out regularization, an optimal network with a finite number of parameters exists. This provides confidence that the method is well-grounded and not merely a heuristic. While the result is intuitive and reminiscent of sparsity-inducing regularizers in linear models (e.g., Lasso), its formal extension to this deep learning context is valuable. The experimental results, though mixed, demonstrate the method's potential. On two of the three datasets, the nonparametric networks are competitive with, and in some cases superior to, parametric networks found through extensive search. Even in cases where performance is comparable, the ability to automatically discover a reasonable architecture without an external search loop is a significant practical advantage.

A key point that requires further exploration is the observation that a dynamically grown network can sometimes outperform a parametric network of the same final size that is trained from scratch. This suggests that the growth and pruning process itself may act as a powerful regularizer or guide the optimization process to a better basin of attraction. Expanding on the intuition behind this phenomenon would strengthen the paper's claims about the benefits of dynamic training.

To further improve the paper, I offer the following suggestions and questions for clarification:

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   **Intuition for Theorem 1:** The paper would benefit from a more detailed, high-level discussion of Theorem 1 in the main body. Providing more intuition about why the fan-in/fan-out regularizer guarantees a finite optimal network would make this key theoretical contribution more accessible and impactful. This could be achieved by slightly condensing the related work section.
*   **Sensitivity Analysis:** The final network architecture is a key output of your method. An experiment analyzing the stability of this final architecture with respect to different random initializations would be very insightful. Do different runs converge to similarly sized and structured networks?

**Questions for the Authors:**
*   Could you provide more insight as to why the nonparametric training process can yield a model with higher accuracy than a standard parametric model of the same final architecture trained from scratch?
*   Theorem 1 appears to hold when using either a fan-in or a fan-out regularizer. Does this guarantee still hold if a combination of both regularizers is used?
*   How critical is the regularization hyperparameter `lambda` in determining the final network size? It seems to be the primary mechanism for balancing model complexity and training error. Is it possible that `lambda` itself could be adapted or annealed during training?
*   In Section 3, you state that adding a "zero unit" changes the value of the regularizer. Is this because a new unit is initialized with a zero fan-out but a *randomly initialized, non-zero* fan-in, thereby immediately incurring a penalty from the fan-in regularizer despite being f-equivalent at the moment of creation?
*   Could you clarify the condition for removing a unit? A "zero unit" is defined as having a zero fan-in or a zero fan-out. If a unit's fan-in weights become zero, its activation will be zero (assuming `sigma(0)=0`), and it no longer contributes to the network output, justifying its removal. Is this the mechanism that allows for removal even if the fan-out weights are non-zero? A confirmation of this logic would be helpful.
