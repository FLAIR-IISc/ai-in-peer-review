### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces a novel and elegant framework, termed "nonparametric neural networks," for automatically determining the size of a neural network's hidden layers during a single training run. The core idea is to dynamically grow the network by adding new units while simultaneously shrinking it by removing redundant ones. This process is driven by a group sparsity regularizer (an l2 penalty on the fan-in or fan-out of each unit), which encourages the weights of unnecessary units to become zero, facilitating their removal. The authors provide a key theoretical contribution by proving that, under this framework, the training objective is guaranteed to have a global minimum at a finite network size. To operationalize this framework, the paper also proposes a novel optimization algorithm, Adaptive Radial-Angular Gradient Descent (AdaRad), which is specifically designed to manage the distinct needs of learning unit direction and shrinking unit magnitude. The empirical results on several benchmark datasets demonstrate that this method can find compact, high-performing networks that are competitive with, and in some cases superior to, parametrically trained networks of the same size or those discovered through an exhaustive random search.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Weak Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The paper presents a well-motivated, simple, and theoretically-grounded framework for a significant problem in deep learning: automatic architecture selection. The proof that a finite optimal network size exists under their regularization scheme provides a strong foundation for the approach.
2.  While the experimental results are promising, the practical advantages over existing paradigms are not yet conclusively established. The method introduces its own set of sensitive hyperparameters that govern network growth and shrinkage, and it is not compared against the strong baseline of post-hoc network pruning.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The primary strength of this work is its conceptual clarity and theoretical rigor. The problem of automating model selection is highly relevant, and the proposed solution of dynamically adjusting network size in a single training run is a compelling alternative to expensive black-box optimization methods. The mechanism for achieving this—periodically adding new, randomly initialized units and using a group sparsity regularizer to prune them—is intuitive. Theorem 1, which proves the existence of a global minimum at a finite network size, is a significant result that provides confidence in the framework's stability and prevents unbounded network growth. The development of the AdaRad optimizer and CapNorm also shows careful consideration of the practical challenges involved, such as allowing new units sufficient time to learn useful features before being pruned.

However, the paper's claims of practical utility could be strengthened. My main concerns are:

1.  **Hyperparameter Trade-off:** The proposed method aims to alleviate the burden of choosing the number of units per layer. However, it replaces this hyperparameter with several new ones: the regularization strength `λ`, the radial and angular step sizes (`αr`, `αφ`), and the unit addition rate and frequency (`ν`, `νfreq`). It is not clear from the experiments whether tuning this new set of hyperparameters is fundamentally easier or more robust across different tasks than tuning the network width in a standard parametric setting. The paper itself notes that a good angular step size was determined through preliminary experiments, suggesting a degree of sensitivity.

2.  **Comparison to Pruning Methods:** The paper's goal of producing small, efficient networks is shared by the field of network pruning. The experiments compare the nonparametric approach to training parametric networks from scratch. A more compelling comparison would be against a standard "train-then-prune" pipeline: train an intentionally oversized parametric network to high accuracy, and then apply a state-of-the-art pruning method to reduce its size. It is unclear whether the dynamically-grown networks produced by this method offer a better performance-vs-size trade-off than networks obtained through post-hoc pruning.

3.  **Training Efficiency:** As shown in Figure 3A, the nonparametric network takes significantly longer to reach a low error rate compared to its parametric counterpart, as it must gradually build up its capacity. While the experiments on the larger 'poker' dataset suggest this overhead becomes a smaller fraction of the total training time for longer runs, this initial inefficiency could be a practical barrier for very large-scale problems where training time is a critical constraint.

Despite these concerns, the novelty of the approach, its theoretical underpinning, and the encouraging results on two of the three initial datasets make this a valuable contribution to the field.

### **Additional Feedback and Questions for Authors**

The following points are intended as constructive feedback to help improve the paper and are not central to my decision.

*   The initialization of new units is a simple random initialization. Have you considered more sophisticated strategies, such as function-preserving transformations inspired by work like Net2Net (Chen et al., 2016), to potentially integrate new units more effectively and speed up convergence?
*   The schedule for adding units (one per layer per epoch) is fixed. It would be interesting to explore adaptive schedules, for instance, adding units more aggressively at the start of training or when the loss plateaus.
*   The analysis in section 4.2 is excellent and provides great insight into the training dynamics. In particular, the discussion of fan-in/fan-out lengths and the role of CapNorm is very clear.

I would appreciate it if the authors could address the following questions in their rebuttal:

1.  Could you provide more intuition on the trade-off between the hyperparameters of your method (λ, α_r, α_φ, ν) and the traditional hyperparameter of layer width? Do you have evidence to suggest that the new parameters are more robust or easier to set than simply choosing a network size?
2.  The goal of finding compact models is also addressed by post-hoc pruning. How do you see your method comparing to an approach where one trains a large, over-parameterized network and then applies a strong pruning technique to achieve a similar model size?
3.  Regarding training time, do you foresee the slower initial convergence being a significant obstacle for scaling to much larger models and datasets (e.g., ImageNet), and have you considered any strategies to mitigate this, such as adding units in larger batches?
