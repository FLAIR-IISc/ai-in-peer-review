This paper introduces a simple yet elegant method for dynamically adjusting the number of hidden units in a neural network during training. The core mechanism involves adding or removing units based on whether their corresponding input and output weights are zero. To encourage this dynamic resizing, the authors employ a group sparsity regularization term, which effectively pushes the weights of unnecessary units towards zero.

A key theoretical contribution is a proof that, under this regularization scheme, the loss function is minimized by a network with a finite number of units. While this theoretical result does not inherently guarantee optimal generalization on unseen data, the preliminary experiments suggest that the resulting networks are well-behaved and do not suffer from significant over- or under-fitting.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

A potential benefit of methods that learn network architecture is the reduction of manual hyperparameter tuning. However, the proposed approach does not fully realize this benefit. Instead of tuning the number of units directly, a practitioner must now tune a new set of hyperparameters that implicitly control the final network size, such as those governing the frequency of unit addition and the rate of weight decay. It remains an open question whether tuning these new parameters is any less burdensome than tuning the network size in a conventional fixed-architecture setting. While the authors do not explicitly claim to simplify the training process, this is a notable limitation.

The authors highlight the ability of their method to produce compact networks that achieve performance comparable to their larger, statically-defined counterparts. This is a significant advantage, as smaller models reduce inference time, power consumption, and memory footprint, which is particularly critical for deployment on resource-constrained devices like mobile phones. However, the experimental evaluation is missing a crucial comparison. The paper does not benchmark its approach against established network compression techniques, such as post-training pruning. Without this comparison, it is difficult to ascertain whether this method is competitive with or superior to the current state-of-the-art in producing efficient neural networks.

Another concern is the interplay between the controlling hyperparameters, the final network size, and the overall training duration. This coupling might lead to significantly longer training times compared to training a network with a fixed architecture. While conventional approaches often necessitate computationally expensive hyperparameter searches (e.g., grid search), this cost is not always incurred, especially when prior experience on similar tasks can guide the choice of architecture. In contrast, the potential slowness of the proposed method may be an inherent aspect of the dynamic training process. The paper does not address the scalability of this training dynamic to much larger, state-of-the-art networks. There is a valid concern that the overhead of dynamically adding and removing units could render the approach impractical for large-scale problems where training efficiency is paramount.

The provided experiments are encouraging and serve as a good proof of concept. However, they are not comprehensive enough to be fully convincing. To establish the practical viability and widespread utility of this approach, a more thorough empirical evaluation on larger and more complex benchmark datasets is necessary.

**Conclusion**

In summary, this is an interesting and well-written paper that introduces a promising idea. The vision of creating networks that can grow and adapt, potentially paving the way for life-long learning systems, is compelling, and this work represents a valuable step in that direction. Nonetheless, given the limitations in the experimental validation and the open questions regarding scalability, hyperparameter sensitivity, and comparisons to alternative methods, the current results should be considered preliminary and somewhat speculative.
