### **Summary**

This paper introduces a simple, elegant, and well-motivated framework called "nonparametric neural networks" for dynamically adjusting the number of units in a network during a single training run. The core idea is to continuously add new units while simultaneously using a group sparsity regularizer (specifically, a fan-in L2 penalty) to encourage the weights of redundant units to decay to zero, after which they are removed. The primary theoretical contribution is a proof demonstrating that, under this regularization scheme, the training objective is guaranteed to have a global minimum at a finite network size. To facilitate this dynamic training process, the authors also propose a novel optimization algorithm, Adaptive Radial-Angular Gradient Descent (AdaRad), designed to work with the group regularizer, and a compatible normalization layer, CapNorm. The experimental results on several benchmark datasets are promising, showing that the method can automatically find compact networks that are competitive with, and in some cases superior to, fixed-size architectures found via extensive hyperparameter search.

### **Decision: Weak Accept**

This is an interesting and clearly written paper that proposes a novel framework for learning network architecture. The core idea is well-motivated and supported by a sound theoretical justification. However, the practical utility of the method remains speculative due to concerns about the introduction of new, hard-to-tune hyperparameters and a lack of comparison to standard model compression baselines.

### **Supporting Arguments**

1.  **Hyperparameter Burden:** A primary motivation for methods that learn network architecture is to reduce the burden of hyperparameter tuning. While the proposed approach removes the need to specify layer widths *a priori*, it introduces its own set of crucial hyperparameters that implicitly control the final network size. These include the regularization strength `λ`, the unit addition rate `ν`, and the radial step size `αr` in the AdaRad optimizer. It is not clear from the paper whether tuning these new, less intuitive hyperparameters is any easier than tuning the network size directly in a standard parametric setting. The paper would be more convincing if it provided an analysis of the sensitivity to these parameters.

2.  **Lack of Comparison to Pruning Methods:** The authors emphasize that their method produces compact networks that achieve strong performance. This is a significant potential benefit, as smaller models are critical for applications on resource-constrained devices. However, the paper only compares its results to parametrically trained networks of fixed sizes found via grid or random search. It lacks an experimental comparison to established network compression techniques, most notably post-training pruning. Without this comparison, it is difficult to assess whether the proposed "grow-and-prune" method is competitive with a standard "train-large-then-prune" workflow for creating compact, efficient models.

3.  **Concerns about Training Time and Scalability:** The dynamic process of adding and removing units appears to incur a significant training time overhead. Figure 3A shows that the nonparametric network converges much more slowly than its parametric counterparts. While standard models often require a costly, upfront hyperparameter search, practitioners can frequently leverage experience from similar problems to select a good architecture, avoiding an exhaustive search. In contrast, the slower training of the proposed method seems inherent to the grow-and-prune process. The authors do not sufficiently address how this training cost scales to the much larger networks common in modern deep learning, raising concerns about the method's practicality for large-scale problems.

### **Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not central to my decision.

*   The vision of building networks that can grow and adapt, possibly enabling lifelong learning, is inspiring. This work is a valuable step in that direction. The experimental results are encouraging, but the claims of practicality would be stronger with experiments on larger-scale datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10/100).

*   The introduction of AdaRad and CapNorm are interesting contributions in their own right. A more detailed ablation study isolating their benefits would be valuable. For instance, how does a standard optimizer like Adam perform when combined with the group sparsity regularizer and the grow-and-prune scheme?

*   **Question 1:** Could you provide more intuition on how to set the key hyperparameters (`ν`, `αr`, `λ`) that control the network's growth and shrinkage dynamics? Do you have a heuristic for setting them, or do they need to be tuned carefully via cross-validation for each new task?

*   **Question 2:** How does the final performance (accuracy and model size) of your method compare to a standard baseline of training an oversized network and then using a state-of-the-art pruning technique to shrink it to a similar size?

*   **Question 3:** Regarding scalability, the slower convergence shown in Figure 3A is a concern. Do you see this as a fundamental trade-off of the dynamic approach, or are there ways to accelerate the convergence of the network's size and parameters to be more competitive with standard training?
