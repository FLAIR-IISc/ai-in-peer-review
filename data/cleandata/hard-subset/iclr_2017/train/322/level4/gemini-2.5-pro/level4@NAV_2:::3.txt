This paper introduces a novel framework, termed "nonparametric neural networks," for automatically determining the size of a neural network's hidden layers within a single training run. The authors present an elegant and well-motivated approach where networks dynamically grow by adding new units and shrink by eliminating redundant ones. This process is driven by an l2 group sparsity penalty on the fan-in of each unit, which encourages entire units to become zero. A key theoretical contribution is a proof that, under this regularization scheme, the training objective is minimized by a network of finite size. The paper also proposes a novel optimization algorithm, AdaRad, tailored to this dynamic setting. The empirical results on several datasets are promising, demonstrating the viability of the core concept.

I recommend a Weak Accept for this paper. The framework for dynamically adapting network size is conceptually appealing and theoretically grounded. However, my enthusiasm is tempered by two main concerns: 1) the approach replaces the hyperparameter of network width with a new set of hyperparameters whose tuning complexity is not well understood, and 2) the practical benefits over existing, simpler methods for obtaining compact networks are not clearly established.

My decision is based on the following supporting arguments:

1.  **Hyperparameter Complexity:** A primary motivation for methods that learn network architecture is to reduce the burden of hyperparameter tuning. While this work successfully removes the need to specify layer widths *a priori*, it introduces new, sensitive hyperparameters. Specifically, the unit addition rate (ν), the unit addition frequency (ν_freq), and the radial step size (αr) all appear to implicitly control the final size and convergence of the network. It is not clear from the paper whether tuning this new set of parameters is any less difficult than performing a search over the discrete layer widths in a standard parametric network. The paper does not claim to make training easier, but the lack of analysis on the sensitivity to these new hyperparameters makes it difficult to assess the overall practical utility of the framework.

2.  **Insufficient Comparison and Scalability Concerns:** The paper emphasizes that the proposed method can find smaller networks that achieve performance comparable to or better than larger, fixed-size networks. This is a valuable goal, particularly for deployment on resource-constrained devices. However, the experiments do not compare against the extensive literature on network pruning and compression. It is unclear if dynamically growing and shrinking a network produces better final architectures than simply training an oversized network and pruning it post-hoc, which is a widely used and effective strategy. Furthermore, the dynamic nature of the training process, which involves continuously adding and removing units, seems inherently slower than training a fixed-size network. The paper does not provide a thorough analysis of this computational overhead or discuss how the approach would scale to the much larger models used in state-of-the-art applications. This raises concerns about whether the method is practical for large-scale problems where training time is a critical factor.

To help improve the paper, I offer the following suggestions and questions for the authors:

These points are intended as constructive feedback and are not the primary basis for my decision.
*   **Comparison to Baselines:** The claims about model compactness would be significantly strengthened by including experimental comparisons to established network pruning techniques (e.g., training a larger network and then applying l1 or l2-based pruning to remove units).
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** An ablation study on the key new hyperparameters (ν, ν_freq, αr) would be very insightful. How does the final network size and performance vary as these parameters are changed? This would help readers understand the robustness and tuning difficulty of the proposed method.
*   **Scalability Analysis:** While the poker dataset experiment is a good first step, a more detailed discussion on scalability would be beneficial. Could the authors comment on the expected computational overhead compared to training a parametric network of similar final size? How does the training time scale with the number of units added throughout the entire process, not just the final number of units?

I would appreciate it if the authors could address the following questions in their response:
*   What is the intuition for why dynamically growing a network from a small size should be superior to starting with a larger, overparameterized network and pruning it?
*   In the analysis of the training process (Figure 3C), the network size grows linearly for a long time before plateauing. This suggests a significant period of training with a large number of "transient" units. Could you quantify the computational cost of this "overhead" compared to a standard training run of the final, converged network?
