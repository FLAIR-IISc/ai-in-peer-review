I agree with Reviewer 2 that the core idea of this paper is compelling. The concept of dynamically adding or removing units, allowing a model to grow or shrink based on the demands of the problem and data rather than on user-defined priors, is a promising research direction.

The authors present a key theoretical result (Theorem 1) proving that under fan-in or fan-out regularization, the optimum of the error function is achieved with a finite number of parameters. This guarantees that the network will not grow indefinitely and simply overfit the training data. This result is reminiscent of traditional methods like Lasso or Elastic Net, where regularization induces sparsity in the weights.

While this is a valuable theoretical contribution, I found the intuition behind it underdeveloped in the main text. The result feels somewhat intuitive, and I believe the paper would be significantly strengthened by dedicating more space to exploring its implications. For instance, some of the discussion on prior work, while useful, could be condensed to make room for a deeper analysis of this central theorem.

I have several comments and suggestions for improvement:

1.  A crucial experiment to demonstrate the practical utility of this method would be to compare its performance against a standard, fixed-architecture network that has the same final number of units. In other words, once your method has converged to a final architecture, train a network with that same architecture from scratch. This would directly test the efficiency of the proposed learning procedure. Does dynamically adding and removing units find a better configuration than a fixed one of the same size? Does it effectively optimize a trade-off between performance and model complexity (e.g., memory)?

    I recognize that Figure 2 presents experiments along these lines, but the results appear mixed and the figure itself is not immediately clear. While it's understandable that the dynamically-sized nets might sometimes underperform, the cases where they *outperform* the fixed model are particularly intriguing and counter-intuitive. If the final, optimal architecture is known, why would the dynamic training process yield a better result than training that same architecture from scratch? I encourage the authors to provide more insight into this phenomenon, as it could highlight a key benefit of their approach (e.g., avoiding poor local minima).

2.  As mentioned earlier, Theorem 1 and its implications deserve a more thorough discussion. The theorem currently feels disconnected from the rest of the narrative. Beyond the formal proof in the appendix, what is the key insight? Can the authors provide a plain-English explanation of what the theorem implies for practitioners? While the conclusion—that the network size remains finite—may seem intuitive, a deeper discussion could reveal more powerful insights. This theoretical result would be more convincing if supported by targeted experiments. Specifically:
    *   **The role of lambda:** How sensitive is the final network structure to the choice of the regularization parameter, lambda? It seems that lambda is the primary factor controlling the final architecture; is this correct? Is it possible to estimate an appropriate lambda based on properties of the dataset?
    *   **Initialization Sensitivity:** How much does the final learned structure depend on the initial random weights? Do different initializations lead to significantly different final networks?
    *   **Combined Regularizers:** Does the theoretical result of Theorem 1 still hold when fan-in and fan-out regularizers are combined?

I also have a few requests for clarification:

1.  The paper states that adding zero units changes the regularizer's value. This is not immediately obvious. For example, the L2 norm of a weight vector does not change if you append zeros to it. Could the authors clarify this point?

2.  The definition of a "zero unit" seems ambiguous. It is defined as a unit where either the fan-in or fan-out weights are all zero. However, to remove a unit without altering the network's output function `f`, it seems necessary for *both* the fan-in and fan-out weights to be zero. Please clarify this definition.

I have raised my score to 7 in light of the paper's novel contributions, and I am hopeful that the authors can address these points to further strengthen their work.
