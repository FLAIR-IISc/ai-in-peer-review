This paper addresses the problem of automatically determining the optimal size of a neural network during training, allowing the network to adapt its capacity. This is achieved by dynamically adding and removing units based on weight magnitudes, coupled with a group sparsity regularization to encourage efficient network structures. The core theoretical contribution lies in demonstrating that, with appropriate regularization, the training error is minimized by a network with a finite number of units. Initial experimental results suggest this framework is viable and competitive.

I recommend **rejecting** the paper in its current form. While the idea is elegant and the theoretical grounding is sound, the practical benefits and scalability remain unconvincing given the current experimental evaluation. The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison to established techniques for network size optimization and reduction.

The motivation for this work is clear: to reduce the burden of hyperparameter tuning and potentially find more efficient network architectures. However, the proposed method introduces its own set of hyperparameters (addition rate, shrinkage rate) that require tuning, shifting rather than eliminating the hyperparameter optimization challenge. The authors acknowledge this, but the extent to which these new hyperparameters are easier or harder to tune than traditional ones is not explored. Furthermore, the paper demonstrates that networks can be trained with fewer units, achieving comparable performance to parametrically trained networks. This is promising, but a critical comparison with existing network pruning and compression techniques is missing. Do the proposed networks outperform pruned networks of similar size in terms of accuracy, training time, or resource usage?

A key concern is the potential trade-off between training time and network size. The dynamic addition and removal of units, coupled with the continuous optimization process, could lead to significantly longer training times compared to training fixed-size networks, even when accounting for hyperparameter search in the latter case. The paper does not address this scalability concern adequately. How does the training time scale with network size and dataset complexity? Is this approach practical for large-scale problems? The experiments, while helpful, are limited in scope and do not provide sufficient evidence to address these questions.

The experimental evaluation, while well-executed, feels preliminary. The results are encouraging, but more extensive experiments on larger and more diverse datasets are needed to demonstrate the generalizability and practical utility of the proposed approach.

To improve the paper, I suggest the following:

*   **Comparative Analysis:** Conduct a thorough comparison against state-of-the-art network pruning and compression techniques (e.g., magnitude pruning, knowledge distillation).  Compare not only accuracy but also training time, inference time, and model size.
*   **Scalability Analysis:** Investigate the scalability of the proposed approach to larger datasets and network architectures.  Present empirical results showing how training time scales with network size and data complexity.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity Analysis:**  Provide a more detailed analysis of the sensitivity of the results to the hyperparameters of the proposed method. Explore techniques for automating the tuning of these hyperparameters.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Perform ablation studies to evaluate the contribution of each component of the proposed framework (e.g., the specific form of the regularization, the AdaRad optimizer).
*   **Theoretical Extension:** Investigate the theoretical guarantees of convergence and generalization for the proposed approach.

I have the following clarifying questions for the authors:

*   How does the choice of the radial and angular step sizes (αr, αφ) affect the final network size and performance?
*   What is the computational cost of the AdaRad algorithm compared to standard optimization algorithms like SGD or Adam, especially concerning memory usage?
*   How sensitive is the performance to the choice of the initial network size (d0)?
*   What is the practical impact of the unit addition rate (ν) on the training process and the final network size?
*   Could you elaborate on the limitations of the current framework and potential directions for future research?
