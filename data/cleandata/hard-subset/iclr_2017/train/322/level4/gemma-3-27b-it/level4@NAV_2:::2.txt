I agree with reviewer 2 on the interesting core idea of the paper: dynamically adjusting network size during training to match the problem's complexity, rather than relying on user-defined priors.

The authors present an interesting theoretical result demonstrating that, under fan-in or fan-out regularization, the error function's optimum is achieved with a finite number of parameters – preventing indefinite network growth and overfitting. This is reminiscent of techniques like Lasso or Elastic Net, which induce sparse weights through regularization. I would have appreciated more intuitive explanation surrounding this theorem; while a valuable result (and intuitively plausible to me), the paper could benefit from dedicating more space to discussing its implications and providing further insight. Perhaps reducing the discussion of prior work could create room for a deeper exploration of these theoretical foundations. Please also see specific questions regarding Theorem 1 below.

I have several comments and questions:

1. A compelling experiment would compare the performance of your dynamically-sized network to a conventionally trained network with a *fixed* number of nodes, matched to the final size of the dynamic network. This would directly demonstrate the efficiency gained by automatically adding or removing units. Does your method effectively eliminate unnecessary nodes while adding those that are needed, optimizing for performance versus model complexity? Figure 2 attempts to address this, but is difficult to interpret. The results are mixed, with nonparametric networks sometimes outperforming, and sometimes underperforming, their parametric counterparts. Even in cases where parametric networks achieve higher accuracy, the adaptive structure discovery process of your method remains valuable. However, I’m unclear why the nonparametric approach can *sometimes* surpass a parametrically-trained network of the same final size. Could you provide more insight into this phenomenon?

2. The discussion surrounding Theorem 1 feels incomplete. Beyond the mathematical proof (in the Appendix), what is the central insight of the theorem? What does it convey in accessible terms? To me, the conclusion appears almost self-evident. Is there a subtle yet powerful implication that isn't being highlighted? I believe this theoretical result deserves more attention, potentially with additional experiments designed to validate it. For example, can the optimal regularization parameter (lambda) be predicted from the data itself? Is there a data property that guides the selection of lambda? My impression is that lambda is a key determinant of the final network structure – is this accurate? Additionally, how sensitive is the final network structure to the initial random weight initialization? Do different initializations lead to significantly different networks? What happens when fan-in and fan-out regularizers are combined – does the theorem still hold?

I have a few specific questions:

1. You state that adding zero units changes the regularizer value. Could you clarify this? For example, does the L2 norm change when you add zero-valued weights?

2. The definition of a zero unit – having either the fan-in *or* the fan-out weights being zero – is potentially misleading. I believe you intend both fan-in and fan-out weights to be zero, as that’s the only scenario permitting unit removal without altering the network’s output, *f*. This point should be clarified.

I rate this paper a 7, with the expectation that the authors will address these comments and questions.
