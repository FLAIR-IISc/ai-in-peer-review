This paper introduces a novel nonparametric neural network capable of learning its own size during training. The core concept involves randomly adding zero-valued units and employing sparse regularization to effectively prune irrelevant connections. While the approach shares similarities with a randomized search over a discrete model space, the use of sparse regularization provides a principled way to eliminate unnecessary units. Addressing the challenge of determining optimal network capacity is an important contribution, and the paper presents encouraging results.

However, several points require further clarification. Primarily, the computational complexity of the proposed algorithm warrants a more detailed analysis. The decomposition of fan-in weights into parallel and orthogonal components, coupled with the transformation to radial-angular coordinates, potentially introduces significant overhead. The authors should discuss the additional computational cost relative to training a parametric neural network with a fixed architecture, quantifying the increase in operations. Supporting this analysis with running time experiments would be highly valuable. 

Furthermore, the observation that the nonparametric network tends to converge to smaller architectures on convex datasets, resulting in performance inferior to parametric networks, is intriguing. Understanding *why* this occurs is crucial. The authors should offer insights into this behavior – perhaps relating it to the nature of the convex dataset and the regularization strength, or discussing limitations of the method in such scenarios. Exploring this discrepancy could reveal important considerations for applying this technique to different problem domains.
