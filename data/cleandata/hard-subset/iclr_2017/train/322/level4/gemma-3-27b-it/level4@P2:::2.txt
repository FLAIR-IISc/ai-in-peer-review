This paper presents an interesting approach to automatically determining neural network size during training, termed nonparametric neural networks. The core idea – dynamically growing and shrinking the network – is a valuable contribution and addresses a significant challenge in deep learning: efficient model selection. The authors provide a theoretical result guaranteeing a finite optimal network size under specific regularization conditions, which is a positive aspect of the work.

**Decision: Reject** with a strong encouragement to revise and resubmit. While the idea is promising and the theoretical result intriguing, the paper falls short in clearly articulating the advantages of the proposed method and providing compelling empirical evidence of its superiority over well-established techniques. The experimental results are mixed, and the discussion lacks sufficient depth to fully understand the implications of the findings.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Lack of Clear Advantage:** The central question—does this method outperform training a parametric network of comparable size or using standard hyperparameter optimization techniques like random search?—is not convincingly answered. Figure 2 demonstrates mixed results, with nonparametric networks sometimes outperforming and sometimes underperforming parametric networks. The paper needs to more clearly articulate *why* the nonparametric approach should be preferred. Is it improved performance, reduced computational cost, or a more robust search of the model space? Currently, the benefits are unclear. The statement that the method "helps discovering the structure" is insufficient justification.

2. **Insufficient Discussion of Theorem 1:** The theoretical result regarding the finite optimal network size is a key contribution, but its significance is not adequately explained. The authors acknowledge its intuitive nature, but fail to elaborate on its implications beyond stating that the network doesn’t grow indefinitely. What practical consequences does this theorem have for the training process? Does it inform strategies for setting the regularization parameter λ? A more in-depth discussion, perhaps with illustrative examples, is crucial. Is there an expectation that the optimal λ is data-dependent, and can it be estimated?

3. **Experimental Clarity & Scope:** The experimental setup could be strengthened. A direct comparison to a parametric network trained *from scratch* with the same final number of nodes is essential to demonstrate the advantage of dynamic growth/shrinkage.  Currently, the comparisons feel somewhat indirect. The experiments on the poker dataset are positive, but further analysis and comparison to other methods on larger datasets are needed to bolster the claims.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Clarify the Motivation:** Expand on the scenarios where this method would be most beneficial. Is it particularly useful when prior knowledge about the optimal network size is limited?
* **Address the Questions Raised in the Original Review:**
    * **Regularizer Impact:** Investigate how the regularization parameter λ influences the final network structure. Is there a relationship between data properties and the optimal λ?
    * **Initialization Sensitivity:** Analyze the impact of different random initializations on the final network architecture.
    * **Combined Regularization:** Explore the effects of combining fan-in and fan-out regularizers.
    * **Zero Units Definition:** Clarify the definition of a "zero unit." It should explicitly state that *both* fan-in and fan-out weights must be zero for a unit to be removable without affecting the network output.
    * **Regularizer Change with Zero Units:** Explain why adding zero units changes the regularizer value. Specifically, address if the L2 norm changes when zero values are added.
* **Algorithm 2 (AdaRad-M):** While the inclusion of momentum is a positive addition, a comparative analysis of AdaRad and AdaRad-M's performance would be valuable.
* **Theoretical Insights:** Explore the connection between the proposed method and existing regularization techniques like Lasso and Elastic Net, drawing parallels and highlighting the unique contributions of this work.
* **Computational Cost:** While alluded to, a more detailed analysis of the computational cost associated with AdaRad compared to other optimization methods would be beneficial.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more intuitive explanation of Theorem 1, detailing its practical implications for training and hyperparameter selection?
2. What specific advantages does the nonparametric approach offer over simply training a parametric network of a similar size obtained through standard hyperparameter optimization techniques?
3.  Do you observe a correlation between data characteristics and the optimal value of the regularization parameter λ?
4. How sensitive is the final network architecture to different random initializations? What is the variance in performance across different runs with different initializations?
