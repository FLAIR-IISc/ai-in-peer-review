This paper introduces a method for dynamically adjusting the number of units in a neural network during training. The approach is both conceptually simple and well-motivated: units are added or removed based on having zero-valued input or output weights, guided by a group sparsity regularization term. A key theoretical result demonstrates that, with appropriate regularization, the loss function can be minimized by a network with a finite number of units. While this doesn’t guarantee optimal generalization performance, initial experiments suggest the method doesn’t inherently lead to over- or under-fitting.

A potential benefit of learning network size is a reduction in hyperparameter tuning. However, the paper acknowledges, and rightly so, that this benefit is not fully realized. The proposed method introduces new hyperparameters – controlling unit addition frequency and weight decay – which govern the effective network size and may not be demonstrably easier to tune than those used in standard, fixed-size networks. While the authors don’t claim simplified training, the lack of clear improvement in this area is a noticeable drawback.

The authors rightly emphasize the potential for achieving comparable performance with smaller networks, which could translate to reduced computational cost, power consumption, and memory footprint—particularly important for deployment on resource-constrained devices. However, a critical omission is a comparative analysis against existing network compression techniques, such as pruning. Without such a comparison, it’s difficult to assess the competitiveness of this approach relative to established methods for reducing network size.

A significant concern is the coupling between hyperparameters controlling network size and training time. This may lead to substantially longer training times compared to parametric approaches, even accounting for the hyperparameter search often required in those scenarios. While grid search can be computationally expensive, practical experience often allows for informed hyperparameter selection in many cases. The proposed method’s inherent slowness, on the other hand, might be unavoidable. The paper does not address the scalability of this issue to larger networks, raising doubts about the practicality of the approach for complex, real-world problems.

The included experiments are helpful as a proof-of-concept and offer encouraging initial results, but they are not extensive enough to be fully convincing. Further investigation on larger and more challenging datasets is needed to validate the approach’s practical viability and potential benefits.

Overall, this is a well-written and interesting paper that presents a potentially valuable contribution. The long-term vision of networks that can dynamically adapt and grow – crucial for lifelong learning – is inspiring. However, the current results remain preliminary and speculative. Further research is required to demonstrate the method’s practical scalability and its advantages over existing network compression techniques.
