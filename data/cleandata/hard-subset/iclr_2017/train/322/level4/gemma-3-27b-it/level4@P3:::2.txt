## Review of NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS

This paper presents an intriguing approach to neural network architecture selection, automatically adapting network size during training. The core idea of dynamically adding and removing units based on regularization penalties is promising, and the theoretical result demonstrating a finite optimum under fan-in or fan-out regularization is a valuable contribution. The method shares similarities with established techniques like Lasso and Elastic Net, promoting sparsity, but operates within a dynamic training framework.

**Decision:** Reject. While the ideas are interesting and the theoretical foundation is solid, the empirical results are mixed and lack sufficient clarity to justify acceptance. The paper feels somewhat incomplete, with key theoretical aspects underexplored and experimental analysis requiring further refinement.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The primary weakness lies in the limited convincingness of the experimental validation. Figure 2, while attempting to compare non-parametric and parametric networks, is difficult to interpret and the results are inconsistent across datasets. While the authors acknowledge some instances where non-parametric networks outperform their parametric counterparts, the reasons for this improvement remain unclear. The claim that the non-parametric approach can *discover* network structure is interesting but needs stronger support. It’s not immediately evident why a network allowed to evolve during training should consistently outperform a parametrically-sized network trained from scratch, given sufficient optimization. 

Furthermore, the paper dedicates considerable space to describing related work but comparatively little to a detailed, intuitive explanation of Theorem 1. The theorem itself, guaranteeing a finite optimum, feels somewhat intuitive and could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of its implications. Why is this result significant? What are its limitations? What practical guidance does it offer? The potential link between the regularization parameter λ and data properties is a particularly compelling avenue for further investigation that is only briefly touched upon.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

The following suggestions are aimed at strengthening the paper and addressing the identified weaknesses:

*   **Clarify Experimental Results:** Re-present Figure 2 with improved clarity, perhaps using separate plots for each dataset and clearly highlighting the performance differences between the methods. Provide statistical significance testing to support any claims of superiority.
*   **In-depth Discussion of Theorem 1:** Expand the discussion surrounding Theorem 1. Explain its core insight in accessible terms and explore its implications for network design. Investigate whether λ can be estimated from the data and how this impacts the resulting network structure.
*   **Initialization Sensitivity:** Conduct experiments to assess the sensitivity of the method to initial weight values. Do different initializations lead to significantly different final network structures?
*   **Combined Regularization:** Explore the effects of combining fan-in and fan-out regularization. Does this yield improved performance or stability? Does the theoretical guarantee still hold?
*   **Addressing the Zero Unit Definition:** Clarify the definition of a "zero unit." As noted, a unit requires *both* fan-in and fan-out weights to be zero for removal without altering the network's output.
*   **Regularizer Impact of Zero Units:** Elaborate on why adding zero units changes the regularizer value. While it's true the regularization term changes, it seems counterintuitive that adding zero-valued weights directly impacts the L2 norm. A more detailed explanation is needed.
*   **Ablation Study:** A key experiment would be to compare the performance of a non-parametric network trained to a final size with a parametrically-sized network *of the same final size* trained from scratch. This would isolate the benefit of the dynamic training process and directly address the question of whether the method optimizes performance versus memory.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  What is the primary reason you believe non-parametric networks sometimes outperform comparable parametric networks trained from scratch? Is it the dynamic adaptation to the data, the exploration of a wider architecture space, or another factor?
2.  Could you provide a more intuitive explanation of Theorem 1? What is the key insight this theorem provides beyond simply stating that the optimization problem has a finite optimum?
3.  How much does the final network structure depend on the initial random weight initialization? Have you quantified the variability in network structure across different initializations?
4.  Why does adding zero units alter the regularizer value, particularly in the context of an L2 penalty? Can you clarify this point?
5.  Do you anticipate any challenges scaling this approach to very large datasets and complex network architectures? What computational optimizations might be necessary?
