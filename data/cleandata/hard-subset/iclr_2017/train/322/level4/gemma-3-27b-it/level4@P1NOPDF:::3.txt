This paper addresses the problem of dynamically adjusting the number of units used during network training. The approach is simple, elegant, and well-motivated: units with zero input or output weights are added or removed during training, guided by a group sparsity norm for regularization. The main theoretical contribution is demonstrating that, with appropriate regularization, the loss is minimized by a network with a finite number of units. While this result doesn’t guarantee optimal performance – the resulting network could still over- or under-fit – initial experiments suggest this isn’t a major issue.

A potential benefit of learning the network size is easing hyperparameter tuning. However, this approach doesn’t fully resolve that challenge. The network still relies on several hyperparameters that indirectly control the final number of units, including those governing unit addition frequency and weight decay rates. It’s unclear whether these hyperparameters are easier to tune than those used in standard network training. The authors rightly don’t claim simplified training, but it's somewhat disappointing that it doesn’t appear to be the case.

The authors do highlight their ability to train networks with fewer units while achieving comparable performance to parametrically trained networks. This is potentially significant, as smaller networks reduce runtime at inference, as well as power consumption and memory footprint – particularly important for mobile devices. However, they don’t experimentally compare against existing methods for reducing the size of *already trained* networks (e.g., network pruning), making it difficult to assess the competitiveness of this approach.

Another potential drawback is that the same hyperparameters govern both the network’s size and training time. This could lead to substantially slower training compared to parametric approaches with fixed hyperparameters. While many parametric methods require computationally expensive grid searches for hyperparameter selection, experience often allows for relatively easy choices in similar problems. This means the cost of grid search isn’t always incurred, whereas the slowness of the proposed approach may be inherent. The authors don’t discuss the scalability of this issue to larger networks, raising concerns about the practical viability of this method for large-scale applications due to potentially prohibitive training times. 

Overall, the experiments are helpful and encouraging, but not comprehensive enough to be fully convincing. I would like to see results on much larger problems before I’m confident this approach is truly practical or broadly applicable.

In summary, this is an interesting and clearly written paper that presents a potentially useful idea. The broader vision of building networks capable of growing and adapting through lifelong learning is inspiring, and work of this nature may be crucial for realizing that vision. However, the current results remain somewhat preliminary.
