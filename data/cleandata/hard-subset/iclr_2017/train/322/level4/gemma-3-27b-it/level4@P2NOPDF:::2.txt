This paper presents an interesting approach to network growth and shrinkage, allowing the model to adapt its size to the complexity of the data rather than being constrained by predefined architectural choices. I concur with Reviewer 2 regarding the novelty of this direction. The authors demonstrate a compelling theoretical result: under fan-in or fan-out regularization, the error function's optimum is achievable with a finite number of parameters, preventing indefinite network growth and potential overfitting.

This result is reminiscent of sparsity-inducing regularization techniques like Lasso or Elastic Net, and I believe the paper would benefit from more intuitive explanation of the theorem’s underpinnings. While the theorem itself is a valuable contribution—and intuitively plausible—the paper currently prioritizes related work over a deeper discussion of its implications. Reallocating space from the literature review to elaborate on the key insights of Theorem 1 would strengthen the paper considerably. (See also point 2 below.)

I have several specific comments and suggestions:

1. A crucial experiment to demonstrate the efficacy of this dynamic network adjustment would be a comparison against a statically sized network. Specifically, a network with the *same final number of nodes* as the dynamically adjusted network – determined after complete learning – but with a fixed architecture determined a priori. Success in this comparison would validate the benefits of allowing nodes to be added or removed, demonstrating that the method effectively allocates resources, saving unnecessary nodes and creating those that are most impactful. This directly highlights the efficiency of the proposed approach in optimizing both performance and potentially memory usage. While Figure 2 attempts to address this, the figure is difficult to interpret and requires careful scrutiny. The mixed results shown – sometimes outperforming and sometimes underperforming static networks – are interesting, suggesting the method can reveal underlying structure, but necessitate further investigation. Why does the non-parametric learning process sometimes yield better results than training a parametric network from scratch, given that the final network size is known in the latter case? Providing more insight into this observation would be valuable.

2. Theorem 1 feels somewhat underexplored. Beyond the mathematical proof (provided in the appendix), what is the central takeaway in a conceptual sense? The conclusion feels almost self-evident, and the paper lacks a clear articulation of its power or significance. Expanding the discussion to address this would significantly improve the paper. Furthermore, exploring the practical implications of the theorem could yield valuable research avenues. For instance, can the optimal regularization parameter, lambda, be predicted based on data characteristics? My intuition suggests lambda is a crucial determinant of the final network structure – is this accurate? How sensitive is the final network structure to the initialization scheme? Do different random initializations lead to substantially different architectures, and if so, how can this be quantified? Finally, how does the theorem hold up when fan-in and fan-out regularizers are combined?

I also have a few clarifying questions:

1. The paper states that adding zero-valued units changes the regularizer value. This seems counterintuitive; for example, adding zero values shouldn’t alter an L2 norm. Could you please elaborate on this point?

2. The definition of a “zero unit” appears to require *both* fan-in and fan-out weights to be zero. Otherwise, removing the unit would alter the function f. The current wording is ambiguous and should be clarified. 

I have adjusted my rating to a 7, with the hope that the authors will address these comments and further develop the theoretical and experimental aspects of this promising work.
