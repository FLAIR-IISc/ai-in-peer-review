I agree with reviewer 2 regarding the most interesting aspect of this paper: the idea of dynamically adjusting network capacity by adding or removing units. This approach allows a model to grow or shrink based on the problem and data requirements, rather than being constrained by user-defined prior knowledge.

The authors present a compelling theoretical result demonstrating that, under fan-out or fan-in regularization, the optimum of the error function is achievable with a finite number of parameters, preventing indefinite network growth and potential overfitting. This result is reminiscent of more traditional regularization techniques like Lasso or Elastic Net, which induce sparsity in weights. While the theorem is a valuable contribution – and somewhat intuitive, at least to me – I would have appreciated greater discussion of the intuition behind it. Allocating less space to prior work discussion, while still important, could allow for a more thorough examination of this core theoretical result. Please also see point 2 below for further suggestions regarding the theorem.

I have several other comments:

1. A compelling experiment would be to demonstrate that a model employing automatic node addition/removal can outperform a network with the same *final* number of nodes, but with a fixed architecture determined a priori. This would directly illustrate the efficiency gains of this approach. The core interest lies in whether the method effectively prunes unnecessary nodes and allocates resources to those that are needed – essentially optimizing the trade-off between performance and memory usage.
I acknowledge that experiments along these lines are presented in Figure 2, though with mixed results. The figure is, unfortunately, not very clear, requiring careful inspection for interpretation. In some cases, the non-parametric nets perform better, while in others they underperform compared to the parametric ones. Even in cases where the parametric nets are superior, the method’s ability to *discover* a suitable network structure is valuable. However, I am unclear as to why the non-parametric learning can sometimes outperform a parametric network trained from scratch, given that the final network architecture is known in advance. Further insight into this phenomenon would be beneficial.

2. The discussion surrounding Theorem 1 feels incomplete. While the proof is provided in the Appendix, the key insight of the theorem itself requires more elaboration. Beyond the mathematical details, what is the takeaway in plain English? To me, the conclusion feels almost self-evident. Is there a more profound implication that is not being highlighted? I believe this theoretical result deserves more extensive exploration, potentially including additional experiments to support it. For instance, could the optimal regularization parameter, lambda, be predicted based on the characteristics of the data? My intuition suggests that lambda plays a crucial role in determining the final network structure. Is this accurate? Furthermore, how sensitive is the final network structure to the initialization of weights? Do different initializations lead to significantly different network configurations, and if so, how substantial are these differences? Finally, what happens when fan-in and fan-out regularizers are combined – does the theorem still hold?

I have a few additional questions:

1. The claim that adding zero units changes the regularizer value is unclear. For example, does the L2 norm change when zero-valued weights are added?
2. The definition of “zero units” as having either the fan-in or fan-out weights equal to zero is potentially misleading. I believe the intention is that *both* fan-in and fan-out weights must be zero to allow for unit removal without altering the function f. This point requires clarification.

I have adjusted my rating to a 7, with the hope that the authors will address these comments and further refine the presentation of their work.
