This paper addresses the problem of automatically determining the optimal size of a neural network during training. This is achieved in a conceptually simple and well-motivated manner: units with zero-valued input or output weights are added or removed during training, while a group sparsity norm acts as a regularizer, encouraging unit weights to become zero. The main theoretical contribution is a proof demonstrating that, with appropriate regularization, the loss function is minimized by a network with a finite number of units. While this result doesn’t guarantee optimal generalization, initial experiments suggest it doesn't lead to obvious overfitting or underfitting.

I recommend **rejection** based on a lack of compelling evidence that this approach offers substantial advantages over existing methods, and concerns about scalability. While the idea is promising, the results presented do not yet demonstrate a clear benefit that justifies the added complexity.

The potential advantage of learning network size during training is to reduce the burden of hyperparameter tuning. However, this approach doesn't truly eliminate this burden. The network still relies on several hyperparameters – controlling unit addition frequency and weight decay rate, for example – that indirectly govern the final network size. It's unclear if tuning these parameters is easier than tuning those in standard, parametric approaches. While the authors don’t explicitly claim simplified training, the lack of demonstrable improvement in this area is somewhat disappointing.

The authors emphasize the ability to train networks with fewer units, achieving comparable performance to parametrically trained networks. This is a potentially valuable outcome, as smaller networks offer benefits like reduced runtime at inference, and lower power consumption and memory footprint – particularly relevant for mobile devices. However, the paper doesn’t compare performance against existing methods for reducing the size of *pre-trained* networks (e.g., pruning techniques). Therefore, it’s unclear whether this approach is competitive with the current state-of-the-art in network compression.

A further potential drawback is the coupling between hyperparameters governing network size and training time. This could lead to significantly slower training compared to parametric approaches with fixed hyperparameters. While grid search can be time-consuming for parametric models, experience often allows for reasonably efficient hyperparameter selection in many cases. The slowness of this method might be inherent, and the authors don’t adequately address how this will scale to larger networks. The scalability issue raises concerns about the practical applicability of this approach to real-world, large-scale problems.

The experiments are helpful and provide initial encouragement, but they are not comprehensive enough to be entirely convincing. I would need to see results on significantly larger and more complex datasets before I could confidently assess the practicality and widespread utility of this approach.

Overall, I found this to be an interesting and well-written paper with a potentially valuable idea. The long-term vision of building networks that can grow and adapt through lifelong learning is inspiring, and this type of work might be necessary to achieve such a vision. However, the current results remain largely speculative. 

Here are some questions for the authors:

*   Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of training, specifically comparing the time required to train a network with your approach versus a standard, parametrically trained network of comparable performance?
*   How does the performance of your method compare to state-of-the-art network pruning techniques (e.g., those based on magnitude pruning, or more advanced methods)?
*   You mention the importance of the angular and radial step sizes. Could you elaborate on a strategy for automatically tuning these hyperparameters, or provide more guidance on how to select appropriate values for different datasets?
*   How sensitive is the performance of the method to the choice of initial network size? Are there guidelines for selecting a reasonable starting point?
*   The theoretical guarantee relies on the p-norm regularization. Have you explored other regularization schemes and their impact on the network size and performance?
