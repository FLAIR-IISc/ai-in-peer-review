This paper addresses the problem of dynamically adjusting the number of units in a neural network during training. The approach is both simple and elegant, motivated by the idea of adding or removing units with effectively zero input or output weights. A group sparsity norm is employed for regularization, and the authors provide a theoretical contribution demonstrating that, with appropriate regularization, the loss function is minimized by a network with a finite number of units. While this theoretical guarantee doesn’t preclude overfitting or underfitting in practice, initial experiments suggest these issues are not prevalent.

A key potential benefit of learning network size is the reduction in hyperparameter tuning. However, the paper acknowledges – and this reviewer agrees with – that this is not a complete solution. The proposed method introduces new hyperparameters that govern the network’s growth and weight decay, which indirectly control the final number of units. It remains unclear whether tuning these new hyperparameters will be demonstrably easier than tuning the architecture-related hyperparameters in standard, parametrically-sized networks. While the authors wisely refrain from claiming simplified training, the lack of evident improvement in this area is somewhat disappointing.

The authors rightly emphasize the potential for training networks with fewer units while maintaining comparable performance to larger, parametrically trained networks. This is a significant advantage, as smaller networks offer reduced runtime during inference, alongside decreased power consumption and memory footprint – crucial considerations for deployment on resource-constrained devices like mobile phones. However, the experimental evaluation does not include a comparison to existing network compression techniques, such as pruning, which aim to reduce the size of already-trained networks. Without such a benchmark, it's difficult to assess the competitiveness of this approach relative to established methods for achieving network efficiency.

A potential drawback is the coupling of hyperparameters governing network size and training time. This could lead to substantially longer training times compared to parametric approaches with fixed architectures. While hyperparameter optimization via grid search can also be time-consuming for standard networks, practical experience often allows for informed choices based on prior work. The proposed method’s potential for inherent slowness, however, is a more fundamental concern. The authors do not address the scalability of this approach to larger networks, raising questions about its practicality in those scenarios, where extended training times might become prohibitive.

The presented experiments are helpful and provide initial encouragement, but they lack the breadth and depth needed for a fully convincing demonstration. Further evaluation on significantly larger and more complex problems is necessary to establish the practical viability and broader applicability of this approach. 

Overall, this is a well-written and interesting paper that raises a worthwhile point. The vision of creating networks that can grow and adapt – potentially enabling lifelong learning – is inspiring. This work represents a step in that direction. However, the current results are preliminary and remain largely speculative. Further research and more comprehensive experiments are needed to solidify the potential of this approach.
