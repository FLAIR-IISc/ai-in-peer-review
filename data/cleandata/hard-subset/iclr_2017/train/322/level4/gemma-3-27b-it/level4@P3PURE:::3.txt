This paper addresses the problem of dynamically adjusting the number of units in a neural network during training. The approach is simple, elegant, and well-motivated: units with zero input or output weights are added or removed, guided by a group sparsity norm that encourages weights to approach zero. A key theoretical contribution is demonstrating that, with appropriate regularization, the loss is minimized by a network with a finite number of units. While this result does not guarantee optimal generalization performance – the network may still over- or under-fit – initial experiments suggest this is not a significant issue.

A potential benefit of learning network size is a reduction in hyperparameter tuning. However, this approach doesn't fully realize that benefit. The network still relies on several hyperparameters that indirectly control the final number of units, including those governing unit addition frequency and weight decay rates. It remains unclear whether these hyperparameters are easier to tune than those in standard, parametrically-sized networks. While the authors rightly avoid claiming simplified training, the lack of demonstrable improvement in this area is somewhat disappointing. 

The authors appropriately emphasize the ability to train networks with fewer units while maintaining comparable performance, which is significant because smaller networks can reduce inference time, power consumption, and memory footprint – particularly important for mobile devices. However, the paper lacks a direct experimental comparison to existing network compression techniques applied to parametrically trained networks (e.g., pruning). Consequently, it’s unclear whether this approach is competitive with current best practices for reducing network size.

A further potential drawback is the coupling of hyperparameters controlling network size and training duration. This could lead to substantially slower training compared to parametric approaches with fixed hyperparameters. While grid search can be time-consuming for parametric models, experience often allows for informed hyperparameter choices. The authors' method, however, may exhibit inherent slowness. The paper also doesn’t address the scalability of this issue to larger networks, raising concerns about practical applicability in such scenarios. 

The included experiments are helpful and encouraging, but not exhaustive or entirely convincing. I would require evidence from significantly larger problems before concluding that this approach is truly practical or broadly useful.

Overall, this is an interesting and clearly written paper that presents a potentially valuable contribution. The overarching vision of networks that can grow and adapt through life-long learning is inspiring, and work of this nature may be essential to achieving that goal. However, the current results remain largely speculative.
