This paper investigates dynamic network architectures, enabling the number of units to vary during training. The method is both conceptually simple and well-justified, adding or removing units based on zero-valued input or output weights, coupled with group sparsity regularization to promote weight reduction. A key theoretical result demonstrates that, with appropriate regularization, the loss function is minimized by a network containing a finite number of units. While this doesn't preclude overfitting or underfitting, initial experiments suggest these are not immediate concerns.

A potential benefit of learning network capacity is a reduction in hyperparameter tuning. However, the approach doesn't fully eliminate this need; several hyperparameters govern the evolving network size – addition frequency and weight decay rate, for instance – and their optimization isn’t demonstrably easier than in standard, fixed-size networks. The authors acknowledge this, but the lack of apparent simplification is somewhat disappointing.

The authors rightly emphasize the potential to achieve comparable performance with smaller networks, which is valuable for reducing inference time, power consumption, and memory usage, especially for resource-constrained devices. However, the work lacks comparison to existing network compression techniques like pruning, making it unclear how competitive this method is against state-of-the-art size reduction strategies. 

A further potential drawback is the coupling of unit number and training time via shared hyperparameters. This could lead to substantially longer training times compared to parametric approaches. While parametric training often involves costly hyperparameter searches, this isn't always the case due to transferrable experience; the proposed method’s slowness may be inherent. The authors do not address scalability to larger networks, raising concerns about practicality given potentially protracted training durations. 

The presented experiments are helpful and promising, but lack the breadth needed to be fully persuasive. More extensive evaluation on larger, more complex problems is necessary to establish the practical viability and broader applicability of this approach. 

Overall, this is a well-written and interesting paper presenting a potentially valuable contribution. The overarching goal of creating adaptable, lifelong-learning networks is inspiring, and work of this nature will likely be critical to achieving that goal. However, the current findings are still preliminary and largely theoretical.
