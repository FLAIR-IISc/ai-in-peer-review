## Review of "Nonparametric Neural Networks"

This paper introduces a novel nonparametric neural network model capable of automatically learning its size during training. The core idea centers around randomly adding “zero units” and employing sparse regularization to effectively prune irrelevant weights, dynamically adapting the network’s capacity. This approach addresses an important problem in deep learning – determining optimal network architecture – and presents promising initial results.

The method’s conceptual foundation appears akin to a guided random search over a discrete space of network sizes, leveraging sparse regularization to eliminate redundant units. While conceptually straightforward, the implementation introduces potentially significant computational overhead. Specifically, the decomposition of fan-in weights into parallel and orthogonal components, coupled with the subsequent transformation into radial-angular coordinates, warrants further investigation. The authors should detail the additional computational complexity of these operations relative to a standard parametric neural network. A quantitative comparison of the operational count, or ideally, running time experiments demonstrating the practical scalability of the proposed method, would substantially strengthen the paper. It's crucial to understand if the benefits of automated architecture search outweigh the increased computational burden, especially for large-scale datasets and complex models.

The paper reports an interesting observation: nonparametric networks tend to converge to smaller networks when trained on convex datasets, resulting in performance inferior to comparable parametric networks. The authors should delve deeper into this phenomenon. Is this behavior a consequence of the sparse regularization, the specific initialization scheme, or a limitation inherent to the method itself? Exploring this finding, perhaps through ablation studies or theoretical analysis, could offer valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed approach and guide future improvements. Understanding *why* the method performs differently on different data distributions is critical. 

Further points for consideration:

* **Zero Unit Initialization:** The method relies on initializing units with zero weights and introducing them randomly. While justified, a more detailed discussion of the potential impact of this initialization strategy on optimization, particularly in the early stages of training, would be beneficial.
* **Regularization Strength:** The performance of sparse regularization is heavily dependent on the regularization strength (lambda). The paper should explicitly justify the chosen value of lambda and investigate its sensitivity. A brief analysis of how different lambda values affect the final network size and performance would be valuable.
* **Comparison to Existing Methods:** The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison to existing techniques for dynamic network sizing, such as network pruning, growing neural gas, or methods based on reinforcement learning for architecture search. Highlighting the advantages and disadvantages of this approach relative to existing solutions would contextualize its contributions.
* **Clarity of Notation:** While generally well-written, some of the notation relating to the weight decomposition could be clarified for improved readability. Providing a concise summary of the key equations and their interpretations would be helpful.



Overall, this paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable contribution to the field of adaptive neural networks. The core idea is sound, and the initial results are promising. However, addressing the computational complexity concerns and providing a more in-depth analysis of the observed behavior on convex datasets are crucial for strengthening the paper and demonstrating its practical utility.
