I concur with Reviewer 2 regarding the paper’s most compelling aspect. The concept of dynamically adjusting model capacity by adding or removing units is a promising direction, allowing the network to adapt to the problem’s complexity and the data’s characteristics rather than being constrained by pre-defined architectural choices.

The authors present a noteworthy theoretical result demonstrating that, under fan-in or fan-out regularization, the optimal solution is achievable with a finite number of parameters—preventing indefinite growth and potential overfitting. This result is reminiscent of techniques like Lasso or Elastic Net, where regularization promotes sparse weight distributions. However, I would have appreciated greater intuition surrounding this theorem. While it's a valuable outcome—and intuitively plausible, at least to me—the paper would benefit from a more detailed exploration of its underlying principles. For instance, streamlining the discussion of related work could create space to more thoroughly analyze the theoretical findings. Please also see point 2 below for further suggestions.

I have several additional comments:

1. A compelling experiment would compare the performance of your dynamically sized model to a fixed-size network with the same final number of nodes, trained from the outset. This would directly demonstrate the efficacy of the adaptive capacity approach. The core strength of this method lies in its ability to prune unnecessary nodes and allocate resources to those that are beneficial; does it optimize for performance, memory usage, or both? 
While Figure 2 presents relevant experiments, the results are mixed and the figure itself lacks clarity, requiring careful inspection for interpretation. The observation that non-parametric nets sometimes outperform parametric ones, and vice-versa, underscores the method’s potential for structure discovery. However, it remains unclear why the non-parametric approach can occasionally surpass a parametrically trained network of known size. Further insight into this phenomenon would be valuable.

2. The discussion of Theorem 1 needs expansion. Currently, it feels presented without sufficient context or explanation. Beyond the proof in the Appendix, what is the central insight of the theorem? What does it convey in accessible terms? The conclusion appears almost self-evident, and I wonder if there's a more profound implication being overlooked. As previously mentioned, this theoretical result warrants more emphasis and supporting experiments. For example, could the optimal regularization parameter, lambda, be predicted based on the data itself? My intuition suggests that lambda is crucial in determining the final network structure. Is this accurate? How sensitive is the final network structure to the initialization? Do different random weight initializations lead to substantially different networks? What happens when fan-in and fan-out regularization are combined—does the theoretical result still hold?

I also have a few specific questions:

1. The claim that adding zero-valued units alters the regularizer value is unclear. For instance, does the L2 norm change when zero values are added?
2. The definition of “zero units” – having either fan-in or fan-out weights equal to zero – is potentially misleading. I believe you intend to define a zero unit as one with *both* fan-in and fan-out weights equal to zero; otherwise, removing the unit would alter the network’s output. This should be clarified.

I have adjusted my rating to 7, with the expectation that the authors will address the points raised above.
