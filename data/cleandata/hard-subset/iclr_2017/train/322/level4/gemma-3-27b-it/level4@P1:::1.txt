This paper proposes a nonparametric neural network model, which automatically learns the size of the model during the training process. The core idea is to dynamically add units and utilize sparsity-inducing regularization to prune irrelevant connections, effectively conducting a search over possible network sizes during a single training run. This addresses an important problem – automatically determining optimal network size without prior information – and the paper presents promising results. My main comments are listed below:

What is the computational overhead of the proposed algorithm? The decomposition of each fan-in weight into parallel and orthogonal components, and the subsequent transformation into radial-angular coordinates, may introduce significant computational cost. The authors should discuss this overhead in relation to parametric neural networks and, ideally, present some runtime experiments to quantify the difference.

The results on the convex dataset show that nonparametric networks converge to smaller models with inferior performance compared to parametric networks. Is there any insight into why this occurs? 

**Decision:** Reject.

**Reasons:** While the idea of dynamically growing and shrinking networks is interesting, the paper lacks sufficient evidence of consistent improvement over well-tuned parametric networks. The results are mixed, with strong performance on MNIST and rectangles, but notably *worse* performance on the convex dataset. The computational cost is also a concern, and the lack of a detailed analysis of this cost makes it difficult to assess the practicality of the approach.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The authors convincingly argue for the limitations of existing black-box methods for hyperparameter optimization, which require full network training runs for each evaluation. The proposed nonparametric approach, in principle, addresses this by allowing for continuous adaptation during training. The theoretical justification, specifically Theorem 1, is also a positive aspect.

However, the empirical evaluation doesn’t consistently demonstrate a clear advantage. While the nonparametric networks match or exceed the performance of parametric networks on two datasets, the performance drop on the convex dataset is significant and unexplained.  The authors acknowledge this, but do not offer a compelling explanation. The claim that nonparametric networks can achieve higher accuracy than is “possible by black-box methods” feels unsubstantiated given the demonstrated results.

The added computational complexity of the AdaRad algorithm, while acknowledged, is not thoroughly analyzed. A comparison of runtime performance is crucial for evaluating the practical value of this method. The fact that AdaRad requires maintaining running averages (φ̃l and associated variables) adds to the memory footprint, and this trade-off between performance and computational cost should be discussed.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement, not part of the decision):**

*   **Ablation Study:** Conduct an ablation study to assess the contribution of different components of the AdaRad algorithm, such as the radial and angular updates, and the CapNorm normalization.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Investigate the sensitivity of the results to the choice of hyperparameters, particularly λ, αφ, β, and ν.
*   **Scaling to Larger Datasets:** The poker dataset experiment is a good step, but further evaluation on other large-scale datasets would strengthen the conclusions.
*   **Theoretical Analysis:** Expand the theoretical analysis to provide a more comprehensive understanding of the convergence properties of the algorithm.
*   **Connection to Existing Work:** Explicitly discuss how this work relates to recent advances in neural architecture search (NAS) methods.
*   **Clarify Terminology:** The term “f-equivalence” feels somewhat vague and could benefit from a more precise definition.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of AdaRad, including a comparison to standard optimization algorithms like Adam or RMSprop, both in terms of operations and memory usage?
*   What is the intuition behind the poor performance on the convex dataset? Is it related to the specific characteristics of this dataset, or are there limitations in the algorithm that contribute to this result?
*   How sensitive is the performance to the choice of the angular step size (αφ)? Did you observe different optimal values for different datasets?
*   How does the unit addition rate (ν) affect the convergence and final performance of the network?
