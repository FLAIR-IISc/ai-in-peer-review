I agree with reviewer 2 regarding the interesting nature of this paper. The idea of dynamically removing or adding units is a compelling direction, allowing a model to grow or shrink based on the requirements of the problem and the data, rather than being constrained by prior user knowledge.

The authors present an interesting theoretical result proving that, under fan-out or fan-in regularization, the optimum of the error function is achievable with a finite number of parameters – thus preventing indefinite network growth and potential overfitting.

This approach reminds me of more traditional methods like Lasso or Elastic Net, where regularization induces sparse weights. I would have appreciated more intuition behind this theorem. While it’s a nice result, and somewhat intuitive (at least to me), I believe dedicating more space to discussing and exploring its implications would strengthen the paper. For example, reducing the discussion of prior work (which is valuable, but less crucial than dissecting the paper’s core result) could allow for a more thorough examination of the theoretical findings. Please also see point 2 below for further suggestions.

I have a few other comments:

1. An insightful experiment would be to demonstrate that a model like yours – where nodes are automatically added or removed – can outperform a network with the same *final* number of nodes, but with a fixed architecture determined from the outset. This would clearly illustrate the efficiency of your method. The core interest lies in whether your approach effectively saves unnecessary nodes and allocates resources to those that are needed, optimizing the trade-off between performance and memory usage.

I understand that experiments along these lines are presented in Figure 2, but the results are mixed. The figure itself is not particularly clear, requiring careful inspection for interpretation. 

In some cases, the non-parametric nets perform better, while in others, they perform worse than their parametric counterparts. Even when the parametric nets are superior, I still see value in your method, as it aids in discovering effective network structures. However, I don’t fully understand *why* the non-parametric version can sometimes outperform a network trained from scratch with a known architecture. What insight can you offer regarding this phenomenon?

2. Could you elaborate on the meaning and implications of Theorem 1? Currently, the theorem feels somewhat isolated, presented without sufficient discussion. Beyond the proofs provided in the Appendix, what is the key insight? What does it convey in plain language? To me, the conclusion feels almost natural and obvious. Is there a deeper, more powerful implication that isn't being highlighted?

As mentioned previously, I believe this theoretical result deserves greater attention, perhaps with additional experiments to support it. For example, could the regularization parameter, lambda, be predicted based on the data? Is there a property of the data that can help estimate an appropriate lambda value? My intuition is that lambda is a key determinant of the final network structure – is this correct?

Furthermore, how sensitive is the final network structure to the initial weights? Do different random initializations lead to significantly different networks? If so, how substantial are these differences? What happens when fan-in and fan-out regularizers are combined? Does the theoretical result still hold?

I have a few additional questions:

1. Why do you state that adding zero units changes the regularizer value? For example, does the L2 norm change if you add zero values?
2. Zero units are defined as having either the fan-in or fan-out weights equal to zero. I believe you mean that *both* fan-in and fan-out weights must be zero; otherwise, removing the unit would alter the output f. This should be clarified.

I have adjusted my rating to 7, with the hope that the authors will address these comments.
