This paper addresses the problem of dynamically adjusting the size of neural networks during training, allowing the number of units to evolve. The approach is elegantly motivated: units are added or removed based on zero-valued weights, guided by a group sparsity regularization term. A key theoretical contribution is proving that, with appropriate regularization, a finite-sized network will minimize the loss. Initial experiments suggest this doesn’t necessarily lead to overfitting.

I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit. While the core idea is promising and the theoretical justification solid, the experimental validation lacks sufficient breadth and convincing comparison to established techniques, raising concerns about practical scalability and utility.

The central motivation – easing hyperparameter tuning – appears largely unfulfilled. While the method avoids *explicitly* setting network size, it introduces new hyperparameters (unit addition rate, weight decay speed) that require tuning, and it’s unclear if these are demonstrably easier to optimize than traditional architectural choices. The authors acknowledge this, but it diminishes a key potential benefit.

The paper demonstrates the potential to achieve comparable performance with smaller networks. This is valuable, particularly regarding runtime, memory footprint, and power consumption. However, a crucial missing element is a direct comparison to existing network compression techniques like pruning, which often operate on pre-trained, parametrically-sized networks. Without this comparison, it's difficult to assess the competitiveness of this approach. 

A significant concern is the potential for increased training time. The proposed method ties network size and training duration together, potentially leading to substantially slower training than parametric approaches, even accounting for the hyperparameter search costs of the latter. The authors do not adequately address this scalability concern, particularly for larger networks. The experiments, while helpful, are not extensive enough to convincingly demonstrate practical viability. 

Overall, the paper presents an interesting and clearly written contribution. The long-term vision of adaptable, lifelong-learning networks is inspiring. However, the current results are preliminary. More comprehensive experiments, particularly comparisons to existing network compression techniques and a thorough investigation of scalability, are necessary to establish the practical value of this approach.

**Additional Feedback (for potential revision):**

*   **Comparison to Pruning:** A critical addition would be a comparison to state-of-the-art pruning methods. Train networks with fixed architectures, then prune them to similar sizes as those achieved by the nonparametric approach. Evaluate performance, training time, and resource usage.
*   **Scalability Analysis:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost, particularly as network size (and training data) increase.  Consider profiling the training process to identify bottlenecks.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Conduct a sensitivity analysis of the new hyperparameters (unit addition rate, weight decay rate).  How robust is the performance to variations in these parameters?
*   **Larger Datasets:**  Evaluate the method on more challenging, large-scale datasets beyond MNIST, rectangles, and convex.  The poker dataset experiment is a good start, but further validation is needed.
*   **Algorithm 1 clarity:** The notation in Algorithm 1 is dense. Consider clarifying variable definitions and breaking down complex operations into smaller steps.
*   **Justification of AdaRad:** Explain more intuitively *why* normalizing only the orthogonal component of the gradient is beneficial. A more detailed discussion of the intuition behind AdaRad would be helpful.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  How does the training time of your nonparametric approach scale with network size compared to a standard parametric network of equivalent performance? Can you provide empirical evidence or theoretical analysis?
2.  Have you experimented with techniques to dynamically adjust the unit addition rate or weight decay rate during training? If so, what were the results?
3.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of why you chose to penalize the fan-in rather than the fan-out, particularly concerning the benefits for dense weight matrices?
4.  What are the limitations of this approach regarding the types of activation functions that can be used? You mention the requirement of σ(0) = 0 – is this strictly necessary, or could it be relaxed with modifications to the algorithm?
5.  The appendix provides extensive mathematical proofs. Could you summarize the key insights of Theorem 1 in a more accessible way for readers less familiar with the mathematical details?
