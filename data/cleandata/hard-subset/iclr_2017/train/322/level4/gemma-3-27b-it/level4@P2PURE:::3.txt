This paper introduces a method for dynamically adjusting the number of units in a neural network during training. The approach is both simple and well-motivated: units with zero-valued input or output weights are added or removed, guided by a group sparsity norm to encourage weight decay. A key theoretical result demonstrates that, with appropriate regularization, the loss function is minimized by a network with a finite number of units. While this theoretical guarantee doesn’t preclude overfitting or underfitting, initial experiments suggest these issues are not prominent.

A potential benefit of learning network size is a reduction in hyperparameter tuning. However, the method doesn’t entirely eliminate this burden, as hyperparameters controlling unit addition and weight decay still need to be set, and it's unclear whether these are easier to optimize than those used in standard parametric approaches. The authors rightly acknowledge this, but the lack of apparent simplification is somewhat disappointing.

The authors highlight the ability to train networks with fewer units while maintaining comparable performance, which could be valuable for reducing computational cost, power consumption, and memory usage – particularly on resource-constrained devices. However, the work lacks comparison to existing network compression techniques like pruning, leaving it unclear whether this approach is competitive with state-of-the-art methods for reducing network size.

A further potential drawback is the coupling of hyperparameters governing network size and training time. This could lead to substantially slower training compared to parametric methods with fixed architectures. While hyperparameter search can also be time-consuming for standard networks, this approach’s slowness may be inherent. The authors do not address the scalability of this issue to larger networks, raising concerns about the practicality of this method for large-scale applications.

The experiments are helpful and provide initial encouragement, but are not exhaustive or fully conclusive. Further evaluation on larger, more complex problems is necessary to establish the practical utility and scalability of this approach.

Overall, this is an interesting and clearly presented paper with a potentially valuable contribution. The broader vision of adaptive, lifelong learning networks is compelling, and this work represents a step towards that goal. However, the current results remain preliminary and speculative.
