I concur with Reviewer 2 regarding the novelty of this work. The concept of dynamically adjusting model size through unit addition and removal is a promising direction, allowing the network to adapt to the problem and data rather than being constrained by pre-defined architectural choices.

The authors present a compelling theoretical result demonstrating that, under fan-in or fan-out regularization, the error function optimum is achieved with a finite number of parameters, preventing indefinite network growth and overfitting. This is reminiscent of regularization techniques like Lasso or Elastic Net which induce sparsity in weights. However, I believe the paper would benefit from a more detailed intuitive explanation of this theorem. While the result is interesting, and intuitively plausible to me, a dedicated discussion exploring the core insight behind it – potentially at the expense of some of the existing prior work discussion – would strengthen the paper. Please also see point 2 below for related suggestions. 

I have several further comments:

1. A compelling experiment would compare a model trained with dynamic unit adjustment to a model with a fixed, equivalent-sized architecture (same number of nodes post-training). This would directly demonstrate the efficacy of the proposed approach. Does the method effectively prune unnecessary nodes while adding those that contribute to performance, and does this translate into improved performance or efficiency (e.g., memory usage)? Figure 2 attempts to address this, but its presentation is unclear and requires careful inspection to interpret. The observed mixed performance – where non-parametric nets sometimes outperform parametric ones – still suggests a valuable method for structure discovery. However, the rationale for *why* the non-parametric approach occasionally surpasses a parametrically-trained network of the same size requires further clarification. What insights can be offered?

2. Theorem 1 requires a more thorough discussion. Beyond the mathematical proof in the Appendix, what is the central takeaway in more accessible terms? The current presentation feels somewhat axiomatic; the conclusion appears almost self-evident. Is there a deeper, more nuanced implication? Furthermore, this theoretical result deserves expanded coverage, possibly with additional experiments to support it. For example, could the optimal regularization parameter (lambda) be predicted from the data itself? My intuition is that lambda heavily influences the final network structure. Is this accurate? How sensitive is the final network structure to the initial weight initialization? Would different initializations yield significantly different architectures? What happens when combining fan-in and fan-out regularization – does the theorem still hold?

I also have a few specific questions:

1. The paper states that adding zero-valued units alters the regularizer value. Is this true for norms like the L2 norm, where adding zeros doesn’t change the value?
2. The definition of a “zero unit” specifies zero-valued weights for either fan-in *or* fan-out. However, to remove a unit without affecting the function f, both fan-in and fan-out weights should be zero. This point needs clarification.

I have adjusted my rating to 7, contingent on the authors addressing these concerns.
