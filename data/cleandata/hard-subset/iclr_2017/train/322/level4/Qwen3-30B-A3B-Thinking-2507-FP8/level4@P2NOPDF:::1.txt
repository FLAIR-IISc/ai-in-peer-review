

This paper introduces a nonparametric neural network architecture that dynamically learns its optimal size during training. The core approach involves introducing zero-valued units and employing sparse regularization to automatically prune irrelevant connections. While the concept resembles a random search over discrete model sizes facilitated by sparsity constraints, it addresses an important challenge in model efficiency. The authors present promising empirical results, though several technical concerns warrant clarification.  

**Key Strengths:**  
The automatic model-size adaptation mechanism is conceptually novel and aligns with growing interest in efficient neural architectures. The sparse regularization strategy effectively suppresses redundant units, yielding compact models on non-convex tasks. The experimental validation across multiple benchmarks demonstrates the method’s viability, particularly for scenarios where fixed-size networks struggle.  

**Critical Concerns:**  
1. **Computational Overhead:** The algorithm’s additional complexity requires explicit quantification. The decomposition of fan-in weights into parallel/orthogonal components and subsequent transformation into radial-angular coordinates likely incurs significant computational overhead. The authors should:  
   - Provide a detailed analysis of the extra operations (e.g., FLOPs) relative to standard parametric networks.  
   - Include runtime benchmarks comparing training/inference times against baseline architectures. Without this, the practicality of the approach remains unclear.  

2. **Performance on Convex Datasets:** The paper notes that the model produces *smaller* networks on convex datasets (e.g., MNIST), where it underperforms compared to parametric counterparts. This counterintuitive result needs deeper investigation:  
   - Why does the model size reduction correlate with performance degradation here? Is it due to insufficient capacity, or does the sparsity mechanism disrupt beneficial inductive biases?  
   - Could the authors discuss how the method’s behavior differs between convex (e.g., linearly separable) and non-convex tasks? A theoretical or empirical analysis of this discrepancy would strengthen the contribution.  

**Minor Suggestions:**  
- Clarify the "zero units" mechanism: Are these units pre-allocated and then pruned, or dynamically added during training? A brief schematic would aid understanding.  
- The claim that the method "automatically learns model size" should be nuanced—size is determined by sparsity thresholds, not purely learned.  

**Overall Assessment:**  
The work proposes a compelling direction for adaptive neural networks, with strong empirical validation on non-convex problems. However, the unresolved computational cost and the performance gap on convex datasets significantly impact its practical utility. Addressing these points would substantially elevate the paper’s contribution. If the authors can quantify the overhead and explain the convex-dataset anomaly, this becomes a valuable addition to the literature. As currently presented, the technical gaps prevent full endorsement.
