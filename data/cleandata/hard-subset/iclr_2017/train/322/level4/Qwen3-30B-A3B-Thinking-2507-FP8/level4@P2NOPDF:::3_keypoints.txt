- Introduces a simple dynamic-size method: add/remove units with zero input/output weights and use a group-sparsity norm to encourage weights to zero.
- Main theoretical result: with appropriate regularization, the loss is minimized by a network with a finite number of units.
- Theory does not guarantee avoidance of over- or under-fitting, though initial experiments suggest the method can avoid these issues in practice.
- Claim of reducing hyperparameter tuning is weakened: the method introduces new hyperparameters (e.g., add frequency, weight decay) that implicitly control network size.
- Unclear whether the new hyperparameters are easier or harder to tune than standard ones; authors avoid overstating reduced tuning effort.
- Potential practical benefit: comparable performance with fewer units could lower inference time, memory, and power for deployment.
- Lacks comparison to established model-size reduction techniques (e.g., pruning), so competitiveness with state-of-the-art compact models is unknown.
- Training-time concerns: the same hyperparameters affect network size and training speed, possibly making training slower than fixed-size networks.
- Scalability is not addressed; authors do not demonstrate practicality for large networks or real-world training efficiency.
- Experiments are encouraging but limited to small-scale tasks and do not establish robustness or scalability.
- Strong writing and sound technical ideas, but empirical validation is preliminary and insufficient to support broad practical claims.
- Verdict: promising, well-motivated contribution that warrants further work (scalability and pruning comparisons) but is not yet a compelling real-world alternative.
