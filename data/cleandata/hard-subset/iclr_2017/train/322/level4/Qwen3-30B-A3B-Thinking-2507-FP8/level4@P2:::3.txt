

This paper introduces a novel framework called "nonparametric neural networks" that dynamically adjusts network size during training by adding and removing units based on a regularization scheme. The authors prove theoretically that with proper regularization (specifically using a fan-in or fan-out regularizer with p=2), the optimization problem achieves a global minimum at finite network size. They develop a new training algorithm called AdaRad, which handles the dynamic unit addition and removal while maintaining training stability. The approach is theoretically grounded and shows promising results on MNIST and rectangles datasets, achieving comparable performance with fewer parameters than parametric networks.

I recommend rejection. The primary reasons are: (1) the paper lacks meaningful comparison to existing methods for reducing network size, and (2) the training time concerns aren't adequately addressed, potentially making the approach impractical for larger networks.

The paper claims to reduce the burden of hyperparameter tuning by automatically determining network size, but this appears misleading. The method introduces new hyperparameters (unit addition rate, decay rate, angular step size) that require careful tuning, potentially making hyperparameter selection as challenging as standard approaches. The authors don't provide evidence that these hyperparameters are easier to tune than conventional ones, and the paper's own experimental protocol shows extensive hyperparameter tuning was required.

Most critically, the paper doesn't compare its approach to existing methods for reducing network size, such as pruning trained networks (e.g., Molchanov et al. 2017, Guo et al. 2016). Without such comparisons, it's impossible to determine whether the proposed method offers meaningful advantages over current state-of-the-art techniques for creating compact networks. The results showing smaller networks with comparable performance could potentially be achieved through post-training pruning, which would be computationally more efficient.

The training time concerns are significant and inadequately addressed. The paper mentions that "the time it took those networks to converge to a stable network size was a much smaller fraction of the overall training time under poker compared to the smaller datasets," but this is a limited analysis. The authors don't provide concrete training time comparisons between their approach and standard parametric training on the same datasets, which is essential for evaluating practical utility. The experiments on the poker dataset (1.025 million datapoints) show promising results, but the training process appears to be substantially slower due to the incremental unit addition. For large-scale applications where training time is critical, this could be a major drawback.

The theoretical contribution is interesting but not sufficiently connected to practical implementation. While the proof of finite network size is mathematically sound, the paper doesn't adequately address how this theoretical guarantee translates to practical benefits in real-world scenarios. The experiments are limited to small datasets, and the authors don't demonstrate how the approach scales to more complex, real-world problems where network size optimization is most valuable.

Additional feedback: The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison to pruning methods for reducing network size. The authors should provide detailed training time comparisons between their approach and standard parametric training on the same hardware. The theoretical analysis would be strengthened by discussing how the fan-in regularizer with p=2 relates to existing sparsity-inducing methods. The paper could also better articulate the specific scenarios where this approach would be preferable to standard methods.

Clarifying questions:
1. How do the hyperparameters of your approach compare in difficulty to those required for standard network architecture search? Do you have evidence that these are easier or harder to tune?
2. Have you compared your method's network size reduction capabilities to existing pruning techniques for parametric networks? If not, can you provide an analysis of why your approach might outperform or underperform compared to these methods?
3. Given that your method requires incremental unit addition, how do you plan to address the potential training time overhead when scaling to much larger datasets and networks, such as those used in modern computer vision applications?

While the paper presents an interesting theoretical framework and shows promising initial results, the lack of meaningful comparison to existing network size reduction methods and the unaddressed training time concerns prevent me from recommending acceptance. The authors have made an interesting theoretical contribution, but the practical impact remains speculative without more comprehensive evaluation.
