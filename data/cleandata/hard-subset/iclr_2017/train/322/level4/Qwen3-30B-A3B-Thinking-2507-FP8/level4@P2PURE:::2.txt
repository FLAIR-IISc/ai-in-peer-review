

I concur with Reviewer 2 regarding the paper's compelling contribution: the concept of dynamically adding or removing units to allow a model to adapt its structure to the problem and data, rather than relying on user-specified architecture. The authors present a theoretically significant result demonstrating that under fan-in or fan-out regularization, the error function's optimum is achieved with a finite number of parameters, preventing unbounded growth and overfitting.  

This result resembles traditional sparsity-inducing methods like Lasso or Elastic Net, where regularization yields sparse weights. The paper would benefit from greater intuitive explanation of this theorem. While the result is logically sound and expected, dedicating more space to its interpretation—rather than extensive prior work discussion—would strengthen the presentation.  

Additional comments:  
1.  An experiment comparing the proposed method (with dynamic unit adjustment) against a fixed-size network of identical final capacity would substantiate the approach's efficiency. Specifically, it should demonstrate whether the method avoids unnecessary units and optimizes performance versus memory usage. Figure 2 presents mixed results, but the figure lacks clarity despite being interpretable with careful inspection. While non-parametric networks occasionally outperform parametric counterparts, the reason for this advantage—particularly when the final network size is known in advance—remains unexplained. Why does the non-parametric approach sometimes yield better results than a fixed-size network trained from scratch? This requires further clarification.  

2.  Theorem 1 warrants deeper discussion. Beyond the proof in the appendix, the core insight in plain language should be articulated: What does the theorem *actually* state, and why is it significant? The conclusion appears intuitive, but the key insight deserves explicit emphasis. This theoretical result merits more space, including supplementary experiments. For instance:  
    - Can the regularization parameter λ be predicted from data properties, or is it purely heuristic?  
    - How sensitive is the final network structure to λ?  
    - Does initialization affect the resulting architecture (e.g., do different random seeds yield substantially different networks)?  
    - What occurs when fan-in and fan-out regularizers are combined? Does the finite-parameter guarantee persist?  

Additional queries:  
1.  The claim that adding zero units alters the regularizer value requires justification. For example, does the L2 norm change upon adding units with zero weights?  
2.  Zero units are defined by having either fan-in or fan-out weights zero. However, to maintain the output function *f* upon removal, *both* fan-in and fan-out weights must be zero. This definition needs clarification.  

I have revised my rating to 7, with the hope that the authors will address these points.
