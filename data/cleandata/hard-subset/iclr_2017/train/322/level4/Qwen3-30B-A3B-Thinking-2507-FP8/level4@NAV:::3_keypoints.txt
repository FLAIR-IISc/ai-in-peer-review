- Proposes dynamic addition/removal of units by zeroing input/output weights and using a group sparsity norm for regularization.  
- Theoretical result: with appropriate regularization, the loss is minimized by a network with a finite number of units.  
- Theory does not guarantee avoidance of over- or under-fitting; experiments suggest this is not a major issue in practice.  
- Claimed benefit of reducing hyperparameter tuning is limited: method introduces new hyperparameters (unit-addition frequency, weight decay) whose tuning difficulty is unclear.  
- Authors correctly avoid overstating reduced tuning complexity, but it is disappointing that the advantage is not demonstrated.  
- Shows networks with fewer units can match parametric counterparts, benefiting inference runtime, power, and memory (useful for mobile).  
- Lacks experimental comparisons to established size-reduction methods (e.g., pruning), so competitiveness is uncertain.  
- Same hyperparameters control final unit count and training duration, potentially making training slower than fixed-size approaches.  
- Scalability to large networks is not addressed; prolonged training raises practicality concerns for large-scale applications.  
- Experiments are encouraging but limited; larger-scale validation is needed and current results remain speculative.  
- Paper is clearly written and presents an intriguing vision of networks that grow and adapt for lifelong learning.
