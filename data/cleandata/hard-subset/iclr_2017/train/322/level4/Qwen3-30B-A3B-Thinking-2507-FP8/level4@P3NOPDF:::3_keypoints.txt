- Proposes dynamic adjustment of network unit count during training via group sparsity that zeroes unit input/output weights for automatic addition/removal.

- Theoretical contribution: with appropriate regularization, loss minimization yields a network with a finite number of units.

- Practical impact is unclear despite theoretical elegance; real-world implications need careful validation.

- Promising potential to reduce inference time, memory, and power for mobile deployment by producing smaller networks.

- Fails to benchmark against state-of-the-art pruning techniques (structured/magnitude pruning), leaving comparative advantage unproven.

- Introduces additional hyperparameters (unit-addition frequency, weight-decay rates) without showing they are easier to tune than conventional ones.

- Hyperparameters jointly control final network size and training duration, creating a trade-off that may require iterative, slow tuning.

- No analysis or experiments demonstrating scalability to larger networks or datasets, raising concerns about real-world applicability.

- Theoretical guarantee of finite units doesn't preclude overfitting or underfitting; empirical evidence is limited and preliminary.

- Lacks ablation studies and hyperparameter-sensitivity analyses that would strengthen experimental claims.

- Vision for lifelong/adaptive networks is compelling but currently speculative due to unresolved practical challenges.

- Overall experimental depth is insufficient; authors should provide pruning comparisons and scalability analysis before broader adoption.
