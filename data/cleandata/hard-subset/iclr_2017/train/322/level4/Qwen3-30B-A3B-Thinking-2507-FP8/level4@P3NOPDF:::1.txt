

This paper introduces a nonparametric neural network architecture that dynamically learns its optimal size during training. The core innovation involves randomly introducing zero-valued units and employing sparse regularization to automatically prune irrelevant connections, thereby eliminating superfluous model components. The approach effectively explores discrete model size configurations through random initialization combined with sparsity constraints, addressing a significant challenge in neural network design. The empirical results demonstrate the method's efficacy on non-convex datasets, offering a promising direction for adaptive model scaling.

The paper's technical contribution is well-motivated and the experimental validation is generally sound. However, two critical aspects require deeper discussion to assess the method's practical viability and theoretical boundaries:

1. **Computational Overhead**: The algorithm's decomposition of fan-in weight vectors into parallel and orthogonal components, followed by radial-angular coordinate transformation, introduces non-trivial computational complexity. The authors should quantify this overhead relative to standard parametric networks. Specifically, they should:  
   - Provide a detailed analysis of additional floating-point operations per layer (e.g., "X% increase in FLOPs per forward pass").  
   - Include runtime benchmarks comparing the proposed method against baseline architectures on standard datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10, MNIST), reporting both training time per epoch and total wall-clock time.  
   Without this, it remains unclear whether the model-size adaptation capability justifies the computational cost, particularly for resource-constrained applications.

2. **Performance on Convex Datasets**: The paper notes that the nonparametric model produces smaller architectures on convex datasets (e.g., synthetic linearly separable tasks), where it underperforms parametric counterparts. This observation warrants deeper investigation:  
   - *Why does the sparsity mechanism favor minimal architectures on convex data?* Is this due to the inherent simplicity of the task (making large networks redundant), or does the random initialization/regularization strategy inherently bias toward small models?  
   - *How does this relate to the theoretical guarantees of the method?* If the convex case requires a specific model capacity, the automatic pruning mechanism may fail to adapt appropriately. The authors should discuss whether this limitation stems from the algorithmic design or the nature of the sparsity constraint.  
   Clarifying this would strengthen the paper’s claim about the method’s general applicability and help identify scenarios where it is most beneficial.

While the core idea is novel and the results on complex tasks are compelling, these points are essential for evaluating the work’s broader impact. The computational analysis is particularly crucial—without evidence that the method scales efficiently, its practical adoption may be limited despite promising theoretical insights. The convex dataset behavior also raises questions about the method’s robustness across task types, which should be addressed to position the work within the wider landscape of adaptive architectures.

Overall, the paper presents an interesting approach to model-size adaptation with clear potential. Addressing the above concerns would significantly enhance its contribution and utility for the community. The method’s ability to eliminate manual hyperparameter tuning for network depth is a valuable asset, but its real-world utility hinges on resolving the computational and task-specific limitations noted above.
