

# Review for "Nonparametric Neural Networks"

## Summary
This paper introduces a novel framework for automatically determining optimal neural network size during a single training cycle, termed "nonparametric neural networks." The authors propose a theoretically grounded approach that dynamically adds and removes units during training, guided by L2 fan-in or fan-out regularization. They prove that under this regularization scheme, the network size converges to a finite optimum (Theorem 1), and introduce a novel training algorithm, AdaRad, that effectively implements this framework. The experimental results demonstrate promising performance on multiple benchmark datasets, with the nonparametric approach achieving competitive accuracy compared to parametric networks of the same size and outperforming them on some tasks. The work presents a compelling theoretical foundation for dynamic network size adaptation, addressing a practical challenge in neural network model selection.

## Decision
Reject. The paper makes an interesting theoretical contribution, but the empirical results are insufficiently rigorous and the theoretical insights are not sufficiently developed to justify acceptance.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper's core contribution is Theorem 1, which establishes that nonparametric networks achieve a global minimum at finite dimensionality under L2 fan-in/fan-out regularization. While the proof appears mathematically sound, the paper fails to adequately explain the practical significance of this theoretical result. The theorem is presented without sufficient intuition or discussion of why this convergence behavior is meaningful in practice. The authors should have spent more time explaining how this result enables practical advantages in network size selection.

The empirical evaluation is the paper's most significant weakness. The results shown in Figure 2 are mixed: while nonparametric networks outperform parametric networks of the same size on MNIST and rectangles datasets, they underperform on the convex dataset. The paper does not sufficiently explain why this occurs or provide enough context to understand when the method is likely to succeed. The paper also fails to demonstrate a clear advantage over parametric networks of the same final size (i.e., networks trained from scratch with the same number of units), which would be the most compelling evidence for the method's value.

The paper's approach to network size determination is conceptually interesting, but the experimental validation is inadequate to support the claim that this method "obtains promising results" as stated in the abstract. The authors do not provide sufficient evidence that this approach consistently outperforms standard practices for network size selection.

## Additional Feedback
To strengthen this work, I recommend:

1. Expanding the theoretical discussion: The paper should spend more time explaining the practical implications of Theorem 1. Why is it significant that the network size converges to a finite optimum? How does this differ from other approaches to network size selection?

2. Improving empirical evaluation: The paper should include a direct comparison between nonparametric networks and parametric networks of the same final size (trained from scratch). This would provide the most compelling evidence for the method's value. The authors should also provide more detailed analysis of why the method works well on some datasets but not others.

3. Clarifying the unit removal mechanism: The paper states that adding zero units changes the value of the regularizer, but this is not immediately clear. The relationship between zero units and the regularization term should be explicitly explained.

4. Adding more experiments: The authors should test the method on more datasets and provide more detailed analysis of the sensitivity to hyperparameters like λ.

## Questions for Authors
1. The paper states that adding zero units changes the value of the regularizer. Could you clarify why the L2 norm would change when adding zero weights, since the L2 norm of zero weights is zero? 

2. The paper defines zero units as having either fan-in or fan-out weights as zero. However, to maintain the same output function, a unit must have both fan-in and fan-out weights as zero. Could you clarify this definition?

3. The paper claims that nonparametric networks achieve higher accuracy than parametric networks of the same size. However, the paper does not compare to parametric networks of the same final size (trained from scratch). Could you provide this comparison, as it would be the most compelling evidence for your approach?

4. The paper shows that the method works well on MNIST and rectangles datasets but not on the convex dataset. What specific properties of the convex dataset cause the method to fail, and how might the approach be modified to handle such cases?

5. How sensitive is the final network structure to the initialization of weights and the choice of λ? Could you provide analysis of this sensitivity?
