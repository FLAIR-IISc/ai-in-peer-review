- Paper proposes "nonparametric neural networks" that grow/shrink units during a single training run, with f-equivalence maintenance and a proof that regularization yields finite size.  
- Introduces CapNorm (normalization layer), AdaRad (optimization algorithm), and reports competitive results with fewer parameters on some benchmarks.  
- Decision: Reject.  
- Main reason 1: Insufficient empirical evidence and missing comparisons to state-of-the-art pruning methods (e.g., Molchanov et al., Guo et al.).  
- Main reason 2: Method appears to substantially increase training time and the paper does not justify computational cost or scalability to large problems.  
- Theoretical result (Theorem 1) is sound and well-motivated, providing a solid foundation.  
- Experimental evaluation is limited: good results on MNIST/rectangles but poor on the convex dataset, raising robustness concerns.  
- Lack of direct comparisons with modern pruning techniques makes it unclear if the approach offers real advantages for compact models.  
- Poker dataset experiment shows feasibility on larger data but fails to analyze how computational cost scales with network size or dataset complexity.  
- Paper should include thorough comparisons with pre- and post-training pruning methods and runtime comparisons to standard training plus hyperparameter search.  
- Authors need a detailed analysis of computational trade-offs and scaling behavior (training time vs. dataset/network size).  
- Clarify how regularization hyperparameters (λ, αr, αφ) interact and concretely control network size instead of describing them as “implicit” controllers.  
- Key questions for authors: how does cost scale vs. standard methods, which dataset characteristics favor this approach, how does accuracy compare to pruning, and how do poker results compare to OpenML/SOTA?
