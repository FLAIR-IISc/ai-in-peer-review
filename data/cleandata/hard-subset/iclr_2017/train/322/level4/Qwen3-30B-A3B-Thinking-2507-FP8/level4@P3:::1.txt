

# Review for "NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS"

## Summary
This paper introduces a compelling framework for automatically determining optimal neural network size during a single training cycle, addressing a significant challenge in deep learning model selection. The authors propose "nonparametric neural networks" that dynamically adjust network architecture by continuously adding new units while eliminating redundant ones through a novel optimization algorithm (AdaRad) and a specific regularization strategy. The theoretical foundation is well-developed, with a proof demonstrating that the framework achieves a global training error minimum under certain conditions. The experimental results show promising performance on several benchmark datasets, with nonparametric networks achieving competitive accuracy while being substantially smaller than their parametric counterparts.

## Decision
I recommend a **weak accept**. The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to the problem of automatic network size selection, but the empirical evaluation lacks sufficient depth and the computational complexity discussion is inadequate.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper successfully addresses a meaningful problem in deep learning: the inefficiency of current model selection approaches that require expensive global searches over network architectures. The theoretical contribution is the strongest aspect of the paper, with Theorem 1 providing a solid foundation for the approach. The authors prove that nonparametric networks achieve a global minimum under specific regularization conditions, which is a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of neural network architecture selection.

The empirical results are promising for two of the three datasets tested (MNIST and rectangles images), showing that nonparametric networks can achieve performance competitive with exhaustive random search while using substantially fewer parameters. This demonstrates the practical value of the framework. However, the poor performance on the convex dataset is concerning and not adequately explained, raising questions about the generalizability of the approach.

The most significant limitation is the lack of discussion regarding computational complexity. The AdaRad algorithm introduces substantial computational overhead through its radial-angular decomposition and coordinate transformations. The paper fails to quantify this overhead or provide any timing experiments comparing AdaRad to standard optimization methods like SGD or Adam. Without this information, it's difficult to assess the practical viability of the approach.

## Additional Feedback
To strengthen the paper, I recommend the following improvements:

1. **Computational complexity analysis**: Provide a detailed analysis of the computational overhead of AdaRad relative to standard optimization methods. Include timing experiments comparing training times for parametric and nonparametric networks on the same hardware.

2. **Convex dataset analysis**: Conduct a more thorough analysis of why the approach performs poorly on the convex dataset. This could include examining the final network structures, error distributions, or comparing with other pruning methods.

3. **Broader empirical evaluation**: Extend the experiments to include more challenging datasets and architectures to better establish the generalizability of the approach.

4. **Clarify relationship to existing work**: More clearly position the work relative to recent advances in network pruning and growth, particularly the connection to the "f-equivalence" concept and how this differs from prior approaches.

## Questions for Authors
1. Could you provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of AdaRad compared to standard optimization methods (e.g., SGD, Adam)? Specifically, what is the additional computational cost per iteration, and how does this scale with network size?

2. The paper shows that nonparametric networks perform poorly on the convex dataset, while achieving strong results on MNIST and rectangles images. What specific characteristics of the convex dataset might explain this discrepancy, and how might the framework be modified to address this limitation?

3. The paper mentions that the authors found no significant performance difference between parametric networks trained with RMSprop and Adam. Given the importance of optimization methods in deep learning, could you elaborate on why these methods performed similarly in this context?
