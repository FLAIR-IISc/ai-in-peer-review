

1. The paper introduces nonparametric neural networks, a novel framework for automatically determining and adapting network size during a single training cycle without requiring prior information or extensive hyperparameter search. The authors propose a method that continuously adds new units while eliminating redundant ones via an L2 penalty, supported by a rigorous theoretical proof demonstrating that their framework achieves a global training error minimum at finite dimensionality under fan-in or fan-out regularization. They introduce a novel optimization algorithm called AdaRad and provide experimental validation across multiple benchmark datasets, showing promising results where nonparametric networks sometimes outperform parametric networks of the same size and achieve results competitive with exhaustive random search.

2. The paper should be accepted with minor revisions. The theoretical contribution is significant and well-justified, and the experimental results are promising and well-conducted.

3. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of neural network architecture search by addressing a key limitation of current black-box optimization methods. The proof of Theorem 1 is rigorous and addresses a fundamental challenge in automatic network size selection, ensuring networks do not grow indefinitely. The experimental results on MNIST and rectangles images datasets demonstrate clear advantages of the proposed approach, with nonparametric networks achieving comparable or better performance with substantially fewer parameters. While the results on the convex dataset are less promising, this is a common challenge in machine learning and does not undermine the overall contribution.

4. The paper would benefit from additional discussion of the practical implications of Theorem 1. While the proof is provided in the appendix, the main text could better convey the intuitive meaning of this result for practitioners. Specifically, the paper could explore how the regularization parameter λ relates to dataset characteristics, as this appears to be a key factor in determining the final network structure. The discussion of why nonparametric networks sometimes outperform parametric networks of the same final size (even when the final size is known in advance) could be expanded to provide more insight into the mechanism of this advantage.

5. 1. Could you elaborate on why the nonparametric training approach sometimes achieves better performance than parametric networks of the same final size, even when the final size is known in advance? What specific mechanism allows the nonparametric approach to achieve this advantage?

2. How sensitive is the final network structure to the choice of λ? Is there a way to predict an appropriate λ value based on dataset characteristics, or is it primarily determined through hyperparameter search?

3. To what extent does the final network structure depend on initialization? Would different random seeds produce significantly different network architectures?

4. What happens when combining fan-in and fan-out regularizers? Does the theoretical guarantee of Theorem 1 still hold?

5. Regarding zero units: The paper defines them as having either fan-in or fan-out weights being zero. However, for the output f to remain unchanged when removing such a unit, wouldn't both fan-in and fan-out need to be zero? Could you clarify this point in the paper?
