

This paper introduces nonparametric neural networks, a novel framework for automatically determining optimal network size during a single training process. The authors address the significant challenge of model selection in neural networks, which typically requires expensive global searches across many network architectures. Their key contribution is a theoretical framework that proves the existence of a global minimum for network size under specific regularization conditions, along with a practical training algorithm (AdaRad) that dynamically adds and removes units during training. The approach uses a fan-in regularizer with p=2 to induce sparsity, allowing redundant units to be eliminated while new units are added. The paper demonstrates promising results on multiple benchmarks, showing that nonparametric networks can achieve competitive or superior performance compared to parametric networks of the same size, while using significantly fewer parameters.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The core contribution is theoretically sound and addresses an important problem in neural network training. The authors provide a rigorous proof of the framework's theoretical soundness and demonstrate practical effectiveness on multiple datasets, with the most compelling results on MNIST and rectangles images. The approach represents a meaningful advance in the field of neural architecture search.

The main supporting argument for acceptance is the paper's strong theoretical foundation combined with practical results. The proof of Theorem 1 (which establishes the existence of a global minimum for network size) is thorough and well-motivated. The experimental results are well-designed, with careful comparisons to parametric networks and exhaustive random search. The MNIST results are particularly convincing, showing that nonparametric networks can achieve comparable accuracy to the best parametric networks while using substantially fewer parameters (144 vs. 694 units). The authors also demonstrate scalability to larger datasets like poker, where their approach outperforms published results.

I have two primary concerns that would benefit from clarification in the revision:

First, the computational complexity of the AdaRad algorithm requires more detailed discussion. The algorithm's key operations—decomposing fan-in weights into parallel and orthogonal components, transforming to radial-angular coordinates, and maintaining running averages—appear to add significant computational overhead compared to standard training algorithms. The authors should provide a detailed analysis of the additional computational complexity relative to standard approaches like SGD or Adam. A concrete comparison of training time on standard datasets would be particularly helpful to assess the practical viability of their approach.

Second, the poor performance on the convex dataset warrants further investigation. The paper notes that nonparametric networks performed substantially worse than parametric networks on this dataset, with the best nonparametric network having a larger error and more variable performance. It would be valuable to understand why this particular dataset presents such a challenge for the proposed approach. Is it related to the specific characteristics of the convex dataset, or does it indicate a fundamental limitation of the framework that should be addressed?

Additional feedback to improve the paper:
1. The paper's description of the theoretical framework could be made more accessible by providing a more intuitive explanation of why the fan-in regularizer with p=2 leads to the desired behavior. A simple example would help readers grasp the core concept more quickly.
2. The connection between the theoretical framework and the practical implementation (AdaRad) could be strengthened. The paper states that the algorithm implements the theoretical framework, but a more explicit explanation of how the algorithm's operations correspond to the theoretical requirements would be valuable.
3. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the hyperparameter sensitivity, particularly regarding λ and the unit addition rate. The current description of hyperparameter selection through grid search is somewhat opaque.

Questions to clarify for the authors:
1. Could you provide more specific details on the computational complexity analysis of AdaRad compared to standard training algorithms? Specifically, what is the asymptotic complexity increase for each training iteration, and what empirical speedup factors can be observed on standard benchmarks?
2. Can you provide any insights into why the convex dataset presents such a challenge for the nonparametric approach, and whether there are any modifications to the framework that might improve performance on this type of problem?
3. The paper states that the framework works with self-similar nonlinearities like ReLU, but does not discuss whether the approach would work with other popular nonlinearities like Leaky ReLU or Swish? What would be the implications for the framework's theoretical guarantees?
