- Proposes a simple method to dynamically adjust network size during training by adding/removing units with zero-weight inputs/outputs and using group sparsity to shrink unit weights.  
- Theoretical result: with appropriate regularization, the loss is minimized by a network with a finite number of units.  
- The theoretical guarantee does not ensure avoidance of overfitting or underfitting in practice.  
- Initial experiments indicate the method may avoid over-/underfitting, but evidence is limited.  
- Main motivation is to reduce hyperparameter tuning by not predefining network size.  
- In practice, tuning burden is shifted to hyperparameters controlling unit addition frequency and weight decay, which still determine final network size.  
- It is unclear whether these new hyperparameters are easier to tune than standard size selection.  
- Authors correctly avoid overclaiming reduced tuning complexity, but this limitation is disappointing.  
- Claimed benefit: trains smaller networks with comparable performance, potentially reducing inference time, power, and memory for mobile use.  
- Paper omits comparisons to established compression/pruning methods, so competitive performance vs. state-of-the-art is unknown.  
- Practical concern: same hyperparameters affect both network size and training speed, potentially making training much slower than fixed-size approaches.  
- No analysis of scalability to larger networks, raising doubts about viability for large-scale applications.  
- Experimental results are encouraging but not comprehensive; larger benchmark testing is needed to validate practicality.  
- Overall: clearly written and conceptually interesting with promise for adaptive lifelong learning, but current hyperparameter sensitivity, training efficiency, and benchmarking gaps prevent strong endorsement.
