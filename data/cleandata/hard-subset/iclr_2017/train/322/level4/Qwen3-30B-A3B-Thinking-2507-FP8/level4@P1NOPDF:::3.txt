

This paper proposes a simple yet elegant approach for dynamically adjusting network size during training by adding or removing units with zero-weight inputs or outputs, while using group sparsity regularization to encourage unit weights to shrink to zero. The main theoretical contribution demonstrates that with appropriate regularization, the loss is minimized by a network with a finite number of units. While this result does not guarantee against over- or underfitting, initial experiments suggest the approach avoids these issues in practice.

A key motivation is reducing hyperparameter tuning burden—specifically, eliminating the need to predefine network size. However, the approach merely shifts this burden: hyperparameters controlling unit addition frequency and weight decay rates still implicitly determine final network size. It remains unclear whether tuning these is easier or harder than standard parametric methods. While the authors correctly avoid claiming reduced tuning complexity, this limitation is somewhat disappointing.

The authors highlight that their method trains smaller networks achieving performance comparable to fixed-size counterparts, which could reduce inference time, power consumption, and memory footprint—critical for mobile applications. Yet, they omit comparisons to established network compression techniques (e.g., pruning trained networks), making it impossible to assess whether this approach competes with state-of-the-art size-reduction methods.

A significant practical concern is that the same hyperparameters govern both network size and training speed. Consequently, training may become significantly slower than standard parametric approaches with fixed hyperparameters. While grid search for hyperparameters in standard methods can be time-consuming, experience often allows for efficient hyperparameter selection without exhaustive searches. In contrast, the proposed method’s inherent slowness may persist regardless of hyperparameter expertise. The authors provide no analysis of scalability for larger networks, raising serious doubts about its viability for large-scale applications where training speed is critical.

The experimental results are encouraging but lack comprehensiveness. More extensive testing on larger benchmarks is needed to validate practicality and broader utility. Without such evidence, the current results remain largely speculative.

Overall, this is a clearly written and conceptually interesting paper that advances a promising direction: networks capable of adaptive growth for lifelong learning. The core idea holds potential for future work, but the current implementation’s practical limitations—particularly regarding hyperparameter sensitivity, training efficiency, and lack of competitive benchmarks—prevent strong endorsement. The vision is compelling, but the execution requires further validation before it can be considered a practical solution.
