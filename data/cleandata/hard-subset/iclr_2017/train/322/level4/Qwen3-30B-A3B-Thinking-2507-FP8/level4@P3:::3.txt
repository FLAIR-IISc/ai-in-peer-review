

# Review for "Nonparametric Neural Networks"

**Summary of Contributions:** The paper introduces a novel framework for automatically determining the optimal size of neural networks during a single training cycle, termed "nonparametric neural networks." The authors propose a theoretically grounded approach that dynamically adds and removes units while maintaining network functionality through f-equivalence, with a theoretical proof that proper regularization ensures finite network size. The work includes a novel normalization layer (CapNorm), a new optimization algorithm (AdaRad), and promising experimental results showing competitive performance with fewer parameters compared to parametric networks on several benchmark datasets.

**Decision:** Reject.

**Key Reasons for Decision:** 
1. The paper lacks sufficient empirical evidence to demonstrate the practical advantages of the proposed approach over existing methods, particularly in terms of scalability and comparison to state-of-the-art network pruning techniques.
2. The paper does not adequately address the computational cost implications of the proposed method, which appears to significantly increase training time without clear benefits for larger-scale problems.

**Supporting Arguments:**
The theoretical contribution (Theorem 1) is well-motivated and appears sound, providing a strong foundation for the approach. However, the experimental evaluation has significant limitations. While the results on MNIST and rectangles datasets are promising, the poor performance on the convex dataset raises concerns about the robustness of the approach. More critically, the paper fails to compare with state-of-the-art network pruning methods that reduce the size of parametrically trained networks (e.g., Molchanov et al., 2017; Guo et al., 2016). Without such comparisons, it's impossible to determine if the proposed method offers any real advantages over existing techniques for producing compact networks.

The scalability concerns are particularly troubling. The authors acknowledge that training time is increased due to the gradual network growth, but they do not provide sufficient evidence that this overhead is acceptable for practical applications. The poker dataset experiment (Section 4.3) shows that the method can be applied to larger datasets, but the paper doesn't address how the computational cost scales with network size or dataset complexity. For large-scale applications, the training time penalty may outweigh the benefits of reduced model size.

**Additional Feedback:**
1. The paper would benefit from a thorough comparison with existing network pruning methods, including both pre-training pruning (e.g., Molchanov et al.) and post-training pruning techniques. This would clarify whether the proposed approach offers any meaningful advantages over current state-of-the-art methods for producing compact networks.

2. The authors should provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost trade-offs. A clear discussion of how training time scales with network size, including a comparison to standard training approaches with hyperparameter search, would strengthen the paper significantly.

3. The theoretical framework is compelling, but the paper would benefit from a clearer explanation of how the regularization parameters (λ, αr, αφ) interact to control network size. The current explanation of these hyperparameters as "implicit" controllers of network size is insufficient.

**Questions for Authors:**
1. How does the computational cost of your approach scale with dataset size and network depth compared to standard training methods with hyperparameter search? Can you provide specific runtime comparisons for larger datasets?

2. In your experiments, you mention that nonparametric networks outperform parametric networks of the same size on MNIST but underperform on the convex dataset. What are the key characteristics of datasets where your approach is most effective, and how would you recommend practitioners determine if their problem falls into this category?

3. You state that "the network ultimately chosen can achieve a higher accuracy than networks of the same size that are trained from scratch." How does this compare to the accuracy achieved by standard pruning methods applied to networks trained from scratch? Could you provide quantitative comparisons with recent pruning techniques?

4. The paper mentions that "the best nonparametric network made no mistake on the test set of 100,000 examples" for the poker dataset. How does this compare to the best results reported in the OpenML database for this dataset, and how does it compare to state-of-the-art methods specifically designed for this dataset?

Overall, while the paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution with promising initial results, the lack of comprehensive experimental evaluation and insufficient discussion of practical computational implications prevent it from being convincing for a conference submission. The authors have made a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of neural network size optimization, but the paper would need significant additional experimental validation and analysis to demonstrate practical utility.
