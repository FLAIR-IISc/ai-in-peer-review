- Addresses dynamic adjustment of network units during training by adding/removing units whose input/output weights are zero, using group sparsity to drive weights to zero.
- Elegant, well-motivated approach with the main theoretical result that appropriate regularization yields a loss-minimizing network with a finite number of units.
- Theory does not guarantee avoidance of overfitting or underfitting; initial experiments suggest it may not be a major issue but need further validation.
- Method could reduce hyperparameter tuning burden in principle, but does not truly solve it because new implicit hyperparameters control unit emergence.
- Key implicit hyperparameters include frequency of unit addition and rate of weight decay, and it is unclear if these are easier to tune than standard hyperparameters.
- Authors' claim of training with fewer units for comparable performance is promising for inference cost, power, and memory savings on devices.
- Paper lacks experimental comparison to established network-compression methods (e.g., pruning), so competitiveness is unclear.
- Same hyperparameters control both final network size and training duration, potentially causing substantially slower training than fixed-parametric approaches.
- Scalability to larger networks is not addressed; training latency and practicality for large-scale applications remain concerns.
- Experiments are encouraging but insufficiently comprehensive; more large-scale testing is needed to assess practicality and utility.
- Paper is clearly written and presents an inspiring vision of lifelong growth/adaptation, but current results are still speculative.
