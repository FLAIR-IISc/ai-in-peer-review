

This paper tackles the challenge of enabling networks to dynamically adjust their unit count during training through a straightforward yet elegant and well-justified methodology. It introduces the dynamic addition or removal of units with zero input or output weights, leveraging a group sparsity norm for regularization to drive unit weights toward zero. The primary theoretical contribution demonstrates that, with appropriate regularization, the loss is minimized by a network with a finite unit count. While this theoretical result does not ensure the trained network avoids over- or under-fitting, initial experiments suggest this is not a significant issue in practice.

A potential benefit of learning the optimal unit count is reducing the need for hyperparameter tuning. However, this approach does not truly resolve the hyperparameter challenge; the network still requires tuning parameters controlling unit addition frequency and weight decay rates. It remains unclear whether these new hyperparameters are easier or harder to tune than those in conventional methods. The authors appropriately avoid claiming reduced tuning complexity, though it is somewhat disappointing that this advantage is not realized.

The authors highlight that their method trains networks with fewer units achieving performance comparable to parametrically trained counterparts, which is significant for reducing inference runtime, power consumption, and memory usage—critical for mobile applications. However, the absence of experimental comparisons to established network size-reduction techniques (e.g., pruning) leaves uncertainty about the approach's competitiveness with state-of-the-art size optimization methods.

A further drawback is that the same hyperparameters govern both the final unit count and training duration. Consequently, training may inherently be slower than fixed-hyperparameter parametric approaches. While grid search for hyperparameters in standard methods can be costly, it is often avoidable through prior experience, unlike the proposed method’s potential inherent slowness. The authors neglect to address scalability for larger networks, raising concerns about practicality for real-world, large-scale applications due to prolonged training times.

Overall, the experiments are encouraging but lack comprehensiveness and definitive evidence. Larger-scale validation would be necessary to assess practicality and broad utility. Despite this, the paper is clearly written and presents an intriguing, potentially valuable contribution. The vision of networks that grow and adapt through lifelong learning is compelling and may be essential for realizing such systems. Nevertheless, the current results remain speculative.
