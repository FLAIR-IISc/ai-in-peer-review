

This paper introduces a novel framework for automatically determining the optimal size of neural networks during training, rather than through costly hyperparameter search. The authors propose "nonparametric neural networks" that dynamically grow and shrink network size while training, using a combination of sparse regularization and a novel optimization algorithm called AdaRad. The key insight is to continuously add new units while eliminating redundant ones via L2 regularization, with the network's dimensionality being optimized as part of the training process. The approach is theoretically grounded through a soundness proof showing that the framework converges to a global minimum under appropriate regularization conditions.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a significant contribution to the problem of model selection in neural networks, offering a practical solution that avoids the computational expense of black-box hyperparameter search. The theoretical foundation is strong, and the empirical results demonstrate clear advantages over standard parametric networks for two of the three benchmark datasets tested.

The paper effectively addresses a critical problem in neural network training: the high computational cost of finding optimal network architectures through traditional methods. The authors position their work well within the literature, acknowledging the limitations of existing approaches (such as black-box optimization and pruning techniques) and explaining how their framework overcomes these limitations. The theoretical soundness of the approach is well-established through Theorem 1, which proves that the nonparametric framework achieves a global training error minimum at some finite dimensionality when using appropriate regularization. The experimental evaluation provides compelling evidence that the method can achieve comparable or better performance than parametric networks while automatically determining optimal network size.

The main concerns I have relate to the computational efficiency of the proposed method and the inconsistent performance on the convex dataset. First, while the paper mentions that AdaRad incurs additional computational cost, it doesn't provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity compared to standard training methods. The reviewers would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the additional operations required for the radial-angular decomposition and how these scale with network size. A brief comparison of training times between the nonparametric and parametric approaches would strengthen the paper.

Second, the poor performance on the convex dataset compared to parametric networks is concerning. The authors note that nonparametric networks tend to converge to smaller network sizes, which appears to be detrimental for this particular dataset. It would be valuable to understand why the convex dataset behaves differently from the others and whether there are specific characteristics of the dataset that make it particularly challenging for the proposed framework. Understanding this could help improve the method's robustness.

Additional feedback to help improve the paper:
1. The paper would benefit from a clearer explanation of why L2 regularization is preferred over L1 for this framework, particularly given the recent interest in sparse networks. The discussion of L1 vs. L2 in Section 3.3 is somewhat brief and could be expanded.

2. The connection between the self-similarity property of nonlinearities and the effectiveness of the framework could be made more explicit. While the paper states that self-similarity eliminates the need to tune regularization per layer, it would be helpful to see a concrete example demonstrating this benefit.

3. The experimental section could include more detailed analysis of the convergence behavior during the training process, particularly regarding how the network size evolves over time.

Questions for the authors:
1. Can you provide a more detailed computational complexity analysis of AdaRad compared to standard optimization algorithms (SGD, Adam, etc.)? Specifically, how does the time per iteration scale with network size, and what is the impact on total training time?

2. Could you elaborate on why nonparametric networks perform particularly poorly on the convex dataset compared to the other datasets? Is this due to specific properties of the dataset, or are there architectural considerations that could be adjusted to improve performance?

3. The paper mentions that AdaRad-M produces similar results to AdaRad. Could you clarify why the authors chose to omit AdaRad-M results from the main paper, and whether there are specific scenarios where the momentum variant might provide advantages?
