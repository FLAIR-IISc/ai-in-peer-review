

I agree with Reviewer 2 on the paper’s intriguing contribution. The core idea—dynamically adding or removing units to let the model adapt to problem-specific data demands, rather than relying on user-specified architecture—is compelling and promising.  

The authors present a theoretically sound result: under fan-in or fan-out regularization, the optimal error solution requires a finite number of parameters, preventing unbounded growth and eventual perfect overfitting. While this resembles traditional sparse regularization (e.g., Lasso or Elastic Net), the paper would benefit from deeper intuition about *why* this theorem holds. It’s a strong result, and while the conclusion feels intuitive to some, explicitly unpacking the key insight in plain language would strengthen the paper. For instance, the discussion of prior work could be condensed to make space for a more thorough exploration of this theorem’s implications.  

My main concerns are:  

1. **Experimental validation of efficiency**: The paper claims dynamic architecture offers advantages, but it lacks a critical comparison: *Can a model trained with automatic unit adjustment outperform a fixed-size network with the same final size (trained from scratch)?* Figure 2 shows mixed results, but the figure is unclear and requires careful inspection to interpret. When dynamic networks outperform fixed-size ones, *why* does this happen? If the final network size is known in advance, why would a dynamically grown network generalize better than one trained from scratch with that exact size? Clarifying this would demonstrate whether the method genuinely saves computational resources or merely discovers structure.  

2. **Theoretical depth for Theorem 1**: The theorem is stated without sufficient discussion. Beyond the proof in the appendix, what is the *key insight*? In plain English: What does it *mean* for the model’s behavior? To me, the conclusion seems almost obvious—regularization should cap the network size—but the paper doesn’t articulate why this is non-trivial or *how* it enables the method’s practical utility. This deserves more space. Additionally:  
   - Can the regularization strength (λ) be predicted from data properties? If λ is the primary driver of the final structure, identifying data-driven heuristics for λ would be valuable.  
   - How sensitive is the final architecture to initialization? Do different random starts yield significantly different networks?  
   - What happens when fan-in and fan-out regularizers are combined? Does the finite-parameter guarantee still hold?  

**Additional clarifications needed**:  
- **Zero units**: The definition states a unit is "zero" if *either* fan-in or fan-out weights are zero. But if only one is zero, removing the unit would alter the network’s output function. The paper should clarify that *both* fan-in and fan-out weights must be zero for the unit to be safely removed without affecting output.  
- **Regularizer behavior**: The claim that "adding zero units changes the regularizer value" requires justification. For example, does adding units with zero weights alter the L2 norm? This needs explicit explanation.  

Overall, the paper’s core idea is strong and novel, but the theoretical contribution and experimental validation require deeper treatment. The current presentation risks understating the method’s significance. Addressing these points—especially providing clearer intuition for Theorem 1, strengthening the comparative experiments, and clarifying the zero-unit definition—would significantly elevate the work.  

I’ve updated my rating to 7, pending thorough revisions addressing these concerns.
