

This paper introduces a novel framework for automatically determining the optimal network size during a single training cycle through a nonparametric approach. The authors propose continuously adding new units while eliminating redundant ones using an L2 penalty, with a novel optimization algorithm called Adaptive Radial-Angular Gradient Descent (AdaRad). The key theoretical contribution proves that with proper regularization (fan-in or fan-out Lp regularizer with 1 ≤ p < ∞), the network will converge to a finite size that minimizes training error. The framework is tested on MNIST, rectangles images, and convex datasets, demonstrating that nonparametric networks can achieve comparable or better accuracy than parametric networks of the same size while using fewer parameters.

I recommend rejection. The paper's main limitation is the lack of comprehensive experimental comparison to established methods for network size reduction, particularly pruning techniques. While the authors claim their approach avoids the need for a high-performing starting model (a requirement for most pruning methods), they fail to demonstrate that their method outperforms state-of-the-art pruning approaches in terms of final model size or accuracy. The experiments only compare against parametric networks of fixed size and random search, not against methods specifically designed to reduce network size.

Additionally, the paper does not adequately address the computational cost of the proposed approach. While the authors acknowledge that training nonparametric networks initially requires more time as units are added, they fail to provide sufficient evidence that this overhead is justified by the resulting model's performance. The experiments on the larger poker dataset show that the nonparametric approach can be applied to larger problems, but the training time comparison to standard parametric approaches is insufficient. Without clear evidence that the method's computational cost is acceptable or that the resulting models consistently outperform alternatives, the practical value of the approach remains unclear.

The theoretical contribution is sound but limited in scope. Theorem 1 proves that the loss function attains a global minimum at finite size with the specified regularizers, but this does not guarantee that the minimum will correspond to a model that generalizes well to unseen data. The experiments on the convex dataset show that the approach can underperform compared to parametric networks, raising questions about the reliability of the method across different problem types.

The paper's claim about "automatically finding a good network size during a single training cycle" is somewhat misleading. While the framework does adjust network size during training, it still requires tuning several hyperparameters that control the size of the final network (including αr, αφ, λ, ν, and νfreq), which are as complex as the hyperparameters in standard model selection approaches. The authors do not convincingly demonstrate that these hyperparameters are easier to tune than traditional network size selection methods.

To strengthen the paper, I recommend:
1. Adding comprehensive comparisons against state-of-the-art pruning methods (e.g., Molchanov et al. 2017, Figurnov et al. 2016) to demonstrate whether the proposed approach produces smaller or more accurate models.
2. Providing detailed training time comparisons between the nonparametric approach and standard parametric training with fixed network sizes, particularly for larger datasets.
3. Including analysis of how the final model's performance varies across different hyperparameter settings, to better understand the method's robustness and ease of use.
4. Clarifying the relationship between the hyperparameters (particularly αr and λ) and the final network size, to better explain how the method controls network complexity.

I have several questions to help clarify the authors' approach:
1. How do the authors justify their choice of p=2 for the regularizer over p=1, especially since p=1 would produce sparser weight matrices that might better align with hardware optimizations for sparse networks?
2. What is the expected scaling behavior of training time for nonparametric networks as the dataset size increases, and how does this compare to standard parametric training?
3. Can the authors provide more insight into why the method performs poorly on the convex dataset compared to parametric networks, and whether these issues could be addressed with modifications to the framework?
4. How do the authors envision the proposed framework being integrated into practical machine learning pipelines, and what specific advantages does it offer over existing approaches to network size selection?
