

This paper proposes a novel approach for networks to dynamically adjust their unit count during training, using group sparsity regularization to drive unit weights toward zero, enabling automatic addition/removal of units with vanishing input/output weights. The core theoretical contribution establishes that with appropriate regularization, the loss is minimized by a network with a finite number of units. While this result is elegant and well-motivated, its practical implications require careful consideration.

The authors rightly emphasize that the method yields networks with fewer units achieving performance comparable to fixed-size networks, which could reduce inference time, memory footprint, and power consumption—critical for mobile deployment. However, the paper fails to benchmark against state-of-the-art network pruning techniques (e.g., structured pruning, magnitude-based pruning) that also reduce model size post-training. Without such comparisons, it remains unclear whether the proposed method offers a meaningful advantage over established size-reduction approaches, undermining the claimed practical significance.

A key limitation lies in the hyperparameter burden. While the authors acknowledge the method does not eliminate hyperparameter tuning, they do not address whether the new hyperparameters (controlling unit addition frequency and weight decay rates) are easier to tune than conventional ones. Crucially, these same hyperparameters simultaneously govern both the final network size *and* training duration. This creates a fundamental tension: to achieve a desired network size, one must iteratively adjust these parameters, potentially leading to prohibitively slow training. In contrast, standard parametric training often leverages experience to select hyperparameters without exhaustive grid search, making the proposed method’s inherent slowness a significant practical drawback. The paper offers no analysis of scalability to larger networks or datasets, raising serious concerns about applicability to real-world, large-scale problems.

The theoretical guarantee of finite units does not inherently prevent over- or under-fitting, as the authors note. Initial experiments suggest the method avoids these pitfalls, but the lack of comprehensive empirical validation—particularly on larger datasets and architectures—limits confidence in its robustness. For instance, the absence of ablation studies on hyperparameter sensitivity or comparisons to pruning baselines weakens the experimental narrative.

The vision of lifelong learning through adaptive networks is compelling and aligns with broader goals in neural architecture design. However, the current work remains largely speculative due to the unresolved practical challenges. The paper does not demonstrate that the proposed method *solves* the hyperparameter tuning problem it ostensibly addresses; instead, it introduces a new layer of complexity without clear benefits over existing techniques. The claim that the approach "eases the burden of tuning" is unsupported and potentially misleading.

In summary, the paper presents a theoretically sound and conceptually interesting mechanism for dynamic network growth. Its potential value in reducing model size is promising but unproven against existing methods. The unresolved issues around hyperparameter sensitivity, training efficiency, and scalability to large-scale tasks prevent the results from being considered robust or practically compelling. While the writing is clear and the core idea is novel, the experimental section lacks the depth required to justify broader adoption. The authors should address these gaps—particularly through rigorous comparisons to pruning methods and scalability analysis—before the approach can be deemed viable for real-world applications. As it stands, the work offers an intriguing direction but falls short of demonstrating tangible advantages over current practices.
