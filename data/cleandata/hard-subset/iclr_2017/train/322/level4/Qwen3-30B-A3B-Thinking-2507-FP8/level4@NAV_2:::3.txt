

This paper introduces nonparametric neural networks, a framework that dynamically adjusts network size during training by continuously adding new units while eliminating redundant ones through an l2 penalty. The authors prove that with proper regularization, the loss is minimized by a network with finite dimensions, and they develop a novel training algorithm, AdaRad, to implement this approach. The work is theoretically grounded and demonstrates promising results on standard benchmark datasets, showing that nonparametric networks can achieve comparable or better performance than parametric networks while using significantly fewer parameters.

Reject. The paper's theoretical contribution is sound, but the empirical results are insufficiently comprehensive to support the claims of practical utility, and the approach does not demonstrate clear advantages over established network pruning techniques.

The theoretical proof of Theorem 1 is rigorous and well-motivated, establishing that nonparametric networks achieve a global minimum at finite dimensionality with proper regularization. However, the empirical evaluation is limited to three relatively small datasets (MNIST, rectangles images, convex) with minimal comparison to state-of-the-art network pruning methods. The paper claims that nonparametric networks "achieve higher accuracy than networks of the same size that are trained from scratch and, in some cases, achieve a higher accuracy than is possible by black-box methods," but fails to provide a meaningful comparison to existing network compression techniques that also aim to reduce model size while maintaining performance. The scalability experiments on the poker dataset are promising but insufficient to address concerns about training time, which the authors acknowledge may be significantly longer than parametric approaches.

The paper could be strengthened by:
1. Including a thorough comparison to existing network pruning techniques (e.g., those by Molchanov et al., 2017; Guo et al., 2016) that reduce the size of parametrically trained networks.
2. Providing more detailed analysis of the computational cost and training time compared to standard parametric approaches, particularly for larger datasets.
3. Exploring the relationship between the hyperparameters (λ, αr, αφ) and the resulting network size to better understand how the approach might be tuned in practice.

To clarify my assessment, I would appreciate the authors addressing:
1. How the proposed approach compares in terms of final model size and accuracy to recent network pruning methods that operate on fully trained networks (not just during training).
2. What evidence exists that the training time penalty is acceptable for practical applications, especially as network sizes increase beyond those tested in the paper.
3. Whether the observed benefits on MNIST and rectangles images generalize to more challenging vision tasks, particularly those where network pruning is commonly applied.
