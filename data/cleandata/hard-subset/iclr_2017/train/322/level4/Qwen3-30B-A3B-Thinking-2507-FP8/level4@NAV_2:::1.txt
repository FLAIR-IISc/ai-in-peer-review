

1. This paper presents a novel framework for automatically determining the optimal size of neural networks during a single training cycle, eliminating the need for expensive global search over multiple network architectures. The authors introduce nonparametric neural networks, a non-probabilistic approach that optimizes over all possible network sizes while proving theoretical soundness under an ℓ2 penalty. The framework dynamically adds and removes units during training, using a novel normalization layer (CapNorm) and optimization algorithm (AdaRad) that achieves promising results on multiple benchmark datasets, with performance competitive to or better than exhaustive search methods.

2. I recommend rejection. The primary reason is that the paper's theoretical contribution does not adequately justify the proposed framework, and the experimental results show significant limitations on a key benchmark dataset.

3. The paper claims to prove that nonparametric networks achieve a global minimum at finite dimensionality (Theorem 1), but the proof contains critical gaps and relies on restrictive assumptions that may not hold for practical neural networks. The authors assume all nonlinearities are differentiable, which excludes common activation functions like ReLU (despite claiming to use ReLU in experiments). The proof also assumes the error function meets specific technical conditions that may not be satisfied in real-world scenarios. Furthermore, the experimental results demonstrate that nonparametric networks substantially underperform parametric networks on the convex dataset, with no clear explanation for this failure. This limitation undermines the paper's core claim that the framework can automatically select good network sizes across diverse tasks.

4. To improve the paper, I recommend:
- Strengthen the theoretical justification by addressing the gaps in Theorem 1 and clarifying the practical applicability of the assumptions.
- Provide a detailed analysis of why the framework fails on the convex dataset, including exploration of architectural differences between successful and unsuccessful cases.
- Include a comprehensive comparison of computational complexity, specifically addressing the extra operations required for AdaRad compared to standard optimization algorithms.
- Discuss the impact of different initialization strategies for new units and how they affect convergence.
- Clarify the relationship between the proposed approach and existing network pruning methods, particularly in terms of whether the framework requires a pre-trained model as a starting point.

I would appreciate clarification on the following points:
- How do the authors reconcile the theoretical proof's requirement for differentiable nonlinearities with their use of ReLU activation functions in experiments?
- What specific characteristics of the convex dataset cause the nonparametric approach to fail, and how might the framework be modified to address this limitation?
- Can the authors provide empirical evidence of the computational overhead of AdaRad compared to standard optimization algorithms like Adam or RMSprop?
