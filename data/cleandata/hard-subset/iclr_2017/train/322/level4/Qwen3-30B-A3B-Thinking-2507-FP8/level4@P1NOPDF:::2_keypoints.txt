- Core idea is compelling: dynamically adding/removing units lets models adapt architecture to data rather than relying on user-specified size.  
- Theorem: under fan-in or fan-out regularization, optimal solution uses a finite number of parameters, preventing unbounded growth/overfitting.  
- Request clearer intuition: explicitly unpack why the finite-parameter result holds in plain language and its practical implications.  
- Condense related work to free space for deeper explanation of the theorem’s insight.  
- Experimental validation is insufficient: compare dynamically adjusted models to fixed-size networks trained from scratch with the same final size.  
- Clarify when and why dynamic networks outperform fixed-size ones, and whether dynamics actually save compute or just discover structure.  
- Figure 2 is unclear and requires clearer presentation and interpretation.  
- Theorem 1 needs more discussion beyond the proof: what it means for model behavior and why it’s non-trivial.  
- Address whether regularization strength (λ) can be predicted from data or guided by data-driven heuristics.  
- Report sensitivity to initialization: do different random seeds produce significantly different final architectures?  
- Explain combining fan-in and fan-out regularizers: does the finite-parameter guarantee still hold?  
- Clarify "zero unit" definition: removal should require both fan-in and fan-out weights to be zero, otherwise the output changes.  
- Justify the claim that adding zero units changes the regularizer value and specify effects on norms (e.g., L2).
