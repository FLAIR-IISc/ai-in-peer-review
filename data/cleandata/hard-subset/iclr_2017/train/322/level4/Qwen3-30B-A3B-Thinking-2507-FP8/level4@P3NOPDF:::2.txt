

Review  

This paper presents a compelling approach to dynamic neural network architecture design, where units are added or removed during training to adapt to problem complexity. I agree with Reviewer 2 that the core idea—allowing the model to grow/shrink based on data demands rather than user-specified structure—is highly interesting and potentially impactful. The theoretical contribution (Theorem 1) is particularly noteworthy: it rigorously establishes that fan-in/fan-out regularization ensures the error function converges to a finite-parameter solution, preventing unbounded growth. This parallels sparse regularization techniques (e.g., Lasso), though the paper could better articulate how this work extends or differs from them.  

**Major Strengths**  
The theoretical result is novel and well-motivated. The insight that regularization inherently limits network size—avoiding the need for manual architecture tuning—addresses a key practical challenge in deep learning. The paper’s focus on *why* this occurs (via the optimization landscape) is a significant contribution, though it requires deeper exposition.  

**Key Concerns**  
1. **Insufficient Theorem Discussion**: The paper states Theorem 1’s conclusion ("finite parameters at optimum") but fails to elaborate on its *implications*. Why is this non-obvious? The intuition—regularization penalizes unnecessary parameters, forcing sparsity—should be developed in the main text (not buried in the appendix). For instance:  
   - *Why does fan-in/fan-out regularization specifically induce finite growth, unlike standard L2?*  
   - *How does the theorem’s guarantee interact with practical training dynamics?*  
   The current treatment feels like a proof without context. A concise "plain English" summary of the theorem’s insight (e.g., "The regularizer acts as a structural prior, ensuring only *useful* units persist") would greatly strengthen the paper.  

2. **Ambiguous Experimental Claims**: Figure 2 compares non-parametric (dynamic) and parametric (fixed-size) networks, but the results are unclear. The caption states "non-parametric models outperform fixed-size counterparts in some cases," yet the plot lacks error bars, statistical significance, or clear metrics. Crucially, the paper does not address *why* dynamic networks sometimes exceed fixed-size models *even when the final size is known*. If the optimal size is predetermined, why would iterative growth yield better performance than direct training of that size? This undermines the core argument for the method’s efficiency. I recommend:  
   - A clearer visualization of Figure 2 (e.g., with error bars and explicit metrics).  
   - An ablation showing whether dynamic networks *save parameters* (e.g., final size vs. fixed-size baseline) while matching or exceeding accuracy.  
   - A discussion of *when* dynamic growth fails (e.g., for tasks where structure is trivially known).  

3. **Critical Definitions & Mechanics**:  
   - **Zero units**: The paper defines them as units with *either* fan-in or fan-out weights zero. This is ambiguous—removing a unit with only fan-in weights zero would alter the network’s output. Clarify that *both* fan-in and fan-out weights must be zero to maintain the output function.  
   - **Regularizer behavior**: The claim that "adding zero units changes the regularizer value" requires justification. For L2 regularization, adding zero weights does not alter the norm. The paper must specify how the fan-in/fan-out regularizer *differs* from standard L2 in this context.  

**Additional Questions for Authors**  
- *Lambda selection*: Theorem 1 hinges on λ (regularization strength). Is λ predictable from data properties (e.g., noise level, feature correlation), or must it be tuned empirically? This is critical for practical adoption.  
- *Initialization sensitivity*: How much does the final network structure depend on random initialization? Do different seeds yield structurally similar networks?  
- *Combined regularizers*: What happens if fan-in and fan-out regularizers are used together? Does the finite-parameter guarantee still hold?  

**Recommendation**  
This paper tackles a meaningful problem with a theoretically sound approach. However, the current draft lacks sufficient discussion of the theorem’s significance, and Figure 2’s conclusions are not rigorously supported. Addressing the points above—particularly clarifying the theorem’s insight, resolving Figure 2’s ambiguity, and defining key terms—would substantially strengthen the contribution. I recommend **acceptance with major revisions** (rating: 7). The core idea is promising, but the paper must better justify *why* this method is valuable beyond the theoretical guarantee.  

*Note*: The authors should reduce discussion of prior work (e.g., on sparsity) to make space for deeper analysis of their theorem and experiments. The current balance prioritizes context over insight.
