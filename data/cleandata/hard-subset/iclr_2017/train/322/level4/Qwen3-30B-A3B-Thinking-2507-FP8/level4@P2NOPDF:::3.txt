

This paper introduces a method for dynamically adjusting the number of units in a neural network during training. The approach is elegantly simple: units with zero input or output weights are added or removed, while a group sparsity norm encourages weights to decay to zero. The key theoretical contribution proves that with appropriate regularization, the loss is minimized by a network with a finite number of units. While this does not guarantee against over- or under-fitting, initial experiments suggest the method avoids these issues in practice.

A stated advantage of learning network size is reducing hyperparameter tuning burden. However, the method does not truly resolve this issue: it introduces new hyperparameters (e.g., frequency of unit addition, weight decay rate) that implicitly control network size. It remains unclear whether these are easier or harder to tune than standard hyperparameters, and the authors rightly avoid claiming reduced tuning effort. This limitation is disappointing, as the paper's core promise hinges on simplifying design.

The authors highlight that their method achieves comparable performance with fewer units than parametric networks, potentially reducing inference time, memory, and power—critical for mobile deployment. However, the paper lacks comparison to established network size reduction techniques like pruning, making it impossible to assess whether this approach is competitive with state-of-the-art methods for compact model generation.

A significant practical concern is that the same hyperparameters control both network size and training speed. Consequently, training may be inherently slower than fixed-size parametric networks, unlike grid search (which is optional for standard methods but often avoided through experience). The authors do not address scalability to large networks, raising doubts about practicality for real-world applications where training efficiency is paramount.

The experimental section is encouraging but insufficient. Results on small-scale tasks demonstrate feasibility but fail to establish robustness or scalability. Comprehensive evaluation on larger benchmarks (e.g., ImageNet) or direct comparison with pruning techniques is essential before claiming practical utility. Without these, the method's advantages remain speculative.

Overall, the paper presents a clear, well-motivated approach with a promising theoretical foundation. The vision of adaptive, lifelong learning networks is compelling and potentially foundational for future work. However, the current results are preliminary and lack the empirical rigor needed to demonstrate practical advantages over existing techniques. While the method is technically sound and the writing is strong, the experimental validation falls short of supporting broader claims. The approach warrants further investigation, particularly regarding scalability and comparison to pruning, but does not yet offer a compelling alternative to standard network design for real-world deployment. The core contribution is interesting but remains speculative without stronger evidence of efficacy and efficiency gains.
