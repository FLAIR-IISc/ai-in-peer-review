

This paper addresses the problem of dynamically adjusting the number of units in a neural network during training. The approach is simple yet elegant and well-motivated: units with zero input or output weights are added or removed during training, while group sparsity regularization encourages unit weights to decay to zero. The main theoretical contribution demonstrates that, with appropriate regularization, the loss is minimized by a network with a finite number of units. However, this theoretical result does not guarantee that the resulting network will avoid overfitting or underfitting the training data; initial experiments suggest this is not a significant issue, though further validation is needed.

A potential advantage of learning the number of units is reducing the burden of hyperparameter tuning. However, a key limitation is that this approach does not truly resolve the hyperparameter tuning problem. The network still requires multiple hyperparameters implicitly controlling the emergence of units, including those governing the frequency of unit addition and the rate of weight decay. It remains unclear whether these hyperparameters are easier or harder to tune than those in standard parametric approaches. While the authors do not claim to simplify training, it is disappointing that this benefit does not materialize.

The authors highlight that their method trains networks using fewer units to achieve performance comparable to parametrically trained networks. This is potentially significant for reducing inference time, power consumption, and memory footprint—critical for mobile devices. However, the paper lacks experimental comparison to established methods for reducing network size (e.g., pruning trained networks), leaving it unclear whether this approach is competitive with state-of-the-art techniques for network compression.

Another drawback is that the same hyperparameters control both the final network size and training duration. Consequently, training may be substantially slower than with parametric approaches using fixed hyperparameters. While grid search for hyperparameters in standard methods can be slow, experience often enables efficient hyperparameter selection without exhaustive search. In contrast, the proposed method’s slowness may be inherent, and the authors do not address scalability to larger networks. This raises practical concerns for large-scale applications, where training latency could become prohibitive.

Overall, the experiments are encouraging but insufficiently comprehensive. More rigorous testing on larger-scale problems is necessary to assess the approach’s practicality and broader utility. 

This is an interesting, clearly written paper that makes a potentially useful contribution. The vision of building networks capable of lifelong growth and adaptation is inspiring and may be essential for realizing such systems. However, the current results remain relatively speculative.
