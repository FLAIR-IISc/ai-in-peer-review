

This paper proposes a method for dynamically adjusting the number of units in a neural network during training. The approach is simple yet elegant: units with zero input or output weights are added or removed throughout training, while a group sparsity norm encourages unit weights to decay to zero. The main theoretical contribution demonstrates that, with appropriate regularization, the loss is minimized by a network with a finite number of units. While this result does not guarantee against over- or under-fitting, initial experiments suggest the approach avoids these issues.

A potential advantage of learning the network size is reducing hyperparameter tuning burden. However, this approach does not truly solve the problem: the network still requires hyperparameters implicitly controlling unit emergence (e.g., frequency of unit addition and rate of weight decay to zero). It remains unclear whether tuning these is easier or harder than standard hyperparameters. While the authors do not claim to simplify training, this limitation is somewhat disappointing.

The authors correctly emphasize that their method achieves comparable performance to parametrically trained networks using fewer units, which is significant for reducing runtime, power consumption, and memory footprint—particularly on mobile devices. However, they do not compare against existing network size reduction techniques (e.g., pruning trained networks), making it unclear whether this approach is competitive with state-of-the-art methods for model compression.

Another disadvantage is that the same hyperparameters control both the final network size and training time. Consequently, training may be significantly slower than parametric approaches with fixed hyperparameters. Although grid search for hyperparameters in standard methods can be slow, it is often avoided through experience. In contrast, the inherent slowness of the proposed method may be unavoidable. The authors do not address how this issue scales to larger networks, raising concerns about practicality for large-scale applications.

Overall, the experiments are encouraging but insufficiently comprehensive. More extensive testing on larger-scale problems is needed before concluding the approach is practical or widely applicable.

The paper is well-written and presents an interesting, potentially useful contribution. The vision of networks that grow and adapt through lifelong learning is inspiring and may be essential for realizing such systems. However, the current results remain speculative.
