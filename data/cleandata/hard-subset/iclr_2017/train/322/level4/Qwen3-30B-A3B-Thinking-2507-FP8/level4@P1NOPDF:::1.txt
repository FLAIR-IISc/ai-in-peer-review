

This paper introduces a nonparametric neural network architecture that dynamically determines model size during training. The core approach stochastically introduces zero-weight units and employs sparse regularization to prune redundant connections. While the problem is significant and the results are promising, the method appears to resemble a random search over discrete model structures facilitated by sparsity constraints. My primary concerns are as follows:

First, the computational overhead of the proposed algorithm requires clarification. The decomposition of each fan-in weight into parallel and orthogonal components, followed by transformation into radial-angular coordinates, likely incurs substantial additional computational cost. The authors should quantify this overhead relative to standard parametric networks—specifically, by detailing the extra operations per layer or per training iteration. Including empirical runtime comparisons would significantly strengthen the evaluation.

Second, the paper observes that the nonparametric model yields substantially smaller architectures on convex datasets, where it underperforms compared to parametric counterparts. This counterintuitive result warrants deeper analysis. What explains the model's tendency to produce overly compact structures on these datasets? Could this be due to the sparsity mechanism misinterpreting signal as noise, or are there inherent limitations in how the random pruning interacts with the data's geometric properties? A thorough investigation of this phenomenon would clarify the method's applicability boundaries and potential improvements.
