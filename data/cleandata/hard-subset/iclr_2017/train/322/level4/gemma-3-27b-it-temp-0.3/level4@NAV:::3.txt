This paper introduces a method for dynamically adjusting network size during training, a compelling problem in neural network research. The approach is both conceptually simple and well-justified: units are added or removed based on zero-valued input or output weights, guided by a group sparsity regularization term. A key theoretical result demonstrates that, with appropriate regularization, the loss function converges to a minimum achieved by a network with a finite number of units. While this doesn’t preclude overfitting or underfitting – and initial experiments suggest this isn’t a major issue – it provides a valuable theoretical foundation.

A potential benefit of learning network size is a reduction in hyperparameter tuning. However, the paper acknowledges, and this review concurs, that this doesn’t fully alleviate the issue. The method introduces new hyperparameters governing unit addition and weight decay, and it’s unclear if tuning these will be simpler than tuning hyperparameters in standard, fixed-size networks. While the authors rightly avoid claiming simplified training, the lack of demonstrable improvement in this area is somewhat disappointing.

The authors rightly highlight the potential for training smaller networks that achieve comparable performance, which is significant for reducing inference time, power consumption, and memory usage – particularly for mobile applications. However, a direct experimental comparison to existing network compression techniques (e.g., pruning) is missing, leaving the competitive advantage of this approach uncertain.

A further consideration is the coupling of hyperparameters controlling network size and training time. This could lead to substantially slower training compared to parametric approaches, even accounting for the hyperparameter search often required in those methods. While grid search isn’t always necessary due to prior experience, the potential for inherent slowness in this approach is a concern, especially as network scale increases. The paper doesn’t address this scalability issue, raising doubts about the practicality of the method for large networks.

The presented experiments are valuable and encouraging, but lack the breadth needed to be fully persuasive. More extensive evaluation on larger, more complex problems is required to establish the practical viability and broader applicability of this work. 

Overall, this is a well-written and interesting paper that presents a potentially valuable contribution. The long-term vision of adaptable, life-long learning networks is inspiring, and this work represents a step in that direction. However, the current results are preliminary and require further investigation.
