I concur with Reviewer 2 regarding the novelty of this work. The concept of dynamically adjusting model capacity by adding or removing units is a compelling direction, allowing the network to adapt to the problem’s complexity rather than being constrained by pre-defined architectural choices.

The authors present a significant theoretical result demonstrating that, under fan-in or fan-out regularization, the error function’s optimum is achievable with a finite number of parameters, preventing indefinite growth and potential overfitting. This result is reminiscent of regularization techniques like Lasso or Elastic Net, which induce sparsity in weights. While the theorem is a valuable contribution, I believe the paper would benefit from a more intuitive explanation of its implications. Currently, the discussion of prior work feels comparatively more extensive than the exploration of this central theoretical finding. Allocating more space to dissecting and contextualizing the theorem – perhaps by streamlining the related work section – would strengthen the paper. Please also see point 2 below for further suggestions regarding the theorem.

I have several further comments:

1. A particularly insightful experiment would involve comparing the performance of a model that dynamically adds/removes nodes to a network with a fixed number of nodes (equal to the final size of the dynamic model after training). This would directly assess the efficiency gains of the proposed approach. The core appeal lies in the potential to eliminate unnecessary nodes and prioritize those that contribute most to performance – effectively optimizing the trade-off between accuracy and model size. While Figure 2 attempts to demonstrate this, its presentation is unclear and requires careful scrutiny to interpret. The observed mixed results – with non-parametric nets sometimes outperforming and sometimes underperforming parametric ones – still highlight the method’s utility in discovering appropriate network structure. However, the reason for the occasional superiority of the non-parametric approach over a parametrically-trained network of the same size remains unclear. Further insight into this phenomenon would be appreciated.

2. The discussion surrounding Theorem 1 feels insufficient. Beyond the mathematical proof in the Appendix, what is the core takeaway? The conclusion appears almost intuitive, and I question whether there is a deeper, more powerful insight being conveyed. This theoretical result warrants more detailed exploration and supporting experimentation. For example, could the optimal regularization parameter (lambda) be predicted based on data characteristics? My intuition suggests that lambda is a critical determinant of the final network structure. Is this accurate? Furthermore, how sensitive is the final network structure to the initial weight initialization? Do different initializations lead to substantially different architectures? What happens when fan-in and fan-out regularization are used in combination – does the theoretical result still hold?

I also have a few specific questions:

1. The claim that adding zero-valued units alters the regularizer value is unclear. For instance, does the L2 norm change when zero values are added?
2. The definition of a “zero unit” – having either fan-in or fan-out weights equal to zero – is potentially misleading. To remove a unit without affecting the output function *f*, both fan-in and fan-out weights must be zero. This point requires clarification.

I have adjusted my rating to 7, with the expectation that the authors will address these comments.
