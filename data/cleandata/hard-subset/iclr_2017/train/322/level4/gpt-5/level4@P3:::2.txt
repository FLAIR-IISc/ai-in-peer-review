Summary and contributions
The paper proposes nonparametric neural networks, a framework that adapts a network’s width during a single training run by adding units and pruning them via group regularization. The main theoretical contribution (Theorem 1) shows that, under a fan-in or fan-out group Lp penalty (1 ≤ p < ∞), the training objective attains a global minimum at a finite network size; thus, optimization over all widths is well-posed and does not diverge to infinite width. On the algorithmic side, the paper introduces (i) CapNorm, a variant of batch normalization that preserves the effect of weight shrinkage by capping variance normalization, and (ii) AdaRad, an optimizer that decomposes gradients into radial (length) and angular (direction) components to normalize only the angular part, thereby enabling fair learning of newly added units while preserving the regularizer’s effect. Empirically, the paper demonstrates that networks grown in this nonparametric fashion can match or exceed the accuracy of parametric networks selected via grid/random search on MNIST, rectangles, and a large OpenML “poker” dataset, while transparently discovering compact architectures; performance is mixed on the convex dataset.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- The paper addresses an important problem—choosing network size—by integrating model selection into training with a principled objective and a clear theoretical guarantee of finite optimum.
- The proposed training machinery (CapNorm, AdaRad) is technically interesting and practically motivated, with evidence that nonparametric training can be competitive with or better than standard parametric baselines of the same final size.

Supporting arguments
- Problem significance: Automatically determining network size typically requires expensive black-box searches. This work frames width selection as part of a single objective, enabling continuous growth and pruning with standard stochastic optimization.
- Motivation and placement: The paper clearly positions itself relative to pruning, hyperparameter optimization, and prior network-growing methods, and explains why conventional batch normalization and standard adaptive optimizers are ill-suited in the presence of group shrinkage.
- Claims and evidence: Theorem 1 supplies a strong existence result ensuring that the objective over all widths has a finite minimizer when using fan-in or fan-out regularization. The proof conditions are reasonable for common activations with σ(0)=0. Empirically, the method (a) converges to finite sizes, (b) can outperform parametric networks of the same size on some datasets, and (c) scales to a million-example dataset. While results are mixed on the convex dataset, the evidence overall supports the core claims that width can be selected during training and that the resulting models are competitive.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (suggestions, not all part of the accept decision)
- Intuition for Theorem 1: Provide a plain-English explanation. For example: with a group Lp penalty over fan-ins/fan-outs, each added unit incurs a penalty proportional to the norm of its incoming/outgoing weights; because activations satisfy σ(0)=0, entire units can be set exactly to zero, so any unconstrained growth that does not materially reduce training loss is dominated by the regularization term. This is closely analogous to group Lasso: the penalty is coercive in width, forcing a finite number of nonzero groups (units) at a minimizer. A brief proof sketch highlighting the key inequalities would help readers.
- Why can the nonparametric model outperform a parametric model of the same final size? Offer hypotheses and ablations: (i) growth acts as a curriculum, avoiding poor local minima by starting small; (ii) the path-dependent interaction of shrinkage and CapNorm provides beneficial implicit regularization; (iii) AdaRad’s angular normalization may facilitate better feature shaping early on. An experiment that retrains the discovered final architecture from scratch (same optimizer, CapNorm, and matched schedules) would help isolate trajectory vs architecture effects.
- Clarify Figure 2: Make axes, sizes, and units per epoch explicit; add error bars across seeds; clearly distinguish “same-size parametric retrain” versus “best parametric via search.”
- Sensitivity to λ and its role in structure: Since λ is crucial for controlling growth, please include curves showing final width and accuracy versus λ on each dataset. If possible, discuss heuristics for λ selection (e.g., relate to dataset size/label noise) or demonstrate simple annealing schedules. Proposition 1 hints that layerwise λs collapse into a geometric mean under self-similar activations; leveraging this to reduce tuning burden is appealing—please emphasize it in the main text.
- Initialization and variability: Report variability of the discovered architectures across seeds (histograms of widths and accuracy). How much do final widths differ across seeds for a fixed λ? Do different runs converge to functionally similar models?
- Combining fan-in and fan-out penalties: Do the existence results extend when both are used together (e.g., sum of fan-in and fan-out group penalties)? Even if the current proof does not directly transfer, commenting on whether the objective remains coercive in width would be helpful.
- CapNorm ablations: Show that the uncapped BN indeed negates the regularizer (e.g., same training but with standard BN and decoupled weight decay), and that CapNorm resolves this while preserving optimization stability.
- AdaRad comparisons: Include comparisons against Adam/RMSprop with decoupled weight decay and per-group normalization baselines, as well as wall-clock/iteration overhead analyses.

Questions for the authors
- Theorem 1 (plain English): Beyond existence of a finite minimizer, what is the key insight readers should take away? Can you indicate how the bound on the number of nonzero units scales with λ, p, and simple properties of the data?
- λ selection: Do you see systematic relationships between λ and final width or accuracy that could guide default choices? Could simple schedules (e.g., start larger then anneal) improve both stability and final performance?
- Retraining from scratch: When you fix the final discovered architecture and retrain it from the same initialization distribution, optimizer, and CapNorm, how close do you get to the nonparametric model’s accuracy?
- Initialization dependence: How much do the final architectures vary across seeds for a given λ, and does that variability correlate with test performance?
- Convex dataset: Can you diagnose why the method underperforms there (e.g., requires larger width than the growth policy provides, different λ regime, or optimizer interaction)?
- Fan-in vs fan-out: Empirically, do you observe differences in the learned structures or stability when using fan-out instead of fan-in?
- Clarification on zero units and the regularizer: Adding a unit with zero fan-out but nonzero fan-in leaves f unchanged yet increases the group penalty (hence the objective), which restrains gratuitous growth—this matches your design choice. Conversely, adding a unit with both fan-in and fan-out zero would leave the penalty unchanged but offers no chance to learn. Please confirm this is the intended rationale. Also, to remove a unit without changing f, it suffices that either its fan-in or its fan-out is the zero vector (since σ(0)=0), correct?

Overall, this is a well-motivated, principled contribution that advances automated architecture sizing with a clear theoretical foundation and practical training tools. I recommend acceptance.
