1. Summary of contributions
The paper introduces nonparametric neural networks, a framework that learns the width of each layer during a single training run. The core idea is to dynamically add units initialized as f-equivalent zero units and remove redundant units via group sparsity (ℓ2) penalties on fan-ins (or fan-outs). The authors provide a theoretical guarantee (Theorem 1) that with fan-in or fan-out regularization (λ > 0, 1 ≤ p < ∞), the global training objective attains a minimum at a finite network size. They further argue for self-similar nonlinearities (e.g., ReLU) and prove that per-layer regularization strengths can be consolidated into a single λ up to weight rescaling (Proposition 1). To train these models, they propose two practical innovations: (i) CapNorm, a capped batch-normalization variant compatible with ℓ2 fan-in/fan-out penalties, and (ii) AdaRad, an optimizer that decomposes fan-in gradients into radial (parallel) and angular (orthogonal) components, normalizing only the angular component to allow fair learning progress for newly added units while keeping shrinkage behavior intact. Experiments on MNIST, rectangles-images, convex, and a large OpenML “poker” dataset show that nonparametric networks can converge to compact architectures and often match or surpass parametric baselines of the same size, sometimes approaching the best performance found by extensive black-box searches.

2. Decision: Weak Accept
Key reasons:
- Well-motivated formulation with a clean theoretical result ensuring finite optimal size under structured regularization, coupled with a practical training recipe (AdaRad + CapNorm).
- Empirical evidence that the approach can discover compact, competitive models without expensive outer-loop architecture search, including on a large dataset.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem significance: Automatic selection of network size typically requires black-box hyperparameter optimization. The proposed approach integrates size selection into a single training run, addressing both efficiency and performance.
- Theory: Theorem 1 is nontrivial and provides a strong existence guarantee for a finite minimizer under fan-in/out penalties, mitigating concerns that optimization could “prefer” unboundedly wide models. The self-similarity discussion and Proposition 1 help reduce hyperparameter complexity.
- Algorithms: AdaRad is thoughtfully designed to preserve the intended effect of group shrinkage and to stabilize learning for newly added units via angular normalization. CapNorm is a minimal modification of batch normalization that avoids neutralizing the effect of the regularizer.
- Empirics: On MNIST and rectangles-images, nonparametric models outperform parametric networks of the same size and sometimes approach the global best found by extensive searches. The poker results demonstrate scalability and that good performance can be achieved with compact models learned on the fly. The convex dataset reveals a limitation (models shrink too aggressively), which is honestly reported and provides a direction for refinement.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (not all part of the accept decision)
- Report computational overhead: Although Table 1 (cost comparison vs SGD/RMSprop) is helpful, readers will benefit from wall-clock measurements. Please provide per-epoch time, memory footprint, and total time-to-target-accuracy for (i) parametric + RMSprop/Adam, (ii) parametric + SGD/momentum, and (iii) nonparametric + AdaRad, across datasets.
- Stronger baselines: Include a fixed, over-parameterized network with the same ℓ2 fan-in group penalty (no unit addition) trained with standard optimizers, to separate the benefit of dynamic unit addition from the effect of structured shrinkage alone. Also compare against representative pruning/growing methods that do not require a high-performing seed (if available).
- Ablations: Quantify the contributions of CapNorm and AdaRad independently. For instance, show nonparametric training with standard BN (and explain the failure mode), CapNorm with Adam/RMSprop, and AdaRad without CapNorm.
- Sensitivity and schedules: Provide sensitivity analyses for λ, ν (unit addition rate), νfreq, αr, and αφ. In particular, visualize the trade-off between growth/shrinkage rates and final accuracy/size.
- Convex dataset: Offer a deeper analysis of the underperformance (e.g., is the validation curve consistent with premature elimination? does increasing αr or decreasing λ prevent over-shrinking? would a λ-annealing schedule or AdaRad-M help?). A small experiment demonstrating recovery with an adjusted schedule would be valuable.
- Clarity/implementation details: The AdaRad pseudocode is dense. A higher-level schematic, plus notes on numerical stability, parallelization, and how the angular normalization is implemented efficiently (e.g., vectorized operations per fan-in) would help reproducibility.
- Generalization vs optimization: The observation that nonparametric models do not drive training cross-entropy to near zero yet generalize well is interesting. Consider reporting calibration metrics or margin analyses to support the regularization effect of the framework.

5. Questions for the authors
- Computational cost: Can you quantify the per-iteration and total training time overhead of AdaRad relative to SGD/RMSprop/Adam on your datasets? Please include wall-clock times, FLOPs estimates if possible, and memory usage for the additional caches. How does overhead scale with number of units vs number of weights?
- Parameter schedules: How sensitive is performance to ν and νfreq? Did you explore adaptive unit addition based on validation gradients or learning progress rather than fixed schedules?
- Initialization: You initialize new units with random fan-in and zero fan-out. Did you test alternatives (e.g., small nonzero fan-out, data-dependent initialization, or borrowing from existing units)? How does this choice affect the probability that new units survive and contribute?
- CapNorm design: Why cap the standard deviation at 1 specifically? Did you try other caps or learnable caps? How does CapNorm compare empirically to BN without learned scale/shift but with careful λ tuning?
- AdaRad vs momentum: AdaRad-M did not improve results. Can you provide intuition (or experiments) on when momentum in the angular component helps or hurts? Is momentum potentially destabilizing when units are frequently added/removed?
- Convex dataset: Do you attribute the small learned architectures mainly to λ being too strong, to the data/task characteristics, or to the optimizer’s angular normalization? Would a λ-annealing or a minimal-width floor prevent premature shrinking without harming generalization?
- Theoretical scope: Theorem 1 addresses existence of a finite minimizer for the regularized training objective. Do you see a path to statements about validation risk or generalization bounds when the architecture is learned this way?
- Extensions: How would you adapt the framework to convolutional layers (e.g., filter-wise group penalties) and to depth selection? Do you expect AdaRad’s angular/radial decomposition to transfer naturally to convolutional filters?

Overall, this is a thoughtful and promising contribution that combines a sound theoretical foundation with practical algorithmic tools for learning network size in situ. With clearer runtime analysis, stronger baselines/ablations, and deeper discussion of failure cases, the paper would be even stronger.
