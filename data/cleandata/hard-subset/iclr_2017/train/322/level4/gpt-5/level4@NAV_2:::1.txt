This paper presents a nonparametric neural network framework that automatically adjusts model size during a single training run. The core idea is to keep adding units (initialized so their addition does not change the current function) and to use sparsity-inducing regularization to drive unhelpful units to zero, thereby pruning them. The overall procedure resembles on-the-fly exploration of discrete architectures guided by a sparsity penalty rather than an external black-box search. The problem is important, and the reported results are interesting.

My main comments are listed below:
- What is the computational overhead of the proposed training procedure? Decomposing each fan-in gradient into components parallel and orthogonal to the current fan-in and then applying updates in radial–angular coordinates could introduce significant extra cost. It would be helpful to quantify the additional operations relative to standard parametric training (e.g., SGD/RMSprop/Adam) and to include wall-clock comparisons or throughput (examples/sec) on representative tasks.
- The method tends to produce relatively small models on the convex dataset and underperforms parametric baselines there. Do the authors have insight into why the procedure converges to undersized architectures on this task?
