- Paper studies dynamic capacity allocation by adding/removing units during training; direction is interesting and timely.
- Theorem: fan-in or fan-out regularization yields an optimal error at finite parameter count, preventing unbounded growth.
- The theorem evokes Lasso/ElasticNet but needs more plain-English intuition and discussion of what structural sparsity it promotes (weights, units, or groups).
- Trim related-work to make room for clearer explanation of the main theorem.
- Empirical: require a head-to-head comparison where the discovered final architecture is trained from scratch with matched size and training budget.
- Quantify whether nonparametric training saves parameters or compute versus comparable fixed models and report performance–memory/compute trade-offs.
- Figure 2 is hard to parse; results are mixed (nonparametric sometimes better, sometimes worse) and need clearer presentation and interpretation.
- Explain why nonparametric models can outperform parametric models with the same final architecture; run ablations (grow-only, prune-only, delayed growth, schedules) to isolate mechanisms.
- Theorem clarification: provide plain-English intuition on how fan-in/out penalties prevent unbounded growth and their relation to group/unit sparsity.
- Sensitivity: analyze how regularization strength λ controls final architecture and give practical guidance or heuristics for choosing λ.
- Report variability across random seeds: distributions of final sizes, architectures, and performance to assess dependence on initialization.
- Analyze combining fan-in and fan-out regularizers: does finite-parameter optimality still hold and under what conditions?
- State limitations and whether the theorem extends beyond the loss/activation assumptions used.
- Clarify the claim “adding zero units changes the regularizer value” and precisely explain which regularizer term changes and why.
- Refine the definition of a “zero unit”: removing a unit without changing f requires both incoming and outgoing weights to be zero or the unit to be functionally inert.
- Improve figure presentation: larger fonts, clearer legends, consistent y-axes, error bars over multiple runs, and ensured matched training budgets.
- Report computational overhead of growth/pruning relative to fixed training (wall-clock time, memory fragmentation, GPU efficiency).
- Add diagnostics showing how units are added/removed over time and layer-wise concentration of added/removed units.
- Strengths: interesting problem, principled regularization inducing finite-size optima, promising architecture discovery.
- Weaknesses: limited intuition/discussion of theorem, unclear experimental baselines at matched size, presentation issues, and open questions about λ selection and stability.
- Overall: direction valuable and theoretically appealing; acceptance contingent on stronger empirical baselines, clearer presentation, and deeper theorem discussion (Rating: 7 accept).
