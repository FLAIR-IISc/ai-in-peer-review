1. Summary of contributions
The paper proposes a simple, well-motivated framework for learning the number of units in a neural network during a single training run. The method adds units with zero fan-out and randomly initialized fan-in, and removes units whose fan-in or fan-out are driven to zero by a group sparsity penalty (an ℓ2 fan-in/fan-out regularizer). The main theoretical result shows that, under this regularization, the training objective attains a global minimum at a finite network size, addressing the risk that the optimal solution might otherwise require unbounded width. Empirically, the authors report that networks trained nonparametrically can match or improve upon parametric networks of the same size, often yielding smaller models with comparable accuracy.

2. Decision
Reject. My two primary reasons are:
- The empirical evaluation is not sufficiently comprehensive to establish practicality, scalability, or competitiveness with strong baselines (notably pruning/compression methods).
- The approach appears to introduce new hyperparameters and training-time trade-offs that may not simplify model selection in practice; their tunability and impact are not convincingly demonstrated.

3. Supporting arguments
- Scope and baselines: The experiments are encouraging but limited. They are conducted on relatively small benchmarks and do not include direct comparisons with established methods that reduce network size after training (e.g., pruning). Since a key claimed benefit is producing smaller networks with comparable performance, it is essential to compare against modern pruning/compression pipelines that are designed for precisely that goal. Without this, it is unclear whether the proposed method is competitive.
- Hyperparameter burden: Although the method adapts network size automatically, several additional hyperparameters implicitly control the final width (e.g., unit addition rate/frequency, the strength of the group sparsity penalty, and the schedule that governs how quickly weights shrink to zero). It is not evident that these are easier to tune than the width/depth hyperparameters of standard parametric models. The paper is careful not to overclaim here, but the hoped-for simplification of model selection is not demonstrated.
- Training efficiency and scalability: The same hyperparameters that regulate model size also affect training time. Growing a network gradually can make training slower, and the paper does not show that total wall-clock time or data efficiency is improved relative to strong parametric baselines, nor how the approach scales to large models and datasets. While black-box hyperparameter search can be expensive, in many practical settings prior experience narrows the search space substantially; the proposed method’s potential slowness could be intrinsic.
- Theoretical result vs. practice: The theorem ensures existence of a finite-size minimizer under the proposed regularization, which is a solid and relevant contribution. However, this does not directly speak to generalization or to the risk of under/over-fitting induced by the dynamics of addition and shrinkage. The experiments suggest this may not be a major issue on the tested tasks, but the evidence is preliminary.

4. Additional feedback and questions for the authors
These points are intended to help strengthen the paper; they are not all decisive for my recommendation.

- Comparisons and scope:
  - Include direct empirical comparisons with competitive pruning/compression methods (e.g., structured pruning by group penalties, importance-based pruning) on the same architectures/datasets. Report accuracy, parameter counts, FLOPs, and inference latency.
  - Evaluate on larger-scale tasks and architectures to demonstrate practicality and scalability. Even a subset of a standard image benchmark with convolutional layers would help.
  - Report wall-clock training time and number of optimization steps to reach a target validation accuracy, for both nonparametric and tuned parametric baselines.

- Hyperparameter sensitivity and ablations:
  - Provide sensitivity analyses for the key hyperparameters that control growth and shrinkage (e.g., regularization strength, unit addition rate/frequency, and any coupling between them). How robust are results to these choices?
  - Clarify whether the same settings transfer across datasets, or if per-dataset retuning is required. If retuning is needed, how does that compare to selecting widths/depths in parametric models?
  - Ablate the unit addition schedule and the shrinkage schedule to show their individual effects on final size, training time, and accuracy.

- Clarifications on claims and methodology:
  - The theoretical result guarantees a finite-size minimizer of the training objective. Can you characterize how the minimizing size depends on λ and p, and whether this interacts with depth?
  - Do you observe consistent tendencies toward under-sized models on certain tasks (e.g., convex), and can you mitigate this by adjusting the growth/shrinkage schedule or regularization strength without sacrificing accuracy?
  - Since the regularizer governs both learning dynamics and final size, how do you recommend setting λ in practice? Is there a principled annealing strategy that stabilizes training and improves final performance?
  - To what extent is the success of the approach tied to self-similar activations and the specific normalization choice? Have you tested alternatives, and how do they affect stability and size selection?

- Practical considerations:
  - Provide detailed runtime, memory, and computational cost comparisons versus parametric training with standard optimizers, including any overhead from unit addition/removal.
  - Discuss how the method might extend to convolutional or recurrent architectures, where structured sparsity and hardware efficiency are central.
  - Since the method can yield smaller models, consider reporting energy or latency metrics where relevant.

Overall, the paper is clearly written and presents an appealing idea with a solid theoretical underpinning. The vision of networks that grow and adapt is compelling, especially for lifelong learning. However, to justify publication, the empirical section needs stronger baselines, larger-scale demonstrations, and clearer evidence on hyperparameter robustness and efficiency.
