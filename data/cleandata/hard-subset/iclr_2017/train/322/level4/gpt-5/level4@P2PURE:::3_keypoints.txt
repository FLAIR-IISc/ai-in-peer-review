- Proposes a simple, elegant mechanism to add/remove units during training with a group-sparsity regularizer that encourages whole units’ weights to shrink to zero for pruning.  
- Main theoretical result: with appropriate regularization, the loss is minimized by a network with a finite number of units.  
- Theory doesn’t guarantee avoidance of over- or underfitting in practice, though initial experiments do not show this issue.  
- Potential advantage: methods that learn unit count may reduce hyperparameter tuning burden.  
- Drawback: the method still depends on several hyperparameters (e.g., unit-add frequency, weight decay speed) that implicitly control model size, so it doesn’t truly eliminate tuning.  
- Authors do not claim easier training, but it is disappointing that tuning isn’t simplified.  
- Claim: achieves comparable performance with fewer units, which could reduce latency, power, and memory for deployment (e.g., mobile).  
- Missing comparisons to existing network compression/pruning techniques, so competitiveness with state-of-the-art compression is unclear.  
- Same hyperparameters control both model size and training time, potentially making training substantially slower than fixed-parametric approaches.  
- Scaling concerns: paper lacks discussion/experiments on large-scale networks, raising practicality and speed issues for big models.  
- Experiments are encouraging but not comprehensive or wholly convincing; results on much larger problems are needed.  
- Overall assessment: interesting, clearly written, and potentially useful with an inspiring vision of lifelong adaptable networks, but current results remain fairly speculative.
