This paper proposes a nonparametric neural network that automatically learns its size during training. The key idea is to randomly add zero-initialized units and use a sparsity-inducing regularizer to drive irrelevant weights to zero. Conceptually, the approach resembles a randomized search over a discrete architecture space, guided by sparsity regularization to eliminate useless units. This addresses an important problem, and the empirical results are interesting. My main comments are below:

- Computational complexity and overhead: What is the additional computational complexity of the algorithm? The decomposition of each unit’s fan-in weights into parallel and orthogonal components, and the transformation to radial–angular coordinates, may introduce substantial overhead. Please quantify the extra operations relative to a standard parametric network, both per iteration and over the entire training process. It would be helpful to provide running-time experiments comparing the proposed method to comparable parametric baselines (e.g., fixed-size models with similar final capacity), ideally across multiple datasets and model sizes, to make clear the practical trade-offs between adaptivity and compute.

- Behavior on the convex dataset: It is observed that the nonparametric networks return very small architectures on the convex dataset, leading to inferior performance compared to parametric networks. Do you have insight into why this occurs? For example, is the sparsity regularizer too aggressive in this setting, does the unit-addition mechanism fail to expand sufficiently, or is this a property of the dataset/optimization that favors overly compact models? A discussion clarifying the underlying cause would be valuable, as well as any guidance on how to mitigate this issue in practice.

Overall, the paper tackles an important question and presents promising results, but it would benefit from a more thorough analysis of computational costs and empirical runtime, together with an explanation of the observed underperformance on the convex dataset.
