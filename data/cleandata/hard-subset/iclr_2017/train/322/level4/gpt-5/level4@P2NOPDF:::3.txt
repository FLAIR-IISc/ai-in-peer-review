This paper studies neural networks that adapt their number of units during training. The approach is simple, elegant, and well motivated: new zero-weight units are occasionally added, and units whose incoming or outgoing weights shrink to zero are pruned. A group-sparsity regularizer encourages entire units to go to zero. The main theoretical result shows that, with appropriate regularization, the loss is minimized by a network with finitely many units. While this does not guarantee the learned model will avoid over- or underfitting, preliminary experiments suggest this is not a major issue.

A potential benefit of learning the number of units is reduced hyperparameter tuning. However, this method does not fully deliver on that hope: several hyperparameters still implicitly control model size, including how often new units are added and how quickly weights decay to zero. It is unclear whether these are easier or harder to tune than in conventional setups. To the authors’ credit, they do not claim to have simplified training, but it is somewhat disappointing that tuning remains a challenge.

The authors highlight that their method achieves competitive performance with fewer units. This could matter in practice, as smaller networks can reduce test-time latency, power consumption, and memory footprint—especially on mobile devices. Yet the paper does not compare against established post-training compression techniques (e.g., pruning), so it is unclear how competitive this method is with the best current approaches to model size reduction.

Another concern is that the same hyperparameters govern both the number of units and training time. Training may therefore be substantially slower than with fixed-size networks. While parametric models often rely on grid search, in many cases prior experience makes hyperparameter selection relatively straightforward, so the full cost of grid search is not always incurred. By contrast, the potential slowness of the proposed method may be intrinsic. The paper also does not discuss scaling to much larger networks, raising concerns about practicality at scale.

Overall, the experiments are informative and encouraging but not comprehensive. Results on significantly larger tasks would strengthen the case for practicality and broad utility.

In sum, this is an interesting and clearly written paper that makes a potentially useful contribution. The vision of networks that can grow and adapt through lifelong learning is compelling, and work of this kind may be essential to achieve it. For now, however, the empirical evidence remains preliminary and somewhat speculative.
