1. Summary of claims and contributions
The paper tackles the problem of learning the number of units to use in a neural network during a single training run. The approach is simple, well-motivated, and conceptually elegant: units with zero input or output weights are added or removed on the fly, and a group sparsity regularizer encourages entire fan-ins or fan-outs to go to zero, making deletion principled. The main theoretical result shows that, under appropriate regularization, the empirical objective attains its minimum at a network of finite size. While this result does not itself ensure against over- or under-fitting, the initial experiments suggest that the method can find compact models without clear signs of either problem and achieve performance comparable to parametric baselines.

2. Decision
Reject.
Key reasons: (1) Insufficient empirical validation against strong baselines that explicitly target model size (e.g., pruning of trained parametric networks), and (2) unresolved issues around hyperparameter burden and training-time/scalability that make practicality on larger problems uncertain.

3. Supporting arguments
- Soundness and motivation: The formulation and the finite-minimum theorem are compelling and justify optimizing over network width during training. However, the theorem does not translate into guarantees about generalization or learned size in practice, and the experiments provided do not fully close that gap.
- Hyperparameterization: Although the method aims to reduce manual model-size selection, several new hyperparameters implicitly determine the final size (e.g., the rate of adding units and the strength/rate at which weights decay to zero). It is unclear whether these are easier to set than standard width hyperparameters; the paper does not show that tuning burden is reduced in practice.
- Missing baselines: The empirical section does not compare against established approaches that shrink models after training (e.g., pruning and related structured sparsity techniques). Without such comparisons, it is hard to assess whether the proposed method is competitive for producing small, accurate models.
- Training-time concerns: The same knobs that control emergent size also influence optimization dynamics and runtime, potentially making training substantially slower than a fixed-width parametric alternative. While exhaustive grid/random searches can be expensive, in many settings prior experience yields reasonable hyperparameters without such searches, so the cost of the proposed approach may be inherent rather than optional. The paper does not convincingly address scaling to much larger networks or datasets.
- Scope of experiments: Results are encouraging but limited; they do not demonstrate that the approach is practical or robust at larger scales, which is critical for impact.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all points affect the decision)
- Include direct comparisons to competitive pruning/compression baselines on the same architectures and datasets, reporting final accuracy, parameter count, and test-time metrics (latency, memory).
- Provide guidance or heuristics for the new hyperparameters (unit addition frequency, regularization strength/decay rates), and analyze sensitivity. If possible, demonstrate partial automation of these choices.
- Report wall-clock training time and compute cost alongside accuracy and model size, and separate the effects of “size-setting” hyperparameters from pure optimization hyperparameters.
- Explore larger-scale experiments to establish practicality, or provide strong evidence-based arguments for expected scalability.
- Clarify the circumstances under which the approach yields smaller networks with equal or better accuracy than training a fixed-width model and then shrinking it.

Questions for the authors
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: How sensitive are final model size and accuracy to the unit addition schedule and the strength/rate of the group sparsity regularization? Can you provide default settings or adaptive rules of thumb that work across tasks?
- Comparative efficacy: How does your method compare empirically to pruning-based pipelines that start from a strong parametric model (e.g., magnitude pruning or structured pruning) in terms of final accuracy, parameter count, and test-time efficiency?
- Training-time vs. parametric baselines: What is the wall-clock overhead relative to training a well-tuned fixed-width model (with typical practitioner effort rather than exhaustive search)? Can you quantify the trade-off between emergent size control and convergence speed?
- Scalability: How does the approach behave on substantially larger networks/datasets? Are there known failure modes (e.g., very slow growth, oscillations in unit counts) or stabilizing strategies when scaling up?
- Decoupling goals: Is there a way in your framework to decouple the hyperparameters that govern “how big the network becomes” from those that determine optimization speed, so that one can target a size without unduly slowing training?
