I agree with Reviewer 2 that the paper tackles a compelling idea: letting the network grow or shrink by adding or removing units during training so the architecture is driven by the data rather than designer priors.

The theoretical contribution is also appealing. Under fan-in or fan-out regularization, the optimum of the objective is achieved with a finite number of parameters, preventing unbounded growth and trivial overfitting. This evokes classical sparsity-inducing regularizers like Lasso and Elastic Net. However, the paper would benefit from more intuition for this result—what mechanism forces finiteness, and how does it relate to sparsity at the unit level? I would suggest trimming some of the prior work discussion in favor of a deeper, plain-English explanation, supported by targeted experiments (see below).

Comments and suggestions:

1) Empirical evaluation of adaptive vs. fixed architectures
- A key experiment would compare your adaptive method to a fixed architecture with the same final number of units (after training completes), trained from scratch with standard procedures. This would directly test whether adaptivity yields better optimization, generalization, or memory/performance trade-offs at fixed capacity.
- Relatedly: does your method “reallocate” capacity—removing unhelpful units and adding beneficial ones—in a way that improves accuracy per parameter or wall-clock efficiency? If so, please quantify.
- My reading of Figure 2 is that results are mixed; sometimes the nonparametric nets outperform, sometimes they underperform, and the figure is not easy to parse. Even with mixed outcomes, adaptivity could be useful for structure discovery. But an important question remains: when the final architecture is known, why can the nonparametric route outperform training that same architecture from scratch? Please provide insight or ablations (e.g., optimization path benefits, implicit curriculum from progressive growth/shrinkage, improved conditioning, or regularization effects).

2) Theorem 1: meaning, implications, and practical guidance
- The theorem appears to formalize a natural intuition, but the paper currently presents it with minimal discussion. What is the key insight in plain English? What are the exact conditions under which finiteness holds, and how do they map to practical setups?
- Is the regularization coefficient λ the principal driver of the final architecture (e.g., effective number of units per layer)? If so, can you provide guidance or data-driven heuristics for choosing λ? Are there dataset characteristics that correlate with the optimal λ (e.g., sample size, noise level, complexity)?
- How sensitive is the final structure to initialization? Do different random seeds yield materially different architectures or similar ones? Please report variability (e.g., distribution of unit counts or sparsity patterns across runs).
- What happens when fan-in and fan-out regularizers are combined? Does an analogous finiteness theorem hold? If not, what changes?
- More broadly, can you provide diagnostic curves (e.g., unit counts vs. epochs vs. λ; validation accuracy vs. unit count) to illustrate how the theorem’s implications play out during training?

Clarifications:
1) You state that adding zero units changes the regularizer value. For L2 norms, adding zeros should not affect the penalty. Are you using a regularizer that depends on the number of units (e.g., group norms, biases, or other terms)? Please clarify precisely which regularizer is affected and why.
2) You define a “zero unit” as having either its fan-in or its fan-out weights equal to zero. But to remove a unit without changing the network function, both its fan-in and fan-out must be zero. Please clarify the definition and the pruning criterion to avoid ambiguity.

Overall, I find the idea and the theory interesting and potentially impactful, especially if accompanied by clearer intuition and stronger, more targeted empirical evidence. I have updated my rating to 7, contingent on the authors addressing the points above.
