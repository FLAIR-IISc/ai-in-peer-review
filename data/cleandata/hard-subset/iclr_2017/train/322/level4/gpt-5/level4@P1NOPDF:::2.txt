I agree with Reviewer 2 that this paper explores an interesting direction. The idea of automatically adding or removing units allows the model to grow or shrink according to the data and task, rather than the user’s prior knowledge.

The authors present an appealing theoretical result: under fan-in or fan-out regularization, the error function attains its optimum with a finite number of parameters, so the network does not grow without bound to perfectly fit the data. This recalls classical approaches such as the Lasso or Elastic Net, where regularization induces sparsity. I would have appreciated more intuition for this theorem. The result is nice—and, to me, largely expected or at least intuitive—and the paper would benefit from devoting space to convey that intuition. For example, a shorter discussion of prior work (useful but less central than analyzing the main result) could free space to discuss the theory in depth. Please also see my suggestions under point 2 below.

I have a few additional comments:

1. Experiments: An informative experiment would compare your method—which adds and removes neurons automatically—to a network with the same final number of neurons (measured after training with your method) but with that size fixed from the start. This would directly test the efficiency of the idea. Does your procedure eliminate unneeded units and replace them with useful ones? Does it trade off performance against memory? I understand that Figure 2 aims at this, with mixed results. However, the figure is not very clear and requires careful inspection to interpret. In some cases the nonparametric networks do better, and in others worse, than the parametric ones. Even so, the method seems useful for discovering structure. What I do not fully understand is why the nonparametric variant can sometimes outperform the final fixed architecture trained from scratch, when that final architecture is known in advance. Could you provide more insight?

2. Theorem 1: Please discuss the meaning and implications of Theorem 1 more thoroughly. Beyond the appendix proofs, what is the key insight, in plain English? The conclusion feels almost natural and obvious; is there a deeper takeaway? As noted above, I believe this theoretical result deserves more space, ideally with additional experiments to support it. For instance:
   - Can the regularization parameter λ be predicted from properties of the data? Is there any data characteristic that helps select λ? My impression is that λ is the key factor determining the final network structure—is that correct?
   - How sensitive is the final structure to initialization? Do different random initializations yield different architectures, and how different are they?
   - What happens when fan-in and fan-out regularizers are combined? Does the same theoretical guarantee still hold?

Additional questions:
1. Why do you state that adding zero-valued units changes the value of the regularizer? For example, would the L2 norm change if we add zero entries?
2. You define zero units as those with either their fan-in or fan-out weights equal to zero. I think you mean both should be zero; otherwise, removing the unit would change f. Please clarify.

I have changed my rating to 7, and I hope the authors will address the comments above.
