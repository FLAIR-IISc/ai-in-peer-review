Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes “nonparametric neural networks,” a framework that allows the number of hidden units to grow and shrink during a single training run. Units are added as zero-units and removed when their fan-in or fan-out goes to zero under a group-norm penalty.
- Theoretical contribution: with a fan-in or fan-out ‘p penalty (1 ≤ p < ∞, λ > 0), the training objective attains its minimum at a finite network size. This provides a sound basis for optimizing over architectures within training.
- Practical components: a new optimizer (AdaRad) that separates radial (length) and angular (direction) gradient components at the fan-in level, and a modified normalization layer (CapNorm) to avoid neutralizing the regularizer.
- Empirically, the method can match or improve accuracy with fewer units than fixed-size baselines on some tasks, suggesting potential test-time benefits in memory and computation.

Decision: Reject
Primary reasons:
1) Insufficient empirical evidence and missing baselines to establish practical competitiveness, especially versus strong pruning/compression pipelines and conventional hyperparameter/architecture search.
2) Unclear scalability and hyperparameter burden: the approach introduces new tuning knobs that appear to jointly control both training time and the final model size, with limited guidance on robustness.

Supporting arguments
- Hyperparameter burden remains high: model capacity is governed by λ, unit addition rate/frequency, and the radial/angular step sizes in AdaRad, all of which influence both when units survive and overall training time. It is not shown that these are easier to tune than standard choices for fixed architectures.
- Missing comparisons: the paper does not compare against modern pruning/compression or distillation approaches that are specifically designed to reduce model size after training. Without such results, it is hard to judge whether the proposed method is competitive for producing compact, accurate models.
- Training efficiency and scalability: growing networks from scratch can delay convergence and may slow training substantially compared to well-chosen fixed architectures. The paper does not provide wall-clock or compute comparisons, nor scaling experiments on large, high-dimensional benchmarks (e.g., deep convnets on CIFAR/ImageNet). The poker experiment is welcome but does not address modern large-scale neural models.
- Mixed empirical outcomes: while some datasets show favorable results (e.g., MNIST), performance degrades on others (e.g., convex), and the final advantages over strong parametric baselines are not consistently demonstrated. The reliance on a bespoke optimizer (AdaRad) and CapNorm also leaves unclear how much each component contributes versus the core nonparametric idea.
- Fairness and rigor of experimentation: results against parametric networks depend on training schedules and optimizers; robust baselines typically include careful tuning of SGD with momentum/Adam/RMSprop plus batch norm/weight decay, and matched training budgets. Ablations (e.g., removing CapNorm or replacing AdaRad) are limited.

Suggestions to improve the paper (helpful, not necessarily decisive)
- Add comparisons to state-of-the-art size-reduction techniques: structured pruning (e.g., channel- or unit-pruning), sparsity-inducing regularization, and distillation/teacher-student compression.
- Report end-to-end training cost: wall-clock time, number of updates, and compute/energy for nonparametric vs parametric runs with matched budgets. Include test-time latency and memory.
- Provide sensitivity analyses and ablations: vary λ, unit addition rate/frequency, αr/αφ; isolate the effects of AdaRad and CapNorm; compare p=1 vs p=2 group penalties; test standard optimizers.
- Evaluate on larger and more realistic settings, particularly convolutional architectures (CIFAR-10/100, ImageNet) and deeper networks, since the claimed advantages are most impactful when model size and deployment constraints matter.
- Clarify selection protocol: how the final size is chosen, whether retraining the discovered architecture as a fixed parametric model helps, and how early stopping interacts with size evolution.
- Release code and reproducible scripts to facilitate broader testing.

Questions for the authors
1) Hyperparameter selection: How did you choose λ, unit addition rate/frequency, and αr/αφ across datasets? How sensitive are results to these choices, and can you provide default, robust settings?
2) Training cost: What are the wall-clock times and total optimization steps vs best-tuned parametric baselines? How does AdaRad’s overhead scale with depth and width?
3) Baselines: How does your approach compare to recent pruning/distillation methods when starting from a reasonably tuned parametric network? Can your method match their size-accuracy trade-offs?
4) Failure modes: On the convex dataset, what explains the weaker results? Is the method biased toward smaller networks that underfit? Would retraining the discovered architecture from scratch close the gap?
5) Role of components: How much of the gain comes from AdaRad and CapNorm versus the nonparametric growth/shrinkage itself? Do standard optimizers with your regularizer and unit addition/removal achieve similar results?
6) Initialization and growth policy: How crucial is the zero fan-out/random fan-in initialization? Have you tried alternative seeding or adaptive addition schedules (e.g., data-driven triggers)?
7) Extension to convnets: How do you envision adapting unit addition/removal to convolutional channels/filters, and does the finite-minimum theorem extend straightforwardly?
8) Stopping criteria: What practical rule do you recommend for halting growth? Is there a principled criterion beyond validation performance plateaus?

Overall assessment
The paper presents an appealing idea with solid theoretical grounding and neat algorithmic elements. However, stronger empirical validation, clearer evidence of practical benefits, and comparisons to competitive compression/pruning baselines are needed to support acceptance.
