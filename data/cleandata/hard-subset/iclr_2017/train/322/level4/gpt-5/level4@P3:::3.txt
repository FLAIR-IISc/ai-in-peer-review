1. Summary and contributions
The paper tackles the problem of selecting network size during training, avoiding expensive outer-loop hyperparameter searches over widths. It proposes “nonparametric neural networks,” in which the number of units per layer can grow or shrink within a single training run. Two ideas enable this:
- Model-side: Add zero units to hidden layers (preserving function equivalence) and remove units when their fan-in (or fan-out) goes to zero under group sparsity. A central theoretical result (Theorem 1) proves that, with an appropriate fan-in or fan-out lp regularizer (λ > 0, 1 ≤ p < ∞), a global minimum occurs at finite network size. A further proposition shows that with self-similar nonlinearities (e.g., ReLU), per-layer regularization weights can be reduced to a single λ up to rescaling.
- Optimization-side: Introduce AdaRad, which decomposes gradients into “radial” (length) and “angular” (direction) components per fan-in and normalizes only the angular component. This is intended to let newly added units learn useful features without being immediately shrunk to zero. The paper also proposes CapNorm, a variant of batch normalization that avoids negating the regularizer by capping the rescaling at standard deviation 1.

Experiments on MNIST, rectangles-images, convex, and a large OpenML poker dataset show that networks can grow to a data-driven size and, on some datasets, match or exceed parametric networks of the same size. The paper emphasizes parameter-efficiency (fewer units for comparable accuracy) and outlines computational and memory trade-offs between AdaRad and RMSprop.

2. Recommendation
Decision: Reject.
Key reasons: (1) Empirical evaluation is not yet convincing for broad applicability, missing comparisons to strong baselines in model compression/pruning and lacking large-scale benchmarks; (2) The proposed approach introduces several new hyperparameters (e.g., λ, αr, αφ, unit addition rate/frequency) whose tuning burden and scalability are not adequately assessed.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The goal of learning network size during training is timely and important; the paper is well motivated and related to both pruning and hyperparameter optimization literature. The theoretical result establishing existence of a finite-size minimizer under group regularization is sound and valuable.
- Method novelty and placement: The use of f-equivalence via zero-unit insertion/removal is clean and connects to constructive/growing-net methods. The focus on l2 group sparsity (fan-in/out) distinguishes this from weight pruning aiming at extreme sparsity. AdaRad’s radial/angular decomposition is an interesting contribution tailored to the nonparametric setting.
- Empirical support: Results are mixed. On MNIST and rectangles-images, nonparametric networks can be competitive or better than parametric networks of the same size; on convex, they underperform and appear to converge to too-small models. The poker experiment is encouraging but lacks broader baselines and variability analysis. Critically, there is no direct comparison to modern pruning/structured sparsity and compression methods (e.g., Molchanov et al., Wen et al., Alvarez & Salzmann), which also produce smaller networks and often report strong test-time efficiency.
- Practicality and tuning: Although the approach aims to replace outer-loop size search, it introduces multiple interacting hyperparameters that implicitly control both model size and training dynamics (λ, αr, αφ, unit addition rate ν and frequency). The paper does not establish that tuning these is simpler or faster in practice than standard width selection with conventional optimizers. Moreover, growth-from-scratch training can be slower to reach good accuracy because useful capacity is acquired gradually; the paper acknowledges additional compute for AdaRad but does not provide comprehensive wall-clock comparisons under matched accuracy and hardware.
- Scope and scalability: The study is restricted to fully connected networks and relatively modest datasets (except poker). There is no demonstration on modern convolutional architectures or tasks where model size and inference cost are critical. This limits confidence in scalability and broader impact.

4. Feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions, not all decision-critical)
- Baselines and metrics:
  - Compare against state-of-the-art pruning and structured sparsity methods starting from overparameterized models (including unit/channel pruning). Report accuracy, parameter count, FLOPs, memory, and wall-clock training and inference time.
  - Include ablations: (i) AdaRad vs RMSprop/Adam under your nonparametric regime, (ii) CapNorm vs BN-without-gamma/beta vs no normalization, (iii) l1 vs l2 group penalties.
  - Provide multiple seeds and dispersion (mean/median±std) for all settings, including poker.
- Hyperparameter sensitivity:
  - Systematically study sensitivity to λ, αr, αφ, ν, νfreq. Show how these affect final size, convergence time, and accuracy. Clarify recommended defaults and tuning heuristics.
  - Quantify the claimed advantage of self-similar nonlinearities by contrasting ReLU with tanh/ELU under CapNorm.
- Efficiency and scaling:
  - Report end-to-end wall-clock training time to a fixed validation target for nonparametric vs parametric approaches (with and without outer-loop search), and the fraction of time attributable to growth phase.
  - Validate the computational and memory cost claims for AdaRad with profiling on CPU/GPU.
- Theory and assumptions:
  - Clarify the exact assumptions on σ (directional differentiability, σ(0)=0) used in Theorem 1 and their practical scope. Discuss how CapNorm interacts with the regularizer and whether it affects the theorem’s conditions.
  - Discuss whether the result might extend to learned depth or convolutional layers.
- Scope expansion:
  - Demonstrate on at least one convolutional benchmark (e.g., CIFAR-10/100) to establish relevance to modern architectures.
  - Explore initialization and unit addition schedules (e.g., importance sampling, gradient-based triggers) beyond fixed-rate insertion.

5. Questions for the authors
- Hyperparameters: How do you recommend choosing αr and αφ in practice? Can you provide a heuristic that targets a desired “survival time” for new units while maintaining fast descent? How sensitive is final performance to ν and νfreq?
- CapNorm: Since CapNorm omits learnable scale/shift and caps variance at 1, does it limit representational power or slow optimization relative to BN? Can you quantify its effect with and without the regularizer?
- AdaRad: Can you provide wall-clock comparisons to RMSprop/Adam achieving the same validation accuracy, including normalization and shrinkage costs? Does AdaRad still help when used with parametric networks?
- Convex dataset: Why do nonparametric networks underperform there? Is the issue early elimination (too aggressive shrinkage) or an optimizer/normalization mismatch? Can adjusting αr/λ or unit-addition schedules fix this systematically?
- Comparisons to pruning: How does your method compare to training a larger network and pruning (magnitude, structured, or variational methods) in terms of final accuracy, compression ratio, and total training time?
- Scalability: For larger models/datasets, can you quantify the time until layer widths stabilize relative to total training time? Do the costs of growth diminish as dataset size increases, as suggested for poker?
- Code and reproducibility: Will you release code and scripts to reproduce the training schedules, including the annealing/rewind procedures?

Overall, the paper is clearly written, the idea is appealing, and the theory is solid. With stronger empirical validation, broader baselines, and clearer guidance on tuning and efficiency, this line of work could have significant impact.
