This paper tackles the problem of enabling networks to vary the number of units during training. The method is simple, elegant, and well motivated. Units are introduced with zero input or output weights and pruned when their corresponding weights reach zero, while a group sparsity norm is used to encourage entire units’ weights to vanish. The main theoretical contribution shows that, with appropriate regularization, the loss is minimized by a network with a finite number of units. In practice, this does not guarantee that the learned model will avoid over- or under-fitting, but preliminary experiments suggest this is not a major issue.

A potential advantage of approaches that learn the number of units is reduced hyperparameter-tuning burden. A drawback of this method (and perhaps others like it) is that it does not truly solve that problem. The model still has several hyperparameters that implicitly control how many units emerge, including how frequently new units are added and how quickly weights decay to zero. It is unclear whether these are easier or harder to tune than those in standard methods. To their credit, the authors do not claim to simplify training, but it is somewhat disappointing that this does not appear to be the case.

The authors emphasize that they can train networks with fewer units while achieving performance comparable to parametrically specified models. This could be important, since smaller networks can reduce test-time latency, power consumption, and memory footprint—particularly on mobile devices. However, the paper does not experimentally compare against existing techniques for compressing parametrically trained networks (e.g., pruning), so it remains unclear whether this approach is competitive with the best current methods for reducing model size.

Another possible disadvantage is that the same hyperparameters govern both how many units appear and the training time. As a result, training may be substantially slower than in a parametric approach with fixed hyperparameters. While many parametric methods rely on grid search, which can be slow, in many cases experience makes hyperparameter selection relatively straightforward. Thus, the cost of grid search is not always incurred, whereas the slowness of the proposed method may be intrinsic. The authors do not discuss how this scales to much larger networks, raising concerns that the approach may be impractical for large-scale models due to slow training.

Overall, the experiments are helpful and encouraging, but not comprehensive or fully convincing. I would want to see results on much larger problems before concluding that this approach is truly practical or broadly useful.

In sum, I found this to be an interesting and clearly written paper that makes a potentially useful point. The vision of networks that grow and adapt through lifelong learning is compelling, and work of this kind may be needed to realize it. However, the current results remain fairly speculative.
