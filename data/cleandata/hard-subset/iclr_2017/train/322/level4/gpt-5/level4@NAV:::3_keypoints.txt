- Tackles adaptive network size during training with a simple, well-motivated method.  
- New units are added with zeroed input/output weights and pruned when their weights reach zero; group sparsity encourages whole-unit removal.  
- Theoretical contribution: with appropriate regularization, the loss is minimized by a network with a finite number of units.  
- Theory does not guarantee avoidance of over- or under-fitting; preliminary experiments suggest this is not a major issue.  
- Potential advantage: could reduce hyperparameter-tuning burden in principle.  
- Drawback: method still has hyperparameters (unit-add frequency, weight-decay rate) that implicitly control unit count, so tuning burden isn’t truly solved.  
- Authors acknowledge they do not simplify training, which is disappointing given the motivation.  
- Claim that learned networks can be smaller with comparable performance, useful for reducing latency, power, and memory.  
- No experimental comparison to existing compression/pruning methods, so competitiveness is unclear.  
- Same hyperparameters affect both model size and training time, so training may be substantially slower than parametric approaches.  
- Scalability to much larger networks is not discussed, raising concerns about practicality for large-scale models.  
- Experiments are encouraging but limited; results on much larger problems are needed to conclude practicality.  
- Overall: interesting and clearly written, promising for lifelong/adaptive learning, but current results remain speculative.
