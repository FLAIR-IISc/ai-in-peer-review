I concur with Reviewer 2 on the most interesting aspect of the paper: dynamically adding or removing units so the model grows or shrinks based on the problem and data rather than the user’s prior knowledge.

The authors provide an appealing theoretical result showing that, under fan-in or fan-out regularization, the optimum of the objective occurs with a finite number of parameters. Thus, the network will not grow without bound to perfectly overfit the data. This evokes classical approaches such as Lasso or Elastic Net, where regularization induces sparsity. I would have appreciated more intuition for this theorem. The result is nice and, to me, somewhat expected; it would be useful to allocate space to develop the intuition behind it. For instance, reducing the discussion of prior work (which is good, but less critical than analyzing the main result) could make room for a deeper treatment. Please also see point 2 below for specific suggestions.

I have a few additional comments:

1. A compelling experiment would compare your adaptive model—which adds/removes units automatically—to a fixed-size network with the same final number of units (measured at the end of training), where layer sizes are fixed from the start. This would directly test the efficiency of your approach. The key question is: do you eliminate unnecessary units and redeploy capacity where it is needed? Do you optimize performance versus memory? I understand that Figure 2 presents experiments along these lines, with mixed results. The figure is not very clear, though it can be interpreted with careful inspection. In some cases the nonparametric networks outperform the parametric ones; in others they underperform. Even with mixed results, I can see the method’s value for discovering structure. What I do not fully understand is why the nonparametric approach can sometimes outperform a final architecture that could be trained from scratch: why would nonparametric training be better than parametric training when the final network is known in advance? More insight here would help.

2. Please discuss the meaning and implications of Theorem 1 more thoroughly. As presented, it appears without sufficient interpretive discussion. Beyond the Appendix proofs, what is the core insight in plain English? To me the conclusion seems almost natural/obvious; is there a deeper takeaway? I believe this theoretical result deserves more space and additional experiments. For example:
- Can the regularization parameter λ be predicted from the data? Are there data properties that help choose λ? My sense is that λ largely determines the final network structure—is that correct?
- How much does the final structure depend on initialization? Do different random seeds produce different architectures? How different are they?
- What happens when fan-in and fan-out regularizers are combined? Do the same theoretical guarantees still hold?

I also have a couple of clarifying questions:
1. Why do you claim that adding zero units changes the value of the regularizer? For example, would the L2 norm change if you add parameters with zero values?
2. You define zero units as those with either fan-in or fan-out weights equal to zero. I believe you mean both fan-in and fan-out must be zero; otherwise, removing the unit would not preserve the function f. This should be clarified.

I have updated my rating to 7, with the expectation that the authors will address the comments above.
