Summary and contributions
The paper proposes “nonparametric neural networks,” a framework for automatically adapting the width of a network during a single training run by adding new units and pruning redundant ones. The key theoretical contribution is Theorem 1, which shows that, under fan-in or fan-out group-ℓp regularization (λ > 0, 1 ≤ p < ∞), the training objective attains a global minimum at a finite network size—i.e., the network will not grow without bound. The paper also introduces two practical components for training: (i) a capped batch normalization layer (CapNorm) compatible with the regularizer, and (ii) a new optimizer, AdaRad, which separates radial and angular updates of fan-ins to allow new units to learn despite shrinkage. Experiments on MNIST, rectangles images, convex, and a large OpenML poker dataset demonstrate that the approach can discover network sizes on the fly and, in some cases, achieve accuracy comparable to or better than parametric baselines, sometimes with far fewer parameters.

Decision: Accept (rating 7)
- Reason 1: The paper addresses an important problem—automated model size selection—with a clear and elegant formulation, a sound theoretical guarantee, and concrete algorithmic innovations (AdaRad, CapNorm).
- Reason 2: The empirical results are promising and demonstrate the viability of the approach, even if performance is mixed on some datasets. The framework is likely to stimulate further work on structure learning in neural networks.

Supporting arguments
- The central theorem is valuable: it formalizes why growth under group-ℓp fan-in/fan-out regularization does not diverge, anchoring the proposed nonparametric view on a solid footing. The result is in the spirit of Lasso/Elastic Net providing sparsity at the level of groups (fan-ins/fan-outs).
- The training method is well thought out. AdaRad addresses a real tension: new units initially receive weak loss gradients but strong regularization; by normalizing only the angular component, the method gives new units a chance to learn without undermining shrinkage. CapNorm adapts batch norm to avoid neutralizing the regularizer.
- The empirical evaluation covers several datasets and includes a large-scale case. On MNIST and rectangles images, the method is competitive and sometimes superior to parametric networks, sometimes with substantially fewer parameters; on convex, performance is weaker, which is transparent and informative.
- The paper is well-situated in the literature and identifies pruning/growth, black-box search, and Bayesian approaches.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (not all part of my accept decision)
- Theorem 1 deserves more space and intuition. The result is intuitive (akin to group sparsity promoting finite effective width), but the paper largely defers to the appendix. Please articulate the key ideas in plain language: what mechanism prevents unbounded growth, and how does group shrinkage lead to elimination of whole units? A brief sketch or illustrative example would help.
- Clarify and strengthen the main empirical test of the core claim: show that a nonparametric model that grows/prunes during training outperforms a parametric model of the same final size trained from scratch (same architecture at the end). Figure 2 seems to target this but is hard to parse (mixed results and unclear presentation). A cleaner, dedicated comparison would isolate the benefit of dynamic sizing (e.g., better optimization trajectory, implicit curriculum, or regularization).
- Discuss the role of λ more explicitly. Is λ the dominant determinant of final width in practice? Can any data property guide a good λ choice? Even heuristic guidance or sensitivity plots would be valuable.
- Report variability across random initializations: how much does final structure vary with different seeds, and how different are the learned widths and accuracies?
- Consider analyzing combined fan-in and fan-out regularization empirically, and clarify whether Theorem 1 extends to the combined case.
- Streamline the related work section slightly to free space for the above analyses and for clearer figure captions.
- Improve Figure 2’s readability (legends, axes, exact sizes/parameter counts, clearer mapping between nonparametric representatives and parametric counterparts).

Questions for the authors
- Why do nonparametric runs sometimes outperform parametric models of the same final size trained from scratch? What mechanisms do you hypothesize (e.g., a better optimization path due to gradual growth, implicit regularization, improved conditioning)? Can you provide diagnostic evidence (learning curves, margin statistics, layerwise norms) to support this?
- Theorem 1: beyond the formal proof, what is the key insight in plain English? In particular, how does group-ℓp regularization guarantee that adding units cannot indefinitely reduce the objective?
- λ selection: Is λ effectively the main control knob for final width? Do you observe robust patterns linking λ to final size/performance across datasets? Any practical guidance or proxy measures to pick λ?
- Initialization sensitivity: How much do final widths and test errors vary over seeds? Do structurally different solutions (widths per layer) achieve similar performance?
- Combined regularizers: What happens if fan-in and fan-out penalties are used together? Does the finiteness guarantee still hold? If not, what breaks?
- Objective under zero-unit operations: You state that adding/removing zero units leaves f unchanged but alters the fan-in/fan-out regularizer; however, adding a unit with both fan-in and fan-out equal to zero would contribute zero to an ℓ2-based group penalty. Can you clarify the precise scenario in which the regularizer value changes?
- Definition of zero units: You define a zero unit as having either fan-in or fan-out equal to zero. For f-equivalence after removal, both fans must be zero; otherwise removing the unit changes f. Please clarify the definition and the conditions under which removal preserves f.
- Resource trade-offs: Does the dynamic procedure save parameters or memory overall by replacing unhelpful units with useful ones? Can you quantify the parameter budget and training-time overhead vs. the performance gains?

Overall, this is a strong and thought-provoking paper with solid theory and promising practice. I recommend acceptance and encourage the authors to expand the theorem’s intuition, sharpen the comparative experiments, and clarify the zero-unit/regularizer points.
