Summary
The paper proposes a simple, elegant, and well-motivated method that allows a neural network to change its number of units during training. Units whose input or output weights shrink to zero are removed, and new units can be added; a group sparsity regularizer encourages entire units to go to zero. The main theoretical result shows that, under appropriate regularization, the loss is minimized by a network with finitely many units. While this does not by itself guarantee good generalization, the initial experiments suggest neither pronounced overfitting nor underfitting.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and elegance: The mechanism for growing and pruning units is straightforward and well grounded in group sparsity.
- Theoretical contribution: The proof that the loss is minimized by a finite network provides a clean justification for adaptively controlling model size.
- Practical promise: The method often yields smaller networks with comparable accuracy, which is attractive for deployment where inference cost, power, and memory are constrained.

Weaknesses and Concerns
- Hyperparameter burden: Although the method adapts the number of units, it introduces several hyperparameters that implicitly govern model size (e.g., the rate of adding units and the strength of sparsity/decay). It is unclear whether these are easier to tune than standard hyperparameters in fixed-size networks. The paper does not claim simplification of tuning, but this limitation is still disappointing.
- Missing baselines for model compression: The experimental evaluation does not compare against strong existing approaches to reducing model size after training, such as pruning, quantization, or low-rank factorization. Without such comparisons, it is difficult to assess whether the proposed approach is competitive as a route to compact models.
- Training efficiency and scaling: The same hyperparameters that influence the number of units also affect training time, and the approach may be significantly slower than training a fixed-architecture model with well-chosen hyperparameters. While exhaustive hyperparameter search in parametric models can be costly, in many practical settings prior experience narrows the search substantially. The paper does not discuss how training time scales to much larger networks and datasets, raising concerns about practicality at scale.
- Experimental scope: The experiments are informative but limited. They do not convincingly demonstrate robustness across larger or more diverse tasks, where the benefits and costs (especially training time) would be most apparent.

Suggestions for Improvement
- Add strong compression/comparison baselines: Include state-of-the-art pruning (magnitude and structured), quantization, and knowledge distillation baselines, and report both accuracy and model size/inference speed comparisons.
- Report training cost: Provide wall-clock time, FLOPs, and number of optimization steps for both the proposed method and fixed-architecture baselines. Clarify how often units are added/removed and the overhead of these operations.
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: Present a thorough sensitivity analysis showing how final accuracy, model size, and training time vary with the key hyperparameters controlling growth and sparsity. Guidance on default settings would aid reproducibility.
- Larger-scale validation: Evaluate on substantially larger models/datasets to test scalability (e.g., deeper convnets on more complex benchmarks). This would strengthen claims of practicality and general utility.
- Ablations and stability: Include ablations isolating the effect of the group sparsity term, the unit-addition policy, and removal criteria. Discuss mechanisms to avoid oscillatory add/remove behavior and to ensure stable convergence.
- Inference-time benefits: Quantify real-world speedups and memory/power savings on representative hardware, especially mobile or edge devices.

Overall Assessment
This is an interesting and clearly written paper that proposes a compelling approach to adaptive network capacity with a solid theoretical underpinning. The vision aligns with lifelong learning systems that can grow and adapt over time, and this work could be a step toward that goal. However, in its current form, the empirical support is not comprehensive, particularly regarding comparisons to strong compression baselines and evidence of scalability. I view the contribution as promising but somewhat speculative. Strengthening the experimental section along the suggested lines would make the case for practicality and competitiveness much more convincing.
