I agree with Reviewer 2 about the interesting aspects of this paper. The idea of dynamically removing or adding units is a compelling direction, allowing the model to expand or contract according to the problem and the data rather than the user’s prior assumptions.

The authors present an appealing theoretical result showing that under fan-in or fan-out regularization, the error function attains its optimum with a finite number of parameters—so the network does not grow without bound until it perfectly overfits the data.

This brings to mind more classical approaches such as Lasso or Elastic Net, where regularization induces sparsity in the weights. I would have appreciated more intuition for this theorem. It is a nice result, and somewhat expected (at least it feels intuitive to me), so I would have liked the paper to devote space to building that intuition. For instance, trimming the discussion of prior work (which is good, but less critical than analyzing the main contribution) could make room to better develop and interpret the theory. Please also see below (point 2) for suggestions.

I have a few other comments:
1. An informative experiment would be to demonstrate that a model like yours—where neurons are automatically added or pruned—can outperform a network with the same number of units (as measured at the end of training) whose layer sizes are fixed from the start. This would directly support the effectiveness of the approach. This is the heart of your contribution: do you eliminate unnecessary units and reallocate capacity where it is needed? Do you optimize the trade-off between performance and memory?
I understand that experiments along these lines appear in Figure 2, with mixed outcomes. The figure, I must say, is not very clear, though it can be interpreted with careful reading. In some cases the nonparametric networks do better, in others they do worse than their parametric counterparts. Even so, I can see the method’s value for uncovering structure.
What I do not fully understand is why they sometimes outperform the final fixed network that could have been trained from scratch: why is the nonparametric learning process better than the parametric version when the final architecture is known in advance? Could you provide more insight?

2. Could you more thoroughly discuss the meaning and implications of Theorem 1? I feel the theorem is presented without sufficient interpretation. Beyond the Appendix proofs, what is the key intuition? In plain English, what does it say? To me, the conclusion seems almost natural and obvious. Is there a deeper insight we should take away?
As mentioned earlier, I believe this theoretical result warrants more space, along with additional experiments to substantiate it. For example, can the regularization strength λ be predicted from properties of the data—does the data suggest a reasonable λ? My impression is that λ is the primary factor determining the final architecture. Is that correct?
How sensitive is the final structure to initialization? Do different random starts yield different networks? How different are they?
What happens when fan-in and fan-out regularizers are combined? Does the same theoretical guarantee still hold?

I have a few additional questions:
1. Why do you claim that adding zero units changes the value of the regularizer? For example, would the L2 norm change if you add parameters that are exactly zero?
2. You define zero units as having either the fan-in or the fan-out weights equal to zero. I believe you mean that both fan-in and fan-out should be zero; otherwise, removing the unit would change f. This should be clarified.

I have updated my rating to 7, with the expectation that the authors will address the comments above.
