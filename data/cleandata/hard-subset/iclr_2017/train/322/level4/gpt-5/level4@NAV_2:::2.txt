1. Summary and contributions
The paper tackles automatic selection of neural network size within a single training run by growing and pruning units on the fly, rather than performing expensive black-box hyperparameter searches over architectures. It introduces a non-probabilistic “nonparametric neural network” framework that optimizes over architectures by continuously adding units and removing redundant ones using a group-regularization penalty on fan-in (or fan-out). The central theoretical contribution is a proof that, under a fan-in or fan-out ℓp penalty with λ > 0 and 1 ≤ p < ∞, the training objective attains a global minimum at a finite network size, ensuring the model does not grow unboundedly. On the algorithmic side, the work proposes CapNorm (a capped batch normalization variant compatible with the regularizer) and AdaRad, an optimizer that decomposes gradients into radial and angular components to balance shrinkage and learning for newly added units. Experiments on several datasets and a large-scale OpenML task show promising results: the method can converge to a reasonable size and, in some cases, match or outperform parametric networks trained from scratch.

2. Decision
Accept.
Key reasons: (i) The core idea—jointly optimizing network size and weights during one training run—is compelling and practically relevant, and (ii) the paper provides a soundness theorem plus a concrete training recipe (CapNorm + AdaRad) with empirical evidence that the approach can be competitive and sometimes superior to fixed-size training.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper addresses the high cost and inefficiency of black-box architecture search by enabling architecture adaptation during training. The notion of f-equivalence for adding/removing zero units and the use of self-similar nonlinearities to avoid scale sensitivity are thoughtful choices that make the framework coherent.
- Positioning: The work is well placed relative to pruning, hyperparameter optimization, and network growing literature. While related to sparsity-inducing methods (e.g., Lasso/group-lasso analogues), the focus on dynamic architecture adaptation within a single run is a clear differentiator.
- Evidence for claims: The theoretical result that ℓp fan-in/fan-out regularization yields a finite-size global minimizer is valuable, even if intuitively in line with sparsity-inducing regularization. Empirically, Figure 2 indicates mixed but encouraging outcomes: nonparametric training often discovers compact models that perform on par with or better than parametric counterparts, though not universally (e.g., convex). The poker experiment demonstrates scalability and competitiveness at large scale. These results support the main claim that joint size/weight training can be effective, while also highlighting dataset-dependent behavior.

4. Feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions; not all tied to the decision)
- Theorem 1 intuition and implications: Please expand the main text with plain-language intuition for why the ℓp fan-in/fan-out penalty curbs unbounded growth and leads to finite optima. A short, example-driven explanation (e.g., group penalty compactness and why excessively wide layers become suboptimal under the induced bounds) would make the result more accessible beyond the appendix.
- Experimental clarity: Figure 2 is hard to parse on first read. Consider clearer legends, explicit mapping between nonparametric final sizes and matched parametric baselines, and a more direct comparison highlighting “same final architecture, trained from scratch” vs “grown-then-pruned” performance.
- Direct ablation: Include a targeted experiment that fixes the final discovered architecture and retrains it from scratch with the best parametric recipe, to isolate whether dynamic growth/pruning provides a training advantage beyond architecture selection. The text suggests this is partly done, but a cleaner, side-by-side ablation would strengthen the case.
- Hyperparameter guidance: Provide more practical guidance on choosing λ and the AdaRad step sizes (αr, αφ), perhaps with rules-of-thumb, sensitivity plots, or diagnostics. Given the role of λ in determining final size, a brief heuristic for setting/annealing it would be valuable.
- Robustness analyses: Report variability across random seeds for (i) final sizes, (ii) performance, and (iii) the discovered architectures’ similarity. This would illuminate how much initialization influences the outcome.
- Combined regularizers: A short discussion/experiment on jointly using fan-in and fan-out penalties (and whether the theorem still holds, or under what conditions) would round out the story.
- Clarifications:
  - The text states that adding/removing zero units changes the value of the fan-in/fan-out regularizer. Under the definitions given (e.g., Ωin sums norms of fan-in vectors), adding a truly zero unit contributes a zero term and thus should not change Ω; please clarify the intended statement or any subtlety (e.g., interactions with normalization layers, implementation details, or counting conventions).
  - The definition of “zero unit” (fan-in zero or fan-out zero or both) is correct for f-equivalence because either case makes the unit functionally inert, but this point could be made more explicit to preempt confusion about needing both fans to be zero.

5. Questions for the authors
- Why can the nonparametric training sometimes outperform training an identical final architecture from scratch? Is the advantage due to curriculum-like growth, implicit regularization from shrinkage and angular updates, better conditioning via CapNorm, or improved exploration of feature subspaces as units are added?
- Theorem 1: Beyond the formal proof, what is the core mechanism that forces a finite optimum under fan-in/fan-out penalties? Can you provide a concise geometric or optimization-theoretic intuition?
- Choosing λ: Is λ effectively the main knob controlling final width? Do you observe predictable relationships between λ, dataset scale/complexity, and final model size that could guide setting or annealing λ a priori?
- Sensitivity to initialization: How much do final architectures and accuracies vary across seeds? Are the discovered structures similar up to permutations, or are there substantive differences in layer widths and unit survival?
- Combining penalties: If both fan-in and fan-out regularizers are used together, do your existence/finite-optimum results still apply (perhaps with modified constants), and how does this affect training dynamics and final sparsity patterns?
- Regularizer invariance under zero units: Given Ωin/Ωout as defined, does adding a zero unit truly change the objective via Ω in your implementation, or is the change due to other components (e.g., normalization behavior or additional penalties)? A short clarification would help.
- Zero unit removal: Please confirm explicitly that units with either zero fan-in or zero fan-out are guaranteed to be functionally redundant under σ(0) = 0 and thus removable without changing f. A brief proof sketch in the main text would improve clarity.
