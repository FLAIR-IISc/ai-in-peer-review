1) Summary of contributions
The paper proposes nonparametric neural networks that dynamically grow and shrink during a single training run. The key idea is to optimize over network size by adding units and eliminating entire units via group penalties on fan-in or fan-out weights. The authors prove a central result (Theorem 1): with a fan-in or fan-out p-norm penalty (λ > 0, 1 ≤ p < ∞), the training objective achieves a global minimum at a finite network size—preventing unbounded growth. They introduce two practical components to make this work: (i) CapNorm, a capped batch normalization variant compatible with group penalties, and (ii) AdaRad, a new optimizer that decomposes updates into radial (length) and angular (direction) components, normalizing only the angular part so new units can learn without being immediately shrunk to zero. They use self-similar nonlinearities (e.g., ReLU) to avoid layer-wise regularization tuning. Experiments on MNIST, rectangles-images, convex, and a large OpenML “poker” dataset show the approach can find compact models and, in several settings, match or outperform fixed-size networks and extensive black-box searches.

2) Decision: Accept
Reasons:
- Novel and well-motivated framework that integrates architecture selection with training, supported by a clear theoretical guarantee (finite optimum under group penalties).
- Practical algorithmic contributions (CapNorm and AdaRad) and empirical results that are often competitive with or better than strong parametric baselines, including at scale.

3) Supporting arguments
- Theorem 1 addresses a core concern for nonparametric growth: with suitable group regularization, the optimum occurs at finite width. This anchors the framework conceptually.
- The training scheme is thoughtful: self-similar activations and CapNorm prevent the penalty from being neutralized by rescaling, and AdaRad’s angular normalization lets newly added units learn while the radial component handles shrinkage and unit removal.
- Empirically, the method can discover useful sizes and sometimes outperforms same-size parametric networks trained from scratch (e.g., on MNIST and rectangles-images). The poker results show scalability and strong accuracy with compact models.
- There are caveats: mixed results (notably on convex) and some presentation issues (e.g., Figure 2 is hard to parse). Still, the overall promise and demonstrated feasibility justify acceptance.

4) Suggestions to improve the paper (to help, not all part of the decision)
- Clarify and motivate Theorem 1. Provide intuition linking group penalties to unit-level sparsity (group-lasso analogy), and explain in plain language why this prevents unbounded growth. A short, self-contained sketch before the appendix would help.
- Add a direct “apples-to-apples” comparison: take the final architectures learned nonparametrically and train those fixed architectures from scratch with matched training budgets and optimizers. Analyze when and why the nonparametric path yields better generalization (e.g., implicit curriculum/regularization, better feature reuse).
- Analyze sensitivity to λ and initialization. Report distributions of final sizes and test errors across seeds; discuss how much λ drives final size vs. optimizer choices (αr, αφ) and CapNorm. Consider simple schedules or adaptive tuning of λ (motivated by Proposition 1).
- Explore combining fan-in and fan-out regularizers (theoretically and empirically). Does Theorem 1 extend straightforwardly to the sum of the two, or are additional conditions needed?
- Clarify “zero unit” and the regularizer statement. You define zero units as having either zero fan-in or zero fan-out (or both). Removing such a unit can preserve f if the corresponding row/column is also removed. However, the text states that adding/removing zero units changes the regularizer value. With group-l2, adding a zero fan-in contributes zero to the sum; please explain under what convention this changes Ω (e.g., layer-wise λ scaling, additional terms, or interaction with CapNorm).
- Improve Figure 2 readability. Separate panels for (a) nonparametric vs. matched-size parametric and (b) nonparametric vs. best-over-sizes parametric; add legends, consistent color/marker coding, and error bars or distributional summaries across seeds.
- Provide ablations: AdaRad vs. RMSprop/Adam with group-lasso shrinkage; CapNorm vs. BN and no normalization; unit addition rate/schedule and initialization choices.
- Discuss resource trade-offs: report parameter counts, FLOPs, and memory vs. accuracy over time, highlighting whether nonparametric training saves parameters without hurting performance.

Questions for the authors
- Why can the nonparametric training path outperform training-from-scratch on the final discovered architecture? Is it due to a curriculum over capacity, implicit regularization from group shrinkage, or better exploration of feature subspaces? Can you provide controlled experiments with matched training budgets to isolate these effects?
- Theorem 1: Please restate its key insight in plain English, and indicate whether it extends to combined fan-in + fan-out penalties. Are there activation or data assumptions under which failure modes might occur in practice?
- Choosing λ: Do you observe predictable relationships between λ and final layer widths across datasets? Any data-driven heuristics or adaptive schemes (e.g., annealing or bilevel tuning) that work reliably?
- Sensitivity to initialization: How variable are the final architectures and errors across seeds? Are different runs functionally similar (e.g., via CKA or feature similarity) despite architectural differences?
- CapNorm: Could you show ablations versus BN and no normalization under the group penalty? How sensitive are results to the cap at σ ≥ 1 and to removing affine re-scaling?
- Regularizer and zero units: mathematically, adding a zero fan-in contributes 0 to a sum of group norms. Under what precise definition does Ω change upon adding/removing a zero unit?
- Zero-unit definition: Please clarify that removing a unit with zero fan-in (or zero fan-out) preserves f when the corresponding column (and next-layer row) is removed. A figure or short proof sketch would help.
- Practicality: What is the compute/memory overhead of AdaRad and dynamic resizing versus standard training for a fixed architecture? Can you report wall-clock comparisons?
