I agree with Reviewer 2 that the paper tackles an interesting and promising direction: allowing a network to add or remove units so that model capacity grows or shrinks according to the data and task rather than the user’s prior choice.

The paper’s theoretical result is also appealing. Under fan-in or fan-out regularization, the optimum of the error function is achieved with a finite number of parameters; thus the network does not grow indefinitely until it perfectly overfits the data. This connection evokes classical results such as Lasso and Elastic Net, where regularization induces sparsity. I would have appreciated more intuition for the theorem. The result is nice—indeed somewhat expected and intuitive to me—and the paper would benefit from devoting more space to explaining it in plain terms. For instance, reducing the space allocated to prior work (which is useful but less critical than analyzing the paper’s central result) could free room to discuss and study the main theorem more deeply. Please also see below (point 2) for specific suggestions.

I have a few additional comments:

1) Experiments and evaluation of the adaptive procedure
- A compelling experiment would directly compare your adaptive model (which adds/removes neurons during training) to a fixed-size network that has the same final number of units as your model after training. This would help demonstrate the efficiency of the adaptive approach: do you discard unhelpful units and replace them with useful ones, and do you optimize performance versus memory?
- I understand that Figure 2 attempts to address this, but the figure is not very clear; its interpretation requires careful inspection. The results appear mixed: in some cases the nonparametric networks outperform their parametric counterparts, and in other cases they underperform. Even with mixed outcomes, the method remains useful for structure discovery.
- What I do not fully understand is why the nonparametric approach sometimes outperforms the “end” network trained from scratch, when the final architecture is known in advance. Why can the nonparametric learning procedure be better than the parametric one in that scenario? Additional insight here would be helpful.

2) Meaning and implications of Theorem 1
- Please discuss the theorem’s meaning in plain English and articulate its key insight beyond the proofs in the appendix. To me, the conclusion feels natural and almost obvious; is there a deeper or more surprising implication that readers should take away?
- As mentioned above, I believe this theoretical result deserves more space, ideally supported by additional experiments.
- Can the regularization parameter λ be predicted from properties of the data? Is there a data-dependent heuristic that helps select λ? My intuition is that λ is the key factor controlling the final network structure—is that accurate?
- How sensitive is the final structure to initialization? Do different random seeds produce substantially different architectures, and if so, how different are they in size and performance?
- What happens when fan-in and fan-out regularizers are combined? Do you still obtain the same theoretical guarantee?

Additional questions:
1) Why do you state that adding zero units changes the value of the regularizer? For example, would an L2 norm change if you add parameters that are identically zero?
2) You define “zero units” as having either the fan-in or the fan-out weights equal to zero. I believe you mean that both fan-in and fan-out weights must be zero; otherwise, removing the unit would not preserve the function f. This point should be clarified.

I have updated my rating to 7, with the hope that the authors will address the comments above.
