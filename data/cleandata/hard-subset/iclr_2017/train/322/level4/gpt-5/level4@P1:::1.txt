Summary of contributions
- The paper introduces nonparametric neural networks, a framework that adapts layer widths during a single training run by repeatedly adding zero units and removing redundant units via an ℓ2 fan-in (or fan-out) group penalty. The method relies on self-similar nonlinearities (e.g., ReLU) to avoid rescaling issues and includes a theoretical result (Theorem 1) showing that, under ℓp fan-in/out regularization, the training objective attains a global minimum at finite dimensionality.
- The authors propose CapNorm, a variant of batch normalization that avoids canceling the effect of the weight penalty by capping the variance scaling at 1 and eliminating learned affine parameters.
- They introduce AdaRad, an optimizer that decomposes each fan-in gradient into parallel (radial) and orthogonal (angular) components, normalizes only the angular part, and updates in radial–angular coordinates. This is designed to let new units learn despite weak loss gradients while preserving the shrinkage dynamics induced by the ℓ2 group penalty.
- Experiments on MNIST, rectangles-images, and convex, plus a large OpenML “poker” dataset, demonstrate that the approach often finds compact architectures and can match or outperform parametric baselines of the same size; however, performance is weaker on convex.

Decision: Weak Accept
- Reason 1: The paper tackles an important problem—automating architecture size selection during a single training run—and offers a principled, novel combination of theory (finite minimizer under group penalties), optimization (AdaRad), and normalization (CapNorm).
- Reason 2: Empirical results are promising on two of three standard datasets and on a large-scale dataset, suggesting practical value, although some gaps remain in runtime analysis and understanding failure modes (e.g., convex).

Supporting arguments
- Specific problem: The work targets model selection over network width without expensive black-box searches that retrain from scratch. This aligns well with the community’s interest in resource-efficient architecture discovery.
- Motivation and placement: The approach is well motivated relative to pruning and black-box hyperparameter optimization. Unlike pruning methods that assume a high-performing seed model, this framework grows networks from scratch. The conceptual link to “f-equivalence” and zero-unit insertion/removal is clear and connects to prior dynamic growth ideas while providing stronger theoretical grounding.
- Claims vs. support:
  - Theoretical claim: Theorem 1 is technically sound under the stated assumptions and addresses a key worry—unbounded growth. The proposition about self-similar nonlinearities justifying a single λ across layers is also well argued.
  - Algorithmic claim: AdaRad’s radial/angular decomposition is plausible for stabilizing learning of new units without undermining shrinkage. However, while the cost analysis argues better scaling than RMSprop in terms of caches and some operations, empirical wall-clock comparisons are missing.
  - Empirical claim: On MNIST and rectangles-images, nonparametric nets outperform same-size parametric baselines and are competitive with broader searches; on poker, the approach performs very well; on convex, the method underperforms, reflecting a limitation that should be better understood. The methodology for selecting representatives and conducting parametric baselines is carefully described, which is appreciated.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions; not all are decision-critical)
- Runtime/complexity: Please quantify the additional computational overhead of AdaRad. The per-fan-in decomposition into parallel and orthogonal components and rotations in radial–angular coordinates likely add nontrivial cost. Providing wall-clock time, FLOPs, or throughput comparisons against SGD, RMSprop, and Adam, for both parametric and nonparametric settings, would greatly strengthen the practical case.
- Fairness and ablations:
  - Include ablations isolating AdaRad vs. standard optimizers, and CapNorm vs. BatchNorm, ideally on the same final architectures. This would clarify how much each component contributes.
  - Consider retraining the final architectures found by the nonparametric procedure as fixed parametric networks under standard training to measure whether the benefit is due to the discovered size or to the training dynamics.
- Sensitivity and schedules: Report sensitivity to the unit addition rate and frequency (ν, νfreq), and to αr/αφ. These hyperparameters control survival time and learning speed and may strongly affect results and the convex failure case.
- Convex dataset failure mode: Provide analysis explaining why the method converges to undersized models on convex (e.g., interaction between CapNorm and ℓ2 shrinkage, weak loss gradients for new units, or feature scale issues in deeper layers). A controlled study varying λ, αr, φ-schedules, and the use of fan-in vs. fan-out penalties would be informative.
- Relation to “random search” characterization: While the approach samples new units randomly, it is guided by gradient-based training and structured shrinkage. Clarifying how this differs theoretically and practically from random search over discrete widths would preempt misunderstandings.

Questions for the authors
1. Computational overhead: What is the asymptotic and practical (wall-clock) overhead of AdaRad per iteration compared to SGD/RMSprop/Adam? Can you provide timing results on the reported datasets, including memory usage?
2. Gradient decomposition details: How do you implement the per-fan-in orthogonal/parallel decomposition efficiently on GPU/CPU? Are there numerically sensitive cases (e.g., near-zero fan-in norms) and how are they handled?
3. CapNorm vs. BatchNorm: How much does CapNorm alone contribute to performance compared to standard BatchNorm (without affine parameters) when used with your regularizer? Any failure cases where capping harms optimization?
4. Undersizing on convex: Why does the method settle on small networks there? Does tuning αr (i.e., unit survival time), ν/νfreq, or using fan-out regularization alleviate this? Have you tried p=1 (sparser weights) or different initial fan-in scales?
5. Role of self-similarity: If ReLU is replaced with tanh/ELU/LeakyReLU, how do results change? Does the theory or practice break down without strict self-similarity?
6. Stability of discovered sizes: If you reinitialize and retrain the fixed architectures obtained by the nonparametric procedure, do you recover similar accuracy and is the optimal size stable across seeds?
7. Comparison to pruning: How does your method compare, in final accuracy and total training time, to a strong train-then-prune pipeline on the same tasks?

Overall, this is a thoughtful and ambitious paper addressing a timely problem with a coherent theoretical and algorithmic package. Providing concrete runtime evidence and deeper analysis of the convex case would significantly strengthen it.
