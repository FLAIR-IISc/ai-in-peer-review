Paper proposes on-the-fly addition/removal of units with group-sparsity regularization and proves the empirical objective attains a finite-size minimum; experiments show compact models with comparable performance to baselines.

Decision: Reject.

Key reasons for rejection: insufficient empirical comparison to strong pruning/compression baselines; unresolved hyperparameter burden and training-time/scalability concerns.

Soundness/motivation: theoretical finite-minimum result is compelling but doesn’t guarantee generalization or practical learned size.

Hyperparameterization issue: several new hyperparameters (unit-add rate, regularization strength/decay) implicitly determine final size and may not reduce tuning burden.

Missing baselines: no direct comparisons to post-training pruning or structured sparsity methods to assess competitiveness.

Training-time concerns: size-control knobs affect optimization dynamics and runtime, potentially making training slower than fixed-width alternatives; scalability to large networks is unaddressed.

Scope of experiments: experiments are limited and do not establish practicality or robustness at larger scales.

Recommendation: include direct comparisons to pruning/compression baselines reporting accuracy, parameter count, latency, and memory.

Recommendation: provide guidance/heuristics and sensitivity analysis for new hyperparameters, or demonstrate partial automation.

Recommendation: report wall-clock training time and compute cost, and separate effects of size-setting hyperparameters from optimization hyperparameters.

Recommendation: run larger-scale experiments or present strong evidence-based scalability arguments.

Recommendation: clarify when the method yields smaller or better-accuracy networks than fixed-width training followed by shrinking.

Questions for authors: how sensitive are final size and accuracy to unit-add schedules and regularization; can you provide default/adaptive rules?

Questions for authors: how does the method empirically compare to magnitude/structured pruning in accuracy, parameter count, and test-time efficiency?

Questions for authors: what is the wall-clock overhead versus a well-tuned fixed-width model under typical practitioner effort?

Questions for authors: how does the approach scale to much larger networks/datasets and are there failure modes or stabilizing strategies?

Questions for authors: can hyperparameters controlling final size be decoupled from those governing optimization speed?
