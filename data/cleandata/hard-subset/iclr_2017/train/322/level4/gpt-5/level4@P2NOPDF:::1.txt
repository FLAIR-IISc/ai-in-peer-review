This paper presents a nonparametric neural network that learns its own size during training. The core idea is to randomly insert zero-valued units and apply a sparsity-inducing regularizer that drives irrelevant weights to zero, effectively pruning them. Conceptually, this resembles a randomized search over discrete architectures guided by sparsity. The problem is important, and the empirical findings are intriguing. My main comments are:

- Computational complexity: What is the additional computational overhead of the proposed method? Decomposing each unit’s fan-in weights into parallel and orthogonal components, and reparameterizing them in radial–angular coordinates, seems potentially costly. Please quantify the extra operations per update/epoch relative to a standard fixed-architecture network. It would be helpful to discuss how the cost scales with depth/width and with the frequency of unit insertions, and to provide wall-clock comparisons. Including runtime experiments (e.g., time to reach a target accuracy) would significantly strengthen the evaluation.

- Behavior on the convex dataset: The nonparametric models appear to shrink to very small architectures on the convex dataset and perform worse than fixed-size baselines. Do you have an explanation for this behavior? Any insight into whether it is due to underfitting from strong regularization, the chosen parameterization, or the interaction between insertion and pruning would be valuable. Analyses that vary the sparsity strength, insertion rate, or minimum layer width, and that report learning curves and final selected sizes, could clarify whether this is an inherent limitation or a tuning issue.
