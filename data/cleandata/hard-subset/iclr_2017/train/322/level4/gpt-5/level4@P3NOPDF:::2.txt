This paper explores dynamic capacity allocation by adding and removing units during training. I agree with Reviewer 2 that this is an interesting and timely direction: letting the model grow or shrink in response to data and task demands, rather than fixing capacity via user prior, has clear practical appeal.

The theoretical contribution—that with fan-in or fan-out regularization the error function attains its optimum at a finite number of parameters—is compelling. It suggests the network will not grow without bound to perfectly overfit. The result evokes classical sparsity-inducing penalties such as Lasso and Elastic Net. However, the paper would benefit from more intuition and discussion around this theorem. A concise, plain-English statement of the key insight, why it holds, and what structural sparsity it promotes (weights, units, or groups) would improve accessibility. Consider trimming some related-work discussion to make room for this.

Empirical evaluation and questions
1) Dynamic vs. fixed architecture at matched final size:
- Please include a clear head-to-head comparison where the final architecture discovered by your method is then trained from scratch as a fixed network with the same number of units per layer and the same training budget. Does the nonparametric training outperform this matched baseline? If so, what mechanism explains the gain—better allocation of units during training, improved optimization due to stagewise growth/pruning, or a regularization effect?
- Clarify whether your approach saves parameters or compute versus comparable fixed models, and quantify performance–memory/compute trade-offs.

I understand Figure 2 aims at this comparison, but it is difficult to parse. In some cases nonparametric nets do better, in others worse. Even with mixed outcomes, the method could be valuable for structure discovery, but please clarify:
- Why can the nonparametric model sometimes outperform a parametric model with the same final architecture trained from scratch? If the final net is known, in what sense is the adaptive path beneficial? Ablations (e.g., grow-only, prune-only; delayed growth; different schedules) could help isolate the effect.

2) Theorem 1: meaning, scope, and implications:
- Please articulate the theorem’s intuition in plain English. What specific property of the fan-in/out penalties prevents unbounded growth? How does this relate to group sparsity at the unit level?
- Is the regularization strength λ the principal driver of the final architecture? Provide a sensitivity analysis and practical guidance for choosing λ (e.g., ranges, proxies from data complexity, or validation heuristics). Can any data-dependent quantity help set λ a priori?
- How dependent is the final structure on initialization? Report variability across seeds: distribution of final sizes, architectures, and performance.
- What happens when fan-in and fan-out regularizers are combined? Does the finite-parameter optimality result still hold under their combination, and under what conditions?
- If applicable, discuss whether the theorem extends beyond the specific loss/activation assumptions used, and note any limitations.

Clarifications and minor issues
- Clarify the statement that “adding zero units changes the regularizer value.” For standard L2 penalties, adding strictly zero weights does not change the norm. If your regularizer counts units or uses group norms that introduce nonzero contributions even when some weights are zero, please explain precisely what term changes and why.
- Definition of a “zero unit”: The text suggests a unit is removable if either its fan-in or its fan-out is zero. But to remove a unit without changing f, both its incoming and outgoing weights must be zero (or the unit must be functionally inert via a constant). Please refine this definition and the corresponding argument.
- Figure presentation: Improve Figure 2 legibility (larger fonts, clearer legends, consistent y-axes, error bars over multiple runs). Ensure matched training budgets when comparing parametric and nonparametric models.
- Report the computational overhead of growth/pruning relative to fixed-architecture training (wall-clock time, memory fragmentation, GPU efficiency) to contextualize the practical trade-offs.
- Where possible, add diagnostics showing how units are added/removed over time and where they concentrate across layers; this will help readers understand the discovered structure.

Overall assessment
- Strengths: Interesting problem; principled regularization that induces finite-size optima; promising results suggesting the method can discover useful architectures.
- Weaknesses: Limited intuition and discussion around the main theorem; unclear experimental comparisons to fixed architectures at matched size; figure clarity; open questions about λ selection, stability across initializations, and combined regularization.

Despite these concerns, I find the direction valuable and the theoretical result appealing. With stronger empirical baselines, clearer presentation, and deeper discussion of Theorem 1 and its implications, this work could make a solid contribution.

Rating: 7 (accept), contingent on addressing the above comments.
