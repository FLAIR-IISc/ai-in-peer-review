Summary
The paper introduces nonparametric neural networks, a framework that selects network width during a single training run by dynamically adding units and pruning them via a group L2 (fan-in or fan-out) penalty. The authors prove that, under this regularization, a global minimum occurs at finite width. They propose two practical components to make this feasible: (i) CapNorm, a capped variant of batch normalization compatible with shrinkage-based regularization, and (ii) AdaRad, an optimizer that decomposes gradients into radial (length-changing) and angular (direction-changing) components at the level of each unit’s fan-in, normalizing only the angular component so new units can learn before shrinkage removes them. Experiments on MNIST, rectangles-images, convex, and a large OpenML “poker” dataset show that the approach can find compact, competitive architectures and, in some cases, outperform parametric networks of the same size and strong tuned baselines. The contribution is timely, well-motivated, and supported by both theory and empirical results.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- The paper tackles an important problem—automated width selection during training—and provides a sound, unified approach (theory + algorithm) rather than a black-box outer loop.
- The proposed training machinery (CapNorm and AdaRad) is novel, well-motivated, and demonstrably effective on multiple datasets, including a large-scale case.

Supporting arguments
- The theoretical guarantee that, with a fan-in or fan-out group penalty, the objective has a finite-width global minimizer is valuable and addresses concerns about unbounded growth.
- The training pipeline is coherent: self-similar activations mitigate scale issues; CapNorm avoids nullifying the effect of the regularizer; AdaRad’s angular/radial decomposition addresses the tension between normalization and shrinkage.
- Empirically, nonparametric networks match or outperform same-size parametric baselines on MNIST and rectangles-images and scale well to the “poker” dataset, indicating practicality beyond toy settings.
- The paper is well-situated in the literature (pruning/growth, hyperparameter optimization, Bayesian model selection) and clearly explains how the approach differs from post-hoc pruning or black-box search.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not central to the accept decision)
- Quantify computational overhead. While the text claims better scaling than RMSprop in memory and some operations, the per-unit orthogonal/parallel decomposition and radial–angular updates introduce extra cost. Please report:
  - Operation counts per forward/backward pass vs SGD/Adam/RMSprop.
  - Memory footprint.
  - Wall-clock time per epoch and to a target validation error on representative datasets.
- Provide ablations isolating contributions:
  - AdaRad vs standard optimizers (SGD, RMSprop, Adam), holding CapNorm and the add/prune scheme fixed.
  - CapNorm vs BatchNorm and vs no normalization (with and without regularization).
  - Effect of using fan-in vs fan-out regularization and p=1 vs p=2.
- Sensitivity analyses:
  - Regularization strength λ, unit addition rate/schedule (ν, νfreq), and the two step sizes (αr, αφ).
  - Initialization scale for new units (does a different fan-in norm change survival/utility?).
- Comparisons to growth/pruning baselines that add and remove units (e.g., dynamic network surgery, structured sparsity methods), beyond parametric fixed-width baselines.
- Clarify reproducibility details and consider releasing code; the training procedure involves several interacting components and schedules.

Questions for the authors
- Computational complexity and runtime:
  - What is the precise per-iteration cost of AdaRad relative to SGD/RMSprop/Adam (in big-O and measured FLOPs/epoch)? How does the per-unit decomposition and rotation scale in practice with width and depth?
  - Please include wall-clock comparisons to reach a fixed validation error on MNIST and rectangles-images.
- Failure case on convex:
  - You observe that nonparametric networks converge to too-small models and underperform. Do you have diagnostics indicating whether this is driven by λ being too large, the unit addition schedule, CapNorm behavior (e.g., σ < 1 too early), or AdaRad’s radial step size?
  - Would annealing λ, delaying shrinkage (smaller αr or warm-up), or increasing ν mitigate this? Any results with alternative regularizers (fan-out, p=1) on convex?
- Role of CapNorm:
  - Can you report performance vs standard BatchNorm when correcting for the regularizer interaction (e.g., freezing BN scales vs learned scales)? Does CapNorm’s “cap at 1” ever limit representation and slow learning?
- Role of AdaRad:
  - If you retain your add/prune mechanism but swap AdaRad for Adam or RMSprop while keeping shrinkage unnormalized (or normalizing only the orthogonal component), how much accuracy do you lose? Any convergence or stability issues?
- Theory and assumptions:
  - ReLU is non-differentiable at zero; your proof relies on directional derivatives. Could you briefly restate how the linearized-network argument covers ReLU in practice?
  - Proposition 1 suggests per-layer λ can be consolidated when using self-similar activations. In experiments, did a single λ suffice across layers, or did you find benefits to layer-wise tuning?

Overall, the paper presents a compelling, principled approach to learning network width within a single training run, with novel optimizer and normalization components and a useful theoretical guarantee. Addressing the questions above—especially the computational overhead and the convex failure mode—would further strengthen the work.
