- Proposes a method to adaptively grow and prune network units during training using group sparsity; proves loss is minimized by a finite network.
- Mechanism is conceptually clear, elegant, and well grounded in group sparsity.
- Theoretical contribution provides a clean justification for adaptive control of model size.
- Method often produces smaller networks with comparable accuracy, useful for constrained inference.
- Introduces additional hyperparameters (growth rate, sparsity/decay strength) that may still govern model size and tuning burden.
- No comparisons to strong post-training compression baselines (pruning, quantization, low-rank factorization).
- Training efficiency and scalability are unclear; hyperparameters affect training time and method may be slower than fixed architectures.
- Experiments are informative but limited in scope and do not demonstrate robustness across larger/diverse tasks.
- Suggest adding state-of-the-art compression baselines and reporting accuracy, model size, and inference speed comparisons.
- Suggest reporting training cost metrics: wall-clock time, FLOPs, optimization steps, and overhead/frequency of unit add/remove.
- Suggest thorough hyperparameter sensitivity analysis and guidance on default settings for reproducibility.
- Suggest larger-scale validation on deeper networks and more complex benchmarks to test scalability.
- Suggest ablations isolating group sparsity, unit-addition policy, and removal criteria; address oscillatory add/remove behavior and stability.
- Suggest measuring real-world inference-time benefits (speed, memory, power) on representative hardware.
- Overall: promising, well-written work with solid theory but currently speculative due to insufficient empirical comparisons and scalability evidence.
