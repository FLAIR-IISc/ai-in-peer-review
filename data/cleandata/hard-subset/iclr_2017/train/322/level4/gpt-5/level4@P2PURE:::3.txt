This paper tackles the problem of allowing neural networks to adjust the number of units during training. The proposed mechanism is simple, elegant, and well motivated: units are added or removed during training, and a group-sparsity regularizer encourages entire units’ weights to shrink to zero so that units with zero input or output weights can be pruned. The main theoretical contribution shows that, with appropriate regularization, the loss is minimized by a network with a finite number of units. In practice, this does not guarantee the final model will avoid over- or underfitting, although the initial experiments suggest this is not an issue.

A potential advantage of methods that learn the number of units is reduced hyperparameter tuning. A drawback of this approach (and perhaps any similar approach) is that it does not truly solve that problem. The method still depends on several hyperparameters that implicitly control how many units emerge, including how often new units are added and how quickly weights decay to zero. It is unclear whether these hyperparameters are easier or harder to tune than those in standard approaches. To their credit, the authors do not claim to have made training easier, but it is somewhat disappointing that this appears not to be the case.

The authors emphasize that they can achieve comparable performance with fewer units than parametrically trained networks. This could be important, as smaller networks can reduce test-time latency, power consumption, and memory footprint—particularly on mobile devices. However, the paper does not include experimental comparisons to existing techniques for reducing the size of parametrically trained networks (e.g., pruning trained models), so it is unclear whether the proposed approach is competitive with state-of-the-art methods for network compression.

Another potential disadvantage is that the same hyperparameters govern both the number of units that appear and the training time. Consequently, training may be substantially slower than for a parametric approach with fixed hyperparameters. While many parametric approaches rely on grid search, in many cases prior experience makes hyperparameter selection relatively straightforward; the cost of grid search is not always incurred, whereas the slowness of the proposed approach may be inherent. The paper does not discuss how this will scale to much larger networks, raising concerns that the method may be impractical for large-scale models because training could be very slow.

Overall, the experiments are helpful and encouraging, but not comprehensive or wholly convincing. I would want to see results on much larger problems before concluding that the approach is practical and broadly useful.

In sum, this is an interesting and clearly written paper that makes a potentially useful point. The broader vision of networks that can grow and adapt through lifelong learning is inspiring, and work of this kind may be necessary to realize it. However, the current results remain fairly speculative.
