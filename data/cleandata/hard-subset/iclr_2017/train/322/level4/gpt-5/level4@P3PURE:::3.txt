This paper tackles the problem of allowing neural networks to change the number of units during training. The method is simple, elegant, and well motivated: units with zero input or output weights are added or removed during training, and a group sparsity regularizer encourages unit weights to go to zero. The main theoretical result shows that, with appropriate regularization, the loss is minimized by a network with a finite number of units. While this does not guarantee against over- or under-fitting in practice, initial experiments suggest this is not a significant issue.

A potential advantage of learning the number of units is reduced burden in hyperparameter tuning. However, this approach does not truly solve that problem. The network still has several hyperparameters that implicitly control the number of units that emerge, including how frequently new units are added and how quickly weights decay to zero. It is unclear whether these are easier or harder to tune than those in standard approaches. To the authors’ credit, they do not claim to make training easier, but it is somewhat disappointing that the method does not appear to reduce tuning effort.

The authors highlight that their approach can train networks with fewer units while achieving performance comparable to parametrically trained networks. This is potentially important, as smaller models can reduce test-time latency, power consumption, and memory footprint—especially relevant on mobile devices. However, the paper does not compare against existing techniques that reduce model size after parametric training (e.g., pruning), leaving it unclear whether the proposed method is competitive with the best current approaches for model compression.

Another concern is that the same hyperparameters govern both the number of units and the training time. This coupling could make training substantially slower than with a fixed-architecture, parametric approach. Although parametric methods sometimes require expensive grid searches, in many cases domain experience makes selecting hyperparameters relatively straightforward, so the cost of grid search is not always incurred. By contrast, the slowness of the proposed approach may be inherent. The paper also does not discuss how the method scales to much larger networks, raising concerns about practicality for large-scale settings due to potentially long training times.

Overall, the experiments are helpful and encouraging but not comprehensive or fully convincing. Demonstrations on substantially larger problems would be needed to establish the method’s practicality and broader utility.

This is an interesting and clearly written paper that makes a potentially useful contribution. The long-term vision of networks that can grow and adapt through lifelong learning is compelling, and this line of work may be an important step toward that goal. However, the current results remain somewhat speculative.
