This paper tackles the problem of allowing a network to change its number of units during training. The mechanism is simple, elegant, and well motivated: new zero-weight units are added and units whose incoming or outgoing weights are driven to zero are removed, while a group-sparsity regularizer encourages entire units to vanish. The main theoretical contribution shows that, with appropriate regularization, the objective is minimized by a network with finitely many units. Although this does not, by itself, guarantee freedom from over- or under-fitting, initial experiments suggest this is not a major issue in practice.

A potential advantage of learning the number of units is reduced hyperparameter tuning. However, this approach does not truly solve that problem. The network still depends on several hyperparameters that implicitly determine the final model size, such as how frequently new units are introduced and how strongly weights are shrunk toward zero. It is unclear whether these hyperparameters are easier or harder to tune than in standard, fixed-size models. To their credit, the authors do not claim to simplify training, but it is nonetheless disappointing that the burden of tuning is not clearly reduced.

The authors emphasize that their method achieves comparable performance with fewer units than parametrically trained networks. This could be important, as smaller models can reduce test-time latency, power consumption, and memory footprint—particularly relevant for mobile devices. However, there is no experimental comparison to existing techniques for shrinking parametrically trained networks (e.g., pruning or compression). Without such baselines, it is unclear whether the proposed approach is competitive with the best current methods for reducing trained model size.

Another potential drawback is that the same hyperparameters govern both the number of units that emerge and the training dynamics. As a result, training may be significantly slower than for fixed-size networks with tuned hyperparameters. While standard approaches sometimes require costly grid search, in many cases prior experience makes choosing hyperparameters relatively straightforward, so this cost is not always incurred. By contrast, the slowness of the proposed method may be intrinsic. The paper also does not discuss how training time scales to substantially larger architectures. I am concerned that the approach may be impractical for large-scale networks because of slow training.

Overall, the experiments are helpful and encouraging but not comprehensive or fully convincing. I would need to see results on much larger problems to be persuaded that the approach is practical and broadly useful.

In sum, this is an interesting and clearly written paper that makes a potentially valuable contribution. The broader vision—networks that grow and adapt through lifelong learning—is compelling, and work of this kind may be necessary to realize it. However, at present the empirical support remains speculative.
