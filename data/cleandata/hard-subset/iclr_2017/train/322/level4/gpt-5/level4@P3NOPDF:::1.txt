Summary:
The paper introduces a nonparametric neural network framework that learns the model size during training. New units are stochastically added as zero-initialized “candidates,” while a sparsity-inducing regularizer prunes irrelevant weights, effectively growing and shrinking the network as learning progresses. The approach targets an important problem—automatically adapting capacity—and the empirical results are promising.

Strengths:
- Addresses a relevant and challenging problem: selecting network size without extensive manual tuning.
- Conceptually simple mechanism (random addition + sparsity) that can be implemented within standard training loops.
- Empirical results indicate the method can discover compact architectures, suggesting potential gains in parameter efficiency.
- The proposed decomposition into radial–angular coordinates appears to provide a structured way to regularize fan-in weights.

Weaknesses / Questions:
1) Computational complexity and wall-clock overhead:
- The paper introduces additional steps, including decomposition of each fan-in weight vector into parallel/orthogonal components and a radial–angular transformation. These operations could add nontrivial overhead compared with a parametric baseline.
- Please quantify the per-update computational cost and memory footprint relative to a fixed-size network of comparable accuracy. For example:
  - Asymptotic cost per layer/unit for the decomposition and coordinate transform.
  - How cost scales with width and depth, and whether it increases linearly or superlinearly with the number of candidate units.
  - Any additional backpropagation complexity due to the chosen regularizer (e.g., group sparsity) and coordinate parameterization.
- Provide wall-clock training curves and epoch times on the main benchmarks. A simple and informative metric would be the total time to reach a target validation accuracy, as well as final time-to-convergence. Comparisons to:
  - A fixed architecture with the same final parameter count discovered by the nonparametric model.
  - A standard, well-tuned baseline of typical size for the dataset.

2) Behavior on convex datasets and underfitting:
- You report that the method tends to return very small models on a convex dataset and performs worse than parametric networks. Please provide insight into why capacity collapses in this regime. Is the regularizer overpowering the data signal, or is the unit-addition mechanism too conservative?
- Suggested analyses:
  - Sensitivity to the sparsity strength and schedule (e.g., delaying regularization, annealing λ).
  - Effect of the unit-addition frequency and criteria (purely random vs. performance-triggered).
  - Constraints that prevent premature pruning (e.g., a minimum width per layer, a warm-up period before pruning).
  - Diagnostics showing how width evolves over training and where performance deteriorates (training vs. validation gaps).
- If the issue is intrinsic (e.g., convex problems are well-solved by simple models but require certain features not easily captured by your growth dynamics), please discuss mitigation strategies or characterize where the approach should and should not be used.

3) Stability and variance:
- Because unit addition is stochastic, please report variance over multiple seeds in terms of final size, accuracy, and training time. How robust is the discovered architecture?

4) Comparisons and ablations:
- Position the method relative to related lines of work: network pruning (magnitude and structured pruning), dynamic growth/pruning methods, and architecture search (including simple width search). What advantages does your approach offer in terms of final size, accuracy, and compute?
- Include ablations for:
  - The specific regularizer used (e.g., L1 vs. group sparsity).
  - The coordinate parameterization (radial–angular) vs. a standard parameterization with equivalent regularization.
  - The unit-addition strategy (rate, placement, initialization).
- A comparison to a strong pruning baseline that starts from a moderately overparameterized model would clarify whether growing-then-pruning outperforms prune-only strategies at equal compute.

5) Theoretical and practical clarity:
- It would help to formalize the optimization objective under the coordinate transform and the conditions under which weights are driven exactly to zero.
- Discuss convergence behavior: does the method converge to a stationary point of a well-defined objective as units are added/removed? Are there guarantees that the architecture will not oscillate?
- Provide implementation details needed for reproducibility: frequency of unit addition, how many units per addition, initialization scheme, thresholds for pruning, and how these interact with batch normalization or other layers.

Clarity and presentation:
- The paper is generally clear, but the description of the parallel/orthogonal decomposition and radial–angular transformation could be made more concrete with pseudocode, computational cost annotations, and a small worked example.
- Figures or tables showing the evolution of layer widths over training would improve interpretability.

Overall assessment:
This is a timely and interesting contribution toward adaptive-capacity neural networks. The empirical results are encouraging, and the approach is conceptually appealing. However, the paper would be considerably strengthened by a thorough analysis of computational overhead (both asymptotic and wall-clock), a clearer explanation and mitigation strategy for the underperformance on convex datasets, and more extensive comparisons and ablations. With these additions, the work would present a more compelling case for practical adoption.
