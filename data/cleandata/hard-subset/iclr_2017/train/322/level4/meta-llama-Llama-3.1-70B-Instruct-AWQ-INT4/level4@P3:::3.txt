This paper addresses the problem of automatically determining the optimal size of a neural network for a given task without prior information. The authors propose a nonparametric neural network framework that allows the network to change the number of units during training. This is done by adding or removing units with zero input or output weights, while using a group sparsity norm for regularization to encourage unit weights to go to zero.

The main theoretical contribution is to show that with proper regularization, the loss is minimized by a network with a finite number of units. This result is proven through a sequence of lemmas, which provide bounds on the values of the weights and the gradients of the loss function. The proof is tedious but instructive, and it highlights the importance of the regularization term in preventing the network from growing indefinitely.

The authors also propose a novel training algorithm, called Adaptive Radial-Angular Gradient Descent (AdaRad), which is designed to work with the nonparametric neural network framework. AdaRad uses a combination of radial and angular updates to adjust the weights of the network, and it is shown to be effective in training nonparametric neural networks.

The experimental results are promising, and they show that nonparametric neural networks can achieve competitive performance with parametric neural networks on several benchmark datasets. The authors also demonstrate that nonparametric neural networks can be applied to large-scale datasets, and they achieve good performance on the poker dataset from OpenML.

However, there are some limitations to the current approach. One potential disadvantage is that the same hyperparameters control both the number of units that will appear in the network and the training time. This means that training might potentially be much slower for this approach than for a parametric approach with fixed hyperparameters. Additionally, the authors do not compare experimentally to existing approaches that attempt to reduce the size of parametrically trained networks, such as pruning trained networks.

Overall, I found this to be an interesting and clearly written paper that makes a potentially useful point. The overall vision of building networks that can grow and adapt through life-long learning is inspiring, and this type of work might be needed to realize such a vision. However, the current results remain pretty speculative, and more comprehensive experiments are needed to fully evaluate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
