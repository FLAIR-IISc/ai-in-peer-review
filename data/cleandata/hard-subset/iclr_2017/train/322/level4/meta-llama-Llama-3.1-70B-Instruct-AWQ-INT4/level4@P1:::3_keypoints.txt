- Paper proposes adding/removing units during training using zero-weight units and group-sparsity regularization to encourage unit removal.
- Main theoretical result: with proper regularization, the loss is minimized by a network with a finite number of units.
- Practical experiments suggest networks trained this way can use fewer units while achieving comparable performance to parametric networks.
- Approach could reduce test-time runtime, power and memory, which is valuable for mobile devices.
- Method does not eliminate hyperparameter tuning: several hyperparameters still implicitly control emergent unit count and dynamics.
- It is unclear whether these new hyperparameters are easier or harder to tune than standard ones.
- Authors did not compare experimentally to existing size-reduction methods (e.g., pruning), so competitiveness is unknown.
- The same hyperparameters influence both model size and training time, potentially making training much slower than fixed-parametric approaches.
- Concerns that the approach may not scale well to large networks and may be impractical for large-scale training.
- Experiments are limited to small-scale problems and are not comprehensive or fully convincing.
- Overall assessment: interesting, well-written, and promising for life-long adaptive networks, but currently speculative.
- Decision: Reject (for now) because practicality, scalability, and competitiveness are not demonstrated.
- Recommendations: add comparisons to pruning and other compression methods, provide scaling strategies, and include larger-scale experiments.
- Questions for authors: how will they address potential slowness, tune hyperparameters in practice, scale to larger networks, and compare to pruning-based methods?
