I agree with Reviewer 2 on the interesting aspect of the paper. The idea of dynamically removing or adding units is an intriguing direction, allowing a model to adapt and grow or shrink according to the problem and data requirements, rather than relying on prior knowledge.

The authors present an interesting theoretical result that proves the optimum of the error function can be achieved with a finite number of parameters under fan-out or fan-in regularization, preventing the network from growing indefinitely and overfitting the data. This concept bears resemblance to traditional approaches such as Lasso or Elastic Net, where regularization leads to sparse weights. I would have appreciated more intuition behind this theorem, as it seems somewhat expected, yet still warrants explanation. Providing more insight into this result could be achieved by allocating less space to prior work discussions and focusing more on the main result. Please see below for suggestions.

I have several additional comments:

1. A compelling experiment would be to demonstrate that a model with dynamically added or removed nodes can outperform a network with a fixed number of nodes (determined after complete learning), where the size and number of nodes per layer are predetermined. This would validate the efficiency of the proposed idea. The key question is whether this method optimizes performance versus memory by eliminating unnecessary nodes and replacing them with necessary ones. Although experiments in Figure 2 show mixed results, they are not entirely clear and require careful interpretation. In some cases, non-parametric networks perform better, while in others, they perform worse than parametric ones. Even in the latter case, the method's usefulness lies in its ability to discover the structure. However, I am unclear as to why non-parametric learning can sometimes outperform parametric learning when the final network is known in advance. Could you provide more insight into this?

2. I would appreciate a more in-depth discussion on the implications and meaning of Theorem 1. The theorem seems to be presented without sufficient explanation, and its significance is not entirely clear. Beyond the proof in the Appendix, what is the key takeaway from the theorem? What does it imply in simple terms? To me, the conclusion seems almost natural and obvious, but I may be missing a powerful insight. As I mentioned earlier, this theoretical result deserves more attention, accompanied by additional experiments to support it. For instance, can the regularizer parameter lambda be predicted based on the data? Is there a property in the data that can help guess the optimal lambda? My intuition suggests that lambda plays a crucial role in determining the final network structure. Is this correct? Furthermore, how much does the final network structure depend on initialization? Do different initializations with random weights result in distinct networks, and if so, how different are they? What happens when fan-in and fan-out regularizers are combined? Does the theoretical result still hold?

I also have a few additional questions:

1. Could you clarify why adding zero units changes the regularizer value? For example, does the L2 norm change when zero values are added?

2. Zero units are defined as having either fan-in or fan-out weights equal to zero. I believe you meant to say that both fan-in and fan-out weights must be zero; otherwise, removing the unit would alter the output f. This point requires further clarification.

I have revised my rating to 7, with the hope that the authors will address my comments above.
