This paper addresses the problem of dynamically adjusting the number of units in a network during training, which is achieved through a simple yet elegant approach. Units with zero input or output weights are added or removed during training, while a group sparsity norm is employed for regularization to encourage unit weights to approach zero. The primary theoretical contribution of this work is to demonstrate that, with proper regularization, the loss is minimized by a network with a finite number of units. Although this result does not guarantee that the resulting network will not overfit or underfit the training data, preliminary experiments suggest that this is not the case.

One potential advantage of approaches that learn the number of units to use in a network is the potential to alleviate the burden of hyperparameter tuning. However, one disadvantage of this approach, and possibly any similar approach, is that it does not fundamentally solve this problem. The network still has several hyperparameters that implicitly control the number of units that will emerge, including parameters that govern the frequency of new unit additions and the rate at which weights decay to zero. It is unclear whether these hyperparameters will be easier or harder to tune than those in standard approaches. In fairness, the authors do not claim to have simplified the training process, but it is somewhat disappointing that this does not appear to be the case.

The authors emphasize that they can train networks with fewer units to achieve comparable performance to parametrically trained networks. This is potentially significant, as smaller networks can reduce runtime, power consumption, and memory footprint during testing, which is particularly important for mobile devices. However, the authors do not experimentally compare their approach to existing methods that aim to reduce the size of parametrically trained networks (e.g., by pruning trained networks), making it unclear whether their approach is competitive with the best current methods for reducing the size of trained networks.

Another potential drawback of the proposed approach is that the same hyperparameters control both the number of units that will appear in the network and the training time. Consequently, training may be significantly slower with this approach than with a parametric approach that uses fixed hyperparameters. Although many parametric approaches require methods like grid search to select hyperparameters, which can be time-consuming, experience with similar problems can often make hyperparameter selection relatively straightforward. This means that the cost of grid search is not always incurred, but the slowness of the authors' approach may be inherent. The authors do not discuss how this issue will scale when training much larger networks, raising concerns about the practicality of this approach for large-scale networks, where training may be very slow.

In general, the experiments are helpful and encouraging but not comprehensive or entirely convincing. Further experiments on larger problems would be necessary to demonstrate the practicality and widespread applicability of this approach. Overall, this paper is interesting and clearly written, making a potentially useful point. The vision of building networks that can grow and adapt through lifelong learning is inspiring, and this type of work may be necessary to realize such a vision. However, the current results remain somewhat speculative.
