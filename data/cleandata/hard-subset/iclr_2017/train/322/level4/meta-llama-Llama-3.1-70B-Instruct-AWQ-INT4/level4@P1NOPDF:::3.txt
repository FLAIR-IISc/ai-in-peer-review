This paper addresses the problem of dynamically adjusting the number of units in a network during training, proposing a simple yet elegant solution. Units with zero input or output weights are added or removed during training, while a group sparsity norm is employed for regularization to encourage unit weights to approach zero. The primary theoretical contribution lies in demonstrating that, with proper regularization, the loss is minimized by a network with a finite number of units. Although this result does not guarantee that the resulting network will not over- or under-fit the training data, initial experiments suggest that this is not typically the case.

One potential advantage of approaches that learn the number of units to use in a network is the alleviation of hyperparameter tuning burdens. However, a disadvantage of this approach (and possibly any such approach) is that it does not fundamentally resolve this problem. The network still relies on several hyperparameters that implicitly control the number of units that emerge, including parameters governing the frequency of new unit additions and the rate at which weights decay to zero. It is unclear whether these hyperparameters will be easier or harder to tune than those in standard approaches. While the authors do not claim to have simplified training, it is somewhat disappointing that this does not appear to be the case.

The authors emphasize their ability to train networks that utilize fewer units to achieve comparable performance to parametrically trained networks. This is potentially significant, as smaller networks can reduce runtime, power consumption, and memory footprint during testing, which is particularly important for mobile devices. However, the authors do not experimentally compare their approach to existing methods that aim to reduce the size of parametrically trained networks (e.g., by pruning trained networks), making it unclear whether their approach is competitive with the best current methods for reducing network size.

Another potential drawback of the proposed approach is that the same hyperparameters control both the number of units in the network and the training time. Consequently, training may be significantly slower with this approach than with a parametric approach using fixed hyperparameters. Although many parametric approaches require time-consuming methods like grid search to select hyperparameters, experience with similar problems can often facilitate relatively easy hyperparameter selection. This means that the cost of grid search is not always incurred, whereas the slowness of the authors' approach may be inherent. The authors do not discuss how this issue will scale when training much larger networks, raising concerns about the practicality of this approach for large-scale networks due to potentially slow training times.

In general, the experiments are helpful and encouraging but not comprehensive or entirely convincing. Further experiments on much larger problems would be necessary to convince me of the practicality and broad applicability of this approach. 

Overall, I found this to be an interesting and clearly written paper that makes a potentially useful point. The vision of building networks that can adapt and grow through lifelong learning is inspiring, and this type of work may be essential to realizing such a vision. However, the current results remain somewhat speculative.
