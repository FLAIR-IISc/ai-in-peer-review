- Proposes dynamically adding/removing units during training using zeroed input/output weights and group sparsity regularization.  
- Theoretical result: proper regularization yields a loss-minimizing network with a finite number of units.  
- The theory does not guarantee avoidance of overfitting or underfitting, though initial experiments are encouraging.  
- Potential benefit: reduces the need to fix network size a priori, potentially easing hyperparameter tuning.  
- Limitation: method still depends on hyperparameters (unit-addition frequency, weight-decay rate) that implicitly control unit count.  
- Authors acknowledge they have not eliminated hyperparameter tuning complexity.  
- Empirical advantage: can train smaller networks with comparable performance, lowering runtime, power, and memory at test time.  
- Missing experimental comparison to established size-reduction methods (e.g., pruning), so competitiveness is unclear.  
- Drawback: same hyperparameters affect both network size and training time, possibly leading to much slower training.  
- No discussion of scaling behavior to large networks, raising practicality concerns for large-scale settings.  
- Experiments are encouraging but limited; more comprehensive, larger-scale evaluations are needed.  
- Paper is clearly written and inspiring for lifelong/adaptive learning, but results remain preliminary and speculative.
