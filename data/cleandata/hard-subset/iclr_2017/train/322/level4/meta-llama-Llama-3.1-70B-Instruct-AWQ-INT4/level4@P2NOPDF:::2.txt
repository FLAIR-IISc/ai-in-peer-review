I agree with Reviewer 2 that the idea of dynamically adding or removing units in a neural network is an interesting direction, as it allows the model to adapt to the problem and data without relying on user prior knowledge. The authors provide a theoretical result that proves the optimum of the error function can be achieved with a finite number of parameters under fan-out or fan-in regularization, preventing the network from growing indefinitely and overfitting the data.

This result bears some resemblance to traditional approaches like Lasso or Elastic Net, where regularization leads to sparse weights. I would have appreciated more intuition behind this theorem, as it seems somewhat expected. Allocating more space to discuss and study the main result, potentially at the expense of prior work, would have been beneficial. I elaborate on this point below.

I have several comments to make:

1. A compelling experiment would be to compare the performance of a model with dynamically added or removed nodes to a model with a fixed number of nodes, demonstrating the efficiency of the proposed method. This would show whether the approach can optimize performance while reducing unnecessary nodes and replacing them with needed ones. Although Figure 2 presents some experiments along these lines, the results are mixed, and the figure could be clearer. However, even in cases where the non-parametric nets perform worse, the method still offers value in discovering the structure.

What is not entirely clear is why the non-parametric version of learning can sometimes outperform the parametric version, even when the final network is known in advance. Further insight into this phenomenon would be helpful.

2. I believe Theorem 1 warrants more discussion on its implications and meaning. Beyond the proof in the Appendix, what is the key insight of the theorem? In plain English, what does it convey? While the conclusion may seem natural, I wonder if there is a more profound insight. As I mentioned earlier, this theoretical result deserves more space, accompanied by additional experiments to support it.

For instance, can the regularizer parameter lambda be predicted based on the data? Is there a property in the data that can help guess the correct lambda? I suspect that lambda is crucial in determining the final network structure. Is this accurate?

How much does the final network structure depend on initialization? Do different random weights lead to different networks? How distinct are they?

What happens when fan-in and fan-out regularizers are combined? Does the theoretical result still hold?

I have a few additional questions:

1. Could you clarify why adding zero units changes the regularizer value? For example, does the L2 norm change when adding zero values?
2. The definition of zero units as having either fan-in or fan-out weights being zero seems incomplete. I think you meant to say that both fan-in and fan-out weights must be zero to remove the unit without affecting the output f. This point should be clarified.

In light of these comments, I have revised my rating to 7, hoping that the authors will address these points.
