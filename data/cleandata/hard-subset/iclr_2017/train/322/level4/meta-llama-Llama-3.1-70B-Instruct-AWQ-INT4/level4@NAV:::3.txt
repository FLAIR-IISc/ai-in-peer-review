This paper tackles the issue of dynamically adjusting the number of units in a network during training. The authors propose a straightforward yet effective solution, where units with negligible input or output weights are added or removed, and a group sparsity norm is employed for regularization to encourage unit weights to approach zero. The primary theoretical contribution lies in demonstrating that, with suitable regularization, the loss function can be minimized by a network with a finite number of units. While this result does not guarantee optimal generalization in practice, preliminary experiments suggest that over- or under-fitting may not be a significant concern.

One potential benefit of this approach is the reduced need for hyperparameter tuning, as the network can adaptively determine the optimal number of units. However, this advantage is partially offset by the introduction of new hyperparameters that implicitly control unit growth and weight decay. It is unclear whether these hyperparameters will be easier to tune than those in traditional approaches. Although the authors do not claim to have simplified the training process, it is somewhat disappointing that this does not appear to be the case.

The authors highlight the ability to train networks with fewer units while maintaining comparable performance to parametrically trained networks. This is significant, as smaller networks can lead to reduced runtime, power consumption, and memory footprint, particularly on mobile devices. Nevertheless, the authors do not experimentally compare their approach to existing methods for reducing network size, such as pruning, making it uncertain whether their approach is competitive with state-of-the-art methods.

A potential drawback of this approach is that the same hyperparameters control both unit growth and training time. Consequently, training may be significantly slower than with traditional parametric approaches. While many parametric methods require time-consuming hyperparameter tuning, experience with similar problems can often simplify this process. However, the authors do not discuss how this issue will scale with larger networks, raising concerns about the practicality of their approach for large-scale networks.

The experiments are encouraging but not exhaustive or entirely convincing. Further experiments on larger problems are needed to demonstrate the practicality and usefulness of this approach. Overall, this paper presents an interesting and well-written idea that makes a potentially valuable contribution. The vision of building adaptive networks through lifelong learning is inspiring, and this type of research may be necessary to achieve this goal. However, the current results are still speculative and require further validation.
