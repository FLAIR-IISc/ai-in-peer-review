I concur with Reviewer 2's assessment of the paper's interesting aspects. The concept of dynamically adding or removing units to enable a model to adapt to the problem and data requirements, rather than relying on prior knowledge, is a promising direction.

The authors present a notable theoretical finding, demonstrating that under fan out or fan in regularization, the error function's optimum is achieved with a finite number of parameters, thereby preventing the network from growing indefinitely and overfitting the data. This echoes traditional approaches like Lasso or Elastic Net, where regularization yields sparse weights. However, I would have appreciated more intuitive explanations for this theorem, which, although expected, deserves more discussion. Allocating less space to prior work and more to the main result could enhance the paper.

I have several additional comments:

1. A compelling experiment would be to compare the performance of a model with dynamically added or removed nodes to a network with a fixed number of nodes. This would demonstrate the efficiency of the proposed method, particularly in optimizing performance versus memory. Although Figure 2 presents mixed results, it is unclear and warrants further clarification. I am intrigued by the method's ability to discover structure, even when the non-parametric nets perform worse than the parametric ones. However, I am unclear on why the non-parametric version sometimes outperforms the parametric version when the final network is known in advance.

2. I suggest providing a more in-depth discussion of Theorem 1's implications and meaning. The theorem appears to be presented without sufficient context, and its key insight is not immediately apparent. Beyond the proof, what is the theorem's significance in plain English? Is there a powerful insight that is not immediately obvious? I believe this theoretical result merits more space, accompanied by additional experiments to substantiate its claims. For instance, can the regularizer parameter lambda be predicted based on the data, or is there a property that can help guess the optimal lambda? My intuition suggests that lambda is crucial in determining the final network structure.

Additional questions and clarifications:

1. Could you explain why adding zero units affects the regularizer value? For example, does the L2 norm change when zero values are added?

2. The definition of zero units as having either fan in or fan out weights being zero is unclear. I assume you meant that both fan in and fan out weights must be zero to remove the unit without altering the output f. This requires further clarification.

I have revised my rating to 7, with the expectation that the authors will address these comments and provide additional insights.
