This paper addresses the problem of dynamically adjusting the number of units in a network during training, which is achieved through a simple yet elegant approach. Units with zero input or output weights are added or removed during training, while a group sparsity norm is employed for regularization to encourage unit weights to approach zero. The primary theoretical contribution of this work is demonstrating that, with proper regularization, the loss is minimized by a network with a finite number of units. Although this result does not guarantee that the resulting network will not overfit or underfit the training data, initial experiments suggest that this is not the case.

One potential advantage of approaches that learn the number of units in a network is the alleviation of hyperparameter tuning burdens. However, a disadvantage of this approach is that it does not entirely resolve this problem, as the network still relies on several hyperparameters that implicitly control the number of units, including parameters governing the frequency of new unit additions and the rate at which weights decay to zero. It is unclear whether these hyperparameters will be easier or harder to tune than those in standard approaches. The authors acknowledge this limitation and do not claim to have simplified the training process.

The authors highlight their ability to train networks with fewer units that achieve comparable performance to parametrically trained networks. This is a significant advantage, as smaller networks can reduce runtime, power consumption, and memory footprint during testing, particularly on mobile devices. Nevertheless, the authors do not experimentally compare their approach to existing methods that reduce the size of parametrically trained networks, such as pruning trained networks. Therefore, it is uncertain whether this approach is competitive with state-of-the-art methods for reducing network size.

Another potential drawback of the proposed approach is that the same hyperparameters control both the number of units in the network and the training time. Consequently, training may be significantly slower for this approach than for parametric approaches with fixed hyperparameters. Although many parametric approaches require time-consuming methods like grid search to choose hyperparameters, experience with similar problems can facilitate the selection of hyperparameters. The authors do not discuss how this issue will scale for larger networks, raising concerns about the practicality of this approach for large-scale networks, where training may be excessively slow.

The experiments presented in the paper are helpful and encouraging but not comprehensive or entirely convincing. Further experiments on larger problems are necessary to demonstrate the practicality and usefulness of this approach. Overall, this paper is interesting and clearly written, presenting a potentially useful concept. The vision of building networks that can adapt and grow through lifelong learning is inspiring, and this type of research may be essential for realizing such a vision. However, the current results remain speculative, and more work is needed to establish the efficacy of this approach.
