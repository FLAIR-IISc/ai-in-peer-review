I agree with Reviewer 2 that the paper's central idea of dynamically adding or removing units in a neural network is intriguing. This approach allows the model to adapt to the problem and data requirements, rather than relying on user prior knowledge.

The authors present an interesting theoretical result, which proves that under fan-out or fan-in regularization, the optimum of the error function is achieved with a finite number of parameters. This means that the network does not grow indefinitely, thereby avoiding overfitting. This concept bears some resemblance to traditional approaches like Lasso or Elastic Net, where regularization produces sparse weights. I would have appreciated more intuitive explanations for this theorem, as it seems somewhat expected. Allocating more space to discuss and study the main result, potentially at the expense of prior work discussion, would enhance the paper.

I have several additional comments and suggestions:

1. A compelling experiment would be to demonstrate that a model with dynamically added or removed nodes can outperform a network with a fixed number of nodes, even after complete learning. This would showcase the efficiency of the proposed method. It would be interesting to see whether the method optimizes performance versus memory by replacing unnecessary nodes with needed ones. Although experiments in Figure 2 provide mixed results, they are not entirely clear. Upon closer inspection, it appears that non-parametric nets sometimes outperform and sometimes underperform parametric ones. Even in the latter case, the method's usefulness in discovering the structure is evident. However, I do not fully understand why the non-parametric version of learning can sometimes outperform the parametric version, even when the final network is known in advance. Further insight into this phenomenon would be beneficial.

2. I believe Theorem 1 deserves more in-depth discussion. Beyond the proof in the Appendix, what is the key insight of the theorem? What does it imply in plain English? To me, the conclusion seems almost natural and obvious. Is there a powerful insight that I am missing? I feel that this theoretical result warrants more space, accompanied by additional experiments to support it. For instance, can the regularizer parameter lambda be predicted based on the data? Is there a property in the data that can help guess the right lambda? My intuition suggests that lambda is crucial in determining the final network structure. Is this correct? Furthermore, how much does the structure of the final network depend on the initialization? Do different random weight initializations yield different networks? How different are these networks? What happens when fan-in and fan-out regularizers are combined? Does the theoretical result still hold?

I also have a few additional questions:

1. Could you clarify why adding zero units changes the regularizer value? For example, does the L2 norm change when zero values are added?

2. The definition of zero units, having either fan-in or fan-out weights being zero, seems incomplete. I believe you meant to say that both fan-in and fan-out weights must be zero to remove the unit while maintaining the same output f. This point requires clarification.

After considering these points, I have revised my rating to 7, with the hope that the authors will address my comments and enhance the paper accordingly.
