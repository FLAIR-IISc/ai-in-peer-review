I concur with Reviewer 2 on the intriguing aspects of the paper. The concept of dynamically adding or removing units is a promising direction, enabling the model to adapt and evolve in response to the problem and data, rather than relying on user prior knowledge.

The authors present an interesting theoretical result, which demonstrates that under fan-out or fan-in regularization, the optimum of the error function can be achieved with a finite number of parameters, thereby preventing the network from growing indefinitely and overfitting the data. This finding bears resemblance to traditional approaches, such as Lasso or Elastic Net, where regularization yields sparse weights. I would have appreciated more intuition and explanation of this theorem, as it seems somewhat expected. Allocating more space to discuss and analyze the main result, potentially at the expense of prior work discussion, would have strengthened the paper.

I have several additional comments:

1. A compelling experiment would be to demonstrate that a model with dynamically added or removed nodes can outperform a network with a fixed number of nodes, showcasing the efficiency of the proposed approach. This would highlight the method's ability to optimize performance versus memory by removing unnecessary nodes and replacing them with needed ones. While Figure 2 presents some experiments, the results are mixed, and the figure could be improved for clarity. Although the non-parametric networks sometimes perform better and sometimes worse than the parametric ones, the method's value in discovering structure is still evident. However, I am unclear on why the non-parametric version of learning can sometimes outperform the parametric version, even when the final network is known in advance. Further insight would be appreciated.

2. I suggest providing a more in-depth discussion of Theorem 1, including its implications and key insights. The theorem's conclusion appears almost natural, and I am unsure if there is a powerful insight that I am missing. As mentioned earlier, this theoretical result warrants more space and potentially additional experiments to support it. For instance, can the regularizer parameter lambda be predicted based on the data, or is there a property that can help estimate the optimal lambda? My intuition suggests that lambda is crucial in determining the final network structure. Is this correct? Additionally, how much does the final network structure depend on initialization, and do different random weights yield different networks? What happens when fan-in and fan-out regularizers are combined, and does the theoretical result still hold?

I also have a few additional questions:

1. Could you clarify why adding zero units changes the regularizer value? For example, does the L2 norm change when adding zero values?
2. The definition of zero units, having either fan-in or fan-out weights being zero, seems incomplete. I believe you intended to state that both fan-in and fan-out weights must be zero to remove the unit while preserving the output f. This point should be clarified.

I have revised my rating to 7, hoping that the authors will address these comments and strengthen the paper.
