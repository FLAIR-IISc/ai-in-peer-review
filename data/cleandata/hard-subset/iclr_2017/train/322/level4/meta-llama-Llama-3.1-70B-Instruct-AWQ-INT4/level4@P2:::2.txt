The paper presents an interesting idea of automatically adapting and choosing the size of a neural network during a single training run. The authors introduce nonparametric neural networks, a non-probabilistic framework for conducting optimization over all possible network sizes, and prove its soundness when network growth is limited via an Lp penalty. They also propose a novel optimization algorithm, Adaptive Radial-Angular Gradient Descent (AdaRad), and obtain promising results.

The specific question tackled by the paper is how to automatically determine the optimal size of a neural network for a given task without prior information. The approach is well-motivated, and the authors provide a thorough discussion of the limitations of existing methods and the benefits of their proposed framework.

The paper supports its claims through a combination of theoretical analysis and empirical evaluation. The authors provide a proof of the soundness of their framework and demonstrate its effectiveness on several benchmark datasets. The results show that nonparametric neural networks can achieve competitive performance with parametric networks of the same size and even outperform them in some cases.

However, the paper could benefit from more discussion of the implications and insights of Theorem 1. The theorem is presented as a standalone result, but its significance and relevance to the broader context of neural networks could be explored further. Additionally, the authors could provide more intuition and explanation of the theorem's proof, as it is a key component of their framework.

The experimental evaluation is thorough, but some of the results are mixed, and the authors could provide more insight into why nonparametric neural networks perform better in some cases and worse in others. The authors could also explore the relationship between the regularizer parameter lambda and the final network structure, as well as the impact of initialization on the final network.

Overall, the paper presents an interesting and promising approach to neural network optimization, and with some additional discussion and analysis, it has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field.
