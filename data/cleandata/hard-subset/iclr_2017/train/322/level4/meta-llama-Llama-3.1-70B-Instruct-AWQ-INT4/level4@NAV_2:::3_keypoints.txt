- Proposes adapting network size during training by adding/removing units with zero input/output weights and group-sparsity regularization.  
- Main theoretical result: with proper regularization the loss is minimized by a network with a finite number of units.  
- Initial experiments suggest no clear over- or under-fitting, but this is not guaranteed by theory.  
- Potential benefit: may reduce need for manual tuning of network size/hyperparameters.  
- Key drawback: approach introduces new hyperparameters (unit-addition rate, weight decay) that still control final size and may be hard to tune.  
- Authors do not claim easier training; reviewer disappointed that tuning burden remains.  
- Demonstrates smaller networks achieve comparable performance, which could reduce test-time runtime, power, and memory.  
- Lacks experimental comparison to existing size-reduction methods (e.g., pruning), so competitiveness is unclear.  
- Same hyperparameters influence both network size and training time, raising risk of much slower training.  
- Scalability concern: authors do not discuss behavior or practicality for large-scale networks.  
- Experiments are encouraging but limited; need evaluations on much larger problems for stronger claims.  
- Overall: interesting, clearly written, and potentially useful with an inspiring lifelong-learning vision, but results are speculative.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Reasons for decision: novel, well-motivated approach and thorough theoretical analysis proving soundness of formulation.  
- Suggested improvements: experimentally compare to pruning/size-reduction methods and address scalability and training-time concerns.  
- Questions for authors: how will they mitigate slow training on large networks; how will the approach scale; how will they compare experimentally to existing methods?
