I concur with Reviewer 2's assessment of the paper's interesting aspects. The concept of dynamically adding or removing units to make a model adapt to the problem and data, rather than relying on user prior knowledge, is indeed an intriguing direction.

The authors present a notable theoretical result, proving that under fan-out or fan-in regularization, the optimum of the error function is achieved with a finite number of parameters. This implies that the network does not grow indefinitely, thereby avoiding overfitting. This concept bears resemblance to traditional approaches like Lasso or Elastic Net, where regularization yields sparse weights. I would have appreciated more intuitive explanations for this theorem, as it seems somewhat expected. Providing more space for discussing and analyzing the main result, potentially at the expense of prior work discussion, would have strengthened the paper.

I have several additional comments:

1. A compelling experiment would be to demonstrate that a model with dynamically added or removed nodes can outperform a network with a fixed number of nodes (determined after complete learning). This would highlight the efficiency of the proposed method. Specifically, it would be interesting to see if the approach optimizes performance versus memory by replacing unnecessary nodes with needed ones. Although experiments in Figure 2 show mixed results, they are not entirely clear and require careful interpretation. In some cases, non-parametric networks perform better, while in others, they perform worse than parametric ones. Even in the latter case, the method's usefulness in discovering the structure is apparent. However, I am unclear as to why non-parametric learning can sometimes outperform parametric learning when the final network is known in advance. Further insight into this phenomenon would be appreciated.

2. I believe Theorem 1 warrants more in-depth discussion. The theorem seems to be presented without proper analysis, and its implications are not fully explored. Beyond the proof in the Appendix, what is the key insight of the Theorem? How can it be explained in plain English? While the conclusion may seem natural, I wonder if there are more profound implications. I think this theoretical result deserves more space, accompanied by additional experiments to support it. For instance, can the regularizer parameter lambda be predicted based on the data? Is there a property in the data that can help guess the optimal lambda? My intuition suggests that lambda is crucial in determining the final network structure. Is this correct? Furthermore, how much does the final network structure depend on initialization? Do different random weight initializations yield different networks, and if so, how different are they? What happens when fan-in and fan-out regularizers are combined? Does the theoretical result still hold?

I also have a few additional questions:

1. Could you clarify why adding zero units changes the regularizer value? For example, does the L2 norm change when zero values are added?
2. The definition of zero units, having either fan-in or fan-out weights being zero, seems to imply that both fan-in and fan-out weights must be zero to remove the unit without affecting the output f. This point could be clarified further.

I have revised my rating to 7, hoping that the authors will address these comments.
