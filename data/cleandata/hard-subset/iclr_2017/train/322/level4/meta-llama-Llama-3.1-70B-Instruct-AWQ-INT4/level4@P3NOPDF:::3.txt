This paper addresses the problem of dynamically adjusting the number of units in a neural network during training. The authors propose a simple yet elegant approach, where units with zero input or output weights are added or removed, and a group sparsity norm is employed for regularization to encourage unit weights to converge to zero. The primary theoretical contribution is the demonstration that, with proper regularization, the loss function is minimized by a network with a finite number of units.

In practice, this result does not guarantee that the resulting network will not suffer from over- or under-fitting, but initial experiments suggest that this is not the case. One potential advantage of approaches that learn the number of units to use in a network is the alleviation of the burden of hyperparameter tuning. However, this approach also has a disadvantage: it does not entirely solve the problem of hyperparameter tuning. The network still relies on several hyperparameters that implicitly control the number of units, including parameters that govern the frequency of new unit additions and the rate at which weights decay to zero.

It is unclear whether these hyperparameters will be easier or harder to tune than those in standard approaches. The authors acknowledge this limitation and do not claim to have simplified the training process. Nevertheless, it is somewhat disappointing that this approach does not seem to offer a significant advantage in terms of hyperparameter tuning.

The authors emphasize that their approach enables the training of networks with fewer units, achieving comparable performance to parametrically trained networks. This is a potentially important result, as smaller networks can reduce runtime, power consumption, and memory footprint, particularly on mobile devices. However, the authors do not experimentally compare their approach to existing methods that aim to reduce the size of parametrically trained networks, such as pruning. Therefore, it is unclear whether this approach is competitive with the best current methods for reducing network size.

Another potential drawback of the proposed approach is that the same hyperparameters control both the number of units and the training time. This may result in slower training times compared to parametric approaches with fixed hyperparameters. While many parametric approaches require time-consuming methods like grid search to choose hyperparameters, experience with similar problems can often simplify the selection process. However, the authors' approach may be inherently slower, and it is unclear how this issue will scale with larger networks. This raises concerns about the practicality of this approach for large-scale networks, where training times may be prohibitively slow.

The experiments presented in the paper are helpful and encouraging, but not comprehensive or entirely convincing. Further experiments on larger problems would be necessary to demonstrate the practicality and usefulness of this approach.

Overall, this is an interesting and clearly written paper that makes a potentially useful point. The vision of building networks that can adapt and grow through lifelong learning is inspiring, and this type of research may be necessary to realize such a vision. However, the current results remain somewhat speculative, and further work is needed to fully demonstrate the potential of this approach.
