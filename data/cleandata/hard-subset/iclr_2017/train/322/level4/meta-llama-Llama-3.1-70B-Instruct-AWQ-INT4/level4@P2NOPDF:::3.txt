This paper proposes a novel approach to dynamically adjust the number of units in a neural network during training. The method is simple yet elegant, involving the addition or removal of units with zero input or output weights, and utilizing a group sparsity norm for regularization to encourage unit weights to approach zero. The primary theoretical contribution is the demonstration that, with proper regularization, the loss function is minimized by a network with a finite number of units.

Although this result does not guarantee that the resulting network will avoid over- or under-fitting the training data, initial experiments suggest that this is not a significant issue. One potential advantage of this approach is that it may alleviate the burden of hyperparameter tuning, as the network can adaptively determine the number of units required. However, a drawback of this method is that it does not entirely eliminate the need for hyperparameter tuning, as several parameters still need to be set, including those controlling the frequency of unit addition and weight decay. It is unclear whether these hyperparameters will be easier or more challenging to tune than those in standard approaches.

The authors highlight the ability of their approach to train networks with fewer units, achieving comparable performance to parametrically trained networks. This is a significant advantage, as smaller networks can reduce runtime, power consumption, and memory footprint, particularly on mobile devices. Nevertheless, the authors do not experimentally compare their approach to existing methods for reducing network size, such as pruning trained networks, making it unclear whether their method is competitive with state-of-the-art approaches.

A potential disadvantage of this approach is that the same hyperparameters control both the number of units and training time. Consequently, training may be significantly slower than with parametric approaches using fixed hyperparameters. While many parametric approaches require time-consuming hyperparameter tuning methods, such as grid search, the cost of these methods is not always incurred, as experience with similar problems can facilitate hyperparameter selection. In contrast, the slowness of the proposed approach may be inherent. The authors do not discuss the scalability of their method for training large networks, raising concerns about its practicality.

The experiments presented in the paper are helpful and encouraging but not comprehensive or entirely convincing. Further experiments on larger problems are necessary to demonstrate the practicality and usefulness of this approach.

Overall, this paper is interesting, well-written, and presents a potentially useful idea. The vision of developing networks that can adapt and grow through lifelong learning is inspiring, and this type of research may be essential for realizing such a vision. However, the current results are still speculative, and further work is needed to fully explore the potential of this approach.
