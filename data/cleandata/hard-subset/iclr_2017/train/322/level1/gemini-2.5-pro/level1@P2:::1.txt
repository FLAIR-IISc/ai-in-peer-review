### **Review of "NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS"**

#### **1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper addresses the challenging and computationally expensive problem of selecting an appropriate neural network width for a given task. Instead of relying on conventional black-box hyperparameter optimization methods that require training many networks from scratch, the authors propose a novel framework called "nonparametric neural networks." The core idea is to treat the network width as a parameter to be optimized during a single training run. This is achieved by continuously adding new, randomly initialized "zero units" to the network while simultaneously encouraging sparsity at the unit level through an L2 fan-in or fan-out regularizer, which effectively prunes redundant units.

The paper makes several key contributions to support this framework. First, it provides a theoretical justification (Theorem 1), proving that under this regularization scheme, the training objective attains a global minimum at a finite network size, ensuring the method's soundness. Second, to facilitate training, it introduces two novel components: a modified batch normalization layer called "CapNorm," which prevents the regularizer from being negated, and a custom optimization algorithm, "Adaptive Radial-Angular Gradient Descent" (AdaRad). AdaRad is cleverly designed to handle the dynamics of new units by separating the gradient update into radial (magnitude-changing) and angular (direction-changing) components, normalizing only the latter to allow new units to learn effectively without being prematurely pruned. The empirical results on several benchmark datasets demonstrate that this approach can find well-performing network architectures and, in some cases, achieve higher accuracy than both fixed-size networks and architectures discovered via random search.

#### **2. Decision: Accept**

I recommend **Accepting** this paper. The primary reasons are:
1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper presents a well-motivated and theoretically-grounded framework for a highly practical and important problem in deep learning. The combination of a continuous growth/pruning mechanism with a custom optimizer designed for its specific dynamics is a significant and novel contribution.
2.  **Rigorous Development:** The proposal is supported by a formal proof of soundness (Theorem 1) and the development of necessary algorithmic components (CapNorm, AdaRad) that directly address the challenges introduced by the framework. This demonstrates a deep understanding of the problem.

#### **3. Supporting Arguments**

The paper's strength lies in its comprehensive and principled approach. The problem of architecture selection is a major bottleneck in applying deep learning, and the proposed method offers a promising alternative to brute-force search.

The theoretical result (Theorem 1) is a cornerstone of the paper. It provides confidence that the optimization problem is well-posed and will not degenerate by favoring infinitely large networks. This elevates the work beyond a purely heuristic method.

The introduction of the AdaRad optimizer is particularly insightful. The authors correctly identify that standard optimizers like RMSprop or Adam would conflict with the fan-in regularizer, either preventing new units from learning or incorrectly scaling the regularization effect. AdaRad's decomposition of the update into radial and angular parts is a clever solution. It allows the learning direction of a unit's weights to be adapted quickly (the angular update) while the unit's "importance," as measured by the norm of its weight vector, is managed separately by the loss gradient and the regularizer (the radial update). This decoupling appears crucial for the success of the method.

The empirical evaluation is thorough and, importantly, transparent about its mixed results. The strong performance on MNIST and `rectangles-images`, where the nonparametric approach not only finds a compact architecture but also outperforms fixed-size networks and even the best models from a random search, is compelling evidence of the framework's potential. The scalability experiment on the larger `poker` dataset further suggests that the method is not limited to small-scale problems. While the poor performance on the `convex` dataset is a limitation, the authors' honesty in presenting this result is commendable and invites valuable future research into the method's failure modes.

#### **4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical to my decision to accept.

*   **Analysis of the `convex` Dataset Failure:** The paper would be strengthened by a more in-depth discussion or hypothesis about why the method failed on the `convex` dataset. Is it related to the loss landscape, the nature of the required features, or an interaction with the AdaRad optimizer? Providing some intuition here would be very valuable for understanding the limitations of the proposed framework.
*   **Sensitivity to New Hyperparameters:** The method aims to automate the selection of network width, but it introduces its own set of hyperparameters (λ, αr, αφ, the unit addition schedule ν). A brief discussion on the sensitivity of the final architecture and performance to these new parameters would be helpful. For instance, how does the relationship set between αr and λ (αr = 150λ) affect the "lifespan" of new units and the final model? This would help practitioners understand the trade-offs involved.
*   **Comparison with Pruning:** The introduction correctly positions the work as distinct from pruning methods that require a large, pre-trained network. However, a direct empirical comparison would be powerful. For example, one could take the final, largest architecture found by the nonparametric method on MNIST, train it from scratch as a parametric network, and then apply a state-of-the-art pruning algorithm. Comparing the performance of that pruned network to the one found by the proposed method would provide a very clear benchmark.
*   **Clarification on AdaRad's Intuition:** While Algorithm 1 is clear, a more intuitive, high-level explanation of why the angular component is normalized using a running average of its squared norm could improve readability for a broader audience.

#### **5. Questions for the Authors**

I would appreciate clarification on the following points:

1.  Regarding the poor performance on the `convex` dataset: Do you have any hypotheses as to the cause? Could it be that the growth/pruning dynamic gets stuck in a poor local minimum for this specific problem, or is there a fundamental mismatch between the dataset's structure and the assumptions of the method?
2.  Could you comment on the practical computational overhead of AdaRad compared to a standard optimizer like Adam? Table 1 provides a theoretical complexity analysis, but in practice, how much slower is one training epoch?
3.  The CapNorm layer is introduced to prevent the regularizer from being nullified. Did you run any experiments using standard Batch Normalization to confirm this failure mode empirically? Seeing this comparison would further motivate the necessity of CapNorm.
4.  The unit addition schedule is fixed at one unit per layer per epoch. Have you experimented with adaptive schedules, for example, adding more units when the validation loss plateaus?
