

# Review of "NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper introduces "nonparametric neural networks," a framework for automatically determining optimal network size during a single training cycle without requiring expensive global search over multiple network architectures. The authors prove that their approach achieves a global training error minimum at finite dimensionality under specific regularization conditions. They develop a novel optimization algorithm, "Adaptive Radial-Angular Gradient Descent" (AdaRad), and a new normalization technique, "Capped Batch Normalization" (CapNorm), to enable continuous network growth and pruning during training. The framework dynamically adds new units while eliminating redundant units via an L2 penalty, allowing the network to adapt its size without requiring multiple full training runs.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution with a rigorous proof of the framework's soundness and provides compelling empirical evidence for its effectiveness across multiple datasets.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
**Theoretical soundness:** The paper's key contribution is Theorem 1, which proves that nonparametric neural networks achieve a global training error minimum at finite dimensionality when using fan-in or fan-out regularizers with λ > 0 and 1 ≤ p < ∞. The proof in Appendix 7.1 is comprehensive, addressing potential concerns about degenerate solutions with infinitely large networks. The authors carefully establish the necessary conditions for the nonlinearities and error functions, making the proof robust and applicable to common neural network architectures.

**Empirical validation:** The paper provides strong empirical evidence supporting the claims. On MNIST, nonparametric networks substantially outperform parametric networks of the same size (144 vs 694 units) while achieving comparable performance. For the rectangles images dataset, nonparametric networks achieve the globally best performance with significantly fewer parameters. The scalability analysis on the poker dataset (1.025 million datapoints) demonstrates the approach's applicability to larger real-world problems. The detailed analysis in Figure 3 provides valuable insight into the training dynamics, showing how units are added and eliminated during training.

**Literature positioning:** The paper thoroughly situates its work within the literature, clearly differentiating from existing approaches. It correctly identifies the limitations of black-box model selection methods (requiring multiple full training runs) and previous pruning techniques (requiring a high-performing starting network). The authors properly acknowledge related work on network growth (Wei et al., Chen et al.) and Bayesian approaches, positioning their contribution as a novel solution to the network size selection problem.

## 4. Additional Feedback
The paper would benefit from more detailed discussion of the limitations of the approach, particularly regarding the convex dataset results. The authors note that nonparametric networks performed poorly on this dataset compared to parametric networks, but could provide more insight into why this occurs and potential modifications to address this limitation.

The paper could include a more thorough comparison of computational costs between the proposed method and standard approaches. While the paper mentions that AdaRad has better computational scaling than RMSprop, a concrete timing comparison would strengthen the practical relevance of the method.

The authors should consider discussing how the approach might be extended to handle different types of neural networks (e.g., convolutional networks) beyond the feed-forward networks tested in the paper.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. The paper reports poor performance on the convex dataset. Could the authors elaborate on why the nonparametric approach fails for this particular dataset, and whether they believe the framework could be modified to handle such problems better?

2. The experimental setup begins with 10 units per layer. How would the approach perform if it started with a single unit or no units at all, and what implications would this have for the theoretical guarantees?

3. The paper demonstrates promising results on MNIST and rectangles images, but less so on the convex dataset. Could the authors provide more insight into which types of problems (in terms of dataset properties or task complexity) benefit most from the nonparametric approach?
