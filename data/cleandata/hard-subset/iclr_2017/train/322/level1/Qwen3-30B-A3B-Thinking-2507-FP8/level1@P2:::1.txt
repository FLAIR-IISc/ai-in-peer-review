

# Review of "NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS"

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contribution

The paper introduces "nonparametric neural networks," a framework that dynamically adjusts network size during training to automatically select the optimal network architecture without prior information. The authors prove theoretically that with appropriate regularization (specifically, an `2 fan-in regularizer), nonparametric networks achieve a global minimum at finite dimensionality. They propose a novel training algorithm called AdaRad and a normalization layer called CapNorm to implement this framework. The authors claim that their approach can find good network sizes during a single training run, outperform parametric networks of the same size, and achieve results competitive with exhaustive search methods. The paper demonstrates promising results on MNIST and rectangles images datasets, though performance on the convex dataset was disappointing.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **acceptance** with reservations. The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution with a well-proven theorem and introduces a novel algorithmic framework that addresses a meaningful problem in neural network architecture selection. The core theoretical contribution is compelling and well-justified.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

**Theoretical Soundness (Key Strength):** The paper's strongest contribution is Theorem 1, which proves that nonparametric networks with appropriate regularization achieve a global minimum at finite dimensionality. The proof is rigorous and addresses a critical concern about the potential degeneration of network size with standard approaches. The theoretical foundation is exceptionally strong, and the proof is comprehensive, addressing differentiability conditions and providing a clear mathematical justification for the approach. This theoretical contribution alone makes the paper valuable to the field.

**Novel Algorithm and Implementation:** The authors introduce AdaRad, a novel training algorithm that handles the unique challenges of nonparametric networks. The algorithm's design addresses the need for separate control of radial (magnitude) and angular (direction) updates, which is crucial for maintaining the ability to add and remove units without destabilizing the training process. The CapNorm layer is a thoughtful adaptation of batch normalization to work with the fan-in regularizer, and the paper clearly explains why standard batch normalization would interfere with the regularization.

**Empirical Results (Mixed):** The empirical results are promising for MNIST and rectangles images, where nonparametric networks outperform parametric networks of the same size and achieve competitive results with exhaustive search methods. The analysis of the training process (Figure 3) provides valuable insights into how the network size evolves during training. However, the poor performance on the convex dataset (Section 4.1) is concerning and requires more thorough explanation. The authors note that "nonparametric networks performed badly on the convex dataset" but don't provide a compelling analysis of why this occurs. This limitation needs to be addressed in the final version.

The paper is well-placed in the literature, with a thorough discussion of related work in Section 5, including connections to pruning, model selection, and network growth methods. The authors clearly articulate how their approach differs from and improves upon existing methods.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

- **Clarify the convex dataset failure:** The paper needs a more detailed analysis of why the approach fails on the convex dataset. Is this due to the specific properties of the dataset (e.g., linearity, noise level)? How does this failure compare to the performance of parametric networks on the same dataset? This analysis would strengthen the paper's claims about the general applicability of the approach.

- **Expand the scalability discussion:** While the poker dataset experiment demonstrates scalability, the paper could better articulate the computational cost of the approach compared to standard training. The paper mentions that AdaRad "incurs additional computational cost" but doesn't quantify it. A comparison of training time per epoch for nonparametric vs. parametric networks would be helpful.

- **Clarify the relationship between unit addition rate and convergence:** The paper mentions adding one unit per epoch for MNIST but states that "this does not lead to fast convergence." It would be helpful to discuss how unit addition rate affects convergence speed and whether there are principled ways to determine the optimal rate for a given dataset.

- **Provide more details about the regularization parameter λ:** The paper uses different λ values for different datasets, but doesn't provide a systematic analysis of how λ affects network size and performance. A more thorough exploration of the λ landscape would strengthen the empirical claims.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. Could you provide more details about why nonparametric networks fail on the convex dataset while succeeding on MNIST and rectangles images? Is this related to the specific properties of the dataset (e.g., linearity, noise, dimensionality) or is there a fundamental limitation to the approach?

2. In the convex dataset experiment, the authors note that "even if the model found by training nonparametric networks were re-trained as a parametric network, the apparent tendency of nonparametric networks to converge to relatively small sizes hurts us." Could you elaborate on how the small size affects performance in this specific case, and whether this is a general limitation of the approach or specific to the convex dataset?

3. The paper states that "nonparametric networks performed badly on the convex dataset" but doesn't quantify how much worse. Could you provide specific numbers comparing the test error of the best nonparametric network to the best parametric network on this dataset?

4. The paper mentions that AdaRad-M performs similarly to AdaRad, but doesn't provide detailed comparisons. Could you share more specific results (e.g., test error differences, convergence speed) to help readers understand the marginal benefit of adding momentum to the algorithm?
