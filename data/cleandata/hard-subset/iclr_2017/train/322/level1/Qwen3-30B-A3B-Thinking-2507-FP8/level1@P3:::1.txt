

# Peer Review: NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims/Contribution

The paper proposes a novel framework called "nonparametric neural networks" that automatically determines optimal network size during a single training cycle without requiring expensive global search over multiple network architectures. The authors introduce a theoretical foundation proving that network growth can be controlled via an L2 fan-in regularizer, enabling the network to dynamically add and remove units during training. They develop a novel normalization layer (CapNorm) and optimization algorithm (AdaRad) to implement this framework. The approach is theoretically justified by proving that nonparametric networks achieve a global training error minimum at finite dimensionality under specific regularizer conditions. Empirical results demonstrate that nonparametric networks can achieve performance comparable to or better than parametric networks of the same size on two of three benchmark datasets, while using fewer parameters.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **reject** the paper for ICLR 2017. The key reasons for rejection are the inconsistent empirical performance (particularly on the convex dataset) and insufficient theoretical justification for the proposed approach's general applicability.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution with Theorem 1, which establishes that nonparametric networks with fan-in/out regularizers achieve a global minimum at finite dimensionality. This is a meaningful theoretical contribution to the understanding of neural network capacity control. However, the empirical results fail to support the broader claims of the paper.

The most significant weakness is the poor performance of nonparametric networks on the convex dataset (Section 4.1). The authors report that "parametric networks of the same size perform substantially better and also have a smaller range of performance across random reruns." This undermines the paper's central claim that nonparametric networks "achieve a higher accuracy than networks of the same size that are trained from scratch and, in some cases, achieve a higher accuracy than is possible by black-box methods." The fact that nonparametric networks perform worse than parametric networks on one of three datasets, and that the gap is significant, is a major concern for a method claiming to be a general solution to network size selection.

Furthermore, the paper's theoretical foundation appears to be too restrictive. The proof of Theorem 1 requires specific conditions on the nonlinearities (σ(0) = 0) and error functions that may not hold for many practical applications. The authors acknowledge this limitation in the appendix but don't thoroughly discuss how common these conditions are in real-world neural network architectures.

The paper also lacks sufficient justification for key design choices. For example, the choice of αr = 150λ (Section 4.1) appears arbitrary and lacks theoretical or empirical justification for why this specific relationship between the radial step size and regularization parameter should work well across different datasets.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

1. **Expand on the convex dataset failure**: The authors should conduct a thorough analysis of why nonparametric networks fail on the convex dataset. Is this due to the specific properties of the dataset, or is there a fundamental limitation in the approach? This would strengthen the paper's theoretical foundation and provide valuable insights for practitioners.

2. **Clarify the scope of the theoretical results**: The paper should more explicitly state the conditions under which the framework is theoretically sound and practical. The current proof assumes specific properties of the nonlinearities (σ(0) = 0) and error functions that may not hold for many common architectures.

3. **Provide more detailed ablation studies**: The paper would benefit from experiments comparing different regularization strengths, unit addition schedules, and the impact of CapNorm versus standard batch normalization.

4. **Address the scalability of the approach**: While the poker dataset experiment demonstrates scalability, the paper doesn't provide sufficient analysis of the computational cost relative to standard training methods.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper states that "nonparametric networks performed badly on the convex dataset" but doesn't explain why. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the failure mode? Is the issue related to the specific properties of the convex dataset, or is it a fundamental limitation of the approach?

2. The proof of Theorem 1 requires that σ(0) = 0 for all nonlinearities except possibly σL. How common is this condition in practical neural network architectures? For example, how would your framework handle networks using sigmoid or tanh activations, which do not satisfy σ(0) = 0?

3. The choice of αr = 150λ appears to be a heuristic without theoretical justification. Can you provide a more principled derivation of this relationship, or at least a more thorough empirical justification?

4. The paper claims that nonparametric networks "achieve a higher accuracy than networks of the same size that are trained from scratch." However, the results in Figure 2 show that for MNIST, the best nonparametric network is "close in performance to the best parametric network, while being substantially smaller." How much of this performance difference is due to the smaller size of the nonparametric network, and how much is due to the network selection mechanism itself?

5. The paper mentions that "the best nonparametric network made no mistake on the test set of 100.000 examples" for the poker dataset. Given that this is a binary classification task, is this result statistically significant, and how does it compare to the best results reported on OpenML for this dataset?
