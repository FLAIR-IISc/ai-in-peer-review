

This paper introduces nonparametric neural networks, a novel framework for automatically determining optimal network size during a single training cycle without requiring prior information or expensive global search. The authors propose a theoretically grounded approach that dynamically adds and removes units while maintaining network functionality through f-equivalence. They prove Theorem 1 establishing that their nonparametric framework achieves a global minimum under specific conditions, and introduce AdaRad, a novel training algorithm with radial-angular gradient updates. The experimental evaluation demonstrates promising results across multiple datasets, with nonparametric networks often achieving comparable or better performance than parametric networks of the same size, while using fewer parameters.

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a significant contribution to the important problem of model selection in deep learning. The theoretical foundation is strong with a well-proven theorem, and the empirical results are scientifically rigorous with careful comparison to relevant baselines. The approach addresses a genuine limitation in current model selection methods by enabling size adaptation during training rather than requiring separate training runs.

The theoretical proof of Theorem 1 is particularly compelling, demonstrating mathematical soundness for the framework. The experimental design is thorough, with proper evaluation across multiple datasets and careful comparison to both parametric networks of equivalent size and exhaustive search methods. The results on MNIST and rectangles images are especially convincing, showing clear advantages of the nonparametric approach.

To further strengthen the paper, I suggest including a more detailed discussion of computational efficiency, particularly regarding the trade-off between training time and parameter count. The authors could also provide more insight into why the convex dataset proved challenging for their method, and whether there are characteristics of datasets that would make them particularly suitable or unsuitable for this approach.

I have two questions for the authors: First, could you elaborate on why the convex dataset was particularly challenging for your method, and whether there are specific dataset properties that would make nonparametric networks less effective? Second, while AdaRad-M showed similar performance to AdaRad, could you discuss potential scenarios where AdaRad-M might offer advantages, or why the momentum aspect might not be beneficial for this particular problem?
