

# Peer Review for ICLR_2017

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contributions

The paper introduces "nonparametric neural networks" as a framework for automatically determining optimal network size during a single training cycle, eliminating the need for expensive global search over network architectures. The authors propose a novel training algorithm called AdaRad with a new normalization layer (CapNorm) and prove that with an l2 fan-in regularizer, the optimization problem has a global minimum at some finite dimensionality (Theorem 1). They claim that their approach can dynamically shrink and expand networks during training, achieving higher accuracy than parametric networks of the same size, and competing with exhaustive random search results. Empirical results on MNIST and rectangles images datasets show promising performance, though the method performs poorly on the convex dataset.

## 2. Decision

I recommend rejection. The key reasons are: (1) The empirical results are inconsistent across datasets, with poor performance on the convex dataset undermining the main claim of the approach's generality; and (2) The theoretical contribution is incremental and not sufficiently novel compared to existing work on network pruning and growth.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The main claim of the paper is that nonparametric networks can automatically select optimal size during a single training cycle and outperform parametric networks of the same size. However, the empirical results reveal a significant limitation: on the convex dataset, nonparametric networks substantially underperform parametric networks of the same size, and even when retrained as parametric networks, they fail to match the performance of larger parametric networks. This contradicts the paper's central claim and raises serious questions about the method's general applicability.

The theoretical contribution (Theorem 1) is technically sound but represents an incremental advancement. The paper builds on established work on sparse regularization (l1 and l2 norms) and network pruning. The proof, while detailed, doesn't offer fundamentally new insights beyond what could be inferred from existing literature on sparse regression applied to neural networks. The authors cite relevant work by Alvarez & Salzmann (2016) and Molchanov et al. (2017), but don't sufficiently differentiate their contribution from these prior approaches.

The empirical evaluation is limited to only three datasets with mixed results. The paper doesn't provide sufficient analysis of why the method works well on MNIST and rectangles images but fails on convex. The authors state that "nonparametric networks performed badly on the convex dataset" without explaining the underlying causes or providing comparative analysis to other methods. The claim that "the best nonparametric network has the globally best performance" for rectangles images is misleading since it's based on median test error, not absolute performance.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

- Provide a more thorough analysis of dataset characteristics that might explain the performance differences across datasets. What specific properties of the convex dataset cause the method to fail?
- Expand the experimental comparison to include state-of-the-art methods for network size selection, such as Chen et al. (2016), Guo et al. (2016), and other network growth approaches.
- Clarify the relationship between the nonparametric approach and existing pruning methods. The paper states that "all of these strategies require a high-performing network model as a starting point," but doesn't sufficiently explain how their approach differs from dynamic network surgery (Guo et al., 2016) which also allows adding back units.
- Include computational efficiency comparisons with state-of-the-art methods, not just memory footprint.
- Provide more details about the unit addition schedule and how it affects convergence. The paper states "growing nets from scratch is a good test case" but doesn't justify this choice or discuss how different unit addition rates affect performance.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper claims that nonparametric networks "substantially outperform parametric networks of the same size" for MNIST, but the results show a nonparametric network with 144 units versus a parametric network with 694 units. How does the performance compare when the parametric network is scaled to the same size as the nonparametric network?

2. Why does the method perform so poorly on the convex dataset compared to the other two datasets? What specific characteristics of the convex dataset might be causing this issue?

3. The paper states that "the best nonparametric network made no mistake on the test set of 100.000 examples" for the poker dataset. What was the test set size, and how does this compare to state-of-the-art results on this dataset?

4. How does your method compare to the recent work by Chen et al. (2016) "Net2Net" or Guo et al. (2016) "Dynamic Network Surgery" in terms of both performance and computational efficiency?

5. The paper mentions that "the apparent tendency of nonparametric networks to converge to relatively small sizes" hurts performance on the convex dataset. What would be a suitable modification to your framework to prevent this behavior while maintaining the benefits observed on MNIST and rectangles images?

The paper presents an interesting approach with theoretical justification, but the inconsistent empirical results and incremental theoretical contribution, combined with the lack of sufficient analysis of failure cases, make it difficult to recommend acceptance for ICLR 2017.
