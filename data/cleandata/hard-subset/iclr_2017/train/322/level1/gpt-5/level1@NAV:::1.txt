Summary and contributions
The paper proposes “nonparametric neural networks,” a framework that adapts layer widths during a single training run by adding zero-units and pruning entire units via an l2 fan-in (or fan-out) group regularizer. The core theoretical claim is Theorem 1: with a fan-in/out lp penalty (1 ≤ p < ∞), the training objective admits a global minimum at finite dimensionality, addressing the risk of unbounded width growth. Practically, the paper introduces two training components: CapNorm, a variant of batch normalization designed to avoid negating the regularizer, and AdaRad, an optimizer that decomposes gradients into angular and radial components to normalize learning without interfering with l2 shrinkage. Experiments on MNIST, rectangles images, convex, and a large Poker dataset indicate the method can discover compact architectures and occasionally match or exceed strong parametric baselines.

Decision: Reject
Primary reasons: (1) Empirical support is mixed and ablations are insufficient to isolate which components (growth/pruning vs CapNorm vs AdaRad) drive the gains; (2) The scope of evaluation (mostly MLPs on a small set of datasets) and baseline choices leave uncertainty about robustness and generality.

Supporting arguments
- Soundness: Theorem 1 is carefully stated with a lengthy appendix proof, and Proposition 1 about self-similar nonlinearities is a nice practical insight for regularization calibration. However, the theory guarantees existence of finite-width minima, not convergence or generalization benefits, and does not analyze AdaRad.
- Empirics: Improvements over parametric networks of the same size are shown on MNIST and sometimes rectangles; performance degrades notably on convex. The large-scale Poker result is encouraging but lacks runtime/compute comparisons. Baselines use uncapped BN with λ=0, which disadvantages weight decay; modern decoupled weight decay (e.g., AdamW) or BN-compatible regularization is not tested. Crucially, there is no ablation to separate the effects of (a) width adaptation with standard optimizers, (b) CapNorm alone, (c) AdaRad alone, and (d) unit-addition schedules.
- Methodology: The protocol involves early stopping/rewinding and aggressive hyperparameter heuristics (e.g., αr=150/λ), raising concerns about sensitivity and reproducibility.

Suggestions for improvement (helpful, not decisive)
- Provide ablations: fixed-width with group-lasso vs growing; AdaRad vs RMSprop/Adam under CapNorm; CapNorm vs BN/capped BN variants.
- Include modern baselines (AdamW/SGDW), l0/l1 group penalties, and dynamic-width/pruning methods; add convolutional settings.
- Report wall-clock, FLOPs, and memory vs baselines; analyze sensitivity to ν, νfreq, αr, αφ, λ; formalize zero-unit detection criteria.

Questions for authors
- How sensitive are final widths and accuracy to αr, αφ, ν/νfreq, and the 50-epoch “grace period”? Can you provide robustness plots?
- Can the benefits be reproduced with standard optimizers if CapNorm is used, or with BN-compatible weight decay (AdamW)?
- What explains the convex dataset failure? Does starting from a larger network with pruning close the gap?
- What is the computational overhead of AdaRad and dynamic resizing in practice relative to RMSprop/Adam at equal accuracy?
