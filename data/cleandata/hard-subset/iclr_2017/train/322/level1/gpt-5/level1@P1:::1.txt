Summary and contributions
- Proposes “nonparametric neural networks,” a framework that jointly optimizes weights and layer widths during a single training run by (i) continuously adding zero units and (ii) removing units via group-sparsity on fan-ins/fan-outs. The key theoretical result (Theorem 1) proves that, with ℓp (1 ≤ p < ∞) fan-in/out regularization and λ > 0, the joint optimization over all widths attains a global minimum at a finite dimensionality.
- Introduces two training components tailored to this setting: (a) CapNorm, a variant of batch normalization that avoids neutralizing the regularizer by capping the rescaling; and (b) AdaRad, an optimizer that decomposes gradients into “radial” (parallel) and “angular” (orthogonal) components w.r.t. each fan-in, normalizes the angular component only, and uses separate step sizes to balance learning vs. shrinkage. A momentum variant (AdaRad-M) is also presented.
- Provides empirical results on MNIST, rectangles images, convex (small/medium) and OpenML poker (large). On two datasets, nonparametric training achieves better or comparable accuracy with fewer parameters than parametric baselines; on convex it underperforms. The paper includes insightful analysis of growth/shrinkage dynamics and unit lifetimes.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) Empirical validation is limited and confounded: gains are not clearly attributable to the nonparametric framework versus the new optimizer (AdaRad) and CapNorm; comparisons to strong baselines and ablations are missing. 2) While the existence theorem is neat, its practical impact is limited (it guarantees a finite-width minimizer for the penalized training objective, not model selection optimality or generalization), and the experimental scope (datasets, architectures) is narrow for the claimed contribution.

Supporting arguments
- Attribution and ablations: The method stacks three ingredients (growth with group-sparsity, AdaRad, CapNorm). There is no systematic ablation isolating (i) growth/removal with standard optimizers and normalizations, (ii) AdaRad on parametric networks, (iii) CapNorm’s effect alone. Since the paper reports that parametric nets with RMSProp/Adam plus BN perform strongly, it is crucial to quantify how much of the reported nonparametric gains stem from AdaRad/CapNorm rather than dynamic width selection.
- Comparisons: The baselines omit closely related pruning/growing methods (e.g., Alvarez & Salzmann 2016; Dynamic Network Surgery; other structured sparsity/group-lasso approaches; Wei et al., Chen et al. for growth). The comparison is mostly to parametric MLPs with BN, and sometimes with λ = 0, making the setup not strictly apples-to-apples (NP uses ℓ2 group regularization while BN baselines avoid weight decay).
- Protocol concerns: Nonparametric models are selected by choosing a “typical representative” from 10 runs; parametric models of matching sizes are then tuned. This introduces a selection step that could bias comparisons; reporting medians across equal numbers of runs for each size would be clearer. The unit addition schedule (one per epoch) and αr = 150/λ are hand-tuned and not systematically studied; sensitivity is unknown.
- Scope and rigor: Experiments use small fully connected nets on MNIST/rectangles/convex and one tabular large dataset. No convolutional architectures, no depth selection, and no tasks where model size selection is particularly impactful (e.g., modern vision or NLP). The poker result shows competitiveness but also that parametric nets attain similar performance; the advantage of nonparametric selection is not compelling.
- Theory: Theorem 1 establishes existence of a finite-width minimizer under the penalized training objective. This is an elegant formalization, but it does not connect to validation performance/model selection or characterize the preferred width. Proposition 1 (self-similarity scaling) is intuitive given ReLU homogeneity. No convergence guarantees for the proposed training procedure.
- Practicality: The method adds/removes units online, which complicates implementation. The claimed computational advantage of AdaRad versus RMSProp is not empirically quantified (no wall-clock or memory metrics), and the unit addition rate slows early learning.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions, not all decision-critical)
- Provide thorough ablations:
  - Fixed-width MLPs trained with AdaRad vs. standard optimizers to quantify AdaRad’s contribution.
  - Nonparametric growth/removal trained with Adam/RMSProp (with a compatible normalization) to quantify the framework’s effect independent of AdaRad.
  - CapNorm vs. BN (with/without affine parameters) on both parametric and nonparametric settings.
- Compare against structured sparsity and dynamic growth/pruning baselines, including group-lasso-based neuron removal and methods like Dynamic Network Surgery, Net2Net growth, and Wei et al.’s approach, using matched training budgets.
- Report results across all 10 seeds for each selected size (median and variability), rather than picking a representative, and ensure fairness in hyperparameter tuning budgets across methods.
- Explore sensitivity to unit addition rate ν, νfreq, αr/αφ, and λ; show how width evolves and whether conclusions are robust.
- Include computational metrics: training time, memory, and overhead of width changes versus fixed-width training; analyze scaling of AdaRad in practice.
- Extend to convolutional layers and evaluate on modern benchmarks; or narrow the claims to MLPs if broader generalization is not yet supported.
- Clarify the interaction between CapNorm and the loss gradients (e.g., when σ < 1) and provide intuition/analysis for why it helps learning new units without negating regularization.

Questions for the authors
1) How much of the performance gain is due to AdaRad vs. nonparametric growth? Can you show fixed-width ablations with AdaRad and nonparametric ablations with Adam/RMSProp?
2) How sensitive are results to λ, αr, αφ, and the unit addition schedule? Do different schedules converge to similar widths/accuracies?
3) Can CapNorm be replaced with BN without learned affine parameters and still preserve the regularizer’s effect? What is the empirical gap?
4) How does the method perform with convolutional layers and on datasets where architecture selection matters more (e.g., CIFAR-10/100)?
5) Do different random seeds converge to similar widths (distribution over widths), and is width correlated with validation error as one would expect for model selection?
6) Can you provide wall-clock and memory comparisons of AdaRad vs. RMSProp/Adam on your experiments, including the cost of adding/removing units?
7) Beyond existence of a finite minimizer, can you characterize properties of the selected width (e.g., bounds in terms of λ, data, or margin) or provide generalization guarantees?
