Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes “nonparametric neural networks” that optimize network width during a single training run by alternately adding units with zero fan-out and eliminating units via a group-regularizer on fan-ins (or fan-outs). The core theoretical result (Theorem 1) shows that, under an ℓp fan-in/out penalty with λ>0 and 1≤p<∞, the nonparametric objective attains a global minimum at finite dimensionality, addressing the concern that the best solution might be infinitely wide.
- Training machinery includes: (a) a growth/prune schedule realized by adding zero units (f-equivalence) and removing zero-fan units produced by group shrinkage; (b) CapNorm, a capped batch normalization variant designed to preserve the effect of the group penalty by preventing compensatory scaling; (c) AdaRad, an optimizer that decomposes per-unit gradients into radial (parallel to fan-in) and angular (orthogonal) components, normalizes only the angular component, and uses separate step sizes to decouple learning from regularization-induced shrinkage.
- Empirically, the approach trains MLPs from very small initial width to a data-driven size, often outperforming parametric nets of the same final size on MNIST and rectangles-images, and scales to a 1M-example “poker” dataset with competitive results. The paper provides insightful analysis of the growth/shrink dynamics and unit lifetimes.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- The empirical support is limited and not sufficiently rigorous: comparisons are not cleanly controlled (different normalization/optimizers across methods, manual model selection of “representatives,” changing objectives mid-training), ablations are missing, and performance is inconsistent (convex dataset).
- The main theoretical contribution guarantees a finite-width minimizer of the training objective, but it does not inform generalization or the proposed training procedure’s convergence to good solutions in practice; empirical validation is insufficient to bridge this gap.

Supporting arguments
- Comparisons and fairness: The nonparametric model uses CapNorm + ℓ2 fan-in group penalty + AdaRad, while parametric baselines use BN (with or without affine params) and RMSprop/Adam, with λ=0 (stated incompatibility with BN). Nonparametric training also includes a phase where λ is set to zero after size selection and angular step-size annealing, while parametric baselines were not given an analogous post-hoc fine-tuning schedule. These differences confound whether observed gains stem from the nonparametric framework, the optimizer, or the normalization scheme.
- Selection bias and evaluation protocol: The procedure identifies a “typical representative” nonparametric run by median size and test error over 10 seeds, then trains parametric nets to match that size. This introduces selection bias (using test error in the representative choice) and couples the parametric search space to outcomes of the nonparametric method. A more rigorous protocol would fix sizes a priori (or sweep sizes) and compare across identical training pipelines and compute budgets.
- Missing ablations: Key design choices—CapNorm vs BN, p=2 vs p=1, fan-in vs fan-out penalty, AdaRad vs standard optimizers within the nonparametric framework, and the unit addition/removal cadence—are not systematically ablated. AdaRad-M is introduced but then dismissed without results. It is therefore unclear which component drives robustness or accuracy improvements.
- Limited scope of experiments: Only fully-connected MLPs on relatively small benchmarks (MNIST, rectangles, convex) and one tabular large dataset are considered. No convolutional results, despite claims about the method’s general utility for model selection. On convex, nonparametric underperforms substantially and is biased toward small widths—suggesting the growth/removal heuristics or regularization can select underpowered models.
- Theoretical scope: Theorem 1 is technically interesting and carefully proven (under directional differentiability and boundedness assumptions), but it guarantees existence of a global minimizer of the regularized empirical objective at finite width—not that the proposed algorithm finds it, nor that generalization is improved. Proposition 1 (self-similar nonlinearities) is useful but narrow; CapNorm is ad-hoc and its effect relative to BN/LayerNorm is not characterized theoretically or empirically.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Provide clean apples-to-apples baselines: run the nonparametric framework with RMSprop/Adam; run parametric nets with CapNorm without ℓ2; allow both sides comparable fine-tuning schedules; match compute budgets (epochs, number of forward/backward passes).
- Ablate each component: CapNorm vs BN (with fixed affine disabled), AdaRad vs RMSprop/Adam within the nonparametric framework, p=1 vs p=2 (sparse matrices vs dense), fan-in vs fan-out, unit addition rates and initialization scales, and the effect of switching λ to zero.
- Report stability/sensitivity: variance across seeds, sensitivity to αr and αφ, and to λ. Include criteria for stopping growth that do not rely on manual annealing or retrospective rewinds.
- Strengthen experiments: add CIFAR-10/CIFAR-100 MLP baselines (and ideally a convolutional extension); compare against strong pruning-from-scratch or structured sparsity/group-lasso baselines; report wall-clock compute and memory overhead vs black-box hyperparameter search.
- Clarify CapNorm: analyze gradients through CapNorm, its interaction with group shrinkage, and whether a principled alternative (e.g., freezing BN scales, decoupled weight decay) would suffice.
- Release code to ensure reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
1. Fairness and protocol: Could you rerun comparisons where both nonparametric and parametric models use the same optimizer (RMSprop/Adam and AdaRad), the same normalization (CapNorm or BN without affine parameters), identical annealing/rewind schedules, and matched training budgets? How do the conclusions change?
2. Ablations: Within the nonparametric framework, what happens if you replace AdaRad with RMSprop/Adam and keep everything else fixed? Conversely, can AdaRad benefit parametric nets?
3. CapNorm: Why is capping at σ>1 the right threshold? Did you test BN with frozen γ/β and/or stop-gradient on BN scales as a control? Please provide ablations isolating CapNorm’s contribution.
4. Regularizer choice: How do results change for p=1 (group lasso promoting sparse connections) and for fan-out penalties? Do you observe different selected widths or generalization?
5. Growth/pruning schedule: How sensitive are results to the unit addition frequency, initialization scale of new units, and the αr = 150/λ heuristic? Could an adaptive addition criterion (e.g., based on gradient norms or loss decrease) yield more stable sizes?
6. Convex dataset: Why does the method select too-small models there? Is this due to CapNorm, the λ schedule, or AdaRad’s angular normalization? Any diagnostics?
7. Poker dataset: Achieving 0% test error is surprising. Can you rule out leakage or duplicated examples across splits? Please detail preprocessing, split methodology, and feature encoding.
8. Theory vs practice: Do you have any convergence guarantees (even local) for the alternating add/prune + AdaRad procedure, or bounds connecting the selected finite width to generalization?
