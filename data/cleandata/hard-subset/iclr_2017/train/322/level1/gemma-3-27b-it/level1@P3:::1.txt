This paper introduces nonparametric neural networks (NNPs), a framework for dynamically adjusting network size during training. The core idea is to optimize not only the weights but also the network’s dimensionality. This is achieved by continuously adding and removing units, guided by an L2 penalty and a novel optimization algorithm, AdaRad. The authors prove theoretical soundness under certain conditions and demonstrate promising results on several datasets.

**Decision: Reject** with a major revision. While the idea is interesting and potentially impactful, the paper suffers from a lack of convincing empirical evidence demonstrating a significant advantage over well-established methods, particularly given the increased complexity. The experimental setup feels somewhat ad-hoc, and the claims of achieving higher accuracy than possible with black-box methods are not strongly supported.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Limited Empirical Advantage:** The core claim is that NNPs achieve higher accuracy than standard parametric networks *and* black-box methods. The results on MNIST are encouraging, showing some improvement over fixed-size networks, but the performance on the Rectangles dataset is mixed, and, critically, the results on the Convex dataset are significantly *worse* than parametric networks. The claim about surpassing black-box methods is not substantiated with a direct comparison; the results only compare against a fixed-size parametric network selected via grid/random search. This weakens the central argument considerably. The poker dataset results are more positive, but the lack of comparison against other methods makes it difficult to evaluate the significance.

2. **Algorithmic Complexity & Justification:**  AdaRad, while novel, adds considerable complexity compared to standard optimizers (SGD, Adam, RMSprop). The justification for its components (radial and angular shifts, running averages) feels somewhat hand-wavy and lacks a strong theoretical grounding beyond the initial soundness proof. The empirical benefits of AdaRad, compared to simply using Adam with appropriate regularization, are not clearly demonstrated. The discussion of computational costs is good, but doesn't provide concrete timing results to show whether the benefits outweigh the additional complexity.

3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity & Tuning:** The paper acknowledges the sensitivity to hyperparameters (λ, αφ, β) but doesn’t offer robust guidance on how to select them. The process of step-size annealing seems ad-hoc and may require significant manual tuning for different datasets. A more systematic hyperparameter optimization strategy would strengthen the findings.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Stronger Baselines:**  A direct comparison against state-of-the-art black-box optimization methods (e.g., Bayesian optimization with Gaussian processes) is crucial. This would convincingly demonstrate whether NNPs truly offer an advantage. A fairer comparison would also involve using a similar computational budget (total training time) for both NNPs and the baseline methods.
* **Ablation Studies:** Perform ablation studies to evaluate the contribution of different components of AdaRad (radial vs. angular shifts, running averages). This would help determine which parts are essential for the observed performance.
* **Scalability Analysis:** Provide more detailed scalability analysis, including training time as a function of dataset size and network complexity.  The poker dataset results are a good start, but a more comprehensive evaluation would be valuable.
* **Theoretical Refinements:** While the soundness proof is a good starting point, it relies on certain assumptions (differentiability, bounded nonlinearities).  Exploring the robustness of the framework to violations of these assumptions would be beneficial.
* **Clarify CapNorm:** The motivation for capping the batch normalization standard deviation isn't fully clear. A more intuitive explanation of why this is necessary in the context of the L2 regularizer would be helpful.
* **Code Release:** Releasing the code would significantly enhance reproducibility and facilitate further research.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Regarding the poor performance on the Convex dataset, can you provide insight into why NNPs struggled in this case? Was it related to the choice of hyperparameters or an inherent limitation of the framework?
2.  What is the computational overhead of AdaRad compared to Adam on a per-iteration basis, especially for large networks? Can you provide timing results?
3.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of the βarith and βquad parameters in AdaRad-M?  Did you perform any hyperparameter optimization for these values?
4.  The step size annealing procedure seems quite specific. What is the rationale behind dividing the angular step size by 3 repeatedly?  Did you explore other annealing schedules?
5.  The paper states that the framework “does not require a starting point” but then performs initialization. Can you clarify the role of initialization in the performance of NNPs? Would completely random initialization also yield comparable results?
