## Review of "NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS"

This paper introduces a novel framework called Nonparametric Neural Networks (NNN) for automatically determining the optimal size of a neural network during a single training cycle. The core idea is to dynamically grow and shrink the network, guided by an L2 penalty on weights, enabling optimization over all possible network sizes. The authors propose a new optimization algorithm, Adaptive Radial-Angular Gradient Descent (AdaRad), to facilitate this process. This is a promising approach to a challenging problem in deep learning, aiming to avoid the computationally expensive grid or random searches typically used for hyperparameter tuning.

I recommend **rejecting** the paper in its current form. While the ideas presented are intriguing, the experimental validation lacks sufficient robustness and the presentation requires significant clarity improvements. Specifically, the results are only compelling for two out of three datasets, and the explanation of AdaRad is complex and hard to follow, hindering reproducibility and evaluation of its contributions.

The paper's strength lies in its theoretical grounding – the soundness of the framework is proven, which is a significant contribution. However, the experimental section, while demonstrating potential, doesn't convincingly establish the superiority of NNNs across a diverse set of problems. The performance on the convex dataset is particularly concerning, where parametric networks significantly outperform the proposed method. Furthermore, the explanation of the algorithm, especially the radial-angular gradient descent, is dense and requires substantial effort to understand. The ablation studies are also lacking; it’s unclear how much of the performance gain comes from the non-parametric aspect versus AdaRad or other specific design choices (like CapNorm). The comparison with exhaustive random search is valuable, but a more comprehensive comparison to other automated machine learning (AutoML) techniques would be beneficial. The choice of hyperparameters for the comparison isn’t clearly justified.

To improve the paper, I suggest the following:

*   **Expand experimental evaluation:** Include more diverse datasets and a wider range of network architectures.
*   **Clarify AdaRad:** Provide a more intuitive explanation of the algorithm, potentially with pseudocode and illustrative examples. A visual representation of the radial-angular gradient descent would be extremely helpful.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct ablation studies to assess the contribution of each component (non-parametric nature, AdaRad, CapNorm).
*   **Comparison to AutoML:** Compare the performance of NNNs to established AutoML techniques (e.g., NAS, Hyperopt).
*   **Address the convex dataset result:** Investigate why NNNs perform poorly on this dataset and discuss potential limitations.
*   **Provide more details on hyperparameter selection:** Justify the choices of hyperparameters used in the experiments.



I have the following questions for the authors:

*   The paper mentions that choosing λ is key. How sensitive is the performance to the choice of λ, and are there any guidelines for choosing an appropriate value?
*   What is the computational overhead of AdaRad compared to standard optimizers like Adam or SGD, and how does this overhead scale with network size?
*   Could you explain the intuition behind the angular and radial components of the AdaRad algorithm and why separating these updates is beneficial?
*   How does the choice of the self-similar nonlinearity impact performance, and would the framework still work with other types of nonlinearities?
