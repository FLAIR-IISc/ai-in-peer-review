- Paper proposes nonparametric neural networks (NNPs) that dynamically add/remove units during training using an L2 penalty and a new optimizer (AdaRad), with a theoretical soundness proof and empirical results.  
- Decision: Reject (major revision) — idea promising but empirical evidence and comparisons are insufficient.

Supporting arguments:
- Limited empirical advantage: mixed results (MNIST slight gain, Convex significantly worse, Rectangles mixed), and no direct comparison to black-box optimization baselines.  
- Algorithmic complexity & justification: AdaRad adds complexity (radial/angular shifts, running averages) with weak theoretical/empirical justification and no concrete timing comparisons to standard optimizers.  
- Hyperparameter sensitivity & tuning: method is sensitive to λ, αφ, β and uses ad-hoc step-size annealing without systematic selection guidance.

Additional feedback:
- Stronger baselines: compare directly to state-of-the-art black-box methods (e.g., Bayesian optimization) under comparable computational budgets.  
- Ablation studies: evaluate the contribution of AdaRad components (radial vs angular shifts, running averages).  
- Scalability analysis: report training time and scalability across dataset sizes and network complexities beyond the poker results.  
- Theoretical refinements: examine robustness when assumptions (differentiability, bounded nonlinearities) are violated.  
- Clarify CapNorm: explain why capping batch-norm std is necessary with the L2 regularizer.  
- Code release: publish code to improve reproducibility.

Questions for the authors:
- Why did NNPs perform poorly on the Convex dataset — hyperparameter choice or framework limitation?  
- What is AdaRad’s per-iteration computational overhead compared to Adam, with timing results for large networks?  
- How sensitive are results to βarith and βquad in AdaRad-M, and was hyperparameter optimization performed?  
- What is the rationale for dividing the angular step size by 3 repeatedly in annealing, and were other schedules explored?  
- The paper claims “does not require a starting point” but uses initialization — what is the role of initialization and would random initialization suffice?
