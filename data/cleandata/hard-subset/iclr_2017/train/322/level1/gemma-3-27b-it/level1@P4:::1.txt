This paper introduces nonparametric neural networks (NNPs), a framework for dynamically adjusting network size during training. The core idea is to optimize not only the weights but also the network dimensions themselves, guided by a p-penalty to prevent unbounded growth. A novel optimization algorithm, AdaRad, is presented to facilitate this process.

**Decision:** Reject. While the core idea is interesting, the paper lacks sufficient empirical evidence to demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods, and the theoretical justification, though presented, feels somewhat disconnected from the practical implementation and results. The presentation also suffers from a lack of clarity in several areas.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Limited Empirical Validation:** The primary weakness lies in the experimental results. While the paper demonstrates performance on three datasets (MNIST, rectangles, and convex), the improvements over standard parametric networks are inconsistent. The results on the convex dataset are particularly concerning, showing *worse* performance than parametric counterparts. The poker dataset results, while promising, aren't explored with sufficient depth. The experiments lack a comprehensive comparison to modern neural architecture search (NAS) techniques like those using reinforcement learning (Zoph & Le, 2017) or differentiable architecture search, which represent the state-of-the-art in automatic network design. The comparison to a simple grid search, as it stands, isn’t sufficient justification.

2. **Clarity and Justification:** The paper presents a significant number of concepts (CapNorm, AdaRad, f-equivalence, etc.) with limited detailed explanation. The motivation for several design choices isn’t always clear. For example, the rationale behind choosing p=2 for the penalty term isn't thoroughly discussed beyond mentioning hardware considerations. While the theorem establishes a theoretical guarantee for finite dimensionality, the connection between this theorem and the practical implications of the p-penalty on network performance is not clearly articulated. The explanation of AdaRad is also quite dense and difficult to follow without careful study. The claims regarding the benefits of AdaRad over other optimizers (RMSprop, Adam) aren't convincingly substantiated by the provided experimental data.

3. **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The paper doesn’t include ablation studies to isolate the impact of different components of the NNP framework. For instance, how much does CapNorm contribute to the performance compared to standard batch normalization? How does AdaRad perform without the angular component of the gradient descent? Without such analysis, it is difficult to assess the relative importance of each innovation.

**Additional Feedback (to improve the paper):**

*   **Expand the Experimental Section:** Include a more thorough comparison with state-of-the-art NAS techniques on a wider range of datasets. Provide more detailed results on the poker dataset, including comparisons with other models.
*   **Clarify the Motivation:** Provide a more intuitive explanation for the choice of p=2 and the advantages it offers. Explain the connection between the theoretical results and the practical behavior of the NNP framework.
*   **Conduct Ablation Studies:** Systematically evaluate the impact of different components of the NNP framework (CapNorm, AdaRad, p-penalty) through ablation experiments.
*   **Improve Clarity:** Restructure the paper to improve clarity and readability. Provide more detailed explanations of key concepts and algorithms. Consider adding diagrams or illustrations to aid understanding.
*   **Address the Convex Dataset Results:** Investigate the poor performance on the convex dataset. Is it a limitation of the framework, or a specific issue with the experimental setup?
*   **Address the Relationship to Existing Work:** Clarify how NNP differs from other dynamic network approaches (Wei et al., 2016; Chen et al., 2016). What specific problems does NNP address that are not handled by these existing methods?

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you elaborate on the justification for choosing p=2, beyond hardware considerations? Are there theoretical reasons why p=2 might be preferable in this context?
*   The paper states that AdaRad is designed to avoid issues with normalization when using a fan-in/out regularizer. Can you provide a more detailed explanation of this problem and how AdaRad specifically addresses it?
*   What is the computational overhead of AdaRad compared to standard optimizers like SGD and Adam? How does this overhead scale with network size?
*   Why does the NNP framework perform poorly on the convex dataset? Are there specific characteristics of this dataset that make it unsuitable for this approach?
*   How does the NNP framework compare to modern NAS techniques like reinforcement learning-based NAS (Zoph & Le, 2017) in terms of computational cost, performance, and ease of implementation?
