This paper introduces Nonparametric Neural Networks (NNPs), a framework for dynamically adjusting network size during training. The core idea is to continuously add and remove units based on an l2 penalty, effectively searching for an optimal network architecture within a single training run. The authors present a novel optimization algorithm, AdaRad, to facilitate this process and demonstrate promising results on several datasets.

**Decision: Reject**

While the idea is interesting and the authors attempt to provide a theoretical justification and a novel training algorithm, the paper suffers from several shortcomings. Primarily, the experimental results aren’t convincingly better than existing methods and the core concept lacks sufficient clarity and practical demonstration of benefit. The novelty, while present, doesn't translate into a substantial enough advantage to warrant acceptance at this time.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Limited Empirical Advantage:** The results presented in section 4 are mixed. While NNPs outperform parametric networks of *the same size* on MNIST and rectangles images, this comparison feels somewhat artificial. The crucial comparison is against well-tuned parametric networks with sizes found through a more conventional hyperparameter search (e.g., random search, Bayesian optimization). While the authors do include this comparison, the NNPs only match or slightly outperform the best parametric networks, and only on rectangles images. On the convex dataset, NNPs perform significantly worse. The claim that NNPs can achieve higher accuracy than "is possible by black-box methods" isn't sufficiently supported by these results. The poker dataset results are promising, but less detailed and harder to interpret given the brief discussion.

2. **Complexity & Clarity of AdaRad:** The AdaRad algorithm is complex and involves several hyperparameters (αr, αφ, β, etc.). The justification for many design choices, particularly the separation of radial and angular updates, is not entirely clear. The explanation feels somewhat hand-wavy and lacks a strong intuitive rationale. Moreover, the explanation of how AdaRad avoids simply shrinking all weights to zero while still allowing for growth is not sufficiently clear. The description lacks specific details on how the parameters are tuned, making it difficult to assess the algorithm’s sensitivity.

3. **Theoretical Justification:** Theorem 1 provides a theoretical foundation, but the conditions for its applicability (specifically, the restriction on 'p' in the penalty term and the requirements on the non-linearities) are relatively strong. The practical relevance of these restrictions isn't fully explored. The proof itself is referenced as being in the appendix, making it difficult to readily verify.

**Additional Feedback:**

* **Comparison to Existing Dynamic Architectures:** The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work, particularly methods that dynamically adjust network architecture during training (e.g., progressive neural networks, neural architecture search). How does NNP differ from and improve upon these existing approaches?
* **Ablation Studies:** Conducting ablation studies to assess the importance of different components of AdaRad (e.g., the radial vs. angular updates, the use of the running averages) would provide valuable insights.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A sensitivity analysis of the key hyperparameters (λ, αr, αφ, β) would help understand the robustness of the approach.
* **Computational Cost:** A more detailed analysis of the computational cost of NNPs compared to training standard networks would be useful. While the authors briefly mention the cost, a more quantitative comparison is needed.
* **Visualization of Network Growth:** Visualizing how the network architecture evolves during training (e.g., a plot of the number of units in each layer over time) could provide a better understanding of the learning process.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1. The claim is made that nonparametric networks can potentially achieve *higher* accuracy than black-box methods. Given that more sophisticated black-box methods like Bayesian optimization and reinforcement learning have been shown to be effective, can you elaborate on the scenarios where you believe NNPs would truly outperform these approaches? Please provide quantitative evidence if possible.
2. The AdaRad algorithm has several hyperparameters. Can you provide more details on how these hyperparameters were tuned during your experiments? What sensitivity analysis did you perform?
3. The conditions on the nonlinearities in Theorem 1 seem restrictive. How sensitive are the results to violations of these conditions? Have you experimented with nonlinearities that do not strictly satisfy the stated requirements?
4. You mention that the benefits of nonparametric networks are most pronounced when there is no prior information about the task. Can you quantify this claim? How does the performance of NNPs degrade as the amount of prior information increases?
5. Could you provide more detail on the computational resources (e.g., GPUs, training time) required to train a NNP compared to a similarly performing parametric network on the poker dataset?
