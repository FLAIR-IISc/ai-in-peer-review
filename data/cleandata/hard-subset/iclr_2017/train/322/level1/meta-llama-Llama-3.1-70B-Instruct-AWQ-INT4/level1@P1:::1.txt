This paper introduces a novel framework for automatically adapting and choosing the size of a neural network during a single training run, which the authors term "nonparametric neural networks." The key idea is to dynamically add and remove units from the network as needed, allowing the network to adapt its size to the task at hand.

The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis of the framework, including a proof that the objective function attains a global minimum at some finite dimensionality when using a fan-in or fan-out regularizer. They also introduce a new optimization algorithm, Adaptive Radial-Angular Gradient Descent (AdaRad), which is designed to work effectively with the nonparametric framework.

The experimental results are promising, with nonparametric networks outperforming parametric networks of the same size on two out of three datasets. The authors also demonstrate the scalability of the framework by applying it to a large dataset.

Overall, the paper presents a compelling case for the use of nonparametric neural networks, and the theoretical analysis and experimental results provide strong support for the approach.
