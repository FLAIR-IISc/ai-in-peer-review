

# Peer Review: "Nonparametric Neural Networks"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces a novel framework for automatically selecting neural network size during training, termed "nonparametric neural networks." The key contribution is a method that dynamically grows and shrinks network size during training to find an optimal architecture without requiring prior information about the task. The authors prove that their approach achieves a global minimum at finite dimensionality when using fan-in or fan-out regularizers (Theorem 1). They introduce two key components: CapNorm (a modified batch normalization layer) and AdaRad (an adaptive training algorithm that handles the nonparametric nature of the optimization). The method is theoretically grounded and empirically evaluated across multiple datasets, demonstrating promising results on MNIST and the poker dataset, though with mixed results on the convex dataset.

## 2. Decision

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper makes a significant theoretical and methodological contribution to the problem of neural network architecture selection. The core idea is novel, well-motivated, and supported by strong theoretical foundations. The empirical results demonstrate the potential of the approach, though some aspects of the evaluation require clarification.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

### Theoretical Contribution
The paper's theoretical contribution is particularly strong. Theorem 1 provides a rigorous justification for the nonparametric approach, proving that the optimization problem attains a global minimum at finite dimensionality under reasonable conditions. This is a significant advance over existing methods that either require manual architecture selection or use black-box optimization with no theoretical guarantees. The proof is thorough and addresses potential degeneration issues that could arise with infinitely large networks.

### Methodological Innovation
The authors' introduction of CapNorm and AdaRad is well-motivated and addresses key challenges in training nonparametric networks. The theoretical insight that self-similar nonlinearities (like ReLU) eliminate the need for layer-specific regularization parameters (Proposition 1) is particularly elegant and practical. The AdaRad algorithm cleverly decouples radial and angular updates to handle the unique optimization challenges of nonparametric networks.

### Empirical Results
The empirical results are generally positive and demonstrate the method's potential:
- On MNIST, the nonparametric approach achieves accuracy comparable to the best parametric networks while using substantially fewer parameters (144 vs. 694 units).
- On the poker dataset, the method achieves near-perfect accuracy (0% test error) on a large-scale problem with no prior information.
- The analysis of the training process (Figure 3) provides valuable insights into how the network size evolves and stabilizes.

The main limitation is the poor performance on the convex dataset, which suggests the method may not generalize universally. However, the authors acknowledge this limitation and provide a reasonable explanation (the tendency to converge to smaller networks hurts performance when the task requires more capacity).

### Comparison to Related Work
The paper effectively situates itself within the literature, distinguishing from both model selection methods (grid search, Bayesian optimization) and network pruning approaches. The key differentiator is that their method dynamically adjusts network size during training rather than requiring a pre-trained network as a starting point.

## 4. Additional Feedback

1. **Clarify the "same size" comparison**: The paper claims nonparametric networks achieve higher accuracy than parametric networks of the same size, but the MNIST results show a smaller nonparametric network achieving similar accuracy to a larger parametric network. The authors should explicitly demonstrate that their nonparametric network achieves higher accuracy than a parametric network of *exactly the same size* (not just the size of the final nonparametric network).

2. **Address the convex dataset issue**: The authors should provide a more detailed analysis of why the method fails on the convex dataset. Is it a specific property of the dataset, or does it indicate a fundamental limitation of the approach? This would strengthen the paper's contribution by clarifying the method's applicability boundaries.

3. **Report variance across runs**: The paper mentions training 10 networks for each λ value and selecting a representative, but doesn't report the variance in results across these runs. Including standard deviations or error bars would strengthen the empirical claims.

4. **Computational efficiency**: While the paper mentions that AdaRad's computational cost scales more gracefully than RMSprop, a more detailed comparison of wall-clock time would be valuable for practitioners.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. In the convex dataset experiments, the nonparametric networks performed worse than parametric networks of the same size. Could this be due to the specific properties of the convex dataset, or does it suggest a fundamental limitation of the method? What would be the expected behavior if the convex dataset were more complex?

2. The paper states that "nonparametric networks achieve a higher accuracy than networks of the same size that are trained from scratch." Could the authors clarify how they measured this, given that the nonparametric networks end up with different sizes than the parametric networks they're compared to?

3. The authors use a fan-in regularizer with p=2, which produces dense weight matrices. Why not use p=1 for sparser matrices, especially given the interest in sparse networks for hardware efficiency? How would this change the performance and the theoretical guarantees?

4. The paper mentions that AdaRad-M (the momentum variant) performed similarly to AdaRad. Given that momentum is widely used in practice, why did the authors choose to focus on AdaRad rather than AdaRad-M in the main text?

This paper makes a compelling contribution to the problem of neural network architecture selection. The theoretical foundation is strong, the methodological innovation is clear, and the empirical results are promising. With minor clarifications to the evaluation and comparisons, this work would be a valuable addition to the ICLR proceedings.
