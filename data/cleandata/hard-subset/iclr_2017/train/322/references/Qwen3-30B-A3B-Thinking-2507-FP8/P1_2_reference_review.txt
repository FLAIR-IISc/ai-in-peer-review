

# Peer Review: "NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces a novel framework called "nonparametric neural networks" that automatically adapts network size during training, eliminating the need for expensive black-box model selection methods. The authors propose a theoretically grounded approach where the network dimensionality is treated as a parameter to be optimized alongside weights, with a fan-in or fan-out regularizer ensuring convergence to a finite network size. They introduce two key components: CapNorm (a modified batch normalization technique) and AdaRad (an adaptive gradient descent algorithm that handles the unique challenges of dynamically changing network size). The paper makes several significant contributions: (1) a theoretical proof showing that nonparametric networks achieve a global minimum at finite dimensionality under appropriate conditions; (2) a practical framework for training such networks; and (3) empirical evidence demonstrating that nonparametric networks can outperform parametric networks of the same size while achieving results competitive with exhaustive search methods.

The paper is well-structured and clearly motivated, with a strong theoretical foundation supporting the practical contributions. The authors demonstrate their approach on multiple standard datasets, showing that nonparametric networks can achieve higher accuracy than parametric networks of the same size on MNIST and rectangles images, and competitive performance with exhaustive search on these datasets. The framework also scales to larger datasets like poker, maintaining strong performance.

## 2. Decision

**Accept** with minor revisions.

The key reasons for acceptance are:
1. The problem addressed (automatically determining optimal network size) is important and well-motivated, with a clear explanation of the limitations of existing black-box model selection approaches.
2. The theoretical foundation is strong, with a rigorous proof of Theorem 1 showing that nonparametric networks converge to finite size under appropriate conditions.
3. The experimental results are promising and provide credible evidence supporting the main claims, with careful comparisons to parametric networks and exhaustive search methods.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper makes a compelling case for its contribution. The theoretical contribution is particularly strong - Theorem 1 is proven in the appendix with careful attention to the necessary conditions, and the proof is technically sound. The authors demonstrate that their approach avoids the degeneration problem that would occur if networks could grow indefinitely by using appropriate regularization.

The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-designed. The authors compare nonparametric networks to parametric networks of the same size (finding that nonparametric networks outperform them on MNIST and rectangles images), and to exhaustive random search (finding competitive results on MNIST and rectangles images). The ablation studies on the training process (Figure 3) provide valuable insights into how the network size evolves during training and why the approach works.

The paper is well-placed in the literature. The authors thoroughly review related work on network pruning, growing networks during training, and model selection methods, clearly articulating how their approach differs and improves upon previous work. The theoretical connection to sparse regression (via the p-norm regularization) is well-explained and well-referenced.

The main concern is the performance on the convex dataset (Section 4.1), where nonparametric networks performed worse than parametric networks of the same size. While the paper acknowledges this limitation, it would be helpful to have a more detailed analysis of why the approach fails on this particular dataset. The authors note that "the apparent tendency of nonparametric networks to converge to relatively small sizes hurts us here," but it would be valuable to know whether this issue is specific to the convex dataset or could be addressed with modifications to the approach.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong overall, I have a few suggestions to improve it further:

1. **Clarify the computational cost**: The paper mentions that AdaRad incurs additional computational cost but argues it scales more gracefully than RMSprop. A more detailed analysis of the computational overhead (especially in terms of wall-clock time) compared to standard training would be helpful. The authors should provide a clear comparison of the time required to train a nonparametric network versus a parametric network of the same final size.

2. **Address the convex dataset issue more thoroughly**: The poor performance on the convex dataset is surprising given the strong results on other datasets. The authors should provide more analysis of why this particular dataset is problematic for their approach. Is it related to the nature of the data distribution, the specific architecture used, or something else? This would strengthen the paper by addressing a limitation and potentially suggesting future improvements.

3. **Discuss practical considerations for large-scale applications**: While the poker dataset experiment is promising, the paper could provide more concrete guidance on how to apply this approach to very large-scale problems. For example, how should the unit addition rate be set for extremely large datasets? What are the memory requirements for maintaining the dynamic network structure?

4. **Improve the presentation of Theorem 1**: The theorem is stated in Section 2, but the proof is in the appendix. While this is acceptable, the paper could provide a more intuitive explanation of why the theorem holds, especially for readers who might not be familiar with the technical details of the proof.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper states that "nonparametric networks performed badly on the convex dataset" and attributes this to their tendency to converge to smaller sizes. However, it's surprising that a method that can outperform parametric networks of the same size on MNIST and rectangles images would fail on the convex dataset. Could you provide more insight into why this particular dataset is challenging for your approach? Is there a specific property of the convex dataset that makes it difficult for the nonparametric framework?

2. In the scalability experiment with the poker dataset, you mention adding a unit every tenth of an epoch instead of every epoch. How did you determine this rate, and how sensitive is the approach to this parameter? Would it be possible to automatically adjust this rate based on the dataset size or other characteristics?

3. The paper mentions that "the time it took those networks to converge to a stable network size was a much smaller fraction of the overall training time under poker compared to the smaller datasets." Could you quantify this difference? For example, how many epochs did it take to reach a stable network size relative to the total training time for MNIST versus poker?

4. The authors claim that "nonparametric networks can achieve higher accuracy than networks of the same size that are trained from scratch." However, in the MNIST results, the nonparametric network is substantially smaller than the parametric network (144 vs 694 units). Could you clarify whether the accuracy comparison is between networks of the same size, or whether the nonparametric network achieves better accuracy with fewer parameters? The paper would benefit from a clearer explanation of this point.

These questions are intended to help clarify aspects of the paper that would strengthen the contribution and ensure the results are properly understood. The paper is already strong, and these suggestions would help make it even better.
