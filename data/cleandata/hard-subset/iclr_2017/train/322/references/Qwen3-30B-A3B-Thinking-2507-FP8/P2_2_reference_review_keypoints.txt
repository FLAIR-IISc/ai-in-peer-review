- Introduces "nonparametric neural networks" that adapt network size during a single training run.  
- Proves (Theorem 1) that with fan-in/fan-out regularizers (λ>0, 1≤p<∞) the optimization has a global minimum at finite dimensionality.  
- Proposes a new normalization layer (CapNorm) tailored to the nonparametric framework.  
- Introduces AdaRad, a novel training algorithm designed for nonparametric networks.  
- Empirical results: nonparametric networks often outperform parametric networks of the same size and can match or beat exhaustive random search.  
- Demonstrates scalability on a large dataset (poker, ~1.025M examples).  
- Recommendation: accept — significant contribution with strong theory and empirical evidence.  
- Theoretical contribution is rigorous and handles directional differentiability of nonlinearities.  
- Practical contribution: AdaRad and CapNorm are well-designed and effective in experiments (MNIST, rectangles).  
- Positions work clearly against pruning and black-box optimization, highlighting differences and advantages.  
- Experiments are thorough with careful hyperparameter tuning and detailed training analysis.  
- Limitation: performs poorly on the convex dataset; needs deeper analysis of failure modes.  
- Suggests comparing with recent automatic architecture methods (e.g., NAS) and adaptive pruning techniques.  
- Calls for a more detailed analysis of AdaRad’s computational cost versus SGD and trade-offs.  
- Requests clearer explanation of the role and necessity of self-similar nonlinearities (e.g., ReLU).  
- Asks for clarification on convex dataset failure and dataset characteristics that cause it.  
- Requests empirical comparison between p=1 and p=2 regularizers within the framework.  
- Asks how different unit-addition rates affect performance and what rates are optimal per dataset.  
- Requests explanation why nonparametric training can yield higher accuracy than same-size networks trained from scratch.  
- Asks how computational cost scales with dataset size and compares to traditional black-box model selection methods.
