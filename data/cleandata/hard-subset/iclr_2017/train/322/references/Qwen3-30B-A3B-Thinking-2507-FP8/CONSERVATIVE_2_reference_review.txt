

# Review of "NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS"

## Summary of Contribution
The paper introduces "nonparametric neural networks" that dynamically adjust their size during training by adding and removing units based on a regularization scheme. The authors claim this approach can automatically select optimal network size during a single training run without needing to run multiple separate training runs (as in traditional hyperparameter search), and that the resulting networks achieve better accuracy than networks of the same size trained from scratch. They present theoretical justification (Theorem 1) and experimental results on three small datasets (MNIST, rectangles images, convex dataset).

## Decision
Reject. The paper presents a novel idea but lacks sufficient empirical validation against established baselines and fails to demonstrate the approach's value for real-world deep learning challenges.

## Supporting Arguments

The paper's core claim—that nonparametric networks can outperform parametric networks of the same size and even surpass exhaustive random search for model selection—is not sufficiently supported by the evidence provided. The experimental evaluation is severely limited in scope:

1. The paper evaluates on only three small datasets (MNIST, rectangles images, convex dataset), which are not representative of modern deep learning challenges. The convex dataset results are particularly concerning—the nonparametric approach performed poorly compared to parametric networks, yet the paper doesn't adequately address this failure or its implications for the approach's generalizability.

2. The comparison with parametric networks is superficial. The authors don't compare with state-of-the-art methods for dynamic network size adjustment (e.g., progressive resizing, dynamic pruning methods) that have been shown to be effective in recent literature. The claim that "nonparametric networks substantially outperform parametric networks of the same size" on MNIST is not convincingly demonstrated against the most relevant baselines.

3. The theoretical justification (Theorem 1) is limited to a specific case (p=2, fan-in regularizer) and doesn't address the broader applicability of the approach. The proof in the appendix is complex and doesn't clearly state the limitations of the theoretical results.

4. The paper claims to outperform exhaustive random search, but doesn't provide sufficient detail on how the random search was conducted or why their method is better. The comparison in Figure 2 is limited to a small number of network sizes and doesn't demonstrate the approach's value on larger, more complex tasks.

5. The paper's "scalability" experiment on the poker dataset is misleading—it shows the approach works on a large dataset, but the dataset itself is simple (14 features, binary classification), which doesn't represent the complexity of modern deep learning tasks.

## Additional Feedback

1. The authors should conduct experiments on more challenging datasets (CIFAR-10, ImageNet) to properly evaluate the approach's value for modern deep learning tasks.

2. The paper should provide a thorough comparison with recent dynamic architecture search methods (e.g., Progressive Neural Architecture Search, Dynamic Filter Pruning) that also adjust network size during training.

3. The failure on the convex dataset needs to be thoroughly analyzed to understand the limitations of the approach. Why does it work on MNIST but not on the convex dataset? This is critical for understanding the approach's applicability.

4. The paper should provide more details about computational costs and training time compared to traditional hyperparameter search methods, especially for larger datasets.

5. The theoretical results should be more clearly contextualized with their limitations and assumptions.

## Questions for Authors

1. Why does the approach fail on the convex dataset when it works well on MNIST and rectangles images? What are the fundamental limitations of the method that cause this failure?

2. How does the proposed method compare to state-of-the-art dynamic architecture search methods (e.g., Progressive Neural Architecture Search, Dynamic Filter Pruning) on standard benchmarks like CIFAR-10?

3. What are the computational costs of the proposed method compared to traditional hyperparameter search methods, especially on larger datasets?

4. How does the approach scale to convolutional networks and more complex architectures? The paper focuses only on fully connected networks.

5. How sensitive is the approach to the choice of hyperparameters (λ, αr, αφ), and how would the authors recommend selecting these for new tasks without extensive tuning?

The paper's core idea is interesting, but the lack of thorough empirical validation against established baselines and the limited scope of experiments prevent it from making a convincing contribution to the field. The authors would need to substantially strengthen their empirical evaluation to support their claims.
