

# Peer Review: "NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contribution

The paper introduces a novel framework called "nonparametric neural networks" for automatically selecting optimal neural network architecture during a single training run. The authors address the computational inefficiency of current model selection methods (grid search, random search, Bayesian optimization) that require multiple full training runs for different network sizes. Their approach dynamically adds and removes neurons during training based on a regularization scheme, allowing the network to adapt its size while training. The paper provides a theoretical foundation (Theorem 1) proving that with appropriate regularization (fan-in or fan-out regularizers with p=2), nonparametric networks achieve a global training error minimum at some finite dimensionality. They introduce a novel normalization layer (CapNorm) and training algorithm (AdaRad) to enable this process. Empirical results demonstrate that nonparametric networks achieve higher accuracy than parametric networks of the same size on MNIST and rectangles images datasets, and sometimes outperform exhaustive random search methods. The approach also scales to larger datasets like the poker dataset from OpenML.

## 2. Decision

**Decision: Accept**

**Key reasons for acceptance:**
1. The paper addresses a meaningful and practical problem in neural network design with a novel, theoretically sound approach.
2. The theoretical contribution (Theorem 1) is rigorous and well-motivated, providing a solid foundation for the proposed method.
3. The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate clear advantages over baseline approaches on two of three benchmark datasets.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of neural network architecture design. The problem it addresses is well-posed: current model selection methods are computationally expensive because they require training multiple networks of different sizes, and they don't exploit the opportunity to improve the network size during training. The authors' approach solves both problems by dynamically adjusting the network size during a single training run.

The theoretical foundation is particularly strong. Theorem 1 proves that with the right regularization, nonparametric networks will converge to a finite size that minimizes the training error. The proof is thorough and addresses potential degeneration issues where networks could grow indefinitely. The paper provides a detailed analysis of the training process (Figure 3), showing how units are added and removed, and how the network size converges.

The empirical results are convincing. On MNIST and rectangles images, nonparametric networks outperformed parametric networks of the same size and even achieved results competitive with exhaustive random search methods. The detailed analysis of the training process (Figure 3) provides valuable insights into how the network adapts its size during training. The scalability test on the poker dataset (1.025 million datapoints) demonstrates that the approach can work on larger datasets.

The paper is well-motivated and places itself well in the literature. It clearly distinguishes itself from related work on network pruning, dynamic network growth, and Bayesian methods by emphasizing that it doesn't require a pre-trained network as a starting point. The authors also provide a thorough discussion of related work in Section 5.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, there are a few areas where the authors could improve the presentation and clarity:

1. **Terminology clarification**: The paper uses "nonparametric" to describe networks with variable size, which is confusing because in statistics, "nonparametric" refers to methods without fixed parameter counts. The authors should clarify this terminology upfront to avoid confusion.

2. **More detailed comparison with recent NAS methods**: While Section 5 discusses related work, the authors could more explicitly compare their approach to recent Neural Architecture Search (NAS) methods that also aim to automatically find good network architectures.

3. **Computational cost analysis**: The paper mentions that AdaRad incurs additional computational cost but scales more gracefully than RMSprop. Providing concrete numbers on how much slower AdaRad is compared to standard optimizers like Adam would strengthen the paper.

4. **Analysis of convex dataset failure**: The paper notes that the approach performed poorly on the convex dataset. A more in-depth analysis of why this happened and what modifications could make the approach more robust would strengthen the paper's contribution.

5. **Unit addition schedule justification**: The paper adds one unit per epoch but doesn't fully justify this choice. An ablation study showing the effect of different unit addition rates on performance would be valuable.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper uses "nonparametric" to describe networks with variable size, which conflicts with the standard statistical meaning of the term. How do you justify this terminology, and what alternative terms might be better?

2. On the convex dataset, nonparametric networks performed worse than parametric networks. Could you provide more insight into why this might be the case? Are there specific characteristics of the convex dataset that make it particularly challenging for this approach?

3. The paper states that AdaRad has "additional computational cost" but scales more gracefully than RMSprop. Could you provide specific numbers on how much slower AdaRad is compared to Adam or RMSprop on standard datasets?

4. In the theoretical proof (Section 7.1), the authors assume σ(0) = 0 for the nonlinearities. How would the approach change if we used nonlinearities like sigmoid or tanh (which don't satisfy σ(0) = 0)? Would the approach still work with modifications?

5. The paper mentions that the approach is most useful when there's no prior information about the dataset. Could you elaborate on how the approach would differ if there were some prior information (e.g., knowledge about appropriate network size for similar tasks)?

---

This paper presents a strong contribution that addresses a meaningful problem in neural network design with both theoretical rigor and empirical validation. The proposed framework offers a practical solution to an important computational challenge in model selection, and the results demonstrate clear advantages over existing approaches on multiple datasets. I recommend acceptance with the suggested improvements.
