

# Peer Review: NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Contribution

This paper introduces a novel framework called "nonparametric neural networks" for automatically selecting optimal network architecture during a single training run. The authors address the problem of neural network model selection, which traditionally requires expensive multiple training runs to evaluate different architectures. Their key insight is that by allowing the network size to dynamically change during training (with regularization to prevent unbounded growth), they can find an optimal architecture without needing prior knowledge about the task.

The paper makes several significant contributions: (1) a formal theoretical foundation proving that nonparametric networks with fan-in or fan-out regularizers attain a global minimum at finite dimensionality; (2) the introduction of CapNorm (a variant of batch normalization compatible with their framework); (3) the AdaRad training algorithm that handles the nonparametric nature of the optimization; and (4) empirical validation across multiple datasets showing that nonparametric networks can achieve higher accuracy than parametric networks of the same size, while using fewer parameters.

The authors demonstrate that their approach can outperform traditional model selection methods on MNIST and rectangles images datasets, while providing a theoretically sound framework for automatic network size selection. The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and makes a significant contribution to the field of neural network architecture search.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper makes a strong theoretical and empirical contribution to the problem of neural network model selection. The theoretical foundation is rigorous, the methodological innovations are well-motivated and novel, and the empirical results compellingly support the authors' claims.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

**Theoretical Soundness (Key Strength):** The paper's most compelling contribution is its theoretical foundation. Theorem 1 proves that with appropriate regularization (fan-in or fan-out regularizers), nonparametric neural networks attain a global minimum at finite dimensionality. The proof in the appendix is thorough and addresses potential concerns about unbounded network growth. This theoretical justification is crucial for establishing the validity of the framework and distinguishes it from other approaches that lack such guarantees.

**Methodological Innovation:** The introduction of CapNorm and AdaRad are well-motivated responses to the specific challenges of training nonparametric networks. The paper clearly explains why standard batch normalization and gradient descent methods cannot be directly applied, and how their modifications address these challenges. The detailed explanation of how AdaRad handles radial and angular components of the gradient is particularly elegant.

**Empirical Validation:** The empirical evaluation across multiple datasets (MNIST, rectangles images, convex, and poker) demonstrates that nonparametric networks can achieve higher accuracy than parametric networks of the same size on two out of three benchmark datasets, while using significantly fewer parameters in some cases. The analysis of the training process (Figure 3) provides valuable insights into how the network size evolves during training. The scalability results on the large poker dataset further demonstrate the practical applicability of the approach.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

While the paper is strong overall, a few points could enhance its impact:

1. **Clarify the computational overhead:** The paper mentions that AdaRad incurs additional computational cost but scales more gracefully than RMSprop. A more detailed comparison of training time per epoch for nonparametric vs. parametric networks would strengthen this claim. How much longer does training take with nonparametric networks compared to standard parametric networks?

2. **Address the convex dataset failure:** The nonparametric networks performed poorly on the convex dataset. A brief analysis of why this might be the case (e.g., the nature of the dataset, the regularization strength, or the specific architecture) would provide valuable insights and help readers understand the limitations of the approach.

3. **Compare to more recent architecture search methods:** While the paper mentions related work on model selection, it would benefit from a more direct comparison to recent neural architecture search (NAS) methods like DARTS or ENAS, which also aim to find optimal architectures but through different mechanisms.

4. **Clarify the relationship to pruning:** The paper correctly notes that their approach differs from pruning methods, but it could more explicitly explain how it relates to the broader literature on neural network pruning and compression.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper states that the network size is chosen during training, but it's not entirely clear how the final network size is determined. Is it simply the size at which validation error stops improving, or is there a specific stopping criterion? How does this compare to traditional early stopping?

2. The authors mention that p=2 is preferred over p=1 for hardware considerations. Could you elaborate on the specific hardware considerations that make dense weight matrices more favorable than sparse ones in practice?

3. The paper states that nonparametric networks "achieve a higher accuracy than networks of the same size that are trained from scratch." What specific improvements in accuracy did you observe compared to training a parametric network of the same size from scratch (rather than comparing to a parametric network of the same size selected via random search)?

4. The theoretical proof requires that σ(0) = 0. How would the approach be modified to work with activation functions like sigmoid that don't satisfy this condition (e.g., by adding a small constant to the input)?

These questions would help clarify the practical implementation and theoretical scope of the proposed framework. The paper makes a strong contribution to the field, and addressing these points would further strengthen its impact.
