

# Review

## 1. Summary of the paper's claims

This paper introduces "nonparametric neural networks," a fundamentally novel approach to neural network architecture selection. The authors propose a framework where network size is dynamically determined during a single training run, rather than through black-box optimization methods that require multiple training runs for different architectures. The core contribution is a theoretical guarantee (Theorem 1) showing that with proper fan-in or fan-out regularization, nonparametric networks converge to a finite size. The paper also introduces a novel normalization layer (CapNorm) and a training algorithm (AdaRad) that enables this dynamic adjustment of network size. Experimental results demonstrate that nonparametric networks can outperform parametric networks of the same size on two out of three benchmark datasets, while achieving results competitive with exhaustive random search. The approach fundamentally shifts the paradigm from static architecture selection to dynamic architecture adaptation during training.

## 2. Decision

Accept

## 3. Supporting arguments for decision

This paper presents a highly novel and bold approach to a fundamental problem in deep learning: automatic neural network architecture selection. The core concept of nonparametric neural networks represents a significant departure from conventional wisdom, moving from static, post-hoc architecture selection to dynamic, in-training architecture adaptation. This is not merely an incremental improvement to existing model selection methods but a paradigm shift in how we think about neural network design.

The theoretical foundation (Theorem 1) is strong and well-motivated, providing a rigorous justification for the approach. The proof, while complex, is carefully constructed and addresses a critical concern about network degeneration that would otherwise undermine the entire approach.

The experimental results are compelling, particularly for MNIST and rectangles images datasets where nonparametric networks substantially outperform parametric networks of the same size. The scalability to the large poker dataset further demonstrates the practical relevance of the approach. While performance on the convex dataset is disappointing, this is common with new methods and doesn't invalidate the core concept.

As an ICLR reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold approaches over methodological perfection, I find this paper exceptionally strong. It introduces a significant new concept that could potentially reshape how we approach neural network architecture design, and the authors have provided sufficient evidence to support their claims. The paper's contribution represents a clear step forward in the field rather than an incremental improvement to existing methods.

## 4. Additional feedback

- The authors should provide more detailed discussion about why the approach underperforms on the convex dataset. Is this a fundamental limitation of the approach, or could it be addressed with additional hyperparameter tuning or modifications to the framework?

- While the paper mentions future work on extending to convolutional layers, a more concrete discussion of how the approach would need to be adapted for different architectures would strengthen the paper.

- The computational cost of AdaRad compared to standard optimizers like Adam should be more thoroughly analyzed, including runtime measurements on larger datasets.

- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the relationship between the regularization parameter λ and the resulting network size, as this is a key design choice for practitioners.

## 5. Questions for the authors

- What specific characteristics of the convex dataset cause the nonparametric approach to underperform, and how might these be addressed?

- Can you provide more intuition about why the fan-in regularizer with p=2 works particularly well for this approach, rather than other regularization schemes?

- How would the nonparametric framework need to be modified to handle convolutional layers, and what potential challenges might arise in that context?

- The paper mentions that "the best nonparametric network made no mistake on the test set of 100,000 examples" for the poker dataset. How does this compare to the best results reported on OpenML for this dataset, and what does this suggest about the potential of the approach for real-world applications?
