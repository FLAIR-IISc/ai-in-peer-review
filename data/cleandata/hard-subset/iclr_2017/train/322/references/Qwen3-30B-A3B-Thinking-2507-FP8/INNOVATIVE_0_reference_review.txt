

# Review of "NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims

This paper introduces a novel framework called "nonparametric neural networks" that dynamically adjusts network size during a single training run to automatically select an optimal architecture without prior information. The key innovation is the theoretical justification (Theorem 1) that nonparametric networks with fan-in or fan-out regularizers achieve a global minimum at finite dimensionality, avoiding the degeneration problem of infinitely growing networks. The paper presents two technical contributions: CapNorm (a modified batch normalization layer compatible with the framework) and AdaRad (an adaptive training algorithm that handles the unique optimization challenges of nonparametric networks). The authors demonstrate through experiments that their approach can achieve competitive or better performance than parametric networks of the same size, and sometimes outperform exhaustive random search methods, on two out of three benchmark datasets.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper introduces a fundamentally novel approach to neural network architecture selection with strong theoretical foundations, addressing a significant problem in deep learning with a bold and creative solution.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's core contribution—nonparametric neural networks—represents a genuinely novel direction in model selection. While there is related work on network pruning and growing networks during training, this paper is the first to provide a theoretically sound framework for dynamically adjusting network size during training without requiring a high-performing starting network. The theoretical justification (Theorem 1) is rigorous and well-motivated, resolving a key concern about degeneration in such approaches.

The novelty of the approach is particularly compelling because it tackles the fundamental problem of model selection in a way that doesn't require multiple expensive training runs. As the authors note, current methods (grid search, random search, Bayesian optimization) require multiple full network training runs, which is computationally prohibitive for large models. The nonparametric framework solves this problem by selecting the optimal architecture during a single training run.

The experimental results are promising for two of three datasets (MNIST and rectangles images), showing that nonparametric networks can achieve comparable or better performance than parametric networks of the same size, while being substantially smaller. The analysis in Section 4.2 provides valuable insights into how the nonparametric training process works, showing that units are added and removed in a principled way that leads to an optimal architecture.

While the results on the convex dataset are disappointing, this is not a fatal flaw for a novel approach. The authors provide a thoughtful explanation for why this might be the case (the tendency to converge to small sizes hurts performance when the problem requires larger models), and this limitation actually highlights an important direction for future work rather than undermining the core contribution.

## 4. Additional Feedback

The paper would benefit from:
- A more thorough comparison with recent neural architecture search (NAS) methods that have emerged since the cited literature (e.g., DARTS, ENAS, etc.), as these have become the state-of-the-art for automatic model selection.
- A more detailed discussion of the computational cost comparison between the nonparametric approach and traditional black-box optimization methods, including both training time and resource requirements.
- Clarification on how the approach scales to larger datasets and more complex architectures beyond the simple feedforward networks tested in the paper.
- A discussion of potential limitations in real-world applications where training time might be constrained, as the gradual addition of units could lead to longer training times than some alternatives.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper mentions that nonparametric networks performed poorly on the convex dataset. Could you elaborate on why this might be the case, and whether there are specific modifications to the framework that could improve performance on such datasets?

2. The paper claims that nonparametric networks can achieve higher accuracy than black-box methods. However, the experiments only compare to exhaustive random search. How would the approach scale to more complex model spaces (e.g., with convolutional layers, different activation functions), and how would it compare to state-of-the-art NAS methods?

3. In Figure 3, the nonparametric network shows a slow improvement in validation error over many epochs. Given that the approach requires adding units gradually, how would the authors suggest balancing the trade-off between training time and final performance in practical applications?

4. The paper uses p=2 regularization for the fan-in. Could you explain why p=2 was chosen over p=1 (which would produce sparser weight matrices), and how the choice of p might affect the final architecture?

The paper presents a bold, novel approach to a significant problem in deep learning with strong theoretical foundations and promising experimental results. The minor limitations noted do not undermine the core contribution, which represents a meaningful step toward automating neural network architecture selection.
