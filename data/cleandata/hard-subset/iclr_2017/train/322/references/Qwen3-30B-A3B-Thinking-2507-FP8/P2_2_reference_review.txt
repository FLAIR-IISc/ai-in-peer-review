

# Review of "NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces a novel framework for automatically adapting and choosing the size of a neural network during a single training run, termed "nonparametric neural networks." The authors propose a theoretical foundation showing that the optimization problem for nonparametric networks has a global minimum at finite dimensionality when using appropriate regularizers. They introduce a novel normalization layer (CapNorm) that works with the nonparametric framework and a new training algorithm (AdaRad) designed specifically for nonparametric networks. The paper demonstrates through experiments that nonparametric networks can achieve higher accuracy than parametric networks of the same size on multiple datasets, and sometimes outperform even exhaustive random search methods. The approach addresses a significant practical challenge in machine learning model selection by eliminating the need for multiple full training runs, which is computationally expensive.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of automatic model selection in neural networks, with a strong theoretical foundation and compelling experimental results on multiple datasets. The approach is well-motivated, well-placed in the literature, and the claims are supported by both theoretical proofs and empirical evidence.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

**Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides a rigorous proof (in Section 7.1) of Theorem 1, showing that nonparametric neural networks achieve a global training error minimum at some finite dimensionality when using fan-in or fan-out regularizers with λ > 0 and 1 ≤ p < ∞. This is a significant theoretical contribution that justifies the approach. The proof is comprehensive, addressing the conditions under which the theorem holds, and carefully handles the directional differentiability of the nonlinearities, which is important for practical neural networks.

**Practical Contribution:** The proposed AdaRad algorithm and CapNorm layer are well-designed to work with the nonparametric framework. The paper demonstrates that nonparametric networks can achieve better performance than parametric networks of the same size on MNIST and rectangles images datasets, and are competitive with exhaustive random search methods. The scalability results on the poker dataset (1.025 million data points) show that the approach can be applied to large-scale problems.

**Comparison to Related Work:** The paper effectively positions itself against prior work on model selection, pruning, and network growth. It clearly articulates how its approach differs from existing methods, particularly noting that it doesn't require a high-performing starting point (unlike pruning methods) and doesn't require multiple full training runs (unlike black-box optimization).

**Empirical Rigor:** The experimental evaluation is thorough, with multiple datasets, careful hyperparameter tuning, and comparisons to both parametric networks of the same size and exhaustive random search. The paper also provides detailed analysis of the training process (Section 4.2), which helps explain why the approach works.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

*Clarify the limitations of the approach:* The paper mentions that nonparametric networks performed badly on the convex dataset, but doesn't provide a clear explanation for this failure. A deeper analysis of why the approach fails on certain datasets would strengthen the paper and provide valuable insights for future work.

*Compare with more recent methods:* The paper would benefit from comparisons with recent methods for automatic network size selection, such as neural architecture search (NAS) methods or adaptive network pruning techniques. This would help position the work more clearly against the current state of the art.

*Discuss computational cost:* The paper mentions that AdaRad incurs additional computational cost compared to SGD, but doesn't provide a detailed analysis of the trade-off between computational cost and performance gains. A more thorough discussion of the computational trade-offs would be helpful for practitioners.

*Clarify the connection to self-similarity:* The paper uses self-similar nonlinearities (like ReLU) as a key component of the approach, but the relationship between self-similarity and the effectiveness of the method could be explained more clearly.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper states that nonparametric networks performed badly on the convex dataset. Could you provide more details about why this happened, and whether there are specific characteristics of the convex dataset that make it particularly challenging for the nonparametric approach?

2. The paper mentions that p=2 is preferred over p=1 due to hardware considerations, but doesn't provide empirical evidence that p=2 outperforms p=1 in the nonparametric framework. Could you provide results comparing p=1 and p=2 for the same datasets?

3. The paper uses a fixed unit addition rate (one unit per epoch) for the MNIST experiments. How would the performance change with different unit addition rates, and what would be the optimal rate for different datasets?

4. The paper claims that the nonparametric approach can achieve higher accuracy than networks of the same size trained from scratch. Could you elaborate on why this is the case, and what specific properties of the nonparametric training process lead to this improvement?

5. The paper shows that the nonparametric approach can be applied to large datasets like poker (1.025 million data points). How does the computational cost of the nonparametric approach scale with dataset size, and how does it compare to the computational cost of traditional black-box model selection methods for the same dataset?

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of neural network model selection with a well-motivated approach, strong theoretical foundation, and compelling empirical results. The minor suggestions for improvement would further strengthen an already strong paper.
