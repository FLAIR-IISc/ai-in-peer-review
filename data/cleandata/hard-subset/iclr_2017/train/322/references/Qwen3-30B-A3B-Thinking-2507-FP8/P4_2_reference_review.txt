

# Review of "NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS"

## 1. Summary
The paper introduces a novel framework for automatically selecting neural network size during a single training run through "nonparametric neural networks." The authors propose a dynamic approach where network size is adjusted during training by adding and removing zero units, guided by fan-in or fan-out regularizers with p=2. The paper provides a theoretical foundation proving that nonparametric networks achieve a global training error minimum at finite dimensionality. It also introduces a novel normalization layer (CapNorm) and training algorithm (AdaRad) to enable effective training of these networks. The experimental evaluation demonstrates promising results on MNIST and rectangles images datasets, with nonparametric networks outperforming parametric networks of the same size and achieving results competitive with exhaustive random search. The paper is well-structured, with clear theoretical contributions and thorough experimental analysis.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a theoretically sound contribution to the problem of automatic network size selection with solid experimental validation on multiple datasets. The main reason for acceptance is the strong theoretical foundation supporting the core claim (Theorem 1), which is rigorously proven in the appendix. The experimental results demonstrate clear advantages over standard parametric networks for two out of three datasets, validating the practical utility of the approach.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper successfully addresses a meaningful problem in neural network training: the need to automatically determine network size without requiring multiple full training runs. The authors provide a compelling theoretical justification for their approach through Theorem 1, which proves that nonparametric networks achieve a global minimum at finite dimensionality under their formulation. This proof is thorough and appears mathematically sound, addressing a potential concern about network growth degeneration.

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, with careful analysis of the training process on MNIST, including detailed examination of network size evolution, error curves, and fan-in/fan-out dynamics. The results on MNIST and rectangles images datasets clearly demonstrate the advantages of the proposed approach over parametric networks of the same size. The paper also provides appropriate comparisons to grid search and random search baselines, showing that nonparametric networks can achieve competitive results with less computational effort.

The paper is well-motivated and properly positioned within the literature, with a thorough discussion of related work on model selection, pruning, and network growth. The authors clearly identify the limitations of existing approaches and position their work as addressing these limitations.

## 4. Additional Feedback
The paper would benefit from the following improvements:

1. The convex dataset results are problematic, with nonparametric networks underperforming compared to parametric networks. A more detailed analysis of why the approach fails on this dataset would strengthen the paper, as this represents a significant limitation that needs to be addressed.

2. The paper claims to outperform "an exhaustive random search" but doesn't compare to state-of-the-art neural architecture search (NAS) methods, which is a notable omission given the relevance to model selection. A comparison to modern NAS approaches would provide stronger evidence of the method's practical utility.

3. The paper mentions that "nonparametric networks can be applied to large datasets" but doesn't provide a thorough computational cost analysis. A more detailed discussion of training time and memory requirements compared to standard methods would help assess the practical viability of the approach.

4. The paper uses a relatively small network architecture (two hidden layers) for most experiments. It would be valuable to demonstrate the approach on larger, more complex architectures to show scalability to state-of-the-art models.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. The convex dataset results show nonparametric networks underperforming compared to parametric networks. Could you provide more insight into why this particular dataset is problematic for the approach, and whether there are specific modifications that could address this issue?

2. The paper mentions that "nonparametric networks can be applied to a large dataset" (poker dataset), but the experimental setup required several ad-hoc parameter adjustments (changing unit addition rate, radial step size). How generalizable are these adjustments, and would they be necessary for other large-scale datasets?

3. The paper claims to outperform exhaustive random search, but doesn't compare to modern neural architecture search (NAS) methods. How does your approach compare to state-of-the-art NAS methods in terms of both performance and computational efficiency?

4. The paper focuses on p=2 regularizers for practical reasons (hardware compatibility), but mentions that p=1 leads to sparse weight matrices. Have you explored the trade-offs between sparse and dense weight matrices for different hardware platforms, and how this might affect the practical implementation of your approach?
