

# Review: Nonparametric Neural Networks

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper introduces a novel framework for automatically selecting and adapting neural network size during a single training run, termed "nonparametric neural networks." The authors formulate network size as part of the optimization problem (rather than a fixed hyperparameter) and prove that with their specific fan-in/fan-out regularizer (with p=2), the optimization problem attains a global minimum at finite dimensionality. They develop a new normalization layer (CapNorm) and training algorithm (AdaRad) to handle the dynamic network size, and demonstrate promising empirical results on multiple datasets. The key contribution is a theoretically sound approach that avoids the time-consuming hyperparameter search typically required for network architecture selection.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **accepting** this paper with minor revisions. The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution with a proof of Theorem 1 (showing global minimum at finite dimensionality), and presents promising empirical results that demonstrate the practical value of the approach on two of three benchmark datasets. The work addresses a meaningful problem in deep learning with significant potential impact.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

**Theoretical Soundness**: The paper's theoretical foundation is robust. Theorem 1 is well-motivated and rigorously proven in the appendix (7.1). The proof carefully addresses the non-convex nature of the optimization problem and demonstrates that with the specific regularizer (p=2 fan-in/fan-out), the optimization problem attains a global minimum at finite dimensionality. This is a crucial theoretical result that justifies the entire framework.

**Practical Value**: The approach solves a real problem in deep learning - the time-consuming process of selecting network architecture via black-box optimization methods. The paper demonstrates that nonparametric networks can converge to good network sizes during training, outperform parametric networks of the same size on MNIST and rectangles images, and achieve results competitive with exhaustive search methods. The scalability results on the poker dataset (1.025 million examples) show the approach is practical for larger problems.

**Literature Placement**: The paper is well-placed in the literature, with a thorough discussion of related work on network pruning, network growth, and model selection. It clearly differentiates itself by focusing on dynamic size selection during training rather than requiring a well-performing network as a starting point (as in pruning) or requiring training to convergence before adding units (as in prior growth methods).

**Empirical Rigor**: The experimental design is thorough, with careful ablation studies and comparisons to multiple baselines. The authors conducted extensive hyperparameter searches and multiple random seeds to ensure results are reproducible. The results on MNIST and rectangles images are particularly compelling, with nonparametric networks achieving better performance than parametric networks of the same size.

## 4. Additional Feedback

1. **Clarify the convex dataset limitation**: The paper shows nonparametric networks underperform on the convex dataset. Please provide more analysis on why this occurred and whether it's a fundamental limitation of the approach or specific to the dataset characteristics.

2. **More detailed computational analysis**: While the paper mentions AdaRad has better computational scaling than RMSprop, provide specific numbers on the time and memory overhead compared to standard training methods. This would help readers assess practical adoption.

3. **Address the size-performance tradeoff**: On MNIST, the best nonparametric network had fewer parameters than the best parametric network (144 vs. 694 first-layer units). Clarify whether the performance advantage is due to the smaller size or the nonparametric approach itself. A direct comparison of nonparametric networks at the same size as the best parametric networks would strengthen the claims.

4. **Clarify the poker dataset results**: The paper states "the best nonparametric network made no mistake on the test set of 100,000 examples" but earlier mentions "less than 1% test error." Please clarify this discrepancy and provide the exact error rate.

## Questions for Authors

1. On the convex dataset, where nonparametric networks underperformed, could you provide more details on the training dynamics? Why did the nonparametric approach fail here, and what specific changes might improve performance?

2. The paper claims the nonparametric approach achieves higher accuracy than parametric networks of the same size. However, on MNIST, the best nonparametric network has fewer parameters than the best parametric network. Could you provide results comparing nonparametric networks of the same size as the best parametric networks?

3. The paper mentions that p=2 was chosen over p=1 for hardware efficiency (dense matrices vs. sparse). Could you provide more theoretical justification for this choice beyond hardware considerations?

4. The paper states that "the best nonparametric network made no mistake on the test set of 100,000 examples" on the poker dataset. Could you confirm the exact error rate and test set size?

This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of neural network architecture selection. With minor clarifications, it would be an excellent contribution to ICLR.
