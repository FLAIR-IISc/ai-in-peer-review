

# Review of "NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS"

## Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes "nonparametric neural networks" that dynamically adjust their size during training by adding and removing zero units. The authors claim their framework can automatically select optimal network size in a single training run, achieving higher accuracy than parametric networks of the same size and outperforming black-box model selection methods. The paper introduces a theoretical foundation (Theorem 1), a novel normalization layer (CapNorm), and a training algorithm (AdaRad). The authors provide experimental validation on MNIST, rectangles images, and convex datasets.

## Decision
Reject

## Key Reasons for Rejection
1. **Theoretical justification is incomplete**: The proof of Theorem 1 (Section 7.1) is excessively lengthy and complex for a main paper, and the restrictive conditions (e.g., requiring nonlinearities to be left- and right-differentiable everywhere) are not adequately justified in terms of practical implications. The paper claims the framework is "theoretically sound" but fails to address how these conditions limit applicability to real-world networks.

2. **Critical experimental gap**: The paper reports poor performance on the convex dataset (Section 4.1), contradicting their core claim that nonparametric networks "achieve a higher accuracy than networks of the same size that are trained from scratch." The authors fail to analyze why this dataset behaves differently, leaving a significant vulnerability in their approach.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper contains numerous minor but substantive flaws that collectively undermine the paper's credibility:

- The paper states they "focus on p=2 because current hardware is geared towards dense weight matrices" but provides no evidence for this claim, which is a key justification for their methodological choice.

- The choice of αr = 150λ (Section 4.1) is presented without justification or sensitivity analysis. The authors claim this "allows each new unit ≈50 epochs to train before being eliminated," but provide no evidence this is optimal.

- The unit addition schedule (adding one unit per epoch) is presented as a "good test case" but is never justified, with no exploration of how this schedule affects performance.

- In Section 3.2, CapNorm is introduced as a modification to batch normalization, but the paper fails to provide a clear theoretical explanation for why standard batch normalization wouldn't work with their regularizer.

- The paper uses inconsistent formatting (e.g., "f -equivalent" with space, inconsistent citation styles) and contains minor technical errors (e.g., confusing notation in the regularizer definition: "||[Wl(1, j),Wl(2, j), ..,Wl(dl−1, j)]||p" should have spaces between elements).

- The AdaRad algorithm (Algorithm 1) is described without sufficient intuition or comparison to existing methods beyond stating it's "novel," making it difficult to assess the significance of the contribution.

These flaws, while individually minor, collectively indicate a lack of rigorous attention to detail that is essential for a high-quality machine learning paper. The rejection is not due to lack of novelty but rather due to insufficient attention to the technical and methodological details required for a convincing contribution.

## Additional Feedback
1. The poor performance on the convex dataset is a major issue that needs addressing. The authors should investigate why this dataset behaves differently and whether the framework can be adapted to handle such cases.

2. The proof of Theorem 1 should be significantly condensed or moved to an appendix. The current presentation is unnecessarily verbose for a main paper.

3. Provide evidence for the claim about hardware being "geared towards dense weight matrices" when justifying p=2.

4. Justify the choice of αr = 150λ with sensitivity analysis or theoretical arguments.

5. Fix the formatting inconsistencies throughout the paper (e.g., "f-equivalent" without space, consistent citation style).

6. Provide a clearer explanation of why standard batch normalization cannot be used with their regularizer, rather than presenting CapNorm as an ad-hoc fix.

## Clarification Questions
1. Can you provide evidence or references supporting the claim that "current hardware is geared towards dense weight matrices" to justify focusing on p=2?

2. Why did the nonparametric networks perform poorly on the convex dataset while outperforming parametric networks on the other two datasets?

3. What is the theoretical basis for choosing αr = 150λ rather than other values?

4. Why is the unit addition schedule (one unit per epoch) presented as a "good test case" without justification?

These questions are necessary to assess whether the paper's claims are fully supported by the presented evidence. Without clear answers to these points, the paper's technical contribution cannot be confidently evaluated.
