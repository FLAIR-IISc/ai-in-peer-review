

# Review for "Nonparametric Neural Networks"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper introduces a novel framework for automatically selecting neural network size during training through "nonparametric neural networks." The core contribution is a method to dynamically grow and shrink network architectures during a single training run, avoiding the need for multiple separate training runs required by traditional model selection approaches. The authors provide a theoretical foundation with Theorem 1 proving that the optimization problem achieves a global minimum at finite dimensionality under specific conditions (fan-in/fan-out regularizers with p=2). They introduce two key innovations: CapNorm (a modified batch normalization technique) and AdaRad (a novel adaptive training algorithm). The experimental results demonstrate promising performance on MNIST and rectangles images datasets, with nonparametric networks achieving better accuracy than parametric networks of the same size and competitive results with exhaustive search methods. The paper also provides a detailed analysis of the training process, showing how units are added and removed during training.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The paper presents a theoretically interesting approach with strong mathematical foundations, but the empirical evidence does not sufficiently demonstrate practical advantages over existing methods. The results on the convex dataset are particularly concerning, undermining confidence in the generalizability of the approach.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's main weakness is the lack of compelling evidence that the proposed method offers practical advantages in real-world scenarios. While the theoretical foundation is strong (Theorem 1 is rigorously proven in the appendix), the empirical evaluation fails to address two critical questions: (1) How much faster is the approach in practice compared to training multiple parametric networks? (2) How does the approach scale to larger networks and datasets beyond the small-scale experiments presented?

The results on the convex dataset are particularly problematic, as nonparametric networks substantially underperform parametric networks of the same size, with a smaller range of performance across random reruns. This suggests the approach may not generalize well to all problem types. While the MNIST results are promising (144 units vs. 694 for parametric networks), the paper does not clearly explain why the nonparametric approach achieves this without the computational overhead of training multiple networks.

The paper also lacks a thorough discussion of practical considerations: How much additional memory does the approach require? How does the training time compare to traditional approaches when accounting for the single training run vs. multiple training runs? The paper claims to avoid the computational cost of black-box optimization, but doesn't provide evidence that this is actually achieved in practice.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
To strengthen the paper, I recommend:

1. **Include practical efficiency metrics**: Provide concrete comparisons of total training time (including the time to converge to the optimal size) versus traditional approaches. A simple calculation showing that training one nonparametric network is faster than training multiple parametric networks would be valuable.

2. **Expand experimental evaluation**: Include more datasets where the approach works well, and analyze why it fails on the convex dataset. A detailed comparison of the learned architectures (e.g., why the nonparametric approach converges to smaller networks on MNIST but not on convex) would provide important insights.

3. **Clarify the theoretical limitations**: Explicitly discuss the conditions under which the approach is applicable (e.g., the requirement for self-similar nonlinearities like ReLU, and the need for specific regularization). This would help readers understand the scope of the method's applicability.

4. **Provide more implementation details**: The paper mentions that AdaRad has "additional computational cost," but doesn't quantify this. A comparison of computational complexity (time and memory) between AdaRad and standard optimizers would strengthen the practical case.

5. **Discuss scalability to larger networks**: The poker dataset experiment is promising, but the paper doesn't thoroughly analyze how the approach scales to larger networks. How does the unit addition/removal schedule change for deeper networks?

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of why the nonparametric approach performs poorly on the convex dataset, compared to the other two datasets? What specific characteristics of the convex dataset make it challenging for this method?

2. The paper claims the approach avoids the computational cost of black-box optimization. Can you provide a concrete example showing how much faster the approach is in practice (e.g., training time for MNIST with nonparametric vs. training multiple parametric networks via random search)?

3. The paper mentions that "the best nonparametric network had the globally best performance" on the rectangles images dataset. How does this performance compare to the best network found via traditional methods (e.g., Bayesian optimization) in terms of training time and computational resources?

4. The theoretical foundation relies on specific regularizers and self-similar nonlinearities. How would the approach perform with other common nonlinearities (e.g., tanh, sigmoid) or with different regularization schemes?
