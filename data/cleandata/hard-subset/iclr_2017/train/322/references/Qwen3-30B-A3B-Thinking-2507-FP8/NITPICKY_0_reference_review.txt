

# Review of "NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS"

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contributions
The paper proposes "nonparametric neural networks" that dynamically adjust their size during training by adding and removing units based on regularization. The key contributions include: (1) a theoretical framework showing that with appropriate regularization, nonparametric networks achieve a global minimum at finite dimensionality; (2) a novel normalization layer (CapNorm); (3) a new training algorithm (AdaRad); and (4) experimental results demonstrating that nonparametric networks can achieve better accuracy than parametric networks of the same size on certain datasets.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The paper contains significant methodological flaws in the experimental protocol that undermine the validity of the reported results, combined with numerous technical errors and inconsistencies that indicate a lack of rigorous review.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

### Critical Methodological Flaw
The most serious issue is in the experimental protocol for Section 4.1. The paper states: "We trained 10 NP networks for each chosen value of λ, with 10 different random seeds. Out of the 10 nets produced, we manually chose a single net as a typical representative by approximating the median of both network size, measured in number of weight parameters, and the test classification error (TCE) across the 10 runs." This is a severe methodological flaw because it introduces subjective bias into the selection of results. The authors should have used an objective criterion (e.g., median TCE, mean TCE, or the run with median TCE) rather than manually selecting a representative. This approach could have led to overestimation of performance, particularly since the authors claim the selected networks represent "typical" performance.

### Technical Errors and Inconsistencies
The paper contains numerous technical errors and inconsistent notations that suggest a lack of careful review:

1. In equations (2) and (3), the summation notation has missing indices: "dl∑ j=1" should be "dl∑ j=1" (missing 'j' in the subscript).

2. In Section 3.2, "CapNorm replaces each pre-activation z with z−µmax(σ,1)" should be "z−µ/max(σ,1)" (division symbol missing).

3. In Algorithm 1, line 14: "φadj = √φmax cmax√φ̄l(j) cl(j) + φ" uses confusing notation that would be difficult to implement.

4. In the proof of Theorem 1 (Section 7.1), Claim 3 states "∑N n=1 |g(n)l (j)| ≥ B2" but the derivation relies on "|| 1 N ∑ n de d[Wl(i,j)]i || p p−1 = || dΩd[Wl(i,j)]i || pp−1" which is not clearly justified.

5. In Section 7.1, the proof of Lemma 2 states "Since dL is fixed, we can simply pick DL = dL" - this is misleading as dL refers to the output dimension, not the number of units in the output layer.

### Experimental Results Concerns
- The paper claims "the best nonparametric network made no mistake on the test set of 100.000 examples" for the poker dataset, but provides no details about the actual test set size or standard deviations. This is an extremely strong claim for a binary classification task that requires statistical validation.

- For the convex dataset, the paper states nonparametric networks performed badly but provides no explanation for this discrepancy, which undermines the paper's broader claim of general applicability.

- The paper doesn't provide statistical tests to show that the performance differences between nonparametric and parametric networks are significant.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

1. **Experimental Protocol**: Completely rewrite the experimental protocol to use objective criteria for selecting representative networks. Use the median TCE across runs rather than manual selection.

2. **Technical Corrections**: Fix all the notation errors and inconsistencies identified above. Pay special attention to the summation indices and mathematical notation in the regularizers.

3. **Statistical Validation**: Provide statistical tests (e.g., t-tests) to validate whether the performance differences are significant. Include standard deviations for all reported results.

4. **Computational Cost Analysis**: The paper mentions that black-box methods require "days on many cores or multiple GPUs" but doesn't compare the computational cost of the proposed method to standard methods. This is a significant omission.

5. **Theoretical Justification**: Provide stronger justification for the claim that self-similar nonlinearities are necessary. The current justification ("if pre-activations have very small absolute values, tanh mimics a linear function") is a general property of many nonlinearities, not specific to tanh.

### Questions for Authors

1. How would you justify the manual selection of "typical representative" networks rather than using an objective metric like median TCE? What potential biases might this introduce?

2. Can you provide a detailed explanation of why "z−µmax(σ,1)" should be interpreted as "z−µ/max(σ,1)" rather than "z−µ*max(σ,1)"? This notation is critical for implementation.

3. For the convex dataset where nonparametric networks underperformed, what specific properties of the dataset caused this discrepancy, and how would you address it in future work?

4. What statistical tests did you use to validate the significance of the performance differences between nonparametric and parametric networks?

5. How does the computational cost of your method compare to standard parametric networks, particularly in terms of time per epoch and total training time?
