

# Review of "NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper introduces a novel framework for automatic neural network size selection called "nonparametric neural networks." The authors propose a method to dynamically adjust network size during training by adding and removing "zero units" (units with zero fan-in or fan-out), eliminating the need for multiple full training runs required by traditional model selection methods. The approach is theoretically grounded with a proof showing that the optimization problem achieves a global minimum under certain conditions, and the authors develop a new training algorithm (AdaRad) and normalization layer (CapNorm) to support this framework. The paper demonstrates promising results on MNIST and rectangles images datasets, where nonparametric networks achieve comparable or better accuracy than parametric networks of the same size, while using fewer parameters.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The main reason is that the empirical results do not fully support the paper's central claim that nonparametric networks achieve higher accuracy than parametric networks of the same size, as demonstrated by poor performance on the convex dataset where parametric networks substantially outperformed nonparametric ones.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution with a rigorous proof (Theorem 1) showing that nonparametric networks achieve a global minimum under certain conditions. The theoretical foundation is sound, and the AdaRad algorithm is well-motivated to address the unique challenges of nonparametric training. However, the empirical results present a significant limitation: on the convex dataset, nonparametric networks performed substantially worse than parametric networks of the same size, contradicting the paper's main claim. The authors acknowledge this limitation but do not provide a clear explanation for why the convex dataset behaves differently, which undermines the general applicability of their approach.

While the paper demonstrates promising results on MNIST and rectangles images, the inconsistent performance across three datasets means the core claim is not sufficiently supported. The paper states nonparametric networks "achieve a higher accuracy than networks of the same size that are trained from scratch and, in some cases, achieve a higher accuracy than is possible by black-box methods," but the convex dataset example directly contradicts this claim. For a paper to be accepted at ICLR, the main claims must be adequately supported by empirical evidence, which is not the case here.

## 4. Additional Feedback
The theoretical contribution is strong and could be valuable for future work. To strengthen the paper, the authors should:
- Provide a more thorough analysis of why the convex dataset behaves differently, including examining the training dynamics and network size convergence patterns for this dataset.
- Consider extending the experiments to more diverse datasets to better establish the generalizability of the approach.
- Clarify the computational cost comparison between nonparametric networks and traditional model selection methods, as this is a key practical advantage claimed by the paper.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. Can you provide a more detailed explanation of why the convex dataset shows such different behavior compared to MNIST and rectangles images? Specifically, what characteristics of the convex dataset cause nonparametric networks to converge to a size that is too small for optimal performance?

2. The paper states that "nonparametric networks substantially outperform parametric networks of the same size" for MNIST, but the best nonparametric network is "close in performance to the best parametric network." Can you clarify the quantitative difference in accuracy between the best nonparametric and best parametric networks for MNIST, and whether this difference is statistically significant?

3. The paper claims that nonparametric networks can "achieve a higher accuracy than is possible by black-box methods" for rectangles images. However, the paper does not provide a direct comparison with the best black-box method (e.g., Bayesian optimization) in terms of accuracy, but rather compares to parametric networks of the same size. Can you clarify how the paper's results compare to the best possible black-box model selection approach?
