

# Review of "NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper proposes a novel framework for automatically selecting neural network size during training through a nonparametric approach. The authors introduce the concept of nonparametric neural networks where network dimensions are not fixed but can be dynamically adjusted during training. They present a theoretical justification for their approach (Theorem 1), a new normalization layer (CapNorm), a training algorithm (AdaRad), and experimental results demonstrating that their approach can achieve higher accuracy than parametric networks of the same size on several benchmark datasets. The paper claims that their method can achieve performance competitive with exhaustive black-box search methods while requiring only a single training run.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper contains numerous methodological inconsistencies, theoretical gaps, and presentation issues that undermine the validity of the claims. While the core idea is interesting, the execution lacks sufficient rigor to warrant acceptance.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

### Theoretical Inconsistencies

The paper's central claim relies on Theorem 1, which states that nonparametric networks achieve a global minimum at finite dimensionality with fan-in or fan-out regularizers. However, the proof in section 7.1 contains critical gaps:

1. The proof assumes differentiability of nonlinearities (Lemma 2 and 3), but Theorem 1 only requires left/right differentiability. The extension to the non-differentiable case (Lemma 4) is not properly justified. The paper states "the proof is a rather tedious extension," but fails to provide sufficient detail to verify this.

2. The paper claims that "p = 2, on the other hand, sets entire fan-ins (for the fan-in regularizer) or fan-outs (for the fan-out regularizer) to zero at once." This is factually incorrect. The p=2 norm would cause the fan-in vector to shrink proportionally, not necessarily to zero. The paper confuses the effect of the regularizer with the effect of the removal of zero units.

3. The proof of Proposition 1 states that "the value of the objective is preserved when we replace all regularization parameters with the same value λ = (∏λl)^(1/L) and rescale Wl by λl/λ." However, this is only true for the fan-in regularizer, not the fan-out regularizer. The proof in section 7.2 only covers the fan-in case.

### Methodological Issues

1. The paper claims "nonparametric networks trained without batch normalization could not compete with parametric networks trained with it," but provides no evidence for this claim. There is no comparison showing the performance difference with and without batch normalization.

2. The justification for CapNorm (capped batch normalization) is flawed. The paper states "batch normalization cannot be applied directly to nonparametric networks with a fan-in or fan-out regularizer, as it would allow us to shrink the absolute value of individual weights arbitrarily while compensating with the batch normalization layer, thus negating the regularizer." This is incorrect because batch normalization normalizes activations, not weights, so it wouldn't allow weights to be arbitrarily shrunk.

3. The paper claims "the fan-in and fan-out regularizers naturally produce such units as they induce sparsity," but fails to explain how p=2 (which they use) leads to zero units. The paper states that p=1 sets individual weights to zero, but they're using p=2, which doesn't set individual weights to zero.

### Experimental Presentation Issues

1. The paper references "rectangles images dataset" and "convex dataset" without providing standard references or clear descriptions. These are not established benchmarks in the literature.

2. The paper claims "the best nonparametric network made no mistake on the test set of 100.000 examples" on the poker dataset but doesn't provide the actual test set size or the number of test examples.

3. The paper uses "10 random reruns" for MNIST but doesn't provide standard deviations or confidence intervals to demonstrate statistical significance of the claims.

4. The paper states "the nonparametric networks had a higher error for larger values of λ and a slightly lower error for smaller values of λ" on poker, but provides no statistical significance testing.

### Formatting and Presentation

The paper contains numerous inconsistent formatting issues:

1. Inconsistent use of "nonparametric" vs "non-parametric"
2. Inconsistent use of mathematical symbols (e.g., "||" vs "||" for norms)
3. Inconsistent use of "fan-in" vs "fan-in"
4. Inconsistent use of "p-norm" vs "p-norm"
5. Inconsistent use of "unit" vs "units"
6. Inconsistent capitalization of "ReLU" vs "ReLU"

## 4. Additional Feedback

1. The paper would benefit from a clearer explanation of how p=2 regularizers lead to zero units, as this is central to their approach.

2. The paper should provide a more rigorous comparison with existing methods for dynamic network size adjustment (e.g., dynamic network surgery, adaptive pruning), not just parametric networks.

3. The paper should provide statistical significance testing for all experimental claims, including standard deviations and confidence intervals.

4. The paper should clarify the relationship between the regularization parameter λ and the network size, as well as provide a more detailed justification for the default values used (e.g., αr = 150λ).

5. The paper should provide a clear description of the "rectangles images dataset" and "convex dataset" to allow reproducibility.

## Questions for Authors

1. The paper claims that "p = 2 sets entire fan-ins to zero at once" but this is mathematically incorrect. Can you clarify how the p=2 regularizer leads to zero fan-ins?

2. The justification for CapNorm appears to be based on a misunderstanding of how batch normalization works. Can you provide a more rigorous explanation for why standard batch normalization cannot be used with the regularizer?

3. The paper states that "the value of the objective is preserved when we replace all regularization parameters with the same value λ = (∏λl)^(1/L)" but this appears to be only true for the fan-in regularizer. Can you clarify whether this holds for the fan-out regularizer as well?

4. The paper claims that nonparametric networks "achieve a higher accuracy than networks of the same size that are trained from scratch" but doesn't provide a comparison to other methods that adjust network size during training. Can you provide such a comparison?
