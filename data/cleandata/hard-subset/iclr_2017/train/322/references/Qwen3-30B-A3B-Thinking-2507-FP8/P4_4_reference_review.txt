

# Review of "NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper introduces a novel framework for automatic neural network model selection called "nonparametric neural networks." The authors propose a method that dynamically adjusts network size during a single training run by adding and removing units, rather than selecting from a fixed set of architectures. The core theoretical contribution is Theorem 1, which proves that nonparametric networks achieve a global training error minimum at finite dimensionality when using appropriate fan-in or fan-out regularizers. The paper develops a novel normalization layer (CapNorm) and training algorithm (AdaRad) to enable this approach, demonstrating empirically that their method can outperform parametric networks of the same size on two out of three benchmark datasets while requiring no pre-trained network as a starting point.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to the problem of automatic neural network model selection with strong theoretical grounding and promising empirical results. The key reasons for acceptance are: (1) the paper provides a rigorous theoretical foundation for the proposed framework, and (2) the empirical results demonstrate that the approach can achieve better performance than parametric networks of the same size on two out of three datasets.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's theoretical contribution is substantial. Theorem 1 establishes that nonparametric networks achieve a global minimum at finite dimensionality under appropriate conditions, addressing a fundamental limitation of black-box model selection methods. The proof is thorough and well-structured, with supporting lemmas that handle both differentiable and directionally differentiable nonlinearities. The paper correctly identifies the limitations of existing model selection approaches and positions their work within the literature, distinguishing it from pruning methods that require a pre-trained network.

The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed. The authors demonstrate that nonparametric networks outperform parametric networks of the same size on MNIST and rectangles images datasets, while achieving results competitive with exhaustive random search. The detailed analysis of the training process (Figure 3) provides valuable insights into how units are added and removed, and how the network size converges. The scalability experiment on the poker dataset (1.025 million datapoints) further validates the practical applicability of the approach, showing that the computational overhead of gradual network growth becomes negligible on larger datasets.

The paper's approach has clear advantages over existing methods: it avoids the computational cost of multiple full training runs required by black-box optimization, and it can potentially achieve higher accuracy than networks of fixed size trained from scratch.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
- The paper could provide more detailed analysis of why the approach underperforms on the convex dataset compared to parametric networks. Understanding this limitation would strengthen the paper's contribution.
- The comparison to recent neural architecture search methods (e.g., Zoph & Le, 2017; Baker et al., 2017) would help contextualize the approach's strengths and limitations relative to state-of-the-art.
- The theoretical results assume self-similar nonlinearities (like ReLU). A discussion of how the approach would perform with other nonlinearities (like tanh) would be valuable.
- The paper could provide more details on the computational cost of AdaRad compared to standard optimizers, particularly in terms of memory and time complexity.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. The paper demonstrates good performance on MNIST and rectangles images but poorer results on the convex dataset. Could you provide more insight into why the approach fails on this particular dataset, and whether there are modifications that could improve performance?
2. The theoretical analysis assumes self-similar nonlinearities (e.g., ReLU). How would the approach be affected if used with other nonlinearities (e.g., tanh), and what modifications would be necessary?
3. The paper shows scalability to the poker dataset (1.025 million datapoints), but what would be the computational cost (in terms of training time and memory) compared to standard parametric networks for the same task, especially considering the gradual growth of network size?
