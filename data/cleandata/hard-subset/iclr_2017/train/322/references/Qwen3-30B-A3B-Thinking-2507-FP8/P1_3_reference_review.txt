

# Peer Review: NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims/Contribution

The paper introduces a novel framework called "nonparametric neural networks" that automatically adapts and selects the optimal size of a neural network during a single training run, without requiring prior information about the task. The authors address a fundamental limitation of current model selection methods (grid search, random search, Bayesian optimization) that require multiple full training runs and cannot improve the model during training. Their approach dynamically shrinks and expands the network by adding and removing zero units during training, guided by fan-in regularizers and a novel training algorithm (AdaRad) with a specialized normalization layer (CapNorm). The key theoretical contribution is Theorem 1, which proves that nonparametric networks achieve a global training error minimum at finite dimensionality when using fan-in or fan-out regularizers. The authors demonstrate that their approach outperforms parametric networks of the same size on MNIST and rectangles images datasets, with competitive results to exhaustive random search.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **Accept** for this paper.

Key reasons:
- The paper addresses a significant and well-motivated problem in neural network model selection
- The theoretical foundation is rigorous with a complete proof of Theorem 1
- The empirical results are compelling, showing clear advantages over parametric networks of the same size on multiple datasets

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper makes a substantial contribution to the field of neural network architecture selection. The problem of automatically determining network size is important, as current black-box methods require multiple full training runs (each taking days) and cannot improve the model during training. The authors correctly identify these limitations and propose a principled solution.

The theoretical contribution is particularly strong. Theorem 1 is a major achievement, proving that nonparametric networks achieve a global minimum at finite dimensionality with the specified regularizers. The proof is thorough, with multiple lemmas building toward the main result, and the appendix provides complete details. Proposition 1 about the equivalence of using different regularization parameters across layers is a valuable practical insight.

The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed. The comparison of nonparametric networks to parametric networks of the same size (shown in Figure 2) demonstrates clear advantages on MNIST (144 vs 694 units) and rectangles images datasets. The detailed analysis of the training process (Figure 3) provides valuable insights into how the network size evolves during training. The scalability test on the poker dataset (1.025 million examples) further validates the approach's practical utility.

The paper is well-placed in the literature, with thorough discussion of related work on pruning (Alvarez & Salzmann, Molchanov et al.), network growth (Wei et al., Chen et al.), and model selection. The authors clearly differentiate their approach from prior work, particularly noting that their method does not require a high-performing starting network, which is a limitation of many pruning approaches.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

While the paper is strong, the following improvements would enhance its clarity and impact:

- **Clarify the "f-equivalence" concept**: The paper introduces f-equivalence as crucial to the approach but doesn't provide an intuitive explanation. A simple example showing how two parameter values with different dimensionality can produce identical network outputs would help readers grasp this key insight.

- **Expand discussion of limitations**: The paper notes poor performance on the convex dataset but doesn't thoroughly analyze why. A brief discussion of the dataset's characteristics (e.g., linear separability) that might make it challenging for the approach would strengthen the paper and help readers understand the method's boundaries.

- **Include computational cost analysis**: The paper mentions AdaRad's computational cost scales more gracefully than RMSprop but doesn't provide concrete comparisons. A table showing wall-clock time per epoch for AdaRad vs. RMSprop would help readers assess the practical trade-offs.

- **Discuss connection to recent NAS work**: The paper cites Zoph & Le (2017) on neural architecture search but doesn't discuss how their approach relates to modern NAS methods. A brief comparison would help position the work within current trends.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

To help clarify my understanding and assess the paper more confidently, I would like to ask:

1. The paper states nonparametric networks underperform on the convex dataset. Can you elaborate on what specific characteristics of this dataset make it challenging for your approach, and how you might modify the framework to better handle such cases?

2. The proof of Theorem 1 requires σ(0) = 0 for nonlinearities. While ReLU satisfies this, how would the approach perform with tanh (which also satisfies σ(0)=0) compared to ReLU, and would the theoretical guarantees still hold?

3. The paper mentions that the best nonparametric network on MNIST had 144 first-layer units versus 694 for parametric networks. How does the training time compare between these approaches? Given that the nonparametric network must grow during training, does it ultimately require more or less total training time than parametric networks?

4. The paper uses a fixed unit addition rate (one unit per epoch for MNIST). How sensitive is the final performance to this choice? Is there a principled way to determine the optimal unit addition schedule for a given dataset, or is it primarily a hyperparameter to tune?

5. The paper demonstrates success on MNIST, rectangles images, and poker datasets. How do you expect the approach to scale to more complex tasks like ImageNet classification, where network size would be significantly larger? Are there additional considerations needed for very large-scale applications?

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of neural network architecture selection with a theoretically sound and empirically validated approach. The reviewers will be interested in the authors' responses to these questions as they further refine the paper.
