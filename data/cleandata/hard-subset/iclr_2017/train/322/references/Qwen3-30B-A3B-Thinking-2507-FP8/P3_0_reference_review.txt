

# Peer Review: "Nonparametric Neural Networks"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces a novel framework called "nonparametric neural networks" that dynamically adjusts network size during training. The key contribution is a theoretically grounded approach to automatically selecting network architecture by allowing units to be added and removed during training, rather than using black-box model selection methods that require multiple full training runs. The authors prove that with appropriate regularization, nonparametric networks achieve a global minimum at finite dimensionality (Theorem 1), develop a novel normalization layer (CapNorm) to prevent regularizer negation, and introduce a new optimization algorithm (AdaRad) that handles the unique challenges of nonparametric networks. The approach demonstrates promising results on MNIST and poker datasets, substantially outperforming parametric networks of the same size on these tasks.

## 2. Decision

**Decision: Weak Accept**

**Key reasons:** 
1) The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution with a well-constructed proof of Theorem 1, addressing a fundamental concern about nonparametric networks potentially growing indefinitely.
2) The approach directly addresses a real limitation of current model selection methods (black-box optimization requiring multiple full training runs), with promising empirical results on several datasets.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

### Theoretical Soundness

The theoretical contribution is the paper's strongest aspect. Theorem 1 is rigorously proven through a sequence of well-constructed lemmas, demonstrating that nonparametric networks with fan-in or fan-out regularizers achieve a global minimum at finite dimensionality. The proof handles the non-differentiable case (via directional derivatives) and is comprehensive. The authors correctly identify that standard batch normalization would negate their regularizer, leading to the novel CapNorm layer, which is a thoughtful practical contribution.

### Methodological Innovation

The AdaRad algorithm is well-motivated and addresses the specific challenges of nonparametric networks. The radial-angular decomposition is insightful, and the distinction between controlling learning speed (angular step size) and unit elimination rate (radial step size) is particularly valuable. The authors correctly note that standard adaptive optimization methods (like RMSprop) would disrupt their regularization strategy.

### Experimental Results

The empirical results are generally positive but have notable limitations:
- On MNIST and rectangles images, nonparametric networks substantially outperform parametric networks of the same size, and achieve performance comparable to exhaustive random search.
- On the convex dataset, parametric networks significantly outperform nonparametric networks, which is concerning for a general framework.
- The scalability experiment on the poker dataset (1.025M examples) is promising, showing the approach can handle larger datasets with good performance.

The paper acknowledges the slow convergence (one unit per epoch) but doesn't thoroughly address computational efficiency compared to standard methods.

### Limitations

The paper's limitations need more attention:
- The poor performance on the convex dataset is unexplained and represents a significant gap in the framework's applicability.
- The comparison to state-of-the-art neural architecture search (NAS) methods is limited (only mentioned in the related work section).
- The paper doesn't discuss how to extend to convolutional networks, despite acknowledging this as future work.

## 4. Additional Feedback

### Strengths
- The theoretical foundation is exceptionally strong and well-justified.
- The f-equivalence concept provides a solid mathematical basis for the dynamic network size adjustment.
- The CapNorm and AdaRad contributions are practical, well-motivated, and well-explained.

### Areas for Improvement
1. **Convex dataset explanation:** The authors should provide a more detailed analysis of why their approach fails on the convex dataset. Is this due to the dataset's properties, or is there a fundamental limitation of the approach? This is critical for assessing the framework's general applicability.

2. **NAS comparison:** The paper mentions NAS in the related work but doesn't compare with state-of-the-art NAS methods (e.g., DARTS, ENAS) on the same datasets. A brief comparison would strengthen the position of their approach relative to current architecture search methods.

3. **Computational efficiency:** The paper claims AdaRad has lower computational cost than RMSprop, but doesn't provide concrete timing comparisons. A table showing training time comparisons would be valuable.

4. **Beyond fully-connected networks:** The authors state they plan to extend to convolutional layers, but don't discuss the challenges or potential adaptations. A brief discussion of how convolutional layers would fit into their framework would be helpful.

### Questions for Authors

1. The convex dataset results are concerning. Could you provide more insight into why the approach fails on this dataset, and whether there's a way to modify the approach to handle such cases?

2. How does your approach compare to recent NAS methods (e.g., DARTS) on the MNIST and poker datasets? The paper mentions NAS briefly but doesn't provide direct comparisons.

3. The paper states "the best nonparametric network made no mistake on the test set of 100,000 examples" for poker. Could you clarify the exact test error rate, as this is a significant claim?

4. The paper focuses on p=2 regularization, while acknowledging p=1 would produce sparser networks. Could you provide more intuition about why p=2 was chosen over p=1, especially given the recent interest in sparse networks?

5. The paper mentions unit initialization as an area for future work. Could you share preliminary results or insights about how different initialization strategies might impact performance?

## Conclusion

This paper presents a theoretically grounded and practically implemented approach to automatic network architecture selection that addresses a real limitation of current black-box model selection methods. The theoretical contribution is strong, and the empirical results are promising on several datasets. While the poor performance on the convex dataset is concerning, the paper makes a valuable contribution to the field. With additional clarification on the limitations and more thorough comparison with state-of-the-art methods, this would be a strong contribution to ICLR. I recommend a weak accept, as the paper makes a significant contribution to the field despite some limitations.
