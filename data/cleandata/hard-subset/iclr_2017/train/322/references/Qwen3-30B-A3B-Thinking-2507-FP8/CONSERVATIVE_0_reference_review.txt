

# Review of "NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes "nonparametric neural networks" as a framework for automatically selecting network size during training. The authors define a nonparametric network as one where the dimensionality of weight matrices is a variable to be optimized rather than a fixed hyperparameter. They introduce a fan-in regularizer with p=2, a novel normalization layer (CapNorm), and a training algorithm (AdaRad) that dynamically adds and removes units during training. The authors claim their approach can achieve higher accuracy than parametric networks of the same size and outperform exhaustive random search for some datasets.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The paper presents an interesting theoretical concept but lacks sufficient empirical evidence to support its claims. The experimental results are inconsistent across datasets, and the paper fails to demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods for network architecture selection.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's core claim—that nonparametric networks can automatically select optimal size and outperform parametric networks of the same size—lacks robust empirical validation. The results presented in Section 4.1 are highly dataset-dependent: nonparametric networks outperform parametric networks on MNIST, underperform on rectangles images for large λ, and perform poorly on the convex dataset. This inconsistency undermines the paper's central claim of a general solution.

The paper's theoretical justification (Theorem 1) is mathematically rigorous but does not translate into clear practical advantages. The authors prove that the optimization problem has a global minimum under certain conditions, but this is not particularly surprising given that regularization is known to prevent overfitting. The proof is lengthy and technical, but it doesn't address the more important question of whether this approach actually leads to better performance on standard benchmarks.

The paper fails to provide a convincing comparison against established methods for network architecture selection. The authors compare against parametric networks of the same size, but don't compare against modern architecture search methods like NAS (Zoph & Le, 2017) or Bayesian optimization (Snoek et al., 2012) that are commonly used for this problem. The paper's claim that "the network ultimately chosen can achieve a higher accuracy than networks of the same size that are trained from scratch and, in some cases, achieve a higher accuracy than is possible by black-box methods" is not adequately supported by the experimental results.

The paper also doesn't demonstrate a significant computational advantage, which was one of the main motivations stated in the introduction. The authors claim that their approach avoids the "days on many cores or multiple GPUs" required by black-box methods, but they don't provide training time comparisons with standard methods. The experiments on the poker dataset (Section 4.3) show that nonparametric networks require many more mini-batches to converge, which contradicts the stated efficiency advantage.

The paper's experimental design is problematic in several ways. The authors conduct a "grid search" over λ but don't explain how they chose the range of values. The paper also doesn't provide statistical significance testing for the performance differences they report, making it difficult to assess whether the observed differences are meaningful.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Provide a more comprehensive comparison against established architecture search methods, including computational efficiency metrics.

2. Conduct experiments on larger, more challenging datasets beyond MNIST, such as CIFAR-10 or ImageNet, to demonstrate broader applicability.

3. Provide statistical significance testing for all performance comparisons.

4. Clarify the relationship between the proposed method and recent work on network pruning (e.g., Molchanov et al., 2017) and neural architecture search.

5. Address why the convex dataset performed poorly and what limitations this reveals about the approach.

6. Provide a clear explanation of how the proposed method's theoretical properties translate to practical advantages.

The paper presents an interesting theoretical idea, but the empirical evidence is insufficient to support the claims of superior performance and automatic network size selection. The authors would need to provide more rigorous experimental validation across multiple datasets and against relevant baselines before this approach could be considered a viable alternative to established methods for network architecture selection. The current evidence does not meet the high standards required for acceptance at ICLR.
