

# Peer Review: "NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces a novel framework called "nonparametric neural networks" for automatically selecting network size during a single training run, rather than using black-box optimization methods that require multiple full training runs. The authors propose a dynamic approach where the network can add and remove units during training, using a fan-in or fan-out regularizer to induce sparsity (zero units), which are then eliminated. They introduce two key innovations: a new normalization layer called "Capped Batch Normalization" (CapNorm) that works with their regularizer, and a novel training algorithm called "Adaptive Radial-Angular Gradient Descent" (AdaRad) that handles the unique challenges of training nonparametric networks. The authors prove a key theoretical result (Theorem 1) establishing that nonparametric networks achieve a global minimum at finite dimensionality with their regularizers. Empirical results show that their approach can outperform parametric networks of the same size on several benchmark datasets, with the nonparametric network achieving higher accuracy than exhaustive random search for two out of three datasets.

## 2. Decision

**Accept** with minor revisions.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of neural network architecture design by presenting a theoretically sound and practically effective approach to automatic network size selection. The key reasons for acceptance are:
1. The paper addresses a genuinely important problem in deep learning (automated network architecture selection) with a novel, theoretically grounded solution.
2. The theoretical foundation (Theorem 1) is rigorous and well-motivated, and the empirical results demonstrate clear advantages over existing approaches on several datasets.
3. The proposed framework is simple, general, and can be applied to standard feed-forward networks without requiring specialized hardware or training infrastructure.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's contributions are significant and well-positioned in the literature. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing black-box model selection approaches (requiring multiple full training runs and not exploiting opportunities to improve during training) and propose a compelling alternative. The theoretical foundation (Theorem 1) is particularly strong, as it establishes the key property that nonparametric networks can achieve a global minimum at finite dimensionality with their regularizers, addressing a critical concern about potential degeneration with larger networks.

The empirical evaluation is thorough and well-designed. The authors compare their approach against parametric networks of the same size (showing the nonparametric networks can achieve better accuracy) and against exhaustive random search (showing they can achieve competitive results with less computation). The results on MNIST and the rectangles images dataset are particularly compelling, with the nonparametric approach outperforming parametric networks of the same size. The analysis of the training process (Figure 3) provides valuable insights into how the network size evolves during training.

The proposed AdaRad algorithm is well-motivated and addresses the unique challenges of training nonparametric networks. The authors carefully explain why standard optimization methods (like RMSprop) are not suitable, and their solution (normalizing only the orthogonal component of the gradient) is elegant and effective.

The paper is well-written, with clear exposition of the core ideas and good organization. The theoretical proofs (in the appendix) are rigorous and well-structured.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, I recommend the following minor improvements:

1. **Clarity on limitations**: The paper mentions that nonparametric networks performed poorly on the convex dataset. It would be helpful to provide more insight into why this happened and whether there are specific conditions under which the approach might fail. This would help readers understand the scope of applicability.

2. **Comparison with related work**: The paper mentions several related works on network growth and pruning. It would strengthen the contribution to explicitly compare with the most recent approaches to network size adaptation (e.g., "Dynamic Network Surgery" by Guo et al., 2016, or "Net2Net" by Chen et al., 2016).

3. **Computational complexity analysis**: While the paper mentions that AdaRad has better computational scaling than RMSprop, it would be helpful to provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity (time and memory) of the proposed approach compared to standard methods, especially for larger networks.

4. **Practical implementation details**: The paper mentions that for the poker dataset, they added units every tenth of an epoch instead of every epoch. It would be helpful to include a brief analysis of how the unit addition rate affects performance, as this is likely an important hyperparameter for practical application.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. In the convex dataset experiments, the nonparametric networks performed worse than parametric networks. Could you provide more insight into why this might be the case, particularly regarding the relationship between the dataset characteristics and the effectiveness of your approach?

2. The paper mentions that they used p=2 for the regularizer because current hardware is geared toward dense weight matrices. Have you explored the trade-offs between sparse and dense representations in terms of both performance and hardware efficiency? Would a hybrid approach (using p=1 for some layers and p=2 for others) yield better results?

3. In Figure 3, the nonparametric network shows a slow but steady improvement in validation error, while parametric networks quickly reach a good accuracy. Could you elaborate on why the nonparametric network's validation error continues to improve even after the training error has plateaued? Is this related to the dynamic network size adjustment?

4. The paper states that the nonparametric approach can be applied to larger datasets like poker (with 1.025 million datapoints). How would the computational requirements scale with even larger datasets, and what modifications would be needed to maintain the same efficiency?

5. The theoretical result (Theorem 1) assumes that nonlinearities satisfy σ(0)=0. How would the approach need to be modified to work with standard nonlinearities like sigmoid that don't satisfy this property?
