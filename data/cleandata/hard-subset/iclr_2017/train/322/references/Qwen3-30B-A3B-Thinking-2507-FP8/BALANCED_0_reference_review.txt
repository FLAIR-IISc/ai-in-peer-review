

# Review of "Nonparametric Neural Networks"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces a novel framework for automatic neural network architecture selection called "nonparametric neural networks." The authors propose a method that dynamically adjusts network size during a single training run, eliminating the need for multiple full training runs with different architectures as required by traditional black-box optimization methods. The approach includes a theoretical foundation (Theorem 1), a novel normalization layer (CapNorm), and a new training algorithm (AdaRad). The authors demonstrate that their method can achieve competitive performance compared to parametric networks of the same size and sometimes outperform exhaustive search methods, while requiring only a single training run.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper makes an interesting theoretical contribution to architecture search, but the empirical results are inconsistent across datasets and the framework appears to have significant practical limitations that are not adequately addressed.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's main strengths include a clear problem statement, a theoretically grounded approach, and a novel training algorithm (AdaRad) that is well-motivated. Theorem 1 provides a solid theoretical foundation showing that the optimization problem has a global minimum under certain conditions. The authors also make a thoughtful comparison to related work on pruning and architecture search.

However, the paper has significant weaknesses that prevent acceptance:

First, the empirical results are inconsistent across datasets. While the method performs well on MNIST and the rectangles dataset, it performs poorly on the convex dataset (Section 4.1), with parametric networks of the same size substantially outperforming the nonparametric approach. This inconsistency raises serious questions about the generalizability of the framework.

Second, the paper does not adequately address the computational cost. Figure 3A shows that the nonparametric training process requires significantly more epochs than standard training to reach similar performance levels. The paper claims that this overhead is "ameliorated" for larger datasets (Section 4.3), but this is not convincingly demonstrated with proper computational cost metrics (e.g., wall-clock time, FLOPs).

Third, the paper lacks a clear comparison to recent neural architecture search (NAS) methods. The comparison to "exhaustive random search" is insufficient, as the authors do not compare to state-of-the-art NAS approaches like ENAS, DARTS, or ProxylessNAS that have become standard benchmarks in the field.

Finally, the theoretical proof (Section 7.1) is extremely complex and may be difficult for readers to verify. While the proof is thorough, it may be more appropriate for a journal publication than a conference paper where brevity and clarity are essential.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen this work for future publication, I recommend:

1. Conduct additional experiments on standard NAS benchmarks (e.g., CIFAR-10) to demonstrate the framework's competitiveness with recent NAS methods.

2. Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost, including wall-clock time comparisons to standard training and NAS methods.

3. Investigate why the approach performs poorly on the convex dataset and provide insights into the limitations of the framework.

4. Simplify the theoretical proof or provide a more intuitive explanation of the key insights to make the theoretical contribution more accessible to readers.

5. Consider extending the framework to convolutional networks, as the current approach is limited to fully connected networks.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The convex dataset results are concerning. Can you provide more insight into why the nonparametric approach performs so poorly on this dataset compared to parametric networks? Is this related to the dataset characteristics or the specific hyperparameters used?

2. How does the computational cost of your approach scale with network size and dataset size? Can you provide a wall-clock time comparison to standard training with the same final network size?

3. The paper mentions that "nonparametric networks had a higher error for larger values of λ and a slightly lower error for smaller values of λ" (Table 2). Why is there a trade-off between λ and performance, and how should λ be selected in practice?

4. The paper states that "the nonparametric networks had a higher error for larger values of λ" but doesn't provide sufficient justification for the specific λ values chosen. How should λ be tuned in practice, and how sensitive is performance to this hyperparameter?

5. The paper claims the framework "achieves a higher accuracy than networks of the same size that are trained from scratch and, in some cases, achieve a higher accuracy than is possible by black-box methods." However, the paper doesn't provide a clear comparison to the best possible black-box method (e.g., Bayesian optimization with a well-tuned acquisition function). Can you clarify this comparison?
