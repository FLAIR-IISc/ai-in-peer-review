

# Review of "NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
This paper introduces a fundamentally novel framework called "nonparametric neural networks" that dynamically adapts network size during training rather than requiring separate training runs for different architectures. The authors propose a theoretical foundation showing that nonparametric networks achieve a global minimum at finite dimensionality under certain regularizers. They develop a novel normalization layer (CapNorm) and training algorithm (AdaRad) to implement this framework, which allows the network to grow and shrink units during training. The approach represents a bold departure from current model selection practices, which typically require multiple training runs with different architectures. The paper demonstrates promising results on MNIST and rectangles images datasets, showing that nonparametric networks can achieve comparable or better accuracy than parametric networks of the same size, while being substantially smaller. The theoretical contribution with Theorem 1 is particularly strong, providing a solid foundation for the approach.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper introduces a significant new concept that fundamentally changes how we approach neural network architecture selection. The theoretical foundation is robust, and the approach represents a bold, novel direction in neural network research.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
This paper deserves acceptance for two key reasons:

First, the core concept of nonparametric neural networks is exceptionally novel. While there's existing work on network pruning and architecture search, the authors' approach of dynamically growing and shrinking the network during a single training run represents a paradigm shift. This is not just an incremental improvement but a fundamentally new way of thinking about model selection. The theoretical justification (Theorem 1) is strong, showing that the optimization problem has a global minimum at finite dimensionality under certain regularizers, which is a significant contribution to the field.

Second, the paper demonstrates the practical viability of the approach. On two of three benchmark datasets (MNIST and rectangles images), nonparametric networks outperform parametric networks of the same size, achieving comparable or better accuracy while being substantially smaller. The scalability experiment on the large poker dataset further demonstrates the approach's potential for real-world applications. The authors' acknowledgment of the approach's limitations (failure on the convex dataset) and their analysis of why it might occur shows intellectual honesty.

The paper's contribution is more valuable than many methodologically perfect papers that offer only incremental improvements. The authors have created a new research direction that could significantly impact how we approach neural network architecture selection.

## 4. Additional Feedback
While the paper is strong, here are some suggestions to further improve it:

1. The failure on the convex dataset deserves more thorough analysis. Why does the approach fail here while succeeding on the other two datasets? A deeper exploration of the properties of the convex dataset that make it challenging for this approach would strengthen the paper.

2. The paper should include a more direct comparison with recent state-of-the-art architecture search methods (e.g., NAS with reinforcement learning or evolutionary algorithms) rather than just random search. This would better establish the relative position of the proposed approach.

3. The theoretical proof (Section 7.1) is very detailed but could benefit from a more intuitive explanation of why the nonparametric approach converges to a finite size. A high-level summary of the key insights from the proof would help readers grasp the theoretical contribution more easily.

4. The paper mentions that AdaRad-M performs similarly to AdaRad, but it would be valuable to include a brief comparison of computational complexity between AdaRad and standard optimizers like Adam, especially since the paper mentions that AdaRad has a more graceful computational cost scaling.

5. The paper could benefit from a clearer discussion of when this approach would be most useful. For example, how does it compare to existing methods in terms of time and computational resources? The paper mentions that the "overhead" of growing networks is ameliorated on larger datasets, but a more quantitative comparison would be helpful.

These suggestions aim to strengthen the paper's presentation and impact, not to indicate that the paper is deficient. The core contribution is sufficiently novel and compelling to warrant acceptance as is.
