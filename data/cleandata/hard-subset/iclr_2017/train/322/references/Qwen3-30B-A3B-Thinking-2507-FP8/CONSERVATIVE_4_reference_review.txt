

# Review of "NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS"

## 1. Summary of Contribution
The paper proposes "nonparametric neural networks" as a framework for automatically adapting network size during training. The authors claim their approach can dynamically shrink and expand network architectures to select optimal size during a single training run, without requiring multiple full training runs as in standard hyperparameter optimization. They introduce a theoretical foundation (Theorem 1), a novel normalization layer (CapNorm), and a new training algorithm (AdaRad) based on an `2 fan-in regularizer. The paper claims their method outperforms parametric networks of the same size on MNIST and rectangles images datasets, while being substantially smaller, and achieves results competitive with exhaustive random search.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The paper presents an unproven, unconventional approach without sufficient evidence of practical advantages over established methods. The theoretical claims are overly complex, the empirical validation is incomplete and inconsistent, and the comparison to baselines is not sufficiently rigorous.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's core contribution appears to be a reimagining of network pruning with a focus on dynamic network size adjustment during training. However, the authors fail to demonstrate that their approach provides meaningful advantages over existing methods that already address similar problems. 

First, the theoretical foundation (Theorem 1) is highly complex and buried in the appendix. While the authors claim to prove that nonparametric networks achieve a global minimum at finite dimensionality, the proof is extremely technical and not sufficiently accessible to verify. More critically, the conditions under which this theorem holds are restrictive (e.g., requiring specific properties of nonlinearities), making it unclear how broadly applicable the theoretical result is to practical neural network architectures.

Second, the empirical validation is problematic. The authors report mixed results across datasets: strong performance on MNIST and rectangles images, but poor performance on the convex dataset. This inconsistency suggests the approach may not generalize well. More importantly, the comparison to parametric networks is not fair. The authors trained parametric networks using different hyperparameters (including different optimization algorithms and potentially better initialization) than the nonparametric networks, which undermines the comparison. For example, they state that "the parametric network benefits from an increased step size - in this case α = 300" - yet they don't explain why they used such a different step size for parametric networks.

Third, the paper claims to solve two drawbacks of black-box optimization: the time required for multiple training runs and the inability to adjust network size during training. However, the paper doesn't demonstrate that their method significantly reduces training time compared to standard practices. The authors note that "nonparametric networks must wait for their units to be added," which actually increases training time compared to standard approaches. The paper also fails to compare against existing methods that dynamically adjust network size (e.g., network pruning approaches that can be applied during training), making it unclear what novel contribution this work offers.

## 4. Additional Feedback and Questions

The authors should address the following points to strengthen their contribution:

1. Provide a more comprehensive comparison against established methods for dynamic network size adjustment (e.g., pruning methods that can be applied during training, or network architecture search techniques that don't require multiple training runs).

2. Conduct a fair comparison with parametric networks by using the same hyperparameters and training protocols for both approaches, rather than optimizing each separately. The current comparison is not scientifically rigorous.

3. Demonstrate the time savings of their approach compared to standard practices. The paper claims to avoid the "days on many cores or multiple GPUs" required by black-box methods, but provides no evidence to support this claim.

4. Provide a clearer explanation of how their method differs from prior work by Wei et al. (2016) and others who have explored similar concepts of dynamically adjusting network size. The paper cites these works but doesn't sufficiently differentiate their contribution.

5. Address the inconsistent results on the convex dataset. Why does the method fail on this dataset when it works well on others? This inconsistency is a major red flag for the approach's generalizability.

6. The paper claims "the network ultimately chosen can achieve a higher accuracy than networks of the same size that are trained from scratch and, in some cases, achieve a higher accuracy than is possible by black-box methods." However, the paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence to support this claim. The authors should provide statistical significance tests for their results.

7. The paper states that "current hardware is geared towards dense weight matrices" and therefore focuses on p=2 regularization. However, the paper doesn't provide evidence that this choice of regularization is optimal for the nonparametric framework, or that it's the only viable approach.

I would appreciate clarification on how this approach provides meaningful advantages over existing methods for network size selection, and why the authors believe it should be considered a significant contribution to the field rather than a minor modification of existing pruning techniques.
