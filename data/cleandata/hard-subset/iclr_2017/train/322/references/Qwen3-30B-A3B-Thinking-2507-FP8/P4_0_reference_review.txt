

# Review for "NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper introduces "nonparametric neural networks," a framework for automatically selecting network size during a single training run. The authors present a theoretical foundation proving that nonparametric networks achieve a global training error minimum at finite dimensionality when using fan-in or fan-out regularizers with p=2. They develop a novel normalization layer (CapNorm) and training algorithm (AdaRad) that enable dynamic unit addition and removal during training. The approach addresses two key limitations of existing model selection methods: the computational expense of training multiple networks (black-box optimization) and the failure to exploit opportunities to improve network size during training. Empirical results demonstrate that nonparametric networks achieve higher accuracy than parametric networks of the same size on MNIST and rectangles images datasets, while requiring less computational effort than exhaustive search methods.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution with a well-proven theorem, and provides compelling empirical evidence supporting the approach.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper successfully addresses a meaningful problem in neural network model selection with a well-motivated approach. The theoretical foundation is rigorous, with Theorem 1 proven in section 7.1. The proof carefully handles the conditions for nonlinearities and error functions, and the lemmas build a solid foundation for the main result. This theoretical contribution is particularly valuable as it justifies the approach and prevents the degeneration of network size during optimization.

Empirically, the paper provides thorough evaluation across three datasets (MNIST, rectangles images, convex) and a large-scale poker dataset. The experimental design is sound, with careful comparison to parametric networks of the same size and an exhaustive random search baseline. The results for MNIST and rectangles images are particularly compelling, showing nonparametric networks achieving competitive or superior performance compared to parametric networks while requiring less computational effort. The analysis in Figure 3 provides valuable insights into the dynamic network size adjustment process, demonstrating how units are added and removed during training.

The paper is well-placed in the literature, with a thorough discussion of related work on pruning, network growth during training, and model selection methods. The authors clearly differentiate their approach from existing methods, particularly by not requiring a high-performing starting network (as pruning methods do) and by enabling dynamic size adjustment during training.

## 4. Additional Feedback
- The claim that "nonparametric networks achieve a higher accuracy than networks of the same size that are trained from scratch and, in some cases, achieve a higher accuracy than is possible by black-box methods" should be qualified to acknowledge the performance on the convex dataset, which contradicts this claim. The paper does acknowledge this limitation, but the claim is stated as a general assertion without qualification.

- The justification for preferring p=2 over p=1 could be strengthened. While the paper states that "current hardware is geared towards dense weight matrices," it would be valuable to provide evidence about the hardware efficiency of p=2 versus p=1 networks, such as inference speed or memory usage comparisons.

- The explanation of why standard batch normalization cannot be applied directly with fan-in regularizers could be expanded to provide more detail about the specific mechanism by which batch normalization would negate the regularizer.

## 5. Questions for Authors
- On the convex dataset, why did nonparametric networks underperform compared to parametric networks? Could this be due to specific characteristics of the dataset that make the dynamic adjustment approach less suitable?

- The paper states that "nonparametric networks trained without batch normalization could not compete with parametric networks trained with it." Could you provide more detailed performance metrics (validation and test errors) for networks trained with and without batch normalization?

- The paper mentions that "the best nonparametric network made no mistake on the test set of 100,000 examples" for the poker dataset. Could you provide the exact test error rate and how it compares to the best parametric network on this dataset?

- The paper claims that "p = 2 sets entire fan-ins to zero at once" and "obtains dense weight matrices." How does this differ from simply using L1 regularization to achieve sparsity followed by pruning to obtain dense matrices, and why is the dynamic approach superior?
