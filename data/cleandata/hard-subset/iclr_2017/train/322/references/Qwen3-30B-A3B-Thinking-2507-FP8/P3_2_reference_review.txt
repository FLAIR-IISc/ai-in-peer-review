

# Peer Review: NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contributions

This paper introduces "nonparametric neural networks," a framework for dynamically adjusting network size during training without requiring prior information about the task. The authors claim their method:
- Automatically selects optimal network size during a single training run
- Achieves higher accuracy than parametric networks of the same size
- Outperforms exhaustive random search on two of three benchmark datasets
- Has theoretical guarantees of convergence to a global minimum with proper regularization
- Introduces novel components: CapNorm (modified batch normalization) and AdaRad (adaptive radial-angular gradient descent)

The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution by proving that nonparametric networks achieve a global minimum at finite dimensionality with fan-in/fan-out regularizers (Theorem 1). The empirical results on MNIST and rectangles images are promising, showing that nonparametric networks outperform parametric networks of the same size while using fewer parameters.

## 2. Decision

**Accept** with minor revisions.

**Key reasons for acceptance:**
1. The theoretical contribution is strong, rigorous, and addresses a significant problem in neural network model selection
2. The framework has clear practical benefits over current black-box optimization methods
3. The empirical results are generally positive and well-designed

## 3. Supporting Arguments

### Theoretical Contribution
The paper's strongest aspect is its theoretical foundation. The proof of Theorem 1 is comprehensive, addressing potential degeneration issues in nonparametric networks. The authors carefully establish conditions under which the optimization problem has a global minimum at finite dimensionality. This theoretical guarantee is rare in neural network papers and represents a significant contribution.

### Novel Methodology
The introduction of CapNorm and AdaRad is well-motivated and addresses key challenges in training nonparametric networks. The theoretical justification for using p=2 (rather than p=1) due to hardware considerations is reasonable, though the paper could better justify this choice beyond hardware considerations.

### Empirical Results
The results on MNIST and rectangles images are compelling, with nonparametric networks achieving comparable or better accuracy than parametric networks while using significantly fewer parameters. The analysis of the training process (Figure 3) provides valuable insights into how the framework dynamically adjusts network size.

### Limitations and Concerns
The main limitation is the poor performance on the convex dataset, where nonparametric networks underperformed compared to parametric networks. The authors attribute this to the framework's tendency to converge to small network sizes, but this limitation needs more thorough discussion. The paper would benefit from a more detailed analysis of why the framework struggles with certain dataset characteristics.

The comparison to neural architecture search (NAS) methods is notably absent, despite NAS being a direct competitor in the space of automatic network structure selection. This omission weakens the positioning of the work in the literature.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

### 1. Expand Experimental Evaluation
The paper should be extended to more challenging datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10 or ImageNet) to demonstrate broader applicability. The current evaluation on MNIST and poker is limited in scope.

### 2. Address Performance on Convex Dataset
The authors should provide a more detailed analysis of the convex dataset results, including:
- A comparison of the final network sizes between nonparametric and parametric approaches
- Analysis of why the framework converges to small network sizes on this dataset
- Discussion of whether this limitation is inherent to the framework or can be mitigated

### 3. Position Against NAS Methods
The paper should explicitly compare and contrast with neural architecture search (NAS) methods like Zoph & Le (2017) and others. This would help clarify the unique contribution of the nonparametric framework.

### 4. Computational Cost Analysis
While the paper claims computational benefits, it lacks a detailed comparison of total training time versus black-box optimization approaches. A more rigorous analysis of computational efficiency would strengthen the practical claims.

### 5. Clarify Theoretical Assumptions
The proof relies on specific assumptions about nonlinearities (e.g., differentiability). The paper should more clearly discuss the implications of these assumptions and how the framework might be extended to handle non-differentiable nonlinearities like ReLU (which is differentiable almost everywhere but not at 0).

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The convex dataset results show clear underperformance. Can you provide more insight into why the framework struggles with this particular dataset, and whether you believe this is a fundamental limitation or a tunable parameter issue?

2. You state that you use p=2 because "current hardware is geared towards dense weight matrices." Is this the only reason for choosing p=2, or would you expect p=1 to yield better performance on certain tasks despite potential sparsity?

3. How would you expect your framework to scale to very deep networks (e.g., ResNet architectures), where layer depth is also a hyperparameter? The paper mentions future work on automatically choosing depth, but this is not addressed in the current work.

4. In the proof of Theorem 1, the authors assume differentiability of nonlinearities. How would you modify the proof (or the framework) to handle ReLU, which is not differentiable at 0, yet is the most common nonlinearity in modern networks?

5. The paper claims that "nonparametric networks achieve a global training error minimum at some finite dimensionality." However, this is a training error minimum, not necessarily a test error minimum. How do you reconcile this theoretical guarantee with the practical goal of minimizing test error?

## Conclusion

This paper presents a theoretically sound and practically promising approach to automatic network size selection. The theoretical contribution is strong and well-justified, and the empirical results demonstrate clear benefits on several benchmark tasks. While the evaluation could be expanded and the comparison to NAS methods would strengthen the paper, the core contribution is significant and represents a valuable advancement in neural network model selection. I recommend acceptance with the suggested revisions.
