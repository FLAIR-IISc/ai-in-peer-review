

# Review of "NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS"

## 1. Summary of Contribution

The paper proposes a novel framework called "nonparametric neural networks" that dynamically shrinks and expands network size during training to automatically select an optimal network architecture. The authors claim their approach eliminates the need for black-box model selection methods (like grid search or Bayesian optimization) by selecting network size during a single training run. They introduce a theoretical framework with a proof of convergence, a novel normalization layer (CapNorm), and a training algorithm (AdaRad). The paper claims their method achieves higher accuracy than parametric networks of the same size and even outperforms traditional black-box model selection approaches.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper fails to provide sufficient empirical evidence to support its claims, and the theoretical justification, while complex, lacks the clarity and rigor expected for a major contribution.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The primary issue is the lack of compelling evidence that this approach significantly outperforms established methods. The paper claims "nonparametric networks substantially outperform parametric networks of the same size" on MNIST, but the comparison is limited to networks of the same size, not the best possible parametric networks. The paper does not demonstrate that the nonparametric approach achieves better accuracy than the best possible parametric networks (which might have different sizes), which is the crucial comparison needed to justify the approach.

The empirical evaluation is severely limited. The authors evaluate on only three small datasets (MNIST, rectangles images, convex) with a fixed architecture (two hidden layers, starting with 10 units each, adding one unit per epoch). This is insufficient to establish the generality of the approach. The paper claims success on the poker dataset, but the results show only marginal improvement over parametric networks, and the paper doesn't demonstrate that the nonparametric approach outperforms black-box methods when those methods are properly applied.

The theoretical claim (Theorem 1) is presented as a key contribution, but the proof is extremely complex (over 70 pages in the appendix) and difficult to verify. For a skeptical reviewer, the lack of clarity in the proof is a significant concern. The paper states "the proof is found in the appendix," but the complexity of the proof undermines confidence in the claim without careful verification, which would require substantial effort beyond a standard review.

The paper also fails to adequately address why the approach works on some datasets (MNIST, rectangles) but fails on others (convex), which is a critical concern for a method claiming broad applicability.

## 4. Additional Feedback

1. **Empirical Rigor**: The paper needs to provide a more comprehensive comparison to established model selection methods (grid search, Bayesian optimization, random search) with proper hyperparameter tuning. The current comparison to parametric networks of the same size is insufficient to demonstrate the claimed advantage.

2. **Theoretical Clarity**: The theoretical proof should be simplified or at least summarized in a way that makes the key insights more accessible. The complexity of the proof is a major barrier to acceptance.

3. **Broader Evaluation**: The paper should evaluate on a wider range of datasets and tasks to demonstrate the robustness of the approach. The limited dataset coverage (three small datasets) is insufficient to claim general applicability.

4. **Comparison to Related Work**: The paper should more thoroughly compare to related work on network growth and pruning (e.g., "Dynamic Node Creation in Backpropagation Networks" by Ash, "Net2Net: Accelerating Learning via Knowledge Transfer" by Chen et al., "Learning the Structure of Deep Convolutional Networks" by Feng and Darrell). The current comparison is superficial.

5. **Ablation Studies**: The paper should include ablation studies to show the contribution of each component (CapNorm, AdaRad, unit addition/removal) to the overall performance.

6. **Computational Cost**: The paper claims the approach avoids the computational cost of black-box methods, but it doesn't provide a clear comparison of the total training time (including the time to add/remove units) versus traditional methods.

7. **Understanding the Failure on Convex Dataset**: The paper should provide a deeper analysis of why the approach fails on the convex dataset, as this is a critical limitation that undermines the claimed generality of the approach.

## Questions for Authors

1. Can you provide a more direct comparison between the best nonparametric network and the best possible parametric network (with any size) for each dataset, rather than just networks of the same size?

2. Why does the approach fail on the convex dataset, while succeeding on MNIST and rectangles images? Is there a fundamental limitation to the approach that could be identified?

3. How does the total training time of your approach compare to traditional black-box model selection methods when properly applied (with sufficient hyperparameter exploration)?

4. Can you provide a simpler explanation or summary of the key insights from Theorem 1 that would make the proof more accessible to reviewers?

5. Why did you choose to add only one unit per epoch? What would happen if you added more units per epoch, and how would that affect the results?
