1. Introduces "nonparametric neural networks": a framework that dynamically grows/shrinks network size during training to find an optimal architecture without prior task knowledge.

2. Proves a theoretical guarantee (Theorem 1) that the optimization attains a global minimum at finite dimensionality using fan-in or fan-out regularizers.

3. Proposes two new components: CapNorm (modified batch normalization) and AdaRad (adaptive optimizer separating radial and angular updates) for nonparametric training.

4. Shows the insight that self-similar nonlinearities (e.g., ReLU) remove the need for layer-specific regularization parameters (Proposition 1).

5. Empirically on MNIST the method matches top accuracy with far fewer units (144 vs. 694).

6. Empirically on the poker dataset the method reaches near-perfect accuracy (0% test error) without prior architecture tuning.

7. Performs poorly on the convex dataset, indicating potential limits or failure modes when tasks require larger capacity.

8. Decision: Accept with minor revisions — novel, well-motivated, theoretically strong, but evaluation needs clarification.

9. Comparison to related work: distinguishes itself from grid/Bayesian model selection and pruning by adjusting size during training rather than post hoc or black-box search.

10. Request to clarify the "same size" comparison: need explicit comparisons to parametric networks of exactly the same size, not just final nonparametric size.

11. Request deeper analysis of the convex dataset failure to determine if it’s dataset-specific or a fundamental limitation.

12. Ask to report variance across runs (e.g., standard deviations/error bars) instead of selecting a representative run.

13. Ask for more detailed computational-efficiency comparisons (wall-clock time) between AdaRad and optimizers like RMSprop.

14. Question whether the fan-in regularizer with p=2 (dense matrices) was chosen over p=1 (sparser matrices) and how p affects performance and theory.

15. Question why AdaRad rather than AdaRad-M (momentum variant) is emphasized, given similar performance and common use of momentum.
