- Introduces nonparametric neural networks that grow/shrink units during training to automate architecture selection.
- Proves (Theorem 1) that with proper regularization, nonparametric networks reach a finite-dimensional global minimum.
- Proposes CapNorm normalization to avoid negating the regularizer and a new optimizer AdaRad for radial-angular updates.
- Decision: Weak Accept.
- Main reason for acceptance: strong theoretical contribution and addressing costly black-box model selection.
- Theorem 1 is rigorously proven, including handling non-differentiability via directional derivatives.
- CapNorm is a practical design choice motivated by avoiding regularizer cancellation from standard batch norm.
- AdaRad’s radial-angular decomposition is well-motivated to separately control learning speed and unit elimination.
- Empirically, nonparametric nets outperform parametric nets of the same size on MNIST and rectangle images.
- Scales to a large poker dataset (1.025M examples) with promising results reported.
- Shows poor performance on the convex dataset, indicating a significant limitation and unexplained failure mode.
- Convergence is slow (roughly one unit added per epoch), and computational efficiency versus standard methods is not thoroughly evaluated.
- Limited comparison with state-of-the-art NAS methods (e.g., DARTS, ENAS) weakens positioning against current architecture search work.
- No discussion or experiments extending the framework to convolutional networks, despite claiming it as future work.
- Strengths: exceptional theoretical foundation, the f-equivalence concept, and practical contributions (CapNorm, AdaRad).
- Requested improvements: analyze convex dataset failure, compare with NAS methods, provide training-time comparisons, and discuss adapting to convolutional layers.
- Questions for authors include clarifying convex dataset issues, direct NAS comparisons, exact poker test error rate, rationale for using p=2 regularization over p=1, and effects of unit initialization.
- Conclusion: valuable and theoretically strong contribution that needs more empirical analysis and comparisons; recommend weak accept.
