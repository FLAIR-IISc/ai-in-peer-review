

# Review of "NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes "nonparametric neural networks," a framework for dynamically adjusting network size during training by adding and removing units. The authors claim their approach finds an optimal network size within a single training run, achieving better accuracy than networks of fixed size trained from scratch, and outperforming black-box model selection methods. The paper introduces CapNorm (a modified batch normalization) and AdaRad (an adaptive gradient descent algorithm) to support this framework. The authors prove a theorem (Theorem 1) that nonparametric networks achieve a global minimum at finite dimensionality under specific regularizer conditions, and provide experimental results on MNIST, rectangles images, and convex datasets.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The paper contains multiple significant methodological errors and theoretical inconsistencies that undermine the core claims. The most critical issue is a fundamental flaw in the theoretical foundation that makes the main theorem unproven under the conditions stated.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

### Theoretical Inconsistency
The most severe issue is the disconnect between Theorem 1 and its proof. Theorem 1 states that nonparametric networks achieve a global minimum with "λ > 0 and 1 ≤ p < ∞" without requiring differentiability of nonlinearities. However, the proof in Section 7.1 (Lemma 2 and 3) explicitly assumes that "the σl are differentiable everywhere" (lines 1-2 of Lemma 2 proof). This is a critical oversight because:

1. The experiments use ReLU, which is not differentiable at 0 (contradicting the differentiability assumption)
2. The paper claims "most commonly used nonlinearities are admissible under this theorem as long as σ(0) = 0" (Section 7.1), but ReLU is not differentiable at 0, making it inadmissible under the actual proof conditions

This theoretical inconsistency means the main claim of the paper (that the approach finds a global minimum) is not proven for the actual methods used in the experiments.

### Algorithmic Error
Algorithm 1 (AdaRad) contains a clear mathematical error in line 15: "[Wl(i, j)]i = [Wl(i, j)]i - αrr". Here, r is defined as the parallel component (a scalar value), but it's being subtracted from a vector [Wl(i, j)]i. This operation is mathematically nonsensical and would produce an error in any implementation. The paper doesn't provide a correction for this error.

### Experimental Protocol Flaws
The experimental methodology contains significant issues:

1. The paper claims to manually choose a "typical representative" from 10 networks based on "approximating the median of both network size and test classification error" (Section 7.4.1). This is non-standard, introduces subjectivity, and makes results difficult to reproduce.

2. The paper states "we used the same random seed as in step 1" for parametric networks, but doesn't specify which seed was used, undermining reproducibility.

3. For the convex dataset, the paper reports poor performance but provides insufficient analysis of why the approach fails on this dataset, which is a significant counterexample to the general claims.

### Minor Technical Issues
- In the proof of Lemma 2, line 24 states "BBx,l-1/λ" but should be "BBx,l-1/λ" (missing fraction bar)
- The paper uses inconsistent notation for dimensionality (sometimes "d", sometimes "dl")
- The paper claims "p = 2, on the other hand, sets entire fan-ins (for the fan-in regularizer) or fan-outs (for the fan-out regularizer) to zero at once" but doesn't clarify how this differs from p=1
- The computational cost comparison in Table 1 is vague and lacks concrete timing measurements

## 4. Additional Feedback

1. **Theoretical Correction:** The authors should either revise Theorem 1 to include differentiability as a condition (which would exclude ReLU, the main nonlinearity used in experiments) or provide a proper proof that doesn't require differentiability. The current proof is incomplete for the methods they actually use.

2. **Algorithm Correction:** The AdaRad algorithm (Algorithm 1) needs to be fixed. The error in line 15 must be corrected to subtract the scalar r from each component of the vector.

3. **Experimental Protocol:** The paper should use a more rigorous method for selecting representative networks (e.g., using the median network size and test error across all 10 runs). The current subjective selection method weakens the experimental results.

4. **Convex Dataset Analysis:** The paper should provide a detailed analysis of why the approach fails on the convex dataset. This is a critical counterexample to the general claims of the paper.

5. **Reproducibility:** The paper should specify all random seeds used and provide more detailed experimental protocols to enable full reproducibility.

6. **Clarity on Nonlinearity Choice:** The paper should clarify why ReLU was used despite its non-differentiability at 0, and how this affects the theoretical guarantees.

## Questions for Authors
1. How do you reconcile the differentiability requirement in the proof with the use of ReLU in the experiments?
2. Can you provide a corrected version of Algorithm 1 that addresses the mathematical error in line 15?
3. Why does the approach fail on the convex dataset, and what modifications would make it work for this case?
4. Can you provide the exact random seeds used for all experiments to enable full reproducibility?
5. How does the theoretical guarantee hold for ReLU, which is not differentiable at 0, as required by the proof?

These issues are fundamental to the paper's claims and cannot be easily overlooked. While the concept of dynamic network size adjustment is interesting, the current theoretical foundation is insufficiently supported, and the experimental methodology contains significant flaws that undermine the results.
