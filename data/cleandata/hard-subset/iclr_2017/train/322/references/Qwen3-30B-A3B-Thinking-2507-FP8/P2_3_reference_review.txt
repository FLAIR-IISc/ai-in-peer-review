

# Review of "Nonparametric Neural Networks"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper introduces "nonparametric neural networks," a framework for automatically adapting network size during a single training run without requiring multiple full training runs for model selection. The authors propose a novel approach where network size dynamically shrinks and expands based on a fan-in regularizer with p=2, supported by theoretical guarantees (Theorem 1), a new normalization layer (CapNorm), and a specialized training algorithm (AdaRad). The key contribution is the ability to select optimal network size during training, potentially eliminating the need for expensive black-box optimization methods. The paper claims nonparametric networks outperform parametric networks of the same size on MNIST and rectangles images, and achieve competitive results with exhaustive search on these datasets, while being significantly more efficient computationally.

## 2. Decision

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper makes a well-motivated contribution to a significant problem in machine learning (automatic model selection), with strong theoretical foundations and promising empirical results. The key reasons for acceptance are: (1) The paper clearly identifies and addresses a genuine limitation in current model selection practices, and (2) The theoretical claims are rigorously proven, and the empirical results are scientifically sound.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

**Problem identification and motivation (Strength):** The paper effectively articulates the problem of expensive black-box model selection (grid search, random search, Bayesian optimization) that requires multiple full training runs. The authors correctly identify two key limitations: computational cost (each training run takes days) and lack of opportunities to improve the network during training. Their proposed solution—dynamically adjusting network size during a single training run—directly addresses both limitations.

**Theoretical soundness (Strength):** The paper provides a rigorous proof of Theorem 1 in the appendix, demonstrating that nonparametric neural networks achieve a global training error minimum at finite dimensionality with fan-in or fan-out regularizers. The proof is detailed, mathematically sound, and carefully addresses the necessary conditions for the theorem to hold. This theoretical foundation is a significant strength.

**Empirical results (Strength with reservation):** The experiments on MNIST and rectangles images show compelling results, with nonparametric networks substantially outperforming parametric networks of the same size. The comparison to exhaustive random search is well-executed and demonstrates the practical advantage of their approach. The poker dataset experiment shows the method scales to larger problems. However, the poor performance on the convex dataset is a notable limitation that the authors acknowledge, suggesting the approach may not be universally applicable.

**Methodological innovation (Strength):** The introduction of CapNorm (a modified batch normalization) and AdaRad (a novel training algorithm) is well-motivated and effectively addresses the challenges of training nonparametric networks. The careful design of AdaRad to handle the orthogonal and radial components of gradient updates is particularly impressive.

**Comparison to literature (Weakness):** The paper could better position itself against modern neural architecture search (NAS) methods, which are more directly related to automatic model selection. While the authors mention some NAS work (Zoph & Le, 2017), they don't clearly differentiate their approach from contemporary NAS techniques that also aim to automatically select network architectures.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To further strengthen the paper, I recommend the following improvements:

1. **Expand the discussion of related work:** Clarify how this work relates to modern NAS methods (e.g., DARTS, ENAS, etc.). While the authors mention NAS in section 5, they don't explicitly contrast their approach with these more recent methods.

2. **Address the convex dataset limitation:** The authors acknowledge poor performance on the convex dataset but don't provide insights into why this occurs or how the approach might be adapted for such problems. A brief discussion of the limitations of the approach would strengthen the paper.

3. **Provide computational efficiency analysis:** While the paper claims to avoid the cost of multiple training runs, it doesn't provide a clear comparison of total training time between their approach and standard black-box optimization. A table comparing total training time (including the time for unit addition/removal) would be valuable.

4. **Clarify the relationship between λ and network size:** The paper mentions λ as a regularization parameter but doesn't provide clear guidance on how to set it in practice. A more detailed discussion of the relationship between λ and final network size would be helpful for practitioners.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. How would you expect your approach to scale to more complex architectures (e.g., convolutional networks) that the authors mention as future work? What challenges would arise?

2. The paper mentions that the convex dataset performed poorly with nonparametric networks. Could you provide more insight into why this particular dataset was problematic for your approach, and whether you have hypotheses about what modifications might improve performance on such datasets?

3. The paper states that "nonparametric networks achieved a higher accuracy than networks of the same size that are trained from scratch." Could you clarify whether this is a general claim or specific to the datasets and architectures tested? What evidence do you have that this is a consistent phenomenon across different tasks?

4. The paper claims that "nonparametric networks can achieve a higher accuracy than is possible by black-box methods." How do you reconcile this with the results on the convex dataset, where nonparametric networks underperformed parametric networks of the same size? Is the claim that nonparametric networks can achieve better performance than black-box methods in some cases, rather than universally?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the problem of neural network model selection. With the suggested revisions to address the limitations and strengthen the discussion, it would be an excellent contribution to ICLR.
