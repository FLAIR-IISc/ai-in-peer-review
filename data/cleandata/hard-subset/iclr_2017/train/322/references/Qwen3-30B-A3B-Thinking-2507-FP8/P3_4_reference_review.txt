

# Peer Review: "NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims

The paper introduces a novel framework called "nonparametric neural networks" that dynamically adjusts network size during training to automatically select the optimal architecture. The authors propose a theoretical foundation showing that with specific regularizers (fan-in or fan-out with p=2), the network size converges to a finite dimensionality that minimizes the training error. They introduce CapNorm (a modified batch normalization), AdaRad (a novel training algorithm with radial-angular gradient descent), and a unit addition/removal strategy. The paper claims that their approach:
- Achieves better accuracy than parametric networks of the same size
- Outperforms exhaustive search methods on two of three benchmark datasets
- Avoids the need for a pre-trained network (unlike pruning approaches)
- Scales to larger datasets while maintaining computational efficiency

The theoretical contribution is significant, with a detailed proof showing that the proposed regularizers prevent unbounded growth of network size. The empirical results are promising on MNIST and rectangles images datasets, with good scalability to the poker dataset.

## 2. Decision

**Reject**

**Key reasons:** While the theoretical contribution is strong and the paper is well-written, the empirical evaluation has significant limitations that undermine the main claims. The approach fails on the convex dataset benchmark, and the "dynamic" aspect of network size adjustment is not truly dynamic during training but rather a slow growth followed by pruning at the end. Additionally, the paper lacks comparison to state-of-the-art neural architecture search methods.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The theoretical foundation (Theorem 1) is a significant contribution with a rigorous proof in the appendix. The authors convincingly demonstrate that their specific regularizer prevents unbounded growth, which is a meaningful theoretical advance. The CapNorm and AdaRad algorithms are well-motivated and properly justified.

However, the empirical evaluation has several critical issues:

1. **Failure on convex dataset**: The paper claims to "achieve results competitive with exhaustive random search" but fails to demonstrate this on the convex dataset, where nonparametric networks underperform significantly compared to parametric networks. This is a major limitation since the convex dataset is a standard benchmark for model selection methods. The authors don't adequately explain why their method fails here.

2. **Misrepresentation of "dynamic" training**: The paper claims to "dynamically and automatically shrink and expand the network as needed during a single training run." However, the experiments add units at a fixed rate (one per epoch) and only remove units after training has completed (following a period of no growth). This is not truly dynamic during training but rather a slow growth followed by pruning at the end. The authors should clarify this limitation.

3. **Lack of comparison to modern NAS methods**: The paper compares only to traditional model selection methods (grid search, random search), but fails to compare with state-of-the-art neural architecture search (NAS) methods like Zoph & Le (2017) or recent reinforcement learning approaches. This undermines the claim that their method "achieves results competitive with those of an exhaustive random search."

4. **Limited architectural scope**: The experiments are restricted to fully connected networks with only two hidden layers, while the approach is claimed to be general. The paper doesn't address how the method would scale to convolutional networks or more complex architectures, which are standard in modern deep learning.

5. **Computational cost**: While the paper notes AdaRad has higher computational cost than SGD, it doesn't quantify the practical cost difference or demonstrate that the accuracy gains justify the additional computation in real-world scenarios.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

1. **Clarify the "dynamic" aspect**: The paper should explicitly acknowledge that the network size adjustment isn't truly dynamic during training but rather a slow growth followed by pruning. This would prevent misleading readers about the method's capabilities.

2. **Expand empirical evaluation**: The authors should include comparisons to modern NAS methods (e.g., Zoph & Le, 2017; Liu et al., 2018) and test on more complex architectures like convolutional networks. This would strengthen the claim that their approach is competitive with state-of-the-art.

3. **Explain the convex dataset failure**: The paper should analyze why the method fails on the convex dataset and explore potential modifications to address this limitation. This would demonstrate a deeper understanding of the approach's boundaries.

4. **Quantify computational cost**: Provide concrete numbers on the computational overhead of AdaRad compared to standard optimizers, and demonstrate that the accuracy gains justify the additional cost.

5. **Refine the terminology**: The term "nonparametric" is potentially misleading, as the paper still has parameters (weights) but the dimensionality is not fixed. Consider an alternative name that better reflects the approach.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper claims "nonparametric networks achieve a higher accuracy than networks of the same size that are trained from scratch and, in some cases, achieve a higher accuracy than is possible by black-box methods." However, on the convex dataset, nonparametric networks underperform significantly. Can you explain why this dataset is particularly challenging for your approach, and whether you see a path to addressing this limitation?

2. The unit addition strategy (adding one unit per epoch) seems arbitrary and likely suboptimal. How would you adapt this approach for more efficient training, and what evidence do you have that this slow growth strategy is optimal for the method?

3. The paper states "the best nonparametric network made no mistake on the test set of 100.000 examples" for the poker dataset. However, the paper doesn't provide the exact test error rate or compare this to the best published result on OpenML. What was the specific error rate, and how does it compare to the best published result on that dataset?

4. The paper claims their approach "avoids the need for a high-performing network model as a starting point from which to prune." However, the experimental setup requires selecting a good value for λ (regularization parameter) through a grid search. How does this compare to the need for selecting a good starting point in traditional pruning approaches?

5. The paper focuses on p=2 regularizers due to hardware considerations, but the theoretical guarantee (Theorem 1) requires p<∞. How would the method behave with p=1 regularizers, and would the theoretical guarantee still hold?
