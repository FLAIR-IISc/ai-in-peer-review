

# Review of "Nonparametric Neural Networks"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes a novel framework called "nonparametric neural networks" that dynamically adapts network size during a single training run. The authors treat network dimensions as part of the optimization problem rather than fixed hyperparameters, proving theoretically that with appropriate fan-in/fan-out regularizers, the training error attains a global minimum at finite dimensionality. They introduce a novel training algorithm (AdaRad) and a normalization layer (CapNorm) to enable this framework. The experimental results show that nonparametric networks outperform parametric networks of the same size on MNIST and rectangles images datasets, achieving results competitive with exhaustive random search. The theoretical contributions are significant, and the framework represents a novel approach to automatic model selection.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The key reasons are: (1) the results are inconsistent across datasets, with poor performance on the convex dataset that is not adequately explained, and (2) the computational cost of the proposed method is not sufficiently justified compared to the marginal gains observed in some experiments.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's theoretical contribution (Theorem 1) is strong and well-proven in the appendix. The framework is well-motivated and positioned correctly in the literature, addressing the drawbacks of black-box optimization methods (high computational cost, not exploiting opportunities to improve during training). The experiments on MNIST and rectangles images are compelling, showing that nonparametric networks can achieve higher accuracy than parametric networks of the same size.

However, the failure on the convex dataset is concerning and not adequately addressed. The paper states that "nonparametric networks performed badly on the convex dataset" with parametric networks of the same size performing substantially better. This represents a significant limitation of the approach, especially since the convex dataset is a standard benchmark. The paper does not provide any analysis of why this failure occurs, which is critical for understanding the framework's limitations.

The paper claims that nonparametric networks can achieve higher accuracy than networks of the same size trained from scratch, but the results don't consistently support this claim across all datasets. The computational cost of the proposed method is also not thoroughly analyzed. While the paper mentions that AdaRad has "more graceful computational cost scaling," it doesn't provide concrete measurements of training time or resource usage compared to standard parametric networks. Given that the framework adds unit addition/removal during training, it's likely to have higher computational overhead, which the paper doesn't adequately address.

## 4. Additional Feedback
- The paper should provide a detailed analysis of why the method fails on the convex dataset. This is critical for understanding the framework's limitations and would strengthen the paper significantly.
- Include concrete computational cost comparisons with standard parametric networks, including training time and memory usage, for both small and large datasets.
- Clarify how the proposed framework differs from other network growth approaches (e.g., Wei et al. 2016) and why the proposed framework is superior.
- Discuss how the framework might be extended to handle more complex architectures (e.g., convolutional networks) as mentioned in the conclusion.
- Include ablation studies to isolate the impact of different components (e.g., AdaRad vs. SGD, CapNorm vs. batch norm).

## 5. Questions for Authors
- The paper shows that nonparametric networks underperform on the convex dataset compared to parametric networks of the same size. Can you provide a detailed analysis of why this happens? Is it related to the specific characteristics of the convex dataset, or does it point to a fundamental limitation of the framework?
- How does the computational cost of the proposed method scale with dataset size? The paper mentions experiments on the poker dataset, but it doesn't provide concrete measurements of training time or resource usage compared to standard parametric networks.
- The paper claims that the framework avoids the need for a high-performing starting point, but many network growth approaches also start from scratch. Can you clarify how this framework differs from those methods in terms of the mechanism for adding/removing units?
- The paper uses λ to control the regularization strength, but it's not clear how this parameter should be set in practice. Did you try to automate this parameter selection, or is it still a hyperparameter that needs to be tuned?
