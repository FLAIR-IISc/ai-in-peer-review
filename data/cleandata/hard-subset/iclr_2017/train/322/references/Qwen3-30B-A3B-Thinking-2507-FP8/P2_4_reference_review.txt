

# Review of "NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

The paper introduces "nonparametric neural networks," a novel framework for automatically selecting optimal network size during training without requiring prior knowledge of the task. The key contribution is a method that dynamically adds and removes units during training, using fan-in or fan-out regularizers to induce sparsity. The authors prove that with appropriate regularization, the network converges to a finite size with optimal performance (Theorem 1). They introduce a novel training algorithm (AdaRad) and a new normalization layer (CapNorm) to enable this process. The approach aims to overcome two major drawbacks of existing black-box model selection methods: (1) the need to train multiple full networks for different sizes, and (2) the inability to improve network performance during training by adjusting size. Experiments show that nonparametric networks outperform parametric networks of the same size on MNIST and rectangles images, while achieving competitive results to exhaustive random search. The paper also demonstrates scalability to the poker dataset.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **acceptance**. The paper makes a significant theoretical and practical contribution to the problem of neural network model selection. The theoretical foundation is rigorous, the approach is well-motivated, and the experimental results support the claims for most datasets. The paper addresses an important problem with clear practical implications for deep learning practitioners.

Key reasons for acceptance:
1. The theoretical contribution (Theorem 1) is mathematically rigorous and provides a fundamental justification for the approach.
2. The experimental results are comprehensive, with detailed analysis of the training process, and demonstrate clear advantages over parametric networks of the same size on two out of three datasets.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's theoretical contribution is its strongest aspect. Theorem 1 proves that with fan-in or fan-out regularizers (with λ > 0 and 1 ≤ p < ∞), nonparametric neural networks achieve a global training error minimum at finite dimensionality. The proof in the appendix is detailed and thorough, addressing potential concerns about degeneration with increasing network size. The proof is structured as a sequence of lemmas that build toward the main result, with careful attention to the conditions required for the nonlinearities and error functions. Proposition 1 further strengthens the theoretical foundation by showing how different regularization parameters across layers can be unified.

The experimental results are well-designed and comprehensive. The authors compare nonparametric networks to:
- Parametric networks of the same size (trained with optimal hyperparameters)
- Exhaustive random search over network sizes

On MNIST, the nonparametric network achieves nearly the same performance as the best parametric network but with significantly fewer parameters (144 vs. 694 units). On rectangles images, nonparametric networks outperform parametric networks of the same size when λ is small, achieving the globally best performance with substantially fewer parameters. The detailed analysis in Figure 3 provides valuable insights into the training dynamics, showing how units are added, learn, and are eliminated during training.

The paper is well-positioned in the literature, with thorough discussion of related work on model selection (Bergstra & Bengio, Snoek et al.), network pruning (Alvarez & Salzmann, Molchanov et al.), and dynamic network architectures (Wei et al., Chen et al.). The authors clearly articulate how their approach differs from and builds upon existing methods.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong overall, the following points would improve it:

1. **Address the convex dataset results**: The paper reports poor performance on the convex dataset, which is a significant concern. The authors should provide a more detailed analysis of why this dataset is problematic for their approach. What specific characteristics of the convex dataset make it challenging for nonparametric networks? This would strengthen the paper's claim that the approach is generally applicable.

2. **Compare to recent neural architecture search (NAS) methods**: The paper should discuss how their approach relates to recent NAS methods (e.g., Zoph & Le, 2017; Baker et al., 2017), which also aim to automatically select network architectures. While NAS methods typically search over architectures rather than dynamically adjust size during training, the comparison would help situate the work more precisely in the current literature.

3. **Expand computational cost analysis**: The paper mentions that AdaRad has better computational scaling than RMSprop, but a more detailed analysis of the computational cost (including wall-clock time) compared to standard black-box model selection methods would strengthen the practical contribution.

4. **Clarify relationship to sparse networks**: The paper mentions p=2 is preferred for hardware efficiency, but doesn't discuss how this relates to recent work on sparse networks (e.g., Guo et al., 2016). A brief comparison would help readers understand the trade-offs between dense and sparse architectures.

5. **Discuss limitations**: The paper should more explicitly acknowledge limitations, such as the current focus on fully-connected networks rather than convolutional networks, which are more commonly used in practice.

## Questions for Authors

1. The convex dataset results are concerning. Can you provide more insight into why nonparametric networks perform poorly on this dataset compared to others? Is this a fundamental limitation of the approach or related to specific dataset characteristics?

2. How does the computational cost of your approach compare to standard black-box model selection methods (e.g., random search, Bayesian optimization) for the same datasets? What is the actual time savings in practice?

3. The paper mentions that "current hardware is geared towards dense weight matrices" (p. 6). How does your approach compare to recent work on sparse networks (e.g., Wen et al., 2016) that target sparse weight matrices while maintaining hardware efficiency?

4. The paper focuses on fully-connected networks. How would the proposed method extend to convolutional networks, which are more commonly used in practice? Are there any specific challenges to address?

5. How would your approach interact with recent work on neural architecture search (NAS)? Could nonparametric networks be used as a component within NAS frameworks?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of neural network architecture design. The theoretical foundation is rigorous, the approach is well-motivated, and the experimental results support the claims. With minor improvements to address the convex dataset results and situating the work more precisely in the literature, this paper would be an excellent contribution to ICLR.
