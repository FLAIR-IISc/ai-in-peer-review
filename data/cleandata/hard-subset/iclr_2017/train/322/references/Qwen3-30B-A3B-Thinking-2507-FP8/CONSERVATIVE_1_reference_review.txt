

# Review of "NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS"

## 1. Summary of Contribution

The paper introduces a novel framework called "nonparametric neural networks" for automatically adapting and choosing the size of neural networks during a single training run. The authors claim that their approach dynamically shrinks and expands the network as needed to select a good network size, achieving higher accuracy than networks of the same size trained from scratch and sometimes outperforming established black-box model selection methods. The paper presents a theoretical justification (Theorem 1) and proposes new components: CapNorm (a modified batch normalization) and AdaRad (an adaptive gradient descent algorithm). The authors evaluate their approach on three datasets (MNIST, rectangles images, and convex) and claim promising results for the first two datasets.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper fails to provide sufficient empirical evidence to support its claims of superiority over established model selection methods, and the theoretical justification does not clearly translate to practical benefits.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The primary reason for rejection is the insufficient experimental validation. The paper makes bold claims that "nonparametric networks achieve a higher accuracy than networks of the same size that are trained from scratch and, in some cases, achieve a higher accuracy than is possible by black-box methods," but provides no meaningful comparison to established black-box methods like Bayesian optimization or random search. The authors only compare to parametric networks of the same size (which they claim are selected via exhaustive search), not to the actual black-box methods they claim to outperform.

The experimental validation is limited to only three datasets, with inconsistent performance (the method performs poorly on the convex dataset). The paper doesn't provide a thorough ablation study to isolate the contribution of each component (CapNorm, AdaRad, etc.), nor does it demonstrate that the approach scales efficiently in practice.

Furthermore, the paper's theoretical justification (Theorem 1) is highly complex and doesn't clearly connect to the practical benefits claimed. The proof in the appendix is lengthy and technical, but the connection between the theoretical result and the practical performance claims is not well-established.

The paper also fails to clearly differentiate its approach from prior work on growing networks during training (Wei et al., 2016; Chen et al., 2016), which makes the contribution seem incremental rather than novel.

## 4. Additional Feedback

1. **Experimental Validation**: The most critical issue is the lack of meaningful comparison to established model selection methods. To validate the claim that the approach "achieves a higher accuracy than is possible by black-box methods," the authors must directly compare to Bayesian optimization, random search, and other standard methods on the same datasets, using the same computational resources.

2. **Practical Efficiency**: The paper claims to avoid the need for "full network training runs" for each model size, but the approach described (adding units every epoch) seems to require training a network from scratch with gradually added units, which would still require significant computation. The authors should provide a clear analysis of the computational cost compared to established methods.

3. **Theoretical-Practical Connection**: The theoretical result (Theorem 1) is presented as the foundation for the approach, but the connection to practical performance is not well-established. The authors should provide more insight into how the theoretical properties translate to improved performance in practice.

4. **Baseline Comparisons**: The comparison to parametric networks of the same size is insufficient. The authors should compare to networks trained with the same computational budget (not just the same number of parameters) to demonstrate a genuine advantage.

5. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity**: The paper mentions that "nonparametric networks had a higher error for larger values of λ and a slightly lower error for smaller values of λ," indicating high sensitivity to hyperparameters. This should be addressed more thoroughly, as it's a significant practical concern.

## Key Questions for Authors

1. Can you provide a direct comparison of your approach to established black-box model selection methods (e.g., Bayesian optimization, random search) on the MNIST, rectangles images, and convex datasets, using the same computational resources?

2. How does your approach scale in terms of computational cost compared to established methods, especially considering the need to train networks from scratch with gradually added units?

3. Can you provide a more detailed explanation of how your theoretical result (Theorem 1) translates to practical performance improvements, particularly given the inconsistent results across datasets?

4. How does your approach differ from prior work on growing networks during training (e.g., Wei et al., 2016; Chen et al., 2016), and what specific advantages does it offer over those methods?
