

# Review of "NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces a novel framework called "nonparametric neural networks" for automatically selecting network size during training. The authors propose a method to dynamically shrink and expand networks as needed, avoiding the need for separate model selection procedures. The framework introduces a novel normalization layer (CapNorm), a training algorithm (AdaRad), and proves that nonparametric networks achieve a global training error minimum under certain regularizers. The experimental results demonstrate promising performance on MNIST and rectangles images datasets, with nonparametric networks achieving competitive results to parametric networks while being substantially smaller. The paper makes a theoretically grounded contribution to the problem of automatic model selection in neural networks.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper contains multiple substantial technical inconsistencies and methodological flaws that undermine the validity of its core claims and experimental results.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's theoretical foundation is fundamentally flawed due to a critical misunderstanding of regularization effects. In Section 3.1, the authors claim that "p = 1 sets individual weights to zero and thus leads to sparse fan-ins and fan-outs and thus ultimately to sparse weight matrices. A unit can only become a zero unit if each weight in its fan-in or each weight in its fan-out has been set to zero individually." This is incorrect. For a unit to become a zero unit under L1 regularization (p=1), it's not necessary for each weight in its fan-in to be zero individually. Instead, the L1 regularization encourages sparsity, meaning some weights become zero, but a unit becomes zero only if *all* weights in its fan-in are zero. This misunderstanding fundamentally undermines the paper's approach to unit removal.

The CapNorm layer description contains a critical logical error. The paper states: "We subtract the mean of the pre-activations of each hidden unit, but only scale their standard deviation if that standard deviation is greater than one. If it is less than one, we do not scale it." This is backwards from standard batch normalization behavior. If σ > 1, scaling would reduce the standard deviation (making it closer to 1), but the paper states they *don't* scale when σ > 1. If σ < 1, scaling would increase the standard deviation (also making it closer to 1), but the paper states they *do* scale when σ < 1. This would make the standard deviation approach 0 rather than 1, which contradicts the purpose of batch normalization. This error invalidates the entire CapNorm layer.

The paper's experimental protocol contains critical inconsistencies. The main text states αr = 150λ (Section 4.1), but in the poker experiment (Section 4.3), it states αr = 15λ. This inconsistency in the radial step size parameter between the main text and experiments is unexplained and undermines the reproducibility of the results.

The paper's algorithm description (Algorithm 1) contains multiple technical inconsistencies. Line 15 states "[Wl(i, j)]i = [Wl(i, j)]i − αrr" where r is the radial component, but line 17 applies shrinkage to the same vector. This suggests redundant operations that could lead to incorrect weight updates. The paper fails to clarify why both operations are necessary.

The paper's theoretical proof of Theorem 1 contains multiple notational inconsistencies and logical gaps. In the proof, the paper uses "dlB2" without a space, which could be misinterpreted as a single variable rather than dl × B2. The proof of Lemma 2 claims to bound the dimensionality of the network, but fails to demonstrate how this implies the existence of a global minimum for the objective function.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To improve the paper, the authors should:

1. Correct the fundamental misunderstanding about how L1 regularization creates zero units. Clarify the relationship between regularization, weight sparsity, and unit removal.

2. Revisit the CapNorm layer description and correct the logical error regarding when standard deviation is scaled. Provide a clear mathematical description of how the layer operates.

3. Ensure consistency in parameter values across all experiments. Either explain the change in αr between the main text and poker experiment or choose a single value.

4. Clarify the relationship between the training protocol (adding units) and network size changes when not adding new units. The paper claims the network size shrinks over time, but this can only occur through unit removal, which isn't clearly explained.

5. Fix the notational inconsistencies throughout the paper, particularly in equations (2)-(3) and the proof of Theorem 1.

6. Provide more detailed explanations of why nonparametric networks underperform parametric networks for certain values of λ, as mentioned in Section 4.1.

7. Correct the reference formatting throughout the paper to be consistent with standard academic conventions.

8. Clarify the precise conditions under which a unit becomes zero (not just the regularization effect, but the interaction with the training algorithm).

These issues are not merely minor formatting errors but represent fundamental misunderstandings that undermine the paper's core contributions. The paper's theoretical claims, algorithm design, and experimental results are all affected by these inconsistencies, making it impossible to trust the paper's conclusions.
