

# Review of "NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces a fundamentally novel framework for neural network architecture selection called "nonparametric neural networks." The core idea is to dynamically adjust network size during training rather than using black-box optimization methods that require multiple full training runs with different architectures. The authors present a theoretical foundation (Theorem 1) proving that nonparametric networks achieve a global training error minimum at finite dimensionality under certain regularization conditions. They introduce several key innovations: a novel normalization layer (CapNorm), a training algorithm (AdaRad) that handles both radial and angular updates, and a mechanism for dynamically adding and removing units during training. The paper demonstrates that nonparametric networks can achieve higher accuracy than parametric networks of the same size on MNIST and rectangles images, while using significantly fewer parameters.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper introduces a bold, novel concept that fundamentally shifts how we think about neural network architecture selection. While the results aren't perfect across all datasets, the core idea represents a significant conceptual advance that could inspire new directions in neural architecture search.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

This paper excels in novelty and conceptual boldness, which are my highest priorities as a reviewer. The core idea—dynamically adjusting network size during a single training run rather than through multiple expensive black-box searches—represents a paradigm shift from current practice. Most existing work on network architecture search requires multiple full training runs (with different architectures), while this paper proposes a single training process that adapts the network size automatically.

The theoretical foundation (Theorem 1) is strong and well-motivated, providing mathematical justification for the approach. The authors clearly distinguish their work from related literature on pruning and network growth, particularly noting that they don't require a high-performing network as a starting point (which is often a limitation of prior approaches).

While the results on the convex dataset are disappointing, the authors acknowledge this limitation and provide a reasonable explanation. More importantly, the approach shows strong results on MNIST (outperforming parametric networks of the same size) and achieves results competitive with exhaustive random search on rectangles images. These results are significant given that the approach requires only a single training run rather than the many runs required by traditional methods.

The paper's theoretical contribution (Theorem 1) is particularly compelling. It's rare to see a paper that not only proposes a novel algorithm but also provides a rigorous mathematical foundation for why it should work. This elevates the paper beyond a simple engineering contribution to a principled new direction.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To further strengthen the paper, I recommend:

1. More detailed explanation of why the convex dataset is problematic for the approach. The authors note that "nonparametric networks performed badly on the convex dataset" but don't provide a thorough analysis of why this might be the case. Understanding this limitation could help refine the approach or identify its most suitable application domains.

2. A more detailed discussion of computational complexity. While the paper mentions that AdaRad has "more graceful" computational cost than RMSprop, a formal analysis of time and memory complexity would strengthen the practical appeal of the approach.

3. More thorough comparison with recent methods for dynamic network growth. The paper cites Wei et al. (2016) as inspiration, but a more comprehensive comparison with contemporary approaches to dynamic network architecture (e.g., progressive neural networks, dynamic network pruning) would better situate this work in the current landscape.

4. A more detailed analysis of the unit addition/removal process. The paper provides some insights in Figure 3, but a deeper analysis of when units are added/removed and how this affects learning dynamics would provide more insight into the mechanism.

## Questions for Authors

1. Can you provide more insight into why the convex dataset is particularly challenging for nonparametric networks, and whether this limitation might be overcome with modifications to the approach?

2. The paper mentions that "parametric networks of the same size perform substantially better" on the convex dataset. Could you elaborate on the specific differences in network behavior between parametric and nonparametric networks on this task?

3. In section 4.3, you note that "the time it took those networks to converge to a stable network size... was a much smaller fraction of the overall training time under poker compared to the smaller datasets." Could you quantify this ratio (e.g., 10% vs. 50%) to help readers understand the scalability of your approach?

This paper presents a genuinely novel and promising direction for neural network architecture search. While there are some limitations to address, the core idea represents a significant conceptual advance that should be published and discussed within the community.
