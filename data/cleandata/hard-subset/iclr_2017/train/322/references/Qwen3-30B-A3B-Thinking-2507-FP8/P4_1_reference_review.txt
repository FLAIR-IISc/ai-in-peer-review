

# Review of "NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper introduces a novel framework called "nonparametric neural networks" for automatically selecting network size during a single training run. The authors address a significant limitation of current model selection methods (grid search, random search, Bayesian optimization), which require multiple full training runs to determine optimal network size. Their approach dynamically shrinks and expands the network during training, eliminating the need for multiple training runs while achieving higher accuracy than parametric networks of the same size and competing with exhaustive random search on some datasets. The paper makes three key contributions: a theoretical foundation proving that nonparametric networks achieve a global minimum at finite dimensionality with appropriate regularizers, a novel normalization layer (CapNorm), and a new training algorithm (AdaRad) that handles the unique challenges of training nonparametric networks.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of automatic model selection with a theoretically sound approach that is well-motivated and empirically validated across multiple datasets.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Acceptance
The paper excels in theoretical rigor, with a complete proof of Theorem 1 demonstrating that nonparametric networks achieve a global minimum at finite dimensionality when using fan-in or fan-out regularizers with p=2. This theoretical foundation is critical for justifying the entire approach and distinguishes it from prior work.

The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed, covering multiple datasets (MNIST, rectangles images, convex, and poker) with appropriate baselines. The results are statistically sound, with multiple random seeds and careful comparison to parametric networks of the same size and exhaustive random search. The paper demonstrates that nonparametric networks can achieve better accuracy than parametric networks of the same size on MNIST and rectangles images, and achieve near-perfect accuracy on the large poker dataset with fewer parameters.

The paper also provides a detailed analysis of the nonparametric training process (Figure 3), showing how units are added, learned, and eliminated during training, which gives valuable insight into the dynamics of the approach. The theoretical and empirical results are well-aligned, with the training dynamics supporting the theoretical claims.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
The paper would benefit from:
- A more detailed explanation of why the convex dataset presents challenges for the approach, beyond simply stating that parametric networks outperform nonparametric ones. Understanding this limitation would strengthen the paper's theoretical and practical contributions.
- More discussion about the relationship between network size and performance on the convex dataset. The paper notes that the best nonparametric network has a smaller size than the best parametric network, but doesn't explain why this size difference leads to worse performance.
- A more detailed comparison of training time between nonparametric and parametric networks, especially for the large poker dataset where the paper mentions the relative impact of training time is reduced.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. For the convex dataset, why do nonparametric networks converge to smaller sizes that result in lower performance compared to parametric networks? Is this a fundamental limitation of the approach, or could it be mitigated with different hyperparameter choices?

2. The paper mentions AdaRad-M performs similarly to AdaRad but doesn't provide detailed performance metrics. Could you share the specific test error rates for AdaRad-M across the datasets?

3. In the poker dataset experiments, you mention multiplying the radial step size by 10 (αr = 15λ). Could you elaborate on the reasoning behind this specific scaling factor and whether it generalizes to other datasets?

4. The paper states that "nonparametric networks trained without batch normalization could not compete with parametric networks trained with it." Could you provide a brief explanation of why CapNorm (your modified batch normalization) is necessary and how it differs from standard batch normalization in this context?

5. In Figure 3, the nonparametric network has a higher training error than parametric networks but better validation/test performance. Could you elaborate on how this relates to the regularization properties of your approach and why this might be beneficial for generalization?
