

# Review for "NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
This paper introduces a novel framework for automatically selecting neural network size during a single training run. The authors propose "nonparametric neural networks" that dynamically grow and shrink network architecture by adding and removing units with zero fan-in or fan-out, while maintaining network output invariance. They prove theoretically that with appropriate regularization, the framework achieves a global minimum at finite dimensionality (Theorem 1), and introduce novel components including CapNorm (a modified batch normalization) and AdaRad (an adaptive radial-angular gradient descent algorithm). The experimental results demonstrate that nonparametric networks can achieve better performance than parametric networks of the same size on MNIST and rectangles images datasets, and compete with exhaustive search methods.

## 2. Decision
Accept

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's core contribution is exceptionally novel: it fundamentally rethinks model selection by embedding it within the training process rather than treating it as a separate black-box optimization problem. This is a bold conceptual leap that challenges conventional wisdom in neural network architecture design. The authors successfully move beyond incremental improvements in model selection to propose a framework where network size is dynamically optimized during training.

The theoretical foundation (Theorem 1) provides strong justification for the approach, demonstrating that with proper regularization, the framework avoids degeneration to infinite network sizes. While the proof is technically complex, the core idea is sound and addresses a genuine problem in neural network training.

The empirical results are promising, particularly on MNIST and rectangles images datasets where nonparametric networks substantially outperform parametric networks of the same size. The detailed analysis of the training process (Figure 3) provides valuable insights into how the framework dynamically adjusts network size. The authors acknowledge limitations (poor performance on convex dataset), but this doesn't undermine the core novelty of their approach.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty over methodological perfection, I find this paper's conceptual contribution sufficiently significant to warrant acceptance. The framework opens up an entirely new direction for neural network architecture design that could inspire future work across multiple domains.

## 4. Additional Feedback
While the paper is strong conceptually, a few improvements would enhance its impact:

1. The authors should provide more intuition about why the convex dataset is particularly challenging for their approach, and what modifications might address this limitation.

2. The paper mentions "unit initialization" as an area for future work but could briefly discuss how initialization choices might affect the learning dynamics in the current framework.

3. The theoretical proof (Section 7.1) is quite dense. A high-level explanation of the key insights would help readers grasp the core intuition behind the proof without getting lost in technical details.

4. The authors could more explicitly discuss the computational cost of their approach compared to standard training, especially for very large networks.

## Key Questions for Authors
1. How does the framework scale to extremely large networks (e.g., ResNet-50 or larger) where the computational overhead of dynamic unit addition/removal might become significant?

2. Could the framework be extended to automatically select other architectural hyperparameters (e.g., depth, filter size) beyond just hidden layer width?

3. How do the authors envision this framework fitting into the broader landscape of neural architecture search (NAS), particularly with respect to methods like AutoML or reinforcement learning-based approaches?

This paper presents a genuinely novel and bold approach to neural network architecture selection that challenges conventional wisdom in the field. While not perfect, its conceptual contribution is sufficiently significant to warrant acceptance at ICLR, especially given the reviewer's prioritization of novelty over methodological completeness.
