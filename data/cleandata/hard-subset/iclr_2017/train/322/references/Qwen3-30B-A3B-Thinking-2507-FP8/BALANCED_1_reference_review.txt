

# Review of "NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper introduces a novel framework called "nonparametric neural networks" that dynamically adjusts network size during a single training run, eliminating the need for multiple full training runs required by traditional model selection methods. The authors prove that their approach has a global minimum under certain regularization conditions (Theorem 1), and propose a training algorithm (AdaRad) with a novel normalization layer (CapNorm) to enable dynamic network growth and pruning. The method demonstrates promising results on MNIST and rectangles images datasets, achieving better accuracy than parametric networks of the same size, and competitive results with random search on two of three datasets.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The paper has strong theoretical foundations but suffers from significant empirical limitations, particularly the poor performance on the convex dataset, which undermines the claimed generality of the approach. The comparison to state-of-the-art network size selection methods is also inadequate.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's theoretical contribution (Theorem 1) is rigorous and well-motivated, providing a solid foundation for the approach. The proposed AdaRad algorithm and CapNorm layer are well-explained and address important challenges in dynamic network training.

However, the empirical evaluation has critical weaknesses. The method fails to outperform parametric networks on the convex dataset, and the paper does not adequately explain this failure or discuss whether the method could be adapted to handle such cases. This is particularly concerning as the convex dataset is a standard benchmark for neural network performance, and the paper claims their method is generally applicable.

Additionally, the paper's comparison to relevant prior work is insufficient. While they compare to parametric networks of the same size and to random search, they do not compare to more recent methods for automatic network size selection (e.g., recent work on neural architecture search or network pruning techniques). The paper also lacks a detailed computational cost analysis of their approach compared to standard training methods.

The theoretical analysis is strong, but the practical implications are not fully explored. For instance, the paper does not address how the method scales to larger networks or datasets beyond MNIST, which limits the practical significance of their claims.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
- **Expand empirical evaluation**: Include more datasets, particularly those where neural networks typically struggle, to better establish the generality of the approach. The convex dataset failure needs thorough analysis.
- **Strengthen comparison to prior work**: Compare directly to recent methods for automatic network size selection, not just parametric networks of the same size and random search.
- **Provide computational cost analysis**: Quantify the additional computational burden of AdaRad compared to standard optimizers, and discuss whether the benefits outweigh this cost.
- **Address the convex dataset failure**: Investigate why the method fails on this dataset and propose potential solutions or limitations to the approach.
- **Clarify theoretical-practical relationship**: Explain more concretely how the theoretical guarantees translate to practical performance improvements.

## 5. Clarifying Questions
1. The paper claims the method "achieves a higher accuracy than networks of the same size that are trained from scratch and, in some cases, achieve a higher accuracy than is possible by black-box methods." However, the results on the convex dataset contradict this claim. Can the authors explain why the method fails on this dataset and whether this is a fundamental limitation or a parameter tuning issue?

2. The paper compares to random search, but not to more recent automatic network size selection methods (e.g., recent work on neural architecture search or network pruning). Why was this comparison omitted, and how would the results change if compared to these methods?

3. The paper states that "nonparametric networks trained without batch normalization could not compete with parametric networks trained with it." Can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of their method compared to standard training, especially considering the additional operations in AdaRad?

4. The paper mentions "the best nonparametric network made no mistake on the test set of 100.000 examples" for the poker dataset. How does this compare to state-of-the-art results on this dataset, and what is the computational cost of achieving this result?
