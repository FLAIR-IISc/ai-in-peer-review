

# Review for "Nonparametric Neural Networks"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper introduces a novel framework called "nonparametric neural networks" for automatically selecting and adapting network size during a single training run. The authors address a significant limitation of current model selection methods (like grid search, random search, or Bayesian optimization), which require multiple full training runs and do not exploit opportunities to improve network size during training. Their key insight is that by using a fan-in regularizer with p=2, they can dynamically add and remove units during training while maintaining a globally optimal solution at finite dimensionality (proven in Theorem 1). The paper introduces two critical components: CapNorm (a modification to batch normalization to work with their regularizer) and AdaRad (an adaptive gradient descent algorithm that handles the unique challenges of nonparametric training). The experimental results demonstrate that nonparametric networks can achieve better accuracy with fewer parameters than parametric networks of the same size on MNIST and rectangles images datasets, and are competitive with exhaustive search methods.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of automatic neural network architecture selection, with strong theoretical foundations and compelling empirical results. The main strength is the elegant theoretical foundation that proves the method's soundness, combined with clear experimental validation on multiple datasets.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

**Theoretical Contribution (Strength):** The paper's theoretical contribution is its strongest aspect. Theorem 1 is a substantial contribution to the field, proving that with their specific regularizer, nonparametric networks achieve a global minimum at finite dimensionality. This is not just a heuristic but a rigorous mathematical result. The proof in the appendix is thorough and addresses the technical challenges of the problem. This theoretical foundation is rare in papers on network architecture search and provides strong justification for the method.

**Methodological Innovation (Strength):** The proposed AdaRad algorithm and CapNorm layer are well-motivated and address the specific challenges of nonparametric training. The key insight that AdaRad normalizes only the orthogonal component of the gradient (while leaving the radial component unnormalized) is clever and directly addresses the challenge of training new units without premature shrinkage. The paper clearly explains why existing optimization methods (like RMSprop) don't work with their framework.

**Empirical Validation (Strength):** The experiments are comprehensive and well-designed. The results on MNIST and rectangles images are particularly compelling, showing that nonparametric networks can achieve better accuracy with significantly fewer parameters than parametric networks of the same size. The ablation study (Figure 3) provides valuable insight into the training dynamics, showing how the method gradually adds and removes units to find an optimal size.

**Limitation (Weakness):** The poor performance on the convex dataset is a notable limitation. However, the authors provide a reasonable explanation: the method's tendency to converge to smaller network sizes hurts performance on this specific dataset. This is not a fundamental flaw in the method but rather a limitation that the authors acknowledge. The paper demonstrates success on two out of three datasets, which is a strong result for a new method.

**Comparison to Related Work (Moderate Weakness):** While the paper properly positions itself in the literature, it could better compare to recent methods that also grow networks during training (e.g., Wei et al. 2016, Chen et al. 2016). The paper mentions these works but doesn't explicitly contrast their approach with the proposed method.

## 4. Additional Feedback

**For Clarity and Impact:**
- The convex dataset failure is notable but not thoroughly analyzed. The authors should provide more insight into why this particular dataset is challenging for their method (e.g., is it related to the nature of the problem, dataset size, or noise level?).
- Consider adding a comparison to recent methods for network size selection (e.g., DSD by Klein et al., 2017) to better position the contribution.
- The paper mentions "nonparametric networks can achieve higher accuracy than networks of the same size that are trained from scratch" but doesn't provide a clear comparison to other methods that grow networks during training. This would strengthen the contribution claim.

**For Technical Rigor:**
- The computational cost analysis is somewhat vague. While the paper states that AdaRad "incurs additional computational cost" but "scales more gracefully than RMSprop," it would be helpful to include concrete timing comparisons between AdaRad, RMSprop, and standard SGD for the same network size.
- The paper could better address the sensitivity of the method to the unit addition schedule. The current approach adds one unit per epoch, but how would the method perform with a different schedule (e.g., adding more units initially)?

**Questions for Authors:**
1. The paper states that "nonparametric networks performed badly on the convex dataset" but doesn't explain why. Could you provide more insight into why this dataset is particularly challenging for your method, and whether the failure is related to the dataset's characteristics (e.g., linearity, noise level, or sample size)?
2. The paper claims "nonparametric networks can achieve higher accuracy than networks of the same size that are trained from scratch," but doesn't provide a clear comparison to other methods that also grow networks during training (e.g., Wei et al. 2016). How does your method compare to these approaches?
3. The best nonparametric network on the poker dataset "made no mistake on the test set of 100,000 examples." How does this compare to the best published results on this dataset (as mentioned in section 4.3), and what are the implications for real-world applications?

Overall, this is a strong paper with significant theoretical and practical contributions to the field of automatic neural network architecture selection. The method is novel, well-justified, and empirically validated on multiple datasets. The convex dataset limitation is acknowledged and explained, but the paper's main contribution remains compelling. With minor revisions to address the questions above, this would be an excellent paper for ICLR.
