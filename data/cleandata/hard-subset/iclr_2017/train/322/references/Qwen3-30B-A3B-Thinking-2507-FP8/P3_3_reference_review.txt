

# Peer Review: "Nonparametric Neural Networks"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces a novel framework called "nonparametric neural networks" that dynamically adjusts network size during training by adding and removing units based on a regularization approach. The authors formalize a nonparametric neural network where the dimensionality of weight matrices is not fixed but determined during training. They prove Theorem 1, showing that with fan-in or fan-out regularization (p=2), the framework achieves a global minimum at finite dimensionality. The paper introduces two key innovations: CapNorm (a modified batch normalization layer) and AdaRad (a novel training algorithm that handles radial and angular weight updates separately). The experimental results demonstrate that nonparametric networks can outperform parametric networks of the same size on MNIST and the poker dataset, while achieving competitive results with exhaustive random search.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **weak acceptance** with the following key reasons:
- The theoretical foundation (Theorem 1) is rigorous and well-proven, addressing a fundamental question about network size selection.
- The approach tackles a meaningful practical problem in neural network training (avoiding multiple expensive training runs for model selection).

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of neural network architecture design. The theoretical proof of Theorem 1 is particularly strong, providing a solid mathematical foundation for the framework. The authors correctly identify the two main drawbacks of existing model selection methods: computational expense (requiring multiple full training runs) and failure to exploit opportunities to improve accuracy during training.

The experimental results are generally promising. On MNIST, nonparametric networks substantially outperform parametric networks of the same size (144 vs. 694 units) while achieving comparable accuracy. On the poker dataset, the best nonparametric network achieved near-perfect performance (no test errors on 100k examples). The ablation studies in Section 4.2 provide valuable insights into the training dynamics of the proposed framework.

However, I have significant concerns about the paper's claims due to two critical issues:

First, the poor performance on the convex dataset (Section 4.1) is not adequately explained. The authors state that "nonparametric networks performed badly on the convex dataset" and that "parametric networks of the same size perform substantially better." This directly contradicts their main claim that nonparametric networks can achieve higher accuracy than parametric networks of the same size. The authors should provide a thorough analysis of why the approach fails on this dataset, as this is crucial for understanding the limitations of their method.

Second, the paper lacks comparison to standard model selection baselines like Bayesian optimization (Snoek et al., 2012), which is the most commonly used method for hyperparameter optimization in neural networks. Without this comparison, it's impossible to assess whether the proposed method actually improves upon existing approaches in terms of both accuracy and computational efficiency.

The theoretical assumptions (e.g., σ(0)=0, self-similarity) are quite restrictive, and the paper doesn't adequately discuss how widely applicable the framework is in practice. While the authors mention that ReLU satisfies these conditions, they don't address how the framework would work with other popular nonlinearities like Leaky ReLU or Swish.

## 4. Additional Feedback

1. **Address the convex dataset failure**: The authors should provide a detailed analysis of why the method fails on the convex dataset. Is this due to the specific nature of the dataset, or does it reveal a fundamental limitation of the approach?

2. **Add comparison to standard model selection methods**: The authors should compare their method to Bayesian optimization and other standard model selection techniques to better position their contribution.

3. **Expand computational cost analysis**: While the paper claims AdaRad has lower computational cost than RMSprop, it lacks concrete numbers showing how this scales with dataset size and network complexity. This is crucial for assessing practical utility.

4. **Clarify the relationship to existing growing network methods**: The paper references Wei et al. (2016) as inspiration but doesn't clearly differentiate their approach from other growing network methods. A more detailed comparison would strengthen the paper.

5. **Discuss practical initialization strategies**: The claim that "growing nets from scratch is a good test case for investigating the robustness of our framework" needs more justification. The authors should provide evidence that this is a robust approach rather than just stating it as a claim.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. Can you explain why the nonparametric approach performs poorly on the convex dataset while working well on MNIST and poker? What specific characteristics of the convex dataset cause this failure?

2. How does the computational cost of your method scale with dataset size and network complexity compared to standard model selection methods like Bayesian optimization? Could you provide concrete numbers?

3. How would your framework need to be modified to work with nonlinearities that don't satisfy σ(0)=0, such as sigmoid or tanh?

4. What is the relationship between your approach and the recent "neural architecture search" literature (e.g., Zoph & Le, 2017)? How does your method differ from those approaches?

The paper makes a theoretically sound contribution that addresses an important practical problem. With the concerns addressed, particularly regarding the convex dataset results and comparison to standard baselines, this would be a strong paper for ICLR.
