

# Review of "NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
This paper introduces a novel framework for automatic network size selection through "nonparametric neural networks," which dynamically adjust their architecture during training by adding and removing units. The authors propose a theoretically sound approach using fan-in/fan-out regularizers that guarantees convergence to a global minimum at finite dimensionality (Theorem 1). They introduce a new normalization layer (CapNorm) and training algorithm (AdaRad) to enable this dynamic architecture adjustment. The method aims to overcome two key drawbacks of current black-box model selection approaches: computational cost of training multiple networks and inability to improve network size during training. The authors demonstrate promising results on MNIST and rectangles images datasets, achieving comparable or better accuracy than parametric networks of the same size while using fewer parameters.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The paper presents an interesting theoretical framework with strong mathematical foundations, but the empirical results are inconsistent across datasets, and the practical advantages of the approach over standard methods are not sufficiently demonstrated.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper makes a theoretically compelling contribution with a rigorous proof of Theorem 1, establishing the existence of a global minimum for the nonparametric network training problem. The AdaRad algorithm and CapNorm layer are well-motivated and represent a novel technical contribution. However, the empirical evaluation has significant limitations:

1. The method performs poorly on the convex dataset (Section 4.1), showing substantially worse results than parametric networks of the same size. This undermines the paper's claim of a general framework for automatic network size selection.

2. The authors report that nonparametric networks "achieve a higher accuracy than networks of the same size that are trained from scratch and, in some cases, achieve a higher accuracy than is possible by black-box methods." However, the results on MNIST show that the nonparametric network is "close in performance to the best parametric network" but substantially smaller. This is not a strong claim of superiority - it's merely comparable performance with fewer parameters.

3. The paper doesn't adequately compare the computational cost of the nonparametric approach to standard methods. Training a nonparametric network requires adding units over time, which likely increases training time compared to training a parametric network of the optimal size (which is what black-box methods would find).

4. The hyperparameter sensitivity (particularly αφ and λ) is not thoroughly analyzed. The paper states they "chose a typical representative from among those 10 trained nets" for each dataset, which suggests the method requires careful tuning to achieve good results.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
To strengthen the paper, I recommend:

1. Conduct more thorough experiments across a wider range of datasets, including more challenging benchmarks beyond the three used in the paper. The poor performance on the convex dataset needs to be explained or addressed.

2. Provide a more comprehensive comparison of computational cost. How much longer does training a nonparametric network take compared to training a parametric network with the optimal size (found through black-box search)?

3. Analyze the sensitivity of the method to hyperparameters more thoroughly. A grid search over αφ and λ should be presented with detailed results showing how performance varies.

4. Discuss the limitations of the approach more honestly. The claim that nonparametric networks "achieve a higher accuracy than is possible by black-box methods" is not supported by the results on the convex dataset.

5. Consider including experiments with more complex architectures (e.g., convolutional networks) to demonstrate broader applicability.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. The paper states that nonparametric networks "achieve a higher accuracy than networks of the same size that are trained from scratch." However, the results in Figure 2 show that for MNIST, the nonparametric network achieves 99.1% accuracy compared to 99.2% for the best parametric network of the same size. How does this represent "higher accuracy"?

2. For the convex dataset, why do nonparametric networks underperform so significantly? Is this related to the specific characteristics of this dataset, or is it a fundamental limitation of the approach?

3. The paper claims that "by altering network size during training, the network ultimately chosen can achieve a higher accuracy than networks of the same size that are trained from scratch." However, the results in Figure 3B show that the nonparametric network had a higher training error (non-zero) compared to parametric networks. How does this higher training error not negatively impact generalization performance?

4. How does the computational cost of the nonparametric approach compare to standard black-box model selection methods that would find an optimal network size? The paper states that "each run can take days on many cores or multiple GPUs" for black-box methods, but doesn't quantify the time for the nonparametric approach.
