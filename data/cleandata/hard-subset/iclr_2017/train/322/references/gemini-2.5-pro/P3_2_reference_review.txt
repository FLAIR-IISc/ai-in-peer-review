### **Review of "NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS"**

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces a novel framework called "Nonparametric Neural Networks" (NP-NNs) to address the challenge of neural network architecture selection, specifically the width of hidden layers. Instead of relying on computationally expensive black-box optimization methods that require multiple full training runs, the proposed method dynamically adapts the network size within a single training pass. The core idea is to start with a small network and periodically add new "zero units" (units with zero fan-out) while a group sparsity regularizer (a fan-in L2 norm) simultaneously prunes useless units by shrinking their entire fan-in weight vector to zero.

This framework is supported by three key contributions:
1.  **A theoretical foundation:** The authors prove that for a class of regularizers (fan-in/fan-out) and common activation functions, the joint optimization over network dimensions and weights is well-posed and admits a global minimum at a finite network size (Theorem 1).
2.  **Novel training components:** To realize the framework, the authors introduce CapNorm, a variant of batch normalization that does not counteract the regularizer's shrinking effect, and AdaRad, a novel optimization algorithm. AdaRad decouples the gradient update into radial (length-changing) and angular (direction-changing) components for each unit's fan-in vector, applying adaptive normalization only to the angular component. This allows new units to learn a useful direction quickly without being prematurely pruned by the regularizer.
3.  **Empirical validation:** The method is tested on three benchmark datasets and a larger dataset from OpenML. On two of the three benchmarks, NP-NNs achieve performance competitive with or superior to an exhaustive random search over architectures, often yielding smaller models. A detailed analysis of the training dynamics provides strong evidence that the proposed mechanisms work as intended.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

This is a high-quality paper that presents a complete and compelling story. It tackles a significant and practical problem with a principled and elegant solution. The key reasons for my recommendation are:

1.  **Novel and Sound Framework:** The paper moves beyond standard pruning (which requires a good oversized network to start) and black-box search (which is prohibitively expensive). The proposed method of simultaneously growing and shrinking the network based on a theoretically-grounded group sparsity regularizer is a novel and powerful concept. The proof of Theorem 1 provides a crucial guarantee that elevates the work from a heuristic to a well-founded optimization framework.
2.  **Excellent Supporting Methodology and Analysis:** The authors identify the practical challenges of their framework (interaction with batch norm, learning for new units) and propose thoughtful solutions (CapNorm, AdaRad). The design of AdaRad is particularly clever, as its radial/angular decomposition is perfectly suited to the problem of learning a new unit's function while its "lifespan" is controlled by regularization. The empirical analysis in Section 4.2, which visualizes the birth, life, and death of individual units, provides exceptional insight and builds strong confidence in the method's mechanics.

**3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The paper's strength lies in its coherence. The central idea (dynamic size adjustment via regularization) is simple, but its successful implementation requires careful consideration of the entire training pipeline. The authors have done this masterfully. The fan-in regularizer necessitates the AdaRad optimizer, which in turn benefits from self-similar activations like ReLU. The interaction between regularization and normalization is handled by the custom CapNorm layer. Each component is well-motivated and serves a clear purpose in support of the main framework.

The empirical evaluation is strong and, importantly, honest. The authors compare against the right baselines: parametric networks of the same final size (to show the dynamic process helps) and a random search over architectures (the main competitor). The fact that the NP-NN outperforms the best model found by random search on the "rectangles images" dataset is a significant result. The inclusion of the "convex" dataset, where the method underperforms, adds to the scientific credibility of the work.

Finally, the theoretical contribution should not be understated. Formulating model selection as the non-standard optimization problem in Eq. (1) and then proving the existence of a finite-sized global minimum (Theorem 1) is a substantial achievement. It provides the assurance that the method is not just an ad-hoc procedure but is optimizing a well-defined objective with desirable properties.

**4. Additional Feedback and Suggestions for Improvement**

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical for my decision to accept.

*   **Analysis of the Convex Dataset Failure:** The paper acknowledges the poor performance on the convex dataset but does not speculate on the cause. This is a missed opportunity for deeper insight. Is the optimization landscape fundamentally different? Does this dataset genuinely require a very large network, and the regularization in the NP-NN framework is too aggressive or converges prematurely to a poor local minimum in the space of architectures? A brief discussion of potential reasons would be valuable.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The method automates the selection of layer widths but introduces its own set of hyperparameters, namely the regularization strength `λ`, the radial and angular step sizes (`αr`, `αφ`), and the unit addition rate `ν`. The paper proposes a reasonable heuristic for `αr` based on `λ`, but the sensitivity to `αφ` and `ν` is not explored. A short discussion or experiment on how the final architecture and performance change with these parameters would strengthen the paper's practical claims.
*   **Practical Overhead of AdaRad:** Table 1 provides a theoretical cost comparison. However, in practice, how does the wall-clock time of AdaRad compare to Adam or RMSprop for a network of a fixed, large size? The vector decompositions and rotations may introduce non-trivial overhead on modern hardware. A brief practical timing comparison would complement the theoretical one and add context to the overall computational savings claimed by avoiding multiple training runs.
*   **Initialization of New Units:** New units are initialized with a random fan-in of expected length 1. Have the authors experimented with other initialization strategies, such as initializing a new unit to reconstruct the residual error or to mimic an existing, successful unit (as in Net2Net)? This could potentially speed up the process of making new units useful.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on your hypothesis for why the Nonparametric Neural Network framework performed poorly on the convex dataset compared to the other two benchmarks? Does this suggest a limitation of the fan-in/fan-out regularization approach for certain types of data distributions or decision boundaries?
2.  The rate of unit addition (`ν`) is set to one per layer per epoch. How sensitive is the final model's performance and size to this parameter? For instance, would adding units more aggressively (`ν=10`) or more cautiously (`ν=0.1`) lead to substantially different outcomes?
3.  Regarding Theorem 1, the proof is extensive and relies on a number of assumptions on the activation and error functions. Are there any common deep learning components that would violate these assumptions? For example, would an attention mechanism or a residual connection complicate the theoretical guarantees?
