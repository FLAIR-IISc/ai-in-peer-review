### Summary

This paper introduces a framework termed "nonparametric neural networks" for automatically selecting the width of hidden layers during a single training run. The core idea is to start with a small network, periodically add new units, and use a group-sparsity-inducing regularizer (a p-norm on the fan-in or fan-out of each unit) to prune unnecessary units. To support this framework, the authors propose a novel normalization layer, Capped Batch Normalization (CapNorm), designed to be compatible with the regularizer, and a new optimization algorithm, Adaptive Radial-Angular Gradient Descent (AdaRad), designed to effectively train newly added units. The paper provides a theoretical justification for the approach, proving that under certain conditions, a global training error minimum is achieved at a finite network size. Empirical results on several datasets show that the method can find well-performing networks and sometimes outperforms both fixed-size networks and models found via random search.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The primary contribution is confounded by the simultaneous introduction of three new components (the growing/shrinking framework, the CapNorm layer, and the AdaRad optimizer) without an adequate ablation study. This makes it impossible to determine whether the reported performance gains stem from the dynamic architecture search itself or from the novel, and potentially superior, optimization and normalization techniques.
2.  The experimental methodology lacks rigor in key areas. The practice of manually selecting a single "typical representative" from ten runs is subjective and not scientifically reproducible. Furthermore, the unexplained yet significant failure of the method on the `convex` dataset raises serious concerns about its robustness and generality.

### Supporting Arguments

While the paper addresses the important and challenging problem of model selection with a novel and conceptually appealing approach, the execution is marred by numerous methodological and presentational flaws.

**1. Confounding Methodological Contributions:** The paper presents a system with multiple interacting novelties. The core idea is the dynamic sizing of the network. However, to make this work, the authors introduce CapNorm and AdaRad. The experiments then compare this entire system against baselines using standard components (e.g., Adam, Batch Norm). This is not a fair comparison. A crucial missing experiment is to evaluate a standard parametric network trained with AdaRad and CapNorm. If such a network outperforms one trained with Adam and Batch Norm, then a significant portion of the paper's reported gains might be attributable to the optimizer and normalization layer, not the "nonparametric" framework. Without this ablation, the central claims of the paper are not adequately supported.

**2. Lack of Experimental Rigor:**
*   **Subjective Result Reporting:** In Section 4.1, the authors train 10 networks for each setting but then "manually chose a single net as a typical representative." This is unacceptable for a rigorous empirical paper. Results should be reported as aggregates (e.g., mean/median with standard deviation or confidence intervals) over all runs to provide an unbiased estimate of the method's performance and stability. Hand-picking a single result opens the door to confirmation bias.
*   **Unexplained Failure Case:** The method performs "badly on the convex dataset," substantially underperforming parametric networks of the same size. The paper notes this failure but offers no analysis or hypothesis as to why. A robust method should be understood in its failure modes. Is it the optimizer? The regularization? The nature of the dataset? This omission undermines confidence in the framework's applicability.
*   **New Hyperparameters:** The method aims to automate model selection but introduces its own set of non-trivial hyperparameters, including the radial and angular step sizes (`αr`, `αφ`), the unit addition rate (`ν`), and the frequency (`νfreq`). The relationship `αr = 150/λ` is an ad-hoc heuristic. The claim of automating model selection is therefore weakened, as the problem has been shifted to tuning these new, less-understood hyperparameters.

**3. Minor but Numerous Presentational Flaws:** A perfectionist approach to research requires meticulous attention to detail, which is lacking throughout the manuscript.
*   **Misleading Terminology:** The term "nonparametric" is used in a non-standard way. In statistics, nonparametric models are typically those whose complexity (e.g., number of parameters) grows with the size of the dataset. The proposed framework's size is determined by a regularization hyperparameter `λ` for a fixed dataset. A more accurate term would be "dynamic network sizing" or "adaptive network structure learning."
*   **Typos and Formatting:** The paper contains distracting errors. For instance, the caption for Algorithm 1 reads "bot not fastest" instead of "but not fastest." The reference list is severely malformed, with artifacts like "? \Q2016\E," suggesting a lack of proofreading. Footnotes on page 4 are poorly placed.
*   **Lack of Clarity:** The description of AdaRad is dense. The notation `[]i` is non-standard. The claim in Section 4.2 that "the CapNorm layer filters the component of the gradient" is an imprecise oversimplification; CapNorm affects activations on the forward pass, which in turn influences the gradient, but it does not directly "filter" gradient components. The proof of Theorem 1 in the appendix is exceptionally long and complex, and its key assumptions (e.g., differentiability of activation functions) are discussed in a confusing manner, particularly concerning the common non-differentiable ReLU activation.

These issues, while some may be minor individually, collectively create an impression of rushed work and detract from the paper's credibility.

### Additional Feedback

The core concept of dynamically growing a network from scratch and using group sparsity to prune it is very promising. I believe this work could be of interest to the community if the aforementioned issues are addressed.
1.  **Conduct a Thorough Ablation Study:** Please disentangle the contributions of your three main ideas. Show results for: (a) Parametric Net + Adam + BatchNorm, (b) Parametric Net + AdaRad + CapNorm, and (c) Nonparametric Net + AdaRad + CapNorm. This would clarify the true benefit of the dynamic sizing.
2.  **Improve Experimental Protocol:** Report aggregate statistics over multiple runs instead of selecting representatives. This will make your results more robust and convincing.
3.  **Analyze Failure Modes:** Investigate and provide a hypothesis for the poor performance on the `convex` dataset. This will provide valuable insight into the limitations of your method.
4.  **Proofread and Refine:** The paper requires a thorough pass for clarity, consistency, and correctness. Please fix the notation, typos, and especially the reference list. A clearer, more intuitive explanation of AdaRad would also be beneficial.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  Could you justify the use of the term "nonparametric" in light of its established meaning in the statistics and machine learning literature?
2.  How sensitive is the final network architecture and performance to the unit addition schedule (i.e., `ν` and `νfreq`)? Have you experimented with adaptive schedules (e.g., adding units based on a training stagnation signal)?
3.  In AdaRad, the radial and angular updates are decoupled. What is the intuition behind not normalizing the radial component of the gradient, and have you experimented with a variant that does?
