### **1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces a novel framework termed "Nonparametric Neural Networks" to address the problem of automatically selecting the size (number of units per layer) of a neural network. Instead of relying on computationally expensive black-box optimization methods that require multiple full training runs, the proposed method dynamically adapts the network architecture within a single training procedure. The core idea is to start with a small network and periodically add new, randomly initialized "zero units" while simultaneously using a group sparsity regularizer to prune unnecessary units.

The paper makes three main contributions. First, it provides a theoretical justification for this framework. It proves that for networks using fan-in or fan-out p-norm regularization (for 1 ≤ p < ∞), a global minimum of the training objective exists at a finite network size, preventing uncontrolled growth. Second, it develops two novel technical components required to make the framework effective: (i) Capped Batch Normalization (CapNorm), a modification of batch normalization that does not interfere with the norm-based regularizer, and (ii) Adaptive Radial-Angular Gradient Descent (AdaRad), a new optimization algorithm that decomposes gradient updates into magnitude (radial) and direction (angular) components. This allows for adaptive normalization of the direction updates without nullifying the shrinkage effect of the regularizer. Third, the paper provides an empirical evaluation on several benchmark datasets, demonstrating that the proposed method can find well-performing network architectures that are competitive with, and in some cases superior to, those found by extensive random search, often using fewer parameters.

### **2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**

1.  The paper presents a novel, well-motivated, and practical framework for automatically determining network width during a single training run, tackling a significant and costly problem in deep learning.
2.  The proposed method is supported by both a rigorous theoretical analysis ensuring the existence of a finite optimal network size and strong empirical results that are thoroughly analyzed, including the introduction of a novel and well-designed optimization algorithm (AdaRad).

### **3. Supporting Arguments**

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clear. The problem is well-defined, and the motivation for moving beyond black-box optimization is compelling. The core concepts of adding and removing "zero units" are explained intuitively. The technical contributions, CapNorm and AdaRad, are introduced with clear justifications for why existing methods (standard Batch Normalization and optimizers like RMSprop) are insufficient. The experimental section is detailed, and the analysis of a single training run in Section 4.2 provides valuable insight into the dynamics of the method.

**Technical Soundness:** The work is technically robust.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** Theorem 1 provides a crucial guarantee that the optimization problem is well-posed and will not lead to infinite networks. The proof provided in the appendix, while extensive, appears rigorous and establishes bounds on network dimensionality for which "good" local minima can exist.
*   **Methodology:** The design of the AdaRad optimizer is a key technical strength. The radial-angular decomposition of the gradient is an intelligent solution to the problem of using adaptive learning rates in conjunction with group sparsity regularization. It allows new units sufficient time to learn useful features before being pruned. CapNorm is a simple but necessary modification to make normalization compatible with the framework.
*   **Empirical Rigor:** The experiments are well-designed, comparing the proposed nonparametric (NP) networks against parametric (P) networks of the same final size and against P networks found via random search. The inclusion of a failure case on the "convex" dataset, while demonstrating a limitation, speaks to the scientific integrity of the evaluation. The scalability experiment on the "poker" dataset further strengthens the claim of the method's applicability.

**Novelty:** The paper's contributions are highly novel. While network pruning and growth have been studied before, this work integrates them into a single, principled optimization framework guided by group sparsity. The formulation of architecture search as an optimization problem over a union of parameter spaces, solved by moving between spaces via f-equivalent transforms (adding/removing zero units), is a fresh perspective. Furthermore, the AdaRad optimizer is a novel contribution in its own right, with potential applicability beyond the specific framework presented.

### **4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The following points are intended as constructive feedback to further strengthen the paper and are not critical to the decision.

*   The reporting of results for the NP method relies on manually selecting a "typical representative" from 10 runs. This introduces a degree of subjectivity. A more objective approach would be to report the median (or mean) performance and size from the 10 runs. For comparison, for each of the 10 final NP architectures, a corresponding P network could be trained, allowing for a comparison of the performance distributions.
*   The poor performance on the "convex" dataset is an interesting result that could be explored further. A brief discussion of potential hypotheses for this failure (e.g., the nature of the dataset's feature space, a need for much larger networks than the method converges to, etc.) would add valuable context about the method's potential limitations.
*   The name "Nonparametric Neural Networks" could be potentially confusing, as in statistics this term often implies that the number of model parameters grows with the size of the dataset. Here, the number of parameters is selected based on the dataset, but does not necessarily scale with it. While the authors define their usage, an alternative name or a brief clarifying sentence acknowledging the standard definition might be helpful.

### **5. Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you provide more insight into the failure case on the "convex" dataset? Have you investigated why the proposed method struggles on this particular task? For instance, do parametric networks require a significantly larger size to perform well on this dataset than the sizes to which the nonparametric method converges?
2.  The AdaRad optimizer is a significant contribution. Have you evaluated its performance in a standard, fixed-architecture setting against optimizers like Adam or RMSprop? Understanding its behavior outside of the dynamic architecture framework would help to better characterize its properties and potential as a general-purpose optimizer.
3.  Regarding the comparison protocol, how sensitive are the paper's conclusions to the choice of the "typical representative" NP network? Would the conclusions remain the same if, for example, the median-performing network from the 10 runs was chosen systematically?
