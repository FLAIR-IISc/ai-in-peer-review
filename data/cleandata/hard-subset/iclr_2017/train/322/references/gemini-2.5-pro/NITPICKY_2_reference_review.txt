### **Summary**

This paper introduces a framework termed "nonparametric neural networks" for automatically selecting the width of a neural network's hidden layers during a single training run. The core idea is to combine a group-sparsity-inducing regularizer (a fan-in or fan-out L2 penalty on weights) with a mechanism for dynamically adding and removing units. The paper provides a theoretical guarantee (Theorem 1) that, under certain conditions, a global minimum of the training objective exists at a finite network size. To enable this dynamic training, the authors propose two novel components: "CapNorm," a capped version of batch normalization designed to be compatible with the regularizer, and "AdaRad," an adaptive learning rate algorithm that decouples updates to the direction (angular) and magnitude (radial) of a unit's fan-in weight vector. The method is evaluated on several benchmark datasets, where it sometimes achieves performance competitive with or superior to fixed-size networks found through search, although results are mixed.

### **Decision & Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Significant Presentation and Clarity Issues:** The paper is marred by a substantial number of formatting errors, notational inconsistencies, and missing information (most notably a referenced but absent Figure 1) that collectively undermine its scientific rigor and reproducibility.
2.  **Incomplete and Unconvincing Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental results are mixed, with a notable failure on one dataset that is presented without analysis or explanation. Furthermore, several key algorithmic descriptions and analytical claims are vague or potentially inaccurate, making it difficult to assess the method's true effectiveness and limitations.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The core concept of this work is promising, and the theoretical analysis is a valuable contribution. However, the execution and presentation of the paper are not yet at the level required for publication at ICLR.

**1. Presentation, Formatting, and Reproducibility:**

*   **Missing Figure:** The text in Section 4 explicitly refers to "the network architecture shown in Figure 1," but no such figure exists in the paper. This is a critical omission, leaving the reader to guess the exact architecture used.
*   **Reference Formatting:** The bibliography is severely malformed, containing raw BibTeX-like entries (e.g., `Chen et al\.,? \Q2016\E, Chen et al\.,`). This indicates a lack of care in the final preparation of the manuscript.
*   **Notational Inconsistencies:** The paper uses non-standard and inconsistent notation throughout. For example, `Rdl−1∗dl` is used for matrix dimensions instead of the standard `R^{d_{l-1} \times d_l}`. Subscripts and indexing are often written unclearly (e.g., `(W )l`). Minor formatting issues like using a backtick for the L2 norm ("`2 penalty") are frequent. While individually small, their cumulative effect detracts from the paper's professionalism and clarity.
*   **Vague Algorithm Description:** The description of the novel AdaRad optimizer in Algorithm 1 is incomplete. Key steps are described verbally (e.g., "rotate" in line 16, "shrink" in line 17) without providing the precise mathematical formulas. This makes the algorithm impossible to reproduce from the paper alone.

**2. Methodological and Experimental Weaknesses:**

*   **Unexplained Failure Case:** The method performs very poorly on the `convex` dataset compared to standard parametric networks (Figure 2). The paper simply states this result without offering any hypothesis or analysis as to why the method fails here. Does this point to a fundamental weakness, for instance, in dealing with certain data distributions or loss landscapes? This omission is a major weakness of the empirical study.
*   **Questionable Analysis:** The analysis in Section 4.2 contains a confusing claim: "the CapNorm layer filters the component of the gradient parallel to the fan-ins as long as the standard deviation of the pre-activations σ exceeds 1." CapNorm is a forward-pass operation on activations; it does not directly "filter" gradients in the backward pass. Its effect on the gradient is indirect, and this statement as written is misleading and requires significant clarification.
*   **Heuristic Hyperparameter Coupling:** The radial step size is set as `αr = 150λ`, directly tying a learning rate parameter to the regularization strength. The justification is based on a heuristic about the lifetime of a new unit. This tight coupling seems brittle and may not generalize well to other problems or settings.

### **Additional Feedback**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper for a future submission and are not the primary basis for my decision.

*   The term "nonparametric" is used in a non-standard way. In statistics, nonparametric models typically have a number of parameters that grow with the dataset size. Here, the final model size depends on the optimization trajectory and the hyperparameter λ, not directly on N. Terms like "Adaptive-Size" or "Dynamic-Structure" might be more accurate and less confusing.
*   The paper introduces both a new framework and a new optimizer (AdaRad). It would strengthen the paper to evaluate AdaRad as a general-purpose optimizer, separate from the nonparametric framework, to better understand its properties.
*   The proof of Theorem 1 in the appendix is exceptionally long and dense for a conference paper. A more effective approach would be to provide an intuitive proof sketch in the appendix and move the full, unabridged proof to supplementary material.
*   The strategy for adding units (one per layer per epoch) is fixed and non-adaptive. A more sophisticated strategy, such as adding units when performance plateaus, might lead to faster convergence and better results.

### **Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you please provide the explicit mathematical formulas for the "rotate" (Alg. 1, line 16) and "shrink" (Alg. 1, line 17) operations in AdaRad?
2.  Can you clarify the mechanism by which you claim CapNorm "filters" the parallel component of the gradient? Is this a direct operation, or an indirect effect of normalizing the pre-activations, and if the latter, could you elaborate on the dynamics?
3.  What is your hypothesis for why the proposed framework fails on the `convex` dataset while succeeding on others?
4.  In AdaRad, the adaptive scaling depends on `φmax` and `cmax`, which are running maximums of the squared angular gradient norm. What was the motivation for using a maximum rather than a more conventional exponential moving average as seen in RMSprop and Adam? This choice seems sensitive to outlier gradients early in training.
