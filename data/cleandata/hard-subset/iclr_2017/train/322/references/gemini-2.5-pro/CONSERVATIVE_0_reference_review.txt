### **Review of "NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS"**

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper proposes a framework called "Nonparametric Neural Networks" (NPN) to address the challenge of selecting neural network layer widths. Instead of using conventional black-box hyperparameter optimization techniques, the proposed method dynamically grows and shrinks the network's hidden layers within a single training run. The core mechanism relies on periodically adding new, randomly initialized "zero units" and subsequently pruning useless units via a group sparsity-inducing regularizer (L2 norm on the fan-in of each neuron). The authors provide a theoretical proof showing that, under this regularization scheme, an optimal network with finite size exists. To operationalize this framework, the paper introduces two novel components: a modified normalization layer named "CapNorm" to prevent interference with the regularizer, and a custom optimization algorithm, "AdaRad," designed to handle the distinct update requirements for the direction and magnitude of weight vectors under their proposed regularization. The experimental results on several benchmark datasets suggest that this method can find effective architectures and, in some cases, outperform fixed-size networks found through grid or random search.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Inconsistent Empirical Performance and Lack of Robustness:** The method's effectiveness is not consistently demonstrated across the presented benchmarks. Its significant underperformance on the `convex` dataset undermines the central claim of providing a reliable, automatic method for model selection. An automated procedure that fails on a standard benchmark is not yet ready for general adoption.
2.  **Confounding Contributions and Insufficient Ablation:** The paper introduces a new framework, a new normalization layer (CapNorm), and a new optimizer (AdaRad) simultaneously. The experiments do not adequately disentangle the effects of these components. It is unclear whether the observed benefits stem from the core dynamic-sizing idea or from the specifics of the novel optimizer and normalization scheme. This lack of rigorous ablation makes it difficult to assess the true value of the primary contribution and introduces too many unverified variables.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The core idea of dynamically adapting network size during training is appealing and theoretically grounded by Theorem 1. However, the practical realization and empirical validation presented in the paper have several weaknesses that prevent a confident recommendation for acceptance.

*   **Failure on the `convex` dataset:** The results in Figure 2 show that NPNs perform substantially worse than both same-sized parametric networks and models found via random search on the `convex` dataset. The paper notes that NPNs converge to "relatively small sizes," which in this case is detrimental. This is a critical failure for a method whose purpose is to *find a good size*. It suggests that the interplay between unit addition, regularization, and optimization can lead to premature convergence to a suboptimal architecture. This instability raises serious questions about the method's reliability in practical scenarios where a user would not know a priori whether the method is suitable for their specific problem.

*   **Necessity and Evaluation of AdaRad and CapNorm:** The paper argues that standard optimizers (like Adam or RMSprop) and normalization layers (like BatchNorm) are incompatible with the proposed fan-in regularization. To solve this, the authors introduce AdaRad and CapNorm. While the motivation is clear, this introduces significant complexity and confounds the evaluation.
    *   The experiments compare NPNs trained with AdaRad/CapNorm against parametric networks trained with Adam/RMSprop/BatchNorm. This is not an apples-to-apples comparison of the architectural search strategy. The performance differences could be attributed, in part, to the unique properties of the novel optimizer.
    *   A crucial missing piece of the evaluation is an ablation study. What happens if one attempts to use a standard optimizer like Adam with the NPN framework, perhaps with a carefully designed learning rate schedule for new units? Even if suboptimal, this would establish a baseline and demonstrate the performance gain specifically attributable to AdaRad. Without this, the claim that AdaRad is *necessary* and *superior* in this context is not sufficiently supported. The optimizer itself is a significant methodological contribution that requires more rigorous validation than is provided.

*   **Comparison to Baselines:** While the comparison to fixed-size networks and a random search is a good start, the evaluation against established hyperparameter optimization (HPO) methods is incomplete. The primary selling point of the NPN framework is its efficiency compared to running multiple full training sessions. A more compelling baseline would be to compare the final NPN model against the best model found by a state-of-the-art black-box optimizer (e.g., Bayesian optimization using Gaussian Processes or TPE) given the *same total computational budget* as the single NPN training run. The current random search baseline may not represent the strongest possible result from established HPO techniques.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

*   **Ablation Studies:** To strengthen the paper, it is essential to isolate the impact of each new component. I would strongly recommend including experiments that attempt to use the dynamic sizing mechanism with more standard components, for example:
    1.  NPN with Adam/RMSprop instead of AdaRad.
    2.  NPN with standard Batch Normalization or Layer Normalization instead of CapNorm.
    This would clarify the necessity of the proposed novel components and highlight the specific contribution of the dynamic architecture search.

*   **Analysis of Failure Cases:** A deeper analysis of why the method failed on the `convex` dataset would be highly valuable. Is it an issue with the regularization strength `λ`, the unit addition schedule, or a fundamental incompatibility between the dataset's properties and the NPN's inductive biases? Understanding the failure modes is critical for developing a more robust method.

*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The proposed method introduces its own set of new hyperparameters, such as the unit addition rate `ν` and the radial/angular step sizes `α_r` and `α_φ`. A sensitivity analysis with respect to these parameters would improve the reader's confidence in the method's practicality. For instance, how does the final architecture's size and performance change with different unit addition schedules?

*   **Computational Overhead:** While Table 1 provides a theoretical comparison of optimizer costs, a practical comparison of wall-clock training time would be more illustrative. For example, how much longer does a single epoch of NPN training with AdaRad take compared to a standard parametric network with RMSprop for a network of equivalent size? This would help quantify the overhead of the proposed on-the-fly computations.

**5. Questions for Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on your hypothesis for the significant underperformance on the `convex` dataset? Does this point to a fundamental limitation of the proposed regularization and growth strategy?
2.  Have you experimented with using a standard, well-established optimizer like Adam in place of AdaRad for your framework? If so, how did it perform? An ablation that replaces AdaRad would be critical to understanding its contribution.
3.  The random search for the parametric baselines is described as "exhaustive." Could you provide more details on the search space and the number of trials, to better contextualize how it compares to the single-run NPN approach in terms of total computational cost?
