### Summary of Contributions

The paper introduces a framework termed "nonparametric neural networks" to address the problem of automatically selecting the width of hidden layers during a single training run. The key idea is to formulate model selection as a joint optimization problem over both the network weights and the layer dimensions. This is made tractable by using a fan-in/fan-out $\ell_2$ regularizer which can shrink the weight vectors of entire neurons to zero, effectively removing them. The authors prove that this formulation guarantees the existence of a global minimum at a finite network size (Theorem 1). To train these dynamically-sized networks, the paper proposes three novel components: (1) "Self-similar" nonlinearities (like ReLU) which allow for a single, global regularization hyperparameter; (2) "CapNorm", a capped version of batch normalization that does not interfere with the regularizer; and (3) "AdaRad", a novel optimization algorithm that decomposes gradients into radial and angular components, applying normalization only to the angular part to allow new units sufficient time to learn. The framework is evaluated on three benchmark datasets and one larger dataset, showing promising results on some but not all of them.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Inconsistent Empirical Performance:** The method's performance is highly inconsistent across the tested datasets. While it shows promising results on MNIST and Rectangles-Images, its significant underperformance on the Convex dataset is a major concern that is acknowledged but not explained. This lack of robustness undermines the claim of a generally applicable framework for automatic model selection.
2.  **Numerous Methodological and Presentational Flaws:** The paper suffers from a substantial number of small-to-medium issues that collectively detract from its quality and rigor. These include unclear descriptions of core algorithmic components (e.g., the rotation in AdaRad), a subjective experimental evaluation protocol, and severe formatting errors, most notably a completely corrupted bibliography.

### Supporting Arguments for Decision

**1. Empirical Weaknesses and Lack of Analysis:**
The experimental results are mixed. On the Convex dataset, the paper states that "nonparametric networks performed badly," significantly underperforming even parametric networks of the same size. This is a critical failure for a method designed to find a good network size automatically. The paper offers no analysis or hypothesis for this failure. Is it a limitation of the AdaRad optimizer? Does the fan-in regularizer introduce an unfavorable inductive bias for this task? Without this analysis, the reader is left to question the general applicability of the proposed framework. The success on the Poker dataset is encouraging, but it does not offset the unexplained failure on a standard benchmark.

**2. Methodological Clarity and Complexity:**
The proposed AdaRad optimizer is a central contribution, but its description is imprecise. Line 16 of Algorithm 1 instructs to "rotate" a high-dimensional weight vector. This is a non-trivial operation that is not uniquely defined by an angle and a direction vector; it requires a plane of rotation. This lack of mathematical formalism makes the algorithm difficult to understand and reproduce. Furthermore, while the goal is to automate model selection, the proposed training scheme introduces a host of new, sensitive hyperparameters: `αr`, `αφ`, `β`, `ν`, `νfreq`, plus the unit addition schedule. This seems to trade one form of hyperparameter tuning (network size) for another, potentially more complex one.

**3. Experimental Rigor:**
The protocol for comparing models in Section 4.1 is questionable. The authors report results for a "typical representative" chosen manually from 10 runs to approximate the median size and test error. This is a subjective and not easily reproducible process. Standard practice would be to report the median performance and confidence intervals (e.g., interquartile range) across all runs for a given hyperparameter setting. The current approach makes it difficult to assess the true stability and average performance of the method.

**4. Severe Presentation and Formatting Issues:**
The paper is marred by a lack of attention to detail, which is concerning for a submission to a top-tier conference.
*   **Bibliography:** The reference list is completely unusable. Entries are malformed (e.g., "Alvarez and Salzmann.,? \Q2016\E, Alvarez and Salzmann.,"), making it impossible to identify the cited works. This is a major flaw.
*   **Notation and Typos:** There are numerous inconsistencies. The penalty is referred to as "`2 penalty" instead of the standard $\ell_2$. Matrix dimensions are written as `Rdl−1∗dl` instead of `R^{d_{l-1} \times d_l}`. There is a typo ("bot not fastest") in the caption of Algorithm 1. The footnotes in Section 4.3 are not formatted correctly.
*   **Theorem Statement:** Theorem 1 is the theoretical cornerstone of the paper, yet its formal statement, including the required conditions on activation and loss functions, is relegated entirely to the appendix. The main paper should contain a formal statement for clarity and completeness.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

*   I strongly recommend a thorough investigation into the failure on the Convex dataset. Understanding the method's limitations is as important as showcasing its strengths.
*   The description of AdaRad must be formalized. Please provide the exact mathematical equations for the update step, particularly the rotation in Line 16.
*   The experimental reporting should be revised to follow standard practices. Please report median performance over all random seeds, along with a measure of variance, instead of using a manually selected "representative."
*   The paper requires meticulous proofreading to fix the numerous formatting, notational, and citation issues before it can be considered for publication. The bibliography, in particular, must be completely regenerated.
*   A brief proof sketch for Theorem 1 in the main paper would be beneficial, as the full proof in the appendix is exceptionally long and dense. This would help readers appreciate the theoretical contribution without needing to navigate the entire appendix.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  Could you please provide the precise mathematical formula for the rotation step in AdaRad (Algorithm 1, line 16)?
2.  Do you have a hypothesis for the stark underperformance of your method on the Convex dataset? Does this suggest that the inductive bias of the fan-in regularizer is not universally beneficial?
3.  The choice of adding one unit per layer per epoch seems arbitrary. How sensitive is the final performance and discovered architecture to this specific growth schedule?
4.  Why was a single "typical representative" chosen for the plots in Figure 2, rather than plotting the median test error (with error bars showing the interquartile range) across all 10 random seeds? The latter would provide a more objective and robust evaluation.
