### Review of "NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS"

**Summary of Claims:**
This paper proposes a framework called "nonparametric neural networks" to automatically determine the width of hidden layers during a single training run. The goal is to circumvent the computationally expensive process of black-box hyperparameter optimization for network architecture. The core idea is to dynamically add and remove units from the network, guided by a novel "fan-in" or "fan-out" group-L2 regularizer that encourages entire units (i.e., their corresponding weight vectors) to become zero. The authors provide a theoretical justification, proving that their regularized objective function achieves a global minimum at a finite network size. To facilitate this dynamic training, they also introduce two new components: a modified normalization layer called CapNorm, designed to be compatible with their regularizer, and a new optimization algorithm, AdaRad, which separates gradient updates into radial and angular components to better handle newly added units. The experimental results on three benchmark datasets show that the proposed method can yield networks that are competitive with, and in some cases superior to, architectures found via random search, though the performance is not consistent across all tasks.

**Decision: Reject**

**Key Reasons for Decision:**
1.  The empirical evaluation is not sufficiently rigorous to support the paper's claims. The method shows mixed results, underperforming significantly on one of the three benchmark datasets, which questions its general applicability as an automatic model selection tool.
2.  The paper introduces a suite of new, complex, and unconventional components (a novel regularizer, a custom normalization layer, and a new optimizer) without sufficient ablation studies to justify the necessity of each part. This makes it difficult to attribute the observed performance to the core idea of dynamic network sizing versus the specifics of the new training machinery.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The central premise of automating architecture selection in a single run is compelling. However, the proposed solution introduces significant methodological complexity, and the evidence provided is not strong enough to warrant its adoption over established practices.

My primary concern lies with the empirical results. While the performance on MNIST and rectangles-images is promising, the poor performance on the convex dataset is a major weakness. An automatic model selection framework should be robust; its failure on a standard benchmark without a clear explanation undermines confidence in the method. The authors note the underperformance but do not investigate the cause, leaving the reader to wonder about the conditions under which this method is expected to succeed or fail. This inconsistency suggests the framework may not be as general-purpose as claimed.

Furthermore, the paper stacks several novelties on top of one another: the fan-in L2 regularizer, CapNorm, and the AdaRad optimizer. This approach is methodologically concerning without a thorough ablation analysis. It is unclear how much each component contributes to the final result. For instance:
*   How would a standard network trained with Adam and the proposed fan-in regularizer (without dynamic sizing) perform? This would establish the regularizer's effectiveness for inducing structured sparsity.
*   What is the performance if one uses Adam instead of AdaRad in the full nonparametric framework? The justification for AdaRad rests on the need to normalize gradients for new units without interfering with the regularizer's shrinkage effect. While plausible, this claim needs to be empirically verified. The introduction of a new optimizer is a significant step that requires very strong evidence of its necessity and superiority over well-established methods like Adam or RMSprop.

Without these comparisons, it is possible that the observed benefits stem from one of the auxiliary components rather than the dynamic sizing itself. The paper replaces the familiar problem of tuning network width with the less familiar problem of tuning AdaRad's hyperparameters (`αr`, `αφ`), the regularization strength `λ`, and the unit addition schedule (`ν`). The paper provides a heuristic for setting `αr` relative to `λ`, but this coupling feels ad-hoc and it is not clear that this new hyperparameter search space is any simpler to navigate than the original one.

Finally, the comparison to "black-box methods" is not entirely fair. The paper compares its final model against models found by random search. However, a key claim is efficiency. A more direct comparison would involve measuring the total computation time (e.g., in GPU hours) for the nonparametric run against the total time for a full random search or a more sophisticated method like Bayesian optimization or Hyperband to achieve a similar validation error. It is plausible that the single, long training run of the proposed method is more expensive than several shorter runs of a parallel random search.

**Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors:**

The paper is well-written, and the core idea of using functional equivalence to grow and shrink a network is elegant. The theoretical proof for the existence of a finite-sized minimum is a solid contribution that adds rigor to the framework. The detailed analysis of the training process in Section 4.2 is also insightful. However, to strengthen the paper, I would suggest the following:

*   **Ablation Studies:** Provide experiments that isolate the contribution of each new component (fan-in regularizer, CapNorm, AdaRad). This is critical for justifying the overall complexity of the proposed system.
*   **Analysis of Failure Case:** Investigate and explain the poor performance on the `convex` dataset. Understanding the method's limitations is as important as understanding its strengths.
*   **Computational Cost Comparison:** Frame the efficiency gains in terms of wall-clock time or total FLOPs against a standard hyperparameter optimization process.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A study on the sensitivity to the new hyperparameters (`αφ`, `αr`, `ν`) would be valuable for assessing the practical usability of the method. How much tuning is required to make the "automatic" procedure work well?
*   **Broader Architectures:** The experiments are limited to simple, fully-connected networks. Demonstrating the approach on more contemporary architectures, such as convolutional layers, would significantly broaden the impact of this work.

I would like to ask the authors the following questions to better understand their work:
1.  Can you provide any intuition or analysis for why the method failed on the `convex` dataset? Does this relate to the dataset's properties or the specific dynamics of the fan-in regularization and AdaRad optimizer?
2.  In your random search baseline, what was the total computational budget? How does it compare to the budget for a single nonparametric training run?
3.  Have you experimented with using a standard optimizer like Adam instead of AdaRad? If so, how did it perform, and what specific failures motivated the development of AdaRad?
