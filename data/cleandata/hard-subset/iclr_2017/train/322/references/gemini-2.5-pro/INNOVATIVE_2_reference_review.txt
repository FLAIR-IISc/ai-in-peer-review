**Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces a novel and ambitious framework termed "Nonparametric Neural Networks" to address the challenge of selecting network layer widths. Instead of treating network architecture as a hyperparameter to be tuned via expensive, black-box outer-loop optimization, the authors propose a method to dynamically grow and shrink the network during a single training run. The core mechanism relies on a group-sparsity inducing regularizer (L2 norm on the fan-in of each unit) to identify and remove redundant units, coupled with a simple schedule for adding new, randomly initialized units. The framework is supported by two novel technical contributions: CapNorm, a capped version of batch normalization that does not interfere with the regularizer's shrinking effect, and AdaRad, a custom gradient descent algorithm that decouples the radial (magnitude-changing) and angular (direction-changing) components of the weight updates. Crucially, the authors provide a theoretical proof (Theorem 1) that their formulation guarantees the existence of a global training error minimum at a finite network size, preventing uncontrolled growth. The empirical evaluation shows that on several datasets, this approach can find networks that are competitive with, or even superior to, architectures found by extensive search, while being significantly more efficient.

**Recommendation: Accept**

This is a bold and highly innovative paper that I am excited to recommend for acceptance. My decision is based on two key factors:

1.  **Significant Conceptual Novelty:** The paper's main contribution is a paradigm shift. It reframes the problem of architecture selection from an external search problem into an intrinsic part of the network's optimization dynamics. This is a fundamentally new and powerful direction for research that moves beyond incremental improvements to existing neural architecture search (NAS) or pruning methods.
2.  **Principled and Theoretically Grounded Approach:** The proposed framework is not an ad-hoc collection of heuristics. The use of group-sparse regularization is a well-understood principle for inducing structural sparsity. The accompanying theoretical proof that an optimal finite-sized network exists under this framework provides essential justification and confidence in the method's stability.

**Supporting Arguments**

The core idea of allowing a network to find its own size during training is compelling and directly addresses a major pain point in deep learning practice. I am particularly impressed by the authors' thoughtful approach to the technical challenges that arise. Standard components like batch normalization and adaptive optimizers (e.g., RMSprop, Adam) would indeed conflict with the proposed regularization scheme. The development of CapNorm and AdaRad demonstrates a deep understanding of the problem and represents a solid methodological contribution in its own right. The decomposition of gradients into radial and angular components in AdaRad is particularly elegant, providing a natural way to allow newly added units to learn a useful function (angular updates) without being immediately penalized and removed by the regularizer (radial updates).

The empirical results, while not universally perfect, strongly support the paper's claims. The success on MNIST and the rectangles images dataset is very encouraging, showing that the method can find compact, high-performing models that outperform parametric networks of the same size and are competitive with a much more expensive random search. The scalability demonstration on the large "poker" dataset further strengthens the case for the method's practical utility.

I see the poor performance on the "convex" dataset not as a critical failure, but as a valuable and honestly reported result. It is rare for a completely new method to work perfectly across all problem types. This finding provides a clear boundary on the method's current capabilities and points toward interesting avenues for future work. For a paper introducing such a novel concept, demonstrating strong performance on a majority of testbeds is more than sufficient. The detailed analysis in Section 4.2, which visualizes the lifespan and dynamics of individual units, is excellent and provides compelling insight into how and why the method works.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While these points do not detract from my recommendation, they may help improve the final version of the paper:

*   The unit addition strategy (adding a fixed number of units each epoch) is simple and effective but seems to be a major hyperparameter. It would be interesting to see a discussion on more adaptive strategies, for example, adding units only when the training loss plateaus, or linking the number of added units to the rate of removal.
*   The paper successfully replaces the hyperparameter of "layer width" with new hyperparameters for the training dynamics (e.g., `ν`, `αr`, `αφ`). A brief discussion on the sensitivity of the final model to these new parameters would be helpful for practitioners looking to apply this method.
*   The analysis of fan-in norms in Figure 3 (E/F) is fascinating. The observation that the second hidden layer in the MNIST experiment does not reach the `σ=1` CapNorm threshold, and instead compensates with larger weights in the final layer, is a great insight into the network's self-organizing properties under this framework. This point could be emphasized even more as a strength of using self-similar activations.

**Questions for the Authors**

I would be interested to hear the authors' thoughts on the following:

1.  Do you have any hypotheses for the failure on the `convex` dataset? Does its loss landscape have specific properties (e.g., sharp minima, poor conditioning) that interact badly with the AdaRad optimizer or the growth/shrinkage dynamic?
2.  The paper focuses on finding the width of the layers. Could the core principle of adding/removing units based on a group-sparsity regularizer be extended to dynamically determine network depth, for instance by adding or collapsing entire layers?
