### Review of "NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS"

#### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a novel and principled framework, termed "nonparametric neural networks," for automatically selecting the width of hidden layers in a neural network during a single training run. The core idea is to treat the network architecture not as a fixed hyperparameter but as a variable to be optimized alongside the weights. The authors formulate this as a joint optimization problem and prove a key theoretical result (Theorem 1), which guarantees that under a fan-in or fan-out group-sparse regularizer, a global minimum of the training objective exists at a finite network size.

To operationalize this framework, the paper introduces a dynamic training process where units are periodically added and subsequently removed if the regularizer shrinks their corresponding weights to zero. This process is enabled by two significant technical contributions:
1.  **CapNorm:** A modified batch normalization layer that prevents the regularizer's effect from being nullified, a critical issue when combining regularization and normalization.
2.  **AdaRad:** A novel optimization algorithm that decomposes gradient updates into radial (length-changing) and angular (direction-changing) components. By selectively normalizing only the angular component, AdaRad allows newly added units to learn useful representations without being immediately pruned by the regularizer.

The authors provide a thorough empirical evaluation on three benchmark datasets and a large-scale classification task. The results show that their method can discover compact and high-performing architectures, in some cases outperforming statically-sized networks found through extensive search, demonstrating the potential of dynamically adapting the model to the data during training.

#### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Novel and Principled Framework:** The paper addresses the important and challenging problem of neural architecture selection with a novel, elegant, and theoretically-grounded approach. The combination of a formal optimization objective, a proof of its well-posedness (Theorem 1), and a cohesive set of practical components (AdaRad, CapNorm, dynamic resizing) represents a significant and well-executed research contribution.
2.  **Strong Technical Contributions and Empirical Validation:** The proposed AdaRad optimizer and CapNorm layer are clever solutions to concrete problems that arise in this dynamic setting. The empirical results, despite being mixed on one dataset, are compelling on the others, including a large-scale one. The detailed analysis of the training dynamics provides valuable insight and compellingly illustrates that the proposed mechanisms work as intended.

#### 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

The primary strength of this work lies in its principled and cohesive approach. Instead of relying on heuristics, the authors begin with a clear optimization objective (Eq. 1) and provide a rigorous proof that this objective is well-behaved, which lends significant credibility to the entire framework.

The technical components introduced are well-motivated and directly tackle the challenges inherent to the proposed method. The design of the AdaRad optimizer is particularly insightful. By separating the updates to a weight vector's magnitude and direction, it elegantly resolves the tension between learning (which requires gradient signal) and pruning (which shrinks magnitudes). This allows new, un-initialized units a "grace period" to become useful before being considered for removal. Similarly, CapNorm is a simple but effective solution to the known problem of batch normalization interfering with weight decay regularizers.

The experimental evaluation is thorough. The comparison against both parametric networks of the same final size and models found via random search provides a solid baseline. The success on MNIST and the rectangles-images dataset demonstrates that the dynamic training process can lead to better solutions than training a fixed architecture from scratch. The positive result on the large "poker" dataset suggests the method is scalable. The detailed analysis in Section 4.2 is exemplary, clearly visualizing the network's growth and pruning phases and the lifecycle of individual units, which greatly enhances the reader's understanding of the method's internal workings. The honest reporting of the negative result on the "convex" dataset, while a weakness in performance, is a strength in terms of scientific rigor.

While the method introduces new hyperparameters (`α_r`, `α_φ`, etc.), it trades the difficult, discrete search over layer sizes for a search over a few continuous parameters that appear more amenable to tuning. The overall contribution is a compelling new direction for automated model selection that elegantly sidesteps the immense computational cost of traditional black-box methods.

#### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

The following points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical for the acceptance decision.

*   **Analysis of the Convex Dataset:** The paper would be strengthened by a deeper analysis of why the method underperforms on the convex dataset. Is the optimal architecture for this problem particularly large, and the method converges prematurely to a smaller, suboptimal size? Or is the optimization landscape somehow different? Applying the same detailed analysis from Section 4.2 to a run on this dataset could provide valuable insights into the method's limitations.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A brief discussion or ablation study on the sensitivity to the new hyperparameters, particularly the unit addition rate (`ν`) and the radial step size (`α_r`), would be helpful. Understanding how these choices affect the final architecture and performance would make the method easier for others to adopt.
*   **Contextualization with Pruning Methods:** The introduction distinguishes the method from pruning by stating that pruning requires a good starting network. However, many modern techniques perform pruning during training from scratch ("sparse training"). It would be valuable to position this work more directly relative to those methods. The key conceptual difference appears to be the ability to *grow* the network. An experiment that starts with an oversized network and only prunes it using the same regularizer and optimizer could help isolate and quantify the specific benefit of the growth phase.
*   **Clarity in Algorithm:** For maximum clarity and reproducibility, the "rotate" operation in the AdaRad algorithm (line 16 of Alg. 1) could be specified with a precise mathematical formulation (e.g., via application of a rotation matrix constructed from the current vector and the orthogonal update vector).

#### 5. Questions for the Authors

I would appreciate it if the authors could address the following points in their rebuttal:

1.  What is your main hypothesis for the performance discrepancy on the convex dataset? Does your analysis suggest this is a fundamental limitation of the growth/shrink dynamics, or an issue with the specific hyperparameter settings used for this task?
2.  The computational cost of the random search baseline is a key point of comparison. Could you provide details on the search budget (i.e., the number of full training runs) used for the random search experiments in Section 4.1? This would allow for a more direct comparison of the total computational effort.
3.  The AdaRad optimizer is a novel contribution in its own right. Have you performed any experiments evaluating AdaRad as a general-purpose optimizer for standard, fixed-architecture networks to see how it compares to Adam or RMSprop in a conventional setting? This could help clarify how much of the performance gain comes from the optimizer itself versus the dynamic architecture.
