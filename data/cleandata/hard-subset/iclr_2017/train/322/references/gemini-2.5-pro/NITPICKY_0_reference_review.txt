### Review of "NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS"

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces a framework, termed "nonparametric neural networks," for dynamically adapting the width of a neural network's hidden layers during a single training run. The goal is to automate the selection of layer sizes, thus avoiding the computationally expensive process of black-box hyperparameter optimization. The core contributions are threefold: (1) A formal optimization problem is defined over both weights and layer dimensions, and a theoretical guarantee (Theorem 1) is provided, stating that a global minimum of the training objective exists at a finite network size under group-sparse (fan-in/fan-out) regularization. (2) A specialized training methodology is developed to solve this problem, including "CapNorm," a novel variant of batch normalization compatible with the group-sparse regularizer, and "AdaRad," a novel optimization algorithm that decouples the updates to the direction (angular) and magnitude (radial) of the weight vectors (fan-ins). (3) The framework is evaluated on several benchmark datasets, demonstrating cases where it can find well-performing, compact models that are competitive with or superior to those found by extensive search over parametric models.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**

1.  The proposed method, intended to simplify model selection, introduces a significant number of new, apparently sensitive hyperparameters and relies on a complex, multi-stage training protocol. This undermines the primary motivation of automating network design, effectively trading one difficult tuning problem for another.
2.  The paper suffers from a lack of methodological clarity in its core algorithm (AdaRad) and is marred by a substantial number of formatting errors, typos, and inconsistencies, which detract from the work's credibility and suggest a lack of careful preparation.

**3. Supporting Arguments for Decision**

**Methodological Obscurity and Complexity:** The paper's central claim is to provide a more automatic way to select network size. However, the proposed solution is far from automatic.
*   **Hyperparameter Proliferation:** The AdaRad algorithm and the unit addition/removal scheme introduce at least seven new hyperparameters: `αr`, `αφ`, `λ`, `β`, `ε`, `ν`, and `νfreq`. The paper provides a heuristic for setting `αr` relative to `λ`, but the sensitivity and tuning process for the others, particularly the crucial unit addition rate `ν` and angular step size `αφ`, are not adequately addressed. This complexity counters the goal of simplifying model selection.
*   **Ambiguous Algorithm:** The description of the AdaRad algorithm is critically imprecise. Line 16 of Algorithm 1 states to "rotate" a weight vector. This is not a standard operation in optimization literature. A vector addition of an orthogonal component does not constitute a pure rotation, as it changes the vector's norm. A true rotation that preserves the norm would require a more complex operation (e.g., applying a rotation matrix or using Rodrigues' rotation formula), which is not specified. This ambiguity in a core component of the proposed method is a major flaw.
*   **Complex Training Heuristics:** The experimental protocol described in Appendix 7.4 reveals a highly complex, multi-stage training process. It involves training until convergence, "rewinding" the training state, ceasing unit addition, continuing training, setting `λ` to zero, rewinding again, and then performing multiple learning rate decays. This is not the "single training run" advertised in the abstract but rather a complex meta-heuristic that requires careful manual supervision, fundamentally undermining the paper's pitch.

**Inconsistent Empirical Performance and Analysis:** The experimental results are mixed, and the analysis is incomplete. The method fails notably on the `convex` dataset, underperforming even a parametric network of the same suboptimal size it converged to. The paper provides no hypothesis or investigation into this failure mode. A robust method for automatic model selection should not fail so significantly on a standard benchmark without a clear explanation. This makes it difficult to trust the method's general applicability.

**Poor Presentation and Formatting:** The paper exhibits a lack of polish that is not up to the standards of ICLR.
*   There are numerous typos and grammatical errors (e.g., "bot not fastest" in Algorithm 1 caption, `l2` penalty written as `` `2 ``).
*   Mathematical notation is often non-standard and cumbersome (e.g., `Rdl−1∗dl` instead of `R^{d_{l-1} \times d_l}`).
*   The reference list is exceptionally poorly formatted, containing raw BibTeX-like fields (e.g., `\Q2016\E`), question marks, and inconsistent styling. This level of carelessness throughout the manuscript makes it difficult to properly evaluate the work and its placement within the literature.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While the core concept is interesting and the theoretical result is non-trivial, the execution and presentation require substantial revision.
*   **Clarify AdaRad:** Provide the exact, unambiguous mathematical formula for the update in line 16 of Algorithm 1. If it is a true rotation, explain how it is implemented. If it is not, the term "rotate" is misleading and should be replaced with the correct description.
*   **Simplify the Method:** The true value of this work would be demonstrated by a much simpler protocol. Can the method work without the multi-stage training, rewinding, and manual `λ` annealing? Ablation studies are needed to justify the complexity.
*   **Address Hyperparameters:** A sensitivity analysis for the new hyperparameters (`αφ`, `ν`, `β`, etc.) is essential. Provide guidance on how a practitioner should set these values.
*   **Analyze Failure Case:** Investigate and discuss the poor performance on the `convex` dataset. Is it a limitation of the regularizer, the optimizer, or the growth strategy? Understanding failure modes is as important as highlighting successes.
*   **Proofreading:** The entire manuscript needs to be meticulously proofread and edited for clarity, consistency, and correctness in formatting, grammar, and mathematical notation. The reference list must be completely rebuilt.

**5. Questions for Authors**

1.  Could you provide the precise mathematical operation for the "rotation" step in Algorithm 1, line 16? Specifically, if `w` is the fan-in vector and `v` is the normalized orthogonal update direction (`− φadj||φadj||2`), what is the exact formula for the updated vector `w'`?
2.  How critical is the complex, multi-stage training protocol (with rewinding, stopping unit addition, and annealing `λ`) to the method's performance? How do the results change if a simple, single-pass training schedule is used until validation error plateaus?
3.  What is your primary hypothesis for why the method fails to find a good model size and performs poorly on the `convex` dataset, while succeeding on MNIST and rectangles?
