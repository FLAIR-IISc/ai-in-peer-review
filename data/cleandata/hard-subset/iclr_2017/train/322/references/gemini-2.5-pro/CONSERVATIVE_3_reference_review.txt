### Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces a framework called "nonparametric neural networks" to address the problem of model selection, specifically the width of hidden layers. The proposed method dynamically grows and shrinks the network during a single training run, aiming to circumvent the high computational cost of conventional black-box hyperparameter optimization techniques. The core mechanism relies on a group-sparsity inducing regularizer (L2 norm on the fan-in of each neuron) to prune unnecessary units, while new units are periodically added. To facilitate this process, the authors propose two novel components: 1) "CapNorm," a modified version of batch normalization designed to be compatible with the regularizer, and 2) "AdaRad," a new optimization algorithm that decomposes gradient updates into radial and angular components to prevent premature pruning of newly added units. The paper provides a theoretical proof that, under this framework, the training objective achieves a global minimum at a finite network size. Experimental results on several benchmark datasets show that the method can find good architectures and is sometimes competitive with or superior to fixed-size networks found via grid or random search.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Inconsistent and Unconvincing Empirical Performance:** The primary claim is that this method provides an effective alternative to standard model selection. However, the empirical results are not consistently strong enough to justify the method's complexity. While it performs well on the "rectangles images" dataset, it performs poorly on the "convex" dataset and is only marginally better or on par with strong baselines on MNIST and "poker." The significant failure on one of the three main benchmark datasets undermines the claim of general applicability and robustness.

2.  **Over-reliance on Novel, Under-validated Components:** The proposed framework's success hinges on two new and complex components: the CapNorm layer and the AdaRad optimizer. These are introduced to solve problems specific to the nonparametric framework itself, but their general utility and behavior are not thoroughly investigated. Introducing a novel optimizer to make a novel architecture search method work adds too many confounding variables and makes the overall contribution less convincing. The work would be stronger if it could achieve its goals using modifications of established techniques.

### Supporting Arguments for Decision

The core idea of dynamically adjusting network size is appealing, but the execution and evaluation presented here have several weaknesses.

**Regarding Empirical Evidence:** The experimental results are mixed at best. On the MNIST dataset, the best nonparametric network is outperformed by the best network found via random search. On the "convex" dataset, the method fails outright, consistently underperforming parametric networks of the same size and converging to suboptimal small architectures. The authors acknowledge this failure but do not provide an analysis of why it occurs. This is a critical omission, as understanding the failure modes of a new method is essential for assessing its reliability. The success on "rectangles images" is promising, but one strong result out of three is insufficient to validate a general-purpose framework. The "poker" dataset experiment shows scalability but the performance differences are in a high-accuracy regime where they are less impactful, and it's unclear if the complex machinery is truly necessary for such a well-structured dataset.

**Regarding Methodological Soundness:** The introduction of both CapNorm and AdaRad adds significant complexity.
*   **AdaRad:** This new optimizer decomposes gradients into radial and angular components and applies different update rules to each. This is a non-trivial departure from standard optimizers like Adam or RMSprop. The paper does not provide evidence that AdaRad is a superior optimizer in a general context; it is presented as a necessity for this specific framework. This raises the question of whether the problem has been shifted from architecture search to a difficult new optimizer-tuning problem. The optimizer itself has new hyperparameters (αr, αφ, β) that need to be set, and the interaction between them seems delicate (e.g., αr is coupled to λ).
*   **CapNorm:** This modification to batch normalization is a necessary but ad-hoc fix. Standard batch normalization is a cornerstone of modern deep learning, and replacing it with an unproven variant is a significant change that requires more justification and analysis than provided.

**Regarding Theoretical Justification:** The paper provides Theorem 1, proving that the training objective has a global minimum at a finite network size. While mathematically sound, the practical relevance of this finding is limited. The goal of model selection is to find models that *generalize* well (i.e., have low validation/test error), not necessarily those that reach a global minimum on the training objective (which includes the regularizer). The failure on the "convex" dataset is a clear example where minimizing the proposed objective does not lead to a good generalizing solution.

In summary, the paper tackles an important problem with an interesting idea but fails to provide the rigorous and consistent evidence needed for adoption. The reliance on bespoke, complex machinery that is not independently validated makes the approach less appealing than established, if expensive, methods like Bayesian optimization or even random search coupled with modern early-stopping techniques.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

*   **Ablation Studies:** The paper would be significantly strengthened by ablation studies. How does the method perform with a more standard optimizer like Adam, perhaps with a modified update rule for the regularization term? How crucial is CapNorm versus, for example, using no normalization or layer normalization?
*   **Analysis of Failure Case:** A deep dive into why the method fails on the "convex" dataset is essential. Is it an issue with the loss landscape, the optimizer, the initialization, or the interaction with the regularizer? Understanding this could lead to crucial improvements and would increase confidence in the method.
*   **Simpler Baselines:** While comparison to random search is included, a comparison to a simple pruning-from-a-large-model baseline (e.g., magnitude pruning) would be valuable. The authors argue this requires a good starting model, but for these benchmark tasks, starting with an oversized model is standard practice and computationally feasible.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The unit addition schedule (one unit per layer per epoch) and the setting of αr = 150/λ seem arbitrary. An analysis of the method's sensitivity to these choices would be beneficial.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  Could you provide some intuition or analysis for the poor performance on the "convex" dataset? What properties of this task make your method converge to small, suboptimal networks?
2.  The AdaRad optimizer is presented as a key component. Have you attempted to make your framework work with more standard optimizers like Adam or RMSprop? For instance, one could apply the optimizer only to the loss gradient and then apply the shrinkage step for the regularizer separately. Did such simpler approaches fail, and if so, why?
3.  The framework replaces a search over discrete layer widths with a search over the continuous regularization hyperparameter λ. Given that λ still needs to be tuned via grid search in your experiments to find a model of the desired size/performance trade-off, what is the primary practical advantage of this framework over simply searching for the layer width directly?
