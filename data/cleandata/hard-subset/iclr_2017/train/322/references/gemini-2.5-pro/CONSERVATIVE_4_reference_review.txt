### Review of "NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS"

#### Summary of Contributions

This paper addresses the computationally expensive problem of model selection for neural networks, specifically the selection of layer widths. The authors propose a "nonparametric" framework where the network architecture is not fixed but is dynamically adjusted during a single training run. The core idea is to periodically add new, randomly initialized units to the network and simultaneously use a group-sparse regularizer (a fan-in or fan-out L2 norm) to prune entire units that are not useful. The paper provides a theoretical justification, proving that under this regularization scheme, a global minimum of the training objective exists at a finite network size. To make this framework practical, the authors introduce two novel components: "CapNorm," a modified batch normalization layer designed not to interfere with the regularizer's magnitude-shrinking effect, and "AdaRad," a custom optimization algorithm that decouples the gradient update into radial (length-changing) and angular (direction-changing) components. The authors claim this optimizer is necessary to allow new units to learn useful features before being pruned. Empirical results on several benchmark datasets suggest the method can find compact, well-performing models, sometimes outperforming fixed-size networks found via random search.

#### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Reject

1.  **Insufficient Empirical Justification for Novel Components:** The paper introduces a suite of new and unconventional methods—a dynamic training process, a new normalization layer (CapNorm), and a new optimizer (AdaRad). However, the necessity and superiority of these components over established techniques are not rigorously demonstrated. The experimental results are mixed, with a notable failure on one of the three benchmark datasets, which undermines the claim of a general and robust framework for model selection.
2.  **Lack of Ablation Studies and Sensitivity Analysis:** The simultaneous introduction of CapNorm and AdaRad makes it impossible to disentangle their individual contributions. The paper lacks crucial ablation studies that would isolate the effects of each component (e.g., using Adam with CapNorm, or AdaRad with standard Batch Normalization). Furthermore, the method replaces the hyperparameter of network width with a new set of non-trivial hyperparameters (`λ`, `αr`, `αφ`, `ν`), but provides little analysis of the model's sensitivity to these new parameters.

#### Supporting Arguments

The main weakness of this paper lies in the gap between its ambitious claims and the supporting evidence. The proposal to replace the entire standard training pipeline (fixed architecture, Adam/RMSprop optimizer, Batch Normalization) requires an exceptionally high burden of proof, which is not met here.

*   **Mixed Empirical Performance:** While the results on MNIST and rectangles-images are promising, the method performs poorly on the `convex` dataset compared to a standard parametric network of the same size. The paper offers no investigation into this failure. A new, general-purpose method for model selection should demonstrate robustness across a variety of tasks. This failure suggests that the intricate dynamics of unit addition, learning, and pruning are brittle and may not be broadly applicable without significant task-specific tuning.
*   **Unjustified Complexity of the Optimizer:** The paper argues that standard adaptive optimizers like RMSprop or Adam are incompatible with the proposed regularization scheme. This motivates the development of AdaRad, a complex algorithm based on radial-angular coordinate decomposition with its own set of hyperparameters (`αr`, `αφ`). This is a strong claim that requires more evidence. It is plausible that a standard optimizer like Adam, combined with a carefully scheduled group-lasso weight decay, could achieve similar results. Without this baseline, the introduction of an entirely new optimizer seems premature. The claim of better scaling in Table 1 is also secondary, as the overhead of modern optimizers is typically dwarfed by the cost of gradient computation.
*   **The Hyperparameter Problem is Shifted, Not Solved:** The paper is motivated by the challenge of hyperparameter optimization (i.e., choosing layer widths). However, the proposed framework introduces its own set of crucial hyperparameters: the regularization strength `λ`, the radial and angular step sizes `αr` and `αφ`, the unit addition rate `ν`, and the schedule for adding units. The paper uses a heuristic `αr = 150/λ` and a fixed addition schedule. This suggests that the problem of finding a good architecture has been replaced by the potentially more difficult problem of finding good hyperparameters for the search process itself. A thorough sensitivity analysis is needed to show that the method is not overly dependent on these choices.
*   **Insufficient Baselines and Missing Ablations:** The paper's central components, CapNorm and AdaRad, are presented as a package deal. It is unclear if both are truly necessary. What is the performance if one uses a standard optimizer with CapNorm? Or AdaRad with standard Batch Norm? Without such ablations, it is difficult to credit the specific contributions or understand their interplay. A more compelling evaluation would also include a direct comparison of the total computational budget (e.g., in GPU hours) between a single nonparametric run and the full random search baseline it aims to replace.

#### Additional Feedback for Improvement

*   The theoretical contribution in Theorem 1 is a definite strength of the paper, providing a solid mathematical foundation for the idea that group-sparse regularization can bound the required model size. This is a valuable insight.
*   The concept of decomposing updates into magnitude and direction is intriguing. This idea might be worth exploring in its own right, perhaps in a separate paper focused purely on optimization, with a more thorough comparison to existing adaptive methods.
*   The empirical evaluation would be significantly stronger if performed on more challenging and widely used datasets where architecture search is more critical, such as CIFAR-10/100.
*   The paper would benefit from a deeper analysis of the training dynamics. For instance, in the failed case of the `convex` dataset, what went wrong? Were units pruned too aggressively? Did new units fail to learn useful representations? Answering such questions would provide valuable insights into the method's limitations and potential avenues for improvement.

#### Questions for the Authors

1.  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the failure on the `convex` dataset? What properties of this dataset or task do you believe are challenging for your proposed method?
2.  To better understand the contribution of your novel optimizer, could you provide results for an ablation where AdaRad is replaced by a standard optimizer like Adam? A simple approach might be to decouple Adam's updates from a separate, scheduled group-lasso weight decay step.
3.  How was the heuristic relationship `αr = 150/λ` determined? Could you provide a sensitivity analysis showing how the final model size and performance vary with changes to `αr` and the unit addition rate `ν`? This would help clarify whether you have simply traded one difficult hyperparameter search problem for another.
