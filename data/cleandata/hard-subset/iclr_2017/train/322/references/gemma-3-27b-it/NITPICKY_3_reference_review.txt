This paper introduces nonparametric neural networks, a framework for dynamically adjusting network size during training, aiming to circumvent the drawbacks of black-box hyperparameter optimization. The core idea is to allow the network to grow and shrink by adding or removing units with zero fan-in/fan-out, guided by a regularizer, during a single training run. The authors also propose a novel normalization layer (CapNorm) and training algorithm (AdaRad) tailored for this setting.

**Reject.** While the concept is intriguing, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws, inconsistencies, and a lack of thorough experimental validation, ultimately undermining confidence in the presented approach. The sheer number of these issues suggests a lack of meticulousness in the development and presentation of the work.

The primary concerns stem from the following:

1. **Overly Detailed and Pedantic Presentation:** The writing style is excessively verbose and bogged down in minute details. Many sections, particularly the proofs in the appendix, feel unnecessarily complex and detract from the core ideas. This level of detail isn’t conducive to clear communication and makes it difficult to assess the actual contribution. For instance, the lengthy proof of Theorem 1, while attempting rigor, obfuscates the key intuition.

2. **Inconsistent Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments, while spanning multiple datasets, lack a consistent evaluation protocol. The choice of hyperparameters seems arbitrary, and the justification for adding units at specific frequencies is weak. The claims of outperforming parametric networks are not universally supported across all datasets; performance on the convex dataset is particularly concerning. The ablation studies (e.g., comparing AdaRad and AdaRad-M) are minimal and provide little insight.

3. **Lack of Justification for Design Choices:** Several key design choices lack clear justification. The rationale for choosing a fan-in regularizer over a fan-out regularizer feels somewhat arbitrary, despite the author's explanation. The initialization of newly added units, while described, isn't convincingly motivated. The `2 shrinkage operator is chosen, yet the practical advantages over `1 regularization in the context of dense matrices are not thoroughly discussed beyond mentioning hardware considerations.

4. **Normality Assumption Issue:** CapNorm introduces a limitation by only scaling the standard deviation if it's greater than one. This design choice fundamentally alters the behavior of batch normalization and may introduce unintended biases, particularly in layers with low activation variance. The potential consequences of this modification aren't adequately discussed.

5. **Algorithm 1's complexity**: Algorithm 1, AdaRad, is presented with an unnecessary level of detail. Many operations are explicitly described which could have been omitted for readability. Moreover, the step-by-step description makes the algorithm's computational complexity unclear.

6. **Missing Baselines**: While comparisons to parametric networks are made, the inclusion of more established and modern neural architecture search (NAS) methods would strengthen the evaluation.



**Additional Feedback (for potential revision):**

*   **Simplify the Presentation:** Rewrite the paper with a focus on clarity and conciseness. Eliminate unnecessary details and streamline the proofs.
*   **Strengthen Experimental Validation:** Conduct more extensive experiments with a wider range of hyperparameters, datasets, and baseline methods. Provide a clear and consistent evaluation protocol.
*   **Justify Design Choices:** Provide a more robust justification for all key design choices. Discuss the trade-offs involved and explain why the chosen approach is superior to alternatives.
*   **Analyze CapNorm:** Investigate the impact of CapNorm on network performance and compare it to standard batch normalization.
*   **Address Scalability Concerns:** While the paper touches upon scalability, a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of AdaRad is needed.
*   **Clarify Algorithm 1:** Summarize the algorithm using pseudocode or a higher-level description, focusing on the core logic and omitting unnecessary details.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Why was the fan-in regularizer specifically chosen over the fan-out regularizer? What benefits does it offer in this context?
2.  How sensitive is the performance of nonparametric networks to the choice of initial angular step size (αφ)? Did you perform a sensitivity analysis on this parameter?
3.  What is the rationale behind the specific choice of βarith and βquad values used in AdaRad-M?
4.  The CapNorm approach introduces a deviation from standard batch normalization. Could this potentially lead to instability or slower convergence in certain scenarios?
5.  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of AdaRad compared to standard optimization algorithms like Adam and RMSprop? Specifically, how does the computational cost scale with network size and dataset size?
6.  What strategies could be employed to further improve the performance of nonparametric networks on datasets like the convex dataset, where they currently underperform parametric networks?
