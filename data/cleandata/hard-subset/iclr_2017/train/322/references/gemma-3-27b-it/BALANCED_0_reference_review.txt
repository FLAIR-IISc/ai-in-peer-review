This paper introduces a novel framework called Nonparametric Neural Networks (NNs) for automatically selecting network size during training. The core idea is to dynamically shrink and expand the network, driven by a fan-in regularizer, allowing the network to adapt its capacity without requiring a separate model selection phase. The authors also present a novel normalization layer (CapNorm) and a training algorithm (AdaRad) tailored for nonparametric networks. The theoretical soundness of the approach is demonstrated with a theorem establishing a global training error minimum under certain conditions.

**Decision: Reject**

While the idea of dynamically adjusting network size during training is intriguing and potentially valuable, the paper falls short of convincingly demonstrating its practical benefits. The experimental results are mixed, with strong performance on some datasets (MNIST, rectangles) but poor performance on others (convex). The gains over well-tuned parametric networks are not consistently significant, and the analysis lacks sufficient depth to understand *why* the method succeeds in some cases and fails in others. The theoretical contribution, while interesting, feels somewhat isolated from the empirical findings, and its practical implications aren't fully explored.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Inconsistent Experimental Results:** The performance on the convex dataset is a major concern. The paper acknowledges this poor performance and offers limited explanation. This raises doubts about the generalizability of the proposed method. While the authors state nonparametric networks “achieve a higher accuracy than networks of the same size trained from scratch”, this claim is not consistently supported across all benchmarks. Furthermore, the comparison to black-box methods isn’t fully compelling; the authors report the best performing parametric network found *after* a careful search, creating a high bar for nonparametric networks to surpass.

2. **Limited Ablation Studies & Parameter Sensitivity:** The paper would benefit greatly from ablation studies to understand the contribution of each component (CapNorm, AdaRad, the fan-in regularizer). The sensitivity of the method to hyperparameters, especially the angular and radial step sizes, is not thoroughly investigated. How do different choices of these parameters affect the network’s ability to converge and generalize?

3. **Theoretical Connection to Empirical Results:** The theorem demonstrating a global minimum is a positive contribution, but its connection to the practical behavior of the algorithm isn't explicitly highlighted. How does the theorem inform the choice of regularization parameters or the design of AdaRad?  Is the theoretical guarantee actually achievable in practice, given the complexity of real-world datasets and optimization landscapes?

4. **Complexity & Scalability:** The algorithm introduces additional complexity compared to standard training procedures, as evidenced by the detailed description of AdaRad. While the authors mention computational costs, a more thorough analysis of the scaling behavior, especially for large datasets, is needed.



**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Dataset Selection:**  The choice of datasets feels somewhat arbitrary. Including a wider range of datasets, representing different domains and complexities, would strengthen the evaluation.
* **Comparison Baselines:** The comparison with black-box optimization methods could be improved.  Instead of simply stating the best results obtained via grid/random search, it would be more informative to track the optimization *process* of these methods alongside the nonparametric network’s training. This would provide a fairer comparison of the time and resources required to achieve a given level of performance.
* **Visualization:** Visualizing the network's growth and shrinkage during training (e.g., plotting the number of units in each layer over time) would offer valuable insights into the algorithm’s behavior.
* **Discussion of Limitations:** A more candid discussion of the limitations of the approach would be appreciated. For example, what types of datasets or architectures are likely to be less suitable for nonparametric networks?
* **Clarify the Role of λ:** A clearer explanation of how the regularization parameter λ affects the network's capacity and generalization performance would be helpful.  How does the choice of λ interact with the angular and radial step sizes?
* **Investigate Alternative Regularizers:** While the fan-in regularizer is a reasonable choice, exploring other regularization techniques (e.g., group lasso) could lead to improved performance or robustness.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*  The performance on the convex dataset is significantly worse than with parametric networks. What is your hypothesis for this discrepancy, and what modifications to the algorithm might improve performance on such datasets?
*  How sensitive is the performance of the nonparametric network to the initial choice of angular step size (αφ)?  Do you have any heuristics or guidelines for choosing a good initial value?
*  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of AdaRad compared to standard optimization algorithms like Adam or RMSprop, especially for large-scale problems?
* The paper mentions "self-similar nonlinearities". Why is this property important for the nonparametric framework, and are there any alternatives that could achieve similar benefits?
*  The theorem guarantees a global minimum under certain conditions.  Are there any practical strategies for verifying whether these conditions are met for a given dataset and network architecture?
