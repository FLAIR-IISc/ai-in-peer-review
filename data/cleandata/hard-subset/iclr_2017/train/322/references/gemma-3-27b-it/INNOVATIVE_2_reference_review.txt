This paper introduces nonparametric neural networks, a novel framework for dynamically adjusting network size during training. The core idea – allowing the network to grow and shrink based on data and training progress – is a compelling departure from traditional fixed-architecture approaches and addresses the costly problem of hyperparameter optimization for network size. The theoretical justification provided, establishing conditions for global minimum attainment, further strengthens the contribution.

**Reject.** While conceptually exciting and demonstrating promising initial results, the paper feels incomplete. The performance gains aren’t consistently demonstrated across datasets, and the experimental setup, while thorough, lacks sufficient comparison to strong baselines, particularly more recent automated architecture search methods. The theoretical contribution, while interesting, feels somewhat detached from the practical implementation and its limitations aren’t fully explored.

The strength of this work lies in its innovative approach. The concept of f-equivalence and the AdaRad training algorithm are well-motivated and attempt to address key practical challenges in neural network training. The inclusion of CapNorm is a clever adaptation to maintain the benefits of regularization within the dynamic network framework. The experiments on MNIST and the poker dataset are encouraging, showing that nonparametric networks can achieve competitive accuracy, and in some cases, surpass traditional methods. The analysis of the training process, particularly Figure 3, provides valuable insight into how the network evolves and adapts during training.

However, several points raise concerns. First, the performance on the convex dataset is significantly worse than parametric networks, raising questions about the method’s generalizability. The authors acknowledge this but don’t provide a satisfying explanation or potential remedies. Second, the comparison to baseline methods feels somewhat limited. While grid search is included, more modern and sophisticated automated architecture search techniques (e.g., those based on reinforcement learning or evolutionary algorithms) are not considered. This makes it difficult to assess the true advantage of the proposed approach. Third, the theoretical results, while mathematically sound, lack a clear connection to the practical limitations of the algorithm. For example, what are the implications of the assumptions made in Theorem 1 for real-world datasets? Finally, while AdaRad is well-explained, its computational cost compared to established optimizers isn’t fully justified. The claim of graceful scaling needs further quantitative support.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Expand Baseline Comparisons:** Include comparisons to more recent and robust automated architecture search methods.
*   **Address Convex Dataset Failure:** Investigate the reasons for the poor performance on the convex dataset and propose potential solutions.
*   **Theoretical Limitations:** Discuss the limitations of the theoretical results and their applicability to real-world scenarios.
*   **Computational Cost Analysis:** Provide a more detailed and quantitative analysis of the computational cost of AdaRad compared to other optimization algorithms.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Investigate the sensitivity of the results to the choice of hyperparameters, such as αr, αφ, and β.
*   **Scaling to Larger Datasets:** Conduct experiments on larger and more complex datasets to demonstrate the scalability of the approach.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   What specific characteristics of the convex dataset might explain the observed poor performance of nonparametric networks?
*   How does the computational cost of AdaRad scale with network size and dataset size? Can you provide a more detailed cost analysis?
*   What are the practical implications of the assumptions made in Theorem 1? Are these assumptions likely to hold for real-world datasets?
*   Have you explored other regularization techniques beyond the fan-in regularizer? If so, what were the results?
*   How does the performance of AdaRad-M compare to AdaRad? Were the differences statistically significant?
