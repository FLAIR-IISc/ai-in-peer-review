## ICLR 2024 Review: Nonparametric Neural Networks

**1. Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel framework called "Nonparametric Neural Networks" (NPNs) which dynamically adjusts network size *during* training, rather than relying on pre-defined architectures or post-training pruning.  The authors propose a method for shrinking and expanding networks by adding or removing units based on a fan-in regularizer, alongside a novel normalization layer (CapNorm) and training algorithm (AdaRad) designed to function effectively within this dynamic setting. They provide a theoretical justification for the method, demonstrating that a global training error minimum exists at finite dimensionality under certain conditions.  Experimental results on MNIST, a synthetic rectangles dataset, and a convex dataset suggest NPNs can achieve comparable or superior performance to static, optimized networks, and demonstrate scalability to a large dataset.

**2. Decision:**

**Reject.** While the ideas presented are interesting and potentially impactful, the paper suffers from several weaknesses including insufficient empirical validation, particularly a lack of comparison to strong, modern baselines, and a presentation that lacks clarity in crucial areas (e.g., the precise benefit of CapNorm). The convex dataset results are particularly concerning, raising doubts about the generalizability of the approach.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Limited Baselines & Modern Comparisons:** The comparison to standard parametric networks often involves a grid search or random search with limited hyperparameter exploration.  More robust comparisons against state-of-the-art methods like NAS (Neural Architecture Search) or modern optimization algorithms like AdamW are missing.  Simply demonstrating competitive performance against a manually tuned network is not sufficient.  The authors mention these algorithms in the related work section, but do not adequately address how their approach compares.
* **Unconvincing Justification for CapNorm:** The motivation for CapNorm is understandable (preventing the regularizer from being circumvented by batch norm), but the explanation is somewhat hand-wavy. The paper doesn’t clearly articulate *why* simply capping the standard deviation is the optimal solution, and whether other strategies (e.g., adjusting the regularization strength) might be equally or more effective.  There's no empirical evidence showing CapNorm is essential for the method's success.
* **Problematic Convex Dataset Results:**  The poor performance on the convex dataset is a significant red flag. This dataset is relatively simple, and the authors admit NPNs underperform significantly. This casts doubt on the broader applicability of the method, especially as the authors highlight the benefit in scenarios *without* prior information.  The authors don’t offer a compelling explanation for this failure.
* **Lack of Ablation Studies:** It's unclear how critical each component of the NPN framework is (CapNorm, AdaRad, the specific choice of regularization).  Ablation studies, systematically removing or replacing components, would help isolate their contributions and demonstrate the overall effectiveness of the proposed system.
* **Complex Algorithm Presentation:** AdaRad-M, while interesting, is added late in the paper with limited explanation of its benefits compared to the standard AdaRad. The algorithmic descriptions, while detailed, are difficult to follow and could benefit from greater clarity.

**4. Additional Feedback (to improve the paper):**

* **Stronger Empirical Validation:**  Focus on a more rigorous evaluation.  Include comparisons to modern NAS techniques on relevant datasets. Explore different architectures beyond the one presented.
* **Clarity on CapNorm:** Provide a more detailed justification for the design of CapNorm. Include ablation studies to demonstrate its impact on performance.
* **Analyze Convex Dataset Failure:** Investigate *why* the NPN approach fails on the convex dataset. Is it a limitation of the regularizer? The dynamic network size adaptation?  Addressing this will strengthen the paper's conclusions.
* **Ablation Studies:** Conduct systematic ablation studies to assess the contribution of each component (CapNorm, AdaRad, regularization strength, etc.).
* **Simplified Presentation:**  Streamline the algorithmic descriptions and focus on the core principles.  Use visual aids to clarify the dynamics of network growth and shrinkage.
* **More Details on Hyperparameter Tuning:**  Provide more specifics on the hyperparameter tuning process for both NPNs and the baseline networks. This would increase the reproducibility of the results.
* **Discussion of Computational Cost:** While the paper touches upon cost, a more detailed analysis of the computational overhead of the dynamic network adaptation is necessary.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1. The convex dataset performance is substantially worse than parametric networks. What specific aspects of this dataset prevent the NPN framework from succeeding, and what potential modifications to the approach could address this?
2. How sensitive is the performance of NPNs to the initial random seed? Are the observed results consistent across multiple runs, or are they heavily dependent on the initial conditions?
3. Can you provide a more detailed comparison of the computational cost of training an NPN versus a statically sized network, accounting for both the training time and the overhead of dynamic adaptation?
4.  What is the impact of different choices for the angular step size (αφ) on the final network architecture and performance?  Is there a principled way to determine an appropriate value for this parameter?
5. The paper highlights the benefit of dynamic network adaptation, but doesn’t address the potential for instability. How does the proposed framework mitigate the risk of oscillations or divergence during training?
