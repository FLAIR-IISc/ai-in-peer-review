This paper introduces a framework called Nonparametric Neural Networks (NNPs) aiming to dynamically adjust network size during training, circumventing the need for extensive hyperparameter search. The authors claim that NNPs can achieve comparable or superior accuracy to conventionally sized, pre-defined networks, and even outperform results obtained through black-box optimization methods. They support this claim with theoretical analysis demonstrating a global training error minimum exists under certain regularization conditions, and empirical results on MNIST, rectangles images, and a large poker dataset.

Reject. While the idea of dynamically adjusting network size is appealing, the presented work feels underdeveloped and relies too heavily on untestable assertions and lacks sufficient comparative rigor against well-established baselines, particularly for the larger, more complex dataset. The theoretical justification feels tangential and doesn’t fully address the practical challenges of optimization in a non-parametric space.

The primary issue is the lack of convincing empirical validation. While improvements are shown on MNIST and rectangles, the claims of outperforming black-box methods are not substantiated with a thorough comparison. The comparison to random search, a standard baseline, is absent. Furthermore, the reliance on a specific angular step size decay schedule, without extensive sensitivity analysis, raises concerns about the robustness of the results. The results on the poker dataset, while promising, are presented without sufficient detail about the training setup of the parametric networks used for comparison. Specifically, the hyperparameters for those networks should be reported to facilitate reproducibility and a fair assessment. The improvement on the rectangles dataset appears conditional on using a small regularization parameter, which raises the question of whether the benefits are solely due to regularization and not the non-parametric aspect of the method. The claim that NNPs perform poorly on the convex dataset is concerning and needs further investigation. Why does this method fail so dramatically on this dataset? What characteristics of the convex dataset make it unsuitable for this approach?

The theoretical framework, while interesting, appears disconnected from the practical implementation. The proof of Theorem 1 relies on a specific type of regularization (fan-in or fan-out) and doesn’t fully address the complexities of the AdaRad training algorithm, which introduces additional hyperparameters and stochasticity. The discussion of the relationship between regularization and network size feels incomplete. How does the choice of regularization parameter influence the final network size and its generalization performance?

Several aspects of the method require further clarification. What is the computational overhead of dynamically adjusting network size compared to training a fixed-size network? While the authors mention some computational considerations, a more detailed analysis is needed. The choice of self-similar nonlinearities is justified theoretically, but its practical impact on performance needs to be assessed. What happens if other nonlinearities are used? The initialization scheme for new units is simple but potentially suboptimal. Have the authors explored other initialization strategies? The interplay between the radial and angular step sizes requires more attention. How sensitive are the results to the choice of these parameters? Finally, the use of batch normalization with a capping mechanism (CapNorm) is ad hoc and requires a more rigorous justification. Why does capping the scaling factor prevent the regularizer from being circumvented?

Specific questions for the authors:

1.  Can you provide a more detailed comparison to random search on all datasets, including hyperparameter ranges and the number of trials?
2.  For the poker dataset, please report the hyperparameters used for the parametric networks and their performance metrics (training/validation/test error).
3.  Why does the NNP approach perform poorly on the convex dataset? What modifications could be made to address this issue?
4.  Can you quantify the computational overhead of the dynamic network adjustment process compared to training a fixed-size network?
5.  Have you explored other initialization strategies for new units, and if so, what were the results?
6.  Could you elaborate on the theoretical justification for the CapNorm mechanism and provide empirical evidence to support its effectiveness?
7.  What are the implications of the choice of βarith and βquad in the AdaRad-M algorithm? How sensitive are the results to their values?
