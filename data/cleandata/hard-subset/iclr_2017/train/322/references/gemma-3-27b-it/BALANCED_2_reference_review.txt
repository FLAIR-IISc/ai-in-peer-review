This paper introduces a framework called Nonparametric Neural Networks (NNNs) for dynamically adjusting network size during training, aiming to alleviate the computational cost and potential sub-optimality of traditional black-box hyperparameter optimization methods. The core idea is to allow the network to grow and shrink during a single training run, guided by a fan-in regularizer and a novel training algorithm called AdaRad. The authors also propose CapNorm, a modification of batch normalization suitable for NNNs. The paper presents theoretical justification for the approach and experimental results on MNIST, rectangles images, and a larger dataset from OpenML.

**Reject** – While the ideas presented are interesting and the theoretical grounding is a positive aspect, the paper suffers from a lack of compelling empirical evidence demonstrating a significant advantage over existing methods, especially considering the complexity introduced. The results are mixed, showing improvement on some datasets but underperforming on others, and the gains aren’t consistently substantial enough to warrant a paradigm shift. Furthermore, the experimental setup and reporting could be significantly improved to strengthen the conclusions.

Here's a breakdown of my reasoning:

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The concept of dynamically adjusting network size during training is a valuable contribution to the field.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The proof that NNNs achieve a global training error minimum under certain conditions provides a solid theoretical basis.
*   **AdaRad & CapNorm:**  The proposed training algorithm and batch normalization modification are well-motivated and address specific challenges posed by NNNs.
*   **Detailed Algorithm Description:** Algorithm 1 for AdaRad is clearly presented and provides a good understanding of the training process.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Inconsistent Results:** Performance varies across datasets. The underperformance on the convex dataset is particularly concerning, given its relative simplicity. The claim that NNNs can achieve better accuracy *than is possible by black-box methods* feels unsupported by the evidence presented.
*   **Limited Baselines:** The comparison with hyperparameter-optimized parametric networks isn’t comprehensive. While grid and random search are mentioned, the details of these searches (search space, number of trials) aren’t provided. It’s difficult to assess whether the NNN’s performance truly surpasses optimized parametric models.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions the importance of step size annealing and appropriate choice of angular step size, but doesn’t delve into the sensitivity of the approach to these parameters. A more thorough analysis of hyperparameter impact would be beneficial.
*   **Scalability Concerns:** While the poker dataset results are promising, the added computational cost of the dynamic resizing isn’t adequately addressed. The authors mention graceful scaling, but the actual training time compared to standard methods isn't detailed.
*   **Overly Detailed Appendices:** The extensive mathematical proofs in the appendix, while rigorous, detract from the overall clarity and accessibility of the paper. Some of the proofs could be shortened or moved to supplementary material.

**Additional Feedback & Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **More Thorough Baselines:** Include more comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art hyperparameter optimization techniques like Bayesian optimization (e.g., using libraries like Optuna or Scikit-Optimize). Specifically, report the configuration of the search space and number of trials used for these methods.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct ablation studies to evaluate the individual contributions of AdaRad and CapNorm. How much does each component contribute to the overall performance?
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity Analysis:** Systematically explore the impact of different hyperparameter settings (radial step size, angular step size, regularization parameter, mixing rates) on the performance of NNNs.
*   **Computational Cost Analysis:** Provide a detailed comparison of training time and resource usage between NNNs and standard parametric networks. Include a breakdown of the computational cost associated with the dynamic resizing process.
*   **Visualization of Network Evolution:** Include visualizations showing how the network size changes during training. This would provide insights into the dynamic resizing process and help understand how the network adapts to the data.
*   **Clarify the Role of λ:** It would be helpful to provide more intuition regarding the selection of the regularization parameter λ.
*   **Revisit the Convex Dataset:** Investigate why NNNs perform poorly on the convex dataset. Is it due to the simplicity of the dataset, the choice of hyperparameters, or a fundamental limitation of the approach?
*   **Address the "Initial Size" Question:**  The paper mentions starting with 10 units per layer. How sensitive is performance to the initial network size?



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Regarding the poker dataset, how does the training time of NNNs compare to that of an optimized parametric network of similar performance?
2.  The authors mention that results can vary substantially depending on the random seed.  What is the variance in performance across different seeds, and how does this compare to the variance observed with traditional methods?
3.  How sensitive is the performance of NNNs to the choice of the initial network size (i.e., the number of units in each layer at the start of training)?
4.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation of why NNNs underperform on the convex dataset?
5.  What are the limitations of this approach? Are there types of datasets or problems where NNNs are likely to be less effective?
