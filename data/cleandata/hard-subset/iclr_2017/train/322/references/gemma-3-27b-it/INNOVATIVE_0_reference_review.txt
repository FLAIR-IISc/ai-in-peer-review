This paper introduces a novel framework called Nonparametric Neural Networks (NNs), aiming to dynamically adjust network size during training, eliminating the need for manual hyperparameter tuning of network architecture. The core idea is to allow the network to grow and shrink during a single training run, potentially achieving better accuracy and efficiency than traditional black-box optimization methods. The paper presents theoretical justification for this approach, a novel normalization layer (CapNorm), and a new training algorithm (AdaRad).

**Reject.** While the idea is incredibly bold and potentially impactful, the presented experimental validation is insufficient to support the strong claims made. The performance gains are dataset-dependent, and the results on the convex dataset are particularly concerning. The theoretical justification, while present, feels somewhat disconnected from the practical implementation and could benefit from further elaboration relating to the observed experimental behavior.

The novelty of this work is undeniably its strength. The concept of dynamically adapting network size during training is a compelling alternative to fixed-architecture networks and laborious hyperparameter searches. The introduction of CapNorm and AdaRad demonstrates a thoughtful attempt to address the challenges inherent in training such networks. The theoretical work establishing a global minimum under certain conditions is also a significant contribution. However, the empirical results are not convincing enough to outweigh the inherent risks of a non-standard approach. 

Specifically, the following concerns require addressing:

*   **Inconsistent Performance:** The paper reports significant performance improvements on MNIST and rectangles images, but underperforms on the convex dataset. The reason for this discrepancy is not sufficiently explored. Why does this method fail on a seemingly simpler dataset? A deeper analysis is needed to understand the conditions under which this framework excels or fails.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are limited to three datasets. While these are standard benchmarks, expanding the evaluation to a wider range of tasks (e.g., NLP, reinforcement learning) would strengthen the findings.
*   **Lack of Comparison with Modern Baselines:** While comparisons to grid and random search are made, it’s unclear how this approach stacks up against more recent and sophisticated Neural Architecture Search (NAS) methods, even if computationally more expensive.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions tuning of angular step size. How sensitive is the performance to other hyperparameters, like the radial step size, and the mixing rates in AdaRad? A more thorough ablation study is required.
*   **Scaling Concerns:** The poker dataset experiment, while promising, could be further analyzed. The claim that the training time overhead is mitigated as the network grows requires more quantitative evidence.
*   **Theoretical Connection to Experiments:** The proof of Theorem 1 demonstrates the existence of a global minimum under specific conditions. However, the paper doesn’t explicitly connect these theoretical guarantees to the observed experimental behavior. Do the conditions of the theorem hold in the experimental settings? How does the choice of regularization parameter (lambda) influence the convergence to this minimum?



**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Clarify the intuition behind CapNorm:** The rationale for capping the scaling factor in batch normalization is sound, but a more intuitive explanation would be helpful.
*   **Provide pseudocode for AdaRad-M:** The description of AdaRad-M is somewhat buried in the appendix. A clearer presentation within the main body of the paper would enhance readability.
*   **Discuss potential limitations:** A frank discussion of the potential limitations of this approach (e.g., increased computational complexity, sensitivity to hyperparameters) would strengthen the paper.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Why does the proposed method perform poorly on the convex dataset? What modifications could be made to improve performance on this type of dataset?
2.  How does the computational cost of Nonparametric NNs compare to NAS methods, particularly in terms of wall-clock time?
3.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of the radial step size (alpha\_r) and the mixing rates in AdaRad (beta\_arith and beta\_quad)?
4.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation of how Theorem 1 relates to the observed experimental results, specifically regarding the choice of the regularization parameter (lambda)?
5.  What are the practical considerations for applying this framework to very large datasets and deep architectures?
