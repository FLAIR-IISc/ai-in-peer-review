This paper introduces Nonparametric Neural Networks (NNs), a framework for dynamically adjusting network size during training. The core idea – to shrink and expand networks *during* training to find a good architecture – is a compelling and novel approach to model selection, moving beyond the typical black-box optimization methods. The authors also present theoretical justification for the approach and novel training techniques (CapNorm and AdaRad) to enable it.

**Reject.** While the idea is highly promising and the work demonstrates a clear understanding of the challenges, the empirical results are not consistently convincing. The significant performance gains on MNIST and rectangles are encouraging, but the poor performance on the convex dataset and the lack of clear advantages over thoroughly tuned parametric networks raise concerns. The presented work feels more like a strong proof-of-concept than a fully validated, robust method.

The novelty of dynamically adjusting network size *during* training is the strongest aspect of this paper. Current model selection techniques generally involve training complete networks and comparing their performance. This approach directly addresses the inefficiencies of that process and the potential to discover architectures beyond those pre-defined. The theoretical grounding, specifically Theorem 1, is also a significant contribution, providing a mathematical basis for the method’s potential success. The introduction of CapNorm and AdaRad, while building on existing techniques, are sensible adaptations required to make the core idea workable. The analysis of the training process, particularly Figure 3, provides valuable insight into how the network evolves.

However, the experimental validation is where the paper falls short. While the improvements on MNIST and rectangles are interesting, the failure on the convex dataset is a significant red flag. This suggests the method may be sensitive to dataset characteristics or require further tuning. Moreover, the comparisons to parametric networks aren't consistently favorable. While NP networks can achieve comparable performance with *fewer* parameters, they don’t consistently *outperform* carefully tuned parametric networks. The scalability experiment on the poker dataset is a positive addition, but doesn’t fully address the concerns raised by the earlier results. The authors state that AdaRad-M showed similar performance to AdaRad, yet provide no empirical results, reducing confidence in that claim. The ablation studies are also lacking – it's unclear how crucial each component (CapNorm, AdaRad) is to the overall performance.

Here are suggestions for improvement:

*   **More robust experimental evaluation:** Conduct a more extensive evaluation on a wider range of datasets, including those where parametric networks typically excel.
*   **Ablation Studies:**  Systematically remove or replace components (CapNorm, AdaRad) to understand their individual contributions.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Investigate the sensitivity of the method to hyperparameters (λ, αφ, ν, νfreq) and provide guidance on their selection.
*   **Detailed Comparison to Baselines:**  Provide more detailed comparisons to state-of-the-art parametric networks, including extensive hyperparameter tuning for the baselines. Specifically, show training curves and discuss why the proposed method does or does not converge faster.
*   **Explain the failure on the convex dataset:** This is crucial. What properties of this dataset make it unsuitable for the proposed approach?
*   **Clarify the computational cost:** While the authors mention the lower memory footprint of AdaRad, a more comprehensive cost analysis (training time, FLOPs) would be valuable.

I have the following questions for the authors:

1.  Why do you think the method performs poorly on the convex dataset? Is it related to the simplicity of the task, or some specific characteristic of the dataset?
2.  The statement that "networks trained without batch normalization could not compete with parametric networks trained with it" suggests batch normalization is critical. How does CapNorm specifically address the issues with using batch normalization in conjunction with the fan-in/fan-out regularizers?
3.  The authors mention using a step size annealing scheme. Could you provide more details about how this was implemented and why it was beneficial? What stopping criteria were used?
4.  In section 4.3, you mention adding units at a rate of one per tenth of an epoch. How was this determined? Does this rate impact performance, and if so, how?
