This paper introduces Nonparametric Neural Networks (NPNs), a framework for dynamically adjusting network size during training to circumvent the need for manual hyperparameter tuning of network architecture. The core idea is to allow networks to grow and shrink during training via the addition and removal of units, guided by a fan-in regularizer and a novel training algorithm (AdaRad) incorporating a capped batch normalization (CapNorm) layer. The authors claim that this approach can achieve higher accuracy than statically sized networks and even surpass the performance of black-box optimization methods.

Reject. While the idea of dynamically adjusting network size is interesting, the presented work lacks sufficient validation and relies on several novel components without robust justification or comparison to well-established alternatives. The performance gains demonstrated are not convincing, and the experimental setup feels somewhat contrived, especially the choice of datasets and training regimes.

The primary concern lies with the lack of thorough comparison. The authors compare NPNs to parametric networks of *the same size* and networks found via random search, but this comparison feels incomplete. The strength of NPNs is supposedly their ability to *find* a good size, so comparing to a pre-defined size undermines this claim. A fairer comparison would involve establishing a clear budget for total parameters and then comparing the performance of NPNs to networks optimized within that budget using more standard methods like grid search or Bayesian optimization, *allowing the parametric networks to also vary in size*. The claim of surpassing black-box methods is weakly supported, and the reported performance on the convex dataset is particularly troubling, showing a clear disadvantage compared to parametric networks.

Furthermore, the introduction of AdaRad and CapNorm adds complexity without sufficient justification. While the authors provide intuition, the necessity of these components isn’t clearly demonstrated. How much does each contribute to the overall performance? Would NPNs perform comparably with standard optimizers (e.g., Adam) and standard batch normalization? The paper needs to provide ablation studies to isolate the benefits of each proposed component. The theoretical justification, while present, feels somewhat detached from the practical implementation and doesn’t fully address the potential for instability or poor convergence due to the dynamic network structure. The dependence on carefully tuned step sizes (αr, αφ) also raises concerns about the sensitivity of the method.

Finally, the experimental section lacks detail regarding the hyperparameter search for the baseline parametric networks. It’s unclear how exhaustive the grid search was and how the "best" network was selected. Reporting more details about the baseline training process is crucial for assessing the fairness of the comparison. The analysis of the training process (Figure 3) is insightful but doesn’t fully explain *why* NPNs perform as they do.

Here are some suggestions for improvement:

* **Comprehensive Baselines:** Include a comparison to parametric networks optimized with grid search or Bayesian optimization *while varying network size* within a defined parameter budget.
* **Ablation Studies:** Conduct experiments to evaluate the individual contributions of AdaRad and CapNorm.
* **Sensitivity Analysis:** Analyze the sensitivity of the results to the choice of step sizes (αr, αφ) and regularization parameter (λ).
* **Theoretical Justification:** Strengthen the theoretical analysis to address potential convergence issues and provide more insights into the behavior of the dynamic network structure.
* **Dataset Diversity:** Evaluate the method on a wider range of datasets, including more challenging benchmarks.
* **Hyperparameter Details:** Provide more detailed information about the hyperparameter search process for the baseline parametric networks.
* **Computational Cost:** A more detailed analysis of the computational cost compared to standard training procedures would be helpful.

Specific questions for the authors:

* How sensitive are the results to the initial network size (d0)?
* What is the computational overhead of AdaRad compared to standard optimizers like Adam?
* Can you provide a more detailed explanation of why NPNs perform poorly on the convex dataset?
* How does the performance of NPNs change when using standard batch normalization instead of CapNorm?
* What is the impact of different unit addition/removal strategies on the final performance?
