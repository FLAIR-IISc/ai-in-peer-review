This paper introduces Nonparametric Neural Networks (NNs), a novel framework for dynamically adjusting network size during training. The core idea is compelling: rather than searching for the optimal network architecture, the framework learns it concurrently with weight training, eliminating the need for lengthy hyperparameter sweeps and potentially achieving better generalization by adapting the network to the data. The theoretical justification provided, demonstrating global minimum attainment under certain conditions, adds significant weight to the proposal.

**Reject.** While the core idea is exceptionally promising and demonstrably novel, the experimental validation, while showing encouraging results on some datasets, lacks the robustness and clarity needed for acceptance at ICLR. The performance on the convex dataset is particularly concerning, and the ablation studies (e.g., comparing AdaRad to other optimizers) aren’t sufficiently detailed to understand the benefits of the proposed method definitively.  The paper prioritizes novelty, and succeeds, but falls short on providing comprehensive validation.

The strength of the paper lies in its originality. The concept of dynamically growing and shrinking a network during training is a fresh perspective on network architecture search and addresses the significant computational cost of traditional methods. The mathematical framework presented appears sound, and the theorems provide a strong theoretical foundation. The introduction of CapNorm is a clever adaptation of batch normalization to accommodate the dynamic network size. AdaRad is a novel and sensible approach to addressing the challenges of training such networks.

However, several areas require significant improvement. The experiments, while insightful, feel somewhat limited. The jump to a more complex dataset like Poker is a positive step, but the analysis of *why* the method works (or doesn't) in different scenarios is lacking. For instance, the poor performance on the convex dataset requires a more in-depth investigation. Why does the approach struggle with this specific dataset, and what modifications might address this? 

Furthermore, the comparison to standard methods isn't exhaustive enough. While the paper demonstrates competitive performance, a more thorough comparison – perhaps with more sophisticated NAS methods – would strengthen the claims. The ablation studies examining the contribution of CapNorm and AdaRad could be more granular.

The writing, while generally clear, could benefit from more concise explanations of certain concepts. For example, the radial-angular gradient descent is described in detail, but the intuition behind this approach could be further emphasized. The connection between the proposed framework and existing work on pruning or dynamic network surgery could also be more explicitly discussed.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Scalability:** While the poker dataset experiment hints at scalability, a more detailed analysis of the computational cost as a function of dataset size and network complexity would be valuable.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  A sensitivity analysis of the key hyperparameters (e.g., radial step size, angular step size, regularization parameter) would help understand the robustness of the method.
*   **Visualization:** Visualizing the network's evolution during training—showing how the size and connectivity change over time—would provide valuable insights.
*   **Theoretical Limitations:** A more thorough discussion of the limitations of the theoretical guarantees (e.g., the assumptions on the nonlinearities and error function) would be beneficial.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The convex dataset performance is significantly worse than parametric networks. What is your hypothesis for *why* Nonparametric NNs fail to generalize well on this dataset, and what changes to the framework could potentially address this?
2.  AdaRad-M is mentioned but results are omitted. Were there any substantive differences in performance between AdaRad and AdaRad-M, and if so, why were the AdaRad-M results not included?
3.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost of AdaRad compared to other optimizers, particularly in terms of memory usage and per-iteration time? Is there potential for further optimization?
4.  How sensitive is the performance of the framework to the initial network size and the unit addition rate? Are there guidelines for setting these parameters based on the characteristics of the dataset?
5.  The claim is made that the framework doesn’t require a well-studied task. Are there specific properties of tasks which make this method perform well/poorly?
