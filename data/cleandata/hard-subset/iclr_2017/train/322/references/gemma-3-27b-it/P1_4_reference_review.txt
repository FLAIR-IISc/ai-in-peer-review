## ICLR 2024 Review: Nonparametric Neural Networks

**1. Summary**

This paper introduces a novel framework called "Nonparametric Neural Networks" (NPNs) which dynamically adjusts network size during training, aiming to automatically select an appropriate network architecture without prior information.  The authors address the limitations of black-box hyperparameter optimization methods (like grid/random search and Bayesian optimization) by allowing the network to grow and shrink during a single training run. They theoretically ground their approach by demonstrating that a global training error minimum exists at finite dimensionality under certain regularization conditions (fan-in/fan-out).  They also propose a new normalization layer (CapNorm) and training algorithm (AdaRad) specifically designed for NPNs, to handle the changing network dimensions.  The authors demonstrate promising results on MNIST and a rectangles image dataset, achieving competitive or superior performance compared to parametric networks of similar size, and in some cases, exceeding results from exhaustive searches.  They also demonstrate performance on a large classification dataset from OpenML.

**2. Decision**

**Accept (with minor revisions)**. The paper presents a compelling and theoretically sound approach to automated network architecture search.  The experimental results, while not overwhelming, are promising and demonstrate the potential of NPNs. The framework offers a unique and potentially valuable alternative to existing black-box optimization methods, particularly in scenarios where prior knowledge about the optimal architecture is limited.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

* **Novelty and Significance:**  The concept of dynamically adjusting network size during training is a significant contribution. It moves beyond the traditional "train and evaluate" paradigm of hyperparameter optimization, and allows for a more efficient exploration of the architecture space.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The theorem establishing the existence of a global minimum at finite dimensionality is a crucial theoretical grounding for the approach, addressing concerns about uncontrolled network growth. The regularization analysis is well-motivated.
* **Technical Contributions:** The introduction of CapNorm and AdaRad are necessary and well-justified adaptations for training NPNs.  The reasoning behind these designs, particularly addressing the challenges of regularization and normalization with variable network dimensions, is clear and convincing.
* **Empirical Results:** The experiments demonstrate that NPNs can achieve competitive performance on the tested datasets. The results on the rectangles dataset, where NPNs outperform both parametric networks of the same size and exhaustive searches, are particularly noteworthy. The scaling to a larger dataset is also encouraging.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and the key concepts are explained clearly.  The algorithm descriptions are detailed and well-structured.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement)**

* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the results are positive, the paper could benefit from a more thorough analysis of the sensitivity of the performance to the hyperparameters (αr, αφ, λ, β, ν). How robust are the results to different settings of these parameters?
* **Computational Cost:** Although the paper acknowledges the computational cost of AdaRad compared to standard SGD, a more detailed comparison, potentially with profiling data, would be valuable. Specifically, how does the cost scale with network size and dataset size?
* **Ablation Studies:**  An ablation study to assess the individual contributions of CapNorm and AdaRad would strengthen the paper. How much does each component contribute to the overall performance?
* **Deeper Dive into Failure Case (Convex Dataset):** The authors mention that NPNs performed poorly on the convex dataset.  A more detailed analysis of *why* this happened would be beneficial. Was it related to the specific characteristics of the dataset, or a limitation of the current framework?  What modifications could potentially improve performance on such datasets?
* **Generalization to Other Architectures:** The experiments focus on relatively simple feedforward networks. It would be interesting to explore the applicability of NPNs to other architectures, such as convolutional neural networks.
* **Connection to Pruning:** While the authors correctly position their work against existing pruning methods, a more detailed discussion of the similarities and differences – and potentially a comparison – would be helpful.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

1.  **Hyperparameter Tuning:**  What strategies did you employ to choose the initial values for αr and αφ? Did you find that these parameters are highly dataset-dependent?
2.  **Scaling to Larger Datasets:**  The OpenML experiment is promising. How did the training time for NPNs compare to training similarly-performing parametric networks on that dataset? What were the memory requirements?
3.  **Regularization Strength:**  The paper mentions finding optimal regularization parameters.  Can you elaborate on the process used to determine the best value of λ for each dataset? Was there a significant difference in optimal λ values across datasets?
4.  **AdaRad-M:** You mention AdaRad-M showing similar performance to AdaRad. Were there any specific cases or datasets where AdaRad-M exhibited advantages or disadvantages?
5. **Theoretical Implications:**  The theorem guarantees a global minimum. Does this guarantee translate into practical convergence during training, or are there scenarios where the algorithm might get stuck in local optima despite the theoretical guarantee?



Overall, this is a well-written and promising paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of automated machine learning. The minor revisions suggested above would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
