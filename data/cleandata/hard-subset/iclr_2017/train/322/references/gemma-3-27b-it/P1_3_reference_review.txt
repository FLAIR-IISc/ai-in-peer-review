## Review of "Nonparametric Neural Networks"

**1. Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel framework called "Nonparametric Neural Networks" (NPNs) that dynamically adjusts network size during training, aiming to circumvent the need for costly hyperparameter search for network architecture. The authors propose a method to grow and shrink networks based on fan-in regularization, coupled with a novel normalization layer (CapNorm) and a training algorithm (AdaRad). They provide a theoretical justification for their approach and demonstrate its effectiveness on several datasets, showing competitive or superior performance compared to traditional parametric networks. The core idea is compelling – to learn the network size itself during training rather than predefining it.

**2. Decision:**

**Reject** with a strong encouragement to revise and resubmit. While the ideas presented are interesting and the theoretical grounding is promising, the empirical results presented are inconsistent and, crucially, the ablation studies and comparisons to strong baselines are insufficient to convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed approach. The experimental section feels underdeveloped and lacks the rigor expected for a conference like ICLR.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Inconsistent Results:** The performance of NPNs varies significantly across datasets. While promising results are shown on MNIST and rectangles, performance on the convex dataset is demonstrably worse than parametric networks. This lack of robustness raises concerns about the generalizability of the proposed method. The authors acknowledge this but lack a compelling explanation.
* **Insufficient Baseline Comparison:** The comparison with parametric networks is somewhat uneven. While a grid search is performed for parametric networks, the selection criteria for choosing the best representative NPN seems less systematic (choosing a “typical” representative). It’s unclear if the chosen NPN truly represents the best performance achievable with the framework.  The comparison to exhaustive random search, while present, lacks detailed reporting on the search space and settings.
* **Lack of Ablation Studies:**  The paper introduces several novel components (CapNorm, AdaRad). However, there's a limited analysis of the individual contribution of each component.  For example, how much does CapNorm contribute compared to standard batch normalization, or AdaRad compared to other optimization algorithms?  Without these ablations, it's hard to understand what’s driving the observed performance.
* **Complexity and Scalability:** While the authors discuss computational cost, the practical implementation seems complex.  The algorithm introduces many hyperparameters (αr, αφ, β, ν, νfreq) that need tuning, potentially offsetting the benefit of automating network size selection. The scalability discussion is largely theoretical and lacks detailed empirical support for large datasets beyond the poker dataset.
* **Theoretical Justification:** While the theorem regarding global minima is interesting, its practical implications are unclear. The conditions for the theorem (specifically regarding the nonlinearities) may be restrictive, and it's not clear how often these conditions are met in practice. A deeper discussion of the practical relevance of this theorem is needed.

**4. Additional Feedback:**

* **Clarify the Advantages:**  The paper would benefit from a more focused discussion of *when* NPNs are expected to outperform parametric methods.  Is it primarily beneficial when prior knowledge about network architecture is limited?  For specific types of datasets?
* **Expand Experimental Evaluation:**
    * Include more datasets, particularly those with different characteristics (e.g., image classification, NLP tasks).
    * Perform a more thorough hyperparameter search for both NPNs and parametric networks.
    * Provide standard deviations for all performance metrics to demonstrate the robustness of the results.
    * Conduct ablation studies to evaluate the contribution of CapNorm and AdaRad.
* **Analyze Training Dynamics:** The analysis of the training process in Section 4.2 is insightful. Expanding this analysis with visualizations of weight distributions, activation patterns, and network growth trajectories would further strengthen the paper.
* **Discuss Limitations:** A more thorough discussion of the limitations of the proposed approach, including potential challenges with specific datasets or network architectures, would increase the paper's credibility.
* **Code Availability:** Making the code publicly available would greatly facilitate reproducibility and allow the community to build upon this work.

**Questions for the Authors:**

* The convex dataset results are significantly worse than parametric networks. Can you provide further analysis into why NPNs struggle on this dataset?  Are there specific characteristics of the convex dataset that hinder the performance of the proposed approach?
* The selection of the “typical” NPN representative (Section 4.1) seems somewhat subjective. What objective criteria were used to ensure this selection was not biased towards specific results? Could a more systematic method, like averaging results over multiple runs or selecting based on a specific metric, be employed?
* Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational cost of training NPNs compared to training parametric networks, especially as the network size grows?
*  What are the practical implications of the theorem presented in Section 2? Are there common neural network architectures that satisfy the conditions of the theorem?
*  How sensitive are the results to the choice of the angular and radial step sizes (αr and αφ)?  What strategies can be used to effectively tune these hyperparameters?
