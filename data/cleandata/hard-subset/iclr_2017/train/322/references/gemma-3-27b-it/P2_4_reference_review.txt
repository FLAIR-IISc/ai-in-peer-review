## ICLR Review: NONPARAMETRIC NEURAL NETWORKS

**1. Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel framework called "Nonparametric Neural Networks" (NNNs) that dynamically adjusts network size during training, aiming to alleviate the challenges of manual hyperparameter tuning for neural network architecture.  The authors propose a method for shrinking and expanding networks based on fan-in regularization, coupled with a new normalization layer (CapNorm) and a training algorithm (AdaRad) to facilitate stable training. They theoretically ground their approach with a theorem demonstrating that a global training error minimum exists at finite dimensionality under certain conditions.  The paper demonstrates promising results on MNIST and a synthetic rectangles dataset, showing that NNNs can achieve comparable or superior performance to hand-tuned parametric networks, and scale effectively to larger datasets like the poker dataset.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept (Weakly)**.  The paper presents a novel and interesting approach to automating neural network architecture selection. The theoretical foundation is a valuable contribution, and the experimental results, while not overwhelmingly conclusive, are promising. However, the complexity of the method and a lack of comprehensive comparison to strong baselines (e.g., efficient neural architecture search methods) warrant cautious optimism.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty:** The idea of dynamically adjusting network size *during* training is a distinct departure from most existing approaches.  This is more efficient than training multiple models with different architectures.
* **Theoretical Grounding:**  The theorem establishing the existence of a global minimum with finite dimensionality is a significant contribution, justifying the core principle of the NNN framework. While the assumptions of the theorem are somewhat restrictive, it provides a solid foundation for the method.
* **Experimental Results:** The results on MNIST and the rectangles dataset are encouraging, demonstrating the ability of NNNs to match or outperform manually tuned networks, and achieve competitive performance with less manual effort.  Scaling to the poker dataset is also positive.
* **Technical Contributions:** CapNorm and AdaRad are well-motivated additions that address challenges specific to training nonparametric networks. AdaRad is particularly well-justified and appears to be a crucial component for stable training.

However, the following weaknesses temper my enthusiasm:

* **Complexity:** The framework involves several components (fan-in regularization, CapNorm, AdaRad, dynamic unit addition/removal) making it somewhat complex to understand and implement.  This complexity might hinder wider adoption.
* **Limited Baselines:** The comparisons to parametric networks, while present, are not exhaustive.  Specifically, the paper doesn't adequately compare against more recent and sophisticated neural architecture search (NAS) techniques (e.g., DARTS, ENAS) that automate architecture selection more broadly.  Demonstrating superiority over these established methods would significantly strengthen the paper.
* **Convex Dataset Results:** The poor performance on the convex dataset is concerning and not fully explained. It suggests potential limitations of the approach that warrant further investigation.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  The paper acknowledges the sensitivity of the results to hyperparameters like αr and αφ.  A more systematic study of these hyperparameters and their impact on performance is needed.



**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Clarity of AdaRad:** The explanation of AdaRad, while detailed, is still complex. Consider adding a more intuitive explanation of *why* separating the radial and angular shifts is beneficial. Perhaps a simple analogy would help.
* **Ablation Studies:**  Conduct ablation studies to isolate the contributions of CapNorm and AdaRad.  How much does each component contribute to the overall performance?
* **NAS Comparison:** Include a more thorough comparison against state-of-the-art NAS methods. This is crucial to position the NNN framework within the broader landscape of automated architecture selection.
* **Convex Dataset Investigation:** Investigate the reasons for the poor performance on the convex dataset.  Is it a limitation of the regularization scheme, the dynamic size adjustment, or something else?
* **Computational Cost Analysis:**  Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of NNNs compared to training traditional parametric networks and running NAS algorithms.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*  The authors state that hardware is geared towards dense weight matrices.  Could the performance of NNNs be improved by exploiting sparsity, even if it requires modifications to the hardware or software stack?
*  What strategies were considered to address the hyperparameter sensitivity (specifically αr and αφ)?  Would an automated hyperparameter tuning method (e.g., Bayesian optimization) be beneficial?
*  Could the framework be extended to support dynamic adjustment of network depth, in addition to width?  What challenges would this introduce?
*  Are there any theoretical insights into why NNNs perform poorly on datasets like the convex dataset?



Overall, this paper represents a promising contribution to the field of automated neural network architecture selection. While further investigation and comparison to stronger baselines are needed, the novelty, theoretical grounding, and encouraging experimental results warrant acceptance.
