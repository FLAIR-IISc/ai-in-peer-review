This paper introduces a novel framework called Nonparametric Neural Networks (NNNs) for automatically selecting network size during training. The core idea is to dynamically shrink and expand the network based on gradients, avoiding the costly full training runs required by traditional black-box optimization methods. The authors also propose a novel normalization layer (CapNorm) and training algorithm (AdaRad) to facilitate this process, and provide theoretical justification for their approach.

**Decision: Reject**

While the ideas presented in this paper are interesting and potentially valuable, the experimental results are not compelling enough to warrant acceptance at ICLR. Specifically, the performance gains over well-tuned parametric networks are inconsistent across datasets, and the results on the convex dataset are particularly weak. The paper lacks a thorough comparative analysis with more recent and sophisticated neural architecture search (NAS) techniques. Furthermore, the theoretical justification, while present, feels somewhat disconnected from the practical implementation and doesn’t fully address potential issues of optimization instability during dynamic network resizing.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Inconsistent Performance Gains:** The primary claim of improved accuracy compared to parametric networks is only consistently supported on the MNIST dataset. On the rectangles images dataset, NNNs perform well only with small regularization parameters, and on the convex dataset, they significantly underperform. This inconsistency raises concerns about the robustness and generalizability of the proposed approach. The authors acknowledge this, but don’t offer convincing explanations or propose avenues for improvement.

2. **Limited Comparison to NAS Techniques:** The paper positions NNNs as an alternative to black-box optimization, but fails to adequately compare them to more modern NAS techniques like differentiable architecture search (DARTS) or evolutionary algorithms. A more comprehensive comparison is needed to understand the strengths and weaknesses of NNNs relative to the state-of-the-art. The authors should discuss why their method offers advantages over existing NAS approaches in terms of computational cost, scalability, or theoretical properties.

3. **Theoretical Disconnect & Practical Concerns:** While the theoretical results regarding the existence of a global minimum are interesting, they do not directly translate into practical guarantees of successful optimization. The dynamic resizing of the network during training could lead to instability, and the paper doesn't fully address this concern. The choice of parameters like αr and αφ seems somewhat arbitrary and could significantly impact performance. A more detailed analysis of the sensitivity of the algorithm to these parameters would be beneficial.

4. **Experimental Setup and Reporting:** The experimental setup feels somewhat dated. The datasets are relatively small and the network architectures are simple. It would be valuable to evaluate NNNs on larger, more challenging datasets and with more complex network architectures. The presentation of results could also be improved. More detailed tables and error bars would enhance the clarity and trustworthiness of the findings.



**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct ablation studies to assess the contribution of each component of the framework (CapNorm, AdaRad, dynamic resizing) to the overall performance. This will help identify the key factors driving the results.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity Analysis:** Investigate the sensitivity of the algorithm to the choice of hyperparameters like αr, αφ, β, and ν. Provide guidance on how to tune these parameters for different datasets and network architectures.
*   **Scalability Analysis:**  Evaluate the scalability of NNNs to larger datasets and more complex network architectures.  How does the computational cost scale with the size of the dataset and the network?
*   **Theoretical Extension:** Explore theoretical extensions to address potential optimization instability issues during dynamic resizing. Can guarantees be provided on the convergence of the algorithm?
*   **Connection to Pruning:**  Elaborate on the relationship between NNNs and existing pruning techniques.  How does NNNs differ from pruning in terms of its approach and performance?
*    **Visualization:** Provide visualizations of the network resizing process during training. This could offer insights into how the network adapts to the data and why certain architectures are favored.
*   **Code Release:**  Releasing the code would greatly facilitate reproducibility and allow other researchers to build upon this work.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Why do NNNs perform so poorly on the convex dataset, and what steps could be taken to improve performance on this type of data?
2.  How does the computational cost of NNNs compare to other NAS techniques, such as DARTS or evolutionary algorithms, on a larger dataset like ImageNet?
3.  The choice of αφ seems crucial. How was the initial value of 30 chosen, and what is the impact of different starting values?
4.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the rationale behind the CapNorm normalization layer, and why it is necessary for the framework to work effectively?
5.  What strategies could be employed to address potential optimization instability during the dynamic resizing of the network?
