This paper introduces a novel framework, Nonparametric Neural Networks (NNNs), for dynamically adjusting network size during training, aiming to circumvent the expensive process of hyperparameter optimization for network architecture. The core idea is compelling: to allow the network to grow and shrink during a single training run, potentially leading to better generalization and efficiency compared to traditional black-box methods. The theoretical grounding, particularly Theorem 1, is a strong positive, establishing conditions under which a global minimum can be reached. The introduction of CapNorm and AdaRad further contribute to the framework, addressing challenges posed by the dynamic network structure.

**Reject.** While the paper presents a technically sound and interesting approach, the experimental results, particularly on the more complex poker dataset, and the somewhat limited scope of experiments (primarily focusing on relatively small datasets) prevent me from recommending acceptance at this time. The improvements demonstrated on MNIST and rectangles are promising, but the underwhelming performance on the convex dataset and the lack of a convincing advantage over well-tuned parametric networks raise concerns about the practical applicability of the method in its current form. The presentation, while thorough, is dense and could benefit from more illustrative examples and a clearer explanation of the intuition behind some of the design choices.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Limited Empirical Evidence:** The most significant weakness lies in the empirical evaluation. While improvements are shown on MNIST and rectangles, the convex dataset highlights a failure case, suggesting the method is not universally effective. The poker dataset experiment is positive, but the relative scale of experimentation feels limited. A more comprehensive evaluation across a wider range of datasets, including larger and more complex ones (e.g., ImageNet), is crucial to establish the robustness and scalability of NNNs. Comparing against state-of-the-art AutoML approaches (beyond grid/random search) is also essential.

2. **Theoretical Result Practicality:** Theorem 1 provides a solid theoretical foundation, but the assumptions regarding the regularizer and nonlinearities may limit its applicability. It’s unclear how sensitive the performance is to deviations from these assumptions in real-world scenarios.  Further, the theorem guarantees a *global* minimum, but doesn’t offer guarantees on the *speed* of convergence. The experiments suggest relatively long training times.

3. **Complexity and Presentation:** The paper is technically dense, and the motivation for certain design choices (e.g., the specific form of AdaRad) isn’t always immediately clear.  More intuitive explanations, alongside visual aids, would enhance understanding. The numerous equations, while mathematically sound, make the paper challenging to follow for a broader audience. The algorithmic details (especially AdaRad) are complex and would benefit from pseudocode that is more concise and easier to interpret.



**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct ablation studies to understand the contribution of each component of the framework (CapNorm, AdaRad, the specific regularization scheme). For example, how does performance change when using standard batch normalization instead of CapNorm? What happens when using a simpler optimization algorithm like Adam instead of AdaRad?
*   **Scalability Analysis:** More detailed analysis of the computational cost of NNNs is needed. While the paper touches on this, providing concrete numbers (e.g., training time per epoch, memory usage) for different network sizes and datasets would be valuable. How does the training time scale with the dataset size and network complexity?
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Investigate the sensitivity of the method to the choice of hyperparameters (αr, αφ, λ, ν, νfreq).  A sensitivity analysis would reveal how robust the performance is to different hyperparameter settings.
*   **Visualization:** Include visualizations of the network architecture during training.  Showing how the network grows and shrinks over time would provide valuable insights into the learning process. Plots of layer sizes versus training iterations would be particularly helpful.
*   **Connection to Pruning:** Expand on the connection between the proposed approach and existing pruning techniques. How does NNN differ from pruning methods, and what are the advantages and disadvantages of each?
*   **Explore different regularizers**: Investigate the performance with different regularizers (e.g., L1 regularization). Could alternative regularizers lead to more efficient or effective network growth?
*  **Consider alternative activation functions**: While the justification for self-similar activation functions is well laid out, explore the impact of using more commonly used activation functions such as Swish or GELU and the implications for the theoretical guarantees.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The performance on the convex dataset is significantly worse than the parametric networks. What are the potential reasons for this, and what modifications to the framework could improve performance on datasets with such characteristics?
2.  The poker dataset experiment shows promising results. However, the description of the experimental setup is somewhat brief. Could you provide more details about the computational resources used (e.g., number of GPUs, training time), and a more detailed comparison to other AutoML methods on this dataset?
3.  The AdaRad algorithm appears quite complex. Could you provide a more intuitive explanation of its underlying principles and how it differs from other adaptive gradient descent methods? What is the practical impact of the radial and angular step sizes?
4.  How sensitive is the performance to the choice of the initialization scheme for new units? Have you experimented with different initialization strategies?
5.  Theorem 1 establishes the existence of a global minimum, but it doesn't provide any guarantees on the convergence rate.  Do you have any empirical observations or theoretical insights regarding the convergence behavior of NNNs?
6.  What are the main limitations of the proposed framework, and what are the most promising directions for future research?
