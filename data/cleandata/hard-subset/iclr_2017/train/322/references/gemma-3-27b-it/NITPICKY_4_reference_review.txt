This paper introduces nonparametric neural networks, a framework for dynamically adjusting network size during training to automatically select an appropriate architecture. The core idea is to allow the network to grow and shrink by adding/removing units with zero fan-in/fan-out, guided by a fan-in regularizer and a novel training algorithm (AdaRad) coupled with a modified batch normalization technique (CapNorm). The authors claim this approach achieves comparable or superior accuracy to traditional methods, and importantly, avoids the expensive upfront cost of hyperparameter optimization via black-box methods.

Reject. While the core idea of dynamically adjusting network size is interesting, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws, inconsistencies, and a lack of thorough experimentation that, cumulatively, undermine confidence in the results. The presentation is also overly verbose and lacks clarity in several crucial sections.

Here's a breakdown of the issues:

1. **Theoretical Weaknesses:** Theorem 1’s conditions on the nonlinearities are excessively restrictive. The requirement for differentiability *and* boundedness of derivatives feels unnecessary and limits applicability. The proof itself, referenced as being in the appendix, is not readily accessible for evaluation and its robustness feels questionable without scrutiny. The connection between the theoretical justification and the practical implementation (specifically, the impact of the approximation introduced by the f-equivalence relation) is not sufficiently discussed.

2. **Methodological Concerns:** The choice of adding/removing units only when their fan-in is zero, while conceptually simple, appears arbitrary. The authors provide some justification, but a more thorough exploration of alternative strategies (e.g., based on activation magnitudes or gradient norms) would strengthen the approach.  The AdaRad algorithm, while novel, lacks sufficient comparison to existing adaptive optimization methods beyond the limited experiments. The justification for the separate radial and angular step sizes feels somewhat hand-wavy. The initialization of new units with random fan-in is not well-motivated and could potentially hinder learning. The rationale behind using only the fan-in regularizer is also weak.

3. **Experimental Deficiencies:** The experimental setup is inconsistent. While the poker dataset experiment is a positive sign of scalability, the results on MNIST and rectangles-images are presented without sufficient detail.  The authors state that the best nonparametric network on MNIST is "close in performance" to the best parametric network, but the figures don’t explicitly quantify this closeness, and the size difference, while mentioned, isn’t visualized clearly. The comparison to an "exhaustive random search" is not well-defined—how many samples were used?  The claim that nonparametric networks perform badly on the convex dataset is concerning, and the explanation provided is not entirely convincing.  The choice of hyperparameters, particularly the angular step size, seems largely empirical and lacks a systematic justification. The ablation studies are missing. What is the impact of CapNorm? Of the radial vs angular step sizes? Of the running averages in AdaRad?

4. **Presentation Issues:** The paper is excessively long and verbose. Many sections, especially the introduction, contain redundant information. The notation is often inconsistent and unclear. The description of AdaRad (Algorithm 1) is particularly difficult to follow due to the numerous lines and the lack of clear separation between the core logic and the implementation details. The discussion of related work is extensive but lacks a critical assessment of the strengths and weaknesses of existing approaches.

5. **Minor Errors/Inconsistencies:**  Numerous minor typographical errors and inconsistencies throughout the text detract from the paper’s professionalism. For instance, the description of CapNorm is confusing and the mathematical notation is not consistently used.



**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **Clarify the theoretical foundations:** Provide a more accessible and rigorous proof of Theorem 1, or at least a more detailed discussion of its limitations.
*   **Conduct ablation studies:** Evaluate the contribution of each component of the proposed framework (CapNorm, AdaRad, the choice of the fan-in regularizer, the zero-fan-in criterion, etc.).
*   **Perform a more thorough comparison to existing methods:**  Compare AdaRad to state-of-the-art optimization algorithms (e.g., AdamW, RAdam) on a wider range of datasets and network architectures.
*   **Provide a more detailed experimental setup:**  Clearly specify all hyperparameters used in the experiments, including search ranges and selection criteria.
*   **Improve the presentation:**  Streamline the writing, clarify the notation, and provide more informative figures and tables.
*   **Address the convex dataset result:** Investigate the reasons for the poor performance on the convex dataset and propose potential solutions.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   What is the computational cost of adding/removing units compared to a single forward/backward pass? Is this cost significant, especially for large networks?
*   How sensitive is the performance of the framework to the choice of the initial network size?
*   Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the rationale behind using only the fan-in regularizer?
*   What is the impact of the βarith and βquad parameters in AdaRad-M? How were these values chosen?
*   Can you provide a visualization of the network size evolution during training?
*   Regarding the "f-equivalence" relation, what is the practical impact of this approximation on the optimization process? Is there a theoretical bound on the error introduced by this approximation?
*   Why are the results from the convex dataset so poor? Is it a characteristic of the approach, or a particular tuning issue?



In summary, while the idea of nonparametric neural networks is intriguing, the paper lacks the rigor, clarity, and thoroughness needed for acceptance. The numerous weaknesses outlined above necessitate significant revisions before this work could be considered suitable for publication in a competitive venue like ICLR.
