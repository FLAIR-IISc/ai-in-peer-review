This paper introduces nonparametric neural networks, a framework for dynamically adjusting network size during training, aiming to circumvent the expensive process of black-box hyperparameter optimization. The core idea is to allow the network to grow and shrink via the addition and removal of units, guided by a fan-in regularizer and a novel training algorithm (AdaRad) with a capped batch normalization layer (CapNorm). The authors provide theoretical justification for their approach and present experimental results on several datasets, demonstrating competitive performance compared to traditional methods.

**Reject.** While the idea of dynamically adjusting network size is intriguing, the paper lacks sufficient evidence to demonstrate a clear advantage over well-established methods, and relies heavily on unproven components and insufficiently validated claims. The theoretical justification, while present, feels disconnected from the practical implementation, and the experimental results, while promising in some cases, are not robust enough to warrant acceptance.

The primary concern lies with the novelty and validation of the proposed methods. AdaRad and CapNorm, central to the framework’s success, are presented with limited comparative analysis against existing optimizers and normalization techniques. The justification for their specific design choices feels intuitive rather than empirically driven. The claim that nonparametric networks can achieve higher accuracy than networks trained with black-box optimization is not consistently supported across all datasets – the convex dataset shows significantly poorer performance, and the improvement on MNIST, while present, isn't overwhelming. The scaling experiments on the large poker dataset are encouraging, but the presentation of results feels cursory, and a deeper analysis of the computational cost and memory footprint compared to standard techniques would be beneficial.

Furthermore, the heavy reliance on specific hyperparameters (e.g., the radial step size) and the sensitivity of the method to those parameters is not adequately addressed. The lack of a thorough ablation study to dissect the contribution of each component (AdaRad, CapNorm, the fan-in regularizer) weakens the argument that the observed improvements are genuinely attributable to the nonparametric network framework as a whole. The analysis of the training process (Section 4.2) is detailed, but focuses primarily on *how* the network evolves rather than *why* it achieves its performance. A more critical evaluation of the learned network structures – Are the dynamically added units genuinely contributing to feature extraction, or are they effectively being pruned shortly after addition? – would enhance the paper.

The theoretical section (Section 7.1 and related propositions) feels somewhat detached from the practical implementation. While the theorem establishing a global minimum under certain conditions is a valuable contribution, its relevance to the observed performance in real-world scenarios isn't clearly articulated. The connection between the theoretical guarantees and the practical training process, especially the impact of the hyperparameters and the choice of nonlinearities, requires further elaboration.

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **Comparative Analysis:** Conduct a more comprehensive comparison of AdaRad against standard optimizers (SGD, Adam, RMSprop) *with* appropriate hyperparameter tuning for each. Similarly, compare CapNorm against standard batch normalization, ideally demonstrating its necessity within the nonparametric framework.
*   **Ablation Study:** Systematically evaluate the impact of each component of the framework – AdaRad, CapNorm, the fan-in regularizer, and the dynamic unit addition/removal mechanism – through ablation experiments.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity Analysis:** Investigate the sensitivity of the performance to the key hyperparameters (αr, αφ, λ, βarith, βquad) and provide guidelines for their selection.
*   **Network Structure Analysis:** Analyze the structure of the learned networks – the distribution of layer sizes, the sparsity patterns of the weights, and the roles of the dynamically added units.
*   **Theoretical Connection:** Provide a clearer explanation of how the theoretical guarantees translate into practical benefits in the context of the experimental results.
*   **Scaling Analysis:** A more in-depth analysis of memory usage and training time for the poker dataset versus well-established techniques.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   What is the computational overhead of AdaRad compared to standard optimization algorithms, particularly in terms of memory requirements?
*   How sensitive is the performance to the choice of the initial network size and the unit addition rate (ν)?
*   Can you provide examples of learned network structures that illustrate how the dynamic adjustment mechanism leads to more efficient or accurate models?
*   What is the justification for using the fan-in regularizer specifically, as opposed to the fan-out regularizer? Does the performance vary significantly with the choice of regularizer?
*   Could you elaborate on the practical implications of the theorem presented in Section 7.1? Specifically, how does it inform the selection of hyperparameters or the design of the training process?
