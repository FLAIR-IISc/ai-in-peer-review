This paper introduces a framework called Nonparametric Neural Networks (NNNs) that dynamically adjusts network size during training, aiming to circumvent the computational cost of traditional black-box hyperparameter optimization methods. The core idea is compelling: rather than training multiple networks with fixed architectures, NNNs grow and shrink during a single training run, potentially achieving better accuracy with fewer resources. The authors also present theoretical justification for their approach, alongside novel components like the CapNorm normalization layer and the AdaRad training algorithm.

**Decision: Reject**

While the ideas presented are interesting and the theoretical results are noteworthy, the experimental validation lacks sufficient convincing evidence to justify acceptance at ICLR. Specifically, the performance gains are not consistently demonstrated across datasets, and the presentation of results feels somewhat selective. The theoretical contribution, while solid, feels less impactful without a more substantial empirical demonstration of the practical benefits of NNNs.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Inconsistent Performance:** The results in Section 4 show significant variance in performance across different datasets. While NNNs outperform parametric networks on MNIST and rectangles, they *underperform* on the convex dataset. This raises concerns about the generalizability of the approach. The authors acknowledge this but don’t offer a clear explanation for why NNNs struggle on convex data. A more thorough investigation of the conditions under which NNNs excel (or fail) is needed.

2. **Limited Scalability Demonstration:** The poker dataset experiment, intended to demonstrate scalability, feels somewhat tacked on and lacks detailed comparison to other scalable methods. The claim that training time increases less dramatically with NNNs needs further justification; a more detailed timing analysis and comparison with standard training protocols would be beneficial.  The fact that the results are presented with a limited number of comparisons to existing approaches weakens the claim of demonstrating scalability.

3. **Presentation of Results:** The chosen representative networks and the justification for selecting them aren't entirely clear.  The description of the grid search and random search processes in Section 4.1 feels overly complex and could be streamlined for better clarity.  More detailed reporting of the full range of results, including standard deviations, would enhance the transparency and reproducibility of the experiments. The extensive reliance on manual selection of representative networks feels subjective.

4. **Novelty of Components:** While CapNorm and AdaRad are presented as novel contributions, their advantages over existing techniques are not fully substantiated. The justification for CapNorm, specifically regarding negating the regularizer, feels somewhat hand-wavy. A more rigorous comparison against standard batch normalization techniques, possibly with ablation studies, would be valuable. AdaRad’s performance advantage isn't clearly demonstrated.




**Additional Feedback (For Improvement):**

* **Theoretical Extension:** Explore the conditions under which the theoretical guarantees of Theorem 1 hold. Are there specific dataset characteristics or network architectures that favor the convergence properties described in the theorem?
* **Regularization Sensitivity:** Conduct a sensitivity analysis of the regularization parameter (λ). How does the performance of NNNs vary with different values of λ, and how does this compare to parametric networks?
* **Ablation Studies:** Perform ablation studies to assess the contribution of CapNorm and AdaRad to the overall performance of NNNs.  What happens if you use standard batch normalization instead of CapNorm? Does AdaRad provide a significant advantage over other optimization algorithms?
* **Hyperparameter Optimization:** Explore more sophisticated hyperparameter optimization techniques (e.g., Bayesian optimization) to tune the learning rate and other hyperparameters of AdaRad.
* **Comparison to Neural Architecture Search:** Position NNNs more explicitly within the broader context of Neural Architecture Search (NAS).  What are the key advantages and disadvantages of NNNs compared to NAS methods like reinforcement learning or evolutionary algorithms?
* **Visualization:** Include visualizations of the network structure during training. How does the network grow and shrink over time? Are there patterns in the addition and removal of units?
* **Clarify the Scaling Advantage:** Provide a detailed computational cost breakdown. How does the training time of NNNs scale with the size of the dataset and the complexity of the network?


**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Why do NNNs underperform on the convex dataset? What characteristics of this dataset make it particularly challenging for the proposed framework?
2.  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of training NNNs, including a comparison to standard training protocols and other NAS methods?  Specifically, what is the overhead of the dynamic network resizing?
3.  How sensitive is the performance of NNNs to the initial value of the angular step size (αφ)?  Did you explore different initialization strategies?
4.  What is the rationale behind choosing the specific mixing rates (βarith and βquad) used in AdaRad-M?  Were these values tuned, and if so, how?
5.   The selection of representative networks seems subjective. Can you provide a more objective metric for this selection, or demonstrate that the conclusions are robust to different choices of representatives?
