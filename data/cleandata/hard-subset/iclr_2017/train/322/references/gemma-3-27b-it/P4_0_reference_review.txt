This paper introduces a framework called Nonparametric Neural Networks (NNs) designed to dynamically adjust network size during training, aiming to automate model selection and potentially achieve higher accuracy than fixed-size networks. The core idea is to allow the network to grow and shrink by adding or removing units with zero fan-in/fan-out, guided by a regularization term that prevents unbounded growth. The authors also propose a novel normalization layer (CapNorm) and a training algorithm (AdaRad) tailored for this non-parametric setting.

**Reject.** The paper presents an interesting idea, but the experimental validation is insufficient to demonstrate a clear benefit over standard techniques, and critical details regarding the training procedure and hyperparameter tuning are lacking, hindering reproducibility and thorough evaluation. While promising results are shown on MNIST and rectangles, the poor performance on the convex dataset, and the lack of robustness shown across the tested datasets raises concerns. The theoretical contributions, while present, feel secondary to a well-validated practical methodology.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Insufficient Empirical Evidence:** The most significant weakness is the limited and somewhat inconsistent experimental results. While the authors show improvements on MNIST and rectangles, the failure on the convex dataset is concerning. The reasons for this failure are not thoroughly investigated. The claims of achieving higher accuracy than black-box methods rely heavily on comparing against randomly searched parametric networks, which may not represent the most optimized configurations. The hyperparameter search space for these parametric networks is not fully described and it’s not clear if the comparisons are entirely fair. Furthermore, the improvement on rectangles is heavily dependent on a small λ value, suggesting sensitivity to hyperparameter settings. The scaling experiments on the large poker dataset, while interesting, lack comparison to thoroughly optimized parametric networks and don't demonstrate substantial advantages.

2. **Lack of Reproducibility and Detail:** The experimental setup lacks crucial details that would allow for reproducibility. The specific initialization schemes for weights, the precise optimization procedure (beyond AdaRad), the batch size, and learning rate schedules are not fully specified. The manual selection of a representative network from the 10 trained with different random seeds introduces subjectivity. The authors mention step size annealing, but details are scarce.  The explanation of how the angular and radial step sizes are chosen isn’t precise enough.  The interplay between unit addition/removal frequency (νfreq), unit addition rate (ν), and αr is not fully clarified.

**Additional Feedback:**

* **Ablation Studies:** The paper would benefit greatly from ablation studies. For example, what is the impact of CapNorm compared to standard batch normalization? How does AdaRad compare to other optimizers (e.g., Adam) when used with parametric networks of similar size?
* **Theoretical Justification:** While Theorem 1 is a useful result, its practical implications are not fully explored. What is the significance of the finite dimensionality guarantee in the context of training?
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A more thorough analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity is needed. How does the performance vary with different regularization strengths (λ), learning rates, and step sizes?
* **Computational Cost:** While the authors mention computational costs, a more detailed analysis comparing the training time of Nonparametric NNs to that of parametric networks (including the hyperparameter search) would be valuable.
* **Connection to Existing Work:** Expand the related work section to include more recent advancements in dynamic network architectures, such as those based on reinforcement learning or evolutionary algorithms.
* **Code Release:**  Release of code is *essential* for a paper proposing a new training framework.
* **Clarification of terminology:** The term "proper dimensionality" is used without complete explanation, and could be more clearly defined.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The performance on the convex dataset is significantly worse than parametric networks. Can you provide a more in-depth analysis of why Nonparametric NNs struggle on this dataset? Was the training procedure adjusted at all for this dataset?
2.  What specific criteria were used to select the “typical representative” network from the 10 trained with different random seeds? Provide more details about the distribution of performance across these runs.
3.  Could you provide a more detailed description of the hyperparameter search space and procedure for the parametric networks used for comparison?
4.  What is the typical range of values for the angular and radial step sizes used during training, and how are these values determined?
5.  How does the training time of Nonparametric NNs compare to that of parametric networks of similar size and performance, including the time spent on hyperparameter optimization?
6.  Can you provide a pseudocode implementation of the AdaRad algorithm, clearly outlining all the steps involved?
