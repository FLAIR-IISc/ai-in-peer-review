This paper introduces nonparametric neural networks, a framework for dynamically adjusting network size during training to avoid the costly hyperparameter search typically required for model selection. The core idea is to allow the network to grow and shrink by adding and removing units based on a regularizer, ultimately aiming for a network that achieves high accuracy with an appropriate size. The authors also introduce a novel normalization layer (CapNorm) and training algorithm (AdaRad) tailored for this nonparametric setting.

**Reject.** While the idea of dynamically adjusting network size is appealing, the paper is riddled with minor flaws and inconsistencies, and the empirical validation, while present, feels incomplete and occasionally contradictory. The sheer number of these issues overshadows the novelty of the approach.

Here's a breakdown of my concerns:

1.  **Lack of Rigor in Experimental Setup & Reporting:** The experimental section feels somewhat haphazard. The choice of hyperparameters seems largely arbitrary, lacking a clear explanation for the specific values chosen. The ablation studies are minimal. For instance, the claim that AdaRad-M performed similarly to AdaRad is stated without any quantitative comparison. More concerning is the inconsistent reporting – the discussion of performance on the poker dataset states the network “made no mistake on the test set”, which is a very strong claim and requires significantly more rigorous presentation. The comparisons to other methods, while attempted, are often unclear – were the parametric networks optimized *specifically* for the size of the best nonparametric network? This crucial detail is missing.

2.  **Theoretical Claims & Proofs:** The theoretical grounding, while present, feels underdeveloped. The proof of Theorem 1 relies on several lemmas, some of which introduce additional complexities (e.g., the signature S) without fully explaining their necessity or impact. The assumptions about the nonlinearities, while reasonable, are stated rather abstractly, and it's unclear how readily they apply to common activation functions in practice. The proof itself is very dense and difficult to follow, lacking sufficient intermediate explanations.

3.  **Notational Inconsistency & Clarity:** The paper suffers from numerous notational inconsistencies and a lack of clarity. For example, the use of superscripts and subscripts is often confusing and inconsistent. The explanation of AdaRad and CapNorm is dense and could benefit from a more step-by-step, intuitive explanation. Terms like "f-equivalence" are introduced but not consistently used or reinforced throughout the paper.

4.  **Algorithm Details:** Algorithm 1 (AdaRad) is presented in a highly optimized (and therefore less readable) manner. A simpler, more pedagogical presentation would significantly improve understanding. The rationales for specific choices within the algorithm (e.g., the initialization of new units) are not always clear. The mention of computational costs feels tacked on, with insufficient justification for the stated claims about scalability.

5.  **Minor Errors & Typos:** The paper contains numerous minor errors, typos, and grammatical inconsistencies. While individually minor, they accumulate to detract from the overall professionalism of the work.



**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Focus on a Stronger Empirical Validation:** Instead of attempting to evaluate the framework on multiple datasets with limited depth, it would be more impactful to focus on one or two datasets and conduct a thorough empirical investigation, including ablation studies and comparisons to strong baselines.
*   **Simplify the Theoretical Presentation:** The theoretical sections could benefit from a more intuitive explanation of the key concepts and a more streamlined proof. Consider providing a high-level overview of the proof before diving into the technical details.
*   **Improve Clarity and Consistency:** Thoroughly review the paper for notational inconsistencies, ambiguous language, and grammatical errors.
*   **Provide More Justification for Design Choices:** Explain the rationale behind the specific choices made in the algorithm (e.g., the choice of radial and angular step sizes, the initialization of new units) and the experimental setup (e.g., the choice of hyperparameters).
*   **Address the Scalability Claims:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational costs of AdaRad, including a comparison to other optimization algorithms.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of how the hyperparameters were chosen for each dataset, and justify why these values are considered reasonable?
2.  Can you provide a quantitative comparison of the performance of AdaRad and AdaRad-M, including statistical significance testing?
3.  For the poker dataset, what measures were taken to ensure the claim of "no mistakes" on the test set is truly valid (e.g., multiple runs with different random seeds, confidence intervals)?
4.  Can you clarify the conditions under which the simplification in Proposition 1 holds, specifically regarding the choice of regularization parameters?
5.  What steps were taken to ensure the stability of the training process, and how sensitive is the performance to the choice of the regularization parameter λ?
