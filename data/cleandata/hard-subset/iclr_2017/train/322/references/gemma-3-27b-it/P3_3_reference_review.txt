## ICLR 2024 Review: Nonparametric Neural Networks

**1. Summary of Claims**

This paper introduces a novel framework called Nonparametric Neural Networks (NNNs) that dynamically adjusts network size during training. The core idea is to bypass the traditional, often costly, hyperparameter search for optimal network architecture by allowing the network to grow and shrink based on learned parameters and a regularization term. The authors theoretically ground this approach, demonstrating a global minimum training error under certain conditions, and propose specific training techniques (CapNorm and AdaRad) to enable effective training of these dynamically sized networks.  They demonstrate the method's efficacy on several datasets, showing competitive and, in some cases, superior performance compared to traditional parametric networks.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Reject.** While the idea of dynamically adjusting network size is intriguing, and the theoretical work is a positive contribution, the experimental results are inconsistent and the paper lacks a convincing demonstration of substantial advantages over well-established parametric network training and hyperparameter optimization methods. Specifically, the performance gains are dataset-dependent, and the improvement on the benchmark datasets doesn’t clearly justify the added complexity of the proposed framework.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

*   **Inconsistent Results:** The performance on the convex dataset is notably poor, undermining the broader claims.  The authors acknowledge this but don’t provide a strong explanation for *why* NNNs struggle in this scenario.  The superior performance on the MNIST and rectangles datasets is encouraging, but the gap isn't substantial enough to outweigh the added complexity.
*   **Lack of Robustness Comparison:** The paper compares NNNs to parametric networks with both grid search and random search hyperparameter optimization. However, the comparison isn't comprehensive. Modern hyperparameter optimization methods (e.g., Bayesian optimization, Population Based Training) are significantly more effective than simple grid or random search and would be a fairer benchmark.  Without this comparison, it's difficult to assess the true advantage of NNNs.
*   **Computational Cost:** The authors acknowledge the computational cost of AdaRad but don't provide a detailed comparison to the cost of hyperparameter optimization with more advanced methods (like those mentioned above). If NNNs require substantially more compute to achieve comparable or slightly better performance, this is a significant drawback.
*   **Experimental Setup Concerns:** The initialization procedure, specifically the random initialization of new units, is not discussed in detail. The impact of this initialization on the learning process and the final performance is unclear.
*   **Limited Scalability Demonstration:** The poker dataset experiment is a welcome attempt to show scalability, but the description is brief. More details about the training process and comparison with state-of-the-art methods for that dataset would be crucial.

**4. Additional Feedback (For Potential Improvement)**

*   **Thorough Ablation Studies:**  Conduct ablation studies to understand the contribution of CapNorm and AdaRad.  For instance, what happens if you use standard batch normalization or a different optimizer?
*   **Theoretical Clarification:** The theorem presented is intriguing, but the conditions required (e.g., specific non-linearities, regularization terms) limit its applicability.  Discuss these limitations more explicitly.
*   **Explain the Convex Dataset Failure:**  Investigate *why* NNNs perform poorly on the convex dataset. Is it related to the dataset's characteristics (e.g., linearity)? This insight would be valuable.
*   **Compare to State-of-the-Art Hyperparameter Optimization:**  Include comparisons to Bayesian optimization or Population Based Training methods for a more comprehensive evaluation.
*   **Provide more detailed runtime analysis**: Include a breakdown of the time required for training with NNNs compared to parametric networks with different hyperparameter tuning strategies.
* **Discuss the practical implications of non-stationarity**. NNNs inherently involve a non-stationary optimization problem, as the network architecture changes during training. This could lead to instability or require careful tuning of learning rates and other hyperparameters. Address these potential challenges and provide guidance for practitioners.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

*   You state that the network size selection is automatic. How does the algorithm respond when the optimal network size is very large or very small? Are there any mechanisms to prevent the network from growing indefinitely or shrinking to a trivial size?
*   Could you elaborate on the choice of initial angular and radial step sizes (αr and αφ)?  How sensitive are the results to these initial values?
*   How does the performance of NNNs change with different regularization strengths (λ)?  Is there a "sweet spot" for λ, and how does it vary across datasets?
*   Regarding the poker dataset, what were the computational resources (GPUs, training time) used, and how did they compare to training a similarly performing parametric network?
*   You mention that adding units doesn't initially contribute to the quality of the output.  How do you address the potential for noisy gradients or instability during the initial stages of unit addition?



In conclusion, while the NNN framework holds some promise, the current paper does not provide sufficiently compelling evidence to warrant acceptance. A more thorough experimental evaluation, particularly with comparison to state-of-the-art hyperparameter optimization methods, and a clearer explanation of the observed inconsistencies are needed to strengthen the claims and demonstrate the practical value of this approach.
