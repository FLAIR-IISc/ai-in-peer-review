This paper introduces a framework called Nonparametric Neural Networks (NNs) for automatically selecting network size during training. The core idea is to dynamically shrink and expand the network, altering its size based on training progress, without requiring a separate model selection phase. The authors provide a theoretical justification for their approach, demonstrating that a global training error minimum can be achieved at a finite dimensionality under certain conditions. They also present novel components, CapNorm (a modified batch normalization) and AdaRad (an adaptive gradient descent algorithm), designed to facilitate training of these NNs.

**Reject.** While the idea of dynamically adjusting network size is intriguing, the paper lacks sufficient empirical evidence to support its claims of superior performance and practicality. Specifically, the results presented are somewhat mixed, showing improvements on some datasets (MNIST, rectangles) but performing poorly on others (convex), and the gains are not substantial enough to justify the increased complexity of the proposed method. The experimental setup, particularly the unit addition rate and initial network size, appears somewhat arbitrary, and a more thorough ablation study would be needed to understand the impact of these choices. The complexity of the AdaRad algorithm, while theoretically motivated, isn’t clearly demonstrated to be critical for the observed performance.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Inconsistent Performance:** The most significant weakness lies in the inconsistent results across datasets. The convex dataset specifically demonstrates substantially worse performance for the nonparametric approach compared to parametric networks. This suggests that the method is highly sensitive to the dataset characteristics and might not generalize well. The claim that nonparametric networks achieve higher accuracy "in some cases" feels weak given the substantial effort presented.

2. **Limited Practical Advantage:** Even on the datasets where nonparametric networks perform comparably to or slightly better than optimized parametric networks, the improvements aren’t dramatic. The authors acknowledge that training can take longer, and the added complexity of the algorithm (CapNorm, AdaRad) doesn’t seem to deliver a significant enough return in performance to justify its adoption. The benefit of avoiding a separate hyperparameter search is undermined if the training process itself is significantly more computationally expensive.

3. **Lack of Rigorous Comparison:** The hyperparameter optimization for the parametric networks is described but lacks complete detail. It’s not entirely clear how exhaustive the grid/random search was, and a fairer comparison would require a more rigorous, standardized optimization process for both types of networks. The use of different optimization algorithms (RMSprop/Adam for parametric, AdaRad for nonparametric) also introduces a potential confounding factor.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Theoretical Justification:** The theoretical result (Theorem 1) is valuable but feels somewhat detached from the practical implementation. Exploring the implications of the theorem more thoroughly, and relating them directly to the observed empirical results, would strengthen the paper.
*   **Ablation Study:** Conducting an ablation study to assess the contribution of each component (CapNorm, AdaRad) would be beneficial. For example, how does performance change when using standard batch normalization instead of CapNorm? Is AdaRad essential, or would a simpler optimizer suffice?
*   **Scalability & Computational Cost:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of training nonparametric networks compared to parametric networks, especially as network size increases.
*   **Unit Addition/Removal Schedule:** Justify the chosen unit addition/removal rates. How sensitive is performance to these parameters? What happens if you add/remove units more or less frequently?
*   **Initialization:** A discussion of the initialization scheme used for added units, and its potential impact on training dynamics, would be helpful.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Explore the sensitivity of the method to the regularization parameter λ. A more systematic investigation of its influence on network size and performance is warranted.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Why does the nonparametric approach perform so poorly on the convex dataset? What characteristics of this dataset make it particularly challenging for this method?
2.  What is the expected increase in training time when using nonparametric networks compared to optimized parametric networks, and how does this increase scale with dataset size and network complexity?
3.  How was the step size annealing schedule for AdaRad determined, and how sensitive is the performance to its parameters?
4.  Could you provide a more detailed description of the hyperparameter optimization process used for the parametric networks, including the search space, evaluation metric, and computational budget?
5.  Is there a clear criterion for choosing between using the fan-in or fan-out regularizer, or does it depend on the specific dataset/task?
