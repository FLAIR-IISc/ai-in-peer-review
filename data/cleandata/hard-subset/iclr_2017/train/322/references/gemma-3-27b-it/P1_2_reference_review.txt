## ICLR 2024 Review: Nonparametric Neural Networks

**1. Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel framework called “Nonparametric Neural Networks” (NNNs) for dynamically selecting network size during training. The core idea is to allow the network to grow and shrink, adding or removing units based on their contribution, eliminating the need for separate, costly hyperparameter searches for optimal network architecture. The authors provide theoretical justification for their approach, demonstrating that a global training error minimum can be achieved at a finite dimensionality with appropriate regularization. They also introduce novel training components: CapNorm, a modified batch normalization technique suitable for dynamically sized networks, and AdaRad, an adaptive gradient descent algorithm tailored for this setting. The framework is evaluated on three datasets (MNIST, rectangles, and convex), showcasing its ability to achieve competitive accuracy, sometimes surpassing networks selected through traditional methods, while often requiring fewer parameters.

**2. Decision:**

**Reject.** While the idea presented is interesting and addresses a relevant problem in deep learning (automatic architecture search), the experimental results are not sufficiently compelling, and the theoretical contributions, while present, lack detailed exploration and impact. The empirical improvements are dataset-dependent and often marginal, and the proof of the core theorem feels abstract without a more intuitive explanation of its practical implications.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

*   **Limited Empirical Advantage:** The performance improvements demonstrated are not consistently observed across all datasets. The most significant result (superior performance on the rectangles dataset) is noteworthy, but the performance on the convex dataset is significantly worse than parametric networks.  The MNIST results show some improvement in parameter efficiency, but the improvement in accuracy is relatively small.
*   **Theoretical Contribution Lacks Depth:** While Theorem 1 establishes a theoretical foundation for the approach, the practical implications and limitations are not thoroughly explored. The theorem states that a global minimum *exists*, but doesn't provide guidance on *how to find it* efficiently. The significance of the regularizer choice (fan-in vs. fan-out) and its impact on the optimization landscape isn't fully discussed.
*   **Complexity and Scalability:** While the authors acknowledge the additional computational cost of AdaRad, a detailed comparative analysis with other methods for automatic architecture search (e.g., NAS) is missing. It's unclear if the benefits of NNNs outweigh the increased complexity and potential computational overhead, particularly for very large datasets.
*   **Insufficient Ablation Studies:** The paper lacks thorough ablation studies to demonstrate the contribution of each component (CapNorm, AdaRad).  For example, what is the performance of AdaRad *without* CapNorm, or vice-versa?
*   **Limited Baseline Comparison:** The comparison with random search, often a strong baseline for hyperparameter optimization, is weak. A more robust comparison showing how the NNN framework compares to a thorough random search (with comparable computational budget) is needed.

**4. Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **Expand Empirical Evaluation:**  Include experiments on larger, more complex datasets (e.g., ImageNet). This would demonstrate the scalability and generalizability of the framework.
*   **Strengthen Theoretical Analysis:** Provide a more intuitive explanation of Theorem 1.  What specific properties of the regularization term guarantee the existence of a finite-dimensional optimum? Explore the implications of different regularization strengths.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct comprehensive ablation studies to assess the individual and combined contributions of CapNorm and AdaRad.
*   **Comparison with NAS:** Position the NNN framework relative to existing Neural Architecture Search (NAS) techniques.  Discuss the trade-offs in terms of computational cost, search space, and performance.
*   **Elaborate on Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Discuss the sensitivity of the framework to hyperparameter choices (e.g., learning rate, radial step size, angular step size, beta values).
*   **Consider Alternative Regularizers:**  Explore the use of other regularization techniques beyond the fan-in/fan-out regularizers.
*   **Code Release:** Releasing the code would greatly enhance the reproducibility and impact of the research.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

*   The paper mentions that the convex dataset was problematic for the NNN framework. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of *why* it failed in this case?  What characteristics of the convex dataset make it unsuitable for this approach?
*   The training process seems sensitive to the choice of αr. How do you recommend setting this parameter in practice, and how does its value affect the training dynamics and final performance?
*   What is the computational cost of AdaRad compared to standard optimization algorithms like Adam and RMSprop, especially as the network size grows? Can you provide a more detailed cost analysis?
*   Can you elaborate on the choice of self-similar nonlinearities? Are there specific reasons why they are crucial for the success of the framework, beyond the regularization aspect?
*   How does the framework handle situations where the optimal network size is very large? Does the initial low dimensionality present a significant bottleneck?
*   You mention the potential for lifelong learning. How would the NNN framework be adapted to continuously learn and grow over time, incorporating new data without catastrophic forgetting?
