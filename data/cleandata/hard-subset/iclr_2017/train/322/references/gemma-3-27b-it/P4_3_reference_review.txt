This paper introduces Nonparametric Neural Networks (NNs), a framework for dynamically adjusting network size during training to automatically select an appropriate architecture. The core idea is to allow the network to grow and shrink by adding or removing units, guided by a fan-in regularizer, and to train the network concurrently. The authors also propose a new normalization layer (CapNorm) and training algorithm (AdaRad) to address challenges posed by this dynamic structure.

**Reject.** While the concept of dynamically adjusting network size is intriguing, the paper lacks sufficient empirical evidence to support its claims of outperforming well-established methods, especially considering the complexity introduced. The results are mixed – promising on MNIST and rectangles, but poor on the convex dataset – and the ablation studies are limited. The theoretical justification, while present, feels disconnected from the practical implementation and benefits.

The paper's primary contribution is the Nonparametric NN framework itself. The authors convincingly argue that standard black-box methods are inefficient and fail to exploit the potential for improving accuracy through dynamic resizing. They formally show that a global minimum can be reached under certain regularization conditions (Theorem 1). They address the training challenges by proposing CapNorm and AdaRad. CapNorm is a sensible adaptation of batch normalization to the dynamic setting, and AdaRad attempts to balance gradient normalization with the regularization penalty. However, the empirical validation is not robust enough. 

The experiments are conducted on three datasets. The performance on MNIST and the “rectangles” dataset is encouraging, showing competitive results compared to hand-tuned parametric networks. However, the performance on the “convex” dataset is significantly worse, raising concerns about the generalizability of the approach. The authors acknowledge this and suggest it might be due to the dataset characteristics, but a more thorough investigation is needed. The experimental setup appears reasonable but lacks a comprehensive comparison to more recent AutoML techniques (e.g., those based on differentiable architecture search). The authors primarily compare against manually tuned networks, which isn't a fair benchmark given the automated nature of their method. The choice of hyperparameters seems somewhat arbitrary, and the sensitivity of the results to these choices is not adequately explored. The ablation study for AdaRad vs. standard SGD is limited and doesn't fully justify the added complexity of the proposed algorithm.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Theoretical Connection to Practice:** The theorem guaranteeing a global minimum feels somewhat detached from the practical implementation. It would be beneficial to discuss how the choice of the fan-in regularizer specifically leads to the observed benefits in practice.
*   **Dataset Choice:** The “convex” dataset result is a significant weakness. Further investigation is crucial – perhaps exploring different regularization strengths or network architectures. Including more datasets with varying characteristics would strengthen the evaluation.
*   **Comparison to AutoML:** The paper would benefit from a comparison to recent AutoML methods. How does this approach compare in terms of accuracy, efficiency, and computational cost?
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A more comprehensive hyperparameter study is needed. How sensitive are the results to the choice of learning rate, regularization strength, and step sizes?
*   **Computational Cost:** While the authors mention computational considerations, a more detailed analysis of the training time and memory requirements compared to standard methods would be valuable.
*   **Clarity on AdaRad-M:** The authors mention AdaRad-M but report no performance difference. The reasoning behind this is unclear and could be investigated further.
*   **Justification of Architectural Choices**: The choice of ReLU and the two-layer structure is somewhat arbitrary.  Is there a reason these were preferred over other common architectures?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Why does the method perform so poorly on the convex dataset? What specific characteristics of this dataset make it challenging for Nonparametric NNs?
2.  How does the computational cost (training time and memory usage) of Nonparametric NNs compare to that of training standard parametric networks and other AutoML methods, particularly on larger datasets?
3.  What is the sensitivity of the results to the choice of the angular and radial step sizes (αφ and αr)? How can these parameters be tuned effectively?
4.  Could you elaborate on the theoretical connection between the fan-in regularizer and the observed improvement in accuracy? How does this regularization specifically encourage the network to find a good size?
5.  Why did AdaRad-M not show any performance improvement over AdaRad? Was the momentum coefficient properly tuned?
