## ICLR 2024 Review: Nonparametric Neural Networks

**1. Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel framework called "Nonparametric Neural Networks" (NPNs) which dynamically adjusts network size during training, aiming to bypass the need for costly hyperparameter search for optimal network architecture. The authors propose a method to grow and shrink networks based on a fan-in regularizer, proving a theoretical guarantee of reaching a finite dimensionality. They also introduce a new normalization layer (CapNorm) and training algorithm (AdaRad) designed to handle the dynamic nature of NPNs.  The paper demonstrates promising results on several datasets, showing that NPNs can achieve comparable or superior accuracy to hand-tuned parametric networks with fewer parameters.

**2. Decision:**

**Reject**. While the ideas presented are intriguing and the theoretical grounding is solid, the empirical results, particularly the variability and dataset dependence, are not convincing enough to warrant acceptance at this time. Furthermore, the complexity of the proposed method and the limited scalability discussion raise concerns about its practical applicability.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Inconsistent Performance:** The results are highly dataset-dependent. While MNIST shows significant improvements, the performance on the convex dataset is substantially worse than parametric networks. The poker dataset result is promising but doesn’t fully justify the added complexity.  The lack of a consistent advantage across datasets weakens the overall claim.
* **Limited Scalability Analysis:** The authors acknowledge computational cost but don’t provide a detailed comparison of training time relative to traditional methods.  The poker dataset experiment is a step in the right direction, but more extensive scalability testing is needed, particularly for larger and more complex datasets. The claim that the overhead of dynamic network resizing is mitigated in larger datasets feels anecdotal without quantitative support.
* **Complexity and Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The method introduces several new components (CapNorm, AdaRad, αr, αφ, β, ν) requiring careful tuning.  The paper doesn’t adequately address the sensitivity of performance to these hyperparameters, nor does it offer practical guidance on their selection.  This complexity could hinder adoption.
* **Theoretical vs. Practical Gap:** While the theoretical proof of convergence to a finite dimensionality is valuable, it doesn't guarantee practical efficiency or optimal performance. The dependence on the fan-in regularizer and the chosen parameters could lead to suboptimal architectures in practice, despite the theoretical guarantees.
* **Lack of Comparison to Modern NAS Techniques:**  The paper frames the work against traditional hyperparameter optimization, but doesn't adequately compare to more recent Neural Architecture Search (NAS) techniques that have shown impressive results. A comparison would contextualize the value proposition of this approach.

**4. Additional Feedback (For Improvement):**

* **Robustness Analysis:** Perform a more comprehensive hyperparameter sensitivity analysis. Show how changes in λ, αr, αφ, and other parameters affect performance on all datasets. Consider presenting results using box plots to illustrate the variability across different hyperparameter settings.
* **Scalability Experiments:** Conduct detailed scalability tests on larger datasets, comparing training time and resource usage to traditional methods and NAS techniques.  Investigate the impact of network size on training time.
* **Ablation Studies:**  Perform ablation studies to evaluate the contribution of each component (CapNorm, AdaRad, dynamic resizing) to the overall performance.  How much does each component improve the results?
* **NAS Comparison:**  Include a comparison to state-of-the-art NAS algorithms, such as those based on reinforcement learning or evolutionary algorithms, on at least one of the datasets.
* **Clarify the Role of the Regularizer:**  The paper focuses heavily on the fan-in regularizer. Explore other regularization techniques and their impact on network size and performance.
* **Discuss Practical Challenges:**  Address the practical challenges of implementing and deploying NPNs, such as the need for specialized hardware or software support.
* **Improve Clarity:** Some sections, particularly those detailing the training algorithm (AdaRad) and the theoretical proofs, are dense and difficult to follow.  Consider simplifying the language and adding more intuitive explanations.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* How sensitive is the performance of NPNs to the initial random seed?  Are the results reproducible across different random seeds?
* What is the computational overhead associated with dynamically resizing the network during training compared to training a fixed-size network?
* Can you provide more insight into why NPNs perform poorly on the convex dataset? What characteristics of this dataset make it particularly challenging for this approach?
* Have you experimented with different regularization techniques besides the fan-in regularizer? If so, what were the results?
* How would you characterize the architectures learned by NPNs? Are they qualitatively different from architectures discovered by other methods?
* Given the complexity of AdaRad, are there simpler alternatives that could achieve comparable performance?



In conclusion, while the core idea of NPNs is innovative, the current presentation lacks the empirical rigor and scalability analysis needed to justify acceptance at a competitive conference like ICLR. Addressing the concerns outlined above would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its potential impact.
