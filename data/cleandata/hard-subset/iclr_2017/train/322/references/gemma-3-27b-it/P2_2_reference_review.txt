## ICLR 2024 Review: Nonparametric Neural Networks

**1. Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel framework called "Nonparametric Neural Networks" (NNNs) that dynamically adjusts network size during training, aiming to overcome the limitations of traditional, fixed-architecture neural networks and black-box hyperparameter optimization methods. The authors propose a method for shrinking and expanding the network based on a fan-in regularizer, and introduce a new normalization layer (CapNorm) and training algorithm (AdaRad) to facilitate this process. They demonstrate the effectiveness of NNNs on several datasets, including MNIST, rectangles, convex and a large poker dataset, showing competitive or superior performance compared to parametric networks with similar sizes. The theoretical soundness of the approach is also established.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a compelling and well-motivated approach to a challenging problem – automatic network architecture selection. The theoretical foundations are solid, and the empirical results, especially on the poker dataset, are promising. While the improvements on smaller datasets aren't dramatic, the demonstration of scalability and the potential for discovering good architectures without extensive search are significant.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

*   **Novelty and Motivation:** The idea of dynamically adjusting network size *during* training, rather than as a separate optimization step, is a fresh perspective. The authors clearly articulate the drawbacks of existing methods (high training cost of black-box optimization, inability to exploit architectural changes during training) and position their work as a solution.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** The theorem establishing the existence of a global minimum for NNNs with fan-in/fan-out regularization is a strong theoretical foundation. This provides confidence in the framework's stability and ability to converge.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The results are reasonably convincing.  While the gains on MNIST and rectangles-images are modest, the performance on the large poker dataset is impressive and demonstrates the potential of the approach to scale.  The ablation studies (comparing to fixed-size networks) are well-designed.
*   **Technical Contributions:** CapNorm and AdaRad are valuable contributions. The need for a modified batch normalization scheme due to the dynamic network size and the development of a gradient descent method suited for this setting are well justified.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed methods and experimental setup.

**4. Additional Feedback (Suggestions for Improvement):**

*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A more detailed analysis of the sensitivity of the approach to the hyperparameters (λ, αr, αφ, β) would be beneficial.  While the grid search provides some insight, a systematic exploration of the hyperparameter space and their impact on performance would strengthen the conclusions.
*   **Computational Cost:** While the paper mentions the theoretical benefits of AdaRad, a more comprehensive comparison of training time (wall-clock time) against comparable parametric network training runs (e.g., using Adam) would be valuable.  Providing a figure or table showing training time vs. performance would strengthen the practical appeal of the method.
*   **Discussion of Limitations:** The disappointing performance on the convex dataset should be discussed more thoroughly. What characteristics of this dataset make it unsuitable for the NNN approach?  Acknowledging limitations will enhance the credibility of the work.
*   **Visualization of Network Dynamics:** Providing visualizations of how the network size evolves during training (e.g., plots showing the number of units in each layer over time) would help readers better understand the behavior of the NNN framework.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you elaborate on the choice of the self-similar nonlinearities?  What are the potential drawbacks of using other nonlinearities?
*   In section 3.3, you mention that AdaRad normalizes at the granularity of fan-ins.  Could you explain why this is computationally more efficient than normalizing at the level of individual weights?
*   On the poker dataset, the NNNs achieved competitive performance. What specific network architectures (number of units in each layer) did the NNNs converge to?  Were there any consistent patterns in the final network architectures?
*   For the convex dataset, is there a specific range of λ values where the NNN performs reasonably? Can you relate the parameters or the data itself to the underperformance?
*   What is the complexity of adding/removing a unit in terms of computational costs?




Overall, this is a strong paper with the potential to significantly impact the field of neural network architecture design. The acceptance recommendation is based on the novelty, theoretical soundness, and promising empirical results. Addressing the suggested improvements would further enhance the paper's quality and impact.
