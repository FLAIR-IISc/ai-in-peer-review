## ICLR 2024 Review: Nonparametric Neural Networks

**1. Summary of the Paper:**

This paper introduces a novel framework called "Nonparametric Neural Networks" (NNNs) aimed at automatically selecting the size of neural networks during training, bypassing the need for manual hyperparameter tuning or expensive black-box optimization methods.  The core idea is to dynamically grow and shrink the network by adding and removing units based on a fan-in regularizer and a novel training algorithm called AdaRad.  The authors provide theoretical justification for their approach, demonstrating that a global training error minimum exists at finite dimensionality under certain conditions. They also introduce a new normalization layer, CapNorm, to address the challenges of applying batch normalization with their regularization scheme.  The paper presents experimental results on several datasets, demonstrating that NNNs can achieve comparable or superior performance to carefully tuned parametric networks, sometimes with a smaller network size.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons:**

**Reject.** While the ideas presented are interesting and the theoretical foundations are solid, the experimental results are not compelling enough to warrant acceptance. The improvements over well-tuned parametric networks are inconsistent across datasets, and the practical benefits (e.g., reduced training time or ease of use) are not convincingly demonstrated. Furthermore, the extensive experimental section feels somewhat disconnected and lacks a clear, focused analysis of the benefits and drawbacks of the proposed method in a compelling manner.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Inconsistent Performance:** The results in Section 4 show significant variation in performance depending on the dataset. While promising results are shown on MNIST and rectangles, performance on the convex dataset is significantly worse than parametric networks. This raises concerns about the robustness and generalizability of the approach. The authors acknowledge this but don't provide sufficient insight into *why* the method struggles on convex data.
* **Limited Practical Advantage:**  The authors mention that NNNs can potentially reduce the need for manual tuning, but the experimental setup still involved significant hyperparameter tuning (e.g., step sizes, regularization parameters).  The gains in accuracy, where present, don’t clearly justify the added complexity of the algorithm and the tuning effort required.
* **Complex Methodology:** The combination of dynamic network resizing, CapNorm, and AdaRad adds significant complexity compared to standard training procedures. The paper attempts to explain these components, but the details are dense and can be difficult to follow. A clearer articulation of the key advantages and trade-offs is needed.
* **Lack of Ablation Studies:**  The paper doesn't adequately perform ablation studies to dissect the contributions of CapNorm, AdaRad, and the dynamic resizing mechanism.  For example, how much does CapNorm improve performance compared to standard batch normalization? What is the impact of the specific choices made for unit addition/removal criteria?
* **Limited Scalability Discussion:** While a scalability experiment is shown in section 4.3, it feels tacked on. It doesn’t provide a clear understanding of how the method scales with extremely large datasets or model sizes, which is a critical concern for practical applications.



**4. Additional Feedback (to improve the paper):**

* **Focus on a Key Benefit:** The paper attempts to address several problems simultaneously (automatic model selection, improved accuracy, potentially faster training). Narrowing the focus to a single, compelling benefit would strengthen the paper. For example, focus on the scenario where prior information about the dataset is limited, and demonstrate that NNNs outperform other methods in this setting.
* **More Thorough Analysis:** Provide a more in-depth analysis of the training dynamics. How does the network size evolve over time? What is the relationship between network size and performance? Are there patterns in the units that are added and removed?
* **Comparative Analysis:**  Compare NNNs to more recent and sophisticated automated machine learning (AutoML) techniques (e.g., differentiable architecture search).  Demonstrate a clear advantage over these competing approaches.
* **Simplify the Presentation:**  Streamline the explanations of CapNorm and AdaRad. Provide more intuitive explanations and visual aids to help readers understand the key concepts.
* **Clarify the Motivation for Design Choices:** Explain *why* specific choices were made for the regularization parameter, step sizes, and unit addition/removal frequency. What is the intuition behind these choices?
* **Investigate the Convex Dataset Failure:** A detailed investigation of why NNNs perform poorly on the convex dataset is crucial. Is it related to the dataset’s inherent simplicity, the choice of regularization, or other factors?


**5. Questions for the Authors:**

*  The regularization parameter (lambda) seems crucial.  How sensitive is performance to the choice of lambda, and how could this be automatically tuned within the NNN framework?
*  The AdaRad algorithm introduces several hyperparameters (alpha_r, alpha_f, beta). What is a good strategy for tuning these parameters, and are there any sensitivities to their values?
*  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of NNNs compared to training standard parametric networks with black-box optimization? What is the trade-off between the dynamic resizing overhead and the potential reduction in training iterations?
*  You mention the importance of self-similar nonlinearities. What happens if you use a non-self-similar nonlinearity like tanh? Does the performance significantly degrade, and if so, why?
*  The experiment on the poker dataset feels somewhat isolated. Could you provide more context on why this dataset was chosen and how it represents a challenging scenario for NNNs?




In conclusion, while the NNN framework presents an interesting and theoretically sound approach to neural network architecture selection, the experimental evidence and clarity of presentation are insufficient to warrant acceptance at this time.  Addressing the feedback provided above could significantly strengthen the paper and make it more compelling.
