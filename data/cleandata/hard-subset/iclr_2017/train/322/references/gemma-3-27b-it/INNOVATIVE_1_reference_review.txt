This paper introduces Nonparametric Neural Networks (NPNs), a novel framework for dynamically adjusting network size during training, aiming to circumvent the computationally expensive black-box hyperparameter optimization typically used for neural network architecture search. The core idea is to allow the network to grow and shrink, guided by a fan-in regularizer, effectively learning its optimal size during the training process. The authors also present a new normalization layer (CapNorm) and a training algorithm (AdaRad) tailored for this framework, alongside theoretical justification for achieving a global training error minimum at a finite dimensionality.

**Reject.** While the idea is strikingly original and ambitious, the experimental results presented, particularly on the convex dataset, are not compelling enough to outweigh the numerous conceptual and practical complexities introduced. The paper prioritizes novelty at the expense of thorough validation and analysis, leaving crucial questions unanswered.

The primary strength of this work lies in its conceptual leap. The idea of a network "discovering" its own optimal size during training is a genuinely interesting departure from conventional methods. The theoretical grounding, specifically Theorem 1, is a valuable contribution, even if its practical implications require further investigation. The introduction of CapNorm and AdaRad demonstrates a commendable effort to address the challenges inherent in training these dynamically sized networks. The analysis of the training process in section 4.2 provides valuable insights into the dynamics of network growth and shrinkage, highlighting the interplay between regularization, nonlinearity, and gradient flow. 

However, the empirical evaluation is significantly lacking. The performance on the convex dataset is particularly concerning, where parametric networks vastly outperform the proposed NPN approach. This casts doubt on the generalizability of the method and raises questions about its suitability for problems where a well-defined, fixed-size network is crucial. While the mnist and rectangles datasets show promise, the improvements over standard methods are not substantial enough to justify the added complexity of the NPN framework. The ablation studies are limited, and the sensitivity of the results to hyperparameters like the radial and angular step sizes remains unclear. The experiments on the large-scale poker dataset are interesting but lack a comprehensive comparison to state-of-the-art methods specifically designed for large datasets. The limited experimentation with different nonlinearities and regularization strategies restricts the scope of the findings.

The writing, while generally clear, suffers from a degree of verbosity and occasional lack of precision. The justification for certain design choices (e.g., the choice of p=2 in the regularizer) feels somewhat superficial. The connections to existing work, particularly in the areas of dynamic network structures and meta-learning, could be more thoroughly explored.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Theoretical Generalization:** The theorem relies on specific conditions on the nonlinearities and error functions. Expand on the implications of relaxing these conditions and investigate the robustness of the theoretical guarantees.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Conduct a more comprehensive sensitivity analysis of the key hyperparameters, including αr, αφ, λ, and the mixing rates in AdaRad-M.
*   **Computational Cost:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of training NPNs compared to standard methods, considering both training time and memory usage.
*   **Alternative Regularizers:** Explore the use of other regularization techniques, such as group sparsity or structural sparsity, to further enhance the framework's ability to discover efficient network architectures.
*   **Connection to Meta-Learning:** Discuss the potential connections between NPNs and meta-learning approaches for neural architecture search.
*   **Explain why a ReLU activation is used:** Mention why other activations are not used in conjunction with your approach.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The performance on the convex dataset is substantially worse than that of parametric networks. What factors contribute to this discrepancy, and what modifications could be made to the NPN framework to improve its performance on this type of problem?
2.  The choice of p=2 for the regularizer is motivated by hardware considerations. How sensitive are the results to the choice of p, and could alternative values offer improved performance or stability?
3.  The authors state that adding and removing zero units is "f-equivalent". However, the impact of this process on the training dynamics and the convergence rate is not thoroughly investigated. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of these effects?
4. The use of the angular step size seems crucial to performance. Can this parameter be learned or adapted during training to make the algorithm less sensitive to the initial hyperparameter selection?
