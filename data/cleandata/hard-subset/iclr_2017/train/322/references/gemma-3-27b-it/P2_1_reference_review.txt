## ICLR 2024 Review: Nonparametric Neural Networks

**1. Summary:**

This paper introduces a framework called "Nonparametric Neural Networks" (NNNs) that dynamically adjusts the size of a neural network during training, aiming to automatically select a good network size without prior knowledge. The core idea is to allow the network to grow and shrink by adding/removing units based on a fan-in regularizer, while maintaining functionality through f-equivalence. The authors also propose a novel normalization layer (CapNorm) and training algorithm (AdaRad) tailored for this dynamic setting. They demonstrate the effectiveness of NNNs on several datasets, showing competitive or superior performance compared to traditional parametric networks, especially when prior knowledge is limited.

**2. Decision:**

**Reject.** While the ideas presented are interesting and the exploration of dynamic network size is valuable, the paper suffers from a lack of consistently convincing empirical results, coupled with a somewhat verbose and difficult-to-follow presentation. The improvements over standard baselines are not substantial enough to warrant acceptance given the complexity introduced.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

*   **Inconsistent Empirical Performance:** The results are mixed. While MNIST shows significant improvement, the performance on the convex dataset is considerably worse than parametric networks. The poker dataset shows promise, but the comparison isn't robust enough to draw strong conclusions. The lack of consistent improvement across datasets raises concerns about the generalizability of the approach.
*   **Complexity vs. Benefit:** The proposed method introduces significant complexity in terms of the training algorithm (AdaRad, CapNorm) and the theoretical justification. The performance gains don't appear to justify this added complexity in all cases. The improvement over a well-tuned parametric network is often marginal.
*   **Presentation & Clarity:** The paper is quite dense and lengthy. The motivation behind some design choices (e.g., the specific choice of the radial and angular step sizes) isn't always clear. The multiple lemmas and proofs in the appendix, while potentially valuable, make it difficult to grasp the core ideas without significant effort.
*   **Limited Ablation Studies:** The paper would benefit from more detailed ablation studies. For instance, the impact of CapNorm and AdaRad independently needs to be better understood. What is the contribution of each component?

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Simplify the Presentation:** Focus on the core contribution – the dynamic network size adjustment – and streamline the explanations. Reduce the density of mathematical notation where possible.
*   **More Robust Empirical Evaluation:** Conduct more comprehensive experiments on a wider range of datasets, including those representative of real-world applications. Perform more rigorous hyperparameter tuning for both NNNs and the baselines.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Thoroughly analyze the individual contributions of CapNorm and AdaRad. How much does each component contribute to the overall performance?
*   **Scalability analysis:** While poker is a large dataset, it would be helpful to see performance on even larger datasets to assess the scalability of the proposed approach.
*   **Connection to Pruning:** The paper briefly mentions pruning. Elaborate on the relationship between NNNs and pruning techniques. Is NNN a form of dynamic pruning? Can the two be combined?
*   **Explain the choice of hyperparameters:** Explain the heuristic behind setting certain hyperparameters such as αr= 150λ, and the justification for the chosen values for the mixing rates in AdaRad-M.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

*   You mention the convex dataset yielded poorer performance with NNNs. What specific characteristics of this dataset might make it less suitable for this approach?
*   Can you provide a more intuitive explanation for the role of CapNorm? Why is it necessary to cap the scaling factor in batch normalization?
*   What is the computational overhead of AdaRad compared to standard optimization algorithms like Adam or RMSprop, especially as network size increases?  A more detailed computational analysis would be helpful.
*   You state that AdaRad normalizes at the granularity of fan-ins, which is more efficient. Can you quantify this efficiency improvement (e.g., in terms of memory usage or computation time)?
*   The paper focuses on the fan-in regularizer. Have you explored the performance of NNNs with the fan-out regularizer? If so, what were the results?
