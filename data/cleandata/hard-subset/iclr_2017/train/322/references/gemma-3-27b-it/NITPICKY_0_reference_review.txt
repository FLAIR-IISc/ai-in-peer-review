This paper introduces nonparametric neural networks (NNs), a framework for automatically selecting network size during training. The core idea is to dynamically shrink and expand the network, guided by a fan-in regularizer, to achieve optimal performance without a separate model selection phase. The authors provide theoretical justification and a novel training algorithm (AdaRad) designed for this dynamic setting. They demonstrate the approach on several datasets, showcasing its potential to achieve competitive or superior accuracy compared to traditional parametric NNs.

**Reject.** While the idea of dynamically adjusting network size during training is interesting, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws, inconsistencies, and a lack of rigorous evaluation that collectively undermine confidence in the claims. The presentation is overly detailed and verbose, obscuring the key contributions, and the experimental validation lacks sufficient depth and comparison to established baselines.

Here’s a detailed breakdown of the concerns:

1. **Theoretical Justification:** Theorem 1’s conditions on the nonlinearities seem overly restrictive. The requirement for differentiability is not well-motivated, and the authors acknowledge that common choices like sigmoid are excluded. This limits the practical applicability of the framework. The proof itself is relegated to the appendix, making it difficult for reviewers to verify its correctness. While the theorem is stated, its practical impact and limitations are not thoroughly discussed.

2. **Training Algorithm (AdaRad):** The introduction of AdaRad, while attempting to address the challenges of non-parametric optimization, feels unnecessarily complex. The motivation for separately normalizing the radial and angular components of the gradient is not entirely clear, and the benefits are not convincingly demonstrated. The justification relies heavily on arguments about the behavior of the fan-in regularizer, but a more direct comparison to standard adaptive optimizers (like Adam) would be helpful. The description is dense and difficult to follow, with numerous variables and a complex algorithm structure.  The claim that AdaRad scales better than RMSprop requires more detailed benchmarking and analysis, especially concerning memory usage.

3. **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental results are mixed. While the MNIST results are promising, the performance on the other datasets is less compelling. The comparison to parametric networks is not consistently fair. The grid search for parametric networks is described vaguely.  The authors claim that nonparametric networks perform poorly on the convex dataset because of their tendency to converge to smaller sizes, but don't explore strategies to mitigate this (e.g., stronger regularization or a different initialization scheme). The scalability experiment on the OpenML poker dataset is interesting, but lacks detailed analysis of the computational costs.  The claim that the results are “competitive with those of an exhaustive random search” is not substantiated by a clear comparison to the best results from that search. Reporting only the "median" performance metric is also problematic; reporting means and standard deviations is crucial. 

4. **Presentation and Clarity:** The paper is extremely verbose and contains many tangential discussions that detract from the main message. The numerous subsections (e.g., 3.1, 3.2, 3.3) make it difficult to grasp the overall flow of the argument. The notation is inconsistent and often unnecessarily complex. There are also numerous minor formatting issues (e.g., inconsistent spacing, awkward phrasing). For example, defining `f` as a function and then also using it to denote an equivalence relation is confusing.

5. **CapNorm:** The justification for capped batch normalization is weak. The argument that it prevents the regularizer from being circumvented is not fully convincing.  The choice to not include learnable scale and shift parameters seems arbitrary.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Simplify the presentation:** Focus on the core ideas and remove unnecessary details.
*   **Provide a more thorough comparison to baselines:**  Include a more comprehensive comparison to standard optimization algorithms (Adam, SGD with momentum) and established model selection techniques (random search, Bayesian optimization).
*   **Conduct ablation studies:**  Investigate the contribution of each component of AdaRad (radial vs. angular normalization) and CapNorm.
*   **Analyze the regularization parameter λ:** Provide a more systematic study of the impact of λ on network size and performance.
*   **Clarify the theoretical assumptions:** Discuss the limitations of the assumptions made in Theorem 1.
*   **Address the issue of scalability:**  Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational costs of training nonparametric networks, especially for large datasets.
*   **Improve the writing quality:** Proofread the paper carefully to eliminate errors and improve clarity.



**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Can you provide a more intuitive explanation of why the radial and angular components of the gradient need to be normalized separately?
*   How sensitive are the results to the choice of the mixing rates (βarith, βquad) in AdaRad?
*   Have you explored other regularization techniques beyond the fan-in regularizer?
*   What is the computational overhead of AdaRad compared to standard optimization algorithms?
*   What specific choices were made during the grid search for the parametric networks? Can you provide more details on the range of hyperparameters explored?
*   Why did the nonparametric networks perform poorly on the convex dataset? What strategies could be used to address this issue?
*   Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the network size distribution during training? Does the network tend to converge to a specific size, or does it continue to fluctuate?
