## ICLR Review: Nonparametric Neural Networks

**1. Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel framework called "Nonparametric Neural Networks" (NNNs) for dynamically adjusting network size during training. The authors address the challenge of automatic model selection by allowing networks to grow and shrink, guided by a fan-in regularizer and a new training algorithm (AdaRad) with a specialized normalization layer (CapNorm). They demonstrate that NNNs can achieve competitive or superior performance compared to statically sized networks, potentially eliminating the need for costly hyperparameter searches. The paper provides theoretical justification for the approach and presents experimental results on benchmark datasets including the poker dataset.

**2. Decision:**

**Reject**. While the ideas presented are interesting and the theoretical grounding is commendable, the experimental results are not consistently convincing, and the presentation lacks sufficient clarity and detail to fully evaluate the method's effectiveness.  Specifically, the performance gains are dataset-dependent and the claims of superior performance are often qualified. The complexity of the method and the lack of a clear advantage across a wider range of tasks raise concerns about its practical applicability.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Inconsistent Performance:** The results in Section 4 show that NNNs significantly outperform parametric networks on MNIST and the rectangles dataset, but *underperform* on the convex dataset. This inconsistency raises concerns about the method’s robustness and generalizability. The authors acknowledge this but do not provide a clear explanation for why NNNs struggle on convex data.  The reliance on a single positive result (rectangles) feels insufficient for a novel approach.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:**  The experiments, while including a large dataset (poker), are still relatively limited in scope. The comparison with exhaustive random search is only presented for MNIST and rectangles, leaving open the question of how NNNs fare against robust optimization methods on other tasks.
* **Complexity and Clarity:**  The proposed method is complex, involving a novel training algorithm (AdaRad), a specialized normalization layer (CapNorm), and careful parameter tuning (αr, αφ, etc.). The descriptions of AdaRad and CapNorm are dense and difficult to follow without a deep understanding of optimization techniques. The rationale behind many design choices isn't always clear.  For example, the choice of initializing new units with random fan-in and zero fan-out feels somewhat arbitrary.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The results seem sensitive to the choice of hyperparameters (especially λ and αφ). The reported ranges for these parameters suggest a significant tuning effort was required, which defeats the purported benefit of automated model selection.
* **Theoretical Justification - Limited Practical Impact:** While the theorem regarding the global minimum is appreciated, its practical implications aren’t fully explored.  The conditions for the theorem (specifically the differentiability requirements) and their impact on real-world applications need further clarification.



**4. Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

* **Ablation Studies:** Conduct thorough ablation studies to isolate the contributions of AdaRad and CapNorm. How much of the performance gain comes from each component? Would a simpler optimizer or normalization technique suffice?
* **Robustness Analysis:** Investigate the method’s sensitivity to different initialization schemes, learning rates, and dataset characteristics.  Provide a more detailed analysis of *why* NNNs perform poorly on the convex dataset.
* **Computational Cost:**  Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of training NNNs compared to traditional methods. The paper mentions scalability, but specific timings and resource usage would be helpful.
* **Improved Clarity:**  Simplify the descriptions of AdaRad and CapNorm. Use more intuitive notation and provide illustrative examples. A diagram illustrating the information flow within AdaRad would be beneficial.
* **Broader Experimentation:**  Evaluate the method on a wider range of datasets, including more complex real-world problems (e.g., image classification with larger datasets like ImageNet).
* **Comparison to Existing Methods:** A more comprehensive comparison to existing automated machine learning (AutoML) techniques (e.g., Auto-Keras, TPOT) would be valuable.




**Questions for the Authors:**

* The choice of initializing new units with random fan-in and zero fan-out feels somewhat arbitrary. What other initialization strategies were considered, and why was this approach chosen?
* Could you provide a more detailed explanation of why NNNs perform poorly on the convex dataset? Is it a limitation of the fan-in regularizer, or is it related to the specific characteristics of the convex data?
* How does the computational cost of training NNNs compare to training a similarly performing parametric network using a standard optimization algorithm (e.g., Adam)?
* Could you elaborate on the practical implications of the theorem presented in Section 2? What guarantees does it provide in real-world scenarios?
* What steps have been taken to ensure the stability and reproducibility of the results, given the sensitivity to hyperparameters?



In conclusion, while the NNN framework presents an interesting idea, the lack of consistent results, the complexity of the method, and the limitations in the experimental evaluation prevent me from recommending acceptance at this time. The authors should address the concerns outlined above before resubmitting.
