This paper introduces nonparametric neural networks, a framework for dynamically adjusting network size during training to avoid the computationally expensive hyperparameter search typical of traditional neural network design. The authors argue that this approach can lead to networks that achieve comparable or superior accuracy to hand-tuned models, while potentially being smaller and more efficient. They present a theoretical justification for their approach, introduce a novel normalization layer (CapNorm) and training algorithm (AdaRad), and provide experimental results on several datasets.

**Reject.** While the core idea is interesting and the authors demonstrate a significant effort in developing the framework, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor, yet significant, flaws in presentation, implementation details, and experimental validation. The density of these issues raises concerns about the robustness and generalizability of the proposed approach, leading to a recommendation for rejection.

The primary issues stem from a lack of clarity and rigor. Several key components, like the choice of hyperparameters for AdaRad, are not thoroughly justified. The empirical results, while presented, are difficult to interpret due to inconsistencies in reporting and a lack of comprehensive ablation studies. The theoretical justification, while present, feels disconnected from the practical implementation and lacks sufficient detail to fully assess its validity. Specifically:

*   **Lack of Hyperparameter Sensitivity Analysis:** The influence of key hyperparameters like αr, αφ, βarith, and βquad is not adequately explored. Simply stating choices were made through "preliminary experiments" is insufficient. A more systematic analysis, perhaps with a limited grid search, is needed to demonstrate the robustness of the method to hyperparameter selection.
*   **Inconsistent Reporting of Results:** The description of experiments in Section 4 lacks crucial details. For instance, the initial layer dimensions used in the poker dataset experiment are not explicitly stated. It’s unclear how the scaling of αr was determined. Further, the justification for selecting certain results for presentation over others is unclear.
*   **Insufficient Ablation Studies:** The impact of CapNorm and AdaRad is not isolated. Experiments comparing NP networks with and without these components are missing, making it difficult to assess their individual contributions to the overall performance. Similarly, the comparison to other adaptive learning rate methods (beyond RMSprop and Adam) is limited.
*   **Unclear Motivation for Design Choices:** The rationale behind specific design choices, such as the choice of p=2 for the regularizer and the fan-in regularizer specifically, is not always clear. A more thorough discussion of the trade-offs involved would strengthen the paper.
*   **Theoretical Disconnect:** The theoretical results (Theorem 1) feel somewhat detached from the practical implementation. The conditions required for the theorem to hold seem stringent, and it is not explicitly shown how these conditions are met in the experiments.
*   **Formatting & Typographical Errors:** Numerous minor formatting inconsistencies and typos distract from the clarity of the presentation (e.g., inconsistent use of notation, unclear figure captions).
*   **Overly Assertive Claims:** The claim of outperforming exhaustive random search on all datasets is not fully substantiated. The methodology for the random search is not detailed enough to confirm this claim.
*   **Ambiguous Language:** Certain phrases, like "growing nets from scratch," are vague. What constitutes "scratch" in the context of initialization and hyperparameter settings?

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Clarify the Relationship to Existing Pruning Techniques:** The paper briefly mentions pruning but doesn't fully explore the connections and differences between this approach and existing methods. A more detailed comparison would be beneficial.
*   **Provide Pseudocode:** While Algorithm 1 is helpful, providing pseudocode for other key components like CapNorm and the initialization procedure would improve reproducibility.
*   **Investigate Different Network Architectures:** The experiments are limited to a specific network architecture. Evaluating the approach on different architectures (e.g., CNNs) would broaden the scope of the study.
*   **Computational Cost Analysis:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of training NP networks compared to traditional methods.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Can you provide a more detailed justification for the choice of hyperparameters, particularly αr and αφ, and demonstrate their influence through sensitivity analysis?
*   What specific criteria were used to select the representative NP network from the 10 trained with different random seeds?
*   Can you provide pseudocode for the CapNorm and initialization procedures?
*   How do the results change when using different values of p in the regularizer (e.g., p=1)?
*   Can you elaborate on the connection between the theoretical conditions for Theorem 1 and the practical implementation of the framework?
*   What is the computational overhead of AdaRad compared to standard SGD or Adam?
