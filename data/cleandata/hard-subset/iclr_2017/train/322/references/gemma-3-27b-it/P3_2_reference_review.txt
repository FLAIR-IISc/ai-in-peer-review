## ICLR 2024 Review: Nonparametric Neural Networks

**1. Summary:**

This paper introduces a framework called "Nonparametric Neural Networks" (NPNs) aimed at dynamically adapting network size during training, eliminating the need for explicit hyperparameter tuning of network architecture. The core idea is to allow the network to grow and shrink by adding/removing units based on a fan-in regularizer, while maintaining a constant output. The authors provide theoretical justification for this approach, demonstrating that a global training error minimum exists at a finite dimensionality under certain conditions. They also present a novel training algorithm, AdaRad, along with a specialized batch normalization technique, CapNorm, to facilitate training of these dynamically sized networks. Empirical evaluation on MNIST, a rectangles dataset, and a convex dataset demonstrates competitive, and in some cases superior, performance compared to statically sized parametric networks.

**2. Decision:**

**Reject**. While the ideas presented are interesting and the theoretical foundation is solid, the experimental results lack robustness and the improvements over well-tuned parametric networks are not sufficiently compelling. The performance on the convex dataset is particularly concerning. The paper feels somewhat underdeveloped and lacks a thorough ablation study to pinpoint the contributions of each component (AdaRad, CapNorm, NPN framework).

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

*   **Inconsistent Performance:** The results are mixed. While NPNs outperform parametric networks on MNIST and the rectangles dataset, they *underperform* significantly on the convex dataset. This suggests the method is highly sensitive to the dataset characteristics, and its generalizability is questionable.
*   **Limited Scalability Demonstration:**  The poker dataset experiment is a positive step toward demonstrating scalability, but the presentation of results is sparse. The statement that "networks required many more mini-batches…but the time it took those networks to converge to a stable network size…was a much smaller fraction of the overall training time" is vague and lacks quantifiable support.  How much more training time was required overall?
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The reliance on careful tuning of step sizes (αr, αφ) and regularization parameters (λ) is evident.  The process described sounds manual and potentially requires substantial expertise. The paper doesn’t adequately address how sensitive the performance is to these settings.  A more systematic hyperparameter search and reporting would be crucial.
*   **Lack of Ablation Study:**  The paper presents AdaRad and CapNorm as essential components, but lacks a clear ablation study to demonstrate their individual contributions.  How much of the performance gain is due to NPNs themselves versus these specific training techniques?  For example, can AdaRad be effectively used to train standard parametric networks?
*   **Theoretical Rigor vs. Practical Benefit:**  While the theoretical justification (Theorem 1) is commendable, the practical benefit of achieving a global minimum isn't fully demonstrated. The authors acknowledge that parametric networks can reach higher accuracy faster, and the NPNs require a longer training period and often settle for a slightly higher error.



**4. Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **Expand Dataset Evaluation:** Include a wider range of datasets, particularly those commonly used in the literature (e.g., CIFAR-10, ImageNet) to establish more generalizable conclusions.
*   **Thorough Ablation Study:**  Conduct a rigorous ablation study to quantify the impact of each component: NPN framework, AdaRad, and CapNorm.  Compare against standard training methods (SGD, Adam) with and without batch normalization.
*   **Hyperparameter Optimization Strategy:**  Develop a more automated hyperparameter optimization strategy (e.g., Bayesian optimization) to demonstrate the robustness of the approach and reduce the reliance on manual tuning.
*   **Convergence Analysis:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the convergence behavior of NPNs.  Plot the network size over time during training, and investigate the relationship between network size and validation error. The description in section 4.2 is a good start, but needs more quantitative backing.
*   **Computational Cost Analysis:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of NPNs compared to standard parametric networks, considering both training and inference time.
*   **Clarify the "Unit Addition Rate":** The meaning of  ν and νfreq is not completely clear, and the discussion of adding units "roughly every epoch" is imprecise. Provide clearer definitions and explain how these parameters affect training dynamics.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

*   **Convex Dataset:**  Why do NPNs perform so poorly on the convex dataset? Is there a specific property of this dataset that hinders the effectiveness of the proposed approach?
*   **Scaling Behaviour:** On larger datasets like poker, what is the total training time for NPNs compared to a well-tuned parametric network?  How does this scale with dataset size?
*   **Regularization Parameter Selection:** How do you recommend choosing a good starting value for the regularization parameter λ?  Is there a principled way to adapt λ during training?
*   **Generalization to Different Architectures:** Have you explored applying the NPN framework to different network architectures, such as convolutional neural networks?
*    **Implementation Details:** Will you release the code for your experiments?



In conclusion, while the core idea of NPNs is novel and potentially valuable, the current presentation lacks sufficient evidence to demonstrate its practical effectiveness and generalizability.  Addressing the concerns outlined above would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its potential for acceptance.
