Summary
The paper proposes “nonparametric neural networks” that adapt layer widths during a single training run via unit addition and removal. The core theoretical contribution is a proof that, with fan-in or fan-out group-ℓp (1 ≤ p < ∞) regularization (with λ > 0), the training objective attains a global minimum at a finite network width. Practically, the paper introduces (i) a training algorithm (AdaRad) that decomposes gradients into radial (length) and angular (direction) components per fan-in and normalizes only the angular component, (ii) a modified normalization layer (CapNorm) that caps standardization to avoid negating the effect of the regularizer, and (iii) a mechanism to add zero-initialized units and prune units whose fan-in/out shrink to zero. Experiments on MNIST, rectangles-images, convex, and OpenML poker suggest that the approach can converge to reasonable sizes and sometimes outperforms parametric networks of the same size; however, on convex it underperforms. The paper includes detailed algorithms and an extensive (if dense) proof for the existence result.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- Empirical validation is limited and not on contemporary architectures/tasks; baselines are insufficient to substantiate the central claims over established methods.
- The contribution hinges on several coupled innovations (group regularizer, CapNorm, AdaRad, growth schedule), but lacks ablations and rigorous comparisons to strong, established alternatives (e.g., structured sparsity with proximal methods, pruning/growth methods, layer-wise normalization/optimizers) to isolate what drives gains.

Supporting arguments
- Scope and generality. The method is only evaluated on small MLPs and a tabular dataset; there is no demonstration on convolutional or modern benchmarks where model selection is truly challenging and where the risks of width mis-specification matter. The paper itself notes future work for conv/depth adaptation.
- Baselines. Comparisons are to parametric networks trained with SGD family optimizers and BN, and to a random search over sizes. Missing are:
  - Structured sparsity baselines that are closest in spirit (group lasso on fan-ins/outs with standard proximal gradient; gradual magnitude pruning; dynamic sparsification/growth).
  - Practical NAS/growth methods and network morphism approaches the paper cites (Wei et al., Chen et al., Wen et al.) on the same search space.
  - Optimizer/normalization baselines with similar normalization granularity (e.g., layer-wise/lars-like normalization, AdamW/decoupled weight decay), and weight norm/scale-invariant training that may address the “new units learn slowly” issue without bespoke AdaRad.
- Ablations. The approach combines multiple novel components:
  - Fan-in group-ℓ2 regularization
  - CapNorm
  - AdaRad (and AdaRad-M)
  - Unit addition schedule (ν, νfreq), λ, and αr/αφ coupling
  There is no systematic ablation to disentangle their effects. For instance, how far can one get with standard proximal gradient for group-ℓ2 plus a simple growth/pruning policy and RMSprop/AdamW? Is CapNorm necessary vs. e.g., frozen BN statistics or LayerNorm/WeightNorm? How much does AdaRad contribute vs. per-fan-in gradient clipping/normalization?
- Fairness and tuning budget. The training protocols differ across methods (e.g., CapNorm for NP vs BN for P; λ ≠ 0 vs λ = 0; different step-size schedules, rewinding heuristics, and multiple annealing cycles). It is unclear whether parametric baselines received comparable tuning/budget, especially considering early stopping and the admitted advantage of larger step sizes for parametric training. The manual selection of “typical representatives” for NP runs introduces selection bias.
- Mixed results and robustness. The method underperforms on convex with large variance across seeds; even where NP wins (MNIST, rectangles-images), margins are modest and could plausibly be explained by training/regularization differences. The poker result is encouraging but tabular datasets often favor simple MLPs; there is no runtime/efficiency accounting relative to baselines (e.g., wall-clock to a given validation accuracy).
- Theory vs practice. The existence theorem is technically interesting but quite weak in practical implications: it guarantees a finite optimum for a fixed λ with group regularization, not that the proposed stochastic growth/prune dynamics will find it, nor that the discovered widths are near-optimal for generalization. Assumptions and the heavy technical development overshadow the empirical part, and the theorem does not involve CapNorm/AdaRad, the most practically impactful elements.

Feedback to improve the paper
- Strengthen baselines and scope:
  - Include structured sparsity baselines (group lasso with proximal updates; gradual magnitude pruning) and regrowth methods (Wei et al., Guo et al. “dynamic surgery”) under identical training budgets.
  - Evaluate on at least one convolutional setup and a modern benchmark to demonstrate applicability beyond small MLPs.
  - Compare to simple, strong alternatives for “new unit learning”: WeightNorm/LayerNorm, LARS/LAMB, AdamW with appropriate scaling, showing whether AdaRad is necessary.
- Provide ablations:
  - Isolate CapNorm, AdaRad, and λ/αr/αφ/ν settings. Show final accuracy and width distributions with each component on/off.
  - Replace CapNorm with BN variants (e.g., no learnable affine, frozen statistics) or LayerNorm to test the claimed incompatibility with regularization.
  - Replace AdaRad with proximal gradient for group-ℓ2 and report differences.
- Fair comparisons:
  - Match regularization across NP and P (e.g., compare to parametric with group-ℓ2 + prox; or use CapNorm-like constraints in P).
  - Control training budgets (epochs, annealing/rewinds), report wall-clock and number of forward/backward passes to a target validation error.
- Analysis:
  - Sensitivity to λ and unit addition rate ν; report convergence of widths and variance across seeds.
  - Provide learning curves and width trajectories with confidence intervals.
  - Discuss failure on convex: is it due to converging to too-small width? Can annealing λ or altering ν mitigate?
- Clarity and positioning:
  - Shorten and streamline the theory section; emphasize the practical meaning and limitations of the existence result.
  - Clarify relationships to group sparsity literature, ARD/Bayesian approaches, and modern NAS/pruning methods; position the novelty precisely.

Questions for the authors
1. How much of the gain comes from CapNorm vs. AdaRad vs. group-ℓ2 with prune/grow? Please provide a full ablation.
2. Can you match training budgets and regularization across NP and parametric baselines (e.g., group-ℓ2 + proximal on fixed-width nets) and report wall-clock/compute?
3. Why is BN fundamentally incompatible with your regularizer if one freezes affine parameters and statistics? Would LayerNorm or WeightNorm suffice without CapNorm?
4. How sensitive are results to λ, αr, αφ, and ν? Do widths converge reliably across seeds? Can annealing λ or adapting ν address the convex dataset failure?
5. Can AdaRad be replaced by a simpler per-fan-in gradient normalization or by standard optimizers (AdamW, LARS) without loss?
6. Do your theoretical guarantees extend in any form to the training dynamics (e.g., existence of a stable finite-width attractor under your add/prune process), or are they divorced from practice?
