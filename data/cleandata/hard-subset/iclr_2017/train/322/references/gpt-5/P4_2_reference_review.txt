Summary of contributions
- The paper proposes “nonparametric neural networks,” a framework that adjusts network width during a single training run by adding zero units and eliminating units whose fan-in or fan-out becomes exactly zero under a group regularizer. This aims to perform model selection (layer widths) within training rather than via external black-box search.
- It proves a core theorem: with a fan-in or fan-out group regularizer (λ > 0, 1 ≤ p < ∞), the training objective admits a global minimum at finite dimensionality, avoiding degenerate growth. A proposition shows that with self-similar nonlinearities (e.g., ReLU), per-layer regularization coefficients are essentially equivalent to a single global λ after weight rescaling.
- It introduces two training components tailored to this setting: (i) CapNorm, a BN variant that caps the standardization (no scaling if σ < 1 and no learnable affine parameters) to prevent negating the effect of the regularizer; (ii) AdaRad, which decomposes gradients into radial (parallel) and angular (orthogonal) components per fan-in, normalizes only the angular part, and combines with group shrinkage, plus a momentum variant (AdaRad-M).
- Experiments on MNIST, Rectangles-Images, Convex, and OpenML Poker show that the nonparametric approach converges to specific widths; on two datasets it is competitive or better than parametric counterparts and black-box search at similar or smaller sizes; on Convex it underperforms. Detailed analysis traces unit lifetimes and norms.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- Empirical support is limited and uneven, with methodological concerns (fairness of baselines, selection protocol, ablations, and a surprising Poker result that warrants stronger validation).
- Novelty relative to prior structured sparsity and dynamic growth/pruning is incremental; core ideas (group-lasso inducing unit removal, dynamic addition, growth-based training) exist, and the new components (CapNorm, AdaRad) lack ablation and strong comparative evidence.

Supporting arguments
1) What problem is tackled? The paper addresses automatic width selection during training, aiming to avoid costly black-box hyperparameter search. This is an important and timely question.

2) Is the approach well-motivated and well-placed in the literature?
- The use of group regularizers to induce unit-level sparsity is well-grounded (group lasso; prior structured sparsity in deep nets). The f-equivalence via adding/removing zero units is standard.
- Prior art on growing/pruning networks (Cascade-Correlation, dynamic node creation, Net2Net, pruning literature, Wei et al., Wen et al.) is cited, but empirical baselines from these families are missing. The claimed advantage of not requiring a good initial model is compelling but needs direct comparison to recent growth/pruning methods that also operate during training.

3) Do the claims hold and are they rigorously supported?
- Theorem 1’s existence result appears technically sound under the stated assumptions; the proof strategy (bounding activations/gradients, using KKT-like conditions with group norms to bound widths) is reasonable.
- CapNorm’s rationale (preventing BN from canceling regularization) is plausible, but there is no ablation isolating its effect on nonparametric training (CapNorm vs standard BN vs no BN with the same λ).
- AdaRad is clearly described, but there is no controlled comparison to RMSprop/Adam variants adapted to avoid scaling the shrinkage term (e.g., normalizing only loss gradients). Claims about cost/memory would benefit from wall-clock and throughput metrics.
- Experimental methodology raises concerns:
  - Parametric baselines use BN with λ=0, while nonparametric models rely on λ>0. This mismatch makes it hard to attribute generalization differences to the proposed framework vs. regularization choice.
  - Choice of a single “typical representative” run for the NP model injects selection subjectivity, though ranges are reported.
  - Missing ablations for unit addition rate, initialization scale, and αr/αφ sensitivity.
  - The Poker result (including zero test errors) is surprising; details about splits, preprocessing, and leakage prevention are insufficient. Given that simple models reportedly perform near chance, a perfect test score requires strong corroboration.
- Overall, the approach is promising and partly supported (two datasets), but the empirical case is not strong enough for acceptance.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not all decision-critical)
- Provide ablations:
  - CapNorm vs BN (with/without affine) vs no normalization for NP nets; quantify how BN can negate the regularizer.
  - AdaRad vs SGD/momentum vs RMSprop/Adam with a modified pipeline that normalizes only the loss gradient’s orthogonal component while keeping shrinkage unscaled; include wall-clock and memory profiling.
  - Sensitivity to λ, αr, αφ, unit addition rate/frequency, and initialization norm; plot size/accuracy trade-offs.
- Strengthen baselines:
  - Compare against recent dynamic growth/pruning and structured-sparsity methods that do not require pretrained large models.
  - For parametric baselines, include L2-regularized models with BN (weight decay can still regularize despite scale invariance) and early stopping to match regularization strength.
- Clarify Poker setup: data splits, any stratification, feature engineering, preprocessing, prevention of duplicates across splits, and evaluation protocol. Report multiple seeds and confidence intervals; check against OpenML standard splits if available.
- Report wall-clock times and compute budget comparisons against black-box searches; quantify how much training-time overhead nonparametric growth incurs vs final accuracy/size.
- Improve clarity and reproducibility: fix typos, formalize algorithmic “rotation” operations, release code, and provide full hyperparameter settings. Tighten proofs (e.g., assumptions on σ(0)=0 for f-equivalence vs those used in Theorem 1).
- Discuss limitations more explicitly (e.g., failure on Convex; when self-similar nonlinearities are not used; extension to conv layers).

Questions for the authors
1) CapNorm: Can you provide an ablation showing NP performance with standard BN (with/without affine) and with no normalization, holding λ and AdaRad fixed? How often does σ<1 cap actually activate during training?
2) AdaRad: How does it compare to RMSprop/Adam when the normalization is applied only to the loss gradient orthogonal component, leaving the shrinkage untouched? Any wall-clock/time-to-target comparisons?
3) Baselines: Why set λ=0 for parametric networks with BN? Did you try weight decay with BN and early stopping? How does that affect fairness?
4) Selection protocol: How sensitive are results to the manual “typical representative” choice? Can you report median across 10 seeds for NP models and compare parametric baselines to that median size/accuracy?
5) Poker dataset: What splits and preprocessing were used? Are there duplicate or near-duplicate examples across splits? Can you reproduce the zero-error result across seeds/splits?
6) Practicality: What is the training-time overhead relative to a fixed-width parametric model that achieves similar accuracy? Any memory constraints as width grows?
7) Generality: Have you tried convolutional layers with group regularization per channel? Any obstacles to extending AdaRad/CapNorm to convs?

Overall, the idea is interesting and the theory is solid, but broader and more rigorous empirical validation and fairer baselines are needed.
