Summary and contributions
- Proposes “nonparametric neural networks” that grow/shrink width during a single training run by adding zero-units and removing units whose fan-in (or fan-out) norms are driven to zero by a group-lasso penalty (p=2). Provides a theorem stating that, with fan-in/out regularization, the training objective attains a global minimum at finite width.
- Introduces CapNorm, a modification of batch normalization designed to prevent the penalty from being neutralized by rescaling. Specifically, subtract mean and divide by max(σ,1) with no learnable affine parameters.
- Introduces AdaRad, an optimizer that decomposes gradients into radial (parallel to fan-in) and angular (orthogonal) components; only the orthogonal component is normalized, and shrinkage is applied in the radial direction.
- Empirical evaluation on three small fully connected benchmarks (MNIST, rectangles-images, convex) and a larger tabular dataset (OpenML poker). Claims that, for some datasets, nonparametric training attains competitive or superior accuracy with fewer parameters than parametric networks trained from scratch.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) Experimental rigor and fairness are insufficient, with data leakage and numerous confounds that undermine claims; (2) Significant clarity and presentation issues (algorithmic ambiguities, inconsistent notation, numerous formatting/citation errors) that impede verification. The conceptual novelty beyond existing growth/pruning/group-lasso literature is incremental, and the theoretical result, while technically interesting, has limited practical impact without stronger empirical support.

Supporting arguments
- Test-set leakage: In Section 4.1, “typical representatives” of NP models are selected using median test error across 10 runs. This invalidates subsequent comparisons (you cannot use test performance to select models). The baseline P models’ hyperparameters are selected by validation; the NP representatives are selected using test. This alone is grounds for rejection.
- Unfair/unclear baselines: P networks use BN with λ=0, while NP uses group-lasso and CapNorm and later sets λ=0 mid-training. There is no control where P networks are trained with a comparable regularizer (e.g., group-lasso proximal updates) and capacity schedule, nor an ablation of CapNorm/AdaRad. The advantage of progressive growth (curriculum/regularization) is not disentangled from the proposed ingredients.
- Heavy manual training protocol: Frequent “rewinding” and ad hoc schedule changes (turning λ off, step-size anneals) complicate reproducibility and mix multiple interventions. Compute budgets are not controlled across methods.
- Limited and dated empirical scope: Only small FC benchmarks and one tabular dataset; no modern convnets (e.g., CIFAR-10/100) or transformers. Claims about outperforming black-box HPO are not substantiated under equal compute.
- Algorithmic ambiguities:
  - AdaRad updates: “rotate by angle αφ||φadj||2 in direction −φadj/||φadj||2” is unclear (angle depends on a vector norm? units?). Global φmax/cmax normalization ties all units/layers together without justification; no bias correction; choice of β not detailed in main text; numerical stabilizer not specified. Shrinkage uses αr λ |Dt|/|D| but G is scaled by 1/|D| over a minibatch Dt—scaling seems inconsistent.
  - CapNorm undermines the self-similarity invariance used in Proposition 1. The proposition excludes σL but CapNorm is applied within hidden layers, breaking scale-equivalence; the text does not reconcile this.
  - Zero-unit add/remove: New units have zero fan-out and random fan-in; removal depends on fan-in L2→0. This asymmetry plus CapNorm’s non-scaling for σ<1 complicates the stated mechanism; no analysis of false eliminations.
- Theory vs practice gap: Theorem 1 ensures existence of a finite-width global minimizer for the regularized training objective. However, the practical protocol changes λ to zero and uses CapNorm; the final reported models need not correspond to the minimizer asserted by the theorem. The theorem does not guide the schedule or explain when/why the grown architecture generalizes better.
- Claims and interpretations:
  - “CapNorm filters the component of the gradient parallel to the fan-ins” is asserted but not derived; the coupling of normalization to gradient geometry is nontrivial, especially with ReLU nondifferentiability.
  - On poker, reporting zero test errors on 100k examples is surprising given literature; needs stronger auditing (data leakage splits? preprocessing?). Also, prior OpenML results may be incomparable without standardized splits.
- Presentation/formatting issues: Numerous typos and broken references (e.g., “bot not fastest,” “durgery,” inconsistent dataset footnotes embedded in text, malformed citations with “\Q2015\E,” repeated/incorrect titles, unconventional number formatting “1.025.010”). Section numbering glitches (.2, .3), figure/table references not included, and algorithm pseudocode uses undefined variables or unclear operations. These collectively signal low polish and hinder verification.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Eliminate test-set usage for any model/hyperparameter selection. Redo Section 4.1 with proper validation-only selection, report means/medians and confidence intervals across fixed seeds, and control compute budgets.
- Provide clean ablations: (a) with/without CapNorm, (b) with/without AdaRad (e.g., compare to RMSprop/Adam with appropriate shrinkage handling), (c) with/without unit growth (fixed-width group-lasso with proximal operator), (d) vary p=1 vs p=2. Report sensitivity to ν, νfreq, αr, αφ, and λ schedules.
- Clarify and correct AdaRad math; specify all hyperparameters and stabilizers; justify normalization across units/layers; add bias correction if needed. Provide convergence diagnostics and cost/memory comparisons with wall-clock benchmarks.
- Reconcile Proposition 1 with CapNorm; either restrict the claim to architectures without CapNorm or provide a rigorous statement of when equivalence survives.
- Strengthen baselines: include group-lasso training with proximal gradient on fixed-width nets; dynamic growth/pruning baselines (e.g., Net2Net, NeST, structured sparsity Wen et al.) under equal compute; curriculum growth without penalty; and black-box HPO comparisons under the same training budget.
- Extend experiments to modern benchmarks (CIFAR with conv layers), release code, and include architectural choices for convolutional settings.
- Fix all citation and formatting errors.

Questions for the authors
1. Why was test error used to pick “typical” NP representatives? Will you rerun all selection via validation only and update results?
2. How exactly is the “rotation by angle αφ||φadj||2” implemented? Please provide a precise vector update formula and units. Why is the angle proportional to a gradient norm?
3. How do CapNorm and Proposition 1 coexist? Since CapNorm breaks scale-invariance for σ<1, does the per-layer λ equivalence still hold?
4. Can you provide an ablation showing the effect of (i) CapNorm vs standard BN without affine parameters, (ii) turning λ off late in training, and (iii) growth without shrinkage?
5. On poker, can you confirm dataset splits, preprocessing, and leakage prevention? How many epochs/updates; what is the exact compute used compared to baselines?
6. How sensitive is the method to the unit addition rate and the choice αr=150/λ? What happens if the gradient magnitude differs substantially (e.g., other datasets)?
7. Could you compare to a proximal group-lasso baseline (fixed-width) and to dynamic pruning methods under identical training schedules?

Overall, the direction is interesting, but the current empirical methodology and presentation issues prevent confident acceptance.
