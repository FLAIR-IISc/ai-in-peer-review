Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes “nonparametric neural networks,” an approach to learn network width during a single training run by dynamically adding zero-units and removing units whose entire fan-in or fan-out shrinks to zero under a group-regularization penalty. The main theoretical result (Theorem 1) shows that, with fan-in or fan-out group ℓp regularization (λ>0, 1≤p<∞), the joint optimization over weights and widths achieves a global training objective minimum at finite dimensionality, addressing the pathological possibility of unbounded growth.
- The paper introduces two algorithmic components to make the framework practical: (i) CapNorm, a variant of batch normalization that prevents rescaling from nullifying the effect of the group regularizer; and (ii) AdaRad, a “radial–angular” optimizer that normalizes only the component of the gradient orthogonal to each unit’s fan-in while leaving the radial component (and the associated shrinkage) unnormalized, to allow new units to learn.
- Empirically, the method is evaluated on MNIST, rectangles-images, convex, and a large OpenML poker dataset. The authors report that nonparametric networks can converge to a “good” size; outperform same-sized parametric networks on some datasets; and be competitive with broader parametric searches. The paper includes informative analyses of training dynamics (e.g., unit survival, fan-in/out trajectories).

Decision: Reject (borderline)
- Key reasons: (1) The empirical evidence is limited, somewhat inconsistent across datasets, and lacks crucial baselines and ablations to support the central claims; (2) The practical gains (e.g., “one training run instead of model search”) are not convincingly demonstrated in terms of compute/time-to-accuracy.

Supporting arguments
- Limited and inconsistent empirical validation. Results vary across datasets: gains on MNIST and rectangles-images, a clear underperformance on convex, and surprising poker results (including 0% test error on 100k examples). The latter warrants stronger scrutiny to rule out leakage or label determinism; without such controls, it weakens confidence in the evaluation.
- Baseline selection and fairness. Comparisons are against parametric MLPs with BN/RMSprop/Adam, but important baselines are missing:
  - Group-lasso training on fixed-width networks (fan-in group ℓ2) with post-hoc unit pruning—this is the closest parametric analogue to your regularization and removal mechanism.
  - Modern grow/prune methods and NAS-style baselines (Wei et al., 2016; other contemporary adaptive-width/structured-sparsity methods) on the same tasks.
  - A reasonably tuned BN alternative with weight decay (or decoupled weight decay, or L2 on pre-BN weights), given that “BN is incompatible with L2” is an overstatement in light of common practice.
- Ablations. The paper bundles several ingredients (dynamic add/remove, CapNorm, AdaRad). There is no ablation to isolate what’s essential. For instance:
  - Nonparametric with standard optimizers (Adam/RMSprop) and CapNorm
  - Nonparametric with BN plus decoupled regularization strategies
  - Parametric networks with CapNorm
  - AdaRad on standard parametric networks
  Absent these, it is hard to attribute the observed performance to the proposed components rather than training heuristics or normalization specifics.
- Selection procedure risks bias. The procedure of selecting a “typical representative” run for nonparametric models by approximate median test error/size (chosen manually) introduces bias and complicates reproducibility. Statistical reporting (mean/median with credible intervals) over all runs, and fixing the comparison set a priori, would be preferable.
- Practical benefits not quantified. The motivation is to reduce black-box search cost. However, experiments do not show wall-clock time or GPU-hours to reach a given validation error, nor time-to-accuracy curves comparing (a) one nonparametric run vs (b) a small grid/random search. The added compute cost of AdaRad and dynamic structure (and the overhead of gradual growth) should be counterbalanced by these curves.
- Theoretical contribution is sound but limited to the training objective. Theorem 1 is technically interesting (finite-width global minimizer under group regularization), and the proof is carefully laid out. However, the result doesn’t address generalization, and the link between the theorem and the practical add/remove schedule (which uses local, stochastic optimization and batch stat layers) is indirect. That is fine, but it leaves the empirical section as the crux—hence the need for stronger evidence.

Additional feedback and suggestions (to help improve the paper)
- Strengthen baselines and ablations:
  - Include fixed-width group-lasso with pruning (and possibly grow-back) using standard optimizers as a strong baseline.
  - Compare to recent width-adaptive or structured sparsity methods (e.g., modern group-sparsity pruning, slimmable networks, dynamic neuron growth) on the same datasets.
  - Provide ablations for CapNorm vs BN (with suitable regularization schemes) and for AdaRad vs RMSprop/Adam within your nonparametric framework.
- Report compute. Include time-to-accuracy curves and total training cost for nonparametric vs parametric searches to substantiate the “single-run model selection” claim.
- Dataset choice. Consider more standard modern benchmarks (CIFAR-10/100, SVHN) and convolutional layers, or make a compelling case why fully connected MLPs suffice. The extension to conv layers is claimed as future work; at minimum, a pilot conv experiment would increase relevance.
- Reproducibility. Replace manual “representative” selection with a predefined statistical protocol and release code. Report means/medians with variance across seeds for all settings.
- Clarify CapNorm rationale and alternatives. BN with weight decay is widely used; explain precisely why it “negates” the regularizer and compare against common remedies (decoupled weight decay, regularizing γ, normalizing preactivations, or weight normalization).
- Analyze stability. Provide sensitivity analyses for λ, addition rate, αr/αφ, and the effect of p=1 vs p=2. Consider adaptive λ schedules (you mention this as future work).
- Complexity. Empirically validate the claimed memory/compute overhead of AdaRad vs RMSprop and the scalability with width.

Questions for the authors
1. Poker dataset: How do you ensure no leakage or distribution shift (e.g., hand encoding implies deterministic labels)? Can you provide stratified splits, baseline sanity checks, and replicate 0% test error across seeds/splits?
2. Why is BN considered “incompatible” with L2 here? Did you try alternatives such as decoupled weight decay or applying L2 to pre-BN weights or γ only? How do results compare to CapNorm?
3. How sensitive are results to the unit addition schedule (rate/frequency) and initialization of new units? Did more adaptive criteria (e.g., gradient norm-based additions) help?
4. Can you provide ablations isolating (a) AdaRad, (b) CapNorm, and (c) add/remove dynamics? In particular, does AdaRad alone improve parametric training?
5. The “typical representative” selection: can you replace manual selection with an objective protocol (e.g., median over seeds) and re-run comparisons accordingly?
6. What is the wall-clock time/GPU-hour comparison for achieving the reported validation errors with nonparametric training vs your best parametric random/grid searches?
7. Theorem assumptions: CapNorm uses batch statistics; during training, f-equivalence under adding/removing zero-units can alter batch statistics. Does this affect the invariance argument or the regularization effect in practice?
8. How does the method fare with convolutional layers and on CIFAR/SVHN? Any preliminary results?

Overall, the paper proposes an appealing idea with a solid theoretical backbone and interesting training heuristics. With stronger empirical validation, clearer baselines, and ablations, this could become a compelling contribution.
