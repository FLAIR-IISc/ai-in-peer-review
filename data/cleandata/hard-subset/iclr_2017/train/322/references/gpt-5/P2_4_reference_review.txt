Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes “nonparametric neural networks,” where layer widths are treated as optimization variables and are adapted during a single training run. The key device is a group regularizer applied to either fan-in or fan-out weights (group-lasso with p=2), which both encourages entire units to go exactly to zero and serves as a size control.
- A central theoretical result (Theorem 1) shows that, under mild conditions and a fan-in or fan-out regularizer with λ>0 and 1≤p<∞, the training objective attains a global minimum at finite width. A useful proposition shows that with self-similar nonlinearities (e.g., ReLU), per-layer regularization strengths are equivalent (up to rescaling), simplifying hyperparameterization.
- Practically, the method alternates gradient updates with addition/removal of zero units, using self-similar nonlinearities and a normalization layer “CapNorm” (a variant of BN that caps scaling below unit std to avoid canceling the regularizer). Training uses a new optimizer, AdaRad, which decomposes gradients per fan-in into radial (length) and angular (direction) components, normalizing only the angular component to allow new units to learn without undermining regularization.
- Experiments on MNIST, Rectangles-Images, Convex, and a large Poker dataset show: (i) the network widths stabilize; (ii) for two datasets, nonparametric models outperform parametric networks of the same size and are competitive with broader searches; (iii) on Poker (1M+ examples), the method scales and achieves strong accuracy.

Decision: Reject (borderline)
Key reasons:
1) Empirical support is mixed and limited: improvements are dataset-dependent, and the strongest baselines and ablations are missing or potentially unfair.
2) Novelty and positioning: the core mechanism is closely related to known structured sparsity/group-lasso unit selection and network growing/pruning paradigms; the contribution blends known ideas with heuristics, but evidence that it advances state-of-the-art practice is not yet convincing.

Supporting arguments
- Fairness and strength of baselines: Parametric baselines use BatchNorm (with or without learnable affine params) and no L2 regularization, while nonparametric models use CapNorm plus an explicit group-lasso penalty. Because standard BN can neutralize L2 on the weights, the comparison is not apples-to-apples. Stronger baselines should include: (i) overparameterized networks with group-lasso on fan-ins/fan-outs (no dynamic resizing), followed by pruning; (ii) modern optimizers with per-filter normalization (e.g., RMSprop/Adam with filter-wise normalization or weight normalization) and decoupled weight decay; (iii) pruning/growing baselines during training (e.g., dynamic sparse methods, modern pruning with regrowth).
- Experimental scope: Datasets are small/classical (MNIST, Rectangles, Convex) plus one large tabular dataset. No convolutional experiments or standard modern vision benchmarks (e.g., CIFAR) are shown, even though the method is motivated as a practical model selection alternative. On Convex, nonparametric models underperform by a large margin, and the paper does not fully analyze failure modes.
- Evaluation protocol: The selection of a “typical representative” nonparametric model (chosen manually among 10 seeds) and then training parametric networks of the same size introduces selection bias. Reporting of error bars and statistical tests is minimal; ablations of CapNorm and AdaRad are limited; the schedule involves rewinding, annealing, and multiple heuristics that make reproducibility and attribution of gains harder.
- Novelty and relation to prior work: The theorem that a group-lasso-regularized objective attains a finite-width minimum echoes well-established properties of group-lasso (finite active groups). While the formalization in a deep network setting is valuable, it is not surprising. The dynamic addition/removal of units is related to constructive networks and recent pruning/regrowth methods; the paper positions these but does not compare experimentally to the most relevant modern counterparts. AdaRad’s idea of decomposing per-fan-in gradients into radial/angular parts is interesting, but its advantage over strong, simpler optimizers (Adam/RMSprop) is not clearly established.
- Clarity and reproducibility: The paper is generally clear and mathematically careful (long, detailed proofs). However, the training protocol is complex (rewinding, per-phase schedules, many hyperparameters), and code is not provided. Some algorithmic choices (e.g., CapNorm details, annealing rules) would benefit from ablations and sensitivity plots.

Positive aspects
- A clear, principled formulation of variable-width training with a sound theoretical guarantee of finite optimal width.
- Practical engineering to make dynamic resizing work (self-similar activations, CapNorm, AdaRad), with insightful analysis of training dynamics and unit lifetimes.
- Demonstration of scalability to a large dataset and careful per-step accounting in the optimizer.

Suggestions to improve the paper (not all affect the acceptance decision)
- Strengthen baselines: compare to group-lasso on an overparameterized fixed-width network with pruning; dynamic sparse methods; modern pruning/regrowth; and weight normalization/decoupled weight decay.
- Add ablations: (i) with/without CapNorm; (ii) AdaRad vs Adam/RMSprop with per-filter normalization; (iii) unit addition frequency ν and αr/αφ sensitivity; (iv) fan-in vs fan-out regularization.
- Expand to convnets on CIFAR-like tasks; show results with depth selection or structured convolutional channel pruning to validate broader applicability.
- Report variance across seeds and use unbiased model selection (avoid manual representative picking).
- Provide code and a simpler default schedule to enhance reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
1) Fairness: How do results change if parametric baselines use a group-lasso penalty (compatible with BN or with a BN variant) and pruning, rather than no regularization?
2) AdaRad: Can you show head-to-head learning curves and final accuracy vs Adam/RMSprop with per-filter normalization or weight normalization, controlling for wall-clock?
3) CapNorm: Could you justify theoretically why capping at std<1 is the right choice, and provide ablations vs “BN without affine” and other BN variants (e.g., freezing statistics)?
4) Initialization rationale: New units are added with random fan-in and zero fan-out. Please clarify the gradient pathways that allow fan-out to grow from zero and then propagate learning to the fan-in; provide empirical evidence on the time-to-usefulness of added units vs alternative initializations.
5) Sensitivity: How sensitive are final width and accuracy to λ and ν? Can you provide guidance for setting λ without extensive tuning?
6) Theory: Theorem 1 guarantees a finite-width training optimum, but does not address generalization. Do you observe a systematic validation optimum near the training optimum width? Any empirical relation between active-unit count and generalization?

Overall, the paper presents an interesting and coherent approach with solid theoretical underpinnings and some promising results, but the empirical evidence and comparisons need to be substantially strengthened for ICLR.
