Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes “nonparametric neural networks,” which dynamically grow and shrink the width of fully connected layers during a single training run. The core ingredients are: (i) a fan-in (or fan-out) group-regularizer that induces unit-level sparsity, enabling removal of zero units; (ii) occasional addition of new zero-output units with random fan-in; (iii) a normalization layer, CapNorm, that caps variance normalization to avoid negating the effect of the regularizer; and (iv) a novel optimizer, AdaRad, which decomposes gradients into radial (length) and angular (direction) components per fan-in, normalizes only the angular part, and applies group shrinkage to the radial part.
- Theoretically, Theorem 1 shows that with fan-in or fan-out group regularization (λ>0, 1≤p<∞) the objective (empirical loss + regularizer) attains a global minimum at a finite network width, i.e., the problem does not degenerate to infinite width. The result is fairly general and is extended to directionally differentiable activations. Proposition 1 shows that for self-similar nonlinearities (e.g., ReLU), per-layer regularization strengths reduce to a single λ up to rescaling.
- Empirically, the authors evaluate on MNIST, rectangles-images, convex, and a large tabular dataset (Poker). Nonparametric nets sometimes outperform parametric networks of the same size and, on some datasets, achieve accuracy competitive with or better than networks found by random search, often with fewer parameters. They also demonstrate scalability on the million-example Poker dataset.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: The empirical evaluation is too limited and not sufficiently rigorous to support the paper’s ambitious claims, and the novelty beyond known ideas (group lasso pruning, dynamic network growth) is not convincingly demonstrated. Important baselines and ablations are missing, and several design choices (CapNorm, AdaRad, unit addition schedule) remain ad hoc and insufficiently validated.

Supporting arguments
- Significance and novelty: The high-level idea—use group sparsity to prune units and add units during training—is appealing and has a long history (grow/prune, cascade-correlation, group lasso for units). The formal existence result (finite optimum under group regularization) is a nice theoretical contribution, but it does not address learnability or generalization, and it is not tied to the proposed training procedure (no convergence or consistency guarantees for the discrete add/remove dynamics). CapNorm and AdaRad are interesting engineering ideas but are relatively incremental; closely related notions (decoupling scale/direction, weight normalization, decoupled weight decay, orthogonal gradient components) exist, and the paper does not sufficiently position against them.
- Empirical scope: Experiments are restricted to small fully connected benchmarks and one large tabular dataset; there are no modern convolutional or sequence tasks where capacity selection is most impactful. The negative result on the convex dataset also highlights instability of the approach. Without demonstrations on CNNs (CIFAR-10/100) or transformers/RNNs, it is difficult to assess practical utility for ICLR’s audience.
- Baselines and fairness: Parametric baselines are strong in some places, but comparisons are not fully convincing. For parametric nets, batch norm is used (with trainable or fixed parameters) and λ=0, whereas nonparametric nets use CapNorm and group regularization; this makes it hard to disentangle the effects of CapNorm, the regularizer, and the add/remove scheme. The hyperparameter selection protocol includes manual selection of a “representative” nonparametric run and rewinding/annealing schedules; this introduces selection bias. There is no comparison to alternative pruning/growing strategies (e.g., group-lasso-trained parametric nets with unit pruning only; dynamic expansion methods; knowledge distillation baselines).
- Ablations: There is no ablation isolating the contributions of CapNorm vs standard BN, AdaRad vs RMSprop/Adam with appropriate handling of shrinkage, or the unit addition rate/schedule. The claim that normalizing only the angular component is essential is plausible but not empirically validated.
- Efficiency: A core motivation is to replace expensive black-box search with a single training run. However, the proposed training requires many epochs because capacity is added slowly, and there is no wall-clock or compute comparison to random/Bayesian search over widths. The optimizer cost is analyzed theoretically, but empirical runtime vs accuracy curves are missing.
- Clarity and correctness: The paper is generally well written and technically careful. Theorem 1 appears sound under the stated assumptions; however, the theorem ensures existence of a finite-width minimizer for the penalized empirical objective, not that the dynamic method finds it, nor that it improves generalization. CapNorm’s behavior (capping std at 1, removing scale/shift) is reasonable to prevent cancellation with regularization but might hamper representational flexibility; empirical evidence for this tradeoff is not provided.

Suggestions to improve the paper
- Strengthen empirical evaluation:
  - Include CNN experiments (e.g., CIFAR-10/100) and, if possible, a transformer/RNN benchmark. Demonstrate depth/width adaptation where it matters.
  - Provide ablations: CapNorm vs BN/LayerNorm; AdaRad vs RMSprop/Adam with properly decoupled weight decay; effect of unit addition rate and schedule; fan-in vs fan-out; p=1 vs p=2.
  - Compare to strong pruning/growing baselines: start from larger nets and prune via group lasso; existing dynamic expansion methods; distillation from larger models.
  - Report wall-clock time and compute (FLOPs) to reach a target accuracy vs black-box width search.
- Positioning and related work: Clarify connections to weight normalization, decoupled weight decay, orthogonal gradient methods, and structured sparsity (group lasso) in neural nets. Discuss differences to prior grow-and-prune frameworks and why your method offers advantages.
- Theory–practice link: Provide any guarantees (or empirical evidence) that the add/remove dynamics coupled with the regularizer converge to stable architectures; analyze sensitivity to λ, αr, αφ; discuss failure cases (e.g., convex dataset).
- Reproducibility: Release code and specify all hyperparameters automatically (avoid manual “representative” selection).

Questions for the authors
1. How sensitive is performance and final width to λ, αr, and the unit addition schedule? Can you provide systematic sweeps and guidelines?
2. Can AdaRad’s benefits be reproduced by standard optimizers with decoupled weight decay and unit-wise normalization? What unique advantage does the radial–angular decomposition offer empirically?
3. How does CapNorm compare to vanilla BN without learnable scale/shift, or to LayerNorm/GroupNorm, under your regularization?
4. Can you demonstrate the method on convolutional architectures and show that width adaptation translates to competitive accuracy/compute trade-offs?
5. What is the wall-clock training cost relative to a basic random search over widths that trains fewer models for fewer epochs using early-stopping proxies?

Overall, the idea is interesting and the theory is neat, but the current empirical evidence and comparisons are not strong enough for acceptance.
