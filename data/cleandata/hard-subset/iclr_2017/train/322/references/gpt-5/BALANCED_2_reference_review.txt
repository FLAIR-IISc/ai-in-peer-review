Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes “nonparametric neural networks,” in which hidden-layer widths are not fixed a priori. Instead, units are added and removed during a single training run while optimizing a regularized training objective. The central mechanism is a fan-in (or fan-out) group-ℓp regularizer, with p=2 emphasized, that induces entire units to be set to zero, enabling principled removal of redundant units. A key theoretical result (Theorem 1) shows that with such regularization, the overall objective attains a global minimum at finite dimensionality, justifying the nonparametric formulation.
- The paper introduces two training components tailored to this setting: (i) CapNorm, a variant of batch normalization that avoids negating the effect of the regularizer by capping the rescaling, and (ii) AdaRad, an optimizer that decomposes the gradient of each fan-in into radial (parallel) and angular (orthogonal) components, normalizing only the orthogonal component to let new units learn while preserving the intended shrinkage from the regularizer.
- Empirical evaluation on MNIST, rectangles images, convex, and a large “poker” dataset suggests that the approach can find reasonable widths and can match or outperform parametric models of the same size in some cases, though it underperforms on convex. The paper also provides analysis of training dynamics, and complexity considerations arguing that AdaRad scales more gracefully in some respects than RMSProp.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons
- Limited and uneven empirical validation: experiments are confined to MLPs on relatively simple datasets, with mixed results (notably negative on convex) and with selection and comparison protocols that risk bias.
- Novelty and placement vs. literature: the central mechanism (group sparsity on fan-ins/outs to eliminate neurons) is well known; the paper’s incremental additions (CapNorm and AdaRad) are interesting but not convincingly shown to be necessary or superior to simpler baselines.

Supporting arguments
1) Problem being tackled. The paper addresses selecting hidden-layer widths during a single training run to avoid expensive black-box hyperparameter search. This is a relevant and important problem.

2) Motivation and relation to prior work. Using group ℓ2 regularization on fan-ins (or fan-outs) to induce neuron-level sparsity has a substantial literature (e.g., group lasso for neural networks, structured sparsification, network slimming/grow-and-prune). The paper cites related work (e.g., Wen et al., Molchanov et al., Wei et al.), but the positioning could be sharper: the presented method largely combines known ingredients—group sparsity plus dynamic addition of zero units—into a cohesive framework. The novelty lies more in the training recipe (CapNorm, AdaRad) and the “nonparametric” framing with an existence theorem.

3) Do the claims hold?
- Theory: Theorem 1 ensures a finite-width global minimum exists for the specified training objective. This is technically sound and useful for justification, but it does not address generalization or the behavior of the proposed training dynamics (convergence, optimality, or selection of a “good” size under validation error). The proposition about self-similar nonlinearities is sensible.
- Empirics: The claim that nonparametric networks can “achieve higher accuracy than is possible by black-box methods” is not convincingly supported. The comparisons are limited, sometimes favor the proposed method on small datasets, and fail on convex. The selection procedure for “typical representatives” (manual choice of a single run among 10; then training parametric models of that size) risks selection bias. Parametric baselines differed in training recipe (e.g., no weight decay + BN vs. NP using CapNorm + regularization), which complicates fairness. There are no wall-clock comparisons, convergence speed evaluations, or ablations isolating CapNorm and AdaRad effects. For AdaRad, the computational/memory advantages are argued but not measured empirically.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (constructive; not all are decision-critical)
- Strengthen baselines and fairness:
  - Compare against standard structured sparsity methods that prune neurons via group lasso during training without dynamic addition, and prune-and-retrain pipelines (including allowing re-growth where applicable).
  - Include baselines with simple grow-and-prune heuristics and with more recent width learning approaches.
  - Ensure comparable normalization/regularization across methods (e.g., compare CapNorm vs BN vs WeightNorm/LayerNorm, and allow appropriate weight decay for BN via decoupled weight decay).
  - Avoid manual “representative” selection; report averages and variances over multiple runs; use consistent validation-based model selection for all methods.

- Ablations and diagnostics:
  - Is CapNorm essential? Show results with and without CapNorm; with BN without affine; with LayerNorm/WeightNorm; and quantify the regularizer–normalization interaction.
  - Is AdaRad necessary? Compare to RMSProp/Adam with per-fan-in normalization or adaptive learning rates; provide wall-clock and step efficiency comparisons.
  - Analyze the unit addition schedule (rate, frequency), initialization of new units (fan-in/out choices), and αr/αφ sensitivity. Report how often added units survive and their contribution.

- Scope and significance:
  - Demonstrate viability beyond small MLPs: include convolutional architectures (dynamic channels) on CIFAR or similar; this also tests the claim about hardware efficiency of dense matrices after pruning.
  - Provide code and hyperparameter search spaces; simplify the “rewind and anneal” training protocol if possible or justify its necessity.

- Clarity:
  - Streamline the algorithm presentation; clarify bias terms (are biases used/regularized?), exact handling of output layer normalization, and the numerical stability details (epsilon settings).
  - Summarize the proof ideas more intuitively in the main text; relegate technicalities to the appendix.

Questions for the authors
1) How sensitive are results to the unit addition schedule and the choice αr=150/λ? Can you provide a principled criterion for addition/removal (e.g., based on validation or contribution metrics) and ablations showing robustness?
2) Can you report wall-clock time and number of optimizer steps to reach a given validation error for NP vs. parametric baselines (SGD, RMSProp, Adam)? How does AdaRad’s per-step overhead translate to end-to-end efficiency?
3) What is the performance of NP without CapNorm, and of parametric models with CapNorm? Does CapNorm alone explain part of the gains?
4) For fairness, can you redo comparisons where both NP and parametric models use the same normalization family and comparable regularization (e.g., decoupled weight decay with BN)? Also, please report mean±std test accuracy over runs rather than a handpicked representative.
5) How does the approach extend to convolutional layers (fan-in/out at the channel level)? Any preliminary results?
6) The theorem guarantees a finite-width minimizer for the training objective. Do you observe a monotone improvement in validation performance with the learned size, and can you relate the learned λ to typical model selection via validation?
7) Are biases included and regularized? If excluded, how do they interact with CapNorm and the self-similarity assumption?

Overall, the paper presents a cohesive and appealing framework with a useful existence result and inventive training components. However, stronger empirical evidence, clearer comparisons to established structured-sparsity methods, and broader demonstrations are needed to meet the bar for acceptance.
