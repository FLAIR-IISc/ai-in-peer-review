Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes “nonparametric neural networks”: during a single training run, the width of fully connected layers is adapted by adding zero-output units and removing units whose fan-in (or fan-out) is shrunk to zero by a structured regularizer. The key theoretical result shows that, with a fan-in or fan-out group lp (1 ≤ p < ∞) regularizer and λ > 0, the training objective attains a global minimum at a finite dimensionality, ensuring the problem is well-posed despite allowing unbounded width.
- Two training components are introduced to make this practical: (i) CapNorm (capped batch normalization) to avoid trivial cancellations between normalization and the regularizer; (ii) AdaRad, which decomposes the gradient into components radial and orthogonal to each unit’s fan-in, normalizes only the angular (orthogonal) part, and performs separate radial and angular updates.
- Empirically, on MNIST, Rectangles-Images, Convex, and a large OpenML “Poker” dataset, the approach can outperform size-matched parametric MLPs, sometimes approaches or exceeds black-box search baselines, and sometimes underperforms (Convex). Analysis illustrates dynamic width adaptation and the training dynamics of unit addition/removal.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) Empirical evaluation is limited and lacks strong baselines and ablations to convincingly support the broad claims; the method underperforms on an entire dataset (Convex), and comparisons omit competing structured sparsity and grow/prune methods. 2) The contribution mixes multiple new components (regularization scheme, CapNorm, AdaRad, growth/pruning policy) without dissecting which pieces are necessary or superior, making it hard to attribute gains and assess generality.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: Automatically adapting width during training is important and underexplored relative to black-box hyperparameter search. The framing via f-equivalence and zero-unit insertion/removal is simple and compelling. The choice of group-regularization to induce unit-level sparsity is well-motivated and consistent with prior structured sparsity literature.
- Theoretical soundness: The main theorem (existence of a finite-width global minimum under fan-in/fan-out regularization) is plausible and carefully proved under reasonable regularity conditions (directional differentiability suffices). This gives a nice conceptual foundation but does not address optimization convergence or generalization. Proposition 1 about self-similar nonlinearities and layer-wise λ equivalence is insightful and reduces hyperparameter burden.
- Empirical support: 
  - Positives: The paper demonstrates that dynamic width selection can match or beat size-matched parametric baselines on MNIST and Rectangles-Images; the Poker result, where the best nonparametric net achieves extremely low test error with a compact width, is encouraging for scalability.
  - Limitations: (i) The benchmarks are limited to MLPs on tabular/image-like toy datasets; no convolutional experiments, which are central to modern ICLR papers. (ii) A strong comparative evaluation is missing: there are no head-to-heads with modern pruning/grow methods (e.g., dynamic network surgery, channel pruning, structured sparsity training) or with neural architecture search/growth methods that are close in spirit. (iii) The Convex dataset result shows clear underperformance and high variance; the paper acknowledges this but does not analyze failure modes or propose remedies. (iv) The claims that the approach can “achieve a higher accuracy than is possible by black-box methods” are not convincingly demonstrated: the black-box comparators are limited and not state-of-the-art, and the search space is restricted to MLP widths.
- Methodological clarity and rigor:
  - CapNorm is a reasonable tweak to avoid scaling cancellations, but it deviates from standard practice; it would be useful to show that CapNorm is necessary and beneficial via ablations, and to compare parametric nets with CapNorm for fairness.
  - AdaRad is a novel optimizer tailored to the regularizer; however, the benefit over RMSprop/Adam is not convincingly isolated, and AdaRad-M (momentum variant) yields similar results, suggesting optimizer choice might be secondary.
  - The unit addition policy is simplistic (fixed-rate addition regardless of utility); more adaptive growth criteria might improve both efficiency and accuracy. Sensitivity analyses (addition rate, αr/αφ, λ) are only partially explored, and the training protocol includes “rewinding” and manual annealing, which complicates reproducibility and fairness.
- Positioning in the literature: The paper cites relevant work (pruning, growth, Bayesian optimization, NAS), but experimental comparisons to structured sparsity or grow/prune approaches are absent. The “nonparametric” terminology is somewhat overloaded in statistics; here it really means “adaptive dimensionality,” which is fine, but a discussion distinguishing the usage would improve clarity.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Provide ablations: (i) Nonparametric with standard BatchNorm vs CapNorm; (ii) AdaRad vs RMSprop/Adam with proper handling of shrinkage; (iii) p=1 vs p=2, fan-in vs fan-out; (iv) no growth but pruning-only vs growth+pruning.
- Include stronger baselines: modern structured sparsity methods, dynamic network surgery, iterative magnitude pruning with regrowth, and, if feasible, simple NAS baselines for width.
- Analyze failures on Convex: Is underperformance due to conservative growth (λ too high), optimizer dynamics, or the CapNorm/activation interaction? Can adaptive addition rates or different nonlinearity help?
- Extend to convolutional layers and at least one modern vision benchmark to demonstrate broader relevance.
- Clarify fairness: allow parametric baselines to use CapNorm; explain the choice to disable weight decay with BN and discuss known interactions (many practitioners use weight decay with BN).
- Discuss computational overheads with wall-clock measurements, not just big-O or memory arguments. Report training time vs parametric baselines at comparable accuracy.
- Tighten writing: the paper currently introduces several innovations; focus the narrative around the core contribution and move secondary components to appendix, emphasizing which are necessary.

Questions for the authors
1) How sensitive is performance to the unit addition rate and the choice of αr/αφ? Could you provide a systematic sensitivity study and/or an adaptive addition policy based on utility signals?
2) Can you provide ablations demonstrating that CapNorm is necessary and that standard BN leads to the claimed cancellation pathologies in practice?
3) How does AdaRad compare to RMSprop/Adam when you adjust shrinkage consistently (e.g., rescaling regularization to account for per-parameter normalization)? What benefits are unique to the radial/angular decomposition?
4) Why not p=1 (sparser weights) or fan-out regularization? Do these variants perform worse empirically, and if so, why?
5) Can you compare to at least one strong pruning/growth baseline on the same tasks (e.g., dynamic network surgery or structured channel pruning analogs for MLPs)?
6) Any evidence the method improves generalization beyond fitting curves (e.g., calibration, robustness, or reduced overfitting when growth is allowed)?
7) How would you extend the approach to CNNs and attention layers? Are there pitfalls with CapNorm or AdaRad in these settings?
