Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes “nonparametric neural networks,” where layer widths are not fixed a priori. Instead, units are dynamically added (as “zero units”) and removed during a single training run via group-ℓ2 fan-in (or fan-out) regularization that induces unit-level sparsity. A key theorem shows that, under mild conditions and with these regularizers, the training objective attains a global minimum at finite dimensionality despite optimizing over a union of spaces.
- Two algorithmic components enable practical training: (1) CapNorm, a capped batch-normalization variant designed to avoid neutralizing the effect of the regularizer; (2) AdaRad/AdaRad-M, an optimizer that decomposes fan-in gradients into radial (length) and angular (direction) components, normalizes only the angular component, and couples naturally with the group-ℓ2 shrinkage.
- Experiments on MNIST, rectangles-images, convex, and a large OpenML Poker dataset show that the approach can grow/shrink networks during training, often finds compact models, sometimes outperforms parametric networks of the same size, and scales to >1M examples. Informative analyses illustrate the dynamics of unit addition/removal and fan-in/fan-out norms.

Decision: Reject (borderline)
Key reasons:
- The empirical evidence is not yet sufficiently compelling or controlled to isolate the benefits of nonparametric training from confounding factors (CapNorm, AdaRad), and comparisons to strong/prior dynamic architecture baselines are missing.
- The evaluation is limited to small fully connected architectures and largely classical datasets; on convex the method underperforms, raising concerns about robustness.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and theory: The existence theorem is well-motivated and technically nontrivial, giving a solid foundation for optimizing over variable-width architectures with group sparse regularization. The self-similarity discussion and Proposition 1 (single λ equivalence) are insightful. However, the theorem concerns existence of finite-width minimizers for the penalized training objective; it does not address generalization or characterize when the learned width is near-optimal for validation/test performance. The theoretical framing is valuable but not yet predictive of empirical behavior.
- Methodological novelty: AdaRad’s radial-angular decomposition at the level of fan-ins is interesting and seems practically useful. CapNorm is a clear, simple modification to avoid the conflict between BN and weight shrinkage. Together, they form a coherent pipeline; however, the gains attributable to nonparametric growth/shrinkage versus these training heuristics are not disentangled.
- Experimental design and baselines: 
  - Parametric baselines are trained with RMSprop/Adam and BN, whereas the nonparametric models use AdaRad + CapNorm. This mismatch hinders attribution of improvements. A stronger, fair test would train parametric networks with CapNorm + AdaRad to match optimization and normalization, and likewise train nonparametric networks under RMSprop/Adam to test sensitivity.
  - The claim that BN is “incompatible” with ℓ2 regularization is debatable; weight decay with BN is common (albeit acting differently from pure ℓ2). The decision to set λ=0 for parametric baselines may disadvantage them, especially since CapNorm removes learned BN affine parameters on the NP side. Ablations are needed.
  - The breadth of comparisons is limited. There are no empirical comparisons to pruning/growing methods (e.g., dynamic growth, DSD, Net2Net follow-ups, neuron-level group sparsity, or recent structured pruning/growth methods), nor to regularizers that induce unit sparsity (group lasso) in standard parametric settings.
  - Dataset/architecture scope: Fully connected MLPs on MNIST/rectangles/convex and Poker do not stress modern architectures. No convolutional or transformer experiments, no image benchmarks (CIFAR/ImageNet), and no wall-clock or compute comparisons. The convex underperformance suggests the method may converge to under-sized models, but there is no remedy demonstrated (e.g., adaptive λ, unit addition criteria).
  - Selection and tuning: The protocol involves manual “representative” selection and extensive step-size annealing/rewinding; it’s unclear how reproducible or automated this would be in practice. Hyperparameters (αr, αφ, ν, CapNorm choices) appear task-specific with nontrivial hand-tuning.

Additional feedback and suggestions (to help improve the work)
- Provide ablations to disentangle components:
  - NP vs P with identical optimizer/normalization (parametric AdaRad + CapNorm; NP with RMSprop/Adam + BN-like layer without affine parameters).
  - CapNorm vs BN vs LayerNorm/WeightNorm; show why CapNorm is necessary and how it affects both NP and P models.
  - Fan-in vs fan-out regularization; p=1 vs p=2; effect on sparsity patterns, training speed, and final accuracy.
  - Unit addition schedule (rate/frequency), initialization scale, and radial vs angular step sizes; show sensitivity and practical defaults.
- Baselines: Compare to strong dynamic/pruning/growth baselines (e.g., methods that remove/add neurons or channels, group-lasso on units for parametric nets, more recent network morphism/growth approaches).
- Fairness: Allow parametric models to use regularization compatible with their normalization (e.g., decoupled weight decay), and include the same “no affine” normalization variant for a fair capacity match.
- Robustness: Demonstrate on convolutional networks (CIFAR-10/100) and possibly transformers/MLPs on tabular data; report wall-clock/compute overhead and memory footprint.
- Theory: Discuss when the penalty-induced width aligns with validation-optimal width; could adaptive λ or bilevel tuning address underfitting cases (e.g., convex dataset)? Any convergence guarantees for AdaRad?
- Clarity: Tighten the write-up (typos, formatting issues in algorithms; make CapNorm and AdaRad pseudo-code concise). Consider releasing code to aid reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
1. How much of the performance gain over same-size parametric networks remains when training those parametric networks with CapNorm + AdaRad (matching optimization and normalization)?
2. Can you quantify the impact of CapNorm alone (vs BN without affine parameters vs no normalization) on the ability of the ℓ2 fan-in penalty to prune/grow effectively?
3. Why is λ=0 used for parametric baselines with BN? Did you try decoupled weight decay or group penalties on parametric networks under BN?
4. How sensitive are results to ν (addition rate), αr, and αφ? Do you have heuristics that generalize across datasets, and can you provide variance/sensitivity plots?
5. Could adaptive λ (e.g., annealing or bilevel tuning) mitigate the under-sizing observed on the convex dataset?
6. Do results extend to CNNs (e.g., channel-wise group penalties) and modern benchmarks? Any preliminary findings?
7. What is the wall-clock and memory overhead of AdaRad/CapNorm versus RMSprop/Adam+BN at equal accuracy? Can you report compute-normalized comparisons?
8. For the Poker dataset, how do you ensure no leakage and stable calibration, and why do linear models perform near random? Could you share more diagnostics (class balance, features)?

Overall, the paper presents an appealing framework with solid theoretical grounding and promising ideas. With stronger, controlled experiments, broader baselines, and clearer ablations, it could become a compelling contribution.
