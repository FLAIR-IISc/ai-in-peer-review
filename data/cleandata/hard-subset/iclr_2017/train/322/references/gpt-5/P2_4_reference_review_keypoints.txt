- Proposed nonparametric neural networks that adapt layer widths during a single training run via group-lasso (p=2) on fan-in or fan-out to zero out units and control size.
- Theorem 1: with λ>0 and 1≤p<∞, the group-lasso-regularized training objective attains a global minimum at finite width; self-similar nonlinearities let per-layer regularization be rescaled.
- Practical method: alternate gradient updates with adding/removing zero units, use self-similar activations, CapNorm (BN variant), and a new optimizer AdaRad that separates radial/angular gradient components.
- Experiments on MNIST, Rectangles-Images, Convex, and a large Poker dataset show width stabilization, competitive accuracy on some tasks, and scalability to 1M+ examples.
- Decision: Reject (borderline).
- Main concern 1: Empirical support is mixed and dataset-dependent, with missing or potentially unfair baselines.
- Main concern 2: Novelty/positioning limited — approach closely related to structured sparsity/group-lasso and existing grow/prune methods; contribution mixes known ideas and heuristics without convincing SOTA advance.
- Baseline fairness issue: Parametric baselines use BatchNorm without L2 while nonparametric models use CapNorm plus group-lasso, making comparisons potentially biased.
- Missing stronger baselines: fixed overparameterized networks with group-lasso/pruning; dynamic sparse methods; modern optimizers with filter-wise normalization or decoupled weight decay.
- Limited experimental scope: small/classical datasets and one tabular set only; no convolutional/CIFAR-style experiments despite practical motivation.
- Failure modes not analyzed: nonparametric models underperform notably on Convex without sufficient analysis.
- Selection bias and evaluation gaps: manually chosen representative seed for nonparametric model, minimal error bars/statistics, and limited ablations of key components.
- Theoretical novelty limited: Theorem echoes known group-lasso properties (finite active groups); dynamic unit add/remove related to prior constructive/pruning/regrowth methods.
- AdaRad novelty uncertain: gradient radial/angular decomposition is interesting but its advantage over Adam/RMSprop is not clearly established experimentally.
- Reproducibility concerns: complex training protocol (rewinding, annealing, many hyperparameters), no code provided, and some algorithmic choices lack sensitivity analyses.
- Positive: clear principled formulation of variable-width training with a finite-width optimality guarantee.
- Positive: practical engineering (self-similar activations, CapNorm, AdaRad) enabling dynamic resizing, with analysis of training dynamics and unit lifetimes.
- Positive: demonstration of scalability to a large dataset and detailed optimizer accounting.
- Suggested improvement: add baselines comparing group-lasso on fixed networks, dynamic sparse methods, pruning/regrowth, and weight-normalized optimizers with decoupled weight decay.
- Suggested improvement: add ablations for CapNorm, AdaRad vs Adam/RMSprop with per-filter normalization, unit addition frequency and learning-rate sensitivities, and fan-in vs fan-out regularization.
- Suggested improvement: extend experiments to convolutional nets and CIFAR-like tasks and explore depth selection/channel pruning.
- Suggested improvement: report variance across seeds, avoid manual representative selection, and simplify/provide a default schedule for reproducibility.
- Suggested improvement: release code and provide sensitivity plots for algorithmic choices.
- Question 1: How do results change if parametric baselines use group-lasso (compatible with BN variants) and pruning rather than no regularization?
- Question 2: Can you compare AdaRad head-to-head with Adam/RMSprop with per-filter normalization, including wall-clock-controlled learning curves and final accuracy?
- Question 3: Why is capping at std<1 in CapNorm theoretically justified, and how does it compare empirically to BN without affine and other BN variants?
- Question 4: Explain/init rationale for new-unit initialization (random fan-in, zero fan-out), gradient pathways for fan-out growth, and empirical time-to-usefulness vs alternative initializations.
- Question 5: How sensitive are final width and accuracy to λ and ν, and can you provide practical guidance for setting λ without extensive tuning?
- Question 6: Theorem 1 is about training optimum width — is there an empirical relation between active-unit count and validation/generalization, i.e., does validation-optimal width align with training-optimal width?
