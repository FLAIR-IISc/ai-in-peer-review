- Proposes "nonparametric neural networks" that adapt layer widths during one training run by alternately updating weights and adding/removing units.  
- Key mechanisms: unit-wise group regularizer (fan-in/out Lp, p=2 group lasso), insertion of "zero units", CapNorm (capped batch norm without scale/shift), and AdaRad optimizer (radial/angular gradient decomposition).  
- Theoretical result: with fan-in/out regularization (λ>0, 1≤p<∞) a finite-width global minimizer exists; per-layer regularization can be rescaled for self-similar nonlinearities.  
- Empirics: on MNIST and rectangle images nonparametric nets can outperform same-size parametric models and find smaller architectures; they underperform on convex data; poker dataset result reaches 0% test error.  
- Decision: Reject.  
- Main rejection reason 1: Empirical evidence is limited, small-scale, mixed, and lacks modern architecture/dataset evaluations (e.g., convnets on CIFAR/ImageNet) or rigorous efficiency comparisons.  
- Main rejection reason 2: Training pipeline is complex with many hyperparameters and lacks thorough ablations and wall-clock/compute comparisons versus simpler baselines or black-box search.  
- Novelty/motivation: Problem is important and the use of group regularization with add/remove is sensible; AdaRad and CapNorm are well-motivated contributions.  
- Positioning weakness: Empirical comparisons do not appropriately match structured sparsity or growing/pruning baselines, weakening the claim of superiority.  
- Scope concern: Experiments limited to fully connected models; absence of convolutional experiments despite claimed applicability.  
- Mixed outcomes: Failure on convex data is unexplained and raises robustness concerns.  
- Protocol concerns: Manual selection of a representative nonparametric model introduces selection bias; baseline regularization and normalization differ, confounding comparisons.  
- Compute/accounting: No wall-clock, GPU-hours, or compute budget comparisons to hyperparameter optimization or width sweeps.  
- Ablation gap: Key components (CapNorm vs BN/LN/WN, AdaRad vs Adam/RMSprop, addition schedule, p=1 vs p=2, fan-in vs fan-out) are not systematically ablated.  
- Poker result flagged: 0% test error is surprising and may indicate data leakage; needs verification.  
- Clarity: Paper is detailed and generally clear; proofs are long but readable; some reference and protocol presentation issues.  
- Suggested improvements: Broaden experiments to convnets/CIFAR, add thorough ablations, compare to group-lasso pruning pipelines and NAS baselines, and report wall-clock/compute trade-offs.  
- Suggested analysis: Investigate failure modes (e.g., convex), sensitivity to λ/αr/αφ and unit-addition schedule, and CapNorm vs other normalization methods.  
- Reproducibility: Release code and hyperparameter defaults; reduce hand-tuned knobs or provide automatic schedules.  
- Questions for authors: sensitivity to αr/αφ and addition schedule; absence of conv experiments and conv-layer implementation issues; direct comparisons to group-lasso pruning and dynamic surgery; wall-clock/GPU-hour comparisons to random/Bayesian search; verification of poker dataset integrity; causes of convex underperformance; CapNorm vs BN/WeightNorm under same regularization.
