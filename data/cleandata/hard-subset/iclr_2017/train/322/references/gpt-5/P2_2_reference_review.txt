Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes “nonparametric neural networks,” a framework to select hidden-layer widths during a single training run by dynamically adding zero-units and removing units whose fan-in or fan-out are shrunk to zero by a group-lasso-like regularizer. The core theoretical claim (Theorem 1) is that with a fan-in or fan-out Lp (1 ≤ p < ∞) regularizer and σ(0) = 0 activations, the training objective attains a global minimum at a finite network size.
- On the algorithmic side, the paper introduces: (i) a capped batch normalization layer (CapNorm) meant to preserve the effect of the regularizer by avoiding rescalings below unit variance; and (ii) AdaRad, an optimizer that decomposes gradients into radial (parallel) and angular (orthogonal) components per fan-in, normalizing only the orthogonal component to help newly added units learn while preserving the intended shrinkage dynamics.
- Empirically, on MNIST, rectangles-images, convex (Bergstra & Bengio), and a large “poker” dataset, the method grows from small seeds and converges to stable sizes. On two datasets (MNIST, rectangles) nonparametric networks are competitive or better than parametric baselines of the same size; on convex they underperform; on poker they achieve very low error with reasonable sizes.

Decision: Reject (borderline)
Key reasons:
- Empirical validation is limited in scope and mixed: results are shown only on fully-connected MLPs and largely on toy/legacy datasets; there is a notable failure case (convex), and no evaluation on standard modern benchmarks (e.g., CIFAR/convnets) where dynamic width selection would be compelling.
- Several design choices are ad hoc and critical (CapNorm, αr relative to λ, unit-add schedule), but their necessity and robustness are not rigorously ablated; fairness of comparisons to parametric baselines is questionable due to differing regularization/normalization regimes.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and positioning: The paper targets a relevant problem (automated width selection within a single run) and situates itself against black-box hyperparameter optimization and pruning/growing literature. The combination of group sparsity, dynamic unit addition/removal, and an optimizer tailored to unit-level geometry is interesting and plausibly novel. The “f-equivalence” via zero-units is a simple but useful device for topology changes.
- Theory: Theorem 1 is a meaningful existence result: with fan-in/out group regularization and σ(0)=0, the objective admits a finite-dimensional global minimizer. The proof strategy—bounding activations/gradients, exploiting group-norm subgradients at local minima, and handling nondifferentiable activations via directional derivatives and linearizations—appears technically sound at a high level. Assumptions are explicit; the result concerns training error, not generalization. Proposition 1 (self-similarity) is a helpful scaling observation but requires ReLU-like activations.
- Algorithms: CapNorm addresses a real issue (BN’s scale invariance can nullify norm regularization), but it is ad hoc and its behavior relative to alternatives (e.g., freezing BN scale, weight norm, layer norm, no affine BN) is not studied. AdaRad’s angular/radial decomposition and per-fan-in normalization is an interesting idea; the rationale (help new units receive normalized learning signal while preserving shrinkage) is convincing. Complexity claims are plausible, but wall-clock/throughput measurements are missing.
- Empirics: Positives—shows size trajectories stabilizing, and in two datasets achieves better accuracy than parametric nets of same size; poker suggests scalability to large N. Negatives—datasets are limited for ICLR 2025 standards; the convex failure is acknowledged but not analyzed; no convnets; no comparison to strong structured sparsity/pruning/growing baselines (e.g., group-lasso unit pruning with standard training; network morphism/growth like Net2Net/Wei et al.; dynamic surgery; L0/ARD). Baseline fairness: parametric nets use BN with λ=0, while nonparametric use CapNorm with λ>0; comparisons “at same size” are informative but mix training regimes. Selection of a “typical representative” from 10 seeds by the authors could unintentionally bias results; better would be reporting medians across seeds consistently. Ablations for the necessity of CapNorm, AdaRad vs Adam/RMSprop with group-lasso, p=1 vs p=2, and sensitivity to ν, αr, αφ, λ are missing or minimal.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Strengthen empirical evaluation:
  - Include at least one convolutional benchmark (e.g., CIFAR-10/100) with width selection over channels; compare against modern pruning/growth methods (structured pruning, dynamic channel selection).
  - Report median and variance across seeds systematically; avoid manual “representative” selection. Ensure baselines use matched regularization/normalization where possible (e.g., compare CapNorm to BN with frozen affine params or weight norm; test parametric nets with group l2 fan-in regularizer and CapNorm).
  - Provide wall-clock training time and FLOPs as networks grow; show overhead of AdaRad vs RMSprop/Adam and discuss hardware implications.
  - Add ablations: (i) remove CapNorm; (ii) replace AdaRad with Adam+group shrinkage; (iii) compare p=1 vs p=2 group norms; (iv) different unit addition schedules and αr/λ couplings.
- Clarify methodology:
  - Justify αr=150/λ and “≈50 epochs” rationale more formally; provide guidelines for choosing ν, νfreq, αφ.
  - Detail inference-time handling of CapNorm (statistics and determinism) and its effect on calibration.
  - Provide code to aid reproducibility.
- Analysis:
  - Investigate why convex fails (optimization dynamics? growth bias to small sizes? CapNorm interaction?) and propose remedies (annealing λ, adaptive unit-add rate).
  - Discuss generalization: does the implicit bias from growth/shrinkage act like regularization beyond explicit λ? Could early stopping or λ annealing be principled?

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide results on at least one convolutional architecture and standard dataset, comparing against structured pruning/growing baselines and reporting compute/time?
- How sensitive is performance and final size to λ, αr, αφ, and unit addition rate ν? Can you provide robustness plots and a principled tuning guide?
- How do results change if parametric baselines use CapNorm (or BN with frozen affine parameters) and the same group-l2 fan-in regularizer?
- What is the wall-clock overhead of AdaRad vs Adam/RMSprop across your tasks? Does the per-fan-in normalization become a bottleneck for wide layers?
- Could CapNorm be replaced with simpler fixes (e.g., weight normalization or freezing BN’s scale) without losing performance? Any ablation?
- For the “convex” dataset failure, what is the main culprit: the growth schedule, the regularizer pushing to small nets, or optimization? Would annealing λ or delaying shrinkage (larger αr) help?
- Does re-training the discovered architectures from scratch with the best parametric training consistently match the nonparametric performance (you mention mixed observations)? Please provide numbers.

Overall, the paper contains interesting theoretical and algorithmic ideas, but the empirical validation and comparisons are not yet at the level needed for acceptance. I encourage the authors to broaden and strengthen experiments, add ablations, and tighten the fairness of baselines.
