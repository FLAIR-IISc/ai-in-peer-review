Summary
The paper proposes “nonparametric neural networks” that adapt layer widths during training by adding zero units and removing units whose fan-in or fan-out vectors shrink to zero under a group regularizer. Theoretical support is given via a theorem showing the regularized training objective attains a global minimum at finite width when using fan-in or fan-out Lp regularization (λ>0, 1≤p<∞). Two practical components are introduced: (i) CapNorm, a variant of batch normalization that subtracts the mean but only scales if the batch std>1 and has no trainable scale/shift; (ii) AdaRad, an optimizer that splits gradients into components parallel/perpendicular to each unit’s fan-in, normalizing only the orthogonal component and applying separate angular/radial updates along with group-shrinkage. Experiments on MNIST, rectangles-images, a convex synthetic dataset, and a large Poker dataset suggest the approach can find compact models and sometimes outperform parametric baselines.

Decision: Reject
Primary reasons: (1) serious issues in experimental protocol and validity (explicit test-set usage in model selection; suspiciously perfect performance on Poker; unclear fairness of baselines), and (2) significant theory-practice mismatch plus numerous clarity and formatting problems that impede reproducibility and confidence.

Supporting arguments
1) Experimental protocol flaws and questionable results
- In Section 7.4.1: “we manually chose a single net as a typical representative by approximating the median of both network size, and the test classification error across the 10 runs.” Using test error to select a representative introduces data leakage and biases subsequent parametric comparisons tied to that representative size. This is a major methodological flaw.
- Poker results report zero test errors on 100,000 examples for a 4-hidden-layer MLP, while linear models “perform roughly as random guessing.” This is highly unexpected for dataset 354 on OpenML and suggests possible leakage, an unreported preprocessing that trivializes the task, or issues with the train/validation/test split. No details on the exact split protocol are provided beyond generalities; this demands verification.
- Baseline fairness is unclear. Parametric baselines were tuned over algorithms and step sizes but with λ=0 because “ℓ2 regularization is not compatible with batch normalization.” In practice, weight decay is commonly used with BN (albeit with scale-invariance caveats), and other regularization choices (dropout, early stopping) could be included systematically. Meanwhile, the proposed method uses an explicit group regularizer and CapNorm. This asymmetry undermines claims of superiority.
- The comparison set omits strong, directly relevant baselines that grow/prune during training with structured sparsity (e.g., Wen et al. 2016; Molchanov et al. 2017; Alvarez & Salzmann 2016), Net2Net for growth, or methods with dynamic unit addition/removal. Citing is not a substitute for empirical comparison.

2) Theory-practice mismatch and assumptions
- Theorem 1 and Proposition 1 are framed for standard feed-forward models with elementwise nonlinearities. The training recipe inserts CapNorm layers (batch-statistics-dependent, non-elementwise, non-self-similar), breaking key assumptions used in Proposition 1 (self-similarity) and not covered by Theorem 1. The paper does not reconcile this mismatch nor discuss whether the theorem extends to normalization layers with data-dependent scaling and capping.
- The training scheme critically uses σ(0)=0 to add/remove zero units without changing f. This assumption is not reflected in Theorem 1’s conditions (which allow tanh but exclude sigmoid), creating confusion about which parts of the theory support which parts of the training procedure.
- The claim that BN “negates” the regularizer is asserted to motivate CapNorm, but no formal analysis or controlled ablation is provided (e.g., BN without gamma/beta, BN with decoupled weight decay), nor evidence that CapNorm preserves regularization effects while maintaining conditioning. Proposition 1’s scale-invariance argument is then cited as reducing per-layer λ tuning, but CapNorm explicitly breaks scale invariance when std<1.

3) AdaRad clarity and correctness
- The “rotate by angle” operation in high-dimensional weight space is nonstandard. The paper lacks a precise, implementable definition and complexity analysis for this rotation in the 2D subspace spanned by the fan-in and its orthogonal gradient component; numerical stability and computational cost are not rigorously addressed.
- The claim that AdaRad cost “scales with units rather than weights” is unconvincing: all core vector operations are over fan-ins (which are weight-dimensional). Without concrete complexity and wall-clock benchmarks versus RMSprop/Adam on the same hardware, the efficiency argument is speculative.
- The rationale that normalizing only orthogonal components avoids interfering with shrinkage is plausible but not established; ablations with standard optimizers plus group-lasso, and with/without gradient normalization, are needed.

4) Presentation issues
- Numerous typos, formatting errors, and missing section numbers (e.g., “.2 CAPPED BATCH NORMALIZATION,” “bot not fastest”), inconsistent notation (“box principle”), and algorithm pseudo-code that is hard to follow (e.g., unspecified epsilon symbol) hinder readability and reproducibility.
- Figures and tables are referred to but not included; crucial details (e.g., data splits on Poker, preprocessing, exact hyperparameters beyond coarse grids) are insufficient for replication. No code is provided.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper)
- Fix the evaluation protocol: never use test metrics for any model selection decisions; report all selections based solely on validation. Re-run all experiments accordingly.
- Provide rigorous Poker dataset details (split source, preprocessing, feature encoding). If truly 0% test error is achieved, include an independent verification and strong ablations; otherwise, correct the result.
- Add strong baselines that perform structured pruning/growth during training and report clear apples-to-apples comparisons (same normalization, same regularization budget, same search effort). Include weight decay with BN and decoupled weight decay variants.
- Provide ablations: with/without CapNorm; with standard BN (no gamma/beta); AdaRad vs RMSprop/Adam with group-lasso; effect of αr, αφ, addition rate, and initialization. Report training time and memory.
- Clarify AdaRad’s “rotation” operator mathematically and give its computational complexity. Include wall-clock comparisons.
- Reconcile theory with practice: either extend Theorem 1 to models with CapNorm-like layers or limit claims accordingly. State assumptions (σ(0)=0) consistently across theory and algorithmic sections.
- Clean up writing, notation, and references; correct citation formatting and typos; include figures/tables.

Questions for the authors
- Can you confirm and correct the test-set usage in Section 7.4.1 for selecting a “representative” network? Please re-run without any test-set involvement.
- How exactly were train/val/test splits obtained for Poker? What preprocessing and categorical encodings were used? How do you explain the 0% test error?
- Can you provide ablations showing CapNorm vs BN (with/without gamma/beta) and the effect on the regularizer’s efficacy?
- How exactly is the high-dimensional rotation implemented in AdaRad, and what is its per-iteration complexity vs RMSprop/Adam? Please provide wall-clock benchmarks.
- Do your theoretical guarantees extend to architectures that include CapNorm layers? If not, what guarantees hold for your actual experimental setting?
- How sensitive is performance to αr = 150/λ, unit addition frequency, and the initial expected fan-in length? Please include sensitivity plots.
