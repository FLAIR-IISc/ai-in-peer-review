1. Summary of contributions
The paper proposes “nonparametric neural networks,” an approach to select network width during a single training run by dynamically adding and removing units. The key components are:
- A theoretical result (Theorem 1) showing that when using a fan-in or fan-out group-regularizer (p-norm with 1 ≤ p < ∞, λ > 0), the training objective attains a global minimum at finite dimensionality, justifying the existence of a finite optimal width.
- Capped Batch Normalization (CapNorm), which modifies batch normalization to avoid negating the effect of the group regularizer by capping the normalization scale below 1 and removing affine parameters.
- Adaptive Radial-Angular Gradient Descent (AdaRad), which decomposes each fan-in’s gradient into parallel (radial) and orthogonal (angular) components, normalizes only the angular component, and combines this with group shrinkage to enable unit addition/removal without destabilizing learning.
- Empirical evaluation on MNIST, rectangles images, convex, and a large tabular dataset (poker), showing that nonparametric training can match or exceed parametric networks of the same size on two datasets, underperform on one, and scale to large datasets. The paper includes a detailed analysis of the training dynamics and unit lifetimes.

2. Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- Empirical evaluation is limited and has methodological issues that undermine the strength of the claims (e.g., reliance on small MLP benchmarks, potential test-set leakage in selecting representatives, and fairness concerns in baselines).
- Novelty is moderate and fragmented across multiple components (group-regularized width selection, CapNorm, AdaRad), with insufficient ablation to isolate contributions or to establish clear advantages over strong existing methods (e.g., structured sparsity, modern optimizers, or NAS/pruning baselines).

3. Supporting arguments
What problem is tackled?
- Automatic width selection during training without expensive black-box hyperparameter search. The approach aims to adapt width dynamically, leveraging f-equivalence via adding/removing zero units.

Is the approach well motivated and placed in the literature?
- The motivation is clear: reduce repeated full trainings and exploit the opportunity to change architecture within a run. The paper cites pruning, structured sparsity, and NAS/hyperparameter optimization, and makes a case for dynamic growth/shrinkage and group penalties. However, the positioning against structured sparsity (group-lasso) and grow-and-prune methods could be stronger: the main novelty over prior structured sparsity appears to be the coupling with dynamic growth and a tailored optimizer/normalizer; prior work on growing networks is acknowledged but not compared empirically.

Do the claims have adequate support and rigor?
- Theorem 1 is technically detailed; the assumptions are explicit (σ(0)=0, directional differentiability bounds, regularizer form), and the proof strategy is careful. Proposition 1 on self-similar nonlinearities is straightforward and useful.
- AdaRad and CapNorm are plausible, but there is no convergence analysis and limited ablation. The empirical section shows mixed results: clear gains on MNIST and rectangles images, underperformance on convex, and competitive performance on poker. However:
  - Comparisons may be confounded by methodological choices. For parametric networks, L2 is disabled to remain compatible with standard BN, while nonparametric models rely critically on L2 group regularization and CapNorm. The impact of this mismatch is not disentangled.
  - Representative nonparametric models are selected using the median test error over 10 runs (Sec. 7.4.1), which involves test-set information in model selection.
  - Ablations are missing: there is no direct measurement of the incremental effect of CapNorm vs BN (or BN without affine), AdaRad vs RMSprop/Adam for nonparametric training, or p=2 vs p=1 vs fan-out.
  - Benchmarks are narrow (MLPs on classic datasets) and do not include convolutional architectures where model selection is most impactful today.

Clarity and reproducibility
- The paper is generally clear about the algorithms (pseudocode provided). There are minor typographical issues and dense notation in the proof. Hyperparameter protocols are documented, but the total number of knobs and heuristics (e.g., schedules, αr=150/λ) is high.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not necessarily decision-critical)
- Strengthen empirical evaluation:
  - Avoid any test-set usage for model selection. Redo representative selection and all tuning purely based on validation.
  - Add ablations: CapNorm vs BN (with/without affine), AdaRad vs RMSprop/Adam under identical regularization, p=2 vs p=1, fan-in vs fan-out, and the effect of the unit addition rate ν and αr/αφ.
  - Compare to strong structured sparsity baselines that start overparameterized and prune with group lasso, and to a simple grow-only baseline, under matched training budgets. Include wall-clock and memory comparisons.
  - Include convolutional architectures and modern datasets to demonstrate generality, or clearly scope the claim to MLPs.
- Improve fairness of baselines:
  - Train parametric networks with comparable regularization (e.g., decoupled weight decay, BN variants compatible with group regularization, or WeightNorm) to separate the benefit of regularization from architecture search.
- Analyze AdaRad:
  - Provide theoretical or empirical evidence for stability/convergence advantages, and show when it outperforms Adam/RMSprop in the nonparametric setting. Report sensitivity to αφ and β.
- Clarify design choices:
  - Justify CapNorm’s “cap at 1” design and lack of affine parameters; discuss potential loss in expressivity and whether learnable scales with gradient stopping could retain regularization effect.
  - Detail computational overhead in FLOPs and memory relative to baselines for each dataset, including the cost of add/remove operations.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance and final width to λ, αr, αφ, ν, and the addition schedule? Can λ be adapted online (e.g., annealing or hypergradient methods) to reduce tuning?
- Can you provide ablations showing (i) AdaRad vs RMSprop/Adam within your nonparametric framework, and (ii) CapNorm vs BN variants, all else equal?
- Did you evaluate p=1 (L1 group) or fan-out regularization, and how do these affect sparsity patterns and accuracy?
- On Sec. 4.1, representatives are chosen using test error statistics. Can you re-run with validation-only selection and report updated comparisons?
- How does the method behave with convolutional layers and skip connections? Are the theoretical guarantees and CapNorm readily extensible?
- What are the wall-clock and compute overheads relative to parametric baselines under matched epochs/mini-batches, especially on poker?
