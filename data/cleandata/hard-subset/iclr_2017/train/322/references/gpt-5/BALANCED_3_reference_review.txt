1) Summary of contributions
This paper proposes “nonparametric neural networks,” a framework that adapts layer widths during a single training run by adding zero units and removing units whose fan-in or fan-out is shrunk to zero. The key technical ingredient is the use of group penalties on fan-in (or fan-out) with p-norm (primarily p=2), together with a theorem guaranteeing that, under such regularization, the global minimum occurs at a finite network size. The paper also introduces two training components to make this practical: (i) CapNorm, a batch-normalization variant that avoids neutralizing the effect of the group regularizer, and (ii) AdaRad, an optimizer that decomposes gradients into radial and angular components to let new units learn without being immediately eliminated by shrinkage. Experiments on MNIST, rectangles-images, convex, and a large OpenML poker dataset show that the approach can grow networks from small seeds, sometimes outperform parametric networks of the same size and, in some cases, come close to (or beat) black-box hyperparameter search. The analysis section provides insights into the dynamics of unit addition/removal and the interaction of CapNorm and AdaRad with the regularizer.

2) Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- The empirical evidence is not yet convincing: comparisons are limited, mixed, and sometimes not apples-to-apples; key ablations and stronger baselines are missing.
- The scope is narrow (mostly small MLPs on classic datasets), leaving unclear whether the approach scales to modern architectures/tasks beyond one tabular dataset.

3) Supporting arguments
On the core questions:
- Problem tackled: automatic selection of network width during training to avoid expensive black-box model selection and to potentially yield better models than fixed-width training.
- Motivation and placement: The motivation is clear and timely. The formulation connects to group sparsity and model pruning/growth lines of work. Related work is discussed, including pruning, hyperparameter optimization, and constructive methods.
- Do the claims hold?
  • Theory: Theorem 1 (finite minimizer under fan-in/fan-out group regularization) is well-motivated and technically careful. However, it speaks to existence of a finite optimum, not to optimization dynamics or generalization; it does not guarantee that the proposed training procedure finds good models.
  • Algorithms: CapNorm and AdaRad are interesting, but their necessity and relative contributions are insufficiently isolated. There is no ablation disentangling: (a) group penalty alone with standard optimizers/normalization; (b) effect of CapNorm vs BN variants; (c) AdaRad vs RMSprop/Adam when used within the same nonparametric framework.
  • Empirics: 
    - Comparisons to parametric baselines are not strictly aligned: nonparametric models use CapNorm + AdaRad + group penalty; parametric models use BN (with/without affine params) + RMSprop/Adam and λ=0. This confounds conclusions about “nonparametric vs parametric” with optimizer/normalization/regularization differences.
    - Reported results are mixed: strong on MNIST/rectangles-images, weak on convex. The poker result (even 0% test error) is striking but on a tabular dataset with possible idiosyncrasies; more checks are needed.
    - The selection of “typical representatives” and the heavy reliance on validation with rewinding and step size annealing make comparisons sensitive and hard to reproduce; no wall-clock or compute budget normalization is provided.
    - Missing stronger baselines: modern pruning/growth with group-lasso, dynamic network surgery, Net2Net/network morphism, recent structured sparsity methods, and contemporary NAS/BO approaches under matched budgets.

Other concerns and clarity:
- The approach requires self-similar activations (e.g., ReLU), limiting generality.
- CapNorm is ad hoc but plausible; however, without ablations it’s difficult to judge whether simpler alternatives (e.g., BN with fixed scale/offset or frozen statistics) would suffice.
- Sensitivity to hyperparameters (λ, αr, αφ, unit addition rate) appears high; more guidance is needed.

4) Additional feedback and suggestions (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Provide clean ablations:
  • Nonparametric with/without CapNorm (try BN with frozen affine parameters, or normalization on pre-activations with clipped scale).
  • Nonparametric with AdaRad vs RMSprop/Adam (ensuring shrinkage schedules are handled fairly).
  • Group-penalty p=2 vs p=1; fan-in vs fan-out; self-similar vs non-self-similar activations.
- Ensure apples-to-apples baselines:
  • Train fixed-width parametric networks using the same optimizer and normalization as the nonparametric model (e.g., AdaRad + CapNorm but without growth/removal).
  • Compare against group-lasso structured pruning methods applied during training to fixed overparameterized nets (same total training budget).
  • Include recent dynamic growth/pruning baselines and a BO/NAS baseline under a matched compute budget.
- Report compute:
  • Number of epochs, total gradient steps, FLOPs, and wall-clock time for all methods. Quantify the overhead of AdaRad and the growth/removal mechanics.
- Expand scope:
  • Apply to convolutional models on CIFAR-10/100 or other vision benchmarks; even shallow convs would help. Show whether width adaptation works in CNNs and interacts well with BN.
- Poker dataset sanity checks:
  • Clarify splits, deduplication, and leakage prevention. A 0% test error on 100k examples is unusual; include confusion matrices and robustness checks.
- Training protocol:
  • The “rewind and anneal” schedule is elaborate; consider a simpler, predetermined schedule, and report sensitivity.
  • Provide heuristics for setting αr relative to λ beyond the fixed αr=150/λ rule; show sensitivity curves.
- Theory:
  • Discuss whether similar finite-minimum guarantees extend to convolutional layers and other common architectural components.
  • Comment on generalization: does the group penalty bias to smaller solutions that generalize better, or can it over-shrink (as on convex)?
- Reproducibility: Release code and scripts; clarify how zero-unit detection and numerical thresholds are handled.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide ablations quantifying the separate contributions of CapNorm and AdaRad within the nonparametric framework?
- How sensitive is performance to ν (unit addition rate/frequency), αr, αφ, and λ? Are there practical default ranges?
- Can you compare to a fixed-width network trained with the exact same optimizer/normalization (AdaRad+CapNorm) and identical training budget?
- For poker, what are the exact train/val/test splits, and how did you guard against duplicate hands or leakage? Is 0% test error reproducible across seeds?
- Have you tried p=1 group penalties or mixed fan-in/fan-out penalties? Any qualitative differences?
- What is the wall-clock overhead of AdaRad vs RMSprop/Adam in your implementation, and how does the growth/removal frequency affect it?
- Do results transfer to CNNs on CIFAR or similar? If not tried, what obstacles do you foresee?

Overall, the paper presents an appealing idea with an interesting theoretical justification and promising elements, but it needs more thorough, controlled empirical validation and stronger baselines before it meets the bar for ICLR.
