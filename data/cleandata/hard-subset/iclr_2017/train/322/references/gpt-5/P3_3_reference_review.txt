Summary and contributions
- Problem: The paper tackles automatic selection of network width during training, avoiding expensive black-box model selection. It proposes to treat the number of hidden units as part of the optimization variable and to add/remove units on the fly.
- Core ideas: (1) A theoretical guarantee (Theorem 1) that, under fan-in or fan-out group-ℓp regularization (p < ∞, λ > 0), the training objective attains a global minimum at finite width. This gives a principled basis for nonparametric width selection. (2) A practical training scheme that alternates gradient steps with adding zero units and removing units whose fan-in/out have shrunk to zero, exploiting f-equivalence when σ(0) = 0. (3) A self-similar nonlinearity requirement (e.g., ReLU) and Proposition 1 showing that with self-similar layers, per-layer regularization strengths can be consolidated into a single λ by rescaling. (4) CapNorm, a batch-normalization variant that avoids neutralizing the norm-based regularizer by only downscaling when batch std > 1 and removing learned affine parameters. (5) AdaRad optimizer that decomposes each fan-in gradient into radial (parallel) and angular (orthogonal) components, normalizes only the angular component, and uses a separate step size for angular vs radial directions; a momentum variant (AdaRad-M) is provided.
- Empirics: Experiments on MNIST, rectangles-images, convex, and OpenML poker with two fully connected hidden layers (or four for poker). Nonparametric (NP) models often outperform same-size parametric (P) models and can be competitive with random-search-tuned P models; however, NP underperforms on convex.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) Empirical validation is limited, inconsistent, and lacks comparisons to strong, directly relevant baselines for width learning/pruning/growing; improvements are dataset-dependent and fail on convex. 2) The practical recipe is heavy (CapNorm + AdaRad + hand-tuned unit-addition/removal schedule + rewinding), raises fairness concerns in comparisons, and the gains over simpler, established methods are not convincingly demonstrated.

Supporting arguments
- Theory vs practice: Theorem 1 is a nice existence result under group-norm regularization, ensuring a finite optimum. However, it does not inform the dynamics of the proposed algorithm (no convergence or approximation guarantees for the add/remove procedure), and the practical method relies on several ad hoc design choices (rate of unit addition ν, αr setting tied to λ, rewinding schedules).
- CapNorm: The need for CapNorm arises because standard BN makes scale-invariant penalties ineffective. While the rationale is sound, the comparison to parametric baselines is muddled: P models use BN (with λ = 0) while NP models use CapNorm with λ > 0. This confounds the effects of normalization and regularization. Alternatives such as decoupled weight decay (AdamW), weight normalization, layer normalization, or penalties on scale parameters (as in Network Slimming) could offer fairer and stronger baselines. There is no ablation showing that CapNorm is preferable to these options for NP training.
- Optimizer: AdaRad is interesting and well-motivated for group penalties, but no ablation shows that AdaRad (or AdaRad-M) is necessary: could RMSprop/Adam with a simple block-wise normalization and a proper proximal/group-shrink step suffice? The claimed compute/memory advantages over RMSprop are not backed by wall-clock/time-per-epoch or throughput statistics.
- Empirical scope: The main results are on small MLPs. The single large-scale poker result is positive but not diagnostic (binary, tabular). There is no evaluation on modern image/text tasks or convolutional settings where structure and scale matter. Importantly, the approach underperforms on convex, and the paper does not sufficiently analyze when/why NP converges to too-small models or how to detect/correct it.
- Missing baselines and related work: The paper situates itself among pruning and NAS, but omits or does not compare to several closely related width-adaptation approaches that leverage group penalties or BN scales, e.g., Network Slimming (Liu et al., 2017), MorphNet (Gordon et al., 2018), L0 regularization (Louizos et al., 2018), dynamic sparse training (RigL/Evci et al., 2020), grow-and-prune frameworks (NeST/Dai et al., 2017), or differentiable architecture search (DARTS/Liu et al., 2019). These works provide strong empirical baselines and often simpler pipelines.
- Reproducibility and complexity: The training protocol uses multiple rewinds, step-size annealing, and hand-crafted αr = 150/λ to give ≈50 epochs of “grace” per added unit. The sensitivity to these hyperparameters is unclear. The unit addition schedule (one per epoch) is acknowledged as slow; no guidance is given for scaling to deeper/wider nets beyond poker.

Constructive feedback to improve the paper
- Strengthen baselines: Compare against modern width-adaptation/pruning methods that use group penalties and BN scales (Network Slimming, MorphNet), L0-based sparsity, and dynamic sparse training during training. Include an apples-to-apples comparison where both NP and P models use the same normalization family (e.g., LayerNorm or WeightNorm) and decoupled weight decay.
- Ablations: (1) CapNorm vs BN vs WeightNorm/LayerNorm; (2) AdaRad vs RMSprop/Adam with proximal/group shrinkage; (3) effect of p = 1 vs p = 2; (4) effect of ν, νfreq, and initial width; (5) eliminating rewinds to show robustness. Report wall-clock time and memory for all algorithms.
- Fairness and clarity in comparisons: Avoid mixing normalization and regularization regimes across NP vs P. If BN truly breaks the regularizer, use a shared alternative for both (e.g., CapNorm or LayerNorm) to isolate the effect of nonparametric training.
- Scale and domains: Demonstrate on convolutional networks and at least one modern image benchmark (e.g., CIFAR-10/100) to show generality. Evaluate in settings where width matters and overfitting is nontrivial.
- Theory-practice link: Provide analysis of the add/remove dynamics (e.g., stability, expected time to prune/grow under simple assumptions). Consider proximal updates for the group penalty (closed-form group shrink), which may yield a cleaner optimizer than the current radial/angular decomposition.
- Release code and detailed hyperparameter settings to improve reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
1) How sensitive is performance to αr, αφ, ν, and the rewinding schedule? Can you provide robustness plots and a default setting that works across datasets without tuning?
2) Can you provide wall-clock time, throughput, and memory comparisons for AdaRad vs RMSprop/Adam, including the overhead of unit addition/removal?
3) How would your method perform with LayerNorm or WeightNorm and decoupled weight decay, removing the need for CapNorm? Have you tested parametric models with CapNorm to decouple normalization effects?
4) Why not use a proximal gradient step for the group-ℓ2 penalty (group shrink) with standard optimizers, avoiding a custom optimizer?
5) What causes failure on the convex dataset? Is it premature pruning due to the group penalty? Could adaptive λ or a different unit-addition schedule fix it?
6) How does the method extend to convolutional layers? Do you regularize per-channel fan-ins/outs, and how do you manage zero-channel addition/removal while preserving f-equivalence with BN/CapNorm?
7) Can you compare to methods that learn width via BN scales (Network Slimming) and MorphNet on at least one shared benchmark?

Overall, the paper presents an appealing conceptual framework with a solid existence result and thoughtful optimizer/normalization design, but the empirical evidence and baseline comparisons are not yet strong enough for acceptance.
