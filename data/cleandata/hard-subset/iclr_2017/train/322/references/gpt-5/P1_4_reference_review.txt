Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes “nonparametric neural networks,” a framework that jointly optimizes the weights and the width of each hidden layer during a single training run. The key idea is to add zero units (which are f-equivalent and do not change the function) and remove units whose fan-in/out becomes exactly zero under a group-regularizer, thereby growing and shrinking the network on the fly.
- Theoretical contribution: Theorem 1 shows that with fan-in or fan-out group regularization (λ > 0, 1 ≤ p < ∞), the nonparametric objective attains a global minimum at a finite dimensionality, addressing the concern that the best model might be “infinitely wide.” A proposition shows that for self-similar nonlinearities (e.g., ReLU), per-layer regularization strengths reduce to a single effective λ up to rescaling.
- Algorithmic contributions: (1) CapNorm, a variant of batch normalization that avoids neutralizing the regularizer by scaling only when batch std > 1 (and without learnable affine parameters). (2) AdaRad, an optimizer that decomposes the gradient for each fan-in vector into radial (parallel) and angular (orthogonal) components, normalizing only the angular component to facilitate learning for newly added units while preserving the effect of shrinkage. A momentum variant (AdaRad-M) is described.
- Empirics: On MNIST and Rectangles-Images, nonparametric nets outperform same-sized parametric nets and are competitive with a broader random search over sizes/optimizers; on Convex they underperform. On a large OpenML Poker dataset, the method scales and achieves strong results (including one run with 0% test error on 100k examples). The paper provides detailed analysis of the training dynamics and layer size evolution.

Decision: Reject (borderline)
Key reasons:
- The empirical validation is limited and uneven: improvements over parametric baselines are modest and dataset coverage is narrow (MLPs on classic datasets; one large tabular dataset), with a notable failure case on Convex. There are no comparisons against strong, closely related baselines for dynamic network sizing/pruning/growing.
- The methodology involves several moving parts (CapNorm, AdaRad, grow/prune schedules, rewinding), but ablations and fair apples-to-apples baselines (e.g., with standard optimizers or alternative norms/regularizers) are insufficient to isolate which pieces are necessary and to substantiate the claimed advantages.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and positioning: The high-level goal—to avoid expensive black-box model selection by learning the size during training—is timely and well-motivated. The paper thoughtfully connects to pruning, hyperparameter optimization, and earlier constructive/growing networks. However, the empirical comparison to modern dynamic/pruning methods (e.g., structured sparsity, dynamic network surgery, L0/L1 group regularization with standard optimizers, Net2Net) is missing.
- Theory: Theorem 1 is interesting and nontrivial; the proof appears technically careful and shows that group penalties constrain layer widths at any proper local minimum, implying existence of a global finite minimizer. Assumptions (directional differentiability, bounded derivatives on compact intervals) are reasonable and encompass commonly used activations (with σ(0)=0). The proposition on self-similar nonlinearities is a nice practical insight. While the theorem is about empirical-risk plus penalty (not generalization), it directly supports the central modeling choice.
- Algorithms: CapNorm addresses a real interaction (BN can negate scale penalties). The design is simple, plausible, and practically motivated. AdaRad’s angular/radial decomposition is intuitive and may reduce sensitivity for newly added units while keeping group shrinkage effective. However, novelty over existing per-parameter normalizations and weight-norm/direction-preserving updates is incremental, and there is no direct comparison to simpler alternatives (e.g., Adam/RMSprop with appropriate scaling of the penalty, layer norm/group norm, weight normalization, or freezing BN’s affine parameters).
- Experiments: The evaluation protocol shows effort (multiple seeds, size-matched comparisons, random search for param nets). Still, (1) the selection of a “typical representative” nonparametric model and then matching size can bias comparisons; reporting medians over seeds for both sides would be more convincing. (2) The Convex dataset underperformance suggests sensitivity to λ/size dynamics—this needs deeper investigation. (3) The Poker result with 0% test error on 100k examples is surprising; a sanity check for leakage, splits, or target definition would strengthen credibility. (4) No convolutional experiments or modern vision tasks, where adaptive width is highly relevant. (5) Ablations (CapNorm off, AdaRad vs Adam/RMSprop, p=1 vs p=2, fan-in vs fan-out) are missing; these are crucial to validate which components matter.

Suggestions for improvement (constructive, not central to the decision)
- Provide stronger, controlled baselines: dynamic network surgery, structured sparsity methods with group-lasso under standard optimizers, Net2Net growth, and pruning-from-scratch methods. Include ablations for CapNorm, AdaRad, the unit-addition schedule, and p=1 vs p=2.
- Report results as medians and confidence intervals over seeds for both nonparametric and parametric runs (not a handpicked representative), especially when comparing same-sized models.
- Expand to CNNs and at least one modern vision benchmark; the framework should naturally extend to convolutional channels/filters. Show that the theory and training scheme still apply and yield practical benefits.
- Clarify and justify “rewinding” procedures for fair comparisons (use equivalent schedules for param/nonparam), and discuss computational cost wall-clock vs baselines.
- Analyze sensitivity to λ, αr, αφ, and the unit addition rate ν; provide practical default recommendations.
- Consider comparing AdaRad to weight normalization or projection-based optimizers that explicitly control direction/scale.
- Release code and scripts to ensure reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
1. Fairness of comparisons: Why select a single “typical representative” NP model for size-matched comparisons instead of reporting median performance at fixed sizes across seeds for both NP and parametric models?
2. Ablations: What is the performance of NP training with CapNorm replaced by BN without affine parameters? How much does AdaRad contribute vs Adam/RMSprop when the penalty term’s shrinkage step is decoupled appropriately?
3. Regularizer choice: Did you try p=1 (sparser weights) or fan-out penalties? How do these affect convergence speed, final size, and accuracy?
4. Sensitivity: How sensitive are results to αr, αφ, ν, and the addition frequency? Any adaptive schedule that reduces the need for tuning?
5. Poker result: Can you provide details on the data splits and checks against leakage? How robust is the 0% test error across seeds/splits?
6. Convex dataset: Can you diagnose why NP converges to too-small models? Would annealing λ or an adaptive λ alleviate this without manual intervention?
7. Computational trade-offs: What is the wall-clock overhead of AdaRad + grow/prune relative to standard training plus random search over widths, for comparable final accuracy?

Overall, this is a creative and promising direction with a solid theoretical underpinning, but it needs stronger empirical validation and clearer ablations to meet the ICLR bar.
