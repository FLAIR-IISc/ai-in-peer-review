- Proposal: "Nonparametric neural networks" that adapt layer widths during one training run by adding zero-initialized units and pruning units whose fan-in/out weights shrink to exactly zero under a group-norm penalty.
- Theory: Theorem 1 states that with a fan-in or fan-out group-ℓp penalty (λ>0, 1≤p<∞) the training objective has a global minimum at finite dimensionality, preventing unbounded growth.
- CapNorm: A batch-normalization variant that subtracts mean, caps variance normalization at 1, and removes affine rescaling so regularization cannot be neutralized by BN.
- AdaRad optimizer: Decomposes each fan-in gradient into radial (parallel) and angular (orthogonal) components, normalizes only the angular part, and uses separate radial/angular step sizes to help new units learn under shrinkage.
- Grow-and-prune schedule: Periodically add new units with random fan-in and zero fan-out; prune units whose fan-in becomes zero under the ℓ2 penalty.
- Empirical evaluation: Experiments on small two-hidden-layer MLPs (MNIST, rectangles-images, convex) and a large Poker dataset; mixed results—wins on MNIST and some cases, underperforms on convex, competitive with tuned parametric models in some settings.
- Decision: Reject—the empirical evaluation is limited and confounded, and the novelty relative to prior structured sparsity and growth/pruning work is incremental.
- Main critique: Confounded comparisons—nonparametric models use CapNorm+AdaRad while parametric baselines use BN + SGD/momentum/RMSprop/Adam, so improvements cannot be attributed cleanly to adaptive width.
- Missing ablations: No isolations of AdaRad vs RMSprop/Adam, CapNorm vs BN/no norm, or fan-in vs fan-out regularization impact.
- Sensitivity and robustness concerns: Method relies on hand-chosen schedules and hyperparameters (unit addition frequency, αr≈150/λ, angular annealing/rewinds) with unclear robustness; results show higher variance on some tasks.
- Incremental novelty: Similar ideas exist (group-lasso neuron pruning, growth/pruning pipelines); Theorem 1’s practical relevance is limited since global optimality isn’t attained in practice.
- Efficiency claims unsubstantiated: No wall-clock, FLOP, or parameter-update cost measurements for the nonparametric pipeline; training initially converges slower due to growth.
- Experimental scope limited: No convolutional architectures or modern benchmarks (e.g., CIFAR/ImageNet) to demonstrate scalability beyond small MLPs.
- Fairness issues: Need to apply comparable normalization and optimizers to parametric baselines (e.g., CapNorm with parametric nets) to avoid confounding factors.
- Method details requested: Formal analysis of CapNorm’s effect on scale invariance and regularization, and implementation/overhead details for the parallel/orthogonal gradient decomposition.
- Theory clarity requested: Clarify assumptions and tightness of Theorem 1, relation to known group-sparsity results, and how normalization layers affect theoretical claims.
- Reporting improvements: Report medians, dispersion, multiple seeds, and avoid cherry-picked representative runs; include calibration or confidence intervals.
- Concrete ablation suggestions: Fix an optimizer+normalization and compare parametric vs nonparametric, vary optimizer/normalization, test p=1 vs p=2, and quantify sensitivity to λ, αr, angular step, and unit addition rate ν.
- Benchmark/efficiency suggestions: Include conv nets and CIFAR-like datasets, report wall-clock time, parameter counts over time, and total parameter-update counts.
- Questions for authors: Provide ablations fixing optimizer/norm, sensitivity to ν/αr/fan-out initialization, wall-clock and compute costs vs parametric training with hypersearch, AdaRad vs RMSprop/Adam within nonparametric regime, extension to convolutional layers, CapNorm’s effect where BN-affine is critical, and why convex experiments converge to smaller-than-optimal sizes and whether tuning can fix it.
