Summary and contributions
- Concept: The paper proposes “nonparametric neural networks,” a framework that treats width as a learnable quantity during training. Width is adapted by adding zero-units and removing units whose fan-in or fan-out are shrunk to zero, yielding functionally equivalent transitions across architectures.
- Theory: With fan-in or fan-out group regularization (ℓp over groups, p ∈ [1, ∞)), the objective attains a global minimum at finite dimensionality. This is a clean and useful result that alleviates degenerate growth and justifies the formulation.
- Methods: Two training innovations are introduced to make the framework practical.
  - CapNorm: A capped batch normalization variant that subtracts the mean and only scales if the batch std > 1 (no learnable affine), preventing the regularizer from being neutralized by normalization.
  - AdaRad: An optimizer that decomposes the gradient into radial (parallel to fan-in) and angular (orthogonal) components; it normalizes only the angular part and performs angular/radial updates with separate step sizes, preserving the shrinkage behavior of the group regularizer while letting new units learn.
- Empirics: Fully-connected ReLU networks on MNIST, rectangles-images, convex, and a large tabular dataset (poker). Nonparametric nets usually outperform equal-sized parametric nets and sometimes match or exceed the best parametric model found by extensive search; they underperform on convex. The analysis section provides insightful diagnostics on growth/shrink dynamics.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Novel, well-motivated direction: a principled and practical framework for automatic capacity selection within a single training run, with a nontrivial theorem guaranteeing finite optimal width under group regularization.
- Useful new training tools (CapNorm, AdaRad) that address real obstacles (BN–regularization interaction; gradient normalization vs shrinkage) and may be of broader interest.

Supporting arguments
Strengths
- Conceptual originality: Treats architecture size as a continuous optimization target via regularization and f-equivalence, rather than outer-loop black-box search. The group regularizer’s role in creating/deleting units is elegant.
- Theoretical backbone: The finite-minimum theorem is technically solid and strengthens the framing beyond heuristic growing/shrinking methods.
- Methodological ingenuity: AdaRad’s radial–angular decomposition is a clever compromise that allows normalization without undermining ℓ2,1 shrinkage. CapNorm offers a simple, targeted fix for a subtle incompatibility with BN.
- Empirical signals: On MNIST and rectangles, nonparametric nets surpass same-sized parametric models and can be close to the best overall models with fewer parameters; the poker experiment demonstrates feasibility at scale.
- Transparency: Diagnostic plots illuminating unit life cycles and fan-in/out dynamics are valuable and make the mechanism believable.

Weaknesses and threats to validity
- Scope: All core results are on MLPs; no convolutional models or modern vision benchmarks. The approach’s impact would be far stronger with CNNs on CIFAR/ImageNet or transformers on standard NLP tasks.
- Ablations: It is hard to disentangle gains due to (i) adaptive width, (ii) AdaRad, (iii) CapNorm, and (iv) training schedules (rewinding, annealing). A systematic ablation is missing.
- Baseline fairness: Parametric baselines omit weight decay with BN (common in practice via decoupled weight decay, e.g., AdamW) and do not explore normalization alternatives; the nonparametric pipeline enjoys bespoke optimizer and normalization.
- Heuristics and hyperparameters: Unit addition frequency, initial fan-in norms, αr/αφ coupling to λ, and rewinding/annealing are somewhat ad hoc; sensitivity is only partially explored. The negative result on convex suggests brittleness.
- Efficiency: Although some per-minibatch complexity analysis is given, there is no wall-clock comparison versus competitive hyperparameter/architecture search regimes (e.g., partial training with early stopping or multi-fidelity BO).

Additional feedback (to improve the paper)
- Add ablations to isolate contributions:
  - Group-ℓ2 fan-in regularizer + standard optimizer (RMSprop/Adam/SGD) + simple add/remove vs full AdaRad/CapNorm.
  - CapNorm vs vanilla BN (no affine), LayerNorm, WeightNorm.
  - AdaRad vs RMSprop/Adam with careful treatment of shrinkage (e.g., gradient normalization applied only to orthogonal components, but with standard optimizers).
- Evaluate on at least one convolutional benchmark (CIFAR-10/100) with standard data augmentation; show that width adapts meaningfully in conv layers.
- Fairness: Include parametric baselines with decoupled weight decay (AdamW) and BN; or exclude decayed parameters from BN scale/shift when using weight decay. Consider weight decay for non-BN parameters to align with practical setups.
- Report wall-clock time and compute budget comparisons (e.g., vs random search with early stopping), not just number-of-ops scaling.
- Sensitivity: Provide curves for performance vs λ, αr, αφ, and unit addition rate; explore simple adaptive schedules (e.g., add units only when training loss plateaus; prune aggressively when validation loss degrades).
- Clarify CapNorm: Justify the threshold at 1; consider an adaptive or per-layer threshold. Compare to BN-without-scale and to simply freezing BN statistics for new units.

Questions for the authors
- How does the method perform with convolutional architectures on CIFAR/ImageNet? Do the finite-minimum guarantees and training dynamics carry over straightforwardly?
- Can you provide ablations showing the independent effect of AdaRad and CapNorm on both nonparametric and fixed-width training?
- How sensitive are results to the unit addition schedule (ν, νfreq) and to αr/αφ? Could you propose an automatic controller (e.g., gradient-norm-based or validation-triggered) to reduce tuning?
- Baseline fairness: How do results change if parametric baselines use decoupled weight decay with BN (e.g., AdamW), or BN without affine parameters, or LayerNorm?
- Efficiency: What is the wall-clock ratio vs random search with early stopping for selecting width? How does compute scale when seeking comparable final accuracy?
- Theory: Do your existence results extend to convolutional layers, and to common non-self-similar activations in intermediate layers? Any insights into convergence guarantees for the add/remove algorithm itself?

Overall, this is a bold and promising direction with solid theory and inventive training machinery. Despite limited scope and missing ablations, the novelty and potential impact justify acceptance.
