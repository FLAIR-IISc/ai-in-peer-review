Summary and contributions
The paper proposes “nonparametric neural networks,” a framework that treats layer widths as variables to be optimized during a single training run by dynamically adding and removing units. The key ingredients are: (1) a theoretical result (Theorem 1) showing that with fan-in/fan-out group-ℓp regularization (λ>0, 1≤p<∞), the training objective attains a global minimum at a finite network size; (2) a practical training scheme that adds zero-effect units and removes units whose fan-in/fan-out are shrunk to zero; (3) Capped Batch Normalization (CapNorm), a minor but clever modification to batch norm designed to avoid nullifying the effect of the group regularizer; and (4) a tailored optimizer, AdaRad, which decomposes gradients into “radial” and “angular” components at the level of neuron fan-ins and normalizes only the angular component to enable newborn units to learn. Experiments on MNIST, rectangles images, convex, and the large Poker dataset show that the proposed approach can discover reasonable widths, often matches or surpasses parametric baselines of the same size, and scales to a million-sample tabular dataset.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (i) The paper introduces a bold, conceptually novel framing—treating width as a nonparametric variable—with a clean existence theorem that justifies the formulation; (ii) a practical, end-to-end training recipe (CapNorm + AdaRad + unit birth/death) that embodies the idea and yields promising results without black-box hyperparameter search. While some components are heuristic and empirical coverage is limited, the direction is fresh and could stimulate follow-on work.

Supporting arguments
- Novelty and potential impact: The nonparametric lens and the theorem guaranteeing finite optimal width under group penalties provide conceptual clarity missing from prior pruning/growing approaches. AdaRad’s radial/angular decomposition aligned with group-ℓ2 shrinkage is a neat optimizer design that could be more broadly useful. CapNorm is a simple yet appropriate tweak to reconcile normalization with structured penalties. Together, these form a coherent pipeline.
- Motivation and positioning: The paper is well motivated: black-box HPO wastes training signal and time; pruning assumes a strong seed model. Here, structure is learned during training with a guarantee that the problem is well-posed. Related work on pruning, constructive algorithms, and NAS is discussed; Wei et al. (2016) is appropriately highlighted as inspiration. While the literature is vast, the paper makes a clear case for its distinct angle.
- Theoretical support: Theorem 1 is the right foundational statement: with group fan-in/out penalties, the nonparametric objective achieves a finite minimizer, removing the risk of unbounded width. The proof is long but appears technically sound; the key lemmas bound activations/gradients and then use the penalty geometry to bound width. Proposition 1 about self-similar nonlinearities and layer-wise λ equivalence is a useful invariance that simplifies practice.
- Empirical support: Results on MNIST and rectangles images show that nonparametric training can produce models that outperform fixed-width models of the same size and approach the best parametric models discovered via search, while using fewer parameters. The Poker experiment demonstrates the approach scales and can be competitive on large data without prior architecture choices. The failure on convex is candid and informative, suggesting limits and avenues for improving addition/elimination schedules or regularization.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Ablations and component analysis: Please provide ablations isolating CapNorm, AdaRad, and unit birth/death. For example: (a) NP + SGD vs NP + AdaRad; (b) NP + BN (without caps) vs NP + CapNorm; (c) fixed-width + group-ℓ2 (no unit addition) + AdaRad vs full NP. This would clarify which pieces drive gains.
- Baseline breadth/fairness: Consider comparing against: (i) fixed-width group-lasso (ℓ2,1) with iterative pruning and retraining; (ii) recent pruning during training methods; (iii) simple heuristic width growth (e.g., grow when gradient norms saturate) without regularizer-driven shrinkage. Also report wall-clock/compute costs for NP vs parametric tuning to substantiate the “single-run” advantage.
- Convex dataset behavior: Analyze why NP underperforms (e.g., strong bias toward smaller widths due to λ/αr; insufficient unit addition rate; optimization stalling?) and show that adjusting λ or ν can recover performance. A sensitivity study would help.
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: The scheme relies on αφ, αr, λ, ν, and CapNorm behavior. Provide guidance (plots) showing performance vs these knobs, and how Proposition 1 helps reduce per-layer λ tuning in practice.
- Clarity on unit initialization and gradient flow: You initialize new units with random fan-in and zero fan-out. Although the fan-in initially gets no loss gradient, the fan-out does receive gradient and becomes non-zero after one step; then fan-in starts learning. A brief derivation to make this explicit would improve clarity.
- Complexity and memory: The paper claims AdaRad scales with number of units (not weights). Please include a concrete per-epoch cost and memory comparison to RMSprop/Adam on your MLPs, and, if possible, wall-clock measurements.
- Scope: While the focus on MLPs is acceptable for an initial study, even a small convolutional experiment or a discussion on extending group penalties and AdaRad to conv kernels (fan-in groups per output channel) would increase relevance.

Questions for the authors
1. Component importance: How much do CapNorm and AdaRad contribute individually? Can NP with standard BN (without learned scale/shift) and RMSprop approach your results if λ is tuned?
2. Group norm choice: You focus on p=2 (group-ℓ2). Did you try p=1 (sparser weights) or mixed norms? How do they affect stability, network density, and performance?
3. Failure modes: On convex, can you recover parity with parametric models by increasing ν or reducing αr to delay eliminations? Is the underperformance due to premature shrinkage, insufficient capacity at early phases, or optimizer dynamics?
4. Computational budget: Relative to the best parametric baseline found via random search, how much total compute did NP training consume (including periods before width stabilizes)? Any speedups possible via batched unit additions or more aggressive pruning?
5. Generality: Any obstacles to applying the method to CNNs (e.g., choosing groups, handling BN/CapNorm in convs) or to learning depth (e.g., adding/removing whole layers)?
6. Theory to practice: The theorem guarantees a finite minimizer under the penalty, but do you observe consistent convergence of width across seeds/λ? Any empirical signs of multiple local optima with quite different sizes?

Overall, I find the paper’s core idea fresh and its theoretical/practical integration compelling. With a stronger ablation and expanded baselines, this could be a very influential direction.
