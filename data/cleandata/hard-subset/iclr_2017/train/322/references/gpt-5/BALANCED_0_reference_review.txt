1) Summary (positive and generous)
This paper proposes “nonparametric neural networks,” a framework that adapts the width of an MLP during a single training run by adding zero units and removing units whose fan-ins/fan-outs are shrunk to zero. The key theoretical result (Theorem 1) shows that with a per-unit group regularizer (fan-in or fan-out, p∈[1,∞)), the training objective attains a global minimum at a finite width, justifying the formulation. The paper further introduces two training components tailored to this setting: (i) CapNorm, a variant of batch normalization that avoids canceling the effect of the per-unit regularizer; and (ii) AdaRad, an optimizer that decomposes gradients into radial (along the fan-in) and angular (orthogonal) components, normalizing the latter to help new units learn without negating shrinkage. A proposition shows self-similar nonlinearities make layerwise regularization parameters effectively equivalent up to rescaling. Experiments on MNIST, rectangles images, convex, and a large OpenML poker dataset demonstrate that the approach can discover reasonable widths and sometimes outperform parametric baselines of the same size or even a random search over sizes. The paper includes insightful analyses of training dynamics (unit survival, fan-in/out norms) and provides detailed protocols.

2) Decision
Reject. Key reasons:
- Empirical evidence is limited and missing strong, directly comparable baselines; improvements are inconsistent across datasets, with a clear failure on convex.
- Novelty and significance are moderate: the core ingredients (group sparsity, growth/pruning, normalized training) are known; the main contributions (CapNorm, AdaRad, and the finite-width existence theorem) are interesting but not yet convincingly shown to provide robust, state-of-the-art benefits.

3) Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper addresses selecting network width during training, a relevant and challenging problem. Positioning relative to pruning, growing, and NAS literature is reasonable, and the motivation for per-unit group regularizers and self-similar nonlinearities is clear.
- Technical soundness: Theorem 1 (existence of finite-width minimizers with fan-in/out regularization) is a solid and useful insight, with assumptions spelled out and a substantial proof. Proposition 1 is neat and clarifies why ReLU-like activations help. However, there is no convergence or optimality guarantee for the proposed training scheme (unit addition/removal + AdaRad/CapNorm), so the theory does not tightly support the practical algorithm’s claims.
- Algorithms: CapNorm is a pragmatic tweak to BN to preserve the effect of group-regularization; AdaRad’s radial/angular decomposition is conceptually appealing and matches the geometry induced by group shrinkage. Both are plausibly helpful for new-unit training under shrinkage. That said, the paper lacks ablations isolating CapNorm and AdaRad from the unit growth/removal mechanism, making it difficult to quantify their individual contributions versus standard optimizers or normalization.
- Empirics: Evaluations are conducted on shallow fully-connected networks and relatively simple datasets. Results are mixed: NP outperforms same-size parametric models on MNIST and (at small λ) rectangles images, but underperforms on convex. On poker, both NP and parametric nets do very well; NP slightly edges parametric in some settings. The claims that NP training can match/exceed black-box searches are not strongly established: comparisons rely on exhaustive grid/random searches within limited ranges and do not include structured sparsity baselines (e.g., group lasso pruning and retraining), dynamic network surgery, Net2Net-style growth, or modern NAS/regularization methods. Moreover, training protocols differ (CapNorm vs BN; explicit rewinding; growth schedule) and compute budgets are not aligned, complicating fairness. The manual selection of a “representative” NP model among 10 runs introduces potential selection bias.

4) Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all points affected my decision)
- Stronger baselines:
  - Fixed-width group-lasso (fan-in) with pruning and fine-tuning; compare performance and final sparsity/density.
  - Recent grow/prune or dynamic capacity methods; Net2Net-style growth; dynamic network surgery; modern structured sparsity methods.
  - Same normalization across methods (e.g., compare CapNorm to BN-without-affine and to no-normalization) and analyze the interplay with group regularization.
- Ablations:
  - Remove AdaRad (use RMSprop/Adam) while keeping growth/removal; remove CapNorm while keeping AdaRad; vary self-similar vs non-self-similar activations; quantify the effect of each component on learning speed, final size, and accuracy.
  - Sensitivity to ν (addition rate), νfreq, αr, αφ, and λ scheduling; show robustness across a range of settings.
- Fairness and compute:
  - Report wall-clock time, total steps, and number of forward/backward passes for NP vs parametric and search baselines; ensure equalized compute budgets when claiming advantages over black-box searches.
  - Avoid manual selection of “representatives”; report medians and confidence intervals across seeds for all methods, including NP.
- Scope and generality:
  - Include convolutional architectures and more challenging datasets; demonstrate that the approach scales beyond MLPs and simple datasets.
  - Discuss/measure how often group shrinkage exactly hits zero in float arithmetic, the thresholding used, and its impact on stability.
- Theory/practice connections:
  - Clarify whether CapNorm affects the assumptions or conclusions of Theorem 1.
  - Provide any convergence insights for the add/remove scheme under AdaRad, or empirical diagnostics for stability (e.g., oscillatory growth-prune cycles).
- Clarity and presentation:
  - Fix typos and minor formatting issues; improve Algorithm readability; release code to improve reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
- Baseline fairness: Why not compare against a fixed wide MLP with group-lasso fan-in regularization, prune zeroed units, and retrain/fine-tune? This seems the most direct competitor.
- CapNorm vs BN: Can you provide an ablation showing that BN-without-affine (or with frozen scale) truly negates the group-regularizer while CapNorm does not, and that CapNorm is necessary for your gains?
- Compute budget: How do NP vs parametric vs search baselines compare in GPU hours and total training steps? If we allocate the same compute to a simple black-box search, do NP networks still come out ahead?
- Sensitivity and robustness: How sensitive are results to the unit addition schedule (ν, νfreq), αr=150/λ heuristic, and the initial width? Any failures or instabilities?
- Generalization: Have you tried convolutional layers or deeper MLPs? Does AdaRad/CapNorm continue to help, and does the finite-width phenomenon persist?
- Theory: Does CapNorm preserve the assumptions in Theorem 1? Can you comment on whether the existence result suggests any guidance for choosing λ dynamically during training?

Overall, the paper has appealing ideas and a solid existence result, but the current empirical evidence and comparisons are not yet strong enough for acceptance. I encourage a revised version with stronger baselines, ablations, broader scope, and clearer compute-normalized comparisons.
