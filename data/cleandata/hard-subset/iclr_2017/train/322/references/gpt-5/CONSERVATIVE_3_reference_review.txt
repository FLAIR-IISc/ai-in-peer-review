Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes “nonparametric neural networks,” a training-time framework that adaptively grows and prunes hidden units, guided by a fan-in (or fan-out) group-regularization penalty. The central theoretical claim is that, with λ>0 and p∈[1,∞), the objective attains a global minimum at a finite dimensionality, thereby avoiding degenerate growth.
- Two implementation components are introduced to make this practical: (i) CapNorm, a modified batch normalization that prevents rescaling from negating the regularizer; and (ii) AdaRad, an optimizer that decomposes gradients into radial (parallel) and angular (orthogonal) components, normalizing only the angular part to let newly added units learn without being immediately shrunk out.
- Empirical results on MNIST, rectangles-images, convex, and a large “poker” dataset show mixed performance: clear wins over matched-size parametric nets on MNIST and rectangles (for some λ), clear underperformance on convex, and strong performance on poker. Analysis of training dynamics suggests the approach converges to a stable size and that the dual step sizes influence learning vs. pruning.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) The empirical validation is too limited and not convincingly benchmarked against strong, established baselines; (2) Claims about efficiency and accuracy benefits over black-box model selection and standard training are not substantiated with fair, comprehensive comparisons and wall-clock analyses.

Supporting arguments
- Limited and uneven empirical scope. All core experiments use shallow fully-connected architectures on small or synthetic datasets. The method aims to replace expensive architecture/width selection yet is not demonstrated on standard settings where width search matters (e.g., modern CNNs on CIFAR-10/100, ImageNet-scale subsets, or tabular benchmarks with well-established baselines). The convex dataset results show notable underperformance and instability, calling into question robustness.
- Baselines are not competitive enough or not comparable. The most relevant established baselines for “automatic width selection” are: (a) training an overprovisioned model with structured sparsity penalties (group lasso over fan-ins/outs) and proximal updates, optionally followed by pruning and finetuning; (b) modern pruning/NetAdapt/AutoSlim/Slimmable-type baselines; (c) architecture/width search using efficient methods (Hyperband/BOHB) with early stopping; and (d) decoupled weight decay optimizers (AdamW/SGDW) and proximal gradient for group sparsity. The paper does not compare to these, making it hard to attribute gains to the proposed innovations vs. known techniques. The comparison to “parametric networks of the same size” is informative but incomplete.
- Efficiency claims are not backed by wall-clock or compute budget analyses. The method repeatedly adds units, prunes, and uses multiple rewind/anneal phases, and the authors sweep λ and seeds to pick a representative. Meanwhile, parametric baselines receive grid/random search with many trials. There is no apples-to-apples accounting (GPU hours vs. accuracy). The claim that the approach can outperform black-box selection “in a single run” is not convincingly demonstrated given the hyperparameter exploration for λ and the number of runs.
- AdaRad and CapNorm are interesting but ad hoc and insufficiently ablated. It is not shown that AdaRad materially improves outcomes over simpler proximal/ISTA-style updates with group-lasso, normalized gradients (e.g., RMSProp/Adam variants) plus appropriate handling of regularization (e.g., AdamW-style decoupling), or weight normalization constraints. For CapNorm, alternatives like weight normalization, spectral norm constraints, or ordinary BN with decoupled penalties are not compared. The ablations provided do not isolate the necessity of either component.
- Theoretical result is plausible but incremental relative to known effects of group-sparsity regularizers. Showing that a group-lasso penalty over units yields existence of a finite-dimensional minimizer is valuable, but conceptually aligns with established structured sparsity literature. The heavy proof burden would be more compelling if it underpinned a method that consistently outperforms strong baselines on modern tasks.
- Terminology. Calling the approach “nonparametric” is potentially misleading in the statistical sense; the learned model is finite-dimensional and the procedure is a particular regularized training dynamic with unit birth/death. This may overstate the conceptual novelty.

Feedback to improve the paper (not all tied to the accept/reject decision)
- Add strong, modern baselines and standardized datasets:
  - CNNs on CIFAR-10/100 with width multipliers; compare to group-lasso with proximal updates on an overprovisioned network + pruning + finetuning; NetAdapt/AutoSlim/Slimmable Networks where applicable.
  - Use Hyperband/BOHB with early stopping as the black-box baseline; report accuracy vs. total compute.
  - Include decoupled weight decay (AdamW/SGDW) and proximal gradient for group lasso as optimizer baselines.
- Provide rigorous compute accounting (GPU-hours) and convergence-time plots for all methods; show final accuracy at equal compute budgets.
- Strong ablations:
  - With/without AdaRad; replace with AdamW+prox for fan-in group-lasso to test necessity.
  - With/without CapNorm; compare to BN with frozen affine parameters, weight normalization, or norm constraints.
  - p=1 vs p=2; fan-in vs fan-out; unit addition rates; effect of the “cap at 1” hyperparameter in CapNorm.
- Clarify the poker dataset setup. A 0% test error on 100k examples is surprising. Rule out data leakage, near-duplicate examples across splits, or leakage via preprocessing. Provide details on splits, shuffling, categorical encodings, and per-class metrics.
- Strengthen the narrative on when/why this approach helps. The failure on convex suggests sensitivity to the schedule/regularization; discuss diagnostics, failure modes, and guidelines.

Questions for the authors
1. Poker dataset: How were the splits constructed? Are there duplicates or near-duplicates across splits? What target definition and class balance are used? Can you share per-class metrics and confirm no leakage in preprocessing?
2. Why not compare against a standard group-lasso proximal gradient baseline on an overprovisioned net (ISTA/FISTA) with subsequent pruning + finetuning? This seems the most directly comparable established approach.
3. Can AdamW (or RMSProp/Adam with decoupled regularization) plus group-lasso proximal steps close the gap to AdaRad? What specific failure arises when using these established optimizers with your regularizer?
4. How sensitive is performance to the unit addition schedule and αr/αφ? Do you have an automatic tuning strategy for λ and schedules that does not require multiple runs?
5. Can you provide wall-clock compute comparisons (including your multiple runs over λ and seeds) versus Hyperband/BOHB on the same tasks?
6. For CapNorm, why cap at std>1 rather than using weight normalization or BN with frozen affine parameters? Any theoretical rationale or ablations?
7. Theorem assumptions: how do they relate to known existence results for group sparsity in neural networks? Is there a simpler argument leveraging proximal regularization and compactness over bounded-norm parameter sets?

Overall, the paper contains interesting ideas and a substantial theoretical appendix, but the empirical evidence and baseline comparisons fall short of the bar needed to recommend acceptance.
