Summary
The paper proposes “nonparametric neural networks,” which treat layer widths as variables to be optimized jointly with weights during a single training run. The core idea is to use a fan-in or fan-out group penalty (p-norm, especially p=2) that induces entire units to go exactly to zero, enabling on-the-fly deletion, while periodically adding zero-output units (with random fan-in) to allow growth. The authors prove that, under mild conditions, the regularized training objective attains a global minimum at finite width. They introduce two training components: (i) CapNorm, a batch-normalization variant that subtracts the batch mean and scales only if batch std>1, without learned affine parameters, to avoid nullifying the regularizer; and (ii) AdaRad, an optimizer that decomposes gradients into components parallel/orthogonal to the fan-in, normalizing only the orthogonal part. Experiments on MNIST, rectangles, convex, and a large “poker” dataset suggest that nonparametric models can reach strong accuracy while discovering compact widths; on two datasets they outperform same-size parametric baselines and in one they underperform.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) The empirical evidence is not yet convincing or fair enough to support the claims; comparisons, protocol, and ablations have several issues. (2) The presentation has many methodological and formatting inconsistencies that reduce confidence in correctness, clarity, and reproducibility.

Supporting arguments
- Novelty and positioning: Using group penalties on fan-in/out to prune units is well established (e.g., group lasso for NNs; Alvarez & Salzmann, Collins & Kohli). The paper’s theorem on existence of a finite-width global minimum under such penalties is a useful formalization, but the practical training method largely combines known elements (group regularization, adding zero units) with ad-hoc choices (unit-add schedule, CapNorm) and a new optimizer (AdaRad) lacking convergence guarantees. Related work on progressive growing/pruning and recent NAS/growth methods is cited but not compared empirically.

- Theory: Theorem 1’s conditions (directional differentiability, bounds on derivatives, error-gradient bounded when e≤S) seem crafted to make the existence proof go through; this is reasonable but specialized. The proof is long and hard to verify in detail, with nontrivial notational issues. The theory guarantees existence of a finite optimum of the regularized objective, not that the proposed training heuristic finds it or that test performance improves.

- CapNorm: The rationale (“BN would negate the regularizer”) is plausible if BN includes learned scale/shift, but the proposed fix is ad-hoc: max(σ,1) introduces a non-differentiable kink at σ=1; its statistical properties and gradient behavior are not analyzed. No ablation is provided to isolate CapNorm’s contribution vs standard BN without affine params (which the authors sometimes use for parametric baselines). It is unclear whether improvements stem from CapNorm rather than the nonparametric mechanism.

- AdaRad: The orthogonal/parallel decomposition is interesting, but the normalization scheme uses per-unit running averages and a global per-layer maximum (φmax/cmax), which implicitly couples units; the motivation for this choice and its effect on stability are not analyzed. No convergence results or comparisons to proximal-gradient methods for group lasso are provided. The pseudocode has formula/notation issues (e.g., φadj expression, missing stabilizer symbol, “bot not fastest,” indices like d0l) that hinder reproducibility.

- Unit addition/removal: The schedule (ν, νfreq) and αr=150/λ are highly heuristic; the paper claims ≈50 epochs grace per unit but provides no sensitivity analysis. Initializing fan-out to zero and fan-in randomly is reasonable (gradients will grow fan-out), but there is no empirical study of alternatives, and the interaction with CapNorm and AdaRad is only qualitatively described.

- Experimental protocol and fairness:
  - The nonparametric model uses CapNorm; parametric baselines use BN (with/without affine params). This confounds conclusions; a fair baseline should include parametric models with CapNorm as well.
  - The selection of a “typical representative” nonparametric model by manual inspection of median size and test error, followed by tuning parametric models at that fixed size, introduces selection bias. Later random search includes size, but the reporting focuses on top performers; aggregate statistics and significance are missing.
  - Datasets are small fully connected benchmarks; no convolutional experiments, despite the claimed practical relevance. The convex dataset results show clear underperformance; failure analysis is minimal.
  - The poker experiment reports a model with 0% test error on 100k examples, which is extraordinary and warrants careful checks for class/task definition, preprocessing, splits, and potential leakage. No ablations or baselines beyond simple references are shown.
  - No wall-clock or compute cost comparisons; claims about AdaRad’s cost scaling are not validated empirically.

- Clarity and formatting: The paper contains many typographical and reference issues (broken citations, stray symbols, inconsistent notation, overloaded σ for std/activation, algorithm typos, footnotes inline). This materially impairs clarity and trust.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Provide rigorous, controlled comparisons: parametric baselines with exactly the same normalization (CapNorm), optimizer variants (with/without AdaRad), and regularization. Include ablations isolating each component: group penalty p=2 vs p=1, CapNorm vs BN (with/without affine), AdaRad vs RMSprop/Adam with proper shrinkage handling, unit-add schedule.
- Report aggregate results over multiple seeds with confidence intervals; avoid manual selection of “representatives.” Include compute/time and memory overheads.
- Revisit poker claims: detail task definition, data splits, preprocessing, target imbalance, and verify no leakage. If 0% test error holds, provide analysis; otherwise correct.
- Improve pseudocode and notation; define all symbols (including the stabilizer), verify equations (e.g., φadj), and fix typos. Release code for reproducibility.
- Discuss convergence or at least training stability of AdaRad, and connect to proximal-gradient methods for group lasso.
- Evaluate on convolutional architectures and more modern benchmarks to demonstrate broader applicability.
- Tighten theory presentation; clarify the relation between σ(0)=0 required for f-equivalence and the theorem’s admissible nonlinearities.

Questions for the authors
1. Can you provide an ablation showing the effect of CapNorm vs standard BN without affine parameters in both parametric and nonparametric settings?
2. Please clarify the exact φadj formula in Algorithm 1, define the numerical stabilizer, and fix index inconsistencies; can you release code?
3. How sensitive are results to ν, νfreq, αr, αφ, and initialization (“expected length 1”): what is the exact distribution?
4. For the poker dataset, what is the precise task (binary vs multiclass), split protocol, and preprocessing? How do you explain 0% test error?
5. Can you compare to a proximal gradient (or block coordinate) method for group-lasso regularization with periodic unit-additions, and to recent growth/pruning baselines?
6. Do parametric baselines using CapNorm match or close the reported gaps?
