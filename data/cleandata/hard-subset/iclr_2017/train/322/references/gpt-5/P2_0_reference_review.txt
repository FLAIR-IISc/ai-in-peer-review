Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes “nonparametric neural networks,” a framework that adapts layer widths during a single training run by alternating between gradient-based weight updates and adding/removing hidden units. The main ingredients are:
  - A unit-wise group regularizer (fan-in or fan-out Lp, focusing on p=2, i.e., group lasso) that induces exact zero fan-ins/outs, enabling unit removal.
  - Addition of “zero units” (zero fan-out, random fan-in), which are f-equivalent at insertion time and can learn if useful.
  - CapNorm, a capped batch normalization variant that avoids negating the regularizer by not scaling when the batch std < 1 and removing learned scale/shift parameters.
  - AdaRad, an optimizer that decomposes gradients into components parallel (radial) and orthogonal (angular) to each unit’s fan-in, normalizing only the angular component to equalize feature learning while preserving the regularizer’s shrinkage effect on radial length.
- Theory: Theorem 1 proves that with fan-in or fan-out regularization (λ>0, 1≤p<∞), the objective attains a global minimum at finite dimensionality, addressing the concern that larger networks could indefinitely improve the objective. A proposition shows that with self-similar nonlinearities (e.g., ReLU), per-layer regularization strengths are equivalent up to rescaling.
- Empirics: On MNIST and rectangles images, nonparametric nets outperform parametric models of the same size and can be competitive with black-box search in accuracy while discovering smaller architectures; on convex, they underperform. On a large tabular dataset (poker), both parametric and nonparametric models reach <1% error; the best nonparametric achieves 0% test error.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) Empirical evidence is limited and mixed, with small-scale, fully connected benchmarks and a failure on convex; there is no demonstration on modern architectures/datasets (e.g., convolutional nets on CIFAR/ImageNet) or rigorous efficiency comparisons against contemporary hyperparameter/model selection baselines.
2) The proposed training pipeline introduces substantial complexity and multiple hyperparameters but lacks thorough ablations and wall-clock analyses to substantiate practical advantages over simpler pruning/growing or black-box search approaches.

Supporting arguments
- Novelty and motivation: The problem of adapting architecture size during training is important, and the use of group regularization to delete units with an explicit add/remove mechanism is sensible and well-motivated. AdaRad is a neat idea: separating radial and angular components elegantly aligns with the geometry induced by group lasso and the need to let new units learn without being immediately shrunk out. CapNorm is a minimalistic and principled tweak to BN to avoid undermining the regularizer. The theoretical result that a finite-width minimizer exists under these regularizers is reassuring (though unsurprising to some readers familiar with group-lasso behavior).
- Positioning: The paper cites pruning, dynamic network surgery, Net2Net, and NAS/AutoML lines, but empirical comparisons focus on standard parametric training with RMSprop/Adam rather than direct apples-to-apples to structured sparsity baselines (e.g., training with group lasso at fixed overparameterization then pruning) or to prior growing methods. This weakens the empirical case that the proposed end-to-end nonparametric training is superior.
- Experimental rigor:
  - Scope: Only fully connected models; no convolutional experiments despite CapNorm and the method being applicable in principle. Results on three small benchmarks and one tabular dataset are insufficient for ICLR-level claims about model selection in neural networks.
  - Mixed outcomes: The convex dataset underperformance is acknowledged, but not deeply probed. This calls into question robustness and when the approach helps vs hurts.
  - Protocol concerns: Choosing a “typical representative” nonparametric model manually and then matching parametric sizes introduces selection bias. For parametric baselines, regularization choices differ (no L2 with BN), whereas the nonparametric method critically relies on the fan-in regularizer plus CapNorm; confounding factors remain. No wall-clock or compute budget comparisons to black-box hyperparameter optimization or to simple width-sweeps are presented.
  - Ablations: Limited. Key components (CapNorm vs BN/LayerNorm/WeightNorm; AdaRad vs RMSprop/Adam with group lasso; unit addition rate and initialization; p=1 vs p=2; fan-in vs fan-out) are not systematically ablated, making it hard to attribute gains to specific design choices.
  - Poker result: Achieving 0% test error on a 100k-example holdout is surprising; possible data quirks or leakage should be discussed and verified.
- Clarity: The paper is detailed and generally clear; proofs are long but readable. Some references are loosely formatted, and the empirical section is heavy on protocol heuristics (rewinds, annealing) without enough analysis of sensitivity.

Suggestions to improve (not required for decision)
- Broaden the empirical evaluation:
  - Include convolutional networks on CIFAR-10/100 and compare against state-of-the-art pruning/growing baselines and simple width-sweep + early stopping. Provide wall-clock and compute metrics to support the single-run advantage claim.
  - Add ablations isolating CapNorm, AdaRad, unit-addition schedule, and regularizer p and direction (fan-in vs fan-out).
  - Compare against a strong group-lasso baseline trained in an overparameterized regime followed by pruning and fine-tuning.
  - Report fairness-controlled comparisons: same normalization, same data preprocessing, similar regularization where applicable.
- Provide time/compute trade-offs versus Bayesian optimization/random search for width selection and versus regular parametric training with early stopping.
- Analyze failure modes (e.g., convex). Is the method biased toward small widths due to the regularizer/αr setting? Can automatically tuning λ or αr mitigate this?
- Examine CapNorm versus BN/LayerNorm/WeightNorm under group regularization, and justify the no-parameter choice.
- Release code and hyperparameter defaults; reduce the number of hand-tuned knobs or provide automatic schedules.

Questions for the authors
1) How sensitive are results to αr, αφ, and the unit addition rate/schedule? Can you provide systematic ablations and guidance for setting them?
2) Why not include convolutional experiments? Are there implementation hurdles for fan-in/out regularization at the channel level and AdaRad in conv layers?
3) Can you compare directly to a group-lasso pruning pipeline trained in an overparameterized regime (same total compute), and to dynamic network surgery methods?
4) Can you report wall-clock time and GPU-hours relative to random/Bayesian search baselines for width selection, to support the “single run” efficiency claim?
5) On the poker dataset, how do you rule out leakage or accidental duplication between train and test, given 0% test error?
6) What causes the convex dataset underperformance? Is the method’s implicit size bias too strong? Would tuning λ online or using p=1 change the outcome?
7) How does CapNorm compare to BN without learned scale/shift, or to WeightNorm, under the same regularization?
