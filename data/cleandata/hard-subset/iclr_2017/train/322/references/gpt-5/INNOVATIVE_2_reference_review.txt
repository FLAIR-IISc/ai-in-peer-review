Summary and contributions
- Proposes “nonparametric neural networks,” a framework that treats hidden-layer widths as variables optimized during a single training run by adding/removing zero units while training weights with a group regularizer.
- Key theoretical result: with fan-in or fan-out group-ℓp regularization (λ>0, 1≤p<∞), the training objective attains a global minimum at finite dimensionality. This gives a principled basis for dynamically choosing width.
- Practical machinery to make this work:
  - CapNorm: a capped batch-normalization variant that centers activations but only scales when batch std>1 and uses no learnable affine parameters, preventing the regularizer from being negated by normalization.
  - AdaRad: an optimizer that decomposes gradients into radial (parallel) and angular (orthogonal) components, normalizes only the angular part, and applies group-ℓ2 shrinkage to fan-ins. This preserves the intended effect of the regularizer while letting new units learn.
- Empirical evaluation on MNIST, rectangles, convex, and a large OpenML poker dataset. The approach often matches or exceeds parametric baselines of the same size, sometimes achieving strong performance with fewer parameters. Analysis illustrates how width stabilizes and how units are added/removed over time.

Decision: Accept
Primary reasons:
1) Novel, coherent formulation of dynamic width selection with a clean theoretical guarantee that avoids “runaway width,” plus concrete training tools (CapNorm, AdaRad) that make the idea practical.
2) The framework opens a promising research direction (single-run, width-adaptive training) that is distinct from conventional black-box hyperparameter search or post-hoc pruning, and the initial results are encouraging.

Supporting arguments
- Conceptual novelty: Treating width as a continuous training-time choice via f-equivalent “zero-unit” moves and a group regularizer is a neat synthesis. The proof that a finite optimum exists under the proposed regularizers is valuable and nontrivial.
- Algorithmic novelty: AdaRad’s radial/orthogonal decomposition with selective normalization is well-motivated by the interaction with group shrinkage; CapNorm is a simple but clever tweak to preserve the effect of regularization.
- Practicality: The method operates in a single training run, potentially reducing the massive cost of black-box architecture search. The poker experiment suggests scalability in terms of examples and shows the overhead is manageable relative to total training.
- Transparency: The paper includes clear pseudocode, ablations (e.g., AdaRad vs AdaRad-M), and insightful diagnostics of how units survive/prune and how fan-in/out norms evolve.

Caveats (do not outweigh the novelty for me)
- Empirics are not as broad/deep as desired for ICLR: primarily MLPs; no CNNs or modern architectures; limited comparisons to recent dynamic/pruning/AutoML methods. The convex dataset failure suggests sensitivity to settings (λ, addition rate, schedules).
- Baselines: Comparison to parametric networks is careful, but omits strong structured-sparsity and growth baselines (e.g., group-lasso pruning with re-growth, Net2Net/morphism, DSD, modern pruning with re-training, variational dropout/L0, NAS/DARTS). Also, the BN vs CapNorm comparison raises questions about fairness and λ usage.
- Engineering choices: Performance seems sensitive to αr, αφ, λ, and the addition schedule ν; guidance and robustness analyses could be stronger.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions, not central to the accept decision)
- Baselines and scope:
  - Add comparisons to state-of-the-art pruning-with-growth or dynamic-width methods and a strong structured sparsity baseline (group-lasso on fan-ins/outs with a fixed-width model).
  - Include at least one convolutional setting (even small-scale) to demonstrate applicability beyond MLPs.
- Fairness and ablations:
  - Provide controlled ablations for CapNorm and AdaRad (e.g., AdaRad vs RMSprop/Adam under the same regularizer; CapNorm vs BN-without-affine vs no norm).
  - Report time-to-accuracy and wall-clock cost vs random/BO hyperparameter search for width to demonstrate efficiency claims concretely.
- Robustness/UX:
  - Analyze sensitivity to ν, νfreq, αr, αφ, and λ; offer heuristics for default choices. The rule αr=150/λ is interesting—show how it generalizes.
  - Discuss non-self-similar activations and their practicality; test leaky-ReLU/ELU to demonstrate the theory/guidance in practice.
- Theory:
  - Clarify the implications of Theorem 1 for overparameterized minima: does the group penalty avoid degenerate wide minima in practice? Are there conditions ensuring that optimization will reach a near-minimal width, not just that one exists?
  - Briefly discuss extending the proof idea to convolutional layers (filters as “units”) and to depth selection.
- Reproducibility: Consider releasing code and scripts; the training schedules (rewind, add/stop adding, λ→0, step-size annealing) are intricate.

Questions for the authors
1) How sensitive is performance and final width to the unit addition rate and schedule (ν, νfreq)? Could you adapt ν online (e.g., based on validation or gradient statistics) to reduce overhead?
2) CapNorm removes learnable affine parameters; does this ever harm expressivity/generalization compared to BN-without-negating-regularizer alternatives? Could fixed small-gain scaling mitigate slow learning in later layers?
3) AdaRad normalizes only the angular component. How does it compare to simply normalizing per fan-in without the radial/angular split but scaling the shrinkage consistently? Any failure cases where AdaRad harms convergence vs Adam/RMSprop?
4) The convex dataset underperformance: can you diagnose whether it is due to premature shrinkage, insufficient width growth, or optimization (afφ too small/large)? Does adjusting αr/λ or allowing temporary overgrowth fix it?
5) Extension to CNNs: do you view filters as units with fan-in group penalties per output channel? Any preliminary results or challenges (e.g., interaction with spatial BN and modern architectures)?
6) Could the global-minimum-at-finite-width theorem be strengthened to say something about the width at local minima under stochastic training, or about typical widths under realistic data assumptions?
7) Have you tried p=1 (sparse weights) variants with modern sparse kernels to see if the method can target hardware-efficient sparsity while retaining the dynamic-width benefits?

Overall, I find the idea bold and well-motivated, with solid theory and promising initial results. Despite empirical limitations, the direction is exciting and deserves a place at ICLR.
