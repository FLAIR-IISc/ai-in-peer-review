Summary
The paper proposes “nonparametric neural networks,” an approach to adapt the width of fully connected networks during a single training run by (i) adding/removing units based on group-regularization (fan-in/out Lp with p=2), (ii) using a custom normalization layer (CapNorm) to avoid the interaction between batch norm and the regularizer, and (iii) a novel optimizer (AdaRad) that separates updates into radial (length) and angular (direction) components, normalizing only angular components. Theoretically, the authors prove that with fan-in/fan-out group regularization the nonparametric optimization admits a global minimum at finite width. Empirically, they evaluate on MNIST, rectangles-images, convex, and a large “poker” dataset. They report that nonparametric networks can outperform parametric networks of the same size on some datasets and can be competitive with random search over sizes, though they underperform on the convex dataset.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) Insufficient empirical validation against established and directly relevant baselines; (2) Heavy reliance on several new, interacting components without adequate ablations or fairness controls; (3) Limited and somewhat idiosyncratic experimental protocol, leaving central claims (accuracy and efficiency of single-run model selection) unconvincing.

Supporting arguments
- Missing strong baselines. The most natural comparators—structured sparsity/group-lasso training with pruning (e.g., Alvarez & Salzmann; Wen et al.), dynamic network surgery (Guo et al.), grow-prune methods (Wei et al.), Net2Net/knowledge transfer, and modern hyperparameter/model-size search (e.g., BO, multi-fidelity)—are not included. The comparisons are mainly to standard parametric networks trained from scratch with batch norm and common optimizers. This leaves unclear whether the gains arise from the proposed “nonparametric” framework or from simply using group-lasso-style regularization with a better training protocol.
- Ablations are lacking. The method combines: group regularization, zero-unit add/remove, CapNorm, AdaRad, and specific unit addition schedules. There is no thorough ablation to quantify: CapNorm vs (a) batch norm variants that do not undo regularization (e.g., stop-gradient scale, fixed gamma; weight norm/layer norm), (b) AdaRad vs standard optimizers when made compatible with the regularizer, (c) the impact of unit addition rate/schedule and initial widths, (d) fan-in vs fan-out penalties. Without these, it is difficult to attribute improvements to the proposed innovations rather than confounding choices.
- Fairness issues. Nonparametric models use CapNorm; parametric baselines use standard batch norm without regularization (authors state L2 reg + BN is “not compatible”). This asymmetry may favor NP models. A more balanced setup would match normalization choices (e.g., CapNorm-like baseline or BN variants that do not negate regularization) and allow structured regularization in baselines.
- Experimental scope and rigor. Evaluations are on small, fully connected benchmarks; no convolutional models or modern image/NLP tasks. The “poker” 0% test error result is surprising and calls for careful checks of splits and leakage. Results depend on manually selected “representative” NP runs (median by eye across seeds), non-standard rewinding protocols, and considerable tuning differences across settings. No runtime or wall-clock budget comparisons are provided, despite claims about single-run efficiency. Figures and tables referred to (cost comparisons, detailed metrics) are not included here.
- Theory vs practice. The existence-of-minimum result under group regularization is mathematically plausible but primarily confirms boundedness of optimal width; it does not address optimization dynamics, convergence of the alternating add/prune process, or generalization. The practical utility of the theorem for choosing λ or schedules remains unclear.
- Mixed results. The method underperforms on the convex dataset and requires careful step-size/rate choices. Claims about achieving higher accuracy than parametric networks of the same size hold only on some tasks and are modest; without stronger baselines and tighter controls, the general claim is not substantiated.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper)
- Provide ablations: (i) CapNorm vs alternative normalizations designed to coexist with structured regularization; (ii) AdaRad vs RMSprop/Adam with matched handling of the shrinkage term; (iii) removal of unit addition (pure group-lasso pruning) vs full NP; (iv) fan-in vs fan-out; (v) effect of p=1 vs p=2.
- Strengthen baselines: compare to group-lasso training with pruning and re-training; dynamic network surgery; grow-prune methods; and hyperparameter/model-size search under matched training budgets. Include wall-clock and GPU-hour comparisons for single-run NP vs search.
- Expand experimental scope: CNNs on CIFAR-10/100 (with BN alternatives), at least one larger vision/NLP task. Report statistical significance across many seeds with confidence intervals.
- Clarify fairness: ensure identical normalization families across methods; if BN is incompatible with your regularizer, provide an equally competitive normalization for parametric baselines.
- Report computational overhead: per-step time, memory, total time-to-accuracy, and time-to-model-selection (stabilized width).
- Reproducibility: release code and hyperparameter search spaces; avoid manual “representative” selection by predefining metrics (median across seeds).

Questions for the authors
1) How sensitive is performance and final width to ν (unit addition rate), νfreq, αr, αφ, and the choice of initial widths? Can you provide robustness plots and guidelines?
2) Can you provide ablations quantifying the isolated impact of CapNorm and AdaRad? Are similar gains achievable with standard optimizers if the shrinkage term is handled carefully?
3) For fairness, can you compare against parametric baselines using CapNorm (or an equivalent BN variant that does not undo regularization) and with group-lasso regularization plus pruning?
4) On the poker dataset, how are train/validation/test splits constructed? How many classes and what is the label noise? Can you reproduce the 0% test error across seeds? This result seems unusually strong and merits scrutiny.
5) What are the wall-clock time and compute budget comparisons vs (a) training fixed-size networks selected by random/BO search under the same budget and (b) structured-pruning pipelines?
6) How does the method perform on convolutional architectures? Are there obstacles to applying CapNorm/AdaRad with conv layers?
7) Can you report stability of the add/prune dynamics (oscillations, premature pruning) and any convergence guarantees beyond the existence of a finite optimum?
8) Does the self-similarity requirement restrict usefulness beyond ReLU-family activations? How does the method fare with tanh/gelu/SiLU when using your λ-equalization proposition?

Overall, the ideas are interesting and the theoretical boundedness result is neat, but the empirical evidence is not yet convincing enough against established alternatives, and the experimental methodology needs strengthening for a confident recommendation.
