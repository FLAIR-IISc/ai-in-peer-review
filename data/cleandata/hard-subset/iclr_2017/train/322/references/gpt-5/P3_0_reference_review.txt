Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes “nonparametric neural networks,” i.e., training procedures that adapt the width of each hidden layer during a single run by (i) adding zero-output units and (ii) pruning units whose fan-in (or fan-out) is driven exactly to zero via a structured Lp penalty. The central theoretical claim (Theorem 1) shows the training objective attains a global minimum at finite dimensionality when using fan-in or fan-out group penalties (λ > 0, 1 ≤ p < ∞). A scaling-invariance proposition (for self-similar nonlinearities like ReLU) shows layer-wise regularization strengths can be consolidated.
- Two algorithmic components are introduced to make this practical: (a) CapNorm, a capped batch normalization variant that avoids negating the regularizer via arbitrary rescaling, and (b) AdaRad, an optimizer that decomposes the gradient into radial (parallel to the fan-in) and angular (orthogonal) components, normalizing only the latter to allow newly added units to learn while respecting group shrinkage. A momentum variant (AdaRad-M) is given.
- Experiments on MNIST, rectangles-images, convex, and a large tabular dataset (poker) show that the approach can grow networks from scratch, prune them to a data-driven width, and sometimes outperform parametric baselines of the same size; performance is mixed across datasets. Analysis of the growth/pruning dynamics is provided.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) The empirical evidence is limited to small MLPs on relatively simple datasets, with no convincing evaluation on modern architectures or challenging benchmarks. Claims about outperforming black-box selection or achieving better accuracy than same-size parametric models are not robustly supported.
2) Important ablations/comparisons are missing, making it hard to attribute gains to the proposed components (CapNorm vs alternatives, AdaRad vs existing proximal/normalized methods). The fairness of baselines and the experimental protocol (e.g., “typical representative” selection, hyperparameter tuning asymmetries) are concerns.

Supporting arguments
- Novelty and theory: The existence result (finite optimum width under group penalties) is sound and well motivated, connecting to group-lasso ideas applied at the unit level. The scaling proposition for self-similar nonlinearities is insightful and practically useful. However, this does not provide guarantees about the training dynamics or convergence of the proposed algorithm to such optima, and the proof’s scope (directional differentiability) is broader than needed but leads to heavy exposition.
- Methodology: The add/remove scheme via f-equivalence (adding zero fan-out units) is a simple and correct mechanism to traverse architectures while preserving function value at insertion. Using group-l2 on fan-in to prune entire units aligns with hardware efficiency (dense matmuls). AdaRad’s radial/angular decomposition is interesting and addresses an important tension: new units receive small loss gradients and are at risk of being pruned prematurely under group shrinkage; normalizing only the angular component gives them a learning window. CapNorm’s design to avoid trivial rescaling is reasonable, but the trade-off of removing learnable BN affine parameters may harm representational capacity; this is not explored.
- Empirical evaluation: 
  - Datasets and models are limited. No convnets, no ResNets/CIFAR/ImageNet-like tasks, and no sequence models. The poker result is strong but on tabular data where powerful tree/GBDT baselines would be informative. 
  - Baselines: Parametric baselines are reasonably tuned, but comparisons are not apples-to-apples: NP uses structured regularization and CapNorm; parametric models use BN and λ=0 (since standard BN conflicts with L2). A proximal group-l2 baseline (with standard optimizers) is missing. The “exhaustive random search” is not clearly bounded against the NP method’s design space, and the conclusion that NP can beat black-box methods is not substantiated convincingly.
  - Ablations: There is no ablation isolating (i) AdaRad vs RMSprop/Adam with per-fan-in normalization or weight normalization, (ii) CapNorm vs other normalization strategies (e.g., BN with fixed gamma/beta, weight norm, LayerNorm), (iii) fan-in vs fan-out penalties, (iv) effect of the unit addition schedule, and (v) the necessity of self-similar nonlinearities beyond ReLU. Wall-clock/compute overhead is not reported.
  - Statistical rigor: Results are reported for “typical representatives,” and ranges are shown, but broader summary statistics (mean/median across runs with CIs) and standardized training budgets would strengthen claims. On convex, the method underperforms and tends to converge to overly small models; diagnosing this would be valuable.
- Writing and presentation: The paper is generally clear but long; the proofs are dense. There are numerous reference formatting issues and typos. Algorithm pseudocode readability could be improved.

Constructive feedback to improve the paper
- Strengthen empirical validation:
  - Include convnet experiments (e.g., CIFAR-10/100) with channel growth and compare to pruning/growth baselines (structured sparsity/group-lasso pruning, dynamic network surgery, Net2Net, modern width scaling). Report final accuracy vs parameter count vs wall-clock.
  - Add ablations: AdaRad vs RMSprop/Adam/AdamW with proximal group-l2; CapNorm vs BN variants (e.g., freezing scale/shift, stop-gradient through BN statistics), WeightNorm/LayerNorm; p=1 vs p=2; fan-in vs fan-out.
  - Provide a proximal formulation (group soft-thresholding) baseline to show whether AdaRad’s angular normalization is essential.
  - Report compute/memory overhead and runtime, not just per-iteration complexity analysis.
  - Replace “typical representative” selection with standardized medians and confidence intervals across seeds; keep the same training budget for all methods.
- Clarify claims: Temper statements about outperforming black-box HPO; demonstrate on a stronger HPO setup or reframe as a promising heuristic for adaptive width.
- Theory/practice bridge: Discuss conditions under which the training dynamics can be expected to converge to compact models; analyze sensitivity to λ, αr, αφ; provide heuristics or adaptive schemes to tune λ (the paper hints at this future work).
- CapNorm analysis: Provide intuition and empirical evidence that CapNorm does not unduly limit feature learning; consider alternatives that preserve scale control but allow learned affine parameters without neutralizing regularization.
- Reproducibility: Release code and exact hyperparameter settings; improve reference formatting.

Questions for the authors
1) How sensitive is performance and final width to λ, αr, and αφ? Can λ be adapted online (e.g., via hypergradient or a heuristic) to reduce manual tuning?
2) Did you try a proximal gradient method with group-l2 (group soft-thresholding) using standard optimizers, and how does it compare to AdaRad?
3) Can CapNorm be replaced with BN variants (e.g., fixed gamma/beta, stop-gradient through scaling) without neutralizing the regularizer? Any empirical comparisons?
4) How does the method perform on convolutional architectures (channel growth) and on more challenging benchmarks? Are there engineering obstacles to adding/removing channels efficiently on GPUs?
5) You restrict to self-similar nonlinearities; does the method degrade with leaky ReLU, GELU, or ELU? Is c ≥ 0 crucial in Proposition 1?
6) How do you define “zero” numerically for unit removal under finite precision? Any stability issues?
7) On convex, why does the method prefer small widths? Is this due to CapNorm, the addition schedule, or the optimizer? Can a different schedule/λ fix this?

Overall, the paper contains interesting ideas and a solid existence theorem, but it needs stronger and more comprehensive empirical validation and ablations to meet ICLR standards.
