Summary and contributions
- Proposes “nonparametric neural networks” that adapt layer widths during a single training run by adding zero-units and removing units whose entire fan-in (or fan-out) is shrunk to zero by a group-norm regularizer. Key ingredients:
  - Theory: With fan-in or fan-out regularization (λ>0, 1≤p<∞), the global training objective attains a minimum at finite dimensionality (Theorem 1). Also, for self-similar nonlinearities (e.g., ReLU), layerwise regularization strengths are equivalent up to rescaling (Proposition 1).
  - Methods: (i) A capped batch normalization variant (CapNorm) that avoids neutralizing the regularizer; (ii) AdaRad, a training method that decomposes per-fan-in gradients into radial and angular components, normalizes only the orthogonal component, and uses separate step-sizes for radial/ angular updates; (iii) A simple schedule to add randomly initialized units and remove dead ones.
  - Experiments: MLPs on MNIST, rectangles-images, convex, and OpenML poker. On some tasks (MNIST, rectangles) the approach outperforms parametric baselines of the same size and competes with a random search; on convex it underperforms. Poker indicates scalability w.r.t. dataset size.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) Experimental rigor and fairness are insufficient to substantiate the central empirical claims (especially vs strong hyperparameter search and pruning/growing baselines), and protocols appear ad hoc (rewinding, schedules, λ handling, differing normalization/regularization across methods).
2) Presentation quality is poor: numerous formatting, notation, and bibliographic errors impede understanding and reproducibility.

Supporting arguments
- Baselines and fairness:
  - Parametric baselines are trained with BatchNorm but weight decay (ℓ2) is set to λ=0 with the justification that ℓ2 is “not compatible” with BN. In practice, weight decay with BN is standard (and decoupled WD is widely used). Disallowing ℓ2 for baselines while relying on strong regularization for the proposed method biases the comparison.
  - Nonparametric runs use CapNorm (no learnable affine parameters, capped scaling); parametric runs use BN (with/without affine). This makes comparisons confounded by different normalization layers. Ablations isolating CapNorm’s impact are missing.
  - Hyperparameter search budgets appear unequal and heterogeneous: NP uses a small grid for (λ, αφ) and a fixed unit-addition schedule; parametric uses grid+random search over optimizer/step-size/sizes. There is no matched wall-clock or training-step budget comparison, and multiple “rewind and retune” cycles for NP complicate fairness.
  - No comparisons to strong growing/pruning or architecture search baselines (e.g., contemporary pruning-with-regrowth, Net2Net, dynamic surgery, modern AutoML/BOHB/MF methods). The claim of outperforming “black-box methods” isn’t convincingly validated.

- Methodological clarity:
  - AdaRad requires per-fan-in decomposition into parallel/orthogonal components and uses a global normalization term via φmax/cmax spanning layers. The motivation and stability implications of coupling layers through global maxima are not adequately explained. Complexity claims vs RMSprop are asserted, but Table 1 is referenced without sufficient detail here; a thorough complexity and wall-clock study is needed.
  - CapNorm divides by max(σ,1) and removes affine parameters. Its effect on representational capacity, gradient flow, and interaction with group regularization is only qualitatively described. There is no demonstration that CapNorm (vs BN/no-norm) is the key to NP’s performance, nor sensitivity to batch size.
  - Unit addition schedule (one per layer per epoch; altered on poker) and αr=150/λ heuristic are hand-tuned; no principled criterion or sensitivity analysis is provided.
  - The “rewind” protocol (retraining after halting criteria, stopping addition, setting λ=0, repeatedly annealing αφ) is complex and risks overfitting to validation dynamics; clearer justification and controls are needed.

- Theory:
  - Theorem 1’s existence result is interesting and likely useful; the proof handles non-differentiable nonlinearities via directional derivatives. However, the conditions are heavy and the practical link to the training recipe (shrinkage operator, discrete add/remove) is not formalized (no convergence guarantees for AdaRad+shrinkage+CapNorm).
  - Proposition 1’s rescaling argument for self-similar nonlinearities is plausible, but empirical ablation where different λ per layer are used would help.

- Empirical scope:
  - Only small fully-connected MLP tasks are used (except poker with 14 features). No convolutional experiments, where the unit concept and group-regularization on fan-ins/outs interacts differently and where the approach would matter most.
  - The convex dataset is a negative result with limited analysis; diagnosing failure modes would be instructive.

- Presentation and reproducibility:
  - Numerous formatting/notation issues: section numbering (.2, .3), typos (“bot not fastest”, “durgery”), broken footnotes, unclear indices (d0l), missing parentheses, inconsistent notation for vectors [·]i, ambiguous variables (φ̄l, cl, φmax/cmax), and missing tables/figures in context.
  - References are often malformed or incorrect (e.g., “Back & Teboulle 2006” for shrinkage; spelling of authors/titles; duplicate/mismatched titles; odd placeholders “? \Q2015\E”). This is below ICLR standards.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper)
- Provide carefully controlled baselines:
  - Use the same normalization (e.g., CapNorm variants) for parametric baselines, and include BN+weight decay baselines. Match training budgets (steps/wall-clock), early stopping, and annealing rules across methods.
  - Compare to modern growing/pruning methods and multi-fidelity hyperparameter optimization under matched budgets.
- Add ablations:
  - With/without CapNorm; with/without group regularization; sensitivity to λ, αr, αφ; different unit addition rates; per-layer λ vs shared λ; fan-in vs fan-out.
- Clarify and justify AdaRad:
  - Derive and motivate the φmax/cmax normalization; provide per-iteration complexity, memory, and wall-clock comparisons vs SGD/RMSprop/Adam on identical hardware. Include stability diagnostics.
- Expand to CNNs to demonstrate practicality on contemporary benchmarks.
- Clean up writing, notation, and references thoroughly; ensure all algorithms, tables, and figures are self-contained and reproducible. Release code.

Questions for the authors
1) Why prohibit weight decay for BN baselines? Please include BN+weight decay (and decoupled weight decay) baselines and report matched-budget results.
2) What is the isolated contribution of CapNorm? Can you show ablations comparing BN (with/without affine), CapNorm, and no normalization on both NP and parametric settings?
3) How sensitive is performance to the global φmax/cmax normalization and to the choice of αr=150/λ? What happens if you normalize per-layer or per-unit only?
4) Can you provide wall-clock and FLOP measurements showing AdaRad’s claimed efficiency vs RMSprop/Adam at scale?
5) On convex, why does the method converge to small networks and underperform? Does altering unit-addition pace or λ annealing fix this?
6) How does the approach extend to convolutional layers (what is a “fan-in” group in conv kernels), and have you tried CIFAR/ImageNet-scale experiments?
7) Can you make your rewind procedure and stopping rules precise and justify them statistically to avoid validation leakage?
