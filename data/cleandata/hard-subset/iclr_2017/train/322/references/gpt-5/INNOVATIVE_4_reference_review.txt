1. Summary of contributions
The paper proposes “nonparametric neural networks,” a principled framework to adapt layer widths during a single training run by dynamically adding zero-units and removing units whose fan-in or fan-out collapses under structured regularization. The key elements are:
- A theoretical result (Theorem 1) showing that, with fan-in or fan-out p-norm regularization (λ>0, 1≤p<∞) and σ(0)=0, the training objective attains a global minimum at a finite network size, justifying width search within the training objective itself.
- A practical training recipe combining: (i) unit addition/removal via f-equivalence of adding/removing zero units; (ii) a capped batch normalization variant (CapNorm) that prevents BN from canceling the regularizer; (iii) a new optimizer, AdaRad, that splits gradients into radial (parallel to fan-in) and angular (orthogonal) components, normalizing only the angular component so new units can learn without interfering with shrinkage.
- Empirical evidence across MNIST, rectangles images, convex, and a large poker dataset (1M+ examples). The approach often matches or outperforms same-size parametric networks and is competitive with black-box searches, while scaling to large data.

I find the central idea bold and refreshing: making width a learnable degree of freedom with a theoretical guarantee that the objective prefers finite solutions, plus a training scheme that makes this work in practice. The analysis of training dynamics is valuable and insightful.

2. Decision
Accept. Key reasons:
- Novelty and potential impact: a principled nonparametric formulation of NN width with a finite-minimum guarantee, together with practical machinery (CapNorm, AdaRad) to make it work.
- Empirical and scalability evidence that the method can be competitive with standard model selection and can outperform same-size parametric baselines, while offering a new direction for architecture selection during training.

3. Supporting arguments
- Theoretical soundness: Theorem 1 is a strong and nontrivial guarantee that addresses a fundamental concern (unbounded growth). Proposition 1 on self-similar nonlinearities provides an elegant normalization of layerwise regularization strengths. The proofs are careful and broadly applicable given common activations (e.g., ReLU).
- Algorithmic contributions: The radial–angular decomposition is an original design that addresses the core tension between gradient normalization (needed so new units can learn) and shrinkage (needed for deletion). CapNorm is a small but clever modification to make regularization and normalization compatible. The unit addition/removal scheme is simple, well-motivated via f-equivalence, and easily implementable.
- Empirical results: On MNIST and rectangles images, nonparametric training often beats same-size parametric models; in rectangles images, it achieves the best median test error with fewer parameters. The poker experiment convincingly demonstrates scalability and strong performance on a large dataset. The convex dataset underperformance is candidly discussed, with analyses of dynamics that can guide future improvements.
- Analysis and clarity: The paper includes useful ablations and dynamics visualizations (e.g., fan-in/out lengths, survival times), and concrete hyperparameter schedules. The pseudocode for AdaRad is clear.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper
These points aim to strengthen the work; they are not all decisive for my recommendation.
- Scope and benchmarks: Most experiments are shallow MLPs on non-convolutional tasks. Including at least one CNN benchmark (e.g., CIFAR) where channels are grown/removed would make the case much stronger, especially given hardware arguments favoring dense operations.
- Fairness and budgets: Clarify and, if possible, equalize compute budgets between nonparametric training and black-box or parametric baselines. Report wall-clock times and memory for AdaRad vs RMSprop/Adam, not just asymptotics.
- CapNorm ablation: Provide a direct ablation showing CapNorm’s necessity and its effect relative to standard BN with properly tuned weight decay (or BN without affine parameters), and to no normalization.
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: The method introduces several knobs (λ, αr, αφ, unit addition rate/frequency, initialization of new units). Include a sensitivity study and guidance for practitioners. In particular, probing whether different addition schedules or αr alleviates the convex dataset failure would help.
- Comparison breadth: Consider comparisons to structured sparsity/pruning methods that remove channels/units during training and to prior growing methods (Wei et al., Chen et al., Guo et al.), run under similar conditions. Even if those require a good starting model, a discussion of trade-offs would be useful.
- AdaRad vs AdaRad-M: Since momentum is standard, more discussion on why AdaRad-M does not help (and when it does) would be helpful.
- Theory–practice bridge: While Theorem 1 is about training objective minima, a brief discussion on generalization (e.g., whether the regularizer implicitly controls capacity in a way that aligns with test error) would strengthen the story.

Questions for the authors
- Sensitivity and schedules: How sensitive is performance to ν and νfreq? Could a data-driven or adaptive unit addition policy improve convergence speed and help on convex?
- Initialization choice: You initialize new units with random fan-in and zero fan-out (for fan-in regularization). Did you test the opposite or other schemes (e.g., small nonzero fan-out)? How does this affect survival and learning speed?
- CapNorm specifics: Can you quantify how often σ<1 vs σ≥1 occurs during training, and how that interacts with αr to control elimination rates? Any downsides of CapNorm in later stages?
- Budget parity: When claiming superiority to black-box search in some cases, what is the relative compute budget (GPU-hours) spent by each method?
- Failure case: Do you have a hypothesis for the convex dataset underperformance (e.g., need for larger persistent widths, mismatch between λ and learning dynamics, feature linearity)? Any adjustments that recover performance?
- Extension to CNNs: Any preliminary results where channels/filters are added/removed? Are there obstacles to applying AdaRad and CapNorm to convolutional layers?
- Proposition 1 with normalization: Does the equivalence still hold in the presence of CapNorm layers interleaved between affine and nonlinearity?

Overall, this paper introduces a compelling and novel direction with solid theory and practical algorithms. Despite some limitations in scope and mixed results on one dataset, I believe it merits acceptance and will stimulate follow-up work on dynamic capacity selection in neural networks.
