- Problem: automatically choose network width during a single training run instead of costly hyperparameter search or post-hoc pruning.
- Proposal: nonparametric neural networks that dynamically add/remove units using a unit-wise group Lp regularizer (focus on fan-in, p=2) to induce exact zero fan-ins.
- Theory: proves existence of a finite-dimensional global minimum under fan-in/out regularization (Theorem 1).
- Theory: shows scaling result for self-similar activations (Proposition 1) allowing a single λ to represent layerwise regularization up to rescaling.
- Method: CapNorm, a modified batch normalization that subtracts mean and only rescales when batch std > 1, with no learned γ/β to preserve regularizer effect.
- Method: AdaRad optimizer that decomposes fan-in gradients into radial (shrinkage) and angular (normalized) components, plus a momentum variant AdaRad-M.
- Empirics: evaluated on MLPs for MNIST, rectangles-images, convex, and a large poker dataset; often converges to reasonable sizes.
- Empirics: nonparametric nets sometimes match or beat parametric nets of the same size (notably MNIST and rectangles-images); underperform on convex.
- Empirics concern: a suspicious 0% test error on poker (100k test set) calls for auditing for leakage or data issues.
- Decision: Reject (borderline).
- Key reason: empirical validation is limited and partly unconvincing (small MLPs, classic benchmarks, one notable failure, and a suspicious poker result).
- Key reason: comparisons raise fairness and methodological questions (different normalization/regularization treatments, manual representative selection, many hyperparameters/schedules).
- Novelty: framing width as nonparametric, existence proof for finite minimizer, and radial/angular optimizer design are interesting and potentially impactful.
- Soundness: Theorem 1 appears technically substantial and correct under stated assumptions; Proposition 1 and CapNorm rationale are reasonable.
- Practicality: AdaRad is well-motivated to let new units learn while enabling shrinkage; complexity claims vs RMSprop are plausible.
- Weakness: scope limited to fully-connected ReLU nets; no convolutional or modern large-scale benchmarks and no depth adaptation experiments.
- Weakness: experimental fairness—parametric nets used BN with λ=0 while nonparametric used CapNorm with group-L2, confounding comparisons.
- Weakness: selection/tuning—manual choice from 10 seeds, extensive annealing/rewinding, many hyperparameters and schedules; missing ablations.
- Weakness: negative case (convex) shows underperformance and is not analyzed in depth.
- Feedback: use identical normalization where possible and include CapNorm/BN baselines to make comparisons fair.
- Feedback: add ablations (AdaRad vs SGD/RMSprop/Adam, CapNorm vs BN vs none, unit addition strategies, λ/αr sensitivity, effect of late λ→0).
- Feedback: rigorously audit and document the poker experiment (splits, preprocessing, duplicates, leakage checks, permuted-label sanity check).
- Feedback: extend experiments to convolutional architectures or modern tasks and explore depth adaptation.
- Feedback: clarify CapNorm intuition and relate methods to weight normalization/spherical optimization; clean up presentation/references.
- Questions for authors: details on poker splits/preprocessing/duplicates and parametric baselines under same splits.
- Questions for authors: why exclude weight decay with BN for parametric nets and can you provide CapNorm-for-parametric and BN-for-nonparametric baselines?
- Questions for authors: ablation results replacing AdaRad with RMSprop/Adam under CapNorm and same group regularizer— is AdaRad necessary?
- Questions for authors: sensitivity to αr, αφ, ν, νfreq, λ and robustness across seeds.
- Questions for authors: wall-clock and memory overhead of AdaRad vs RMSprop/Adam and scalability to convolutional layers.
- Questions for authors: does Theorem 1 extend to piecewise-linear non-self-similar activations or architectures with BN-like layers, and implications for generalization?
