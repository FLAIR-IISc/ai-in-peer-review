Summary
This paper proposes “nonparametric neural networks,” a framework that treats network width as part of the optimization, adding/removing units during a single training run. The central idea is to regularize fan-ins (or fan-outs) with an Lp group penalty (focus on p=2), which induces entire units to become exactly zero and hence removable. The authors prove an important result: with such group regularization (λ>0, 1≤p<∞), the training objective attains a global minimum at finite width, ensuring the problem is well-posed despite an unbounded architecture space. They introduce two practical components to make this work: (i) CapNorm, a variant of batch normalization that avoids “cancelling out” the regularizer by only scaling when batch std>1 and removing learned affine parameters; and (ii) AdaRad, a new optimizer that decomposes gradients into radial (parallel) and angular (orthogonal) components per fan-in, normalizing only the orthogonal component to let new units learn without overwhelming the shrinkage. Empirically, on MNIST, rectangles images, convex, and a large OpenML poker dataset, the approach learns widths on the fly, sometimes outperforming parametric nets of the same size and in a few cases approaching or exceeding best black-box baselines. The paper includes an insightful analysis of the training dynamics and a small ablation (AdaRad vs AdaRad-M).

Decision: Accept
Reason: The paper introduces a novel, principled, and bold direction—treating width as a nonparametric quantity to be learned within a single run—with nontrivial theory (existence of finite-width minima), new training mechanisms (CapNorm, AdaRad), and promising empirical evidence. While the empirical scope is limited and some comparisons could be tightened, the conceptual contribution and accompanying mechanisms are strong and likely to stimulate follow-up work.

Supporting arguments
- Novelty and significance: Casting width selection as part of the optimization, plus a theorem guaranteeing finite optimal width under group fan-in/out regularization, is both elegant and practically meaningful. The idea to traverse “f-equivalent” parameterizations by adding/removing zero units is neat and well-motivated.
- Algorithmic contributions: CapNorm is a simple, targeted modification that addresses a real incompatibility between BN and group shrinkage. AdaRad’s radial/orthogonal decomposition per fan-in is a thoughtful way to normalize learning for newly added units without undermining regularization. These are concrete, reusable contributions.
- Empirical evidence: Although not exhaustive, results show (i) the method converges to a reasonable size; (ii) can outperform parametric nets of the same size on MNIST and rectangles and be competitive in broader searches; (iii) scales to a 1M-example dataset. The detailed training dynamics provide useful intuition about why the mechanism works.
- Theoretical support: The main theorem and the proposition on self-similar nonlinearities are well-placed and bolster the core claim that the nonparametric formulation is sound.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Baselines: Please compare directly to a simple but strong alternative—train a single, sufficiently wide network with group-L2 fan-in (or fan-out) regularization, then prune zeroed units at the end. This tests whether dynamic add/remove plus AdaRad/CapNorm adds value over “static large + group sparsity.”
- Fairness/Protocol clarity: The comparison with parametric nets disables weight decay due to BN but uses CapNorm+group-L2 in your method. Could you include parametric baselines using CapNorm (no affine) + group-L2 as well? Also clarify how test-time CapNorm is applied (batch vs running estimates).
- Scope: Currently limited to fully-connected MLPs. Even one convolutional experiment (e.g., CIFAR-10) would greatly strengthen the case that the approach is broadly applicable.
- Claims vs evidence: The statement that you can surpass black-box model selection is compelling but not rigorously established here. A controlled budgeted comparison against BO/Random Search would make this more convincing.
- Compute/time: Please report wall-clock comparisons and memory footprint vs RMSprop/Adam and vs black-box searches (including the benefit of a single run).
- Sensitivity/ablations: Include sensitivity to ν/νfreq (unit addition), αr/αφ, and λ; an ablation on CapNorm vs BN (and BN without learned affine) would help. Also show results for p=1 vs p=2 and fan-in vs fan-out, at least on one dataset.
- Theory scope: Does the existence theorem still hold under CapNorm layers (which modify pre-activation scaling) and self-similar activations? It would help to state conditions precisely for networks with normalization layers.
- Editing/references: Several references have formatting/typos. Polishing would improve readability.

Questions for the authors
1. How does your method compare to “static wide + group-L2 + prune” in accuracy and training time? This is a critical baseline to isolate the benefit of dynamic growth and AdaRad.
2. How is CapNorm applied at test time? Do you maintain running means/variances? Any accuracy drop from train to test?
3. Can RMSprop/Adam be adapted to avoid undermining shrinkage (e.g., normalizing only orthogonal components) and how would that compare to AdaRad?
4. How sensitive is performance to ν (units per epoch) and αr relative to λ? Any principled tuning heuristics?
5. Does the finite-minimum theorem cover networks augmented with CapNorm layers, or do additional assumptions apply?
6. Have you tried leaky-ReLU (approximately self-similar) and do Proposition 1’s practical implications persist?
7. On the convex dataset, what diagnostics suggest why the method converges to widths that are too small? Could annealing λ or adaptive unit addition mitigate this?

Overall, the paper is a creative and well-argued step toward single-run architecture selection. Despite limitations in empirical breadth, the conceptual novelty, theoretical underpinning, and practical mechanisms justify acceptance.
