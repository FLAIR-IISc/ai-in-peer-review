1. Summary of claims and contributions
The paper proposes “nonparametric neural networks,” a framework that adapts the width of a feedforward network during a single training run. The key ideas are:
- Formulation: Treat width as part of the optimization variable and use group-sparsity regularizers on fan-in or fan-out (ℓ2 over each unit’s incoming or outgoing weights). Theorem 1 states that with a positive λ and 1 ≤ p < ∞, the training objective attains a global minimum at finite dimensionality, addressing the risk of unbounded growth.
- Training machinery: (i) Add and remove zero units on the fly while preserving functional equivalence, (ii) Capped Batch Normalization (CapNorm), which normalizes means and scales only when batch std > 1 to avoid negating the regularizer, and (iii) AdaRad, which decomposes gradients per fan-in into radial (parallel) and angular (orthogonal) components, normalizes only the angular part, and uses separate step sizes for radial vs angular updates to allow new units to learn without being immediately shrunk to zero. A momentum variant (AdaRad-M) is provided in the appendix.
- Practical guidance: Use self-similar nonlinearities (e.g., ReLU) for scale invariance and to obviate per-layer λ tuning (Proposition 1).
- Empirical results: On MNIST and rectangles-images, the method finds compact models and sometimes outperforms parametric networks of the same size; on convex, performance lags. On the large Poker dataset, results are competitive or slightly better than tuned parametric counterparts. The experiments include analyses of unit lifetimes, layer sizes over time, and fan-in/fan-out dynamics.

2. Decision
Reject. Primary reasons: (i) empirical validation is limited and mixed, with modest or task-dependent gains and a notable failure case; (ii) limited scope (fully connected MLPs only), missing comparisons and ablations that would isolate the contributions of CapNorm, AdaRad, and the regularizer; (iii) no convergence or consistency guarantee for the proposed growth–shrink training algorithm beyond the existence theorem.

3. Supporting arguments
- Technical soundness: Theorem 1 (existence of a finite global minimizer under fan-in/out regularization) is carefully stated with clear assumptions and a detailed proof sketch relying on boundedness arguments and directional derivatives. Proposition 1 on self-similar nonlinearities is straightforward and correct. These results justify the formulation but do not establish that the proposed training procedure finds good minima or widths.
- Algorithmic design: AdaRad’s radial–angular decomposition is coherent; normalizing only the orthogonal component while leaving shrinkage unscaled is a reasonable solution to the “new unit learns slowly” problem under group-lasso. CapNorm’s design prevents the regularizer from being nullified by re-scaling, addressing a real incompatibility with standard BN. However, the paper does not include convergence analysis or stability guarantees for AdaRad; empirical evidence is relied upon.
- Empirical evaluation: 
  - Breadth: Only fully connected networks on a few tabular/image datasets. There are no convolutional or more modern benchmarks, which limits generality.
  - Comparisons: Parametric baselines are tuned via grid/random search with BN/RMSprop/Adam, which is appropriate; however, there is no ablation isolating the effects of CapNorm vs BN, AdaRad vs standard optimizers under the same regularizer, or p=1 vs p=2, fan-in vs fan-out. Thus, causal attribution for gains is unclear.
  - Method sensitivity: The convex dataset underperformance suggests the approach can converge to undersized models; although acknowledged, the work does not present remedies or principled guidance to avoid this failure mode beyond heuristic scheduling. The Poker result is encouraging but lacks wall-clock and compute comparisons and rigorous variance analyses across seeds.
  - Selection protocol: Choosing a “typical representative” run for nonparametric nets introduces potential selection bias; reporting aggregate statistics across seeds would be more robust.

4. Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not all affect the accept decision)
- Ablations and controls:
  - Compare AdaRad to Adam/RMSprop under identical fan-in/out regularization and CapNorm to isolate optimizer impact.
  - Compare CapNorm to BN variants under the same regularizer and to layer norm/group norm; quantify its effect on both training dynamics and final performance.
  - Evaluate p=1 vs p=2 and fan-in vs fan-out to validate the choice of group-lasso and the dense/sparse claims.
- Robustness and fairness:
  - Report results as median and dispersion across all seeds; avoid manual “typical representative” selection.
  - Provide wall-clock time, FLOPs, and memory overhead for AdaRad vs RMSprop/Adam, and quantify the extra time caused by gradual growth versus potential gains.
  - Include sensitivity analyses for λ, αr, αφ, and unit addition frequency.
- Scope and applicability:
  - Demonstrate on convolutional architectures (e.g., CIFAR-class benchmarks) and, if possible, transformers/attention layers. Clarify how Theorem 1 extends to convolutional parameter-sharing.
  - Compare to grow-and-prune methods and NAS strategies that adapt width during training; even if method classes differ, strong baselines are needed to position the contribution.
- Theory and guarantees:
  - Provide any convergence or monotonicity properties for AdaRad with shrinkage; even local statements (e.g., descent in a surrogate) would strengthen the case.
- Presentation:
  - Fix reference formatting and minor typos; streamline the proof presentation to improve readability.
  - Release code and scripts to enhance reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
1. CapNorm: Can you quantify how CapNorm alone affects performance vs BN (with/without affine parameters) under the same regularization? Is the cap at std ≤ 1 essential, or are there alternatives?
2. AdaRad necessity: How does the method perform if standard optimizers (Adam/RMSprop) are used with careful handling of the shrinkage term (e.g., decoupled weight decay)? Is AdaRad required to realize the width-adaptation benefits?
3. Failure on convex: Is the undersizing issue mitigated by different λ schedules, addition rates, or αr/αφ choices? Any diagnostics to predict when the method will select too-small widths?
4. Complexity: What are the wall-clock and memory overheads relative to standard training for the same final width? How much additional time is due to growing from small initial networks?
5. Theorem scope: Do the existence results extend to convolutional layers with weight sharing and to other common losses (e.g., hinge)? Are there constraints beyond σ(0)=0 that are practically relevant?
6. Poker results: How are train/val/test splits defined? Given the extremely low error, can you rule out leakage or spurious correlations? Are results stable across seeds/splits?
7. Removal/addition policy: How sensitive are results to the unit addition schedule and the “≈50 epoch grace period” via αr=150/λ? Could an adaptive schedule improve both speed and final accuracy?
