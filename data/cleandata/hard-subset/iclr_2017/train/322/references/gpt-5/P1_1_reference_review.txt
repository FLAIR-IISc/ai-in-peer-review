Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes “nonparametric neural networks,” a framework that treats layer widths as variables to be optimized during a single training run. Networks grow by adding zero units (which are f-equivalent) and shrink via a group l2 (fan-in or fan-out) regularizer that zeroes out full fan-ins/outs, enabling unit removal.
- Theoretical result: With a fan-in or fan-out lp regularizer (1 ≤ p < ∞, λ > 0), the training objective admits a global minimum at a finite dimensionality (Theorem 1). A proposition shows that for self-similar activations (e.g., ReLU), layer-specific regularization strengths are equivalent up to a rescaling, simplifying tuning.
- Two algorithmic components are introduced: (i) CapNorm, a modification of batch normalization that avoids undermining the group regularizer by capping variance normalization and removing affine parameters; (ii) AdaRad, an optimizer that decomposes gradients into radial (parallel to the fan-in) and angular (orthogonal) components, normalizing only the angular component to enable new units to learn without negating shrinkage.
- Empirical results on MNIST, rectangles images, convex, and a large Poker dataset show that the approach can automatically select network size and sometimes outperform same-size parametric networks and even strong black-box searches (mixed results: strong on MNIST and rectangles, weaker on convex; solid on Poker).

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) Empirical support is limited and mixed, with relatively small fully connected benchmarks, one negative result (convex), and limited ablations to disentangle the contributions of the multiple proposed components. 2) Comparisons and baselines are insufficient to convincingly establish that the adaptive nonparametric mechanism (vs. optimizer/normalization choices and schedules) is the primary driver of gains.

Supporting arguments
- Novelty and motivation: The core idea—dynamic width selection through adding/removing units under a group-l2 penalty—is interesting and practically motivated. The finite-dimensionality theorem gives a clear optimization justification, and the use of f-equivalence (zero units) is natural. However, similar ideas exist in pruning/growing literature, and the paper could better position its novelty relative to structured sparsity (group lasso), Net2Net, dynamic network surgery, and modern grow/prune schedules.
- Theory: Theorem 1 is technically careful and broadly applicable under mild assumptions. It ensures existence of a finite-width minimizer but does not speak to generalization or to properties of local minima encountered by SGD-like training. The proposition on self-similar nonlinearities is neat and practically useful.
- Algorithms: CapNorm and AdaRad are nontrivial contributions. Yet, the paper bundles three ideas (nonparametric width, CapNorm, AdaRad) without clear attribution of which is necessary/sufficient. Key missing ablations:
  - Fixed-width training with AdaRad (+/− CapNorm)
  - Fixed-width with group-l2 regularization (p=2) and standard optimizers
  - Nonparametric training with standard optimizers and normalization
  - Fan-in vs. fan-out; p=1 vs. p=2 trade-offs
- Empirics: 
  - Benchmarks: MNIST and rectangles images are small; convex is idiosyncratic; only Poker tests scale. No results on convolutional architectures or more modern datasets/tasks where model size search matters most.
  - Mixed outcomes: The method underperforms parametric counterparts on convex. The paper argues the nonparametric tendency to select smaller widths hurts performance but does not test alternative addition/elimination schedules or adaptive λ to remedy this.
  - Comparisons: The parametric baselines are optimized (grid/random search), but fairness is muddy: they use BN with λ=0, while the proposed method uses CapNorm and group-l2; the impacts of “rewinding,” step-size schedules, and long training horizons with progressive growth are not controlled.
  - Efficiency: Claims about AdaRad’s computational and memory benefits are not backed by wall-clock results. The overhead of growth/shrink cycles and CapNorm vs BN is not quantified.
  - Reproducibility: Many moving parts (rewinding, annealing, frequency of unit addition/removal) and no code availability are concerning.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Provide thorough ablations to separate the effects of: nonparametric width vs. CapNorm vs. AdaRad; fan-in vs. fan-out; p=1 vs. p=2; and sensitivity to λ, αr, αφ, ν, νfreq. Include fixed-width baselines trained with AdaRad and/or CapNorm.
- Compare against strong structured sparsity methods (group lasso on fixed nets, Wen et al. 2016; Collins & Kohli 2014), pruning-after-training methods (Molchanov et al. 2017), grow/prune schedules (Wei et al. 2016), and knowledge transfer schemes (Net2Net).
- Report compute: training time, epochs to convergence, number of unit add/remove events, and size stabilization curves; include wall-clock for AdaRad vs RMSprop/Adam.
- Extend to convolutional layers and at least one modern vision/NLP benchmark; dynamic width is most impactful there.
- Clarify fairness: re-run parametric baselines with CapNorm; add a baseline with group-l2 and static widths; test “train from scratch” with the final selected architecture using the same optimizer/normalization as the proposed method.
- Provide code, hyperparameter ranges, and seeds to facilitate reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
1) How much of the performance gain vs. same-size parametric nets is due to AdaRad or CapNorm rather than nonparametric growth? Can you show fixed-width training with AdaRad (+/− CapNorm) matches or falls short of the nonparametric approach?
2) Why not compare to fixed-width group-l2 (p=2) regularization baselines using standard optimizers? Does dynamic add/remove still help over static group-l2?
3) On convex, could increasing the addition rate or annealing λ prevent premature size under-selection? Any results with adaptive λ schedules?
4) How sensitive is performance to ν, νfreq, αr, and αφ? What guidelines should practitioners follow?
5) Can you report wall-clock cost and memory overhead of AdaRad vs RMSprop/Adam across datasets? 
6) How does CapNorm compare to BN-without-affine and BN-with-affine when combined with group-l2 (e.g., by excluding the normalized layers from regularization)?
7) Any preliminary results for convolutional layers and fan-in/out regularization across channels/filters?
8) Is there a simple criterion to decide between fan-in vs fan-out penalties in practice? 
9) Can you provide code and training scripts to reproduce the reported curves (especially the growth/shrink dynamics)?
