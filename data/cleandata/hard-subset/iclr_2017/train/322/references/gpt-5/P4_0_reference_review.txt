1. Summary of contributions
The paper proposes “nonparametric neural networks,” a framework that jointly optimizes network width (number of hidden units) and weights in a single training run by dynamically adding and removing units. The core technical ingredients are:
- A formulation with fan-in or fan-out group ℓp penalties that promote unit-wise sparsity. A main theorem shows that, under these regularizers (λ > 0, 1 ≤ p < ∞) and mild assumptions on activations and loss, the training objective attains a global minimum at a finite network size, thus justifying the nonparametric formulation.
- A training procedure that alternates gradient-based weight updates with insertion of zero units (ensuring f-equivalent parameterizations) and removal of units whose entire fan-in (or fan-out) shrinks to zero.
- Two algorithmic components to make this work in practice: (i) CapNorm, a variant of batch normalization designed not to cancel out the shrinkage induced by the fan-in/out penalty, and (ii) AdaRad, an optimizer that decomposes the gradient per unit into radial (parallel to fan-in) and angular (orthogonal) components, normalizes only the angular part, and uses separate step sizes to balance learning vs. shrinkage.
Empirically, the paper evaluates on MNIST, rectangles-images, convex (MLP setting), and a large OpenML “poker” dataset. In two datasets, nonparametric models outperform parametric models of the same size and are competitive with black-box hyperparameter search; on convex, they underperform; on poker they achieve strong results and scale to >1M examples. The paper also analyzes training dynamics and provides detailed proofs and implementation descriptions.

2. Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- Empirical validation is limited and not sufficiently rigorous for ICLR: evaluations are confined to shallow MLPs on small/older datasets plus one tabular dataset; comparisons and ablations are incomplete, making it difficult to assess the generality and advantage of the approach.
- Several methodological choices (CapNorm, AdaRad, growth schedule) are novel but ad hoc; comparisons with strong baselines are potentially confounded (e.g., BN vs. CapNorm, regularization setups), and the benefits of each component are not isolated.

3. Supporting arguments
Clarity
- The main ideas are well introduced and the theoretical statements are clearly scoped. Algorithm pseudocode is helpful. However, there are notational inconsistencies/typos, dense proofs, and occasional referencing issues, which reduce accessibility. The experimental setup is lengthy and complex, with many hyperparameters and rewinding schedules that could be summarized more clearly.

Technical soundness
- Theorem 1 (existence of a finite optimal width under group penalties) is well-motivated and appears sound under the stated assumptions, which include directional differentiability and bounded local Lipschitz constants. This is a meaningful theoretical result for the proposed formulation.
- Proposition 1 about self-similar nonlinearities and layer-wise λ equivalence is mathematically straightforward and useful.
- AdaRad’s decomposition and update are plausible and well-motivated for unit-wise group penalties. However, no convergence analysis is provided; the rotation/normalization operations are intricate and their numerical stability and scaling behavior are not theoretically characterized.
- CapNorm is conceptually reasonable to avoid scale-canceling interactions with the regularizer, but its behavior is only justified qualitatively; no formal analysis supports its claimed properties.

Novelty and placement
- The paper provides a unified nonparametric formulation with existence guarantees, which is a valuable conceptual contribution beyond prior pruning/growth work and group lasso unit-sparsity. Related work is broadly covered (constructive networks, pruning, group sparsity, BO/NAS).
- AdaRad and CapNorm are new, but their novelty would be stronger with ablations and comparisons against established optimizers/normalizers within the same nonparametric framework.

Empirical support
- Benchmarks are limited. Modern architectures/tasks (e.g., CNNs on CIFAR/ImageNet, transformers, or strong tabular baselines) are not included. The negative result on convex suggests the method may converge to too-small models, raising concerns about robustness.
- Baselines: parametric models use standard BN and typically no ℓ2 regularization, while nonparametric models use CapNorm with group-ℓ2 penalties. This mismatch, along with extensive per-method schedules (unit addition, rewinding) and selective “representative” runs, complicates fairness. Compute/wall-clock budgets are not equalized or reported.
- Ablations: effects of CapNorm vs BN, AdaRad vs RMSprop/Adam within the nonparametric setup, growth rate ν and step sizes (αr, αφ), p=1 vs p=2, and fan-in vs fan-out are not systematically studied. The AdaRad-M discussion reports “similar performance” but no quantitative detail.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not necessarily decisive for the evaluation)
- Provide thorough ablations isolating: CapNorm, AdaRad, group penalty (p=1 vs p=2), fan-in vs fan-out, unit addition rate, initialization magnitude, and the annealing/rewinding schedule. Include results with standard optimizers inside the nonparametric framework to show AdaRad’s necessity.
- Strengthen baselines and fairness: match normalization and regularization across methods as closely as possible, and report equalized compute/wall-clock. Include fixed-width models trained with group-lasso to compare against your grow/shrink mechanism. Add modern benchmarks (e.g., CNNs on CIFAR-10/100) and, if possible, a NAS baseline.
- Provide uncertainty estimates (median/IQR over many seeds), not just a “representative” run. Clarify tuning protocol and whether test sets are only evaluated once after validation-based selection.
- Analyze failures (e.g., convex): diagnose under-sizing, and propose mitigations (adaptive λ schedules, different growth heuristics).
- Improve clarity and reproducibility: release code; clean up notation, figure/table references; include the complexity/memory comparison table and actual measured overheads; discuss numerical stability of angular rotations and zero-unit detection.

Questions for the authors
- CapNorm: Can you provide a formal argument that CapNorm cannot undo the effect of the fan-in/out penalty (beyond σ≤1 capping)? How sensitive is performance to the “cap at 1” choice?
- Sensitivity: How robust are results to ν, νfreq, αr, αφ, λ, and initialization scale? Please provide systematic sweeps and guidelines.
- AdaRad: Does AdaRad improve training in the parametric setting as well, or is its benefit specific to the nonparametric/group-penalty regime? Any convergence or stability analysis?
- Fairness and compute: What are the wall-clock times and GPU-hours for nonparametric training vs parametric grid/random search? Can you equalize compute budgets and report final validation/test metrics?
- Generality: Does Theorem 1 extend to convolutional layers with weight sharing or to transformers? Are there obstacles to applying the proof to shared-parameter settings?
- Activations: Beyond ReLU, do leaky-ReLU or other self-similar functions maintain Proposition 1? What happens with non-self-similar activations in practice?
- Zero-unit handling: How do you detect exact zeros under floating-point? Do small norms cause premature deletions? Any thresholding heuristics?
- Failure case (convex): What diagnostics indicate why the method converged to suboptimal sizes? Would alternative growth schedules or adaptive λ remedy this?

Overall, the paper offers an interesting theoretical justification and a promising framework, but stronger and broader empirical validation, clearer ablations, and tighter baseline comparisons are needed for acceptance.
