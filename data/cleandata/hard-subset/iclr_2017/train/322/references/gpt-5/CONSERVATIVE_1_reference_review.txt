Summary
- The paper proposes “nonparametric neural networks,” i.e., training procedures that grow and shrink the number of hidden units during a single training run using group-style regularization on fan-ins/outs. The key ingredients are: (i) a theorem stating that with fan-in or fan-out p-norm regularization (1 ≤ p < ∞) the objective attains a global minimum at finite dimensionality; (ii) a training algorithm, AdaRad, that decomposes gradients into radial and angular components, normalizes only the angular component, and uses group-ℓ2 shrinkage to zero out units; (iii) a modified normalization layer CapNorm (a capped form of BN) to avoid neutralizing the effect of the regularizer; (iv) an add/remove scheme for units. Experiments on MNIST, rectangles, convex, and a large “poker” dataset show mixed outcomes: better or competitive performance vs parametric nets in two datasets, worse on convex, and competitive on poker.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) The empirical support is limited and inconclusive, with mixed results on small datasets, no comparisons against several strong and relevant baselines, and no wall-clock/model-selection efficiency evaluation that substantiates the paper’s core motivation.
2) The novelty is incremental relative to established neuron-/group-lasso pruning/growth literature. The added components (AdaRad, CapNorm, specific add/remove schedules) are not convincingly justified by ablations, and Theorem 1, while technically careful, is largely detached from the proposed training algorithm and unsurprising given the regularization structure.

Supporting arguments
- Positioning and novelty: Group-wise penalties to eliminate units (fan-in/out) are well-studied (e.g., Alvarez & Salzmann, Yuan & Lin), and dynamic growth/pruning is a long-standing idea. The paper’s claim that prior approaches “require a high-performing model to prune” is overstated; group-lasso can be trained from scratch on an overparameterized network and pruned, which is a direct baseline. The key novel ingredients here are AdaRad and CapNorm, plus a specific growth schedule; however, these do not receive rigorous validation.
- Theorem 1: The existence of a finite-width global minimizer under fan-in/out group regularization is plausible; indeed, the penalty grows with the magnitude of learned structures, and extra zero-weight units do not reduce loss. While the proof is elaborate, it does not inform algorithm design or provide guarantees for the proposed nonconvex training scheme (with growth/shrinkage and CapNorm). The proposition on self-similar nonlinearities is reasonable but again remains mostly a modeling convenience rather than a driver of the empirical results.
- Training methodology: AdaRad’s angular/radial decomposition is unconventional and intricate. Yet, there is no ablation that (a) replaces AdaRad with a simpler proximal-gradient/group-lasso optimizer, or (b) shows clear gains over RMSprop/Adam with straightforward adjustments. Similarly, CapNorm is introduced because BN conflicts with the regularizer; but no alternatives are evaluated (e.g., BN without learnable affine parameters, freezing γ/β, WeightNorm/LayerNorm, or proximal methods that handle scaling invariances). Without such comparisons, it is unclear whether the complexity is necessary.
- Experiments:
  - Datasets are small/synthetic (MNIST, rectangles, convex) and one large tabular dataset (poker). There are no modern image benchmarks (e.g., CIFAR) or convolutional architectures, where architectural size selection is most impactful.
  - Results are mixed: clear degradations on convex; on poker both approaches do very well, suggesting the problem is relatively easy for nonlinear models. The claims about superior performance “in some cases” are not robustly substantiated across tasks.
  - Baselines: A critical missing baseline is training a sufficiently wide parametric network with fan-in group-ℓ2 (or ℓ1/ℓ2) regularization and proximal updates, then pruning zeroed groups in a single run (i.e., a direct competitor to “one-run model selection”). Also missing are dynamic-growth baselines (Wei et al., Net2Net variants) and modern pruning/growth methods. The parametric baseline choices (BN with λ=0) are not clearly comparable to the proposed regularized, CapNorm-trained models.
  - Efficiency: The primary motivation is to avoid expensive black-box model selection. However, there is no wall-clock or compute comparison showing that one nonparametric run is comparable or better than random/Bayesian search over sizes, especially since λ and angular step sizes are tuned and unit-addition schedules are manually set. The “rewind-and-continue” training protocol further complicates practical efficiency claims.
- Method sensitivity: The procedure depends on several couched hyperparameters (αr, αφ, ν, νfreq, λ), unit initialization, and a specific schedule. The paper provides limited guidance or robustness analysis, making reproducibility and adoption challenging.

Additional feedback (to help improve the work)
- Include ablations: (i) AdaRad vs RMSprop/Adam vs simple proximal gradient with group-ℓ2; (ii) CapNorm vs BN variants (frozen γ/β, capped scaling), LayerNorm, WeightNorm. Show that each component is necessary.
- Add a strong baseline: start with an overparameterized network, apply group-ℓ2 (or group-lasso) with proximal updates, and prune in one run. Compare accuracy, final size, and wall-clock time to your method.
- Report computational cost: number of epochs, FLOPs, memory, and elapsed time versus random/Bayesian search baselines tuned on width. The contribution is primarily about efficient model selection; quantify it.
- Evaluate on at least one modern convnet benchmark (e.g., CIFAR-10/100) using convolutional layers, or demonstrate limits on such settings if not currently supported.
- Clarify fairness in baselines: using λ=0 for BN models may disadvantage parametric baselines. Consider weight decay compatible with normalization or alternative normalization schemes that do not negate regularization.
- Improve clarity of the add/remove schedule and its sensitivity. The repeated “rewind last epochs” protocol is unusual; justify and quantify its impact.

Questions for the authors
1) How does your approach compare (accuracy/compute) to training a single oversized network with group-ℓ2 and proximal updates, then pruning zeroed units—i.e., one-run model selection without dynamic addition?
2) Can AdaRad be replaced by a simpler optimizer without loss? Please provide ablations across optimizers for the nonparametric setting.
3) Why not BN with fixed γ/β (or frozen statistics), or LayerNorm/WeightNorm, instead of introducing CapNorm? How do these compare empirically?
4) What is the wall-clock and computational cost relative to a reasonable random/Bayesian search over width? Include the cost of tuning λ and αφ.
5) How sensitive are results to ν, νfreq, αr, and unit initialization? Are there principled defaults?
6) Do your claims generalize to convolutional architectures? If not, what are the obstacles?
7) Can you release code to facilitate reproducibility?

Overall, while the paper contains interesting ideas and a careful theoretical existence result, the empirical evidence and baselines are insufficient to endorse the method over established alternatives.
