Summary
- The paper proposes “nonparametric neural networks” that adapt layer widths during a single training run by alternating gradient updates with addition/removal of units. The removal is driven by fan-in/fan-out group regularization (p=2), which can zero entire fan-ins/outs; addition appends zero-output units with random fan-ins.
- It introduces CapNorm, a variant of batch normalization that subtracts batch means but scales by 1/max(σ,1) and removes trainable γ/β, to avoid trivial rescalings that would defeat the regularizer.
- It proposes AdaRad, which decomposes the gradient per unit into a radial (parallel) and angular (orthogonal) component; it normalizes the angular component (per unit) and rotates the weight vector while applying unnormalized radial updates plus shrinkage.
- Theory: Theorem 1 claims the objective (empirical loss + fan-in/out regularizer with λ>0, 1≤p<∞) attains a global minimum at some finite dimensionality; Proposition 1 argues self-similar nonlinearities obviate per-layer λ tuning (equivalence to a single λ).
- Experiments on MNIST, rectangles-images, convex, and OpenML poker: on two datasets, same-size nonparametric nets outperform parametric baselines; on convex they underperform; on poker they report very low test error and even 0 errors at best.

Decision: Reject. Key reasons: (1) Experimental methodology and comparisons are not scientifically rigorous and contain questionable protocol choices; (2) Clarity/technical presentation and reproducibility are weak, with many ambiguities and numerous editorial issues; (3) While the overall direction is interesting, novelty relative to prior pruning/growth and group-sparsity approaches is limited and the empirical evidence does not convincingly support the main claims.

Supporting arguments
- Questionable evaluation protocol:
  • The choice of a “representative” nonparametric run is based partly on median test error across seeds (Sec. 7.4.1), which leaks test information into model selection.
  • Baselines for parametric nets are set with λ=0 “as ℓ2 is not compatible with standard BN.” This is not a generally accepted constraint; weight decay is standard with BN. Disallowing regularization on baselines while using it for nonparametric models compromises fairness.
  • Training protocols differ markedly (CapNorm vs BN; step size schedules; “rewinding the last 100 epochs” for NP but not mirrored for parametric). The annealing/rewind heuristics are nonstandard and insufficiently justified. No ablation disentangles the effects of CapNorm, AdaRad, and unit growth/removal.
  • The poker result claims 0 test errors on 100k examples; given that linear models perform near random, this is surprising and requires careful scrutiny of splits, leakage, and preprocessing. Details are insufficient.
- Insufficient breadth and depth of empirical validation:
  • Only small fully connected nets are studied; no convolutional networks despite the method purportedly being general. No results on more modern benchmarks or tasks where architecture search matters most.
  • The convex dataset shows nonparametric networks converge to too-small sizes and underperform. There is no analysis of when/why the framework fails or how to mitigate.
  • Efficiency claims (per-unit normalization leading to better scaling; smaller memory than RMSprop) are asserted but not substantiated with wall-clock, throughput, or memory benchmarks. Referenced Table 1 is not present.
- Theory-practice disconnect:
  • Theorem 1 is for feedforward nets without data-dependent normalization layers; yet CapNorm is central in practice. CapNorm induces batch-dependent, non-elementwise transformations of preactivations that are functions of all examples in a batch and of weights. The theorem’s assumptions (elementwise σl, directional differentiability, bounded gradients) and conclusions are not shown to apply with CapNorm.
  • The conditions on the loss gradient boundedness under a loss bound are asserted (b3) but not verified for the common softmax-cross-entropy instantiation used here.
  • Proposition 1’s equivalence relies on self-similarity; while the derivation is plausible for σ1..σL−1=ReLU, the interaction with the final layer and the actual e (often softmax cross-entropy) deserves a precise statement (e.g., whether logits scaling leaves the loss unchanged—generally it does not).
- Algorithmic clarity concerns:
  • The “rotation by angle αφ||φadj||” update is described at a high level. Precise definition of the rotation operator in high dimensions (the 2D subspace, numerical stability, and cost) is missing.
  • The adaptive normalization φadj uses φmax/cmax across layers and time; rationale for this specific normalization and its sensitivity is unclear.
  • The unit addition scheme (one per epoch, expected fan-in norm 1) lacks exact initialization distribution details and justification. The effect of addition frequency ν, αr, λ annealing, and CapNorm on survival is only qualitatively discussed.
- Presentation and references:
  • Numerous typos/formatting issues (e.g., section numbering “.2”, “bot not fastest”, “durgery”), inconsistent symbols, and several incomplete or malformed references (years, venues) detract from clarity.
  • Figures/Tables are referenced but missing in the submission excerpt; crucial for assessing results and complexity claims.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper)
- Provide clean, reproducible comparisons:
  • Use a single, shared training protocol where possible; include weight decay baselines with BN; include ablations: (i) AdaRad vs SGD/RMSprop/Adam (with per-unit RMS normalization as a control), (ii) CapNorm vs BN (with/without γ/β), (iii) growth/removal with standard optimizers, (iv) fan-in vs fan-out regularization, (v) schedules for ν, αr, αφ.
  • Avoid test-set-driven choices; select hyperparameters using validation only; report confidence intervals over multiple seeds.
  • Report wall-clock time, memory, and FLOPs to substantiate efficiency claims.
- Clarify theory:
  • State precisely whether Theorem 1 applies when CapNorm is present (batch-dependent, piecewise ops). If not, delineate the scope. Verify the loss gradient boundedness assumption for the used e.
  • Tighten Proposition 1 with an exact statement of σL/e assumptions; if softmax CE is used, explain how the rescaling does or does not leave the objective unchanged.
- Improve algorithmic exposition:
  • Provide explicit formulas for the rotation update, computational cost, and numerical stabilization. Explain φadj design and sensitivity; include a simple geometric illustration.
  • Detail unit initialization distributions and scaling with layer width; report how survival/elimination correlates with these choices.
- Expand evaluation:
  • Include convolutional models and modern datasets where adaptive width is impactful. Compare against strong structured sparsity baselines (group lasso, variational dropout, NetSlimming) and dynamic growth/pruning methods (e.g., Dynamic Network Surgery).
  • Analyze the convex dataset failure and propose remedies (e.g., λ annealing, alternative schedules).
- Presentation:
  • Fix typos and references; ensure all figures/tables are present; release code.

Questions to authors
1) Does Theorem 1 still hold when CapNorm is used during training (with batch-dependent μ,σ and the max(σ,1) nonlinearity)? If so, please formalize the assumptions and outline the proof adjustments.
2) Why disallow weight decay in parametric BN baselines? Can you provide baselines with weight decay and/or penalizing BN γ to ensure fairness?
3) How exactly is the rotation implemented per unit (formula, computational complexity)? Is the cost negligible relative to standard updates?
4) On poker: what is the exact dataset (binary vs 10-class), splits, preprocessing, and leakage safeguards? How reproducible is the 0-error result?
5) Can you provide ablations isolating the contributions of CapNorm and AdaRad to the gains over same-size baselines?
6) How sensitive are the results to ν, αr, αφ, and the unit addition frequency? Is there a principled way to set αr relative to λ beyond the heuristic αr=150/λ?
