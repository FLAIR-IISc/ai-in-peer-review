Summary and contributions
The paper proposes “nonparametric neural networks,” a framework that treats layer widths as variables to be optimized during a single training run by dynamically adding and removing hidden units. The key ingredients are:
- A group-regularization scheme on fan-ins (or fan-outs) using an ℓp norm (focus on p=2) that induces whole-unit sparsity and guarantees the existence of a finite optimal width (Theorem 1).
- A training procedure that maintains f-equivalence when adding zero units and deletes units whose fan-in (or fan-out) shrinks to zero.
- CapNorm, a modification of batch normalization that avoids negating the effect of the shrinkage penalty by only standardizing when the batch std > 1 and without learnable affine parameters.
- AdaRad, an optimizer that decomposes the gradient of each fan-in into radial (parallel) and angular (orthogonal) components, normalizing only the angular part to let brand-new units learn while preserving the intended effect of the shrinkage penalty; a momentum variant (AdaRad-M) is described in the appendix.
- Experiments on MNIST, Rectangles-Images, Convex, and a large OpenML Poker dataset; qualitative analyses of width dynamics and unit lifetimes.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- The empirical validation is insufficient and not convincingly fair: core comparisons mix multiple factors (CapNorm vs BN, different regularization, different optimizers/step-sizes) and lack ablations, and the benchmarks are small-scale FC nets with limited relevance to current practice.
- The methodological novelty is incremental relative to prior work on structured sparsity (group lasso), grow-and-prune, and dynamic architecture methods; the main theoretical result (existence of a finite-width minimizer under group regularization) is valuable but unsurprising given known properties of group sparsity.

Supporting arguments
- Positioning and motivation: The paper is well-motivated—reducing black-box architecture search by adapting width during a single training run is attractive. The notion of f-equivalence via zero-unit insertions/removals is natural. The literature review covers pruning, Bayesian optimization, and some grow-network work (e.g., Wei et al., Net2Net), though the empirical section does not compare against competitive grow/prune baselines that also dynamically adjust width.
- Technical soundness: 
  - Theorem 1 provides a rigorous existence result that rules out width divergence under the proposed regularizers. The proof is lengthy but appears technically careful; the directional-derivative handling adds robustness to non-smooth nonlinearities. Still, the result largely formalizes what group sparsity is expected to do: eliminate whole units to a finite number.
  - Proposition 1 (self-similar nonlinearities) is a nice observation: it simplifies per-layer regularization strengths. The constraints (ReLU-like) are practical.
  - CapNorm is a simple modification to BN; intuitively reasonable to prevent canceling the penalty, but there is no ablation to quantify its necessity vs alternatives (e.g., fixed gamma/beta, weight standardization).
  - AdaRad’s decomposition and normalization of only the orthogonal component is an interesting optimization idea tailored to the group penalty. The cost/memory analysis is sketched, but no wall-clock comparisons are given, and the gains vs strong baselines (e.g., RMSprop/Adam with appropriate scaling of shrinkage) are not rigorously established.
- Empirical support:
  - Datasets are small and fully connected (MNIST, Rectangles-Images, Convex). The approach performs well on two datasets but clearly underperforms on Convex, suggesting instability across tasks. The Poker results claim excellent performance, but this dataset is atypical (simple tabular features), and comparisons rely on OpenML summaries rather than strong modern baselines (e.g., calibrated gradient-boosted trees).
  - Fairness of comparisons is questionable: nonparametric models use CapNorm and structured shrinkage, while parametric baselines use BN with λ=0 (since BN + ℓ2 allegedly conflicts), often with different optimal step sizes and optimizers. This confounds whether the advantage stems from the dynamic width mechanism, the CapNorm + group penalty combination, or other training details. The extensive “rewind and reduce step size” schedule differs between NP and P settings.
  - Missing ablations: no controlled study isolating the contributions of (i) unit addition/removal with group penalty vs (ii) CapNorm vs (iii) AdaRad vs standard optimizers. No comparison to alternative dynamic-width methods or to strong structured-sparsity baselines (e.g., group-lasso “Network Slimming”-style, structured pruning with regrowth).
  - Scalability: while the Poker experiment indicates feasibility on a larger dataset, there is no wall-clock/compute accounting or comparison to comparable baselines under the same budget.

Constructive feedback to improve the paper
- Provide controlled ablations: (a) fixed-width + CapNorm + group-lasso vs (b) fixed-width + BN + group-lasso-friendly scaling vs (c) dynamic-width but standard optimizer (RMSprop/Adam) vs (d) AdaRad without CapNorm. Quantify each component’s contribution.
- Ensure fair baselines: use the same normalization strategy (e.g., CapNorm or BN without trainable gamma/beta) and compatible regularization across NP and P models; match compute budgets, early-stopping protocols, and annealing schedules.
- Compare against strong structured-sparsity and grow/prune methods on comparable settings (e.g., group-lasso channel pruning, dynamic network surgery, Net2Net + pruning). Include at least one modern CNN benchmark (e.g., CIFAR-10/100) to demonstrate relevance.
- Report wall-clock time, memory, and number of optimizer steps to reach a target validation error for NP vs P and AdaRad vs RMSprop/Adam; show the cost-benefit tradeoffs of starting small and growing.
- Clarify the unit addition schedule, initialization, and sensitivity analysis (ν, νfreq, αr, αφ). Provide stability across seeds and the variance of the final selected width.
- CapNorm: justify design choices with experiments vs alternative normalizations that maintain the regularizer’s effect (e.g., BN with fixed gamma=1, or normalization layers that do not permit scale compensation).
- Improve clarity: fix typos, streamline the heavy theoretical appendix (move key lemmas into main text as intuition), and provide code for reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
1. How sensitive are results to the unit addition rate/schedule and to αr/αφ? Do you observe mode collapse (too few units) or runaway growth under different settings?
2. Can you provide ablations isolating the effect of CapNorm? Would BN with fixed gamma/beta (or weight standardization) avoid the reported conflict with ℓ2?
3. How does AdaRad compare to RMSprop/Adam when you proportionally scale the shrinkage term to account for per-parameter normalization? Any results on wall-clock efficiency?
4. Are the comparisons matched for compute (epochs, minibatches, flops)? If not, how do NP and P models compare under a fixed budget?
5. Does the method extend cleanly to convolutional layers and modern vision tasks? Any preliminary results?
6. How stable is the selected final width across seeds? Please report distributions, not just typical representatives.
7. Can you release code and scripts to reproduce the training procedures (including “rewind” steps) and hyperparameter searches?
