- Problem: Learn network width during training by treating hidden-unit counts as optimization variables, adding/removing units on the fly.
- Theorem 1: Group-ℓp (p<∞) regularization guarantees a global minimum at finite width, providing a principled basis for nonparametric width selection.
- Practical scheme: Alternate gradient steps with adding zero units and removing units with zeroed fan-in/out, exploiting f-equivalence when σ(0)=0.
- Self-similar nonlinearities: For self-similar layers (e.g., ReLU), per-layer regularization can be unified into a single λ via rescaling (Proposition 1).
- CapNorm: A BN variant that preserves norm-based regularization by only downscaling when batch std > 1 and removing affine parameters.
- AdaRad optimizer: Decomposes fan-in gradients into radial and angular components, normalizes angular updates, and uses separate step sizes; AdaRad-M adds momentum.
- Empirics: On small MLPs (MNIST, rectangles, convex, OpenML poker), NP models often beat same-size parametric models and tuned P models, but fail on the convex dataset.
- Decision: Reject.
- Main rejection reason 1: Empirical validation is limited, inconsistent, and lacks comparisons to strong, directly relevant baselines for width learning/pruning/growing.
- Main rejection reason 2: The practical recipe is complex (CapNorm + AdaRad + tuned add/remove schedule + rewinding), raising fairness concerns and unclear gains over simpler methods.
- Theory vs practice critique: Theorem 1 is an existence result but gives no convergence or dynamics guarantees for the add/remove algorithm; many design choices are ad hoc.
- CapNorm concern: Using CapNorm for NP and BN for P confounds normalization and regularization effects; alternative normalizers and penalties were not fairly compared.
- Optimizer concern: No ablation shows AdaRad is necessary; standard optimizers with block normalization plus proximal/group-shrink might suffice, and compute claims lack wall-clock evidence.
- Empirical scope critique: Experiments are mostly small MLPs; lack of convolutional/modern image/text benchmarks and insufficient analysis of failure cases (e.g., convex).
- Missing baselines: Paper omits comparisons to many related methods (Network Slimming, MorphNet, L0 regularization, RigL, NeST, DARTS) that are strong, simpler baselines.
- Reproducibility/complexity: Protocol uses rewinds, annealing, and hand-tuned hyperparameters (e.g., αr = 150/λ); sensitivity and scalability are unclear.
- Recommendation 1: Strengthen baselines by comparing to modern width-adaptation/pruning methods and ensure shared normalization/regularization for fair comparison.
- Recommendation 2: Provide ablations for CapNorm vs BN/WeightNorm/LayerNorm, AdaRad vs Adam/RMSprop with group shrinkage, p=1 vs p=2, and hyperparameter sensitivities.
- Recommendation 3: Report wall-clock time, throughput, and memory for all methods and avoid mixing normalization regimes across NP vs P.
- Recommendation 4: Scale to convolutional networks and at least one modern image benchmark to demonstrate generality where width matters.
- Recommendation 5: Provide theory-practice links for add/remove dynamics (stability, pruning/growth rates) and consider proximal group-shrink updates to simplify optimizer design.
- Recommendation 6: Release code and detailed hyperparameters to improve reproducibility.
- Key questions for authors: How sensitive are results to αr, αφ, ν, and rewinding; can you provide robustness plots and a default, tuning-free setup?
- Key questions for authors: Provide wall-clock, throughput, and memory comparisons for AdaRad vs Adam/RMSprop including unit add/remove overhead.
- Key questions for authors: Can the method work with LayerNorm/WeightNorm plus decoupled weight decay, and have parametric models been tested with CapNorm for fair comparison?
- Key questions for authors: Why not use proximal group-ℓ2 updates with standard optimizers instead of a custom optimizer?
- Key questions for authors: What causes failure on the convex dataset and can adaptive λ or different addition schedules fix premature pruning?
- Key questions for authors: How does the method extend to convolutional layers (per-channel regularization, zero-channel add/remove) and preserve f-equivalence with normalization?
- Key questions for authors: Can you compare directly to BN-scale methods like Network Slimming and MorphNet on shared benchmarks?
