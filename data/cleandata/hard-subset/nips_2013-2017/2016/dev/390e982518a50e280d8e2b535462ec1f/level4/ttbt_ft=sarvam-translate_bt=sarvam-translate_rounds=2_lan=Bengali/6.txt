The authors have proposed a new neural network architecture for graph prediction problems. They explain it as a generalization of convolution outside of a grid structure. The method has been shown to perform better than several kernel methods. It works well in vertex classification, but not in graph classification. The experimental results look promising, but I have several concerns about the presentation and testing. Presentation: - How exactly is diffusion defined here? It's a fundamental concept, why isn't there a formal definition or at least a reference to previous work? I spent 10 minutes googling it, and I couldn't find a clear definition. I'm not an expert in graph/social network analysis, but it still seems like an important omission. In any case, I assume there are some standard ways to characterize a node's neighbors that are used here. - The authors strongly emphasize how this work generalizes convolution. I think it's only true in the graph setting, not in the edge or vertex prediction setting (since I don't see spatial variation in those settings). Since the contribution is good performance in vertex classification, I don't think this story needs to be emphasized. Testing: - One of the claims is that this architecture handles vertex, edge, and graph prediction in a unified way. Why isn't there an edge test? There should be at least a comment on this. Also, can it be used for structure learning? - In vertex prediction, the authors compare with baselines including kernel methods, logistic regression, and a CRF. But logistic regression only takes features from the vertex, not its neighbors, so it's not surprising that they don't do well. Tests need to be done that train a shallow model on the same signal (i.e., features comparable to the diffusion-based features used here). Then we need to see if the neural network model is more accurate or faster. The same applies to CRF; the authors don't specify which features it uses, but even if it uses edge features, we need to compare with a feature-matched model. The authors mention that they use up to 5 hops of diffusion, which I think they are looking at up to 5 hops of neighbors. CRF and LR should get the same features. - I'm not an expert in neural network applications to graphs, but I'm surprised that there aren't other neural-network-based models to compare it to. Why is this type of work applicable and/or compared to: https://cs.stanford.edu/people/jure/pubs/node2vec-kdd16.pdf In conclusion, I think the architecture is elegant and the paper is very well written (except for the omission of the diffusion definition), but the tests are not entirely convincing and that is the main reason why we cannot judge an applied paper. At the moment, I think this paper is somewhat marginal, but I would be happy to readjust my opinion if the authors could comment on some of the above. Update: After reading the response and looking more closely at the related literature, I am downgrading my review. I strongly disagree with the authors' claim that "[many] other techniques were developed in parallel to our own and there were not easily available open-source implementations". See the node2vec paper published in parallel, for example: - They have a fairly extensive test - They compare with previous neural network-based methods, LINE and DeepWalk - These methods are from 2014 and 2015, so I see no excuse for not comparing them. In fact, they are not even cited, which I think is unacceptable! The quality of this paper is much lower than LINE, DeepWalk, and node2vec, and so I strongly argue to reject this paper.
