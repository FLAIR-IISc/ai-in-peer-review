The authors of the graph prediction proposal offer a novel neural network architecture for problems. They interpret this as a generalization of convolution beyond grid structures. The method has been shown to perform better than many kernel methods. It performs better on top classification, but not on graph classification. The experimental results look promising, but I have several concerns about the presentation and experiments. Presentation: - How is propagation properly defined here? This is such a core concept, why is there no formal definition or at least reference to previous work? I spent 10 minutes googling it, and I couldn't find a single clear definition. I am not an expert on graph/social network analysis, but this still seems like a significant oversight. In any case, I assume there is some standard way of attributing neighbors to a node that is used here. - The authors strongly emphasize how this work generalizes convolution. I think this is only true in the graph setting, not in the edge or top prediction settings (for example, I don't see where the spatial variation is in those settings). Since the contribution is better performance on top classification, I don't think it's worth emphasizing this story. Experiment: - One of the claims is that this architecture handles top, edge, graph prediction in an integrated way. Why are there no edge experiments? There should be at least one comment on this. Also, can it be used for structure learning? - On top prediction, the authors compare against baselines including kernel methods, logistic regression, and CRF. But logistic regression only captures features from the top, not its neighbors, so it's not surprising that they don't do well. There should be experiments that train a shallow model on the same signal (i.e., on features that are comparable to the propagation-based features used here). Then we need to see if the neural network model is more accurate or faster. The same goes for CRF; the authors do not specify which features it uses, but even if it uses edge features, we need to compare against an equivalent feature. The authors mention that they use up to 5 hops of propagation, which I think means they are looking at neighbors up to 5 hops away. CRF and LR need to capture the same features. - I am not an expert on neural networks applied to graphs, but I am surprised that there are no other neural-network-based models to compare against. Why isn't this kind of work applied and/or compared against: https://cs.stanford.edu/people/jure/pubs/node2vec-kdd16.pdf In conclusion, I find the architecture elegant and the paper very well-written (except for the definition of propagation), but the experiments are not entirely convincing, and this is the main factor by which we can judge an applied paper. Right now, I think this paper is somewhat borderline, but I would be happy to readjust my opinion if the authors could comment on some of the above. Update: After reading the response and closely looking at the relevant literature, I am downgrading my review. I strongly disagree with the authors' claim that "[many of the] techniques were developed in parallel to our own and did not have readily available open-source implementations". For example, look at the node2vec paper that came in parallel: - They have quite extensive experiments - They compare against previous neural network-based methods, LINE and DeepWalk - These methods are from 2014 and 2015, so I see no excuse for not comparing against them. In fact, they have not even been cited, which seems unacceptable to me! The quality of this paper is much lower than LINE, DeepWalk, and node2vec, and therefore I strongly argue to reject this paper.
