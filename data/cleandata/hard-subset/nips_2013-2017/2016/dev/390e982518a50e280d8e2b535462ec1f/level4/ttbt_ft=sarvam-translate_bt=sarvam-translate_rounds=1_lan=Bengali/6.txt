The authors have proposed a new neural network architecture for graph prediction problems. They explain it as a generalization of convolution outside of a grid structure. The method has been shown to perform better than several kernel methods. It works well on vertex classification, but not on graph classification. The experimental results look promising, but I have several concerns about the presentation and tests. Presentation: - How exactly is diffusion defined here? This is a fundamental concept, why isn't there a formal definition or at least a reference to previous work? I spent 10 minutes googling it, and I couldn't find a clear definition. I'm not an expert in graph/social network analysis, but it still seems like an important omission. In any case, I assume there are some standard ways to characterize a node's neighbors that are used here. - The authors strongly emphasize how this work generalizes convolution. I think this is only true in graph setting, not in edge or vertex prediction settings (as I don't see where spatial variation exists in those settings). Since the contribution is good performance on vertex classification, I don't think this story is worth emphasizing. Tests: - One of the claims is that this architecture handles vertex, edge, graph prediction in a unified way. Why isn't there an edge test? There should be at least one comment on this. Also, can it be used for structure learning? - On vertex prediction, the authors compare with baselines including kernel methods, logistic regression, and a CRF. But logistic regression only takes features from the vertex, not its neighbors, so it's not surprising that they don't do well. Tests need to be done that train a shallow model on the same signal (i.e., features comparable to the diffusion-based features used here). Then we need to see if the neural network model is more accurate or faster. The same applies to CRF; the authors don't specify which features it uses, but even if it uses edge features, we need to compare it with a feature-matched model. The authors mention that they use up to 5 hops of diffusion, which I think means they are looking at neighbors up to 5 hops away. CRF and LR need to get the same features. - I'm not an expert in neural networks applied to graphs, but I'm surprised there aren't other neural-network-based models to compare it to. Why isn't this type of work applicable and/or compared to: https://cs.stanford.edu/people/jure/pubs/node2vec-kdd16.pdf In conclusion, I think the architecture is elegant and the paper is very well written (except for the omission of the diffusion definition), but the tests are not entirely convincing and that is the main reason why we can judge an applied paper. At the moment, I think this paper is somewhat marginal, but I would be happy to readjust my opinion if the authors could comment on some of the above. Update: After reading the response and looking at the related literature more closely, I am downgrading my review. I strongly disagree with the authors' claim that "[many] other techniques were developed in parallel to our own and there were not easily available open-source implementations". See the node2vec paper, published in parallel, for example: - They have a fairly extensive test - They compare with previous neural network-based methods, LINE and DeepWalk - These methods are from 2014 and 2015, so I see no excuse for not comparing them. In fact, they are not even cited, which I think is unacceptable! The quality of this paper is much lower than LINE, DeepWalk, and node2vec, and therefore I strongly argue to reject this paper.
