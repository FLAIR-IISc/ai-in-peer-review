- Introduces DCNNs with a diffusion-convolution operation that builds isomorphism-invariant graph representations.
- Motivated by the need to balance predictive performance and model complexity, arguing diffusion is a better basis than raw graph structure.
- Shows strong, statistically significant node classification results on datasets like Cora and Pubmed.
- Model is flexible, handling node and graph classification and both structural and feature inputs.
- Paper is clear and well-organized, with solid mathematical formulations and experimental details.
- Scalability is a major issue due to memory-heavy dense tensors, limiting use on very large graphs.
- No runtime (training/inference) statistics are reported, obscuring practical feasibility.
- Experimental comparisons may be unfair because DCNNs use node features+structure while some baselines use only structure; results for a purely structural DCNN are missing.
- Lacks comparisons with recent graph neural architectures (e.g., GCNs, GATs), making state-of-the-art positioning unclear.
- Graph-level classification performance is weak, and simple mean aggregation likely fails to capture necessary information.
- The claimed relationship to CNNs is unclear because there is no explicit generalization of 2D convolution presented.
- Pro: Novel, well-motivated conceptual contribution with promising node classification performance.
- Con: Scalability, missing runtime data, limited comparisons to modern GNNs, and weak graph-classification results reduce practical impact.
- Recommendation: Weak Accept — interesting and promising but needs improved scalability and more comprehensive comparisons.
