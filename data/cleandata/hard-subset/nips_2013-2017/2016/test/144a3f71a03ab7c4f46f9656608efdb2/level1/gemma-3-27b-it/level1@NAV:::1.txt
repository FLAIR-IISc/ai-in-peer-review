## Review of "Learning Bayesian Networks with Ancestral Constraints"

This paper addresses the problem of learning Bayesian networks optimally under ancestral constraints, a valuable task for incorporating prior knowledge. The core idea is to leverage a recent framework for optimal structure learning based on non-decomposable scores, which naturally accommodates ancestral constraints, but requires efficient handling of these constraints within an oracle that typically works with decomposable scores. The authors cleverly propose inferring decomposable constraints from ancestral ones to empower this oracle, significantly improving efficiency.

The paper is well-motivated, connecting to the broader fields of Bayesian network learning, constraint-based modeling, and optimization techniques. It positions itself correctly within the literature, referencing key works like Darwiche (2009), Koller & Friedman (2009), and recent advances in optimal structure learning (Chen et al., 2015, 2016). The approach of translating non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones is a novel and pragmatic way to bridge the gap between theoretical frameworks and practical implementations. The connection to MaxSAT for ordering constraint inference is also a solid contribution.

A major strength of the paper is the empirical evaluation. The authors demonstrate significant performance gains (orders of magnitude faster) compared to an ILP-based approach (GOBNILP), showcasing the effectiveness of their method. The experimental setup, varying network size, dataset size, and constraint percentage, provides a comprehensive assessment. The analysis of structural Hamming distance offers valuable insight into the accuracy of the learned models, and the observation regarding the impact of even a small number of ancestral constraints is notable. 

However, the paper could benefit from more detailed discussion of the limitations. For instance, the scalability to larger networks (beyond n=20) is not fully explored. Furthermore, the complexity of the constraint projection step (especially the MaxSAT reduction) isn’t deeply analyzed. While the authors mention the existence of other approaches to handling ancestral constraints (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos, 2012), a more direct comparison, beyond simply mentioning them, would strengthen the paper. The notation throughout could be slightly improved for readability.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to incorporating non-decomposable constraints into optimal Bayesian network learning.
*   Strong empirical results demonstrating significant performance improvements.
*   Clear connection to and advancement of existing work in the field.
*   Sound theoretical grounding.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Scalability to very large networks remains relatively unexplored.
*   Complexity analysis of constraint projection could be more thorough.
*   Limited direct comparison to alternative approaches that handle ancestral constraints.



**Overall:** I recommend acceptance. The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian network learning, offering a practical and efficient solution to a challenging problem. The strengths outweigh the weaknesses, and the work is of high quality and significance.
