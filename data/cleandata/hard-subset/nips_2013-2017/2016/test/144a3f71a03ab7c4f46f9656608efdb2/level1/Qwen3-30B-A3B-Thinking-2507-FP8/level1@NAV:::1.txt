

This paper addresses the important problem of learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints (non-decomposable background knowledge), extending Chen et al.'s (2015) framework for optimal structure learning with non-decomposable scores. The authors propose a method to convert non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints that can be processed by existing oracles designed for decomposable scores. Their approach leverages the EC tree search space and demonstrates orders-of-magnitude improvements over ILP-based methods.

The paper is technically sound with strong theoretical foundations, including theorems about constraint projection and soundness of pruning. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, using multiple standard benchmarks (ALARM, ANDES, CHILD, CPCS54, HEPAR2) and systematically varying parameters (number of variables, dataset size, constraint percentage). The authors carefully address both strengths (efficiency gains) and limitations (scalability, constraint projection complexity).

The work is well-situated within the literature, referencing Chen et al.'s foundational EC tree work and distinguishing from constraint-based approaches like the PC algorithm (Borboudakis and Tsamardinos, 2012). The paper demonstrates significant practical value by showing that a small number of ancestral constraints (10%-25%) can have similar impact on model quality as substantially larger datasets (e.g., increasing data from 512 to 2048).

The main strength is the novel method for handling non-decomposable constraints through constraint projection, which is both theoretically sound and empirically validated. This work advances the state of the art by enabling the use of important background knowledge that was previously difficult to incorporate.

The primary limitation is scalability to very large networks (the paper shows results up to n=20 variables, while some recent work scales to 26 variables). The constraint projection method, while theoretically sound, might be complex to implement for practitioners.

Pros: Novel constraint projection method, significant efficiency gains, thorough evaluation, clear theoretical foundation, practical significance.
Cons: Limited scalability to very large networks, implementation complexity for constraint projection.

This paper makes a strong, high-quality contribution to Bayesian network structure learning that addresses a practical problem with a well-justified approach. It advances the field demonstrably and should be accepted for NIPS.
