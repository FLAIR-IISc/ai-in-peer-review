

# Review of "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"

This paper addresses a significant challenge in Bayesian network structure learning: incorporating non-decomposable ancestral constraints (specifying cause-and-effect relationships) into optimal structure learning frameworks. The authors build upon Chen et al.'s EC tree search space for non-decomposable scores, which previously could not handle ancestral constraints. Their key contribution is a method to infer decomposable edge and ordering constraints from ancestral constraints, enabling the use of existing oracles optimized for decomposable scores.

The paper is well-positioned within the NIPS literature. It extends Chen et al. (2015, 2016) who introduced the EC tree for non-decomposable scores, and relates to work by Cussens (2008) on ILP-based structure learning with ancestral constraints. Unlike previous approaches that either couldn't handle ancestral constraints (K2, order graph methods) or had not been properly evaluated for this specific task (ILP/CP), this work provides a systematic method to transform non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones that can be leveraged by existing oracles.

**Strengths:**

*Quality:* The paper is technically sound with well-motivated theoretical contributions. The authors prove key theorems (Theorems 1-4) establishing the soundness of their constraint projection methods. Their experimental evaluation is comprehensive, comparing against GOBNILP (an ILP-based system) with and without projected constraints. The results consistently show orders-of-magnitude improvements in efficiency (Table 1), with the EC tree approach scaling to 20 variables while ILP approaches struggle. The paper also demonstrates that a small number of ancestral constraints (10-25%) can have similar impact on model accuracy as increasing training data size by 4x, highlighting the practical significance.

*Clarity:* The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. Key concepts (ancestral constraints, BN graphs, EC trees) are clearly introduced with helpful examples. The transition from theoretical framework to algorithmic implementation is logical and well-explained. The experimental setup is detailed enough for reproducibility, and the results are presented clearly with appropriate statistical reporting (averaged over 50 datasets).

*Originality:* This work represents a significant advancement over previous approaches. While Chen et al. (2015, 2016) established the EC tree for non-decomposable scores, they couldn't handle ancestral constraints. Previous ILP approaches (e.g., Cussens, 2008) could theoretically handle such constraints but had not been properly evaluated for this specific problem. The authors' method for inferring decomposable constraints from ancestral constraints is novel and directly addresses this gap. Their approach is distinct from Borboudakis and Tsamardinos (2012, 2013) who used path constraints for constraint-based learning, not optimal structure learning with non-decomposable scores.

*Significance:* The problem of incorporating background knowledge into Bayesian network learning is highly relevant to practitioners. The paper demonstrates that ancestral constraints can dramatically improve learning efficiency while maintaining model accuracy. The finding that a small number of constraints can substitute for large amounts of training data is particularly compelling for real-world applications where data collection is expensive. The scalability to 20 variables (Table 3) is comparable to state-of-the-art score-based approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of their approach. For instance, they mention that "a relatively small number of ancestral constraints (say 10%-25%) can have a similar impact on the quality of the observed network," but don't explore what happens when the constraints are incorrect or partially incorrect. This is a critical consideration for practical applications where background knowledge may be imperfect.

The MaxSAT encoding for ordering constraints (Section 5.2) is presented as a solution, but the authors don't discuss the computational complexity of solving the MaxSAT problem or how it scales with the number of ancestral constraints. While they report using EVASOLVER, a more thorough analysis of the trade-offs between the time spent on constraint projection versus the time saved in the search would strengthen the empirical evaluation.

**Conclusion:**

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. It solves a meaningful problem (incorporating ancestral constraints) with a technically sound approach that builds upon recent advances in the field. The empirical results are compelling, demonstrating significant efficiency improvements over alternative approaches. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a clear contribution to the literature.

The work is highly relevant to NIPS, addressing a practical challenge in structure learning with a novel technical approach that advances the state of the art. The significance is demonstrated through both theoretical contributions and empirical results that show meaningful improvements in efficiency and model accuracy.

**Recommendation:** Accept

**Pro:** Strong technical contribution, clear presentation, comprehensive evaluation, significant practical impact, builds upon recent NIPS work (Chen et al., 2015, 2016), addresses an important gap in the literature.

**Con:** Limited discussion of constraint errors (imperfect background knowledge), some details about MaxSAT complexity could be expanded.
