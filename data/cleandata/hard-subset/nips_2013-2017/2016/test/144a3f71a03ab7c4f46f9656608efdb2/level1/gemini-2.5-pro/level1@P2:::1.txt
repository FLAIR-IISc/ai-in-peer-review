**Summary of Main Ideas**

This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data, subject to background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints (e.g., variable X must be an ancestor of Y, or X must not be an ancestor of Y). The central challenge is that ancestral constraints are non-decomposable, meaning their satisfaction cannot be checked by looking at local parent-child relationships independently. This property makes them incompatible with many state-of-the-art optimal structure learning algorithms (e.g., those based on dynamic programming or A* search over orderings), which rely on the assumption of a decomposable score function.

The authors' approach builds on a recent framework for exact structure learning with non-decomposable scores which performs an A* search over a space of equivalence classes of DAGs (an "EC tree"). A key component of this framework is an "oracle"—an efficient algorithm for finding optimal structures under decomposable scores—which is used to compute an admissible heuristic for the A* search.

The main contributions of this paper are twofold. First, it shows how to incorporate ancestral constraints into the EC tree search by developing sound pruning rules for nodes (CPDAGs) that are inconsistent with the given constraints. Second, and more significantly, it introduces a method to "empower the oracle" by inferring *decomposable* constraints (edge presence/absence and pairwise variable orderings) from the given *non-decomposable* ancestral constraints. These inferred decomposable constraints can be passed to the oracle, which tightens the A* heuristic and dramatically accelerates the search. The paper provides algorithms and theoretical justifications (Theorems 2-4) for this inference process, including a novel reduction to MaxSAT for inferring a maximal set of consistent ordering constraints. The proposed method is empirically compared to a state-of-the-art Integer Linear Programming (ILP) approach, which can also handle ancestral constraints, demonstrating orders-of-magnitude performance improvements.

**Review**

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of exact Bayesian network structure learning. It tackles an important practical problem, proposes an elegant and effective solution, and supports its claims with strong theoretical and empirical evidence.

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed approach is sound and well-grounded in prior work on A*-based structure search. The core technical contribution—projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints onto decomposable edge and ordering constraints—is well-formalized and justified through a series of theorems. The claims of superior performance are convincingly supported by a thorough experimental evaluation. The authors compare their method against a relevant and strong baseline (the ILP solver GOBNILP) across a variety of network structures, dataset sizes, and constraint densities. The results in Tables 1 and 2 clearly demonstrate not only that the proposed EC tree search is faster than the ILP approach, but also that the constraint projection technique is the critical component responsible for this speedup, often accelerating the search by several orders of magnitude. The inclusion of results on learning accuracy (structural Hamming distance) in Table 3 is also a welcome addition, highlighting the practical benefit of incorporating background knowledge. The work is complete, presenting the problem, solution, theory, and evaluation in a self-contained manner.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and situates the work within the existing literature. The technical sections are presented logically, starting with the necessary preliminaries before introducing the core concepts of the EC tree search and the novel constraint projection methods. The distinction between what the main search can handle (non-decomposable constraints) and what the oracle can handle (decomposable constraints) is made very clear, which effectively highlights the central idea of "empowering the oracle". The algorithms and theoretical results are presented with precision. The experimental setup and results are easy to understand. The paper provides sufficient detail for an expert to grasp the method and its implications.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While the problem of learning with constraints is not new, and the base EC tree search framework was introduced in prior work by the authors, the key idea of systematically inferring decomposable constraints from non-decomposable ones to accelerate an exact search is novel and insightful. This "projection" or "oracle empowerment" technique is a clever bridge between two classes of algorithms: those that are general but potentially slow (handling non-decomposable scores) and those that are fast but restrictive (requiring decomposable scores). The formulation of ordering constraint inference as a MaxSAT problem is also a creative and non-obvious contribution. The paper clearly articulates how its approach differs from and improves upon both standard decomposable-score methods (which cannot handle the constraints) and general-purpose solvers like ILP.

**Significance:** The results presented are significant and likely to have a strong impact on the field. The ability to incorporate ancestral knowledge is crucial in many scientific domains (e.g., genetics, systems biology) where causal priors are available. This paper makes exact, score-based learning with such knowledge significantly more practical, pushing the boundary of solvable problems to over 20 variables, which is competitive with state-of-the-art methods for unconstrained learning. The demonstrated orders-of-magnitude speedup over ILP is a substantial advancement. Furthermore, the core idea of projecting complex constraints to empower a simpler, faster subroutine could be a powerful algorithmic paradigm applicable to other constrained optimization problems in machine learning beyond Bayesian network learning. Researchers and practitioners interested in exact inference, structure learning, and incorporating domain knowledge are very likely to use and build upon these ideas.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Strong Contribution:** Addresses an important problem with a novel, elegant, and effective solution.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The approach is well-justified with theoretical results and is technically solid.
*   **State-of-the-Art Results:** Demonstrates a massive empirical improvement (orders of magnitude) over the primary alternative (ILP) for this problem.
*   **High Clarity:** Exceptionally well-written and easy to follow.
*   **Potential for Broader Impact:** The "empower the oracle" paradigm may be applicable to other constrained optimization problems.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   None of significant weight. One could argue that the core search framework is incremental on the authors' prior work, but the contribution related to constraint handling is substantial and stands on its own. The scalability is still limited, as is the case for all exact methods for this NP-hard problem, but the paper pushes the state of the art forward.

In conclusion, this is a clear-cut accept. It is a well-executed piece of research that presents a significant advance in an important area.
