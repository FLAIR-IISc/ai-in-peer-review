

This paper addresses the problem of learning Bayesian network structures with ancestral constraints, which specify cause-and-effect relationships between variables and are non-decomposable. The authors extend Chen et al.'s (2015, 2016) framework for optimal structure learning with non-decomposable scores by introducing a method to project ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints that can be handled by existing oracles. The key contribution is a theoretically sound approach for transforming non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones, enabling efficient integration of ancestral constraints into structure learning.

The paper builds on recent work in Bayesian network structure learning, particularly Chen et al.'s EC tree search space and oracle-based approach. It correctly identifies that most mainstream structure learning approaches (e.g., K2 algorithm, order graph methods, and ILP-based systems) cannot directly handle ancestral constraints because they are non-decomposable, while the EC tree framework can. The authors provide formal theorems (Theorems 2-4) demonstrating how to infer edge constraints and ordering constraints from ancestral constraints, which can then be used to empower oracles that optimize decomposable scores.

The technical soundness is strong, with clear theoretical foundations for the constraint projection method. The paper correctly acknowledges that ancestral constraints are non-decomposable, while the inferred edge and ordering constraints are decomposable, making them compatible with existing oracles. The authors provide a detailed explanation of the EC tree search space and how ancestral constraints can be incorporated through pruning. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, comparing their approach against ILP-based methods (GOBNILP) across multiple benchmark networks (ALARM, ANDES, CHILD, CPCS54, HEPAR2) with varying numbers of variables, dataset sizes, and constraint percentages. The results consistently show their approach is orders-of-magnitude faster than ILP-based methods, with significant speedups increasing as the number of variables grows. The paper also demonstrates that a small number of ancestral constraints (10-25%) can have a similar impact on model accuracy as significantly more training data.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with appropriate figures (Figures 1a-b) illustrating the BN graph and EC tree concepts. The theoretical results are properly contextualized with respect to related work, including Koivisto and Sood (2004), Parviainen and Koivisto (2013), and Chen et al. (2015, 2016). The authors correctly acknowledge limitations, such as the need to enumerate structures for some CPDAGs when checking constraint satisfaction.

Strengths:
- The paper provides a theoretically sound solution to a well-defined problem (incorporating non-decomposable ancestral constraints into structure learning)
- The constraint projection method is complete and sound, with formal proofs
- The empirical evaluation demonstrates significant practical advantages over existing approaches
- The paper shows that ancestral constraints can substantially reduce learning time while improving model quality
- The approach scales to 20 variables, comparable to state-of-the-art score-based methods

Weaknesses:
- The paper could provide more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the constraint projection step
- The evaluation focuses primarily on speed, with less emphasis on the quality of the learned networks beyond structural Hamming distance
- The assumption of a "maximum set of ancestral constraints" (where all implied constraints are included) might not always hold in practice, but the paper doesn't discuss this limitation in detail

The novelty is significant within the context of Bayesian network structure learning. While Chen et al. (2015, 2016) established the EC tree framework for non-decomposable scores, this paper extends it to handle a practically important class of constraints (ancestral constraints) that were previously difficult to incorporate. The approach differs from previous work (e.g., Borboudakis and Tsamardinos, 2012, 2013; Cussens, 2008) by providing a systematic method for transforming non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones, rather than merely incorporating them into ILP formulations.

The significance is substantial. Ancestral constraints are important for incorporating domain knowledge in practical applications, and the paper demonstrates that these constraints can dramatically improve learning efficiency without sacrificing accuracy. The finding that a small number of ancestral constraints can have a similar impact on model quality as significantly more training data is particularly compelling for practitioners who face expensive data collection challenges.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses a clear gap in Bayesian network structure learning (incorporating non-decomposable ancestral constraints)
- Theoretical contribution is sound and complete
- Empirical results demonstrate orders-of-magnitude efficiency improvements
- The approach is practical and scalable to real-world problems
- Provides valuable insights about the impact of background knowledge on learning

Arguments against acceptance:
- Some might view the contribution as incremental (extending Chen et al.'s work)
- Could benefit from more detailed complexity analysis
- Limited discussion of constraint projection complexity in practice

Overall, this paper makes a strong technical contribution to Bayesian network structure learning with clear theoretical foundations, comprehensive empirical validation, and significant practical implications. The approach is technically sound, well-presented, and addresses an important practical problem in the field. The paper meets all four evaluation criteria (quality, clarity, originality, significance) at a high level.
