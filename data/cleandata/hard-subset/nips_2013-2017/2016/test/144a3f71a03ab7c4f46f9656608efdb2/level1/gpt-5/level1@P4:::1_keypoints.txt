- Paper tackles optimal BN structure learning with ancestral (path) constraints by pruning EC-tree search and projecting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones for an admissible A*-style search.  
- Pruning rule: a CPDAG can be eliminated if no DAG in its equivalence class satisfies the ancestral constraints, allowing monotone pruning of its descendants.  
- Projection: formal conditions infer negative edge constraints X ↛ Y using ancestor/descendant closures α(X), β(Y) (Theorem 2).  
- Projection: formal conditions infer positive edge constraints X → Y when X must reach Y and no intermediate Z can lie on any path (Theorem 3).  
- Projection: compute a maximal consistent set of ordering constraints via a MaxSAT reduction enforcing transitivity and a sufficient acyclicity condition; provide entailment sufficiency (Theorem 4).  
- Empirically compares to ILP-based GOBNILP using BDeu and precomputed family scores; projected constraints fed to both methods.  
- Results: EC-tree approach achieves orders-of-magnitude speedups over ILP when using projected constraints and is often faster even without projection.  
- Experiments reveal an easy-hard-easy pattern as constraint density varies and improved structural accuracy (lower SHD) with more constraints or data.  
- Technical soundness: pruning and A* admissibility are sound; Theorems 2–4 appear logically consistent and the MaxSAT formulation is safe (sufficient constraints).  
- Missing details: algorithms and complexity for closure computation, CPDAG satisfaction testing, and exact MaxSAT clauses are omitted; complexity guarantees and enumeration costs not analyzed.  
- Clarity issues: informal definition of “maximum set of ancestral constraints,” unclear fast vs. enumeration CPDAG checks, and nonstandard MaxSAT acyclicity clause need justification.  
- Originality: novel integration of ancestral constraints into optimal score-based EC-tree pruning and projection to decomposable constraints; MaxSAT reduction is a useful contribution.  
- Significance: practically valuable with strong speedups and broad applicability, but evaluation limited to n ≤ ~20, complete data, simulated consistent constraints, and single score choice.  
- Cons: reliance on potentially exponential enumeration of class(P), lack of robustness tests for noisy/conflicting constraints, missing scalability analysis, and reference/notation issues.  
- Suggestions: provide polynomial-time closure algorithm or complexity bounds, replace enumeration with scalable satisfiability tests, detail MaxSAT encoding and runtime costs, and add experiments on noisy constraints and bounded in-degree.  
- Recommendation: technically sound, novel, and impactful work with strong empirical benefits; reviewer leans toward acceptance despite missing details and scope limits.
