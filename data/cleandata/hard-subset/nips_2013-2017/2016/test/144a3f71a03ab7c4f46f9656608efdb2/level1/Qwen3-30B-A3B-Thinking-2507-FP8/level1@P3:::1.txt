

# Peer Review: "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"

This paper addresses a significant gap in Bayesian network structure learning: incorporating ancestral constraints (non-decomposable constraints specifying cause-effect relationships) into optimal structure learning frameworks. The authors build upon Chen et al.'s (2015, 2016) EC tree framework, which enables learning with non-decomposable scores by leveraging oracles that handle decomposable scores. Their key innovation is a method to project ancestral constraints (which cannot be directly handled by standard oracles) into decomposable edge and ordering constraints that the oracle can process.

The paper makes a valuable contribution by resolving a practical limitation in the field. While previous work (e.g., Koivisto and Sood, 2004; Parviainen and Koivisto, 2013) has explored structure learning with decomposable constraints, ancestral constraints have been largely ignored in optimal score-based learning. The authors correctly identify that ILP-based approaches (like GOBNILP) can encode ancestral constraints but are computationally expensive, and they demonstrate how their method achieves orders-of-magnitude improvements in efficiency.

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Soundness:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical foundations for constraint projection. Theorems 2-4 establish sound and complete methods for inferring edge constraints and ordering constraints from ancestral constraints. The authors correctly identify that ancestral constraints are non-decomposable and cannot be handled by standard oracles, and they provide a principled solution.

2. **Empirical Impact:** The experimental results are compelling. The paper demonstrates consistent and dramatic improvements (orders of magnitude) over GOBNILP across multiple benchmark networks and varying problem sizes. The finding that a small percentage of ancestral constraints (10-25%) can have as much impact on model accuracy as increasing dataset size by 4x (from 512 to 2048 samples) is particularly significant for practitioners.

3. **Clear Organization:** The paper is well-structured, with clear technical background (Section 2), problem definition (Section 3), and methodological contributions (Sections 4-5). Figures 1a and 1b effectively illustrate the BN graph and EC tree.

4. **Practical Relevance:** The work directly addresses a common need in applied Bayesian network learning where domain knowledge (in the form of causal relationships) is available but difficult to incorporate into optimal learning frameworks.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Theoretical Complexity Analysis:** While the constraint projection algorithms are sound, the paper lacks detailed analysis of their computational complexity, particularly for the MaxSAT formulation in Section 5.2. It's unclear how the complexity scales with the number of ancestral constraints or variables, which would be important for understanding practical limitations.

2. **Comparison to Other Non-Decomposable Approaches:** The paper compares against ILP-based methods but doesn't contrast with other approaches for non-decomposable scores, such as constraint programming (CP) methods (e.g., van Beek and Hoffmann, 2015). While the authors note CP can represent ancestral constraints, they don't evaluate their approach against CP, which would strengthen the empirical claim of "orders-of-magnitude more efficient."

3. **Handling of Conflicting Constraints:** The paper doesn't address how their method handles cases where ancestral constraints might be inconsistent (e.g., X → Y and Y → X). While the theoretical framework assumes a maximum set of ancestral constraints, practical applications may encounter such conflicts.

4. **Scalability Limitations:** The paper reports results up to 20 variables, but the EC tree approach has exponential space complexity. The authors should acknowledge this limitation and discuss how it might impact larger-scale applications.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to Bayesian network structure learning. The problem addressed is important, the solution is theoretically sound, and the empirical results are convincing. The work clearly advances the state of the art in structure learning with background knowledge.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS, as it addresses a fundamental problem in probabilistic modeling with clear theoretical and practical significance. It builds upon previous NIPS work (Chen et al., 2015, 2016) while introducing novel techniques that are both conceptually and empirically impactful.

**Recommendation:** Accept

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Solves a well-motivated problem with practical importance
- Presents a theoretically sound and complete method
- Demonstrates substantial empirical improvements over existing approaches
- Clearly written and well-organized
- Makes a significant contribution to the field of structure learning

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- Limited theoretical analysis of constraint projection complexity
- Comparison to CP approaches would strengthen empirical claims
- Doesn't address handling of conflicting ancestral constraints
- Limited discussion of scalability limits

The authors have made a valuable contribution to the field that will be of interest to both researchers and practitioners. The paper is well-positioned to influence future work on incorporating domain knowledge into probabilistic model learning.
