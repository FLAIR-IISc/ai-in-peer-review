This paper tackles the problem of learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints, a practically relevant yet computationally challenging task. The authors leverage a recent framework for optimal structure learning based on non-decomposable scores, addressing the limitations of traditional approaches that struggle with such constraints. Their core contribution lies in a method to “project” the ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints that can be utilized by an existing oracle for efficient learning. The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and presents a solid technical approach.

The paper builds upon the work of Chen et al. [2015, 2016] on optimal Bayesian network structure learning with non-decomposable scores. It correctly identifies the key challenge: ancestral constraints are non-decomposable, preventing their direct use with algorithms relying on decomposable scores (like K2). The authors' strategy of inferring decomposable constraints from the ancestral ones is novel and logically sound.  Related work, including ILP and CP approaches [Jaakkola et al., 2010; Bartlett and Cussens, 2015; van Beek and Hoffmann, 2015], and constraint-based methods like PC algorithm [Borboudakis and Tsamardinos, 2012, 2013] is acknowledged and appropriately positioned. The introduction effectively highlights the limitations of existing methods and sets the stage for the proposed solution.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of projecting ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints is a significant contribution. This bridging of non-decomposable and decomposable frameworks is a clever idea.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical justification for the projection method, particularly the theorems presented in Section 5, appears robust.  The pruning strategy for the EC tree (Section 4.3) based on ancestral constraints seems well-defined and correct.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments are comprehensive, comparing the proposed approach with GOBNILP and demonstrating significant speedups.  The varying parameters (n, N, p) and reporting across multiple datasets increase confidence in the results.  The impact of constraint projection is clearly demonstrated.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The technical concepts are explained clearly and concisely, making the paper accessible to a broad audience. The notation is consistent and well-defined.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Complexity of MaxSAT:** While the reduction to MaxSAT for inferring ordering constraints is a sound idea, the practical scalability of this step could be a concern for very large networks. The paper mentions using EVASOLVER, but a more detailed discussion of the computational cost of MaxSAT and its potential bottleneck would be beneficial.
*   **Limited Exploration of Constraint Types:** The paper focuses primarily on ancestral constraints. It would be valuable to briefly discuss the potential for extending the projection method to other types of non-decomposable constraints.
*   **Oracle Dependence:** The performance of the approach is heavily reliant on the chosen oracle (URLEARNING). A sensitivity analysis exploring the impact of different oracle algorithms on the results would strengthen the paper.
*   **Structural Hamming Distance Discussion:** The discussion of Structural Hamming Distance (∆) in Section 6 could be expanded. Relating this metric back to the specific constraints used and the complexity of the network would provide more meaningful insights.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant challenge in Bayesian network learning.
*   The proposed approach is novel, technically sound, and empirically validated.
*   The paper is clearly written and well-organized, making it easy to understand and follow.
*   The results demonstrate a substantial performance improvement over existing methods.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The MaxSAT component could be a scalability bottleneck for very large networks.
*   The dependence on the chosen oracle requires further investigation.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian network learning. The proposed approach is well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically validated. While the potential scalability limitations of the MaxSAT component and oracle dependence are concerns, they do not outweigh the significant benefits of the approach.  I recommend **acceptance** to NIPS 2016.  The clear writing and well-defined technical contributions make this a worthwhile addition to the conference proceedings.
