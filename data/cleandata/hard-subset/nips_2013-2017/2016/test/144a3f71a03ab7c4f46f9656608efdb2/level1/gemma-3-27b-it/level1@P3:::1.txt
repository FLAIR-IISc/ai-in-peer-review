This paper addresses the important problem of learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints, a common scenario in practice where domain expertise provides valuable structural information. The core idea – leveraging an oracle for learning structures with decomposable scores within a framework capable of handling non-decomposable constraints – is well-motivated and builds upon recent work by Chen et al. [2015, 2016]. The key contribution lies in efficiently inferring decomposable constraints from ancestral ones to empower this oracle, significantly improving performance compared to ILP-based approaches.

The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The introduction effectively sets the stage, highlighting the limitations of existing methods when dealing with ancestral constraints. The technical preliminaries provide a necessary foundation for understanding the proposed approach. Sections 4 and 5 detailing the learning framework and constraint projection are logically structured and easy to follow. The experimental setup is thorough, comparing against a strong baseline (GOBNILP) across a range of network sizes and constraint levels. The empirical results convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the proposed method, particularly the significant speedups achieved through constraint projection.

The originality of the work stems from the clever combination of existing techniques. While the use of oracles for structure learning and ILP for constrained learning are not novel, the specific approach of projecting ancestral constraints to empower the oracle and the associated theoretical grounding (Theorems 1-4) represent a substantial contribution. The exploration of both edge and ordering constraints derived from ancestral constraints is a valuable addition. Related work is adequately cited, including relevant papers on ILP-based approaches [Jaakkola et al., 2010, Bartlett and Cussens, 2015] and constraint-based learning with ancestral constraints [Borboudakis and Tsamardinos, 2012, 2013].

The significance of this work is considerable. Ancestral constraints are frequently encountered in real-world applications, and the ability to efficiently incorporate them into the learning process can substantially reduce search space and improve model accuracy. The observed speedups over ILP, particularly for larger networks, are particularly impactful. The analysis showing that a small number of ancestral constraints can have an effect comparable to increasing the data size is a valuable insight. This work effectively bridges the gap between theoretical advances in non-decomposable score learning [Chen et al., 2015, 2016] and practical applications requiring the incorporation of domain knowledge.

However, some areas could be improved. The discussion of the MaxSAT reduction for inferring ordering constraints feels somewhat brief. While Theorem 4 establishes the soundness of the approach, a more detailed explanation of the practical challenges and potential bottlenecks in the MaxSAT solver’s performance would be beneficial. Also, the choice of BDeu score is somewhat arbitrary. Exploring the impact of different scoring functions (MDL, BDeu) could offer valuable insights. While the empirical evaluation is comprehensive, a more detailed analysis of the quality of the learned structures (e.g., precision and recall of edges) beyond the Structural Hamming Distance (SHD) would strengthen the conclusions. Finally, it would be helpful to discuss the limitations of the approach – for example, how the performance scales with the complexity of the ancestral constraints themselves (e.g., a highly interconnected constraint network versus a sparse one).

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Technically sound and well-supported by empirical results.
*   Addresses an important practical problem with significant potential impact.
*   Novel combination of existing techniques with theoretical justification.
*   Demonstrates substantial performance improvements over existing methods.
*   Clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The MaxSAT reduction discussion is brief and could benefit from more detail.
*   The empirical evaluation could be strengthened by including additional structural quality metrics.
*   The limitations of the approach regarding the complexity of ancestral constraints are not discussed.



**Overall assessment:** I strongly recommend acceptance. This paper presents a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. The proposed approach is innovative, well-motivated, and empirically validated. Despite minor areas for improvement, the strengths of the work far outweigh its weaknesses. It’s a high-quality paper that is likely to be influential in the field.
