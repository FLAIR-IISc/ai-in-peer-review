- Addresses learning Bayesian network structures under non-decomposable ancestral constraints by projecting them into decomposable constraints.
- Main contribution: a theoretically sound method to transform ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints usable by existing oracles.
- Builds on Chen et al.'s EC tree search space and oracle-based framework for non-decomposable scores.
- Provides formal theorems (Theorems 2–4) that derive edge and ordering constraints from ancestral constraints.
- Demonstrates that inferred edge and ordering constraints are decomposable and compatible with existing optimization oracles.
- Explains how ancestral constraints are incorporated into the EC tree via pruning, with clear theoretical foundations.
- Technical approach is sound, complete, and accompanied by formal proofs.
- Experimental evaluation compares the method to ILP-based GOBNILP across multiple benchmark networks and settings.
- Results show orders-of-magnitude speedups over ILP methods, with larger gains as variable count increases.
- Shows that 10–25% ancestral constraints can have similar impact on model accuracy as substantially more training data.
- Paper is well-organized and clearly written, with helpful figures illustrating BN and EC tree concepts.
- Situates contributions relative to related work (Koivisto & Sood, Parviainen & Koivisto, Chen et al., Borboudakis & Tsamardinos, Cussens).
- Acknowledges limitations such as needing to enumerate structures for some CPDAGs when checking constraints.
- Weakness: lacks detailed analysis of computational complexity for the constraint projection step.
- Weakness: evaluation emphasizes speed over deeper analysis of learned-network quality beyond SHD.
- Weakness: assumes a "maximum set of ancestral constraints" without fully discussing practical violations of this assumption.
- Novelty: significant extension of the EC tree framework to a practically important class of constraints, differing from ILP-only approaches.
- Significance: enables practical incorporation of domain causal knowledge, improving efficiency and often model quality.
- Arguments for acceptance: fills a clear gap, theoretically sound, empirically compelling, practical and scalable.
- Arguments against acceptance: seen by some as incremental extension of prior work and needs more complexity/discussion detail.
