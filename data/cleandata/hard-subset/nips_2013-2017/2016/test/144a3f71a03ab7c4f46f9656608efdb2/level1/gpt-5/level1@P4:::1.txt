Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses optimal Bayesian network (BN) structure learning under background knowledge expressed as ancestral (path) constraints, which are non-decomposable and thus not directly handled by mainstream score-based methods that rely on decomposable scores. Building on BN graph and EC tree search spaces for non-decomposable objectives (Chen et al., 2015; Chen et al., 2016), the authors prune the EC tree using satisfiability of ancestral constraints at CPDAG nodes. They use an admissible A*-style search guided by an “oracle” that optimally solves decomposable-score subproblems (e.g., order-graph based), and crucially “empower” this oracle by projecting the non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints.

For pruning, they formalize when a CPDAG can be eliminated: if no DAG in its equivalence class satisfies the constraints, all descendants can be pruned (monotonicity when constraints concern a fixed variable set). For projection, they give conditions to infer:
- Negative edge constraints X ↛ Y from a “saturated” (closed) set of ancestral constraints via ancestor/descendant closures α(X), β(Y) (Theorem 2).
- Positive edge constraints X → Y when X must reach Y and no intermediate Z can lie on a path (Theorem 3).
- A maximal consistent set of ordering constraints via a MaxSAT encoding that enforces transitivity, a necessary condition (Y ≮ X implies not X < Y), and a sufficient acyclicity condition; they also provide sufficient conditions for entailment (Theorem 4).

Empirically, the method is compared against the ILP-based GOBNILP using BDeu scores, precomputed family scores, and simulated ancestral constraints derived from standard BN benchmarks. With projected constraints supplied to both methods, the proposed EC-tree approach achieves orders-of-magnitude speedups; even without projection, it is often faster. Additional experiments show an easy-hard-easy pattern as constraint density increases, and demonstrate improved structural accuracy (lower SHD) with more constraints or more data.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The pruning rule on EC trees is sound given that adding leaf variables cannot alter ancestral relations among the existing variables; this satisfies the “if a CPDAG violates A, so do descendants” property, and the converse under their definition of satisfaction (existence of a DAG in the class) constrained to the same variable set. The A* search is admissible via an oracle that returns optimal extensions, consistent with prior work. The edge entailment conditions (Theorems 2–3) are stated as iff and appear logically consistent with α/β closures; the ordering MaxSAT formulation uses hard constraints that are sufficient to avoid eliminating feasible DAGs, with a supporting sufficiency theorem (Theorem 4).
- Some details are omitted (“details omitted for space”) for computing the maximal closure of ancestral constraints, complexity of CPDAG satisfaction testing when partial checks are inconclusive (they fall back to enumerating class(P)), and the exact MaxSAT clause set beyond the listed templates. Complexity guarantees for the edge-entailment procedure and the practical cost of exhaustive enumeration of class(P) are not analyzed.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well organized: motivation, preliminaries, definition of ancestral constraints, EC-tree integration, projection techniques, and experiments. Definitions of α(X) and β(X) are clear. The distinction between non-decomposable constraints and decomposable scores is well articulated. However, some statements would benefit from precision:
  - The term “maximum set of ancestral constraints” is defined informally; an explicit closure algorithm and complexity would help reproducibility.
  - The CPDAG satisfaction tests: the fast checks vs. when enumeration is necessary should be detailed (e.g., criteria for “no partially directed path”).
  - The acyclicity clause in MaxSAT (sufficient condition) is nonstandard; a brief justification or citation would improve clarity.
- The references section contains formatting issues and placeholders, but this is likely a draft artifact.

Originality:
- Integrating ancestral constraints into optimal score-based learning via EC-tree pruning is a novel application of the non-decomposable-score search framework. The projection of path constraints to decomposable edge constraints with necessary and sufficient entailment conditions is new to score-based optimal learning. The MaxSAT reduction to obtain a large, safe set of ordering constraints is a useful contribution. Prior works either handle path constraints in constraint-based learning (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos) or encode them directly in ILP/CP without leveraging decomposable oracles. The work builds on and extends Chen et al.’s BN graph/EC tree ideas substantively.

Significance:
- The approach offers practical value: substantial speedups over ILP on standard benchmarks and improved effectiveness when projected constraints are used. Empowering existing order-graph or A* oracles with inferred decomposable constraints is broadly applicable. The ability to use background causal knowledge efficiently is important in BN applications.
- Scope limitations: experiments are limited to small-to-moderate n (up to 20 variables) with unconstrained parent set sizes and complete data using BDeu. Constraints are simulated from ground truth and assumed consistent; robustness to noisy or conflicting constraints is not addressed. Still, within the optimal BN learning literature, the contribution is meaningful.

Pros:
- Sound pruning of EC-tree nodes under ancestral constraints.
- Clear, general methodology for projecting ancestral constraints to edge and ordering constraints with formal conditions.
- Effective empirical results with orders-of-magnitude speedups vs. ILP; ablation showing the benefit of projection.
- Leverages and advances a line of work on non-decomposable search spaces (BN graph/EC tree).

Cons:
- Missing algorithmic details and complexity analyses for closure computation, CPDAG satisfaction testing, and MaxSAT encoding scale.
- Reliance on exhaustive enumeration of class(P) in ambiguous cases may be exponential; practical frequency and mitigation are not analyzed.
- Evaluation excludes inconsistent/noisy constraints and broader score choices; scalability beyond n≈20 not demonstrated.
- Reference formatting issues and some notation/wording could be tightened.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide a polynomial-time closure algorithm for ancestral constraints with complexity bounds; include proofs for Theorems 2–4 in the appendix.
- Replace CPDAG class enumeration with a more scalable satisfiability test (e.g., using PAG reachability or SAT-based checks) and report its empirical overhead.
- Detail the MaxSAT formula size and solver settings; report time spent on constraint projection vs. search.
- Add experiments with noisy or partially incorrect constraints, and with bounded in-degree, to assess robustness and scalability.

Recommendation: The paper presents a technically sound and novel approach with strong empirical benefits and clear relevance to optimal BN learning with prior knowledge. Despite some missing details and scope limitations, the contribution meets NeurIPS standards on quality, originality, and potential impact. I lean toward acceptance.
