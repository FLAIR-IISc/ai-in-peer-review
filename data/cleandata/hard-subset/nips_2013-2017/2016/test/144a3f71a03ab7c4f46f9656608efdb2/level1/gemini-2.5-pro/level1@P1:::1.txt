### Review of "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"

**Summary of Main Ideas**

This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data, subject to background knowledge given as ancestral constraints (e.g., variable X must be an ancestor of Y, or X must *not* be an ancestor of Y). The core challenge is that such constraints are non-decomposable, meaning they cannot be verified locally at each node. This makes them incompatible with many efficient, exact structure learning algorithms (e.g., based on dynamic programming) that rely on decomposable scores.

To solve this, the authors build upon a recent A*-based search framework over the space of equivalence classes of DAGs (the "EC tree") [Chen et al., 2016], which can handle non-decomposable scores. The main contributions of this paper are:
1.  Adapting the EC tree search to handle ancestral constraints by developing sound pruning rules for nodes (CPDAGs) that violate the given constraints.
2.  A novel and powerful technique called "constraint projection" to significantly improve search efficiency. The A* search in this framework uses an "oracle" (an exact learner for decomposable scores) to compute its heuristic. The authors show how to infer decomposable constraints (edge presence/absence and topological ordering constraints) from the non-decomposable ancestral constraints. These inferred constraints are then passed to the oracle, leading to a much tighter heuristic and dramatically faster search.
3.  Formal methods for this projection, including an efficient algorithm for inferring all entailed edge constraints and a more subtle formulation using MaxSAT to find a maximal set of sound topological ordering constraints.

The paper provides a strong empirical evaluation comparing their method to a standard Integer Linear Programming (ILP) approach, demonstrating orders-of-magnitude speedups and showing scalability to networks of up to 20 variables.

**Relation to Previous Work**

The paper is well-situated within the literature on exact Bayesian network structure learning. It correctly identifies the limitations of classic dynamic programming [Koivisto and Sood, 2004] and A* search methods [Yuan and Malone, 2013] which are restricted to decomposable scores and simple constraints. The work is a direct and significant extension of the recent BN graph/EC tree framework [Chen et al., 2015, 2016], applying it to a new, important class of non-decomposable constraints. The authors also correctly position their work relative to general-purpose solvers like ILP [Jaakkola et al., 2010; Bartlett and Cussens, 2015] and constraint programming, arguing for their specialized method's superior performance on this specific problem. The connection to work using ancestral constraints in other learning paradigms (constraint-based methods or heuristic search) is also noted [Borboudakis and Tsamardinos, 2012, 2013].

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed methods for pruning the EC tree and for projecting constraints are sound. The theorems presented appear correct and provide a solid foundation for the algorithms. The experimental methodology is rigorous, using standard benchmarks, a relevant and strong baseline (ILP), and thorough analysis. The authors are careful to evaluate the impact of their key contribution (constraint projection) via an ablation study (Table 1 vs. Table 2), which clearly shows its dramatic effect. The claims of superior performance are well-supported by the results.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. It starts with a clear motivation for the problem, provides the necessary technical background concisely, and then walks the reader through the proposed solution. The distinction between the non-decomposable ancestral constraints and the inferred decomposable constraints is made very clear, which is central to the paper's contribution. The figures and tables are informative and easy to understand.

A minor point for improvement would be to provide more intuition for the MaxSAT formulation in Section 5.2. Specifically, the third hard constraint is quite complex; a brief example illustrating why it is a sufficient condition for acyclicity in this context would be helpful for the reader. Similarly, providing a brief proof sketch for Theorem 1 (perhaps in an appendix) would further solidify the paper's technical completeness.

**Originality:** The paper's primary contribution—the idea of "projecting" non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones to empower an oracle within a larger search framework—is highly original. While the underlying EC tree search is from prior work by the same authors, its application here and the projection technique itself are novel. The use of MaxSAT to find a maximal set of sound ordering constraints is a particularly clever and non-obvious contribution. This work is a significant departure from simply encoding the constraints directly into a monolithic solver like ILP.

**Significance:** The results are highly significant for the field of probabilistic graphical models. Incorporating expert knowledge is crucial for learning accurate models, especially from limited data, and ancestral relationships are a natural and powerful way for experts to express causal background knowledge. This paper provides the first scalable, *exact* method for doing so. The experimental results showing that a small percentage of ancestral constraints can have an impact on model accuracy comparable to a 4x increase in data size (Table 3) powerfully illustrates the practical importance of this work. Researchers and practitioners working on structure learning are very likely to build on or use these ideas.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Pro:** Solves an important, practical problem in structure learning.
*   **Pro:** Introduces a novel and elegant technical contribution (constraint projection) that is broadly applicable.
*   **Pro:** Demonstrates state-of-the-art performance with orders-of-magnitude improvements over a standard baseline (ILP).
*   **Pro:** The work is technically sound, clearly written, and the claims are well-supported by strong empirical evidence.
*   **Pro:** The finding that a small number of constraints can significantly improve model accuracy highlights the practical value of the method.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   **Con:** Like most exact score-based methods, the scalability is ultimately limited to small-to-medium sized networks (n=20 in the experiments). While this is a general limitation of the problem class rather than a specific flaw of this paper, it is worth noting.
*   **Con:** Some technical details, especially regarding the MaxSAT formulation, could benefit from slightly more explanation.

In conclusion, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses. I strongly recommend acceptance.
