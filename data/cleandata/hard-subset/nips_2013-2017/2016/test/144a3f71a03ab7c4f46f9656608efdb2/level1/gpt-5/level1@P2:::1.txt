Summary
The paper addresses exact Bayesian network (BN) structure learning with background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints (positive X ≼ Y, negative X ≼/ Y). Mainstream exact methods assume decomposable scores and mostly support decomposable constraints (edge presence/absence, family-size, or precedence constraints), making ancestral constraints difficult to incorporate. The authors extend the BN-graph/EC-tree shortest-path framework (Chen et al. 2015, 2016) to handle non-decomposable ancestral constraints by pruning CPDAG nodes that cannot be extended to any DAG satisfying the constraints (Theorem 1). To recover efficiency, they “empower” a decomposable-score oracle (URLearning) with projected decomposable constraints inferred from the ancestral constraints: (i) edge constraints via closure over ancestor/descendant sets α(·), β(·) with necessary-and-sufficient entailment tests (Theorems 2–3), and (ii) precedence constraints via a MaxSAT formulation that maximizes a safe set of orderings entailed by the ancestral constraints (Theorem 4 provides sufficient conditions). Experiments against GOBNILP (ILP) on standard BN benchmarks with synthetic constraints show orders-of-magnitude speedups and demonstrate the value of projection to tighten the heuristic, along with an easy–hard–easy trend as the number of constraints increases.

Strengths
- Clear problem focus: exact BN learning with hard ancestral constraints, a practically relevant form of causal prior knowledge.
- Sound integration into EC-tree search: pruning at the CPDAG level is principled and exploits equivalence classes.
- Effective projection of non-decomposable knowledge to decomposable constraints:
  - Edge entailment conditions are explicit and efficiently checkable (leveraging α/β closures).
  - Ordering constraints via MaxSAT provide a scalable way to extract a large safe set without eliminating solutions.
- Strong empirical results: consistent, large speedups over an ILP baseline; ablation showing projected constraints substantially tighten the oracle heuristic; analysis of scalability and accuracy trends.
- Good positioning relative to prior work: connects to order-graph methods, ILP/CP, and path constraints in CB methods.

Weaknesses
- Scalability remains modest (n≈20) and depends on the oracle’s limits; the approach inherits the memory/time profile of A*-style exact search with precomputed family scores.
- The projection to ordering constraints relies on a MaxSAT encoding with sufficient (not necessary) conditions; the impact of suboptimality in the inferred ordering set and its computational overhead are not quantified (e.g., runtime spent in MaxSAT vs total).
- Constraint robustness: experiments use noise-free constraints sampled from ground truth; no evaluation with inconsistent or partially incorrect constraints, which are common in practice.
- Some theoretical/algorithmic details are deferred:
  - Closure rules and complexity for computing maximal ancestral sets are described only informally.
  - Complexity bounds for edge entailment (Theorems 2–3) and for the pruning test “enumerate class(P) if inconclusive” are not fully characterized. Exhaustive enumeration of class(P) can be exponential; practical mitigation strategies are not detailed.
- Baseline breadth: only GOBNILP is evaluated (no CP baseline), and tuning details for ILP/cutting planes and family-size limits are minimal; fairness is discussed but could be expanded.

Quality
The technical claims are mostly sound and well-justified. The pruning of EC-tree nodes via CPDAG-level tests is correct (Theorem 1). Edge projection entailment conditions are precise and appear complete for negative edges and for positive edges under the stated assumptions (Theorems 2–3). The MaxSAT formulation yields a safe (sufficient) set of orderings (Theorem 4). Empirically, the methodology is validated across multiple networks and settings, with ablation on projection. However, the lack of robustness tests (noisy/inconsistent constraints) and the reliance on enumerating class(P) in some cases are gaps.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and organized. The distinction between non-decomposable ancestral constraints and decomposable projected constraints is clear, and the role of the oracle is well-motivated. Some sections would benefit from more explicit algorithms and complexity analyses (ancestral closure rules; the precise MaxSAT encoding and variables; when class(P) enumeration is triggered and how often). Providing pseudocode or a small worked example for the edge entailment tests would improve readability. Reference formatting has a few artifacts but does not impede understanding.

Originality
The integration of ancestral constraints into optimal BN learning via EC trees, plus the projection of these non-decomposable constraints into decomposable edge/ordering constraints to empower oracles, is novel. The MaxSAT reduction to extract a maximal safe precedence set is also a creative contribution. Prior work either handled causal priors in local search or used ILP/CP without the oracle-empowerment angle.

Significance
The contribution is practically meaningful: many applications possess causal/ancestral knowledge, and the demonstrated speedups over ILP suggest the approach can broaden the applicability of exact learning with such priors. While scalability is comparable to other exact algorithms, the framework is extensible and complements existing oracles. The projection ideas could influence other hybrid exact/heuristic pipelines.

Suggestions for improvement
- Evaluate robustness to noisy/inconsistent constraints (and propose conflict resolution or soft penalties).
- Provide complexity bounds and detailed algorithms for (i) ancestral closure, (ii) edge entailment computation, and (iii) pruning tests that avoid full class(P) enumeration (e.g., use necessary/sufficient graphical conditions).
- Report MaxSAT overhead and an ablation separating edge-only, order-only, and combined projection.
- Expand baselines (e.g., CP) and discuss ILP tuning choices; include family-size limits to test different regimes.
- Consider additional scores (MDL) and larger networks to stress-test generality.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel, principled method to handle important non-decomposable constraints in exact BN learning.
- Strong empirical gains and informative analysis of projection impact.
- Clear theoretical conditions for safe projection; sound pruning strategy.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited scalability and incomplete complexity characterization for key subroutines.
- Lack of robustness evaluation to imperfect constraints.
- Baseline diversity and tuning limited; MaxSAT projection details/overheads underreported.

Overall assessment
A solid, technically sound contribution that advances exact BN learning with ancestral constraints and demonstrates substantial practical benefits. Despite some gaps in robustness and scalability analysis, I lean toward acceptance.
