Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper tackles optimal Bayesian network (BN) structure learning under background knowledge given as ancestral (path) constraints, which are inherently non-decomposable and hence not directly supported by mainstream score-based optimal learners relying on decomposable scores (e.g., dynamic programming and order-graph/A* approaches; Koivisto & Sood 2004; Silander & Myllymäki 2006; Yuan & Malone 2011, 2013). Building on the BN-graph and EC-tree search spaces introduced by Chen et al. (2015, 2016), the authors show how to prune EC trees soundly with ancestral constraints and how to “empower” a decomposable-score oracle (URLearning) by projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge constraints and ordering constraints. They introduce an efficient algorithm to infer entailed edge presence/absence constraints from a closed set of ancestral constraints using α(·) and β(·) reachability sets, with necessary-and-sufficient conditions (Theorems 2–3). For ordering constraints, they propose a MaxSAT formulation that selects a maximally large set of pairwise orderings consistent with ancestral constraints while maintaining acyclicity; Theorem 4 provides sufficient conditions guaranteeing that the selected ordering constraints are entailed. Empirically, they compare against ILP (GOBNILP), showing orders-of-magnitude speedups, particularly when using projected constraints, and analyze scaling to n up to 20 variables with BDeu.

Strengths:
- Clear problem motivation: ancestral constraints encode causal/temporal knowledge that should reduce search and improve estimation, yet are non-decomposable.
- Technically solid integration: EC-tree pruning criteria for CPDAGs are natural; the pruning is sound with a clear descendant-closure property (Theorem 1).
- Novel projection methods: the edge entailment conditions (Theorems 2–3) are intuitive, checkable, and, if complete as claimed, valuable on their own. The MaxSAT-based selection of compatible ordering constraints is a principled way to harvest as much decomposable information as possible.
- Strong empirical evidence: consistent and large runtime gains over ILP, and a convincing ablation showing that projected constraints dramatically tighten the oracle and reduce search.
- Practical significance: ancestral constraints improve both runtime and estimation accuracy; results suggest that modest amounts of trusted knowledge can substitute for large amounts of data.

Weaknesses and questions:
- CPDAG pruning scalability: when simple tests are inconclusive, the method “exhaustively enumerates class(P).” Enumeration can be exponential; the paper does not quantify how often/when this occurs or its cost. Could SAT/ILP checks or Meek-rule–based orientation extensions avoid enumeration?
- MaxSAT formulation vs. claim of maximality: Theorem 4 gives sufficient (not necessary) conditions for entailment, so the MaxSAT optimum yields a maximum only within this conservative encoding, not necessarily the globally largest entailed set. Please clarify the guarantee and, if possible, tighten the encoding or discuss its gap.
- Experimental scope: evaluations are limited to noiseless constraints on small/medium networks (n ≤ 20). Realistic settings include noisy/inconsistent constraints; the method currently treats them as hard. It would be useful to test robustness to noise or discuss extending the framework to soft constraints (e.g., via priors as in Borboudakis & Tsamardinos 2013).
- Baselines and fairness: GOBNILP is a strong ILP baseline, but constraint programming (van Beek & Hoffmann 2015) is not evaluated. In addition, unrestricted in-degree typically favors heuristic/A*-based methods over ILP (as the authors note); experiments with small max in-degree (a common ILP regime) would provide a fairer comparison.
- Missing details and proofs: key procedures (closure to a “maximum” ancestral set, the edge projection algorithm, and the MaxSAT encoding) are only sketched; formal algorithms, complexity, and proofs should be included in an appendix. In particular, Theorems 2–3 deserve full proofs.
- Clarity and presentation: notation for α(·), β(·), and the use of Xa, Yb could be tightened. The “sufficient condition for acyclicity” clause in the MaxSAT encoding is nonstandard and should be motivated/proved. There are minor typographical/formatting issues and some incomplete references.

Quality:
- The core ideas are technically sound, with clear theorems for pruning and edge entailment. The ordering component is conservative (sufficient conditions), which preserves correctness but may leave performance on the table; this is acceptable but should be stated precisely. Experimental methodology is reasonable, though additional analyses (enumeration frequency, MaxSAT overhead) would strengthen the case.

Clarity:
- Overall well organized. However, important algorithms are omitted for space; including pseudocode/appendix and tightening the MaxSAT part would improve reproducibility. Clarify the generation and percentage p of constraints, and report runtime breakdowns (projection vs. search).

Originality:
- Using EC trees with sound pruning for ancestral constraints and projecting non-decomposable constraints to decomposable ones (edge and ordering) to strengthen a decomposable oracle is novel to my knowledge. The connection to MaxSAT for selecting safe ordering constraints is also original in this context.

Significance:
- The approach advances exact BN learning with non-decomposable knowledge, demonstrating substantial practical gains over ILP. Given the prevalence of causal/temporal priors, this is a meaningful contribution likely to be used and extended.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear, well-motivated problem with practical impact.
- Novel, principled methods (EC-tree pruning; edge/ordering projection).
- Strong empirical gains and insightful analysis (benefits of projection; easy–hard–easy trend).
- Builds on and extends recent influential work (Chen et al. 2015/2016).

Arguments against acceptance:
- Incomplete methodological details and proofs; some claims (maximality of orderings) need careful qualification.
- Potential scalability bottleneck from CPDAG class enumeration is unquantified.
- Limited evaluation scope (noisy constraints, CP baseline, in-degree–bounded regimes).

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide full proofs and algorithms (closure to “maximum” A, edge projection, MaxSAT encoding), with complexity analysis.
- Replace enumeration of class(P) by a satisfiability-based check or provide statistics on its frequency and cost.
- Clarify the exact optimality guarantee of the MaxSAT approach; consider strengthening the encoding (e.g., introduce variables for reachability) or discuss trade-offs.
- Add experiments with bounded in-degree and a CP baseline; report projection/MaxSAT overheads; include noise in constraints.
- Improve clarity/notation and fix reference formatting.

Overall: A strong and timely contribution. With added technical details and clarified guarantees, I lean toward acceptance.
