Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper tackles optimal Bayesian network (BN) structure learning in the presence of background knowledge expressed as ancestral (path) constraints. Because such constraints are inherently non-decomposable, they cannot be directly handled by mainstream exact methods that exploit decomposable scores via order-graph dynamic programming or A* search. Building on Chen et al. (2015, 2016), the authors search in the BN/EC-tree spaces (over DAGs/CPDAGs), which support non-decomposable scores and constraints by pruning infeasible nodes, while using a powerful “oracle” solver for decomposable scores to yield admissible heuristics. The core idea is to “project” non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints that can be passed to this oracle, thereby dramatically tightening the heuristic and reducing search.

Technically, the paper:
- Shows how to prune BN-graph/EC-tree nodes using ancestral constraints, including tests on CPDAGs (necessary/quick checks and, if inconclusive, enumeration of class(P)).
- Derives conditions under which a set of ancestral constraints entails negative and positive edge constraints (Theorems 2–3) using ancestor/descendant closure α(·), β(·) after saturating A under transitivity and simple implication rules.
- Proposes a MaxSAT-based reduction to infer a maximally large set of safe ordering constraints (hard constraints ensure transitivity and acyclicity via sufficient conditions; soft clauses prefer orderings implied by negative ancestral constraints). Theorem 4 provides sufficient conditions for correctness.
- Empirically compares the EC-tree+oracle method (using URLearning as oracle) against an ILP baseline (GOBNILP with linear encodings of ancestral constraints), showing orders-of-magnitude speed-ups, especially when projected constraints are used. The study also examines the effect of constraint density p and dataset size N on runtime and accuracy.

Strengths:
- Conceptually clear and well-motivated: ancestral/path constraints are practically important and poorly served by decomposable-score methods.
- A principled integration of non-decomposable pruning with oracle heuristics, with a clever projection step to empower the oracle without sacrificing soundness.
- The edge entailment rules (Theorems 2–3) are simple to implement and appear powerful in practice.
- The MaxSAT formulation provides a practical way to extract a large set of safe topological constraints; treating these as soft objectives is reasonable.
- Strong empirical gains vs. ILP on standard BN benchmarks, with ablations showing the value of projection.
- Useful discussion of the easy–hard–easy pattern as constraint density increases, and of how modest prior knowledge can improve accuracy comparably to substantial data increases.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Correctness and completeness details are thin. Theorems 2–3 are stated as “iff,” but proofs are omitted. For Theorem 3 in particular, the necessity of the stated condition (no X→Z→Y path permitted) deserves a clear proof. Similarly, Theorem 4 requires a precise definition of “closed set” and a more explicit argument that the hard constraints suffice to avoid eliminating any DAG in G(A).
- CPDAG feasibility checking: when necessary conditions are inconclusive, exhaustive enumeration of class(P) can be exponential. How often does enumeration trigger, and what is its runtime impact? Are there polynomial-time checks for the presence/absence of partially directed paths that could reduce enumeration frequency?
- MaxSAT encoding: Constraint (3) is presented as a sufficient condition for acyclicity using a disjunction of ancestral and ordering relations; the rationale and derivation need more intuition and a complexity discussion. What is the asymptotic size of the encoding (variables/clauses) as a function of n, and empirical solution times for MaxSAT?
- Experimental scope: Only ILP is compared, although constraint programming (CP) formulations can also encode path constraints (van Beek & Hoffmann, 2015). A CP baseline would strengthen the empirical case. Moreover, the experiments do not include indegree bounds, a regime where ILP can be competitive or superior; a bounded-fanin study would make the comparison more balanced.
- Robustness to noisy or conflicting constraints is not studied. Real prior knowledge can be imperfect; a discussion of soft constraints or inconsistency handling (e.g., via slack variables or penalties) would be valuable.
- Minor clarity/typos: Theorem 1 appears misstated (“P satisfies A iff its descendants satisfy A”): monotonicity implies “if P violates A then all descendants do,” not the other direction. There are formatting artifacts and incomplete references; improving these will help readability.

Quality:
- Overall technically sound with reasonable design choices; however, several key proofs/derivations and complexity analyses are deferred or omitted. Adding proofs (main text or appendix) and clarifying the MaxSAT encoding’s sufficiency would strengthen the paper.

Clarity:
- Generally well organized and readable, but some formal definitions (closure of A, closed O) and theorem statements should be tightened. Provide pseudocode or clear steps for the closure of ancestral constraints and for edge entailment.

Originality:
- Combining EC-tree search with automated projection of path constraints into decomposable constraints and MaxSAT-based ordering inference seems novel. Prior work on path constraints focused on constraint-based or heuristic search; this work targets exact optimal learning with strong heuristics.

Significance:
- If the reported speedups generalize, this is a meaningful advance for exact BN learning with background knowledge, enabling practical use of ancestral constraints. The projection approach could be reused beyond this specific framework.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide proofs (or detailed sketches) for Theorems 2–4 and a precise definition of “closed” O.
- Quantify how often CPDAG enumeration is needed and its cost; consider more powerful CPDAG reachability tests to avoid enumeration.
- Report MaxSAT instance sizes and solve times; consider alternative SAT encodings or incremental solving.
- Add CP baseline; include experiments with bounded indegree.
- Study robustness to noisy constraints (e.g., allow a small number of constraint violations with penalties).
- Separate ablations for edge vs. ordering projections to quantify their individual contributions.

Arguments pro acceptance:
- Addresses an important gap: exact BN learning with non-decomposable ancestral constraints.
- Strong empirical improvements over a solid ILP baseline.
- Methodologically elegant integration of pruning, oracle heuristics, and constraint projection.

Arguments con acceptance:
- Incomplete theoretical details and some unclear theorem statements.
- Potential exponential blowup in CPDAG enumeration not fully addressed.
- Limited baselines and experimental regimes (no CP, no fan-in bounds, no noisy constraints).

Overall, a promising and practically useful contribution; with tightened theory/clarity and expanded evaluation, it would be very strong.
