### **Review of "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"**

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the important problem of learning optimal Bayesian network (BN) structures from data, subject to background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints (e.g., variable X must be an ancestor of Y, or X cannot be an ancestor of Y). The primary challenge is that ancestral constraints are non-decomposable, which makes them incompatible with most state-of-the-art optimal structure learning algorithms that rely on the assumption of decomposable scores (e.g., dynamic programming or A* search over variable orderings).

The authors build upon a recent A*-based search framework that navigates a space of equivalence classes of DAGs (the "EC tree") and can, in principle, handle non-decomposable scores/constraints [Chen et al., 2015, 2016]. The search is guided by a heuristic function provided by an "oracle"—an efficient solver for the simpler, decomposable-score problem.

The main contributions are twofold:
1.  An adaptation of the EC tree search to correctly prune branches that violate the given ancestral constraints.
2.  A novel and elegant method for "projecting" the non-decomposable ancestral constraints onto a set of decomposable constraints (i.e., edge presence/absence and topological ordering constraints). These projected constraints are then passed to the oracle, significantly strengthening its heuristic power and dramatically speeding up the A* search. For inferring edge constraints, the authors propose a sound and complete rule-based method. For the more subtle problem of inferring ordering constraints, they propose a clever reduction to a MaxSAT problem to find a maximal set of consistent ordering constraints.

Empirically, the proposed approach is compared against a state-of-the-art Integer Linear Programming (ILP) solver, GOBNILP, which can also encode ancestral constraints. The experiments show that the proposed method is orders of magnitude faster and scales to problems with up to 20 variables, which is competitive for exact score-based learning.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **High Significance and Practicality:** The problem of incorporating rich background knowledge into structure learning is of great practical importance. Ancestral constraints are a natural way to express causal hypotheses or domain knowledge, and this work provides a way to leverage them in a principled, optimal learning setting. The experimental finding that a small percentage of ancestral constraints can have an impact on structural accuracy comparable to a quadrupling of the dataset size strongly underscores the practical value of this work.

2.  **Novel and Elegant Methodology:** The core idea of projecting non-decomposable constraints into a decomposable form that an efficient oracle can handle is novel and powerful. It provides a generalizable blueprint for incorporating other types of non-decomposable information into search-based learning frameworks. The MaxSAT formulation for inferring a globally consistent set of ordering constraints is particularly insightful and sophisticated.

3.  **Strong Technical Quality and Evaluation:** The paper is technically sound. The proposed methods for constraint projection are well-defined and backed by theorems. The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The choice of ILP as a baseline is appropriate, as it represents a major class of general-purpose methods for exact combinatorial optimization. The experiments systematically analyze the effects of problem size, data size, and the number of constraints, providing a clear picture of the algorithm's performance and demonstrating its superiority over the ILP baseline by orders of magnitude.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Clarity on Key Technical Details:** While generally well-written, some technical sections are dense and would benefit from more explanation.
    *   **MaxSAT Formulation (Sec 5.2):** The third hard constraint, described as "a sufficient condition for acyclicity," is complex and its derivation is not intuitive. A brief justification or an illustrative example would greatly improve the clarity of this otherwise excellent section.
    *   **CPDAG Pruning (Sec 4.3):** The paper states that if initial checks are inconclusive, the algorithm resorts to "exhaustively enumerate the structures of class(P)" to check for constraint satisfaction. An equivalence class can contain exponentially many DAGs, so this step seems like a potential computational bottleneck. It would be valuable to discuss the practical impact of this step. For example, how frequently does this exhaustive enumeration occur in the experiments, and does it dominate the runtime for certain types of problems?

2.  **Scope of Experimental Comparison:** The comparison is limited to an ILP-based approach. The introduction also mentions Constraint Programming (CP) as a viable alternative for this problem [van Beek and Hoffmann, 2015]. Including a comparison with a CP-based solver, if available, would further strengthen the empirical claims of the paper.

3.  **Analysis of Results:** The results in Table 1 and 2 clearly show that constraint projection dramatically helps the proposed A* method and also provides a consistent (though smaller) benefit to the ILP solver. A brief discussion on *why* the impact is so much larger for the A* approach would be insightful. One hypothesis is that tightening the heuristic is more critical for pruning the vast search space of A* than adding redundant constraints to a highly structured ILP formulation.

**Detailed Comments**

*   **Quality:** The work is complete and technically sound. Claims are well-supported by strong empirical results. The authors are honest about the foundations of their work (the EC tree framework) and clearly delineate their own novel contributions.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The motivation is strong, and the overall approach is easy to follow. The aforementioned points in the "Weaknesses" section are relatively minor suggestions for further improvement.
*   **Originality:** This work is highly original. While it builds on the Chen et al. (2015, 2016) framework, the core contributions—the specific handling of ancestral constraints and, most importantly, the constraint projection methodology—are entirely novel. This is not an incremental contribution but a significant extension that enables a new, important class of constraints to be handled efficiently.
*   **Significance:** The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of BN structure learning. It advances the state of the art in exact learning by providing a much more scalable solution for learning with ancestral constraints than existing general-purpose methods. The ideas presented are likely to be influential and could be extended to other types of non-decomposable constraints or even adapted for approximate learning algorithms.

In conclusion, this is an excellent paper that presents a novel, elegant, and highly effective solution to an important problem. The technical contributions are significant, and the experimental results are compelling. It is a clear accept for NIPS.
