This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data when given background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints (e.g., variable X must be an ancestor of Y, or X cannot be an ancestor of Y). The central challenge is that ancestral constraints are non-decomposable, meaning they cannot be evaluated by looking at individual variable-parent families. This violates the decomposability assumption that underlies most efficient, exact score-based learning algorithms, such as those based on dynamic programming over variable orderings [Koivisto and Sood, 2004].

The authors build upon a recent A*-based search framework that is designed for non-decomposable scores [Chen et al., 2015, 2016]. This framework searches over a graph of equivalence classes (the EC tree) and uses an "oracle"—an optimal learner for decomposable scores—to compute an admissible heuristic that guides the search. The paper's main contribution is to adapt this framework to handle ancestral constraints. The proposed method has two key components. First, it uses the ancestral constraints to directly prune nodes in the EC tree search space that represent equivalence classes containing no DAGs that satisfy the constraints. Second, and more innovatively, it proposes a method to "project" the non-decomposable ancestral constraints onto a set of decomposable constraints (edge presence/absence and pairwise variable ordering constraints). These inferred decomposable constraints can be handled by the oracle, thereby "empowering" it to compute a much tighter heuristic, which significantly accelerates the A* search. The paper provides sound theoretical results for inferring edge constraints and uses a MaxSAT formulation to find a maximal set of consistent ordering constraints. The proposed method is empirically evaluated against an Integer Linear Programming (ILP) approach, which is another general method for handling such constraints, showing orders-of-magnitude performance improvements.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles a practical and important problem. Incorporating expert knowledge is crucial for learning reliable models, and ancestral relations are a natural way to express causal priors. The core idea of projecting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones to empower a heuristic oracle within a larger A* search is novel and powerful. It provides a new and effective way to bridge the gap between algorithms designed for decomposable scores and problems that inherently involve non-decomposable aspects. The results suggest this approach makes optimal learning with this type of background knowledge feasible for problem sizes that were previously out of reach for exact methods.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The work is technically sound and well-supported. The method for pruning the EC tree is justified by Theorem 1. The algorithms for inferring edge constraints are formally presented and backed by Theorems 2 and 3. Recognizing the subtlety of inferring ordering constraints (i.e., that pairwise entailment does not guarantee joint consistency) and formulating it as a MaxSAT problem is a solid and principled solution (supported by Theorem 4). The overall framework is a cohesive and intelligent extension of prior work.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. It provides the necessary background on score-based learning, decomposability, and the EC tree framework before clearly motivating the problem. The core contributions—pruning and constraint projection—are explained in dedicated sections with clear examples and theoretical justification. The logical flow is excellent, guiding the reader from the problem definition to the proposed solution and its validation.

4.  **Experimental Validation:** The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and convincing. The authors compare their method against a strong and appropriate baseline (ILP via GOBNILP). The experiments systematically vary key parameters (number of variables, dataset size, number of constraints) and demonstrate the proposed method's superior performance, often by orders of magnitude. The ablation study in Tables 1 and 2 effectively highlights the critical importance of the constraint projection technique for achieving this speedup. Furthermore, the analysis of how constraints improve model accuracy (structural Hamming distance) provides valuable practical insight, showing that background knowledge can be as impactful as collecting significantly more data.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Overhead of Constraint Projection:** The projection of ordering constraints requires solving a MaxSAT instance. While this is a one-time pre-processing step, its computational cost is not discussed or measured in the experiments. For problems with a large number of variables, this step could become a bottleneck. Including the runtime for the MaxSAT solver would provide a more complete picture of the method's overall performance profile.

2.  **Scalability Discussion:** While the method scales well up to 20 variables, which is competitive for exact learning, the observed "easy-hard-easy" trend as the number of constraints increases suggests a computational cliff. A more detailed discussion of the factors that make the intermediate-constraint settings difficult and the practical limits of the approach would be beneficial for potential users.

3.  **Oracle Dependency:** The performance of the overall system is fundamentally tied to the performance of the chosen oracle for decomposable scores (URLEARNING in this case). While empowering this oracle is the paper's focus, a brief mention of how the choice of a different oracle (e.g., a dynamic programming-based one) might interact with the projected constraints could add further context.

In summary, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. It offers a novel, technically sound, and empirically verified method for optimally solving a practical problem that has been difficult for existing state-of-the-art learners. The strengths in originality, technical quality, and empirical validation are substantial and clearly outweigh the minor weaknesses.
