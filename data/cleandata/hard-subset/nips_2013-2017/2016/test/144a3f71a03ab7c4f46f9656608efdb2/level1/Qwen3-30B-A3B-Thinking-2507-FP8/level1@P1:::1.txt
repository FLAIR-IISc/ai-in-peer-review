

# Peer Review: "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"

## Summary
This paper addresses the important problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures when subject to ancestral constraints (background knowledge about cause-effect relationships), which are non-decomposable and thus incompatible with most mainstream structure learning approaches. The authors build upon Chen et al.'s recent framework for non-decomposable scores (NIPS 2015, 2016), which introduced the BN graph and EC tree search spaces. They show how to incorporate ancestral constraints into this framework by (1) pruning the EC tree based on ancestral constraints, and (2) inferring decomposable edge and ordering constraints from non-decomposable ancestral constraints to empower the oracle. The approach is evaluated against ILP-based methods (GOBNILP) on standard Bayesian network benchmarks, demonstrating orders-of-magnitude efficiency improvements.

The paper relates well to previous NIPS work, particularly Chen et al. (2015, 2016) which introduced the EC tree framework, and contrasts with earlier work like Koivisto and Sood (2004) and Parviainen and Koivisto (2013) that assumed decomposable scores. It also engages with the constraint-based learning work by Borboudakis and Tsamardinos (2012, 2013), though the current approach is score-based rather than constraint-based.

## Strengths

**Quality (Strong):** The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical contributions. Theorems 1-4 provide a sound and complete method for pruning the EC tree and inferring decomposable constraints. The experimental design is thorough, with multiple benchmarks (ALARM, ANDES, CHILD, CPCS54, HEPAR2), varying problem sizes (n=12-20 variables), dataset sizes (N=512-8192), and constraint percentages (p=0-100%). The results consistently show the proposed method outperforms ILP-based approaches by orders of magnitude.

**Originality (Strong):** The core contribution of projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable ones is novel. While ILP approaches can technically represent ancestral constraints, the paper is the first to evaluate them for this specific purpose and demonstrate significant efficiency gains from the constraint projection approach. The paper clearly differentiates from previous work, particularly in addressing the limitation of decomposable score assumptions.

**Significance (Strong):** The problem of incorporating background knowledge into Bayesian network learning is highly relevant for real-world applications where causal knowledge is available. The efficiency gains (orders-of-magnitude) make optimal learning with ancestral constraints practical for larger networks. The paper demonstrates that a small percentage of ancestral constraints (10-25%) can have similar impact on model quality as increasing training data by 4-16x, highlighting the value of background knowledge.

**Clarity (Good):** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with helpful figures (Fig 1) illustrating the EC tree. The technical concepts are well-explained, and the paper provides sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the results. The relationship between ancestral constraints and decomposable constraints is particularly well articulated.

## Weaknesses

**Minor Clarity Issue:** The explanation of Theorem 4 (on ordering constraints) is somewhat dense. A more intuitive explanation of why the specific conditions are both necessary and sufficient would strengthen the paper.

**Limited Comparison with State-of-the-Art:** While the paper compares with ILP (GOBNILP), it doesn't discuss how the approach relates to recent score-based methods that handle some constraints (e.g., Parviainen and Koivisto, 2013 on order-modular constraints). It would strengthen the significance to explicitly discuss how this work extends beyond previous constraint handling approaches.

**Practical Limitations:** The paper doesn't discuss how to handle inconsistencies in ancestral constraints (e.g., X→Y and Y→X). While this is a general challenge with constraints, a brief discussion would make the approach more complete.

## Pro/Con Acceptance

**Pro:**
- Novel contribution: First to show how to incorporate ancestral constraints into optimal structure learning with non-decomposable scores
- Strong theoretical foundation with complete proofs
- Significant empirical results (orders-of-magnitude efficiency gains)
- Practical relevance: Demonstrates how background knowledge can reduce data requirements
- Well-motivated problem addressing a clear limitation in existing approaches

**Con:**
- Limited discussion of constraint inconsistencies
- Could better position against recent constraint-handling work
- Some theoretical concepts (Theorem 4) could be more accessible

## Overall Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to Bayesian network structure learning. The problem is well-motivated, the solution is novel and technically sound, and the empirical results are compelling. The authors have clearly demonstrated how to integrate ancestral constraints into the EC tree framework through constraint projection, addressing a limitation of most existing approaches. The efficiency gains are substantial and would be of interest to both practitioners and researchers.

The paper meets all NIPS criteria: it's high quality (rigorous theory and experiments), clear (well-organized with good figures), original (novel constraint projection approach), and significant (practical efficiency gains with important implications for incorporating background knowledge).

I recommend acceptance at NIPS 2016. The minor weaknesses noted above could be addressed in the camera-ready version with relatively minor revisions.
