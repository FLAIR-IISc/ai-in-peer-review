### **Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data, subject to background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints (e.g., X must be an ancestor of Y, or X cannot be an ancestor of Y). Such constraints are highly relevant for incorporating causal domain knowledge but are non-decomposable, making them incompatible with many state-of-the-art optimal structure learning algorithms that rely on decomposable scores.

The authors build upon a recent A*-based search framework that explores a space of equivalence classes (the EC tree) using an "oracle" for a heuristic. The core contribution is a method to integrate these non-decomposable ancestral constraints into this framework. This is achieved in two ways: (1) A sound pruning strategy for nodes in the EC tree that violate the given constraints. (2) A novel and powerful technique to "project" the non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints. These inferred decomposable constraints are then passed to the oracle, significantly tightening the A* heuristic and accelerating the search. The authors formulate the inference of ordering constraints as a MaxSAT problem. The proposed method is empirically evaluated against a modern Integer Linear Programming (ILP) approach, demonstrating orders-of-magnitude improvements in runtime.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Practical Relevance:** The problem is well-motivated and important. In many real-world applications, expert knowledge is more naturally expressed as high-level causal relationships (ancestors/descendants) rather than specific parent-child relationships (edges). This work provides a principled and efficient way to incorporate such knowledge into the search for an optimal network structure, which can lead to both faster learning and more accurate models, especially with limited data.

2.  **Novel and Elegant Technical Contribution:** The central idea of "projecting" non-decomposable constraints onto decomposable ones to empower an existing optimal solver (the oracle) is clever and highly effective. Instead of developing an entirely new search algorithm, the authors elegantly bridge the gap between the expressive power of ancestral constraints and the efficiency of algorithms designed for decomposable scores. The use of MaxSAT to find a maximal set of consistent ordering constraints is a principled and suitable approach for this sub-problem.

3.  **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed and convincing.
    *   The comparison against GOBNILP, a state-of-the-art ILP-based system, is a strong and appropriate baseline for an exact learning method. The results in Table 1 clearly show a dramatic performance advantage for the proposed approach.
    *   The paper includes an excellent ablation-style comparison (Table 1 vs. Table 2) that quantifies the impact of the constraint projection technique. This experiment successfully demonstrates that empowering the oracle is critical to the method's performance, validating the core technical contribution.
    *   The analysis of the "easy-hard-easy" pattern as the number of constraints increases provides valuable insight into the search problem's complexity.
    *   The paper also shows (Table 3) that incorporating ancestral constraints leads to more accurate models (lower Structural Hamming Distance), reinforcing the practical value of the work.

4.  **Clarity and Soundness:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured. The theoretical claims (Theorems 1-4) appear sound and provide a solid foundation for the proposed algorithms. The distinction between the BN graph and the EC tree is explained clearly, justifying the choice of the latter.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Clarity on a Computationally Intensive Step:** The method for pruning the EC tree requires checking if a CPDAG `P` satisfies the ancestral constraints. The paper states that if simple checks fail, the algorithm must "exhaustively enumerate the structures of class(P)". The size of an equivalence class can be exponential in the number of variables, and this step could potentially become a computational bottleneck, especially for CPDAGs with many undirected edges. The paper does not provide any analysis of how often this exhaustive enumeration is required in their experiments or what its performance impact is. A discussion of this would strengthen the paper's claims about scalability.

2.  **Omission of Key Algorithmic Details:** The paper omits details on how a "maximum set of ancestral constraints" is computed (Section 5.1), stating this is for space reasons. This transitive closure-like step is a crucial preprocessing component of the method. While the general idea is intuitive, providing a reference or a brief algorithmic sketch would improve the completeness and reproducibility of the work. Similarly, the MaxSAT formulation in Section 5.2 is very compact; a little more intuition for clause 3 (the sufficient condition for acyclicity) would be helpful for the reader.

3.  **Limited Comparison to Other Frameworks:** The experimental comparison is focused solely on ILP. While ILP is a major competitor, the introduction also mentions Constraint Programming (CP). Given the close relationship between these fields, a discussion or comparison against a CP-based approach would have broadened the evaluation's scope. Furthermore, the paper focuses exclusively on optimal learning. It would be interesting to briefly discuss how these constraint projection techniques could also be used to guide and accelerate popular heuristic search algorithms (e.g., greedy hill-climbing, TABU search), which are often used when optimal search is intractable.

### **Overall Assessment**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. It addresses a practical and important problem with a technically sound and elegant solution. The core idea of projecting non-decomposable constraints to empower an oracle for decomposable scores is a powerful concept that may be applicable to other constrained optimization problems. The empirical results are strong and convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over a relevant state-of-the-art baseline. While there are minor weaknesses regarding the clarity of some computational steps and the scope of the comparison, they do not detract from the overall strength and impact of the work. The paper is well-written, well-supported, and advances the state of the art in optimal BN learning. I strongly recommend acceptance.
