### NIPS Review

**Paper Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network (BN) structures from data in the presence of ancestral constraints (e.g., variable X must be an ancestor of Y, or X must *not* be an ancestor of Y). The key challenge is that such constraints are non-decomposable, rendering them incompatible with many efficient, state-of-the-art optimal structure learning algorithms that rely on decomposable scores (e.g., dynamic programming methods).

The authors build upon their recent work (Chen et al., 2015, 2016), which introduced an A*-based search framework over a space of equivalence classes (the EC tree) capable of handling non-decomposable scores. The search is guided by a heuristic function provided by an "oracle"—an optimal learner for problems with decomposable scores.

The main contribution of this paper is a novel method to effectively integrate ancestral constraints into this framework. They propose two key ideas:
1.  Directly enforce constraints by pruning nodes (CPDAGs) in the EC tree search space that provably violate the given ancestral constraints.
2.  A more impactful technique called "constraint projection," where the non-decomposable ancestral constraints are analyzed to infer a set of *decomposable* constraints (i.e., required/forbidden edges and topological ordering constraints). This is done via a sound enumeration method for edges and a MaxSAT formulation for orderings. These inferred decomposable constraints are then passed to the oracle, significantly improving the heuristic's accuracy and pruning power, which in turn dramatically accelerates the main A* search.

The authors provide a thorough empirical evaluation comparing their approach to a standard Integer Linear Programming (ILP) formulation, demonstrating orders-of-magnitude speed improvements. They also analyze the scalability and the impact of adding constraints on both search efficiency and the accuracy of the learned network structure.

**Strengths (Reasons for Acceptance)**

1.  **Significance and Practicality:** The problem is highly relevant. In many real-world domains (e.g., biology, economics), experts possess causal background knowledge that can be naturally expressed as ancestral relationships. Providing a method to learn *optimal* structures that respect this knowledge is a significant contribution. The paper clearly demonstrates that even a small amount of such knowledge can improve model accuracy as much as a substantial increase in data size.

2.  **Novelty and Technical Depth:** The core technical idea of "projecting" non-decomposable constraints into a decomposable form to "empower" an oracle is both elegant and powerful. It provides a generalizable template for incorporating other types of non-decomposable knowledge into search-based learning frameworks. The specific methods for this projection are non-trivial contributions in their own right, particularly the use of a MaxSAT solver to find a maximal set of consistent ordering constraints. This shows a sophisticated understanding of the interplay between different types of graph constraints.

3.  **Technical Quality and Soundness:** The approach is well-founded. The pruning strategy for the EC tree is justified by Theorem 1. The algorithms for inferring edge constraints (Theorems 2 and 3) and ordering constraints (Theorem 4) are presented as sound, ensuring that the search remains exact. The overall framework correctly finds the optimal BN structure satisfying the given constraints.

4.  **Thorough and Convincing Experiments:** The experimental evaluation is a major strength.
    *   The comparison against a strong and appropriate baseline (ILP via GOBNILP) clearly establishes the state-of-the-art performance of the proposed method. The orders-of-magnitude speedup is impressive.
    *   The ablation study (Tables 1 vs. 2) that isolates the effect of constraint projection is crucial and effectively proves that this is the key to the method's efficiency, not just the underlying EC-tree search.
    *   The analysis of scalability and the "easy-hard-easy" phenomenon as the number of constraints increases provides valuable insight into the behavior of the algorithm.
    *   Measuring the Structural Hamming Distance shows the practical benefit of incorporating prior knowledge for learning more accurate models.

**Weaknesses (Areas for Improvement)**

1.  **Clarity and Density:** The paper is quite dense, particularly in Section 5. While the core ideas are communicated, the details of the constraint inference algorithms (especially the MaxSAT formulation) are brief. For reproducibility, a more detailed explanation, perhaps in an appendix, would be beneficial. For example, the justification for the clauses in the MaxSAT instance could be expanded upon.

2.  **Limited Discussion of Scalability:** While the method scales better than the ILP baseline, exact BN structure learning is fundamentally hard. The experiments go up to 20 variables, which is respectable for exact methods but still limits applicability to small-to-medium sized problems. The paper could be more explicit about this inherent limitation and briefly discuss how the approach might relate to approximate methods for larger-scale problems.

3.  **Incremental Nature:** The work heavily builds on the authors' previous papers on BN and EC graphs. The core search algorithm is not new. The novelty is confined to the specific application of this framework to ancestral constraints and the constraint projection technique. While this new contribution is significant and powerful, it is important to frame it as an extension of an existing framework rather than a completely new one.

**Overall Assessment**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a strong and valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. It tackles an important practical problem with a novel and technically sound solution. The central idea of empowering a decomposable-score oracle with projected non-decomposable constraints is clever and impactful. The empirical results are comprehensive and convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed approach over a relevant baseline. The weaknesses are minor, mostly related to the density of the presentation and the inherent scalability limits of any exact learning algorithm. The paper advances the state of the art in optimal structure learning and is very likely to be influential and built upon by other researchers. It is a clear accept for NIPS.
