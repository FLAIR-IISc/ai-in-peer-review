Summary
The paper tackles exact Bayesian network (BN) structure learning with non-decomposable background knowledge in the form of ancestral (reachability) constraints. It builds on Chen et al.’s BN graph/EC tree shortest-path perspective for non-decomposable scores, and shows how to incorporate hard ancestral constraints by pruning EC-tree nodes (CPDAGs) that cannot be extended to any DAG satisfying the constraints. To recover efficiency lost when the heuristic oracle (order-graph methods) cannot natively handle non-decomposable constraints, the paper proposes projecting ancestral constraints onto decomposable edge and ordering constraints. For edges, it gives polynomial-time entailment tests (Theorems 2–3) using ancestor/descendant closures α(·), β(·). For orderings, it poses a MaxSAT problem to compute a maximally large set of safe ordering constraints implied by ancestral constraints (Theorem 4 gives sufficient conditions). Empirically, the proposed EC-tree+A* method with an oracle (URLEARNING) and projections is compared against the ILP solver GOBNILP, showing orders-of-magnitude speedups on benchmarks with simulated constraints; it scales up to ~20 variables and shows an easy–hard–easy pattern as constraint density increases.

Relation to prior work
The paper situates itself against order-graph/dynamic programming/A* methods which require decomposable scores and modular constraints (Koivisto & Sood; Silander & Myllymäki; Yuan & Malone), and ILP/CP approaches that can handle global constraints but have scalability issues (Jaakkola et al.; Bartlett & Cussens; van Beek & Hoffmann). It also relates to path constraints in constraint-based learning and soft priors (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos). The key novelty is combining EC-tree pruning by ancestral constraints with projected decomposable constraints to empower a decomposable-score oracle.

Strengths
- Addresses a practically important class of background knowledge (ancestral relations) that mainstream score-based methods cannot natively enforce.
- Clear conceptual integration: prune EC-tree nodes that provably cannot satisfy constraints; use an admissible heuristic from a decomposable oracle augmented with only constraints entailed by the ancestral knowledge.
- Sound rules for inferring negative edge constraints (Theorem 2) and a simple, implementable test for positive edge entailment (Theorem 3), plus a conservative MaxSAT formulation to harvest many safe orderings (Theorem 4).
- Empirical evaluation indicates large speedups over ILP, and demonstrates the value of projection in tightening heuristics.
- The easy–hard–easy trend analysis and accuracy trends vs. constraints/data size provide useful insight.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Completeness/complexity gaps:
  - CPDAG pruning relies, when local path tests are inconclusive, on “exhaustively enumerating” class(P). Since class sizes can be exponential, the worst-case complexity is unclear. How often does enumeration trigger in practice? Are there polynomial-time graph-theoretic tests (or sufficient conditions) that could avoid enumeration more broadly?
  - The “maximum set of ancestral constraints” closure used by Theorems 2–3 is defined informally (“details omitted”). The soundness of edge entailment depends critically on the completeness of these closure rules. Please provide the full set of inference rules and a proof (or citation) of closure correctness.
  - Theorem 3’s condition for inferring X→Y checks all Z outside α(X)∪β(Y) and requires either Xa 6 Zb or Za 6 Yb for all Z. The argument that this blocks all longer paths (not just length-2 paths) needs a rigorous proof; otherwise there is a risk of inferring edges too aggressively.
  - For ordering constraints, the MaxSAT encoding uses sufficient conditions (Theorem 4). This yields conservative entailment, but the encoding in item 3 (acyclicity sufficient condition) is nonstandard and not obviously implied by standard partial order reasoning. A proof or intuition why this prevents cycles while preserving soundness of entailment is needed.
- Heuristic admissibility relies on passing only constraints that are entailed by the ancestral knowledge. Any unsound projection could make h(G) inadmissible. The paper should make this dependency explicit and verify empirically (e.g., via bounds) that admissibility holds.
- Experimental scope:
  - Only BDeu is tested; MDL or different equivalent sample sizes would strengthen generality claims.
  - No experiments with indegree bounds. ILP benefits substantially from small indegree; to be fair, include a setting with indegree ≤ 3–4 (common in practice).
  - Constraints are sampled noiselessly from ground truth. Real background knowledge can be noisy or contradictory. How does the method behave with inconsistent constraints (detect/report) or with soft/path-belief priors?
  - Overheads of projection steps (especially MaxSAT) are not quantified. Provide timings and the fraction of inferred edges/orderings.
  - Memory/time for precomputing all parent-set scores without indegree bounds can dominate; report these costs and how they scale.
- Clarity/Presentation:
  - Some editorial issues: stray date text, footnote markers, and incomplete/broken references. Proof sketches and algorithmic details (e.g., enumeration strategy, oracle interface, constraint filtering on partial CPDAGs) are missing or deferred; a supplement seems necessary.
  - Define precisely how constraints are applied on partial CPDAGs during search: ignore constraints mentioning unseen variables, or do you maintain partial implications?

Quality
Technically promising with solid ideas; pruning soundness is straightforward, and the projection strategy is well motivated. However, several key claims (Theorems 2–4, completeness of closure, MaxSAT constraints) need fuller proofs and complexity discussion. The empirical evidence is encouraging but could be broadened and made fairer (indegree bounds, overheads).

Clarity
Generally well written at a high level, but marred by formatting errors and missing details. Add formal definitions, proof sketches, and algorithm pseudocode (even in the supplement), and clean up references.

Originality
The combination of EC-tree pruning with projected decomposable constraints and a MaxSAT-based ordering entailment appears novel and nontrivial relative to prior order-graph, ILP/CP, and path-constraint works.

Significance
If the pruning and projection are as effective as suggested, this provides a practical exact method for incorporating strong causal prior knowledge at scale, which is valuable to both BN learning and causal discovery communities.

Suggestions and questions for authors
- Provide the full closure rules for ancestral constraints and proofs of Theorems 2–4 (or precise citations).
- Quantify how often class(P) enumeration is needed, its cost, and any heuristics to avoid it.
- Report MaxSAT overhead and the number of inferred ordering constraints; ablate the effect of edge vs ordering projections separately.
- Include experiments with indegree bounds, MDL, and noisy/inconsistent constraints (e.g., detect conflicts, or soften via penalties).
- Clarify constraint handling on partial CPDAGs and ensure admissibility of the heuristic is preserved.
- Clean up references and formatting.

Pros
- Addresses an important, under-supported form of prior knowledge.
- Sound pruning in an EC-tree formulation; practical and conceptually clean.
- Effective projection to empower a decomposable oracle; strong empirical speedups.

Cons
- Missing proofs/details for key entailment rules; potential soundness/complexity concerns.
- Reliance on class enumeration in ambiguous CPDAGs may be costly.
- Experimental scope could be broader and fairer to ILP baselines.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on providing proofs/algorithmic details and strengthening experimental evaluation as suggested. Confidence: high.
