## NIPS 2016 Review: Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning Bayesian networks (BNs) when background knowledge is provided in the form of *ancestral constraints* – relationships dictating ancestor/descendant relationships between variables.  The authors highlight the limitations of existing optimal structure learning methods that rely on decomposable scoring functions, as these struggle to incorporate the non-decomposable nature of ancestral constraints. They build upon their previous work utilizing the EC tree search space for optimal learning with non-decomposable priors, extending it to handle ancestral constraints.  Crucially, they also present a method to *project* these constraints into decomposable forms (edge and ordering constraints) to empower an oracle used within the EC tree search, significantly improving efficiency.  The paper demonstrates empirically, through comparison with Integer Linear Programming (ILP) approaches, that their method offers substantial performance gains.

**Related Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the context of BN structure learning. It references foundational work (Darwiche, Koller & Friedman, Murphy) and more recent advancements in optimal learning (Chen et al., Koivisto & Sood). The discussion of limitations in existing methods due to decomposability assumptions is accurate.  It appropriately acknowledges related work on constraint-based learning with path constraints (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos) and ILP formulations, noting the lack of comprehensive empirical evaluation of the latter in the context of ancestral constraints. The connection to MaxSAT for inferring topological ordering constraints is also well-placed.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an important problem:** Integrating background knowledge into BN structure learning is crucial for real-world applications. Ancestral constraints are a powerful and commonly-used form of such knowledge.
* **Novel approach:**  The combination of the EC tree search space with constraint projection is a significant contribution. The idea of translating non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones to leverage existing efficient algorithms is clever and effective.
* **Strong empirical results:** The experimental evaluation convincingly demonstrates the superiority of the proposed approach over ILP-based methods, often by orders of magnitude. The scalability analysis to larger networks is particularly valuable.
* **Theoretical soundness:** The theoretical results (Theorems 1-4) provide a solid foundation for the proposed method and clearly define the conditions under which constraint projection is valid.
* **Clear presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, and the technical details are presented in a reasonably accessible manner. The use of examples helps to illustrate key concepts.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Dependency on the Oracle:** The performance relies heavily on the effectiveness of the "oracle" (URLEARNING). While the authors acknowledge this, it’s a critical point. The performance gains could be diminished if a less effective oracle were used.  A more thorough investigation of oracle sensitivity would be beneficial.
* **MaxSAT Complexity:** The MaxSAT reduction for inferring ordering constraints, while theoretically sound, could potentially become a bottleneck for very large networks.  The scalability of the EVASOLVER used is not fully addressed, and it's unclear how the approach would handle even larger constraint sets.  Exploring alternative methods for inferring ordering constraints (perhaps approximate methods) could be worthwhile.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the experimental results are strong, the evaluation is primarily focused on synthetic data generated from existing BN benchmarks. Evaluating the approach on real-world datasets with naturally occurring ancestral constraints would further strengthen the findings.
* **Discussion of Markov Equivalence:** The EC tree handles Markov equivalence classes, but the implications of this for ancestral constraints are not fully explored.  It would be useful to discuss how the approach deals with situations where multiple DAGs within an equivalence class satisfy the constraints, and whether any specific DAG is preferred.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important problem with a novel and effective solution. The combination of the EC tree search space and constraint projection is a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. The empirical results are compelling, and the theoretical foundations are solid. While there are some areas for improvement (oracle sensitivity, MaxSAT scalability, real-world data evaluation), these do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.  

**Arguments Pro Acceptance:**

* Solves a practically relevant problem in BN learning.
* Presents a novel and theoretically sound approach.
* Demonstrates significant performance improvements over existing methods.
* Is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments Con Acceptance:**

* Reliance on oracle performance.
* Potential scalability issues with the MaxSAT reduction.
* Limited evaluation on real-world data.




**Recommendation:**

**Strong Accept.**  I believe this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field and is well-deserving of acceptance to NIPS 2016.  I recommend the authors address the minor weaknesses mentioned above in a future iteration of the work.
