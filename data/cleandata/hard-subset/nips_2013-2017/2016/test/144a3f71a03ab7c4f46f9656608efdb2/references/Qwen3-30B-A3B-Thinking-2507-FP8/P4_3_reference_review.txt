

# Review of "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"

This paper addresses the problem of learning Bayesian network structures subject to ancestral constraints, which specify cause-and-effect relationships between variables (e.g., "X is an ancestor of Y" or "X is not an ancestor of Y"). The authors note that such constraints are non-decomposable, making them difficult to incorporate into standard Bayesian network learning approaches that assume decomposable scores. The paper builds upon Chen et al.'s EC tree framework for non-decomposable score learning, extending it to handle ancestral constraints through two key contributions: (1) a method for pruning the EC tree search space based on ancestral constraints, and (2) a technique to project non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints that can empower a standard oracle (a system for learning with decomposable scores).

The paper relates well to previous NIPS work, particularly Chen et al. (2015, 2016) who introduced the EC tree framework, and also connects to constraint-based learning approaches like Borboudakis and Tsamardinos (2012, 2013) that used ancestral constraints in different learning paradigms. The authors clearly distinguish their work from ILP-based approaches (e.g., Cussens, 2008; Bartlett and Cussens, 2015) that can represent ancestral constraints but have not been evaluated for this specific problem.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with a strong theoretical foundation. The authors provide formal definitions, theorems (Theorems 1-4), and proofs for their constraint projection techniques. The experimental evaluation is thorough, with controlled variations in problem size (n=12-20 variables), dataset size (N=512-8192), and constraint density (p=0-100%). The comparison against GOBNILP (a state-of-the-art ILP-based system) is fair, with both approaches using the same input data and scoring function (BDeu). The authors carefully evaluate the impact of constraint projection on efficiency, showing dramatic improvements in both EC tree (up to orders of magnitude) and ILP (moderate improvements). The paper also includes an analysis of the structural Hamming distance to the ground-truth network, demonstrating that ancestral constraints significantly improve model quality.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The background on Bayesian network structure learning (Section 2) provides sufficient context without being overly verbose. The key contributions (Sections 4 and 5) are clearly presented with helpful visualizations (Figures 1a and 1b). The paper provides precise definitions of technical terms (e.g., ancestral constraints, Markov equivalence classes) and builds the argument methodically. The experimental setup is described in sufficient detail for reproducibility, including the benchmark networks used and the parameters varied.

**Originality:** The paper makes a clear contribution by extending the EC tree framework to handle ancestral constraints, which are non-decomposable and thus incompatible with most standard structure learning approaches. While ILP-based methods can represent ancestral constraints, the authors are the first to evaluate them for this specific purpose and demonstrate the dramatic efficiency gap between ILP and their approach. The constraint projection techniques (both for edge constraints and ordering constraints) are novel, with the MaxSAT encoding for ordering constraints being particularly innovative. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work, especially Chen et al. (2015, 2016) who did not consider ancestral constraints.

**Significance:** The results are significant for both theoretical and practical reasons. Theoretical significance comes from providing a sound and complete method for handling non-decomposable constraints in Bayesian network learning. Practically, the work demonstrates that a small number of ancestral constraints (10-25%) can have a similar impact on model quality as increasing training data size by 4-16x, which is valuable for applications where data collection is expensive. The efficiency gains (orders of magnitude faster than ILP) make the approach practically useful for real-world applications.

**Strengths:** 
- Strong theoretical foundation with clear theorems and proofs
- Thorough empirical evaluation across multiple dimensions
- Practical demonstration of how background knowledge (ancestral constraints) improves model quality
- Clear distinction from previous work and careful comparison with ILP baseline
- Well-organized and clearly written

**Weaknesses:**
- The evaluation is limited to standard benchmark networks (ALARM, ANDES, CHILD, CPCS54, HEPAR2), without testing on more diverse or real-world datasets
- The paper doesn't discuss the practical challenges of implementing the MaxSAT encoding for ordering constraints at scale
- The discussion of scalability is limited to n=20 variables, while some prior work (e.g., Yuan and Malone, 2013) has demonstrated scalability to 26 variables

**Pro/con for acceptance:**
Pro: This is a technically sound paper with clear theoretical contributions that address a meaningful practical problem. The results demonstrate significant improvements over the state of the art, and the paper is well-written and clearly organized. The work advances the state of the art in Bayesian network structure learning with non-decomposable constraints.

Con: The experimental evaluation could be strengthened with additional datasets beyond the standard benchmarks, and the paper could provide more discussion about the practical implementation challenges of the constraint projection techniques.

**Overall assessment:** The paper makes a significant contribution to Bayesian network structure learning by providing a theoretically sound and empirically validated approach for incorporating ancestral constraints. The results demonstrate both theoretical novelty and practical significance, with substantial efficiency gains over competing methods. The paper meets all four NIPS criteria (quality, clarity, originality, significance) and is a strong candidate for acceptance. The work is clearly different from previous NIPS papers on Bayesian network structure learning, and it addresses an important practical problem in the field.
