Summary
The paper addresses optimal Bayesian network structure learning under non-decomposable ancestral (path) constraints. Existing exact methods typically assume decomposable scores and constraints (e.g., edge presence/absence or precedence constraints), which do not accommodate ancestral constraints directly. Building on BN graph and EC tree search spaces (Chen et al. 2015, 2016), the authors:

- Use EC trees (CPDAG-based, canonicalized into a tree) with A* and an oracle for decomposable scores to search over equivalence classes while pruning CPDAGs that cannot satisfy the given ancestral constraints.
- Provide procedures to “project” non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints to strengthen the oracle:
  - Edge constraints: an algorithm (Theorems 2–3) that infers entailed negative and positive edge constraints using ancestor/descendant closures α(·), β(·).
  - Ordering constraints: a MaxSAT formulation to infer a maximally large, acyclicity-safe set of precedence constraints entailed by the ancestral constraints (with sufficient conditions, Theorem 4).
- Empirically compare against an ILP solver (GOBNILP) that encodes ancestral constraints via linear constraints, showing orders-of-magnitude speedups on benchmarks (ALARM, ANDES, CHILD, CPCS54, HEPAR2 subnets) across variable counts, data sizes, and fractions of provided constraints. They also show an easy–hard–easy trend as constraint density increases and improvements in structural Hamming distance with more constraints.

Relation to prior work: ILP/CP encodings can represent path constraints, but there has been little empirical evaluation in this setting; order-graph/Dynamic Programming/A* typically rely on decomposability and cannot enforce ancestral constraints directly. The paper extends the EC-tree framework to support non-decomposable constraints and contributes inference mechanisms to convert them into decomposable constraints that can be exploited by standard oracles.

Strengths
- Problem relevance: Ancestral constraints encode practically important causal/temporal knowledge; enabling exact learning under such constraints is valuable.
- Methodological contribution: Clear integration of non-decomposable constraints into EC-tree search via pruning; principled projection of ancestral constraints into edge/order constraints to power decomposable oracles.
- Theoretical insights: Simple, checkable conditions for entailed edge constraints (Theorems 2–3) and sufficient conditions for safe ordering constraints (Theorem 4). The negative-edge entailment rule is especially elegant and likely useful beyond this setting.
- Empirical results: Strong speedups over ILP, particularly as n grows; ablation shows the benefit of constraint projection; accuracy improves with constraint quantity, supporting practical utility.
- Conceptual clarity: Nicely positions the EC-tree approach as a “meta” framework that can exploit powerful oracles while handling non-decomposable constraints via search-space pruning.

Weaknesses
- Clarity/Correctness of pruning condition (Theorem 1): The statement “A CPDAG P satisfies A iff its descendants satisfy A” is confusing and likely misstated. Satisfaction is not monotone under adding leaves in general; violations are monotone (once violated, cannot be repaired by adding a leaf whose edges point only into the new node). The text needs to:
  - Disambiguate the variable sets: pruning at partial CPDAGs vs full CPDAGs “over the same set of variables.”
  - State precisely which constraints can be checked safely at partial nodes (negative constraints among existing variables), and which must be deferred (positive constraints that might be realized through future nodes).
- Completeness claims: The paper claims a “sound and complete method for pruning the EC tree,” but the described tests include a fallback to enumerating class(P). Completeness depends on whether P and A are defined over the same variable set; otherwise pruning for positive constraints may be unsafe. This needs a precise theorem and proof (or restrict pruning to levels where the condition holds).
- Missing formal proofs and algorithmic details: Key results (closure rules for ancestral constraints; proofs of Theorems 2–4; complexity bounds) are omitted. The MaxSAT encoding’s acyclicity clause (Constraint 3) is unusual and warrants justification and discussion of tightness.
- Potentially expensive CPDAG enumeration: When tests are inconclusive, enumerating class(P) can be exponential. The paper should quantify how often this happens, the typical class sizes, and its impact on runtime.
- Evaluation scope: Constraints are randomly sampled from ground-truth relations; no study of noisy or conflicting constraints, which are common in practice. Comparisons are limited to ILP; a baseline that uses order-modular/precedence constraints (e.g., Parviainen & Koivisto 2013) strengthened by the projected ordering constraints would be informative. Reporting solver time breakdowns (EC-tree search vs oracle vs MaxSAT) would improve reproducibility.
- Presentation issues: Some citation formatting is broken; figures referenced but not shown here. A few notational slips (e.g., “both over the same set of variables”) add ambiguity.

Quality
- Technically promising with sensible constructions and strong empirical evidence. However, the pruning theorem and completeness claim need clarification/correction, and formal proofs (or an extended appendix) should be provided. More detail on computational costs of the projection steps and class(P) enumeration is needed.

Clarity
- Generally well written, but key definitions/theorems need to be tightened. Provide a clear description of when pruning is safe at partial nodes, and state assumptions for all theorems. Include pseudo-code for edge-constraint inference and the exact MaxSAT encoding.

Originality
- Novel combination: EC-tree search with non-decomposable constraint pruning plus projection to decomposable constraints (edge/order) is original and useful. The entailment rules for edges and the MaxSAT-based extraction of safe ordering constraints are fresh contributions.

Significance
- High practical impact: background knowledge can dramatically shrink search and improve accuracy; showing exact learning with ancestral constraints and large speedups over ILP is valuable. The projection techniques may benefit other exact/approximate learners.

Questions for the authors
- Please clarify Theorem 1 and the pruning policy at partial CPDAGs. Under what precise conditions is pruning sound, and what (if any) completeness guarantees hold?
- How often do you need to enumerate class(P), and what are the typical sizes encountered? Any heuristics to avoid enumeration?
- What is the complexity of the edge-constraint inference algorithm in n and |A|? Any worst-case bounds?
- How sensitive is performance to noisy or inconsistent ancestral constraints? Can the framework handle soft constraints?
- Could you compare against an order-graph/A* baseline equipped with your projected ordering constraints?

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, underexplored setting (non-decomposable ancestral constraints).
- Strong empirical speedups and accuracy gains.
- Conceptually clean framework likely to influence follow-up work.

Cons for acceptance
- Ambiguity around pruning theorem and completeness.
- Missing proofs/details for core theoretical claims.
- Limited evaluation breadth (no noisy constraints; limited baselines).

Recommendation
Weak accept. The contribution is significant and timely, with solid empirical evidence. Acceptance should be contingent on clarifying/correcting the pruning theorem, adding formal proofs (or an appendix), and expanding implementation details and limitations.
