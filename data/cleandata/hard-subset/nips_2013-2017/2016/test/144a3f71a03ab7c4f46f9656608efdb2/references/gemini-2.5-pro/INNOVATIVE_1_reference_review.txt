**Summary:**
This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data when given background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints (e.g., X must be an ancestor of Y; Z must not be an ancestor of W). Such constraints are a natural way to encode prior knowledge but are non-decomposable, which makes them incompatible with most efficient, optimal structure learning algorithms that rely on decomposable scores.

The authors build on a recent A*-based search framework that navigates a space of equivalence classes (the EC tree) and uses an "oracle"—an optimal solver for problems with decomposable scores—to compute an admissible heuristic. The paper's main contributions are twofold. First, it shows how to directly incorporate ancestral constraints by pruning the EC tree search space. Second, and more significantly, it introduces a novel method to "project" the global, non-decomposable ancestral constraints into a set of local, decomposable constraints (i.e., required/forbidden edges and partial variable orderings). These inferred decomposable constraints are then passed to the oracle, dramatically improving the heuristic's quality and, consequently, the overall search efficiency. The authors formalize this projection for edge constraints and propose a MaxSAT formulation to find a maximal set of consistent ordering constraints. The proposed method is empirically evaluated against a strong baseline, Integer Linear Programming (ILP), and shown to be orders of magnitude faster, scaling to problems with up to 20 variables.

**Strengths:**
1.  **High Novelty and a Bold Approach:** The central idea of this paper—projecting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones to "empower" a heuristic oracle within a larger search—is exceptionally elegant and powerful. This is not merely an application of a prior framework but a significant conceptual advance. It provides a new blueprint for tackling problems that mix decomposable and non-decomposable components, which could have impact beyond Bayesian network learning. This is precisely the kind of creative and bold thinking that pushes the field forward.

2.  **Significant Contribution to an Important Problem:** The inability of standard optimal methods to handle rich background knowledge like ancestral constraints has been a long-standing limitation. This work provides the first practical and exact algorithm to solve this problem effectively. The experiments convincingly show that incorporating even a small amount of ancestral knowledge can improve model accuracy as much as collecting significantly more data, highlighting the immense practical importance of this work for domains where data is scarce but expert knowledge is available.

3.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Evaluation:** The paper's claims are thoroughly supported by experiments. The choice of ILP as a baseline is appropriate, as it is one of the few other methods capable of handling such constraints exactly. The orders-of-magnitude speedup is impressive. Crucially, the comparison between running the algorithm with and without constraint projection (Tables 1 vs. 2) clearly demonstrates that the novel projection idea is the key driver of the performance gains, not just the underlying EC-tree search.

4.  **Clarity and Technical Soundness:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clear, the proposed method is well-described, and the key claims are formalized in theorems. It presents a complete and polished piece of research.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Complexity of Constraint Projection:** The MaxSAT formulation for inferring ordering constraints, while principled, introduces a non-trivial and potentially expensive sub-problem. The paper relies on an off-the-shelf solver and does not discuss the scalability or potential bottlenecks of this step. While the overall results suggest this is not a limiting factor for the tested problem sizes, a brief analysis of the complexity of this projection step would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Scalability Limitations:** The method is demonstrated on problems up to 20 variables. While this is state-of-the-art for *exact* score-based learning and a significant achievement, it is still a limitation for larger real-world applications. This is more an inherent challenge of the problem domain than a specific flaw of the paper, but it is worth acknowledging.

**Recommendation:**
I strongly recommend this paper for acceptance.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a highly novel and powerful concept of "constraint projection" to bridge the gap between non-decomposable global constraints and efficient decomposable solvers.
*   It solves a significant, practical, and previously difficult problem in structure learning, making optimal learning with ancestral priors feasible.
*   The empirical results are outstanding, demonstrating a clear and dramatic improvement over a strong and relevant baseline.
*   The work is technically sound, clearly presented, and likely to inspire follow-up research on hybrid constraint and search methods.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The work is an extension of the authors' previous research on EC trees. (However, the projection concept is a major, non-obvious addition that fundamentally enhances the framework's capability.)
*   The method's scalability is limited, as is typical for exact approaches. (However, pushing the boundary of exact methods to n=20 on this difficult problem is a valuable contribution in itself.)

In my view, the paper's strengths, particularly its conceptual novelty and significant practical implications, far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It is an excellent example of creative problem-solving and a standout contribution.
