Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies exact Bayesian network (BN) structure learning with background knowledge given as ancestral constraints (X is/is not an ancestor of Y). The key challenge is that these constraints are non-decomposable, while most optimal structure-learning methods assume decomposable scores and constraints. Building on recent search spaces over DAGs and CPDAGs—the BN graph and EC tree [Chen et al., 2015; Chen et al., 2016]—the authors show how to integrate ancestral constraints by pruning nodes (DAGs/CPDAGs) that cannot satisfy them. They further “empower” a decomposable-score oracle (e.g., order-graph based algorithms such as URLearning [Yuan and Malone, 2013]) by projecting ancestral constraints to decomposable edge- and ordering-constraints that the oracle can exploit.

Concretely: (i) in EC trees, a CPDAG is pruned if it necessarily violates X 6 Y (contains a directed X→…→Y path) or cannot possibly satisfy X Y (no partially directed X→…→Y path), and if inconclusive, by enumerating class(P) to test feasibility; (ii) they derive closure rules to enlarge a given set of ancestral constraints and define α(X) (forced ancestors) and β(X) (forced descendants); (iii) they give sufficient and necessary conditions for entailed negative and positive edge constraints (Theorems 2–3); and (iv) they reduce inference of a maximally consistent set of ordering constraints to a partial MaxSAT problem with hard clauses for transitivity and acyclicity and soft clauses to include orderings suggested by negative ancestry, with sufficient conditions stated in Theorem 4.

Experiments compare the proposed EC-tree-based A* method (with an oracle) to the ILP system GOBNILP [Jaakkola et al., 2010; Bartlett and Cussens, 2015] on standard BN benchmarks (ALARM, ANDES, CHILD, CPCS54, HEPAR2), varying number of variables, sample size, and proportion of ancestral constraints. With projected constraints provided to both systems, the proposed method is reported to be orders of magnitude faster, with larger gains as n grows. Withholding projection degrades both, but much more so the EC-tree approach, showing the practical importance of projection. Additional experiments show an easy–hard–easy trend as the number of constraints increases, scalability to about 20 variables, and improved structure accuracy with more data and more constraints. The paper also notes that ILP tends to benefit monotonically from more constraints.

Quality
- Soundness: The pruning idea is standard and, combined with enumeration of class(P), should be sound and (for pruning) complete. The edge-projection theorems are plausible and precisely stated; however, proofs are omitted. The MaxSAT formulation for ordering constraints uses sufficient (not necessary) conditions for acyclicity, which preserves soundness (does not exclude feasible DAGs) but may be conservative. A statement in the conclusion claims a “sound and complete method for pruning,” consistent with enumeration, but the formal Theorem 1 as written (“P satisfies A iff its descendants satisfy A”) is unclear or misstated for partial CPDAGs. Overall, the approach appears technically reasonable, but several key claims lack proof details.
- Empirical support: The experimental protocol is sensible (standard networks, BDeu, precomputed family scores). However, the paper text does not include the actual tables/figures, error bars, or detailed numerical results, limiting independent assessment. The comparison is primarily to ILP; no CP baseline [van Beek and Hoffmann, 2015] is reported.
- Limitations not discussed: Enumeration of class(P) can be exponential; no bounds or empirical measurements of how often enumeration is triggered are given. The approach assumes consistent (error-free) constraints; robustness to noisy/mistaken constraints is not evaluated. Proofs and complexity analyses for the closure algorithm (to obtain a “maximum set” A) are omitted.

Clarity
- The paper is generally understandable and well-motivated, but contains several clarity issues:
  - Theorem 1’s statement appears incorrect/ambiguous with respect to partial CPDAGs vs full variable sets.
  - The “maximum set” of ancestral constraints is referenced without giving the closure rules or their complexity.
  - The MaxSAT encoding is only sketched; it would help to specify exactly which clauses are hard vs soft and why Clause (3) suffices for acyclicity of combined orderings.
  - There are numerous typographical and bibliographic artifacts (e.g., “th Conference,” stray non-English characters, incomplete citations with placeholders). These should be corrected.
- Suggestions: Provide precise definitions, a small worked example for projection (edge and ordering), pseudocode for the closure and edge-projection algorithms, and a proof sketch for Theorems 2–4.

Originality
- Integrating non-decomposable ancestral constraints into the EC-tree search and projecting them into decomposable edge/order constraints to guide a decomposable-score oracle is, to my knowledge, novel. Prior work integrated path beliefs in constraint-based or local search [Borboudakis and Tsamardinos, 2012; 2013] and used ILP/CP to encode global constraints; this paper contributes an exact A*-based alternative with principled constraint projection.

Significance
- The work addresses a meaningful practical setting where background knowledge exists and can reduce search. If the reported speedups hold broadly, the approach is a useful addition to exact BN learning under constraints, with potential impact on applications requiring optimal structures. The scalability demonstrated is in line with exact BN learning literature; constraints can extend it. The projection machinery may be of independent interest.

Pros
- Clear motivation for ancestral constraints and their non-decomposability.
- Conceptually appealing integration of EC-tree pruning with a strong oracle.
- Formal characterization of entailed edge constraints; sufficient conditions for safe ordering constraints.
- Empirical evidence of large speedups over a strong ILP baseline; analysis of the effect of projection.
- Uses standard datasets and scores.

Cons
- Missing proofs and algorithmic details (closure procedure, proofs for Theorems 2–4, justification of acyclicity clause).
- Theorem 1 appears misstated; CPDAG satisfaction criterion needs clarification.
- Potential exponential enumeration of class(P) is not analyzed; no reporting on its frequency.
- Evaluation omits CP baselines; tables/figures are not shown in the text provided.
- No robustness analysis to noisy/incorrect constraints; limited ablations (e.g., edge vs ordering projection).

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify and correct Theorem 1; define satisfaction/violation for partial CPDAGs precisely.
- Include proofs or proof sketches for Theorems 2–4; provide the closure rules and complexity of computing the “maximum set” of ancestral constraints.
- Provide pseudocode and complexity for the edge projection algorithm; quantify how many constraints are typically inferred and their effect on oracle runtime.
- Detail the MaxSAT encoding (variables, hard vs soft clauses, objective) and justify the acyclicity constraint; report solver runtime.
- Add ablations (no projection, only edge projection, only ordering projection) and report enumeration rates of class(P).
- Evaluate robustness to a small fraction of incorrect constraints.
- Include a CP baseline and a BN-graph baseline to isolate the value of EC-tree canonization.
- Clean up references and typographical issues.

Overall, the paper presents a technically interesting and potentially impactful method; with stronger theoretical exposition and more comprehensive empirical reporting, it would be a solid contribution.
