## NIPS 2016 Review: Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of learning Bayesian network structures when prior knowledge is available in the form of ancestral constraints (specifying ancestor/descendant relationships between variables). The key challenge lies in the non-decomposable nature of these constraints, which clashes with the decomposability assumptions underlying many efficient Bayesian network learning algorithms. The authors build upon their previous work leveraging the “EC tree” search space and an oracle for learning with decomposable scores, demonstrating how to incorporate ancestral constraints by pruning the search space and, crucially, projecting these constraints into decomposable constraints usable by the oracle.  They empirically show significant performance improvements compared to Integer Linear Programming (ILP)-based approaches, showcasing the efficacy of their approach.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper clearly situates itself within the broader context of Bayesian network structure learning (citing Darwiche, Koller & Friedman, Murphy). It directly builds upon the authors' previous work on optimal Bayesian network learning with non-decomposable scores using BN graphs and EC trees (Chen et al., 2015, 2016).  The limitations of traditional decomposable score-based approaches (K2, dynamic programming) and ILP/CP methods in handling non-decomposable constraints are well-articulated, citing relevant works (Koivisto & Sood, Jaakkola et al., Bartlett & Cussens).  The connection to constraint-based methods with path constraints (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos) and greedy local search with path beliefs (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos) is appropriately mentioned.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an Important Problem:** Incorporating background knowledge into Bayesian network learning is crucial for practical applications. Ancestral constraints are a natural way to represent this knowledge, yet existing methods struggle to handle them efficiently.
* **Novel Approach:**  The combination of the EC tree search space, pruning based on ancestral constraints, and, importantly, the *projection* of these constraints into decomposable forms for the oracle is a clever and novel idea.  This enables the oracle to be effectively utilized, significantly boosting performance.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and demonstrates a clear and substantial advantage (orders of magnitude speedup) over the ILP-based baseline. The systematic variation of network size, data size, and constraint percentage provides a robust assessment.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:** The theorems regarding pruning the EC tree and the conditions for constraint projection provide a sound theoretical basis for the approach.
* **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized.  The technical details are presented clearly, and the notation is consistent.
* **Scalability:** The experiments demonstrate the scalability of the approach up to 20 variables, which is competitive with state-of-the-art methods.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of MaxSAT Reduction:** While the idea of projecting constraints to ordering constraints is powerful, the reduction to MaxSAT seems complex and potentially computationally expensive. The paper acknowledges using an external solver (EVASOLVER) but doesn’t provide detailed runtime analysis of this step. This could be a bottleneck for larger networks.
* **Oracle Dependence:** The performance heavily relies on the efficiency of the chosen oracle (URLEARNING).  The paper acknowledges this but doesn’t thoroughly investigate the sensitivity of the approach to different oracle choices. A more detailed analysis of oracle performance and its impact on the overall runtime would be beneficial.
* **Limited Comparison to Other Approaches:** While the comparison to GOBNILP is valuable, it would be beneficial to compare the proposed approach with other recent advancements in Bayesian network structure learning, particularly those leveraging approximate inference or heuristic search.
* **Real-World Datasets:** The experiments are based on simulated data. Evaluating the approach on real-world datasets with naturally occurring ancestral constraints would strengthen the results and demonstrate its practical applicability.
* **Discussion of Structural Hamming Distance:** The discussion of structural Hamming distance is present, but feels somewhat cursory. More detailed analysis of the types of errors made by the model, and how they relate to the ancestral constraints, would be insightful.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant problem in Bayesian network learning.
* The proposed approach is novel, theoretically sound, and empirically effective.
* The experimental results demonstrate a clear and substantial improvement over existing methods.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented.
* The contributions are likely to be of interest to the NIPS community.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The MaxSAT reduction introduces potential computational complexity.
* The reliance on the oracle requires further investigation of its impact on performance.
* The lack of evaluation on real-world datasets limits the assessment of practical applicability.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian network learning. The proposed approach is innovative and demonstrates impressive performance gains. While there are some minor weaknesses related to the complexity of the MaxSAT reduction and the dependence on the oracle, the strengths of the paper far outweigh these concerns. I believe this paper is a valuable addition to the NIPS conference and **recommend acceptance**.

**Confidence:** 9/10
