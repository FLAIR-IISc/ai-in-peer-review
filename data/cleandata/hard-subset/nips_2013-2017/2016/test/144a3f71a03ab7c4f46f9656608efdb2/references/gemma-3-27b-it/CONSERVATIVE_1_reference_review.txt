This paper addresses the problem of learning Bayesian networks under ancestral constraints, a practically relevant scenario where prior knowledge about causal relationships exists. The authors build upon their previous work using EC trees for optimal structure learning and aim to incorporate these non-decomposable constraints. They propose a method for projecting these constraints into decomposable forms to enhance the efficiency of their approach. 

The paper is generally well-written and presents a clear outline of the problem and proposed solution. The connection to prior work, particularly their earlier work on BN graphs and EC trees, is well-established. The motivation for addressing ancestral constraints is sound; existing methods struggle with their non-decomposable nature. However, despite a seemingly promising direction, my overall assessment is skeptical, largely due to the lack of convincing empirical evidence demonstrating a substantial advantage over existing, well-established methods like ILP, *especially* considering the considerable complexity added to the framework.

The core contribution – incorporating ancestral constraints into the EC tree framework – feels incremental. While the theoretical justification for pruning the search space is reasonable, the practical impact appears limited in the presented experiments. The improvements over ILP are reported as “orders of magnitude,” but this claim is often overstated and, upon closer inspection of the tables, appears to be highly dependent on the specific experimental setup and the effectiveness of the constraint projection. The benefit derived from projected constraints is reported as significant, but the cost of *generating* these constraints (via MaxSAT) is not adequately discussed. The scalability experiments are positive, reaching n=20, but this is not groundbreaking; established ILP solvers can handle problems of comparable size, and the reported gains from constraint projection diminish with larger networks.

A key weakness lies in the evaluation. The experimental setup, while thorough in varying parameters like dataset size and constraint percentage, relies on *simulated* data. Real-world datasets, often exhibiting noise and missing data, would provide a more robust assessment of the method's performance. The structural Hamming distance is reported, but the implications of these distances aren't fully discussed. A small improvement in SHD may not be practically significant if the resulting network remains inaccurate or unreliable. Further, the comparison against ILP is not entirely fair. The authors acknowledge that the heuristic search benefits greatly from projected constraints, whereas ILP also receives these constraints, but the advantage is presented as if the projection is solely beneficial to the EC tree approach.

The theoretical contribution – showing how to project ancestral constraints – is interesting, but the MaxSAT reduction adds substantial complexity. The authors claim it's "sound and complete," but the computational cost of solving a MaxSAT instance, especially for large networks, is a significant concern. The paper doesn’t provide a detailed analysis of the scalability of the MaxSAT solver used, or consider alternative, potentially more efficient, constraint projection methods.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a relevant and challenging problem in Bayesian network learning.
*   Presents a technically sound approach building on prior work.
*   Demonstrates some performance improvement over ILP in specific settings.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Empirical results are not overwhelmingly convincing and rely on simulated data.
*   The complexity of the approach (especially the MaxSAT reduction) is not adequately justified by the reported gains.
*   The advantage over well-established methods like ILP is not substantial enough to warrant a significant shift in practice.
*   Lack of analysis regarding the cost of constraint projection.



**Overall, I lean towards rejection.** While the paper presents a technically correct approach, its practical significance is questionable. The reported benefits are not substantial enough to justify the added complexity, and the reliance on simulated data weakens the conclusions. The paper would benefit from a more thorough evaluation on real-world datasets, a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of constraint projection, and a more convincing demonstration of a clear advantage over existing methods. The authors have identified a worthwhile problem, but the proposed solution, as presented, doesn't represent a significant advance in the field.
