

# Review of "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"

This paper proposes a method for incorporating ancestral constraints (non-decomposable background knowledge about cause-effect relationships) into Bayesian network structure learning. The authors build upon Chen et al.'s EC tree search space and introduce a method to project ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints, which can then be used to "empower" an oracle (a standard structure learning algorithm for decomposable scores).

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with good technical explanations of the EC tree and the constraint projection method. The theoretical contributions, including the algorithms for inferring edge and ordering constraints from ancestral constraints, appear sound. The authors claim their approach is orders-of-magnitude faster than ILP-based methods (specifically GOBNILP) when handling ancestral constraints.

However, as a reviewer who prioritizes methodological rigor and extensive validation, I have significant concerns about the paper's claims. The experimental evaluation is limited in scope and fails to provide sufficient evidence for the strong claims made. The authors only evaluate their method on small networks (up to 20 variables) and a limited set of parameter combinations (n ∈ {12,14,16,18,20}, p ∈ {10%, 20%, 30%}, N ∈ {512, 2048, 8192}). This is insufficient to claim "orders-of-magnitude" improvements across realistic scenarios.

The paper's most significant weakness is the lack of comprehensive comparison with established methods. The authors compare only against GOBNILP, an ILP-based approach, but fail to compare with other state-of-the-art structure learning methods that might handle constraints differently. For example, constraint programming approaches (cited in the paper) have been used for Bayesian network learning with constraints but are not thoroughly evaluated against the proposed method.

The claim that "a relatively small number of ancestral constraints (say 10%-25%) can have a similar impact on the quality of the observed network as increasing the amount of data" is not sufficiently supported. The paper reports structural Hamming distance improvements, but these are only shown for one specific dataset configuration (n=16, p=30%, N=8192) and do not demonstrate consistent behavior across different network structures or constraint sets.

I'm also concerned about the paper's claim that "to our knowledge, the ILP and CP approaches have not been previously evaluated" for learning with ancestral constraints. The authors cite Borboudakis and Tsamardinos [2012, 2013], which specifically uses ancestral constraints for constraint-based learning methods like the PC algorithm. The paper should clarify how their approach differs from these earlier methods.

The paper's theoretical contribution is novel in the specific context of projecting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones for the EC tree search space. However, the significance of this contribution is limited by the narrow scope of the experimental validation. While the method appears theoretically sound, the paper fails to demonstrate that it provides a substantial practical advantage over existing approaches in realistic settings.

I'm also concerned about the paper's scaling claims. The authors state that their approach scales to n=20 variables, which they claim is "comparable to the scalability of modern score-based approaches." However, they don't provide evidence that their method scales better than existing approaches for larger networks (beyond 20 variables), which is essential for real-world applications.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1) A more comprehensive experimental evaluation across a wider range of network sizes, constraint types, and data sets
2) Comparison with more recent state-of-the-art structure learning methods
3) Analysis of how the method handles noisy or incomplete constraints
4) Discussion of limitations when the constraint projection method fails to infer useful decomposable constraints

While the paper presents a theoretically interesting approach to a challenging problem, the lack of thorough empirical validation and limited comparison with existing methods makes it difficult to assess the practical significance of the contribution. The paper makes strong claims about efficiency improvements that are not fully supported by the evidence provided.

The paper addresses an important problem (incorporating background knowledge into Bayesian network learning), but the evidence for the proposed solution's superiority is insufficient. The authors should provide more extensive experimental validation before claiming that their method represents a significant advance over existing approaches.

In conclusion, while the paper has a theoretically sound approach to a meaningful problem, the experimental evidence does not sufficiently support the strong claims made about the method's efficiency and practical utility. The paper would benefit from a more comprehensive evaluation against a wider range of baselines and in more realistic scenarios before being considered for publication at NIPS.
