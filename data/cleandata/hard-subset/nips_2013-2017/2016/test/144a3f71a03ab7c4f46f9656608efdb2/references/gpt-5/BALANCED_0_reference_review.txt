Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles exact Bayesian network (BN) structure learning in the presence of non-decomposable ancestral constraints (X is/is-not an ancestor of Y). Mainstream optimal BN learning methods rely on decomposable scores and constraints (edge presence, parent set size, or order-modular constraints), which makes integrating ancestral constraints challenging. The authors build on the BN-graph and EC-tree search spaces introduced by Chen et al. (2015, 2016) for non-decomposable scores, and show how to (i) prune these search spaces using ancestral constraints and (ii) project non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints that can be exploited by an oracle (URLearning) that optimizes decomposable scores. They also present an algorithm to infer all entailed edge constraints and a MaxSAT reduction to infer a large, cycle-free set of ordering constraints. Empirically, they compare against an ILP baseline (GOBNILP), reporting orders-of-magnitude speedups across several benchmark networks, with further gains from the projection step.

The work relates to: (a) score-based optimal BN learning via dynamic programming and A* over order graphs (Koivisto & Sood 2004; Silander & Myllymäki 2006; Yuan & Malone 2011, 2013), which do not support non-decomposable constraints; (b) ILP/CP approaches that can encode global constraints (Jaakkola et al. 2010; Cussens 2008; Bartlett & Cussens 2015; van Beek & Hoffmann 2015); and (c) use of causal/ancestral path constraints in constraint-based learning and soft priors (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos 2012, 2013). The paper’s novelty is to bring ancestral constraints into exact score-based learning via EC trees and to leverage projected decomposable constraints to strengthen a decomposable-score oracle.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: ancestral constraints are natural for causal prior knowledge and should reduce search space, yet are hard to use in decomposable frameworks.
- Methodological contribution: integrating pruning by ancestral constraints directly into the EC-tree search space; the pruning is sound and, by exhaustive class(P) checking when necessary, complete.
- Projection mechanism: practical and well-motivated approach to infer decomposable edge and ordering constraints from ancestral constraints, with theorems characterizing when negative/positive edges are entailed, and a MaxSAT formulation for order constraints.
- Empirical results: consistent, often dramatic speedups vs. GOBNILP, and clear evidence that projected constraints materially aid search. The easy–hard–easy pattern with increasing constraint density is insightful. Accuracy analysis (SHD) shows constraints can substitute for data to some extent.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Theoretical clarity/precision: Some statements appear inaccurate or need tightening. For example, Theorem 1 as written (“P satisfies A iff its descendants satisfy A”) seems mis-stated given the pruning logic; the intended property is presumably monotonicity of violation (if P violates A, all descendants do). The paper also states the pruning is “sound and complete,” but completeness depends on (potentially exponential) class(P) enumeration.
- Ordering constraints via MaxSAT: The paper frames the MaxSAT encoding as yielding a “maximally large” set of entailed orderings, but the constraints presented are sufficient conditions for entailment (Theorem 4). It is unclear whether the method is complete for inferring all entailed order constraints; if not, “maximally large” needs qualification. More discussion of the tightness and complexity of the encoding would help.
- Scalability and cost of class enumeration: When simple path checks are inconclusive, enumerating all DAGs in a CPDAG’s equivalence class can be exponential. The paper does not quantify how often this occurs or its practical overhead.
- Baselines and fairness: Only ILP (GOBNILP) is evaluated; CP is mentioned but not included. Parent sets are unrestricted, a regime known to favor heuristic/A* approaches relative to ILP; evaluation with bounded in-degree (common in practice) would be informative. Also, experiments are limited to up to 20 variables, which is comparable to prior exact methods but still small; larger cases or more diverse networks would strengthen claims.
- Robustness: Constraints are simulated from ground truth and assumed consistent. Real background knowledge can be noisy or contradictory. The method as presented appears to require consistency; no analysis is provided for inconsistent/soft constraints (contrast Borboudakis & Tsamardinos).
- Presentation: The paper has numerous typos/formatting artifacts, incomplete references, and some notation inconsistencies that hinder readability.

Quality
- Technical soundness: Mostly solid, but needs careful restatement of Theorem 1, explicit proofs (or pointers) for Theorems 2–4, and a clearer treatment of completeness of the MaxSAT projection and the pruning step’s worst-case complexity.
- Empirical soundness: Experiments are thorough within the chosen regime and clearly support the main efficiency claims, but could use broader baselines and robustness analyses.

Clarity
- Overall organization is good, but the manuscript needs significant polishing: fix theorem statements, tighten notation, remove artifacts, and complete citations. Provide pseudocode for edge-inference and the MaxSAT encoding.

Originality
- Integrating ancestral constraints into EC-tree search and systematically projecting them into decomposable constraints for a decomposable-score oracle is novel and useful.

Significance
- Practical significance is moderate: the approach offers a new way to use causal prior knowledge in exact BN learning and shows large speedups vs ILP, though demonstrated scale is limited. The projection idea may be more broadly applicable.

Constructive suggestions
- Correct and clarify Theorem 1 and explicitly state assumptions for all theorems; include proofs or a detailed appendix.
- Precisely characterize the completeness/optimality of the MaxSAT-based ordering projection. If it’s a sufficient-only scheme, temper claims and quantify the gap empirically.
- Quantify the frequency and cost of full class(P) enumeration during pruning; consider using partial enumeration with certificates.
- Add a CP baseline and experiments with bounded in-degree, and report sensitivity to inconsistent/noisy constraints (e.g., via soft penalties or MaxSAT).
- Expand experiments to larger networks where feasible, and include runtime breakdowns (search vs. projection/MaxSAT).
- Clean up writing and references; provide implementation details and, if possible, code.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important gap: using non-decomposable causal knowledge in exact score-based BN learning.
- Methodologically sound idea with clear speedups vs a strong ILP baseline.
- Projection of constraints is a nice, general technique likely useful beyond this paper.

Arguments against acceptance
- Theoretical statements need correction/clarification; completeness claims are not all substantiated.
- Limited scalability and missing baselines; robustness to real-world noisy constraints not addressed.
- Clarity issues and incomplete references.

Recommendation: Weak accept
The paper presents a useful and well-motivated contribution with solid empirical gains, but it needs tightening of theory and presentation. With the suggested revisions, it would be a valuable addition to NeurIPS.
