

# Review of "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"

## Summary
This paper addresses the challenge of incorporating non-decomposable ancestral constraints (e.g., "X is an ancestor of Y") into Bayesian network structure learning. The authors extend the EC tree search space (previously proposed by Chen et al.) to handle these constraints, and develop techniques to project ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints that can be used to empower a standard structure learning oracle. The approach is evaluated against ILP-based methods, showing orders-of-magnitude improvements in efficiency.

The paper builds upon Chen et al.'s work on learning with non-decomposable scores, while addressing a key limitation of existing approaches that cannot handle non-decomposable constraints like ancestral relationships. It makes a clear contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning by providing a practical method for incorporating important background knowledge.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Contribution**: The paper makes a significant technical contribution by providing a sound and complete method for incorporating non-decomposable ancestral constraints into structure learning. The adaptation of the EC tree search space to handle these constraints is well-motivated and technically sound.

2. **Constraint Projection**: The development of methods to project non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints is particularly clever and valuable. The theorems supporting this projection (Theorems 2, 3, and 4) are rigorous and well-explained.

3. **Empirical Evaluation**: The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing, demonstrating consistent orders-of-magnitude improvements over ILP-based approaches across multiple datasets and problem sizes. The results clearly show that constraint projection significantly enhances efficiency, with improvements increasing as problem size grows.

4. **Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, with clear explanations of both the theoretical concepts and the practical implementation. The relationship between ancestral constraints and decomposable constraints is explained particularly well.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Discussion of Constraint Inconsistency**: The paper doesn't address how the method handles inconsistent ancestral constraints (e.g., X→Y and Y→X). This is a significant limitation in practical applications, as background knowledge may contain contradictions. The authors should discuss this limitation and suggest potential approaches to handle it.

2. **Real-World Relevance of Constraints**: The experiments use randomly generated ancestral constraints, but the paper doesn't demonstrate how well these constraints align with real-world domain knowledge. It would strengthen the paper to include a case study using constraints from a specific application domain.

3. **Comparison with Other Non-Decomposable Approaches**: While the paper mentions constraint programming approaches in the related work, it doesn't compare against them in the evaluation. The authors should include a comparison with CP-based methods to better position their contribution.

4. **Trade-off Analysis**: The paper doesn't analyze the trade-off between the computational cost of constraint projection and the efficiency gains in the search. For small problem sizes, the projection might be costly relative to the search improvement.

## Assessment

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical foundations and well-supported empirical results. The authors are careful to acknowledge the limitations of their approach and provide a thorough comparison with relevant baselines. (4.5/5)

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally clear, well-organized, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility. The explanation of complex concepts like constraint projection is particularly effective. (5/5)

**Originality**: The paper addresses a significant gap in the literature regarding non-decomposable constraints in Bayesian network learning. The EC tree adaptation and constraint projection techniques represent a novel contribution that builds upon but extends Chen et al.'s prior work. (4/5)

**Significance**: The results demonstrate substantial practical improvements in efficiency, which could enable the application of optimal Bayesian network learning to larger and more complex problems. The ability to incorporate meaningful background knowledge through ancestral constraints is particularly valuable for practitioners. (4.5/5)

## Recommendation

This paper presents a significant contribution to Bayesian network structure learning. The technical approach is sound, the empirical results are compelling, and the paper is exceptionally well-written. The main weaknesses relate to the limited discussion of constraint inconsistency and the lack of real-world constraint validation.

**Recommendation**: Accept. The paper makes a strong contribution to the field with clear theoretical and empirical advances. With minor revisions addressing the limitations noted above (particularly regarding constraint inconsistency and real-world constraint validation), this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.

**Suggested Revisions**:
1. Add a section discussing how the method handles inconsistent ancestral constraints.
2. Include a case study with real-world ancestral constraints to demonstrate practical applicability.
3. Add a comparison with constraint programming approaches to better position the contribution.
4. Discuss the trade-offs between constraint projection cost and search efficiency gains.
