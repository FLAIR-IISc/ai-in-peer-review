Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles optimal Bayesian network (BN) structure learning in the presence of non-decomposable background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints (X is an ancestor of Y or not). Most exact, score-based approaches assume decomposable scores and thus only support decomposable constraints (edge presence/absence, parent set size) or order-modular constraints. Building on Chen et al. (2015, 2016), the authors use search spaces over DAGs/equivalence classes (BN graph and EC tree) to support non-decomposable scores/constraints. They show how to enforce ancestral constraints by pruning nodes (CPDAGs) in the EC tree and, critically, how to “project” non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints to empower an oracle (URLEARNING) for heuristic guidance: (i) edge constraints via necessary-and-sufficient conditions (Theorems 2–3) using ancestral closure; and (ii) topological ordering constraints via a MaxSAT formulation with soundness guarantees (Theorem 4). Empirically, they compare to ILP (GOBNILP) with ancestral constraints encoded linearly, showing orders-of-magnitude speedups across multiple benchmark subnets, dataset sizes, and degrees of available constraints, and report an easy–hard–easy trend as constraint density varies. The work is positioned relative to ILP/CP formulations for BN learning with non-decomposable constraints (Jaakkola et al.; Bartlett & Cussens; van Beek & Hoffmann), to order-graph methods (Koivisto & Sood; Silander & Myllymäki; Yuan & Malone), and to path/causal priors used in constraint-based and local search methods (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos).

Strengths
- Clear problem motivation: ancestral constraints capture domain causal knowledge, but are non-decomposable and under-served by mainstream exact learners.
- Methodological contribution: adapting EC-tree search to prune CPDAG nodes using ancestral constraints; projecting ancestral constraints to edge and ordering constraints to strengthen decomposable oracles.
- Theorems for inference: necessary-and-sufficient conditions for entailing negative/positive edge constraints from an ancestrally closed set (Theorems 2–3); sound sufficient conditions for entailing a consistent set of order constraints (Theorem 4).
- Empirical effectiveness: consistent, large speedups over ILP; ablation showing the large impact of projection on heuristic quality; sensible scaling trends; accuracy improvements with more constraints and data.
- Practical relevance: uses standard benchmarks; leverages existing tools (URLEARNING, EVASOLVER); approach applies to exact learning and could extend to k-best enumeration.

Weaknesses and questions
- Completeness/complexity details:
  - EC-tree pruning: The paper notes resorting to enumerating all DAGs in a CPDAG when simple tests are inconclusive. This enumeration may be exponential. How often does this occur in practice, and what is the overhead? Are there known graph-theoretic conditions (e.g., using essential graph properties/Meek rules or reachability constraints in CPDAGs) that can avoid enumeration more often?
  - Ordering projection: The MaxSAT encoding is said to find a “maximally large” set of order constraints, but Theorem 4 only provides sufficient conditions. Is the MaxSAT solution guaranteed to be complete (i.e., maximal and sound), or is it a sound but potentially conservative set? Please clarify the exact guarantees and whether maximizing the number of satisfied soft clauses yields maximality under the hard constraints.
  - Ancestral closure: The algorithm to compute the “maximum set” (closed set) of ancestral constraints is omitted. Please include the closure rules, their soundness, and complexity.
- Empirical scope and fairness:
  - Variable counts are modest (n ≤ 20). While in line with prior exact BN studies using BDeu, it would help to include results with bounded parent sizes to allow larger n and to give ILP a fairer setting (ILP often benefits from parent caps), plus a comparison under both capped and uncapped settings.
  - Only ILP (GOBNILP) is evaluated; CP-based approaches (e.g., van Beek & Hoffmann) can also encode non-decomposable constraints. Including CP or justifying its omission would strengthen the empirical case.
  - Provide additional details: GOBNILP/SCIP versions and settings, acyclicity constraints used (cluster constraints vs. cutting planes), time/memory limits, and whether projected constraints were also provided as redundant constraints to ILP (the text suggests yes for Table 1).
  - Ablations: Separate the contributions of edge vs. ordering projections to heuristic strength; report the number of inferred constraints and their effect on node expansions. Also quantify the runtime of the MaxSAT step.
- Clarity and presentation:
  - Some notation/terminology could be tightened: “maximum set” likely means “closure” or “maximally entailed set”; define it precisely. State proof sketches for Theorems 2–4 or provide an appendix.
  - A few typos/formatting glitches and incomplete references appear in the provided copy; please clean up references and footnotes.

Quality
Technically sound overall. The EC-tree pruning is reasonable, and oracle-based A* admissibility is inherited from prior work. The edge entailment theorems are novel and plausibly correct; proofs and complexity would be welcome. The ordering projection is clearly sound but completeness claims need to be precisely stated. Experiments are carefully set up and show strong gains, though broader baselines and ablations would add weight.

Clarity
Generally clear and well organized, with good motivation and linkage to prior art. Include the omitted algorithmic details and proofs, quantify the fallback enumeration cost, and improve reference formatting.

Originality
The integration of ancestral constraints into exact optimal BN learning via EC-tree pruning and the projection of non-decomposable constraints to decomposable edge/order constraints appear novel. Prior work used path constraints in constraint-based or local search; ILP/CP can encode them but were not empirically evaluated for this purpose.

Significance
Useful advance for incorporating domain knowledge into exact BN learning, with substantial empirical gains over a strong ILP baseline. The projection idea is likely reusable in other exact/approximate BN learners and related structure discovery tasks.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel, principled framework for ancestral constraints in exact BN learning.
- Strong empirical improvements and clear practical relevance.
- Solid theoretical underpinnings for projected constraints.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing algorithmic/proof details and some ambiguity about ordering projection completeness.
- Limited baselines (no CP) and modest problem sizes; fairness issues without parent size caps.
- Potential exponential fallback (enumerating class(P)) not quantified.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide proofs/appendix, detailed closure algorithm, and complexity analyses.
- Quantify frequency/cost of CPDAG enumeration; explore stronger CPDAG-level tests.
- Add CP baseline or justify omission; include parent set caps to broaden scale and fairness.
- Ablate edge vs. ordering projections; report MaxSAT overhead and constraint counts.
- Clean up references/formatting.

Recommendation: Weak accept
Confidence: High in assessment of contributions; medium in empirical generality pending additional baselines/ablations.
