**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data when provided with background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints (e.g., X is an ancestor of Y, or X is not an ancestor of Y). Such constraints are non-decomposable, making them incompatible with many efficient score-based learning algorithms that rely on score decomposability. The authors build upon the A*-based search framework of Chen et al. (2015, 2016), which navigates a search space of equivalence classes (the EC tree) using an oracle for a heuristic. The main contributions are: (1) a method to prune the EC tree search space by checking the consistency of nodes (CPDAGs) with the given ancestral constraints, and (2) a novel technique to "project" the non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints. These projected constraints are then used to "empower" the oracle, leading to a more informed heuristic and a more efficient search. The proposed method is empirically evaluated against an integer linear programming (ILP) approach, demonstrating orders-of-magnitude speedups.

**Strengths**

1.  The problem of incorporating non-decomposable background knowledge, specifically ancestral relations, into optimal Bayesian network structure learning is both important and challenging. The paper offers a novel and practical solution.
2.  The idea of inferring decomposable constraints from non-decomposable ones to improve the heuristic of an A*-based search is clever and appears effective. The reduction to MaxSAT for inferring ordering constraints is a particularly interesting contribution.
3.  The empirical results are strong, showing a significant performance advantage over a state-of-the-art ILP-based system (GOBNILP) on a variety of benchmark networks. The experiments also effectively demonstrate how background knowledge can improve model accuracy.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the promising core ideas, the paper suffers from a significant number of flaws in its presentation, technical detail, and experimental rigor. These issues, taken together, undermine the quality of the submission and suggest it is not yet ready for publication at NIPS.

1.  **Lack of Technical Detail and Rigor:**
    *   Several key theorems (Theorems 1, 2, 3, and 4) are stated without proof or even a proof sketch. For a paper proposing a new theoretical framework, this is a major omission. These results are central to the correctness of the pruning and projection methods, and their validity cannot be taken on faith.
    *   In Section 4.3, the method for checking if a CPDAG P satisfies the constraints involves, as a fallback, to "exhaustively enumerate the structures of class(P)". The size of an equivalence class can be exponential in the number of variables. This crucial step could be computationally prohibitive, yet its complexity and practical impact on runtime are not discussed at all. This glosses over a potentially critical bottleneck in the algorithm.
    *   The process for creating a "maximum set of ancestral constraints" is mentioned as a prerequisite for the projection algorithms but the details are "omitted for space." This is a foundational step for the projection method, and its complexity and algorithm should be described, at least briefly, or included in an appendix.
    *   The MaxSAT formulation in Section 5.2 includes a complex "sufficient condition for acyclicity" (constraint #3) without any derivation or justification. The reader is left to wonder where this intricate logical formula comes from, making the method opaque and hard to verify.
    *   The justification in Footnote 4 for inferring an ordering constraint from a negative ancestral constraint is hand-wavy and lacks formal proof.

2.  **Clarity and Presentation Issues:**
    *   The paper contains distracting and unprofessional formatting errors. The phrase "年6月12⽇日星期五 11 16" appears randomly in the middle of Section 3. The references are poorly formatted, with extraneous characters like `.,? \Q2015\E,`. This indicates a lack of careful proofreading.
    *   The writing is often imprecise. The repeated use of the colloquial phrase "empower the oracle" is less formal than would be expected. Footnote 10 provides a confusing example: "DAG X → Y → Z expresses the same ancestral relations, after adding edge X → Z." Adding the edge X->Z does not change the ancestral relations, but it does change the DAG. The intended point is unclear.
    *   The preliminary section rushes through a large amount of prior work (K2, order graphs, DP, ILP). While space is limited, the description is too dense for a non-specialist to follow comfortably.

3.  **Experimental Evaluation Concerns:**
    *   The choice of baselines is limited. The introduction mentions Constraint Programming (CP) as another viable approach, but no comparison is made, limiting the scope of the empirical claims. Only the ILP-based GOBNILP is used.
    *   The generation of constraints is based on random sampling. While this provides a controlled experimental setup, it may not reflect how background knowledge is available in real-world applications, which is often structured and non-uniform. The robustness of the method to different patterns of background knowledge is therefore unevaluated.
    *   The paper notes that for ILP, the projected constraints offer "consistent improvements" for n=14, which seems to contradict the earlier suggestion that they add "mild overhead" and are merely "redundant constraints". This inconsistency is not adequately explained.

**Decision**

I recommend **rejection**.

While the paper tackles an important problem with a novel and promising approach, the execution is flawed. The accumulation of missing proofs, glossed-over technical complexities, careless formatting errors, and minor experimental limitations prevents me from recommending acceptance. The work is incomplete in its current form. Key algorithmic components are either not detailed sufficiently (e.g., handling large equivalence classes, maximizing the ancestral set) or not proven correct (Theorems 1-4). For a paper to be accepted at NIPS, it must meet a high standard of technical rigor and clarity, which this manuscript unfortunately does not. The core ideas are valuable, and I would encourage the authors to resubmit after thoroughly addressing these significant issues.
