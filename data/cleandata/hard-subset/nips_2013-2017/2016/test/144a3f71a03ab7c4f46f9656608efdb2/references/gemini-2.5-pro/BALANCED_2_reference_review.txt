This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data when given background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints (e.g., X must be an ancestor of Y, or X cannot be an ancestor of Y). The authors correctly identify that such constraints are non-decomposable, which prevents their direct use in mainstream optimal structure learning algorithms (e.g., dynamic programming or A* search over orderings) that rely on the decomposability of the scoring function.

The proposed solution builds upon the recent framework of Chen et al. (2015, 2016), which uses A* search over a graph of DAGs (or equivalence classes, in the EC tree) to handle non-decomposable scores. This search is guided by an admissible heuristic provided by an "oracle"—an efficient solver for the decomposable case. The core contributions of this paper are twofold: (1) It adapts this EC tree search to handle ancestral constraints by developing a sound pruning strategy for nodes (CPDAGs) that violate the constraints. (2) More significantly, it introduces a novel technique called "constraint projection," which infers decomposable edge and ordering constraints from the given non-decomposable ancestral constraints. These projected constraints are then used to "empower" the oracle, making its heuristic tighter and the overall search dramatically more efficient. The inference of ordering constraints is cleverly formulated as a MaxSAT problem to find a maximal set of consistent orderings. The paper empirically validates the approach against a state-of-the-art integer linear programming (ILP) solver, demonstrating orders-of-magnitude speedups and showing the substantial impact of the proposed constraint projection method.

### Strengths

*   **Significance and Relevance:** The problem is highly relevant to the NIPS community. Learning graphical models from a combination of data and prior knowledge is a central challenge in machine learning. Ancestral constraints are a natural and powerful way to encode causal or domain-specific knowledge, and this paper provides the first efficient, exact algorithm for incorporating them into score-based learning. The results showing that a small number of constraints can be as effective as a large increase in data size underscore the practical importance of this work.

*   **Technical Novelty and Quality:** The main technical contribution—the method for projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints—is both novel and technically sound. The formulation for inferring ordering constraints via MaxSAT is particularly insightful, correctly handling the subtleties that arise from the fact that a single DAG can be consistent with multiple, mutually exclusive orderings. The overall framework provides a clear advance over existing methods. The experiments are thorough, comparing against a strong ILP baseline and including a crucial ablation study that isolates and validates the impact of the projection technique.

*   **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and logically structured. It begins with a good overview of the necessary background, clearly explains the limitations of existing methods, and systematically develops its proposed solution. The use of small, illustrative examples (e.g., in Sections 3 and 5) is effective in building intuition for the reader.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

*   **Details on Constraint Inference:** While the core ideas are clear, some technical details that are important for reproducibility are omitted for space.
    *   In Section 5.1, the paper mentions that a set of rules can be used to efficiently compute the closure of a set of ancestral constraints but does not provide these rules. Including them in an appendix would strengthen the paper.
    *   In Section 5.2, the MaxSAT formulation is described at a high level. A more explicit definition of the variables and a precise statement of the clauses (especially for the acyclicity condition) would be very helpful for readers wishing to implement or build upon this method.

*   **Computational Cost of Projection:** The projection of ordering constraints relies on solving a MaxSAT instance. This pre-processing step could itself be computationally expensive, especially for larger numbers of variables. The paper does not report the time taken by the MaxSAT solver. It would be valuable to include these timings to understand if this step represents a significant fraction of the total runtime and how its cost scales.

*   **Scalability Discussion:** The method is shown to scale to 20 variables, which is competitive for exact score-based learning. However, it would be beneficial to include a more detailed discussion of the scalability bottlenecks. Is the primary limitation the size of the EC tree, the runtime of the oracle calls, or the constraint projection step? Understanding this would provide valuable context and highlight directions for future work.

*   **Practicality of CPDAG Pruning:** In Section 4.3, the method for checking if a CPDAG satisfies the constraints involves, in the worst case, exhaustively enumerating all DAGs in its equivalence class. This can be computationally infeasible. It would strengthen the paper to comment on how often this expensive step is necessary in practice during the search, or if the two efficient initial checks are usually sufficient to make a pruning decision.

*   **Minor Justification:** Theorem 1 is stated without proof or intuition. While it appears correct, a brief justification (e.g., in an appendix) explaining that any DAG extending a descendant CPDAG must also be an extension of a DAG from the parent CPDAG would make the pruning argument more rigorous.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses an important and practical problem in Bayesian network learning.
*   Presents a novel and technically sound method for "projecting" non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones, which is a significant conceptual contribution.
*   The empirical results are comprehensive and compelling, showing orders-of-magnitude improvement over a state-of-the-art ILP approach.
*   The paper is well-written and easy to follow.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   Some implementation details regarding constraint inference are omitted, slightly hindering reproducibility.
*   The computational cost and scalability of the constraint projection pre-processing step are not analyzed.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It presents a high-quality, novel, and significant contribution to the field of learning graphical models. The weaknesses are minor and can be readily addressed, for example, by adding an appendix with the suggested details. This is a solid piece of work that I expect will be of high interest to the NIPS community.
