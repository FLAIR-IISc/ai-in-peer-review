Summary
The paper tackles optimal Bayesian network (BN) structure learning under ancestral (reachability) constraints, which are non-decomposable and hence difficult to integrate into standard exact score-based methods that rely on decomposability. Building on BN graphs and EC trees (search over CPDAGs), the authors (i) prune EC-tree nodes that cannot satisfy given ancestral constraints, (ii) “empower” a decomposable-score oracle (URLEARNING) by projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and topological-order constraints, (iii) give rules and theorems for inferring negative/positive edge constraints from ancestral constraints (via α/β ancestor/descendant sets), and (iv) formulate a MaxSAT problem to infer a maximal set of safe ordering constraints. They empirically compare against GOBNILP (ILP with ancestral constraints encoded linearly) and report orders-of-magnitude speedups, plus an easy–hard–easy trend as the fraction of ancestral constraints increases.

Relation to prior work
The paper connects to dynamic programming/order-graph methods (Koivisto & Sood; Silander & Myllymäki; Yuan & Malone), ILP/CP formulations (Jaakkola et al.; Bartlett & Cussens; van Beek & Hoffmann), and causal/ancestral constraints in constraint-based and greedy local search (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos). The central novelty is adapting EC-tree search with pruning under ancestral constraints, and projecting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones to guide a decomposable-score oracle within a non-decomposable search.

Strengths
- Addresses a practically important form of background knowledge (ancestral constraints) and integrates it into exact structure learning.
- Sound pruning tests for CPDAGs in clear special cases (directed path implies violation of negative constraints; no partially directed path implies violation of positive constraints).
- The projection idea is insightful: use non-decomposable knowledge to generate decomposable edge/order constraints that tighten oracle heuristics.
- Empirical results (as described) suggest significant speedups over ILP, and highlight the value of even modest amounts of background knowledge.

Major concerns
1) Missing/unclear theory and correctness issues
- Theorem 1 (“P satisfies A iff its descendants satisfy A”) as stated is implausible. Satisfaction should be downwards-closed (if P violates A then all descendants do), but not necessarily upwards-closed; a descendant satisfying A does not imply P satisfies A. This needs restatement and proof.
- Theorems 2 and 3 claim iff characterizations for entailed negative/positive edges from a “maximum” closure of ancestral constraints. The “only if” directions are not obvious and no proofs are provided. Even with closure, the necessity seems delicate. This is central to the projection’s correctness and should be proven or carefully weakened (e.g., sufficient-only).
- The ordering MaxSAT encoding mixes ordering and ancestral predicates in a way that appears ad hoc. Constraint (3), the “sufficient condition for acyclicity,” is hard to justify, and “closed set of ordering constraints” is undefined. Theorem 4 only gives sufficient conditions; yet the text suggests the MaxSAT maximizes an entailed set. Provide precise semantics, proofs, and complexity bounds.

2) Scalability and algorithmic details
- Pruning positive constraints by enumerating all DAGs in a CPDAG equivalence class can be exponential. No bounds, heuristics, or empirical measurement of this cost are provided. This seems like a potential bottleneck.
- The interaction with the oracle is underspecified. Exactly how are inferred edge/order constraints passed to URLEARNING? Are they enforced as hard constraints on parent sets and precedence? How are inconsistencies handled?
- The “maximum set of ancestral constraints”/closure is referenced but rules are “omitted for space.” This is a key step; completeness/complexity should be detailed.

3) Experiments and reporting
- Tables and figures referenced (Tables 1–3, Figure 1) are missing; numerical results are not shown. This alone prevents verification of the main empirical claims.
- Only GOBNILP is compared; CP-based approaches are cited as capable but not evaluated. Comparisons to order-based exact methods with precedence constraints (Parviainen & Koivisto) would contextualize gains.
- No ablations isolating the contribution of edge vs ordering projection, or the cost/benefit of CPDAG enumeration.
- Only perfect (noise-free) constraints derived from ground truth are considered. Realistic scenarios include inconsistent/noisy constraints; how robust is the method?
- Small scale (n ≤ 20) and single score (BDeu, ESS=1). No results with MDL/BDs, no parent set size constraints (which affect ILP baselines), and no timing for score precomputation.

4) Presentation and formatting
- Numerous formatting issues: broken references (“? \Q2015\E”), missing bibliographic details, stray text (“年6月12日星期五 11 16”), inconsistent notation (boldface sets vs plain), typos (missing spaces, “th Conference...”).
- Several definitions are imprecise (e.g., “closed set of ordering constraints,” “maximum set” vs “maximal/closure”).
- Footnotes/numbering are inconsistent (e.g., note “3” mid-text).

Minor comments
- Clarify whether “partially directed path” corresponds to standard “possibly directed path” in CPDAGs; cite Chickering for CPDAG reachability conditions.
- Define α(X), β(X) once and keep notation consistent.
- State computational complexity for each step (CPDAG pruning, edge projection, MaxSAT size).
- Provide proofs (in appendix) for all theorems or downgrade claims to sufficient conditions.

Pros
- Timely and relevant problem.
- Conceptually elegant melding of non-decomposable search with decomposable oracles via projection.
- Promising empirical story (if substantiated).

Cons
- Theoretical claims lack proofs and some appear incorrect/overstated.
- Potential exponential subroutines without analysis.
- Incomplete/missing experimental evidence and limited baselines.
- Significant clarity and formatting issues.

Recommendation: Reject
Despite an interesting and potentially impactful idea, the paper in its current form has substantial issues in correctness, missing proofs, incomplete experiments (tables absent), and presentation that prevent proper assessment. I encourage the authors to (i) fix Theorem 1 and rigorously prove the projection theorems, (ii) precisely define and justify the MaxSAT encoding, (iii) quantify the cost of CPDAG enumeration or replace it with a polynomial-time test if possible, and (iv) provide complete, reproducible experiments with stronger baselines and ablations, including robustness to noisy constraints.
