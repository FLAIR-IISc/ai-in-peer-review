Summary
The paper addresses exact Bayesian network (BN) structure learning in the presence of non-decomposable ancestral (path) constraints. Building on the BN graph and EC-tree search spaces of Chen et al. (2015, 2016), the authors propose to prune CPDAG nodes in the EC tree using ancestral constraints and to strengthen the heuristic “oracle” (an optimal learner for decomposable scores) by projecting ancestral constraints onto decomposable edge and ordering constraints. They give rules for inferring entailed ancestral constraints, characterize when negative and positive edge constraints can be inferred (Theorems 2–3), and formulate inference of a maximal consistent set of ordering constraints as a MaxSAT problem with hard clauses for transitivity/acyclicity and soft clauses to include as many orderings as possible. Empirically, they compare against the ILP-based GOBNILP on benchmark networks with simulated data and constraints, and report consistent orders-of-magnitude speedups; they also show that constraint projection substantially improves their search.

Relation to prior work
The paper situates itself against dynamic programming and heuristic search methods requiring decomposable scores, and against ILP/CP approaches that can encode non-decomposable constraints. It connects to path constraints and priors (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos) but focuses on exact learning with hard ancestral constraints. The EC-tree and oracle idea is directly based on Chen et al. (2015, 2016); the contributions are the pruning tests and the projection mechanisms to edge/order constraints.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: ancestral constraints are practically relevant and non-decomposable.
- Methodologically grounded: uses established exact EC-tree/A* framework; pruning with constraints is natural.
- Insightful projection: converting path constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints is a useful idea that can benefit any order- or family-based exact solver.
- Empirical evidence that projection matters; and comparisons with a strong ILP baseline (GOBNILP) under exact learning.
- Theoretical statements (Theorems 2–4) provide conditions for safe inference of constraints from ancestral knowledge.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Ambiguity/possible error in Theorem 1: as stated (“P satisfies A iff its descendants satisfy A”), it appears incorrect because EC-tree nodes are over different variable subsets; if P and A are over the full set, P has no descendants; if not, descendant satisfaction does not imply ancestor satisfaction. The pruning soundness condition needs precise formulation (e.g., monotonicity of satisfaction w.r.t. adding leaves) and a rigorous proof.
- Pruning via enumeration of class(P): when simple path tests are inconclusive, the paper proposes exhaustive enumeration of the equivalence class. This can be exponential and its practical impact is not quantified. There exist graph-theoretic criteria for “possibly directed paths” in CPDAGs that avoid enumeration; these should be used/analyzed instead.
- Projection details are incomplete. The paper assumes converting A to a “maximum set” by adding all entailed ancestral constraints, but the inference rules and complexity are omitted. Theorems 2–3 are plausible, yet proofs and computational complexity are not provided. For MaxSAT, the encoding has a “sufficient condition for acyclicity” over pairs that is nonstandard; soundness and completeness relative to Theorem 4 need tighter justification, and the risk of eliminating valid DAGs by over-restrictive orderings should be addressed empirically.
- Experimental setup favors the proposed method: no indegree cap is used; it is well known that ILP scales poorly without bounded parents, while A*/DP-based oracles benefit from unconstrained parent sets. A fairer comparison would include varying parent bounds, stronger ILP cuts, and CP baselines (van Beek & Hoffmann, 2015). Only hard, noise-free constraints sampled from the true network are considered; real constraints can be inconsistent or noisy. Robustness to incorrect constraints is not evaluated.
- Limited scale: n up to 20 variables is modest and comparable to prior exact methods; the practical significance of orders-of-magnitude speedups at this scale should be tempered.
- Presentation issues: numerous typos/formatting glitches (stray date, footnote markers, incomplete references with “?”), and some definitions could be sharper (e.g., “partially directed path” and the exact tests on CPDAGs).

Quality
The paper builds on solid prior methods and proposes reasonable extensions. However, some theoretical statements are imprecise, key algorithmic details are omitted, and the fallback enumeration step raises complexity concerns. The empirical comparison is suggestive but not fully fair or comprehensive.

Clarity
Overall readable, but the above issues (Theorem 1, CPDAG path tests, missing inference rules, and reference/formatting problems) impede clarity and reproducibility. More precise definitions and proofs are needed.

Originality
Moderate. The main conceptual advances are: (i) integrating ancestral-constraint pruning into the EC-tree and (ii) projecting path constraints into edge and ordering constraints (with a MaxSAT formulation). Both are natural extensions; nonetheless, the projection idea is practically interesting.

Significance
Potentially useful for exact BN learning with background knowledge. Given the limited scale and constrained comparison, the impact is currently moderate. Stronger theoretical development and broader, fairer experiments would increase significance.

Questions for the authors
1. Please restate and prove Theorem 1 precisely. Under what conditions does satisfaction of A propagate along EC-tree expansions without requiring enumeration?
2. Can you replace enumeration of class(P) with necessary/sufficient CPDAG reachability tests for possibly directed paths? What is the worst-case and observed cost of your current test?
3. Provide full details and complexity of the closure to a “maximum” ancestral set. Are Theorems 2–3 complete for inferring all entailed edge constraints?
4. For the MaxSAT encoding: prove that the output set O is sound (does not eliminate any DAG in G(A)); discuss completeness; and report runtime/size of the MaxSAT instances.
5. Evaluate robustness to noisy or inconsistent ancestral constraints (e.g., a small fraction flipped).
6. Include baselines with parent-degree bounds, stronger ILP configurations, and a CP solver.

Pros
- Addresses a relevant non-decomposable constraint class with an exact method.
- Practical projection to decomposable constraints; clear empirical benefit.
- Uses established exact search machinery; plausible theoretical underpinnings.

Cons
- Theorem/algorithmic gaps and potential error in Theorem 1.
- Exponential fallback enumeration not analyzed.
- Experimental comparison likely biased; limited scale; no noise robustness.
- Clarity and reference issues.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The direction is promising and the projection idea is valuable, but the paper needs stronger theoretical precision, clearer algorithms (avoiding enumeration), and more balanced, robust experiments to meet the bar. I encourage a revision addressing the above points.
