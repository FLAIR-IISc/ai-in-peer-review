This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data, subject to background knowledge expressed as ancestral constraints (e.g., variable X must be an ancestor of Y). The authors correctly identify that such constraints are non-decomposable, which poses a significant challenge for mainstream score-based learning algorithms that rely on the decomposability of the scoring function.

The proposed approach builds upon the recently developed A* search framework over the space of equivalence classes (the EC tree) [Chen et al., 2016]. This framework can handle non-decomposable scores and uses an "oracle"—an exact algorithm for problems with decomposable scores—to compute an admissible heuristic for the search. The authors adapt this framework by first introducing a sound method for pruning nodes (CPDAGs) from the EC tree that are inconsistent with the given ancestral constraints. The main and most novel contribution, however, is a method to "project" the non-decomposable ancestral constraints onto a set of decomposable constraints (i.e., edge and topological ordering constraints). These projected constraints are then passed to the oracle, effectively "empowering" it by tightening the heuristic and dramatically improving search efficiency. The paper provides theorems and algorithms for inferring edge constraints and uses a MaxSAT formulation to infer a maximal set of consistent ordering constraints. The approach is empirically evaluated against an Integer Linear Programming (ILP) formulation, which is another exact method capable of handling such constraints. The experiments show that the proposed method is orders of magnitude faster than the ILP baseline.

### Quality and Technical Soundness
The paper presents a complete and technically sound piece of work. The theoretical claims appear correct and are well-supported. Theorem 1 provides the formal justification for the pruning strategy within the EC tree search. Theorems 2 and 3 provide a sound basis for the projection of ancestral constraints onto edge constraints. The reduction to MaxSAT for inferring ordering constraints (justified by Theorem 4) is a clever and appropriate use of existing solver technology to tackle a complex combinatorial inference problem.

The experimental evaluation is rigorous and well-designed. The comparison against a strong baseline (GOBNILP, an ILP solver) is appropriate and effectively demonstrates the superiority of the proposed method. The authors carefully control for variables such as network size, dataset size, and the number of constraints, providing a comprehensive picture of the algorithm's performance. A particularly strong aspect of the evaluation is the ablation study (comparing Table 1 and Table 2) that isolates and quantifies the massive performance gain attributable to the novel constraint projection technique. The paper is also honest about the limitations, implicitly acknowledging the scalability limits (~20 variables) that are characteristic of exact score-based methods.

### Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, and the background material is presented concisely and effectively, making the paper accessible even to readers not deeply familiar with the specific search frameworks of Chen et al. [2015, 2016]. The core idea of projecting non-decomposable constraints to empower a decomposable oracle is explained with intuitive examples before the formal details are presented. The structure logically flows from the problem definition to the proposed solution and its evaluation. The tables and figures are clear and support the paper's claims effectively. While some derivations are omitted for space, the provided information is sufficient for an expert to understand the method's mechanics and reproduce the core ideas.

### Originality
The work presents a significant and novel contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. While the underlying search framework (A* on the EC tree) is based on the authors' prior work, its adaptation and enhancement for handling ancestral constraints are new. The central idea of projecting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones to improve a heuristic search is, to my knowledge, a novel technique in this context. It offers a new principle for integrating complex background knowledge into score-based search. The specific algorithms for this projection, particularly the MaxSAT formulation for ordering constraints, represent a novel combination of techniques applied to this problem. Furthermore, the paper provides what appears to be the first systematic empirical evaluation of ILP-based methods for learning with ancestral constraints, which is a valuable contribution on its own.

### Significance
The results presented are highly significant. Ancestral constraints are a natural and powerful way for experts to encode causal or temporal background knowledge, but their non-decomposable nature has been a major barrier to their use in optimal score-based learning. This paper provides the first method that is both exact and efficient enough for practical use on moderately sized problems. The demonstrated orders-of-magnitude speedup over the ILP alternative is a substantial advancement of the state of the art. The general principle of "empowering an oracle" by projecting constraints could inspire similar approaches for other types of non-decomposable constraints or other problems in graphical model learning. Finally, the empirical result that a small number of ancestral constraints can improve model accuracy as much as a large increase in data size powerfully underscores the practical importance of methods that can effectively leverage domain knowledge. This work is likely to be used by practitioners and built upon by researchers in the field.

### Summary of Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
*   **Novelty:** Proposes a novel and effective method (constraint projection) for incorporating non-decomposable ancestral constraints into an exact score-based learning algorithm.
*   **Significance:** Solves an important practical problem and significantly advances the state of the art, demonstrating orders-of-magnitude speedups over a relevant ILP baseline.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The approach is well-founded in theory, and the claims are supported by strong and thorough empirical evidence.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The work is an incremental, though very significant, development upon the authors' own prior work on the EC tree search framework.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that presents a novel, significant, and technically sound contribution to Bayesian network structure learning. It addresses a practical problem with an elegant solution and demonstrates its effectiveness with compelling empirical results. The strengths far outweigh the minor weakness of its incremental nature.
