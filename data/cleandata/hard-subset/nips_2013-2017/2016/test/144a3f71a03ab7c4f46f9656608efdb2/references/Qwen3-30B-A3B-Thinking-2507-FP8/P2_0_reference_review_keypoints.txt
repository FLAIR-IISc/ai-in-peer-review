- Tackles learning Bayesian network structure under non-decomposable ancestral constraints (X ancestor/not ancestor of Y), a practically important but hard setting.  
- Proposes using the EC tree search space with sound pruning to incorporate ancestral constraints efficiently.  
- Introduces a projection technique that converts non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints.  
- Empirically shows their method is orders of magnitude faster than ILP-based alternatives (GOBNILP), with speedup growing with problem size.  
- Theoretically grounded: proves key theorems (Theorems 1–4) on constraint projection and EC tree pruning to ensure correctness.  
- Comprehensive experiments vary network size, dataset size, and constraint density; uses standard networks (ALARM, ANDES) and details solver parameters for reproducibility.  
- Demonstrates that a modest number of ancestral constraints (10–25%) can match the benefit of large increases in training data (e.g., 512→8192).  
- Clear exposition overall; effective figures (Figure 1) and logical organization from problem to evaluation.  
- Novel contribution: first systematic efficient approach for ancestral constraints in optimal BN structure learning and an innovative projection method.  
- Significant practical impact: makes background causal knowledge usable and shows large efficiency gains for practitioners.  
- Some sections lack intuition: EC tree explanation, MaxSAT connection, and certain theorem intuitions (e.g., Theorem 3) could be clearer.  
- Needs better contextualization relative to broader constraint-based learning (e.g., Borboudakis & Tsamardinos work) and explicit comparisons or distinctions.  
- Empirical evaluation could explore trade-offs more deeply—particularly how constraint density affects learning quality and unexplained phenomena (e.g., Δ > 0 when p = 1).  
- Minor suggested additions: expand discussion on trade-offs between constraint density and model quality and clarify links to constraint-based methods.  
- Overall recommendation: accept for NIPS; technically strong, novel, empirically compelling, with minor clarity and evaluation extensions suggested.
