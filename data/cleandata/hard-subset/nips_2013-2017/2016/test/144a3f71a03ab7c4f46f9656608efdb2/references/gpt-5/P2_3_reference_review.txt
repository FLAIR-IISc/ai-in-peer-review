Summary
The paper addresses exact Bayesian network (BN) structure learning when users provide non-decomposable background knowledge in the form of ancestral (path) constraints. Main contributions:
- Search space and pruning: Extend the BN-graph/EC-tree shortest-path framework (Chen et al. 2015, 2016) to incorporate ancestral constraints by pruning CPDAG nodes that cannot be extended to any DAG satisfying the constraints. A* search uses an oracle for decomposable scores (URLearning).
- Constraint projection: Devise methods to “project” non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints to strengthen the oracle:
  - Edge constraints: Polynomial-time rules (Theorems 2–3) infer entailed positive/negative edge constraints.
  - Ordering constraints: A MaxSAT formulation identifies a maximally large, cycle-free set of entailed topological ordering constraints (Theorem 4).
- Empirical evaluation: On subgraphs of standard BN benchmarks with synthetic ancestral constraints (derived from ground-truth), their EC-tree approach is orders of magnitude faster than an ILP baseline (GOBNILP). Projection strengthens both methods but benefits EC-tree far more. They also show an easy–hard–easy trend as the proportion of ancestral constraints increases, and that modest amounts of background knowledge can reduce structural error comparably to large data increases.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The high-level idea is sound: using EC trees allows pruning based on global (non-decomposable) constraints, and projected decomposable constraints can safely tighten the oracle. The A* correctness is inherited from prior work.
- Pruning criteria: The paper proposes simple sufficient checks (directed path implies violation of negative constraint; absence of any partially directed path implies violation of positive constraint), otherwise enumerates DAGs in the CPDAG. Soundness for pruning follows from the monotonicity of ancestral-constraint violations under leaf additions; however Theorem 1 is oddly phrased (“P satisfies A iff its descendants satisfy A”), and likely intends: if P violates A then all its descendants do, which justifies pruning. Please clarify and formalize.
- Projection rules: The negative edge inference (Theorem 2) is plausible and likely correct. The positive edge rule (Theorem 3) is stronger and claims an iff characterization; necessity is nontrivial. It would help to include proofs or a sketch; as stated, the condition might be sufficient but not necessary (e.g., other structural properties could force X→Y without excluding all X→...→Y paths via Z). Similarly, the MaxSAT encoding includes a “sufficient condition for acyclicity” clause that is unusual; Theorem 4 asserts sufficiency for entailment but details are sparse. More formal justification would strengthen the paper.
- Practicality: Enumerating all DAGs in a CPDAG when the quick checks are inconclusive can be exponential; the paper should quantify how often this occurs and its runtime impact. Likewise, report MaxSAT overhead versus overall savings.
- Experiments: Positive and fairly extensive across multiple networks, dataset sizes, and constraint proportions. The comparison to GOBNILP is appropriate; both methods receive the same precomputed local scores and (in one condition) the projected constraints. It would be useful to show timeouts/solved counts, and breakdown of time spent in oracle vs. EC-tree expansion vs. MaxSAT.

Clarity
- The overall narrative is clear and well-organized, with motivation, method, projection, and experiments. However, the current draft contains typos/formatting artifacts (random date text, inconsistent notation, missing figure/table contents) and some references are malformed. Definitions of “partially directed path” and the exact CPDAG path criteria should be made precise with citations (e.g., Meek’s rules, Chickering 2002).
- Theorems are stated without proofs; brief proof sketches or an appendix would help, especially for Theorems 3–4.
- Spell out the closure procedure for producing a “maximum” set of ancestral constraints and its complexity.

Originality
- Integrating non-decomposable ancestral constraints into the EC-tree/shortest-path framework and using an oracle for decomposable scores is novel.
- Projecting path constraints into edge and ordering constraints to empower the oracle is a useful idea; the MaxSAT formulation for extracting a maximal safe set of ordering constraints appears new.
- Prior ILP/CP approaches can encode ancestral constraints but have not been empirically evaluated for this purpose in exact BN learning. The comparison is therefore valuable.
- Related work is broadly cited; consider adding more precise references on CPDAG path reachability and on path-constraint learning in constraint-based methods to contrast exactness vs. heuristic approaches.

Significance
- If widely applicable, the approach makes exact BN learning with rich background knowledge far more practical than ILP on moderate-sized problems. The orders-of-magnitude speedups and the demonstrated benefit of even modest constraint sets could influence practice.
- The projection idea is general and could benefit other exact search frameworks (DP/A* on order graphs) and even approximate learners by shrinking candidate sets.
- Current scalability tops out around 20 variables under BDeu with no indegree bound, which is competitive for exact learning but still limiting for some applications. Showing behavior with indegree bounds or larger n would broaden impact.

Pros
- Clear problem motivation; practical importance of ancestral constraints.
- Sound pruning strategy within EC trees; leverages strong oracle heuristics.
- Effective projection to decomposable constraints; clever MaxSAT use.
- Strong empirical gains over ILP; good analysis of how constraints help both speed and accuracy.

Cons
- Some theoretical claims (esp. Theorems 3–4) lack proofs; necessity vs. sufficiency needs clarification.
- Potential exponential subroutines (CPDAG enumeration) not quantified.
- Experiments use constraints derived from the true network; robustness to noisy or inconsistent constraints is not studied.
- Clarity/formatting issues and some missing implementation details.

Questions for authors
- How often do you need to enumerate class(P)? What is the size distribution and runtime impact?
- Are Theorems 3–4 proved in an appendix? If not, can you provide proofs or relax “iff” to “if” where appropriate?
- How does the approach handle inconsistent ancestral constraints? Do you detect and report infeasibility early?
- What is the overhead of the MaxSAT step relative to total runtime? How large are the inferred ordering sets?
- Can you report solved counts/timeouts and memory usage for both methods?

Recommendation
Leaning toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying the theoretical guarantees (especially Theorems 3–4) and tightening the exposition. The paper addresses a relevant gap, proposes thoughtful algorithms, and shows substantial empirical benefits. With improved rigor and clarity, it would be a solid contribution to exact BN learning with background knowledge.
