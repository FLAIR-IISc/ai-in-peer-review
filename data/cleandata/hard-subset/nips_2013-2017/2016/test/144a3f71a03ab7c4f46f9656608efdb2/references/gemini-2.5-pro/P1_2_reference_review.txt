### Summary of the Paper

This paper tackles the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data when provided with background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints (e.g., variable X must be an ancestor of Y, or X must *not* be an ancestor of Y). The authors correctly identify that such constraints are non-decomposable, which makes them incompatible with mainstream optimal structure learning algorithms (e.g., dynamic programming or A* search over variable orderings) that fundamentally rely on the decomposability of the scoring function.

The core contribution is a novel method that adapts a recent search framework based on EC trees (Equivalence Class trees) for this task. The EC tree search space is amenable to non-decomposable scores and constraints. The proposed method works in two main ways:
1.  It prunes nodes (CPDAGs) in the EC tree that are provably inconsistent with the given set of ancestral constraints.
2.  More significantly, it introduces a "projection" technique to infer decomposable constraints (edge presence/absence and ordering constraints) from the non-decomposable ancestral constraints. This is the key insight, as these inferred decomposable constraints can then be passed to the A* search's heuristic oracle (an efficient solver for the decomposable case), making the oracle "smarter" and the overall search dramatically more efficient.

For inferring edge constraints, the paper provides a sound and complete algorithm based on pre-computed ancestor/descendant sets. For inferring topological ordering constraints, which is more subtle, the authors propose an elegant reduction to a MaxSAT problem to find a large, consistent set of such constraints.

The empirical evaluation compares the proposed approach against a state-of-the-art Integer Linear Programming (ILP) solver (GOBNILP), which can also encode ancestral constraints. The results show that the proposed method is orders of magnitude faster, especially when the projection technique is used. The experiments also demonstrate that incorporating even a modest number of ancestral constraints can significantly improve the structural accuracy of the learned network, highlighting the practical value of the work.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**

1.  **High Significance and Practical Relevance:** The paper addresses a well-motivated and important problem. In many real-world domains (e.g., biology, economics), experts possess causal knowledge that is naturally expressed as ancestral relationships. Enabling optimal structure learning algorithms to leverage this knowledge is a significant contribution that pushes the field towards more practical and accurate modeling.
2.  **Novel and Clever Technical Contribution:** The central idea of "projecting" non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones to empower a heuristic oracle is both novel and highly effective. It provides a generalizable template for incorporating other types of non-decomposable knowledge into A*-style search frameworks. The use of MaxSAT to handle the tricky inference of ordering constraints is also a strong and elegant technical contribution.
3.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed and thoroughly executed.
    *   The comparison against ILP, a powerful and relevant baseline for exact learning, is appropriate.
    *   The results clearly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method, with orders-of-magnitude speedups.
    *   The ablation-style comparison (Table 1 vs. Table 2) convincingly shows that the constraint projection technique is the primary driver of the performance gains, validating the paper's core claim.
    *   The inclusion of structural Hamming distance results effectively connects the algorithmic improvements to the ultimate goal of learning more accurate models.
4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. It provides the necessary background on score-based learning, ILP methods, and the more recent BN/EC graph search spaces before clearly motivating and presenting its own contributions. The progression from problem statement to solution and evaluation is logical and compelling.

**Weaknesses (Suggestions for Improvement):**

1.  **Details of Constraint Checking in EC-Tree Search:** In Section 4.3, the paper states that if initial efficient checks on a CPDAG are inconclusive, it will "exhaustively enumerate the structures of class(P), to check if any of them satisfies the given constraints." This could be computationally prohibitive as an equivalence class can contain exponentially many DAGs. It would strengthen the paper to comment on the practical cost of this step. How often does this worst-case scenario occur in the experiments? Is it a practical bottleneck?
2.  **Limited Discussion on Scalability:** The experiments scale up to n=20 variables, which is competitive for exact score-based learning. However, it would be beneficial to explicitly discuss the scalability limitations. The easy-hard-easy pattern observed in Table 3 is interesting and warrants more discussion. It seems likely that the heuristic is very accurate for low `p` (close to the unconstrained problem) and the search space is small for high `p`, while the middle ground is hardest. Contrasting this with the more monotonic improvement for ILP would add valuable insight.
3.  **Dense Presentation of MaxSAT Formulation:** The MaxSAT formulation in Section 5.2 is presented very concisely. While space is a concern, a bit more intuition behind the clauses, particularly the "sufficient condition for acyclicity" (constraint #3), would improve readability for non-experts in this specific area.

### Overall Evaluation and Recommendation

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant and novel contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. It solves a practical problem with a technically sound and creative solution. The central idea of empowering a decomposable oracle with projected constraints is powerful and likely to be influential. The claims are strongly supported by a thorough and convincing experimental evaluation. The weaknesses are minor and mostly relate to clarifying details or expanding discussion, which do not detract from the core quality of the work. The paper is of high quality, originality, and significance, making it a clear fit for NIPS.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
