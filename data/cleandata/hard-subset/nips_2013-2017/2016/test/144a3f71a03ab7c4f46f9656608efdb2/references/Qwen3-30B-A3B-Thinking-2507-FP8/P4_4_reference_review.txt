

# Review of "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"

This paper addresses the problem of learning Bayesian network structures under ancestral constraints, which specify cause-effect relationships (e.g., "X is an ancestor of Y" or "X is not an ancestor of Y"). Such constraints are non-decomposable, meaning they cannot be enforced by standard score-based structure learning approaches that assume decomposable scores. The authors build upon Chen et al.'s (2015, 2016) EC tree search space for learning with non-decomposable scores, extending it to handle ancestral constraints through two main contributions: (1) a sound method for pruning the EC tree based on ancestral constraints, and (2) techniques for projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints that can be used to empower an oracle (a score-based structure learner for decomposable scores). The paper demonstrates that their approach outperforms ILP-based methods by orders of magnitude in experiments.

**Quality:** The paper demonstrates strong technical soundness. The authors provide rigorous theoretical foundations, including four key theorems establishing the soundness and completeness of their constraint projection methods. The experimental section thoroughly evaluates the approach against GOBNILP (an ILP-based system), showing consistent and significant efficiency improvements. The paper correctly identifies and addresses limitations, such as the need to enumerate structures for some CPDAGs when checking ancestral constraints. The experimental design is sound, with controlled variations of network size, dataset size, and constraint proportion, and results are presented with appropriate statistical measures (averaged over 50 datasets). The paper acknowledges the limitations of their approach (e.g., when constraints are inconsistent) and the computational cost of structure enumeration for certain CPDAGs.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The introduction effectively contextualizes the problem by contrasting ancestral constraints with decomposable constraints and explaining why current approaches cannot handle them. Technical concepts (BN graphs, EC trees, ancestral constraints) are clearly explained with helpful examples and figures (Figure 1). The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined. The paper provides sufficient detail for an expert to reproduce the methods, including the MaxSAT encoding for ordering constraints and the pruning procedure for the EC tree. The connection between theoretical contributions and practical implementation is well-maintained throughout.

**Originality:** The paper makes a clear and significant contribution to the literature. While Chen et al. (2015, 2016) introduced the EC tree for learning with non-decomposable scores, this paper is the first to extend this framework to handle ancestral constraints specifically. The authors correctly identify the gap in the literature (ILP and CP approaches have not been evaluated for learning structures with ancestral constraints) and provide a novel solution through constraint projection. The techniques for inferring edge constraints (Theorem 2) and ordering constraints (Theorem 4) represent novel contributions that are not present in previous work. The paper's distinction from related work (e.g., Borboudakis and Tsamardinos' use of ancestral constraints in constraint-based learning) is well-articulated.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a practical problem with significant real-world implications. Background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints is common in many application domains (e.g., biology, medicine), yet current structure learning approaches cannot effectively incorporate such knowledge. The empirical results demonstrate that a relatively small number of ancestral constraints (10-25%) can have a similar impact on model accuracy as increasing training data from 512 to 2048 samples, highlighting the value of background knowledge. The orders-of-magnitude speed improvements over ILP-based approaches make the method more scalable for practical applications. The work advances the state of the art in Bayesian network structure learning by extending it to handle non-decomposable constraints, which has been a recognized limitation in the field.

**Strengths:** The paper's strongest aspects are its technical rigor, clear presentation, and significant empirical results. The constraint projection techniques are both theoretically sound and practically valuable. The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-designed, demonstrating consistent improvements across multiple problem sizes. The connection between the theoretical contributions and practical implementation is seamless.

**Weaknesses:** The paper acknowledges that for some CPDAGs, they must enumerate all structures to check constraints, which could become computationally expensive for larger networks. The experimental section could benefit from more detailed analysis of constraint patterns that lead to particularly challenging cases. While the paper demonstrates scalability to 20 variables, it does not directly compare against the most recent state-of-the-art approaches for unconstrained structure learning (beyond the citations provided).

**Arguments for acceptance:** The paper addresses a well-defined gap in the literature with a technically sound solution that advances the state of the art. The theoretical contributions are novel and well-supported, and the empirical results demonstrate significant practical improvements. The paper is clearly written and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility. The work has clear potential for impact in domains where causal knowledge is available.

**Arguments against acceptance:** The constraint projection method might face scalability challenges with extremely large constraint sets or complex networks, though the paper demonstrates good scalability to 20 variables. The paper does not deeply explore the limitations of constraint projection in cases of inconsistent constraints.

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to Bayesian network structure learning by providing an efficient solution for incorporating ancestral constraints. It is technically sound, clearly presented, and demonstrates substantial empirical improvements over existing approaches. The work advances the field by addressing a meaningful limitation in current structure learning methods and providing a practical solution with strong theoretical foundations. The paper meets all NIPS quality criteria and represents a valuable contribution to the literature.
