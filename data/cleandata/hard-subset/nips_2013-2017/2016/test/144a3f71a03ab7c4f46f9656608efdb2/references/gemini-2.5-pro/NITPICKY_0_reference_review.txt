### NIPS Review

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data, subject to background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints. Standard optimal structure learning algorithms often rely on decomposable scores (e.g., BDeu, MDL), which precludes the direct integration of non-decomposable constraints like ancestral relations. The authors propose a novel approach that builds upon the EC tree search framework, which is designed for non-decomposable scores. The core contribution is a method to "project" non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and topological ordering constraints. These projected constraints are then used to "empower" an oracle (an exact solver for decomposable scores) that provides the heuristic for an A* search over the EC tree. This projection technique is shown to dramatically speed up the search. The ordering constraint projection is cleverly formulated as a MaxSAT problem. The authors provide an empirical evaluation comparing their method to an Integer Linear Programming (ILP) formulation, demonstrating orders-of-magnitude performance improvements.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The paper tackles a significant and practical problem. Incorporating rich prior knowledge, such as cause-and-effect relationships encoded as ancestral constraints, is crucial in many scientific domains, but has been a major challenge for optimal, score-based learning methods. The proposed solution is novel and elegant. The idea of projecting non-decomposable constraints into a decomposable form to tighten the heuristic for a larger search problem is a powerful and potentially generalizable concept.

2.  **Technical Contribution:** The method for inferring edge constraints (Theorems 2 and 3) and, in particular, the reduction to MaxSAT for inferring a consistent set of topological ordering constraints are solid technical contributions. This demonstrates a creative combination of techniques from different areas (search, graphical models, SAT solving).

3.  **Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are thorough. The authors compare against a strong and relevant baseline (ILP), which is one of the few other exact methods capable of handling such constraints. The results clearly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed approach, often by several orders of magnitude. The analysis across varying numbers of variables, dataset sizes, and constraint densities provides a comprehensive picture of the algorithm's performance, including the interesting "easy-hard-easy" pattern.

**Weaknesses:**

Despite the promising core ideas, the paper is marred by a substantial number of flaws, ranging from severe formatting errors to a lack of technical clarity and rigor.

1.  **Clarity and Technical Precision:**
    *   **Footnote 4:** The justification for inferring an ordering constraint `Y < X` from an ancestral constraint `X ⊄ Y` is unconvincing and potentially incorrect. The argument states that for any DAG `G` satisfying `X ⊄ Y`, one can add an edge `Y → X` without creating a cycle. While this is true, the conclusion that `G(X ⊄ Y) ⊆ G(Y < X)` does not follow. A DAG `X → Y` trivially satisfies `X ⊄ Y` but is incompatible with the ordering constraint `Y < X`. This seems to be a fundamental flaw in the reasoning presented.
    *   **Theorem 1:** The theorem states that a CPDAG `P` satisfies constraints `A` *iff* its descendants do. The "if" direction (a descendant satisfying `A` implies `P` satisfies `A`) is not obvious and is presented without proof or intuition. A more likely property is that if `P` violates `A`, then all its descendants do, which would be sufficient for pruning but is a weaker statement than the one made.
    *   **MaxSAT Formulation:** The constraints for the MaxSAT problem, particularly constraint (3), are presented without adequate explanation. This "sufficient condition for acyclicity" is complex and central to the correctness of the ordering projection, yet its derivation and intuition are completely omitted. A reader cannot be expected to verify or understand this crucial component based on the information provided.
    *   **Inconsistent Notation:** The paper states "upper case letters X to denote variables and bold-face upper case letters X to denote sets," but then uses `X` to denote the set of all variables (e.g., `∑_{X∈X}`) and `X` as a placeholder for a single variable in the same context. In the ILP objective, the notation `U⊆X\X` is ambiguous and likely a typo for `U⊆X\{X}`. The family score notation `score(XU | D)` is unconventional; `score(X, U | D)` would be clearer.

2.  **Presentation and Formatting:** The manuscript exhibits a notable lack of care in its preparation.
    *   There is a stray line "th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016), Barcelona, Spain." at the end of the introduction.
    *   There is a line of Chinese characters ("年6月12⽇日星期五 11 16") in the middle of Section 3.
    *   The reference list is improperly formatted, containing LaTeX-like artifacts (e.g., `Bartlett and Cussens.,? \Q2015\E, Bartlett and Cussens.,`).
    *   These errors are severe and distracting, suggesting the paper was submitted without proper proofreading.

3.  **Reproducibility:** The description of the experimental setup is too vague for reproducibility. The process of generating sub-networks is described as selecting "random sets of nodes and all their ancestors, up to a connected sub-network of a given size," which is not a precise procedure.

**Recommendation:**

**Reject.**

While the paper presents a novel and significant contribution to Bayesian network structure learning, the current manuscript is not acceptable for publication at NIPS. The combination of potentially incorrect technical claims (especially in Footnote 4), a lack of clarity on key theoretical components (the MaxSAT formulation, Theorem 1), and egregious formatting errors makes the paper fall well below the required standard for quality and clarity. The core ideas are strong, but they must be presented in a correct, clear, and professional manner. I would encourage the authors to thoroughly revise the paper to fix the technical arguments, provide necessary proofs and intuitions for their claims, and meticulously proofread the manuscript before resubmission to a future venue.
