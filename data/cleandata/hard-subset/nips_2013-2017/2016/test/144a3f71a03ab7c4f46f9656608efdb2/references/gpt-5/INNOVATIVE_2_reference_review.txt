Summary
The paper tackles optimal Bayesian network (BN) structure learning under non-decomposable ancestral constraints (positive X ≻ Y meaning a directed path exists; negative X ⊁ Y meaning no such path). Mainstream dynamic programming/order-graph methods assume decomposable scores and thus cannot enforce ancestral/path constraints, while ILP/CP encodings can express them but can be slow. Building on the BN graph and EC tree search spaces (Chen et al. 2015, 2016), the authors show how to:
- Integrate ancestral constraints into EC-tree search via sound pruning at CPDAG nodes: prune a CPDAG if every DAG in its equivalence class violates the constraints (using simple path checks and, if inconclusive, enumeration of the class).
- Project non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints that can be fed to a “decomposable oracle” (URLearning) to tighten A* heuristics: (i) infer entailed edge presence/absence constraints using closure rules over ancestor/descendant sets (Theorems 2–3), and (ii) infer a maximally large set of topological ordering constraints consistent with the ancestral constraints via a MaxSAT formulation with hard clauses (transitivity, necessity, acyclicity) and soft clauses that prefer X<Y when Y ⊁ X (Theorem 4).
Empirically, on subnetworks from ALARM, ANDES, CHILD, CPCS, HEPAR with BDeu scores, they compare against GOBNILP (ILP), with and without projected constraints. Their EC-tree+A* approach is often orders of magnitude faster, benefits substantially from projected constraints, shows an easy–hard–easy pattern as the fraction of ancestral constraints increases, and demonstrates that modest amounts of background knowledge can improve structural accuracy to a degree comparable to substantial data increases.

Strengths
- Novelty and conceptual contribution: The paper cleanly bridges non-decomposable constraints with decomposable-score oracles via EC-tree pruning and constraint projection. The MaxSAT reduction for extracting a large, safe set of ordering constraints from ancestral constraints is particularly elegant.
- Soundness where it matters: Clear theorems justify pruning (Theorem 1) and the safety of projected edge/order constraints (Theorems 2–4). Using these to empower a standard “oracle” is a compelling design that others can adopt.
- Practical impact: Strong empirical speedups versus ILP, especially as n grows, and clear ablations showing that projections are critical to performance. The observation that a small fraction of ancestral constraints can substitute for a large data increase is useful and actionable.
- Relevance: BN structure learning with prior causal/ancestral knowledge is important; this work provides the first detailed, evaluated method for exact learning with such constraints outside ILP/CP pipelines.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- CPDAG pruning test may be exponential: When simple path checks are inconclusive, the method “exhaustively enumerates” class(P), which can be large. While Theorem 1 gives soundness, the paper should clarify how often enumeration is needed in practice, include runtime breakdowns, and ideally replace enumeration by a polynomial-time existence test using known PDAG orientation rules/“possibly directed” path checks or a SAT query.
- Projection details and complexity: The “maximum set of ancestral constraints” closure and the edge-projection algorithm (Theorems 2–3) omit algorithmic details and complexity analysis. Please include the closure rules, proof sketches, and complexity bounds, and discuss how dense/contradictory constraints are handled.
- MaxSAT formulation: Clause (3) labeled “sufficient condition for acyclicity” is nonstandard and needs justification. Clarify why these clauses suffice to guarantee that the selected ordering constraints do not eliminate any DAG in G(A), and discuss whether the solution is maximal or maximum and how ties are handled. Providing a small example illustrating the MaxSAT encoding would improve clarity.
- Experimental scope: Comparisons are only to GOBNILP. It would strengthen the case to include CP-based methods (e.g., van Beek & Hoffmann), and/or order-based DP/A* baselines augmented only with projected constraints (to isolate the benefit of EC-tree pruning). Also, provide ablations for edge-only vs order-only projection.
- Scalability: Results up to n=20 are comparable to prior exact BDeu studies, but a discussion about scalability limits (memory/time of EC-tree search vs ILP) and behavior under degree bounds would be useful.
- Clarity and presentation: The manuscript has numerous typos and formatting issues (broken citations with “?”, stray text like “年6月12日星期五 11 16”, “th Conference on NIPS 2016”). Please clean up references, unify notation (X ≻ Y vs X Y), and include omitted details in an appendix.

Quality
Technically sound at the core, with reasonable theorems and clear algorithmic framing. Some important details are omitted (closure rules, CPDAG pruning without enumeration), but the approach is coherent and validated empirically.

Clarity
Generally well-organized, but marred by citation/formatting issues and missing algorithmic details. With a careful revision and an appendix, it should be clear and reproducible.

Originality
High. The combination of EC-tree pruning under non-decomposable constraints plus projection to empower a decomposable oracle, and the MaxSAT-based extraction of safe ordering constraints, is novel and advances the state of the art.

Significance
High for the BN community. The ideas are likely to be adopted in exact and heuristic BN learners that wish to exploit causal prior knowledge beyond edge/precedence constraints.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, well-motivated novelty bridging two previously disconnected regimes.
- Sound pruning and safety guarantees for projected constraints.
- Large empirical speedups; clear impact of background knowledge on accuracy.
- Practical method that can be integrated with existing oracles.

Cons for acceptance
- Some theoretical/algorithmic details omitted; enumeration step may be a bottleneck.
- Limited baselines and scale; presentation issues need fixing.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper introduces a significant new direction for exact BN learning with non-decomposable constraints, with solid empirical gains. The minor gaps are addressable in revision and do not detract from the core contribution.
