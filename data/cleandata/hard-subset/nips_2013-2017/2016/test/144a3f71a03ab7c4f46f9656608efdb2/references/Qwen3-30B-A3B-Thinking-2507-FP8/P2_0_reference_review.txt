

# Review: Learning Bayesian Networks with Ancestral Constraints

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper addresses a significant challenge in Bayesian network structure learning: incorporating non-decomposable ancestral constraints (e.g., "X is an ancestor of Y" or "X is not an ancestor of Y") into the learning process. The authors recognize that while ancestral constraints are important in practice for expressing causal relationships, they are non-decomposable, making them incompatible with most mainstream approaches that rely on decomposable scoring functions. The paper introduces an efficient approach using the EC tree search space (a refinement of the BN graph) to learn optimal Bayesian networks under ancestral constraints. The key contributions include: (1) a sound method for pruning the EC tree based on ancestral constraints, (2) a technique to project non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints, and (3) empirical demonstration that their approach is orders of magnitude faster than ILP-based alternatives for learning with ancestral constraints.

This work builds on recent advances in non-decomposable scoring (Chen et al., 2015) and extends them to handle a practically important class of constraints. The authors clearly distinguish their approach from prior work on decomposable constraints (e.g., edge constraints) and non-decomposable approaches like ILP.

## Strengths

**Quality:**
The paper is technically sound with clear theoretical foundations. The authors prove key theorems (Theorems 1-4) about constraint projection and EC tree pruning, demonstrating the correctness of their approach. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, with careful experimental design varying network size (n), dataset size (N), and constraint density (p). The results consistently show their approach outperforms ILP-based methods (GOBNILP) by orders of magnitude, with the gap increasing as problem size grows. The authors also provide valuable insights about the impact of ancestral constraints on learning quality, showing that 10-25% of ancestral constraints can have a similar impact on model accuracy as increasing training data size from 512 to 8192.

**Clarity:**
The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The authors effectively use Figure 1 to illustrate the BN graph and EC tree concepts, which helps readers grasp the search space. The distinction between decomposable and non-decomposable constraints is well-explained, and the paper's organization flows logically from problem statement to methodology to evaluation. The paper provides sufficient detail for an expert to reproduce the experiments, including the specific networks used (ALARM, ANDES, etc.), experimental parameters, and the EVASOLVER solver details.

**Originality:**
The paper addresses a meaningful gap in the literature. While prior work has considered decomposable constraints (like edge presence/absence) or non-decomposable constraints via ILP, this is the first systematic approach to efficiently incorporate ancestral constraints (a specific type of non-decomposable constraint) into optimal Bayesian network structure learning. The projection method for transforming ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints is novel and significantly enhances the efficiency of the approach. The paper clearly distinguishes itself from related work, particularly noting that ILP approaches haven't been properly evaluated for this specific problem.

**Significance:**
The problem addressed is highly significant for practical Bayesian network learning, where background knowledge in the form of causal relationships is often available. The demonstrated efficiency gains (orders of magnitude faster) make this approach practically valuable. The paper also provides important insights about the relative impact of background knowledge versus data size, showing that a small number of well-chosen ancestral constraints can be as valuable as substantial increases in training data. This has important implications for real-world applications where collecting more data is expensive or difficult.

## Weaknesses

**Clarity:**
While generally clear, some sections could be improved. The explanation of the EC tree in Section 4.2 could be more intuitive for readers unfamiliar with Markov equivalence classes. The connection between the MaxSAT formulation (Section 5.2) and the practical implementation could be more explicit. Some of the theoretical results (like Theorem 3) are stated without sufficient intuition about why they hold.

**Originality:**
While the core idea is novel, the paper could better contextualize its contribution within the broader landscape of constraint-based learning. The authors cite Borboudakis and Tsamardinos (2012, 2013) for path constraints, but don't sufficiently explain how their approach differs from or improves upon these earlier constraint-based methods.

**Quality:**
The empirical evaluation, while strong, could be more comprehensive. The paper focuses on the efficiency of the approach but doesn't thoroughly analyze the trade-offs between the number of ancestral constraints and learning quality. The paper mentions that "∆ can be greater than 0 when p = 1" but doesn't explore this phenomenon in depth.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. The problem is well-motivated, the approach is theoretically sound and empirically validated, and the results demonstrate substantial practical benefits over existing methods. The paper is technically rigorous, well-written, and addresses an important gap in the literature.

**Quality:** 9/10 - The paper is technically sound with strong theoretical foundations and comprehensive empirical evaluation. The only minor weakness is the limited exploration of the trade-offs between constraint density and learning quality.

**Clarity:** 8/10 - The paper is well-written and well-organized, though some technical aspects could be clarified for broader accessibility.

**Originality:** 9/10 - The paper presents a novel approach to a non-trivial problem that has been largely overlooked in the literature. The constraint projection technique is particularly innovative.

**Significance:** 9/10 - The work has clear practical implications and demonstrates substantial efficiency gains that would be valuable to practitioners. The insights about the relative value of background knowledge versus data size are particularly noteworthy.

## Recommendation

I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS. It makes a clear, significant contribution to Bayesian network structure learning with a well-justified approach that addresses an important practical challenge. The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate substantial advantages over existing methods. The paper is well-written and technically sound, with a clear contribution that advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way.

The authors should consider adding more discussion about the trade-offs between constraint density and learning quality, and potentially clarifying the connection between their approach and constraint-based learning methods. However, these are minor improvements that don't detract from the paper's overall strength.

This paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings, as it addresses a practical problem with significant theoretical and empirical contributions that will be of interest to researchers and practitioners in machine learning and probabilistic graphical models.
