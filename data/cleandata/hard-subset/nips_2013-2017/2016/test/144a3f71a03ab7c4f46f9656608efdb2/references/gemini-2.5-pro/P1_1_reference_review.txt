### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data, subject to background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints (i.e., specifying that a variable X must be, or must not be, an ancestor of another variable Y). The authors correctly identify that such constraints are non-decomposable, which makes them incompatible with many efficient, state-of-the-art optimal structure learning algorithms that rely on decomposable scores (e.g., dynamic programming or A* search over orderings).

The proposed solution builds upon the recent work of Chen et al. (2015, 2016), which uses an A* search over a space of equivalence classes of DAGs (an "EC tree"). This framework uses an "oracle"—an optimal solver for the decomposable case—to compute an admissible heuristic that guides the search. The key contributions of this paper are twofold:

1.  It shows how to directly incorporate ancestral constraints into the EC tree search by developing a sound pruning strategy. A node in the search tree (representing an equivalence class of DAGs) is pruned if no DAG within its class can satisfy the given constraints.

2.  More significantly, it introduces a novel method to "project" the non-decomposable ancestral constraints into a set of decomposable constraints (edge presence/absence and pairwise variable ordering constraints). This is the paper's core technical insight. Inferring edge constraints is handled by logical rules, while the more complex task of inferring a consistent set of ordering constraints is elegantly formulated as a MaxSAT problem. These inferred decomposable constraints "empower" the oracle, allowing it to compute a much tighter heuristic, which dramatically prunes the search space and accelerates the learning process.

The authors provide a strong empirical evaluation, comparing their method to a leading Integer Linear Programming (ILP) based solver (GOBNILP), which can also handle such constraints. The results show that the proposed approach is orders of magnitude faster and scales to larger problems. The experiments also clearly demonstrate the massive performance benefit gained from the constraint projection technique.

### Strengths

1.  **High Significance and Practical Relevance:** The problem of incorporating rich background knowledge into structure learning is of great practical importance. Ancestral constraints are a natural and powerful way to express causal assumptions. This work provides an effective and scalable method for optimally solving this problem, representing a significant advance for practitioners.

2.  **Novel and Elegant Technical Contribution:** The core idea of projecting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones to empower an oracle-guided search is highly innovative. It's a clever way to bridge the gap between the power of non-decomposable background knowledge and the efficiency of algorithms designed for decomposable problems. The use of a MaxSAT formulation to find a maximal consistent set of ordering constraints is particularly sophisticated and well-justified.

3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments are thorough and well-designed.
    *   The comparison against a strong, exact baseline (ILP) clearly establishes the superiority of the proposed method.
    *   The ablation study (comparing performance with and without constraint projection, implicitly in Tables 1 & 2) effectively isolates and validates the impact of their main technical contribution. The orders-of-magnitude speedup is compelling.
    *   The analysis of how performance and accuracy vary with the number of variables, dataset size, and number of constraints provides valuable insights, including the practical benefit of background knowledge for improving model accuracy.

4.  **Exceptional Clarity:** The paper is extremely well-written, organized, and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly articulated, the technical approach is explained logically with helpful examples, and the experimental results are presented in a way that directly supports the paper's claims.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Complexity of Pre-processing:** The constraint projection step, particularly the MaxSAT solving, is a pre-processing step whose complexity is not discussed. While clearly effective, it would be useful to understand how the size of the MaxSAT instance scales with the number of variables and constraints, and whether this step could itself become a bottleneck for much larger problems.

2.  **Details of EC Tree Pruning:** The mechanism for pruning the EC tree (Section 4.3) is somewhat brief. It states that if initial checks are inconclusive, the algorithm resorts to exhaustively enumerating the structures within an equivalence class. This can be exponentially expensive. While the overall performance is excellent, some discussion on how frequently this expensive check is needed in practice would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Generalizability:** The work is tightly focused on ancestral constraints. While this is a valuable contribution in its own right, a brief discussion on whether the "projection" framework could be generalized to other forms of non-decomposable constraints (e.g., constraints on path lengths, graph connectivity, etc.) would enhance the paper's perceived impact.

### Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors

-   In Section 5.1, you state "we shall subsequently assume that a given set of ancestral constraints A will already include all ancestral constraints that can be entailed from it." The details of this entailment process are omitted for space. An appendix detailing these inference rules would be valuable for reproducibility.
-   The "easy-hard-easy" trend reported in Table 3 is interesting. This is a known phenomenon in many constraint satisfaction and search problems. It's great that you highlight it, as it shows a nuanced understanding of the problem's computational profile.
-   Could you provide more intuition on the sufficient condition for acyclicity in the MaxSAT formulation (hard constraint 3)? It seems to be preventing certain pairs of ordering constraints from co-existing unless certain ancestral relations are present. A brief justification would be helpful.

### Overall Evaluation

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed methods are sound, the theoretical claims appear correct, and they are backed by rigorous and convincing experimental results. This is a complete and polished piece of work.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear, well-structured, and a pleasure to read. It serves as an excellent example of how to present complex algorithmic ideas.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While it builds on an existing search framework, the central contribution—projecting non-decomposable constraints to empower a decomposable oracle—is novel and powerful. The specific application of MaxSAT to infer ordering constraints is also a new and clever technique in this context.

**Significance:** The results are highly significant. The paper provides a state-of-the-art exact algorithm for a practical and important problem in Bayesian network learning. The demonstrated orders-of-magnitude speedup over a strong ILP baseline is a substantial advance. The core ideas are likely to inspire further research into handling other types of non-decomposable knowledge in graphical models.

**Recommendation:** Strong Accept. This is an excellent paper that makes a significant and novel contribution to an important area. It is technically sound, clearly written, and convincingly validated.
