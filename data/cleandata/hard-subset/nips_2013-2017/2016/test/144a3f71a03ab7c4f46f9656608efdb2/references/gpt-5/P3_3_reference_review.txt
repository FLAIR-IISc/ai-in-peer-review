Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses exact structure learning of Bayesian networks under non-decomposable background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints (X is/is-not an ancestor of Y). Mainstream exact score-based methods exploit decomposability and operate in order-graph spaces, thus are ill-suited for ancestral constraints. The authors build on the BN-graph and EC-tree search spaces introduced by Chen et al. (2015, 2016), which navigate DAGs/CPDAGs directly and can accommodate non-decomposable scores/constraints via pruning. The core contributions are: (i) a pruning scheme on EC trees to enforce ancestral constraints at the level of CPDAGs; (ii) projection of non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints to “empower” a decomposable oracle (URlearning) used as an A* heuristic; (iii) a simple, efficient procedure for inferring all entailed edge constraints (Theorems 2–3) and a MaxSAT reduction for inferring a large, sound set of ordering constraints (Theorem 4). Experiments on standard BN benchmarks (ALARM, ANDES, CHILD, CPCS54, HEPAR2) show large speedups over an ILP baseline (GOBNILP), especially when projected constraints are supplied.

This relates to prior work as follows: ILP/CP formulations can express path/ancestral constraints (Jaakkola et al. 2010; Cussens 2008; Bartlett & Cussens 2015; van Beek & Hoffmann 2015), but this is the first empirical evaluation focused on ancestral constraints for exact score-based learning and the first integration with EC-tree search. It complements work on constraint-based learning with path constraints and soft priors (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos 2012/2013) by addressing exact score-based optimization.

Strengths
- Technically well-motivated idea of combining a non-decomposable search space (EC tree) with a strong decomposable oracle, and further tightening the oracle with projected constraints.
- Clear articulation of why ancestral constraints are non-decomposable and why order-graph methods cannot handle them directly.
- Edge-constraint inference rules are simple and appear implementable with low overhead; they are likely to be very useful in practice.
- Conservative MaxSAT-based ordering inference is a reasonable, sound way to extract additional heuristic power while avoiding elimination of feasible DAGs.
- Empirical results are compelling: orders-of-magnitude improvements over ILP on multiple datasets and settings; insightful observation of easy–hard–easy behavior as constraint density varies; demonstration that moderate amounts of background knowledge can rival substantial increases in data.
- The problem is practically important and underexplored in the exact learning literature.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Theorem 1 as stated appears incorrect: “In an EC tree, a CPDAG P satisfies A iff its descendants satisfy A.” Satisfaction is not monotone under extension; a descendant can violate a negative ancestral constraint by creating a new path. The pruning rationale only needs the one-way property “if P violates A, all descendants violate A.” Please fix the statement and clarify the proof conditions.
- CPDAG pruning requires, in inconclusive cases, “exhaustively enumerating the structures of class(P).” Classes can be exponential; there is no analysis of how often this happens, nor a mitigation (e.g., SAT-based existence checks for a path in some extension). This could be a practical bottleneck.
- The edge-constraint inference claims (Theorems 2–3) require a “maximum” closure of ancestral constraints via rules whose details are omitted. Completeness and computational complexity of this closure are not provided; the “iff” in Theorem 2 and 3 hinges on the closure being well-defined and computable. Proof sketches or an appendix would be valuable.
- The ordering projection is explicitly sufficient but not necessary (Theorem 4). While this guards correctness, it may miss many useful ordering constraints. An empirical ablation isolating the impact of edge vs ordering projections would help.
- Experimental scope: exact structure learning typically scales to ~20–30 variables with BDeu; results here top at n=20. This is in line with prior exact methods, but the claim of scalability would be stronger with n≥26 and comparisons to A*-based order-graph methods with precedence constraints (e.g., Parviainen & Koivisto 2013) and CP-based solvers.
- It is unclear whether projection computation time (edge closure and MaxSAT) is included in the reported wall-clock times; please clarify and, ideally, report separately.
- Robustness to incorrect/noisy ancestral constraints is not studied; in practice, background knowledge may be imperfect.

Quality
The overall approach is sound, and the experimental protocol is careful in terms of datasets and baselines. However, the likely error in Theorem 1, the omission of key proof details, and the lack of complexity analysis for CPDAG enumeration and constraint closure weaken the theoretical rigor.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written, but there are several typos/formatting artifacts, incomplete sentences, and awkward phrasing (likely due to typesetting). Key definitions are present, but important derivations/proofs are missing. Figures referenced are essential to understanding EC-tree operations and should be self-contained. Please correct Theorem 1 and expand the descriptions of the closure rules and MaxSAT encoding.

Originality
Combining EC-tree search with projection of ancestral constraints to decomposable constraints, including a MaxSAT formulation for safe ordering inference, appears novel. The focus on exact learning with hard ancestral constraints is distinct from prior soft-prior and constraint-based treatments.

Significance
If widely adopted, the method could substantially improve exact BN learning in settings with background knowledge. The edge inference component is especially practical.

Suggestions for improvement
- Correct Theorem 1 and provide clear proofs (or pointers to an appendix) for Theorems 2–4; specify the closure rules and their complexity.
- Replace exhaustive enumeration over class(P) with a SAT/MaxSAT-based feasibility check for existence of a DAG in class(P) satisfying A, or empirically quantify how often enumeration is needed and its cost.
- Provide an ablation: EC-tree only, +edge projection, +ordering projection.
- Include projection time in runtime, and report memory usage.
- Compare also to CP-based approaches and to precedence-constraint methods on order graphs.
- Evaluate robustness to a small fraction of incorrect constraints.

Reasons to accept
- Addresses an important, practical gap (exact learning with ancestral constraints).
- Technically sound core idea and a well-executed system with strong empirical gains.
- Likely to be useful to the community; ideas (projection, MaxSAT) are reusable.

Reasons to reject
- Theorem 1 appears wrong as stated; missing proofs/closure details reduce rigor.
- Potential scalability bottleneck in CPDAG-class enumeration not addressed.
- Experimental scope limited and missing some relevant baselines/ablations.

Overall recommendation
Weak accept. The contribution is novel and useful, with strong empirical results. With corrections to Theorem 1, added proof details, and minor experimental clarifications, this would be a solid addition to the literature on exact BN learning with background knowledge.
