Summary
The paper tackles optimal Bayesian network (BN) structure learning under non-decomposable ancestral constraints (asserting that a variable is/is not an ancestor of another). Existing exact methods either assume decomposable scores (order-graph/DAG DP, A* with decomposable heuristics) and thus cannot handle such constraints directly, or use ILP/CP formulations that in principle allow such constraints but can be slow. Building on Chen et al. (2015, 2016), the authors (1) adapt the EC-tree (CPDAG-based) search space to prune nodes that cannot satisfy ancestral constraints, and (2) “project” non-decomposable ancestral constraints to decomposable constraints: an algorithm to infer entailed edge presence/absence constraints (Theorems 2–3) and a MaxSAT-based method to infer a maximally large, cycle-free set of topological ordering constraints (Theorem 4). These projected constraints “empower” the oracle used for admissible heuristics (URLEARNING), dramatically tightening A* search over the EC tree. Experiments on benchmark networks (ALARM, ANDES, CHILD, CPCS54, HEPAR2) with synthetic data and constraints show large speedups vs. GOBNILP, especially when projections are provided, and show an easy-hard-easy pattern as the proportion of constraints increases.

Relation to prior work: The paper clearly positions itself relative to (i) dynamic programming/A* approaches relying on decomposable scores and constraints, (ii) ILP/MaxSAT formulations (Cussens; Bartlett & Cussens; Jaakkola et al.; van Beek & Hoffmann), and (iii) constraint-based/path-belief priors (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos). The core novelty is bringing non-decomposable ancestral constraints into the EC-tree search with sound pruning and deriving decomposable projections to exploit powerful existing oracles.

Strengths
- Clear, practical problem: learning with hard ancestral constraints is important in causal discovery and knowledge integration.
- Elegant integration: pruning EC-tree nodes using CPDAG-level tests; use of an exact decomposable-oracle heuristic is principled and effective.
- Projection methods: theorems and algorithms to infer edge and ordering constraints from ancestral constraints; MaxSAT encoding is a neat way to maximize safe order constraints.
- Empirical results: consistent orders-of-magnitude speedups over GOBNILP on multiple benchmarks; informative ablations with/without projection; analysis of scalability and effect on SHD.
- Good insights: easy-hard-easy trend with p% constraints; benefit of modest constraints comparable to much more data.

Weaknesses and questions
- Theoretical details omitted: proofs for Theorems 2–4 and the closure algorithm for “maximum” set of ancestral constraints are not provided; complexity analyses are missing. Even brief proof sketches and runtime bounds would strengthen the claims.
- CPDAG feasibility test: when simple path tests are inconclusive, the method “exhaustively enumerates class(P)” to decide satisfiability. This can be exponential and may limit scalability; empirical reporting on how often enumeration is triggered and its cost would help. Are there stronger polynomial-time tests (e.g., orientation propagation rules) that could avoid enumeration more often?
- MaxSAT projection: the constraints for acyclicity are sufficient, not necessary, and Theorem 4 guarantees only sufficiency for entailment. This may leave many entailed ordering constraints unused. Quantify the gap (e.g., size of inferred O vs. maximum possible, and the runtime gain from order vs. edge projections).
- Robustness to noisy/miscalibrated constraints: real background knowledge can be wrong or inconsistent. The paper assumes a consistent, closed set A and does not evaluate robustness to noise, partial contradiction, or soft constraints. Even a small study would add practical relevance.
- Baselines and scope: Only GOBNILP is evaluated; CP-based methods (e.g., van Beek & Hoffmann) or other ILP formulations with path variables and cutting planes might behave differently. Also, comparisons to order-modular or pairwise precedence methods (Parviainen & Koivisto) under what can be expressed would provide context.
- Scale: Experiments go up to n=20; while typical for exact BNSL, it would be good to report memory usage, number of EC nodes expanded, and the cost of projections. The approach likely remains limited to small/medium n.

Quality
- Generally sound and well-motivated. The pruning correctness on EC trees is plausible given that only leaf additions are allowed and cannot “repair” violated ancestral constraints; the formal statement (Theorem 1) would benefit from proof/clarification. Edge projection theorems are reasonable; include proof outlines and complexity. The MaxSAT formulation is conservative; clearly state trade-offs.
- Experiments are carefully set up with multiple networks and controlled factors (n, N, p). However, more diagnostic metrics and ablations (edge vs. order projection contributions, enumeration frequency) would improve the analysis.

Clarity
- The paper is mostly clear and well-organized. Some important steps are “details omitted,” which hurts reproducibility. Include:
  - Algorithmic details for closure of ancestral constraints.
  - Explicit pseudo-code for edge inference and complexity.
  - Definition of “closed set” of order constraints O.
  - Clarify Theorem 1 (“P satisfies A iff its descendants satisfy A”): the second direction is non-obvious due to “existential” satisfaction at P.
- Minor issues: reference formatting glitches, stray characters, and figures referenced but not shown in the text provided.

Originality
- Integrating ancestral constraints into EC-tree-based exact learning and projecting to decomposable constraints for oracle empowerment is, to my knowledge, novel. The MaxSAT reduction for order projections is also a fresh angle. The work builds meaningfully on the authors’ prior EC-tree framework.

Significance
- Advances exact BNSL under practically relevant non-decomposable constraints, with strong empirical improvements over ILP on standard benchmarks. Likely to influence subsequent exact and heuristic approaches that can leverage projections and CPDAG-level pruning. Practical impact depends on availability of accurate constraints and scalability beyond ~20 variables.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide proof sketches and complexity for Theorems 1–4 and the closure procedure.
- Report statistics on CPDAG enumeration frequency and cost; consider adding stronger CPDAG feasibility tests to avoid enumeration.
- Separate the contributions of edge vs. order projections empirically.
- Evaluate robustness to noisy/contradictory constraints (e.g., fraction of constraints flipped).
- Add another baseline (CP or alternative ILP) and discuss limits of each.
- Clean up references and minor textual artifacts.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important and under-explored setting (hard ancestral constraints).
- Methodologically solid, well-integrated with prior exact learning frameworks.
- Strong, consistent empirical gains and useful insights.
- Novel projection techniques with potential broader use.

Arguments against acceptance
- Missing proofs and complexity analyses for key theorems/algorithms.
- Potentially exponential CPDAG enumeration without detailed mitigation.
- Limited robustness evaluation and baselines.
- Modest scale typical of exact methods; practical reach may be constrained.

Recommendation: Weak Accept
Confidence: High on the assessment of novelty and empirical value; medium on theoretical completeness due to omitted proofs.
