- Summary: Addresses learning Bayesian networks with non-decomposable ancestral constraints by pruning the EC tree and projecting constraints into decomposable edge/ordering constraints to enable efficient oracle-based optimization.

- Related work: Positions approach against decomposable-score methods, ILP/CP approaches, and prior EC tree research.

- Strength (Quality): Technically sound with Theorems 1–4 proving pruning method is sound and complete.

- Strength (Quality): Thorough experiments on multiple benchmark networks, variable sizes (n=12–20), dataset sizes (N=512–8192), and constraint proportions (10%–50%).

- Strength (Quality): Demonstrates large efficiency gains from constraint projection and outperforms ILP (GOBNILP) by orders of magnitude.

- Strength (Quality): Acknowledges limitations like needing to enumerate Markov equivalence classes when pruning CPDAGs.

- Strength (Clarity): Paper is clearly written and well-organized with logical flow.

- Strength (Clarity): Key concepts (ancestral constraints, EC trees, constraint projection) are well-explained with examples and figures.

- Strength (Clarity): Connection between ancestral constraints and decomposed edge/ordering constraints is clearly presented.

- Strength (Clarity): Provides implementation details and settings for reproducibility.

- Strength (Originality): Identifies a gap in handling non-decomposable ancestral constraints in optimal BN learning.

- Strength (Originality): Novel EC tree–based approach with constraint projection builds on prior work to address that gap.

- Strength (Originality): MaxSAT encoding for ordering constraints is an innovative technique.

- Strength (Significance): Shows small numbers of ancestral constraints can substitute for more data and improve model quality.

- Strength (Significance): Achieves practical efficiency gains and scales competitively (up to 20 variables).

- Weakness (Quality): Theoretical analysis of constraint projection/MaxSAT lacks a formal proof of optimality or completeness.

- Weakness (Quality): Does not discuss behavior when ancestral constraints are extremely numerous (near n(n−1)/2).

- Weakness (Quality): No empirical comparison with constraint programming approaches is provided.

- Weakness (Clarity): EC tree pruning algorithm (Section 4.3) and CPDAG satisfaction checks need clearer exposition.

- Weakness (Clarity): More detail is needed on how the oracle (URLEARNING) is concretely accelerated by projected constraints.

- Weakness (Originality): Some constraint projection techniques reuse ideas from other constraint contexts; novelty is mainly in application to BNs.

- Weakness (Significance): Lacks discussion on how to elicit ancestral constraints from domain experts in practice.

- Weakness (Significance): Experiments use simulated data/constraints; no real-world dataset with known ancestral constraints is evaluated.

- Arguments for acceptance: Well-motivated, technically sound, significant efficiency gains, novel extension of EC tree framework, thorough experiments, and clear writing.

- Arguments against acceptance: Missing CP comparison, unclear pruning exposition, and insufficient discussion of practical elicitation of constraints.

- Overall recommendation: Strong paper; recommend acceptance.

- Suggestion 1: Clarify Section 4.3 with more detail on CPDAG checks for ancestral constraints when initial tests are inconclusive.

- Suggestion 2: Add a formal proof that the MaxSAT encoding correctly identifies maximal ordering constraints without excluding valid DAGs.

- Suggestion 3: Discuss practical considerations and challenges for eliciting ancestral constraints from domain experts.

- Suggestion 4: Include empirical comparisons with constraint programming approaches to broaden evaluation.

- Suggestion 5: Mention future work directions, e.g., handling more complex non-decomposable constraints beyond ancestral constraints.
