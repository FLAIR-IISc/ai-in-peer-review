This paper addresses the problem of learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints, a task hindered by the non-decomposable nature of these constraints within traditional optimal structure learning frameworks. The authors build upon their previous work [Chen et al., 2015, 2016] which introduced a framework for optimal structure learning with non-decomposable priors, leveraging an oracle capable of handling decomposable scores. The core contribution lies in adapting this framework to incorporate ancestral constraints, and importantly, in inferring decomposable constraints from the ancestral ones to further empower the oracle. The paper proposes pruning the search space (EC tree) based on ancestral constraints and demonstrates through empirical evaluation that this approach, coupled with constraint projection, significantly outperforms existing ILP-based methods.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The motivation is clearly articulated, and the connections to prior work in Bayesian network structure learning (K2 algorithm, dynamic programming, ILP, CP) and constraint-based methods (PC algorithm) are well established. The introduction does a good job of positioning the problem and highlighting the limitations of existing approaches. The technical preliminaries are concise and sufficient for understanding the subsequent sections. The presentation of the EC tree and the proposed adaptation for ancestral constraints is logical and easy to follow. The theoretical results (Theorems 1-4) are clearly stated, although their detailed proofs are omitted, which is acceptable for a conference paper. The experimental setup is well described, and the results are presented clearly in tables with concise explanations.

The strengths of this paper lie in its novelty and technical soundness. The approach of integrating non-decomposable constraints into an existing framework for optimal structure learning is a valuable contribution. The idea of projecting ancestral constraints into decomposable ones to improve the efficiency of the oracle is particularly insightful. The empirical evaluation provides strong evidence of the effectiveness of the proposed approach, demonstrating orders-of-magnitude improvements over ILP-based methods. The scalability analysis, though limited to n=20, is encouraging. The authors are careful to acknowledge the limitations of their approach and the potential for further improvements.

The weaknesses of the paper are relatively minor. The reliance on the heuristic function from URLEARNING introduces a dependency on that algorithm's performance. While the authors mention that this functions as a powerful oracle, a more thorough analysis of the heuristic's impact on the overall performance would be beneficial. The MaxSAT solver for inferring ordering constraints is a potential bottleneck for larger networks, and a more detailed discussion of its scalability would be useful. The experiments focus primarily on simulated data, and evaluation on real-world datasets would strengthen the findings. Furthermore, while the performance gains over ILP are significant, a discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed approach relative to ILP would be valuable. The paper could also benefit from a more detailed discussion of the trade-offs between the accuracy of the learned network (as measured by SHD) and the computational efficiency of the approach. The assumption of BDeu scores with a fixed equivalent sample size may limit the generalizability of the results.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach for integrating non-decomposable constraints into optimal Bayesian network structure learning.
*   Significant empirical improvements over state-of-the-art ILP-based methods.
*   Sound theoretical foundation and clear presentation.
*   Insightful idea of projecting ancestral constraints into decomposable ones.
*   Addresses a challenging problem with practical relevance.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Reliance on a heuristic function (URLEARNING) for the oracle.
*   Scalability of the MaxSAT solver for inferring ordering constraints.
*   Evaluation limited to simulated data.
*   Lack of detailed complexity analysis.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. The proposed approach is novel, technically sound, and empirically validated. The weaknesses are relatively minor and do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above, such as a discussion of the heuristic's impact and the scalability of the MaxSAT solver.
