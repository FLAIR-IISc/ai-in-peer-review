

# Review of "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"

This paper proposes a method for learning Bayesian network structures subject to ancestral constraints, which are non-decomposable and pose challenges for standard structure learning approaches. The authors introduce an approach that utilizes the EC tree search space (from Chen et al., 2015, 2016) and develops techniques to project non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints to empower an oracle that uses decomposable scores.

## Strengths

The paper addresses a genuine challenge in Bayesian network structure learning: incorporating non-decomposable background knowledge. The theoretical framework for projecting ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints appears sound, with well-justified theorems (Theorems 2-4). The EC tree approach is a reasonable adaptation of prior work (Chen et al., 2016) to handle non-decomposable constraints, and the pruning technique for ancestral constraints is logically sound.

The empirical results demonstrate significant speed improvements over ILP-based approaches (GOBNILP) when ancestral constraints are present, which is a promising finding. The authors provide evidence that even a small percentage (10-25%) of ancestral constraints can have a similar impact on model quality as substantially increasing the training data size (from 512 to 8192 samples), highlighting the potential value of incorporating background knowledge.

## Weaknesses

Despite the promising theoretical foundation and empirical results, the paper suffers from significant methodological shortcomings that undermine its claims of superiority:

1. **Insufficient Baseline Comparison**: The paper only compares with GOBNILP, which is a specific ILP approach. The authors fail to compare with other established methods for Bayesian network structure learning with constraints, such as constraint programming (CP) approaches (van Beek and Hoffmann, 2015) or other constraint-based methods (Borboudakis and Tsamardinos, 2012, 2013). Without these comparisons, it's impossible to determine if the claimed "orders-of-magnitude" improvements are relative to an outdated baseline.

2. **Lack of Robust Experimental Validation**: The paper claims "orders-of-magnitude" improvements but provides insufficient detail to verify these claims. The experimental setup lacks critical details: How many different network structures were tested? What was the exact distribution of ancestral constraints across datasets? What were the precise time improvements (not just "orders of magnitude")? The paper doesn't provide error bars or statistical significance testing for the reported improvements.

3. **Overstated Claims**: The paper claims that their approach "is orders-of-magnitude more efficient compared to learning systems based on ILP," but the results in Table 1 only show improvements in some cases. The paper doesn't acknowledge when the method performs poorly or fails to provide consistent improvements, which is essential for a rigorous evaluation.

4. **Limited Scalability Analysis**: The paper demonstrates scalability to 20 variables, but this is still quite limited for modern Bayesian network structure learning (Yuan and Malone, 2013 report results up to 26 variables). The paper doesn't provide a thorough analysis of how the method scales beyond 20 variables, which is crucial for assessing practical utility.

5. **Unrealistic Constraint Assumption**: The paper assumes perfect ancestral constraints, which is highly unrealistic in practice. The authors don't address how their method performs with noisy or incomplete ancestral constraints, which would be critical for real-world applications.

6. **Theoretical Justification Gap**: While the theoretical framework is sound, the paper doesn't provide strong theoretical justification for why their approach should outperform existing methods in practice. The empirical results alone don't establish a clear theoretical advantage.

## Overall Assessment

The paper presents a technically sound approach to a relevant problem in Bayesian network structure learning. However, the claims of "orders-of-magnitude" improvements over established methods are not sufficiently supported by the experimental evidence provided. The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with the most relevant baselines, doesn't provide enough detail to verify the claimed improvements, and makes unrealistic assumptions about the availability of perfect ancestral constraints.

While the theoretical insights are valuable, the paper falls short of the rigorous validation expected for a NIPS submission. The authors should provide more detailed experimental results, compare with additional relevant baselines, and address the practical limitations of their approach (particularly regarding constraint quality).

## Recommendation

The paper has potential but requires substantial revision to meet NIPS standards. I recommend rejection for the following reasons:

Pro:
- Addresses a relevant problem in Bayesian network structure learning
- Theoretical framework for constraint projection is sound
- Demonstrates potential value of ancestral constraints

Con:
- Insufficient comparison with established baselines
- Overstated claims without sufficient empirical evidence
- Unrealistic assumption of perfect ancestral constraints
- Limited scalability analysis
- Lack of statistical rigor in experimental results

The authors should provide a more thorough comparison with relevant methods, validate their claims with additional experiments, and address the practical limitations of their approach before reconsideration. Without these improvements, the paper's contribution is not sufficiently validated to warrant publication at NIPS.
