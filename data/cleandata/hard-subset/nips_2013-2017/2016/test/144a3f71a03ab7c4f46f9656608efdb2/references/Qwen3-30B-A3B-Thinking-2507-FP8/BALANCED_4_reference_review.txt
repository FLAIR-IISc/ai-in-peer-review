

# Review of "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"

This paper addresses an important gap in Bayesian network structure learning: incorporating non-decomposable ancestral constraints into optimal learning frameworks. The authors build upon Chen et al.'s EC tree search space to handle ancestral constraints (non-decomposable constraints specifying ancestor-descendant relationships), and introduce methods to project these constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints that can be used to empower existing oracles. The approach is theoretically grounded with sound theorems and empirically validated against ILP-based methods, demonstrating orders-of-magnitude efficiency improvements.

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Soundness & Quality:** The paper makes a clear, well-justified contribution to a meaningful problem. The theoretical foundations are solid, with carefully constructed theorems (1-4) that provide a complete framework for handling ancestral constraints. The EC tree adaptation for pruning based on ancestral constraints is particularly elegant and well-motivated.

2. **Originality:** The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work. While ILP approaches can technically handle ancestral constraints, the authors are the first to evaluate them for this specific purpose and demonstrate their inefficiency compared to the EC tree approach. The constraint projection method (transforming non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge/ordering constraints) is novel and well-executed.

3. **Significance:** The problem is important for real-world applications where domain knowledge (e.g., causal relationships) is available. The empirical results show that a small number of ancestral constraints (10-25%) can have a similar impact on model accuracy as significantly increasing training data size, highlighting the value of incorporating background knowledge. The efficiency gains (orders of magnitude) make the approach practically valuable.

4. **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The technical concepts (EC trees, ancestral constraints, constraint projection) are clearly explained with helpful examples and figures. The experimental setup is thoroughly described, including details about benchmark networks, constraint generation, and evaluation metrics.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Comparison:** While the paper compares against ILP (GOBNILP), it omits recent approaches that handle non-decomposable constraints, such as constraint programming (CP) methods (e.g., van Beek and Hoffmann, 2015) or newer ILP formulations. A more comprehensive comparison would strengthen the claim of superiority.

2. **Scalability Limits:** The experiments only scale to 20 variables, which, while impressive, doesn't demonstrate the approach's viability for larger networks that are increasingly common in modern applications. The paper notes that their method scales "comparable to modern score-based approaches," but doesn't provide a clear benchmark against the current state of the art in scalability (e.g., recent work by Yuan and Malone).

3. **Constraint Sensitivity:** The paper doesn't address how the approach performs when ancestral constraints are imperfect or incorrect, which is a practical concern. It would be valuable to include experiments showing the impact of constraint errors on the learned network structure.

4. **Theoretical Complexity:** The paper doesn't analyze the computational complexity of the constraint projection algorithms (Section 5), which is important for understanding scalability limits. The MaxSAT-based approach for ordering constraints could become computationally intensive for large numbers of constraints.

**Recommendation:**

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical and practical contributions to Bayesian network structure learning. The core idea of using the EC tree with constraint projection is novel and well-executed, and the empirical results are compelling. While the paper has some limitations in scope (particularly regarding comparisons with newer methods and scalability beyond 20 variables), these are fixable with minor additions.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

1. Add a comparison with constraint programming approaches (e.g., van Beek and Hoffmann, 2015) that also handle non-decomposable constraints.

2. Extend the experiments to larger networks (e.g., 25-30 variables) to better demonstrate scalability against recent state-of-the-art methods.

3. Include experiments on the impact of imperfect ancestral constraints to address practical applicability concerns.

4. Add a brief analysis of the computational complexity of the constraint projection algorithms, particularly the MaxSAT-based approach.

5. In the conclusion, briefly discuss how the approach could be extended to handle other types of non-decomposable constraints beyond ancestral ones.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that advances the field of Bayesian network structure learning. With the suggested improvements, it would be an excellent fit for NIPS. The paper's strengths in theoretical contribution, practical significance, and clear presentation outweigh its current limitations. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
