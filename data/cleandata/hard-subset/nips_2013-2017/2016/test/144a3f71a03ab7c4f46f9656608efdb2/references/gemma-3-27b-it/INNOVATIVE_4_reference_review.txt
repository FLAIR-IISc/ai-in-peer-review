This paper tackles the important problem of learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints, a scenario frequently encountered in practice where domain expertise dictates relationships between variables. The core idea – adapting the recent framework of Chen et al. [2015, 2016] which leverages an oracle for efficient optimal structure learning – is compelling. The authors correctly identify the limitations of standard approaches that rely on decomposable scores and propose a method for incorporating non-decomposable constraints by pruning the search space (EC tree) and projecting constraints to empower the oracle. 

The strength of this work lies in its novelty. While ILP and CP approaches *can* represent ancestral constraints, the authors demonstrate empirically a significant performance improvement over these methods, positioning their approach as a practical solution. The concept of projecting ancestral constraints into decomposable ones to enhance the oracle’s performance is particularly insightful and a strong contribution. This is a significant step forward for dealing with complex prior knowledge in Bayesian network structure learning. The theoretical justification provided (Theorems 1-4) adds to the rigor of the approach.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. The empirical evaluation, while showcasing substantial speedups over ILP, feels somewhat limited in scope. The datasets used are based on established benchmarks, but the simulated ancestral constraints seem somewhat arbitrarily generated. More analysis varying the *complexity* and *density* of the constraints would be beneficial. A more detailed ablation study on the effect of constraint projection, showing how much of the speedup can be attributed solely to the pruning vs. the empowered oracle, is also needed. While the authors mention a 2-hour time limit and 32GB memory limit, details about the specific hardware configurations (beyond the CPU) are missing. Furthermore, the discussion of MaxSAT for inferring ordering constraints feels brief, and the scalability of this component (especially for larger networks) isn’t thoroughly assessed. Finally, the writing, while generally clear, could benefit from tighter editing. Several sentences are unnecessarily verbose.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** This work addresses a practical and challenging problem with a novel approach. The projection of non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones to empower the oracle is a clever and impactful idea.
*   **Performance:**  The empirical results demonstrably show significant speedups over existing methods (ILP) on a non-trivial problem.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The theorems provide a solid foundation for the pruning and constraint projection techniques.
*   **Potential Impact:**  This work offers a practical pathway for incorporating rich prior knowledge into Bayesian network structure learning, which could have broad applications.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Empirical Scope:** The dataset generation and constraint simulation seem somewhat arbitrary and lack extensive exploration.
*   **Ablation Study Needed:** The contribution of constraint projection needs further dissection.
*   **Scalability Concerns:** The scalability of the MaxSAT component for ordering constraints is not fully evaluated.
*   **Writing Quality:** While generally clear, the writing could be more concise.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a promising and novel contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. While the empirical evaluation could be more extensive and the scalability of some components requires further investigation, the core idea is strong, the theoretical foundation is solid, and the performance gains are significant. I believe the novelty and potential impact of this work outweigh its minor weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, specifically requesting a more thorough empirical evaluation with a wider range of constraint complexities and a more detailed analysis of the MaxSAT component’s scalability.
