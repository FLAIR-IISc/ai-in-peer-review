

# Peer Review: "Learning Bayesian Networks with Ancestral Constraints"

This paper addresses a significant challenge in Bayesian network structure learning: incorporating ancestral constraints (specifying ancestor-descendant relationships between variables) into the learning process. Ancestral constraints are non-decomposable, making them difficult to integrate into mainstream structure learning approaches that rely on decomposable scoring functions. The authors propose a novel method using the EC tree search space and develop techniques to project ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints.

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Contribution:** The paper makes a clear and important contribution. The authors provide a sound and complete method for pruning the EC tree based on ancestral constraints (Theorem 1), and develop theoretically grounded techniques for projecting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones (Theorems 2-4). The constraint projection methods are particularly well-motivated, with clear conditions for when edge constraints can be inferred from ancestral constraints.

2. **Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are thorough and well-designed. The authors compare their approach against GOBNILP (an ILP-based method) across multiple benchmark networks (ALARM, ANDES, etc.), varying problem sizes (n=12-20 variables), dataset sizes, and constraint percentages. The results consistently show their method is orders of magnitude faster than GOBNILP, especially as the number of variables increases. For example, at n=20 variables, their approach is 100-1000x faster when projected constraints are used.

3. **Practical Significance:** The paper demonstrates that even a small number of ancestral constraints (10-25%) can have a similar impact on model accuracy as significantly increasing the training data size (from 512 to 8192 examples). This highlights the substantial value of incorporating domain knowledge into Bayesian network learning, particularly when data is limited or expensive to collect.

4. **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. The technical concepts (BN graphs, EC trees, constraint projection) are introduced progressively and explained clearly. The experimental setup and results are presented with sufficient detail for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses and Limitations:**

1. **Narrow Scope:** The paper focuses exclusively on ancestral constraints, while real-world domain knowledge often includes other constraint types (e.g., edge presence/absence, ordering constraints). While the authors show how to project ancestral constraints into edge and ordering constraints, they don't discuss how their approach could handle other non-decomposable constraints directly. This limits the general applicability of their method.

2. **Oracle Dependency:** The approach relies heavily on an oracle (URLEARNING) that solves decomposable scoring problems. The paper assumes the oracle is perfect, but doesn't explore how sensitive their approach is to the quality of the oracle. If the oracle is suboptimal for the projected constraints, how does this affect the final solution? This is particularly relevant since the authors note that heuristic search approaches often scale better than ILP approaches.

3. **Scalability Limits:** While the authors show their method scales to 20 variables (a good achievement), the paper doesn't discuss why it might not scale beyond this point. Modern structure learning methods (e.g., Yuan and Malone, 2013) report results up to 26 variables for BDeu scores. It would be valuable to understand the scalability limits of their approach.

4. **Comparison with Constraint-Based Methods:** The paper mentions constraint-based methods like PC can use ancestral constraints (Borboudakis and Tsamardinos, 2012), but doesn't compare their approach to these methods. A comparison with constraint-based approaches that also incorporate domain knowledge would strengthen the evaluation.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. The technical content is sound, the empirical evaluation is thorough, and the practical implications are clear. The paper addresses an important gap in the literature and provides a method that is both theoretically sound and practically efficient.

The main limitation is the narrow focus on ancestral constraints, but this is a reasonable starting point for addressing non-decomposable constraints. The approach is general enough that it could potentially be extended to other constraint types. The authors have clearly demonstrated the value of their approach through comprehensive experiments.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance of this paper. It makes a solid contribution to the field that is both theoretically sound and practically valuable. The paper is well-written, well-researched, and the experiments provide strong evidence for the claims made.

The paper would benefit from a brief discussion of how their approach might be extended to handle other constraint types, but this is not necessary for acceptance. The current work is already a significant step forward in incorporating non-decomposable constraints into Bayesian network structure learning, with clear practical implications for researchers and practitioners who want to incorporate domain knowledge into their models.

The authors have made a strong case for the efficiency and effectiveness of their approach, and their empirical results provide compelling evidence for the value of incorporating ancestral constraints into structure learning. This work represents an important advance in the field of Bayesian network structure learning.
