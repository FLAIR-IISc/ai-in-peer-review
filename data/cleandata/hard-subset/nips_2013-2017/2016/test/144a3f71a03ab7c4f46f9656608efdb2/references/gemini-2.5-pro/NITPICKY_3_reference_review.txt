### **Review of "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"**

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data, subject to a set of user-provided ancestral constraints. Such constraints, specifying which variables must (or must not) be ancestors of others, are non-decomposable and thus cannot be handled by many state-of-the-art optimal structure learning algorithms that rely on score decomposability. The authors adapt a recent search framework based on EC trees (a canonical representation of equivalence classes of DAGs) that can accommodate non-decomposable scores. The core technical contributions are: (1) a method for pruning the EC tree search space based on the satisfaction of ancestral constraints, and (2) a novel technique to "project" the non-decomposable ancestral constraints into a set of decomposable edge and topological ordering constraints. These projected constraints are then used to "empower" the oracle (a learning algorithm for decomposable scores) that provides the heuristic for the A* search on the EC tree, dramatically improving performance. The proposed method is empirically compared against an Integer Linear Programming (ILP) formulation, demonstrating orders-of-magnitude speedups on a variety of benchmark networks.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Problem Significance:** The paper tackles an important and practical problem. Incorporating background knowledge, particularly causal or temporal information in the form of ancestral constraints, is a common need in many domains. Existing optimal methods are often limited in their ability to handle such expressive, non-decomposable constraints.
2.  **Novel Technical Contribution:** The idea of projecting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones to tighten the heuristic for an A* search is clever and appears highly effective. The reduction to MaxSAT for inferring a consistent set of ordering constraints is particularly novel.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments show a clear and significant performance advantage over the ILP-based approach (GOBNILP), which is a relevant state-of-the-art competitor for exact learning. The reported speedups are substantial, often several orders of magnitude, highlighting the practical utility of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**

Despite the promising contributions, the paper is marred by a significant number of flaws, ranging from severe formatting issues to a lack of technical rigor and clarity in key sections.

1.  **Presentation and Formatting:** The manuscript is in a state unsuitable for publication.
    *   There is a stray conference footer ("th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems...") in the middle of the introduction.
    *   A timestamp in Chinese characters ("年6月12⽇日星期五 11 16") appears on the second page.
    *   The entire reference section is incorrectly formatted (e.g., "Bartlett and Cussens.,? \Q2015\E"), suggesting a BibTeX compilation failure. This makes verifying citations difficult and reflects a lack of care in preparation.
    *   Several footnotes (e.g., footnote 1, 2, 4) contain material that is either part of the related work discussion or a key part of a technical argument. This information should be integrated into the main body of the text for better readability and flow. For example, the justification in footnote 4 is critical for understanding an inference claim and should not be relegated to a footnote.

2.  **Lack of Formalism and Missing Details:** The paper frequently omits crucial details and proofs, undermining its technical soundness.
    *   **Theorems without Proofs:** Theorems 1, 2, 3, and 4 are all stated without proof or even a proof sketch. While some may seem intuitive, their formal justification is essential for a technically sound contribution. Theorem 3, in particular, presents a complex condition that is difficult to parse without an example or a formal proof.
    *   **Glossing over Complexity:** The paper states that checking for constraint satisfaction on a CPDAG may require exhaustively enumerating all DAGs in its equivalence class. This is a potentially exponential step, yet the authors do not discuss its practical impact or how frequently this worst-case scenario is encountered in their experiments. This is a critical performance detail that has been ignored.
    *   **Unsubstantiated Claims:** In Section 5.1, the authors claim they can "efficiently enumerate" the closure of a set of ancestral constraints, but omit details and provide no complexity analysis. In Section 5.2, the MaxSAT formulation for ordering constraints is presented with complex clauses (e.g., condition 3) without sufficient justification for their origin or correctness. The link between the MaxSAT solution and the conditions of Theorem 4 is not explicitly made.

3.  **Clarity and Notation:**
    *   The notation is occasionally confusing. In the ILP formulation in Section 2, `X` is used to denote both a single variable and the set of all variables in the same expression (`∑ X∈X ∑ U⊆X\X`), which is poor practice.
    *   The explanation in footnote 10 regarding structural Hamming distance is confusing and appears incorrect. The statement "DAG X → Y → Z expresses the same ancestral relations, after adding edge X → Z" is false; adding an edge changes the DAG. The intended point—that multiple distinct DAGs can satisfy the same complete set of ancestral constraints—is poorly articulated.

4.  **Experimental Evaluation:**
    *   The process for generating sub-networks for experiments is described vaguely. The properties of these networks (e.g., density) are not reported, making it difficult to assess the generality of the results.
    *   While the comparison to GOBNILP is valuable, the paper misses an opportunity to compare against or at least discuss other relevant approaches, such as the Constraint Programming method of van Beek and Hoffmann [2015], which is cited.
    *   The narrative overemphasizes the superiority of the proposed search. Table 2 shows that without the constraint projection, the ILP can be faster. This suggests that the main contribution to performance is the projection method, not necessarily the underlying EC tree search framework itself. A more nuanced discussion is warranted.

**Recommendation:** Reject.

While the core ideas in this paper are innovative and address a significant problem, the execution is severely lacking. The manuscript in its current form falls well below the high standards of NIPS due to numerous formatting errors, a lack of technical rigor (e.g., missing proofs and complexity analysis), and unclear explanations of key methodological components. The work is promising, but it requires a major revision to address these substantial flaws before it can be considered for publication. The authors should focus on providing formal proofs for their theorems, clarifying the complex parts of their methodology (especially the constraint checking on CPDAGs and the MaxSAT formulation), and thoroughly proofreading and reformatting the manuscript.
