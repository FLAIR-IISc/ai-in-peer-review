**Summary**
This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data, subject to background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints (e.g., variable X must be an ancestor of Y). Such constraints are non-decomposable, rendering them incompatible with mainstream dynamic programming and heuristic search algorithms that rely on score decomposability. The authors propose a solution based on the recently introduced EC tree search space [Chen et al., 2016], which represents Markov equivalence classes of DAGs. Their method uses A* search to find the optimal structure, pruning subtrees that violate the given ancestral constraints. The primary contribution is a technique to "project" the non-decomposable ancestral constraints onto decomposable edge and ordering constraints. These projected constraints are then used to "empower" the oracle (a standard exact learner for decomposable scores) that provides the heuristic for the A* search, significantly improving its efficiency. The authors compare their method to an Integer Linear Programming (ILP) formulation, a standard approach for handling complex constraints, and demonstrate orders-of-magnitude speed improvements on a range of synthetic problems.

**Strengths**
*   **Problem Significance:** The ability to incorporate complex, non-local background knowledge like ancestral relationships is of high practical importance in many domains where expert knowledge is available. The paper addresses a clear limitation of many widely used structure learning algorithms.
*   **Methodological Soundness:** The proposed method appears technically sound. The approach of pruning the EC tree based on constraint satisfaction is valid. The core technical contribution—projecting non-decomposable constraints to decomposable ones to tighten the A* heuristic—is a clever and effective idea.
*   **Clear Empirical Validation:** The paper provides a direct and compelling comparison against a strong and appropriate baseline (ILP via GOBNILP), which is the most established method for optimal learning with such arbitrary constraints. The experiments are well-designed, clearly demonstrating both the performance advantage over ILP and the critical importance of the constraint projection technique for the method's efficiency.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written, clearly motivating the problem and explaining the proposed approach, its connection to prior work, and the experimental setup.

**Weaknesses**
*   **Reliance on a Recent, Unproven Framework:** The entire approach is built upon the BN graph/EC tree framework [Chen et al., 2015, 2016]. While interesting, this is a very new search methodology that has not been widely adopted or vetted by the community. Its properties, limitations, and performance characteristics are not as well-understood as those of decades-old established methods like dynamic programming or ILP. The paper essentially demonstrates a successful application of a new framework, rather than advancing a more established one.
*   **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The evaluation is conducted entirely on synthetic data generated from benchmark networks. This is a common practice but represents an idealized scenario. It remains unclear how the method would perform on real-world data, or how robust it is to noisy or even slightly incorrect ancestral constraints provided by domain experts. Furthermore, the comparison is limited to a single ILP solver. While GOBNILP is a strong baseline, other formalisms like Constraint Programming (CP) can also handle such constraints and a comparison would have strengthened the paper's claims.
*   **Scalability Concerns:** The paper reports results up to 20 variables, which is respectable for an exact learning algorithm. However, this does not represent a significant leap in scalability over existing exact methods for unconstrained problems. The reliance on an external MaxSAT solver for projecting ordering constraints introduces another layer of complexity whose own scaling properties are not analyzed. The runtime contribution of this projection step is not reported, making it difficult to assess potential bottlenecks for larger problems.
*   **Novelty Context:** The novelty lies primarily in the projection technique and its application within the EC tree framework. The ability of ILP/CP to handle arbitrary constraints is well-known, even if not previously benchmarked for this specific constraint type. The core search algorithm is an application of A* on a recently proposed search space. Therefore, the contribution is more of an effective combination and extension of recent ideas rather than a fundamentally new paradigm for structure learning.

**Overall Assessment and Recommendation**
This paper presents a solid piece of engineering, combining a new search framework with a clever heuristic-tightening technique to solve an important problem. The empirical results showing a dramatic speedup over a standard ILP approach are compelling and significant.

However, my enthusiasm is tempered by the reliance on the very recent and relatively unestablished EC tree framework. While the results are strong in the presented context, I am cautious about endorsing a methodology whose foundational principles are not yet widely understood or validated. Established methods like ILP, while slower in this specific setting, are built on general, powerful, and extensively studied solver technology. The presented A* approach is highly specialized.

The work is a valuable contribution, demonstrating that for the specific problem of learning with ancestral constraints, this specialized A* search can outperform a general-purpose ILP solver. It highlights the power of using problem-specific knowledge (projecting constraints) to guide a search algorithm. The paper is well-executed and the results are clear. Despite my reservations about the novelty of the underlying framework, the performance gains and the utility of the projection technique are significant enough to warrant attention.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Addresses a practical and important problem in Bayesian network learning.
2.  The method of projecting non-decomposable constraints to empower a heuristic search is novel and shown to be highly effective.
3.  Presents a rigorous and convincing empirical comparison against a strong, established baseline (ILP), showing significant performance improvements.
4.  The paper is technically sound and clearly written.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
1.  The approach is built on a very recent and not yet established search framework (EC trees), making it difficult to assess its broader applicability and robustness.
2.  The experimental evaluation is limited to synthetic data and a single baseline, leaving questions about real-world performance and comparison to other potential methods (e.g., CP).
3.  The claimed scalability (n=20) is comparable to, not a major advance over, existing exact methods in the literature.

I lean towards acceptance. The paper tackles a well-defined problem with a technically sound and empirically validated solution that convincingly outperforms a standard alternative. While I would have preferred to see this work grounded in a more established search paradigm or more extensively validated, the contribution is sufficiently clear and significant for a conference publication.
