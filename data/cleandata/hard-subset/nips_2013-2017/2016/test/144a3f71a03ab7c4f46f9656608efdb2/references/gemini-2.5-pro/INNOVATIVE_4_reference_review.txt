This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data when given background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints (e.g., X is an ancestor of Y, or X is not an ancestor of Y). This is a challenging and important problem because ancestral constraints are non-decomposable, meaning they cannot be verified locally by examining individual parent sets. This violates the core assumption of most efficient, modern, score-based optimal structure learning algorithms.

The main contribution is a novel method that adapts the A*-based search framework over the EC tree (a search space of Markov equivalence classes) proposed by Chen et al. [2016]. The key insight is not merely to prune the search space, which is a straightforward application of the framework, but to develop a method for "projecting" the non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable ones (i.e., edge and ordering constraints). These inferred decomposable constraints are then fed to the "oracle" (an exact solver for the unconstrained problem) that provides the heuristic for the A* search. This "empowers" the oracle, resulting in a much tighter heuristic and a dramatically more efficient search. The paper presents a sound and complete algorithm for inferring edge constraints and a particularly clever reduction to MaxSAT for inferring a maximal consistent set of ordering constraints.

This work builds directly upon the BN/EC graph search paradigm but represents a significant conceptual advance by showing how to bridge the gap between non-decomposable global constraints and the decomposable-score oracles that power these search methods. It positions itself as a more scalable alternative to Integer Linear Programming (ILP) approaches, which can handle such constraints in principle but have not been thoroughly evaluated for this task.

**Strengths:**

1.  **High Novelty and Boldness:** The central idea of "constraint projection" is highly novel and elegant. Instead of treating the non-decomposable constraints as a mere filter, the authors use them to actively guide the search by making the underlying heuristic more intelligent. This is a powerful concept that could potentially be extended to other types of non-decomposable knowledge. The use of a MaxSAT solver to find an optimal set of entailed ordering constraints is a creative and non-obvious methodological contribution. This is precisely the kind of bold, clever approach that pushes the field forward.

2.  **Significant Practical Impact:** Ancestral constraints are a natural and intuitive way for domain experts to provide causal background knowledge. Enabling optimal structure learning to leverage this knowledge is a valuable contribution. The experiments compellingly show that a small number of such constraints can improve the accuracy of the learned structure as much as a several-fold increase in dataset size, highlighting the practical utility of the method.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The proposed method demonstrates orders-of-magnitude speedups over a state-of-the-art ILP-based system (GOBNILP), which is the most natural and strong baseline for this problem. Furthermore, the ablation study (comparing Tables 1 and 2) clearly isolates and validates the massive performance gain attributable to the core contribution of constraint projection.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The problem is clearly motivated, the proposed approach is well-explained, and the connection between the different components (EC tree search, oracle, constraint projection) is made explicit.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Theoretical Detail:** While the paper presents theorems for the correctness of its constraint projection methods, it does so without proofs or significant intuition, particularly for the more complex MaxSAT formulation for ordering constraints. While this is understandable given space limitations, a more detailed justification (perhaps in an appendix) would have made the work more self-contained and complete. However, this is a minor flaw in an otherwise excellent paper.

2.  **Inherent Scalability Limits:** The approach, being an exact method, is ultimately limited to networks of modest size (around 20 variables in the experiments). This is a characteristic of the entire class of optimal score-based learning algorithms rather than a specific weakness of this work, but it is an important limitation to acknowledge.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Introduces a highly novel and powerful concept—constraint projection—for integrating non-decomposable constraints into score-based search.
*   The methodological contribution, particularly the MaxSAT formulation, is creative and technically sophisticated.
*   Addresses a problem of significant practical importance.
*   Empirically demonstrates a massive performance improvement over the most relevant state-of-the-art baseline.
*   The paper is clear, well-structured, and convincing.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The theoretical justifications could be more detailed. This is a minor point that does not undermine the core contribution or the empirical results.

**Recommendation:**

This is an excellent paper that I strongly recommend for acceptance. It tackles a difficult, important problem with a genuinely novel and powerful idea. The work combines existing frameworks with new techniques in a creative way, leading to a significant advance in the state of the art for exact Bayesian network structure learning. The empirical results are striking and leave little doubt about the effectiveness of the proposed method. This paper prioritizes a bold new direction over methodological minutiae, and succeeds in delivering a significant and impactful contribution.
