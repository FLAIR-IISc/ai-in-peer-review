**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a novel exact algorithm for learning the structure of optimal Bayesian networks from data, subject to a set of ancestral constraints (e.g., variable X must be an ancestor of Y, or X cannot be an ancestor of Y). The core challenge is that such constraints are non-decomposable, rendering them incompatible with mainstream efficient structure learning algorithms that rely on decomposable scores (e.g., dynamic programming over variable orderings). The authors' approach builds upon a recent search framework, the EC tree, which navigates the space of Markov equivalence classes and can accommodate non-decomposable constraints by pruning invalid sub-problems. The main contribution is a method to "project" the non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints. These inferred decomposable constraints are then passed to an "oracle" (an efficient solver for unconstrained optimal BN learning), which provides a significantly tighter heuristic for the A* search over the EC tree. This "empowerment" of the oracle is shown to dramatically speed up the search. The authors formulate the projection of edge constraints via a set of logical rules and the projection of ordering constraints as a MaxSAT problem. The proposed method is empirically evaluated against an Integer Linear Programming (ILP) formulation, demonstrating orders-of-magnitude performance improvements on benchmark datasets.

**Strengths**

1.  **Well-Motivated Problem:** The problem of incorporating background knowledge into structure learning is of significant practical importance. Ancestral constraints are a natural and expressive way to represent causal or temporal prior knowledge, and developing efficient methods to handle them is a valuable research direction.

2.  **Technically Sound and Novel Contribution:** The core idea of projecting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones to tighten the heuristic for a search algorithm is clever and well-executed. This "empowering the oracle" concept is the paper's primary strength. The formalization of this projection, including the reduction to MaxSAT for ordering constraints, is a solid technical contribution.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental evaluation is systematic and compelling. The authors compare their method against ILP, which is the most appropriate established baseline for exact learning with such arbitrary constraints. The reported speedups of several orders of magnitude are significant and clearly demonstrate the efficacy of their proposed technique, particularly the constraint projection method (as evidenced by comparing Tables 1 and 2).

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Reliance on a Recent, Unestablished Framework:** The entire approach is built upon the BN graph and EC tree search spaces, which were introduced by the same group in 2015/2016. While this framework is interesting, it is not yet an established, widely-vetted method in the field. The contribution of this paper is thus an improvement to a very new technique, rather than an adaptation of a more classical, well-understood one. This makes it harder to assess the long-term impact and robustness of the approach, as its foundation is not yet solidified within the community.

2.  **Limited Scope of Baselines:** While the comparison to ILP is correct for an exact method, the evaluation feels narrow. The paper would be strengthened by including comparisons to state-of-the-art approximate methods (e.g., greedy or local search algorithms) adapted to respect ancestral constraints. While the proposed method is exact, practitioners often resort to heuristic methods for scalability. Understanding the trade-offs in speed and solution quality against a strong heuristic baseline would provide a more complete picture of the method's practical utility. The paper cites Borboudakis and Tsamardinos [2013] which uses local search, but does not compare against it.

3.  **Scalability Remains a Concern:** The reported scalability up to 20 variables is respectable but does not represent a dramatic leap for exact score-based learning in general. While it is an improvement over the ILP baseline *for this specific constrained problem*, it's important to recognize that the problem remains computationally hard. The "easy-hard-easy" pattern with an increasing number of constraints suggests the method is most challenged in a regime that may be common in practice (a moderate amount of prior knowledge). It is unclear if this approach paves a path towards solving significantly larger constrained problems.

4.  **Clarity on Pre-computation Cost:** The complexity of the constraint projection step, particularly solving the MaxSAT instance, is not thoroughly analyzed or reported. This pre-computation is critical to the method's performance, and its own computational cost should be discussed as a potential bottleneck, especially as the number of variables and constraints grows.

**Overall Assessment and Recommendation**

This is a solid paper that presents a technically sound and clever method for an important problem. The central idea of empowering a heuristic oracle by projecting non-decomposable constraints is novel and shown to be highly effective. The experimental results clearly demonstrate a significant performance advantage over the most direct competitor, ILP.

However, my enthusiasm is tempered by the reliance on the very recent EC tree framework. The work essentially demonstrates that a new technique can be made much better, but it doesn't fundamentally change our understanding of or ability to use more established classes of algorithms. The baseline comparison, while appropriate, could have been broader to include heuristic methods to better situate the work's practical relevance.

Despite these reservations, the paper's contribution is clear, the problem is significant, and the results are strong. It advances the state-of-the-art for exact learning under ancestral constraints.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a well-motivated and practical problem in structure learning.
*   Presents a novel and effective technique (projecting constraints to empower an A* oracle).
*   Demonstrates substantial, orders-of-magnitude speedups over the standard ILP approach for this problem.
*   The paper is methodologically sound and clearly written.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The method is an enhancement of a very new and not yet widely established search framework (EC tree).
*   The experimental comparison is limited to a single exact baseline (ILP), omitting relevant heuristic approaches.
*   Scalability, while improved, is still limited to a level typical of exact methods (~20 variables).

I lean towards acceptance. The core technical idea is strong and the empirical validation is convincing, justifying its place at NIPS. The work is a valuable contribution to the niche of exact score-based learning with complex constraints.
