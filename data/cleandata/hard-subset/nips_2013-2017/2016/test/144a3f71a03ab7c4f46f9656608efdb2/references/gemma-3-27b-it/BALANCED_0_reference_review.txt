This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints, which are common in real-world applications where domain knowledge exists. The authors tackle the limitations of existing approaches that struggle to incorporate non-decomposable constraints, building upon their prior work [Chen et al., 2015, 2016] which introduced a framework for optimal structure learning with non-decomposable priors. Specifically, they propose a method for incorporating ancestral constraints into the EC tree search space, and demonstrate a technique for projecting these constraints into decomposable forms to enhance search efficiency.

The core idea – leveraging the EC tree framework and projecting constraints – is novel and potentially impactful. Existing methods, like ILP, often struggle with the computational complexity of non-decomposable constraints. The approach of converting non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints to empower the oracle within the EC tree is a clever and well-motivated technique. The theoretical guarantees, particularly Theorem 1 regarding pruning the EC tree, and Theorems 2-4 concerning constraint projection, strengthen the paper. The empirical evaluation, comparing against an ILP-based system (GOBNILP), shows significant performance improvements, demonstrating the practical benefits of the proposed approach. The experimental setup appears robust, considering variations in network size, data quantity, and constraint percentage. The observation of an "easy-hard-easy" trend with increasing constraint percentage is intriguing and provides valuable insight into the problem’s complexity.

However, the paper is not without its weaknesses. While the theoretical results are presented, their connection to practical gains is sometimes implicit. For example, the benefit of projecting ancestral constraints into ordering constraints isn't always clearly quantified in the results. While Tables 1 & 2 clearly demonstrate performance improvement with projected constraints, a more focused ablation study specifically isolating the effect of projected *ordering* constraints versus projected *edge* constraints would be valuable. 

The description of the MaxSAT reduction for inferring ordering constraints (Section 5.2) is somewhat brief and lacks detail, especially regarding the clause construction. A more detailed explanation, perhaps with a small example, would make this section more accessible.  The reliance on EVASOLVER also introduces a dependency on an external tool, and its potential impact on reproducibility is not discussed.  Furthermore, the paper lacks a discussion of the limitations of the approach. For instance, are there scenarios where projecting constraints might *hurt* performance, perhaps by introducing too many constraints and unnecessarily pruning the search space? Are there types of ancestral constraints that are more or less effectively handled by the proposed method?

The presentation could also be improved. While the writing is generally clear, some sections (like 4.3) feel dense and could benefit from more intuitive explanations. Figures 1(a) and 1(b) are somewhat small and could be larger for better clarity.  The extensive referencing, while demonstrating awareness of the literature, sometimes interrupts the flow of the main argument.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of integrating non-decomposable constraints into the EC tree framework, combined with constraint projection, is a novel contribution.
*   **Significance:**  The problem addressed is important, and the performance improvements demonstrated against ILP suggest a significant practical impact.
*   **Soundness:**  The theoretical guarantees provided, particularly regarding EC tree pruning and constraint projection, add to the paper's credibility.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The extensive experimental evaluation demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach across various scenarios.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   **Lack of Detailed Analysis:** The effect of projected ordering constraints is not isolated in the results.
*   **Limited Discussion of Limitations:** The paper does not adequately address potential drawbacks or scenarios where the approach might not be effective.
*   **Presentation:** Some sections are dense and could benefit from clearer explanations.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. The proposed approach is novel, theoretically sound, and empirically validated. While some aspects could be improved in terms of detail and clarity, the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Provide a more detailed explanation of the MaxSAT reduction (Section 5.2), perhaps with a small example.
2.  Conduct an ablation study to isolate the impact of projected edge constraints versus projected ordering constraints.
3.  Discuss the limitations of the approach, including potential scenarios where constraint projection might be detrimental.
4.  Address the reproducibility concerns related to the use of EVASOLVER.
5.  Consider improving the clarity of Section 4.3 and enlarging Figures 1(a) and 1(b).
