## Review of "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of learning Bayesian networks (BNs) when prior knowledge is available in the form of ancestral constraints (specifying ancestor/descendant relationships between variables). The key issue addressed is that standard BN learning algorithms often rely on decomposable scoring functions, which prevent them from directly incorporating these non-decomposable constraints. The authors build upon their previous work utilizing EC trees and an oracle for decomposable scores, adapting this framework to handle ancestral constraints by pruning the search space and, crucially, *projecting* the non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones that can empower the oracle. They demonstrate significant performance gains over existing ILP-based approaches.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an important problem:** Incorporating background knowledge into BN learning is crucial for real-world applications, and ancestral constraints are a natural way to express this knowledge.
* **Novel approach:**  The combination of EC trees, oracle-based learning, and constraint projection is a novel and promising technique for dealing with non-decomposable constraints.  The idea of projecting ancestral constraints into decomposable ones to improve oracle efficiency is particularly clever.
* **Strong empirical results:** The experimental results convincingly demonstrate that the proposed approach is significantly faster than existing ILP-based methods, particularly as the problem size grows.  The scaling experiments are also valuable, showing that the approach can handle networks with 20 variables.  The impact of constraint projection is clearly demonstrated.
* **Sound theoretical foundation:** The theorems presented provide a solid justification for the pruning and projection strategies, ensuring correctness.
* **Clear writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear explanations of the concepts and algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited comparison to other structure learning algorithms:** While the comparison to ILP is strong, it would be beneficial to see a comparison to more traditional score-based structure learning algorithms (e.g., those based on the order graph) even if they cannot directly handle the ancestral constraints.  It's unclear how much performance is lost by *not* using ancestral constraints in those algorithms versus the benefits of the proposed approach.
* **Complexity of implementation:** The approach appears complex to implement, relying on several components (EC trees, oracle, constraint projection, MaxSAT solver). This could be a barrier to adoption. The authors do not discuss the computational cost of the constraint projection step, which may be significant.
* **Sensitivity to constraint quality:** The performance gains appear highly dependent on the accuracy of the provided ancestral constraints.  While this is inherent to incorporating prior knowledge, it would be useful to analyze the robustness of the approach to noisy or inaccurate constraints. The discussion of this is minimal.
* **MaxSAT Solver Dependence:** The performance is contingent on the efficiency of the chosen MaxSAT solver. A discussion of the sensitivity to different solvers, or alternative approaches for inferring ordering constraints, would be valuable.



**Addressing the Review Criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theorems provide theoretical guarantees, and the empirical results are compelling. The approach is complete, although complex. The authors are relatively honest about potential limitations.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally clear, although some sections (e.g., the MaxSAT formulation) are dense and require careful reading. More intuitive explanations of the constraint projection step would be helpful.
* **Originality:** The combination of techniques – EC trees, oracle-based learning, and constraint projection – is novel. The idea of projecting non-decomposable constraints is a significant contribution. The paper adequately references related work, although a more comprehensive comparison to other structure learning algorithms would be beneficial.
* **Significance:** The results are important, as they address a critical challenge in BN learning.  The approach has the potential to be widely used in applications where prior knowledge is available. The significant performance improvements over ILP suggest a substantial advance in the state-of-the-art.



**Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

*  Addresses a significant and challenging problem in BN learning.
*  Presents a novel and theoretically sound approach.
*  Demonstrates strong empirical results, showing significant performance improvements over existing methods.
*  Clear and generally well-written.

**Against Acceptance:**

*  Limited comparison to other standard BN learning algorithms.
*  Complexity of implementation could hinder adoption.
*  Potential sensitivity to constraint quality and MaxSAT solver choice.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian network learning. The approach is novel, theoretically well-founded, and empirically validated. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths significantly outweigh them.  I **recommend acceptance** to NIPS, with the caveat that the authors address the points raised in the weaknesses section during the rebuttal period. Specifically, a more thorough comparison to other standard structure learning algorithms and a discussion of the scalability and robustness of the constraint projection step would strengthen the paper considerably.
