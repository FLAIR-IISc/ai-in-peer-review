Summary
The paper addresses exact Bayesian network (BN) structure learning under hard ancestral constraints (X is/is not an ancestor of Y), which are non-decomposable and thus poorly supported by mainstream exact score-based methods. Building on the BN graph/EC tree framework (Chen et al., 2015; 2016), the authors (i) show how to integrate ancestral constraints into EC-tree search via pruning CPDAG nodes that cannot satisfy the constraints, (ii) “project” non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints to empower an oracle that solves decomposable instances (e.g., order-graph-based dynamic programming or A*), and (iii) empirically demonstrate large speedups over an ILP baseline (GOBNILP). For projection, they give necessary-and-sufficient tests to entail edge presence/absence (Theorems 2–3) and encode inference of a maximally consistent set of ordering constraints with a MaxSAT formulation (and a sufficient condition for soundness, Theorem 4). Experiments with constraints sampled from benchmark networks show orders-of-magnitude improvements.

Relation to prior work
- Order/score-modular exact search over order graphs and DP is classical (Koivisto & Sood; Silander & Myllymäki; Yuan & Malone). Prior exact non-decomposable work using BN graphs/EC trees (Chen et al.) did not handle ancestral constraints.
- ILP/CP approaches (Jaakkola et al.; Bartlett & Cussens; van Beek & Hoffmann) can encode global constraints but have not evaluated ancestral constraints specifically.
- Constraint-based methods with path constraints (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos) are related but different (conditional independence-based vs score-based).
The paper’s novelty lies in marrying EC-tree search with pruning by ancestral constraints and in the projection techniques that safely feed decomposable constraints into the oracle.

Strengths
- Addresses a practically important form of background knowledge (ancestral relations) that is not decomposable.
- Clean integration into EC-tree search: pruning on CPDAGs using “must” directed paths for negative constraints and absence of possibly directed paths for positive constraints leverages standard CPDAG semantics.
- Projection of ancestral constraints to edge and ordering constraints is insightful. Theorem 2 gives an elegant characterization of when an edge must be absent; Theorem 3 offers a conservative test for when an edge must be present. The MaxSAT formulation identifies a large safe set of ordering constraints, strengthening the oracle substantially.
- Empirical results are compelling: consistent orders-of-magnitude improvements over GOBNILP across networks, data sizes, and constraint densities; clear easy–hard–easy pattern as constraints increase; and evidence that modest constraints improve structural accuracy comparably to significant data increases.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Theoretical details are underspecified. Several key pieces are stated without proof or with “details omitted”: (a) the closure procedure to compute a “maximum” ancestral constraint set; (b) proofs of Theorems 2–4; (c) formal correctness and complexity of the CPDAG pruning tests when “inconclusive” cases trigger enumeration of class(P), which can be exponential. At minimum, proofs and a complexity discussion should appear in the appendix.
- Theorem 1 as written is confusing. “P satisfies A iff its descendants satisfy A” needs clarifying: the intended monotonicity appears to be that adding a leaf cannot alter ancestral relations among existing variables, hence satisfaction/violation is preserved downward. Please restate with precise variable sets and the leaf-addition property for CPDAGs.
- MaxSAT encoding: the “sufficient condition for acyclicity” (constraint 3) is nonstandard and hard to interpret; more intuition/proof is needed. The approach seems conservative—potentially leaving many safe orderings unexploited. Empirical ablations (edge-only vs order-only vs both) and reporting MaxSAT overhead would help.
- Pruning via enumeration of class(P): how often does enumeration happen, how large do classes get, and what is the cost? Are there alternative polynomial-time graphical tests for existence of a directed path in some extension of a CPDAG (e.g., using possibly directed paths and orientation rules) that could reduce enumeration?
- Experimental scope: n ≤ 20 with unbounded indegree is in line with exact BDeu search literature, but it would help to also report results under bounded indegree to reach larger n and to compare with DP/A* baselines enhanced by the projected constraints. Also, only ILP is compared; CP (van Beek & Hoffmann) or MaxSAT-based BN learning (Cussens, 2008) would be informative baselines.
- Robustness to noisy/inconsistent background knowledge is not studied. Real prior knowledge can be wrong or inconsistent; a soft-constraint variant (e.g., penalized violations or soft MaxSAT) would widen applicability.
- Clarity/polish: some notation slips and formatting issues in references; figures/tables are referenced but not visible; a few footnotes and example explanations are hard to follow.

Quality
Technically promising, but missing proofs and some algorithmic details weaken confidence. The empirical section is solid and supports the main efficiency claims.

Clarity
Generally well organized, but needs clearer statements of theorems, a precise description of the closure algorithm, and more intuition for the MaxSAT constraints. Reference formatting should be fixed.

Originality
Good. The projection of ancestral constraints into edge/order constraints with soundness guarantees and their use to strengthen an order-based oracle within EC-tree search is novel.

Significance
High for the exact BN learning community: enables exploiting a useful class of background knowledge with substantial practical speedups. The ideas should influence both exact and approximate BN learners.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Provide full proofs of Theorems 2–4 and a formal statement/proof of Theorem 1. Clarify the closure algorithm for ancestral constraints and its complexity.
- Quantify how often CPDAG enumeration is invoked, the typical class sizes, and runtime impact. Can you avoid enumeration using stronger graphical criteria?
- Report ablations: EC-tree without projection; projection: edges only vs orderings only; MaxSAT overhead; and effect of projected constraints on the oracle’s search (node expansions).
- Include additional baselines (CP, MaxSAT-based BN learning) and bounded indegree experiments to explore larger n.
- Discuss handling inconsistent/noisy constraints (e.g., detecting infeasibility; soft penalties).
- Fix references and minor typos; restate Theorem 1 precisely.

Pros
- Tackles an important, previously unsupported constraint class in exact BN learning.
- Sound pruning on EC trees and principled projection to decomposable constraints.
- Strong empirical speedups over a relevant baseline.
- Clear practical impact and conceptual novelty.

Cons
- Missing proofs/details for key theorems and procedures.
- Some algorithmic steps may be exponential; limited analysis of when they trigger.
- MaxSAT encoding lacks intuition and may be conservative; ablations missing.
- Limited baselines and scalability exploration; no robustness to noisy constraints.

Overall: A solid and promising contribution; with added proofs, clarifications, and slightly broader experiments, it would be a strong accept. As is, I lean toward acceptance contingent on addressing the theoretical and clarity gaps.
