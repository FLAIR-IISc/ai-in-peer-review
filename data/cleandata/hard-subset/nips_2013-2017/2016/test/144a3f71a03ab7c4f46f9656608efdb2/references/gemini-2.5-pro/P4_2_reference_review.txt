**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network (BN) structures from data, subject to background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints (e.g., variable X must be an ancestor of Y, or X cannot be an ancestor of Y). The authors correctly identify that such constraints are non-decomposable, which prevents their direct use in mainstream optimal structure learning algorithms (e.g., those based on dynamic programming or A* search over orderings) that rely on decomposable scoring functions.

To tackle this, the paper builds upon a recently proposed framework for learning with non-decomposable scores [Chen et al., 2015, 2016], which performs an A* search over a space of completed partially directed acyclic graphs (CPDAGs), called the EC tree. The core idea of that framework is to use an "oracle"—an optimal learning algorithm for decomposable scores—to compute an admissible heuristic for the A* search. This paper's contributions are twofold: First, it adapts this search framework to handle ancestral constraints by developing a sound method for pruning nodes (CPDAGs) in the EC tree that are inconsistent with the given constraints.

Second, and more significantly, the paper introduces a novel technique called "projecting constraints." This involves inferring a set of decomposable constraints (specifically, edge presence/absence and pairwise topological ordering constraints) that are logical consequences of the original non-decomposable ancestral constraints. These inferred decomposable constraints are then passed to the oracle, effectively "empowering" it to compute a much tighter heuristic, which dramatically prunes the search space and improves efficiency. The authors provide algorithms for inferring edge constraints and formulate the more complex problem of inferring a maximal set of consistent ordering constraints as a MaxSAT problem.

The paper provides a strong empirical evaluation, comparing the proposed method against a state-of-the-art Integer Linear Programming (ILP) solver (GOBNILP), which can also encode ancestral constraints. The results show that the proposed approach is orders of magnitude faster and scales to larger problems.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proposed method for pruning the EC tree based on ancestral constraints is well-founded, and its soundness is stated in Theorem 1. The core technical contribution—projecting non-decomposable constraints to empower a heuristic oracle—is a clever and powerful idea. The characterizations for inferring edge constraints (Theorems 2 and 3) appear correct. The reduction of the ordering constraint inference problem to MaxSAT is an appropriate and well-reasoned approach to a non-trivial combinatorial problem. The experimental methodology is rigorous, comparing against a relevant and strong baseline (ILP) across multiple datasets and systematically varying key parameters like the number of variables, dataset size, and the number of constraints. The claims of performance improvement are well-supported by the empirical results.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. It begins with a clear motivation for the problem and an excellent overview of the technical challenges posed by non-decomposable constraints. The proposed approach is explained logically, building from the prior work on EC trees to the novel contributions of pruning and constraint projection. The distinction between empowering the oracle (by passing it inferred decomposable constraints) and pruning the main search space is articulated clearly. Figures and tables are informative. While proofs of theorems are omitted for space, the provided intuitions and formal statements are sufficient to understand the claims. A minor point is that the MaxSAT formulation is presented concisely; a little more intuition for the acyclicity clause (clause 3) could have been beneficial.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While it builds on the authors' prior work on BN graphs and EC trees, the main contribution—the concept of projecting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones to tighten a search heuristic—is novel. To our knowledge, this is the first work to propose such a method in the context of BN structure learning. Furthermore, while ILP and Constraint Programming (CP) methods can theoretically handle ancestral constraints, this paper is the first to provide a systematic empirical evaluation of their performance on this task and to propose a substantially more efficient alternative. The specific algorithms for inferring edge constraints and the MaxSAT formulation for ordering constraints are also novel contributions.

**Significance:** The results presented are significant and likely to be impactful for both researchers and practitioners. The paper addresses a practical and important problem, as incorporating expert background knowledge is crucial in many real-world domains (e.g., computational biology, causal discovery) to guide learning from limited data. The demonstrated orders-of-magnitude speedup over ILP-based approaches makes optimal learning with this important class of constraints feasible for larger and more complex problems than was previously possible. The experimental results also provide a compelling quantitative demonstration of the value of background knowledge, showing that a small percentage of ancestral constraints can improve model accuracy as much as a several-fold increase in data size. The general idea of empowering a decomposable oracle with projected non-decomposable constraints may also inspire similar approaches for other constrained optimization problems in machine learning.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Pro:** The paper tackles an important and difficult problem: exact BN structure learning with non-decomposable ancestral constraints.
*   **Pro:** The core technical idea of "projecting" constraints to empower the search heuristic is novel, elegant, and shown to be highly effective.
*   **Pro:** The work is technically sound, with clear theoretical justifications for the proposed methods.
*   **Pro:** It presents a state-of-the-art algorithm that is orders of magnitude faster than a strong ILP-based baseline, significantly advancing the capabilities of exact structure learning.
*   **Pro:** The paper is exceptionally clear, well-structured, and easy to follow.
*   **Pro:** The findings have practical significance, enabling the effective use of expert knowledge and demonstrating its value in improving model accuracy.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   **Con:** The core search framework is an extension of the authors' own recent work, which could be viewed as incremental. However, the novel techniques for handling constraints and empowering the oracle represent a substantial and non-obvious conceptual leap.
*   **Con:** The paper omits proofs and some algorithmic details for brevity, which can slightly hinder full reproducibility from the manuscript alone (though the core concepts are conveyed very well). This is a common and understandable consequence of conference page limits.
