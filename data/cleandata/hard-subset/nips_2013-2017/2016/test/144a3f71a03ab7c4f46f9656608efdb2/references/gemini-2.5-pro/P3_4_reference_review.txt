### Review of "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the problem of exact Bayesian network (BN) structure learning from data in the presence of background knowledge expressed as ancestral constraints (e.g., X must be an ancestor of Y, or X must not be an ancestor of Y). The authors identify a key challenge: ancestral constraints are non-decomposable, which makes them incompatible with the vast majority of efficient, exact score-based learning algorithms (e.g., those based on dynamic programming or A* search over orderings) that rely on score decomposability.

The authors propose a novel and elegant solution by building upon the recent EC tree search framework of Chen et al. (2016). Their approach has two main components:
1.  They adapt A* search on the EC tree (a search space over Markov equivalence classes) to handle ancestral constraints by developing sound pruning rules. A node in the tree (a CPDAG) is pruned if no DAG within its equivalence class can satisfy the given constraints.
2.  More significantly, they introduce a method to "project" the non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints. This is the paper's core technical contribution. These inferred decomposable constraints are then used to "empower" the oracle (the A* heuristic), leading to a much tighter heuristic and a dramatically more efficient search. The inference of ordering constraints is particularly sophisticated, framed as a MaxSAT problem to find a maximal consistent set.

The proposed method is empirically evaluated against a state-of-the-art Integer Linear Programming (ILP) approach (GOBNILP), which can naturally encode such constraints. The results demonstrate that the proposed method is orders of magnitude faster, scales to a respectable number of variables (n=20), and that the constraint projection technique is critical to its performance.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Novelty:** The paper tackles an important and practical problem. Incorporating rich background knowledge is crucial for learning meaningful causal or dependency models, especially with limited data. The core idea of projecting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones to accelerate a search guided by a decomposable-score oracle is highly novel and powerful. It provides a generalizable template for handling other types of non-decomposable constraints.
*   **Technical Quality:** The methodology is sound and well-conceived. The extension of the EC tree search with pruning rules is a natural and correct application of that framework. The constraint projection methods are the highlight; the reduction to MaxSAT for inferring a consistent set of ordering constraints is a particularly clever solution to a subtle problem, which the authors correctly identify (i.e., naive inference of ordering constraints can improperly prune valid DAGs).
*   **Experimental Rigor:** The experiments are thorough and convincing.
    *   The choice of GOBNILP as a baseline is strong, as ILP is one of the few other existing paradigms for exact learning with such complex constraints.
    *   The results clearly show orders-of-magnitude improvements, validating the approach's efficiency.
    *   The comparison between Table 1 (with projection) and Table 2 (without) serves as an excellent ablation study, demonstrating unequivocally that the projection method is the key to the performance gains.
    *   The analysis of accuracy (Structural Hamming Distance) effectively shows the practical benefit of incorporating background knowledge, which can be as impactful as collecting significantly more data.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It clearly motivates the problem, explains why existing methods fall short, details the proposed solution step-by-step, and presents a compelling empirical case. The examples provided are effective in illustrating complex concepts like non-decomposability and the subtleties of inferring ordering constraints.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Complexity of CPDAG Constraint Checking:** The paper states that if the simple pruning rules for a CPDAG are inconclusive, the algorithm must "exhaustively enumerate the structures of class(P), to check if any of them satisfies the given constraints." This step could be an exponential bottleneck. While the overall performance is excellent, it would be valuable to understand how often this expensive check is triggered and if it limits scalability for certain constraint patterns or graph structures. A brief discussion of the complexity or characterization of this worst-case scenario would improve the paper.
*   **Details of MaxSAT Formulation:** For brevity, the details of the MaxSAT formulation, particularly the "sufficient condition for acyclicity" (hard constraint #3), are presented without much justification. While plausible, this clause is complex, and a brief intuition or a reference to supplementary material would help the reader fully appreciate its correctness and necessity. Similarly, details on the "maximum set of ancestral constraints" are omitted.
*   **Generalizability:** The paper focuses exclusively on ancestral constraints. While this is a significant contribution in its own right, a brief discussion on whether the "projection" paradigm could be applied to other non-decomposable constraints (e.g., constraints on the maximum path length, or limitations on the number of v-structures) would strengthen the paper's claimed significance and inspire future work.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant and novel contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. The approach is elegant, technically sound, and empirically verified to outperform the state-of-the-art by a large margin on a practical and challenging problem. The weaknesses identified are minor and mostly relate to details omitted for space, which is understandable in a conference submission. The work is of high quality, original, clearly presented, and the results are likely to be impactful for both researchers and practitioners in graphical models and causal discovery. It represents a clear advance in the state of the art for exact BN learning.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
