Summary
The paper studies exact Bayesian network structure learning under non-decomposable ancestral (path) constraints. It builds on the BN-graph/EC-tree shortest-path framework of Chen et al. (2015, 2016), which allows searching over DAGs/CPDAGs with non-decomposable scores via an oracle that solves the decomposable case. The contributions are: (i) pruning EC-tree nodes (CPDAGs) that cannot be extended to satisfy a given set of ancestral constraints, with a correctness argument (Theorem 1); (ii) projecting ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints to “empower” the oracle. For edges, the paper gives sufficient/necessary conditions (Theorems 2–3) to infer X→Y or X↛Y from ancestral relations; for orderings, it encodes inference as a MaxSAT problem with sufficient constraints (Theorem 4). Empirically, the method is compared to GOBNILP (ILP) on standard BN benchmarks (subnets of ALARM, ANDES, CHILD, CPCS54, HEPAR2) with simulated data and simulated ancestral constraints. The authors report orders-of-magnitude speedups, and show that projected decomposable constraints substantially improve runtime.

Relation to prior work
The formulation relates to score-based exact structure learning via dynamic programming/order graphs (Koivisto & Sood; Silander & Myllymäki; Yuan & Malone) and MILP/CP formulations (Jaakkola et al.; Cussens; Bartlett & Cussens; van Beek & Hoffmann). Novelty lies in handling hard ancestral constraints in an exact shortest-path framework, and in projecting non-decomposable constraints to decomposable ones. The comparison to GOBNILP is appropriate; related path constraints in constraint-based/soft priors (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos) are discussed.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: ancestral constraints are practically important yet non-decomposable.
- Sensible use of EC-tree/BN-graph framework; pruning w.r.t. constraints is natural and, if correct, soundly reduces search.
- Projection of ancestral constraints to edge/ordering constraints is a good idea; providing conditions for inferring negative/positive edges is useful.
- Empirical comparison against a strong ILP baseline, with and without projected constraints, and reporting of easy–hard–easy trends is informative.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical soundness and completeness
- Theorem 1 is stated ambiguously (“P satisfies A iff its descendants satisfy A”); the intended property for safe pruning is monotonicity: if a CPDAG P violates A, all descendants violate A. Please clarify and provide a proof sketch. As written, the biconditional is puzzling.
- Checking CPDAG feasibility: the paper proposes two quick tests and, if inconclusive, “exhaustively enumerate the structures of class(P).” This guarantees completeness but can be exponential. There is no analysis or empirical accounting of how often enumeration is triggered, how large classes get, or its runtime impact. This is a key potential bottleneck that must be quantified.
- Edge inference (Theorems 2–3): The negative-edge condition looks correct and efficient. The positive-edge condition is non-trivial (requires certifying that all X→Z→Y alternatives are excluded). Complexity and completeness are not discussed; in dense/ambiguous constraints, this could be expensive or overly conservative. Proofs are omitted.
- Ordering projection via MaxSAT: The constraints are sufficient, not necessary; thus the selected ordering set may be far from maximally large, contrary to the stated goal. The encoding uses a “sufficient condition for acyclicity” that is unusual and not justified; without a stronger correctness argument, I am concerned about either (a) inferring too many orderings and excluding feasible DAGs, or (b) inferring too few and losing the promised efficiency. Empirical sensitivity to this choice is not reported.

Experimental evaluation
- Scale: Experiments top out at n=20 variables. Exact BN learning literature frequently reports larger n when indegree bounds are used; here no indegree cap is set, but the claim of “orders-of-magnitude faster” would be stronger if validated beyond 20 variables or with varying indegree bounds (a common constraint in practice).
- Baselines: Comparison is only to GOBNILP. CP-based methods (van Beek & Hoffmann) are cited but not evaluated. Also absent are order-graph/A* baselines augmented with the projected decomposable constraints (i.e., how far do projections alone get you without EC-tree pruning?).
- Ablations and overhead: Runtimes for the projection steps (transitive closure of ancestral constraints, edge inference, and the MaxSAT solve) are not reported; it is unclear whether these are included in totals. Likewise, the cost/benefit of ordering vs edge projections is not disentangled.
- Robustness to noisy/inconsistent constraints: Real background knowledge is often imperfect. The paper assumes hard, consistent constraints; there is no discussion or experiment on handling conflicts or noise, despite related work on soft path beliefs.
- Metrics: Since both methods are exact, SHD to ground truth primarily reflects data size and constraints rather than algorithmic quality; it does not speak to the core claim (efficiency under constraints). Memory usage and node expansions would be more informative.

Clarity and presentation
- Several statements are imprecise (e.g., Theorem 1), and some notation is inconsistent. Definitions such as “closed set of ordering constraints” are used but not defined.
- Numerous citation formatting issues and typos (e.g., stray characters, missing years, malformed references). Tables/figures are referenced but not fully visible here; ensure they are legible and reproducible.
- Important proofs are omitted; at minimum, provide proof sketches or move full proofs to supplementary material.

Pros
- Addresses a relevant and underexplored constraint type in exact BN learning.
- Builds on established, principled frameworks.
- Concept of projecting non-decomposable constraints to decomposable ones is practical and potentially impactful.
- Promising empirical speedups over ILP.

Cons
- Key correctness claims are under-specified; reliance on exponential enumeration without quantification is concerning.
- Limited experimental scope and missing baselines/ablations.
- No treatment of noisy/inconsistent constraints.
- Clarity and polish issues.

Questions for the authors
1. Clarify Theorem 1 and provide a proof sketch. What exactly is the monotonicity property you rely on?
2. How often do you enumerate entire equivalence classes, and how large are they in your runs? Provide statistics and runtime breakdowns.
3. What is the complexity of the edge-inference algorithm and the MaxSAT encoding in practice (variables/clauses)? Report solver times.
4. Can you quantify the separate contributions of edge vs ordering projections?
5. How does the method behave with a small fraction of erroneous ancestral constraints? Can you adapt the framework to soft constraints?
6. Can you compare against a CP baseline and an oracle-only baseline using only projected constraints?

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea is interesting and grounded in established frameworks, and preliminary results are promising. However, I am not comfortable endorsing it without stronger theoretical clarity (especially on pruning and entailment conditions) and a more thorough empirical study that quantifies the potential exponential failure modes, includes stronger baselines/ablations, and reports projection overheads. With these addressed, I would be inclined to support acceptance.

Reviewer confidence: Moderate.
