### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the important problem of learning optimal Bayesian network (BN) structures from data under background knowledge expressed as ancestral constraints. Ancestral constraints (e.g., variable X must be an ancestor of Y, or X must *not* be an ancestor of Y) are a natural way to encode causal assumptions but are non-decomposable, making them incompatible with most state-of-the-art optimal BN learning algorithms that rely on decomposable scores.

The authors build upon a recent search framework [Chen et al., 2015, 2016] that navigates a search space of equivalence classes (the EC tree) using A* search. This framework is capable of handling non-decomposable scores and constraints. The main contributions of this paper are twofold:
1.  It adapts the EC tree search to incorporate ancestral constraints by developing a sound pruning strategy. Nodes in the search tree (CPDAGs) that are inconsistent with the given constraints are pruned, effectively reducing the search space.
2.  It introduces a novel and powerful technique called "constraint projection." This method infers decomposable constraints (specifically, edge presence/absence and partial topological ordering constraints) from the given non-decomposable ancestral constraints. These simpler, projected constraints are then used to "empower" the oracle that computes the A* heuristic, leading to a much tighter heuristic and consequently, a significantly more efficient search. The inference of edge constraints is handled by an efficient algorithm, while the inference of a maximal set of consistent ordering constraints is cleverly formulated as a MaxSAT problem.

The authors provide a strong empirical evaluation, comparing their approach to a state-of-the-art integer linear programming (ILP) method (GOBNILP). The results show that their method is orders of magnitude faster, scales to networks of up to 20 variables, and that the constraint projection technique is critical to its performance. The experiments also highlight the value of ancestral constraints in improving the structural accuracy of the learned networks, often having an impact comparable to a large increase in data.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Practicality:** The paper tackles a problem of high practical importance. Incorporating prior knowledge is often essential for learning meaningful graphical models, especially with limited data. Ancestral constraints are a very natural and expressive form for this knowledge, particularly in causal discovery settings. The proposed method provides an effective and scalable way to use this information to find optimal network structures.

2.  **Novelty and Elegance of the Approach:** The core idea of "projecting" non-decomposable constraints into a decomposable form to tighten the heuristic of an A* search is both novel and elegant. It provides a generalizable recipe for integrating complex global constraints into search algorithms that rely on oracles designed for simpler, decomposable problems. This is a significant conceptual contribution that could inspire work beyond BN structure learning.

3.  **Technical Quality:** The work is technically sound. The pruning method for the EC tree is justified by Theorem 1. The algorithms for inferring edge constraints from ancestral constraints are well-defined and backed by Theorems 2 and 3. The reduction to MaxSAT for finding a consistent set of ordering constraints is a clever way to handle the subtleties of inferring orderings without accidentally eliminating valid DAGs.

4.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are comprehensive and convincing. The comparison against a strong exact baseline (ILP) clearly demonstrates the superior performance of the proposed method. The ablation analysis (comparing performance with and without constraint projection in Tables 1 & 2) effectively proves the value of this key contribution. The scalability analysis and the investigation of the "easy-hard-easy" pattern are insightful. Finally, showing the positive impact of constraints on structural accuracy (Table 3) reinforces the practical motivation for the work.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Computational Cost of Constraint Projection:** The paper proposes using a MaxSAT solver to infer ordering constraints. While effective, this step could be a computational bottleneck itself, especially for larger numbers of variables. The paper does not analyze the runtime of this pre-processing step or discuss how it scales. While the overall search time is low, a brief discussion on the cost of projection relative to the cost of the main A* search would be beneficial for the reader.

2.  **Details of CPDAG Pruning:** The method for checking if a CPDAG node satisfies the ancestral constraints involves an exhaustive enumeration of the DAGs in its equivalence class as a fallback. For CPDAGs representing very large equivalence classes, this check could be expensive. It would strengthen the paper to include some discussion or empirical data on how often this exhaustive check is necessary in practice and what its performance impact is.

3.  **Clarity on MaxSAT Formulation:** The connection between the sufficient conditions for entailed orderings in Theorem 4 and the clauses in the MaxSAT formulation could be made more explicit. While the formulation appears correct, a clearer mapping would help the reader verify that solving the MaxSAT instance indeed finds a set of ordering constraints `O` that is provably entailed by the ancestral constraints `A`.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a significant, practical problem in graphical model learning.
*   Presents a novel and elegant technical approach (constraint projection to empower an oracle) that is likely to be influential.
*   The method is technically sound and well-supported by theorems and a strong, comprehensive experimental evaluation.
*   The results show a dramatic performance improvement (orders of magnitude) over a state-of-the-art exact baseline.
*   The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The computational complexity of some components (MaxSAT-based projection, exhaustive CPDAG pruning) is not fully analyzed, leaving minor open questions about scalability in extreme cases.
*   The comparison is limited to one class of baseline (ILP), though it is arguably the most relevant one for exact learning.

### Conclusion

This is an excellent paper that makes a strong contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. The proposed method for learning optimal BNs with ancestral constraints is novel, technically sound, and empirically shown to be highly effective. The key idea of empowering a decomposable-score oracle with projected non-decomposable constraints is a significant insight. The weaknesses are minor and relate to details that could be clarified or expanded upon, but they do not detract from the core quality and impact of the work. The paper is well-suited for NIPS and I strongly recommend its acceptance.
