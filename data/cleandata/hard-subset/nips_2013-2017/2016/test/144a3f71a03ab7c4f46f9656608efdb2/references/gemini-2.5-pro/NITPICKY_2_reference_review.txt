This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data, subject to background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints. The key challenge is that ancestral constraints are non-decomposable, rendering them incompatible with many efficient, score-based learning algorithms that rely on score decomposability. The authors propose a novel approach that adapts the EC tree search framework, which is designed for non-decomposable scores. Their method uses an A* search over the EC tree, guided by a heuristic function (an "oracle") that can optimally solve problems with decomposable scores. The main contributions are: (1) a sound pruning strategy for the EC tree search based on ancestral constraints; (2) a method to "project" the non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints, which significantly "empowers" the oracle and improves the search heuristic; (3) an empirical demonstration that the proposed approach is orders of magnitude faster than a comparable ILP-based approach.

**Strengths:**

*   The problem is well-motivated and of practical importance. Incorporating rich background knowledge, such as cause-and-effect relationships expressed as ancestral constraints, is a significant challenge for score-based Bayesian network learning.
*   The core technical idea of projecting non-decomposable constraints into a decomposable form to improve a search heuristic for a non-decomposable problem is clever and appears to be effective.
*   The empirical results are compelling, showing dramatic performance improvements over the ILP-based baseline, GOBNILP. The experiments also clearly demonstrate the substantial impact of the proposed constraint projection technique.

**Weaknesses:**

Despite the promising core ideas, the paper is marred by a substantial number of flaws, ranging from critical formatting errors to significant omissions in the methodological description.

1.  **Presentation and Formatting:** The manuscript is riddled with egregious formatting errors that suggest a lack of care in its preparation.
    *   A stray line `th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016), Barcelona, Spain.` appears in the introduction.
    *   Random characters (`年6月12⽇日星期五 11 16`) are present on the second page.
    *   The reference list is corrupted, with entries like `Bartlett and Cussens.,? \Q2015\E, Bartlett and Cussens.,` and `Chen et al\.,?`. This is unacceptable for a camera-ready submission.
    *   In Section 5.1, the example DAGs are depicted ambiguously (e.g., `Y Z X`) without arrows, making it impossible to understand the intended structures.

2.  **Lack of Clarity and Rigor:**
    *   The notation is inconsistent. The symbol `X` is used to denote a single variable, the set of all variables in the network, and as an index in summations, often within the same section (e.g., Section 2, `∑ X∈X ∑ U⊆X\X`). This ambiguity hinders readability. The expression `U⊆X\X` is particularly confusing; it should likely be `U ⊆ V\{X}` where `V` is the set of all variables.
    *   Key theoretical claims are presented without proof or sufficient justification. Theorem 1, which provides the basis for the soundness of pruning the EC tree, is stated without a proof sketch. Theorems 2, 3, and 4, which are central to the constraint projection method, are also presented without formal proofs, leaving the reader to trust their correctness.
    *   The argument in Footnote 4 for inferring an ordering constraint `Y < X` from an ancestral constraint `X ≁ Y` is informal and not entirely convincing without a more rigorous argument.
    *   The MaxSAT formulation in Section 5.2 is presented with minimal explanation. Constraint 3, in particular, is complex and its role as a "sufficient condition for acyclicity" is not justified. The connection between this formulation and Theorem 4 is left to the reader to decipher.

3.  **Methodological Gaps:**
    *   In Section 4.3, the procedure for checking if a CPDAG `P` satisfies the constraints involves exhaustively enumerating `class(P)` if simpler checks are inconclusive. As `class(P)` can be exponentially large, this step could be a major computational bottleneck. The paper provides no discussion on the practical cost of this enumeration or how frequently it is required in their experiments. This is a critical detail for evaluating the algorithm's real-world performance.
    *   The experimental setup lacks important details. The authors state they "pre-computed the scores of candidate parent sets," but do not specify the maximum in-degree (parent set size) allowed. This parameter is crucial, as it determines the feasibility of both the pre-computation and the oracle's search. Without this information, the claims of scalability to `n=20` are difficult to interpret.

4.  **Experimental Evaluation:**
    *   The comparison is limited to a single ILP-based baseline (GOBNILP). While a relevant choice, the authors also mention Constraint Programming (CP) approaches in the introduction but do not compare against them, leaving it unclear if the reported speed-ups would hold against other constraint-based paradigms.

**Decision:**

I recommend **rejection**.

While the paper presents a novel and potentially significant contribution to Bayesian network structure learning, its current state is far below the quality standards expected at NIPS. The sheer number of formatting errors, notational ambiguities, and missing theoretical justifications demonstrates a lack of thoroughness and care. The methodological description omits critical details about potential computational bottlenecks, making it difficult to fully assess the practicality of the proposed algorithm. The core ideas are strong, but the paper requires a major revision to address these numerous and substantial flaws before it can be considered for publication.
