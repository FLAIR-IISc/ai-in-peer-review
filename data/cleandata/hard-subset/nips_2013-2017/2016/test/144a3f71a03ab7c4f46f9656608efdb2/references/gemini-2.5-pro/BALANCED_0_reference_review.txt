**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network (BN) structures from data, subject to background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints (e.g., "X must be an ancestor of Y", or "X must not be an ancestor of Y"). The authors correctly identify that such constraints are non-decomposable and thus cannot be directly handled by mainstream optimal structure learning algorithms (e.g., those based on dynamic programming or A* search over variable orderings), which rely on decomposable scoring functions. While Integer Linear Programming (ILP) or Constraint Programming (CP) methods can encode these constraints, their empirical performance on this task has not been thoroughly evaluated.

The proposed solution extends a recent framework for optimal BN learning that performs an A* search over a space of DAGs (BN graphs) or equivalence classes (EC trees). This framework naturally accommodates non-decomposable constraints by simply pruning nodes in the search space that violate them. The main contribution and novelty of this work lies in a technique called "constraint projection." The authors show how to infer decomposable constraints (i.e., required/forbidden edges and topological ordering constraints) from the given non-decomposable ancestral constraints. These inferred decomposable constraints are then passed to the A* heuristic's "oracle" (an efficient solver for decomposable problems), significantly tightening the heuristic lower bound and dramatically accelerating the search. The inference of ordering constraints is cleverly formulated as a MaxSAT problem. The paper provides a strong empirical evaluation, comparing their method to a state-of-the-art ILP-based system (GOBNILP), demonstrating orders-of-magnitude speedups and showing scalability up to 20 variables.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles a significant and practical problem. Incorporating expert knowledge is crucial for learning reliable graphical models, especially from limited data, and ancestral constraints are a natural and powerful form of such knowledge. The core idea of projecting non-decomposable constraints into a set of decomposable ones to empower a fast, decomposable-score-based oracle is highly novel and insightful. This represents a valuable conceptual contribution that could be applicable beyond the specific constraints studied here.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed method is technically sound and well-founded. The pruning strategy for the EC tree search is correct. The algorithms for inferring edge constraints (Theorems 2 and 3) and the MaxSAT formulation for inferring a maximal set of consistent ordering constraints (summarized by Theorem 4) provide a rigorous foundation for the constraint projection technique. The paper presents a complete and well-thought-out system.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. It clearly motivates the problem, situates the work within the existing literature, explains the technical preliminaries, and builds up the proposed method step-by-step. The distinction between pruning the search space and empowering the oracle is articulated very clearly.

4.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The comparison against a state-of-the-art ILP solver provides a strong baseline and clearly demonstrates the superiority of the proposed approach. The ablation-like study comparing performance with and without constraint projection (Tables 1 vs. 2) effectively validates the central claim of the paper—that projection is the key to efficiency. The analysis of learning accuracy (structural Hamming distance) provides compelling evidence for the practical value of incorporating ancestral constraints, showing they can be as impactful as collecting significantly more data.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Details for Reproducibility:** For space considerations, the paper omits some key details. For example, the rules for computing the "maximum set of ancestral constraints" are not provided, and the full MaxSAT encoding is described at a high level. While understandable for a conference paper, including these details in a supplementary appendix would significantly improve the paper's completeness and reproducibility.

2.  **Computational Cost of Projection:** The paper does not analyze the computational overhead of the constraint projection step itself. The MaxSAT solver for inferring ordering constraints could be computationally intensive. It would be valuable to include a discussion or experimental breakdown of the time spent on projection versus the time spent on the A* search. This would help readers understand the trade-offs and identify potential bottlenecks for problems with a very large number of constraints.

3.  **Scalability Discussion:** While the performance up to 20 variables is competitive for an exact learning algorithm, this remains a practical limitation. The paper could be strengthened by a brief discussion of potential avenues for scaling to larger problems. For instance, could this projection technique be used to guide heuristic search methods (like greedy hill-climbing) for larger networks where optimality is intractable?

4.  **Minor Clarification on Theorem 1:** The statement of Theorem 1 ("a CPDAG P satisfies ancestral constraints A... iff its descendants satisfy A") seems tautological. The intended meaning is likely that if a CPDAG P violates the constraints, all of its descendants in the EC tree will also violate them, justifying the pruning. Clarifying the wording would improve precision.

**Decision**

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a novel and significant contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. The core idea of constraint projection is elegant and shown to be highly effective. The work is technically solid, clearly presented, and backed by strong empirical evidence. The weaknesses identified are minor and primarily relate to suggestions for additional details or future work, rather than fundamental flaws. The paper easily meets the high standards of NeurIPS.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses an important, practical problem in graphical model learning.
*   Presents a highly novel and clever technique (constraint projection) to bridge non-decomposable constraints and decomposable solvers.
*   Technically sound with a rigorous formulation.
*   Empirically demonstrates state-of-the-art performance, with orders-of-magnitude speedups over a relevant baseline.
*   Clearly written and easy to understand.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   None. The identified weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the paper's core contribution.
