## NIPS 2016 Paper Review: Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of learning Bayesian networks (BNs) while incorporating non-decomposable ancestral constraints (specifying ancestor/descendant relationships between variables). Existing optimal structure learning methods struggle with this due to their reliance on decomposable scoring functions. The authors build upon their previous work leveraging an “oracle” capable of optimally learning with decomposable scores, adapting it to handle ancestral constraints. Their approach involves pruning the search space based on the constraints and, crucially, *projecting* the non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints (edge and ordering constraints) to empower the oracle.  The authors demonstrate significant performance improvements over Integer Linear Programming (ILP)-based methods in empirical evaluations.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds directly on the authors’ earlier work [Chen et al., 2015, 2016] introducing the BN graph and EC tree search spaces for optimal BN learning. It acknowledges the limitations of traditional BN learning algorithms (K2, dynamic programming, ILP) in handling non-decomposable constraints, referencing [Koivisto and Sood, 2004, Parviainen and Koivisto, 2013] for existing constraints they *can* handle. The work also positions itself relative to constraint-based approaches using ancestral constraints like [Borboudakis and Tsamardinos, 2012, 2013] and ILP-based methods [Jaakkola et al., 2010, Bartlett and Cussens, 2015, van Beek and Hoffmann, 2015], providing a novel and demonstrably more efficient solution.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a significant problem:** Incorporating background knowledge into BN learning is crucial for real-world applications, and ancestral constraints are a natural way to represent such knowledge. The non-decomposability issue is a fundamental barrier to progress.
* **Novel approach:** The combination of pruning the search space with constraint projection to empower the oracle is a clever and effective strategy.  The theoretical grounding of constraint projection (Theorems 1-4) adds rigor.
* **Strong empirical results:** The experimental evaluation convincingly demonstrates the substantial speed-up (orders of magnitude) compared to ILP-based methods. The ablation study showing the impact of constraint projection is particularly valuable.
* **Scalability:** Achieving results on networks with up to 20 variables is commendable, indicating reasonable scalability for a complex problem.
* **Clear exposition:**  The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and explains the core ideas.
* **Well-defined theoretical foundations:** The theorems presented strengthen the approach and provide a basis for further development.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited discussion of the practical implications of constraint projection:** While the performance gains are clear, the paper doesn't delve deeply into the practical challenges of *identifying* and *representing* ancestral constraints in real-world scenarios. How robust is the projection process to noisy or incomplete constraints?
* **Oracle dependence:** The performance is heavily reliant on the effectiveness of the “oracle” (URLEARNING). While the authors acknowledge this, more discussion on the sensitivity of the results to the choice of oracle would be beneficial.  Could a less efficient oracle significantly degrade performance?
* **Experimental Setup:** The experimental setup, while thorough, relies heavily on simulated data. While this allows for controlled comparisons, it doesn’t fully capture the complexities of real-world datasets.  Results on a few real-world datasets would add further validation.
* **MaxSAT Solver:** The reliance on an external MaxSAT solver (EVASOLVER) introduces a dependency and a potential bottleneck. The computational cost of the MaxSAT solving process isn't fully explored in the context of the overall performance.
* **Theorem 4’s Conditions:**  The conditions in Theorem 4 seem restrictive. It would be helpful to explore how often these conditions are met in practice and how the approach handles situations where they are not.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a challenging and important problem in BN learning.
* The proposed approach is novel, technically sound, and supported by strong empirical results.
* The clear writing and logical organization make the paper accessible to a broad audience.
* The theoretical contributions (constraint projection and the associated theorems) are significant.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The reliance on simulated data and a specific oracle limits the generalizability of the results.
* The discussion of practical considerations (e.g., constraint identification, noise robustness) is somewhat limited.
* The complexity of the MaxSAT solver and its impact on overall performance warrants further investigation.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian network learning. The authors effectively address a challenging problem with a novel and well-supported approach. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths outweigh them significantly.  I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance to NIPS 2016.

**Rating:**  Strong Accept (8/10)

**Recommendations for Improvement:**

* Expand the experimental evaluation to include real-world datasets.
* Provide a more detailed discussion of the practical challenges of constraint identification and representation.
* Investigate the sensitivity of the results to the choice of oracle.
* Analyze the computational cost of the MaxSAT solving process in more detail.
* Explore scenarios where the conditions of Theorem 4 are not fully met and discuss potential mitigation strategies.
