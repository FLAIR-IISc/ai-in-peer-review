

# Peer Review: "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"

## Summary

This paper addresses a significant gap in Bayesian network structure learning: the incorporation of non-decomposable ancestral constraints (e.g., "X is an ancestor of Y" or "X is not an ancestor of Y"). The authors propose a novel approach using an EC tree search space (an extension of the BN graph) combined with a technique to project ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints. This enables them to leverage existing optimal structure learning algorithms for decomposable scores (the "oracle") while efficiently handling non-decomposable background knowledge. The paper demonstrates orders-of-magnitude speed improvements over ILP-based approaches (GOBNILP) in experiments across multiple benchmark networks.

The work builds upon Chen et al.'s previous contributions (2015, 2016) on structure learning with non-decomposable scores and addresses a limitation identified in prior work: that most structure learning approaches assume decomposable scoring functions and cannot effectively incorporate non-decomposable constraints like ancestral relationships. The authors make a clear contribution to the field by providing a theoretically sound and practically efficient solution to this problem.

## Quality

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The theoretical foundation is solid, with well-constructed theorems (Theorems 2-4) that support the constraint projection methods. The EC tree approach for handling ancestral constraints is sound, with a clear pruning mechanism that ensures completeness.

The empirical evaluation is thorough and well-designed. The authors simulate learning problems from standard Bayesian network benchmarks (ALARM, ANDES, etc.) and systematically vary key parameters (number of variables, dataset size, percentage of ancestral constraints). The results consistently show that their approach outperforms the ILP-based GOBNILP system by orders of magnitude. The paper also provides insightful analysis of the structural Hamming distance between learned networks and ground-truth, demonstrating that a small number of ancestral constraints can have a similar impact on model quality as significantly more training data.

One minor technical limitation: The paper doesn't fully address how the approach handles inconsistent ancestral constraints (e.g., X → Y and X ← Y). This could be a significant practical concern in real-world applications where background knowledge might be incomplete or contradictory.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The progression from problem statement to methodology to evaluation is logical and easy to follow. Key concepts (ancestral constraints, EC trees, constraint projection) are clearly defined with helpful examples. The figures (Figures 1a and 1b) effectively illustrate the BN graph and EC tree structures.

The paper provides sufficient detail for experts to understand and potentially reproduce the approach. The description of the constraint projection algorithms (Section 5) is precise, and the MaxSAT formulation for ordering constraints is well-justified. The experimental setup is clearly described, including details about the benchmark networks, dataset simulation, and evaluation metrics.

## Originality

This work makes a significant original contribution to the field. While previous work has explored structure learning with decomposable constraints (e.g., edge presence/absence, order-modular constraints), this is the first to provide a comprehensive solution for ancestral constraints, which are a common form of non-decomposable background knowledge in real-world applications.

The paper effectively distinguishes itself from prior work:
- Unlike approaches based on the order graph (K2, dynamic programming, A* search), which cannot enforce ancestral constraints, this work enables their incorporation.
- Unlike ILP-based approaches that can encode ancestral constraints (Cussens, 2008), this work demonstrates a dramatically more efficient solution.
- The constraint projection technique (Section 5) is novel and provides a general framework for converting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones.

The authors thoroughly review related work, including the seminal K2 algorithm, order graph approaches, and ILP/CP methods, making clear how their contribution advances the state of the art.

## Significance

The significance of this work is substantial. Incorporating domain knowledge through ancestral constraints is a common requirement in practical applications (e.g., medical diagnosis, biological networks), yet this capability has been largely missing from optimal structure learning methods. The paper demonstrates that this incorporation can be done efficiently without sacrificing optimality.

The empirical results are particularly compelling: the authors show that a relatively small number of ancestral constraints (10-25%) can have a similar impact on model quality as increasing training data from 512 to 8192 observations. This has important practical implications for domains where data collection is expensive but domain knowledge is available.

The paper also demonstrates scalability to 20 variables, which is competitive with state-of-the-art score-based approaches (Yuan and Malone, 2013 reported results up to 26 variables). The approach's efficiency makes it applicable to larger problems than previous methods that could handle non-decomposable constraints.

## Strengths

1. **Novel and important problem**: Addressing the incorporation of ancestral constraints fills a significant gap in Bayesian network structure learning.
2. **Theoretical soundness**: The constraint projection methods are rigorously proven (Theorems 2-4).
3. **Empirical impact**: The results demonstrate orders-of-magnitude speed improvements over existing methods.
4. **Practical relevance**: The approach is immediately applicable to real-world problems where domain knowledge is available.
5. **Clear presentation**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized.

## Weaknesses

1. **Inconsistent constraints**: The paper doesn't address how to handle inconsistent ancestral constraints (e.g., contradictory knowledge), which could be a practical limitation.
2. **Constraint quality analysis**: The paper doesn't explore how the quality of ancestral constraints (e.g., accuracy of domain knowledge) affects the learning process.
3. **Comparison with constraint-based methods**: While the paper mentions constraint-based approaches (e.g., PC algorithm), it doesn't compare with them directly, which would strengthen the evaluation.
4. **MaxSAT complexity**: The MaxSAT formulation for ordering constraints is introduced without sufficient intuition about why this approach is necessary or how it scales.
5. **Limited theoretical analysis**: The paper could provide more theoretical analysis of the relationship between constraint density and search space reduction.

## Arguments for Acceptance

- Addresses a meaningful problem with significant practical relevance
- Makes a theoretically sound and practically efficient contribution to the field
- Demonstrates strong empirical results showing orders-of-magnitude improvements
- Builds upon and extends previous work in a clear and logical manner
- Provides a framework that could be applied to other non-decomposable constraints
- Well-written and well-organized paper

## Arguments against Acceptance

- Doesn't fully address how to handle inconsistent ancestral constraints
- Limited comparison with constraint-based learning approaches
- The MaxSAT component may be perceived as complex without sufficient justification
- Some practical limitations (e.g., constraint quality dependence) aren't fully explored

## Overall Recommendation

I strongly recommend acceptance of this paper. It makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning by providing an efficient, theoretically sound solution to incorporating non-decomposable ancestral constraints. The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate clear practical advantages over existing approaches. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a substantial advancement over previous work.

The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and do not detract from the paper's overall contribution. The authors could address the most significant weakness (handling inconsistent constraints) in a brief revision by adding a short discussion of this issue and potential approaches for handling it.

This work advances the state of the art in structure learning and has clear potential for practical application in domains where domain knowledge is available. It represents a high-quality contribution that aligns well with NIPS' focus on innovative machine learning research with theoretical and practical significance.
