This paper addresses the problem of learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints, a challenging task due to the non-decomposable nature of these constraints. The authors build upon their previous work on optimal Bayesian network structure learning and propose a method to incorporate ancestral constraints into the search process. They also introduce a technique for projecting these non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones to empower an oracle used within their approach. The core idea of pruning the search space based on ancestral constraints and leveraging decomposable constraints to improve oracle efficiency is conceptually sound and aligns with established approaches in Bayesian network learning.

However, while the paper presents a potentially valuable contribution, it suffers from a multitude of minor flaws and lacks the polish expected for a NIPS publication. The presentation is frequently imprecise, and the experimental evaluation, while extensive, feels somewhat unfocused and lacks sufficient critical analysis.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses an Important Problem:** Incorporating background knowledge, such as ancestral constraints, into Bayesian network learning is a crucial and practical problem.
*   **Sound Conceptual Approach:** The idea of pruning the search space and projecting constraints is a logical and potentially effective strategy.
*   **Empirical Evaluation:** The authors provide a thorough empirical comparison against an ILP-based approach, demonstrating performance improvements.
*   **Connection to Existing Work:** The paper builds on the authors’ prior work and appropriately references relevant literature.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Precision in Notation:** The use of notation is inconsistent and occasionally ambiguous. For example, the meaning of ‘U’ shifts between representing parents of a single variable and a set of variables. The definition of α(X) and β(X) is unclear without careful parsing of the preceding text.
*   **Redundancy & Verbosity:** The introduction is overly verbose and repetitive. The same ideas are reiterated multiple times, making it difficult to quickly grasp the core contributions. Phrases like "orders-of-magnitude improvements" are used without specific quantification in the introduction.
*   **Missing Definitions:** Key concepts, such as “Markov equivalence class” and “CPDAG”, are introduced without sufficient explanation, assuming prior knowledge not universally held by the NIPS audience. 
*   **Incomplete Justification of Algorithm:** The MaxSAT reduction for inferring ordering constraints is only briefly mentioned and lacks a detailed explanation. The theoretical justification for the soundness and completeness claim (Theorem 4) feels underdeveloped.
*   **Experimental Details:** The experimental setup, while detailed in some aspects, lacks critical information.  What were the specific network structures selected as sub-networks? The choice of equivalent sample size (1) for the BDeu score is not justified. The impact of different MaxSAT solvers on performance is not discussed.
*   **Limited Discussion of Results:** The results are presented largely as tables without in-depth discussion or analysis of the observed trends. The “easy-hard-easy” trend in Table 3 is mentioned, but not adequately explained. Why does the performance gap between the proposed approach and ILP widen with increasing *n*?
*    **Formatting Inconsistencies:** There’s a minor, but noticeable, formatting error in the header - "th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016)".
*   **Theorem Phrasing:** The phrasing of Theorem 1 is awkwardly worded, and could be clearer. "Descendants" must be defined.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The problem addressed is important.
*   The proposed approach shows promising empirical results, demonstrating significant speedups over ILP.
*   The idea of constraint projection is novel and potentially impactful.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The paper’s numerous minor flaws and lack of polish detract from its overall quality.
*   The lack of precision and clarity makes it difficult to fully understand and evaluate the proposed approach.
*   The experimental evaluation, while extensive, lacks sufficient critical analysis.
*   The theoretical justification for some key aspects of the approach (e.g., the MaxSAT reduction) is incomplete.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper, in its current form, is **not suitable for acceptance** to NIPS. While the core ideas are promising, the numerous flaws in presentation, clarity, and theoretical justification prevent it from meeting the high standards of the conference. The paper would significantly benefit from a thorough revision addressing the points raised above. Specifically, the authors should focus on improving the clarity of their writing, providing more detailed explanations of their algorithms and theoretical results, and conducting a more rigorous analysis of their experimental findings. A more streamlined and focused presentation would greatly enhance the impact of their work.
