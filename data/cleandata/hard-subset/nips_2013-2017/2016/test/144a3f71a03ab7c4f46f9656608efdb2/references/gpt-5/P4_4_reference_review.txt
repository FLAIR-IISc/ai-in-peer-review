Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses exact Bayesian network (BN) structure learning in the presence of ancestral (path) constraints, which are non-decomposable and therefore not directly supported by standard exact methods that rely on decomposable scores and/or modular constraints. Building on Chen et al. (2015, 2016), the authors operate in search spaces over full DAGs (BN graphs) and over Markov equivalence classes (EC trees/graphs), using A* with a heuristic supplied by an “oracle” exact learner that optimizes decomposable scores on subproblems. The key contributions are: (1) a pruning strategy for EC trees that enforces ancestral constraints by discarding CPDAG nodes whose entire equivalence class cannot satisfy the constraints; (2) projection methods that infer decomposable constraints from non-decomposable ancestral constraints to “empower” the oracle—specifically, an algorithm to infer necessary/forbidden edges (Theorems 2–3) and a MaxSAT-based method to infer a large, sound set of ordering constraints (Theorem 4); and (3) an empirical evaluation against the ILP-based system GOBNILP, showing large speedups on synthetic problems derived from standard BN benchmarks.

This work connects to dynamic programming and heuristic search over order graphs (Koivisto & Sood 2004; Silander & Myllymäki 2006; Yuan & Malone 2011, 2013), and to non-decomposable priors over DAGs (Chen et al. 2015, 2016). It contrasts with ILP/CP-based exact methods (Jaakkola et al. 2010; Bartlett & Cussens 2015; van Beek & Hoffmann 2015), which can in principle encode path constraints but have not been evaluated for this specific use case. It also relates to path-constraint approaches in constraint-based learning and soft priors (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos 2012, 2013).

Strengths
- Addresses an important and practical form of background knowledge (ancestral constraints) with an exact learning method, overcoming a limitation of decomposable-score frameworks.
- Sound pruning criterion over EC trees: a CPDAG node can be discarded if no DAG in its equivalence class can satisfy the constraints. This is complete for pruning and preserves optimality.
- Projection of path constraints into edge and ordering constraints is well-motivated and practically effective. Theorem 2 (edge exclusion via ancestor/descendant closures) and Theorem 3 (edge inclusion when all alternative mediators are ruled out) are intuitive and useful.
- MaxSAT formulation to infer a maximally large, jointly consistent set of ordering constraints is a practical way to strengthen the oracle without sacrificing exactness; Theorem 4 offers sufficient conditions for entailment.
- Empirical results indicate orders-of-magnitude runtime gains over GOBNILP under both with/without projection settings; the benefit from projection is clearly demonstrated. The easy-hard-easy pattern with increasing constraint density is informative.
- The approach is exact and yields improved sample efficiency when constraints are provided, as evidenced by SHD improvements.

Weaknesses and points for clarification
- Clarity/precision. Theorem 1 is stated as “a CPDAG P satisfies A iff its descendants satisfy A.” This appears too strong as written; the intended property seems to be monotonicity of violation (if P violates A then all descendants violate), which justifies pruning. Please restate and prove formally, making explicit the variable sets at each EC-tree node versus the variable set of A.
- Practicality of class(P) enumeration. The pruning test relies on checking CPDAGs via (1) quick necessary/sufficient tests and (2) exhaustive enumeration of DAGs in class(P) when inconclusive. Since class(P) can be exponentially large, some discussion of how often enumeration occurs, its cost, and any mitigations (e.g., bounded enumeration, SAT-based feasibility check) would strengthen the empirical story.
- MaxSAT encoding details. Clause (3), presented as a sufficient condition for acyclicity over selected ordering constraints, is non-standard and its rationale is not explained. A proof sketch of Theorem 4 and a brief justification of this clause would improve rigor. Also specify whether soft clauses are uniformly weighted and how often the MaxSAT solver times out or returns suboptimal selections.
- Robustness to noisy/inconsistent constraints. All experiments use constraints sampled from ground truth and thus consistent. In practice, background knowledge may be noisy or contradictory. How does the method behave when A is inconsistent? Can the MaxSAT step be adapted to select a maximally consistent subset of ancestral constraints (soft constraints)?
- Scope of empirical comparison. Only GOBNILP is evaluated. While ILP is a natural baseline for non-decomposable constraints, including a CP baseline (van Beek & Hoffmann 2015) or reporting attempts and outcomes would broaden the comparison. An ablation of projected edge vs. ordering constraints (each alone and together) would isolate their contributions to speedups.
- Scaling. The largest problems reported are n up to 20 with BDeu and no parent limits, comparable to prior exact BN learning results. It would be helpful to report memory consumption and behavior beyond 20 variables with varying constraint densities, as well as the oracle’s runtime share versus EC-tree search.
- Presentation issues. The manuscript contains formatting glitches (e.g., stray date text, reference placeholders like “? \Q2015\E”), and some definitions are terse (e.g., closure rules to derive a “maximum set” of ancestral constraints). Cleaning these would aid readability and reproducibility.

Quality
- The approach is technically sound as an exact A* over EC trees with admissible heuristic augmented by provably sound projections. Pruning is sound; projection results are stated with plausible conditions. Some proofs and algorithmic details are omitted; clarifying Theorem 1 and the MaxSAT conditions would improve rigor. The experimental methodology is reproducible and the comparison fair.

Clarity
- Generally well organized with clear motivation. However, several definitions and theorem statements need tightening, and some implementation details (frequency and cost of class enumeration; MaxSAT configuration) should be expanded. Minor formatting errors should be fixed.

Originality
- Novel integration of ancestral constraints into exact structure learning over EC trees, and novel projection of path constraints to decomposable edge and ordering constraints (with a MaxSAT formulation) to strengthen a decomposable-score oracle. This combination is, to my knowledge, new relative to prior BN learning work.

Significance
- Ancestral constraints are practically valuable. The reported speedups over ILP and the demonstrated gains from projection suggest the method will be useful to practitioners needing exact learning with rich prior knowledge. The projection techniques may also be useful beyond this framework.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important limitation of exact BN learning with a principled, exact method.
- Provides practically effective projections that yield large empirical speedups.
- Careful experimental evaluation across datasets, data sizes, and constraint densities.

Arguments against acceptance
- Some theoretical statements lack full clarity/proofs (Theorem 1; MaxSAT clause (3)).
- Evaluation omits noisy/inconsistent constraints and broader baselines; scalability beyond n=20 is not demonstrated.
- Occasional reliance on exponential enumeration without detailed analysis of impact.

Suggestions to improve the manuscript
- Clarify Theorem 1 and provide formal proofs/sketches for Theorems 2–4; justify the MaxSAT encoding, especially clause (3).
- Report how often CPDAG enumeration is invoked, its runtime share, and any pruning heuristics to avoid full enumeration.
- Add experiments with inconsistent/noisy constraints and/or a soft-constraint variant.
- Provide ablations for edge-only vs. ordering-only projection.
- Clean up formatting and references; describe the closure rules used to construct a maximum set of ancestral constraints.
