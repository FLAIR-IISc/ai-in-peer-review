Summary
The paper addresses exact Bayesian network (BN) structure learning under non-decomposable ancestral (path) constraints. Building on the BN graph and EC tree search spaces of Chen et al. (2015, 2016), the authors (i) prune the EC tree by testing whether a CPDAG can be extended to a DAG satisfying a given set of ancestral constraints, and (ii) “project” non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints to strengthen a decomposable-score oracle used as an admissible heuristic in A* search. They give conditions for inferring negative and positive edge constraints from ancestral constraints (Theorems 2–3) and formulate a MaxSAT problem to extract a large, consistent set of ordering constraints entailed by the ancestral constraints (Theorem 4 gives sufficient conditions). Experiments compare their exact approach against GOBNILP (ILP) on benchmark subnetworks with synthetic data and randomly sampled true ancestral constraints, reporting orders-of-magnitude speedups and improved scalability when projected constraints are used.

Relation to prior work
- Score-based exact BN learning with decomposable scores via DP/A* over order graphs (Koivisto & Sood; Silander & Myllymäki; Yuan & Malone) cannot handle non-decomposable constraints. ILP/CP encodings can express path constraints (Jaakkola et al.; Cussens; van Beek & Hoffmann), but empirical evaluation for ancestral constraints has been lacking. Constraint-based learning with path constraints has been studied (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos).
- The paper builds directly on Chen et al. (2015, 2016), extending their BN graph/EC tree framework from non-decomposable scores/priors to non-decomposable constraints, and adds projection to decomposable constraints and a MaxSAT formulation for ordering constraints.

Quality
Strengths:
- The pruning strategy in EC trees is conceptually sound: if no DAG in a CPDAG’s equivalence class can satisfy the constraints, prune. Theorem 1 gives the monotonicity needed for safe pruning in the tree.
- The edge-projection rules are clearly stated, and Theorems 2–3 aim at necessary and sufficient conditions under closure of ancestral constraints. This is a useful bridge from non-decomposable to decomposable constraints.
- The MaxSAT approach for ordering constraints imposes hard logical conditions that preserve soundness (only entailed orderings are selected), keeping the oracle admissible.
- Empirical comparisons against a strong ILP baseline (GOBNILP) and ablations (with/without projection) are appropriate and show consistent gains on the tested instances.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Practicality of CPDAG pruning: When simple path tests are inconclusive, the method “exhaustively enumerates the structures of class(P)” to test satisfiability. This can be exponential and could dominate runtime in hard cases. There is no complexity analysis, bounding argument, or caching strategy described. This is a major algorithmic gap.
- Projection completeness/optimality: For edges, Theorems 2–3 are claimed necessary and sufficient given a “maximum” closure of ancestral constraints, but the closure procedure is not described and proofs are omitted. For orderings, only sufficient conditions are enforced; the MaxSAT yields a maximally large set under these sufficient clauses, not necessarily the true maximal set entailed by A. The impact of this gap on heuristic tightness is not quantified.
- Empirical scope: Experiments are limited to n ≈ 20, synthetic data, and constraints sampled from ground truth. There is no evaluation under noisy or inconsistent constraints, which is common in practice. Comparisons are only to ILP; no CP baseline or other exact solvers supporting path constraints are evaluated. It would also be informative to compare to a decomposable-only baseline that uses projected constraints but not EC-tree pruning, to separate the contributions.
- Reproducibility details are insufficient: enumeration strategy, memoization, MaxSAT encoding size, and the computational cost of the projection (esp. MaxSAT) vs the search are not reported. It is unclear whether the speedups persist when the projection step is accounted for on larger n.
- Clarity and polish: There are typos, formatting issues, and some references are incomplete. Several key statements defer to “details omitted for space,” including the closure algorithm for ancestral constraints.

Clarity
Overall organization is reasonable. The high-level ideas are understandable, but rigor and implementational detail are lacking where it matters (CPDAG pruning, closure computation, complexity). The proofs are not included; at minimum, sketches or a supplemental should be provided. Some notation is overloaded and a few sentences are hard to parse.

Originality
Moderate. The work extends an existing exact search framework (EC tree) to a new constraint class and proposes projection rules and a MaxSAT formulation for exploiting non-decomposable constraints via a decomposable oracle. The idea of deriving edge/order constraints from path constraints is natural, but the specific theorems and the MaxSAT construction are a useful, if incremental, contribution.

Significance
Mixed. Incorporating ancestral constraints into exact BN learning is practically relevant, and the reported speedups over ILP are promising. However, the demonstrated scale is modest, and the reliance on potentially exponential CPDAG enumeration without clear mitigation raises questions about robustness. The lack of experiments with noisy constraints limits impact for real applications.

Pros
- Clear problem motivation; non-decomposable constraints are important and under-supported by mainstream exact methods.
- Conceptually simple integration into EC tree; sound pruning under Theorem 1.
- Useful projection of ancestral constraints to edge and ordering constraints; admissible oracle preserved.
- Empirical gains over a strong ILP baseline; ablation shows projection materially helps.

Cons
- Potentially exponential CPDAG enumeration in pruning with no analysis or engineering details.
- Missing proofs/algorithms for closure; MaxSAT only enforces sufficient conditions; unclear how close to maximal entailment the inferred orderings are.
- Limited empirical scope: small n, no noisy/inconsistent constraints, no CP baseline, and no breakdown of projection overheads.
- Clarity issues and incomplete references.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Provide proofs (or at least detailed sketches) of Theorems 2–4 and the closure procedure for ancestral constraints, plus complexity analyses.
- Explain how often CPDAG enumeration is triggered, how large equivalence classes get in practice, and what optimizations (caching, SAT-based reachability, partial extensions) you use to avoid exponential blowups.
- Report the time spent on projection (edge inference and MaxSAT) and the size of the MaxSAT instances; include an ablation of edges-only vs orders-only projections.
- Evaluate robustness to noisy or inconsistent ancestral constraints (and describe how your method handles inconsistencies).
- Add baselines: CP encodings with path constraints; a decomposable-only search using only projected constraints; and larger n if feasible.
- Improve writing and fix references/formatting issues.

Recommendation
Leaning toward rejection in its current form due to missing theoretical and algorithmic details around pruning and projection, and limited empirical scope. With stronger proofs/analyses and more comprehensive experiments (especially addressing CPDAG enumeration and robustness), this could be a solid contribution. Confidence: high.
