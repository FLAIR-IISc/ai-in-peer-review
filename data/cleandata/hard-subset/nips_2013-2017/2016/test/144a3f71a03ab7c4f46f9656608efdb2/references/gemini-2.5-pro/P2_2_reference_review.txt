**Summary and Relation to Prior Work**

This paper addresses the problem of exact, score-based Bayesian network (BN) structure learning in the presence of ancestral constraints. Ancestral constraints, which specify that a variable X must (or must not) be an ancestor of another variable Y, are a natural form of expert knowledge but are non-decomposable. This non-decomposability makes them incompatible with many state-of-the-art optimal BN learning algorithms that rely on dynamic programming over variable orderings (e.g., Koivisto & Sood, 2004) or A* search on order graphs (e.g., Yuan & Malone, 2013), as these methods fundamentally require decomposable scores and constraints.

The authors build upon the recent EC tree search framework of Chen et al. (2016), which searches over equivalence classes of DAGs (CPDAGs) and uses an "oracle" (an exact solver for decomposable problems) to compute an admissible A* heuristic. The paper's main contributions are twofold:
1.  It adapts the EC tree search to handle ancestral constraints by developing a sound method for pruning nodes (CPDAGs) that are inconsistent with the given constraints.
2.  More significantly, it introduces a novel method to "project" the non-decomposable ancestral constraints into a set of decomposable edge and ordering constraints. This is achieved through a set of logical inference rules for edge constraints and a reduction to MaxSAT for deriving a consistent set of ordering constraints. These inferred decomposable constraints are then passed to the oracle, dramatically tightening the A* heuristic and accelerating the search.

The work is correctly positioned against Integer Linear Programming (ILP) methods, which are the primary existing alternative for exact learning with such non-decomposable constraints. The paper provides, to my knowledge, the first direct and thorough empirical evaluation of this specific task, comparing their proposed search-based approach with a modern ILP solver (GOBNILP).

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality.
*   **Strengths:** The proposed method is sound. The pruning strategy for the EC tree is well-justified (Theorem 1), and the methods for projecting constraints (Theorems 2, 3, and 4) are well-formulated. The core idea of empowering the heuristic oracle with inferred decomposable constraints is both clever and effective. The experimental evaluation is rigorous and convincing. It compares against the most appropriate state-of-the-art baseline (ILP), uses standard benchmarks, and systematically varies key parameters (number of variables, data size, constraint density). The ablation-style comparison between Tables 1 and 2 provides compelling evidence for the efficacy of the constraint projection technique, which is the paper's central technical contribution. The claim of "orders of magnitude" speedup over ILP is clearly substantiated by the results.
*   **Weaknesses:** The work is very complete, with few technical weaknesses. One minor point is that the scalability of the MaxSAT formulation for inferring ordering constraints is not discussed. While it is a pre-processing step, for very large numbers of variables, this could potentially become a bottleneck itself. A brief discussion of its complexity or runtime would have been beneficial.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, though some sections are dense.
*   **Strengths:** The introduction and preliminaries clearly set up the problem and motivate the solution. The distinction between decomposable and non-decomposable constraints is explained well with a clear example. The overall structure follows a logical progression from the base framework to the novel contributions and their evaluation. The experimental tables are easy to interpret and effectively highlight the main findings.
*   **Weaknesses:** Section 5.2 on inferring topological ordering constraints is particularly dense. The example showing the subtlety of inferring ordering constraints is excellent, but the subsequent MaxSAT formulation is presented very concisely. More intuition behind the clauses, especially the "sufficient condition for acyclicity" (clause 3), would improve readability for non-experts in this specific area.

**Originality:** The work presents significant novelty.
*   **Strengths:** While the paper builds on the EC tree framework, its application and extension to ancestral constraints are highly non-trivial. The key innovation is the projection of non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones to improve a search heuristic. This is a powerful and potentially generalizable idea. The systematic method for inferring edge constraints and the MaxSAT formulation for ordering constraints are novel contributions. The paper is the first to provide a practical and highly efficient algorithm for this problem, moving it from a theoretical possibility (in ILP) to a scalable tool.

**Significance:** The results are important and likely to have a strong impact.
*   **Strengths:** The paper tackles a problem of practical importance. Eliciting full network structures from experts is difficult, but providing high-level causal knowledge (e.g., "smoking is an ancestor of lung cancer") is often feasible. This work provides a state-of-the-art tool to integrate such knowledge into the learning process optimally. The dramatic performance gains over ILP significantly advance the state of the art for exact BN learning, pushing the boundary of solvable problem sizes. Furthermore, the results in Table 3, demonstrating that a small percentage of ancestral constraints can improve model accuracy as much as a 4-16x increase in data, provide a powerful quantitative argument for the value of incorporating background knowledge, which is a valuable insight for the broader machine learning community.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a well-motivated and practical problem in structure learning.
*   Presents a novel and highly effective technical contribution (constraint projection) that significantly improves search efficiency.
*   Demonstrates state-of-the-art performance with a rigorous empirical evaluation, showing orders-of-magnitude speedup over the most relevant competitor (ILP).
*   The work is technically sound, clearly presented, and the results are significant for researchers and practitioners in graphical models.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   None of significant weight. Some sections are dense, but this is often necessary in a conference paper format and does not detract from the overall quality and impact of the work.

In conclusion, this is an excellent paper that presents a novel, sound, and highly effective solution to an important problem. It is a complete piece of work with strong theoretical motivation and compelling empirical results. I strongly recommend acceptance.
