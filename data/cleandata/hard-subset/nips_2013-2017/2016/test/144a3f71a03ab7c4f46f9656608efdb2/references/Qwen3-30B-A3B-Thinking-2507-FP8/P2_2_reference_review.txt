

# NIPS Review: "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"

## Summary

This paper addresses an important gap in Bayesian network structure learning: the ability to incorporate non-decomposable ancestral constraints (specifying whether one variable is an ancestor of another) into optimal structure learning. The authors build upon Chen et al.'s EC tree search space framework for learning with non-decomposable scores, extending it to handle ancestral constraints. Their key contributions include: (1) a method for pruning the EC tree based on ancestral constraints, (2) a technique for projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints onto decomposable edge and ordering constraints to empower a standard oracle, and (3) empirical evidence showing their approach is orders-of-magnitude faster than ILP-based approaches for structure learning with ancestral constraints.

The paper makes a clear connection to previous work in Bayesian network structure learning, particularly the work of Chen et al. (2015, 2016) on EC trees and the limitations of mainstream approaches that assume decomposable scores. It also relates to prior work on constraint-based learning (e.g., Borboudakis and Tsamardinos, 2012, 2013) but focuses on score-based learning with non-decomposable constraints.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound and well-supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical results. The authors provide clear theorems (Theorems 1-4) establishing the correctness of their pruning and constraint projection methods. The constraint projection algorithms are well-motivated and their soundness is rigorously proven.

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed. The authors simulate multiple structure learning problems from standard Bayesian network benchmarks, varying key parameters (number of variables, dataset size, and percentage of constraints). They compare their approach against GOBNILP, an ILP-based system, with and without constraint projection, showing consistent and significant speed improvements (often orders of magnitude) across different problem sizes. The results are well-presented in Tables 1-3, with appropriate statistical reporting (averaged over 50 datasets).

The paper also demonstrates a thoughtful evaluation of the impact of constraints on learning accuracy (Table 3 shows structural Hamming distance), revealing that even a small number of ancestral constraints (10%-25%) can have a similar impact on model quality as substantially increasing the training data size.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The technical concepts are clearly defined, and the progression from problem statement to solution is logical and easy to follow. The figures (Figures 1a and 1b) effectively illustrate the BN graph and EC tree search spaces. The paper provides sufficient detail for an expert reader to reproduce the results.

The distinction between decomposable and non-decomposable constraints is particularly well-explained, with a clear example showing why ancestral constraints cannot be enforced by standard approaches like K2 or the order graph. The explanation of the EC tree and its relationship to Markov equivalence classes is also particularly clear.

## Originality

This paper makes a significant original contribution to the field. While the problem of incorporating background knowledge into Bayesian network learning has been studied (e.g., Borboudakis and Tsamardinos, 2012, 2013), this paper specifically addresses the challenging case of non-decomposable ancestral constraints, which have been largely ignored by mainstream optimal structure learning approaches.

The core innovation—projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints onto decomposable edge and ordering constraints—is novel and well-executed. The paper demonstrates how to infer edge constraints (Theorems 2-3) and ordering constraints (via MaxSAT) from ancestral constraints, which is a technically sophisticated contribution that has not been previously explored in this context.

Compared to recent NIPS papers on Bayesian network structure learning (e.g., those using deep learning approaches or new score functions), this work takes a fundamentally different approach by addressing a critical gap in the theoretical foundations of structure learning with constraints.

## Significance

The significance of this work is substantial. Ancestral constraints represent a powerful way to incorporate domain knowledge into Bayesian network learning, which is crucial for real-world applications where causal relationships are known. The paper demonstrates that incorporating such constraints can dramatically improve learning efficiency (orders of magnitude faster) without sacrificing model quality.

The paper also provides a practical method for leveraging existing structure learning algorithms (which assume decomposable scores) to handle non-decomposable constraints, which is a valuable contribution for practitioners. The finding that a small number of ancestral constraints can have a similar impact on model quality as substantially increasing training data size (Table 3) is particularly significant for applications where data collection is expensive or difficult.

The paper advances the state of the art in Bayesian network structure learning, particularly for problems with non-decomposable constraints, which has been a longstanding limitation in the field.

## Weaknesses

1. The paper could better explain why the EC tree is the appropriate search space for this problem, beyond citing Chen et al. It would strengthen the argument to directly compare the EC tree with other search spaces (e.g., order graphs, ILP) for handling ancestral constraints.

2. While the paper acknowledges the limitations of their approach, it could provide more thorough discussion of the limitations of the constraint projection method. For example, how does the method scale when the number of ancestral constraints grows significantly? What happens when constraints are inconsistent?

3. The experimental evaluation, while strong, could be expanded to include more baseline approaches. For instance, the paper mentions constraint programming (CP) approaches but doesn't compare with them as extensively as with ILP.

4. The paper could better clarify the relationship between their work and recent advances in Bayesian network structure learning, particularly those using deep learning or other novel approaches.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong, high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. The problem addressed is important, the solution is technically sound and well-justified, and the empirical results are compelling. The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way, showing orders-of-magnitude improvements in efficiency for learning with ancestral constraints.

The paper meets all four NIPS review criteria at a high level: it is of high quality (technically sound with strong theoretical and experimental support), clear (well-organized and well-written), original (addresses a gap in the literature with novel techniques), and significant (has practical implications for incorporating domain knowledge into Bayesian network learning).

## Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Addresses a significant gap in Bayesian network structure learning (handling non-decomposable ancestral constraints)
- Presents a technically sound solution with strong theoretical foundations (Theorems 1-4)
- Provides compelling empirical evidence of orders-of-magnitude improvement over ILP-based approaches
- Demonstrates practical significance (small number of constraints can replace large amounts of data)
- Well-written, well-organized, and clear exposition of complex concepts

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Could provide more thorough discussion of limitations of constraint projection method
- Could include more comprehensive comparison with alternative approaches (e.g., constraint programming)
- Could better connect with recent advances in Bayesian network learning beyond traditional methods

## Recommendation

I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS. It makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning, addressing a problem that has been largely ignored by mainstream approaches. The theoretical contributions are sound, the empirical results are compelling, and the work has clear practical significance. While there are minor areas for improvement, the paper is of high quality and meets all the criteria for NIPS acceptance.

The paper advances the state of the art in Bayesian network structure learning, particularly for problems with non-decomposable constraints, which is an important and challenging problem in the field. The authors have provided a complete framework for incorporating ancestral constraints into optimal structure learning, and their empirical results demonstrate substantial practical benefits.
