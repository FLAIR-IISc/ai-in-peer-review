Summary
The paper tackles exact Bayesian network structure learning with hard ancestral constraints (X is/is not an ancestor of Y). Main contributions: (1) extending the EC tree search space (built from CPDAGs/equivalence classes) to enforce non-decomposable ancestral constraints via pruning; (2) projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints to empower oracle-based heuristics—specifically, all entailed edge-presence/absence constraints (via closure using α/β sets and Theorems 2–3) and a maximally large, jointly consistent set of topological ordering constraints via a MaxSAT formulation (with sufficient acyclicity conditions, Theorem 4); and (3) an empirical study comparing the proposed approach against an ILP baseline (GOBNILP), showing orders-of-magnitude speedups and demonstrating an easy–hard–easy profile as constraint density varies, plus benefits for structure accuracy (SHD).

Relation to prior work
The work builds on Chen et al. (2015, 2016) BN/EC graphs and oracle-guided A* for non-decomposable scoring. It contrasts with dynamic programming/order-graph-based exact methods (Koivisto & Sood; Silander & Myllymäki; Yuan & Malone) which assume decomposability and thus cannot directly handle ancestral constraints, and with ILP/CP methods (Jaakkola et al.; Cussens; Bartlett & Cussens; van Beek & Hoffmann) that can encode such constraints but have different performance tradeoffs. It also relates to constraint-based learning with path constraints (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos), but here the focus is exact score-based learning under hard ancestral constraints.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The pruning strategy in EC trees is conceptually sound: if a CPDAG/node cannot be extended to any DAG satisfying the ancestral constraints, prune it and its subtree. The paper provides efficient sufficient checks (directed path implies violation of negative constraint; no partially directed path implies violation of positive constraint) and falls back to enumerating class(P) when inconclusive, which is complete albeit potentially exponential. The projection rules for entailed negative and positive edges (Theorems 2–3) are well-motivated; the α/β reasoning is clear, and the necessity/sufficiency claims appear correct under the “maximal closure” assumption on ancestral constraints.
- The MaxSAT formulation for ordering constraints uses hard constraints to ensure transitivity and acyclicity sufficiency (Theorem 4) and soft clauses to encourage inferring Y<X from X⊀Y. This gives a safe but possibly conservative set O, suitable for empowering a decomposable-score oracle. This is a reasonable design.
- Empirics: The evaluation is careful: multiple benchmarks, varying n, data size, and constraint density; comparisons both with and without projected constraints to isolate their effect; time/memory limits; and reporting SHD to ground truth. The reported, large speedups vs. ILP are consistent with leveraging a strong oracle and aggressive pruning.

Caveats/limitations:
- Some theoretical details are omitted. In particular, the statement and role of Theorem 1 are confusing as written (“P satisfies A iff its descendants satisfy A”), especially given nodes in the EC tree span different variable subsets. The paper needs to clearly define satisfaction for partial CPDAGs vs. full variable sets and clarify the monotonicity property it uses for pruning.
- The enumeration of class(P) as a fallback can be exponential; the paper should discuss how often this occurs in practice and whether there are bounds or heuristics to mitigate worst-case blow-ups.
- For projecting edge constraints, the “details omitted for space” on computing the maximal closure of ancestral constraints (e.g., the specific inference rules beyond simple transitivity and cycle-prevention) limits reproducibility; a precise algorithm or appendix is needed.
- The MaxSAT acyclicity constraint (Condition 3) is non-standard and only sufficient; more intuition/derivation and an ablation (impact on inferred O and runtime) would help. Define “closed set” of orderings precisely.
- Robustness: All constraints are assumed correct/hard. No study of inconsistent/noisy constraints (or soft constraints) is provided; this is important in practice.
- Baselines: Only ILP (GOBNILP) is evaluated; CP-based approaches are mentioned but not tested. Also, comparison to exact order-graph methods augmented with order constraints (where applicable) might be informative.
- Scalability: Results up to n≈20 variables for BDeu and unbounded parents are competitive with exact methods but still modest; discussion of scalability bottlenecks (oracle cost, constraint projection, CPDAG enumeration) would be useful.

Clarity
Overall, the paper is well-organized and readable, with a clear motivation and consistent notation. However:
- Clarify Theorem 1 and precisely define when pruning is sound at non-leaf nodes (different variable subsets) versus at full CPDAGs.
- Provide the full set of closure rules for ancestral constraints and a pseudocode for the edge-projection algorithm.
- Expand on the MaxSAT encoding, especially constraint (3) and the definition of a “closed” set O.
- Minor editorial issues and citation formatting glitches should be fixed.

Originality
The integration of hard ancestral constraints into an EC-tree/A*-based exact learner, together with principled projection to decomposable edge and ordering constraints (including the MaxSAT approach), appears novel. Prior ILP/CP work could handle such constraints, but this paper shows a different algorithmic path with significant empirical gains.

Significance
Allowing domain knowledge in the form of ancestral relations while retaining exactness is valuable for causal discovery and structure learning. The method demonstrates strong speedups over a widely used ILP baseline and shows that a modest fraction of constraints can yield accuracy gains comparable to large increases in data. This should be of interest to the BN/PGM and causal discovery communities.

Pros
- Clear problem with practical importance; exact solution.
- Novel, well-motivated integration of non-decomposable constraints into EC-tree search.
- Effective projection to decomposable constraints; MaxSAT formulation for orderings.
- Strong empirical improvements over ILP; informative analyses (easy–hard–easy, SHD trends).

Cons
- Some theoretical statements lack clarity (notably Theorem 1); several details omitted.
- Potential exponential fallback (class(P) enumeration) not analyzed.
- Only hard constraints; no robustness to noisy/inconsistent constraints.
- Limited baseline diversity and scalability.

Questions for authors
- Please clarify Theorem 1 and the precise monotonicity used for pruning across levels of the EC tree.
- How often does class(P) enumeration occur in practice, and how large are these classes?
- Can you provide the full set of closure rules and pseudocode for edge-projection?
- How sensitive is performance to the MaxSAT solver and encoding choices? Any limits as n grows?
- What happens with contradictory/noisy ancestral constraints? Can your framework accommodate soft constraints?

Recommendation
Lean accept. The paper addresses an important gap with a technically sound, novel approach and compelling empirical results. Clarifying the theoretical details and providing fuller algorithmic descriptions would strengthen the paper further.
