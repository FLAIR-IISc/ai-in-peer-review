Summary
The paper tackles optimal Bayesian network (BN) structure learning under non-decomposable ancestral constraints (asserting ancestor/non-ancestor relations). Existing exact methods largely rely on decomposable scores/priors and thus cannot directly accommodate such constraints, aside from edge/order-modular ones; ILP/CP can encode path relations but have not been evaluated for this use case. Building on the BN-graph/EC-tree framework of Chen et al. (2015, 2016), the authors (i) integrate ancestral constraints by pruning EC-tree nodes (CPDAGs) that cannot realize the constraints, (ii) derive tests to detect violations or impossibility at the CPDAG level (with a fallback to equivalence-class enumeration), and (iii) “project” non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints to empower an oracle (an optimal decomposable-score learner used as a heuristic in A* over the EC tree). Edge constraints are inferred via closure rules (Theorems 2–3 using α/β sets); ordering constraints are selected by a MaxSAT formulation to find a largest jointly entailed set (with sufficient conditions for soundness; Theorem 4). Experiments on benchmarks (random subnets of ALARM/ANDES/CHILD/CPCS/HEPAR2) show orders-of-magnitude speedups over GOBNILP with ancestral constraints, and demonstrate that projecting constraints dramatically tightens the search. Additional results show an easy–hard–easy trend as the proportion of constraints increases, scalability to ~20 variables, and improvements in structural accuracy with more constraints.

Relation to prior work
- Extends EC-tree shortest-path search (Chen et al. 2016) and the idea of using a decomposable oracle for non-decomposable objectives (Chen et al. 2015).
- Contrasts with score-based DP/A* over order graphs that cannot enforce non-decomposable constraints.
- Complements ILP/CP formulations capable of encoding path constraints (Jaakkola et al. 2010; Bartlett & Cussens 2015; van Beek & Hoffmann 2015), providing the first empirical evaluation specifically for ancestral constraints and showing strong gains.
- Differs from constraint-based/path-prior work (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos 2012/2013) by providing exact model selection with hard ancestral constraints and an oracle-empowered search.

Strengths
- Clear, impactful problem: integrating meaningful causal background knowledge (ancestry) into exact BN learning.
- Conceptual novelty: using EC-tree pruning with CPDAG-level feasibility checks for ancestral constraints; projection of path constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints to strengthen an exact oracle; MaxSAT-based selection of a maximal consistent set of order constraints.
- Soundness claims are stated formally (Theorems 1–4), with intuitively plausible rules for edge entailment and a principled MaxSAT formulation.
- Strong empirical results: consistent orders-of-magnitude improvements over an established ILP solver (GOBNILP) in the ancestral-constraint regime; ablation showing that projected constraints are key to efficiency.
- Practical significance: shows that modest amounts of causal knowledge can substitute for substantial data increases.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Scale: experiments top out at n=20. While in line with exact BDeu results, it would be valuable to test larger n where constraints meaningfully shrink the space. Also, comparisons to ILP are run with unlimited indegree, known to favor heuristic/A* search; a complementary evaluation with bounded indegree would strengthen claims.
- CPDAG pruning test: when simple reachability checks are inconclusive, the method enumerates class(P), which can be exponential. The paper would benefit from more details or alternative polynomial tests (e.g., orientation rules) to avoid enumeration, or at least empirical evidence that enumeration is rare.
- MaxSAT formulation: constraint set 3 (“sufficient condition for acyclicity”) is nonstandard; more intuition/proof sketch would help. Runtime of the MaxSAT stage and its sensitivity to problem size are not reported.
- Robustness: constraints are sampled from ground truth. There is no study of noisy/inconsistent constraints, which is realistic in practice. How does the method detect/handle contradictions? What is the behavior when the MaxSAT-derived order constraints conflict with the true feasible region (e.g., due to solver suboptimality)?
- Inference of a “maximum” closure of ancestral constraints is mentioned but algorithmic details and complexity are omitted; similarly, the complexity of the edge-projection procedure (Theorems 2–3) is not analyzed.
- Comparisons: CP-based baselines are discussed but not evaluated. Given their ability to express path constraints, including a CP baseline would make the empirical case stronger.

Questions for the authors
1. How often does equivalence-class enumeration occur in CPDAG pruning, and what is its cost in practice? Can you replace it with stronger CPDAG orientation reasoning?
2. What happens with contradictory or noisy ancestral constraints? Can your framework detect infeasibility early and return a minimal conflicting subset?
3. How large are the projected ordering sets (and MaxSAT runtime) as n and the proportion of constraints grow? Any ablation separating the gain from edge vs. order projections?
4. Could you include experiments with bounded indegree and a CP baseline?
5. Are Theorems 2–3 complete for edge entailment, or conservative? Any empirical measure of projection tightness?

Clarity
Overall clear and well organized, but there are formatting glitches (dates, figure/table references, some typos) and several “details omitted for space” in key algorithmic steps. Adding pseudo-code/complexities for closure and projection, and clearer explanation of the MaxSAT constraints, would improve reproducibility.

Significance and originality
The paper introduces a compelling and, to my knowledge, novel route to exact BN learning with hard ancestral constraints by marrying EC-tree search with constraint projection and an oracle. The demonstrated speedups and practical insights (constraints vs. data) are valuable. Despite some missing details and limited scale, the conceptual contribution is strong and likely to influence subsequent work.

Recommendation
Accept (clear accept). High novelty with a bold, well-motivated approach and strong empirical gains in an important setting. Some methodological details and broader comparisons would strengthen the camera-ready version, but the core ideas are sound and impactful.

Confidence
High on the technical assessment and relation to prior exact BN learning; medium on empirical breadth due to missing tables/figures in the submission.
