

# Review of "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"

## Summary
This paper addresses the problem of learning Bayesian network structures with ancestral constraints, which specify cause-and-effect relationships (e.g., X is an ancestor of Y) and are non-decomposable constraints that cannot be handled by standard score-based learning approaches. The authors build upon Chen et al. (2015, 2016), who introduced the BN graph and EC tree search spaces for learning with non-decomposable scores. They extend this framework to handle ancestral constraints by developing a sound pruning technique for the EC tree, and propose methods to project ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints that can empower an oracle (a standard decomposable-score learning algorithm). The paper demonstrates through extensive experiments that their approach is orders-of-magnitude faster than ILP-based methods for structure learning with ancestral constraints.

## Quality
The technical content is sound, with formal proofs (Theorem 1) supporting the pruning technique and well-justified algorithms for constraint projection (Theorems 2-4). The authors carefully address the non-decomposable nature of ancestral constraints and provide a rigorous method for handling them. The experimental design is thorough, with multiple benchmark networks (ALARM, ANDES, CHILD, CPCS54, HEPAR2), varying problem sizes (n=12-20 variables), dataset sizes (N=512-8192), and constraint percentages (p=10-50%). The results are well-presented, showing consistent improvements over the ILP-based GOBNILP approach across all configurations. The paper acknowledges limitations, such as the need for structure enumeration in some pruning cases, and provides appropriate statistical analysis (averaged over 50 datasets).

## Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The technical concepts (ancestral constraints, EC trees, constraint projection) are introduced with precise definitions and illustrative examples. The figures (1a and 1b) effectively demonstrate the BN graph and EC tree structures. The paper provides sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the results, including specific parameters (BDeu scores with equivalent sample size of 1), computational setup (2.67GHz Intel Xeon X5650 CPU), and experimental design (50 datasets from 5 benchmark networks). The writing is concise and avoids unnecessary jargon while maintaining technical precision.

## Originality
The paper makes a significant contribution by addressing a gap in the literature: the incorporation of non-decomposable ancestral constraints into optimal Bayesian network structure learning. While Chen et al. (2015, 2016) introduced the EC tree for non-decomposable scores, they did not consider ancestral constraints. The authors' key novelty is the method to project ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints, which they demonstrate is crucial for the efficiency of their approach. This work is distinct from previous NIPS papers (e.g., Borboudakis and Tsamardinos, 2012, 2013) that used path constraints in constraint-based learning rather than optimal structure learning with non-decomposable scores. The paper also provides the first empirical evaluation of ILP approaches for learning with ancestral constraints, which the authors note "has not been previously evaluated."

## Significance
The paper addresses an important practical problem: incorporating background knowledge (in the form of causal relationships) into Bayesian network learning. The results demonstrate that even a small number of ancestral constraints (10-25%) can have a similar impact on model accuracy as increasing training data size by a factor of 4-16, highlighting the value of background knowledge. The approach is orders-of-magnitude faster than ILP-based alternatives, making optimal structure learning with constraints feasible for larger networks (up to 20 variables, comparable to state-of-the-art score-based approaches). This could enable applications in domains where causal knowledge is available but data is limited (e.g., medical diagnosis, biological networks).

## Strengths
- Solves a well-defined problem with clear practical motivation
- Provides sound theoretical foundation with formal proofs
- Demonstrates significant empirical improvements over existing methods
- Presents a comprehensive experimental evaluation across multiple benchmarks
- Offers practical insights about the impact of background knowledge
- Well-written and clearly organized

## Weaknesses
- Limited discussion of the computational complexity of the constraint projection step
- Does not compare against constraint-based approaches like Borboudakis and Tsamardinos (2012, 2013), which use path constraints but in a different learning framework
- The scalability to very large networks (beyond 20 variables) isn't thoroughly explored
- The paper could provide more insight into when the approach might fail (beyond the cases requiring structure enumeration)

## Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments
**Pro:** The paper makes a technically sound contribution to a meaningful problem in Bayesian network learning. The proposed method is novel, well-motivated, and empirically validated with significant performance improvements. The work aligns with NIPS's focus on advancing machine learning with sound theoretical foundations and practical impact.

**Con:** The paper does not compare against constraint-based approaches that use path constraints (Borboudakis and Tsamardinos), which might be relevant to the problem of handling ancestral constraints. The scalability to very large networks is not thoroughly demonstrated.

## Overall Assessment
This paper presents a technically sound, novel, and significant contribution to Bayesian network structure learning. The authors effectively address an important gap in the literature by extending the EC tree framework to handle ancestral constraints, providing both theoretical foundations and empirical validation of their approach. The work is clearly written, well-organized, and makes a demonstrable advance over existing methods. The paper meets all NIPS quality criteria and is a strong candidate for acceptance.
