

# Peer Review: "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper addresses a significant gap in Bayesian network structure learning: the incorporation of non-decomposable ancestral constraints. Ancestral constraints specify relationships between variables (e.g., "X is an ancestor of Y" or "X is not an ancestor of Y"), which are common in applications requiring causal knowledge but are challenging to integrate into standard structure learning approaches. The authors propose a method that leverages the EC tree search space (introduced by Chen et al. [2016]) to efficiently incorporate these constraints. Their key contributions include:

1. A sound method for pruning the EC tree based on ancestral constraints
2. A novel technique for inferring decomposable edge and ordering constraints from non-decomposable ancestral constraints
3. Empirical evidence demonstrating that their approach is orders-of-magnitude more efficient than ILP-based approaches (specifically GOBNILP) for structure learning with ancestral constraints

The paper builds upon Chen et al.'s recent work on EC trees [Chen et al., 2016] and extends it to handle non-decomposable constraints. It relates to prior work on structure learning with constraints, including constraint-based methods (e.g., PC algorithm) and ILP/CP approaches for enforcing constraints [Bartlett and Cussens, 2015, van Beek and Hoffmann, 2015]. The authors also acknowledge the work of Borboudakis and Tsamardinos [2012, 2013] who used ancestral constraints in constraint-based learning, but note that their approach is different as it focuses on score-based learning with exact optimization.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound and makes a significant contribution to the field. The authors provide rigorous theoretical foundations for their approach, including formal proofs of their pruning method (Theorem 1) and constraint inference algorithms (Theorems 2-4). The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, covering multiple benchmark networks (ALARM, ANDES, CHILD, CPCS54, HEPAR2) with varying numbers of variables, dataset sizes, and constraint percentages. The authors carefully compare their approach against GOBNILP (an ILP-based system) both with and without the projected constraints, demonstrating the substantial impact of their constraint projection technique.

The paper is a complete piece of work rather than a position paper, with well-defined methods, thorough experiments, and clear results. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, such as the need to enumerate structures when ancestral constraint pruning is inconclusive, and they provide empirical evidence about the impact of different constraint types.

## Clarity

The paper is well-written and well-organized, with a logical flow from problem statement to solution to evaluation. The technical concepts are introduced progressively, with helpful figures (Figure 1) illustrating the BN graph and EC tree. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined throughout. The authors clearly explain the relationship between ancestral constraints and decomposable constraints, which is crucial for understanding the paper's contribution.

The paper provides sufficient detail for experts to understand and potentially reproduce the results. The experimental setup is clearly described, including the benchmark networks, constraint generation method, and evaluation metrics (structural Hamming distance). The authors also include details about the computational resources used and the software tools employed (EVASOLVER for MaxSAT).

## Originality

The paper makes a clear original contribution by addressing a specific problem that has been largely overlooked in the Bayesian network structure learning literature. While previous work has considered edge constraints and order constraints [Koivisto and Sood, 2004, Parviainen and Koivisto, 2013], this paper specifically addresses ancestral constraints, which are non-decomposable and therefore more challenging to incorporate.

The approach of projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints onto decomposable edge and ordering constraints is novel. The authors provide both theoretical justification and practical algorithms for this projection, which is a significant methodological contribution. The use of the EC tree search space (a relatively recent development) to handle these constraints is also innovative, as it provides a more efficient search space than the BN graph.

The paper properly cites relevant prior work, including Chen et al.'s EC tree work [Chen et al., 2016] and the ILP/CP approaches for constraint handling [Bartlett and Cussens, 2015, van Beek and Hoffmann, 2015]. The authors clearly articulate how their work differs from previous contributions.

## Significance

The paper addresses a practically important problem: incorporating domain knowledge (in the form of ancestral constraints) into Bayesian network structure learning. The empirical results demonstrate that even a small number of ancestral constraints (10-25%) can have a similar impact on model accuracy as significantly increasing the training dataset size (from 512 to 8192 samples), which is a highly valuable insight for practitioners.

The efficiency gains are substantial, with the proposed approach being orders-of-magnitude faster than the ILP-based GOBNILP system. This makes the approach more practical for real-world applications where structure learning can be computationally expensive. The scalability to 20 variables is also notable, as it is comparable to state-of-the-art score-based approaches for BDeu scores.

The paper advances the state of the art in Bayesian network structure learning by providing a principled approach for handling non-decomposable constraints, which is a significant limitation of many existing methods.

## Strengths

1. The paper addresses a meaningful problem with practical applications in causal discovery and knowledge incorporation.
2. The theoretical foundation is strong, with clear proofs for the key contributions.
3. The empirical evaluation is thorough, covering multiple benchmarks and varying parameter settings.
4. The results clearly demonstrate the substantial efficiency gains of the proposed approach.
5. The paper makes a clear distinction between decomposable and non-decomposable constraints, which is crucial for understanding the problem.
6. The constraint projection technique is clever and has significant practical impact.
7. The paper provides a useful analysis of the trade-offs between constraint types and their impact on search efficiency.

## Weaknesses

1. The paper does not thoroughly compare with other constraint-based approaches (e.g., constraint-based learning methods like PC algorithm) that might handle ancestral constraints differently. While the authors acknowledge Borboudakis and Tsamardinos [2012, 2013], they don't fully explore how their score-based approach differs from constraint-based methods.

2. The theoretical analysis of the complexity of the constraint projection algorithms (especially the MaxSAT reduction) could be more detailed. While the paper mentions that the MaxSAT approach is efficient, it doesn't provide complexity analysis or empirical comparison with alternative constraint inference methods.

3. The paper assumes BDeu scores with an equivalent sample size of 1, which might not reflect all practical scenarios. It would be valuable to mention how the approach would extend to other scoring functions.

4. The paper doesn't discuss potential limitations when dealing with very large numbers of ancestral constraints or when constraints are inconsistent. While the paper mentions that the EC tree pruning is sound, it doesn't address how the method handles contradictory constraints.

## Arguments for and against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- The paper addresses a meaningful problem with clear practical relevance
- The theoretical contributions are sound and well-justified
- The empirical results demonstrate significant efficiency improvements (orders-of-magnitude)
- The approach is scalable to networks with 20 variables, which is competitive with state-of-the-art
- The constraint projection technique is novel and has clear practical impact
- The paper is well-written and clearly organized

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- The comparison with constraint-based learning methods (e.g., PC algorithm) is limited
- The complexity analysis of the constraint projection algorithms is not thorough
- The paper doesn't explore how the approach handles inconsistent constraints
- The focus on BDeu scores might limit generalizability to other scoring functions

## Overall Recommendation

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning by providing a principled approach for incorporating non-decomposable ancestral constraints. The theoretical foundation is strong, the empirical evaluation is comprehensive, and the results demonstrate clear practical advantages over existing approaches. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and addresses an important gap in the literature.

While there are some minor weaknesses regarding comparison with constraint-based methods and complexity analysis, these do not substantially undermine the paper's contributions. The approach has clear potential for practical application, and the efficiency gains demonstrated are substantial.

I recommend acceptance of this paper for NIPS. It represents a significant advance in Bayesian network structure learning that will be of interest to both researchers and practitioners in the field.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Include a brief discussion comparing the proposed approach with constraint-based learning methods (like PC algorithm) that also handle ancestral constraints, highlighting the key differences in approach and potential advantages.

2. Provide a more detailed analysis of the complexity of the constraint projection algorithms, particularly the MaxSAT reduction, and include a comparison with alternative constraint inference methods.

3. Consider adding a small example of inconsistent constraints and how the approach handles them, to strengthen the discussion of limitations.

4. Briefly discuss how the approach might be extended to other scoring functions beyond BDeu.

5. In the experiments, consider including a comparison with a more recent ILP-based approach (e.g., Cussens et al. [2013]) to ensure the comparison is with the most relevant state-of-the-art methods.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a clear contribution to the field and should be accepted for presentation at NIPS.
