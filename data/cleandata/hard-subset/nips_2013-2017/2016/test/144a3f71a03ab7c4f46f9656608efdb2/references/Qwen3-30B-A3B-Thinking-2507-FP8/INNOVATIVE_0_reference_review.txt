

# Review of "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"

This paper presents a novel approach to Bayesian network structure learning that incorporates ancestral constraints, which are non-decomposable and thus challenging for most existing methods. The authors build on Chen et al.'s recent work on non-decomposable scores and introduce the EC tree search space, along with a method to project non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints that can be used to empower existing oracles. The key insight is that by inferring decomposable constraints from ancestral ones, they can leverage efficient oracles (like those based on the order graph) to navigate the search space under non-decomposable constraints.

The paper makes several significant contributions: (1) it formalizes how to prune the EC tree using ancestral constraints; (2) it develops a theoretically sound method for projecting ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints; and (3) it demonstrates orders-of-magnitude improvements over ILP-based approaches in empirical evaluations. The authors effectively show that even a small percentage (10%-25%) of ancestral constraints can have a similar impact on model accuracy as substantially increasing training data size (e.g., from 512 to 2048 samples).

This work is highly original and addresses a meaningful gap in Bayesian network learning. While most structure learning methods assume decomposable scores (limiting their ability to incorporate background knowledge like causal relationships), this paper directly tackles the non-decomposable nature of ancestral constraints. The constraint projection technique is particularly elegant, transforming a challenging problem into one solvable by existing efficient oracles. The authors properly contextualize their work against related approaches (e.g., ILP/CP methods) and clearly articulate how their approach differs.

The significance of this work is substantial. In real-world applications, domain knowledge often takes the form of causal relationships (ancestral constraints), and incorporating this knowledge can dramatically reduce the search space and improve model quality. The empirical results demonstrate that their approach scales to 20 variables (comparable to state-of-the-art methods), with the most impressive finding being that a small number of ancestral constraints can yield results equivalent to much larger datasets. This has important practical implications for reducing data collection costs in applications where domain knowledge is available.

The paper is technically sound with clear theoretical results (Theorems 2-4) and comprehensive empirical validation across multiple benchmark networks (ALARM, ANDES, CHILD, CPCS54, HEPAR2). The experimental setup is well-designed, varying key parameters (number of variables, dataset size, constraint percentage) and reporting both runtime and model accuracy (structural Hamming distance). The comparison to GOBNILP (an ILP-based system) is particularly compelling, showing consistent orders-of-magnitude speedups.

Minor weaknesses include limited discussion of the computational complexity of the constraint projection step (particularly for large numbers of ancestral constraints) and the fact that experiments are based on simulated data. However, these are relatively minor compared to the paper's significant contribution. The authors acknowledge that the EC tree approach is most effective when the oracle is empowered with projected constraints, which is well-supported by their results.

This paper exemplifies the kind of bold, novel approach that NIPS values. While the problem of Bayesian network structure learning is well-studied, the specific challenge of incorporating non-decomposable ancestral constraints has been largely overlooked. The authors' solution is both theoretically sound and empirically validated, with clear practical implications. The constraint projection technique is particularly clever and could potentially be applied to other non-decomposable constraints in graphical model learning.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making it accessible to the NIPS audience. The connection to previous work (Chen et al., ILP approaches) is properly contextualized, and the relationship to the broader field of Bayesian network learning is well-explained.

In summary, this paper makes a significant contribution to Bayesian network structure learning by addressing a previously overlooked challenge (incorporating ancestral constraints) with a novel and effective approach. The method is theoretically sound, empirically validated, and has clear practical significance. While minor limitations exist, the novelty and impact of this work far outweigh these concerns.

**Recommendation**: Accept

**Strengths**: Novel problem formulation, elegant constraint projection technique, strong empirical results showing orders-of-magnitude improvements, clear theoretical grounding, practical significance.

**Weaknesses**: Limited discussion of constraint projection complexity, simulated data experiments (though this is standard practice in structure learning).
