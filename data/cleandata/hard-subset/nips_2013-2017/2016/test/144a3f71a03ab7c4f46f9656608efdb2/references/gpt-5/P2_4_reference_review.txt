Summary
The paper tackles optimal Bayesian network (BN) structure learning in the presence of non-decomposable ancestral (path) constraints. Mainstream exact methods (dynamic programming on the order graph, A*, etc.) rely on decomposable scores and constraints, which prevents them from directly handling path constraints. The authors adapt the BN graph/EC tree perspective of Chen et al. (2015, 2016): search over DAGs/CPDAGs using A*, prune nodes that violate ancestral constraints, and use an oracle (a decomposable-score optimizer such as URLearning) to supply admissible heuristics. The key contribution is projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints to “empower the oracle”: (i) rules for entailed edge presence/absence (Theorems 2–3) and (ii) a MaxSAT encoding to infer a maximally large, sound set of precedence constraints (Theorem 4). Experiments compare against an ILP approach (GOBNILP) with and without projected constraints, showing orders-of-magnitude speedups and a clear easy–hard–easy pattern as the proportion of constraints increases. Accuracy (SHD) improves with more data and constraints.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The overall framework is well-motivated. Pruning on the EC tree is justified by the monotonicity of adding leaves: adding a leaf cannot “fix” a violated ancestral relation among existing variables. The projection rules for edge constraints (Theorems 2–3) are plausible and intuitive: negative edges are induced if any ancestor–descendant pair implied by the constraint sets forbids a path; positive edges are induced when all indirect X→Z→Y routes are blocked by ancestry constraints. The MaxSAT formulation for ordering constraints is conservative (sufficient conditions), which preserves admissibility of the oracle heuristic when these constraints are passed on.
- Completeness and proofs: Some proofs and key algorithmic details are omitted “for space” (e.g., closure rules for generating a maximal set of ancestral constraints; proofs of Theorems 2–4; soundness/completeness of the CPDAG pruning test beyond simple path checks). This limits reproducibility and makes it harder to assess corner cases (e.g., when class(P) enumeration is needed).
- Practicality: The need to enumerate class(P) when partial path tests are inconclusive could be expensive; the paper does not quantify how often this happens or its impact on run time. Similarly, the time spent in MaxSAT is not reported. Nevertheless, empirical results strongly suggest the overall pipeline is efficient in practice on the tested scales.
- Empirical evaluation: The comparison to GOBNILP is fair in that both use the same precomputed family scores and, when applicable, receive the same projected constraints. A broad experimental sweep across networks, data sizes, and constraint densities with 50 replicates per setting is solid. Scaling is demonstrated up to n=20 variables, which is consistent with exact BN learning literature for BDeu.

Clarity
- The paper is mostly clear and well-structured. The high-level idea, the role of the oracle, and the benefit of projection are well explained.
- Some statements are confusing or likely misstated. Theorem 1 reads “P satisfies A iff its descendants satisfy A.” Interpreted as “if P violates A, all descendants violate A” this is correct and sufficient for pruning; the converse as written is odd and should be clarified.
- Important algorithmic steps are only sketched (closure procedure for A; exact CPDAG violation test and when enumeration is triggered; MaxSAT encoding details), which hinders reproducibility.
- There are formatting and citation glitches in the references.

Originality
- Integrating non-decomposable ancestral constraints into exact BN learning by combining EC-tree search with pruning and oracle-empowered heuristics is novel.
- The projection of ancestral constraints into entailed edge and large sets of precedence constraints (via MaxSAT) appears original and practically impactful.
- The work is well positioned relative to ILP/CP and prior work on path constraints in constraint-based learning and soft priors.

Significance
- The ability to enforce path constraints exactly is important for incorporating causal background knowledge. The observed speedups over a strong ILP baseline are substantial, and the projection method improves both search efficiency and learned structure quality.
- The ideas are likely to be adopted by researchers working on exact BN learning and by practitioners who need to inject causal knowledge. Wider practical impact would be enhanced by releasing code.

Pros
- Clear problem formulation and motivation; fills a gap in handling non-decomposable constraints.
- Conceptually elegant integration of EC-tree pruning with oracle-guided A*.
- Effective projection of path constraints into decomposable constraints (edge and order) with theoretical guarantees.
- Strong empirical gains over ILP; thorough experimental sweep; interpretable easy–hard–easy phenomenon.

Cons
- Some theoretical details and proofs are omitted; Theorem 1’s statement needs clarification.
- Potentially expensive class(P) enumeration is not analyzed; frequency and impact are unreported.
- No comparison to CP-based methods despite being cited; MaxSAT and projection overheads are not reported.
- Presentation issues: incomplete references and minor clarity gaps.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify Theorem 1 and provide proofs or extended appendix for Theorems 2–4 and the closure algorithm.
- Report statistics on class(P) enumeration frequency, MaxSAT runtime, and an ablation of edge vs ordering projections.
- Include a comparison to CP or MaxSAT-based exact BN learning that can encode path constraints.
- Consider releasing code to facilitate adoption.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper addresses a relevant and challenging problem with a principled and practically effective approach, demonstrates clear gains over a strong baseline, and proposes generally useful techniques (projection via edges and MaxSAT orderings). Strengthening theoretical details and clarifying a few points would further improve the paper.
