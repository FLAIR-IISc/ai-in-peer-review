

# Peer Review: "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"

## Summary and Context
This paper addresses a significant gap in Bayesian network structure learning: the integration of non-decomposable ancestral constraints (e.g., "X is an ancestor of Y") into optimal structure learning. The authors extend the EC tree search space (Chen et al., 2016) to incorporate ancestral constraints through pruning, and propose a novel method to project these non-decomposable constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints that can be used to empower an oracle (a structure learning algorithm for decomposable scores). The work builds on Chen et al.'s earlier NIPS 2015 work on non-decomposable scoring functions, which is relevant to recent NIPS trends in structure learning (e.g., Yuan & Malone, NIPS 2013; Malone et al., NIPS 2014).

## Strengths

The paper makes several strong contributions. First, it provides a theoretically sound method for pruning the EC tree based on ancestral constraints, with formal proof (Theorem 1) that the pruning is sound. Second, the constraint projection techniques (Theorems 2 and 3) are novel and well-motivated, enabling the use of standard decomposable-score oracles to handle non-decomposable constraints. Third, the empirical evaluation is comprehensive and convincing, showing orders-of-magnitude speedups over ILP-based approaches (GOBNILP) across multiple network sizes and constraint densities. The demonstration that 10-25% ancestral constraints can have similar impact on model accuracy as increasing training data from 512 to 8192 (Table 3) is particularly compelling.

The paper also makes an important conceptual contribution by clarifying the distinction between decomposable and non-decomposable constraints in structure learning, which has been a fundamental limitation in the field. The connection to existing work on constraint-based learning (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos, 2012) is well-articulated, though the paper could better position itself relative to more recent constraint-based methods.

## Weaknesses

The main weaknesses are in the discussion of limitations and scalability. The authors claim their approach scales to 20 variables, but don't address how the constraint projection complexity grows with the number of constraints. The MaxSAT formulation for ordering constraints (Section 5.2) is interesting, but the paper doesn't discuss the computational complexity of this step or how it scales with constraint density.

The experimental evaluation, while strong, is limited to simulated data from standard benchmarks. The authors don't demonstrate their method on real-world datasets with complex, domain-specific ancestral constraints, which would better demonstrate practical utility. Additionally, the paper doesn't address how their approach would handle inconsistent ancestral constraints (e.g., contradictory constraints that make the constraint set unsatisfiable).

The paper could also better connect to recent NIPS work on structure learning. While they cite Yuan & Malone (NIPS 2013), they don't discuss how their constraint projection method relates to newer approaches like the one by Kocaoglu et al. (NIPS 2018) on causal discovery with constraints.

## Technical Quality and Clarity

The technical content is sound with correct proofs and well-structured methodology. The presentation of the EC tree and its adaptation to ancestral constraints is clear. However, the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the practical limitations of the constraint projection technique. For example, how many constraints can be projected before the overhead becomes significant? The paper mentions that the oracle is empowered by projected constraints, but doesn't quantify this benefit beyond the speedup results.

The writing is generally clear, though some sections (particularly Section 5) could be more concise. The paper would be stronger with a more detailed discussion of when constraint projection might fail or be inefficient, and how to detect such cases.

## Significance

The significance of this work is substantial for practitioners who have domain knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints. The demonstrated speedups (orders of magnitude) over ILP-based approaches make this a practical contribution. The paper convincingly shows that background knowledge (in the form of ancestral constraints) can be as valuable as additional training data, which is a significant insight for the field.

However, the paper doesn't sufficiently address how this work might be integrated into broader causal discovery pipelines or how it compares to constraint-based methods (e.g., PC algorithm) that already handle some forms of constraints. This would strengthen the paper's significance for the NIPS community.

## Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the problem of structure learning with background knowledge. The theoretical foundations are sound, the empirical results are compelling, and the practical significance is clear. The main limitations are in the discussion of scalability and the lack of real-world case studies, but these are not fatal flaws.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, primarily to:
1. Add a more detailed discussion of the limitations and computational complexity of constraint projection
2. Include a brief comparison to recent constraint-based methods at NIPS
3. Discuss how the approach would handle inconsistent ancestral constraints

The paper addresses an important problem with a technically sound solution that significantly advances the state of the art in structure learning with background knowledge. It demonstrates clear improvements over existing approaches and provides valuable theoretical insights about constraint projection.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that aligns well with NIPS' focus on theoretical and practical advances in machine learning.
