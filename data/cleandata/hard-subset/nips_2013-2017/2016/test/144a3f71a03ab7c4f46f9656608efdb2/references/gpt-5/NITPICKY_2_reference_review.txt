Summary
The paper tackles optimal Bayesian network (BN) structure learning with non-decomposable ancestral constraints (X is/is not an ancestor of Y). It builds on the BN-graph and EC-tree search spaces of Chen et al. (2015, 2016), using A* over CPDAG nodes and an “oracle” solver for decomposable scores to compute admissible heuristics. The main technical ideas are: (1) pruning CPDAG nodes in the EC tree that cannot satisfy ancestral constraints, (2) projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and topological ordering constraints to strengthen the oracle, with an exact (claimed) characterization for entailed edges (Theorems 2–3) and a MaxSAT-based selection of a maximal sound set of ordering constraints (Theorem 4). Experiments compare against GOBNILP (ILP) and show speedups.

Strengths
- Problem relevance: Incorporating path/ancestral prior knowledge is practically important and under-supported by decomposable-score optimal learners. 
- Leverages recent non-decomposable search spaces (BN graph, EC tree) and provides a route to integrate hard non-decomposable constraints with admissible heuristics.
- The pruning idea at CPDAG level is natural and potentially impactful; monotonicity (violations persist when adding leaves) is a useful property.
- Projection of constraints to edges and orderings to strengthen the oracle is a clever way to get the best of both worlds (non-decomposable pruning + decomposable heuristic).
- Empirical results (as described) suggest substantial runtime gains vs. ILP, especially as n grows.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical correctness/completeness
- Theorem 1 appears misstated. The text relies on monotonicity of violation: if a CPDAG P cannot realize any DAG satisfying A, then all descendants can be pruned. The stated “P satisfies A iff its descendants satisfy A” is implausible (satisfaction is not closed under all descendants since adding new variables may introduce new constraints). Please restate precisely (e.g., violation monotonicity) and provide proof.
- Section 4.3 pruning: The test “no partially directed path X→…→Y in a CPDAG implies impossibility of X Y in all DAGs in class(P)” is plausible but nontrivial. A correctness proof or a reference is needed; otherwise the pruning may be unsound. Similarly, the claim that a directed path in a CPDAG implies a directed path in every DAG in the class deserves a citation (compelled paths).
- “Exhaustively enumerate class(P) if inconclusive”: this can be exponential in class size. The paper provides no bounds, caching, or heuristics; scalability of this step is unclear and could dominate runtime on some instances.
- Projection to edges (Theorems 2–3): The “iff” characterizations are strong and rely critically on computing a “maximum” closure of A (details omitted). Without a precise, correct, and terminating closure procedure (including cyclicity-derived negatives), these theorems can be wrong and the heuristic may become inadmissible if over-pruning occurs. Proofs or counterexample analyses are needed.
- Ordering constraints via MaxSAT (Section 5.2) are underspecified and potentially inconsistent:
  - The MaxSAT encoding appears to treat ancestral constraints as propositional variables rather than hard facts; then the solver could flip them to satisfy more orderings, which breaks entailment. The paper should clarify which constraints are hard (must hold exactly A) vs. soft.
  - Clause (3) “sufficient condition for acyclicity” is unusual and not justified; its variables mix orderings and ancestry atoms. The logical meaning and sufficiency need a proof. 
  - Theorem 4 provides sufficient conditions for O to be entailed by A, but the MaxSAT formulation does not guarantee these conditions are enforced as hard constraints. As written, it is unclear that the output O is guaranteed to be entailed by A and hence safe for admissible heuristics.
- Admissibility: Strengthening the oracle with projected constraints is only safe if those are truly entailed by A. Given the above issues, the paper must explicitly argue heuristic admissibility under all proposed projections.

Experimental evaluation
- The paper repeatedly cites Tables 1–3 and Figure 1, but they are not included here; claims (e.g., orders-of-magnitude improvements, easy-hard-easy trends) cannot be verified. Important details (number of instances solved within limits, wall-clock times, node expansions, variance) are missing from this version.
- Only ILP (GOBNILP) is compared, although CP is suggested as capable of handling ancestral constraints; there is no CP baseline. Also, no comparison to dynamic programming/A* with order-modular priors or k-best enumerators configured with equivalent constraints.
- The reliance on enumerating class(P) is not analyzed empirically (how often triggered, time spent, dependence on p).
- Data generation: The method for selecting sub-networks (“random sets of nodes and all their ancestors, up to a connected sub-network”) is vague. Variable arities, sparsity, and constraint noise (incorrect constraints) are not investigated. Realistic noise in background knowledge is common; robustness is unknown.

Clarity and presentation
- Numerous formatting/copy-editing issues: stray text (“th Conference…”, “年6月12⽇日星期五 11 16”), broken footnotes (1–3), inconsistent notation (e.g., “DAG graph”), and an incomplete/garbled bibliography with missing years and punctuation. This impedes readability and confidence in correctness.
- Some definitions are imprecise (e.g., “maximum set of ancestral constraints” vs. “maximally closed”; define closure precisely). 
- Sign conventions: you minimize “score” (shortest path) while BDeu is typically maximized; clarify whether you minimize negative log-score.
- “We provide such an empirical evaluation in Section 6” for CP is claimed earlier, but only ILP is evaluated.
- The role of URLEARNING (version, configuration) and how ordering/edge constraints are injected should be detailed for reproducibility.

Pros
- Addresses an important, underexplored constraint type in optimal BN learning.
- Conceptually neat integration of EC-tree pruning with decomposable oracle via projection.
- If correct and robust, could be practically useful and faster than ILP.

Cons
- Technical claims (Theorems 1–4, MaxSAT encoding) are underproved/underspecified; potential soundness/admissibility issues.
- Scalability of class(P) enumeration is unaddressed.
- Experimental evidence is incomplete (missing figures/tables here), with limited baselines.
- Significant clarity and formatting problems; references are incomplete.

Questions for authors
1. Provide precise statements and proofs for Theorems 1–4, and a complete description of the closure algorithm producing a “maximum set” A.
2. Clarify the MaxSAT encoding: which constraints are hard? How does the solver ensure O is entailed by A? Justify clause (3) or replace with standard acyclicity encodings.
3. Quantify how often class(P) enumeration is invoked and its overhead; any pruning-by-reachability alternative?
4. Report node expansions, heuristic accuracy (bound gaps), and ablations (no projection / edge-only / order-only).
5. Include a CP baseline; consider noisy constraints.
6. Fix formatting and bibliography.

Recommendation
Reject in current form. The ideas are promising, but the paper needs substantial technical clarifications/proofs, stronger empirical support, and significant writing/formatting fixes to meet NIPS standards.
