Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses optimal Bayesian network (BN) structure learning under non-decomposable background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints (statements that one variable is/is not an ancestor of another). Mainstream exact score-based methods (dynamic programming over order graphs, A* over order graphs, and related k-best enumeration) assume decomposable scores and constraints; they can accommodate edge or ordering constraints but not general ancestral constraints. ILP/CP formulations can, in principle, encode such constraints but have not been systematically evaluated for this use.

Building on BN graphs and EC trees introduced by Chen et al. (2015, 2016), the paper shows how to:
- Enforce ancestral constraints by pruning nodes in the EC tree search space (nodes are CPDAGs), using A* guided by an oracle that solves the decomposable subproblem.
- “Empower” the oracle by projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints:
  - Edge constraints: derive necessary and sufficient conditions (Theorems 2, 3) for entailment of X→Y or X↛Y from a closure of ancestral constraints via α(·), β(·) sets.
  - Ordering constraints: formulate a MaxSAT problem to select a maximally large acyclic set of ordering constraints entailed by ancestral constraints; provide sufficient logical conditions (Theorem 4).

Empirically, the method is compared to GOBNILP (ILP) on synthetic tasks generated from standard BN benchmarks (ALARM, ANDES, CHILD, CPCS54, HEPAR2), varying number of variables, data size, and proportion of ancestral constraints. Results indicate orders-of-magnitude speedups over ILP, especially when projected constraints are used. The study also explores scalability to n≈20 and the accuracy benefits of constraints.

Quality
- Technical soundness:
  - EC-tree pruning under ancestral constraints is sound in spirit: violations at a CPDAG should persist under leaf additions. However, Theorem 1 as stated (“P satisfies A iff its descendants satisfy A”) is unclear; the intended statement likely concerns monotonicity of satisfaction/violation along expansions. Clarifying whether “all” or “some” descendants are meant is important.
  - The pruning test sometimes requires enumerating all DAGs in a CPDAG when quick tests are inconclusive. While correct, this can be exponential; no complexity bounds or practical safeguards are discussed.
  - Edge projection: Theorems 2 and 3 provide necessary and sufficient criteria under a closed (“maximum”) ancestral constraint set. The constructions appear reasonable; proofs and the exact closure rules are omitted for space, making verification difficult.
  - Ordering projection: The MaxSAT encoding uses sufficient, not necessary, conditions (Theorem 4). This ensures soundness (no elimination of feasible DAGs) but may miss entailed orderings (suboptimality vs. the maximal entailed set). The “sufficient condition for acyclicity” clause (3) is nonstandard and would benefit from justification; the notion of “closed set of ordering constraints” is not fully specified.
  - The search relies on an oracle (URLEARNING) for decomposable scores. The overall method remains exact given the pruning/projection soundness, but explicit proof of global optimality under the combined pruning and oracle use would strengthen the paper.

- Empirical evaluation:
  - Clear experimental setup: constraints sampled from ground-truth ancestral relations, BDeu score, precomputed family scores, comparisons to ILP with and without projected constraints, and exploration of constraint density.
  - Strong speedups are reported, increasing with n; the benefits of projection are large for the EC-tree method and somewhat positive for ILP at larger n.
  - Scalability is demonstrated up to n=20 with no parent-set size limit. Given known ILP challenges without parent limits, this favors heuristic/A* approaches; acknowledging this and reporting ILP with and without parent limits would improve fairness.
  - CP baselines are mentioned as capable in principle but not included; a brief justification would help.
  - Accuracy metrics (structural Hamming distance) show expected trends, and the result that modest constraint sets can substitute for significant increases in data is useful.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized, with helpful background and motivation. However:
  - Some formulations (Theorem 1) and the acyclicity clause in the MaxSAT encoding need clearer statements and justification.
  - Details are omitted for space (closure rules, proofs, CPDAG enumeration strategy), making it harder to assess completeness and practical cost.
  - Minor typographical issues and inconsistent reference formatting should be addressed.

Originality
- The integration of ancestral constraints into EC-tree search with exact pruning, combined with systematic projection of non-decomposable constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints to accelerate the oracle, is novel relative to prior exact score-based methods and to earlier BN/EC graph work.
- The edge entailment criteria and the MaxSAT-based ordering selection add methodological contributions.

Significance
- Ancestral constraints are practically relevant in causal discovery. Providing an exact, scalable method to exploit them is valuable.
- The reported speedups over ILP and the demonstrated gains from projection suggest practical impact for exact BN learning under background knowledge.
- Scalability remains moderate (n≈20), comparable to prior exact methods with BDeu, but the approach broadens the applicability to constrained settings.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important gap: exact BN learning with non-decomposable ancestral constraints.
- Conceptually clean integration of EC-tree pruning and oracle-guided A*.
- Novel and sound edge entailment; practical ordering entailment via MaxSAT.
- Strong empirical improvements over a widely used ILP baseline; careful exploration of constraint density effects.

Cons against acceptance
- Some theoretical statements are imprecise (Theorem 1) or only provide sufficiency (ordering constraints), without proofs in the paper.
- Potentially expensive CPDAG enumeration step is required in pruning; no complexity or mitigation strategies are analyzed.
- Evaluation lacks CP baselines and does not explore ILP under parent-set limits; scalability is limited to small/moderate n.
- Clarity issues and reference formatting need polish.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify Theorem 1, provide proofs (or an appendix), and justify the MaxSAT acyclicity clause; define “closed set” precisely.
- Analyze the worst-case and typical costs of CPDAG enumeration during pruning; propose heuristics to avoid full enumeration.
- Include additional baselines (CP) and report ILP results with parent-set limits for completeness.
- Provide ablations showing the contribution of edge vs. ordering projections separately.
- Improve presentation and fix typos/formatting.

Overall
The paper presents a compelling and practically useful approach with solid empirical gains and reasonable theoretical underpinnings, though some formal details and clarity should be strengthened.
