

# NIPS Review: "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"

## Summary

This paper addresses a significant gap in Bayesian network structure learning: the incorporation of non-decomposable ancestral constraints (e.g., "X is an ancestor of Y" or "X is not an ancestor of Y"). The authors build upon Chen et al.'s recent work on EC trees (a search space for learning Bayesian networks with non-decomposable scores) and present a method to incorporate ancestral constraints by developing a sound and complete method for pruning the EC tree, along with a technique to infer decomposable constraints (edge and ordering constraints) from ancestral constraints. Their approach empowers an oracle that learns with decomposable scores, leading to dramatic efficiency improvements over ILP-based approaches. The authors evaluate their method on standard Bayesian network benchmarks (ALARM, ANDES, CHILD, CPCS54, HEPAR2) and demonstrate orders-of-magnitude speedups compared to GOBNILP, an ILP-based system.

## Strengths

### Quality
The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical foundations. The authors provide formal proofs for their constraint projection method (Theorems 2-4) that establish the correctness of inferring decomposable constraints from ancestral constraints. The experimental design is comprehensive, with careful variation of key parameters (number of variables n, dataset size N, percentage of ancestral constraints p), and the results are well-supported by empirical evidence. The authors demonstrate that their method scales to 20 variables, which is comparable to state-of-the-art score-based approaches. The paper acknowledges limitations, such as the "easy-hard-easy" trend in search efficiency as the number of constraints increases.

### Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly frames the problem and its importance, while the technical sections build logically from foundational concepts to the proposed method. The EC tree representation is well-explained with helpful figures (Figure 1), and the constraint projection algorithms are clearly described with illustrative examples. The experimental section provides sufficient detail for reproducibility, including hardware specifications, parameter settings, and implementation details.

### Originality
This work makes a clear and significant contribution to the literature. While ILP and CP approaches can technically represent ancestral constraints, the authors demonstrate for the first time that their EC tree-based approach with constraint projection is orders of magnitude more efficient. The constraint projection method (inferring decomposable constraints from non-decomposable ancestral constraints) is novel and well-motivated. The paper effectively distinguishes itself from prior work by Chen et al. (2015, 2016) and other approaches that cannot handle ancestral constraints.

### Significance
The significance of this work is substantial. Ancestral constraints are common in real-world applications where domain knowledge is available, and the paper demonstrates that even a small number of ancestral constraints (10-25%) can have a similar impact on model accuracy as increasing dataset size by several times. The efficiency gains (orders of magnitude faster than ILP approaches) make this method practically valuable for larger problems. This advances the state of the art in Bayesian network structure learning, particularly for problems with non-decomposable constraints.

## Weaknesses

### Limited Analysis of Constraint Projection Complexity
While the paper demonstrates the effectiveness of constraint projection, it doesn't provide a thorough analysis of how the complexity of the projection step scales with the number of ancestral constraints. The paper mentions varying the percentage of ancestral constraints (p), but doesn't provide detailed complexity analysis of the projection algorithms, which would strengthen the theoretical contribution.

### Limited Comparison with Other Constraint-Based Approaches
The paper focuses on comparing with ILP-based approaches (GOBNILP) but doesn't adequately discuss or compare with other constraint-based approaches for Bayesian network learning, such as those using constraint programming (CP) or other methods that might handle non-decomposable constraints. While the authors note that "ILP and CP approaches have not been previously evaluated" for learning with ancestral constraints, a brief comparison with relevant CP work would have strengthened the paper.

### Limited Discussion of Practical Implementation Challenges
The paper doesn't address practical challenges in real-world applications, such as how to obtain accurate ancestral constraints from domain experts, how to handle conflicting constraints, or how the method would perform with noisy constraints. These are important considerations for practitioners who might adopt this approach.

### Underdeveloped Explanation of Efficiency Gains
While the paper demonstrates that their method is orders of magnitude faster, the explanation for why this is the case is somewhat superficial. A more detailed analysis of the computational complexity differences between the EC tree approach and the ILP approach would have strengthened the paper.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. The technical content is sound, the approach is novel, and the experimental results demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods. The paper addresses an important practical problem (incorporating domain knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints) and provides a method that is both theoretically grounded and empirically validated.

The weaknesses identified are not major flaws but rather opportunities for further development. The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it presents a novel approach to a well-defined problem with clear theoretical and practical significance.

## Recommendation

**Accept**

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field with a technically sound approach, clear presentation, and compelling empirical results. The significance of the work is demonstrated through both efficiency gains and the impact of ancestral constraints on model accuracy. The paper advances the state of the art in Bayesian network structure learning and provides a practical method for incorporating domain knowledge.

The authors should consider addressing the weaknesses noted in their future work, particularly by providing a more detailed analysis of constraint projection complexity and expanding the comparison to include other constraint-based approaches. However, these are not required for acceptance at NIPS.

## Final Thoughts

This paper demonstrates that non-decomposable constraints, which have been largely ignored in mainstream Bayesian network structure learning, can be effectively incorporated with significant efficiency gains. The authors have made a clear and important contribution to the field, and their method has clear practical value for applications where domain knowledge in the form of causal relationships is available. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a significant contribution to the literature.
