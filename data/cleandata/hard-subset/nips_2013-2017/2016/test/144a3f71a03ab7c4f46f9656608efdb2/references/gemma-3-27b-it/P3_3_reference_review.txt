## Review of "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning, specifically addressing the challenging problem of incorporating non-decomposable ancestral constraints. The approach, building upon the recent work of Chen et al. (2015, 2016), is well-motivated, technically sound, and demonstrates compelling empirical results. While the exposition could be slightly improved in places, the paper is generally clear and the significance of the work is high. I recommend acceptance.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper tackles the problem of learning Bayesian networks (BNs) under the constraint of known ancestral relationships between variables.  Traditional BN learning algorithms struggle with such constraints because they are non-decomposable, meaning they cannot be expressed as independent constraints on individual variable-parent relationships. The authors cleverly leverage the framework of Chen et al. (2015, 2016) – based on navigating a search space of DAGs with an oracle for decomposable scores – to address this limitation. They demonstrate how to incorporate ancestral constraints through pruning the search space (EC trees) and, crucially, how to *project* these non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones, thereby empowering the oracle and significantly improving efficiency.  The paper positions itself well within the existing literature, acknowledging the limitations of ILP/CP approaches (Jaakkola et al., 2010; Bartlett & Cussens, 2015) and highlighting the advantages of their framework. They also appropriately contrast their work with constraint-based methods leveraging ancestral constraints like those of Borboudakis & Tsamardinos (2012, 2013), emphasizing the focus on *optimal* structure learning.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The approach of combining the Chen et al. framework with ancestral constraints and, importantly, the projection of these constraints into decomposable forms, is genuinely novel. This projection step is a key insight and a major contribution.
* **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical foundation is solid. The pruning strategy within the EC tree is logically sound (Theorem 1) and the description of the MaxSAT reduction for inferring ordering constraints (Theorem 4) is well-defined.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are convincing. The comparisons with GOBNILP demonstrate significant performance improvements (orders of magnitude faster), and the ablation study demonstrating the benefit of constraint projection is compelling.  The scaling analysis and observation of the "easy-hard-easy" trend with constraint density are insightful.
* **Practical Significance:** The ability to incorporate prior knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints has significant practical implications.  The results showing that even a small amount of ancestral knowledge can yield improvements comparable to increasing the dataset size are particularly noteworthy.
* **Clear Connection to Existing Work:** The paper builds on recent and influential work (Chen et al., 2015, 2016) in a thoughtful and productive way.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity of Exposition:** While generally clear, some sections could benefit from further clarification. For example, the explanation of the "EC tree" in Section 4.2 felt slightly rushed and could be more intuitive, particularly for readers unfamiliar with the original Chen et al. work. A diagram illustrating the pruning process on a small EC tree example would also be helpful.
* **MaxSAT Implementation Details:** The details of the MaxSAT instance construction are somewhat high-level. Providing a more concrete example would enhance understanding.  A discussion of the limitations of using an external solver (EVASOLVER) and potential scalability issues related to the MaxSAT reduction would be valuable.
* **Limited Comparison to Other Approaches:** While GOBNILP is a reasonable comparison point, it might be beneficial to briefly discuss the strengths and weaknesses of other related techniques (e.g., hybrid constraint-based/score-based methods) to further contextualize the contribution.
* **Dataset Selection:** The use of standard BN benchmarks is good, but it would be worthwhile to briefly acknowledge any potential limitations of these datasets in capturing the specific challenges associated with ancestral constraints.




**4. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For:**

* Presents a novel and technically sound approach to a challenging problem.
* Demonstrates significant empirical improvements over existing methods.
* Offers a practical solution with potentially wide-ranging applications.
* Builds upon and extends important recent work in the field.
* Theoretical results are well-stated and seemingly correct.

**Against:**

* Exposition could be improved in certain sections.
* MaxSAT implementation details are somewhat lacking.
* Limited comparison to other relevant approaches.

**5. Final Recommendation:**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper for NIPS 2016.  The strengths of the work – particularly its novelty, technical soundness, and empirical validation – outweigh the minor weaknesses in clarity and comprehensiveness.  The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning and has the potential to be highly influential.  The authors have identified a significant challenge and proposed a creative and effective solution.
