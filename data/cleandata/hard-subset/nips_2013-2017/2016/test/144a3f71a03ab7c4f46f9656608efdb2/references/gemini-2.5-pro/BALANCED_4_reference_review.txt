This paper addresses the important problem of learning optimal Bayesian network (BN) structures from data, subject to background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints. The authors correctly identify that such constraints are non-decomposable, which poses a significant challenge for mainstream exact, score-based learning algorithms that rely on the assumption of score decomposability. The paper builds upon a recent A*-based search framework over the space of Markov equivalence classes (the EC-tree). The primary contributions are twofold. First, the authors develop a sound pruning strategy to enforce the ancestral constraints during the search over the EC-tree. Second, and more significantly, they propose a novel method to "project" the non-decomposable ancestral constraints onto a set of decomposable constraints (edge presence/absence and topological ordering constraints). These projected constraints are then used to "empower" the oracle that computes the heuristic for the A* search, leading to a much tighter heuristic and dramatically improved search performance. The projection of ordering constraints is cleverly formulated as a MaxSAT problem. The paper provides strong empirical evidence that the proposed approach is orders of magnitude faster than a state-of-the-art Integer Linear Programming (ILP) based system, which is one of the few other exact methods capable of handling such constraints.

### Strengths (Arguments for acceptance)

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The paper tackles a long-standing and practical challenge in score-based BN learning: incorporating non-decomposable prior knowledge. The core contribution—projecting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones to tighten the heuristic of an A* search—is a highly novel and powerful idea. It provides an elegant way to bridge the gap between search frameworks designed for decomposable scores and the practical need for richer, non-decomposable constraints. This approach could potentially be extended to other types of non-decomposable constraints beyond ancestral relations.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed method is technically sound. The theorems provided give formal grounding to the pruning and projection methods. The reduction of the ordering constraint inference problem to MaxSAT is particularly clever, as it correctly handles the subtlety that naively inferring ordering constraints can improperly prune valid DAGs from the search space. The overall algorithm integrates these components into a complete and coherent system for exact learning under ancestral constraints.

3.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and convincing. The authors compare their method against a very strong and relevant baseline: an ILP solver (GOBNILP), which represents the state-of-the-art for exact learning with flexible constraints. The reported orders-of-magnitude speedup is a significant result that clearly demonstrates the superiority of the proposed approach. Furthermore, the ablation study (comparing performance with and without constraint projection in Tables 1 and 2) effectively isolates and validates the contribution of the projection technique, showing it is critical to the method's performance.

4.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It starts with a clear motivation, builds the necessary technical background, and presents the new contributions in a logical and easy-to-follow manner. The examples provided, particularly in the discussion of ordering constraints, are effective at illustrating the technical challenges involved.

### Weaknesses (Arguments against acceptance)

1.  **Scalability of the Projection Step:** While the paper thoroughly evaluates the performance of the overall search, it does not discuss the computational cost of the constraint projection step itself. Specifically, solving the MaxSAT instance to infer ordering constraints could become a bottleneck for problems with a large number of variables. A brief analysis or empirical measurement of the time spent in the projection phase relative to the search phase would strengthen the paper and provide a more complete picture of the method's scalability.

2.  **Cost of CPDAG Constraint Checking:** In Section 4.3, the authors state that if initial checks are inconclusive, they must exhaustively enumerate the DAGs within a CPDAG's equivalence class to check for constraint satisfaction. This can be exponentially expensive. While this is an inherent difficulty when working with CPDAGs, the paper would benefit from a discussion on how frequently this worst-case scenario is encountered in their experiments and what its practical impact on runtime is.

3.  **Minor Omissions:** The paper omits details on computing the "maximum set of ancestral constraints" due to space limitations. While this is understandable, this transitive closure computation is a key preprocessing step. A brief mention of its polynomial-time complexity (e.g., via multiple graph traversals) would be helpful for the reader to understand the full computational pipeline.

### Decision and Justification

I recommend **Accept**. The paper makes a clear, significant, and novel contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. It effectively solves an important practical problem with a technically elegant and well-grounded approach. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and do not detract from the core contribution. The impressive empirical results, demonstrating a substantial improvement over a state-of-the-art baseline, provide strong evidence of the work's value and advance the state of the art in exact score-based structure learning. The ideas presented are likely to be influential and built upon by other researchers.

### Suggestions for Improvement

*   Please consider adding a brief discussion in the experiments section on the runtime of the constraint projection step, especially the MaxSAT solver. For the problem sizes tested, how much time does this preprocessing take compared to the A* search itself?
*   It would be valuable to add a comment on the practical cost of the exhaustive CPDAG enumeration mentioned in Section 4.3. Was this a significant contributor to the overall runtime in your experiments?
*   Footnote 4 provides an intuitive argument for inferring `Y < Z` from `Z \not\preceq Y`. The claim that adding edge `Y -> Z` to any DAG `G` satisfying the constraint does not create a cycle is the key step. A slightly more formal justification would strengthen this point.
*   In Section 5.1, please consider adding a sentence to clarify that the computation of the "maximum set of ancestral constraints" can be done efficiently in polynomial time.
