

# Review of "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"

This paper addresses an important problem in Bayesian network structure learning: incorporating non-decomposable ancestral constraints into the learning process. The authors propose a method that extends the EC tree search space (Chen et al., 2015, 2016) to handle ancestral constraints, which specify cause-and-effect relationships between variables (e.g., X→Y). They demonstrate how to prune the search space based on these constraints and how to project them into decomposable edge and ordering constraints to empower the oracle used in the search process.

## Strengths

**Originality (Strong):** The paper tackles a well-known limitation in Bayesian network structure learning—existing methods typically assume decomposable scores and cannot handle non-decomposable constraints like ancestral constraints. The authors make a novel contribution by extending the EC tree search space to accommodate these constraints and developing techniques to project them into decomposable forms. This is a significant advancement over prior work, as the authors correctly note that approaches based on ILP and CP can represent ancestral constraints but have not been evaluated for this specific purpose.

**Significance (Strong):** The empirical results demonstrate impressive improvements over ILP-based methods (GOBNILP), showing orders-of-magnitude speedups. The paper also provides valuable insights into how ancestral constraints affect learning efficiency and accuracy. Crucially, they show that even a small number of ancestral constraints (10-25%) can have a similar impact on model accuracy as substantially increasing dataset size (e.g., from 512 to 8192 samples), highlighting the practical value of incorporating background knowledge.

**Quality (Strong):** The theoretical foundation is solid, with clear definitions, theorems, and proofs. The constraint projection techniques (edge and ordering constraints) are sound and well-motivated. The experimental evaluation is thorough, using multiple benchmark networks (ALARM, ANDES, etc.) and varying problem sizes (n, N, p). The paper correctly acknowledges the need for constraint projection to make the approach efficient.

**Clarity (Good):** The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and uses helpful figures (e.g., Figure 1) to illustrate concepts. The technical explanations are accessible to researchers familiar with Bayesian networks, though some readers may need to consult prior work on EC trees.

## Weaknesses

**Computational Complexity (Moderate):** While the paper demonstrates significant speedups in structure learning, it doesn't provide sufficient detail on the computational cost of the constraint projection step (especially the MaxSAT formulation for ordering constraints). The time required to infer decomposable constraints from ancestral constraints could become a bottleneck for large problems, and the paper should address this.

**Real-World Validation (Moderate):** The experiments rely on simulated data from benchmark networks with randomly generated ancestral constraints. While this provides controlled evaluation, it would strengthen the paper to include experiments on real-world datasets with known ancestral constraints, particularly in domains where such constraints are commonly available (e.g., biological networks or medical diagnosis systems).

**Scalability Beyond 20 Variables (Moderate):** The paper demonstrates scalability to 20 variables, which is competitive with state-of-the-art methods. However, for many real-world applications, scalability to 50+ variables is crucial. The paper should discuss how their approach would perform on larger networks and whether the constraint projection step would become a limiting factor.

**Limited Discussion of Limitations (Minor):** While the paper acknowledges the need for constraint projection, it doesn't fully explore the limitations of their projection techniques. For example, how does the completeness of the projection depend on the specific set of ancestral constraints? When might the projection fail to capture all relevant decomposable constraints?

## Recommendation

This paper makes a significant contribution to Bayesian network structure learning by addressing a fundamental limitation in incorporating non-decomposable ancestral constraints. The technical approach is sound, the experimental results are compelling, and the practical implications are well-documented. The paper meets all the criteria for a high-quality NIPS submission.

While the paper has minor weaknesses regarding computational complexity of constraint projection and real-world validation, these are not severe enough to undermine the paper's core contribution. The authors have made a clear and important advancement that will be valuable to the community.

I recommend **acceptance**. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a significant contribution to the field. The authors should consider addressing the minor limitations in the final version, particularly regarding the computational cost of constraint projection and potential real-world validation.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Include a brief analysis of the computational complexity of the constraint projection step (both edge and ordering constraints), especially for large numbers of ancestral constraints.

2. Add a small section discussing potential real-world applications where ancestral constraints are available (e.g., biological pathways, causal relationships in social sciences) and how the method could be applied.

3. Consider adding a short experiment on a small real-world dataset with known ancestral constraints to complement the simulated experiments.

4. Clarify the relationship between the number of ancestral constraints and the computational cost of constraint projection in the experiments.

5. Discuss how the method might be extended to handle more complex constraint types beyond ancestral constraints (e.g., conditional constraints).
