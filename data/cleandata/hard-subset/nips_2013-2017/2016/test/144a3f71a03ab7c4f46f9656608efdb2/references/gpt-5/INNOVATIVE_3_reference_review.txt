Summary
The paper tackles optimal Bayesian network (BN) structure learning in the presence of non-decomposable ancestral constraints (positive: X is an ancestor of Y; negative: X is not an ancestor of Y). Main contributions: (i) extend the BN graph/EC tree framework of Chen et al. (2015, 2016) to prune search with ancestral constraints at the CPDAG level; (ii) project non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and topological ordering constraints to “empower” a decomposable-score oracle (URLearning) used as an admissible heuristic in A* over the EC tree; (iii) theory for sound pruning and for inferring entailed edge constraints (Theorems 2–3) and a MaxSAT formulation for extracting a maximally large, sound set of ordering constraints (with sufficient conditions formalized in Theorem 4); (iv) empirical evaluation vs. an ILP baseline (GOBNILP), demonstrating orders-of-magnitude speedups and improved scalability, as well as accuracy gains as constraints and data increase.

Relation to prior work
- Prior exact BN learners largely assume decomposable scores and support only structure-modular or order-modular constraints (e.g., DP/A* over order graphs, Koivisto & Sood; Yuan & Malone).
- ILP/CP approaches can encode non-decomposable constraints (cluster constraints, acyclicity), but have not been thoroughly evaluated for ancestral constraints; this paper provides such an evaluation.
- Builds directly on BN graph/EC tree search with non-decomposable scores (Chen et al. 2015, 2016), but innovates by integrating ancestral constraints into CPDAG pruning and by projecting them to decomposable constraints for the oracle. Related path-belief work in constraint-based settings (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos) is acknowledged.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: The projection of non-decomposable ancestral constraints into provably sound sets of decomposable edge and ordering constraints, then using them to strengthen a decomposable oracle inside a non-decomposable search space, is elegant and broadly applicable beyond this specific constraint family.
- Solid theoretical components: sound CPDAG pruning criterion; clear definitions of α(·), β(·) and necessary/sufficient conditions for entailed edge constraints; a MaxSAT construction yielding large, cycle-free ordering sets with sufficient conditions captured in Theorem 4.
- Practical impact: Empirical results show orders-of-magnitude speedups over a strong ILP baseline, with and without projected constraints, across multiple benchmarks and regimes; clear demonstration that projection is key to efficiency.
- Relevance: Ancestral constraints are natural vehicles for background causal knowledge; the observed accuracy improvements from modest constraint percentages are compelling.

Weaknesses and questions
- Completeness/optimality of projection: The edge inference rules (Theorems 2–3) are sound, but how complete are they in practice? Some discussion on missed entailed edges and the tradeoff between aggressiveness and soundness would help.
- CPDAG feasibility test: When quick checks are inconclusive, the method enumerates class(P). This can be exponential; empirical statistics on how often enumeration is needed and its cost would strengthen the case.
- Theorem 1 phrasing is potentially confusing (“P satisfies A iff its descendants satisfy A”); likely intended to formalize monotonicity for pruning but should be clarified (e.g., violation is downward-closed).
- MaxSAT encoding: Only sufficient (not necessary) acyclicity constraints are enforced. Some analysis of the gap between the MaxSAT solution and the true maximum entailed ordering set would be useful. Also, sensitivity to the chosen MaxSAT solver/timeouts is not reported.
- Scope: Focuses exclusively on hard ancestral constraints. The framework seems promising for other non-decomposable priors/constraints (e.g., soft path beliefs), but this remains future work.
- Presentation: Numerous typos/citation formatting issues and stray text fragments make parts hard to parse; some derivation details are omitted “for space,” which harms reproducibility.

Pros for acceptance
- Introduces a fresh and useful idea (constraint projection) that bridges non-decomposable constraints with decomposable oracles in exact BN learning.
- Strong empirical evidence of substantial efficiency gains over ILP and clear practical benefits of background knowledge.
- The method advances the state of the art on a challenging and relevant problem.

Cons for acceptance
- Some theoretical components are sufficient but not characterized for tightness; practical completeness not fully explored.
- Potential exponential subroutines (CPDAG enumeration) are not well quantified.
- Clarity and polish issues (proof details omitted; presentation glitches).

Quality
Technically sound overall, with plausible theorems and a correct high-level integration of components. Experiments are thorough across datasets/settings, though additional diagnostics on bottlenecks would improve rigor.

Clarity
The paper is generally understandable but needs editing for grammar/formatting and clearer statements (esp. Theorem 1). More algorithmic details (pseudo-code, complexity) would aid reproducibility.

Originality
High. While building on EC trees, the projection mechanism and MaxSAT-based ordering extraction for empowering a decomposable oracle under non-decomposable constraints is novel.

Significance
High. Likely to influence exact BN learning under background knowledge and inspire similar projection strategies for other non-decomposable priors/constraints.

Suggestions
- Clarify Theorem 1 and provide a proof sketch.
- Report frequency/cost of CPDAG enumeration.
- Quantify how close the MaxSAT-derived ordering set is to the theoretical maximum.
- Provide pseudo-code for edge inference and the closure to a “maximum” ancestral set.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper proposes a bold and impactful direction with solid theoretical grounding and strong empirical gains. Despite some presentation and analysis gaps, the novelty and demonstrated benefits merit inclusion.
