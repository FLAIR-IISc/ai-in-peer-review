

# Review of "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"

## Summary
This paper addresses the problem of learning Bayesian network structures when given ancestral constraints (e.g., "X is an ancestor of Y" or "X is not an ancestor of Y"). The authors note that while ancestral constraints are important for expressing cause-and-effect relationships in practice, most mainstream Bayesian network structure learning approaches assume decomposable scoring functions, which cannot directly incorporate these non-decomposable constraints. The paper builds on Chen et al. (2015, 2016), who introduced the EC tree search space for learning with non-decomposable scores. The authors extend this work to handle ancestral constraints by: (1) developing a method to prune the EC tree based on these constraints, and (2) proposing algorithms to project non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints that can empower an oracle (a structure learning algorithm for decomposable scores). The paper evaluates this approach against ILP-based methods (GOBNILP), demonstrating orders-of-magnitude speedups.

## Strengths
1. **Technical Soundness**: The paper provides rigorous theoretical foundations with formal proofs (Theorems 1-4) for the constraint projection methods. The authors clearly explain why ancestral constraints are non-decomposable and how they differ from decomposable constraints. The experimental evaluation is thorough, with multiple datasets, varying problem sizes, and careful comparison against a strong baseline (GOBNILP).

2. **Clarity**: The paper is well-organized with clear technical explanations of complex concepts. Figures 1(a) and 1(b) effectively illustrate the BN graph and EC tree. The authors provide sufficient detail for experts to reproduce their results, including experimental setup, constraint projection algorithms, and evaluation metrics.

3. **Originality**: The paper makes a clear contribution by addressing a specific gap in the literature. While ILP approaches can theoretically represent ancestral constraints, the authors demonstrate this has not been previously evaluated for structure learning with ancestral constraints. The constraint projection approach (inferring decomposable constraints from ancestral constraints) is novel and well-justified.

4. **Significance**: The results demonstrate substantial practical improvements (orders of magnitude speedup) over existing methods. The paper shows that a small number of ancestral constraints (10-25%) can have similar impact on model quality as substantially increasing training data size, highlighting the value of incorporating domain knowledge. The approach scales to 20 variables, comparable to state-of-the-art score-based methods.

## Weaknesses
1. The paper does not thoroughly discuss the computational complexity of the constraint projection algorithms, particularly the enumeration of structures when pruning CPDAGs (Section 4.3). While the authors note this is "efficient" for the cases they consider, a more detailed analysis would strengthen the paper.

2. The empirical evaluation, while thorough, focuses primarily on speed improvements. The paper could better quantify the trade-offs between the number of constraints and the quality of the learned networks (beyond the structural Hamming distance reported in Table 3).

3. The paper does not fully address how the proposed approach compares to constraint-based methods (like PC algorithm) that also incorporate domain knowledge through constraints, though the authors note Borboudakis and Tsamardinos (2012) used path constraints for constraint-based learning.

## Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance

**Pro:**
- Addresses a well-motivated problem (incorporating non-decomposable ancestral constraints) that has been overlooked in prior work
- Presents a technically sound approach with strong theoretical foundations
- Demonstrates significant empirical improvements (orders of magnitude faster) over existing methods
- Provides clear algorithms for constraint projection that can be applied broadly
- Well-written and well-organized with sufficient detail for reproducibility

**Con:**
- Limited discussion of computational complexity for constraint projection
- Some potential trade-offs between constraint number and model quality could be explored more deeply
- Does not fully compare with constraint-based approaches that incorporate domain knowledge

## Overall Assessment
This paper makes a significant contribution to Bayesian network structure learning by providing a method to incorporate non-decomposable ancestral constraints, which are important for expressing domain knowledge in practice. The technical approach is sound, well-motivated, and supported by thorough experiments showing substantial speed improvements over existing methods. The paper advances the state of the art in structure learning with non-decomposable constraints, which is a recognized challenge in the field. The work is clearly presented, and the contribution is sufficiently distinct from prior NIPS papers (e.g., Chen et al. 2015, 2016; Borboudakis and Tsamardinos 2012, 2013).

The paper meets all four criteria for high-quality NIPS papers: it is technically sound with strong theoretical and empirical support, clearly written, makes a novel contribution by addressing a specific gap in the literature, and has significant practical implications for incorporating domain knowledge into Bayesian network learning. The minor weaknesses noted do not detract from the overall quality and contribution of the work.
