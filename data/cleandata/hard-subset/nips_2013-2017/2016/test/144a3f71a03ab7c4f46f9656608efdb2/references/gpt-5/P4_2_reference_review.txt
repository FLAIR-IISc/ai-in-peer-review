Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses exact Bayesian network (BN) structure learning under non-decomposable background knowledge in the form of ancestral (path) constraints. It builds on the BN graph and EC tree search spaces introduced by Chen et al. (2015, 2016) for exact learning with non-decomposable priors/scores, and adapts them to handle hard path constraints efficiently. The main algorithmic components are: (i) pruning in the EC tree by testing whether a CPDAG admits any DAG in its equivalence class that satisfies a given set of ancestral constraints, with two efficient sufficient tests and an otherwise exhaustive check; (ii) “projection” of non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints—specifically, entailed edge presence/absence constraints (Theorems 2–3) and entailed topological ordering constraints via a MaxSAT reduction (Theorem 4)—used to strengthen a decomposable-score oracle (URLearning) that provides admissible heuristics for A* search in the EC tree. Experiments compare this exact approach against an ILP-based exact learner (GOBNILP), with and without projected constraints, on benchmark networks with synthetic datasets and synthetic constraint sets.

Prior work on exact score-based learning largely depends on decomposable scores and enforces only local (structure-modular) or order-modular constraints (e.g., K2, dynamic programming over the order graph, A*, Koivisto & Sood; Silander & Myllymäki; Yuan & Malone). ILP/CP formulations can encode non-decomposable constraints (Jaakkola et al.; Cussens; Bartlett & Cussens; van Beek & Hoffmann), but there appears to have been no targeted empirical evaluation for ancestral constraints. Path constraints have also been used in constraint-based learning and as soft priors for local search (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos). This paper positions itself as the first exact score-based method that incorporates hard ancestral constraints efficiently by leveraging the EC tree plus projection to empower a decomposable oracle, and empirically contrasts with an ILP baseline.

Quality (technical soundness and evaluation)
- EC-tree pruning: The pruning strategy is sound because CPDAG nodes whose entire equivalence class violates a positive path constraint (no partially directed path X→…→Y) or a negative path constraint (has a directed path X→…→Y) can be discarded; descendants cannot recover a violated constraint in the former case, and adding leaves cannot destroy an existing path in the latter. Theorem 1 claims an “iff” property along the EC tree; the conditions under which satisfiability of constraints is preserved across expansions are subtle and merit a clear proof (e.g., constraints are defined over the full variable set while EC nodes are over subsets; leaves have only incoming edges). The paper would benefit from a formal argument or a pointer to supplementary material.
- Projection of constraints: Theorems 2–3 provide simple, checkable conditions for entailed negative/positive edge constraints from a closed set of ancestral constraints via ancestor/descendant closures α(·), β(·). The approach is conservative and appears sound; details of the closure rules to obtain a “maximum set” of ancestral constraints are omitted and should be provided for reproducibility (definition of “maximum” vs. “maximal” is also needed).
- Ordering constraints via MaxSAT: The encoding uses hard clauses enforcing transitivity and a sufficient acyclicity condition, and soft clauses to prefer orderings implied by negative ancestral constraints. Theorem 4 gives sufficient conditions under which the resulting set of orderings is entailed by the ancestral constraints. This conservative approach favors soundness over completeness, which is appropriate for an oracle-tightening step. However, the sufficiency condition for acyclicity (pairwise disjunction over four nodes) is non-standard; a proof sketch and discussion of its conservativeness would improve confidence. Reporting the overhead of the MaxSAT step would also be useful.
- Empirical evaluation: The experiments include ablations with and without projected constraints and show consistent, often orders-of-magnitude, speedups over ILP (especially as n increases), and substantial gains from projection (particularly for the EC-tree method). The easy–hard–easy trend as constraint density p varies is clearly observed. The scale (up to n=20) is comparable to prior exact methods with BDeu. The predictive accuracy analysis (structural Hamming distance) shows that modest constraint sets can provide benefits comparable to substantial increases in data. Limitations include: constraints are noise-free and derived from ground truth; no evaluation of CP approaches; little breakdown of costs (e.g., CPDAG satisfiability checks or MaxSAT time); and no ablation separating the effect of edge vs. ordering projections.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable and well organized, with good motivation and clear positioning relative to decomposable-score methods and ILP/CP. Some definitions and notational choices need tightening:
- Precisely define “maximum set of ancestral constraints” and the closure procedure.
- Elaborate the test for CPDAG satisfiability beyond the two quick checks, and discuss the practical cost of enumerating class(P) when needed.
- Provide proofs or at least proof sketches (or a pointer to an appendix) for Theorems 1–4.
- Minor typographical issues and incomplete references (e.g., formatting in the bibliography) should be corrected.

Originality
The combination of (i) exact EC-tree search under hard ancestral constraints, (ii) sound pruning at the CPDAG level, and (iii) projection of non-decomposable constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints to strengthen a decomposable oracle, appears novel. Prior work either encoded constraints in ILP/CP or used path beliefs for constraint-based or local search. The MaxSAT-based inference of a largest sound set of ordering constraints is also a new and useful idea.

Significance
Incorporating causal background knowledge as ancestral constraints is practically important. Demonstrating substantial efficiency gains over a strong ILP baseline, while maintaining exactness, is significant for exact BN learning under constraints. The approach’s reliance on a decomposable oracle suggests broader applicability wherever non-decomposable constraints arise. The main limitation is scale (n≈20) and the restriction to exact learning; nevertheless, this is on par with exact BDeu learners.

Pros
- Sound integration of ancestral constraints into EC-tree search with clear pruning rules.
- Elegant projection of path constraints to edge and ordering constraints; MaxSAT formulation to maximize safe ordering constraints.
- Strong empirical speedups over ILP; clear ablations showing the value of projection.
- Addresses a practically relevant form of background knowledge.

Cons
- Some technical details and proofs are omitted; Theorem 1 requires careful justification.
- Potential exponential enumeration of class(P) for satisfiability checking is not analyzed empirically.
- No assessment under noisy/inconsistent constraints; constraints are synthetically generated from ground truth.
- Limited scale and comparisons (no CP baseline; no breakdown of MaxSAT overhead; no separate ablation for edge vs. ordering projections).

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide formal proofs (or appendix) for Theorems 1–4 and a precise definition/algorithm for ancestral-constraint closure.
- Analyze the worst-case and observed costs of CPDAG satisfiability checks (including enumeration frequency) and of the MaxSAT step.
- Add ablations isolating the contributions of edge vs. ordering projections.
- Evaluate robustness to noisy or contradictory constraints and discuss conflict resolution.
- Include a comparison to a CP-based approach (e.g., van Beek & Hoffmann) if feasible.
- Improve reference formatting and fix minor typos.
