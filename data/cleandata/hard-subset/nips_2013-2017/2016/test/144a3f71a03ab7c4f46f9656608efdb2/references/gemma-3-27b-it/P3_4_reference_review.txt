## NIPS 2016 Review: Learning Bayesian Networks with Ancestral Constraints

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning, specifically addressing the challenging problem of incorporating non-decomposable ancestral constraints. The approach, building upon the recent work of Chen et al. (2015, 2016), is well-motivated, technically sound, and demonstrates substantial empirical improvements over existing ILP-based methods. While the complexity of the framework and the reliance on established oracles might present barriers to widespread adoption, the demonstrated benefits and clear presentation warrant a strong positive recommendation for acceptance.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper tackles the problem of learning optimal Bayesian networks subject to ancestral constraints – relationships specifying ancestor/descendant relationships between variables. These constraints are common in real-world applications, representing domain knowledge or causal assumptions. Existing optimal structure learning methods struggle with these constraints because they rely on *decomposable* scoring functions. The authors cleverly adapt the framework of Chen et al. (2015, 2016) – which utilizes EC trees and an oracle for decomposable scores – to handle the non-decomposability introduced by ancestral constraints. They achieve this through a pruning strategy for the EC tree based on constraint satisfaction and by projecting the ancestral constraints into equivalent decomposable constraints to empower the oracle. 

The authors correctly position their work within the broader literature. They reference key works in Bayesian network learning (Darwiche, Koller & Friedman, Murphy), constraint-based methods (Koivisto & Sood, Parviainen & Koivisto), ILP approaches (Jaakkola et al., Bartlett & Cussens, van Beek & Hoffmann), and relevant prior work from Chen et al. They acknowledge the connection to Borboudakis & Tsamardinos’ work on path constraints in constraint-based learning.  The paper effectively contextualizes its contribution within this landscape.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Addressing a Critical Gap:** The paper directly addresses a major limitation of existing optimal structure learning algorithms – their inability to effectively handle non-decomposable constraints like ancestral relations. This is a significant theoretical and practical advance.
* **Sound Technical Foundation:** The adaptation of the EC tree framework is elegant and well-justified. The pruning strategy based on ancestral constraints is sound, and the theoretical guarantees outlined in Theorem 1 are reassuring.
* **Constraint Projection Technique:** The idea of projecting ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints to improve the oracle’s performance is a particularly clever and impactful contribution. The theoretical support (Theorems 2, 3, and 4) demonstrates the rigor of this approach.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results are compelling. The consistently observed orders-of-magnitude speedup compared to GOBNILP demonstrates the practical effectiveness of the proposed approach. The scalability to 20 variables is impressive.
* **Clear and Well-Organized Presentation:**  The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The technical concepts are explained clearly, and the logical flow is easy to follow.  The use of theorems to formalize key results strengthens the presentation.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Reliance on Oracle Performance:** The performance of the approach heavily relies on the performance of the underlying oracle (URLEARNING in the experiments). While the authors demonstrate that empowering the oracle improves efficiency, the overall performance remains bounded by its capabilities.  It’s not entirely clear how the method performs when the oracle itself struggles.
* **Complexity:** The framework (EC trees, oracles, constraint projection) is complex, potentially hindering broader adoption.  A more accessible implementation or a simplified overview of the approach might be beneficial.
* **MaxSAT Solver Dependency:** The efficiency of constraint projection depends on the performance of the MaxSAT solver.  The authors use EVASOLVER, but the performance might vary with different problem instances and solver implementations.
* **Limited Exploration of Constraint Types:** The paper focuses primarily on ancestral constraints.  Exploring other types of non-decomposable constraints and their potential projection into decomposable forms could broaden the applicability of the approach.



**4. Originality:**

The paper demonstrates a high degree of originality. While it builds on the existing work of Chen et al., the adaptation to handle ancestral constraints and the novel constraint projection technique are significant contributions. The systematic evaluation against ILP-based methods is also valuable, providing concrete evidence of the benefits of the proposed approach.

**5. Significance:**

The results are highly significant. The ability to efficiently learn optimal Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints opens up new possibilities for incorporating domain knowledge and causal assumptions into probabilistic modeling. This has implications for various applications, including causal discovery, medical diagnosis, and risk assessment. The demonstration of substantial speedups compared to existing methods makes the approach practically relevant.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Addresses a significant open problem in Bayesian network structure learning.
* Presents a technically sound and well-motivated solution.
* Demonstrates substantial empirical improvements over state-of-the-art methods.
* Clear and well-written presentation.
* The projected constraint technique is a novel and powerful addition.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Reliance on an external oracle.
* Relatively complex framework.
* Dependency on the performance of a MaxSAT solver.




**Final Recommendation:**

**Strongly Accept.** This paper represents a significant advance in the field of Bayesian network structure learning. The theoretical contributions are sound, the empirical results are compelling, and the paper is exceptionally well-written. While the complexity of the framework is a minor concern, the benefits clearly outweigh the drawbacks. I recommend this paper for acceptance to NIPS 2016.
