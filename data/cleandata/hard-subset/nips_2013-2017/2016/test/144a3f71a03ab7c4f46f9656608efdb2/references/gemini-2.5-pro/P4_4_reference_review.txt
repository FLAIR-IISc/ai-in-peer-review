### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of learning an optimal Bayesian network structure from data, subject to background knowledge expressed as ancestral constraints (e.g., variable X must be an ancestor of Y, or X cannot be an ancestor of Y). The authors correctly note that such constraints are non-decomposable, rendering them incompatible with mainstream optimal structure learning algorithms that rely on decomposable scores (e.g., dynamic programming or A* search over orderings).

The proposed approach extends the recent work of Chen et al. (2015, 2016), which introduced an A* search framework over a search space of equivalence classes of DAGs, represented by an "EC tree". This framework is capable of handling non-decomposable scores and constraints. The first contribution of this paper is to adapt this search by developing a sound method for pruning nodes (CPDAGs) in the EC tree that are inconsistent with the given set of ancestral constraints.

The second, and primary, contribution is a method for "projecting" the non-decomposable ancestral constraints onto a set of decomposable constraints (specifically, edge presence/absence and pairwise topological ordering constraints). These inferred decomposable constraints can then be passed to the A* search heuristic (the "oracle"), which is itself an optimal learner for decomposable problems. This "empowers the oracle," leading to a tighter heuristic and a more efficient search. The authors provide theorems and algorithms for inferring edge constraints and formulate the inference of a maximal set of consistent ordering constraints as a MaxSAT problem.

Finally, the paper provides an empirical evaluation comparing the proposed method against an Integer Linear Programming (ILP) baseline (GOBNILP), demonstrating orders-of-magnitude performance improvements on benchmark networks. The experiments also highlight the practical value of ancestral constraints, showing they can significantly improve model accuracy, sometimes having an impact comparable to a large increase in dataset size.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proposed method for pruning the EC tree based on ancestral constraints is well-justified (Theorem 1). The core technical contribution—the projection of non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones—is well-formalized through Theorems 2, 3, and 4 and the MaxSAT formulation. The claims are well-supported by a rigorous experimental evaluation on standard benchmark datasets. The authors compare their method to a strong and relevant baseline (ILP), which is one of the few other exact methods capable of handling such constraints. The experiments are thorough, varying the number of variables, dataset size, and number of constraints, providing a clear picture of the algorithm's performance. The work constitutes a complete and valuable contribution to the field of exact structure learning.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It begins with a clear motivation, provides necessary background, and builds its contributions incrementally. The figures illustrating the BN graph and EC tree are helpful. However, Section 5, which describes the constraint projection, is very dense. A small, worked-out example illustrating how edge constraints are inferred via Theorems 2 and 3, or how the MaxSAT clauses in Section 5.2 prevent the inference of inconsistent orderings, would significantly improve readability and intuition for these core concepts. Additionally, the paper mentions that checking constraint satisfaction on a CPDAG may require exhaustive enumeration of its consistent DAGs (Section 4.3). This is a potentially severe computational bottleneck, as an equivalence class can contain an exponential number of DAGs. The paper could be improved by discussing how frequently this exhaustive step was necessary in the experiments and what its performance impact was.

**Originality:** The paper presents a novel and significant contribution. While the underlying EC tree search framework is from prior work, its application to ancestral constraints and, more importantly, the method of inferring decomposable constraints from non-decomposable ones, is new. This "projection" technique is a clever way to bridge the gap between powerful non-decomposable priors and efficient decomposable-score-based learners used as oracles. The formulation of ordering constraint inference as a MaxSAT problem is also novel in this context. The work is clearly differentiated from prior art; it extends the framework of Chen et al., provides a practical alternative to ILP/CP methods (which were previously unevaluated for this task), and focuses on optimal score-based learning, in contrast to constraint-based methods.

**Significance:** The results presented are important and likely to be impactful for both researchers and practitioners. The ability to efficiently incorporate complex background knowledge like ancestral relations is a critical step toward making Bayesian network learning more practical in real-world domains where such expert knowledge is available. The demonstrated orders-of-magnitude speedup over a state-of-the-art ILP solver makes exact learning with these constraints feasible for larger problems than was previously possible. Furthermore, the empirical result showing that a small percentage of ancestral constraints can improve model accuracy as much as quadrupling the dataset size provides a compelling argument for the value of incorporating domain knowledge, which could guide data collection efforts in practice. This work advances the state-of-the-art in exact score-based structure learning.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Pro:**
*   Addresses a practical and important problem: learning optimal BN structures with non-decomposable ancestral constraints.
*   Presents a novel and technically sound method for projecting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones to accelerate search.
*   Empirical results demonstrate a significant, orders-of-magnitude performance improvement over a relevant state-of-the-art ILP baseline.
*   The work is a clear advancement for exact structure learning, pushing the boundary of problem sizes that can be solved with this class of prior knowledge.

**Con:**
*   The description of the core constraint projection method in Section 5 is dense and could benefit from more detailed examples to improve clarity.
*   The potential computational cost of verifying constraints on a CPDAG via exhaustive enumeration is not fully analyzed or discussed in the context of the experiments.
