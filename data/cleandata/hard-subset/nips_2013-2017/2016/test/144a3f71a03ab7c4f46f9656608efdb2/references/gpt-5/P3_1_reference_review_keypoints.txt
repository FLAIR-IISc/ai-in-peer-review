- Paper tackles exact BN structure learning with hard ancestral (reachability) constraints by pruning EC-tree nodes and projecting constraints to decomposable forms.
- Builds on EC-tree/A* perspective and uses oracle-based heuristics plus projections to edge and ordering constraints.
- Novelty: combining CPDAG pruning by ancestral constraints with projected decomposable constraints and a MaxSAT ordering entailment.
- Strength: addresses a practically important form of background knowledge not handled by mainstream score-based methods.
- Strength: clear conceptual integration—prune provably infeasible CPDAGs and tighten oracle heuristic with entailed decomposable constraints.
- Strength: provides sound rules for negative edge entailment (Theorem 2) and a implementable positive-edge test (Theorem 3), plus a conservative MaxSAT for orderings (Theorem 4).
- Strength: empirical results show large speedups over ILP (GOBNILP) and reveal an easy–hard–easy pattern with constraint density.
- Major concern: CPDAG pruning may require exhaustive enumeration of class(P) in ambiguous cases, with exponential worst-case complexity and unclear practical frequency.
- Major concern: the closure of ancestral constraints used in Theorems 2–3 is defined informally and lacks the full set of inference rules or proofs of correctness.
- Major concern: Theorem 3’s argument against longer paths is not rigorously proven and may risk overly aggressive edge inferences.
- Major concern: The MaxSAT encoding for orderings (item 3 acyclicity condition) is nonstandard and requires proof or intuition that it is sound and prevents cycles.
- Major concern: heuristic admissibility depends critically on sound projections; any unsound entailment could make the heuristic inadmissible and must be made explicit and empirically verified.
- Experimental limitations: only BDeu score is tested—other scores/ESS values like MDL should be included for generality.
- Experimental limitations: no experiments with indegree bounds; ILP baselines could be advantaged by small indegree settings.
- Experimental limitations: constraints are noiseless and sampled from ground truth; behavior with noisy or inconsistent constraints (detection/softening) is not explored.
- Experimental limitations: projection overheads (especially MaxSAT) and fraction of inferred edges/orderings are not reported; precomputation costs for parent-set scores without indegree bounds are missing.
- Clarity issues: editorial errors, missing proofs, deferred algorithmic details (enumeration strategy, oracle interface, constraint filtering on partial CPDAGs) and need for a supplement.
- Clarity request: precisely define how constraints are applied to partial CPDAGs during search and ensure admissibility is preserved.
- Quality assessment: technically promising and well-motivated but several key theoretical claims need fuller proofs and complexity discussion.
- Originality assessment: the combination of EC-tree pruning with projected decomposable constraints and MaxSAT-based ordering entailment is novel and nontrivial.
- Significance assessment: could provide a practical exact method for strong causal priors if pruning/projection hold up, valuable to BN and causal discovery communities.
- Suggested author actions: provide full closure rules and proofs for Theorems 2–4, quantify enumeration frequency/cost, report MaxSAT overhead and inferred counts, and ablate edge vs ordering projections.
- Suggested experiments: include indegree-bounded settings, alternative scores (MDL/ESS variants), and tests with noisy/inconsistent constraints.
- Suggested fixes: clarify admissibility dependence, add formal definitions/proofs/pseudocode in supplement, and clean up references/formatting.
- Pros summary: important prior supported, sound pruning concept, effective projection yielding strong empirical speedups.
- Cons summary: missing theoretical details and proofs, potential costly enumeration in CPDAGs, and narrower experimental scope than desired.
- Overall recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on adding proofs, algorithmic details, and broader/fairer experimental validation.
