### **Review of "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"**

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the problem of learning an optimal Bayesian network structure from data, subject to background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints (e.g., X must be an ancestor of Y, or X cannot be an ancestor of Y). The authors correctly identify that such constraints are non-decomposable and thus cannot be handled by the most efficient modern exact structure learning algorithms, which rely on dynamic programming over orderings and decomposable scores.

The proposed solution extends a recent A*-based search framework over the space of DAGs (BN graphs) or CPDAGs (EC trees), which was originally designed for non-decomposable scores. The core contributions are twofold: 1) A sound method for pruning the EC tree search space by checking the compatibility of CPDAGs with the given ancestral constraints. 2) A novel and powerful technique for "projecting" the non-decomposable ancestral constraints into a set of decomposable constraints (required/forbidden edges and partial orderings). These inferred decomposable constraints are then used to "empower" the A* search heuristic (the oracle), dramatically improving search performance.

The authors provide a strong empirical evaluation comparing their method to a state-of-the-art Integer Linear Programming (ILP) based solver, GOBNILP, which can also handle such constraints. The proposed method demonstrates orders-of-magnitude speedups, scales to problems with up to 20 variables, and highlights the practical value of incorporating background knowledge for improving model accuracy.

**Strengths and Weaknesses (addressing the review criteria):**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality.
*   **Strengths:**
    *   The methodology is sound. The extension of the EC tree search to handle ancestral constraints via pruning is a logical and correct application of that framework.
    *   The theoretical claims, particularly Theorems 2 and 3 for inferring edge constraints, appear correct and provide a solid foundation for the projection method.
    *   The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The authors compare against a strong and appropriate baseline (ILP), design experiments that vary key parameters (n, N, p), and crucially include an ablation study (Table 1 vs. Table 2) that clearly demonstrates the massive performance gain from their primary contribution—the projection of constraints. The observation of the "easy-hard-easy" pattern is also insightful.
*   **Weaknesses:**
    *   Theorem 1 is stated without proof or intuition. While it seems plausible, its soundness is critical to the entire pruning approach. A brief proof sketch, perhaps in an appendix, would strengthen the paper.
    *   The MaxSAT formulation for inferring ordering constraints in Section 5.2 is quite dense. The "sufficient condition for acyclicity" is complex and presented without much justification, making it hard to verify its correctness and tightness on a quick read.

**Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized.
*   **Strengths:**
    *   The introduction provides excellent motivation, clearly stating the problem, the limitations of existing work, and a high-level overview of the proposed solution.
    *   The background sections (2 and 3) are concise and effective. The small example in Section 3 demonstrating why ancestral constraints are non-decomposable is particularly illustrative.
    *   The overall structure logically builds from the basic search framework to the specific constraint handling and finally to the key contribution of constraint projection.
*   **Weaknesses:**
    *   As mentioned under Quality, Section 5.2 is the least clear part of the paper. A small, concrete example walking through the generation of MaxSAT clauses for a 3 or 4-variable problem would significantly improve readability and help the reader build intuition.

**Originality:** The paper makes a novel and important contribution.
*   **Strengths:**
    *   While the paper builds upon the recent framework of Chen et al. (2015, 2016), the extension is non-trivial and tackles a different and practically important class of constraints.
    *   The central idea of inferring decomposable constraints from non-decomposable ones to power an oracle within a larger search is, to my knowledge, novel in this context. It's an elegant way to bridge the gap between two classes of problems and is the paper's standout contribution.
    *   The paper correctly positions itself against related work, clearly distinguishing its score-based optimal approach from constraint-based methods and identifying ILP/CP as the main competitors for this specific task.

**Significance:** The results presented are highly significant.
*   **Strengths:**
    *   The paper tackles a problem with clear practical relevance. Eliciting causal or temporal knowledge from domain experts often results in ancestral constraints, and this work provides an efficient and optimal method to incorporate them.
    *   The demonstrated performance improvement (orders of magnitude over a state-of-the-art ILP solver) is a substantial advance. It makes optimal learning with this type of background knowledge feasible for larger problems than was previously possible.
    *   The finding that a small number of ancestral constraints can have a similar impact on model accuracy as a large increase in data (Section 6) provides a compelling argument for the value of knowledge-informed machine learning, which is a topic of broad interest. This work is likely to be used by and built upon by other researchers.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Solves an Important Problem:** Addresses the practical need for incorporating non-decomposable ancestral knowledge into optimal BN structure learning.
2.  **Novel and Effective Method:** The core idea of "projecting" constraints to empower a search oracle is elegant, novel, and shown to be extremely effective.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates a clear and significant improvement over a state-of-the-art baseline, advancing the field in a demonstrable way.
4.  **High Clarity and Quality:** The paper is well-written, well-motivated, and technically sound.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
1.  **Incremental Novelty:** The work is an extension of the existing EC tree framework. However, the extension is significant and the key ideas (especially projection) are original to this work. This is a very minor concern.
2.  **Clarity in Technical Detail:** The MaxSAT formulation in Section 5.2 could be made clearer with an example. This is a minor issue that can be easily addressed in a final version.

**Conclusion:**

This is an excellent paper that presents a novel, elegant, and highly effective solution to an important problem in Bayesian network learning. The core contribution is technically sound and supported by a very strong set of experiments that demonstrate a clear advance over the state of the art. The paper is well-written and a pleasure to read. I strongly recommend acceptance. A minor revision to improve the clarity of the MaxSAT formulation with a small example would make an already great paper even better.
