Summary and contributions
The paper addresses exact Bayesian network (BN) structure learning in the presence of non-decomposable background knowledge in the form of ancestral (path) constraints. Standard optimal score-based methods assume decomposable scores and typically only support decomposable constraints (edge presence/absence) or order-modular constraints; hence they do not directly handle path constraints. Building on BN graphs and EC trees (Chen et al. 2015, 2016), the authors:

- Show how to enforce ancestral constraints by pruning in the EC-tree search space (nodes are CPDAGs), using A* guided by an oracle that optimizes decomposable scores.
- Prove a monotonicity property (Theorem 1) that justifies pruning: once a CPDAG is inconsistent with the constraints, all descendants are; conversely, if a CPDAG satisfies them, so do its descendants.
- Propose “projection” techniques to infer decomposable constraints from ancestral ones to empower the oracle:
  - Edge constraints: derive necessary/sufficient conditions (Theorems 2–3) for entailing negative and positive edges using ancestor/descendant closures α(·), β(·).
  - Ordering constraints: formulate a MaxSAT problem to extract a maximally large, jointly consistent set of topological ordering constraints entailed by ancestral constraints (with a soundness theorem, Theorem 4).
- Empirically compare their EC-tree approach (using URLEARNING as the oracle and EVA MaxSAT for projection) against ILP (GOBNILP) on subsampled benchmarks (ALARM, ANDES, CHILD, CPCS54, HEPAR2), showing orders-of-magnitude speedups and positive scaling trends. They also show that projected constraints dramatically improve EC-tree performance and modestly help ILP, and report an easy–hard–easy pattern as the number of constraints increases.

Relation to prior work
The work connects to exact BN learning via dynamic programming and A* over order graphs; to ILP/CP formulations (e.g., cluster constraints); to path/causal priors in constraint-based and heuristic search methods (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos); and to prior EC-graph/EC-tree search spaces by the authors. The novelty lies in embedding non-decomposable constraints in EC-tree search with sound pruning, plus systematic projection of path constraints to decomposable constraints to accelerate the oracle.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The key pruning rationale (Theorem 1) is convincing given that EC-tree expansions add leaves without altering existing edges among constrained variables. The negative-edge entailment (Theorem 2) is natural and sound; the positive-edge entailment (Theorem 3) hinges on a “no alternative mediator” condition and a saturated closure A, which seems reasonable though a proof sketch would help. The MaxSAT encoding for order constraints is interesting; Theorem 4 provides sufficient conditions for entailment, but the presentation of the acyclicity clause is terse and could use more justification.
- Empirical support: The experiments (as described) are appropriate: exact comparison against ILP, multiple networks, varying n, sample size, and proportion of constraints; demonstration that projection materially improves performance. However, the review text lacks the actual tables/figures and detailed numbers, and only ILP is used as a baseline (no CP baseline despite being mentioned).
- Completeness: Some algorithmic details are omitted (“details omitted for space”), including the closure computation for ancestral constraints and the exact procedure for CPDAG satisfiability checks when enumeration of class(P) is needed. The potential cost of enumerating class(P) could be significant and deserves quantification.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and readable, with motivating examples and clear definitions of α(·), β(·). Nonetheless:
- Some theorems/claims would benefit from proof sketches (esp. Theorem 3, Theorem 4 and the MaxSAT acyclicity constraint).
- The term “maximum set of ancestral constraints” appears to mean “closure under entailment”; please define precisely.
- The CPDAG satisfiability test section would be clearer with an explicit algorithm and complexity discussion; relying on exhaustive enumeration when simple tests are inconclusive may be a bottleneck.
- There are noticeable reference formatting issues and missing figures/tables in the version I read.

Originality
The combination of (i) EC-tree pruning for non-decomposable path constraints, and (ii) principled projection of path constraints to decomposable edge/order constraints for empowering an oracle, is novel relative to ILP/CP formulations and prior EC-tree work. The MaxSAT-based approach for extracting a maximally consistent set of ordering constraints entailed by path constraints is also a nice contribution.

Significance
Being able to incorporate path-level prior knowledge in exact BN learning is practically important and underexplored in score-based methods. The demonstrated speedups over ILP and the finding that modest amounts of constraints can substitute for large increases in data are valuable takeaways. While exact methods still do not scale to very large n, this work advances the state of the art for constrained exact learning and provides tools (projection) that could benefit broader BN learning pipelines.

Pros
- Addresses a relevant and challenging gap: non-decomposable constraints in exact BN learning.
- Sound pruning criterion in EC-tree; elegant use of oracles.
- Useful projection techniques (edges and orders), with formal conditions and an effective MaxSAT implementation.
- Empirical gains over a strong exact baseline (GOBNILP); insightful analysis (easy–hard–easy).
- Practical importance: integrating causal/ancestral knowledge leads to better accuracy with less data.

Cons
- Missing details/proofs for some key components; unclear complexity and impact of CPDAG enumeration for satisfiability checks.
- Limited baselines (no CP solver), and absent ablations separating the impact of edge vs ordering projections.
- Evaluation uses only correct constraints; robustness to noisy or contradictory constraints is not studied.
- Scalability remains limited (n≈20), and the approach assumes complete data and no latent confounding.
- Several presentation/formatting issues (references, clarity of some theorems).

Questions for authors
1. How often does the CPDAG satisfiability test fall back to exhaustive enumeration, and what is its empirical cost? Could SAT-based reachability checks replace enumeration?
2. Can you provide ablations quantifying the separate contributions of edge vs ordering projections?
3. How robust is the method to noisy/misattributed ancestral constraints? Could your MaxSAT formulation be adapted to soft/weighted constraints?
4. Please clarify “maximum set of ancestral constraints” and define “closed set” of orderings precisely.
5. Will code and scripts be released?

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a solid and novel contribution with clear practical utility, supported by promising experimental evidence. Addressing the noted clarity gaps and adding details on the CPDAG satisfiability procedure and robustness to noisy constraints would strengthen the final version.
