

# Review: Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints

This paper addresses an important gap in Bayesian network structure learning: incorporating non-decomposable ancestral constraints into optimal structure learning. The authors propose a novel approach using the EC tree search space (introduced by Chen et al., 2016) and show how to project ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints to empower an oracle that optimizes decomposable scores. The work builds on recent advances in structure learning with non-decomposable scores and extends them to handle a practically important class of constraints.

**Strengths:**
1. **Technical Contribution**: The paper makes a clear and well-motivated contribution by addressing a significant limitation in current structure learning methods. The authors provide sound theoretical foundations with four theorems establishing the soundness of their constraint projection approach.

2. **Empirical Evaluation**: The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-designed, varying multiple parameters (number of variables, dataset size, constraint density) across standard benchmarks. The results consistently show orders-of-magnitude improvements over ILP-based approaches, which is a compelling empirical contribution.

3. **Practical Relevance**: The paper demonstrates that a small number of ancestral constraints (10-25%) can have a similar impact on model quality as substantially more training data, highlighting the practical value of incorporating background knowledge.

4. **Clarity and Organization**: The paper is well-written and logically organized, with clear explanations of the technical concepts and a helpful progression from problem statement to solution.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Contextualization of Scalability**: While the paper states that their approach scales to 20 variables, it doesn't sufficiently contextualize this against recent state-of-the-art methods. For example, Yuan and Malone (2013) reported results up to 26 variables for BDeu scores, so the 20-variable claim isn't particularly impressive in the current landscape.

2. **Lack of Discussion on Constraint Acquisition**: The paper doesn't address how difficult it is for domain experts to provide accurate ancestral constraints in practice. Without this information, the practical utility of the approach is somewhat unclear.

3. **Limited Generalization Discussion**: The work is limited to BDeu scoring and doesn't discuss how the approach would generalize to other scoring functions or approximate learning methods.

4. **Insufficient Detail on Constraint Projection**: While the theoretical foundation is strong, the paper could benefit from more concrete examples of constraint projection in action, particularly for the MaxSAT approach to ordering constraints.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- Fills an important gap in Bayesian network learning literature by handling non-decomposable ancestral constraints
- Provides a theoretically sound and empirically validated approach that significantly outperforms existing methods
- Offers practical insights about the value of background knowledge in structure learning
- Well-written and clearly organized

*Con:*
- The scalability claim (n=20) is not particularly impressive compared to recent work
- Doesn't sufficiently address practical challenges of acquiring ancestral constraints
- Limited discussion of how the approach would generalize to other scoring functions
- The paper doesn't clarify whether the constraint projection approach is necessary for the EC tree to work, or if it's just an efficiency improvement

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid technical contribution that addresses a meaningful problem in Bayesian network structure learning. The paper demonstrates significant empirical improvements over existing methods and provides a theoretically sound approach to constraint projection. While the scalability claim is somewhat weak compared to recent literature, the methodological contribution is substantial and the results are convincing.

The paper meets NIPS standards for quality and significance, though it could be strengthened by better contextualizing the scalability claim, discussing practical constraint acquisition, and providing more concrete examples of constraint projection. The work is clearly original in its specific approach to ancestral constraints, though it builds well on the foundational work of Chen et al. (2015, 2016).

**Recommendation:**

Accept. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning with practical implications. While there are some limitations in the presentation of scalability and constraint acquisition, these are minor compared to the strength of the core contribution and the convincing empirical results. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses a problem that is both theoretically interesting and practically relevant.
