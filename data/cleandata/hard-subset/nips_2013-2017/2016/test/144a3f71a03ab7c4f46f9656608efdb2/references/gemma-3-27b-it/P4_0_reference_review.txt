This paper addresses the problem of learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints, a relevant and challenging task. The core idea is to adapt a recent framework for optimal Bayesian network structure learning [Chen et al., 2015, 2016] to incorporate these non-decomposable constraints. The authors propose to infer decomposable constraints from the ancestral ones to leverage an oracle that excels with decomposable scores. The paper is well-structured, clearly presenting the problem, related work, proposed approach, and experimental results.

The paper builds upon a line of work focusing on optimal structure learning, particularly the framework introduced by Chen et al. [2015, 2016], which tackled the issue of non-decomposable priors. This work is positioned as an extension to handle a specific type of non-decomposable constraint – ancestral constraints – which are common in real-world applications where domain knowledge exists.  Prior work on incorporating constraints primarily focused on edge presence/absence or topological orderings [Koivisto and Sood, 2004, Parviainen and Koivisto, 2013]. While ILP and CP approaches can *represent* these constraints [Jaakkola et al., 2010, Bartlett and Cussens, 2015, van Beek and Hoffmann, 2015], the authors rightly point out the lack of empirical evaluation of their efficacy.  The connection to constraint-based methods using path constraints [Borboudakis and Tsamardinos, 2012, 2013] is also noted, offering context within the broader field.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an important problem:** Learning with background knowledge (in the form of ancestral constraints) is crucial for many real-world applications.
* **Novel approach:** The combination of adapting the EC tree framework with constraint projection is a novel way to tackle the problem of non-decomposable constraints.  Inferring decomposable constraints to empower the oracle is a clever idea.
* **Strong empirical evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed, covering a range of network sizes, dataset sizes, and constraint levels. The comparison against ILP provides a valuable benchmark.  The demonstration of significant speedups is compelling.
* **Theoretical justification:** Theorems 1-4 provide sound theoretical foundations for the proposed methods.
* **Clear writing:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, with a logical organization.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of the method:** While the explanation is clear, the overall approach is complex and involves multiple components (EC trees, constraint projection, oracle, MaxSAT). A reader unfamiliar with the base framework by Chen et al. might find it difficult to grasp the entirety of the method quickly.
* **Dependence on the oracle:** The performance heavily relies on the effectiveness of the chosen oracle (URLEARNING). While justified, the paper doesn’t thoroughly explore the sensitivity to the choice of the oracle.
* **MaxSAT Solver:** The use of an external MaxSAT solver (EVASOLVER) introduces a dependency and potential bottleneck. The impact of MaxSAT solving time is not thoroughly reported.
* **Limited comparison:** The comparison is primarily against a single ILP implementation (GOBNILP). Comparing against other state-of-the-art constraint-based or score-based learning algorithms (even without direct ancestral constraint support) might further highlight the advantages of the proposed approach.
* **Structural Hamming Distance Caveat:** The explanation regarding non-zero SHD even with p=1 is adequate but could be expanded.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a challenging problem in Bayesian network learning.
* The experimental results are compelling, demonstrating significant improvements in efficiency compared to existing methods.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed approach and its evaluation.
* The work has the potential to impact both research and practice by enabling the incorporation of background knowledge into Bayesian network learning.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The complexity of the method might limit its accessibility and adoption.
* The reliance on an external MaxSAT solver and the chosen oracle adds dependencies and potential limitations.
* The limited comparison against other algorithms leaves room for further investigation.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper is a strong contribution that addresses an important problem with a novel and well-supported approach. The experimental results are convincing, and the writing is clear. While the method is complex and has some dependencies, the benefits in terms of efficiency and the ability to incorporate non-decomposable constraints outweigh these drawbacks. I recommend acceptance.
