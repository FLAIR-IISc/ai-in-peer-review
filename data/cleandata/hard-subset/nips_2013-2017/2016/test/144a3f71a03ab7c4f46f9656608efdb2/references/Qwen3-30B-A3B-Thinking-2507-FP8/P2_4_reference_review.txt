

# Review: Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints

## Summary
This paper addresses a significant challenge in Bayesian network structure learning: incorporating non-decomposable ancestral constraints (e.g., "X is an ancestor of Y") into the learning process. The authors note that while such constraints are valuable for encoding domain knowledge, most existing approaches assume decomposable scores, making them incompatible with ancestral constraints. The paper proposes a novel approach using the EC tree (Equivalence Class tree) search space, along with a method for inferring decomposable constraints from ancestral constraints. The authors demonstrate through experiments that their method is orders of magnitude more efficient than ILP-based approaches when handling ancestral constraints.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound and well-supported. The authors provide formal proofs for their constraint inference methods (Theorems 2-4), demonstrating the soundness of their approach. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, systematically varying key parameters (number of variables, dataset size, constraint density) across multiple benchmark networks. The comparison against GOBNILP, a state-of-the-art ILP-based system, is fair and well-executed, with the authors using the same datasets and scoring functions. The authors also acknowledge limitations, such as the fact that their approach only handles ancestral constraints (not all non-decomposable constraints), and note that the constraint inference step has negligible overhead compared to the structure search. The paper presents a complete technical solution to the problem, not just a preliminary idea.

## Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The technical concepts (ancestral constraints, EC trees, constraint inference) are introduced clearly with precise definitions. The connection between ancestral constraints and their non-decomposable nature is particularly well-explained, with a compelling example showing why standard approaches like K2 cannot handle them. The relationship between the paper's contributions and prior work (Chen et al. 2015, 2016; ILP/CP approaches) is clearly articulated. The experimental setup is thoroughly described, making it possible to reproduce the results. The figures (not shown here but referenced) would likely further clarify the EC tree and BN graph concepts. The paper's structure follows a logical flow from problem statement through technical contributions to empirical evaluation.

## Originality
This work makes a significant contribution to the literature. While ILP and constraint programming approaches can theoretically represent ancestral constraints, the authors provide the first empirical demonstration that their EC tree approach with constraint projection is orders of magnitude more efficient. The key novelty lies in their method for inferring decomposable constraints from ancestral constraints—specifically, the algorithms for edge constraints (Theorem 2) and topological ordering constraints (reduced to MaxSAT). This is a novel combination of ideas that directly addresses the core challenge of non-decomposability. The paper clearly differentiates itself from Chen et al. (2015, 2016) by extending their framework to handle ancestral constraints, and from ILP/CP approaches by demonstrating superior efficiency. The related work is thoroughly cited and contextualized.

## Significance
The significance of this work is substantial. Ancestral constraints are crucial for incorporating domain knowledge into Bayesian network learning, particularly in applications like medical diagnosis and causal inference. The authors demonstrate that a small number of ancestral constraints (10-25%) can have the same impact on model accuracy as quadrupling the dataset size (from 512 to 2048 samples), highlighting the practical value of domain knowledge. Their method scales to 20 variables, which is comparable to state-of-the-art score-based approaches, making it applicable to meaningful real-world problems. The efficiency gains (orders of magnitude faster than ILP) make the approach practically viable for applications where structure learning with constraints is needed. This represents a demonstrable advance over previous approaches to constrained structure learning.

## Strengths
- Clear problem statement highlighting the gap between existing decomposable approaches and practical non-decomposable constraints
- Theoretical soundness with formal proofs for constraint inference methods
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation across multiple parameters and benchmarks
- Practical demonstration of the value of ancestral constraints (comparing to dataset size increases)
- Well-organized and clearly written, making complex concepts accessible

## Weaknesses
- The paper doesn't explore how to handle inconsistent ancestral constraints (e.g., X → Y and Y → X), though this may be outside the scope
- The evaluation focuses on synthetic data from standard benchmarks; a real-world case study would strengthen the practical significance
- The paper doesn't explicitly compare against other constraint-based approaches beyond ILP (e.g., constraint programming)

## Recommendation
This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. It addresses a practical problem (incorporating domain knowledge via ancestral constraints) with a theoretically sound and empirically validated solution that significantly outperforms existing approaches. The work is well-executed, clearly presented, and makes a clear advance over previous methods. While the limitations noted are minor, the paper's quality, clarity, originality, and significance all strongly support acceptance.

The paper is a valuable addition to the NIPS literature, as it provides a practically useful method for a common problem in structure learning that has been largely overlooked in the literature. The results are compelling and the technical contributions are significant enough to warrant publication at NIPS. The authors have clearly demonstrated the value of their approach through both theoretical analysis and empirical evaluation.

I strongly recommend acceptance.
