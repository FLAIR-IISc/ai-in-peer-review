**Summary**
This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data under a specific type of background knowledge: ancestral constraints (e.g., variable X must be an ancestor of Y, or X must not be an ancestor of Y). Such constraints are non-decomposable, meaning they cannot be verified locally for each variable's parent set, which poses a significant challenge for many state-of-the-art exact structure learning algorithms that rely on score decomposability (e.g., dynamic programming or A* search over variable orderings).

The authors propose a solution by adapting a recent A*-based search framework that operates over a search space of equivalence classes of DAGs (the "EC tree"). Their core contributions are twofold:
1.  A sound pruning strategy for the EC tree search space, where nodes (representing CPDAGs) that cannot satisfy the ancestral constraints are removed.
2.  A novel and powerful method for "projecting" the non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints (i.e., required/forbidden edges and partial variable orderings). This is achieved through a set of logical inference rules for edge constraints and a MaxSAT formulation for deriving a maximal consistent set of ordering constraints. These inferred decomposable constraints are then passed to the A* search's heuristic oracle, significantly improving its accuracy and pruning power.

The approach is empirically evaluated against an Integer Linear Programming (ILP) formulation, a standard method for handling arbitrary constraints. The results demonstrate that the proposed method is orders of magnitude faster and scales to larger problems.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Practicality:** The paper tackles a problem of high practical importance. In many real-world applications (e.g., biology, medicine), domain experts can provide causal or temporal knowledge, which often takes the form of ancestral relations. Integrating this knowledge effectively into the learning process can both improve the quality of the learned model and reduce the search space. This work provides a highly effective method for doing just that.

2.  **Novel and Clever Technical Contribution:** The standout contribution is the projection of non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable ones (Section 5). This is a very clever idea. While the adaptation of the EC tree search is a natural extension of prior work, the projection technique is what makes the approach highly efficient. The use of a MaxSAT solver to find the largest set of consistent ordering constraints is particularly sophisticated and well-suited to the problem's subtleties, which the authors clearly identify (e.g., naively inferring orderings can improperly prune valid DAGs).

3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experimental section is thorough and convincing.
    *   The comparison against a strong and relevant baseline (GOBNILP, an ILP-based exact solver) clearly shows the superiority of the proposed approach, with performance gains of several orders of magnitude.
    *   The comparison between Table 1 (with projection) and Table 2 (without projection) serves as an excellent ablation study. It unequivocally demonstrates that the projection technique is the key driver of the performance gains, validating the paper's central technical claim.
    *   The results on structural Hamming distance (Table 3) effectively show the real-world benefit of the method: adding a small percentage of ancestral constraints can improve model accuracy as much as quadrupling the amount of training data, highlighting the value of incorporating background knowledge.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clear, the technical background is presented concisely, the core problem of non-decomposability is illustrated with a simple example, and the proposed solutions are explained logically. The structure flows well from problem definition to solution and evaluation.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Scope of Constraints:** The paper focuses exclusively on ancestral constraints. While this is a well-motivated and important class of constraints, a brief discussion on whether the "projection" framework could be generalized to other types of non-decomposable constraints (e.g., constraints on the length of the longest path) would have strengthened the paper's broader impact.

2.  **Clarity of MaxSAT Formulation:** The MaxSAT formulation in Section 5.2, particularly the "sufficient condition for acyclicity," is presented very densely. While likely correct, a little more intuition or a brief example illustrating why this complex clause is necessary would improve readability for non-experts in this specific area. This is a minor point, as space is limited in conference papers.

3.  **Incremental Aspect:** The core search framework (A* on the EC tree) is not new and is taken directly from Chen et al. [2015, 2016]. One could view this work as an application of that framework to a new problem. However, I believe the novelty and effectiveness of the constraint projection technique are significant enough to make this a strong, standalone contribution that goes well beyond a simple application.

**Overall Assessment and Justification**

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant and practical contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. It addresses a well-defined and important problem with a technically sound and novel solution. The core idea of empowering a decomposable-score oracle with projected constraints inferred from non-decomposable ones is powerful and could potentially be applied in other contexts. The claims are strongly supported by a well-designed set of experiments that show dramatic improvements over the state of the art. The paper is well-written and meets all the NIPS criteria of quality, clarity, originality, and significance. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the overall strength of the work. I strongly recommend acceptance.
