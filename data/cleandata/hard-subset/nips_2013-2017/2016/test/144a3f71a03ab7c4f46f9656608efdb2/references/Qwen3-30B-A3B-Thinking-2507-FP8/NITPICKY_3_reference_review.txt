

# Review of "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"

This paper proposes an approach for learning Bayesian network structures under ancestral constraints using the EC tree search space, with an oracle that optimizes decomposable scores. The authors aim to integrate non-decomposable ancestral constraints into structure learning, which is a valuable extension to existing methods. However, the paper contains numerous technical inconsistencies, formatting errors, and insufficient methodological details that undermine its scientific rigor.

## Major Issues

1. **Technical Inconsistencies**: 
   - Section 4.1 incorrectly describes A* search in the BN graph, stating h(G) is "the estimated cost to reach a leaf from G" - but the goal is reaching a complete DAG, not "a leaf." This is a fundamental misunderstanding of the search space.
   - Theorem 2 (Section 5.1) states "A entails the negative edge constraint X 6→ Y iff Xa 6 Yb" without defining what "Xa 6 Yb" means. This is either a typo or a critical omission in the formal definition.
   - In the MaxSAT formulation (Section 5.2), the constraint "(X < Y ) ∧ (Z < W )⇒ (X Y ) ∨ (Z W ) ∨ ..." uses ambiguous notation where "X Y" should be "X Y" (ancestral constraint) but is written without proper notation.

2. **Formatting and Citation Errors**:
   - References use inconsistent formatting: "Chen et al\.,? \Q2015\E" instead of standard citation format.
   - The paper contains typos in references (e.g., "Bartlett and Cussens.,? \Q2015\E").
   - The abstract contains a typo: "th Conference" should be "The Conference."
   - Inconsistent use of "Bayesian network" (singular vs. plural) throughout the paper.

3. **Experimental Flaws**:
   - The paper claims to vary "the percentage of the n(n−1)/2 total ancestral relations" but n(n-1)/2 is the number of possible directed edges, not ancestral relations. The correct count for ancestral relations is n(n-1) (since X→Y is different from Y→X).
   - The paper states "we used the EVASOLVER partial MaxSAT solver" without citation or version information.
   - The paper claims "orders-of-magnitude improvements" without providing specific numerical results or statistical significance tests to support this claim.
   - The paper assumes "BDeu scores with an equivalent sample size of 1" without justification for this specific choice.

4. **Methodological Gaps**:
   - The paper claims to "empirically evaluate" against ILP approaches but doesn't specify which ILP-based systems were compared.
   - The paper states "we can infer some decomposable constraints from non-decomposable ones" but provides no concrete examples of how this inference works in practice.
   - The paper claims to use "the EC tree" but doesn't explain why it's superior to the BN graph for this specific task.

## Minor Issues That Accumulate

- Section 3 contains a sentence fragment: "We empirically evaluate the proposed approach against those based on ILP, showing orders of magnitude improvements."
- The paper incorrectly states "the number of possible ancestral relations" as n(n-1)/2, which is the number of possible directed edges, not ancestral relations.
- The paper claims "orders-of-magnitude improvements" but doesn't provide sufficient detail about experimental setup (hardware, software versions, exact parameters).
- In Section 5.2, the MaxSAT problem includes a clause "for all X < Y and Z < W : (X < Y ) ∧ (Z < W )⇒ (X Y ) ∨ ..." where "X Y" should be a clear ancestral constraint notation.

## Strengths

The paper identifies a valuable problem (incorporating ancestral constraints into Bayesian network learning) and proposes a novel approach using the EC tree. The concept of projecting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones is interesting, and the experimental results appear promising. The paper correctly identifies the limitation of existing approaches (K2, order graph) in handling non-decomposable constraints.

## Weaknesses

The technical flaws are too numerous and significant to overlook. The paper's claims about efficiency gains are unsubstantiated by sufficient detail, and the theoretical foundations contain critical inconsistencies. The formatting errors and inconsistent terminology undermine the paper's professionalism. As a scientific contribution, it lacks the rigor expected at NIPS, particularly in the formal definitions and experimental reporting.

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection. While the paper addresses a meaningful problem and contains potentially valuable insights, the numerous technical inconsistencies, formatting errors, and methodological gaps prevent it from being a scientifically sound contribution. The authors have not demonstrated sufficient understanding of the problem to produce a rigorous paper. The paper's flaws are not merely stylistic but affect the core technical claims, making it difficult to evaluate the actual contribution.

The paper would benefit from significant revision: clarifying the theoretical foundations, fixing all technical inconsistencies, providing complete experimental details, and properly formatting all references. Until these issues are addressed, the paper does not meet NIPS standards for technical rigor.
