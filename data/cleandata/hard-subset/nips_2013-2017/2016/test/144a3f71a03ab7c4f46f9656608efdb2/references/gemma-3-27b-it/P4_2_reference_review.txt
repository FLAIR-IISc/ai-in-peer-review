This paper addresses the problem of learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints, a task that is difficult for standard approaches relying on decomposable scores. The core idea is to leverage the recent framework of Chen et al. (2015, 2016) for optimal structure learning with non-decomposable priors and to integrate ancestral constraints by pruning the search space within the EC tree structure. Additionally, the authors propose methods for projecting the non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints to empower an oracle used in the search process. The paper is well-motivated, acknowledging the limitations of existing methods and building on a relevant body of work.

The strengths of this work are significant. First, the problem is important and practical; ancestral constraints represent valuable prior knowledge. Second, the proposed approach effectively extends a promising recent framework (Chen et al.’s work) to handle these constraints. The theoretical results (Theorems 1-4) are valuable in providing guarantees about the soundness and completeness of the constraint projection and pruning steps. Third, the empirical evaluation is thorough, comparing the approach against a relevant ILP-based system (GOBNILP) on benchmark datasets. The results demonstrate significant performance improvements, especially as the network size increases and constraints are effectively utilized. The observation that a small number of ancestral constraints can improve results comparable to significantly increasing dataset size is particularly insightful.

However, there are also some weaknesses. The presentation of the MaxSAT reduction in Section 5.2 is somewhat dense and could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the clause construction. While the authors state the theoretical guarantees, the practical impact of those guarantees is not fully explored – how robust is the performance to imperfect or inconsistent ancestral constraints? Furthermore, while the comparison with GOBNILP is helpful, it would have been beneficial to compare the running time and memory usage of the proposed approach with other standard Bayesian network structure learning algorithms (e.g., those based on hill climbing or greedy search) with and without ancestral constraints. This would provide a broader context for assessing the computational benefits. The paper also assumes the BDeu score, and doesn’t explore the sensitivity of the method to different scoring functions. Finally, the experimental setup relies heavily on simulated data. Exploring real-world datasets would strengthen the conclusions.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and challenging problem in Bayesian network learning.
*   Provides a theoretically sound and practically effective solution building on recent advances.
*   Demonstrates significant empirical improvements over existing approaches (GOBNILP).
*   The proposed constraint projection method is a novel and insightful contribution.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation (aside from the MaxSAT section).

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The MaxSAT reduction explanation could be clearer.
*   Limited comparison with a broader range of existing algorithms.
*   Lack of real-world dataset evaluation.
*   Sensitivity analysis of the approach to the choice of scoring function is missing.
*   Robustness to inconsistent or imperfect ancestral constraints is not evaluated.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper represents a strong contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. While there are some areas for improvement, the novelty, theoretical soundness, and empirical results justify acceptance. The work is likely to be of interest to researchers and practitioners working on causal discovery and probabilistic graphical models. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to improve the clarity of the MaxSAT reduction section and to include a brief discussion of the limitations regarding robustness to inconsistent constraints. Additional experiments on real-world datasets and comparisons with other structure learning methods would further strengthen the paper.
