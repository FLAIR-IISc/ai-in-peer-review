

# Peer Review: "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"

## Summary

This paper addresses an important gap in Bayesian network structure learning: the incorporation of ancestral constraints, which are non-decomposable constraints specifying cause-and-effect relationships between variables. The authors build upon the EC tree search space introduced by Chen et al. (2015, 2016) to develop a method for efficiently learning optimal Bayesian networks subject to these constraints. The key innovation is a method for pruning the EC tree based on ancestral constraints and a technique for projecting these non-decomposable constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints that can be used to empower an oracle (a system that optimizes decomposable scores).

The paper relates well to previous work in the field. It acknowledges the limitations of mainstream approaches that assume decomposable scoring functions (which cannot handle ancestral constraints), and contrasts with ILP and constraint programming approaches that can represent such constraints but have not been empirically evaluated for this specific task. The authors also connect their work to prior research on constraint-based learning (Borboudakis and Tsamardinos) and to the EC tree framework.

## Strengths

### Quality
- The paper presents a technically sound approach with well-supported theoretical claims (Theorems 1-4). The pruning method for ancestral constraints is proven sound and complete.
- The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-designed, with multiple benchmark networks (ALARM, ANDES, CHILD, CPCS54, HEPAR2), varying problem sizes (n=12-20 variables, dataset sizes N=512-8192), and different constraint proportions (p=10%-50%).
- The authors carefully evaluate the impact of constraint projection (Tables 1 vs. 2) and demonstrate the significant efficiency gains (orders of magnitude) of their approach compared to ILP-based methods (GOBNILP).
- The paper acknowledges limitations, such as the need to exhaustively enumerate Markov equivalence classes when pruning CPDAGs (though this is rare in practice), and provides appropriate comparisons.

### Clarity
- The paper is clearly written and well-organized, with a logical flow from problem statement to technical approach to evaluation.
- Key concepts (ancestral constraints, EC trees, constraint projection) are well-explained with illustrative examples and figures.
- The connection between ancestral constraints and their decomposition into edge/ordering constraints is particularly well-explained.
- The paper provides sufficient detail for reproducibility, including implementation specifics (BDeu scores with equivalent sample size of 1, EVASOLVER for MaxSAT).

### Originality
- The paper identifies a specific gap in the literature: handling non-decomposable ancestral constraints in optimal Bayesian network structure learning.
- The EC tree-based approach with constraint projection is a novel contribution that builds on prior work (Chen et al.) while addressing a limitation of that work.
- The MaxSAT encoding for ordering constraints (Section 5.2) is a particularly innovative technique for handling non-decomposable constraints.
- The paper clearly differentiates its approach from prior work (K2, order graph, ILP, CP) and demonstrates how it overcomes their limitations.

### Significance
- Ancestral constraints are important in practice for incorporating domain knowledge, and the paper demonstrates that a small number of such constraints (10%-25%) can have a similar impact on model quality as significantly more training data.
- The demonstrated efficiency gains (orders of magnitude faster) make the approach practically useful for real-world applications.
- The approach scales to 20 variables, which is competitive with state-of-the-art score-based approaches (comparable to Yuan and Malone [2013] who reported results up to 26 variables).
- The paper provides a concrete method for leveraging background knowledge in Bayesian network learning, which has practical implications for applications in domains where causal knowledge is available.

## Weaknesses

### Quality
- While the paper provides strong empirical evidence, the theoretical analysis of the constraint projection method (particularly the MaxSAT encoding) could be strengthened with a formal proof of optimality or completeness.
- The paper doesn't discuss potential limitations when the number of ancestral constraints is very large (close to n(n-1)/2), which might reduce the search space too much and make the problem trivial.
- The comparison with CP approaches (mentioned in the introduction) is not empirically evaluated, which is a missed opportunity to demonstrate the full scope of the contribution.

### Clarity
- The explanation of the EC tree pruning algorithm (Section 4.3) could be clearer. Specifically, the details of how to check whether a CPDAG satisfies ancestral constraints (when the initial tests are inconclusive) need more elaboration.
- The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how the oracle (URLEARNING) is actually empowered by the projected constraints, beyond stating that it's "more efficient."

### Originality
- While the EC tree framework is novel, the constraint projection method (especially for edge constraints) builds on techniques that have been used in other constraint satisfaction contexts, though the application to Bayesian networks is novel.

### Significance
- The paper doesn't provide a thorough discussion of how to obtain ancestral constraints in practice (e.g., from domain experts), which is a crucial consideration for real-world applications.
- The experiments focus on simulated data and constraints. It would be valuable to demonstrate the approach on a real-world dataset where ancestral constraints are known.

## Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments For Acceptance:**
- The paper addresses a well-motivated problem with practical significance (incorporating domain knowledge into Bayesian network learning).
- The proposed approach is technically sound and demonstrates significant efficiency gains over existing methods.
- The paper makes a clear contribution to the literature by extending the EC tree framework to handle non-decomposable ancestral constraints.
- The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-designed, providing strong evidence for the approach's effectiveness.
- The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making it accessible to the NIPS audience.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
- The paper doesn't fully evaluate the approach against constraint programming methods, which could have provided a more comprehensive comparison.
- The explanation of the pruning algorithm for CPDAGs could be clearer.
- The paper could provide more discussion of practical considerations for obtaining ancestral constraints in real-world applications.

## Overall Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. It identifies a meaningful gap in the literature (handling non-decomposable ancestral constraints), proposes a technically sound solution, and provides thorough empirical validation. The efficiency gains demonstrated (orders of magnitude faster than ILP-based approaches) are impressive and have clear practical implications.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a clear contribution that advances the state of the art in Bayesian network structure learning. While there are minor weaknesses in the theoretical analysis and some details that could be clarified, these do not detract from the paper's overall quality and significance.

I recommend acceptance of this paper at NIPS.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. In Section 4.3, provide a more detailed explanation of the algorithm for checking whether a CPDAG satisfies ancestral constraints when the initial tests (directed path or partially directed path) are inconclusive.

2. In Section 5.2, consider adding a formal proof that the MaxSAT encoding correctly identifies the largest set of ordering constraints that can be inferred without eliminating any valid DAGs.

3. Add a brief discussion of practical considerations for obtaining ancestral constraints in real-world applications, including potential challenges in eliciting them from domain experts.

4. Consider including a comparison with constraint programming approaches in the experiments to provide a more complete evaluation of the approach's strengths relative to other methods that can represent non-decomposable constraints.

5. In the conclusion, briefly mention potential directions for future work, such as handling more complex types of non-decomposable constraints beyond ancestral constraints.
