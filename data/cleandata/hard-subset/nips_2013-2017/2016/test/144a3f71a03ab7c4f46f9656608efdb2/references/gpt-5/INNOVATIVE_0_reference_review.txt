Summary
The paper addresses exact Bayesian network structure learning with non-decomposable background knowledge in the form of ancestral (path) constraints. Existing optimal methods largely rely on decomposable scores and support only decomposable constraints (edge presence/absence, order constraints), while ILP/CP can in principle encode path constraints but have not been evaluated for this purpose. The authors adapt the BN-graph/EC-tree shortest-path framework (Chen et al. 2015/2016) to handle ancestral constraints by pruning CPDAG nodes that cannot be extended to satisfy the constraints. The key idea is to (i) integrate non-decomposable constraints directly in the EC-tree search via sound pruning, and (ii) “project” ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and topological ordering constraints that can be passed to the oracle (URLEARNING) to tighten its heuristic. They give rules and theorems for entailed negative and positive edge constraints (Theorems 2–3) and formulate inference of a maximally large consistent set of ordering constraints as a partial MaxSAT problem, with sufficient conditions for entailment (Theorem 4). Experiments on standard BN benchmarks with randomly sampled ancestral constraints show orders-of-magnitude speedups over GOBNILP, and substantial additional gains when using projected constraints. The method scales to n≈20 in these settings and shows an easy–hard–easy pattern as constraint density increases.

Relation to prior work
The work builds directly on the BN-graph/EC-tree framework for non-decomposable scoring (Chen et al. 2015/2016) and contrasts with ILP/CP (Jaakkola et al. 2010; Bartlett & Cussens 2015) that can encode path constraints but lack targeted evaluation here. It also connects to path-constraint priors/soft constraints and constraint-based learning (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos 2012/2013), but differs by providing exact model selection with hard ancestral constraints. The novelty lies in making ancestral constraints first-class citizens in EC-tree search and in the projection machinery that converts non-decomposable knowledge into decomposable constraints to power a decomposable oracle.

Strengths
- Clear, impactful idea: integrate non-decomposable ancestral constraints into exact structure learning and leverage them twice (search pruning and oracle empowerment).
- Theoretical development: sound pruning on CPDAGs; characterization of entailed negative edges (necessary and sufficient) and sufficient conditions for positive edges; a MaxSAT formulation to harvest many safe ordering constraints.
- Empirical gains: consistent, large speedups over a strong ILP baseline on standard networks; compelling evidence that projected constraints are crucial in practice.
- Practical relevance: Ancestral constraints are natural in causal domains; demonstrating that modest amounts of such knowledge can substitute for substantial data increases is useful.

Weaknesses/concerns
- Clarity/precision: Theorem 1 appears misstated (“P satisfies A iff its descendants satisfy A”). Likely intended: if P violates A then all descendants violate A (i.e., pruning is sound). Please correct.
- Projection completeness: For order constraints, the MaxSAT encoding enforces sufficient (not necessary) conditions; the method may leave many entailed orderings on the table. The paper should clarify optimality guarantees (or lack thereof) and the trade-off between safety and completeness.
- Positive edge entailment (Theorem 3): Conditions are strong; complexity and practical frequency of successful positive-edge deductions are not analyzed. Some intuition or statistics on how often Theorem 3 fires would help.
- CPDAG feasibility test: When simple checks are inconclusive, the method enumerates class(P). This can be exponential; there is no report on how often enumeration is triggered, class sizes encountered, or its runtime impact. Such statistics would strengthen the practicality claims.
- Experimental scope: 
  - Only GOBNILP is compared; CP-based approaches (e.g., van Beek & Hoffmann 2015) could be included for completeness, even if expected to be weaker.
  - No ablation on “edge-only” vs “order-only” projection; disentangling their contributions would be valuable.
  - Constraints are noiseless and derived from ground truth. In many applications, path knowledge can be wrong or soft. Robustness to noisy constraints (e.g., with a small fraction flipped) is not studied.
  - Scalability remains modest (n up to 20), albeit consistent with exact BDeu baselines; some discussion of limits and memory usage would help.
- Presentation: Numerous typos/formatting issues (e.g., stray characters, incomplete citations, “th Conference…”, footnotes), and some “details omitted for space” in closure rules that are essential for reproduction. Pseudocode for edge entailment and for the closure of ancestral constraints would improve reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
- Please clarify/correct Theorem 1 and provide a proof sketch. Is pruning complete in the sense that no satisfiable CPDAG is pruned?
- How often does class(P) enumeration occur, and what are the observed sizes? Any caching or partial orientation reasoning to avoid enumeration?
- For the MaxSAT inference: are the hard clauses sufficient to guarantee that the resulting order set is consistent with all DAGs satisfying A (i.e., never eliminates a feasible DAG)? Any cases where the solver returns suboptimal sets due to the sufficiency encoding?
- Ablations: runtime with no projection, edge-only, order-only? How much each contributes across settings?
- How does the method behave under noisy ancestral constraints? Can the framework handle soft constraints (e.g., weighted penalties) via the same BN-graph/EC-tree machinery?

Pros for acceptance
- Introduces a compelling, general mechanism to exploit non-decomposable knowledge in exact BN learning, with solid theoretical underpinnings.
- Demonstrates large, practical gains over state-of-the-art exact ILP.
- The projection idea is elegant and likely to be adopted beyond this specific framework.

Cons for acceptance
- Presentation issues and some ambiguous statements reduce clarity.
- Limited analysis of the expensive subroutines (class(P) enumeration) and of projection completeness.
- Experimental scope could be broader (noise robustness, ablations, alternative baselines).

Recommendation
Lean accept. The paper presents a novel and useful direction—bridging non-decomposable ancestral constraints with decomposable oracles and EC-tree search—and backs it with strong empirical speedups. While some proofs/details and clarity need tightening, the core contribution is original and likely to influence subsequent work on exact BN learning with background knowledge. I would support acceptance conditional on addressing the clarity concerns and adding brief quantitative diagnostics on enumeration and projection ablations. Confidence: high.
