**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data in the presence of ancestral constraints (e.g., variable X must be an ancestor of Y, or X must *not* be an ancestor of Y). The key challenge is that such constraints are non-decomposable, rendering them incompatible with mainstream, efficient, score-based learning algorithms that rely on score decomposability. The authors build upon a recent A*-based search framework over a space of equivalence classes (the EC tree), which can handle non-decomposable scores by using a standard decomposable-score learner as an "oracle" to compute the heuristic.

The central and most novel contribution is a technique the authors term "constraint projection." They propose methods to infer a set of decomposable constraints (edge presence/absence and pairwise variable orderings) from the given non-decomposable ancestral constraints. These simpler, inferred constraints are then passed to the oracle, dramatically improving the heuristic's accuracy and pruning power, which in turn massively accelerates the main A* search. For inferring edge constraints, they provide an efficient algorithm. For the more complex task of inferring a maximal set of consistent ordering constraints, they propose an elegant reduction to MaxSAT. The paper empirically validates this approach against an Integer Linear Programming (ILP) baseline, demonstrating orders-of-magnitude speedups and showing scalability to 20 variables, which is competitive for optimal learning.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

The primary strength of this paper is its outstanding novelty and the elegance of its core idea. The concept of "projecting" high-level, non-decomposable constraints into a simpler, decomposable form to "empower" an underlying oracle is a powerful and generalizable algorithmic paradigm. It represents a significant conceptual leap beyond merely adapting an existing search algorithm to a new constraint type. This is not an incremental paper; it introduces a new way of thinking about how to integrate complex background knowledge into score-based learning.

Further strengths include:
*   **Significance:** The paper tackles a genuinely important problem. Ancestral constraints are a natural way for experts to specify causal background knowledge, but incorporating them into optimal learning frameworks has been a major challenge. The results convincingly show that even a small number of such constraints can improve model accuracy as much as collecting significantly more data, highlighting the practical value of the proposed method.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments are thorough and the results are compelling. The orders-of-magnitude improvement over a state-of-the-art ILP solver (GOBNILP) is a very strong result that clearly demonstrates the efficacy of the proposed approach. The "easy-hard-easy" pattern observed as constraints are added is insightful and well-explained.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. It clearly explains the technical challenge, the proposed solution, and the connection to previous work. The formalization of the projection via theorems and the MaxSAT encoding is clear and precise.

The paper's weaknesses are relatively minor and do not detract from the core contribution:
*   **Scalability:** The method is demonstrated on networks up to 20 variables. While this is state-of-the-art for exact, score-based learning, it is still a practical limitation. However, this is more a reflection of the hardness of the underlying problem than a specific flaw in this approach. Given the novelty of the idea, this level of validation is more than sufficient.
*   **Analysis of Projection Cost:** The paper does not analyze the computational cost of the projection step itself, particularly the MaxSAT solving. It is possible that for very large numbers of variables or constraints, this step could become a bottleneck.
*   **CPDAG Pruning:** The method for pruning CPDAG nodes from the search tree relies on exhaustive enumeration of the equivalence class in ambiguous cases. This could be computationally prohibitive for large classes. A more detailed analysis of how frequently this step is necessary would have strengthened the paper.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Pro:** The core idea of "constraint projection" is highly novel, elegant, and impactful. It provides a new and powerful mechanism for integrating complex, non-decomposable knowledge into optimal structure learning.
*   **Pro:** The work addresses a significant, practical problem and provides a solution that is demonstrably orders of magnitude better than a strong baseline.
*   **Pro:** The paper is likely to inspire follow-up work, both in Bayesian network learning and potentially in other domains where decomposable sub-problems are used to solve non-decomposable global problems.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   **Con:** The scalability is limited, which is typical for exact methods but might limit immediate practical application on very large problems.
*   **Con:** Some computational aspects, like the cost of MaxSAT solving and the frequency of exhaustive CPDAG enumeration, are not fully analyzed.

**Recommendation**

This is an excellent paper that I strongly recommend for acceptance. Its central contribution—projecting non-decomposable constraints to empower an oracle—is a bold and highly innovative idea. This is precisely the kind of conceptual advance that pushes the field forward. The authors have coupled this novel idea with a strong technical implementation and compelling empirical evidence of its power. The weaknesses are minor and relate more to the inherent difficulty of the problem domain and avenues for future work, rather than fundamental flaws in the proposed approach. This work has high significance and originality and represents a clear advance in the state of the art for learning with background knowledge.
