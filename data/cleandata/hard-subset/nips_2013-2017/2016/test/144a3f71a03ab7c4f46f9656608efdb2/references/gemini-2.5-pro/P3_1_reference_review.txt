This paper tackles the important problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data subject to background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints. The authors correctly identify that such constraints, while natural for expressing causal priors, are non-decomposable and thus incompatible with most modern, efficient score-based learning algorithms that rely on score decomposability. While Integer Linear Programming (ILP) methods can handle these constraints, their scalability for this specific task has not been thoroughly evaluated.

The main contribution is a novel approach that adapts the A*-based search framework on the EC tree (a search space of Markov equivalence classes) proposed by Chen et al. (2016). The search is guided by an admissible heuristic provided by an "oracle" that can efficiently find optimal structures for decomposable scores. The authors integrate ancestral constraints in two main ways: (1) by pruning nodes in the EC tree search space that provably violate the constraints, and (2) in a more sophisticated step, by "projecting" the non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints. This projection empowers the oracle, allowing it to compute a much tighter heuristic, which dramatically prunes the A* search. The inference of sound ordering constraints is shown to be non-trivial, and the authors propose a clever reduction to a MaxSAT problem to find a maximal set of such constraints.

Empirically, the proposed method is compared against a leading ILP-based system (GOBNILP). The results demonstrate orders-of-magnitude speedups. The experiments also include an effective ablation study, showing that the constraint projection technique is critical to achieving this performance. The paper also highlights the practical value of this approach, showing that incorporating even a small percentage of ancestral constraints can significantly improve the structural accuracy of the learned network, rivaling the effect of a large increase in data.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Relevance:** The problem of incorporating rich prior knowledge into structure learning is highly significant and of great interest to the NIPS community. Ancestral constraints are a powerful and intuitive form of such knowledge, and developing efficient, optimal algorithms to handle them is a valuable contribution.
*   **Novelty of the Approach:** The core idea of projecting non-decomposable constraints into a set of decomposable ones to tighten the heuristic of an A* search is both elegant and highly effective. This "empowering the oracle" concept is a powerful meta-algorithm that could potentially be applied to other types of non-decomposable constraints or search problems. The formulation of the ordering constraint inference as a MaxSAT problem is particularly novel and well-motivated.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proposed pruning rules for the EC tree are correct. The theorems for inferring edge constraints appear solid and provide a complete method for this sub-problem. The experimental evaluation is rigorous and convincing. The choice of a strong ILP baseline, the use of standard benchmarks, and the ablation study on the effect of constraint projection provide compelling evidence for the method's effectiveness.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It provides the necessary background on score-based learning and the EC tree framework, clearly motivates the problem of non-decomposable constraints, and lucidly explains the proposed solution. The distinction between pruning the search space and empowering the oracle is made very clear.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Authors:**

*   **Details on Theoretical Claims:** The paper presents key theoretical results (Theorems 2, 3, and 4) but omits proofs or even proof sketches, presumably due to space constraints. While the claims are intuitive, including a brief justification or intuition, perhaps in an appendix, would strengthen the paper's technical completeness. For Theorem 4, a more explicit link showing that the solution from the MaxSAT formulation satisfies its conditions would be beneficial.
*   **Clarification on Experimental Setup:** The paper states that parent set scores were pre-computed. This implies an upper bound on the parent set size was used, as enumerating scores for all `n * 2^(n-1)` families would be infeasible for `n=20`. This maximum parent set size should be specified, as it is a critical parameter that affects the performance of both the proposed method and the ILP baseline.
*   **Analysis of Overheads:** The MaxSAT formulation is a key component of the method. While its runtime is likely negligible for the problem sizes tested, a brief analysis or reporting of the time spent in the MaxSAT solver would provide a more complete picture of the computational costs. This is particularly relevant if the method were to be scaled to larger `n`.
*   **Discussion of Scalability:** The experiments show scaling up to `n=20`, which is state-of-the-art for exact learning. However, a brief discussion of the primary scalability bottlenecks would be welcome. Is the bottleneck the size of the EC tree, the runtime of the oracle calls, or the constraint projection step? This could provide valuable insights for future research aiming to push the boundaries of exact learning even further.

**Summary of Recommendation:**

This is a strong paper that presents a significant advance in Bayesian network structure learning. It addresses a practical and important problem with a novel and technically sound solution. The central idea of empowering a decomposable-score oracle with projected non-decomposable constraints is a powerful contribution with potential for broader impact. The empirical results are impressive and clearly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over a relevant state-of-the-art baseline. The weaknesses are minor and mostly relate to providing additional detail rather than any fundamental flaws in the approach. The paper is well-written, the contributions are clear, and the results are significant. I strongly recommend acceptance.
