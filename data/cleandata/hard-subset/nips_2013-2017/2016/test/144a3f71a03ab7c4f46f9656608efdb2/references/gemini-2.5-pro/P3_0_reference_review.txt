This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data, subject to background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints (e.g., X is an ancestor of Y, or X is not an ancestor of Y). The authors correctly identify that such constraints are non-decomposable, rendering them incompatible with standard, efficient, exact structure learning algorithms that rely on decomposable scores.

The main contribution is an approach that adapts the A*-based search framework of Chen et al. [2015, 2016]—which searches over a graph of DAGs or equivalence classes (CPDAGs)—to handle these non-decomposable constraints. The key innovation is a technique the authors term "constraint projection." This involves inferring a set of decomposable constraints (required/forbidden edges and partial topological ordering constraints) from the given non-decomposable ancestral constraints. These projected decomposable constraints are then used to "empower" the oracle—an exact solver for the decomposable case, such as URLEARNING—which provides the admissible heuristic for the A* search. A more constrained oracle yields a tighter heuristic, which significantly prunes the A* search space. The method for inferring ordering constraints via a reduction to MaxSAT is particularly novel. The authors provide a strong empirical evaluation, showing that their method is orders of magnitude faster than a state-of-the-art Integer Linear Programming (ILP) approach, which can also encode such constraints.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The paper tackles a problem of high practical importance. Incorporating rich prior knowledge is often critical for learning meaningful models from limited data, and ancestral relations are a natural way to express such knowledge (e.g., from known causal pathways). The proposed solution is elegant and novel. The core idea of projecting non-decomposable constraints into a decomposable form to tighten a heuristic for a search-based solver is a powerful concept that could potentially be applied to other non-decomposable learning problems. The use of MaxSAT to find a maximal set of consistent ordering constraints is a clever and non-trivial technical contribution.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The work is technically sound. The overall framework builds on established prior work on A* search for BN learning. The proposed extensions—pruning the EC tree based on constraint satisfaction (Theorem 1) and the constraint projection methods (Theorems 2-4)—are well-motivated and appear correct. The experimental methodology is rigorous, comparing against a strong and appropriate baseline (GOBNILP) and thoroughly analyzing the impact of different parameters (n, N, p) and the effect of the constraint projection itself.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It clearly explains the limitations of existing methods, motivates the need for a new approach, and walks the reader through the proposed techniques in a logical manner. The example in Section 3 effectively illustrates why ancestral constraints are non-decomposable. The experimental results are presented clearly and convincingly support the paper's claims.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Clarification:**

1.  **Practicality of CPDAG Pruning:** In Section 4.3, the authors state that if the initial efficient checks for constraint violation on a CPDAG are inconclusive, they "exhaustively enumerate the structures of class(P)" to check for satisfiability. A CPDAG can represent an exponential number of DAGs, so this step could be a significant computational bottleneck. It would be valuable if the authors could comment on how frequently this worst-case scenario occurred in their experiments and discuss whether more sophisticated methods for checking satisfiability (e.g., SAT-based approaches for CPDAG extension) were considered or could be used.

2.  **Scalability of Constraint Projection:** While the authors state that edge constraint inference is efficient, the reduction to MaxSAT for ordering constraints may not scale well to a larger number of variables. Could the authors provide some analysis on the runtime of the MaxSAT pre-computation step and its contribution to the overall learning time? This is particularly relevant for problems with larger `n` but perhaps a sparse set of ancestral constraints.

3.  **Clarity of MaxSAT Acyclicity Constraint:** The MaxSAT formulation in Section 5.2 is a key contribution, but the "sufficient condition for acyclicity" (constraint #3) is quite complex. A brief intuitive explanation or a small example illustrating why this particular disjunction of six negative ancestral constraints is sufficient to prevent cycles among four variables involved in two ordering constraints would significantly improve the reader's understanding.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a practical and important extension to the fundamental problem of Bayesian network structure learning.
*   Presents a novel and elegant technical solution (constraint projection to empower an A* search oracle) that is both effective and potentially generalizable.
*   The MaxSAT formulation for inferring ordering constraints is a significant and clever contribution in its own right.
*   The paper is supported by a strong and convincing empirical evaluation against a state-of-the-art baseline.
*   The work is clearly presented and technically sound, representing a solid contribution to the field.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The potential computational bottlenecks in the CPDAG pruning and MaxSAT-based projection steps are not fully discussed, which may limit the practical scalability of the approach beyond what was tested.

**Conclusion:**
This is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. The problem is well-motivated, the proposed solution is novel and technically sound, and the empirical results are compelling. The weaknesses are minor and primarily relate to requests for additional clarification and discussion of potential scalability limits. This paper clearly meets the high standards of NIPS and I strongly recommend acceptance.
