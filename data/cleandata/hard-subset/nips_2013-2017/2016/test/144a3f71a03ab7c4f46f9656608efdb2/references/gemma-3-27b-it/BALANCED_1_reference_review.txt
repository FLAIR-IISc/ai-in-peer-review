This paper addresses the problem of learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints, a practically relevant task where prior knowledge about causal relationships is available. The core challenge lies in the non-decomposable nature of ancestral constraints, which clashes with the decomposable scoring functions used in many efficient Bayesian network learning algorithms. The authors build upon their previous work [Chen et al., 2015, 2016] and propose a method to incorporate these constraints into their EC tree-based search framework. They also introduce a technique for projecting these non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones, further enhancing the efficiency of the learning process. The paper is well-motivated, and the problem addressed is clearly defined and significant.

The main strength of the paper lies in its theoretical contribution towards handling non-decomposable constraints within an optimal structure learning framework.  The adaptation of the EC tree to accommodate ancestral constraints is a logical and well-explained extension of their earlier work. The technique for inferring decomposable constraints is particularly insightful, as it demonstrates a clever way to leverage prior knowledge to reduce the search space. The empirical evaluation, comparing their approach to an ILP-based method (GOBNILP), provides strong evidence of its superiority in terms of efficiency. The experiments are thorough, varying network size, data size, and constraint density.  The observation of an "easy-hard-easy" trend with increasing constraint density is interesting and provides valuable insight into the impact of constraints on the learning process. The comparison of performance with and without constraint projection convincingly shows the benefits of this technique.  The writing is clear and organized, and the paper is generally well-presented. The related work section is reasonably comprehensive, referencing relevant literature.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses.  While the experimental results demonstrate significant speedups compared to GOBNILP, the practical impact of this improvement needs further contextualization. GOBNILP is known to be computationally expensive, and it's unclear how often it would be a competitive alternative in real-world scenarios.  More comparisons with other commonly used algorithms, even if they don't guarantee optimality (e.g., constraint-based algorithms like PC or greedy approaches), would provide a more holistic view of the method's performance. Although the authors mention that their approach scales to n=20 variables, this is still a relatively small network size. Exploring the scalability to larger networks would be valuable. The discussion on the MaxSAT reduction for inferring ordering constraints feels somewhat cursory. More details on the implementation and its computational cost would be helpful.  The choice of the BDeu score is somewhat arbitrary.  Investigating the performance with other scoring functions could reveal potential sensitivities or limitations of the approach. While Theorem 1 is stated, its practical implications regarding pruning efficiency could be elaborated further. Finally, the notation is a bit dense, and some definitions could be presented earlier for better readability. For instance, the definition of α(X) and β(X) appears only in section 5.1, but a more intuitive explanation earlier on would be helpful.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The paper presents a novel approach to address a challenging problem – learning Bayesian networks with non-decomposable constraints.
*   **Significance:** The ability to incorporate ancestral constraints is important for leveraging prior knowledge and improving the efficiency and accuracy of Bayesian network learning.
*   **Technical Soundness:**  The theoretical framework is well-developed and the proposed algorithms appear to be correct.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments provide strong evidence of the effectiveness and efficiency of the proposed approach compared to a relevant baseline.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Comparison:** The comparison is primarily with GOBNILP, which may not be the most common baseline in practice.
*   **Scalability Concerns:** While the approach scales to n=20, the scalability to larger networks remains an open question.
*   **Implementation Details:** Some implementation details, particularly regarding the MaxSAT reduction, are lacking.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian network learning. The proposed approach is novel, technically sound, and empirically validated. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend **acceptance** to NIPS, with the caveat that the authors address the reviewer's comments regarding the comparison with alternative algorithms, scalability concerns, and implementation details in a revised version. Specifically, I suggest including a comparison with a commonly used constraint-based learning algorithm and providing more details on the MaxSAT implementation.
