## NIPS 2016 Review: Learning Bayesian Networks with Ancestral Constraints

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning Bayesian Networks (BNs) when prior knowledge is available in the form of ancestral constraints (specifying ancestor-descendant relationships between variables). The authors highlight the limitations of existing optimal structure learning approaches, which often rely on decomposable scoring functions and struggle to incorporate these non-decomposable constraints. They build upon their previous work using EC trees and oracles for optimal structure learning, extending it to effectively handle ancestral constraints by pruning the search space and, crucially, by projecting these constraints into equivalent decomposable constraints to empower the oracle. The paper demonstrates significant empirical improvements over ILP-based methods, showcasing the efficacy of their approach.

**Related Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the landscape of BN structure learning, referencing key works like Darwiche, Koller & Friedman, and Murphy. It effectively contrasts its approach with traditional decomposable score-based methods (K2, dynamic programming) and ILP/CP formulations that, while capable of representing the constraints, have not been thoroughly evaluated in this context. The connection to Chen et al.’s previous work on BN graphs and EC trees is well established. The authors also acknowledge related work on constraint-based learning (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos) and MaxSAT approaches, providing a comprehensive overview of relevant techniques. 

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a critical gap:** The paper tackles a practical and important problem: integrating non-decomposable prior knowledge into optimal BN structure learning.  Existing methods often force users to compromise between optimality and the ability to incorporate meaningful constraints.
* **Sound and theoretically grounded approach:**  The adaptation of EC trees and the use of an oracle provide a theoretically sound framework. The theorem establishing the soundness of pruning based on ancestral constraints is particularly strong.
* **Constraint Projection is a key innovation:** The projection of ancestral constraints into decomposable ones is a brilliant idea, significantly improving the oracle's performance and the overall efficiency of the search. This cleverly bridges the gap between non-decomposable constraints and the existing algorithmic framework.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results consistently demonstrate orders of magnitude improvement over ILP-based methods, across a variety of network structures and dataset sizes.  The analysis of scalability and the impact of constraint projection are particularly convincing.
* **Clear and well-structured presentation:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written.  The technical details are presented in a logical and accessible manner.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of the MaxSAT reduction:** While the MaxSAT reduction for inferring ordering constraints is presented, the details are somewhat limited. A more detailed description, perhaps with an illustrative example, would be beneficial. The computational cost of the MaxSAT solver could potentially become a bottleneck for very large networks.
* **Oracle Dependence:** The performance of the approach heavily relies on the effectiveness of the oracle (URLEARNING). While a good choice, the paper doesn't extensively explore the sensitivity of the results to different oracle implementations.
* **Limited comparison to constraint-based methods:**  While the paper mentions constraint-based learning, a more direct comparison to state-of-the-art constraint-based algorithms like PC or FCI, adapted to handle ancestral constraints, would strengthen the evaluation.  The focus is heavily on comparing against ILP, which appears less competitive.
* **Experiment setup details:** While the experimental setup is generally well-described, more details about the simulation process (e.g., how the sub-networks were selected, how the ancestral constraints were randomly generated) would increase reproducibility.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses an important and previously unsolved problem in Bayesian network structure learning.
* The proposed approach is theoretically sound and demonstrates significant empirical improvements over existing methods.
* The constraint projection technique is a novel and impactful contribution.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The complexity of the MaxSAT reduction could be a limitation for very large networks.
* The strong dependence on the oracle requires careful consideration.
* A more comprehensive comparison to relevant constraint-based methods would be valuable.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong and valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning.  The authors have successfully addressed a critical challenge by extending their previous work to incorporate non-decomposable prior knowledge in a principled and efficient manner. The innovative constraint projection technique and the compelling empirical results make this paper worthy of acceptance. While there are minor areas for improvement (detailed explanation of the MaxSAT reduction, more extensive comparison to constraint-based methods), these do not detract significantly from the overall quality and significance of the work.

**Recommendation:** **Accept** with minor revisions.  I suggest the authors provide more detail on the MaxSAT reduction and consider including a comparison to state-of-the-art constraint-based algorithms.
