

# NIPS Review: "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"

## Summary

This paper addresses a significant limitation in Bayesian network structure learning: the inability of existing optimal structure learning approaches to incorporate non-decomposable constraints such as ancestral constraints (specifying cause-and-effect relationships between variables). The authors build upon Chen et al.'s EC tree search space, which is designed for non-decomposable scores, and present a method to incorporate ancestral constraints by pruning the search space. Crucially, they introduce a novel technique to project these non-decomposable constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints that can be used to empower an oracle (a decomposable-score learning algorithm), significantly improving search efficiency. The paper demonstrates through extensive experiments that this approach is orders-of-magnitude faster than ILP-based methods for learning with ancestral constraints, while maintaining comparable accuracy.

The work relates to several important lines of previous research: Chen et al.'s BN graph and EC tree for non-decomposable scores, Borboudakis and Tsamardinos' use of ancestral constraints in constraint-based learning, and ILP/CP approaches that can represent non-decomposable constraints but have not been previously evaluated for this specific problem.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound and makes a significant contribution to the field. The theoretical foundation is strong, with clear proofs (Theorems 1-4) demonstrating the correctness of the pruning method and constraint projection. The authors carefully consider edge cases (e.g., transitivity of constraints, potential cycles) and provide well-motivated solutions.

The empirical evaluation is thorough and well-designed. The authors use standard Bayesian network benchmarks (ALARM, ANDES, CHILD, CPCS54, HEPAR2), vary key parameters (number of variables, dataset size, constraint percentage), and provide results averaged over 50 different datasets. The comparison with the ILP-based GOBNILP system is particularly valuable, as it's the first empirical evaluation of ILP approaches for this specific problem. The results consistently show orders-of-magnitude speedups for the proposed approach, with the performance gap increasing as the problem size grows.

One minor weakness is that the paper doesn't fully explore the computational complexity of the constraint projection step, particularly the MaxSAT formulation for ordering constraints. While the authors mention using EVASOLVER, they don't discuss how the runtime of this step scales with the number of variables or constraints.

## Clarity

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The authors provide excellent figures (Figures 1a and 1b) to illustrate the BN graph and EC tree, which are crucial for understanding the search space. The technical explanations are accessible to readers familiar with Bayesian networks, though the MaxSAT formulation for ordering constraints might be challenging for those less familiar with constraint programming.

The paper adequately informs the reader about the necessary background (Section 2) and provides clear definitions of key concepts (e.g., ancestral constraints, Markov equivalence). The transition from the problem statement to the proposed solution is logical and well-motivated.

## Originality

This paper makes several important original contributions:

1. It presents the first systematic approach to incorporating ancestral constraints (non-decomposable constraints) into optimal Bayesian network structure learning with non-decomposable scores.

2. The method of projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints to empower the oracle is novel and well-motivated.

3. The adaptation of the EC tree search space to handle ancestral constraints (Theorem 1) is a significant contribution that fills a gap in the literature.

4. The empirical evaluation demonstrates the first practical comparison between ILP-based approaches and non-decomposable score-based approaches for learning with ancestral constraints.

While the EC tree was introduced in Chen et al. [2016], the adaptation to handle ancestral constraints is a substantial extension that differentiates this work from previous publications.

## Significance

The significance of this work is high for both theoretical and practical reasons:

1. The paper addresses a practically important problem: incorporating domain knowledge as ancestral constraints, which is common in real-world applications but largely ignored in structure learning literature.

2. The empirical results showing that a small number of ancestral constraints (10-25%) can have a similar impact on model accuracy as increasing dataset size from 512 to 8192 observations is particularly valuable for practitioners facing data collection challenges.

3. The orders-of-magnitude speedup over ILP-based approaches makes the proposed method highly practical for real-world applications with larger networks.

4. The approach scales to 20 variables, which is competitive with state-of-the-art methods for BDeu scores (e.g., Yuan and Malone [2013] reported results up to 26 variables).

5. The paper makes a compelling case that background knowledge can be more valuable than additional data for improving model accuracy.

## Strengths

- Solves a practically important problem that has been overlooked in the literature
- Theoretical foundation is sound with clear proofs
- Empirical evaluation is thorough and well-designed
- Demonstrates significant speedups (orders of magnitude) over ILP-based approaches
- Shows that a small number of constraints can have a large impact on accuracy
- Provides a clear method for projecting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones
- The EC tree adaptation for ancestral constraints is elegant and well-executed

## Weaknesses

- Doesn't fully explore the computational complexity of the constraint projection step (especially MaxSAT)
- Doesn't compare with the most recent constraint programming approaches for structure learning (mentioned in the introduction but not evaluated)
- Doesn't discuss how to handle inconsistent ancestral constraints (e.g., contradictory constraints)
- The paper mentions that the ILP approach with projected constraints shows some overhead for small n (n=12), but doesn't explain why this occurs or how it might be mitigated
- The experimental section could provide more insight into the trade-off between constraint percentage and accuracy

## Recommendations for Improvement

1. Include a brief discussion of the computational complexity of the constraint projection step, particularly for large numbers of variables or constraints.

2. Add a comparison with recent constraint programming approaches for structure learning (e.g., van Beek and Hoffmann [2015]) to provide a more complete picture of the landscape.

3. Discuss how the method handles inconsistent ancestral constraints (e.g., if constraints contradict each other or the data).

4. Provide more detailed analysis of the trade-off between the number of ancestral constraints and model accuracy.

## Final Recommendation

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning by addressing a practical limitation in existing methods. The theoretical foundation is sound, the approach is novel, and the empirical results are compelling. The paper clearly demonstrates that ancestral constraints can be incorporated efficiently into optimal structure learning, with significant practical benefits.

While there are some minor weaknesses in the presentation (particularly regarding the computational complexity of constraint projection), these do not detract from the overall quality and significance of the contribution. The paper would be a strong addition to NIPS, advancing both the theory and practice of Bayesian network structure learning.

**Recommendation: Accept**

## Arguments for Acceptance
- Solves a significant practical problem in Bayesian network structure learning
- Theoretical contribution is sound with clear proofs
- Empirical results demonstrate orders-of-magnitude speedup over the best alternative
- Shows that background knowledge can be more valuable than additional data
- Well-written and well-organized with clear figures and explanations
- Contributes to a growing literature on incorporating domain knowledge into structure learning

## Arguments Against Acceptance
- Minor limitations in the discussion of constraint projection complexity
- Doesn't compare with all relevant constraint-based approaches
- Doesn't fully address inconsistent constraints

The arguments for acceptance significantly outweigh the arguments against, making this a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.
