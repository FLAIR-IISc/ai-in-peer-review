

# Review of "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"

## Summary
This paper addresses the challenge of learning Bayesian network structures under ancestral constraints, which specify cause-and-effect relationships between variables (e.g., "X is an ancestor of Y"). The authors note that most mainstream approaches for optimal Bayesian network structure learning assume decomposable scoring functions, which cannot directly incorporate ancestral constraints (a non-decomposable constraint type). The paper builds on Chen et al. (2015, 2016), who introduced the EC tree search space for learning with non-decomposable scores, and extends it to handle ancestral constraints. The authors propose two key contributions: (1) a method for pruning the EC tree using ancestral constraints, and (2) a technique for projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints to empower a standard oracle for decomposable scores. The paper evaluates these techniques against ILP-based approaches (GOBNILP), demonstrating orders-of-magnitude efficiency improvements.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound with clear theoretical foundations. The authors provide formal proofs (Theorems 1-4) establishing the correctness of their constraint projection methods. The experimental design is rigorous, using multiple standard Bayesian network benchmarks (ALARM, ANDES, CHILD, CPCS54, HEPAR2) with controlled variations in network size, dataset size, and constraint density. The evaluation includes both with and without projected constraints, demonstrating the significant impact of constraint projection on efficiency. The paper acknowledges limitations, such as the need for exhaustive enumeration of Markov equivalence classes when constraints are inconclusive, and carefully discusses the scalability of their approach (up to 20 variables). The authors properly compare against relevant prior work, including Chen et al. (2015, 2016), ILP-based approaches (Bartlett and Cussens, 2015), and constraint-based learning (Borboudakis and Tsamardinos, 2012).

## Clarity
The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem formulation to theoretical contributions and empirical evaluation. Key concepts (ancestral constraints, EC trees, constraint projection) are well-defined with appropriate examples. The figures (Figure 1) effectively illustrate the BN graph and EC tree structures. The paper provides sufficient detail for reproducibility, including experimental parameters (e.g., BDeu scores with equivalent sample size of 1, 50 datasets per condition). The writing is precise and avoids unnecessary jargon, making the technical content accessible to the NIPS audience.

## Novelty
The paper makes a clear technical contribution by extending Chen et al.'s EC tree approach to handle ancestral constraints. While ILP and CP approaches can theoretically represent ancestral constraints (as noted in the paper), this is the first empirical evaluation showing their inefficiency compared to the EC tree approach. The projection method that transforms non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints is novel and significantly enhances the efficiency of the oracle. The paper clearly differentiates its approach from prior work (e.g., the K2 algorithm, order graph approaches, and ILP-based methods) by highlighting how ancestral constraints are non-decomposable and cannot be handled by standard approaches.

## Significance
The results demonstrate that ancestral constraints can dramatically improve learning efficiency (orders of magnitude faster than ILP-based methods) while maintaining or improving model quality. The paper shows that a relatively small number of ancestral constraints (10-25%) can have a similar impact on model accuracy as significantly more training data (e.g., increasing dataset size from 512 to 8192 samples). This is a significant practical contribution, as collecting additional training data is often expensive or difficult. The approach scales to 20 variables, which is competitive with state-of-the-art methods for Bayesian network structure learning (e.g., Yuan and Malone, 2013). The work directly addresses an important practical limitation in Bayesian network learning (incorporating background knowledge) and provides a practically efficient solution.

## Strengths
- Clear theoretical contribution with formal guarantees (Theorems 1-4)
- Strong empirical results showing orders-of-magnitude efficiency improvements
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation with multiple benchmarks and parameter variations
- Practical demonstration that background knowledge (ancestral constraints) can significantly reduce the need for large datasets
- Clear connection to and extension of prior work (Chen et al., 2015, 2016)

## Weaknesses
- The paper does not discuss how ancestral constraints might be obtained in practice (this may be out of scope, but it's a practical limitation)
- The method for handling CPDAGs requiring exhaustive enumeration (when constraints are inconclusive) is not thoroughly analyzed for computational complexity as network size increases
- The comparison against constraint-based learning approaches (e.g., PC algorithm with ancestral constraints) is limited, though the authors note this is a different paradigm

## Recommendation
This paper makes a significant contribution to Bayesian network structure learning by addressing a practical limitation (handling non-decomposable ancestral constraints) with a technically sound and empirically validated approach. The work is well-positioned to advance the state of the art in structure learning, with clear theoretical foundations, rigorous evaluation, and practical significance. The paper meets all NIPS criteria for quality, clarity, novelty, and significance.

Pro: Novel solution to a practical problem in Bayesian network learning, technically sound, strong empirical results, significant efficiency gains, well-written and organized.

Con: Limited discussion of constraint acquisition in practice, no analysis of worst-case complexity for CPDAG enumeration, limited comparison to constraint-based learning approaches.

Overall, this paper is a strong contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning and would be of interest to the NIPS community. It advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way with clear practical implications.
