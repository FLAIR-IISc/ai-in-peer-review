

# Review of "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"

This paper addresses an important but underexplored problem in Bayesian network structure learning: incorporating non-decomposable ancestral constraints into optimal structure learning. The authors make a compelling case that while ancestral constraints (e.g., "X is an ancestor of Y") are common in practice and would improve learning efficiency by reducing the search space, they pose a fundamental challenge because they are non-decomposable. The paper's key contribution is showing how to integrate these constraints into the EC tree search space framework (Chen et al., 2016) by developing techniques to project non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints that can be used by existing oracles.

The paper's main novelty lies in two key insights: First, they demonstrate how to soundly prune the EC tree based on ancestral constraints, which is non-trivial because ancestral constraints cannot be checked locally. Second, they develop a novel method for projecting ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints (both edge and ordering constraints), which significantly empowers the oracle used in their search process. The authors provide formal theorems (Theorems 2-4) to justify their projection techniques, which is a strong theoretical contribution.

The empirical evaluation is particularly impressive. The authors show orders-of-magnitude speedups over ILP-based approaches (GOBNILP) across multiple benchmarks and varying problem sizes. For instance, in Table 1, their approach is consistently faster than GOBNILP by multiple orders of magnitude. More importantly, they demonstrate that a relatively small number of ancestral constraints (10-25%) can have a similar impact on model quality as significantly increasing the amount of training data (e.g., from 512 to 2048 samples). This practical insight about the value of background knowledge is highly significant for practitioners.

The paper builds upon Chen et al.'s EC tree framework but extends it to handle a new class of constraints (ancestral constraints) that have been largely ignored in the literature. Previous work on ILP and CP approaches for handling non-decomposable constraints (e.g., [Jaakkola et al., 2010, Bartlett and Cussens, 2015]) has not been evaluated for learning with ancestral constraints, which the authors correctly identify as a gap in the literature.

I particularly appreciate the paper's focus on practical impact. The authors don't just present a theoretical contribution but demonstrate its real-world significance through extensive experiments showing both efficiency gains and the value of ancestral constraints in improving model quality. Their finding that background knowledge (in the form of ancestral constraints) can be as valuable as increasing dataset size is a powerful message for the community.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides sufficient detail for reproduction. The technical content is sound, with the theoretical results well-supported by empirical evidence. The only minor weakness is that the paper doesn't explore how their approach scales to very large networks (beyond 20 variables), but this is understandable given the computational complexity of exact structure learning.

In terms of significance, this paper makes a substantial contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. It addresses a recognized limitation in the literature (handling non-decomposable constraints) and provides a practical, efficient solution that significantly outperforms existing approaches. The constraint projection technique they develop could potentially be applied to other non-decomposable constraints beyond ancestral constraints.

The authors are careful to acknowledge related work and clearly differentiate their contribution from previous approaches. They correctly note that while ILP and CP approaches could in principle handle ancestral constraints, they have not been evaluated for this purpose, which is exactly what this paper provides.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold approaches, I find this paper to be a strong candidate for acceptance. The core idea—projecting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable forms to leverage existing oracles—is novel, elegant, and has significant practical implications. The empirical results robustly support the claimed advantages, and the paper advances the state of the art in Bayesian network structure learning.

The paper's contribution is not incremental but rather represents a significant new direction for handling constraints in structure learning. It successfully bridges a gap between theoretical constraints (ancestral constraints) and practical learning algorithms, which has been a longstanding challenge in the field.

In summary, this paper presents a novel approach to an important problem with clear theoretical foundations and impressive empirical results. It makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning and is well-suited for publication at NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
