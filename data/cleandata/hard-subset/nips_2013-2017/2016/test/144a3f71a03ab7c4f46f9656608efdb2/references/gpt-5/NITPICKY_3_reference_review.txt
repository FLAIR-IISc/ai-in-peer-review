Summary
The paper tackles optimal Bayesian network structure learning under non-decomposable ancestral constraints. It builds on the BN-graph/EC-tree search spaces (Chen et al. 2015, 2016) to enable pruning of states (CPDAGs) that cannot satisfy given path constraints. To regain efficiency, the authors “project” non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable ones: (i) edge presence/absence constraints inferred via sufficient conditions (Theorems 2–3), and (ii) topological ordering constraints inferred via a MaxSAT formulation (and a sufficient condition, Theorem 4). These projected constraints are fed to a decomposable-score oracle (URLearning) to tighten A* heuristics during EC-tree search. Experiments compare the method to ILP (GOBNILP), reporting orders-of-magnitude speedups and showing an easy–hard–easy pattern as the proportion of ancestral constraints increases.

Strengths
- Addresses an important practical setting: incorporating background causal knowledge in the form of ancestral (path) constraints into exact BN learning.
- Uses EC trees to handle non-decomposable constraints elegantly via pruning; the overall shortest-path perspective is well-motivated.
- The idea of projecting path constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints and using them to empower a decomposable-score oracle is clever and potentially impactful.
- Provides conditions for entailed negative and positive edge constraints (Theorems 2–3) and a MaxSAT approach for order constraints (with sufficient conditions in Theorem 4).
- Empirical results (as described) suggest significant speedups over ILP, and the easy–hard–easy trend is insightful.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Theorem 1 as stated appears problematic/unclear: “In an EC tree, a CPDAG P satisfies A, both over the same set of variables X, iff its descendants satisfy A.” Nodes in the EC tree correspond to CPDAGs over different subsets of variables; stating “both over the same set of variables” is inconsistent. Moreover, “iff” is doubtful: even if some DAG in class(P) satisfies A on the current subset, not all descendants need admit a DAG satisfying A after adding new variables/edges. The intended pruning criterion seems to be: if P is provably incompatible with A, all descendants can be pruned (sound), but the converse is not true. This needs correction and a rigorous proof.
- Exponential enumeration in Section 4.3: when simple tests are inconclusive, the method “exhaustively enumerates class(P).” This can be exponential and undermines scalability; no mitigation, bounds, or heuristics are provided.
- Projection details are incomplete. The “maximum set of ancestral constraints” closure procedure is referred to as efficient but is “omitted for space.” Without an explicit algorithm and complexity, Theorems 2–3 cannot be applied or reproduced.
- MaxSAT formulation and “maximally large set” claim: the hard/soft constraints listed do not convincingly guarantee maximality or even correctness of the induced ordering constraints with respect to entailment by A. The sufficient condition in Theorem 4 is weaker than the stated goal (“find a maximally large set”), and the relation between the MaxSAT encoding and Theorem 4 is unclear. Proofs and completeness/optimality guarantees are missing.
- Completeness claim: The conclusion claims “a sound and complete method for pruning the EC tree, based on ancestral constraints,” yet Section 4.3 relies on exponential enumeration and does not provide completeness guarantees for the test suite beyond trivial sufficiency. This is a strong claim that needs proof or retraction.
- Experimental reporting is insufficient for assessment:
  - Tables/figures with actual numbers are not included; only qualitative statements (“orders of magnitude”) are given. No time, node expansions, or memory statistics are shown.
  - Benchmarks are small (n ≤ 20), and results are compared only to ILP; CP approaches are mentioned as capable of handling ancestral constraints but not evaluated.
  - Only BDeu with ESS=1 is used; no analysis of sensitivity to scoring choices or parent-set size caps.
  - All constraints are correct and sampled from ground truth; no robustness evaluation under noisy/mislabeled constraints—critical in practice.
  - Overhead of MaxSAT, and its effect on total runtime, is not reported.
- Clarity/notation issues:
  - Several imprecisions (e.g., “largest variable in Gj according to some canonical ordering” without specification; mixing BN graph/EC tree terminology; switching between DAG/CPDAG definitions).
  - Equation (1) lacks fully specified indices; the illustrative family-score “table” is malformed.
  - In Section 5.1, the complexity of checking Theorem 3 (enumerating Z, and alpha/beta set computations) is not analyzed; the worst-case cost matters when used repeatedly.
- Citations and formatting are severely inconsistent:
  - Many references are malformed/incomplete, with placeholders (“?\Q2015\E”), missing venues/pages, and inconsistent styles; URLs appear inline in footnotes.
  - Spurious text fragments (e.g., “th Conference…”, “年6月12⽇日星期五 11 16”).
  - Footnotes and numbering are misaligned; some footnote markers appear mid-paragraph.
  - Typos and grammar issues throughout (e.g., “DAG graphs”, “We also make note of…”).
These presentation problems hinder readability and reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
- Precisely state and prove the correct pruning theorem for EC-tree nodes under ancestral constraints, accounting for varying variable subsets. If only a one-way implication holds, adjust the pruning and claims.
- Provide the algorithm (and complexity) for computing the closure to a “maximum set” of ancestral constraints. What rules are used beyond simple transitivity?
- Prove Theorems 2–4, or at least supply formal sketches sufficient for verification. For Theorem 3, clarify necessity vs sufficiency.
- Clarify the MaxSAT encoding, its objective, and whether the solution is guaranteed maximal and safe (does not eliminate any DAG in G(A)). Report the runtime and size of the MaxSAT instances.
- Replace exhaustive CPDAG enumeration with a polynomial-time test if possible, or bound the enumeration cost in practice.
- Evaluate against a CP baseline able to encode path constraints, and include larger n and/or parent limits. Assess robustness to noisy constraints.

Minor issues (non-exhaustive)
- Clean all references; include full bibliographic details. Remove placeholder tokens and stray characters.
- Fix equation typesetting, table formatting, and figure references (Figures/Tables are currently missing).
- Define “canonical ordering” precisely.
- Clarify whether parent-set sizes are unconstrained and how local score caches were constructed.

Pros
- Relevant problem; practical appeal.
- Conceptually elegant integration of non-decomposable constraints into EC-tree search.
- Neat idea of projecting path constraints to decomposable constraints to strengthen oracles.

Cons
- Multiple technical gaps and possibly incorrect theorem statement.
- Heavy reliance on omitted details and exponential enumeration.
- Inadequate experimental reporting and scope.
- Significant clarity/formatting/citation issues.

Recommendation
Reject. While the core idea is promising, the paper in its current form has substantial technical and presentation deficiencies that prevent reliable assessment and reproduction. I encourage a thorough revision with corrected/principled pruning results, full proofs/algorithms, stronger and transparent experiments (including CP baselines and robustness), and a complete cleanup of the manuscript.

Reviewer confidence: High on methodological and presentation concerns; moderate on EC-tree specific technicalities pending full proofs.
