## NIPS 2016 Paper Review: Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of learning Bayesian networks (BNs) while incorporating prior knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints (specifying ancestor-descendant relationships between variables). Existing optimal structure learning methods struggle with non-decomposable constraints like these. The authors build upon a recent framework for optimal BN learning with non-decomposable priors [Chen et al., 2015, 2016], adapting it to handle ancestral constraints.  They achieve this by pruning the search space (EC trees) based on constraint satisfaction and, crucially, by *projecting* these non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones that can empower a decomposable-score learning oracle.  The paper demonstrates significant performance improvements over Integer Linear Programming (ILP) approaches, suggesting a scalable and effective solution.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The work directly builds upon the EC tree and BN graph frameworks introduced by Chen et al. [2015, 2016], addressing a key limitation of those methods – the difficulty of integrating non-decomposable priors.  It acknowledges existing ILP and Constraint Programming (CP) approaches ([Jaakkola et al., 2010, Bartlett and Cussens, 2015, van Beek and Hoffmann, 2015]) but positions itself as a more efficient alternative.  The paper also references related work on constraint-based learning with ancestral constraints ([Borboudakis and Tsamardinos, 2012, 2013]), highlighting a difference in approach (score-based vs. constraint-based).  The reliance on a decomposable-score oracle is a clever reuse of existing efficient algorithms like those based on the order graph [Koivisto and Sood, 2004, Singh and Moore, 2005, Silander and Myllymäki, 2006].

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an important practical problem:**  Integrating prior knowledge into BN learning is crucial for real-world applications. Ancestral constraints represent a natural and expressive form of such knowledge.
* **Novel approach:** The combination of pruning the EC tree with constraint projection to empower the oracle is a clever and original contribution.  The ability to leverage existing efficient algorithms for decomposable scores is a significant advantage.
* **Strong empirical results:** The experiments demonstrate substantial performance gains (orders of magnitude) compared to ILP-based approaches, validating the effectiveness of the proposed method.  The scalability experiments are encouraging, showing ability to handle networks with 20 variables.
* **Soundness and Completeness:** The theorem regarding pruning of the EC tree guarantees correct results, and the MaxSAT reduction provides a principled way to derive decomposable constraints.
* **Clear writing:**  The paper is generally well-written and organized. The explanations of the BN graph, EC tree, and constraint projection are reasonably clear.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of MaxSAT:** While the MaxSAT reduction is theoretically sound, the paper doesn't detail the computational cost of this step.  The use of EVASOLVER suggests it’s potentially a bottleneck, especially for larger networks. More detailed analysis of its scaling behavior would be valuable.
* **Oracle Dependence:** The performance is heavily reliant on the effectiveness of the decomposable-score learning oracle.  The choice of URLEARNING is justified, but the results could vary with a different oracle.  This dependence isn’t explicitly discussed as a limitation.
* **Limited Comparative Analysis:** While the ILP comparison is strong, comparing to other state-of-the-art *score-based* methods (even those without ancestral constraint support) might provide a more complete picture of the method’s advantages.  It would be useful to see how much speed-up is achieved *even without* the ancestral constraints, just from the EC tree structure.
* **Theoretical Depth:** The paper focuses more on the algorithmic aspects than on a deeper theoretical understanding of the interaction between ancestral constraints and the search space. Exploring the theoretical limits of constraint projection would be interesting future work.
* **Experimental Setup:** The simulated data, while reasonable, may not fully capture the complexities of real-world datasets. Testing on more diverse and challenging real-world problems would strengthen the conclusions.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant challenge in Bayesian network learning.
* The proposed approach is novel, technically sound, and empirically validated.
* The performance improvements over existing methods are substantial.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presents its contributions.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The MaxSAT reduction could be a computational bottleneck.
* Reliance on a specific oracle introduces a potential source of variability.
* Limited comparison to other score-based learning methods.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian network learning. The authors successfully adapt a recent framework to handle non-decomposable ancestral constraints, achieving impressive performance gains. While some aspects, like the MaxSAT reduction and oracle dependence, could benefit from further investigation, the overall quality of the work is high. I believe this paper is **worthy of acceptance** to NIPS.



**Rating:** 7/10 (Strong Accept)
