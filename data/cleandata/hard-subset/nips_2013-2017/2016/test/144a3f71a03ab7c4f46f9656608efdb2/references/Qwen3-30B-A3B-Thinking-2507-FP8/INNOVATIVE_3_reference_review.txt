

# Review of "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"

This paper addresses an important and underexplored problem in Bayesian network structure learning: incorporating ancestral constraints, which are non-decomposable and thus challenging to integrate into existing approaches. The authors propose a novel framework that leverages the EC tree search space (introduced by Chen et al., 2016) and introduces a method to project non-decomposable ancestral constraints onto decomposable edge and ordering constraints. This projection enables the use of existing oracles that optimize decomposable scores, leading to significant efficiency gains.

The paper's core novelty lies in the theoretical framework for constraint projection (Theorems 2-4) and the practical implementation of this approach within the EC tree search space. Previous work on structure learning (e.g., K2 algorithm, order graph approaches, ILP/CP methods) has largely assumed decomposable scores and constraints, making them incapable of directly handling ancestral constraints. The authors demonstrate how their approach overcomes this limitation, with empirical results showing orders-of-magnitude improvements over ILP-based approaches (GOBNILP) when ancestral constraints are present.

The paper makes a strong case for the significance of the problem. Ancestral constraints represent important background knowledge in real-world applications, allowing users to encode cause-and-effect relationships directly. The empirical results demonstrate that a small number of ancestral constraints (10-25%) can have a similar impact on model accuracy as substantially increasing training data size (from 512 to 2048 or 8192 samples), highlighting the practical value of incorporating such constraints.

The technical contribution is well-justified and theoretically sound. The authors provide clear definitions of ancestral constraints, establish their non-decomposable nature, and develop a systematic method for projecting them onto decomposable constraints. The EC tree approach is well-motivated and extends the existing BN graph framework in a meaningful way. The empirical evaluation is thorough, covering multiple benchmark networks, varying problem sizes, and different constraint percentages.

The paper excels in originality. While Chen et al. (2015, 2016) introduced the EC tree for learning with non-decomposable scores, this paper extends it specifically to handle ancestral constraints—a significant gap in the literature. The constraint projection technique is novel and provides a practical solution to a challenging theoretical problem. The authors clearly position their work relative to previous approaches, highlighting where their method differs from and improves upon existing techniques.

The writing is clear and well-organized, with a logical progression from problem definition through theoretical development to empirical validation. The theoretical foundations are carefully developed, and the empirical results robustly support the claims made.

As a reviewer prioritizing novelty and bold approaches, I find this paper highly compelling. The core idea of projecting non-decomposable constraints to decomposable ones is elegant and has significant potential impact. While the paper builds on Chen et al.'s work, it makes a substantial and distinct contribution by addressing the specific challenge of ancestral constraints.

The main limitation I see is that the empirical evaluation uses simulated data rather than real-world applications, though this is common in structure learning research. The paper also could have more thoroughly discussed the limitations of the constraint projection approach (e.g., cases where no decomposable constraints can be inferred).

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to Bayesian network structure learning by addressing a practically important problem with a novel theoretical approach and strong empirical validation. The method is theoretically sound, practically valuable, and represents a meaningful advance beyond existing approaches. Given my emphasis on novelty and bold ideas, I believe this paper would be a strong addition to NIPS.

**Strengths:**
- Novel approach to handling non-decomposable ancestral constraints
- Clear theoretical foundation with sound theorems
- Practical implementation that delivers orders-of-magnitude efficiency gains
- Strong empirical validation across multiple benchmarks
- Significant practical implications for incorporating background knowledge

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited discussion of constraint projection limitations
- Simulated data rather than real-world applications
- Could better address related work on constraint-based learning with path constraints (though the paper notes this as a separate approach)

**Recommendation:** Accept. This paper presents a novel and significant contribution to Bayesian network structure learning with clear theoretical and empirical support. The approach is both conceptually elegant and practically valuable, addressing a gap in the literature that has been largely overlooked by existing methods. The results demonstrate substantial improvements over competing approaches, making this a strong candidate for NIPS.
