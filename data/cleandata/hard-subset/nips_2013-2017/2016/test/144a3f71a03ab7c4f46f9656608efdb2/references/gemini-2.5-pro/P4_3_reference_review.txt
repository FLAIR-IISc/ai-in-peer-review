### Summary

This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data when given background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints (e.g., variable X must be an ancestor of Y, or X cannot be an ancestor of Y). The key challenge is that such constraints are non-decomposable, which prevents their direct use in many state-of-the-art optimal structure learning algorithms that rely on decomposable score functions (e.g., dynamic programming or A* search over orderings).

The authors build upon the recent work of Chen et al. [2015, 2016], which introduced a search framework over a graph of equivalence classes (the EC tree) capable of handling non-decomposable scores. This A*-based search uses an "oracle"—an optimal learner for decomposable scores—to compute an admissible heuristic. The core contributions of this paper are twofold. First, it proposes a sound method to prune the EC tree search space by checking the consistency of partial structures (CPDAGs) with the given ancestral constraints. Second, and more significantly, it introduces a novel technique to "project" the non-decomposable ancestral constraints into a set of decomposable constraints (edge presence/absence and topological ordering constraints). These inferred decomposable constraints are then passed to the oracle, significantly improving the heuristic's quality and dramatically speeding up the search. The inference of edge constraints is handled algorithmically, while the more complex inference of a consistent set of ordering constraints is formulated as a MaxSAT problem. The paper provides an empirical evaluation comparing the proposed method to an Integer Linear Programming (ILP) formulation, demonstrating orders-of-magnitude improvements in runtime.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The paper tackles a highly relevant problem. Incorporating complex background knowledge is crucial for learning meaningful models from data, and ancestral relations are a natural and powerful way to express causal priors. The proposed solution is elegant and novel. While prior work like ILP can model such constraints, the idea of projecting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones to empower a search heuristic is a clever and powerful contribution. This "empower the oracle" approach appears to be a generalizable principle for hybrid search algorithms.

2.  **Technical Soundness:** The methodology is well-founded. The pruning strategy for the EC tree is justified with Theorem 1. The procedures for inferring decomposable constraints from ancestral constraints are formally presented and supported by Theorems 2, 3, and 4. The reduction of the ordering constraint inference problem to MaxSAT is a sound approach to finding a maximal consistent set of such constraints. The overall algorithm maintains the guarantee of optimality.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is clearly written and well-structured. It begins with a concise motivation and a good review of the necessary technical background. The proposed method is introduced logically, starting from the base EC tree search and then detailing the constraint handling and projection techniques. The experimental setup and results are presented clearly, and the main takeaways are well-summarized.

4.  **Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are thorough and convincing. The comparison against GOBNILP, a state-of-the-art ILP-based exact learner, is a strong choice of baseline. The results in Tables 1 and 2 compellingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed approach, often by several orders of magnitude. The experiments also effectively isolate the impact of the proposed constraint projection technique, showing its crucial role in achieving this performance. The analysis in Table 3, showing the "easy-hard-easy" pattern and the impact of constraints on model accuracy, provides valuable insights.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Details on Constraint Inference:** Some technical details are glossed over, potentially impacting reproducibility. For instance, the paper mentions using "a few rules" to efficiently compute the transitive closure of ancestral constraints but omits these rules. More critically, the MaxSAT formulation for ordering constraints in Section 5.2 could be better explained. Specifically, clause 3, described as "a sufficient condition for acyclicity," is complex and its derivation or intuition is not provided. A more detailed explanation or a reference would be beneficial.

2.  **Cost of Pre-processing:** The projection of constraints, especially solving the MaxSAT instance to infer ordering constraints, is a pre-processing step. The computational cost of this step is not discussed or reported in the experiments. While likely small compared to the main structure search, understanding how this cost scales with the number of variables and constraints would be important for assessing the practical limits of the approach.

3.  **Scope of Comparison:** The experimental comparison is limited to ILP. While this is a very relevant baseline for exact learning with non-decomposable constraints, a brief discussion or comparison with other paradigms would strengthen the paper. For example, Constraint Programming (CP) approaches [van Beek and Hoffmann, 2015] can also handle such constraints. Furthermore, while the focus is on optimal learning, a comparison with prominent heuristic search algorithms (e.g., greedy hill-climbing) adapted to respect ancestral constraints could provide context on the practical trade-off between optimality and runtime for larger problems.

### Arguments for Acceptance

*   The paper addresses an important and practical problem in Bayesian network learning.
*   The core technical contribution—projecting non-decomposable constraints to empower a search oracle for a decomposable subproblem—is novel, elegant, and effective.
*   The claims are well-supported by both theoretical justification and strong empirical evidence showing significant performance gains over a state-of-the-art baseline.
*   The work advances the state of the art in exact score-based structure learning.

### Arguments against Acceptance

*   The omission of some technical details (e.g., regarding the MaxSAT formulation and transitive closure computation) may hinder full understanding and reproducibility.
*   The computational overhead of the constraint projection pre-processing step is not analyzed.
*   The experimental comparison, while strong, could have been broadened to include other relevant paradigms like Constraint Programming.

Overall, the strengths of this paper significantly outweigh its weaknesses. It presents a novel, sound, and highly effective solution to an important problem, with compelling empirical results. The weaknesses are minor and could be addressed in a final version.
