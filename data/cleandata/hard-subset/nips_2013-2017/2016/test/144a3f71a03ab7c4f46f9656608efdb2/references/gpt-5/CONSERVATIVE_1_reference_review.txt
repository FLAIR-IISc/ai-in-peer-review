Summary
The paper tackles exact Bayesian network (BN) structure learning with non-decomposable ancestral constraints (path constraints of the form X is/is not an ancestor of Y). Building on prior work that searches directly in DAG/CPDAG spaces with an oracle for decomposable scores (BN graph, EC tree), the authors (i) prune CPDAG nodes that cannot satisfy a given set of ancestral constraints, (ii) “project” non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and topological ordering constraints to empower the decomposable-score oracle, via rule-based inference for edges and a MaxSAT formulation for orderings, and (iii) empirically compare against an ILP formulation (GOBNILP). They report large speedups and improved structure accuracy when ancestral constraints are provided.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on Chen et al. (2015, 2016) BN/EC search spaces and on standard decomposable-score oracles (e.g., URLearning). It relates to ILP/CP formulations (Jaakkola et al., 2010; Bartlett & Cussens, 2015; van Beek & Hoffmann, 2015) and to path-constraint priors (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos, 2012/2013). The projection of ancestral constraints to edge/order constraints appears less explored in exact score-based learning and is the most novel component.

Strengths
- Clear high-level goal: support non-decomposable ancestral constraints in exact structure learning.
- Sensible use of EC tree search with pruning; conceptually straightforward and compatible with existing oracles.
- Projection of ancestral constraints to decomposable constraints is intuitively appealing and, if correct, practically useful.
- The empirical comparison suggests substantial runtime gains over a strong ILP baseline under the tested settings.
- The paper emphasizes soundness of pruning and tries to formalize entailment for projected constraints.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Theoretical completeness/rigor: Several claims are under-specified. The key pruning step relies on testing whether a CPDAG can realize a path constraint; the “easy tests, else enumerate class(P)” is exponential and the paper does not show when enumeration is avoided or how often it occurs. Theorem 1 is stated ambiguously (as written, “P satisfies A iff its descendants satisfy A” is unusual; likely means: if P violates A then all descendants violate A). Proofs are omitted.
- Projection correctness: The rule system for closing ancestral constraints and the proofs for Theorems 2–4 are not provided. The MaxSAT encoding includes a “sufficient condition for acyclicity” but not necessity; the gap between what the MaxSAT solution enforces and what is truly entailed by A is not quantified. It is unclear whether the chosen clauses sometimes miss entailed order constraints or, worse, admit spurious ones that could eliminate valid DAGs.
- Experimental validation is limited:
  - Only exact learning with BDeu and precomputed family scores, up to n=20. No larger networks, no continuous variables, no missing data, no priors other than BDeu.
  - Constraints are sampled from the ground-truth ancestral relations (noise-free). Real background knowledge is imperfect; no robustness analysis under incorrect/noisy constraints is provided.
  - The reported speedups may be confounded: (i) GOBNILP is handicapped by unlimited parent-set sizes; (ii) the oracle (URLearning) benefits strongly from projected constraints; (iii) overheads for MaxSAT and CPDAG enumeration are not reported. An ablation (oracle without projection vs with edge-only vs ordering-only) would clarify contributions.
  - Comparisons omit CP-based solvers, dynamic programming/A* baselines adapted with order constraints, or other exact methods with strong cutting planes.
- Clarity and presentation issues:
  - Several typographical errors, incomplete references (years missing), unexplained footnotes, stray date string, and missing tables/figures in the submission. Notation is sometimes inconsistent.
  - Important algorithmic details are “omitted for space,” hindering reproducibility (closure rules; proofs; how CPDAG feasibility under path constraints is decided without full enumeration; MaxSAT encoding details).
- Practicality: Relying on class(P) enumeration for undecided cases may be prohibitive in larger CPDAGs; the paper does not quantify this cost or propose polynomial-time tests (e.g., using reachability in CPDAG with compelled/reversible edge analysis).

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: Promising idea, but theoretical guarantees are not fully established in the paper, and some key procedures appear exponential with no empirical cost breakdown. Experimental scope is narrow and lacks stress tests (noisy constraints, larger n).
- Clarity: The high-level narrative is understandable, but the paper has many presentation issues and omits essential details/proofs.
- Originality: Moderate. The EC tree and oracle framework is prior work; the novelty lies in integrating ancestral constraints via pruning and in the projection mechanisms. The edge entailment rules are interesting; the MaxSAT-based ordering projection is a reasonable formulation but not clearly superior to alternative graph-theoretic approaches.
- Significance: Potentially useful for practitioners who have path-level prior knowledge and want exact learning. However, current evidence is limited to small-scale synthetic setups; robustness and scalability remain unproven.

Questions for authors
1. Can you provide a polynomial-time test for determining whether a CPDAG can realize X ≺ Y (ancestor) without enumerating class(P)? If not, how often did enumeration occur and what was its runtime share?
2. Please clarify Theorem 1 and provide proofs or a pointer to an appendix. As stated it is confusing.
3. For Theorems 2–4, can you provide full proofs and the exact closure rules used for ancestral constraints?
4. How does the approach behave under noisy/incorrect ancestral constraints? Is there a way to detect infeasibility or relax constraints?
5. Provide ablation results isolating the contribution of edge projection vs ordering projection vs pruning only, including MaxSAT overhead.
6. How do results change if you cap parent-set size (to favor ILP) or use stronger ILP cuts/CP solvers?

Pros
- Addresses a practically relevant form of background knowledge.
- Integrates cleanly with established exact BN learning frameworks.
- Promising empirical speedups in tested regimes.

Cons
- Insufficient theoretical detail; unclear correctness/efficiency of key subroutines.
- Limited and potentially biased empirical evaluation; no robustness to noisy constraints.
- Presentation and referencing issues hinder reproducibility.

Recommendation
Score: 5 (leaning reject). The idea is interesting and potentially impactful, but the paper, as written, lacks the necessary theoretical rigor, clarity, and breadth of empirical validation to meet NeurIPS standards. I encourage the authors to strengthen the proofs, provide polynomial-time feasibility tests or quantify enumeration costs, add robust experiments (including noisy constraints and stronger baselines), and improve presentation.

Confidence: High.
