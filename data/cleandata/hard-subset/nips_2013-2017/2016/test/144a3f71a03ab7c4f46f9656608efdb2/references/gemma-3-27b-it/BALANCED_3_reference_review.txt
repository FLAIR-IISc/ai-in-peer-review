This paper addresses the important problem of learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints, a common scenario in applications where domain knowledge exists. The core idea is to leverage the recent framework of Chen et al. [2015, 2016] for optimal Bayesian network structure learning with non-decomposable priors, and extend it to incorporate these constraints. The authors propose a method for pruning the search space (EC trees) based on ancestral constraints and, crucially, for *projecting* these constraints into decomposable ones that can empower the underlying oracle. The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and tackles a practically relevant problem.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an important problem:** Integrating background knowledge into Bayesian network structure learning is critical for many real-world applications. Ancestral constraints are a natural way to represent such knowledge.
* **Novel approach:** Extending the Chen et al. framework to handle non-decomposable constraints, specifically ancestral constraints, is a significant contribution.
* **Constraint projection:** The idea of projecting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones to enhance the oracle’s performance is clever and appears to be highly effective, as demonstrated by the experiments.
* **Empirical validation:** The extensive experimental evaluation against ILP-based approaches shows significant performance improvements, demonstrating the practical value of the proposed method. The analysis of the effect of constraint percentage and network size is insightful.
* **Thorough related work:** The paper appropriately contextualizes the work within the existing literature on Bayesian network structure learning and constraint satisfaction.
* **Clear and organized:** The paper is well-structured and easy to follow, with a logical flow of ideas.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of implementation:** While the concepts are clearly explained, the implementation likely requires significant engineering effort, particularly the MaxSAT solver integration for constraint projection.  This isn't a weakness of the *research* per se, but a practical consideration for adoption.
* **Scalability limitations:** Although the experiments show scalability to n=20, the memory limitations (32GB) suggest that the approach may struggle with significantly larger networks, even with constraint projection. This should be discussed more explicitly.
* **Oracle dependency:** The performance is heavily reliant on the effectiveness of the underlying oracle (URLEARNING).  The sensitivity of the method to the choice of oracle isn't thoroughly investigated. While URLEARNING is a reasonable choice, exploring other oracles would strengthen the analysis.
* **MaxSAT solver performance:** The performance of the MaxSAT solver could be a bottleneck, especially for larger problems. The paper doesn't delve into the solver's performance characteristics or potential optimizations.
* **Theoretical guarantees:** While Theorem 1 establishes the soundness of the pruning step, a more thorough theoretical analysis of the constraint projection process and its impact on the search space would be valuable.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and effective approach to a challenging problem (Bayesian network structure learning with non-decomposable constraints).
* The experimental results are compelling, demonstrating significant improvements over existing methods.
* The concept of constraint projection is innovative and has the potential to be applied to other constraint-based learning problems.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The implementation complexity and scalability limitations might hinder widespread adoption.
* The strong dependency on the oracle and the lack of investigation into alternative oracles are potential weaknesses.
* The theoretical analysis could be more comprehensive.


**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. The proposed approach is both novel and practical, and the experimental results are convincing. While there are some limitations, they do not outweigh the strengths of the work. I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance to NIPS, with the caveat that the authors address the scalability concerns and potentially discuss the sensitivity to the choice of the oracle in the camera-ready version.

**Suggestions for improvement:**

* **Discuss scalability:** Explicitly address the scalability limitations and potential strategies for mitigating them.
* **Oracle sensitivity:** Investigate the impact of using different oracles on the performance of the approach.
* **MaxSAT analysis:** Provide more details about the MaxSAT solver used and its performance characteristics.
* **Theoretical extensions:**  Explore a more detailed theoretical analysis of the constraint projection process.
* **Code release:**  Consider releasing the code to facilitate reproducibility and adoption.
