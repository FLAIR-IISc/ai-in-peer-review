

# Review of "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"

This paper proposes a method for learning Bayesian network structures subject to ancestral constraints, which are non-decomposable and thus challenging for standard structure learning approaches. The authors introduce a search space called the EC tree that allows for pruning based on ancestral constraints, and they propose techniques to project ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints to empower an oracle that uses decomposable scores.

**Strengths:**
The paper addresses a meaningful problem in Bayesian network structure learning, as ancestral constraints are common in domain knowledge and can significantly reduce the search space. The EC tree approach provides a principled way to incorporate these non-decomposable constraints, and the projection techniques for converting ancestral constraints to decomposable ones are theoretically sound. The empirical results suggest substantial efficiency gains over ILP-based approaches, which is promising for practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Formatting and Typographical Errors:**
   The paper contains several formatting inconsistencies that undermine its professionalism. The conference name appears as "th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016)" (should be "The 30th Conference..."), and there's a strange "11 16" appearing in the text. The references section has inconsistent citation formats (e.g., "Bartlett and Cussens.,? \Q2015\E,"), which suggests poor proofreading.

2. **Technical Errors:**
   The paper contains a critical technical error in the example in Section 3. The optimal DAG for the given scores should be X1 → X2 → X3, not "X1 X2 → X3" as stated. This typo fundamentally undermines the example's explanatory value. More significantly, the paper claims "A CPDAG P with no partially directed paths from X1 to X2 violates the given constraint, as no structure in class(P ) contains a path from X1 to X2" (Section 4.3). This is incorrect - a CPDAG with no partially directed paths from X1 to X2 could still have paths in some DAGs within the equivalence class (e.g., if X1 and X2 are connected by an undirected edge).

3. **Misrepresentation of K2 Algorithm:**
   The paper states "We can find the DAG G that optimizes Equation 1 by running the K2 algorithm on all n! variable orderings σ, and then take the DAG with the best score." This is factually incorrect - the K2 algorithm assumes a fixed topological ordering and cannot be run on all orderings to find the globally optimal DAG. The correct approach would be to search over orderings, not apply K2 to each ordering independently.

4. **Inconsistent Notation and Terminology:**
   The paper uses inconsistent notation for parent sets (e.g., "XU" vs "X → U" for parent sets), which creates confusion. The explanation of the EC tree (Section 4.2) is particularly confusing: "X must be the largest variable in Gj (according to some canonical ordering)" but fails to specify what this canonical ordering is or why it matters for the algorithm's correctness.

5. **Lack of Clarity on Key Claims:**
   The paper claims "the EC tree is a recently proposed search space that improves the BN graph along two dimensions" but doesn't clearly explain how this improvement specifically benefits the problem of learning with ancestral constraints. The projection techniques are described theoretically but lack sufficient detail to assess their practical implementation.

6. **Insufficient Evidence for Claims:**
   The paper states "our approach is orders-of-magnitude more efficient compared to learning systems based on ILP" but doesn't provide sufficient evidence for this claim in the provided text. The referenced Tables 1-3 are not included, making it impossible to verify the claims.

**Overall Assessment:**
While the paper addresses an important problem with a theoretically sound approach, the numerous technical errors, inconsistent notation, and lack of clarity significantly undermine its credibility. The fundamental error in the example in Section 3 is particularly concerning, as it suggests the authors may not have thoroughly verified their core example. The paper's claims about efficiency improvements cannot be properly assessed without the missing tables, and the misrepresentation of the K2 algorithm is a serious technical flaw.

The paper's contribution is interesting but not sufficiently validated or clearly presented. The technical errors and inconsistencies make it difficult to trust the paper's claims, and the lack of clarity on key algorithmic details prevents proper evaluation of the proposed methods. While the problem is significant and the approach has potential, the paper as written does not meet the quality standards expected for NIPS.

**Recommendation:** Reject. The paper contains multiple serious technical errors and inconsistencies that prevent it from being a credible scientific contribution. The errors in the core example and the misrepresentation of standard algorithms undermine the entire paper's credibility, making it difficult to assess the validity of the proposed methods.
