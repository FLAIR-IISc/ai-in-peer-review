**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data when given background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints (i.e., specifying that a variable X must be, or must not be, an ancestor of Y). The key challenge is that such constraints are non-decomposable and therefore incompatible with many mainstream efficient, score-based learning algorithms that rely on the decomposability of the scoring function.

The authors build upon a recently proposed search framework based on A* search over a space of equivalence classes (the EC tree) [Chen et al., 2016], which can handle non-decomposable scores. The core contributions are twofold: 1) adapting this search to prune branches that violate ancestral constraints, and 2) a novel "constraint projection" method that infers decomposable edge and ordering constraints from the non-decomposable ancestral constraints. These inferred decomposable constraints are then used to "empower" the oracle (a fast solver for decomposable problems) that computes the A* heuristic, thereby significantly speeding up the search. The authors formulate the inference of ordering constraints as a MaxSAT problem. Empirically, the proposed method is compared against an Integer Linear Programming (ILP) baseline (GOBNILP), demonstrating orders-of-magnitude speedups on benchmark datasets for networks up to 20 variables.

**Critique**

The paper is well-written and tackles a relevant problem. Incorporating rich prior knowledge is crucial for learning reliable graphical models, and ancestral relations are an intuitive form for such knowledge. The proposed method for projecting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones is technically interesting. However, I have several reservations regarding the methodology and the significance of the contribution in its current form.

**Quality and Methodological Soundness:**

The work is technically sound in its formulation. The theorems for inferring edge constraints appear correct, and the use of MaxSAT for ordering constraints is a creative approach. The experimental methodology is standard for this area of research.

My primary concern is the foundation upon which this work is built. The entire approach relies on the BN graph/EC tree framework from Chen et al. [2015, 2016]. This is a very recent and not yet widely established technique. While promising, it is not a standard tool in the BN structure learning literature in the same way that dynamic programming over orderings or ILP formulations are. The paper essentially presents an extension of this new framework to a specific type of constraint. This makes the contribution feel incremental and dependent on the success and adoption of a nascent methodology.

Furthermore, the choice of baseline is narrow. The comparison is solely against an ILP-based solver. While ILP is a valid exact method for this problem, it is well-documented that A*-based search methods often outperform general-purpose ILP solvers on unconstrained BN learning tasks [Yuan and Malone, 2013]. Therefore, the reported speedups, while impressive, may simply reflect the known performance differences between these two families of algorithms rather than the specific merit of the proposed constraint handling method. A more convincing evaluation would require comparison against other potential approaches, or at least a more thorough justification for why ILP is the most relevant baseline. For instance, could other search-based methods be adapted with less elegant, but still functional, constraint-checking mechanisms?

Finally, the complexity of the constraint projection step is not adequately analyzed. The reduction to MaxSAT for inferring ordering constraints is non-trivial. The paper does not report the time required for this pre-processing step or analyze how its own complexity scales with the number of variables. If this step becomes a bottleneck for larger problems, it could undermine the practical benefits of the overall approach.

**Originality and Significance:**

The novelty of the paper lies almost entirely in the constraint projection technique. The adaptation of the EC tree search itself is a straightforward application of the framework. While the projection idea is clever, the overall contribution feels more like an application paper for the EC tree methodology rather than a fundamental advance in structure learning.

The significance of the results is also questionable. The method scales to n=20 variables, which is on par with other modern exact methods but still far from the scale of many real-world problems. The paper successfully demonstrates a performance gain over a specific ILP solver for a specific type of non-decomposable constraint. However, it is unclear if this provides a generalizable lesson for handling other non-decomposable scores or constraints, or if it will spur broader adoption of the underlying EC tree framework. Given the complexity of the overall system (A* search over CPDAGs, a decomposable oracle, plus a MaxSAT solver for pre-processing), it seems unlikely that practitioners will favor this approach over simpler, well-understood heuristic methods when scalability is a concern. The work advances the state-of-the-art on a very specific sub-problem, but the broader impact on the field appears limited.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper addresses an important practical problem of incorporating non-decomposable prior knowledge into BN structure learning.
*   The constraint projection technique, especially the MaxSAT formulation for ordering constraints, is novel and technically sound.
*   The empirical results show a clear and substantial performance improvement over a recognized exact method (ILP).

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The work is highly incremental, functioning as an extension of a very recent and not yet established search framework from the same authors.
*   The experimental comparison is limited to a single baseline (ILP), which may not be the most competitive alternative for this search-based approach.
*   The scalability remains limited, and the practical significance for the broader NIPS community is therefore debatable.
*   The complexity and overhead of the MaxSAT-based pre-processing step are not evaluated, leaving the analysis of the method's efficiency incomplete.

In conclusion, while the paper presents a solid piece of technical work on a niche problem, its reliance on a nascent framework and its narrow experimental validation limit its perceived significance and impact. The contribution seems more suited for a specialized venue on probabilistic graphical models or uncertainty in AI, rather than the broad audience of NIPS. I am skeptical that this work, in its current form, provides a sufficiently novel or significant advance to warrant publication at this conference.
