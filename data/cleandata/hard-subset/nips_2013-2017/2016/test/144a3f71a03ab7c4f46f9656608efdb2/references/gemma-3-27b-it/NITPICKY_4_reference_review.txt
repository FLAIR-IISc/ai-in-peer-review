This paper addresses the problem of learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints, a practically relevant scenario where prior knowledge about causal relationships exists. The authors build upon their previous work utilizing EC trees for optimal structure learning and propose methods to incorporate these non-decomposable constraints. The core idea is to prune the EC tree based on constraint violations and, crucially, to *project* the ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints (edge and ordering constraints) to enhance the oracle's efficiency. The paper is clearly structured and motivated, with a detailed technical presentation.

However, despite the novelty of addressing ancestral constraints within this specific framework and the potential impact of constraint projection, I find several methodological shortcomings and inconsistencies that, in my assessment, outweigh the contributions. My recommendation is **reject**.

**Strengths:**

* **Relevant Problem:** Learning Bayesian networks with constraints is an important area.
* **Novel Approach:** Integrating non-decomposable constraints into the EC tree framework is a novel contribution.
* **Constraint Projection:** The idea of projecting ancestral constraints into decomposable ones to empower the oracle is insightful.
* **Empirical Evaluation:** The paper includes an empirical evaluation comparing the proposed approach to ILP-based methods.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Experimental Scope:** The experimental setup, while extensive in parameter variation, lacks sufficient justification for network choices and dataset sizes. The reliance on synthetic data, generated from existing networks, feels somewhat contrived. A more realistic benchmark with real-world datasets and constraints would strengthen the conclusions.
* **Heuristic Dependence:** The performance is heavily reliant on the quality of the oracle (URLEARNING) and the heuristic function. The paper doesn't adequately address the sensitivity of the results to different oracle choices. Furthermore, claiming "orders of magnitude improvements" requires a more rigorous statistical analysis – are these improvements statistically significant across all settings?
* **MaxSAT Implementation Details:** The reduction to MaxSAT for ordering constraints is mentioned but lacks sufficient detail. How was the MaxSAT solver configured? What were the performance characteristics of the MaxSAT solving process? Without this information, it's difficult to assess the scalability and practicality of this projection step.
* **Inconsistent Notation & Definitions:** There are several instances where notation is used inconsistently. For example, the definition of α(X) and β(X) could be clarified and explicitly linked to the constraints within A, rather than implicitly relying on the reader to infer the relationship. There's also ambiguity around the definition of “maximum set of ancestral constraints A” - is this the set achieved after iterative application of transitivity, or is there an algorithmic process?
* **Overstated Claims:**  The claim that the approach “scales to n = 20 variables” is questionable. While the experiments run up to n=20, the reported runtimes are still substantial, suggesting that scalability remains a concern. Moreover, the comparison to Yuan and Malone [2013] is misleading; that work used BDeu scores, whereas this work relies on a different scoring function.
* **Missing Baseline:** A comparison against simpler approaches to constraint integration – for example, directly modifying the K2 algorithm with constraint checks during parent selection – is missing. This would help to establish the true value added by the more complex EC tree framework.
* **Theorem 2 & 3’s clarity:** Theorem 2 and 3 could be more clear, the conditions presented need a more detailed explanation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper tackles a challenging problem with a novel approach.
* The idea of constraint projection is promising and could lead to significant performance improvements.
* The empirical results demonstrate that the proposed method can outperform existing ILP-based approaches in certain settings.

**Arguments for Rejection (My Position):**

* The experimental evaluation is limited and lacks sufficient statistical rigor.
* The reliance on a specific oracle introduces a significant source of uncertainty.
* The lack of detailed implementation information hinders reproducibility and assessment.
* The inconsistencies in notation and definitions detract from the clarity of the presentation.
* The scalability remains a concern, and the claims of “orders of magnitude improvements” are overstated.
* The omission of a simpler baseline makes it difficult to assess the true benefits of the proposed approach.



In conclusion, while the paper presents an interesting idea, the numerous methodological flaws and inconsistencies, coupled with the limited scope of the experimental evaluation, lead me to believe that it is not ready for publication in NIPS at this time. A significantly revised version, addressing the points raised above, could potentially be considered for future submission.
