

# Review for "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"

This paper addresses a significant challenge in Bayesian network structure learning: incorporating non-decomposable ancestral constraints into the learning process. The authors build upon Chen et al.'s work on non-decomposable scores (2015, 2016) by introducing a novel method for projecting ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints that can be efficiently utilized with existing oracles. This approach enables the use of the EC tree search space (previously limited to non-decomposable scores) for structure learning with ancestral constraints, which are important for expressing cause-and-effect relationships in real-world applications.

The paper's core contribution is the sound and complete method for projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints. The authors prove theoretically that their projection method preserves the set of valid DAGs satisfying the original ancestral constraints. They further demonstrate how these projected constraints can dramatically improve the efficiency of structure search when passed to an oracle that optimizes decomposable scores. The empirical results are compelling, showing orders-of-magnitude speedups over ILP-based approaches for learning with ancestral constraints.

This work represents a meaningful advance in Bayesian network learning, particularly for applications where domain knowledge is available in the form of ancestral constraints. The authors effectively contrast their approach with existing methods (K2, order graph, ILP, CP), demonstrating that while these methods can handle decomposable constraints, they cannot directly incorporate ancestral constraints. The paper's key innovation is bridging this gap through constraint projection, making ancestral constraints practically useful in the context of existing efficient learning algorithms.

The technical contributions are well-grounded with clear theorems and proofs (Theorems 1-4) that establish the soundness and completeness of their approach. The experimental evaluation is thorough, varying the number of variables, dataset size, and proportion of constraints, with results consistently showing the superiority of the proposed method. The authors also provide an insightful analysis of how ancestral constraints impact both learning efficiency and model accuracy.

I believe this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of probabilistic graphical models, particularly for applications where background knowledge is available. The approach is both theoretically elegant and practically impactful, offering a substantial improvement in learning efficiency when ancestral constraints are present. The paper's focus on a practically important problem with a novel solution that leverages existing infrastructure (the oracle) is exactly the kind of work that advances the field in a meaningful way.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a strong connection between theoretical contributions and empirical validation. The related work section adequately contextualizes the paper within the broader literature on Bayesian network structure learning.

Minor weaknesses include:
1. Limited discussion of how frequently ancestral constraints are actually available in real-world applications (though this is understandable given the paper's focus on the methodological contribution)
2. The paper could provide more concrete examples of domain-specific applications where ancestral constraints are naturally available (e.g., biological networks, medical diagnosis systems)

However, these are minor considerations given the paper's strong theoretical and empirical contributions. The authors have made a significant methodological advance that addresses an important practical challenge in Bayesian network learning.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold approaches, I find this paper's contribution particularly compelling. The projection method for converting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones is a clever insight that opens up new possibilities for incorporating background knowledge into structure learning. The dramatic speedups demonstrated in the experiments (orders of magnitude faster than ILP approaches) make this not just theoretically interesting but practically valuable.

The paper meets all four criteria for NIPS acceptance:
- Quality: Technically sound with strong theoretical and empirical validation
- Clarity: Well-written and logically organized
- Originality: The constraint projection method is novel and represents a significant advance
- Significance: Addresses an important practical problem with a substantial impact on efficiency

This is exactly the kind of paper that advances the field in a meaningful way, and I believe it would be a valuable contribution to NIPS. The authors have identified a key limitation in current Bayesian network learning approaches (inability to handle non-decomposable ancestral constraints efficiently) and provided a novel solution that works within existing algorithmic frameworks. The approach is both theoretically rigorous and practically effective, making it a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.
