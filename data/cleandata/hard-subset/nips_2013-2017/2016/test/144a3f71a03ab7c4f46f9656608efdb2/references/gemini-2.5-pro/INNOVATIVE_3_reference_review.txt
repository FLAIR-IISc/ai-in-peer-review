**Summary:**
This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data, subject to background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints (e.g., X must be an ancestor of Y, or X cannot be an ancestor of Y). The primary challenge is that such constraints are non-decomposable, rendering most state-of-the-art exact, score-based learning algorithms inapplicable, as they rely on the assumption of decomposable scores. The authors build upon a recent search framework (the EC tree) designed for non-decomposable scores, which uses an A*-like search with an "oracle" that can solve the decomposable version of the problem to provide an admissible heuristic.

The main and most novel contribution of this paper is a method to "project" the non-decomposable ancestral constraints into a set of decomposable constraints (edge presence/absence and pairwise topological ordering constraints). These inferred decomposable constraints are then fed to the oracle, dramatically improving the heuristic's quality and pruning power, which in turn massively accelerates the overall search. The authors provide an efficient algorithm for inferring edge constraints and a MaxSAT-based formulation for inferring a large, consistent set of ordering constraints. Empirically, the proposed approach is compared against an Integer Linear Programming (ILP) baseline, demonstrating orders-of-magnitude speed improvements and scaling to networks of up to 20 variables.

**Strengths:**
The paper's primary strength lies in its conceptual novelty and elegance. The core idea of projecting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones to empower a fast, specialized subroutine (the oracle) is a bold and powerful approach. It's a genuinely new way to bridge the gap between general but slow methods (like ILP) and fast but restricted ones (like dynamic programming on order graphs). This is not an incremental improvement; it's a significant new technique for tackling a fundamental challenge in structure learning.

The practical significance is also very high. Ancestral constraints are a natural way for experts to encode prior knowledge (e.g., about causality or temporal sequences), but incorporating them into optimal learning has been a major challenge. This work makes doing so practical for moderately-sized problems. The experiment showing that a small number of constraints can be as beneficial as a large increase in data volume powerfully illustrates the value of this line of research.

The methodology is sound, and the experimental evaluation is convincing. The choice to compare against an ILP-based approach is the correct one, as ILP is one of the few other general methods capable of finding optimal structures with these types of constraints. The reported speed-ups are not minor; they are dramatic and leave no doubt about the efficacy of the proposed technique.

**Weaknesses:**
The work is a specialized application and extension of the EC tree framework proposed in prior work by the same authors. While the contribution is significant, it's not a completely new search paradigm.

The scalability is limited to around 20 variables. This is not a flaw of the paper per se, but an inherent limitation of exact score-based methods. The authors are transparent about this, and their performance is state-of-the-art within this problem class.

The proposed MaxSAT formulation for inferring ordering constraints is a pragmatic, heuristic approach to find a large set of entailed constraints, rather than a complete, formally-guaranteed one. While this might be seen as a lack of methodological completeness, I view it as a clever and practical solution to a very difficult subproblem. Given the massive performance gains it enables, this trade-off is more than justified.

**Recommendation: Strong Accept**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **High Novelty:** The concept of projecting non-decomposable constraints to empower a decomposable oracle is a major, elegant, and impactful idea. It presents a new way to tackle a broad class of problems where global constraints must be integrated into locally-decomposable searches.
2.  **Significant Contribution:** The paper provides the first practical and efficient method for finding provably optimal Bayesian network structures under ancestral constraints, a long-standing and important problem.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The method is shown to be orders of magnitude faster than the most relevant competitor (ILP), demonstrating its clear superiority and practical value.
4.  **Bold Approach:** The work tackles the exact, optimal learning problem directly, avoiding the easier path of local search or other approximations. The creativity shown in the projection method is precisely the kind of bold thinking that should be encouraged.

This paper is an excellent example of the kind of work NIPS should highlight. It takes a hard, important problem and offers a novel, elegant, and demonstrably effective solution. It moves the field forward not by inches, but by a significant leap. I am willing to overlook the minor limitations regarding the completeness of the constraint inference, as the core conceptual contribution and its practical realization are outstanding.
