### Summary

This paper addresses the problem of learning the optimal structure of a Bayesian network from data, subject to background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints (e.g., variable X must be an ancestor of Y, or X must not be an ancestor of Y). Such constraints are highly relevant in practice, particularly in causal discovery, but are non-decomposable, which prevents their direct integration into most modern exact score-based learning algorithms that rely on score decomposability (e.g., methods based on dynamic programming or A* search over orderings).

The authors build upon a recent search framework [Chen et al., 2015, 2016] that navigates a space of equivalence classes of DAGs (the EC tree) using A* search. The heuristic for this search is provided by an "oracle"—an exact algorithm that can solve the unconstrained (or decomposably constrained) problem efficiently. The main contributions of this paper are:
1.  An adaptation of the EC tree search to handle ancestral constraints by pruning nodes (CPDAGs) that are inconsistent with the given constraints.
2.  A novel method to "project" the non-decomposable ancestral constraints onto a set of decomposable constraints (edge presence/absence and variable ordering constraints). This projection "empowers the oracle," leading to a much tighter heuristic and consequently a more efficient A* search.
3.  Algorithms for this projection, including a direct inference method for edge constraints and a MaxSAT formulation to find a maximal set of consistent ordering constraints.
4.  An extensive empirical evaluation showing that the proposed approach is orders of magnitude faster than a state-of-the-art Integer Linear Programming (ILP) based method and that incorporating ancestral constraints significantly improves the accuracy of the learned structures.

### Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The paper tackles a well-motivated and important problem. While the idea of using background knowledge is not new, optimally incorporating non-decomposable constraints like ancestral relations into score-based learning remains a significant challenge. The proposed approach provides an elegant and effective solution. The key novel idea is not just to use the constraints for pruning, but to also project them back into the decomposable world to improve the search heuristic. This is a clever and powerful concept that could be applicable to other non-decomposable constraints as well.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed method is technically sound. It correctly identifies the limitations of existing methods and builds upon a state-of-the-art exact learning framework. The formalization of how to infer decomposable edge and ordering constraints from ancestral constraints (Theorems 2, 3, 4) is a solid theoretical contribution. The use of MaxSAT to find a maximal set of consistent ordering constraints is a sophisticated and appropriate choice for this subproblem.

3.  **Experimental Rigor:** The experiments are thorough and convincing.
    *   The comparison against a strong baseline for exact learning with side constraints (ILP via GOBNILP) clearly demonstrates the superior performance of the proposed method.
    *   The paper includes a critical ablation-style comparison (Tables 1 vs. 2) that isolates the performance gain from empowering the oracle with projected constraints, proving this to be a crucial component of the work.
    *   The scalability analysis (up to 20 variables) is respectable for an exact structure learning algorithm and demonstrates the practical viability of the approach.
    *   The evaluation of learning accuracy (Structural Hamming Distance) provides a compelling argument for the practical utility of ancestral constraints, showing they can be as impactful as collecting significantly more data.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. It clearly explains the problem, the limitations of prior work, and the details of its own contributions. The figures and tables are informative and support the main claims effectively.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Details on CPDAG Constraint Checking:** The paper states that when initial efficient checks are inconclusive, it must "exhaustively enumerate the structures of class(P), to check if any of them satisfies the given constraints." A CPDAG can represent an exponential number of DAGs, making this step a potential computational bottleneck. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of this, for instance by reporting how frequently this expensive check is required in the experiments and what its typical cost is. Is there a more efficient characterization or algorithm for checking if *any* DAG in an equivalence class satisfies an ancestral constraint?

2.  **Runtime of the MaxSAT Solver:** The projection of ordering constraints relies on solving a MaxSAT instance. While effective, this can be computationally expensive. The experimental section does not report the time taken by the MaxSAT solver. It would be helpful to know if this preprocessing step constitutes a significant fraction of the total runtime, especially as the number of variables grows.

3.  **Clarity on Pruning Soundness (Theorem 1):** Theorem 1 states that a CPDAG P satisfies constraints A iff its descendants do. The soundness of pruning descendants relies on the property that if a CPDAG *fails* to satisfy the constraints (i.e., *no* DAG in its equivalence class satisfies them), then all of its descendants will also fail. While this property seems correct (as extending a DAG cannot remove an existing path or resolve a violation), the theorem's "if and only if" wording is slightly confusing. For example, a parent CPDAG P might satisfy a constraint X -> Y (because one of its DAGs has the path), but a child P' formed by adding an edge might violate it (if *all* consistent extensions of DAGs from P now violate it). Clarifying that the pruning is based on the contrapositive (if P violates A, all descendants violate A) would improve precision.

4.  **Comparison to Heuristic Methods:** The evaluation focuses exclusively on exact methods. While this is a fair comparison, it would be interesting to contextualize the results by including a comparison to a strong heuristic search method (e.g., Greedy Equivalence Search) adapted to respect ancestral constraints. This is not strictly necessary for a paper on optimal learning but would provide a more complete picture of the trade-offs between optimality, speed, and constraint satisfaction in the broader landscape of BN learning.

### Decision

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. It addresses an important practical problem with a novel and technically sound solution. The experimental results are strong and convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness and efficiency of the proposed method. The weaknesses are minor and mainly relate to requests for additional details and clarification rather than fundamental flaws. The work clearly meets the high standards of NeurIPS. I recommend acceptance.
