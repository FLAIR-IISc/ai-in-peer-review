## NIPS 2016 Paper Review: Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning Bayesian networks (BNs) when incorporating background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints (relationships specifying ancestor-descendant relationships between variables). Existing optimal structure learning approaches struggle with these constraints due to their non-decomposable nature. The authors build upon recent work utilizing the EC tree search space for non-decomposable scores, extending it to handle ancestral constraints by pruning the search space and, crucially, by *projecting* these constraints into decomposable constraints (edge and ordering constraints) that can empower an oracle used within the EC tree search.  They demonstrate significant empirical improvements over an Integer Linear Programming (ILP) based approach, highlighting both the pruning benefits and the power of the projected constraints.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper correctly situates itself within the landscape of Bayesian network structure learning. It acknowledges the limitations of traditional methods like K2 and dynamic programming which rely on decomposable scores and struggle with non-modular constraints.  It builds upon the framework introduced by Chen et al. (2015, 2016) using the BN graph and EC tree, recognizing their ability to handle non-decomposable priors.  The work also acknowledges connections to ILP and Constraint Programming (CP) approaches for BN structure learning (e.g., Jaakkola et al., 2010; Bartlett & Cussens, 2015), but positions their contribution as empirically superior. Importantly, it connects to related work incorporating constraints, like Borboudakis and Tsamardinos (2012, 2013) which uses path constraints and prior beliefs for causal discovery.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an Important Problem:** Integrating prior knowledge into BN structure learning is a crucial step towards building more accurate and interpretable models. Ancestral constraints represent a natural and powerful form of such knowledge.
* **Novel Approach:** The combination of EC tree search with pruning based on ancestral constraints and, *especially*, the projection of non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones, is a significant contribution. This projection dramatically improves the oracle’s efficiency and overall search speed.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The empirical evaluation convincingly demonstrates the superiority of the proposed approach over an ILP-based method.  The orders-of-magnitude speedups are impressive. The ablation study showing the impact of constraint projection is particularly insightful.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The theorems presented (Theorem 1, 2, 3, and 4) provide a solid theoretical foundation for the constraint projection and pruning mechanisms.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a logical flow from problem definition to proposed solution and empirical evaluation.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Experimental Setup - Limited Baselines:** While the ILP comparison is valuable, comparing to more modern, scalable score-based structure learning algorithms (even those *without* explicit constraint handling) would strengthen the empirical results.  How does the approach compare to, for example, methods employing heuristic search within the order graph?
* **Scalability Limits:** While the authors demonstrate scalability to 20 variables, this remains a limitation. More discussion of the computational bottlenecks as the number of variables increases would be helpful.  What are the primary drivers of computational cost (e.g., the MaxSAT solver, the oracle, the pruning)?
* **MaxSAT Solver Dependency:** The approach relies on an external MaxSAT solver.  The performance and scalability of this solver could be a limiting factor in real-world applications. A sensitivity analysis on the performance of the MaxSAT solver would be beneficial.
* **Dataset Generation:** The experiments rely on simulated data. While this allows for controlled evaluation, the results may not generalize perfectly to real-world datasets where noise and data quality can be significant issues.
* **Minor Clarity Issues:** The discussion of Theorems 2 and 3 could be more concise and easier to follow. A more illustrative example would aid understanding.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* This paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a challenging problem in Bayesian network structure learning.
* The empirical results are compelling, demonstrating significant improvements over existing methods.
* The constraint projection technique is a clever and effective way to leverage prior knowledge and improve search efficiency.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited in terms of baseline comparisons.
* Scalability remains a concern, although the authors demonstrate results up to 20 variables.
* Reliance on an external MaxSAT solver introduces a dependency and potential bottleneck.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. The proposed approach is innovative, theoretically grounded, and empirically validated. While the limitations mentioned above are worth considering, they do not overshadow the paper’s strengths. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, specifically addressing the need for more comprehensive baseline comparisons and a deeper discussion of scalability limitations.  The authors should also consider including a brief discussion of the potential impact of data quality and noise on the performance of their approach.



**Confidence:** 8/10
