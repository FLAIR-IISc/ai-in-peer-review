- Addresses exact BN structure learning with non-decomposable ancestral (path) constraints, extending EC-tree/A* search to prune CPDAG nodes that cannot extend to any DAG satisfying constraints.  
- Introduces projection methods that convert ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints: polynomial rules for entailed edge constraints and a MaxSAT formulation for maximal cycle-free ordering constraints.  
- Empirically, the EC-tree approach is orders of magnitude faster than an ILP baseline (GOBNILP) on benchmark subgraphs with synthetic ancestral constraints; projection strengthens both methods and benefits EC-tree most.  
- The A* correctness and high-level approach are sound; pruning via EC trees and tightening the oracle with projected constraints is valid.  
- Theorem 1 is poorly phrased and should be clarified/formalized (intended monotonicity: if P violates A then all descendants do).  
- Pruning checks are simple sufficient conditions; when inconclusive the method may need to enumerate DAGs in a CPDAG—soundness follows from monotonicity but frequency/impact of enumeration is unclear.  
- Theorem 2 (negative edge inference) is plausible; Theorem 3 (positive edge iff condition) may be overstated—necessity is nontrivial and needs proof or relaxation to a one-way claim.  
- The MaxSAT encoding and Theorem 4 claim sufficiency for entailment but lack detailed justification; more formal proof or sketches are needed.  
- Enumerating class(P) can be exponential; the paper should report how often this happens and its runtime effect, plus MaxSAT overhead.  
- Experiments are positive and extensive, comparing fairly to GOBNILP with shared precomputed local scores; more reporting on timeouts/solved counts and time breakdowns (oracle vs. EC-tree vs. MaxSAT) is requested.  
- Paper organization and narrative are clear, but contains typos, formatting artifacts, malformed references, and missing figure/table contents.  
- Definitions like “partially directed path” and CPDAG path criteria need precise statements and citations (e.g., Meek’s rules, Chickering 2002).  
- Theorems lack proofs; brief sketches or an appendix would strengthen rigor, especially for Theorems 3–4.  
- The closure procedure for producing a “maximum” set of ancestral constraints should be spelled out with complexity analysis.  
- Integrating non-decomposable ancestral constraints into EC-tree/A* search and using a decomposable-score oracle is novel and useful.  
- Projecting path constraints into edge and ordering constraints, and using MaxSAT to extract a maximal safe ordering set, appears to be a new and practical idea.  
- Prior ILP/CP approaches can encode ancestral constraints but were not empirically compared for exact BN learning; this comparison is valuable.  
- If broadly applicable, the approach could make exact BN learning with rich background knowledge more practical and influence practice.  
- Projection idea is general and may benefit other exact and approximate learners by reducing candidate sets.  
- Current scalability peaks around ~20 variables without indegree bounds; showing results with indegree bounds or larger n would increase impact.  
- Pros: clear motivation, sound EC-tree pruning strategy, effective projection methods, clever MaxSAT use, and strong empirical gains over ILP.  
- Cons: missing proofs for key theorems, potential exponential CPDAG enumeration not quantified, experiments use ground-truth-derived constraints (no noise study), and clarity/implementation details lacking.  
- Questions for authors: frequency and cost of class(P) enumeration; availability of proofs for Theorems 3–4 or whether statements should be weakened; handling and detection of inconsistent ancestral constraints; MaxSAT overhead and sizes of inferred ordering sets; solved counts/timeouts and memory usage for both methods.  
- Recommendation: leaning toward acceptance conditional on clarifying theoretical guarantees (esp. Theorems 3–4), quantifying expensive subroutines, and improving exposition and rigor.
