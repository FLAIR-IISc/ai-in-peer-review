Summary
The paper addresses optimal Bayesian network (BN) structure learning when provided with non-decomposable background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints (X is/is not an ancestor of Y). Mainstream exact, score-based methods rely on decomposable scores and typically only allow decomposable constraints (edge presence/absence; precedence constraints). Building on the BN graph and EC tree search spaces introduced by Chen et al. (2015, 2016), the authors show how to (i) integrate ancestral constraints into EC-tree-based search via pruning, and (ii) “project” these non-decomposable constraints into decomposable constraints—edge and ordering constraints—to strengthen a decomposable-score oracle used within A* search. They give rules for entailing edge constraints from ancestral constraints (Theorems 2–3), and formulate the inference of a large, jointly consistent set of ordering constraints as a MaxSAT problem with hard constraints ensuring transitivity and acyclicity (Theorem 4). Empirically, on benchmark BNs (ALARM, ANDES, CHILD, CPCS54, HEPAR2) with simulated data and randomly sampled ancestral constraints, their EC-tree-based approach outperforms an ILP baseline (GOBNILP) by orders of magnitude in runtime, especially when projected constraints are supplied. They also observe an easy–hard–easy trend versus the proportion of ancestral constraints and report improved accuracy (structural Hamming distance) as constraints and data increase.

Relation to prior work: Prior exact methods for optimal BN structure with decomposable scores include dynamic programming and A* on order graphs (Koivisto & Sood; Silander & Myllymäki; Yuan & Malone), and ILP/CP formulations (Jaakkola et al.; Bartlett & Cussens; van Beek & Hoffmann). Ancestral/path constraints have been used in constraint-based learning and priors for local search (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos). Chen et al. introduced BN graphs and EC trees for non-decomposable scores and order-modular priors. This paper extends that framework to handle hard ancestral constraints and shows how to leverage them to derive decomposable constraints, enabling more efficient exact search.

Strengths
- Addresses a practically important form of background knowledge (ancestral relations) that is hard to incorporate into decomposable-score exact learners.
- Conceptually clean integration of ancestral constraints via EC-tree pruning, with helpful sufficient tests (directed/partially directed paths) and fallback enumeration of class(P).
- Useful projection techniques: clear rules for entailing edge constraints (negative and positive) from ancestral constraints; MaxSAT formulation for inferring many safe ordering constraints.
- Strong empirical gains over a well-known ILP baseline; careful evaluation across networks, sample sizes, and constraint densities; analysis of the effect of projections on both methods.
- The framework remains exact.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Clarity/technical precision: Theorem 1 is likely misstated. As written (“P satisfies A iff its descendants satisfy A”), the “iff” is not generally true; the monotonicity is one-way: if P violates A, all descendants violate. Please restate/clarify the exact conditions (variable sets at a node vs. full set, and that adding leaves cannot fix violations nor create new violations among existing variables).
- Ordering projection: The text oscillates between “maximally large set” and presenting only sufficient constraints for entailment (Theorem 4). With only sufficient (not necessary) hard constraints, MaxSAT may not guarantee maximality of O. Please clarify optimality guarantees and the potential conservativeness of the encoding.
- Practicality of CPDAG enumeration. When the simple path tests are inconclusive, enumerating class(P) can be exponential. The paper does not quantify how often enumeration is triggered nor its cost. Ablations and statistics would help.
- Missing details: The rules for saturating ancestral constraints (“details omitted for space”) are important for reproducibility and to understand completeness of projections. Similarly, proofs or proof sketches for Theorems 2–4 would strengthen soundness claims.
- Experimental scope: Only ILP is compared; CP approaches are mentioned but not evaluated. An ablation isolating the impact of edge vs. ordering projections on search time would be informative. Reporting search node expansions and time breakdowns (EC-tree pruning, MaxSAT time, oracle time) would improve interpretability.
- Presentation issues: Some formatting errors, stray date text, incomplete bibliographic entries, and missing tables/figures impede readability.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: The approach is sound in spirit, with plausible theorems and a principled integration into EC-tree search. However, some statements (Theorem 1) need correction, and proofs/details are missing. The experimental methodology is reasonable and shows clear performance benefits; more diagnostics would strengthen the empirical case.
- Clarity: Generally well-structured, but several definitions and theorems require tighter statements, and some technical steps are omitted. Notation is mostly consistent; references need cleanup.
- Originality: The combination of EC-tree pruning for ancestral constraints and projection of non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones (with a MaxSAT formulation for orderings) appears novel relative to prior exact methods and prior uses of path constraints.
- Significance: The ability to incorporate ancestral knowledge exactly and efficiently is valuable. The reported speedups over ILP and the demonstrated impact of background knowledge on accuracy suggest the work will be useful to practitioners and spur further research.

Suggestions for improvement
- Correct and precisely state Theorem 1; formalize the monotonicity property under the EC-tree’s leaf-addition operator and specify the scope (variables present in the node).
- Provide proofs or clear proof sketches for Theorems 2–4; include the closure rules for ancestral constraints in an appendix.
- Clarify whether the MaxSAT encoding yields a maximal set of entailed ordering constraints; if conservative, say so and, if possible, tighten the encoding.
- Report how often class(P) enumeration is invoked, its cost, and any heuristics to avoid it.
- Add ablations (edge-only, order-only projections) and, if possible, a CP baseline.
- Clean up references, figures, and tables; ensure reproducibility details (code/parameters) are available.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important gap (non-decomposable ancestral constraints) with an exact, efficient method.
- Strong empirical performance and practical utility of projected constraints.
- Builds meaningfully on and advances recent EC-tree work.

Arguments against acceptance
- Some technical statements are unclear or possibly incorrect; missing proofs and omitted procedural details.
- Limited baselines and diagnostic analysis; unclear worst-case behavior of CPDAG enumeration.
- Presentation issues reduce clarity.

Overall, the contribution is promising and potentially impactful if the technical clarifications and empirical diagnostics are addressed.
