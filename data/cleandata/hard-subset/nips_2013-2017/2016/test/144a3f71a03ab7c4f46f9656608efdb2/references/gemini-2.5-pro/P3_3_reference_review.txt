**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data in the presence of ancestral constraints, which specify required or forbidden ancestor-descendant relationships. The central challenge is that ancestral constraints are non-decomposable, rendering them incompatible with mainstream, efficient, score-based learning algorithms that rely on decomposable scores (e.g., dynamic programming or A* search over orderings). The authors propose a novel approach that extends a recent framework for learning with non-decomposable scores [Chen et al., 2015, 2016], which performs A* search over a graph of equivalence classes (the EC tree). The core contributions are twofold: 1) a sound and complete method for pruning the EC tree search space based on the given ancestral constraints, and 2) a novel and powerful technique for "projecting" the non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints. These projected constraints are then used to significantly tighten the A* heuristic (the "oracle"), leading to dramatic search efficiency improvements. The method for inferring ordering constraints is particularly sophisticated, involving a reduction to MaxSAT to find a maximal set of consistent ordering constraints. The paper provides a strong empirical evaluation, demonstrating that their approach is orders of magnitude faster than a state-of-the-art Integer Linear Programming (ILP) baseline, which is one of the few other exact methods capable of handling such constraints.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The paper tackles a well-motivated and practically important problem. Incorporating rich prior knowledge, such as causal pathways, is crucial for learning meaningful models, especially with limited data. The proposed method for "projecting" non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones is a highly original and powerful idea. While the base search framework (EC tree) is from prior work, its adaptation and, more importantly, the empowerment of its oracle via constraint inference, constitute a significant conceptual advance. The MaxSAT formulation for inferring a globally consistent set of ordering constraints is particularly clever and addresses a subtle and difficult aspect of the problem.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The work is technically sound and rigorous. The proposed pruning strategy for the EC tree is justified with a theorem. The algorithms for inferring edge constraints from ancestral constraints (Theorems 2 and 3) are well-defined. The recognition that naively converting negative ancestral constraints into ordering constraints can be unsound is a critical insight, and the MaxSAT formulation provides an elegant and principled solution. The overall approach of using a fast solver for a restricted problem class (decomposable scores) as a heuristic to guide search for a more general problem class (non-decomposable constraints) is an elegant and effective search paradigm.

3.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The empirical results are comprehensive and convincing. The comparison against GOBNILP, a strong ILP-based baseline, clearly demonstrates the superiority of the proposed approach, with speedups of several orders of magnitude. The ablation study (comparing results with and without the projected constraints in Tables 1 and 2) effectively highlights the critical importance of the constraint projection technique, showing it is the key to the method's efficiency. The scalability analysis up to 20 variables is respectable for exact score-based learning, and the analysis of model accuracy (structural Hamming distance) provides valuable insight into the practical benefit of incorporating background knowledge, showing it can be as effective as acquiring significantly more data.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clear, the technical preliminaries are concise, and the core ideas are introduced with illustrative examples (e.g., the example in Section 3 showing non-decomposability). The structure is logical, guiding the reader from the problem definition to the search framework and then to the main contribution of constraint projection.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Scalability of Constraint Projection:** The MaxSAT formulation for inferring ordering constraints is a core contribution, but MaxSAT itself is NP-hard. The paper does not discuss the computational cost or scalability of this pre-processing step. While likely fast for the tested variable sizes (n ≤ 20), for larger problems, this projection phase could become a bottleneck itself. A brief analysis of the runtime of the MaxSAT solver in the experiments would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Pruning Complexity in EC Trees:** In Section 4.3, the authors state that if simple checks on a CPDAG are inconclusive for constraint satisfaction, they resort to exhaustively enumerating the structures within the equivalence class. This can be exponentially expensive. The paper does not provide any empirical data on how frequently this worst-case scenario is encountered. While the overall search is fast, it's unclear if this specific step could pose a scalability challenge for certain problem structures.

3.  **Generality of the Framework:** The paper focuses exclusively on ancestral constraints. While this is a significant and well-defined contribution, a short discussion on whether the "projection" paradigm could be applied to other types of non-decomposable constraints (e.g., limiting the total number of edges, or constraints on path lengths) would help contextualize the work and highlight the broader potential of the proposed methodology.

**Recommendation: Accept**

This is a high-quality paper that presents a significant advance in the field of Bayesian network structure learning. It addresses the important practical problem of incorporating non-decomposable prior knowledge with a novel, technically deep, and empirically validated solution. The core idea of projecting non-decomposable constraints to empower a decomposable oracle within a larger search framework is elegant and impactful. The strengths in originality, technical quality, and empirical rigor far outweigh the minor weaknesses, which are primarily requests for additional analysis. This work is likely to be influential and built upon by other researchers in the field.
