Summary
The paper tackles optimal Bayesian network (BN) structure learning under non-decomposable background knowledge, specifically ancestral (path) constraints. Building on the BN-graph and EC-tree search spaces (Chen et al., 2015; 2016), the authors (i) show how to prune the EC tree soundly with ancestral constraints (Theorem 1), (ii) “empower” a decomposable-score oracle by projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge constraints (Theorems 2–3) and a maximal set of topological ordering constraints via a MaxSAT formulation (Theorem 4), and (iii) empirically demonstrate large speedups over an ILP baseline (GOBNILP) while retaining exactness. They also explore how the amount of background knowledge affects difficulty and accuracy.

Relation to prior work
- Mainstream optimal BN learning under decomposable scores (DP/A*, order-graph) cannot directly handle ancestral constraints. ILP/CP can encode them but have not been evaluated in this setting. The paper fills this gap.
- The work extends EC-tree/BN-graph ideas to non-decomposable constraints and introduces principled projection to decomposable constraints, which is novel.
- Prior path-constraint work exists for constraint-based learning and soft priors (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos), but not for exact search with non-decomposable constraints; this paper addresses that.

Strengths
- Novel and bold integration: A general recipe to use a decomposable-score oracle for non-decomposable constraints by pruning in EC space plus projection to edge/order constraints. This is a clear conceptual advance.
- The projection step is technically interesting: clean entailed edge rules (negative and positive) and a conservative MaxSAT-based extraction of acyclicity-safe ordering constraints. This is likely reusable beyond ancestral constraints.
- Empirical results are compelling: consistent orders-of-magnitude speedups over a strong ILP baseline, and a clear demonstration that projected constraints greatly tighten the oracle and reduce search.
- Practical relevance: ancestral constraints are a common form of causal/background knowledge; the work shows that modest amounts of such knowledge can substitute for large increases in data.
- Exactness is preserved.

Weaknesses and questions
- CPDAG pruning step (Sec. 4.3): when simple path tests are inconclusive, the method “exhaustively enumerates class(P).” This can be exponential. How often does this trigger and how costly is it in practice? Some statistics would help, or a more scalable sufficient test.
- The projection procedure’s complexity and completeness: edge projection claims are supported by theorems, but proofs and full closure rules for ancestral constraints are omitted “for space.” Given this is a core contribution, a proof sketch and complexity bounds would improve trust.
- MaxSAT encoding: the constraints are sufficient (not necessary) to ensure entailment. How conservative is this in practice? Report the number of ordering constraints inferred, solver runtimes, and overall overhead vs benefits. Also, how sensitive are results to the choice of MaxSAT solver?
- Fairness/coverage of baselines: comparison is only to ILP (GOBNILP). It would be valuable to include CP or ILP variants with additional cutting planes for path constraints, or at least discuss tuning. Also, given unlimited parent-set sizes, ILP is known to struggle; a discussion of this regime choice would be fair.
- Robustness to inconsistent or noisy constraints: the paper assumes a “maximum” closed and consistent set A. How does the method behave if constraints are inconsistent or partially wrong? Any diagnostic or relaxation (e.g., soft constraints) would broaden applicability.
- Clarity issues: minor notational/sign inconsistencies around “minimize score” vs the usual “maximize score,” some formatting glitches and placeholder references. Tightening exposition would help.

Quality
Overall technically sound with reasonable theorems and an exact algorithmic framework. The experimental methodology is solid (multiple benchmarks, varied n/N/p), but could report more ablations on the cost and benefit of each projection component (edges vs orderings) and the frequency/cost of CPDAG enumeration.

Clarity
Generally clear and well organized. Some details are deferred that would be useful for reproducibility (proof sketches, complexity, MaxSAT instance sizes, enumeration stats). Clean up typos, reference metadata, and the score sign convention.

Originality
High. The combination of EC-tree pruning for non-decomposable constraints and the projection to decomposable constraints (especially the MaxSAT approach to safe ordering constraints) is novel and elegant.

Significance
High. This opens a practical path to exact BN learning with realistic causal/path knowledge while delivering substantial efficiency gains. The ideas are likely to be adopted by practitioners working on constrained BN learning and could generalize to other non-decomposable priors/constraints.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear and novel conceptual contribution bridging non-decomposable constraints with decomposable-score oracles.
- Strong empirical gains; practical impact for causal modeling with background knowledge.
- Exactness preserved; theoretically grounded rules for projection.

Cons against acceptance
- Some theoretical and implementation details are omitted; scalability of CPDAG enumeration step not fully characterized.
- Limited baselines; no analysis under inconsistent/noisy constraints.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide statistics on CPDAG enumeration frequency and cost; consider stronger sufficient tests to avoid enumeration.
- Add proof sketches and complexity bounds for Theorems 2–4 and the ancestral-closure rules.
- Report MaxSAT problem sizes, runtime overhead, and the marginal benefit of ordering vs edge projection.
- Discuss behavior under inconsistent/soft constraints and potential extensions.
- Fix presentation issues and clarify the score optimization direction.

Recommendation
Accept (leaning strong). The paper introduces a significant new direction for exact BN learning with non-decomposable constraints and demonstrates substantial empirical benefits. Minor missing details do not detract from the novelty and potential impact.
