This paper addresses the important problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data, subject to background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints. The key challenge is that ancestral constraints (e.g., X is an ancestor of Y) are non-decomposable, rendering them incompatible with the majority of state-of-the-art optimal structure learning algorithms, which rely on the assumption of a decomposable score function.

The authors build upon a recently proposed A*-based search framework (Chen et al., 2015, 2016) that navigates a search space of DAGs (BN graph) or Markov equivalence classes (EC tree). This framework is inherently capable of handling non-decomposable scores and constraints. The search is guided by a heuristic function, or "oracle," which is an optimal solver for a relaxed problem with decomposable scores.

The main contribution of this work is a novel and elegant method for effectively integrating ancestral constraints into this search framework. The core idea is to "project" the non-decomposable ancestral constraints onto a set of decomposable constraints—specifically, edge presence/absence and pairwise ordering constraints. These projected constraints are then passed to the oracle, significantly tightening its heuristic estimate and thereby "empowering" it to prune the search space much more effectively. The paper details sound methods for this projection: a direct inference algorithm for edge constraints and a more sophisticated reduction to MaxSAT for deriving a maximal consistent set of ordering constraints. The authors provide a strong empirical evaluation, comparing their approach to a state-of-the-art Integer Linear Programming (ILP) solver (GOBNILP), demonstrating orders-of-magnitude improvements in runtime.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The paper tackles a problem of high practical relevance. In many domains (e.g., biology, medicine), experts possess causal knowledge that is naturally expressed as ancestral relationships. Providing a method to integrate this knowledge into the *optimal* score-based learning process is a significant step forward. The experiments convincingly show that even a small number of such constraints can drastically improve the structural accuracy of the learned model, rivaling the effect of collecting substantially more data.

2.  **Originality and Technical Depth:** The central idea of projecting non-decomposable constraints to empower a decomposable oracle is highly innovative. While the base search framework is from prior work, this paper presents a non-trivial and powerful extension. The handling of ordering constraints is particularly clever; recognizing the subtlety that naively inferring ordering constraints can improperly prune valid DAGs, and then formulating the problem as a MaxSAT instance to find a globally consistent set, demonstrates a deep understanding of the problem.

3.  **Technical Quality and Rigor:** The proposed methods are well-founded theoretically, with key claims supported by theorems. The experimental evaluation is thorough and compelling. The authors compare against the most appropriate baseline for exact learning with such constraints (ILP). The inclusion of an ablation study (comparing performance with and without the projected constraints) clearly demonstrates the massive performance gain from their primary contribution. The analysis of runtime versus the number of constraints and the scalability study up to 20 variables are well-executed and position the work clearly within the landscape of exact structure learning methods.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It provides a clear motivation, succinctly reviews the necessary background, and presents its novel contributions in a logical and easy-to-follow manner. The use of a small, running example in Section 3 effectively illustrates the core challenge of non-decomposability.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Details of CPDAG Pruning:** The paper states that for a given CPDAG `P`, if simple checks are inconclusive, the algorithm resorts to exhaustively enumerating the DAGs in `class(P)` to check for constraint satisfaction. Since `class(P)` can be exponentially large, this appears to be a major computational bottleneck. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of this issue. For instance, how often did this exhaustive check occur in the experiments? Are there more efficient characterizations or algorithms for checking if *any* DAG in an equivalence class satisfies an ancestral constraint, without explicit enumeration?

2.  **Clarity of MaxSAT Formulation:** The MaxSAT formulation in Section 5.2 is a key contribution, but its presentation is very condensed. Clause (3), the "sufficient condition for acyclicity," is complex and its derivation is not immediately obvious. Providing a brief intuition or a reference for this constraint would improve clarity and reproducibility.

3.  **Inference of Ancestral Closure:** The paper assumes that the initial set of ancestral constraints `A` is already closed under inference rules (i.e., it is a "maximum set"). The process for computing this closure is omitted for space. While likely straightforward (e.g., a variant of transitive closure), explicitly stating or citing the algorithm used would make the work more self-contained.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro-acceptance:**
    *   Addresses a significant and practical problem in graphical model learning.
    *   Presents a novel, elegant, and technically deep solution (constraint projection).
    *   Demonstrates state-of-the-art performance with orders-of-magnitude speedup over a strong baseline.
    *   The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and clearly presented.

*   **Con-acceptance:**
    *   The work is an extension of a previously published search framework.
    *   Some key technical details (CPDAG checking, MaxSAT formulation) could be explained more thoroughly.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It makes a substantial and original contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. The proposed technique is powerful, the results are impressive, and the work is likely to be highly influential for researchers and practitioners who wish to incorporate rich background knowledge into their learning pipelines. This is a high-quality submission, and I recommend it for acceptance.
