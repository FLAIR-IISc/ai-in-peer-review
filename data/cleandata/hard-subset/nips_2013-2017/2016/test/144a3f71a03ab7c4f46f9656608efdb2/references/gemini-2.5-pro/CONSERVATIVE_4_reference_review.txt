**Summary**

This paper presents an exact algorithm for learning the optimal structure of a Bayesian network from data, subject to a set of ancestral constraints (i.e., specifying that a variable X must be, or must not be, an ancestor of another variable Y). The core challenge is that such constraints are non-decomposable, rendering them incompatible with many efficient, state-of-the-art optimal structure learning algorithms that rely on decomposable scores (e.g., dynamic programming over orderings).

The proposed approach adapts the recently introduced A* search framework over the space of equivalence classes (the "EC tree") [Chen et al., 2016]. This framework is suitable for non-decomposable scores and uses an "oracle"—an exact solver for decomposable problems—to compute an admissible heuristic. The authors' main contributions are twofold: 1) They show how to prune the EC tree search space directly using the ancestral constraints. 2) More significantly, they propose methods to "project" the non-decomposable ancestral constraints into a set of decomposable edge and ordering constraints. These projected constraints are then fed to the oracle, resulting in a much tighter heuristic and a dramatically more efficient A* search. For inferring ordering constraints, the authors formulate the problem as a MaxSAT instance. The paper provides an extensive empirical evaluation, comparing their method against a state-of-the-art integer linear programming (ILP) approach, demonstrating orders-of-magnitude improvements in runtime.

**Strengths**

1.  **Methodological Soundness and Rigor:** The proposed method is exact, guaranteeing the discovery of the globally optimal network structure that satisfies the given constraints. This is a significant advantage over heuristic or local search methods. The theoretical underpinnings, particularly the projection of constraints (Theorems 2, 3, and 4), appear sound and provide a solid foundation for the algorithm.

2.  **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The paper presents a thorough and convincing experimental evaluation. The choice of baseline, a state-of-the-art ILP solver (GOBNILP), is highly appropriate as it is one of the few established exact methods capable of handling arbitrary linear constraints. The experiments systematically vary key parameters (number of variables, dataset size, number of constraints) and use standard benchmark networks. The results clearly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed approach over the ILP baseline, often by several orders of magnitude.

3.  **Significant Contribution in Constraint Handling:** The key technical contribution—projecting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones to empower the heuristic oracle—is both clever and demonstrably effective. Tables 1 and 2 clearly show that this projection step is responsible for the bulk of the performance gain. This technique could potentially be adapted for other types of non-decomposable constraints beyond ancestral relations.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Incremental Originality of the Search Framework:** The core search algorithm (A* on the EC tree) is not novel and directly builds on previous work by the authors [Chen et al., 2015, 2016]. The main contribution is the application of this framework to a new type of constraint and, crucially, the development of a specific acceleration technique (projection) for it. While this is a valuable contribution, the paper does not introduce a fundamentally new search paradigm.

2.  **Limited Scope of Baselines:** The evaluation is restricted to a comparison against another exact method (ILP). While this is a fair comparison for an exact algorithm, the paper does not provide context by comparing against or discussing approximate methods. For instance, the work of Borboudakis and Tsamardinos [2013], which uses greedy local search with soft ancestral constraints, is cited but not evaluated against. For problems where the proposed exact method times out (e.g., n > 20), practitioners would likely resort to heuristic methods. An understanding of the trade-off between the optimality of this method and the speed/scalability of approximate ones would have strengthened the paper.

3.  **Scalability and Preprocessing Complexity:** While the method scales better than the ILP baseline, its applicability is still limited to relatively small networks (up to 20 variables in the experiments). This is a common limitation of exact score-based methods. Furthermore, the preprocessing step of inferring ordering constraints via a reduction to MaxSAT introduces its own computational complexity. The paper does not report or analyze the time taken by the MaxSAT solver, which could become a bottleneck for problems with many variables and constraints. This is an important detail for assessing the overall practical performance of the system.

**Arguments for Acceptance**
*   The paper tackles a well-motivated and practical problem: incorporating expert background knowledge in the form of ancestral relations into Bayesian network structure learning.
*   The proposed method is exact and is shown to be significantly more efficient than a strong, established ILP-based baseline.
*   The technical idea of projecting non-decomposable constraints to improve the heuristic of an A* search is elegant and highly effective.
*   The experimental validation is rigorous and provides strong evidence for the claims made.

**Arguments against Acceptance**
*   The work is an incremental, though significant, improvement upon a recently proposed search framework rather than a completely new approach.
*   The evaluation lacks a comparison with approximate/heuristic methods, limiting the assessment of its practical utility in scenarios where exact inference is infeasible.
*   The complexity and performance of the MaxSAT-based preprocessing step are not fully analyzed.

**Conclusion**

This is a solid, well-executed paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of exact Bayesian network structure learning. The core idea of empowering a decomposable oracle with projected constraints is compelling and well-supported by strong empirical results. While the underlying search framework is not entirely novel and scalability remains a concern, the demonstrated performance improvements over a state-of-the-art ILP solver are substantial. The work is technically sound and advances the state of the art in learning optimal networks with complex background knowledge. I recommend acceptance.
