### NIPS Review

**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network (BN) structures from data, subject to background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints (e.g., X is an ancestor of Y, or X is not an ancestor of Y). The key challenge is that ancestral constraints are non-decomposable, which prevents their direct integration into most efficient, score-based learning algorithms that rely on the decomposability of the scoring function.

The authors build upon a recent framework for optimal BN learning with non-decomposable scores [Chen et al., 2015, 2016], which performs an A* search over a space of equivalence classes (the "EC tree"). The main contributions of this paper are:
1.  A method to correctly prune the EC tree search space based on the satisfaction of ancestral constraints.
2.  A novel and powerful technique to "project" the non-decomposable ancestral constraints into a set of decomposable constraints (i.e., required/forbidden edges and topological ordering constraints).
3.  This projection is used to "empower" the oracle that computes the A* heuristic, leading to a much tighter heuristic and consequently, a dramatic speedup in search. For inferring ordering constraints, the authors propose an elegant reduction to MaxSAT.

The paper provides a strong empirical evaluation, comparing their A*-based approach with an Integer Linear Programming (ILP) formulation on several benchmark networks. The results show that their proposed method is orders of magnitude faster than the ILP baseline and that the projection of constraints is critical to its performance.

**Quality**

The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed approach is sound, and the connection between the non-decomposable search space and the decomposable oracle is well-motivated. The core idea of projecting constraints to improve the heuristic is both clever and effective.

The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing.
-   **Baseline:** The choice of an ILP-based system (GOBNILP) is appropriate, as ILP is a standard and powerful framework for exact optimization with arbitrary constraints.
-   **Methodology:** The experiments systematically vary the number of variables (n), dataset size (N), and the percentage of constraints (p), providing a clear picture of the algorithm's performance under different conditions. The use of standard BN benchmarks is also good practice.
-   **Results:** The results are compelling. The consistent orders-of-magnitude speedup over the ILP baseline (Table 1) strongly supports the paper's claims. Furthermore, the ablation study (comparing Table 1 and Table 2) clearly demonstrates the crucial impact of the proposed constraint projection technique. The paper is also honest about the results, showing the "easy-hard-easy" pattern in Table 3, which is a known phenomenon in constrained search.

The theoretical claims (Theorems 1-4) appear correct and provide a formal basis for the proposed algorithms, although proofs are omitted for space. This is a minor weakness but understandable for a conference paper.

**Clarity**

The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. It starts by introducing the necessary background on score-based learning and decomposability, clearly motivating why ancestral constraints are a challenge for standard methods. The proposed solution is built up logically, first explaining the EC tree search space and then detailing how to incorporate constraints through pruning and, more importantly, through projection. The use of a small, 3-variable example effectively illustrates the non-decomposable nature of the problem.

A minor point for improvement would be to provide slightly more intuition for the MaxSAT formulation, particularly for the third hard constraint, which is presented very formally. Additionally, statements like "details omitted for space" regarding the inference of a maximum set of ancestral constraints slightly detract from full reproducibility, though this could be easily rectified with a supplementary appendix.

**Originality**

The work is highly original. While it builds on the existing EC tree framework of Chen et al., the extension to handle ancestral constraints is non-trivial and introduces significant new ideas. The central contribution—projecting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones to tighten the heuristic of an A* search—is a novel and potentially generalizable concept for other non-decomposable search problems. To my knowledge, this is the first work to effectively use this "oracle empowerment" strategy for ancestral constraints in exact BN structure learning. The formulation of ordering constraint inference as a MaxSAT problem is also a novel and principled approach to a complex sub-problem. The paper properly situates its work relative to prior art in ILP, CP, and constraint-based methods.

**Significance**

The results presented are significant and impactful for several reasons:
1.  **Practical Relevance:** It addresses a practical need. In many domains (e.g., computational biology, economics), experts possess causal background knowledge that can be naturally expressed as ancestral relations. This work provides an efficient, exact method to incorporate this knowledge, improving both the speed of learning and the quality of the learned models.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Advancement:** The method dramatically advances the state of the art for *exact* BN learning with ancestral constraints, demonstrating a massive performance gain over the standard ILP approach. It pushes the boundary of what is computationally feasible, scaling to 20 variables, which is competitive for exact score-based methods.
3.  **Illustrates the Value of Knowledge:** The paper provides compelling evidence (Table 3, structural Hamming distance) that a small amount of background knowledge can be as valuable as collecting significantly more data. This is an important message for practitioners, highlighting the synergy between data and domain expertise.

The core ideas, particularly the projection of constraints, could inspire similar approaches in other constrained optimization problems within machine learning.

**Arguments for Acceptance (Pros):**
*   Addresses an important and practical problem in graphical model learning.
*   Presents a novel and clever method (constraint projection) that dramatically improves search efficiency.
*   The empirical evaluation is rigorous, with a strong baseline and clear, compelling results showing orders-of-magnitude speedups.
*   The work significantly advances the state-of-the-art for exact BN learning with this class of constraints.
*   The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow.

**Arguments against Acceptance (Cons):**
*   The proofs for the theorems are omitted, which slightly hinders full verification (though they are plausible). An appendix would solve this.
*   The evaluation is limited to one class of competitor (ILP). A comparison to heuristic search methods that can incorporate such constraints would have been interesting, though perhaps out of scope for a paper focused on optimality.

**Conclusion**

Overall, this is an excellent paper that presents a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. The proposed method is novel, the technical quality is high, and the empirical results are outstanding. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses. This work is likely to be of high interest to the NIPS community and will be built upon by future research. I strongly recommend acceptance.
