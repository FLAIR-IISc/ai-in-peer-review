Summary
The paper addresses exact Bayesian network (BN) structure learning under non-decomposable ancestral (path) constraints. The key idea is to leverage the EC tree search space (Chen et al., 2016), which compactly searches over Markov equivalence classes (CPDAGs), and to prune it using ancestral constraints. Because ancestral constraints are not decomposable, mainstream order-graph/dynamic programming (DP) methods cannot enforce them directly. The paper (i) provides pruning rules for CPDAG nodes in the EC tree, (ii) proposes “projection” methods that infer decomposable constraints—edge presence/absence and topological order constraints—from ancestral constraints to empower a decomposable-score oracle (URLearning), and (iii) evaluates the approach against ILP (GOBNILP) on synthetic experiments derived from standard BN benchmarks. Results show large runtime gains and improved accuracy with more constraints.

Relation to prior work
- Order-graph/DP/A* exact structure learning assumes decomposable scores and typically supports only edge or ordering constraints (Koivisto & Sood 2004; Silander & Myllymäki 2006; Yuan & Malone 2013).
- ILP/CP can encode non-decomposable constraints, including path constraints (Jaakkola et al., 2010; Cussens 2008; Bartlett & Cussens 2015; van Beek & Hoffmann 2015), but have not been systematically evaluated for ancestral constraints.
- Path constraints have been used in constraint-based learning and as priors for local search (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos 2012, 2013).
- The current work builds on the BN/EC graph framework for non-decomposable scores (Chen et al. 2015, 2016), extending it to hard ancestral constraints and adding projection techniques to pass decomposable constraints to the oracle.

Technical quality
- Pruning in the EC tree: The paper defines when a CPDAG P “satisfies” ancestral constraints A (existentially: some DAG in class(P) satisfies A) and proposes pruning tests:
  - Negative constraint X 6 Y: If P contains a directed path from X to Y, then all DAGs in class(P) have that path, so P violates A and is pruned.
  - Positive constraint X Y: If P has no partially directed path from X to Y, then no DAG in class(P) can realize X→…→Y, so P is pruned.
  - Otherwise, the paper enumerates DAGs in class(P) to decide satisfiability.
  These rules are sound. The fallback enumeration may be exponential in the worst case; the paper should quantify how often it is needed and its practical cost.

- Theorem 1: The stated equivalence “P satisfies A iff its descendants satisfy A” appears misstated. The monotonic property used for pruning is: if P violates A, then all descendants violate A (adding a leaf cannot repair a violated ancestral constraint). The converse does not generally hold (a descendant may violate A even if P does not). Please clarify/correct this statement.

- Projection to edge constraints: The closure over A (transitive closure and simple inconsistency propagation) is referenced but not specified; details and complexity should be included or moved to appendices. Theorem 2 (negative edge entails if some Xa 6 Yb with Xa∈α(X) and Yb∈β(Y)) is intuitive and seems correct. Theorem 3 gives necessary and sufficient conditions for inferring a positive edge X→Y when X Y is forced and all indirect paths via any Z outside α(X)∪β(Y) are ruled out by A. The condition is plausible; a proof sketch or examples of edge cases would help build confidence.

- Projection to ordering constraints: The MaxSAT encoding aims to extract a maximal set of ordering constraints entailed by A without excluding any DAG in G(A). The hard clauses (transitivity, necessary condition from Y 6 X ⇒ X < Y, and a sufficient condition for acyclicity) are presented as sufficient. Theorem 4 formalizes a sufficient condition for entailment of an order set O. This is reasonable, but conservative; a discussion of tightness/completeness, and the trade-off between maximality and safety, would be useful. Empirically, please report the overhead of MaxSAT and its impact on total runtime.

- Overall correctness: The pruning and projection steps are presented as sound; apart from the likely misstatement of Theorem 1, the methodology appears technically solid. However, several “details omitted for space” (closure algorithm, proofs, enumeration complexity) are central to reproducibility.

Empirical evaluation
- Setup: Benchmarks derived from ALARM/ANDES/CHILD/CPCS/HEPAR2; constraints sampled from ground-truth ancestral relations; BDeu with ESS=1; no parent set bound; URLearning as oracle; EVASOLVER for MaxSAT. Comparisons to GOBNILP with and without projected constraints.

- Results: The proposed approach often yields orders-of-magnitude speedups over ILP, with larger gaps as n increases. Projected constraints significantly help both methods, especially the EC-tree approach. An easy–hard–easy pattern with respect to the proportion of constraints p is plausible. SHD decreases with more data and more constraints.

- Missing analyses: 
  - Time breakdown (projection vs search), especially MaxSAT overhead.
  - Ablations: edge-only vs order-only projections; pruning alone vs projection alone.
  - Robustness to noisy/mistaken constraints (very relevant for “background knowledge”).
  - Comparisons with CP solvers or ILP with parent-set bounds (common in practice).
  - Larger n with bounded in-degree to situate scalability among exact methods.

Clarity
The core ideas are clearly motivated. However, the manuscript contains multiple formatting issues, broken references, and a few imprecise statements (notably Theorem 1). Some key algorithmic details are omitted. Figures are referenced but not shown. Improving exposition, providing proofs/algorithms in an appendix, and fixing citations would markedly improve readability and reproducibility.

Originality
- Novel integration of ancestral constraints into exact BN learning via EC-tree pruning.
- New projection rules to derive decomposable edge and order constraints from ancestral constraints and use them to strengthen a decomposable oracle.
- To my knowledge, this combination and empirical evaluation against ILP under path constraints is new.

Significance
The paper addresses a practically important setting—injecting causal/ancestral knowledge—and demonstrates substantial runtime gains while maintaining exactness. The projection technique is broadly useful for hybrid exact search. Impact is limited by scale (n≈20 without parent bounds), but this is consistent with exact BN learning literature.

Pros
- Sound and practically effective pruning of EC tree under ancestral constraints.
- Clever projection of non-decomposable constraints to empower decomposable oracles.
- Strong empirical speedups over ILP and clear qualitative insights (easy–hard–easy).
- Topic of broad interest to causal/graphical model learning.

Cons
- Some theoretical statements are unclear/misstated; key details omitted.
- Limited evaluation scope (no noisy constraints; no CP baselines; modest n; no parent bounds).
- Unquantified overhead of MaxSAT/projection steps.
- Clarity and reference issues.

Questions/suggestions
- Clarify/correct Theorem 1 and provide proofs or sketches for Theorems 2–4.
- Describe the closure algorithm for ancestral constraints and its complexity.
- Report how often CPDAG enumeration is needed, its cost, and any heuristics to avoid it.
- Provide runtime breakdown and ablations for projection components.
- Evaluate robustness to a fraction of incorrect constraints and consider soft constraints.
- Include results with bounded in-degree and compare to ILP/CP in that regime.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper provides a meaningful technical and empirical advance in exact BN learning with ancestral constraints. With improved clarity, added proofs/details, and a few additional experiments (especially on projection overhead and robustness), this work would make a valuable contribution. Confidence: high.
