Summary
The paper tackles exact Bayesian network structure learning under hard ancestral (path) constraints, which are non-decomposable and thus poorly supported by mainstream exact methods that rely on decomposable scores and constraints. Building on the BN-graph/EC-tree shortest-path perspective (Chen et al. 2015, 2016), the authors (i) prune the EC-tree by removing CPDAG nodes that cannot be extended to any DAG satisfying the ancestral constraints, and (ii) “project” the non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and topological-ordering constraints to empower a decomposable-score oracle (URLearning/A* over the order graph). They give sufficient tests for pruning CPDAG nodes and, when inconclusive, enumerate members of the equivalence class. They present rules for inferring all entailed negative edges (Theorem 2) and some entailed positive edges (Theorem 3) from a closure of the ancestral constraints, and reduce inference of a maximally large consistent set of ordering constraints to MaxSAT with hard transitivity/acyclicity clauses (Theorem 4 gives sufficient conditions). Experiments on subgraphs of standard networks with simulated ancestral constraints show large (often orders-of-magnitude) runtime gains over GOBNILP (ILP), with and without projected constraints, and demonstrate an easy–hard–easy trend as constraint density varies. They also show that adding a modest fraction of ancestral constraints can substantially improve structural accuracy.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The pruning strategy is natural, and the monotonicity intuition (adding leaves cannot fix a violated path constraint among existing variables) supports pruning descendants. However, Theorem 1 is stated in a way that is easy to misread: as written, “P satisfies A iff its descendants satisfy A” reads stronger than needed for pruning (which only requires that violation is monotone). Please clarify scope (subset vs full variable set) and provide a proof sketch.
- Projection to edge constraints is precise: Theorem 2 gives a complete characterization for entailed negative edges via α/β closures; Theorem 3 gives a sufficient-and-necessary condition for entailed positive edges relative to the closed ancestral set—this is useful, and the algorithmic implications (check all Z outside α(X)∪β(Y)) are clear.
- Ordering projection: The MaxSAT encoding uses only sufficient conditions for entailment and acyclicity (Theorem 4), so the inferred set may be conservative (not maximally entailed in the logical sense). This is fine if acknowledged and if the trade-off (safety vs coverage) is quantified empirically; please clarify whether your MaxSAT objective finds a maximum subject to your sufficient constraints (hence not necessarily the logically maximal entailment set), and report how much is missed in practice.
- Practicality: The fallback to enumerating all DAGs in a CPDAG class when simple tests are inconclusive could be exponential. How often does this happen and what is the cost distribution in practice? A small ablation measuring time spent in enumeration vs total would strengthen the case.
- Experimental methodology is generally fair: same family scores; projected constraints also given to GOBNILP. However, only ILP is compared; a CP baseline (e.g., van Beek & Hoffmann 2015) would help. Also, exact structure learning with BDeu is commonly evaluated on 20–30 variables; n up to 20 is respectable but modest. It would help to show wall-clock breakdown (oracle calls, MaxSAT time) and memory.

Clarity
- The paper is mostly clear, but there are distracting formatting/citation glitches and some ambiguous statements (Theorem 1; “both over the same set of variables” while EC nodes are over subsets; footnote 3). Clearly specify how constraints over the full variable set are evaluated at partial CPDAGs, and the correctness argument for pruning.
- Please provide the closure rules used to make A “maximum,” at least in supplemental material, and a complexity bound for the edge-projection procedure.
- Include more detail on the MaxSAT encoding (variable set size, clause counts, weights) and its runtime impact.

Originality
- Integrating non-decomposable ancestral constraints into EC-tree search with admissible heuristics from decomposable-score oracles is a novel and well-motivated combination. The projection of ancestral constraints into safe decomposable edge/order constraints is a useful contribution beyond prior ILP/CP formulations and beyond path-belief priors used with heuristic search. The work extends Chen et al. 2015/2016 from non-decomposable priors to hard non-decomposable constraints with new inference machinery.

Significance
- Hard ancestral constraints arise in causal discovery and knowledge-informed modeling; supporting them in exact structure learning is valuable. The substantial speedups over ILP and the demonstrated accuracy gains from modest amounts of prior knowledge indicate practical impact. The main limitation is scalability (n ≈ 20) and reliance on exponential fallback enumeration in some cases, which may limit adoption on larger problems; nonetheless, this advances the state of the art for exact learning with such constraints.

Pros
- Sound pruning framework for non-decomposable ancestral constraints in EC trees.
- Effective projection to decomposable constraints; clear theorems and algorithms for edge constraints; principled MaxSAT reduction for order constraints.
- Strong empirical speedups vs a leading ILP system; informative analysis of constraint density effects; improved accuracy with modest prior knowledge.

Cons
- Clarity issues (Theorem 1, evaluation of constraints at partial CPDAGs), formatting glitches.
- Ordering inference is conservative; no analysis of gap to the logically maximal entailed set.
- Potentially expensive enumeration of class(P) when quick tests fail; no quantitative analysis.
- Limited baselines and scale; no CP comparison; n up to 20.

Questions for authors
1) How frequently do you enumerate class(P), and what fraction of runtime does this consume?
2) What is the size of the MaxSAT instances, and how much time do they add?
3) Is your inferred ordering set maximal only under your sufficient constraints, or logically maximal? Any empirical gap analysis?
4) How sensitive are results to noisy/mistaken ancestral constraints?
5) Can your projection rules be extended to other non-decomposable constraints?

Recommendation: Weak accept. The paper addresses an important gap with a principled and empirically strong method. Clarifying the theoretical statements, detailing the expensive steps, and tightening the presentation would strengthen it further.
