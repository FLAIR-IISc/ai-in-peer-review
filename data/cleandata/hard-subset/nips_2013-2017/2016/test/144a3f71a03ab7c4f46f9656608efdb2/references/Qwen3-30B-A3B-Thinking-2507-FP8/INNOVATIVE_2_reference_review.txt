

# Review

This paper addresses an important limitation in Bayesian network structure learning: the inability to incorporate non-decomposable ancestral constraints (which specify cause-and-effect relationships) into standard learning frameworks. The authors build upon Chen et al.'s EC tree framework for learning with non-decomposable scores to develop a novel approach for handling ancestral constraints. Their key insight is that while ancestral constraints are inherently non-decomposable, they can be projected into decomposable edge and ordering constraints that can be used to "empower" a standard oracle (a learning algorithm for decomposable scores).

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it provides a sound and complete method for pruning the EC tree based on ancestral constraints. Second, it introduces an elegant technique for projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints that can be passed to the oracle. Third, it demonstrates through extensive experiments that this approach yields orders-of-magnitude improvements over ILP-based methods for structure learning with ancestral constraints.

The theoretical foundation is solid. The authors prove that their pruning method is sound (Theorem 1) and provide clear algorithms for inferring edge and ordering constraints from ancestral constraints (Theorems 2-4). The constraint projection approach is particularly clever, as it allows the authors to leverage existing decomposable scoring algorithms while still handling non-decomposable constraints.

The empirical evaluation is thorough and convincing. The authors test their approach against GOBNILP (an ILP-based method) across multiple benchmark networks, varying problem size, dataset size, and constraint density. Their results consistently show dramatic speedups (orders of magnitude) for the EC tree approach when using projected constraints. The experiments also reveal that a small number of ancestral constraints (10-25%) can have as much impact on model quality as significantly increasing the training data size, highlighting the practical value of incorporating background knowledge.

This work represents a meaningful advancement in Bayesian network structure learning. While the EC tree framework itself was introduced by Chen et al. (2015, 2016), the application to ancestral constraints is novel and addresses a significant practical limitation. The constraint projection technique is particularly valuable, as it enables the use of existing oracles without requiring fundamental changes to their implementation.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The problem statement is compelling, the technical contributions are well-explained, and the experimental results are presented effectively. The authors appropriately acknowledge the limitations of their approach (e.g., the need for background knowledge about ancestral constraints) and provide a thoughtful discussion of related work.

Compared to the NIPS community's recent work on Bayesian networks (e.g., [Yuan and Malone, 2013, Malone et al., 2014]), this paper makes a clear and valuable contribution by addressing a previously unaddressed practical limitation. While the EC tree framework itself is not new, its application to ancestral constraints represents a significant step forward in making Bayesian network learning more practical for real-world applications where background knowledge about causal relationships is available.

The paper is of high quality, with sound methodology, clear presentation, and significant empirical results. The authors have demonstrated that their approach is not only theoretically sound but also practically superior to existing methods. The results are well-supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical evaluation.

This work advances the state of the art in Bayesian network structure learning by enabling the incorporation of important background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints. The dramatic speedups demonstrated in the experiments make this approach viable for larger-scale problems than previously possible. The constraint projection technique is particularly elegant and could potentially be applied to other types of non-decomposable constraints as well.

I believe this paper makes a significant contribution to the field and would be of great interest to the NIPS community. The novelty of applying the EC tree framework to ancestral constraints, combined with the practical impact demonstrated through empirical results, strongly supports acceptance. The paper's focus on a practically important problem with a theoretically sound and empirically validated solution aligns well with NIPS' emphasis on impactful machine learning research.

The main limitation is that the approach requires background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints, which may not always be available. However, the paper clearly acknowledges this limitation and demonstrates that even a small number of such constraints can have substantial impact, making it a valuable tool for practitioners who have access to such knowledge.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to Bayesian network learning. The novelty of the constraint projection technique, combined with the compelling empirical results, makes this a worthy addition to the NIPS proceedings.
