This paper addresses the problem of learning Bayesian networks under ancestral constraints, a practically relevant scenario where prior knowledge about causal relationships exists. The authors leverage recent work on optimal Bayesian network structure learning with non-decomposable scores [Chen et al., 2015, 2016] and propose a method to incorporate these constraints. They also present a method for projecting the ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints to improve efficiency. The paper is well-motivated, building on a solid foundation of prior work, and identifies a genuine challenge in the field.

However, while the ideas are promising, the presentation and empirical validation leave much to be desired. The paper suffers from a lack of clarity in several sections, particularly concerning the details of the constraint projection and its impact on the oracle. The theoretical contributions, such as Theorems 1-4, feel somewhat disconnected from the practical impact demonstrated in the experiments, and their utility isn’t fully elucidated. While the theorems are stated formally, a more intuitive explanation of *why* they hold and *how* they translate to performance gains would be beneficial. The reliance on existing tools (EVASOLVER, URLEARNING, GOBNILP) without significant modification is acceptable, but the performance gains are presented without sufficient context. How sensitive are the results to the choice of the underlying oracle? What are the limitations of using this oracle?

The empirical evaluation, while attempting a comprehensive comparison with ILP, is difficult to interpret. The description of the experimental setup, particularly the generation of datasets and ancestral constraints, is not detailed enough for reproducibility. The tables present significant speedups, but the absolute runtimes remain unclear (e.g., "orders of magnitude" needs quantification). The effect of dataset size and the proportion of constraints on performance is interesting, but the 'easy-hard-easy' trend requires more in-depth analysis. Why does the ILP approach become easier with more constraints? The authors mention a 2-hour time limit and 32GB memory limit – were these frequently hit, and if so, how does that impact the reported results? The structural Hamming distance is reported, but its relation to the quality of the learned network is not adequately discussed.  Simply stating that "a relatively small number of ancestral constraints… can have a similar impact… as increasing the amount of data" is not convincing without a more nuanced analysis.

**Strengths:**

*   Addresses a relevant and challenging problem: learning Bayesian networks with non-decomposable constraints.
*   Builds on recent advances in optimal Bayesian network structure learning [Chen et al., 2015, 2016].
*   Proposes a method for constraint projection to improve efficiency.
*   Demonstrates empirically that the proposed approach is significantly faster than an ILP-based system, though the significance is hard to assess.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Lack of clarity in several sections, particularly regarding constraint projection and oracle interaction.
*   Theoretical contributions (Theorems 1-4) are not fully connected to the practical impact.
*   Empirical evaluation lacks sufficient detail for reproducibility and nuanced analysis.
*   Performance gains are presented without sufficient context (absolute runtimes, oracle sensitivity).
*   Limited discussion of the limitations of the proposed approach.



**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:** The problem is important, and the authors provide a technically sound approach that demonstrably outperforms a standard method (ILP). The connection to existing work is clearly articulated.

**Con:** The lack of clarity, limited empirical detail, and insufficient analysis of the results raise concerns about the robustness and generalizability of the findings. The paper feels somewhat incomplete, and the impact, while potentially significant, is not convincingly demonstrated.

**Overall Assessment:**

I am leaning towards **reject** with a strong encouragement to resubmit after significant revisions. The paper has potential, but the current version lacks the clarity, detail, and rigorous analysis necessary for acceptance at NIPS. The authors need to address the weaknesses outlined above, provide a more thorough empirical evaluation, and clearly articulate the significance of their contributions. A more intuitive explanation of the theoretical results and their practical implications would also be beneficial. Specifically, a clearer depiction of how and why the projected constraints impact oracle performance is crucial. If these revisions are made, the paper could become a valuable contribution to the field.
