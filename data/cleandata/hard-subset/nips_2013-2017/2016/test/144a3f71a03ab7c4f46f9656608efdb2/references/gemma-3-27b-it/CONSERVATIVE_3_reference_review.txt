This paper addresses the problem of learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints, a practical scenario where prior knowledge about causal relationships exists. The core idea is to leverage recent advancements in optimal structure learning [Chen et al., 2015, 2016] which operate on non-decomposable scores, and adapt them to incorporate these constraints. The authors propose pruning the search space (EC trees) based on ancestral constraints and, crucially, projecting these constraints into decomposable forms to empower an oracle used within the learning process.

The paper is generally well-written and the problem statement is clearly articulated. The connection to prior work in optimal structure learning, particularly the work of Chen et al., is well-established. The technical details regarding the adaptation of EC trees and the projection of constraints are presented adequately, although the MaxSAT reduction for ordering constraints is described somewhat briefly and would benefit from more detail. 

However, my primary concern centers around the lack of extensive empirical validation *relative* to the claims being made. While the authors demonstrate improvements over an ILP-based approach, the experiments feel somewhat limited in scope. The networks used (ALARM, ANDES, etc.) are standard benchmarks, but the simulation setup – random sub-networks and randomly generated constraints – feels artificial. The focus on comparing against a single ILP implementation is also insufficient. A more thorough evaluation would involve comparing against established constraint-based algorithms (e.g., those incorporating path constraints [Borboudakis & Tsamardinos, 2012]) and hybrid approaches, particularly in settings where the ancestral constraints are noisy or incomplete. The effect of varying the quality (accuracy) of the ancestral constraints is not explored.

The claim of “orders-of-magnitude improvements” is somewhat overstated. While the reported speedups are significant, they are often presented without sufficient context regarding the scale of the problems being solved. Furthermore, the scalability experiments, while demonstrating the approach's capability to handle up to 20 variables, do not provide a comprehensive picture of its computational complexity as the number of variables and constraints increase. The discussion of structural Hamming distance is also limited; a more in-depth analysis of the *types* of errors made would be valuable.

The projection of constraints is a novel contribution, but the experiments don’t fully demonstrate its impact outside of the comparison to ILP. While beneficial, the authors don’t systematically investigate the tradeoffs between the cost of performing the projection and the resulting speedup in the search process. Is the projection always worthwhile? Under what conditions does it provide the greatest benefit?

**Strengths:**

*   Addresses a practically relevant problem – learning Bayesian networks with prior knowledge.
*   Leverages recent advances in optimal structure learning with non-decomposable scores.
*   Proposes a sound and complete method for incorporating ancestral constraints into EC trees.
*   Demonstrates improved performance compared to an ILP-based approach.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Limited scope of empirical validation; simulation setup feels artificial.
*   Insufficient comparison to established constraint-based and hybrid algorithms.
*   Overstated claim of “orders-of-magnitude improvements.”
*   Limited analysis of the impact and tradeoffs of constraint projection.
*   Brief description of the MaxSAT reduction.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The work addresses a challenging and important problem in Bayesian network structure learning.
*   The proposed approach is technically sound and represents a novel contribution.
*   The empirical results, while limited, demonstrate the potential of the approach.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The empirical validation is not sufficiently comprehensive to support the strong claims being made.
*   The lack of comparison to a wider range of existing algorithms raises concerns about the true significance of the results.
*   The limited analysis of the constraint projection technique leaves important questions unanswered.



**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards rejection. While the paper presents a technically sound and potentially valuable contribution, the lack of extensive empirical validation and the somewhat overstated claims weaken its impact. The paper feels more like a promising proof-of-concept than a fully mature work. I would encourage the authors to significantly expand their experimental evaluation, comparing against a wider range of algorithms and investigating the robustness and scalability of their approach in more realistic settings. A more thorough analysis of the constraint projection technique is also needed. The novelty and technical soundness are there, but the empirical support isn’t yet sufficient for a top-tier conference like NIPS.
