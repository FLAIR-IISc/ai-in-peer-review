This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data when subject to ancestral constraints. The authors correctly note that such constraints are non-decomposable and therefore cannot be handled by the majority of mainstream exact structure learning algorithms, which rely on dynamic programming over orderings and require decomposable scores.

The proposed solution is to adapt the recently introduced A* search framework over the space of equivalence classes (the "EC tree" from Chen et al., 2016). This framework is designed to accommodate non-decomposable scores/constraints. The core technical contribution of the present work is a method to "empower the oracle" used as the heuristic function in this A* search. Specifically, the authors propose techniques to infer, or "project," a set of decomposable edge and ordering constraints from the given non-decomposable ancestral constraints. For ordering constraints, this inference is non-trivial and is cleverly formulated as a MaxSAT problem. These inferred decomposable constraints are then passed to the oracle (an exact solver for problems with decomposable scores), which produces a tighter heuristic and thus accelerates the A* search. The paper provides an empirical comparison against an Integer Linear Programming (ILP) formulation, demonstrating significant speedups.

**Strengths:**

*   **Problem Formulation:** The paper tackles a well-defined and practically relevant problem. Incorporating expert knowledge is crucial for learning reliable networks from limited data, and ancestral relations are an intuitive way to express such knowledge.
*   **Technical Contribution:** The method for projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints is the main strength of the paper. This is a sound and clever way to bridge the gap between a general search framework and the highly efficient solvers that exist for a restricted class of problems. The use of MaxSAT to find a consistent set of ordering constraints is particularly novel.
*   **Empirical Results:** The experiments show a clear and often dramatic performance improvement over a standard ILP-based approach (GOBNILP). The finding that projecting constraints leads to speedups of several orders of magnitude (Table 1 vs. Table 2) strongly supports the paper's central claim. The analysis of how accuracy (structural Hamming distance) improves with more constraints is also a valuable demonstration of the method's utility.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Reliance on a Nascent Framework:** The entire approach is built upon the EC tree search framework [Chen et al., 2016]. While interesting, this framework is very recent and not yet as established or widely vetted as the canonical dynamic programming [Koivisto and Sood, 2004] or A* search on order graphs [Yuan and Malone, 2013] approaches. As such, the contribution feels more like an incremental improvement to a specific, new algorithm rather than a fundamental advance in structure learning. The paper's impact is thus tied to the future adoption of this particular search framework.
*   **Limited Baselines and Scalability:** The primary comparison is against an ILP solver. While ILP is a valid exact method, it is known that for unconstrained or lightly constrained BN learning, search-based methods often scale better with the number of variables. The paper would have been stronger with a comparison to other exact paradigms, such as Constraint Programming (CP), which is mentioned but not evaluated. Furthermore, the experiments are limited to n=20 variables. While this is a non-trivial size for exact learning, state-of-the-art solvers for decomposable scores have been pushed to n=26 and beyond. It is therefore difficult to assess whether this approach truly advances the state of the art in scalability or primarily offers a solution for a specific subclass of problems at a moderate scale.
*   **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written, but some key technical sections are dense. The MaxSAT formulation in Section 5.2, in particular, would benefit from more intuition and perhaps a small, illustrative example of why the simpler inference method can fail and how the MaxSAT encoding avoids this. The dependence on prior work [Chen et al., 2015, 2016] is heavy, and a reader not intimately familiar with BN graphs and EC trees may struggle to fully grasp the search mechanism.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

1.  The paper provides a novel and effective method for handling an important class of non-decomposable constraints in exact Bayesian network structure learning.
2.  The technique of projecting constraints to "empower" a heuristic oracle is elegant and could potentially be applied in other contexts.
3.  The empirical results demonstrate a very significant performance improvement over a standard and established baseline (ILP), validating the practical value of the proposed technique.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

1.  The work is an incremental extension of a very recent and not yet widely adopted search framework (EC trees), which may limit its broader impact.
2.  The experimental validation is somewhat narrow. It relies on a single class of baseline (ILP) and does not push the boundaries of scalability for exact learning.
3.  The practical need for solving for optimal DAGs with specifically *ancestral* constraints, as opposed to the more common edge or pairwise ordering constraints, is not overwhelmingly established.

**Recommendation:**

While I am cautious about methods built on very new foundations, the technical contribution here is sound, and the empirical speedup over a standard method like ILP is compelling. The paper addresses a valid problem with a clever solution. However, the claims about scalability and its position relative to the broader state of the art in exact inference should be tempered. The contribution is a strong algorithmic enhancement for a specific search framework and constraint type, rather than a breakthrough in general structure learning. On balance, the strengths outweigh the weaknesses. I lean towards acceptance but would urge the authors to better contextualize their work with respect to the broader landscape of exact methods and be more circumspect about its scalability.
