

# Peer Review: "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper addresses a significant gap in Bayesian network structure learning: the incorporation of ancestral constraints, which specify direct or indirect cause-and-effect relationships between variables (e.g., X → Y meaning X is an ancestor of Y in the network). The authors note that ancestral constraints are non-decomposable, meaning they cannot be enforced through local constraints on individual variable parents, which limits most mainstream structure learning approaches that assume decomposable scores.

The paper extends the EC tree search space (Chen et al., 2016) to handle ancestral constraints through a sound pruning mechanism. The key innovation is their method for projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints onto decomposable edge and ordering constraints, which they use to empower an oracle (a system that optimizes for decomposable scores). This approach enables the EC tree to efficiently handle ancestral constraints while maintaining optimality guarantees.

The authors empirically demonstrate that their approach is orders-of-magnitude faster than ILP-based approaches (specifically GOBNILP) when handling ancestral constraints, with the performance gap increasing as problem size grows. They also show that even a small number of ancestral constraints (10-25%) can significantly improve model quality, comparable to increasing training data size by a factor of 4-16.

## Strengths

1. **Clear identification of a significant gap**: The paper effectively identifies that most structure learning approaches cannot handle non-decomposable ancestral constraints, which are important in practice for incorporating domain knowledge.

2. **Strong theoretical foundation**: The authors provide a sound and complete method for pruning the EC tree based on ancestral constraints (Theorem 1) and develop a novel method for projecting non-decomposable constraints to decomposable ones (Theorems 2-4). The theoretical development is well-motivated and carefully reasoned.

3. **Comprehensive experimental evaluation**: The paper conducts thorough experiments across multiple benchmark networks (ALARM, ANDES, CHILD, CPCS54, HEPAR2), varying problem sizes (n, N, p), and demonstrates consistent orders-of-magnitude improvements over the ILP baseline (GOBNILP). The analysis of how constraints affect search efficiency (Table 3) is particularly insightful.

4. **Practical significance**: The paper demonstrates that even a small number of ancestral constraints can have a substantial impact on model quality, comparable to collecting significantly more training data. This has important implications for practitioners who can leverage domain knowledge to reduce data requirements.

5. **Technical soundness**: The paper is technically rigorous, with clear definitions, well-structured arguments, and appropriate use of established concepts from Bayesian network learning and constraint satisfaction.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient comparison with related constraint-based approaches**: The paper mentions Borboudakis and Tsamardinos (2012, 2013) as using ancestral constraints but doesn't provide a detailed comparison with their constraint-based approaches. This is particularly surprising since the authors note that "ILP and CP approaches can represent ancestral constraints" but don't discuss how their approach differs from or improves upon these constraint-based methods.

2. **Lack of complexity analysis**: While the paper provides algorithms for constraint projection (Theorems 2-3), it doesn't analyze their time complexity. For instance, the edge constraint inference algorithm's complexity with respect to the number of ancestral constraints would be valuable for understanding scalability.

3. **Incomplete explanation of MaxSAT formulation**: The MaxSAT formulation for ordering constraints (Section 5.2) is presented without sufficient explanation of why this is the right approach or how it's implemented. A brief discussion of the trade-offs between the MaxSAT approach and alternatives would strengthen the paper.

4. **Limited discussion of limitations**: The paper doesn't thoroughly discuss the limitations of their approach. For example:
   - How does the approach scale when the number of ancestral constraints grows very large?
   - What happens when the ancestral constraints are inconsistent or contradictory?
   - How sensitive is the performance to the quality of the ancestral constraints (e.g., if some constraints are incorrect)?

5. **Inadequate explanation of why ILP is slow**: While the paper shows their approach is faster, it doesn't provide a clear explanation of why the ILP approach is slower when handling ancestral constraints. The authors mention that ILP "can in principle enforce non-decomposable constraints" but don't detail the specific computational challenges.

## Quality Assessment

The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The theoretical results are properly justified, and the experimental evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed. The authors are careful to evaluate both strengths (speedups with increasing constraints) and limitations (e.g., when constraints are sparse).

The paper presents a complete piece of work, not just a position paper. It makes a clear contribution to the field by solving a previously unaddressed problem (handling ancestral constraints in optimal structure learning) with a sound theoretical approach and empirical validation.

## Clarity Assessment

The paper is generally well-written and well-organized, with a logical flow from problem statement to solution to evaluation. The technical background is clearly explained, and the use of examples (like the 3-variable example in Section 3) helps clarify concepts.

However, some sections could be improved for clarity:
- Section 4.3 (EC trees and ancestral constraints) is somewhat dense and could benefit from a more intuitive explanation of why the pruning approach works.
- The MaxSAT formulation in Section 5.2 could be better motivated with a brief explanation of why this approach is suitable for the problem.

## Originality Assessment

The paper makes a clear contribution to the field. While Chen et al. (2015, 2016) introduced the EC tree for learning with non-decomposable scores, this paper extends it specifically to handle ancestral constraints - a non-decomposable constraint type that has been largely overlooked in the literature.

The constraint projection technique (Theorems 2-4) is novel, and the authors clearly differentiate their approach from related work. The paper properly references Chen et al. (2015, 2016) as the foundation for their work, and the combination of EC tree with constraint projection for ancestral constraints appears to be genuinely novel.

## Significance Assessment

The paper addresses a problem with significant practical importance: incorporating domain knowledge (in the form of causal relationships) into Bayesian network learning. The results demonstrate that ancestral constraints can substantially reduce the computational burden of structure learning while improving model quality.

The finding that a small number of ancestral constraints (10-25%) can have a similar impact on model quality as increasing training data by a factor of 4-16 is particularly significant for practitioners, as it suggests domain knowledge can be a more cost-effective way to improve models than collecting more data.

The paper advances the state of the art in Bayesian network structure learning by providing the first efficient method for optimal structure learning with ancestral constraints, which is a significant step forward from the current state (where most methods either ignore ancestral constraints or use heuristics that aren't optimal).

## Recommendation

I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning by addressing a practical problem that has been largely overlooked in the literature. The theoretical approach is sound, the experimental evaluation is comprehensive, and the results demonstrate substantial improvements over existing methods.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Expand the comparison with constraint-based approaches like Borboudakis and Tsamardinos (2012, 2013) to better position the work in the literature.

2. Add a brief discussion of the complexity of the constraint projection algorithms (Theorems 2-3) and how they scale with the number of ancestral constraints.

3. Provide a clearer explanation of why the ILP approach is slower (beyond just stating it's slower), perhaps by analyzing the constraint encoding complexity.

4. Include a section discussing the limitations of the approach (e.g., handling inconsistent constraints, scalability with very large constraint sets).

5. Add more detail to the MaxSAT formulation in Section 5.2, explaining why this approach is suitable for the problem.

## Final Thoughts

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. It addresses a practical problem with significant implications for real-world applications, presents a sound theoretical solution, and provides convincing experimental evidence of its effectiveness. The paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings, advancing the state of the art in structure learning with domain knowledge.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it addresses a fundamental problem in probabilistic modeling with both theoretical and practical significance, and it makes a clear contribution to the field.
