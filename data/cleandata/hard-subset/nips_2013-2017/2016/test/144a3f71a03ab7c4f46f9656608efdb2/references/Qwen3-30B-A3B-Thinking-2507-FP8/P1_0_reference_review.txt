

# Peer Review: "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"

## Summary

This paper addresses a significant challenge in Bayesian network structure learning: incorporating non-decomposable background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints. Ancestral constraints specify whether one variable is an ancestor of another (i.e., whether there exists a directed path between them) in the network structure. The paper builds upon the recent work of Chen et al. (2015, 2016) who proposed the BN graph and EC tree search spaces for learning Bayesian networks with non-decomposable scores. The authors extend this framework to handle ancestral constraints, which are non-decomposable and cannot be directly handled by mainstream approaches that rely on decomposable scores.

The paper makes three key contributions: (1) a sound method for pruning the EC tree search space based on ancestral constraints; (2) a technique for projecting ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints that can be used to empower an oracle (a system that optimizes decomposable scores); and (3) an empirical evaluation demonstrating that their approach is orders of magnitude faster than ILP-based approaches for structure learning with ancestral constraints.

The work relates to several important lines of research at NIPS and beyond, including the seminal work on decomposable score-based structure learning (Koivisto and Sood, 2004; Parviainen and Koivisto, 2013), the development of search spaces for non-decomposable scores (Chen et al., 2015, 2016), and constraint-based approaches to Bayesian network learning (Borboudakis and Tsamardinos, 2012, 2013).

## Quality

The paper is technically sound and makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. The authors provide formal proofs of their key theorems (Theorems 1-4) and demonstrate how ancestral constraints can be incorporated into the EC tree search space. The approach to projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints is particularly well-developed, with the authors providing both efficient algorithms for edge constraints and a MaxSAT-based approach for ordering constraints.

The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed. The authors simulate structure learning problems from standard Bayesian network benchmarks (ALARM, ANDES, CHILD, CPCS54, and HEPAR2), varying the number of variables, dataset size, and percentage of ancestral constraints. They compare their approach against the ILP-based GOBNILP system, demonstrating orders-of-magnitude improvements in efficiency. The evaluation also includes an analysis of how ancestral constraints affect learning accuracy, showing that a relatively small number of constraints (10-25%) can have a similar impact on model accuracy as significantly increasing the training dataset size.

The authors are careful to acknowledge the limitations of their approach, particularly the potential computational cost of enumerating structures in Markov equivalence classes when pruning CPDAGs. They also provide a thorough discussion of related work, including the limitations of existing approaches for handling ancestral constraints.

## Clarity

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The technical concepts are introduced gradually and with appropriate examples. The figures (Figure 1) effectively illustrate the BN graph and EC tree search spaces, which are central to the paper's contributions. The paper provides sufficient detail for experts to reproduce their results, including specific implementation details about the EVASOLVER partial MaxSAT solver and the experimental setup.

The authors could improve clarity by providing a more detailed explanation of why ancestral constraints are non-decomposable, beyond the single example given in Section 3. A more comprehensive discussion of the limitations of existing approaches (beyond just the K2 algorithm and order graph) would also strengthen the paper.

## Originality

This work makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. While the EC tree search space was previously proposed by Chen et al. (2016), the authors' extension to handle ancestral constraints is novel. The key insight is that while ancestral constraints themselves are non-decomposable, they can be projected into decomposable edge and ordering constraints that can be efficiently used by existing structure learning algorithms.

The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work. The authors explicitly state that "nearly all mainstream approaches for optimal structure learning make a fundamental assumption, that the scoring function is decomposable," which limits their ability to incorporate ancestral constraints. They also acknowledge that while ILP and CP approaches can theoretically represent ancestral constraints, they have not been evaluated for this specific purpose.

The paper's approach represents a novel combination of ideas: leveraging the EC tree search space, using an oracle for decomposable scores, and projecting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones. This combination is not present in prior work.

## Significance

The significance of this work is substantial. Ancestral constraints are an important form of background knowledge that can be incorporated into Bayesian network learning to improve both accuracy and efficiency. The paper demonstrates that incorporating such constraints can reduce learning time by orders of magnitude compared to existing approaches.

The empirical results are particularly compelling: the authors show that a relatively small number of ancestral constraints (10-25% of the possible relations) can have a similar impact on model accuracy as significantly increasing the training dataset size (from 512 to 2048 samples, or from 2048 to 8192 samples). This demonstrates the practical value of incorporating domain knowledge in Bayesian network learning.

The paper also makes a contribution to the broader field of structure learning by demonstrating that non-decomposable constraints can be effectively handled by projecting them into decomposable forms. This approach could potentially be extended to other types of non-decomposable constraints.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

### Strengths:
1. The paper addresses a significant gap in Bayesian network structure learning: incorporating non-decomposable ancestral constraints.
2. The theoretical development is sound, with formal proofs for key theorems.
3. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and demonstrates clear advantages over existing approaches.
4. The paper provides a practical method for integrating domain knowledge into structure learning.
5. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations and provide a thorough discussion of related work.

### Weaknesses:
1. The paper could provide more detailed discussion of why ancestral constraints are non-decomposable (beyond the single example given in Section 3).
2. The computational complexity of the constraint projection step is not fully analyzed, particularly for large networks.
3. The paper could include a more detailed comparison with constraint-based approaches that use ancestral constraints (e.g., Borboudakis and Tsamardinos, 2012).
4. The authors could provide more insight into the practical difficulty of obtaining ancestral constraints in real-world applications.

## Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance

### Pro:
- The paper addresses an important problem in Bayesian network structure learning that has not been adequately solved by existing approaches.
- The proposed method is theoretically sound and empirically validated, demonstrating significant improvements over existing approaches.
- The paper provides a practical framework for incorporating domain knowledge into structure learning.
- The work has clear potential for impact in applications where domain knowledge is available but data is limited.

### Con:
- The paper's main contribution (extending EC tree to handle ancestral constraints) builds heavily on Chen et al. (2015, 2016), which may reduce the novelty.
- The empirical evaluation, while comprehensive, focuses on simulated data rather than real-world applications.
- The computational complexity of the constraint projection step is not fully analyzed, which could be a limitation for very large networks.

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS. The work makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning by providing a novel and effective method for incorporating ancestral constraints. The theoretical development is sound, the empirical evaluation is comprehensive, and the results demonstrate clear advantages over existing approaches. The paper is well-written, well-organized, and makes a compelling case for the importance of incorporating domain knowledge into structure learning.

The paper addresses a practical problem with real-world applications, and the results have the potential to impact both researchers and practitioners in the field. The authors have provided a clear demonstration of how their approach outperforms existing methods, with orders-of-magnitude improvements in efficiency.

I believe this paper represents a significant advance in the state of the art for Bayesian network structure learning with constraints, and it would be a valuable contribution to the NIPS conference.
