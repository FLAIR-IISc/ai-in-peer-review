This paper addresses the problem of learning Bayesian network structures with ancestral constraints, which represent known causal or acausal relationships between variables. The authors leverage recent advances in optimal structure learning based on navigating search spaces like the EC tree and BN graph, adapting them to incorporate these constraints. Their key contribution lies in efficiently pruning the search space based on the ancestral constraints and projecting these constraints into decomposable forms (edge and ordering constraints) to empower an oracle used within the search algorithm.

The paper is well-structured, building logically from background on Bayesian network structure learning to the introduction of ancestral constraints, the proposed approach, and finally empirical evaluation. It clearly connects to previous work, citing relevant literature in both the Bayesian network learning and constraint satisfaction communities (e.g., Chen et al., Koivisto & Sood, Cussens, Borboudakis & Tsamardinos). The introduction accurately frames the problem and highlights the limitations of existing approaches in handling non-decomposable constraints. The theoretical foundations (Theorems 1-4) appear sound, and the experiments provide a compelling comparison to an ILP-based approach.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a significant limitation:** The paper tackles a practical and important issue – integrating background knowledge (in the form of ancestral constraints) into optimal Bayesian network structure learning, which is often hindered by the non-decomposable nature of these constraints.
* **Novel approach:** The adaptation of the EC tree search space and the projection of ancestral constraints into decomposable forms represent a novel and potentially effective strategy.
* **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate a substantial performance improvement (orders of magnitude) over a state-of-the-art ILP-based approach, validating the effectiveness of the proposed method. The scalability analysis to 20 variables is also a significant positive point.
* **Sound Theoretical Foundation:** The theorems provide a solid theoretical basis for the proposed pruning and constraint projection techniques.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the concepts and algorithms involved.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Oracle Dependence:** The performance heavily relies on the effectiveness of the “oracle” used for decomposing scores. While URLEARNING is a reasonable choice, the paper doesn't extensively analyze the sensitivity of the approach to different oracle algorithms.
* **MaxSAT Solver:** The use of an external MaxSAT solver for projecting constraints adds complexity and introduces a potential bottleneck. The paper doesn't provide detailed analysis on the performance of the MaxSAT solver and its impact on overall runtime.
* **Limited Comparison:**  While the comparison with GOBNILP is valuable, including a comparison with other constraint-based or hybrid methods (e.g., those based on PC algorithm with path constraints as mentioned in Borboudakis & Tsamardinos) could provide a more comprehensive evaluation.
* **Dataset Limited Scope:** The experimental benchmarks are well-known but might not fully represent the diversity of real-world datasets. Exploring datasets with different characteristics (e.g., high dimensionality, complex relationships) could further validate the approach’s robustness.
* **Projection Overhead:** The benefit of projected constraints is evident, but the computational cost of *generating* those constraints (particularly via MaxSAT) isn’t fully detailed. A more in-depth analysis of the projection time would be helpful.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Addresses a significant challenge in Bayesian network learning.
* Presents a novel and well-motivated approach.
* Demonstrates strong empirical performance improvements.
* Possesses a solid theoretical foundation.
* Clearly written and well-structured.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* Reliance on an external MaxSAT solver could introduce complexity.
* Limited comparison with a broader range of relevant approaches.
* Scope of experimental datasets could be expanded.
* Dependency on oracle performance is not fully explored.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. The proposed approach effectively addresses the challenge of incorporating ancestral constraints and demonstrates significant performance improvements over existing methods. The theoretical foundations are solid, and the experiments are well-designed. While there are some minor weaknesses regarding the dependence on the oracle, the complexity introduced by the MaxSAT solver, and the limited scope of comparisons, these do not outweigh the paper’s strengths.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically requesting a more in-depth discussion of the oracle’s sensitivity and the MaxSAT solver’s performance characteristics.  Expanding the comparisons to other relevant techniques would also strengthen the paper.
