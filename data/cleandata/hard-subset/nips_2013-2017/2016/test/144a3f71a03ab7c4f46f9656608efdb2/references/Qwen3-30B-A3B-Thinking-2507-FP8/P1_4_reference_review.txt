

# Peer Review: "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"

## Summary
This paper addresses a significant challenge in Bayesian network structure learning: incorporating non-decomposable ancestral constraints (e.g., "X is an ancestor of Y" or "X is not an ancestor of Y"). The authors build upon Chen et al.'s EC tree search space framework, which was designed for non-decomposable scores, and show how to incorporate ancestral constraints through two key contributions: (1) a sound pruning strategy for the EC tree based on ancestral constraints, and (2) a method to project these non-decomposable constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints that can be used to empower an oracle (a system that optimizes decomposable scores). The authors empirically demonstrate that their approach is orders-of-magnitude faster than ILP-based approaches like GOBNILP, particularly when the projected constraints are used.

The paper relates well to previous work at NIPS, particularly Chen et al.'s 2015 and 2016 papers on EC trees and non-decomposable scores, as well as the broader literature on Bayesian network structure learning (e.g., Koivisto and Sood, 2004; Yuan and Malone, 2013). It clearly identifies a gap in the literature: while ILP and constraint programming approaches can represent ancestral constraints, they haven't been evaluated for this specific problem, and the authors show their method significantly outperforms these alternatives.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The authors provide solid theoretical foundations (Theorems 1-4) for their pruning method and constraint projection, and the experimental results are thorough and well-designed. They systematically vary key parameters (number of variables, dataset size, constraint percentage) across multiple standard Bayesian network benchmarks (ALARM, ANDES, CHILD, CPCS54, HEPAR2) and report results averaged over 50 different datasets.

The paper is a complete contribution, not merely a position paper. It clearly outlines the problem, presents a solution with theoretical backing, and provides empirical validation. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, such as the need for constraint projection to make their approach efficient, and they provide evidence of this impact through their experiments.

## Clarity
The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and provides sufficient detail for an expert reader to understand and potentially reproduce the work. The structure flows logically from problem statement to background, proposed method, theoretical results, and empirical evaluation. The figures (e.g., Figure 1 illustrating BN graphs and EC trees) effectively complement the text. The authors provide adequate pseudocode and explanations for their constraint projection algorithms.

## Originality
This work makes a clear contribution to the field. While previous approaches have focused on decomposable constraints (e.g., edge presence/absence), this paper addresses the important but challenging problem of incorporating non-decomposable ancestral constraints. The authors' key innovation is the method for projecting these non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones that can be used to empower an oracle, which is novel and well-executed. The paper clearly differentiates itself from Chen et al.'s prior work and from ILP/CP approaches by showing that their method is significantly more efficient for this specific problem.

## Significance
The results are highly significant. The authors demonstrate orders-of-magnitude speedups over ILP-based approaches, which is crucial for practical applications. Their empirical findings also reveal a valuable insight: a small number of ancestral constraints (10%-25%) can have a similar impact on learning quality as substantially increasing the amount of training data (from 512 to 2048, or 2048 to 8192 samples). This highlights the potential value of incorporating background knowledge in Bayesian network learning, which could save significant data collection costs in practice.

## Strengths
1. Addresses a well-identified gap in the literature: incorporating non-decomposable ancestral constraints into structure learning.
2. Provides a sound theoretical foundation with well-motivated theorems.
3. Demonstrates significant empirical advantages over ILP-based approaches.
4. Shows that a small amount of background knowledge can substantially improve learning quality.
5. Well-organized, clearly written, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.

## Weaknesses
1. The paper doesn't thoroughly explore the limitations of the constraint projection approach. For example, how does the method handle inconsistent ancestral constraints? What is the computational cost of the constraint projection step for very large constraint sets?
2. The comparison with constraint-based approaches (like the PC algorithm with path constraints mentioned in the related work) is limited. The authors reference Borboudakis and Tsamardinos (2012, 2013) but don't compare their approach to these constraint-based methods.
3. The MaxSAT encoding for ordering constraints is complex and could benefit from a simple illustrative example to clarify how it works.
4. The paper assumes ancestral constraints are correct, but in practice, background knowledge might be noisy or incomplete, which could impact the method's performance.

## Recommendation
I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a significant contribution to Bayesian network structure learning by addressing a challenging problem (incorporating non-decomposable ancestral constraints) with a novel and effective approach. The theoretical foundation is sound, the empirical results are compelling, and the method has clear practical significance. While there are some limitations to explore further, these don't detract from the paper's main contribution.

The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way, showing orders-of-magnitude improvements over existing approaches for a problem that has been recognized as challenging in the literature. The insights about the value of background knowledge (showing that a small number of constraints can have similar impact as substantially more data) are particularly valuable for practitioners.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS, as it makes a significant contribution to a core machine learning problem with strong theoretical and empirical grounding. The work represents a meaningful advancement over previous approaches and has clear potential for practical impact in domains where causal knowledge is available.
