This paper tackles the important problem of Bayesian network structure learning with ancestral constraints, a setting where prior knowledge about causal relationships should, in principle, significantly aid learning. The core idea – adapting the EC tree framework to incorporate these non-decomposable constraints and, crucially, *projecting* them into decomposable constraints to empower the oracle – is genuinely novel and promising. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing ILP and CP approaches for handling these constraints and demonstrate substantial empirical improvements over a state-of-the-art ILP implementation.

The strength of this work lies in its boldness. It doesn’t incrementally improve existing methods; it proposes a fundamentally different approach to integrating complex prior knowledge into a powerful, optimal structure learning framework. The theoretical connection between non-decomposable and decomposable constraints, and the projection technique, is a significant contribution. The empirical results, while presented with a somewhat limited range of datasets, are compelling, demonstrating orders-of-magnitude speedups. The analysis of the impact of projected constraints is particularly insightful.

However, the paper is not without weaknesses. The empirical evaluation, while convincing, is somewhat limited. The choice of datasets (based on existing benchmarks) is reasonable, but the specific sub-networks selected and the simulation process aren’t described in sufficient detail to allow for easy reproducibility. Furthermore, the impact of different *types* of ancestral constraints (positive vs. negative, transitive vs. non-transitive) isn't explored thoroughly. The MaxSAT formulation for ordering constraint projection, while mentioned, lacks detail and could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of its computational complexity and scalability. While the paper notes scaling to n=20, it's not clear how performance degrades with truly large networks or complex constraint sets. The discussion of related work, while present, feels somewhat cursory. The connection to constraint-based methods like PC, mentioned briefly, could be expanded. The notation is also a little dense and could be streamlined for clarity.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **High novelty:** The approach of projecting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones is a significant and original idea.
*   **Bold approach:** This paper isn’t simply refining existing techniques; it's proposing a new paradigm for constrained Bayesian network learning.
*   **Strong empirical results:** The reported speedups over ILP are substantial and suggest a significant practical benefit.
*   **Theoretical contribution:** The connection between ancestral and decomposable constraints is a valuable theoretical insight.
*   **Addresses a key limitation:** Directly tackles the difficulty of integrating non-decomposable constraints into optimal structure learning.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited empirical scope:** The datasets and experimental setup could be more thoroughly described and expanded.
*   **MaxSAT details lacking:** The MaxSAT projection method requires more explanation regarding complexity and scalability.
*   **Clarity of presentation:** The notation and some explanations could be more accessible.
*   **Related work could be expanded:** The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of relevant literature, particularly in constraint-based learning.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some minor flaws in the presentation and empirical evaluation, this paper presents a highly novel and promising approach to a challenging problem. The core idea – projecting ancestral constraints – is brilliant, and the empirical results are compelling. The originality and potential impact of this work outweigh its weaknesses. I believe this paper makes a significant contribution to the field and is worthy of acceptance to NIPS. I’m willing to overlook the relatively incomplete validation given the boldness of the approach and its potential to open up new research directions. I would suggest the authors address the comments regarding reproducibility and the MaxSAT details in a revision.



**Recommendation:** Accept.
