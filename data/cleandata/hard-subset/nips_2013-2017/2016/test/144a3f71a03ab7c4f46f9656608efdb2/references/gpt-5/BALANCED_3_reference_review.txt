Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses optimal Bayesian network (BN) structure learning when background knowledge is given as ancestral (path) constraints. Mainstream exact score-based methods assume decomposable scores and typically support only decomposable constraints (edge presence/absence, in-degree bounds, precedence constraints). The authors build on the BN graph/EC tree framework of Chen et al. (2015, 2016), which reframes optimal structure learning as shortest-path search over DAGs or Markov equivalence classes (CPDAGs), guided by an oracle that optimizes decomposable scores. The contributions are twofold: (1) integrate non-decomposable ancestral constraints by pruning nodes in the EC tree (CPDAGs) that cannot admit any DAG satisfying the constraints; and (2) “project” non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints—edge constraints (algorithm with necessary-and-sufficient conditions; Theorems 2–3) and ordering constraints (via a MaxSAT formulation; Theorem 4)—to strengthen the oracle’s heuristic.

The proposed pruning includes efficient tests on CPDAGs (e.g., presence of directed paths violating negative constraints, absence of partially directed paths for positive constraints), with enumeration of class(P) when inconclusive. For projection, the paper defines closure of ancestral constraints, then derives conditions under which negative edges X ↛ Y and positive edges X → Y are entailed. For ordering constraints, the authors encode maximization of jointly entailed pairwise orderings as a partial MaxSAT problem with hard clauses ensuring transitivity and acyclicity and soft clauses preferring Y < X whenever X 6 Y.

Empirically, the method (EC tree + oracle URLearning) is compared to GOBNILP (ILP) on subsampled benchmark BNs (ALARM, ANDES, CHILD, CPCS54, HEPAR2), using exact learning with BDeu, and synthetic ancestral constraints consistent with ground truth. With projected constraints, the proposed approach is often orders of magnitude faster than ILP. Without projection, it remains competitive but slower, highlighting the benefit of projection. The authors also study scaling with n up to 20, dataset size, and proportion of constraints, observing an easy–hard–easy pattern and accuracy gains from constraints comparable to increasing data.

Strengths
- Addresses an important practical setting: integrating path/causal knowledge into exact score-based BN learning.
- Clear integration with EC trees enables principled pruning using ancestral constraints; Theorem 1 gives a soundness guarantee for pruning descendants.
- Projection of non-decomposable constraints to decomposable ones is novel and useful; edge entailment conditions are stated as iff theorems; ordering projection via MaxSAT is creative and practically impactful.
- Strong empirical speedups over a well-known ILP baseline (GOBNILP), with controlled ablations showing the value of projection and insight into problem hardness.
- Demonstrates that a modest number of constraints can markedly improve model accuracy, which is of practical significance.

Weaknesses
- Some technical details are omitted (“details omitted for space”), e.g., the complete procedure to close ancestral constraints and the proofs of Theorems 2–4. This limits reproducibility and the ability to verify claims of necessity/sufficiency.
- Pruning test for CPDAGs falls back to exhaustive enumeration when initial checks are inconclusive; the frequency and cost of this enumeration are not quantified, and known CPDAG reachability criteria could avoid enumeration. The lack of analysis here is a potential scalability bottleneck.
- The MaxSAT encoding for ordering constraints is presented as sufficient; the paper does not prove that the resulting set of orderings is maximal among those entailed by A, nor does it quantify how conservative it is. Constraint (3) is a nonstandard sufficient condition for acyclicity; its tightness and impact on recall of entailed orderings are unclear.
- Evaluation uses only constraints consistent with ground truth; robustness to noisy or conflicting constraints (or soft constraints) is not addressed, despite being common in practice.
- Baselines are limited to ILP; no comparison to CP approaches or to alternative exact heuristic search spaces (e.g., A* over order graph with path constraints expressed via precedence constraints when possible).
- Presentation issues: a few citation placeholders and formatting artifacts; definitions such as “maximum set” of ancestral constraints need precise formalization; figures referenced but not shown in the text provided.

Quality
Technically sound at a high level, with solid framing (shortest-path over EC tree + oracle) and sensible algorithms. However, key correctness claims are not fully substantiated in the paper (missing proofs and full algorithms), and the fallback enumeration strategy raises concerns about worst-case behavior.

Clarity
Generally well written and motivated, but clarity suffers where details are omitted, terminology is imprecise (“maximum set”), and some references/figures are incomplete. Including pseudocode and proofs in the supplement would help.

Originality
The combination of EC-tree pruning with ancestral constraints and the projection of non-decomposable constraints to decomposable ones (edge/order) to empower an oracle is original and nontrivial. It meaningfully extends prior work on EC trees and order-modular priors.

Significance
Practically significant for exact BN learning with domain knowledge; the speedups over ILP and the ability to leverage path constraints are valuable. While scaling to only ~20 variables without in-degree bounds is typical for exact learning, the methodological ideas could influence broader toolchains and future approximations.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel, principled integration of ancestral constraints into exact score-based learning.
- Strong empirical gains and informative ablations.
- Potentially useful projection techniques beyond this framework.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing proofs/algorithms and limited analysis of the potentially exponential enumeration step.
- No robustness to noisy/inconsistent constraints.
- Limited baselines and scalability assessment.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide complete proofs for Theorems 1–4 and formalize the closure algorithm for ancestral constraints; clarify “maximum” vs. “closed” sets.
- Replace or reduce enumeration of class(P) by leveraging known CPDAG reachability/orientation rules (e.g., Meek’s rules, Chickering’s characterization) or SAT-based feasibility checks; report how often enumeration is needed and its cost.
- Analyze the MaxSAT encoding: prove that the selected set of orderings is entailed (safety), characterize its maximality or conservativeness, and ablate the contributions of edge vs. ordering projection.
- Evaluate robustness to noisy or conflicting constraints (e.g., soft constraints with penalties), and discuss how the framework could incorporate them.
- Add comparisons to a CP baseline and to ILP with alternative constraint sets; report memory usage and larger n with bounded in-degree.
- Improve presentation and citations; include figures and pseudocode, and release code if possible.

Decision: Weak accept
The paper offers a clear, useful contribution with compelling empirical results. With strengthened proofs/analysis and clarified implementation details, it would be a solid addition to NeurIPS.
