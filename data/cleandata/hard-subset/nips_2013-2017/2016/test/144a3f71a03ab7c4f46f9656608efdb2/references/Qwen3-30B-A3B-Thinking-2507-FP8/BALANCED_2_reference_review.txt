

# Review of "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"

## Summary
This paper addresses a significant challenge in Bayesian network structure learning: incorporating non-decomposable ancestral constraints (specifying cause-and-effect relationships between variables) into optimal structure learning. The authors build upon Chen et al.'s (2015, 2016) EC tree search space for non-decomposable scores, extending it to handle ancestral constraints. They propose two key contributions: (1) a method to prune the EC tree based on ancestral constraints, and (2) a technique to project non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints that can empower an oracle (a system for decomposable scores). The paper demonstrates orders-of-magnitude speed improvements over ILP-based approaches (GOBNILP) in empirical evaluations across multiple benchmark networks.

## Strengths

**Technical Soundness and Theoretical Contributions (Quality):** The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution with clear theorems (Theorems 1-4) that establish the soundness of their pruning method and constraint projection. The approach to transforming non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones is particularly elegant and well-motivated. The authors properly situate their work within the context of existing approaches to non-decomposable scoring (Chen et al., 2015, 2016) and contrast their method with ILP-based approaches (Cussens, 2008; Jaakkola et al., 2010).

**Significance and Practical Impact (Significance):** The paper demonstrates substantial practical significance by showing that a relatively small number of ancestral constraints (10-25%) can have a similar impact on model accuracy as substantially increasing training data (from 512 to 8192 samples). The scalability to 20 variables (Table 3) is also noteworthy, matching state-of-the-art score-based approaches. The results clearly show that ancestral constraints can dramatically improve learning efficiency without requiring more data.

**Comprehensive Evaluation (Quality):** The experimental evaluation is thorough, varying multiple parameters (number of variables, dataset size, constraint percentage) across standard benchmarks (ALARM, ANDES, etc.). The comparison against GOBNILP (an ILP-based system) is particularly valuable, with results consistently showing orders-of-magnitude speedups for the proposed approach. The analysis of the impact of constraint projection on search efficiency (Tables 1 vs. 2) is insightful.

**Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically organized, with clear definitions and good use of figures to illustrate the EC tree and BN graph. The technical background in Sections 2-3 is sufficient for readers familiar with Bayesian networks.

## Weaknesses

**Incomplete Explanation of Key Components:** The paper's approach relies on a MaxSAT encoding for ordering constraints (Section 5.2), but provides insufficient detail about the implementation. The MaxSAT solver (EVASOLVER) is mentioned, but without explaining how the problem is formulated or why this particular solver was chosen. A brief pseudocode or more detailed description of the MaxSAT encoding would strengthen the paper.

**Limited Analysis of Constraint Projection Effectiveness:** While the paper demonstrates that constraint projection improves efficiency (Tables 1 vs. 2), it doesn't analyze *when* or *why* certain constraints are more effective. For instance, it would be valuable to know how the type of ancestral constraint (positive vs. negative) or the position in the network affects the efficiency gain.

**Comparison Against Constraint-Based Methods:** The paper cites Borboudakis and Tsamardinos (2012, 2013) who used ancestral constraints in constraint-based learning (e.g., PC algorithm), but doesn't compare against these methods. Given that constraint-based approaches are common in practice, a comparison would better establish the relative advantages of the proposed approach.

**Handling of Inconclusive Pruning Cases:** The paper mentions that when pruning CPDAGs is inconclusive, they exhaustively enumerate structures (Section 4.3), but doesn't discuss the frequency of this case or how it affects scalability. A brief analysis of the computational cost of this fallback would be helpful.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a strong contribution to Bayesian network structure learning by addressing a practical challenge (incorporating non-decomposable ancestral constraints) with a technically sound and empirically validated approach. The significance of the results (orders-of-magnitude speedups, reduced data requirements) is compelling, and the work is well-situated within the literature.

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper is strong in its theoretical contributions and empirical evaluation, but would benefit from:
1. More detailed explanation of the MaxSAT encoding for ordering constraints
2. Analysis of when constraint projection is most effective
3. Brief comparison against constraint-based methods (Borboudakis and Tsamardinos) to better establish the relative advantages of the proposed approach

The paper meets all NIPS quality standards: it is technically sound, clearly written, makes a meaningful contribution to the field, and demonstrates significant impact. The minor weaknesses identified do not detract from the paper's overall strength and contribution to the literature.
