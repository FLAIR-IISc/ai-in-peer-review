- Paper tackles exact Bayesian network structure learning under hard non-decomposable ancestral (path) constraints by pruning EC-tree nodes and projecting constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints.
- Method: prune CPDAG nodes that cannot extend to any ancestral-satisfying DAG; project ancestral constraints into edge/topological-order constraints and use MaxSAT for ordering inference.
- Theorems: complete characterization for entailed negative edges (Thm.2), sufficient-and-necessary condition for some entailed positive edges relative to closed ancestral set (Thm.3), and sufficient conditions for ordering entailment via MaxSAT (Thm.4).
- Experiments on subgraphs of standard networks with simulated constraints show large runtime gains over GOBNILP and accuracy improvements from modest prior knowledge.
- The pruning strategy is natural and supported by monotonicity intuition, but Theorem 1 is ambiguously stated and needs clarification and a proof sketch about scope (subset vs full variable set).
- Edge-projection results are precise and algorithmically clear; ordering projection via MaxSAT uses only sufficient conditions and may be conservative—this trade-off needs empirical quantification.
- Fallback enumeration of all DAGs in a CPDAG class can be exponential; the paper should report how often this occurs and time spent in enumeration versus total runtime.
- Experimental methodology is fair (same scores, projected constraints given to ILP) but compares only to ILP; adding a CP baseline would strengthen the evaluation.
- Scale is modest (n up to ~20); experiments should include wall-clock breakdowns (oracle calls, MaxSAT time) and memory usage.
- Clarity issues: formatting/citation glitches, ambiguous statements about evaluating constraints at partial CPDAGs, and unclear correctness argument for pruning.
- Authors should provide closure rules used to make A “maximum” (at least in supplement) and a complexity bound for edge-projection.
- Need more detail on MaxSAT encoding (variable/ clause counts, weights) and its runtime impact.
- Integration of non-decomposable ancestral constraints into EC-tree search with decomposable-score oracles is novel and well-motivated.
- Projection of ancestral constraints into safe decomposable edge/order constraints is a valuable contribution beyond prior ILP/CP work and path-belief heuristics.
- Supporting hard ancestral constraints is significant for causal discovery; demonstrated speedups and accuracy gains show practical impact.
- Main limitations: scalability (~20 variables) and reliance on potentially exponential fallback enumeration, which may limit larger-problem adoption.
- Pros: sound pruning framework, effective projection with clear theorems, principled MaxSAT reduction, strong empirical speedups, and insightful analysis of constraint-density effects.
- Cons: clarity and presentation issues, conservative ordering inference without gap analysis, potentially expensive enumeration without quantitative analysis, limited baselines and modest problem size.
- Key questions for authors: frequency and runtime cost of class(P) enumeration; MaxSAT instance sizes and runtime; whether inferred ordering set is logically maximal or only maximal under sufficient constraints and any empirical gap; sensitivity to noisy/mistaken ancestral constraints; extensibility of projection rules to other non-decomposable constraints.
- Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on clarifying theoretical statements, quantifying expensive steps, and improving presentation.
