## NIPS 2016 Review: Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the problem of learning Bayesian networks (BNs) when prior knowledge is available in the form of ancestral constraints (specifying ancestor-descendant relationships between variables). The key challenge is that these constraints are *non-decomposable*, meaning they cannot be easily incorporated into standard BN learning algorithms that rely on decomposable scoring functions. The authors build upon their previous work [Chen et al., 2015, 2016] which introduced a framework for optimal BN structure learning with non-decomposable priors, and propose a method to integrate ancestral constraints into this framework.  They leverage the Efficient and Compact (EC) tree search space, pruning it based on the ancestral constraints.  Crucially, they also demonstrate how to *project* these non-decomposable constraints into decomposable constraints (edge and ordering constraints) to further empower an oracle used within their search algorithm.  Finally, they empirically compare their approach against Integer Linear Programming (ILP)-based methods, demonstrating significant performance improvements.

**Related Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the existing literature on BN structure learning, noting the limitations of decomposable scoring functions in handling non-decomposable constraints.  It acknowledges previous work on structure-modular and order-modular constraints (e.g., [Koivisto and Sood, 2004, Parviainen and Koivisto, 2013]) and recognizes the potential of ILP/CP approaches [Jaakkola et al., 2010, Bartlett and Cussens, 2015, van Beek and Hoffmann, 2015] but highlights the lack of empirical evaluation for those methods in the context of ancestral constraints.  The connection to recent work on path constraints [Borboudakis and Tsamardinos, 2012, 2013] is also appropriately noted. The citations are relevant and comprehensive.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an important practical problem:** Incorporating background knowledge into BN learning is crucial for real-world applications, and ancestral constraints are a natural way to represent such knowledge.
* **Novel approach:** The combination of using the EC tree search space with constraint pruning and projection of non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones is a novel and promising technique.
* **Strong empirical results:** The experimental evaluation demonstrates significant performance improvements over ILP-based methods, particularly as the network size increases. The experiments are well-designed, systematically varying the number of variables, dataset size, and constraint percentage.
* **Theoretical foundation:** The inclusion of theorems regarding constraint projection and soundness of pruning adds to the rigor of the work.
* **Clear exposition:** While the concepts are complex, the paper is generally well-written and organized, making it reasonably accessible. The use of examples helps illustrate the key ideas.
* **Impactful findings:** The observation that even a small percentage of ancestral constraints can significantly improve model accuracy is notable.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:** The paper dives into technical details (EC trees, BN graphs, MaxSAT) without sufficiently motivating *why* these specific search spaces are chosen. A higher-level explanation of the advantages of these structures would be beneficial.
* **MaxSAT implementation details:** While the reduction to MaxSAT is mentioned, details of the implementation (e.g., solver used, specific clause construction) are somewhat lacking. This makes it difficult to assess the computational cost of this step.
* **Limited comparison:** The comparison is primarily against a single ILP-based method. Comparing against other relevant state-of-the-art algorithms (even if they don’t natively handle ancestral constraints) would strengthen the conclusions.  How does the performance compare to methods that *ignore* the ancestral constraints entirely?
* **Scalability limitations:**  While the approach scales to 20 variables, it still faces scalability challenges. The paper acknowledges this but doesn't discuss potential avenues for further scaling (e.g., approximate inference, parallelization).
* **Dataset specifics:** The experiments are performed on standard benchmark datasets. The authors could benefit from investigating the performance on more complex, real-world datasets.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and effective approach to learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints. The technical contributions are significant, and the empirical results are compelling.  While the complexity of the approach and limited comparison represent minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh them. 

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a challenging and practically relevant problem.
* Proposes a novel and theoretically sound solution.
* Demonstrates significant empirical improvements over existing methods.
* Well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The approach is complex and could be difficult for practitioners to implement.
* The comparison is limited to a single ILP-based method.
* Scalability remains a concern.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept** - This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning and is well-suited for presentation at NIPS.  I recommend minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly adding more details about the MaxSAT implementation and including a comparison to approaches that ignore the ancestral constraints.
