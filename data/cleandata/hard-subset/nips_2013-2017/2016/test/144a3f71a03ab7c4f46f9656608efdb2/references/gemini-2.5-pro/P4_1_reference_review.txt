This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data when subject to background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints (e.g., variable X must be an ancestor of Y). The authors correctly identify that such constraints are non-decomposable and thus cannot be handled by mainstream optimal structure learning algorithms (e.g., Koivisto and Sood, 2004; Yuan and Malone, 2013) that rely on the assumption of a decomposable score function.

To tackle this, the paper builds upon the recently proposed EC tree search framework (Chen et al., 2016), which navigates a search space of Markov equivalence classes (represented as CPDAGs) and can accommodate non-decomposable scores. The core contributions are twofold. First, the authors develop a method to prune the EC tree search space by checking whether a given CPDAG can satisfy the set of ancestral constraints. Second, and more significantly, they introduce a method to "project" the non-decomposable ancestral constraints onto a set of decomposable constraints (edge presence/absence and topological ordering constraints). This projection is used to "empower" the oracle—an exact solver for decomposable problems used as the heuristic function in the A* search over the EC tree. A tighter heuristic leads to more efficient search. The paper provides a sound algorithm for inferring all entailed edge constraints and formulates the more complex problem of inferring a maximal set of sound ordering constraints as a MaxSAT instance.

The proposed approach is empirically evaluated against an Integer Linear Programming (ILP) formulation, which represents another class of exact methods capable of handling such constraints. The experiments demonstrate that the proposed method is orders of magnitude faster than the ILP baseline, and that the constraint projection technique is critical to this performance gain.

**Quality:**
The paper is technically sound. The theoretical claims supporting the proposed methods appear correct. Theorem 1 provides a sound basis for pruning the EC tree. The methods for projecting ancestral constraints into edge constraints (Theorems 2 and 3) and ordering constraints (Theorem 4 and the MaxSAT formulation) are well-reasoned. The paper is a complete piece of work, presenting a novel algorithm, theoretical justification, and a thorough empirical evaluation. Claims are well-supported by experimental results on standard benchmark networks. The authors are careful to compare against a relevant and powerful baseline (ILP) and analyze the performance under varying conditions (number of variables, data size, and number of constraints), including an ablation study of sorts by testing their method with and without the projected constraints.

**Clarity:**
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and situates the work within the existing literature. Section 2 provides the necessary technical background on score-based learning. The core ideas—adapting the EC tree search and projecting constraints—are presented logically in Sections 4 and 5. The experiments are well-designed and the results are presented clearly in tables.

A few points could be clarified to improve reproducibility. The paper mentions that a given set of ancestral constraints `A` is assumed to be a "maximum set" including all entailed constraints, but the rules for computing this closure are omitted for space. Similarly, the method for checking if a CPDAG satisfies the constraints mentions "exhaustively enumerate the structures of class(P)" as a fallback; a brief discussion on the practical cost of this step, or how often it is invoked, would be helpful.

**Originality:**
The paper presents a novel and clever approach to a known hard problem. While the underlying search framework (the EC tree) is from previous work by Chen et al. (2016), its adaptation to handle ancestral constraints is new. The primary novelty lies in the idea of projecting non-decomposable constraints onto decomposable ones to improve the heuristic of an A*-style search. This "empowering the oracle" technique is a significant conceptual contribution that could potentially be applied to other types of non-decomposable constraints beyond ancestral ones. The use of MaxSAT to find a consistent set of ordering constraints is also a novel application in this context. The paper correctly references related work in both optimal score-based learning and ILP-based methods. To the best of my knowledge, this is the first work to provide a detailed algorithm and strong empirical results for optimal score-based learning with ancestral constraints in this manner.

**Significance:**
The results presented are significant. The ability to incorporate background knowledge, particularly causal relationships expressed as ancestral constraints, is of high practical importance in many scientific domains. This work provides an exact, operational method for doing so that is demonstrably more efficient than a state-of-the-art ILP solver. The empirical results showing that a modest number of ancestral constraints can improve model accuracy as much as a several-fold increase in data size highlights the practical value of this research. Researchers and practitioners working on Bayesian network structure learning are likely to find these ideas useful and may build upon them. The core idea of constraint projection to strengthen heuristics for search over non-decomposable spaces may also be influential beyond this specific application.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a significant and practical problem in structure learning.
*   The proposed method, particularly the constraint projection to empower the search heuristic, is novel and technically sound.
*   The paper includes a strong empirical evaluation against a relevant baseline, demonstrating orders-of-magnitude performance improvements.
*   The work advances the state of the art in exact score-based structure learning with complex background knowledge.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The scalability of the exact method is still limited to a relatively small number of variables (n=20), which is a general limitation of the field but worth noting.
*   The core search framework is not new, though its application and enhancement are.
*   Some details required for full reproducibility are omitted, likely due to space constraints.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper with clear strengths in originality, technical soundness, and significance. The weaknesses are minor. It presents a valuable contribution to the NIPS community.
