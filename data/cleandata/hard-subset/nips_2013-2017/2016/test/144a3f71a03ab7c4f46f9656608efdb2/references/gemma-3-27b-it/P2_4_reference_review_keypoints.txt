- Tackles learning Bayesian networks with prior ancestral (ancestor/descendant) constraints that are non-decomposable and hard to incorporate into standard scoring methods.  
- Builds on EC trees and an oracle for decomposable scores, adapting them to prune search and project non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable ones.  
- Projection of ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints is a key novel idea that enables use of the oracle.  
- Presents theorems that justify the pruning and projection strategies, providing a sound theoretical foundation.  
- Empirical results show significant speedups over existing ILP-based approaches, especially as problem size increases (demonstrated up to 20 variables).  
- Experiments clearly demonstrate the positive impact of constraint projection on performance.  
- Writing is generally clear and well-organized, with understandable explanations of algorithms.  
- Limited comparison: lacks evaluation against more traditional score-based structure learning algorithms (e.g., order-graph methods) to measure trade-offs when ancestral constraints are not used.  
- Implementation complexity is high, requiring EC trees, an oracle, constraint projection, and a MaxSAT solver, which may hinder adoption.  
- The computational cost of the constraint projection step is not discussed and may be significant.  
- Sensitivity to constraint quality: performance gains depend heavily on the accuracy of provided ancestral constraints; robustness to noisy constraints is minimally analyzed.  
- Dependence on MaxSAT solver: overall performance may hinge on the chosen solver, with no sensitivity analysis across solvers or alternative methods.  
- Quality: technically sound with theoretical guarantees and compelling empirical validation.  
- Clarity: generally clear but some dense sections (e.g., MaxSAT formulation) need more intuitive explanation.  
- Originality: novel combination of EC trees, oracle-based learning, and constraint projection; projection of non-decomposable constraints is a substantial contribution.  
- Significance: important contribution likely useful in applications with prior knowledge; shows notable advances over ILP.  
- For acceptance: addresses a significant problem with a novel, theoretically justified approach and strong empirical gains.  
- Against acceptance: needs broader algorithmic comparisons, discussion of implementation/scalability of projection, and analysis of robustness and solver dependence.  
- Overall recommendation: strong paper recommended for acceptance, contingent on addressing comparisons, scalability, and robustness concerns in the rebuttal.
