This paper addresses the problem of learning Bayesian network structures with ancestral constraints, a challenging area due to the non-decomposable nature of such constraints. The authors build upon their previous work utilizing EC trees for optimal structure learning and propose methods for incorporating and leveraging ancestral constraints. They introduce techniques for projecting these constraints into decomposable forms, effectively empowering an oracle used within their search process. The paper is well-motivated, as ancestral constraints are relevant in many practical applications and existing methods struggle to handle them efficiently.

However, despite presenting a reasonable approach and achieving promising empirical results, the paper suffers from a multitude of small but persistent flaws, ranging from imprecise language to inconsistencies in notation and a lack of thoroughness in experimental details. These issues, while individually minor, accumulate to create a concerning impression of sloppiness and a lack of meticulousness. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of adapting EC trees to handle ancestral constraints is a valuable contribution. The idea of projecting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones to enhance the oracle is clever.
*   **Empirical Results:** The results demonstrate significant speedups compared to ILP-based approaches, establishing the practical benefit of the proposed method. The comparison to GOBNILP is important.
*   **Soundness:** The theoretical results (Theorems 1-4) provide a solid foundation for the proposed algorithms and pruning strategies.
*   **Relevance:** The topic of integrating background knowledge into Bayesian network learning is a significant one, and ancestral constraints represent a natural and important form of such knowledge.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity and Notation:** The writing is often imprecise. For example, the use of “Xa” and “Yb” in Section 5.1 is confusing without more explicit definition within the context of α(X) and β(Y). The paper frequently uses “approaches” when “algorithms” would be more precise, and relies on vague phrasing. The definition of α(X) and β(X) can be slightly confusing and is not directly reflected in the notation used in the following theorems.
*   **Inconsistent Terminology:** Terms like "decomposable constraints" and "ordering constraints" are used interchangeably at times, leading to ambiguity. The distinction between "ancestral constraints" and "non-decomposable constraints" feels blurred – all ancestral constraints *are* non-decomposable, so repeatedly stating this feels redundant.
*   **Missing Details in Experiments:** The experimental setup lacks critical details. What specific sub-networks were chosen from the benchmark datasets? How were the ancestral constraints randomly generated (e.g., what proportion of possible constraints were considered)? While the authors mention averaging over 50 datasets, the rationale behind this number isn’t provided. The choice of equivalent sample size (1) for the BDeu score is arbitrary and should be justified, or the sensitivity to this parameter should be tested.
*   **Minor Errors and Imprecisions:** Several minor errors exist. For instance, the phrase “GOBNILP, where we encoded ancestral constraints using linear constraints, based on [Cussens, 2008]” is followed by a citation of Bartlett and Cussens, 2015, not Cussens, 2008. The claim that “a relatively small number of ancestral constraints (say 10%–25%) can have a similar impact on the quality of the observed network…as increasing the amount of data” feels strong and lacks sufficient supporting evidence (e.g., a graph comparing performance under different data sizes and constraint levels).
*   **Overly Strong Claims:** Assertions like “orders-of-magnitude improvements” should be qualified. While the speedups are substantial, the term “orders-of-magnitude” is often used imprecisely and could be misleading.
*   **Redundancy:** The introduction contains repetitive phrasing, particularly regarding the limitations of existing methods. The paragraph starting "To our knowledge, however…" is unnecessary and could be removed.
*   **Formatting:** There is an odd formatting issue with the dates in the introduction (e.g., "年6月12⽇日星期五"). This suggests a copy-paste error.
* **Theorem 2 and 3 have strange notation, and need further clarification**. The notation Xa 6 Yb may be confusing.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important problem in Bayesian network learning.
*   Presents a novel and promising approach.
*   Demonstrates substantial empirical benefits.
*   Provides theoretical justification for the proposed techniques.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The paper lacks the polish expected of a NIPS publication. The numerous minor flaws detract from the overall quality.
*   The experimental setup is insufficiently detailed, making it difficult to assess the robustness and generalizability of the results.
*   The writing is often imprecise and ambiguous.
*   The claims are occasionally overstated.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **rejection**. While the core ideas of this paper are valuable and the empirical results are encouraging, the numerous small flaws and lack of rigor throughout the manuscript are too significant to overlook. The paper feels rushed and lacks the meticulous attention to detail expected of a NIPS publication. The authors would significantly benefit from carefully revising the manuscript to address these issues. A major revision, with a focus on clarity, precision, and experimental rigor, would be necessary for reconsideration. A more careful edit to address the many minor errors and inconsistencies is also critical.
