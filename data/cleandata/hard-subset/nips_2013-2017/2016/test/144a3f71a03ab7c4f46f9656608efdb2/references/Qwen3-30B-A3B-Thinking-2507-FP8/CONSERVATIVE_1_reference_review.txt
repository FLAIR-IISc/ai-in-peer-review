

# Review of "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"

This paper proposes a method for learning Bayesian network structures under ancestral constraints, which specify whether one variable is an ancestor of another in the network. The authors argue that ancestral constraints are non-decomposable, making them difficult to incorporate into standard Bayesian network learning approaches that rely on decomposable scoring functions. They adapt the EC tree search space (from Chen et al., 2016) to incorporate ancestral constraints and propose a method to project non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints that can be used to improve the efficiency of structure learning.

**Strengths:**
The paper makes a theoretically sound contribution to the problem of incorporating non-decomposable constraints into Bayesian network structure learning. The constraint projection method (Theorems 2 and 3) appears logically sound, and the EC tree adaptation for ancestral constraints is well-motivated. The empirical evaluation demonstrates that their approach can be significantly faster than ILP-based methods (GOBNILP), which is an important practical contribution. The finding that a small number of ancestral constraints can have a similar impact on learning quality as substantially larger datasets is an insightful observation with potential practical value.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper's main weakness is insufficient validation of its key claim of "orders-of-magnitude" improvements. While Table 1 shows speedups compared to GOBNILP, the paper lacks detailed runtime measurements that would allow for proper assessment of the magnitude of improvement. The experimental setup is limited to a small number of benchmark networks (ALARM, ANDES, CHILD, CPCS54, HEPAR2), and the authors don't demonstrate how their method scales to larger networks or more complex constraint sets.

More critically, the paper fails to adequately address why their approach is necessary when established methods like ILP and constraint programming can already handle ancestral constraints. The authors acknowledge that ILP approaches "can in principle enforce non-decomposable constraints" but don't provide a thorough comparison against these methods beyond GOBNILP. The paper doesn't address why the EC tree approach would be preferable to simpler ILP formulations that have been previously developed for similar problems.

The theoretical contribution is also somewhat limited. The EC tree approach builds directly on Chen et al.'s prior work (2015, 2016), and the constraint projection method, while novel, doesn't fundamentally change the underlying problem. The paper would be significantly stronger if it demonstrated how their approach could handle constraints that are impossible for existing methods to incorporate.

The paper also lacks a thorough discussion of limitations. For example, it doesn't address how their method handles inconsistent ancestral constraints or how it scales with very large numbers of constraints. The claim that their method is "orders-of-magnitude" faster than ILP approaches is unsubstantiated without concrete runtime comparisons across a wider range of problem instances.

**Overall Assessment:**
While the paper presents a theoretically sound method for incorporating ancestral constraints into Bayesian network structure learning, it fails to provide sufficient empirical validation of its key claims and doesn't adequately justify why this new approach is necessary given existing methods. The paper's most significant contribution - the constraint projection method - is interesting but not sufficiently validated against the full range of relevant baselines.

The paper would be significantly improved by:
1. Providing more detailed runtime measurements to substantiate the "orders-of-magnitude" claim
2. Conducting a more comprehensive comparison against relevant baselines, including other constraint-based approaches
3. Addressing the limitations of their method more thoroughly
4. Better justifying why their approach is needed when established methods can already handle ancestral constraints

As a reviewer who prefers established methods and is skeptical of new approaches without thorough validation, I find the paper's claims to be overstated relative to the evidence provided. The theoretical contribution is sound but not sufficiently novel or impactful to warrant publication at NIPS given the lack of comprehensive empirical validation.

**Recommendation:**
Reject. While the paper addresses a legitimate problem in Bayesian network learning, the empirical validation is insufficient to support the paper's key claims, and the necessity of the proposed approach is not convincingly demonstrated. The paper would need significantly more thorough empirical evaluation and a stronger justification for why this new approach is needed before it could be considered for publication at NIPS.
