Summary and contributions
The paper tackles optimal Bayesian network (BN) structure learning under non-decomposable ancestral constraints (X is/is not an ancestor of Y). Building on DAG/EC search spaces introduced by Chen et al. (BN graph, EC tree), the authors (i) prune EC-tree nodes (CPDAGs) that cannot satisfy ancestral constraints; (ii) “project” ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints (edge presence/absence and topological ordering) to empower a decomposable-score oracle (URLEARNING); and (iii) use MaxSAT to infer a large set of safe ordering constraints from ancestral constraints. Empirically, the method reportedly outperforms an ILP approach (GOBNILP) by orders of magnitude, with further gains when projected constraints are used.

Relation to prior work
The work is closely related to Chen et al. (2015; 2016) on shortest-path formulations over DAG/EC spaces and k-best enumeration with non-decomposable priors. It complements ILP/CP approaches that can encode global constraints (e.g., Jaakkola et al. 2010; Bartlett & Cussens 2015; van Beek & Hoffmann 2015), and to causal/path-constraint priors for constraint-based and score-based search (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos 2012; 2013). SAT/WCNF-based BN learning (Cussens 2008) is also relevant to the MaxSAT encoding. The paper’s idea of projecting global constraints to decomposable ones is interesting and, to my knowledge, not systematically explored in this context.

Strengths
- Conceptually appealing integration: prune EC tree by global constraints and strengthen a decomposable oracle using projected constraints.
- Practical: uses an existing strong oracle (URLEARNING), and claims large empirical speedups over a strong ILP baseline (GOBNILP).
- Potentially useful methodology: rules to infer edge and ordering constraints from ancestral constraints could be broadly applicable; MaxSAT formulation is a reasonable vehicle.
- The easy-hard-easy trend analysis with increasing constraint density is a nice empirical observation.

Weaknesses and concerns (technical and clarity)
- Theorem 1 statement appears incorrect or at least improperly stated. It reads: “In an EC tree, a CPDAG P satisfies A iff its descendants satisfy A.” Monotonicity should be: if P violates A then all descendants violate A (pruning soundness), and if a descendant satisfies A then its ancestors may or may not (not an iff). As written, it suggests that if P satisfies A then all descendants satisfy A, which is not necessarily true when constraints involve variables that are added later or when negative ancestral constraints could be violated via paths through newly added variables. The paper needs a precise problem setting (are constraints only over variables already present at a node?) and a corrected monotonicity statement/proof.
- Checking CPDAG satisfaction: authors propose, after two quick tests, to “exhaustively enumerate the structures of class(P).” This can be exponential in class size and undermines scalability. There is prior work on deciding existence of orientations/paths in CPDAGs without enumerating all DAGs (e.g., using essential graph orientation rules). A polynomial-time decision procedure or a bounded fallback is needed; otherwise worst-case complexity is not discussed.
- Projection rules:
  - Theorem 2 (characterization of negative edge entailment via α/β sets) is plausible in one direction but “iff” requires proof. There can be cases where X→Y is precluded only via a cycle formed with other nodes yet not captured by any single Xa 6 Yb among α(X), β(Y), unless the closure procedure adds all such consequences. The closure algorithm is said “details omitted,” leaving correctness/complexity unclear.
  - Theorem 3 (positive edge entailment) is strong and nontrivial. The condition “for all Z∉α(X)∪β(Y), A contains Xa 6 Zb or Za 6 Yb” as a complete characterization also needs a rigorous proof. No proofs are provided; this is core to the paper’s contribution.
  - Complexity of computing α/β sets and running all pairwise checks is not discussed. Provide pseudocode and big-O bounds.
- MaxSAT encoding and optimality: The text claims a “maximally large” set of orderings, but the provided constraints (Theorem 4) are explicitly sufficient (not necessary). Maximizing soft constraints subject to only sufficient conditions can yield a submaximal set w.r.t. true entailment. The paper should (i) clarify that the result is conservative, (ii) quantify loss (e.g., compare inferred vs. true entailed orderings on synthetic cases), and (iii) report MaxSAT overhead. Also, Clause 3 (“sufficient condition for acyclicity”) is unusual and under-explained; semantic justification is needed.
- Experimental details and fairness:
  - Time accounting: Are times to compute the ancestral closure, MaxSAT ordering projection, and edge projection included? Are GOBNILP runs provided with the same projected constraints (Table 1 says yes; Table 2 no), but does GOBNILP also benefit from precomputed family scores equally? Please report total wall-clock (end-to-end) and breakdowns.
  - Parent set preprocessing: Precomputing all family scores with no parent-size bound can be prohibitive; memory/time costs and cardinalities are not reported. This favors both methods but must be measured. Report average numbers of candidate parent sets, memory, and pruning ratios.
  - Scalability: n ≤ 20 is modest; best dynamic programming/A* results with BDeu go larger under constraints. Provide results on larger n and/or stronger constraints, or explain limitations.
  - Quality metrics: Only SHD is reported. Please add constraint satisfaction accuracy, number of inferred edge/order constraints, and ablations (edge-only vs. order-only vs. both).
  - Baselines: Consider CP (van Beek & Hoffmann), SAT-based (Cussens 2008), and constraint-based with path constraints (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos) for completeness.
  - Robustness to noisy/inconsistent constraints is not addressed.
- Notational and methodological clarity:
  - The paper alternates between minimizing and maximizing “score” without defining sign conventions.
  - How exactly are projected order constraints enforced in URLEARNING? Are they order-modular precedence constraints à la Parviainen & Koivisto (2013)? Need details.
  - Figures/tables are referenced but not sufficiently described; no algorithm boxes; key steps like CPDAG pruning test need formalization.
- Numerous formatting/reference issues:
  - Stray text (“th Conference…”, “年6月12⽇日星期五 11 16”), inconsistent footnotes, broken citations with “?\Q201x\E”, missing venues/years, inconsistent author formatting.
  - Unconventional notation X 6 Y for “X is not ancestor of Y” without initial definition of symbol 6; later used also as X 6→ Y. Define clearly.
  - Typos and grammar throughout.

Questions for authors
1. Precise statement and proof (or counterexample) for Theorem 1; clarify variable sets at partial nodes.
2. Provide formal algorithms and proofs for Theorems 2–4 and for the closure procedure, with complexity.
3. How do you avoid enumerating class(P) in practice? Any heuristics or completeness guarantees?
4. End-to-end runtime breakdowns including projection/MaxSAT; MaxSAT instance size vs. n, and solver times.
5. Impact of projection: counts of inferred edge/order constraints vs. p; ablations.
6. Behavior under inconsistent/noisy ancestral constraints.

Pros
- Addresses an important, practical constraint type (ancestry) rarely handled in optimal BN learning.
- Clever use of EC tree pruning and constraint projection to boost a strong oracle.
- Promising empirical speedups reported.

Cons
- Core theoretical claims lack proofs/rigor; some statements appear incorrect/ambiguous.
- Potential exponential subroutines (class(P) enumeration) not justified.
- Insufficient experimental reporting and ablations; limited scalability.
- Significant clarity, notation, and formatting problems.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). The ideas are interesting and potentially impactful, but the paper needs substantial revision: fix theoretical statements, add proofs/algorithms with complexity, replace exponential checks, and strengthen/rebalance experiments. Confidence: high.
