This paper addresses the problem of learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints, a challenging area due to the non-decomposable nature of these constraints. The authors build upon their previous work leveraging an oracle for decomposable scores and present a method for incorporating ancestral constraints by pruning the search space and inferring decomposable constraints. The core idea is sound – adapting the EC tree search space to accommodate these constraints and enhance the oracle’s efficiency. However, while the concepts are promising, the presentation is excessively detailed and, critically, the empirical evaluation, despite being extensive, lacks sufficient justification for the observed improvements and suffers from inconsistencies in reporting.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses an important problem:** Learning with ancestral constraints is practically relevant and theoretically challenging.
*   **Solid technical foundation:** The adaptation of the EC tree and the concept of projecting constraints are well-motivated.
*   **Empirical evaluation:** The authors conduct a relatively extensive empirical evaluation, comparing their approach against ILP.
*   **Theoretical grounding:** The theorems regarding constraint projection provide a theoretical basis for the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Excessive detail and redundancy:** The introduction is overly verbose, repeating concepts multiple times. Sections 2 and 3 contain a large amount of background information that could be streamlined, hindering readability.
*   **Minor methodological inconsistencies:** The use of "ancestral constraints" feels somewhat loose. The difference between positive and negative constraints isn’t always consistently applied, and the discussion of transitivity feels incomplete.
*   **Incomplete justification of empirical setup:** The choice of networks (ALARM, ANDES, etc.) is not adequately justified. Why these networks were selected, and how they represent different characteristics of the problem, is missing. The explanation for choosing particular sub-networks is also vague.
*   **Reporting inconsistencies and lack of statistical significance:** Table 1 consistently shows significant speedups, which is excellent. However, the reported speedups are often given as "orders of magnitude" without providing concrete numbers (e.g., “orders-of-magnitude improvements”). More importantly, the reported results lack standard statistical significance testing. Are the observed differences statistically significant, or are they due to random fluctuations?
*   **Overemphasis on implementation details:** A significant portion of the paper focuses on the implementation (e.g., the EVASOLVER and GOBNILP). This detail distracts from the core contributions.
*   **Lack of discussion on scalability limitations:** While the authors demonstrate scalability up to 20 variables, they don't sufficiently discuss the limitations of the approach. The complexity of the constraint projection, particularly with MaxSAT, is not thoroughly analyzed.
*   **Formatting errors:** Several minor formatting issues exist, such as inconsistent spacing and citation styles. The note regarding the 2016 NIPS conference date is awkwardly placed.

**Specific Concerns & Questions:**

*   The claim that ILP approaches haven’t been evaluated with ancestral constraints is inaccurate. Cussens (2008), cited in the paper, *explicitly* details using ILP with acyclicity constraints based on ancestral relations.
*   In Section 5.2, the discussion of inferring ordering constraints feels incomplete. The statement about the easy-hard-easy trend in Table 3 is also vague – it needs further explanation.
*   The use of an "equivalent sample size" of 1 for BDeu is unusual and should be justified.
*   The theorem statements throughout the paper are not always clearly linked to the preceding text.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The problem is important and challenging.
*   The proposed approach is technically sound.
*   The empirical results, despite limitations, suggest a significant improvement over ILP.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The paper is overly detailed and lacks focus.
*   The empirical evaluation lacks statistical rigor and clear justification.
*   The claims regarding novelty are overstated given related work.
*   The numerous minor flaws (formatting, inconsistencies, incomplete explanations) detract from the overall quality.

**Overall Assessment:**

While the core ideas of this paper are promising, the execution is flawed. The overabundance of detail, coupled with the lack of statistical rigor and inconsistencies in reporting, significantly diminishes the impact of the work. The authors' attention to detail, while commendable in some respects, has paradoxically resulted in a paper that feels unnecessarily complex and difficult to assess. I believe the paper, in its current form, does not meet the standards for acceptance at NIPS. A major revision addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly a more focused presentation and a statistically sound empirical evaluation, would be necessary for reconsideration.

**Recommendation:** Reject.
