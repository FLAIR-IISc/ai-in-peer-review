Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses optimal Bayesian network (BN) structure learning in the presence of non-decomposable ancestral constraints (X is/is not an ancestor of Y). Mainstream exact score-based methods (order-graph DP/A*, ILP/CP) typically assume decomposable scores and constraints, which makes ancestral constraints awkward. Building on the BN graph/EC tree framework of Chen et al. (2015, 2016), the authors show how to incorporate ancestral constraints by pruning the EC-tree search space and by “projecting” non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge-presence/absence and ordering constraints that can be handed to a decomposable-score oracle (URLearning) to yield powerful heuristics. They give conditions to infer negative and positive edge constraints from a closed set of ancestral constraints (Theorems 2–3), and formulate a MaxSAT problem to infer a large, safe set of topological ordering constraints entailed by ancestral constraints (with sufficient correctness conditions; Theorem 4). Empirically, they show substantial (often orders-of-magnitude) speedups over the leading ILP system GOBNILP on standard BN benchmarks under various numbers of variables, data sizes, and proportions of known ancestral relations, and they analyze the impact on accuracy (SHD) and search hardness.

The work relates to: (i) score-based optimal BN learning via order-graph DP/A* (Koivisto & Sood; Silander & Myllymäki; Yuan & Malone), which cannot directly enforce ancestral constraints; (ii) ILP/CP encodings that can express non-decomposable constraints (Jaakkola et al.; Cussens; van Beek & Hoffmann), but have not been evaluated for ancestral constraints; and (iii) constraint-based methods and priors based on path beliefs (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos). The paper extends the EC-tree framework to support pruning with ancestral constraints and contributes new inference procedures to derive decomposable constraints from ancestral ones.

Strengths
- Addresses a practically important class of background knowledge (ancestral constraints) that prior optimal score-based methods do not support well.
- Solid conceptual integration: prune the EC tree using constraint satisfaction on CPDAGs, and strengthen the heuristic oracle via projected decomposable constraints.
- Clear theorems for when negative/positive edge constraints are entailed (Theorems 2–3), and a principled MaxSAT formulation to harvest a large safe set of ordering constraints (Theorem 4).
- Strong empirical performance: consistent and often orders-of-magnitude speedups over GOBNILP; informative analysis of easy–hard–easy behavior as constraints increase; accuracy improvements with modest amounts of constraints.
- The approach is exact (finds optimal DAG under constraints) and builds on widely used components (URLearning, GOBNILP, public MaxSAT solver).

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Proofs and completeness. Several key results (especially Theorems 3–4 and the sufficiency clause for acyclicity) are stated without proofs. The unusual sufficient condition in clause (3) requires justification. The MaxSAT-based ordering projection is acknowledged as sufficient but not complete; the paper occasionally uses “maximally large set,” which overstates what is guaranteed by sufficient conditions alone. Please clarify the gap between maximization under the encoding vs. the true maximum set entailed by A.
- CPDAG pruning test. When simple tests are inconclusive, the method enumerates all DAGs in a CPDAG to check feasibility under ancestral constraints. This can be exponential. Are there known polynomial-time graphical criteria for ancestor reachability in CPDAGs that could avoid enumeration? A discussion or improved test would strengthen the practicality guarantees.
- Experimental scope and fairness. 
  - Constraints are simulated from ground-truth and are noiseless; robustness to misspecified or conflicting constraints is not evaluated. This is important for practical use.
  - Only ILP (GOBNILP) is compared; no CP-based baseline despite being mentioned as able to encode such constraints.
  - Indegree is unrestricted, which is known to disadvantage ILP baselines; a controlled comparison with indegree caps would be informative.
  - Scalability is demonstrated to about n=20; it would help to report node/edge expansions and memory footprints to contextualize limits.
- Missing details.
  - Complexity of the edge-projection algorithm (with α(·), β(·)) and the closure process for ancestral constraints is not analyzed; provide worst-case bounds.
  - Overhead of MaxSAT and edge projection vs. the observed speedups; ablation showing “no projection,” “edge only,” and “ordering only” would be useful.
  - How exactly are ordering constraints integrated into URLearning (e.g., precedence/parent set filtering), and are all projected constraints supported?
- Clarity and presentation. Some notation and definitions (e.g., “partially directed path” test on CPDAGs) would benefit from precise references. Several references appear garbled/incomplete, and figures/tables are essential for comprehension but are only briefly described.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with a compelling algorithmic design and solid experiments. However, some theoretical claims (ordering constraints sufficiency, CPDAG pruning checks) need full proofs or stronger justification, and the worst-case complexity of key subroutines should be discussed.

Clarity
Generally well organized and readable, but important algorithmic details and proofs are omitted; references/formatting need cleanup. Adding pseudocode for projection procedures and precise definitions/lemmas for CPDAG tests would improve reproducibility.

Originality
The integration of ancestral constraints into EC-tree search and the projection of non-decomposable constraints into decomposable edge/order constraints (especially the MaxSAT formulation) appear novel and practically meaningful relative to prior optimal BN learning work.

Significance
High practical significance for users who have causal/temporal prior knowledge. The empirical gains over a strong ILP baseline are substantial. The ideas (pruning in EC trees; projection to empower oracles) can likely transfer to other non-decomposable priors/constraints.

Suggestions and questions for the authors
- Provide proofs or an appendix for Theorems 1–4 and the MaxSAT encoding (especially clause (3)), and clarify completeness vs. sufficiency.
- Replace CPDAG enumeration with a polynomial-time criterion for possible ancestor relationships in CPDAGs if available, or bound when enumeration is triggered and how large classes were in practice.
- Evaluate robustness to noisy/misleading constraints; include detection/diagnosis of inconsistent constraint sets.
- Add ablations for edge vs. ordering projection, and report the number of projected constraints and MaxSAT/runtime overhead.
- Compare also with a CP baseline if feasible, and with indegree limits to control for known ILP weaknesses.
- Detail how ordering constraints are enforced in the oracle and how they prune family candidates.
- Improve reference formatting and fix typos; include figures/tables that are referenced.

Overall recommendation
Weak accept. The paper tackles an important gap with a well-motivated, effective approach, and shows strong empirical benefits. With stronger theoretical justification of the ordering projection and more clarity on CPDAG pruning and experimental robustness, this would be a clear accept.
