

# Review of "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"

This paper proposes a method for learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints, which are non-decomposable constraints specifying cause-effect relationships between variables. The authors build on Chen et al.'s (2015, 2016) EC tree search space for non-decomposable scores, adapting it to handle ancestral constraints by pruning the search space and projecting these non-decomposable constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints that can be used to improve the efficiency of an oracle (a system that learns with decomposable scores).

The paper makes several claims: (1) ancestral constraints can significantly reduce the search space, (2) their approach is orders-of-magnitude faster than ILP-based approaches (GOBNILP) when handling ancestral constraints, and (3) a small number of ancestral constraints (10-25%) can have a similar impact on model quality as substantially increasing the dataset size.

**Strengths:**
The paper demonstrates methodological soundness in its theoretical approach to handling ancestral constraints. The theorems regarding constraint projection (Theorems 2-4) provide solid theoretical grounding for their approach. The EC tree framework is well-motivated as a more efficient search space than the BN graph, and the pruning strategy for ancestral constraints appears logically sound. The empirical evaluation is conducted on standard Bayesian network benchmarks, and the authors report consistent improvements over GOBNILP across multiple experimental conditions.

**Weaknesses:**
The primary weakness is the limited scope of the baseline comparison. The paper only compares against ILP-based approaches (GOBNILP), but fails to compare against the most established methods for Bayesian network structure learning, which are based on order graphs (e.g., Koivisto and Sood, 2004; Parviainen and Koivisto, 2013). These approaches are specifically designed for decomposable scores and are the standard in the field. The authors claim their method is orders-of-magnitude faster than ILP approaches, but they don't provide a meaningful comparison against the state-of-the-art for structure learning with decomposable scores.

The paper also lacks sufficient detail on the practical significance of the claimed improvements. While the paper reports "orders-of-magnitude improvements" in Tables 1-3, it doesn't provide enough context about the absolute runtimes or how these improvements translate to real-world applications. The paper doesn't address how the method scales when the number of ancestral constraints increases, or how sensitive the performance is to the quality of the constraints (e.g., if they contain errors).

The paper's novelty is overstated. The EC tree was introduced in Chen et al. (2016), and this paper essentially applies it to a new constraint type. The authors cite Chen et al. but don't clearly distinguish their new contribution from what was already proposed in that work. The paper also fails to adequately address the limitations of their approach, such as the computational cost of the constraint projection step, which could potentially offset the claimed efficiency gains.

Most significantly, the paper doesn't provide evidence that ancestral constraints are commonly available in practice. While the paper mentions "background knowledge," it doesn't demonstrate how these constraints are obtained in real-world applications or how often they would be available with sufficient accuracy to be useful.

**Recommendation:**
While the paper presents a theoretically sound approach that builds on previous work, the empirical evaluation is insufficiently rigorous for a NIPS paper. The lack of comparison against the most relevant baselines (order graph approaches) and the overstated claims about the practical significance of the improvements are major concerns. The paper would be significantly strengthened by:

1. Comparing against order graph-based approaches (the standard for decomposable score structure learning)
2. Providing more detailed analysis of the absolute runtimes and scalability
3. Discussing the practical limitations and requirements for obtaining ancestral constraints
4. Clearly distinguishing the novelty from previous work by Chen et al.

The paper's claim that "a relatively small number of ancestral constraints (say 10%-25%) can have a similar impact on the quality of the observed network as increasing the amount of data available" is particularly concerning without evidence of how often such constraints are available with the required accuracy in real-world applications.

Given the skepticism I hold toward new approaches without thorough validation against established baselines, I cannot recommend acceptance in its current form. The paper would require substantial revision to address these concerns before it could be considered for publication at NIPS. The methodological contribution is sound, but the empirical validation is insufficient to support the claims made, and the novelty is not sufficiently established relative to previous work.
