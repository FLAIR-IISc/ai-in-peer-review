

# Peer Review: "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"

## Summary
This paper addresses a significant gap in Bayesian network structure learning: how to efficiently incorporate non-decomposable ancestral constraints (e.g., "X is an ancestor of Y") into optimal structure learning algorithms. The authors build upon Chen et al.'s recent work on EC trees (a search space for non-decomposable scores) and extend it to handle ancestral constraints. They present a sound pruning method for the EC tree based on ancestral constraints and develop a novel technique to project these non-decomposable constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints that can be used to empower an oracle (a system that optimizes decomposable scores). The authors empirically demonstrate that their approach is orders-of-magnitude faster than ILP-based methods (like GOBNILP) for learning with ancestral constraints, while maintaining model accuracy. This work extends Chen et al.'s EC tree framework and addresses a practical problem that has been largely overlooked in the literature.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Soundness and Novelty**: The paper makes a clear contribution by addressing a fundamental limitation in structure learning: the inability of most approaches to handle non-decomposable ancestral constraints. The authors' theoretical results (Theorems 1-4) are well-justified and provide a sound foundation for their method. The constraint projection technique (Section 5) is particularly novel and valuable.

2. **Empirical Rigor**: The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed. The authors use standard Bayesian network benchmarks (ALARM, ANDES, etc.), vary key parameters (n, N, p), and report results averaged over 50 different datasets. The comparison with GOBNILP (an ILP-based approach) is particularly compelling, showing consistent orders-of-magnitude speedups.

3. **Practical Impact**: The paper demonstrates that even a small number of ancestral constraints (10-25%) can have a similar impact on model accuracy as increasing training data by 4-16x. This is a significant practical insight for practitioners who have domain knowledge but limited data.

4. **State-of-the-Art Comparison**: The authors provide a thorough comparison with existing approaches, noting that while ILP and CP can theoretically represent ancestral constraints, they haven't been evaluated for this specific problem. The paper fills this important gap.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Discussion of Constraints**: The paper doesn't fully explore the limitations of the constraint projection method. For example, it doesn't address cases where the projected constraints might be too restrictive, potentially excluding valid networks that satisfy the ancestral constraints but not the projected edge/ordering constraints.

2. **Scalability Beyond 20 Variables**: While the paper shows scalability to 20 variables (comparable to state-of-the-art), it doesn't discuss how the method would scale to larger networks (e.g., 50+ variables) that are common in real-world applications. The "easy-hard-easy" trend observed in Table 3 suggests potential challenges at certain constraint percentages.

3. **Oracle Dependency**: The performance of the proposed method heavily depends on the oracle (URLEARNING) being effective. The paper doesn't provide a thorough analysis of how different oracles might affect performance, which could be important for practitioners selecting implementations.

4. **Comparative Analysis with Other Methods**: While the paper compares with ILP, it doesn't discuss how their approach might compare with recent constraint-based methods (e.g., Borboudakis and Tsamardinos' path constraints) that also handle ancestral constraints, though for different learning paradigms.

## Evaluation Against Review Criteria

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims and comprehensive empirical evaluation. The authors carefully analyze both strengths (speedups) and limitations (constraint projection limitations, scalability). The work represents a complete piece of research, not a position paper.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The EC tree concept is well-explained with Figure 1, and the constraint projection method is logically presented. Some technical sections (e.g., Section 5.2 on MaxSAT reduction) might be challenging for readers without background in constraint programming, but the paper provides sufficient context.

**Originality**: The paper makes a clear contribution by addressing a problem that has been largely overlooked in optimal Bayesian network structure learning. The constraint projection technique is novel, and the paper effectively demonstrates how this enables efficient handling of ancestral constraints. The work builds on Chen et al.'s EC tree framework but extends it in a significant way to handle non-decomposable constraints.

**Significance**: The results have clear practical significance. The demonstrated speedups (orders-of-magnitude) make the approach feasible for real-world applications, and the finding that small amounts of domain knowledge can substantially improve model accuracy (equivalent to large data increases) is highly valuable. The paper advances the state of the art in structure learning with non-decomposable constraints.

## Overall Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to Bayesian network structure learning. The problem addressed is important, the solution is novel and theoretically sound, and the empirical results are compelling. The paper clearly demonstrates the practical impact of incorporating ancestral constraints, showing both efficiency gains and accuracy improvements.

The main weaknesses are the limited discussion of constraint projection limitations and the lack of analysis for larger networks beyond 20 variables. However, these are relatively minor compared to the paper's substantial contributions.

I recommend acceptance. This work is well-suited for NIPS as it addresses a fundamental problem in probabilistic modeling with a theoretically sound and empirically validated solution, and it makes a clear contribution to the field of structure learning.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Consider adding a discussion of the constraint projection limitations and potential workarounds.
2. Include a brief analysis of how the method would scale to larger networks (50+ variables) or discuss potential extensions for larger problems.
3. Provide a short comparison with constraint-based methods like PC that also handle ancestral constraints (though for a different learning paradigm).
4. Clarify the relationship between the "maximum set of ancestral constraints" and the constraint projection method in Section 5.1.

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a clear contribution to the field. It advances the state of the art in Bayesian network structure learning with non-decomposable constraints and provides significant practical value.
