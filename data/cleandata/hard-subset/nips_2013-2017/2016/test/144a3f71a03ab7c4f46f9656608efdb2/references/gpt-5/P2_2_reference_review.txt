Summary
The paper addresses exact Bayesian network (BN) structure learning under hard ancestral (path) constraints, which are non-decomposable and thus not directly supported by mainstream dynamic programming or A* methods that rely on decomposable scores. Building on BN graphs and EC trees (Chen et al., 2015; 2016), the authors propose to search over CPDAGs using A* while pruning nodes that cannot satisfy the given ancestral constraints. To keep the heuristic strong, they “project” non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge constraints and ordering constraints to feed to a decomposable-score oracle (URLearning). They give tests for pruning CPDAGs using path information, characterize when negative and positive edge constraints are entailed by ancestral constraints (Theorems 2–3), and propose a MaxSAT encoding to infer a large, safe set of ordering constraints (Theorem 4). Experiments on benchmark BNs with synthetic data/constraints show orders-of-magnitude speedups over the ILP method GOBNILP, and demonstrate the practical value of projection.

Relation to prior work: The work is positioned against ILP/CP approaches that can encode non-decomposable constraints but may not scale as well, and extends the non-decomposable-score search spaces (BN/EC trees) to handle hard ancestral constraints. It also connects to constraint-based use of path constraints and to soft priors on paths (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos) but targets exact score-based learning.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The approach to prune EC-tree nodes via path feasibility in CPDAGs is sound. The entailment conditions for edge constraints (Thm 2–3) are crisp and intuitive; if proofs are correct, this provides a complete characterization for edges. The MaxSAT formulation for order constraints is intentionally sufficient but not necessarily complete; Theorem 4 is presented as sufficient conditions, which preserves admissibility of the heuristic. One caveat: the statement of Theorem 1 (“P satisfies A iff its descendants satisfy A”) appears misstated; likely the intended statement is monotonicity of violation (if P violates A, so do all descendants). Please correct/clarify.
- Experimental support: Comparisons against GOBNILP are appropriate and show large speedups, especially when projected constraints are used. The easy–hard–easy trend with increasing constraint density is plausible and informative. Accuracy (structural Hamming distance) improves with more data and more constraints, reinforcing the practical value of constraints.
- Missing analyses: The paper omits runtime/overhead characterization for (i) enumerating class(P) when CPDAG tests are inconclusive, and (ii) MaxSAT solving for order projection. It would help to report how often exponential CPDAG enumeration is triggered and its cost, and the size/time of MaxSAT instances. Robustness to inconsistent or noisy constraints is not addressed (the paper assumes a “maximum” closed, consistent set A). A brief discussion or experiment with contradictory or erroneous constraints would strengthen the paper.

Clarity
- Overall well-organized with a clear narrative: search space, pruning, projection, and experiments.
- Some clarity issues:
  - Theorem 1 statement is likely incorrect or poorly phrased.
  - The CPDAG path tests: define precisely “no partially directed paths” and the algorithm used. The fallback “exhaustively enumerate class(P)” can be exponential; state any practical heuristics/limits.
  - Section 5.1 says details of closure rules for ancestral constraints are “omitted for space”—provide at least a concise list in the appendix/supplement.
  - The manuscript has noticeable typographical and formatting glitches (e.g., stray lines, broken references). Please clean up.
- Reproducibility: Datasets and solver names are given; parent-set scores are precomputed; however, code availability for EC-tree + heuristic + projections is not stated. Parameters for MaxSAT solver and any time limits should be reported. Please include pseudocode for edge-projection algorithm and the MaxSAT encoding.

Originality
- Novelty lies in: (i) integrating hard ancestral constraints into EC-tree search with sound pruning, (ii) an exact edge-projection theory (Thm 2–3) to derive all entailed edge constraints from path constraints, and (iii) a MaxSAT-based method to safely derive many ordering constraints. While ILP/CP can encode ancestral constraints, this paper departs by using a non-decomposable search space guided by a decomposable oracle and by systematically projecting constraints. This is a meaningful and non-trivial combination of ideas, with new theory and an effective algorithmic pipeline.

Significance
- Ancestral constraints are practically valuable (domain knowledge in causal discovery). The proposed method shows strong practical gains vs. a leading ILP baseline and demonstrates that projecting non-decomposable constraints to decomposable ones can dramatically improve exact structure learning. The ideas should be useful to researchers working on exact BN learning and could inspire hybrid methods in other non-decomposable settings. Scope is currently up to ~20 variables in experiments; nonetheless, for exact learning with hard constraints, this is competitive.

Specific strengths
- Clear problem motivation and relevance.
- Sound pruning within EC-tree; admissible heuristic via oracle; careful use of projection to tighten the heuristic.
- Theoretical characterization for edge entailment.
- Strong empirical gains over GOBNILP; informative ablations with/without projection; accuracy analysis.

Specific weaknesses and questions
- Clarify/correct Theorem 1. Provide proofs or at least proof sketches for Theorems 2–4.
- Quantify cost of CPDAG enumeration and MaxSAT solving; how often do they dominate runtime?
- Discuss handling of inconsistent/noisy constraints (detection, diagnosis, or soft relaxation), and whether the MaxSAT framework could be extended to soft constraints.
- Baselines: any comparison to MaxSAT-based BN learning (Cussens 2008) with path constraints encoded? A CP-based baseline (van Beek & Hoffmann) would also strengthen the case.
- Implementation details: how exactly are derived edge/order constraints injected into the oracle (parent-set pruning, ordering graph constraints)? Does this preserve heuristic admissibility (it should if the oracle completes under a superset of constraints)?

Pros
- Addresses an important, practically motivated gap.
- Solid theory for edge entailment; safe and effective order projection.
- Strong empirical performance; clear ablations.

Cons
- Some theoretical and clarity gaps (Theorem 1 wording, omitted details).
- Limited analysis of potential bottlenecks (CPDAG enumeration, MaxSAT).
- No treatment of inconsistent/noisy constraints; limited baselines.

Recommendation
Lean accept. The paper advances exact BN learning under hard ancestral constraints with a principled and effective approach, supported by new theory and convincing experiments. Addressing the noted clarity issues and adding small empirical analyses of projection overhead would further strengthen it.
