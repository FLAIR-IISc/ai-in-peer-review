Summary
The paper tackles optimal Bayesian network structure learning with non-decomposable ancestral constraints. It builds on the BN graph/EC tree framework of Chen et al. (2015/2016), which searches directly in DAG/CPDAG spaces with an oracle that optimizes decomposable scores. The key contributions are: (1) pruning EC-tree nodes (CPDAGs) that cannot satisfy ancestral constraints; (2) projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and topological ordering constraints to “empower” the oracle; (3) an algorithm (with stated theorems) for inferring entailed edge constraints and a MaxSAT reduction to infer a large set of entailed ordering constraints; and (4) empirical comparisons against ILP (GOBNILP), reporting orders-of-magnitude speedups and showing that modest amounts of ancestral constraints can improve accuracy.

Strengths
- Addresses a relevant and underexplored practical need: integrating ancestral (path) constraints in exact score-based BN learning.
- Sensible hybridization: use a non-decomposable search space (EC tree) but leverage a decomposable-score oracle augmented with projected constraints to tighten heuristics.
- The projection idea is potentially impactful in practice; the edge-constraint inference is intuitively motivated.
- Experiments consider multiple benchmarks, varying n, N, and fraction p of ancestral constraints; discuss an easy–hard–easy trend and accuracy improvements.

Weaknesses (technical correctness and rigor)
- Theorem 1 appears incorrect as stated: “In an EC tree, a CPDAG P satisfies ancestral constraints A iff its descendants satisfy A.” The forward direction is false in general: a CPDAG can satisfy A while some descendants (which add variables/edges) violate A. The paper elsewhere only needs the monotonicity that if P violates A then all its descendants violate A; the biconditional is a serious error and undermines the “sound and complete” pruning claim as written.
- Pruning test for CPDAGs: beyond two simple checks, the method “exhaustively enumerates the structures of class(P)” to decide satisfaction. This can be exponential and is at odds with the efficiency claims. No bounds or practical mitigations are provided, and no empirical analysis of how often enumeration is triggered.
- “Sound and complete pruning” is claimed but no proof is provided; given the flawed Theorem 1, the claim is not substantiated.
- Edge-constraint inference (Theorem 2/3): The closure rules for producing a “maximum set” of ancestral constraints are “omitted for space,” yet are required for the theorems. The necessity/sufficiency of Theorem 3 (for positive edge entailment) is not convincingly justified; the condition appears overly strong in practice (it disallows any intervening Z that could be on a path), which may yield few positive edges in realistic settings. No formal proof is provided.
- MaxSAT encoding for ordering constraints is underspecified and questionable:
  - Clause (3) (“sufficient condition for acyclicity”) is an unusual disjunction mixing positive and negative ancestry relations; no proof or intuition is provided that it suffices, and it likely excludes many valid orderings. The conditions in Theorem 4 are very restrictive and appear only sufficient, not necessary; the link between the MaxSAT objective and maximality of O subject to entailment is not established.
  - The result (Theorem 4) again lacks proof and only gives sufficient conditions; no guarantees that the MaxSAT solution is maximal under entailment, or that it never eliminates valid DAGs in G(A).
- No complexity analysis for the proposed projection algorithms or EC-tree pruning with enumeration. Given the centrality of efficiency, this is a notable omission.

Weaknesses (empirical)
- Tables and figures are missing from the submission (only textual references), so the “orders-of-magnitude” claims cannot be verified. No raw numbers, variances, or statistical summaries are shown.
- Details critical for reproducibility are sparse: no code availability, no parent-set size limits (stated), but no details on maximum indegree in precomputation, number of candidate parent sets, or how ordering/edge constraints are passed to URLEARNING. No ablations of projection components (edge vs ordering), nor breakdown of time spent in MaxSAT or CPDAG enumeration.
- Scope: n up to 20 nodes is modest for exact BDeu settings and comparable to prior work; the paper claims scalability but without convincing quantitative evidence.
- Fairness of comparison to ILP: GOBNILP’s configuration and constraints are not fully specified; projected constraints are sometimes provided as “redundant constraints” but their exact form and benefit to ILP are unclear.

Clarity and presentation issues
- Numerous formatting and citation problems: stray lines (“th Conference on…”, Chinese characters/date), misnumbered/inline footnotes (1–4), missing figures, inconsistent notation (DAG graph vs BN graph), and many references with placeholder fields (e.g., “? \Q2015\E”). This significantly impedes readability.
- Several key algorithmic steps are “omitted for space,” including closure rules for ancestral constraints and proofs of theorems.
- Notational overload and ambiguity (e.g., use of α(X), β(Y), partially directed paths) would benefit from formal definitions and examples.

Originality and relation to prior work
- The use of EC trees with an oracle is based on Chen et al.; applying them to handle ancestral constraints + projection to decomposable constraints is a reasonable extension. Related work on path constraints in constraint-based learning is cited, and ILP/CP are acknowledged. However, the paper should more carefully contrast its projection approach with precedence/order-modular constraints (e.g., Parviainen & Koivisto 2013) and prior MaxSAT encodings (Cussens 2008), and clarify novelty.

Significance
- If technically correct and efficiently implemented, the approach could be useful. However, the current submission lacks theoretical rigor (incorrect/unsupported claims), has potential exponential worst cases without mitigation, and presents incomplete empirical evidence. As is, the contribution is not ready for publication.

Suggestions for improvement
- Correct Theorem 1 and rigorously prove pruning soundness; avoid exhaustive enumeration by using known CPDAG reachability tests and Meek’s rules to decide existence of a directed path under consistent extensions.
- Provide full, proved rules for ancestral closure; supply proofs for Theorems 2–4, or clearly state them as sufficient-only with tightness discussion.
- Clarify and justify the MaxSAT encoding; prove that the produced O is entailed and maximally large under entailment (or explain trade-offs).
- Include complete experimental tables/figures, ablations (no projection / only edges / only orderings), time breakdowns, and fairness details for ILP. Release code or at least pseudocode and parameter settings.
- Fix formatting, references, and notation throughout.

Recommendation: Reject
While the problem is important and the high-level idea is promising, the paper contains technical errors, missing proofs, algorithmic steps omitted for space, potential exponential subroutines, and incomplete empirical evidence. With substantial revision and rigor, this work could become a solid contribution.

Confidence: High
