Summary
The paper addresses optimal Bayesian network (BN) structure learning with non-decomposable background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints (positive: X is an ancestor of Y; negative: X is not an ancestor of Y). Mainstream exact methods assume decomposable scores and typically only support decomposable priors/constraints (edge presence/absence or order-modular constraints). The authors build on the BN-graph and EC-tree search spaces (Chen et al. 2015, 2016) to directly search over DAGs/CPDAGs with non-decomposable constraints by pruning nodes that cannot satisfy the ancestral constraints. They further “project” non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints to tighten the heuristic oracle (URLearning) used within A* on the EC tree. Edge constraints are inferred via closure properties over α(X) (forced ancestors of X) and β(X) (forced descendants), yielding necessary-and-sufficient conditions for entailed negative and positive edges (Theorems 2 and 3). Ordering constraints are inferred by solving a MaxSAT problem that selects a maximal set of pairwise orderings entailed by the ancestral constraints while ensuring acyclicity via sufficient conditions (Theorem 4). Empirically, on subgraphs of standard BN benchmarks with synthetic constraints sampled from ground truth, the proposed approach is reported to be orders-of-magnitude faster than ILP (GOBNILP), especially when augmented with projected constraints; scalability is shown up to ~20 variables with BDeu scores.

Relation to prior work
The paper clearly positions itself with respect to (i) exact decomposable-score learners (dynamic programming, A* on order graph), (ii) ILP/CP approaches that can encode global constraints, and (iii) path-constraint priors in constraint-based or greedy score-based learning (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos). It extends Chen et al.’s BN-graph/EC-tree framework to handle non-decomposable constraints and contributes novel projection algorithms that turn global constraints into local ones that standard oracles can exploit.

Strengths
- Problem importance: Many applications have domain knowledge that is naturally ancestral (causal) and not easily expressible as local edge or order constraints. Supporting such constraints in optimal structure learning is valuable.
- Methodological contribution: Using EC trees with pruning is a natural and sound way to incorporate non-decomposable constraints, and the projection of ancestral constraints into edge/order constraints is insightful. The negative-edge entailment (Thm 2) and the positive-edge test (Thm 3) give simple, implementable checks; the MaxSAT formulation provides a principled way to maximize usable order constraints.
- Empirical results: The comparison to a strong ILP baseline (GOBNILP) and the ablation with/without projection show large practical gains. The easy–hard–easy trend with respect to the number of constraints is informative; the improvement in model accuracy with even modest amounts of constraints is noteworthy.
- Practicality: The approach preserves the benefits of powerful decomposable-score oracles while enabling global constraints through pruning and projection.

Weaknesses and questions
- Theoretical details and clarity:
  - Theorem 1 is stated unclearly. As written (“P satisfies A iff its descendants satisfy A, both over the same set of variables”), it seems either trivial or possibly incorrect. I believe the intended monotonicity is: if P violates A then all descendants do; if P satisfies A then all descendants also satisfy A for constraints restricted to the variables already present in P. Please clarify the precise variable sets and give a proof sketch.
  - The closure rules that make A “maximum” (including inferring negative ancestral constraints to avoid cycles) are critical to the “iff” statements in Theorems 2–3, but are omitted. Please provide the exact rule set and complexity, or move them to an appendix.
  - The completeness of Theorem 3 (positive edge entailment) is nontrivial. A proof or more detailed intuition is needed, as the condition must exclude every alternative X→…→Y route via any Z not in α(X)∪β(Y). Are there corner cases with v-structures or compelled orientations in CPDAGs that could break the characterization?
  - For ordering constraints: the “sufficient condition for acyclicity” used as a hard constraint in MaxSAT is unusual and not obviously sufficient. A proof sketch or references are needed. Also define precisely what “closed set of ordering constraints” means in Theorem 4 (transitive closure?).
- Pruning test cost: When simple tests are inconclusive, the method “exhaustively enumerates” class(P), which can be exponential. How often does this happen in practice? Can SAT-based feasibility checks (without full enumeration) be used to test whether any DAG in class(P) can satisfy A?
- Experimental scope:
  - Only ILP is compared; CP (e.g., van Beek & Hoffmann) or SAT-based exact learners are natural baselines for global constraints.
  - All constraints are noise-free and sampled from the ground truth. Robustness to inconsistent or noisy constraints is not studied; in practice, elicited causal knowledge is often imperfect.
  - Scalability remains modest (n≈20 without parent-set bounds). While comparable to prior exact BDeu learners, it would be useful to report wall-time/memory scaling curves and the distribution of time spent in pruning vs oracle vs MaxSAT.
  - More detail on how projected constraints are injected into the oracle (e.g., forbidding parent sets, restricting predecessor sets) would help reproducibility.

Quality
Overall technically sound, but some key claims (Thms 1–4) lack proofs or precise conditions; the enumeration fallback could be a bottleneck. Experiments are carefully designed and support the main claims, though broader baselines and robustness tests are desirable.

Clarity
Generally readable, but several formal statements need tightening. There are formatting glitches in references and a few typos. Please define “maximum set of ancestral constraints,” “closed set of ordering constraints,” and formalize the CPDAG path tests. Providing pseudocode for projection would help.

Originality
Using EC trees with pruning for ancestral constraints and the projection of ancestral constraints into local edge/order constraints (especially the MaxSAT formulation) appear novel and practically useful.

Significance
If validated and made robust, this can become a go-to way to incorporate causal background knowledge into exact BN structure learning, with sizable runtime benefits over ILP. The projection ideas may be independently useful.

Pros
- Addresses a relevant, under-served need (global constraints in exact BN learning).
- Elegant integration of non-decomposable constraints via EC-tree pruning and projection.
- Strong empirical gains over a widely used ILP baseline.
- Clear practical implications.

Cons
- Some theoretical statements are underspecified; proofs/conditions missing.
- Potential exponential fallback (enumerating class(P)) left unanalyzed.
- Limited baselines; no noisy/inconsistent constraints.
- Modest scalability.

Recommendation
Borderline accept leaning accept. With improved clarity/proofs and broader experiments (including robustness), this would be a solid contribution.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide proofs or detailed sketches for Theorems 1–4 and formal definitions of closure conditions.
- Replace enumeration of class(P) with a SAT/ILP feasibility check when pruning CPDAGs, if possible.
- Evaluate sensitivity to incorrect constraints and include CP/SAT baselines.
- Clarify how projected constraints are injected into the oracle; release code or pseudocode.
