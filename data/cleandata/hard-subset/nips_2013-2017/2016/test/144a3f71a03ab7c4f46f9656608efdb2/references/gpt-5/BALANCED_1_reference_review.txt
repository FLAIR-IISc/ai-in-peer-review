Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles optimal Bayesian network (BN) structure learning when given background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints (X is/is not an ancestor of Y). Most exact score-based methods assume decomposability and therefore support only edge- or order-modular constraints. The authors build on the BN-graph/EC-tree shortest-path perspective (Chen et al., 2015; Chen et al., 2016), where A* search over DAGs or equivalence classes is guided by an “oracle” that solves decomposable subproblems. They show how to (i) prune the EC-tree search space using ancestral constraints, and (ii) “project” non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints to empower the oracle. Edge entailments are characterized by necessary-and-sufficient criteria (Theorems 2–3). Ordering constraints are obtained via a MaxSAT formulation with sufficient conditions (Theorem 4). Empirically, their EC-tree approach with projections substantially outperforms an ILP baseline (GOBNILP) across several UCI-style BN benchmarks (ALARM, ANDES, CHILD, CPCS54, HEPAR2), and the authors study the impact of constraint density on runtime and accuracy.

This connects to: dynamic programming and heuristic search exploiting decomposable scores (Koivisto & Sood, Silander & Myllymäki, Yuan & Malone), ILP and CP formulations that can in principle encode non-decomposable constraints (Jaakkola et al., Bartlett & Cussens; van Beek & Hoffmann), and constraint-based use of path constraints and priors (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos). To my knowledge, this is the first exact score-based method that integrates ancestral constraints within an EC-tree search and systematically projects them into decomposable constraints to tighten the oracle.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and well-scoped problem: ancestral constraints are practical and non-decomposable, yet valuable both for accuracy and efficiency.
- Sound pruning of EC-tree nodes using ancestral constraints; the pruning condition is natural and correct (Theorem 1).
- Novel and useful projection of ancestral constraints into edge constraints with necessary and sufficient conditions (Theorems 2–3); this is technically neat and practically impactful.
- Practical MaxSAT-based pipeline to infer a large, safe set of ordering constraints; improves heuristic quality for the oracle.
- Solid empirical gains: often orders-of-magnitude faster than ILP (with and without projection), and ablation showing projections matter.
- Informative study of the easy–hard–easy trend as constraint density increases; accuracy improvements (SHD) vs data size illustrate the value of domain knowledge.

Weaknesses
- The EC-tree pruning test falls back on enumerating class(P) when simple path tests are inconclusive. Enumeration can be exponential; the paper lacks analysis of how often this occurs and its empirical cost.
- The MaxSAT formulation is presented with sufficient conditions; guarantees about achieving a maximal entailed set of orderings are not fully specified (e.g., clause weights, completeness vs maximality). More detail on solver settings and instance sizes is needed.
- No treatment of noisy or inconsistent constraints, which is critical in practice. The approach appears to require fully consistent constraints; robustness or soft handling (e.g., weighted constraints) is not explored.
- Comparisons limited to ILP (GOBNILP). Given the mention of CP, a CP baseline would be informative. Also compare against order-modular approaches with precedence constraints (Parviainen & Koivisto) where applicable.
- Scalability demonstrated up to n ≈ 20 without parent-set limits. While comparable to some BDeu-based exact methods, an evaluation with in-degree bounds could highlight larger-n behavior and fairness to ILP.
- Several algorithmic details are “omitted for space” (e.g., closure to a maximum set of ancestral constraints, exact MaxSAT encoding and weights); this hurts reproducibility. References have multiple formatting errors.

Quality
The core ideas are technically sound. EC-tree pruning is correct. Edge entailment criteria are precise and seem complete for positives and negatives. The MaxSAT approach is sound but only sufficient; more rigor on maximality and complexity would strengthen the contribution. Experiments are careful, with ablations and parameter sweeps, but lack robustness-to-noise and broader baselines. Overall, good quality with some missing details.

Clarity
Generally well written and easy to follow. Key notions (α(X), β(Y), compatibility) are defined. However, some proofs and procedural details are absent; references have formatting mistakes; there are typographical artifacts (e.g., foreign date strings) that should be fixed. Providing pseudocode for edge entailment and a precise MaxSAT encoding would improve clarity.

Originality
The integration of ancestral constraints into EC-tree search and the projection of non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones for oracle empowerment are original and valuable. The exact characterization for edge entailments is novel. Using MaxSAT for safe ordering inference is a reasonable, if not entirely new, twist; the specific sufficient conditions appear new in this context.

Significance
Moderate to high. The work advances exact BN learning with practically important constraints, and the speedups over ILP are substantial. The projection technique is likely useful beyond ancestral constraints. Broader impact would be enhanced by addressing noisy constraints and scaling with in-degree bounds to larger networks.

Specific comments and suggestions
- Quantify how often CPDAG enumeration is triggered, the average size of class(P) in those cases, and the runtime/memory impact. Consider additional sufficient tests to avoid enumeration (e.g., reachability with compelled edges).
- Provide full proofs for Theorems 2–4 in the appendix; include explicit algorithms for computing the maximal ancestral closure and for edge entailment checks.
- Detail the MaxSAT encoding: variable definitions, clause weights, solver parameters, instance sizes vs n and p. Clarify whether the returned set is maximal under entailment.
- Evaluate robustness to noisy/inconsistent constraints (e.g., flip a fraction of constraints) and consider soft-weighted integration (e.g., weighted MaxSAT, or penalized pruning).
- Add a CP baseline and/or an ILP baseline augmented with stronger cutting planes specialized for path constraints.
- Explore in-degree bounds to test scalability to larger n (e.g., 50–100 vars), and report node expansions and heuristic quality (with/without projections).
- Clean up the references and typos.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important and understudied constraint type for exact BN learning.
- Elegant theory for edge entailments; practical MaxSAT-based ordering inference.
- Strong empirical gains over ILP; insightful ablations.
- Integrates well with established EC-tree/oracle framework.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing details on several algorithmic components and proofs.
- Potential exponential fallback (CPDAG enumeration) not analyzed empirically.
- Noisy/soft constraints not handled.
- Limited baselines and scale.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a solid and novel contribution with clear empirical value. Addressing the noted clarity and robustness gaps would make it stronger.
