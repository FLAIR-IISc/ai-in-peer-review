

# Review of "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"

This paper proposes a method for learning Bayesian network structures under ancestral constraints, which are non-decomposable constraints that specify ancestor-descendant relationships. The authors adapt the EC tree search space from Chen et al. (2015, 2016) to incorporate these constraints, and develop techniques for projecting ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints to empower an oracle. The main claim is that this approach outperforms ILP-based methods by orders of magnitude.

## Strengths
The paper identifies an important gap in the literature regarding the integration of non-decomposable ancestral constraints into Bayesian network structure learning. The EC tree approach for handling non-decomposable scores is well-motivated, and the projection techniques for converting ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints are theoretically sound. The empirical results demonstrate significant speedups over ILP-based approaches, which is a valuable contribution to the field.

## Weaknesses

### Technical Errors and Inconsistencies
1. Theorem 2 contains a critical typo: "Xa 6 Yb" should be "Xa 6 Yb ∈ A" (not just "Xa 6 Yb" as stated). This changes the meaning of the theorem and could mislead readers.

2. In Section 5.2, the MaxSAT formulation includes the constraint "for all X < Y and Z < W : (X < Y ) ∧ (Z < W )⇒ (X Y ) ∨ (Z W ) ∨ (X Z) ∨ (Y W ) ∨ (X W ) ∨ (Y 6 Z)". The notation "X Y" appears to be a typo for "X → Y" or "X Y" (which isn't defined elsewhere), causing confusion.

3. Theorem 1 states "a CPDAG P satisfies ancestral constraints A iff its descendants satisfy A," but this is incorrect. A descendant CPDAG represents a larger set of variables, so it cannot satisfy the same constraints (which are defined over a fixed set of variables).

4. In Section 4.3, the paper states "A CPDAG P with no partially directed paths from X1 to X2 violates the given constraint," but the term "partially directed path" is undefined and inconsistent with the standard definition of ancestral constraints.

### Formatting and Presentation Issues
1. Inconsistent citation format throughout (e.g., "Chen et al., 2015" vs. "Chen et al\.,? \Q2015\E" in references).

2. The paper mentions "CPCS54" as a benchmark, but the standard name is "CPCS" (not "CPCS54").

3. The paper states "n = 12" and "n = 14" in the experimental section, but Table 3 lists "n ∈ {16, 18, 20}", creating confusion about the experimental setup.

4. The paper claims "orders-of-magnitude improvements" without providing specific numbers for the speedup (e.g., "100x faster" vs. "10x faster").

5. The reference list contains multiple formatting errors: "Borboudakis and Tsamardinos.,? \Q2012\E" should be "Borboudakis and Tsamardinos, 2012."

### Missing Details
1. The paper states "we used the EVASOLVER partial MaxSAT solver" without justification for this specific choice over other solvers.

2. The paper mentions "BDeu scores with an equivalent sample size of 1" without explaining why this value was chosen.

3. The paper claims "a relatively small number of ancestral constraints (say 10%–25%) can have a similar impact on the quality of the observed network as increasing the amount of data from 512 to 8192," but doesn't provide specific numbers to support this claim.

## Recommendation

While the paper addresses an important problem with a theoretically sound approach, the numerous technical errors, inconsistent notation, and formatting issues significantly undermine the credibility of the work. The paper contains multiple critical errors in theorems and definitions that would confuse readers attempting to implement or build upon this work. The lack of specific performance numbers ("orders of magnitude" without quantification) and the inconsistent experimental setup (different values of n mentioned in different places) further weaken the empirical claims.

The paper's contribution is valuable, but these fundamental issues prevent it from meeting the quality bar expected at NIPS. The technical errors are not minor typographical mistakes but rather substantive problems that affect the correctness of the core contributions.

The paper would benefit from a thorough revision to correct the theorems, clarify the notation, provide specific performance metrics, and fix the numerous formatting inconsistencies. Without these corrections, the paper's claims cannot be properly evaluated.

I recommend rejection due to the substantial technical errors and lack of clarity in the presentation.
