This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data when subject to background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints (e.g., "X is an ancestor of Y" or "X is not an ancestor of Y"). The authors correctly identify that such constraints are non-decomposable, which poses a significant challenge for most modern and efficient score-based learning algorithms that rely on the assumption of decomposable scores (e.g., dynamic programming or A* search over orderings).

The proposed solution builds upon the recent work of Chen et al. [2015, 2016], which introduced an A* search framework over a search space of equivalence classes (the EC tree). This framework is notable because its use of an external "oracle" to compute an admissible heuristic allows it to handle non-decomposable scores and constraints. The core contributions of this paper are twofold: 1) It provides a sound method for pruning the EC tree search space by checking the consistency of a CPDAG node with the given ancestral constraints. 2) More significantly, it introduces a technique to "project" the non-decomposable ancestral constraints onto a set of decomposable constraints (edge and topological ordering constraints). This inferred decomposable knowledge is then passed to the oracle, making its heuristic tighter and thereby dramatically accelerating the A* search. For inferring edge constraints, the paper provides an efficient algorithm based on two theorems. For the more subtle problem of inferring ordering constraints, the authors cleverly formulate the task as a MaxSAT problem to find the largest set of consistent ordering constraints that can be inferred without erroneously pruning valid DAGs.

The paper's approach is empirically evaluated against an Integer Linear Programming (ILP) baseline (GOBNILP), which is a natural competitor as it can also handle non-decomposable constraints. The experiments show that the proposed method is orders of magnitude faster than the ILP approach. Furthermore, an ablation study convincingly demonstrates that the constraint projection technique is the key to this performance gain.

### Strengths:

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The paper tackles an important and practical problem. Incorporating rich background knowledge like ancestral relationships is often crucial for learning meaningful models, especially from limited data. The proposed method for handling these non-decomposable constraints within an exact learning framework is novel and elegant. The core idea of projecting non-decomposable constraints to empower a fast oracle for decomposable problems is a powerful and potentially generalizable concept.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The approach is technically sound. The use of the EC tree framework is well-justified. The methods for pruning the search space and, more importantly, for inferring decomposable constraints appear correct. The reduction to MaxSAT to handle the subtleties of inferring topological ordering constraints is particularly insightful, as it correctly avoids the issue of creating spurious cycles or eliminating valid models.

3.  **Experimental Rigor:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing.
    *   The comparison against ILP, a state-of-the-art method for exact learning with constraints, is appropriate and demonstrates a clear performance advantage.
    *   The ablation study (comparing Table 1 and Table 2) provides compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed constraint projection technique. The performance difference is dramatic, underscoring the importance of this contribution.
    *   The scalability analysis (Table 3) is informative, showing both the limits of the approach (as expected for an exact method) and an interesting "easy-hard-easy" pattern in runtime as the number of constraints increases.
    *   The inclusion of Structural Hamming Distance (SHD) results effectively illustrates the practical benefit of using ancestral constraints to learn more accurate models, linking the algorithmic contribution back to its real-world motivation.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. It clearly motivates the problem, situates the work within the existing literature, explains the proposed method step-by-step, and presents the experimental results in an easy-to-understand manner.

### Weaknesses:

1.  **Reproducibility Details:** Due to space constraints, some technical details are omitted, which could make exact reproduction difficult. For example, the rules for computing the transitive closure of ancestral constraints are mentioned but not provided. Similarly, a more detailed specification of the MaxSAT encoding would be beneficial, perhaps in an appendix.

2.  **Scope of Scalability:** While the performance is excellent compared to the ILP baseline, the experiments scale up to n=20 variables. This is state-of-the-art for *exact* learning, but it is important to remember that the approach, like all known exact methods, is limited to relatively small problems. The authors are honest about this by comparing to relevant literature, but the abstract could be slightly more precise about the scale at which this method is "efficient".

3.  **Minor Clarity Issues:** Theorem 1 is presented as a justification for the pruning step, but its proof or a strong intuition is not provided. While the claim is believable, a brief justification would strengthen the argument. The description of the MaxSAT constraints (specifically constraint #3) is quite dense and could benefit from a short explanatory example.

### Summary and Recommendation

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. It addresses a practical problem with a novel, technically sound, and empirically verified solution. The core idea of projecting non-decomposable constraints to empower an oracle for decomposable scores is the main strength and a valuable insight for the community. The weaknesses are minor and largely pertain to providing more detail for reproducibility, which is understandable in a conference format. The paper is well-written, the experiments are compelling, and the results advance the state of the art in exact structure learning.

**I strongly recommend acceptance.**
