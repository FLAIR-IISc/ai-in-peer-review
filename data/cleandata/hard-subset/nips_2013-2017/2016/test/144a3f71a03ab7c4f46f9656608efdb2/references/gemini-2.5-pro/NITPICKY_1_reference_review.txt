### Review of "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"

**Summary:**
This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data, subject to background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints (e.g., variable X must be an ancestor of Y, or must not be). Such constraints are non-decomposable, which poses a significant challenge for mainstream score-based learning algorithms that rely on score decomposability. The authors propose a novel approach that leverages a search over the space of equivalence classes (the EC tree), which can handle non-decomposable scores. The core technical contribution is a method to "project" the non-decomposable ancestral constraints onto a set of decomposable edge and topological ordering constraints. These inferred decomposable constraints are then used to "empower" an oracle (an exact solver for decomposable problems) that provides a tight heuristic for an A* search over the EC tree. The method for inferring ordering constraints involves a novel reduction to MaxSAT. The authors demonstrate empirically that their approach is orders of magnitude faster than a state-of-the-art integer linear programming (ILP) solver adapted to handle these constraints.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The paper tackles a well-motivated and important problem. Incorporating rich background knowledge is crucial for learning meaningful causal or dependency models, and ancestral constraints are a natural way to express such knowledge. The proposed solution is elegant and novel. The idea of inferring decomposable constraints from non-decomposable ones to tighten the heuristic for a non-decomposable search is a clever way to bridge two classes of problems.
2.  **Technical Contribution:** The projection of ancestral constraints to edge constraints (Theorems 2 and 3) and ordering constraints (via a MaxSAT formulation) is a solid technical contribution. This provides a principled way to leverage the power of existing, highly optimized solvers for decomposable problems within a more general search framework.
3.  **Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are extensive and convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The comparison against GOBNILP, a strong baseline for exact learning, shows orders of magnitude improvement in runtime. The paper also clearly demonstrates the significant performance benefit gained from the constraint projection technique (Table 1 vs. Table 2) and analyzes the impact of constraint density on both runtime and model accuracy.

**Weaknesses:**
Despite the promising core ideas, the paper is marred by a substantial number of flaws in its presentation, methodological detail, and formatting, which collectively undermine confidence in the work.

1.  **Formatting and Presentation:** The manuscript is unacceptably sloppy for a conference submission.
    *   There is a stray line "th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016), Barcelona, Spain." at the end of the introduction.
    *   At the end of Section 3, there is an incomprehensible timestamp in Chinese characters: "年6月12⽇日星期五 11 16".
    *   The reference list formatting is broken. Every entry contains spurious characters like `.,? \Q2015\E,`, suggesting a severe BibTeX or copy-paste error that went unnoticed.
    *   Notation is inconsistent. Section 2 introduces `X` for variables and `X` for sets, but immediately uses `X` as a variable in a summation and then `X, U`. This initial confusion makes the paper harder to follow than necessary.
    *   The placement of footnotes is inconsistent (e.g., Footnote 1 appears after the period).

2.  **Clarity and Omission of Technical Details:** Key technical aspects of the method are either poorly explained or completely unjustified.
    *   **MaxSAT Formulation:** The MaxSAT formulation in Section 5.2 is the cornerstone for inferring ordering constraints, yet it is presented without any justification. In particular, constraint 3, the "sufficient condition for acyclicity," is a complex logical clause involving six ancestral relations. The paper provides no intuition, proof sketch, or citation to support why this specific condition is sufficient. This is a critical omission, as the soundness of the inferred ordering constraints depends entirely on it.
    *   **CPDAG Constraint Checking:** In Section 4.3, the authors state that if initial checks are inconclusive, they "exhaustively enumerate the structures of class(P)" to check for constraint satisfaction. A CPDAG can represent an exponential number of DAGs. This step is a potential computational bottleneck, but its cost, frequency, and practical impact are not discussed or analyzed at all.
    *   **Justification in Footnote 4:** The footnote claims that for any DAG G satisfying `Z ⇏ Y`, one can add the edge `Y → Z` without creating a cycle. This is a non-trivial claim about graph topology that is presented as self-evident, which it is not. A more formal argument is required.

3.  **Methodological and Experimental Nuances:**
    *   **Scalability of Projection:** The paper does not analyze the computational cost of the constraint projection step itself. Finding edge constraints via Theorem 2 requires iterating over all pairs in `α(X)` and `β(Y)`, which could be large. The MaxSAT problem itself can be hard to solve. The experiments only report total time, leaving it unclear if this pre-processing step could become a bottleneck for problems with many variables or constraints.
    *   **Limited Baselines:** While ILP is a reasonable baseline, the authors mention constraint programming (CP) approaches in the introduction but do not include them in the comparison. A more comprehensive evaluation would have strengthened the paper's claims.
    *   **Footnote 9:** The admission that "When no limits are placed on the sizes of families... heuristic-search approaches (like ours) have been observed to scale better than ILP approaches" is a critical detail. This experimental setting favors the proposed method. This information should be stated upfront in the experimental setup, not relegated to a footnote, as it is a major factor influencing the relative performance of the compared methods.

**Decision:**
I recommend **rejection**.

While the paper presents a novel and potentially significant contribution to Bayesian network structure learning, the poor quality of the presentation and the lack of justification for key technical claims are severe. The numerous formatting errors indicate a lack of care in the preparation of the manuscript, which raises concerns about the rigor of the underlying work. More importantly, the central claims regarding the MaxSAT formulation are left completely unsupported. A NIPS paper must not only present good ideas but must also present them clearly, correctly, and with sufficient detail for the community to understand and build upon them. This paper, in its current state, fails to meet this standard. The core ideas are strong enough that I would encourage the authors to thoroughly revise the manuscript—addressing the formatting, clarifying the notation, and providing rigorous proofs or justifications for all technical claims—for a future submission.
