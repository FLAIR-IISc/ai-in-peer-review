Summary
The paper tackles optimal Bayesian network (BN) structure learning under non-decomposable ancestral (path) constraints. Building on the BN-graph/EC-tree framework of Chen et al. (2015, 2016), the authors (i) prune the EC tree by eliminating CPDAG nodes that cannot admit any DAG consistent with the given ancestral constraints, and (ii) “project” non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints to strengthen a decomposable-score oracle (URLEARNING) used by A* search. For edge constraints, they provide conditions (Theorems 2–3) identifying entailed edge absences/presences from a closed set of ancestral constraints. For ordering constraints, they formulate a MaxSAT instance intended to select a maximally large set of safe ordering constraints entailed by the ancestral constraints (Theorem 4 provides sufficient conditions). Experiments on subnets of standard BN benchmarks compare against GOBNILP (ILP) and report orders-of-magnitude speedups, with additional gains from projected constraints; accuracy (SHD) improves with more constraints and data.

Strengths
- Problem relevance: Incorporating path/causal background knowledge into exact BN learning is practically important and under-supported by standard decomposable-score methods.
- Sound pruning principle: Pruning EC-tree nodes that cannot contain any DAG satisfying the ancestral constraints is natural in this search space and theoretically justified (Theorem 1).
- Projection idea: Using ancestral constraints to derive edge/ordering constraints to power existing decomposable oracles is a clever and pragmatic way to reuse strong tools.
- Empirical runtime gains: The reported speedups over GOBNILP are substantial, and the ablation suggests the projection step matters in practice.
- Clear connection to prior work: The paper situates itself relative to order-graph methods, ILP, CP, and causal path beliefs.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Incremental novelty over Chen et al.: The core search machinery (BN graph/EC tree + oracle) is from prior work. The contributions—pruning by ancestral constraints and projecting constraints—while sensible, feel incremental and in parts straightforward. Stronger theoretical development or broader empirical coverage would help justify a NeurIPS contribution.
- Incomplete theoretical details:
  - Checking CPDAG feasibility: When simple path tests are inconclusive, the method “exhaustively enumerates” class(P). This can be exponential and undermines the claimed scalability; no bounds or frequency quantification are provided. There is relevant literature on consistent extensions and orientation via Meek rules that may avoid enumeration; this is neither cited nor leveraged.
  - Edge projection: Theorem 3’s “iff” characterization for positive edge entailment is nontrivial; proofs are omitted and the condition appears strong. Without proofs or complexity analysis, it is hard to assess soundness and practicality.
  - Ordering projection via MaxSAT: The encoding uses sufficient conditions for acyclicity and entailment; it is unclear whether the resulting set is guaranteed maximal or merely a maximal solution subject to those sufficient constraints. The “closed set” notion is not defined precisely. Risk of being overly conservative (missing useful orderings) is not quantified.
  - The process to compute a “maximum set of ancestral constraints” is stated but rules are omitted “for space,” making reproducibility difficult.
- Limited empirical validation:
  - Baselines: Only ILP (GOBNILP) is evaluated; CP-based formulations (e.g., van Beek & Hoffmann) are mentioned but not tested. No comparison to dynamic-programming/A* baselines augmented with any feasible path-constraint handling (e.g., via ordering constraints) is shown.
  - Scale: n ≤ 20 with unconstrained in-degree and precomputed family scores is modest by current standards; the method’s reliance on precomputing all local scores and occasional class(P) enumeration raises concerns about larger n or higher indegree constraints.
  - Constraint realism: Constraints are sampled from ground-truth, i.e., noise-free. Real background knowledge is often noisy or partial; the method does not handle soft/erroneous constraints, and robustness is not investigated.
  - Ablations: The separate contributions of edge projection vs ordering projection vs EC-tree pruning are not dissected; time spent in MaxSAT, in oracle calls, and in CPDAG feasibility checks is not reported.
- Clarity and polish: There are typographical issues (garbled dates, missing years, inconsistent citation formatting), and some central procedures (closure computation, proofs) are omitted. Figures/tables referenced are not visible here, but the text suggests missing detail where precise algorithms matter.

Quality
The high-level approach is reasonable and consistent with prior exact BN learning literature. However, several key steps (CPDAG feasibility checking, projection completeness/soundness, MaxSAT maximality) lack sufficient theoretical detail or empirical stress-testing. The reliance on enumerating class(P) in ambiguous cases is a red flag for scalability.

Clarity
Overall structure is clear, but the manuscript needs editing and more algorithmic specifics. Important definitions (e.g., “closed” O), proofs (Theorems 2–4), and the closure algorithm are missing.

Originality
The work extends known search spaces to incorporate ancestral constraints and proposes simple projection rules plus a MaxSAT encoding. This is a novel combination, but feels incremental relative to Chen et al. and to existing uses of path constraints in BN learning.

Significance
If robust, this could make exact BN learning with path knowledge more practical on small–medium problems, and the projection idea could be adopted by others. But the current evaluation and theoretical guarantees are not strong enough to demonstrate broad impact.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Can you avoid enumerating class(P) by using known algorithms for PDAG consistent extensions and path existence under Meek rules? Please provide complexity or empirical frequency of enumeration.
- Provide proofs (or at least detailed sketches) for Theorems 2–4, and precisely define “closed set” of orderings and “maximum set” of ancestral constraints. What is the worst-case complexity of the edge projection algorithm?
- Does the MaxSAT encoding guarantee maximality of entailed orderings, or only maximality under sufficient conditions? Report the overhead of MaxSAT solving relative to total runtime.
- Evaluate against a CP baseline and include ablations for edge-only vs order-only projection.
- Assess robustness to noisy constraints and to different priors/scores (e.g., MDL, different ESS).
- Report memory usage and the fraction of time spent in the oracle versus pruning/projection.

Recommendation
Lean reject. The problem is relevant and the ideas are promising, but the contribution appears incremental, key components lack rigorous justification and detail, and the empirical evaluation is limited in scope and baselines. Strengthening the theoretical guarantees (eliminating enumeration), broadening experiments (including CP and noisy constraints), and clarifying algorithms would considerably improve the paper.
