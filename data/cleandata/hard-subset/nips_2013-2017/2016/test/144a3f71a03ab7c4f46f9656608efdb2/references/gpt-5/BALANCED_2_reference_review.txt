Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses optimal Bayesian network (BN) structure learning under non-decomposable background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints (X is/is not an ancestor of Y). Most exact score-based methods assume decomposable scores and therefore support only decomposable constraints (edge presence/absence, family-size bounds, or order-modular constraints). Building on the DAG/EC-graph framework of Chen et al. (2015, 2016), the authors adapt the EC tree search space to incorporate ancestral constraints by pruning CPDAG nodes that cannot admit any DAG consistent with the constraints. They further “project” non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints to empower a decomposable-score “oracle” (e.g., order-graph solvers like URLearning) used as a heuristic in A* search over the EC tree.

For projection, they provide: (i) an algorithm with sufficient conditions (Theorems 2–3) to infer entailed edge constraints X→Y or X↛Y from a closed set of ancestral constraints (using α/β ancestor/descendant closures), and (ii) a MaxSAT formulation to infer a maximally large, cycle-free set of entailed ordering constraints from negative ancestral constraints (with hard clauses for order transitivity, a necessary condition Y⊄anc(X) for X<Y, and a sufficient acyclicity condition; and soft clauses encouraging Y<X from X↛Y). They prove sufficiency conditions (Theorem 4).

Empirically, they compare their exact EC-tree+A* approach to the ILP-based GOBNILP. They evaluate on subnets of ALARM, ANDES, CHILD, CPCS54, HEPAR2 with synthetic data and ground-truth ancestral constraints randomly sampled, up to n≈20 variables. They report orders-of-magnitude runtime improvements over ILP, especially as n grows, and show that projected constraints greatly accelerate their method. They observe an easy–hard–easy pattern as the proportion of constraints increases. Structural Hamming distance decreases with more data and constraints.

Strengths
- Clear problem motivation: ancestral constraints are practically useful but non-decomposable; mainstream exact solvers do not handle them well.
- Technically interesting integration: EC-tree search with pruning using satisfiability of ancestral constraints, plus a principled way to feed projected decomposable constraints to an oracle to tighten A* heuristics.
- Soundness of pruning strategy (checking CPDAGs and, if necessary, enumerating class(P)) is conceptually simple and complete.
- The edge-projection rules are intuitive and potentially impactful; the MaxSAT reduction for order constraints is a reasonable approach to safely extracting many entailed orderings.
- Empirical results indicate substantial speedups over a strong ILP baseline, and useful accuracy gains from modest amounts of constraints.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Theorem 1 statement appears problematic. The text argues that violation of ancestral constraints is monotone under adding leaves (hence pruning is safe): if P violates A, descendants also violate. The theorem states “P satisfies A iff its descendants satisfy A,” which is generally false; a CPDAG that currently could admit a satisfying DAG might, after adding a leaf and orienting edges, cease to admit one. Please clarify/correct (likely the intended monotonicity is for violations).
- CPDAG feasibility test: when local tests are inconclusive, the method exhaustively enumerates class(P). This can be exponential; the paper does not analyze or empirically quantify how often enumeration is required, nor its impact on runtime. More detail is needed on practical mitigation (e.g., caching, bounded enumeration, stronger necessary/forbidden path tests).
- Projection completeness and guarantees: The edge rules (Thm 2–3) and the order MaxSAT constraints are sufficient but not necessary. The claim of finding a “maximally large set” of ordering constraints is not theoretically guaranteed by the provided sufficient conditions. Clarify the optimality criterion and whether the MaxSAT solution is maximal among constraints provably entailed by A under your axioms, and discuss potential missed entailments.
- Experimental scope: constraints are sampled from ground truth and assumed consistent; there is no evaluation with noisy or contradictory constraints, which is common in practice. How does the approach behave under inconsistencies (e.g., feasibility detection, partial satisfaction)?
- Baselines: Only ILP is compared; CP-based approaches (e.g., van Beek & Hoffmann) are mentioned but not evaluated. Also, no comparison to order-modular constrained methods that could utilize inferred order constraints directly (to tease apart gains due to EC-tree vs projection).
- Scalability: n up to 20 is typical for exact BDeu learning, but the paper’s claims about orders-of-magnitude improvements would be stronger with larger n or with analyses showing how projection scales with constraint density.
- Clarity/polish: Several typos and reference formatting issues; some derivations are “omitted for space” (e.g., closure rules for A). A brief complexity discussion and more formal proofs (even in appendix) would improve confidence.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: Mostly sound, with a likely error in Theorem 1’s wording and missing proofs. The pruning approach is complete (via enumeration), but the worst-case cost is not analyzed. Experimental methodology is reasonable, but more robustness analyses are needed.
- Clarity: Generally well written, but some ambiguous statements (Theorem 1), omitted details, and formatting/citation issues hinder readability.
- Originality: Good. Bringing ancestral constraints into exact score-based learning via EC-tree pruning and projection to decomposable constraints appears novel relative to ILP/CP and path-constraint work in constraint-based learning.
- Significance: Moderate. Speedups are promising and the method could be a practical tool for exact learning with prior knowledge. The niche is focused (exact BN learning), but the ideas (constraint projection, hybrid search) may transfer.

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix Theorem 1 and clarify monotonicity (violation vs satisfaction). Provide proofs (or an appendix) for Theorems 2–4, and the closure procedure for ancestral constraints.
- Analyze the complexity and frequency of CPDAG enumeration; add ablations showing time spent in projection, enumeration, and oracle calls.
- Evaluate robustness to noisy/inconsistent constraints; report how conflicts are detected/handled (e.g., via SAT/MaxSAT) and the impact on runtime and accuracy.
- Add baselines: a CP solver, and an order-graph method using only projected order constraints, to isolate the benefit of EC-tree pruning vs projection.
- Provide larger-scale stress tests where constraint density reduces search space substantially; include memory/time profiles.
- Improve presentation and references; include tables/figures with detailed results, and release code/scripts if possible.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important gap: exact learning with non-decomposable prior knowledge.
- Conceptually elegant hybrid of EC-tree pruning and oracle empowerment.
- Empirically strong runtime improvements over a widely used ILP baseline.
- Practical utility: small sets of constraints can notably improve accuracy.

Arguments against acceptance
- Theorem 1 issue and missing proofs undermine confidence in correctness.
- Potential exponential CPDAG enumeration not analyzed; unclear practical impact.
- Limited experimental scope (no noisy constraints, small n, limited baselines).
- Some clarity and polish issues.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The contribution is promising, but the theoretical clarity (esp. Theorem 1), completeness of proofs, and experimental breadth need strengthening. With these addressed, this work could be a solid contribution.
