

# Review of "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"

## Summary
This paper proposes a method for learning Bayesian network structures under ancestral constraints (non-decomposable constraints) using an EC tree search space and an oracle that optimizes decomposable scores. The authors claim to significantly outperform ILP-based approaches for this specific problem by projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints onto decomposable edge and ordering constraints. The approach builds on prior work by Chen et al. (2015, 2016) on BN graphs and EC trees, which themselves represent an extension of the order graph search space.

## Strengths
The paper makes a technically sound contribution to the problem of incorporating non-decomposable constraints into Bayesian network structure learning. The theoretical foundation for constraint projection (Theorems 2-4) appears solid, and the EC tree representation with pruning for ancestral constraints is well-motivated. The experimental results showing orders-of-magnitude speedups over ILP-based approaches (GOBNILP) for the specific problem setting are impressive, and the paper provides a clear explanation for why their approach is faster.

## Weaknesses
Despite the technical soundness of the approach, I have significant concerns about the paper's overall contribution and validation. As a reviewer who prioritizes methodological soundness and rigorous validation against established baselines, I find the paper lacking in several critical areas:

1. **Insufficient validation against established methods**: The paper only compares against GOBNILP (an ILP-based approach), but fails to compare against other well-established methods for incorporating background knowledge, such as constraint-based learning (e.g., PC algorithm with path constraints as in Borboudakis and Tsamardinos, 2012). The paper claims to be orders-of-magnitude faster than ILP approaches, but doesn't address how their method compares to constraint-based approaches that are designed to incorporate such background knowledge.

2. **Limited empirical evaluation**: The experimental evaluation is restricted to simulated datasets from five benchmark networks (ALARM, ANDES, CHILD, CPCS54, HEPAR2) with only 50 total datasets (5 from each of 2 sub-networks per benchmark). This is insufficient to establish the robustness of the approach across diverse real-world scenarios. The paper lacks analysis of failure cases or conditions under which the method might not perform well.

3. **Overstated significance**: The paper claims that "a relatively small number of ancestral constraints (say 10%-25%) can have a similar impact on the quality of the observed network as increasing the amount of data available from 512 to 2048." This claim is not sufficiently supported by the data presented, as the paper doesn't show how the quality of the learned networks compares to other approaches that incorporate background knowledge.

4. **Lack of discussion on limitations**: The paper doesn't adequately address the limitations of their approach. For instance, how does their method scale with the complexity of the ancestral constraints? What happens when the constraints are inconsistent or ambiguous? The paper makes strong claims about the efficiency of their approach but doesn't discuss when it might fail or perform poorly.

5. **Questionable practical relevance**: The paper doesn't provide any real-world case studies where this method has been applied to solve a meaningful problem. Without demonstrating practical utility, the contribution remains theoretical.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper presents a technically sound approach to a specific problem (incorporating ancestral constraints into Bayesian network structure learning), it falls short of the rigorous validation expected for NIPS. The authors have made an incremental contribution to a niche problem, but they haven't adequately demonstrated why this specific approach is superior to existing methods for incorporating background knowledge into Bayesian network learning.

The paper's most significant weakness is its failure to compare against established methods for incorporating background knowledge, which are designed precisely for this purpose. The constraint-based approach (e.g., Borboudakis and Tsamardinos, 2012) is a direct competitor to the authors' approach but is not properly evaluated against it. The paper's claim of "orders-of-magnitude" improvements is only meaningful if compared to relevant baselines, which are not properly established here.

The paper is well-written and technically sound, but it fails to meet the high bar for significance and validation expected at NIPS. The problem is narrow, the evaluation is limited, and the comparison to relevant baselines is inadequate. While the method may be efficient for the specific problem it addresses, the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate why this approach should replace or significantly advance current practice in Bayesian network structure learning with background knowledge.

## Recommendation
I recommend rejection. While the technical contribution is sound, the paper lacks sufficient validation against established baselines and fails to convincingly demonstrate the significance of the approach. The authors would need to substantially strengthen the empirical evaluation, compare against relevant constraint-based methods, and provide more thorough analysis of the method's limitations to be competitive at NIPS.
