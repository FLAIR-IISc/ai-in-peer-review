This paper tackles the important problem of Bayesian network structure learning with ancestral constraints, a scenario frequently encountered in practice where prior knowledge about causal relationships exists. The key contribution is adapting a recent framework for optimal structure learning (Chen et al., 2015, 2016) to incorporate these non-decomposable constraints, and demonstrating significant empirical improvements over existing ILP-based approaches. The core idea – leveraging an oracle for decomposable scores and intelligently projecting ancestral constraints into decomposable forms – is both novel and promising.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The approach of adapting the EC tree framework to handle non-decomposable ancestral constraints is genuinely novel. Existing methods largely struggle with such constraints due to their inherent incompatibility with decomposable scoring functions.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The empirical evaluation convincingly demonstrates the superiority of the proposed approach compared to ILP-based methods, particularly in terms of computational efficiency. Orders-of-magnitude speedups are significant.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a sound theoretical basis for projecting ancestral constraints into decomposable forms, bolstering the effectiveness of the oracle. Theorems 1-4 establish important guarantees.
* **Practical Relevance:** The ability to incorporate prior causal knowledge is highly relevant to many real-world applications where expert knowledge is available.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the complex concepts accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Scalability Limitations:** While the experiments demonstrate scaling to 20 variables, this remains a limitation. The computational cost could become prohibitive for networks with substantially more variables, even with constraint projection.
* **Oracle Dependence:** The performance heavily relies on the effectiveness of the chosen oracle (URLEARNING). The impact of different oracle choices isn't thoroughly investigated.  A weaker oracle might severely hinder performance.
* **MaxSAT Solver Dependence:** The MaxSAT reduction for ordering constraints introduces a dependency on an external solver (EVASOLVER). While this is common, it adds a potential bottleneck and requires careful tuning. The performance of the entire pipeline is tied to the MaxSAT solver's efficiency.
* **Limited Dataset Diversity:** The experiments are conducted on a relatively small number of benchmark datasets. Exploring a wider range of data distributions and network structures would further strengthen the evaluation.
* **Heuristic Nature:** While the approach aims for optimality, the use of a heuristic (the oracle) introduces a degree of approximation. A more detailed analysis of the optimality gap would be valuable.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper introduces a truly novel approach to a challenging problem.
* The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate a substantial improvement over existing methods.
* The theoretical framework is well-developed and provides a solid foundation for the proposed approach.
* The work has the potential to significantly impact the field of Bayesian network structure learning.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The scalability remains a concern.
* The strong dependence on the oracle and MaxSAT solver introduce potential bottlenecks.
* The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. The novelty of the approach, coupled with the compelling empirical results, outweighs the existing limitations.  I value the bold approach of tackling non-decomposable constraints directly, rather than relying on approximations or limitations. While scalability and the dependence on external tools are concerns, they do not detract significantly from the overall impact of the work. I believe this paper deserves to be accepted to NIPS, as it introduces a new direction with substantial potential for future research. The theoretical insights and the practical improvements demonstrated warrant a positive evaluation. I recommend acceptance.
