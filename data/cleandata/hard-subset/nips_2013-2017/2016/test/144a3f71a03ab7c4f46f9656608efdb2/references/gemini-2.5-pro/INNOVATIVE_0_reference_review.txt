**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data when given background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints (e.g., "X is an ancestor of Y" or "X is not an ancestor of Y"). The central challenge is that such constraints are non-decomposable, which breaks the fundamental assumptions of most efficient, exact score-based learning algorithms (e.g., those based on dynamic programming or order-graph search). While Integer Linear Programming (ILP) methods can encode these constraints, they often struggle with scalability.

The authors propose a novel approach by adapting a recently developed A* search framework over the space of equivalence classes (the EC tree). This framework is designed for non-decomposable scores and uses an "oracle"—an optimal learner for decomposable scores—to compute an admissible heuristic. The core contribution of this work lies in two parts: (1) A sound method for pruning the EC tree search space using the ancestral constraints. (2) A highly innovative technique to "project" the non-decomposable ancestral constraints onto decomposable edge and ordering constraints. These projected constraints are then fed to the oracle, significantly strengthening the heuristic and pruning power of the A* search. For the particularly subtle task of inferring ordering constraints, the authors propose a clever reduction to a MaxSAT problem. The paper empirically demonstrates that this approach is orders of magnitude faster than a state-of-the-art ILP-based solver on this task.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Significance of the Core Idea:** The standout contribution of this paper is the concept of "projecting" non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones to empower a subroutine that requires decomposability. This is an elegant and powerful meta-idea that addresses a fundamental friction point in constrained optimization for graphical models. It bridges the gap between the expressive power of non-local constraints and the efficiency of algorithms that rely on local, decomposable properties. This approach is likely to inspire similar techniques for other types of non-decomposable background knowledge in structure learning.

2.  **Bold and Creative Technical Approach:** The method for inferring ordering constraints via MaxSAT is particularly impressive. The authors correctly identify that naively inferring ordering constraints from negative ancestral constraints can be unsound, eliminating valid DAGs. Formulating this as a search for the largest consistent set of orderings via MaxSAT is a non-obvious and sophisticated solution that demonstrates a deep understanding of the problem's combinatorial structure. This is precisely the kind of bold, creative problem-solving that pushes the field forward.

3.  **Strong and Relevant Empirical Validation:** The experiments are well-designed and compelling. The authors compare their method against ILP, which is the most appropriate and powerful baseline for this specific problem. The results, showing orders-of-magnitude speedups, are dramatic and leave little doubt about the practical efficacy of the proposed method. Furthermore, the comparison between running the system with and without the projected constraints (comparing Tables 1 and 2) serves as a perfect ablation study, clearly demonstrating that the projection technique is the main driver of the performance gains.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. It clearly motivates the problem, explains why ancestral constraints are difficult for standard methods, and lucidly presents its proposed solution. The use of small examples effectively illustrates the key concepts.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Reliance on an Existing Framework:** The underlying search algorithm (A* on the EC tree) is based on the prior work of Chen et al. The novelty here is not in the search framework itself, but in its adaptation and, more importantly, the constraint projection technique that makes it highly effective for this new problem. This is a minor weakness, as building upon recent, state-of-the-art work is standard practice, but it's worth noting that the core search infrastructure is not developed from scratch.

2.  **Scalability Limitations:** As is common with exact, score-based methods, the approach is demonstrated on problems with up to 20 variables. While this is respectable and state-of-the-art for this problem class, it naturally limits its direct applicability to larger-scale problems. The authors are transparent about this, and it is an inherent difficulty of the problem rather than a flaw in their specific method.

3.  **Overhead of Constraint Projection:** The MaxSAT formulation is a key component, but the paper does not detail the computational overhead of this pre-processing step. While it is very likely negligible compared to the main structure search, quantifying this would make the experimental analysis more complete.

**Recommendation: Accept**

This paper is a strong candidate for acceptance. It tackles a well-defined, important, and difficult problem in graphical model learning. While the methodology has some minor limitations in terms of scalability and builds upon an existing search framework, these are far outweighed by the novelty and significance of the core contribution. The idea of projecting non-decomposable constraints to empower a decomposable oracle is a major conceptual leap. This is not an incremental improvement; it is a novel approach that enables solving a class of problems that was previously computationally prohibitive. The technical execution, particularly the MaxSAT formulation, is creative and sound. The paper's high quality, clear presentation, and compelling empirical results make a strong case for its inclusion at NIPS.
