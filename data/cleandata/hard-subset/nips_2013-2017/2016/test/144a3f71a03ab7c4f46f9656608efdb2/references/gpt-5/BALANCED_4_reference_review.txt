Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles optimal Bayesian network (BN) structure learning subject to non-decomposable background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints (X is/is not an ancestor of Y). The mainstream exact algorithms rely on decomposable scores and search spaces (order graph, dynamic programming, A* over subsets) that cannot directly encode non-decomposable constraints. Building on Chen et al. (2015, 2016), the authors use search spaces over DAGs/equivalence classes (BN graph and EC tree) that support non-decomposable scoring and constraints. They propose: (i) pruning EC-tree nodes (CPDAGs) using checks for satisfaction/violation of ancestral constraints, backed by a monotonicity theorem; (ii) projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable ones to “empower the oracle” (an exact solver for decomposable scores used as the A* heuristic). Projection comprises an algorithm with sound criteria to infer edge presence/absence constraints (Theorems 2–3) and a MaxSAT formulation to infer a safe, maximally large set of topological ordering constraints (Theorem 4). Empirically, they compare against an ILP approach (GOBNILP) with ancestral constraints encoded linearly, showing orders-of-magnitude speedups and demonstrating that projected constraints dramatically improve oracle guidance.

This relates to prior exact BN learning via order graph/dynamic programming (Koivisto & Sood; Silander & Myllymäki; Yuan & Malone), ILP/CP formulations (Jaakkola et al.; Cussens; van Beek & Hoffmann), and prior use of path constraints in constraint-based learning and soft path beliefs (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos). It extends Chen et al.’s BN/EC search spaces to accommodate hard ancestral constraints and introduces principled projection to decomposable constraints.

Strengths
- Addresses an important practical need: incorporating causal/ancestral knowledge while retaining optimality guarantees.
- Clear conceptual integration: EC tree for non-decomposable pruning plus a decomposable oracle strengthened by projected constraints.
- Soundness of pruning and projection is formalized; ordering projection is explicitly safe (sufficient conditions).
- Empirical gains are substantial: orders-of-magnitude speedups over a strong ILP baseline, and clear benefits from projection.
- Informative analysis of easy–hard–easy behavior as constraint coverage increases; accuracy improvements from even modest constraint availability.

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Quality
- Theorem statements and guarantees need tightening. Theorem 1 as written (“a CPDAG P satisfies A iff its descendants satisfy A”) appears misstated; what is needed for pruning is the monotonicity property: if P violates A, all descendants violate A. Please restate precisely and provide a proof or citation.
- Edge-projection completeness is not discussed. Theorems 2–3 give sound criteria; are they complete for entailed single-edge constraints? If not, quantify the gap or discuss conditions under which they are complete.
- The MaxSAT encoding for ordering constraints uses sufficient conditions (Theorem 4). Since these are not necessary, the “maximally large” phrasing is misleading; it is a maximum under the chosen sufficient-constraint encoding. Please clarify optimality scope, and report how many entailed orderings are missed in practice.
- The CPDAG pruning includes an exponential fallback by enumerating class(P) when quick checks are inconclusive. There is no analysis or empirical reporting of how often this occurs, its worst-case frequency, or how it affects runtime. This is important as class(P) can be huge.
- Experimental scope is limited: only ILP is compared; no CP baseline despite claiming CP can represent ancestral constraints. The networks are modest (n ≤ 20), with no tests under degree bounds or sparse parent limits where some methods scale further. There is no evaluation under noisy/inconsistent constraints, which are realistic in practice.
- Reproducibility details are sparse: the exact pruning algorithm, complexity, and pseudocode for edge-projection are omitted; MaxSAT encoding details and solver parameters are minimal.

Clarity
- Generally well structured, but several notational issues: “maximum” vs “maximal” set of ancestral constraints; α(X), β(Y) definitions could be made crisper; explicit definitions of “partially directed path” and test procedures should be centralized. Some formatting glitches and placeholder dates/text in the manuscript and references hinder readability.
- Proof sketches are largely omitted; at least short proofs or an appendix are needed for theorems critical to correctness.

Originality
- The integration of ancestral constraints into EC-tree search, and especially the projection of non-decomposable ancestral constraints into safe decomposable edge and ordering constraints to strengthen decomposable oracles, is novel and practically useful. The idea of a MaxSAT-based safe ordering inference appears new in this context.

Significance
- If robust and scalable, the approach would enable exact BN learning with real causal background knowledge and offer significant speedups over ILP. However, the current empirical scope is limited, and practical aspects (noisy constraints, larger n with degree bounds, more baselines) need strengthening to fully establish impact.

Questions for the authors
- Please restate and prove the precise monotonicity result used for EC-tree pruning. Under what conditions can pruning be done without enumerating class(P)?
- Are Theorems 2–3 complete for single-edge entailment from ancestral constraints? If not, can you characterize missing cases?
- How often did CPDAG enumeration occur in experiments? What was the size distribution of class(P) when enumeration was triggered?
- How many ordering constraints were inferred by MaxSAT relative to the set of all orderings entailed by A? Any measure of tightness?
- How sensitive are results to inconsistent or noisy ancestral constraints? Can the framework accommodate soft/penalized violations akin to Borboudakis & Tsamardinos (2013)?

Suggestions to improve the paper
- Fix Theorem 1 and add proofs or an appendix with proof sketches for Theorems 2–4. Clarify completeness vs soundness explicitly.
- Provide pseudocode and complexity analysis for edge-projection; discuss worst-case and expected cost.
- Describe the fast CPDAG pruning checks in detail, and empirically report the frequency and cost of enumeration.
- Extend experiments: include a CP baseline; add degree-bounded settings; evaluate with noisy/inconsistent constraints; test larger n where degree bounds permit; report ablations isolating the impact of edge vs ordering projections.
- Clean up references and formatting; ensure all citations have complete bibliographic details.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a relevant, underexplored problem with a principled, general approach.
- Strong empirical speedups; clear practical utility of constraint projection.
- Builds coherently on and extends prior non-decomposable search spaces (BN/EC).

Cons against acceptance
- Theoretical statements need correction/clarification; proofs missing.
- Limited experimental scope and missing baselines; no analysis of exponential fallback.
- Clarity and polish issues in writing and references.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The core idea is promising and likely impactful, but the paper needs stronger theoretical presentation, broader and more rigorous empirical evaluation, and improved clarity. I would be inclined to accept a revised version addressing the above points. Confidence: medium.
