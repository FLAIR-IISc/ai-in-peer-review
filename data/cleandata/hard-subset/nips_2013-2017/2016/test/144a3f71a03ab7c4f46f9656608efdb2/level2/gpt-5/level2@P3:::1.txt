Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses optimal Bayesian network (BN) structure learning when background knowledge is given as ancestral constraints (positive: X is an ancestor of Y; negative: X is not an ancestor of Y). These constraints are non-decomposable, hence not directly supported by mainstream exact methods that rely on decomposable scores and order/family decompositions (Koivisto and Sood, 2004; Silander and Myllymäki, 2006; Yuan et al., 2011; Yuan and Malone, 2013). The work builds on the BN graph and EC tree search spaces for non-decomposable scores (Chen et al., 2015; Chen et al., 2016), using A* guided by an “oracle” capable of optimal learning under decomposable scores. The key ideas are:
- Pruning EC tree nodes (CPDAGs) that cannot satisfy the ancestral constraints, using necessary and sufficient tests; if quick tests are inconclusive, enumerating DAGs in the CPDAG to decide.
- Projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints to empower the oracle: (i) an algorithm with sound-and-complete rules to infer edge presence/absence constraints (Theorems 2–3), and (ii) a MaxSAT reduction to obtain a maximal, sound set of topological ordering constraints (with sufficient soundness conditions; Theorem 4).
The approach is compared empirically to ILP-based exact learning (GOBNILP; Jaakkola et al., 2010; Cussens, 2008; Bartlett and Cussens, 2015). Results on standard BN benchmarks show orders-of-magnitude speedups, particularly when projected constraints are supplied to the oracle. The study also explores scalability to 16–20 variables and reports improved structure accuracy (SHD) as data size and the proportion of ancestral constraints increase. Related works on causal/path priors in constraint-based and local-search settings (Borboudakis and Tsamardinos, 2012; 2013) are acknowledged.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation and motivation: ancestral constraints capture causal/domain knowledge and should reduce search space, yet are non-decomposable and ill-suited to standard exact methods.
- Solid integration with existing non-decomposable search spaces (BN graph/EC tree) and heuristic A* search guided by a decomposable-score oracle.
- Technically novel projection of ancestral constraints to decomposable edge and ordering constraints; the edge rules are precise and efficiently checkable; MaxSAT formulation for ordering constraints is a practical way to maximize safe order information.
- Soundness is emphasized: pruning tests in the EC tree are sound; Theorems 2–4 formalize conditions for entailed edges and orderings.
- Strong empirical results: consistent and large speedups over ILP (GOBNILP), clear demonstration of the value of projected constraints, and a nuanced easy–hard–easy trend as constraint density varies.
- Practical relevance: the projected constraints can empower a broad class of decomposable-score learners, not just the specific oracle used.

Weaknesses and limitations
- EC-tree pruning sometimes falls back to exhaustive enumeration of DAGs within a CPDAG when quick tests are inconclusive. The worst-case exponential nature of this step is acknowledged implicitly but not quantified. Guidance on how often enumeration is needed in practice and its impact on runtime would be valuable.
- The MaxSAT-based ordering projection guarantees soundness via sufficient conditions (Theorem 4) but not completeness. The claim of finding a “maximally large” set of orderings could be clarified as maximal under the chosen sufficient constraints; a discussion of the potential gap to the true entailed set would improve precision.
- The construction of a “maximum set of ancestral constraints” (closure under entailment) is assumed, but the rules and complexity are omitted. This step is central to subsequent theorems; moving details to an appendix would improve reproducibility.
- Robustness to inconsistent or noisy constraints is not studied. In real settings, background knowledge may contain conflicts or errors; the behavior of pruning and projection in such cases (e.g., detection, diagnosis, graceful degradation) is not discussed.
- Comparisons are limited to ILP; CP-based approaches (van Beek and Hoffmann, 2015) are mentioned but not evaluated. A brief comparison or justification would strengthen the empirical section.
- Some reference formatting issues and minor typographical artifacts reduce polish.

Quality
The technical development is generally sound, with clear statements of key theorems for pruning and projection. The oracle-empowerment idea is well-motivated and practically impactful. Proof sketches or appendices for Theorems 2–4 and the CPDAG pruning procedure would enhance rigor and reproducibility. Experimental methodology is reasonable and uses commonly adopted BN benchmarks, though constraints are sampled from ground-truth networks (see robustness comment).

Clarity
The paper is well-organized and readable. The roles of EC tree, oracle, and projections are explained clearly. Places that would benefit from elaboration: (i) details and complexity of the ancestral-closure procedure, (ii) frequency and cost of CPDAG enumeration, and (iii) precise scope of the MaxSAT guarantees. Cleaning up reference formatting and stray artifacts would improve presentation.

Originality
The combination of EC-tree pruning under ancestral constraints and the projection of non-decomposable ancestral knowledge into decomposable edge and ordering constraints is novel. Prior work either incorporated path beliefs as soft priors with local search (Borboudakis and Tsamardinos, 2013) or encoded constraints within ILP/CP frameworks; this work provides a distinct, efficient alternative with strong empirical benefits.

Significance
Enabling optimal BN learning with non-decomposable ancestral constraints is practically important and likely to be adopted by researchers and practitioners who use exact BN learning. The projected constraints can benefit any decomposable-score structure learner, broadening impact. The demonstrated efficiency gains over ILP are substantial.

Suggestions for improvement
Essential:
- Provide a formal description (or appendix) of the ancestral-closure rules and their complexity; clarify how inconsistencies are detected and handled.
- Clarify the CPDAG pruning procedure: specify the fast tests for positive/negative ancestral constraints, and quantify how often full class(P) enumeration is needed in practice.
- Refine the claim about “maximally large” ordering constraints to reflect the sufficient (sound but potentially incomplete) nature of the MaxSAT constraints; if possible, add a brief analysis of the potential gap.

Nice-to-have:
- Add experiments with a small fraction of noisy or conflicting constraints to assess robustness.
- Include a brief comparison to a CP-based method (van Beek and Hoffmann, 2015) or discuss why ILP suffices as a representative baseline.
- Report the number of projected edge and ordering constraints produced and correlate with runtime improvements.
- Release code or pseudocode for the projection algorithms.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a relevant and nontrivial problem with clear novelty.
- Strong, consistent empirical improvements over a standard exact baseline.
- Methodologically sound, with broad applicability through constraint projection.

Arguments against acceptance
- Some technical details (closure rules, pruning enumeration behavior) are omitted, affecting reproducibility.
- MaxSAT ordering projection is only guaranteed sound, not complete, and the claim of maximality could be tightened.
- No robustness study to imperfect constraints and limited breadth of baselines.

Overall, the work makes a meaningful and useful contribution to exact BN learning under non-decomposable constraints and appears suitable for NIPS, contingent on clarifying the noted technical details.
