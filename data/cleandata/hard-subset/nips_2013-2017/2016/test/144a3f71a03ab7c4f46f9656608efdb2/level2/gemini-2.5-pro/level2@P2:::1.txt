### Summary

This paper addresses the problem of learning the optimal structure of a Bayesian network from data, given background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints (e.g., X must be an ancestor of Y, or X cannot be an ancestor of Y). The key challenge is that such constraints are non-decomposable, meaning they cannot be evaluated by checking each variable's parent set independently. This makes them incompatible with most efficient, state-of-the-art optimal structure learning algorithms, which rely on decomposable scores and constraints.

The authors build upon a recent framework [Chen et al., 2015, 2016] that performs an A* search over a space of equivalence classes of DAGs (an "EC tree"). This framework can handle non-decomposable scores but relies on an "oracle" (a fast solver for problems with decomposable scores) to compute the A* heuristic. The main contributions of this paper are:
1.  A method to prune the EC tree search space directly using the ancestral constraints.
2.  A novel and powerful technique to "project" the non-decomposable ancestral constraints into a set of decomposable constraints (edge presence/absence and topological ordering constraints).
3.  These projected, decomposable constraints are then passed to the oracle, making the A* heuristic much tighter and dramatically speeding up the search. The projection for ordering constraints is cleverly formulated as a MaxSAT problem to find a large, consistent set of such constraints.

The authors empirically compare their approach to a state-of-the-art Integer Linear Programming (ILP) solver, which is one of the few other methods capable of handling such constraints for optimal learning. The proposed method demonstrates orders-of-magnitude speedups, scaling to networks of up to 20 variables, and highlights the significant impact that a small amount of background knowledge can have on learning accuracy.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed approach is sound, well-motivated, and elegant. The core idea of empowering a decomposable-score oracle with constraints inferred from non-decomposable ones is a powerful concept. The theorems provided appear correct and form a solid theoretical basis for the projection of edge constraints. The use of MaxSAT to handle the more complex case of ordering constraints is a clever and appropriate reduction. The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. It compares against the most relevant and powerful class of competitors (ILP solvers), uses standard benchmarks, and clearly demonstrates the substantial performance gains of the proposed method. The analysis of the "easy-hard-easy" pattern and the impact of constraints on model accuracy further strengthen the empirical section.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clear, the background on structure learning is concise and accurate, and the challenges posed by non-decomposable constraints are explained well. The structure of the paper logically builds from the base framework (EC trees) to the main contributions (pruning and projection). The description of the projection methods in Section 5, while dense, is communicated effectively.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While it builds on the recent EC tree framework of Chen et al., it applies it to a new, important problem and introduces a significant and non-obvious technical contribution. The idea of projecting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones to tighten a search heuristic is, to my knowledge, novel in this context. It provides a general recipe for incorporating other types of non-decomposable knowledge into this search framework. The specific algorithms for inferring edge constraints and the MaxSAT formulation for ordering constraints are novel contributions.

**Significance:** The results are highly significant for the graphical models community. Ancestral relations are a very natural way for experts to specify background knowledge (e.g., from known causal pathways or temporal sequences). Prior to this work, practitioners wishing to find an optimal network structure under such constraints had few options beyond general-purpose, and often slow, solvers like ILP. This paper provides a specialized, exact algorithm that is orders of magnitude faster, making optimal learning with this type of background knowledge far more practical. The demonstration that a few constraints can be as valuable as a large increase in sample size is an important practical takeaway. This work advances the state of the art in exact Bayesian network learning and is likely to be built upon by others.

### Constructive Comments and Suggestions for Improvement

*   The description of the MaxSAT formulation in Section 5.2 is quite compact. In particular, the third hard constraint ("a sufficient condition for acyclicity") is crucial but its justification is not immediately obvious. A sentence of intuition explaining why this complex-looking clause helps prevent cycles among the inferred ordering constraints would be very helpful for the reader.
*   In Section 5.1, the paper states that the set of ancestral constraints A is assumed to be closed under inference ("maximum set"), with details omitted for space. While understandable, it would be useful to cite a standard algorithm for this or include the details in an appendix, as this is a key pre-processing step for the subsequent theorems to hold.
*   The experiments show a dramatic improvement when projected constraints are used. It would be interesting to see a more detailed ablation study that separates the impact of projecting edge constraints versus ordering constraints. This could provide further insight into which type of projected constraint provides the most benefit.
*   Minor point: The caption for Table 3 refers to structural Hamming distance as Δ, but the table itself does not contain this symbol, which could cause momentary confusion. It's clear from the text, but aligning the symbol would be a slight improvement.

Overall, these are minor suggestions for an otherwise excellent paper.

### Arguments for Acceptance

*   **Pro:** The paper solves an important and practical problem: optimal BN structure learning with ancestral constraints.
*   **Pro:** The proposed solution is novel, technically deep (especially the projection method and MaxSAT formulation), and elegant.
*   **Pro:** The empirical results are outstanding, showing orders-of-magnitude improvement over the main competitor (ILP) and demonstrating a clear advance in the state of the art.
*   **Pro:** The paper is extremely well-written, clear, and places itself perfectly within the context of existing literature.
*   **Con:** The work is an extension of a very recent framework [Chen et al., 2016], so it could be viewed as incremental. However, the extension is substantial, non-trivial, and addresses a significant open aspect of that framework, making it a strong contribution in its own right.
