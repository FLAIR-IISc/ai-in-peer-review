Summary: The paper tackles optimal Bayesian network (BN) structure learning under background knowledge expressed as ancestral (path) constraints, which are non-decomposable and thus incompatible with mainstream decomposable-score learners. Building on BN graph/EC-tree search with an oracle for decomposable scores (Chen et al., 2015, 2016; Yuan and Malone, 2011, 2013), the authors (i) prune the EC tree using tests over CPDAGs to enforce ancestral constraints and (ii) “project” non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones to empower the oracle: a complete set of inferable edge constraints via closed-form rules (Theorems 2–3) and a MaxSAT formulation to extract a maximal acyclic set of ordering constraints (Theorem 4). Empirically, the method is reported to be orders-of-magnitude faster than an ILP baseline (GOBNILP; Cussens 2008; Bartlett & Cussens 2015) on standard BN benchmarks, with clear benefit from the projected constraints.

Quality: The technical core is sound and well-motivated. The pruning criterion on CPDAGs (Theorem 1) is appropriate, and the projection rules are plausible and useful. The experimental protocol uses multiple networks, dataset sizes, and constraint densities, and includes SHD to assess model quality. Some details are omitted (“details omitted for space” on closure rules), and proofs are not provided; adding them (supplementary) would strengthen rigor. The enumeration of class(P) when local tests are inconclusive can be exponential; practical safeguards are not discussed.

Clarity: Generally clear and well-organized, with a good connection to prior work at NIPS and elsewhere (order-graph/DAG-graph search; ILP/CP; path constraints in constraint-based learning). Figures/tables are referenced; citation formatting has inconsistencies that should be corrected.

Originality: The combination of EC-tree pruning for path constraints with principled projection to decomposable edge/order constraints and the MaxSAT reduction is novel relative to ILP/CP formulations and prior use of path beliefs (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos, 2012/2013). The shortest-path/oracle view is leveraged in a new way.

Significance: Enabling optimal BN learning with hard causal knowledge is practically valuable. The reported speedups and the observed easy–hard–easy trend as constraints increase are insightful. This work should be useful to both theory and practice.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear, principled integration of non-decomposable constraints with decomposable oracles.
- Strong empirical gains over ILP and informative ablations with/without projection.
- Potentially impactful for domains with reliable causal priors.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Missing proofs and some algorithmic details (closure rules; complexity of class(P) checks).
- Limited baselines (no CP comparison) and sensitivity to incorrect/noisy constraints not evaluated.
- Scalability demonstrated to n≈20; more evidence desirable.

Constructive suggestions:
- Provide proofs and full specification of closure/inference rules (supplement).
- Analyze worst-case and practical complexity of CPDAG enumeration; add heuristics or bounds.
- Evaluate robustness to partially incorrect constraints and to different scores (e.g., MDL) and ESS choices.
- Compare with CP approaches (van Beek & Hoffmann, 2015) and modern GOBNILP configurations.
- Break down the contribution of edge vs ordering projections; release code and projected-constraint statistics.
