

This paper presents a novel approach for learning Bayesian network structures under ancestral constraints, which are non-decomposable and thus challenging for traditional structure learning methods that rely on decomposable scores. The authors build upon Chen et al.'s (2015, 2016) EC tree framework for optimal structure learning with non-decomposable scores, introducing a method to infer decomposable constraints from ancestral constraints that can be passed to existing oracles optimized for decomposable scores. The key technical contribution is a sound and complete method for projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints onto decomposable edge and ordering constraints, which significantly improves search efficiency.

The paper makes a strong case for the importance of ancestral constraints in practical Bayesian network learning, where they can encode cause-and-effect relationships and reduce search space size. The authors correctly identify that most existing approaches cannot handle such constraints due to their non-decomposable nature, while ILP-based methods could theoretically accommodate them but have not been empirically evaluated for this specific purpose.

Strengths:
1. The theoretical contributions are well-developed, with clear theorems (Theorems 1-4) establishing the soundness of the constraint projection approach.
2. The experimental evaluation is thorough, comparing against GOBNILP (an ILP-based approach) across multiple benchmark networks, dataset sizes, and constraint percentages.
3. The empirical results demonstrate significant efficiency gains (orders-of-magnitude faster) for the proposed approach, particularly when using projected constraints (Table 1).
4. The paper effectively demonstrates the practical value of ancestral constraints, showing that a small number (10%-25%) can have similar impact on model accuracy as substantially larger datasets.

Weaknesses:
1. The scalability claims are somewhat limited. While the paper reports results up to n=20 variables, recent work (Yuan & Malone, 2013) has demonstrated scalability to 26 variables for BDeu scores. It would strengthen the paper to show how the proposed method compares to state-of-the-art approaches for larger networks.
2. The paper states that "the ILP and CP approaches have not been previously evaluated" for learning with ancestral constraints, but cites Cussens (2008) which used ancestral relations in ILPs for acyclicity constraints. The authors should clarify whether Cussens' work was specifically evaluated for learning with ancestral constraints (as opposed to just enforcing acyclicity).
3. The MaxSAT encoding for ordering constraints (Section 5.2) is presented as a black box. The paper should provide more detail about the computational complexity of this step and how it scales with problem size.
4. The paper doesn't fully address why decomposable scores are so pervasive in Bayesian network learning. A brief explanation of the computational advantages of decomposable scores would help contextualize the problem.

Constructive suggestions:
1. The paper should include a more detailed comparison of scalability with recent state-of-the-art methods for Bayesian network structure learning, particularly those that handle larger networks.
2. The authors should clarify the relationship between their work and Cussens (2008), specifically whether Cussens' approach was evaluated for learning with ancestral constraints.
3. Consider adding a discussion of the computational complexity of the constraint projection step, as this could be a bottleneck for very large constraint sets.
4. The paper could benefit from a brief analysis of when ancestral constraints are most likely to be available in real-world applications, helping to establish the practical relevance of the work.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. The approach to handling non-decomposable constraints via constraint projection is novel and well-executed. The theoretical results are sound, and the empirical evaluation convincingly demonstrates the efficiency gains over ILP-based approaches. The paper effectively addresses an important practical problem (incorporating background knowledge into structure learning) and provides a method that could have significant impact in real-world applications where causal knowledge is available.

The paper meets NIPS quality standards with technically sound contributions, clear presentation, and significant practical implications. The minor weaknesses noted above are easily addressable and do not detract from the paper's overall contribution. I recommend acceptance.
