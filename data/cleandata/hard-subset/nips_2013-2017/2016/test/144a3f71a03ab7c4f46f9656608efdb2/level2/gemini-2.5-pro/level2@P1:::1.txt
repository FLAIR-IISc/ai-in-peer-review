This paper tackles the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data when given background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints. The key challenge is that ancestral constraints (e.g., X is an ancestor of Y) are non-decomposable, making them incompatible with most efficient, exact structure learning algorithms that rely on decomposable scores.

The authors build upon a recent A*-based search framework [Chen et al., 2015, 2016] that operates over a search space of equivalence classes (the EC tree) and can, in principle, handle non-decomposable scores. This framework uses an "oracle"—an efficient solver for problems with decomposable scores—to compute an admissible heuristic that guides the search. The main contribution of this work is a method to "empower" this oracle by inferring decomposable constraints (edge and ordering constraints) from the non-decomposable ancestral constraints. For inferring edge constraints, they provide an efficient algorithm. For the more complex task of inferring topological ordering constraints, they propose a novel and sound reduction to a MaxSAT problem to find a maximally large set of compatible ordering constraints.

The proposed approach is empirically evaluated against a state-of-the-art integer linear programming (ILP) based system, GOBNILP, which can also handle ancestral constraints. The results show that the proposed method is orders of magnitude faster, scales to problems with up to 20 variables, and that the incorporation of even a small amount of ancestral knowledge can significantly improve the accuracy of the learned network structure.

**Quality:**
The paper presents a high-quality, complete piece of work. The proposed method is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by a thorough experimental evaluation. The core idea of projecting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones to tighten the heuristic for an outer-loop A* search is both clever and effective. The formulation of the ordering constraint inference as a MaxSAT problem is particularly strong. The experiments are well-designed, comparing against a relevant and strong baseline (ILP), demonstrating the impact of the projection technique, and analyzing scalability and accuracy.

**Clarity:**
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem, providing the necessary technical background on structure learning, and situating their work within the existing literature. The main ideas are presented logically, and the experimental results are clearly reported in tables and effectively summarized. The paper provides enough information for an expert to understand the approach, though full reproducibility would require some of the details omitted for space (e.g., proofs for the theorems).

**Originality:**
The work is highly original. While it builds on the existing EC tree search framework, the key contribution—the projection of ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints to empower a decomposable oracle—is novel. To my knowledge, this specific approach has not been proposed before. The use of MaxSAT to find a sound and maximal set of ordering constraints is a particularly novel contribution to the structure learning literature. The paper is careful to cite and differentiate itself from related work in both score-based and constraint-based learning.

**Significance:**
The results presented are significant. Learning with background knowledge is a critical task, as data is often scarce or noisy, and prior expert knowledge can substantially reduce the search space and improve model quality. Ancestral constraints are a natural and powerful form of such knowledge. By providing a method that is orders-of-magnitude faster than existing exact approaches like ILP, this paper makes optimal learning with this important class of constraints much more practical. The demonstration that a small number of constraints can have a similar impact on accuracy as a large increase in data size further highlights the practical importance of this work. Researchers and practitioners working on structure learning are likely to find these ideas useful and build upon them.

**Strengths:**
*   Addresses the important and practical problem of incorporating non-decomposable background knowledge into optimal Bayesian network learning.
*   Presents a novel and elegant technical solution by projecting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones to improve search efficiency. The MaxSAT formulation for ordering constraints is a highlight.
*   The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and convincing, demonstrating significant performance improvements over a state-of-the-art ILP solver.
*   The paper is very well-written and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The paper omits some details for space, such as the full algorithm for ancestral constraint entailment and the proofs for the theorems. While understandable, this makes complete verification difficult.
*   The complexity and practical overhead of the constraint projection step, particularly solving the MaxSAT instance, is not discussed. While the overall system is clearly fast, it would be useful to know how much time is spent in this pre-processing stage.

**Questions and Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   Could the authors clarify what "maximally large set" means in the context of the ordering constraints found by the MaxSAT solver? Does this refer to maximum cardinality, and is this set guaranteed to be unique?
*   It would strengthen the experimental section to briefly comment on the runtime of the constraint projection step itself, to give readers a sense of the overhead of this pre-computation.
*   In Section 4.3, the paper describes a check for whether a CPDAG `P` satisfies constraints by enumerating all DAGs in `class(P)` if simpler checks are inconclusive. Could this exhaustive enumeration be a bottleneck for CPDAGs representing very large equivalence classes? Some discussion of this would be helpful.

In summary, this is an excellent paper that presents a significant, original, and well-executed contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the overall quality of the work. I strongly recommend acceptance.
