

This paper presents a novel approach for learning Bayesian network structures under ancestral constraints, which are non-decomposable and typically challenging for standard score-based methods. The main claim is that their framework, building on Chen et al.'s EC tree search space, can efficiently incorporate ancestral constraints by inferring decomposable constraints from them, resulting in orders-of-magnitude speed improvements over ILP-based approaches.

The paper makes strong contributions to the field. The theoretical foundation is solid, with clear algorithms for projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints (Theorems 2-4). The EC tree pruning mechanism is well-motivated and sound. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, comparing against GOBNILP (an ILP-based system) across multiple benchmark networks (ALARM, ANDES, etc.) with varying problem sizes. Results consistently show their approach is significantly faster, with improvements growing with problem size (n=20 variables). The analysis of how ancestral constraints impact learning quality (Table 3) demonstrates their practical utility, showing that even 10-25% of ancestral constraints can have similar impact as quadrupling training data.

The paper excels in originality, addressing a clear gap in the literature: most structure learning methods assume decomposable scores, making them incompatible with non-decomposable ancestral constraints. The authors properly contextualize their work against related approaches (Borboudakis and Tsamardinos, 2012; Cussens, 2008; ILP/CP methods), demonstrating their approach is distinct and superior in efficiency.

The quality is high with rigorous theoretical claims supported by careful experiments. The clarity is excellent, with well-structured explanations of the EC tree and constraint projection methods. The significance is clear: this work makes optimal Bayesian network structure learning with background knowledge practically feasible for larger problems.

A minor limitation is that the paper doesn't fully explore the trade-offs when ancestral constraints are sparse or inconsistent. While the authors acknowledge some overhead for small n with ILP, a more detailed discussion of constraint quality vs. quantity would strengthen the analysis.

Overall, this is a strong contribution that advances the state of the art in Bayesian network structure learning. The combination of theoretical insight and empirical validation makes it a valuable addition to the field, and the results should be of interest to researchers working on structure learning with background knowledge. The paper is well-suited for NIPS and merits acceptance.
