This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data when subject to background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints. The main challenge is that such constraints are non-decomposable, rendering them incompatible with many efficient, exact structure learning algorithms that rely on decomposable scores. The proposed approach extends a recent framework based on A* search over an equivalence class search space (the EC tree), which utilizes an oracle for solving subproblems with decomposable scores. The key contributions are two-fold: first, a method for pruning the EC tree search space based on the given ancestral constraints; and second, a novel technique called "constraint projection" to infer decomposable constraints (edge and ordering constraints) from the non-decomposable ancestral constraints. These projected constraints are then passed to the oracle, significantly improving its efficiency and thus the overall search performance. The work provides an empirical comparison against an Integer Linear Programming (ILP) formulation, demonstrating orders-of-magnitude speedups.

**Quality**

The paper presents a technically sound and complete piece of work. The approach of combining direct pruning of the non-decomposable search space with a method to empower the decomposable oracle is powerful. The formalization of the constraint projection ideas into algorithms and theorems (Theorems 1-4) provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed methods. Claims of performance improvement are convincingly supported by a thorough experimental evaluation on a variety of benchmark networks. The experiments are well-designed, systematically varying the number of variables, dataset size, and the amount of background knowledge. The comparison to a state-of-the-art exact method (GOBNILP) is appropriate and effectively highlights the benefits of the proposed approach.

To further improve the paper, a more detailed discussion of some technical aspects would be beneficial. For example, Section 5 assumes that the initial set of ancestral constraints `A` has been expanded into a "maximum set." Briefly describing how this transitive closure and inference process is performed would add to the paper's completeness. Similarly, while the reduction of ordering constraint inference to MaxSAT is a clever contribution, a little more intuition behind the "sufficient condition for acyclicity" (clause 3 in Section 5.2) could help the reader grasp the encoding more fully.

**Clarity**

The paper is very well-written and logically structured. The motivation is clear, the necessary background on structure learning and ancestral constraints is presented concisely, and the proposed method is developed in a step-by-step manner. The use of a small example in Section 3 to illustrate the non-decomposable nature of ancestral constraints is particularly effective. The distinction between pruning the EC tree directly and empowering the oracle is articulated well, making the overall contribution easy to understand.

A minor suggestion for improvement would be to include proof sketches for the main theorems, perhaps in an appendix. While space is limited, this would strengthen the paper's self-containedness and formal rigor for interested readers.

**Originality**

This work builds upon the recently introduced BN graph and EC tree framework [Chen et al., 2015, 2016]. However, it represents a novel and non-trivial extension of that framework to a new and important class of constraints. The core contribution—the idea of projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints to accelerate an oracle-based search—is highly original. The specific algorithms for inferring edge constraints and the MaxSAT formulation for finding a maximal set of consistent ordering constraints are novel contributions. The paper also appears to be the first to provide a rigorous empirical evaluation of the efficacy of exact, score-based methods for learning with ancestral constraints, filling a gap in the literature.

**Significance**

The results presented in this paper are important and likely to be impactful. Incorporating background knowledge is crucial for learning accurate graphical models, especially when data is scarce. Ancestral relations are a natural and expressive form for such knowledge, particularly in domains where causal assumptions exist. This work significantly advances the state of the art by making optimal learning with these constraints feasible for problems of up to 20 variables, a scale comparable to leading exact methods for unconstrained learning. The demonstrated orders-of-magnitude speedup over a strong ILP baseline is a substantial practical achievement. The ideas presented are likely to be used by researchers and practitioners needing to learn models from data and expert knowledge. Furthermore, the general concept of "constraint projection" may inspire similar techniques for other types of non-decomposable constraints or in other machine learning contexts.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses an important and practical problem in structure learning.
*   Presents a novel and clever method (constraint projection) that significantly improves the efficiency of an existing state-of-the-art framework.
*   The approach is technically sound and well-supported by strong empirical evidence.
*   The paper is clearly written and easy to follow.
*   The work significantly advances the practical applicability of exact learning with ancestral constraints.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The work is an extension of a recent framework, rather than proposing a completely new paradigm. However, it is a significant and non-obvious extension.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper with clear strengths in originality, technical soundness, and practical significance. It makes a solid contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning.
