This paper addresses the problem of learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints, a challenging task due to the non-decomposable nature of these constraints. The authors build upon their previous work on optimal structure learning with non-decomposable scores, proposing a method to incorporate ancestral constraints by projecting them into decomposable constraints that can be handled by existing oracles. The core idea – leveraging decomposable constraints inferred from non-decomposable ones to improve efficiency – is promising, and the empirical evaluation against ILP-based approaches demonstrates significant speedups.

The paper is well-structured, starting with a clear introduction of the problem and related work, and proceeding through technical details, the proposed approach, and experimental results. The motivation for addressing ancestral constraints is well-articulated, and the limitations of existing methods are effectively highlighted. The connections to previous work, particularly [Chen et al., 2015, 2016], are clearly explained. The description of projecting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones is central to the contribution and is reasonably detailed, with theorems formalizing the approach. The experimental setup appears sound, utilizing benchmark datasets and varying relevant parameters.

A significant strength of the paper lies in the empirical validation. The results convincingly demonstrate the substantial efficiency gains achieved by the proposed approach compared to GOBNILP, particularly as the number of variables increases. The analysis of the impact of constraint projection is insightful, showing its importance in accelerating the learning process. The observation that even a small number of ancestral constraints can significantly improve model accuracy compared to increasing training data is a valuable finding. 

However, the paper could be improved in several areas. While the theoretical underpinnings are present (theorems 1-4), the intuition behind *why* the projection works so well could be elaborated further. Specifically, how the inferred decomposable constraints effectively prune the search space in a way that’s beneficial for the oracle-based learning. A more intuitive explanation of the connection between ancestral constraints and the resulting edge/ordering constraints would be helpful. 

The discussion of the MaxSAT reduction for inferring ordering constraints feels somewhat cursory.  More details regarding the encoding and scalability of the MaxSAT solver in the context of this problem would be beneficial.  The statement that the EVASOLVER solver was used is helpful, but a discussion of its limitations (especially as the problem size grows) is warranted.

The comparison with ILP is strong, but it would be useful to briefly discuss the limitations of ILP-based approaches beyond computational cost. For instance, are there specific types of Bayesian networks (e.g., those with many cycles) where ILP struggles even before considering ancestral constraints? A more nuanced discussion of the trade-offs would strengthen the paper.

Finally, while the paper mentions scalability to 20 variables, it would be valuable to discuss the limitations of the approach and potential avenues for further improvement in terms of scalability. What are the bottlenecks as the problem size continues to grow? 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to incorporating non-decomposable constraints into Bayesian network structure learning.
*   Significant empirical results demonstrating substantial speedups compared to state-of-the-art ILP-based methods.
*   Well-structured and clearly written paper with a strong theoretical foundation.
*   Valuable insight into the role of background knowledge (ancestral constraints) in improving learning efficiency and accuracy.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The intuition behind the efficacy of the constraint projection could be better explained.
*   The discussion of the MaxSAT reduction is brief and lacks detail.
*   The limitations of ILP-based approaches could be discussed more thoroughly.
*   The scalability limitations of the proposed approach need further discussion.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the minor weaknesses mentioned above, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. The proposed approach is novel, well-motivated, and empirically validated. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised regarding intuition, MaxSAT details, and scalability limitations. The potential impact of this work is significant, as it provides a more efficient way to incorporate valuable background knowledge into Bayesian network learning.
