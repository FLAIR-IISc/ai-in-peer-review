

This paper addresses an important problem in Bayesian network structure learning: incorporating ancestral constraints (which specify cause-and-effect relationships between variables) into optimal learning algorithms. The authors note that most mainstream approaches assume decomposable scoring functions, which cannot directly handle non-decomposable ancestral constraints. They build on Chen et al.'s (2015, 2016) framework for learning with non-decomposable scores, which uses an oracle for decomposable scoring. Their key contribution is showing how to infer decomposable constraints from ancestral constraints, empowering the oracle to efficiently handle the non-decomposable constraints.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, they formalize how to prune the EC tree search space using ancestral constraints, providing a sound and complete method for incorporating these constraints. Second, they develop a method to project ancestral constraints into edge and ordering constraints that the oracle can handle, using efficient algorithms for edge constraints and a MaxSAT formulation for ordering constraints. Finally, their empirical evaluation demonstrates that their approach is orders-of-magnitude faster than ILP-based methods like GOBNILP across multiple benchmark networks and varying problem sizes.

The paper is technically sound and well-structured. The theoretical contributions are well-motivated and supported by formal theorems (Theorems 2-4), and the experimental results are thorough and convincing. The authors carefully compare against a strong baseline (GOBNILP) and demonstrate the practical impact of their method through both runtime comparisons and model quality metrics (structural Hamming distance). The finding that a small percentage of ancestral constraints (10-25%) can have similar impact on model accuracy as substantially more training data is particularly valuable for practitioners.

The paper is clearly written with good organization and helpful examples. The figures (Figure 1) effectively illustrate the BN graph and EC tree. The related work is well-cited and properly contextualized, especially regarding the limitations of previous approaches for handling ancestral constraints.

Some minor weaknesses could be addressed to strengthen the paper. The connection between the MaxSAT formulation (Section 5.2) and the practical implementation of the constraint projection could be clarified. The paper mentions that "when these tests are inconclusive, we exhaustively enumerate the structures of class(P)," but doesn't specify how often this occurs or how the enumeration scales with the size of the CPDAG. A brief discussion of this would help readers understand the practical limitations of the pruning step.

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field. The problem of incorporating background knowledge into Bayesian network learning is important for practical applications where domain knowledge is available. The authors' solution is both theoretically sound and empirically effective, with clear practical benefits demonstrated through their experiments. Their approach represents a significant advance over existing methods for handling ancestral constraints, which typically rely on ILP approaches that are much less efficient.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS, as it addresses a significant problem in probabilistic modeling with a technically sound solution that advances the state of the art. The work builds on recent NIPS contributions (Chen et al., 2015, 2016) while making a clear, novel contribution to the field.

Strengths:
- Novel approach to incorporating ancestral constraints into optimal structure learning
- Theoretical contributions with formal proofs of correctness
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation demonstrating orders-of-magnitude speedups
- Practical insights about the impact of background knowledge on learning quality
- Clear and well-organized presentation

Weaknesses:
- The MaxSAT implementation details could be clarified
- More discussion of when exhaustive enumeration is needed during pruning would strengthen the practical understanding

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to Bayesian network structure learning. The technical quality is high, the results are compelling, and the practical implications are clear. I recommend acceptance.
