This paper addresses the problem of learning Bayesian networks optimally under ancestral constraints, a task complicated by the non-decomposable nature of these constraints. The work builds upon a recent framework for optimal structure learning with non-decomposable scores, leveraging oracles designed for decomposable scores by projecting the ancestral constraints into equivalent decomposable constraints. The core contribution lies in demonstrating how to effectively utilize these oracles in the presence of non-decomposable constraints, resulting in significant efficiency gains over alternative approaches like integer linear programming.

The paper is well-structured and clearly presents the problem, the proposed approach, and the experimental results. The introduction provides sufficient background and motivation, outlining the limitations of existing methods when dealing with ancestral constraints. The technical preliminaries section effectively reviews relevant concepts like decomposable scores and the K2 algorithm. The description of the BN graphs and EC trees, along with the proposed method for incorporating constraints, is clear and concise. The detailed explanation of constraint projection, including the use of MaxSAT for ordering constraints, demonstrates a thoughtful and technically sound approach. The experimental setup and results are well-presented, showcasing a substantial performance improvement over ILP-based methods.

A significant strength of the work is its comprehensive approach to handling ancestral constraints. The authors not only address the core challenge of incorporating these constraints into the learning process but also explore techniques for projecting them into decomposable forms, thereby enhancing the efficiency of existing oracles. The empirical evaluation provides strong evidence supporting the effectiveness of their approach, demonstrating orders of magnitude speedups compared to ILP.  The use of standard benchmark datasets and a clear comparison with a relevant baseline (GOBNILP) further strengthens the validity of the results.  The inclusion of analysis on the impact of constraint projection is also valuable.

However, some areas could benefit from further elaboration. While the paper provides a good overview of the theoretical underpinnings, a more intuitive explanation of *why* the constraint projection significantly improves efficiency would be helpful.  Specifically, detailing how the projected constraints reduce the search space explored by the oracle could enhance understanding. The discussion of the MaxSAT reduction feels somewhat brief; elaborating on the complexity and potential bottlenecks in this step would be valuable. The choice of BDeu score is mentioned, but a brief discussion of its suitability (or limitations) for this problem would add context.  Regarding the experimental setup, the choice of sub-networks from the original benchmarks isn’t thoroughly justified.  How representative are these sub-networks?  Additionally, the structural Hamming distance metric, while informative, could be supplemented with other measures to provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the learned network’s accuracy. Finally, the statement about scalability to n=20 variables being comparable to literature results feels slightly understated, given the limitations of prior work with BDeu scores as noted in the references.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to handling non-decomposable constraints in Bayesian network structure learning.
*   Significant empirical improvements in efficiency compared to existing methods.
*   Sound theoretical foundation and a well-structured presentation.
*   Comprehensive experimental evaluation on standard benchmarks.
*   Addresses a practical challenge in Bayesian network learning.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Explanation of *why* constraint projection leads to such large speedups could be more detailed.
*   Discussion of the MaxSAT reduction could be expanded.
*   Justification for the selection of sub-networks is limited.
*   Evaluation could be strengthened with additional metrics.



Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. The proposed approach is novel, technically sound, and demonstrably effective. The minor weaknesses identified above do not detract from the overall strength of the work and can be addressed with relatively minor revisions.  This paper is recommended for acceptance.
