

This paper addresses the problem of learning Bayesian network structures with ancestral constraints, which are non-decomposable and thus incompatible with standard structure learning approaches that rely on decomposable scores. The authors build upon Chen et al.'s EC tree search space for non-decomposable scores, introducing a method to incorporate ancestral constraints through two key contributions: (1) a pruning mechanism for the EC tree based on ancestral constraints, and (2) a projection technique to transform non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints that can be used to empower existing oracles.

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical foundations with formal theorems establishing the soundness of the pruning approach and the correctness of constraint projection. The authors prove that ancestral constraints can be efficiently transformed into decomposable constraints that maintain the same solution space, enabling the use of existing oracles designed for decomposable scores. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, systematically varying the number of variables (n), dataset size (N), and proportion of ancestral constraints (p) across multiple standard benchmarks (ALARM, ANDES, CHILD, CPCS54, HEPAR2). The results consistently show the proposed approach outperforms ILP-based methods by orders of magnitude, with efficiency gains increasing with problem size.

The work demonstrates significant quality through its rigorous theoretical development and thorough empirical validation. The paper clearly establishes the limitations of existing approaches for handling ancestral constraints and provides a sound solution that addresses these limitations. The technical contribution is complete, with well-defined algorithms and clear theoretical guarantees.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-organized and accessible, with appropriate mathematical notation and helpful illustrations (Figures 1a and 1b). The problem statement and contributions are clearly articulated in the introduction, and the technical sections flow logically from the problem definition to the proposed solution. The paper effectively relates the work to previous contributions, particularly Chen et al.'s EC tree framework and the limitations of ILP-based approaches.

The originality of the work is substantial. While ILP-based approaches have been theoretically capable of handling ancestral constraints, this paper is the first to demonstrate their practical inefficiency compared to the proposed framework. The constraint projection technique represents a novel approach to handling non-decomposable constraints by transforming them into decomposable ones that can leverage existing infrastructure. The paper's contribution fills a clear gap in the literature, as prior work has largely focused on decomposable constraints or has not evaluated the practical efficiency of handling non-decomposable constraints.

The significance of this work is substantial. The empirical results demonstrate that ancestral constraints can have a comparable impact on learning accuracy as significantly increasing dataset size (e.g., 10-25% constraints can match the effect of increasing data from 512 to 8192 samples). This highlights the practical value of incorporating background knowledge. The efficiency gains (orders-of-magnitude faster) make the approach feasible for larger networks, scaling to 20 variables - a challenging benchmark in the field. The method's compatibility with existing oracles means it can be readily integrated into current Bayesian network learning pipelines.

The paper is well-positioned within the NIPS community, addressing a fundamental problem in structure learning with clear theoretical and practical implications. The work builds upon recent advances in non-decomposable score learning (Chen et al., 2015, 2016) while making a novel contribution to handling constraints.

A minor limitation is the absence of comparison with constraint-based approaches that might handle ancestral constraints differently (though the authors note Borboudakis and Tsamardinos' work on constraint-based learning with path constraints). The paper could also provide more detail on the MaxSAT implementation for ordering constraints, though the use of EVASOLVER is appropriately cited.

Overall, this paper makes a strong contribution to Bayesian network structure learning with significant theoretical and practical implications. The work is technically sound, well-written, and addresses an important gap in the literature. The empirical results provide compelling evidence of the approach's efficiency advantages. The paper demonstrates clear significance through its practical impact on learning efficiency and accuracy, with potential applications in domains where causal knowledge is available. The contribution is sufficiently novel compared to previous NIPS papers, particularly Chen et al.'s EC tree work, and represents a meaningful advancement in the field.
