Summary
The paper addresses exact Bayesian network (BN) structure learning under non-decomposable background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints (X is/is not an ancestor of Y). Most exact score-based methods rely on decomposable scores and support only decomposable constraints (edge presence/absence, order-modular constraints). Building on the BN-graph/EC-tree framework for exact structure learning with non-decomposable scores (Chen et al., 2015; Chen et al., 2016), the authors show how to incorporate ancestral constraints by pruning the EC-tree search space and by “projecting” the non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints that can be fed to a decomposable “oracle” (e.g., order-graph A* or DP). For edges, they give simple tests (Theorems 2–3) to infer X→Y or X↛Y from closures of ancestral relations; for orderings, they formulate a MaxSAT problem to infer a maximally large, sound set of X<Y constraints (Theorem 4). Empirically, their approach outperforms an ILP approach (GOBNILP) by orders of magnitude on synthetic tasks derived from standard BN benchmarks, and the projections significantly tighten the search. They also report accuracy improvements (lower SHD) as data size and constraint coverage increase.

Relation to previous work
- Exact BN learning with decomposable scores via DP and A* on the order graph (e.g., Koivisto & Sood 2004; Silander & Myllymäki 2006; Yuan & Malone 2011/2013) supports only decomposable constraints; the current work extends the EC-tree framework (Chen et al., 2015/2016) to handle ancestral (path) constraints.
- Ancestral/path constraints have been used in constraint-based methods and local search with priors (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos, 2012/2013), and can be expressed in ILP/CP frameworks (Jaakkola et al., 2010; Bartlett & Cussens, 2015; van Beek & Hoffmann, 2015). The paper provides, to my knowledge, the first empirical comparison focused specifically on ancestral constraints for exact score-based learning, showing substantial speedups over ILP.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: a clear integration of non-decomposable ancestral constraints into an exact global-search framework, including sound pruning on CPDAG nodes in the EC tree.
- Novelty: projection of ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints to empower standard oracles; the MaxSAT formulation for extracting a large, safe set of ordering constraints is neat and practical.
- Empirical results: consistent and often dramatic speedups over GOBNILP; ablations show that projected constraints are critical for performance; sensible analysis of easy–hard–easy trends with constraint density.
- Practical relevance: demonstrates how modest amounts of background knowledge can improve both runtime and accuracy.

Weaknesses and questions
- Soundness/clarity of pruning: Theorem 1 as stated (“P satisfies A iff its descendants satisfy A”) appears counterintuitive; more likely the intended property is monotonicity of violation (if P violates A, then so do all descendants). Please restate precisely and include a proof or intuition.
- CPDAG testing: When quick tests are inconclusive, the method “exhaustively enumerates class(P)” to check satisfiability. This can be exponential. How often does this occur in practice? Can you bound or empirically quantify the fraction of CPDAGs requiring enumeration and the cost? Could consistency checks be implemented via SAT/ILP or by applying Meek rules to avoid enumeration?
- Projection completeness and complexity:
  - Ancestral closure computation is said to be “details omitted.” Please specify the exact closure rules, their complexity, and whether the closure is complete for the entailments you use.
  - Theorem 2–3 conditions: give formal proofs or proof sketches and complexity. What is the worst-case complexity of enumerating all entailed edge constraints (over all pairs X,Y)? Are the tests applied iteratively to a fixpoint with newly inferred edges feeding back into A?
- MaxSAT encoding for ordering constraints:
  - Constraint (3) (“sufficient condition for acyclicity”) is nonstandard; please justify/cite and explain its role. Theorem 4 gives sufficient conditions for entailment; is the solution guaranteed to be maximal among all entailed orderings, or could the sufficient constraints be conservative so that some entailed orderings are missed? Empirically, how many orderings are inferred vs. the theoretical maximum?
  - The text mentions O being “closed”; please define closure and ensure transitive closure is enforced (either via hard clauses or a post-processing step).
- Evaluation details and fairness:
  - ILP settings matter greatly (e.g., family size bounds, cuts, warm starts). You note no parent bounds; could you also report results with standard bounds (e.g., max in-degree 3–5) to give a more balanced picture? List GOBNILP version, parameterization, and any additional constraints used.
  - Report runtime breakdowns (EC-tree node expansions, proportion pruned by projected constraints, number of oracle calls, % of CPDAGs requiring enumeration).
  - Robustness to noisy/mistaken constraints: the current approach assumes correct ancestral knowledge. How does performance degrade with a small fraction of incorrect constraints? Can the framework handle soft constraints (e.g., via penalties) as in Borboudakis & Tsamardinos (2013)?
- Scope: experiments go up to n=20; this is reasonable for exact learning with unrestricted parent sets, but a discussion of scaling when restricting in-degree (common in practice) would be helpful.

Quality
Generally sound and well-motivated. The core pruning and projection ideas are plausible, and the empirical results are compelling. Some theoretical statements (Theorem 1, Theorem 3–4) need clearer statements/proofs and complexity analyses. The CPDAG enumeration fallback is a potential bottleneck; its practical frequency should be quantified.

Clarity
The paper is mostly clear, with helpful motivating examples. A few places would benefit from precision (exact statement of Theorem 1, definition of “maximum set of ancestral constraints,” details of closure rules and MaxSAT encoding, and the notion of “closed” ordering sets). Minor formatting artifacts appear in the draft.

Originality
The combination of EC-tree pruning under ancestral constraints with projection to empower decomposable oracles appears novel. The MaxSAT-based inference of safe ordering constraints from ancestral constraints is also a nice contribution.

Significance
Enabling exact BN learning with practically important non-decomposable knowledge while leveraging fast decomposable oracles is significant. The reported speedups over ILP and the demonstrated accuracy benefits with modest background knowledge suggest real impact for practitioners.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important gap (exact learning with ancestral constraints) with a principled, effective method.
- Strong empirical improvements and insightful ablations.
- Technique (projection to decomposable constraints) is broadly useful.

Arguments against acceptance
- Some theoretical claims and algorithmic details are under-specified; Theorem 1 as written is confusing.
- Potential worst-case exponential step (CPDAG enumeration) is not analyzed.
- Limited exploration of robustness to noisy constraints and comparison with CP frameworks.

Overall recommendation
Weak accept. The contribution is timely and useful, with solid empirical evidence. Clarifying the theoretical statements and adding a bit more algorithmic/detail polish would strengthen the paper further.

Confidence
Medium. I am familiar with exact BN structure learning, EC/BN graphs, and ILP approaches.
