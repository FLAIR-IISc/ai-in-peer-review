Summary
This paper tackles optimal Bayesian network (BN) structure learning under background knowledge expressed as ancestral constraints (positive: X is an ancestor of Y; negative: X is not an ancestor of Y). The key difficulty is that ancestral constraints are non-decomposable, while most exact structure learning methods rely on decomposable scores and constraints. Building on the BN-graph and EC-tree search spaces of Chen et al. (2015, 2016), the authors (i) enforce ancestral constraints within the EC-tree by pruning CPDAG nodes that cannot be extended to any DAG satisfying the constraints, and (ii) “empower” a decomposable-score oracle (URLearning) by projecting ancestral constraints into decomposable edge constraints and topological ordering constraints. For edge constraints, they give necessary-and-sufficient conditions (Theorems 2–3) to infer negative/positive edges entailed by ancestral constraints. For ordering constraints, they propose a MaxSAT formulation to obtain a maximally large set of order constraints safely entailed by the ancestral constraints (with Theorem 4 providing sufficient conditions). Empirically, on standard BN benchmarks with simulated data and constraints, the approach is often orders of magnitude faster than an ILP baseline (GOBNILP), particularly when using projected constraints.

Relation to prior work
The paper is well positioned relative to (i) exact BN learning with decomposable scores (dynamic programming, A*, order graph; e.g., Koivisto & Sood, Silander & Myllymäki, Yuan & Malone), (ii) ILP/CP formulations (Jaakkola et al., Bartlett & Cussens; van Beek & Hoffmann), and (iii) constraint-based and prior-based use of path/ancestral beliefs (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos). The use of EC trees to integrate non-decomposable constraints and the projection to decomposable constraints are, to my knowledge, new in combination.

Quality
- Theory: The pruning strategy in the EC-tree is sound given the construction that only adds leaf nodes (Theorem 1). The edge-projection conditions (Theorems 2–3) are plausible and useful; the negative-edge criterion seems especially powerful and easy to check. The ordering projection via MaxSAT is carefully constrained (hard constraints for transitivity and acyclicity surrogates), but Theorem 4 provides only sufficient conditions for entailment; hence the extracted order constraints are conservative, which is an acceptable design choice here. However, many proofs and closure rules for generating a “maximum set” of ancestral constraints are omitted.
- Algorithms: The step that checks ancestral-constraint satisfaction at a CPDAG node relies on two efficient tests and, if inconclusive, exhaustive enumeration of class(P). While correct, the worst-case cost of enumeration can be exponential; guidance on when this occurs, or mitigation (caching, partial checks), would strengthen the paper.
- Experiments: The comparison to GOBNILP is appropriate and fair (both with and without projected constraints). Results show consistent speedups and a clear advantage of projection for the EC-tree oracle. The easy–hard–easy pattern as the proportion of constraints increases is well explained. Structural accuracy vs. data size and constraint proportion is informative. Scalability to n≈20 variables is comparable to prior exact methods for BDeu without parent limits.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear, with a good high-level narrative. Some technical details are deferred (proofs; the closure procedure for ancestral constraints; complexity and implementation choices for CPDAG enumeration; specifics of MaxSAT encoding). A more explicit statement of the EC-tree expansion policy (new variable added only as a leaf and as the largest variable) helps justify Theorem 1. A small schematic illustrating the two “quick tests” for pruning CPDAGs would aid readability. There are a few minor formatting glitches and typos in the provided copy.

Originality
- Integrating non-decomposable ancestral constraints into an exact structure-learning framework via the EC-tree is novel.
- The projection of ancestral constraints into decomposable edge constraints with necessary-and-sufficient criteria appears new and practically impactful.
- The use of MaxSAT to extract a maximal safe set of entailed ordering constraints from ancestral constraints is an original, pragmatic contribution.

Significance
Background knowledge in the form of ancestral relations is common in causal discovery. Showing orders-of-magnitude gains over ILP on standard benchmarks under such constraints is meaningful. The projection idea is general and could benefit other settings that mix non-decomposable priors/constraints with decomposable-score oracles. The main limitation is scale (n up to ~20), but this is in line with exact BN structure learning under BDeu in 2016.

Pros
- Clear problem formulation and motivation; relevant to causal discovery with background knowledge.
- Sound integration of ancestral constraints into EC-tree search; theoretically supported pruning.
- Effective projection of non-decomposable constraints to decomposable edge and ordering constraints; the edge-projection conditions are tight.
- Strong empirical results versus a standard ILP system, with and without projection; insightful analysis of accuracy and constraint proportion.

Cons
- Proofs and the closure algorithm for “maximum” ancestral constraints are omitted; present only statements.
- Potentially expensive enumeration of class(P) when quick tests are inconclusive; lack of analysis or mitigation.
- MaxSAT encoding provides only sufficient conditions; no guarantees on maximality beyond the chosen soft constraints; runtime overhead of the MaxSAT step is not reported.
- Evaluation limited to ILP; no CP baseline; scalability demonstrated only up to 20 variables and only for BDeu (ESS=1).
- Robustness to noisy or inconsistent constraints is not studied.

Suggestions for improvement
- Include formal proofs (perhaps in an appendix) for Theorems 1–4 and the correctness of the ancestral-closure rules.
- Analyze and report the frequency and cost of enumerating class(P); add caching or upper bounds, or propose additional polynomial tests to reduce enumeration.
- Provide an ablation study quantifying the number of inferred edge vs. ordering constraints and their individual impact on runtime and node expansions; report MaxSAT runtime separately.
- Explore robustness to imperfect constraints (contradictory or noisy), and discuss extension to soft path beliefs (cf. Borboudakis & Tsamardinos, 2013).
- Add experiments with other scores (e.g., MDL) and with/without parent-size limits; consider a CP baseline if feasible.
- Clarify details of the oracle integration (URLearning) with projected constraints and any limitations on candidate parent sets.

Overall assessment
A solid, well-motivated and technically sound contribution that advances exact BN structure learning under practically important ancestral constraints. The projection mechanism is particularly compelling and empirically impactful. I recommend acceptance after addressing clarity and missing-details issues.
