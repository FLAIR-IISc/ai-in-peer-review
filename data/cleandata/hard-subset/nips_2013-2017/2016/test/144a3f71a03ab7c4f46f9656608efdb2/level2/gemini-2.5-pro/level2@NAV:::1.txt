This paper addresses the important problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures when subject to ancestral constraints, a form of non-decomposable background knowledge. The authors build upon a recent search framework [Chen et al., 2015, 2016] that navigates a search space of equivalence classes (the EC tree) using an A* search guided by an oracle that solves structure learning with decomposable scores. The key contributions are a method for pruning the EC tree search based on the ancestral constraints, and, more significantly, a novel technique to infer decomposable edge and ordering constraints from the non-decomposable ancestral ones. These inferred constraints "empower" the oracle, leading to a much more accurate heuristic and a more efficient search.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a practical and relevant problem. Incorporating expert knowledge is crucial for learning accurate models, especially with limited data. The core idea of "projecting" non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones that an efficient underlying solver can handle is both clever and novel. It provides an elegant bridge between expressive constraints and efficient search algorithms.
*   **Technical Quality:** The approach is technically sound, supported by theorems that formalize the constraint inference process. The empirical evaluation is thorough and convincing. The authors compare against a strong and appropriate baseline (Integer Linear Programming) and demonstrate orders-of-magnitude performance improvements. The ablation study (Tables 1 vs. 2) clearly shows the dramatic impact of the proposed constraint projection technique.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and logically structured. It effectively explains the base EC tree framework before detailing its novel extensions, making the contributions easy to understand.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The paper could benefit from a brief discussion on the computational cost and scalability of the constraint projection step itself, particularly the reduction to MaxSAT. It is not clear if this pre-processing step could become a bottleneck for problems with a larger number of variables.
*   While the experiments are strong, a more explicit discussion of limitations would be welcome. For instance, how does the approach scale beyond 20 variables, and what is its performance with different densities or types of ancestral constraints?

In summary, this is a high-quality paper presenting a significant advance in exact Bayesian network structure learning. The methodology is innovative, the results are impressive, and the work has clear practical importance. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the overall strength of the contribution. I strongly recommend acceptance.
