Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies exact structure learning of Bayesian networks under ancestral (path) constraints. The authors work in the EC-tree search space over CPDAGs (Markov equivalence classes), using an A*-style best-first search with an admissible heuristic provided by a decomposable-score oracle (URLearning). The key idea is to prune EC-tree nodes using the ancestral constraints (non-decomposable) while simultaneously empowering the oracle by adding projected decomposable constraints—edge constraints (presence/absence) and ordering constraints—derived from the ancestral constraints. Edge projections are obtained by explicit reasoning over the closure of ancestral relations; ordering constraints are selected via a MaxSAT formulation. Empirically, the approach substantially outperforms an ILP baseline (GOBNILP) on synthetic problems derived from standard BN benchmarks.

The work is closely related to Chen et al. (2015, 2016) on BN graphs and EC trees for non-decomposable priors/scores and to A* search over the order graph (Yuan et al., 2011; 2013). It also connects to prior use of path constraints in constraint-based learning (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos, 2012; 2013), and to exact optimization formalisms based on ILP (Jaakkola et al., 2010; Bartlett & Cussens, 2015) and CP (van Beek & Hoffmann, 2015).

Strengths
- Addresses an important practical need: exact BN learning with path knowledge.
- EC-tree search over CPDAGs provides symmetry breaking and often a much smaller state space than DAG-space search.
- Clear separations of concerns: prune with non-decomposable constraints; strengthen the oracle with decomposable ones.
- Edge-projection rules are intuitive and potentially very useful in practice.
- Empirical results show large speedups and informative ablations on the utility of projected constraints.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Soundness and admissibility of the heuristic with ordering projections: The paper alternates between saying it “can infer” ordering constraints and describing a MaxSAT that “maximizes” selected orderings. If added orderings are merely consistent with A (but not entailed by A), then feeding them to the oracle can make the heuristic inadmissible, jeopardizing A* optimality. Theorem 4 claims sufficiency conditions, but the text still reads as if a maximal consistent set is sought rather than a provably entailed one. This must be made precise: are only entailed orderings passed to the oracle, or is optimality sacrificed? If only entailed orderings are used, please prove that the MaxSAT constraints enforce entailment (not just consistency), and explain why clause (3) suffices.
- Lemma 3 (key pruning result): The proof is unclear/incomplete. Because Lemma 3 underpins pruning EC-tree nodes using ancestral constraints, a rigorous, self-contained proof is needed. Provide a formal statement with precise preconditions (what it means for a CPDAG to “satisfy” a set A), and a proof strategy (e.g., induction on EC-tree depth, or a construction using consistent extensions of CPDAGs).
- “By the EC tree edge generation rules, Gk also contains edge Z -> W”: This step is not obvious. Please spell out the EC-tree edge-generation and orientation-propagation rules (which canonical ordering is used, what compelled orientations are applied, and which Meek rules fire). A minimal example or lemma showing why Z -> W must be oriented in Gk would remove ambiguity.
- Implied/projected vs entailed constraints: The edge projections are claimed as entailed. For orderings, the paper should clearly distinguish:
  1) constraints entailed by A (sound to use in a heuristic), versus
  2) constraints only consistent with A (may improve practical performance but can break admissibility).
  If you intentionally allow (2), please state this and explain how you still guarantee optimality (or else present results as anytime/branch-and-bound with no optimality guarantee).
- MaxSAT formulation details: The rationale for clause (3) (the “sufficient condition for acyclicity”) needs justification and a proof sketch that it indeed ensures the selected orderings are entailed by A (if that is the intent). Also report the size of the MaxSAT instance (variables/clauses ∼ O(n^2)/O(n^4)), solver time, and an ablation comparing: (i) only entailed orderings from Y 6 X; (ii) MaxSAT-selected set; (iii) no ordering projections.
- CPDAG satisfaction test: When local checks are inconclusive, enumerating class(P) can be exponential. Please quantify how often enumeration happens, the average class size encountered, and consider using a consistent-extension test (e.g., Dor–Tarsi/Chickering) or “possibly directed path” reasoning to avoid enumeration.
- Experimental comparisons:
  - GOBNILP: It is unclear whether the poor runtime is due to ancestral constraints per se or to unbounded parent sets, with which GOBNILP is known to struggle. Please include (a) experiments with a reasonable indegree bound, and/or (b) stronger ILP encodings. The comparison presently encodes ancestral constraints using the older Cussens (2008) style, which is not a tight ILP. A tighter ILP would introduce ancestry variables A(x,y) and family-selection variables I(y,U), with:
    • transitivity: A(x,z) ≥ A(x,y) + A(y,z) − 1
    • antisymmetry: A(x,y) + A(y,x) ≤ 1 (x ≠ y)
    • A–I linking: for all y,U with x ∈ U, A(x,y) ≥ I(y,U)
    • plus standard cluster or cutting-plane acyclicity over I.
    Using this stronger ILP (and modern cuts) would make the baseline more competitive and the empirical claim more convincing.
  - CP baseline: van Beek & Hoffmann (CP 2015) is not discussed or evaluated. Their CP approach often outperforms GOBNILP and seems adaptable to path constraints; please include or at least discuss this comparison.
- Self-containedness: Many operational details (URLearning heuristic, EC-tree construction, symmetry breaking) are deferred to prior work. A short recap sufficient to reproduce results would improve clarity.
- Notation and definitions: “maximum set of ancestral constraints” should be defined as the closure under transitivity and conflict-driven negatives, with the complete rule set and complexity provided.

Quality
Technically promising but currently underspecified at key points (Lemma 3 proof, EC-tree orientation argument, and especially the admissibility of the heuristic under ordering projections). The empirical methodology would benefit from stronger baselines and ablations.

Clarity
Readable and well-motivated, but several core arguments are too terse. The distinction between entailed and consistent projected constraints must be explicit.

Originality
Combining EC-tree A* search with projected constraints for empowering a decomposable oracle under path constraints is a novel and interesting synthesis.

Significance
If the theoretical guarantees are clarified and the empirical evaluation strengthened, this could be a useful exact framework for incorporating path knowledge, with practical speedups.

Suggestions for revision
- Provide a rigorous, self-contained proof of Lemma 3 and a detailed explanation of the “Z -> W” step.
- Make explicit whether projected ordering constraints are guaranteed to be entailed; if yes, fix the MaxSAT formulation and prove entailment; if no, state that optimality is no longer guaranteed and present results accordingly.
- Report MaxSAT instance sizes and solve times; add ablations isolating the contribution of edge vs ordering projections.
- Strengthen baselines: bounded indegree runs for GOBNILP, tighter ILP with A/I variables and transitivity/linking constraints, and a CP baseline (van Beek & Hoffmann, 2015).
- Replace exhaustive class(P) enumeration with a consistent-extension or possible-path test, or quantify its impact.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a relevant, underexplored constraint type with a principled search framework.
- Strong empirical speedups and useful practical ideas (edge projections).
- Leverages CPDAG symmetry breaking effectively.

Arguments against acceptance
- Key theoretical steps are underjustified; admissibility/optimality may be at risk.
- Comparisons use a weak ILP encoding and omit a strong CP baseline.
- Important algorithmic details are deferred, limiting reproducibility.
