### Review Summary

This paper presents a novel and exact algorithm for learning the structure of an optimal Bayesian network from data, subject to background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints. Since ancestral constraints are non-decomposable, they cannot be directly handled by many efficient score-based learning algorithms. The authors' main contribution is to integrate these constraints into the EC-tree search framework. This is achieved by translating the global, non-decomposable ancestral constraints into local, decomposable edge and ordering constraints that can be consumed by the oracle used in the EC-tree search. For complex ordering constraints, this translation is framed as a MaxSAT problem. The proposed method is shown to be orders of magnitude faster than a baseline implementation using the Gobnilp integer linear programming (ILP) package.

The paper is well-written and the core idea is conceptually elegant. The approach of using MaxSAT to infer a consistent set of ordering constraints is clever. However, the practical significance of the work may be limited, as the problem setting (exact structure learning with hard ancestral constraints and no hidden confounders) is somewhat niche. Furthermore, there are some concerns about the fairness of the experimental comparison and the technical details of the MaxSAT formulation.

### Quality

The paper is technically solid for the most part, but there are a few points that weaken its claims or require clarification.

**Strengths:**
*   The overall framework, which leverages the EC-tree search with an oracle for decomposable scores, is sound.
*   The proposed translation from ancestral to edge/ordering constraints is a principled way to bridge the gap between non-decomposable global constraints and local search procedures.
*   The empirical results clearly demonstrate a significant performance advantage over the ILP-based approach on the chosen benchmarks.

**Weaknesses and Questions:**
*   **Fairness of Comparison:** The impressive speedup over Gobnilp should be interpreted with caution. Gobnilp is a general-purpose solver for BN structure learning, and ILP formulations are not always the most efficient for problems where specialized search algorithms exist. The performance gap might reflect that the EC-tree approach is highly specialized for this problem, whereas the ILP baseline is not. A more informative comparison would be against a strong greedy search algorithm (like GES) adapted to respect the ancestral constraints. This would help clarify whether the benefit comes from the exact search itself or from the novel constraint handling.
*   **MaxSAT Formulation:** The rules for inferring ordering constraints (lines 257–261) raise some questions.
    *   Rule 3 appears to have a symmetric premise `(X < Y) ∧ (Z < W)` but an asymmetric conclusion, which includes `Y ↛ Z` but not its symmetric counterpart `W ↛ X`. Please clarify the derivation of this rule.
    *   Rule 4, `(X ↛ Y) ⇒ (Y < X)`, seems to preclude situations where two variables are known not to be ancestors of each other (i.e., `X ↛ Y` and `Y ↛ X`). Such knowledge would force the solver to choose between `Y < X` and `X < Y`, potentially eliminating valid DAGs where X and Y are uncoupled.
*   **Theoretical Statements:**
    *   Theorem 1 states that a CPDAG P satisfies constraints A iff its descendants do. This seems imprecise. A descendant CPDAG operates on a superset of variables. The statement should likely be qualified to refer to the projection of constraints onto the relevant variable sets, making it clear that constraint satisfaction by a parent is a necessary but not sufficient condition for its descendants.
    *   Theorem 3, for inferring positive edge constraints, seems unlikely to be triggered in practice unless the set of ancestral constraints is extremely dense. It would be helpful to report how often this rule was successfully applied in the experiments.

### Clarity

The paper is very well-written and organized. The problem, motivation, and proposed solution are explained clearly with helpful examples. The technical preliminaries are sufficient for an expert reader to understand the context.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   The encoding described as ensuring "acyclicity" on line 267 is not immediately obvious from the provided MaxSAT rules. A more explicit explanation of how these rules prevent the elimination of valid acyclic graphs would strengthen the paper.
*   Minor wording choices could be improved for formality and precision: "omniscient oracle" (line 30) could simply be "oracle"; "empower the oracle" (line 38 and elsewhere) could be rephrased as "providing the oracle with inferred constraints"; "optimizing" (line 90) should be specified as "minimizing" the score.
*   The claim that 10–25% of ancestral constraints are available (line 310) represents a substantial amount of prior knowledge. It would be useful to motivate why such a dense set of constraints might be available in practical scenarios.

### Originality

The paper presents a novel combination of existing techniques to solve an important problem. While the EC-tree search and the use of ILP/MaxSAT for BN learning are not new, the specific application of translating ancestral constraints into decomposable ones to be used within this exact search framework is original. The work is well-situated within the existing literature on score-based structure learning. The novelty is sufficient for a NIPS publication, although it is of primary interest to specialists in graphical model structure learning.

### Significance

The impact of this work is likely to be moderate.

**Positive Impact:**
*   It provides the first efficient, exact algorithm for learning BNs with ancestral constraints, a problem that has been addressed by heuristic or constraint-based methods previously.
*   The general idea of "projecting" non-decomposable constraints into a decomposable form for use in specialized solvers could be influential and applicable to other types of global constraints.

**Limiting Factors:**
*   The problem setting itself is somewhat artificial. In many domains (e.g., genetics, economics), the presence of background knowledge about causal pathways strongly suggests the possibility of unobserved confounders. In such cases, practitioners would typically turn to methods that learn models representing latent variables (e.g., MAGs or PAGs), rather than assuming causal sufficiency and learning a DAG.
*   The practical utility is unclear for sparse-constraint settings. If only a few high-confidence ancestral relations are known (a more likely real-world scenario), the benefit of this complex machinery may be minimal, as the search space would not be significantly reduced.

Overall, this is a solid contribution to the specialized area of exact score-based structure learning. It presents a novel and well-executed idea, but its practical impact may be constrained by the specificity of the problem it solves.
