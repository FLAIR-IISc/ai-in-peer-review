Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles optimal Bayesian network (BN) structure learning with background knowledge given as ancestral (path) constraints, which are non-decomposable and thus incompatible with the standard order-graph/DAG DP machinery that assumes decomposable scores. Building on the BN graph and EC tree search spaces of Chen et al. (2015, 2016), the authors search over CPDAGs with A*; they (i) prune CPDAG nodes that cannot contain any DAG satisfying the ancestral constraints, and (ii) leverage an admissible heuristic induced by an oracle that optimizes decomposable scores (e.g., URLearning). The key technical contribution is to “empower” this oracle by projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints: a complete procedure for inferring negative and positive edge constraints (Theorems 2–3) and a MaxSAT-based selection of a maximally large, cycle-free set of ordering constraints entailed by the ancestries (Theorem 4 gives sufficient conditions). This yields a tighter but still admissible heuristic. The pruning rule for CPDAGs is sound (Theorem 1) and uses efficiently checkable sufficient conditions plus exact enumeration of class(P) as a fallback.

Related work includes ILP/CP approaches that can encode ancestral relations but have not been evaluated specifically under such constraints, and prior use of path beliefs in constraint-based/local search settings (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos). The proposed framework unifies exact search over equivalence classes with non-decomposable priors/constraints and introduces practical inference of decomposable projections to guide a decomposable-score oracle.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean integration of non-decomposable constraints into exact EC-tree search, with a clear admissible heuristic story: projected constraints tighten the cost-to-go without overestimation.
- Sound, simple rules for edge-constraint entailment and a principled MaxSAT formulation for maximizing safely entailed ordering constraints.
- Empirical results suggest substantial speedups over an ILP baseline (GOBNILP), and the ablation (with vs. without projection) indicates that projection materially helps the EC-tree search.
- Useful qualitative insights: easy–hard–easy behavior as the density of ancestral constraints increases; accuracy (SHD) benefits from modest amounts of background knowledge.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Experimental design and presentation are confusing. Table 1 appears to compare (EC + projection/new) against (GOBNILP + “redundant” projection/new′). Table 2 withholds projected constraints from both. The paper should explicitly isolate the effect of the new projection machinery on EC itself, i.e., (EC + projection) vs (EC alone), across the same instances. The current narrative mentions improvements but does not present a focused, side-by-side ablation summary.
- The role of Table 3 is unclear relative to Tables 1–2. It changes settings (time/memory limits, n up to 20), includes SHD, but drops the ILP baseline and blends multiple factors (n, N, p). Please state the goal of Table 3 explicitly (scalability and effect of constraints on search difficulty) and tie it back to the core claims.
- Fairness to GOBNILP: the “redundant” projected constraints likely change the ILP polytope and can help or hurt. Clarify precisely which projected constraints are added to GOBNILP, whether they are logically entailed and safe, and quantify their effect via an ablation for GOBNILP as well (GOB vs. GOB + projection).
- Complexity and practicality of CPDAG pruning checks: when sufficient tests are inconclusive, enumerating class(P) can be exponential. Please report how often enumeration is triggered, average class sizes examined, and the runtime impact.
- MaxSAT overhead and benefit: report the number of inferred ordering constraints, MaxSAT time as a fraction of total time, and the incremental speedup from orderings alone vs. edges alone vs. both. This would illuminate how much of the gain comes from heuristic tightening versus search-space pruning.
- Proof coverage: Theorem 1 claims sound pruning; the text calls pruning “sound and complete,” but completeness depends on enumeration. Be precise about conditions under which pruning is complete. For Theorems 2–4, include proof sketches or a supplementary appendix.

Quality
Overall technically sound and careful, with sensible use of admissible heuristics and pruning. The main claims are supported by experiments, but the empirical narrative would benefit from clearer, targeted ablations and additional diagnostics (node expansions, heuristic values, contribution breakdown, MaxSAT overhead). Clarify the completeness/complexity caveats around CPDAG enumeration.

Clarity
The main approach and algorithms are explained clearly. However, the experimental section should be reorganized: state hypotheses; define configurations; present ablations in a consistent grid; explain Table 3’s purpose; and summarize key findings numerically. Minor editorial issues in references can be cleaned up.

Originality
Integrating ancestral constraints into exact EC-tree search with admissible heuristic tightening via projected decomposable constraints is novel. The edge-entailment characterization and the MaxSAT-based selection of safe orderings are also original and practically motivated.

Significance
Important for exact BN learning with prior causal knowledge, offering orders-of-magnitude speedups and a pathway to incorporate non-decomposable priors/constraints into exact search. Likely to be adopted by practitioners and to inform subsequent methods.

Requested revisions and suggestions
- Add an explicit, controlled comparison: (EC alone) vs (EC + edge projection) vs (EC + ordering projection) vs (EC + both), on the same instances used in Tables 1–2, reporting wall-clock, node expansions, and memory. This is the most relevant evidence, given that EC already dominates GOB.
- Clarify Table 3’s role and, if space permits, include the above ablations at n = 16–20 to show scalability of the projection benefit.
- For GOBNILP, present (GOB) vs (GOB + projection) to show whether projected constraints help ILP and to what extent.
- Report statistics on the number of projected constraints found, frequency of CPDAG-class enumeration, and MaxSAT time.
- Provide a brief proof sketch or appendix for Theorems 2–4 and precise statements about pruning completeness.

Pros
- Strong, principled integration of non-decomposable constraints into exact search with admissible heuristic tightening.
- Clear algorithmic contributions (edge and ordering projections).
- Large empirical speedups on standard benchmarks; informative observations on constraint density and accuracy.

Cons
- Experimental comparisons are not organized to isolate the core contribution cleanly.
- Some implementation details and complexity trade-offs (enumeration, MaxSAT overhead) are missing.
- Presentation of Table 3 is underspecified.

Recommendation
Lean toward accept after revision. The contributions are solid and relevant; tightening the experimental section with explicit EC vs EC+projection ablations and clarifying Table 3 would make the case compelling.
