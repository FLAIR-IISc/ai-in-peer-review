

# Review of "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper addresses an important practical problem in Bayesian network structure learning: incorporating background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints (e.g., "X is an ancestor of Y" or "X is not an ancestor of Y"). The authors build upon Chen et al.'s (2015, 2016) EC tree framework for learning with non-decomposable scores, which is a significant advancement over traditional decomposable-score approaches. The key innovation is showing how to incorporate ancestral constraints (which are non-decomposable) by inferring decomposable constraints that can be passed to an oracle (like URLEARNING) that optimizes decomposable scores. This approach avoids the need for ILP-based methods that have been used to handle non-decomposable constraints, but with potentially better efficiency.

## Quality

The paper makes a technically sound contribution to a meaningful problem in Bayesian network structure learning. The EC tree framework is well-established in the literature (Chen et al., 2016), and the authors extend it to handle ancestral constraints. The main technical contribution is Lemma 3, which enables pruning of the search space based on ancestral constraints. However, the proof of this lemma is unclear and lacks sufficient justification, making it difficult to verify the correctness of the pruning procedure. The authors' claim that "by the EC tree edge generation rules, Gk also contains edge Z → W" requires more explanation, as it's not obvious how the edge generation rules relate to the ancestral constraints.

The experimental comparison to GOBNILP shows impressive speedups (orders of magnitude) when ancestral constraints are present. However, the comparison is weakened by the fact that the authors use a suboptimal ILP encoding (based on Cussens, 2008), rather than a tighter formulation that would better handle ancestral constraints. A more appropriate comparison would use the stronger ILP formulation suggested by Cussens (2008) with transitivity and A-I linking constraints. Additionally, the authors do not compare against van Beek and Hoffmann (2015), which often outperforms GOBNILP and could likely be adapted to handle ancestral constraints.

## Clarity

The paper would benefit from significantly improved clarity, particularly in the presentation of the constraint projection methodology. The treatment of "implied/projected constraints" is confusing, as the authors use "can infer" without clarifying whether they are adding entailed constraints or consistent (but not necessarily entailed) ordering constraints. The MAXSAT approach for ordering constraints is mentioned but not sufficiently explained, making it difficult to understand how the authors determine which ordering constraints to add.

The section on "Projecting constraints" (Section 5) is particularly problematic. The authors claim to infer constraints from ancestral constraints but fail to provide clear algorithms or sufficient justification for why their approach correctly identifies the constraints to infer. Theorems 2 and 3 are stated without sufficient context or explanation of their practical implications.

## Originality

The paper extends the EC tree framework (Chen et al., 2016) to handle ancestral constraints, which is a non-trivial extension. The approach of inferring decomposable constraints from non-decomposable ancestral constraints is novel and addresses a practical limitation of existing approaches. However, the novelty is somewhat limited by the fact that the authors build on prior work by Chen et al. (2015, 2016) and do not fully engage with the most recent developments in the field.

## Significance

The problem addressed is significant for practitioners who want to incorporate domain knowledge into Bayesian network learning. The empirical results show that ancestral constraints can dramatically improve learning efficiency (orders of magnitude faster) and that a small number of constraints (10-25%) can have a similar impact on model quality as substantially more training data. This suggests practical value for real-world applications where domain knowledge is available. However, the significance is somewhat diminished by the lack of a fair comparison against stronger baselines and the unclear technical justification of key components.

## Pro and Con Acceptance

**Pros:**
- Addresses an important practical problem in Bayesian network learning.
- Demonstrates significant empirical improvements over existing methods.
- Builds upon a solid theoretical foundation (EC tree framework).
- Shows that background knowledge can have a substantial impact on learning quality.

**Cons:**
- Key technical lemma lacks sufficient justification.
- Constraint projection methodology is poorly explained.
- Comparison to ILP baselines is not fair or convincing.
- Does not engage with the most recent literature (e.g., van Beek and Hoffmann 2015).
- Theoretical contribution is limited by reliance on prior work.

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection. While the problem is important and the empirical results are promising, the paper's technical contributions are undermined by unclear explanations of key components and a flawed experimental comparison. The paper would benefit from significantly improved clarity in the constraint projection methodology, a more rigorous justification of the key lemma, and a more appropriate comparison against stronger baselines. The current version does not provide sufficient evidence to support the claims made about the approach's efficiency and correctness.
