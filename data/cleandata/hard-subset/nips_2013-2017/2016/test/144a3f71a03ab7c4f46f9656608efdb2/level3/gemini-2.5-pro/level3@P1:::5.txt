This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data, subject to background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints. Such constraints, which specify direct or indirect relationships (e.g., X is an ancestor of Y, or X is not an ancestor of Y), are a natural way for experts to encode prior knowledge. However, they are non-decomposable, making them incompatible with many efficient, score-based learning algorithms that rely on the assumption of a decomposable score function (e.g., dynamic programming methods like Koivisto & Sood, 2004).

The authors build upon a recent framework for optimal structure learning with non-decomposable scores by Chen et al. (2015, 2016). This framework uses an A* search algorithm over a space of equivalence classes of DAGs (an "EC tree"). The key insight of the present paper is to improve the efficiency of this search by "empowering" the heuristic function. The A* heuristic is provided by an "oracle"—an optimal learner that assumes decomposable scores. The central contribution is a method to infer, or project, decomposable constraints (specifically, edge presence/absence and topological ordering constraints) from the given non-decomposable ancestral constraints. These inferred decomposable constraints can then be passed to the oracle, resulting in a much tighter heuristic and dramatically pruning the search space. The paper presents a direct algorithm for inferring edge constraints and a novel formulation as a MaxSAT problem to infer a maximal consistent set of topological ordering constraints.

The proposed approach is empirically evaluated against an Integer Linear Programming (ILP) baseline (GOBNILP), which can also handle ancestral constraints. Across a range of benchmark datasets, problem sizes, and constraint densities, the authors' method demonstrates orders-of-magnitude speed improvements over the ILP solver.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Practicality:** The paper tackles a highly relevant problem. Incorporating expert knowledge is crucial for learning robust and meaningful graphical models, especially with limited data. Ancestral constraints are a very natural and powerful form of such knowledge. By providing an exact and efficient algorithm to handle them, this work makes optimal structure learning with this type of prior knowledge significantly more practical. The experiments clearly show that a small number of constraints can improve model accuracy as much as a large increase in data size.

2.  **Novelty and Technical Soundness:** The core technical idea of projecting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones to tighten a heuristic for A* search is both clever and powerful. While the work builds on the Chen et al. framework, the projection methodology is a novel and non-trivial contribution. The formalization of this projection for edge constraints (Theorems 2 and 3) and the use of MaxSAT to find a maximal set of consistent ordering constraints (Section 5.2) appear technically sound and well-conceived.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and structured. It provides a clear background on score-based learning, decomposability, and the challenges posed by ancestral constraints. The motivation for the proposed approach is compelling, and the overall method is explained logically.

4.  **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental section is thorough and convincing. The authors compare against a relevant and strong baseline (ILP). The experiments systematically vary key parameters (number of variables, data size, number of constraints) and the reported speedups are not marginal but span orders of magnitude. The observation and explanation of the "easy-hard-easy" pattern as the number of constraints increases also adds depth to the analysis.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Clarity of Technical Details:** While the paper is generally clear, some key technical sections are quite dense. In particular, the MaxSAT formulation for inferring topological ordering constraints in Section 5.2 is presented with little intuition or illustrative example. A small, worked-out example could have greatly improved the reader's understanding of how the clauses enforce the desired properties and why this reduction is necessary.

2.  **Incremental Contribution:** The work is presented as an application and extension of the EC tree search framework from Chen et al. (2016). While the contribution is significant, it is an incremental improvement on an existing search algorithm rather than a fundamentally new approach to structure learning. The performance is therefore tied to the effectiveness of the underlying A* search on the EC tree and the specific oracle used (URLEARNING).

3.  **Reviewer's Expertise:** I should note that my expertise in the intricacies of exact structure learning algorithms and state-of-the-art ILP solvers is limited. Therefore, I may be missing some subtleties in the comparison. However, the reported runtime differences are so substantial across multiple settings that the paper's primary claim of superior efficiency seems well-supported.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses an important, practical problem in Bayesian network learning.
*   Presents a novel and effective method for handling non-decomposable ancestral constraints.
*   The technical idea of "constraint projection" to empower a heuristic is elegant and potentially generalizable.
*   Demonstrates dramatic, orders-of-magnitude performance improvements over a state-of-the-art ILP baseline in a rigorous empirical study.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The work is an incremental, though significant, extension of a previously published framework.
*   Clarity could be improved in some dense technical sections, particularly the MaxSAT formulation.

In conclusion, this is a high-quality paper that makes a strong contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. The strengths, particularly the significance of the problem and the impressive empirical results, clearly outweigh the minor weaknesses. The proposed method represents a valuable step forward in making optimal learning with rich prior knowledge a more feasible task. I recommend acceptance.
