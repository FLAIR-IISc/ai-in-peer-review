This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data in the presence of ancestral constraints. Such constraints, which specify direct or indirect causal relationships, are a natural form of background knowledge but are non-decomposable. This non-decomposability prevents their use in many efficient score-based learning algorithms that require the scoring function to be decomposable.

The authors build upon a recent A*-based search framework [Chen et al., 2015, 2016] that operates on a search space of equivalence classes (the EC tree) and can handle non-decomposable scores by using an oracle that solves the simpler, decomposable version of the problem. The primary contribution of this work is a method to "project" the non-decomposable ancestral constraints into a set of decomposable constraints, specifically edge presence/absence constraints and topological ordering constraints. These inferred constraints are then passed to the oracle, thereby "empowering" it and making the overall search more efficient. The proposed method is empirically evaluated against an Integer Linear Programming (ILP) approach, demonstrating orders-of-magnitude speed improvements on several benchmark problems.

### Quality and Technical Soundness

The paper is technically sound. The proposed method for inferring decomposable constraints from ancestral constraints (Section 5) is well-founded, with theoretical results (Theorems 2-4) supporting the inference procedures. The use of a MaxSAT solver to find a maximal set of consistent ordering constraints is a clever approach to a non-trivial problem. The experimental methodology is solid, evaluating the algorithm's performance across varied numbers of variables, dataset sizes, and quantities of background knowledge. The results convincingly show a significant performance gain over the GOBNILP baseline, a state-of-the-art exact solver.

### Clarity

The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, and the technical preliminaries are explained concisely. The core contribution in Section 5 is presented with sufficient detail and illustrative examples. The experimental setup and results are easy to understand. One minor suggestion for improving clarity would be to reduce the space dedicated to the BN graph (Section 4.1), as the main algorithm and experiments are based on the more advanced EC tree. The saved space could be used to provide slightly more detail on the A* search process or the specific oracle (URLEARNING) used in the experiments, which would be more directly relevant to the reader trying to understand the implementation.

### Originality and Significance

The paper makes a novel and significant contribution. While ILP and CP methods can theoretically handle non-decomposable constraints, they often struggle to scale. This work provides a new, practical, and much more efficient way to leverage a powerful and natural form of background knowledge within an exact, score-based learning framework. The idea of projecting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones to improve the heuristic for an A*-based search is elegant and potentially applicable to other types of non-decomposable constraints as well. Given the practical importance of incorporating domain knowledge in structure learning, the results are likely to be of high interest to both researchers and practitioners in the field. This work clearly advances the state of the art in optimal Bayesian network learning.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

Despite the paper's strengths, there are a few points that could be addressed to make it even stronger:

1.  **Generalizability of the Projection Method:** The experiments demonstrate that the inferred constraints significantly help the EC tree search but provide inconsistent benefits for the ILP solver (comparing Tables 1 and 2). This raises the question of how general the benefit of this projection method is. The central motivation is to empower methods that *cannot* handle non-decomposable constraints. A more direct demonstration of this would be to show how the inferred decomposable constraints could be used by a classic learning algorithm based on dynamic programming (which cannot handle ancestral constraints directly) to find the correct optimal network. This would provide stronger evidence for the broad utility of the projection technique.

2.  **The "Easy-Hard-Easy" Phenomenon:** In Table 3, the runtime for the EC tree method exhibits an "easy-hard-easy" pattern as the percentage of ancestral constraints increases. The runtime first increases for a small number of constraints before decreasing as the problem becomes highly constrained. This is an interesting behavior that warrants discussion. Why does this occur? Does it suggest that the A* heuristic becomes less effective with a few constraints before the search space shrinks enough to compensate? This contrasts with the ILP approach, which appears to improve monotonically with more constraints, and it implies that the performance of the proposed method might be sensitive to the amount of domain knowledge provided. A brief discussion of this phenomenon would be insightful.

3.  **Completeness of Empirical Comparison:** Table 3 reports the Structural Hamming Distance (SHD) between the learned and ground-truth networks, which is an important measure of accuracy. However, this metric is only provided for the proposed EC tree method. To provide a complete picture, the SHD for the networks learned by the ILP baseline should also be reported. Since both are exact methods, they should find the same optimal structure and thus have identical SHD, but explicitly including this would confirm it and strengthen the comparison.

### Summary

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses the important and practical problem of learning BNs with background knowledge.
*   Presents a novel and elegant method for converting non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable ones.
*   The approach is technically sound and well-explained.
*   Empirical results show a dramatic, orders-of-magnitude performance improvement over a state-of-the-art ILP solver.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The generalizability of the proposed projection technique could be better demonstrated.
*   The paper lacks a discussion of the interesting "easy-hard-easy" performance pattern observed in the experiments.
*   The empirical comparison of accuracy (SHD) with the baseline is missing.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper with a significant and novel contribution. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and relate mostly to the experimental discussion and evaluation, rather than the core methodology. They can likely be addressed in a final version. The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning and is a clear candidate for acceptance at NIPS.
