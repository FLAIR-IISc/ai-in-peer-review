This paper presents a novel approach to learning Bayesian networks optimally, subject to background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints. The authors exploit a recently proposed framework for optimal structure learning based on non-decomposable scores, which can accommodate ancestral constraints. The key contribution of this work lies in the method to project non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and topological-ordering constraints, as described in Section 5. This allows the authors to empower an oracle that optimizes decomposable scores, leading to significant improvements in efficiency.

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the theory and experiments. The authors provide a thorough review of the problem of Bayesian network structure learning, including the challenges posed by non-decomposable constraints. They also provide a detailed explanation of their approach, including the use of EC trees and the A* algorithm.

The experiments presented in the paper demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The authors show that their method is orders-of-magnitude more efficient than alternative frameworks, such as those based on integer linear programming (ILP). They also evaluate the impact of the projection of constraints on the efficiency of learning and show that it has a significant impact on the efficiency of the EC tree approach.

However, there are some concerns that need to be addressed. Firstly, the results in Table 3 show improvement for EC trees but not ILP, raising doubts about whether the projection generalizes to other learning approaches. It would be beneficial to provide evidence that the inferred decomposable constraints improve performance/efficiency of methods that originally cannot handle non-decomposable constraints.

Secondly, Table 2 exhibits an easy-hard-easy pattern as the proportion of ancestral constraints increases. The authors should explain why this pattern occurs and whether EC trees are highly sensitive to the amount of domain knowledge compared to ILP. This is important because it may imply that the number of ancestral constraints needs careful tuning to achieve ideal EC tree performance.

Finally, the paper only reports structural Hamming distance for EC trees. It would be useful to add a comparison of SHD with the ILP approach to provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.

In terms of presentation, it would be beneficial to drop Section 4.1 on BN graph and instead expand explanations of EC trees and the A* algorithm in the EC-tree experimental setting. This would provide a clearer understanding of the approach and its application.

In conclusion, this paper presents a novel and effective approach to learning Bayesian networks optimally, subject to background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints. The authors provide a thorough explanation of their method and demonstrate its effectiveness through experiments. However, there are some concerns that need to be addressed, and some suggestions for improving the presentation.
