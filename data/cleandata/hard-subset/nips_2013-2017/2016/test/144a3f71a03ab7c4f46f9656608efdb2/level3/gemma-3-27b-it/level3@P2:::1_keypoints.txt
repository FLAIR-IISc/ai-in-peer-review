- Addresses the practical problem of optimal Bayesian network structure learning under ancestral constraints.  
- Proposes combining non-decomposable scores with EC-tree search, a pruning strategy, and projection of ancestral constraints into decomposable forms.  
- Core idea is promising: flexibility of non-decomposable scores plus efficient search via constraint projection.  
- Well-motivated and correctly positions limitations of decomposable-score and ILP methods; connects clearly to prior work (Chen et al.).  
- Lemma 3 (basis for pruning) is insufficiently explained; soundness—especially regarding descendants of pruned nodes—needs stronger justification.  
- The statement about EC-tree edge generation (Figure 1(b)) is unclear and requires elaboration.  
- The paper misuses the term “infer”; it adds constraints consistent with ancestral knowledge rather than logically entailing them—this distinction must be clarified.  
- Rationale for using MaxSAT to add ordering constraints is not well justified; the MaxSAT encoding and its effect on search need much more detail.  
- Experimental results show significant speedups over GOBNILP, which is encouraging.  
- Comparison is weakened by choosing GOBNILP as a baseline; performance differences may stem from GOBNILP’s limitations (e.g., unbounded parent sets) rather than the proposed method’s strengths.  
- A stronger ILP baseline using A(X,Y) and I(Z,U) variables with transitivity and A–I linking constraints should be included for a fairer comparison.  
- Omission of van Beek & Hoffmann (CP 2015) is a significant oversight; adapting and comparing to that CP approach would strengthen empirical evaluation.  
- Reporting structural Hamming distance (Δ) is a positive aspect, providing accuracy insight.  
- Strengths summarized: important problem, promising framework, ancestral-constraint pruning strategy, empirical speedups, SHD reporting.  
- Weaknesses summarized: unclear key explanations, misleading “infer” language, insufficient MaxSAT detail, weak baselines and omitted related work, limited discussion of method limitations.  
- Arguments for acceptance: tackles a relevant challenge, promising empirical results, well-connected to prior work.  
- Arguments against acceptance: unclear presentation and theoretical gaps, weakened empirical evaluation, insufficient justification of pruning.  
- Overall assessment: weak reject; significant clarity and experimental issues must be addressed before acceptance.  
- Recommendation: clarify theoretical claims (Lemma 3, EC tree), explicitly distinguish inference vs. constraint addition, justify and detail MaxSAT encoding, and strengthen empirical baselines (include ILP variants and van Beek & Hoffmann).
