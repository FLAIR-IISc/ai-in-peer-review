

This paper addresses the problem of learning Bayesian network structures with ancestral constraints, which are non-decomposable and thus challenging for most existing approaches. The authors build upon Chen et al.'s (2015) framework for optimal structure learning with non-decomposable scores, introducing a novel approach that leverages the EC tree search space and an oracle for decomposable scores. Their key insight is to transform ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints that can be efficiently handled by existing oracles, thereby enabling significant efficiency gains.

The paper makes a clear contribution to the literature on Bayesian network structure learning. Previous work (e.g., Koivisto and Sood, 2004; Parviainen and Koivisto, 2013) has largely focused on decomposable constraints, while approaches using integer linear programming (e.g., Cussens, 2008) have not been evaluated for learning with ancestral constraints. The authors' approach is a significant step toward integrating non-decomposable background knowledge into optimal structure learning, which has practical importance in domains where causal relationships are known.

The technical contribution is well-motivated and presents a theoretically sound approach to handling ancestral constraints. The EC tree representation provides a more compact search space than the BN graph, and the pruning mechanism based on ancestral constraints is sound. The paper demonstrates that their approach can be orders-of-magnitude faster than ILP-based approaches (GOBNILP) when using projected constraints. The empirical evaluation is thorough, with experiments varying network size, dataset size, and constraint density, and the results consistently show significant speedups.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that need addressing. First, Lemma 3, which is central to the pruning mechanism, lacks a clear proof and justification. The statement "By the EC tree edge generation rules, Gk also contains edge Z -> W" requires more detailed explanation to establish its validity. Second, the treatment of inferred constraints is problematic. The paper claims to "infer" constraints from ancestral constraints but fails to clarify that these are consistent (not necessarily entailed) constraints. The use of "can infer" is misleading without explicit explanation of the selection mechanism.

The experimental comparison is also problematic. The authors compare against GOBNILP using Cussens' (2008) ILP formulation, which is a weak encoding for ancestral constraints. A more appropriate comparison would use a tighter ILP formulation with A(X,Y) variables for ancestral relations and I(Z,U) variables for edges, with transitivity constraints linking them. This would provide a fairer comparison and strengthen the empirical claims. The paper also fails to discuss van Beek and Hoffmann (2015), which presents a constraint programming approach that often outperforms GOBNILP and could likely be adapted to handle ancestral constraints.

The paper's significance is clear: ancestral constraints are important in practice for incorporating domain knowledge, and the authors demonstrate substantial efficiency gains. However, the weak comparison to ILP and the unclear theoretical justification for key steps reduce the impact of the contribution. The approach is technically sound, but the paper would benefit from more rigorous theoretical justification and a more comprehensive comparison to state-of-the-art methods.

In terms of clarity, the paper is generally well-written but suffers from the aforementioned theoretical gaps. The explanation of how to infer ordering constraints using MaxSAT is insufficiently detailed, and the paper would benefit from a more thorough description of the MaxSAT encoding and the rationale behind the constraints.

The originality of the work is strong, as it addresses a non-decomposable constraint type that has been largely overlooked in the literature. The authors successfully extend Chen et al.'s framework to handle ancestral constraints, and the EC tree approach represents a meaningful advancement over previous work.

Quality-wise, the theoretical foundation is mostly sound but has gaps in key proofs. The empirical evaluation is strong and well-designed, but the comparison to baseline methods is not as robust as it could be. The paper would benefit from a more rigorous theoretical justification of the pruning mechanism and a more comprehensive empirical comparison to state-of-the-art methods.

In summary, this paper presents a valuable contribution to Bayesian network structure learning with a clear practical application. The approach is theoretically grounded and demonstrates impressive empirical efficiency gains. However, the paper would be significantly stronger with clearer theoretical justification, a more robust empirical comparison, and a better explanation of the constraint inference process. The current work is promising but requires these improvements to fully realize its potential.
