This paper presents a novel approach to learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints, a challenging problem due to the non-decomposable nature of these constraints. The core contribution lies in projecting these non-decomposable constraints into decomposable edge and topological ordering constraints, enabling the use of efficient algorithms leveraging decomposable scores. The paper is well-written and clearly explains the theoretical background and experimental setup. The authors effectively connect their work to prior research in Bayesian network structure learning, highlighting the limitations of existing methods when dealing with ancestral constraints.

The authors build upon their previous work on optimal structure learning with non-decomposable priors [Chen et al., 2015, 2016], specifically leveraging the EC tree search space and an oracle for learning with decomposable scores. The key insight is to empower this oracle by providing it with the inferred decomposable constraints, leading to significant efficiency gains. The theoretical framework is sound, and the theorems presented (Theorems 1-4) provide a solid foundation for the proposed method.

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, employing simulated data from standard Bayesian network benchmarks. The results demonstrate a significant speedup compared to an ILP-based approach (GOBNILP), particularly when utilizing the projected constraints (Table 1). However, a key observation from the experiments (Table 3) is that this improvement isn't consistently seen with ILP; in fact, ILP performance remains relatively stable regardless of projected constraints. This raises a crucial question: does the projection of constraints truly generalize to improving *all* approaches incapable of directly handling non-decomposable constraints, or is the benefit primarily realized within the EC tree framework? Providing further evidence supporting the broader applicability of this projection technique would strengthen the contribution. 

The observation of an “easy-hard-easy” pattern in Table 2 as the proportion of ancestral constraints increases is also noteworthy and warrants further discussion. The authors should investigate and explain why performance initially improves, then degrades, and finally improves again with increasing constraint density. Is the EC tree particularly sensitive to the amount of domain knowledge provided, compared to ILP? Understanding this sensitivity is important for practical application, as it suggests a potential need for careful tuning of the number of ancestral constraints to achieve optimal performance. The implication is that the user needs to be careful while providing the domain knowledge.

Furthermore, Table 2 only reports the Structural Hamming Distance (SHD) for the EC tree approach. Including a comparison of SHD with the ILP approach would provide a more complete picture of the accuracy of both methods under different constraint levels. This would allow for a more nuanced assessment of the trade-off between computational efficiency and model accuracy.

While Section 4.1 introduces the BN graph, it feels somewhat tangential to the main contribution, which focuses on the EC tree and its adaptation for ancestral constraints. The paper would benefit from streamlining the presentation by removing Section 4.1 and expanding the explanation of EC trees and the A* algorithm specifically in the context of the experimental setup. A more detailed explanation of how the A* search is implemented and how the heuristic function is used to guide the search within the EC tree would be valuable.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* **Novelty:** The projection of non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints is a novel and valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning.
* **Efficiency:** The proposed approach demonstrates significant efficiency gains compared to existing methods, particularly for larger networks.
* **Soundness:** The theoretical framework is well-founded, with clear theorems supporting the proposed method.
* **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the complex concepts involved.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* **Generalizability:** The efficiency gains are most pronounced within the EC tree framework, and it's unclear whether the projected constraints generalize to improve *all* methods unable to handle non-decomposable constraints directly.
* **Sensitivity Analysis:** The “easy-hard-easy” pattern in Table 2 raises concerns about the sensitivity of the EC tree to the amount of domain knowledge.
* **Incomplete Comparison:** The lack of SHD comparison with ILP in Table 2 hinders a complete assessment of model accuracy.



**Overall:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important problem in Bayesian network structure learning. While some concerns remain regarding the generalizability of the approach and the sensitivity to domain knowledge, the novelty and efficiency gains demonstrated are significant. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically addressing the points raised above regarding the generalizability of the constraint projection, the explanation of the "easy-hard-easy" pattern, and the inclusion of SHD comparisons with ILP.
