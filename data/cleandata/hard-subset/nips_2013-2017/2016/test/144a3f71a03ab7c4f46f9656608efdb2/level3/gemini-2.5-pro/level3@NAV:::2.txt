This paper presents a novel method for learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data, subject to background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints. Recognizing that such constraints are non-decomposable and thus incompatible with many efficient score-based learning algorithms, the authors adapt the recently proposed EC tree search framework, which can accommodate non-decomposable scores. The core contribution is a technique to leverage an oracle—an exact solver for decomposable scores—as an admissible heuristic for an A* search on the EC tree. Crucially, the authors show how to "empower" this oracle by projecting the non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints that the oracle can handle. This tightens the heuristic, pruning the search space more effectively. The approach is positioned as a more efficient alternative to methods based on Integer Linear Programming (ILP), and builds directly upon recent work by Chen et al. (2015, 2016).

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance:** The work addresses the important and practical problem of incorporating rich, non-decomposable background knowledge into the structure learning process. The empirical results in Table 3 effectively demonstrate how a small number of ancestral constraints can significantly improve model accuracy, rivaling the effect of a large increase in data.
*   **Originality:** The method of inferring decomposable constraints from non-decomposable ones to inform an admissible heuristic for an exhaustive search is both novel and elegant. It provides a generalizable strategy for integrating complex constraints into heuristic search frameworks.
*   **Quality:** The proposed approach is technically sound, and the empirical results show orders-of-magnitude speedups over a state-of-the-art ILP solver (GOBNILP) on the specific task evaluated.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Clarity of Experiments:** The primary weakness lies in the experimental evaluation, which is confusing and undermines the paper's central claims. In Table 1, the main comparison is between (EC tree + constraint projection) and (GOBNILP + constraint projection). By applying their novel projection technique to the baseline solver, the authors have confounded the comparison, making it difficult to isolate the performance gains of their EC-tree-based framework versus the ILP framework.
*   **Missing Baseline Comparison:** For a clearer evaluation of the proposed system, a direct comparison between (EC tree + projection) and the un-enhanced GOBNILP is needed. Furthermore, to explicitly demonstrate the value of the constraint projection technique, the most relevant comparison would be between the full system (EC tree + projection) and the EC tree framework alone (pruning based on constraints but with a non-empowered heuristic). While the data to infer this is present across Tables 1 and 2, the main comparative claims are not presented this way.

Overall, this paper introduces a clever and potentially high-impact technique. However, the claims of superiority are not well-supported by the current experimental design. The paper would be much stronger if the experiments were revised to include clearer, unconfounded comparisons against the relevant baselines.
