

# Review of "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"

This paper addresses the problem of optimal Bayesian network structure learning under ancestral constraints, which specify cause-effect relationships between variables. The authors build upon Chen et al.'s [2015, 2016] framework for structure learning with non-decomposable scores, extending it to incorporate ancestral constraints - a significant challenge as these constraints are inherently non-decomposable. Previous approaches either assumed decomposable scores (limiting their ability to handle ancestral constraints) or used ILP/CP methods that could represent these constraints but were computationally expensive.

The main contribution is a method to transform non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints that can be efficiently handled by existing oracles (algorithms for decomposable scores). The authors introduce a MaxSAT formulation to infer the largest possible set of ordering constraints from ancestral constraints, and demonstrate how to prune the EC tree search space using these inferred constraints. This approach leverages Chen et al.'s EC tree framework, which already provides an efficient search space for non-decomposable scores.

The paper's technical approach is well-motivated and theoretically sound. The authors establish theorems (Theorems 1-4) showing the correctness of their constraint projection method. The MaxSAT formulation for inferring ordering constraints is particularly elegant, with careful attention to avoiding constraint cycles that would eliminate valid networks. The empirical evaluation demonstrates remarkable performance gains: the proposed method is orders-of-magnitude faster than ILP-based approaches (GOBNILP) across all tested parameters, with the speedup increasing as problem size grows. The experiments also reveal that a small number of ancestral constraints (10-25%) can have similar impact on model accuracy as significantly increasing the training dataset size.

In terms of quality, the paper presents a technically sound approach with rigorous theoretical foundations and comprehensive empirical validation. The authors clearly acknowledge limitations (e.g., the need to exhaustively enumerate structures when pruning CPDAGs) and provide appropriate comparisons to relevant prior work. The clarity of the paper is excellent, with well-organized sections and helpful examples illustrating key concepts like the EC tree and constraint projection.

The originality is strong, as the transformation of non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones represents a novel approach to a well-known problem. While ancestral constraints have been used in constraint-based learning (Borboudakis and Tsamardinos [2012, 2013]), and ILP approaches can represent them (Jaakkola et al. [2010]), this paper provides the first efficient exact approach for incorporating them into optimal structure learning. The authors properly contextualize their work within the NIPS literature on structure learning (Koivisto and Sood [2004], Parviainen and Koivisto [2013], Yuan and Malone [2013]).

The significance is substantial. For practitioners, the orders-of-magnitude speedup makes optimal structure learning with background knowledge feasible for larger problems. The paper demonstrates that background knowledge (in the form of ancestral constraints) can significantly improve learning accuracy, potentially reducing the need for large datasets. The scalability to 20 variables represents a meaningful advance in exact structure learning, comparable to state-of-the-art methods.

Strengths:
- Novel and effective method for handling non-decomposable constraints
- Theoretical soundness with well-proven theorems
- Empirical results showing dramatic runtime improvements
- Clear explanation of technical concepts
- Practical impact for incorporating domain knowledge

Weaknesses:
- Limited discussion of cases where constraint projection fails to infer useful constraints
- The paper could provide more insight into how to handle conflicting ancestral constraints
- The MaxSAT solver implementation details could be more thoroughly explained

The reviewer has limited expertise in this specific technical approach but finds the runtime comparisons particularly impressive given the magnitude of the speedup across all tested scenarios. The paper successfully demonstrates that their approach is not just theoretically sound but practically superior to existing alternatives.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that advances the state of the art in Bayesian network structure learning. The paper is well-written, technically rigorous, and demonstrates significant practical impact. The authors have successfully extended Chen et al.'s framework to handle a practically important class of constraints while maintaining computational efficiency.

Pro: Strong theoretical foundation, significant empirical improvements, clear writing, practical significance
Con: Limited discussion of constraint projection limitations, some implementation details could be expanded

The paper meets all the criteria for a high-quality NIPS contribution, with clear significance for the field. I strongly recommend acceptance.
