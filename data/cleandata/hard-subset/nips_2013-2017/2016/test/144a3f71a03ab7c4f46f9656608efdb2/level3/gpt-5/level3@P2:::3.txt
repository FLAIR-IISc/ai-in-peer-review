Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses exact Bayesian network (BN) structure learning under background knowledge expressed as ancestral (path) constraints. Because such constraints are non-decomposable, they are incompatible with the dominant exact methods that rely on decomposable scores and local constraints over families or orders. Building on the BN-graph and EC-tree search spaces introduced by Chen et al. (2015; 2016), the paper proposes to (i) prune the EC-tree using tests that determine whether a CPDAG can be extended to satisfy a set of ancestral constraints, and (ii) “empower” a decomposable-score oracle (e.g., order-graph DP/A*) by projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge constraints (via sound rules; Theorems 2–3) and ordering constraints (via a MaxSAT formulation; Theorem 4). Empirically, the method is compared to an ILP-based system (GOBNILP) augmented to handle ancestral constraints, showing large speedups on benchmark networks.

This contribution connects to score-based optimal BN learning via dynamic programming and A* (Koivisto & Sood 2004; Silander & Myllymäki 2006; Yuan & Malone 2011, 2013), ILP/CP encodings (Jaakkola et al. 2010; Bartlett & Cussens 2015; van Beek & Hoffmann 2015), and to work incorporating path constraints in constraint-based and greedy settings (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos 2012, 2013). The paper advances the EC-tree line by supporting non-decomposable constraints while still leveraging strong decomposable oracles.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The overall architecture is sensible: prune at the CPDAG level, and tighten the oracle’s heuristic by safe projections to edge and order constraints. The pruning is claimed to be sound and complete for ancestral constraints; however, Theorem 1 needs a more careful statement. As written, “P satisfies A iff its descendants satisfy A” is easy to misread. Please make explicit that (a) A is over the full variable set; (b) EC-tree edges add only a leaf (no new edges among existing variables); and (c) “P satisfies A” means ∃G ∈ class(P) that satisfies A. With these quantifiers, the monotonicity argument is plausible, but the proof sketch should be clarified.
- Edge-constraint inference: The negative-edge rule (Theorem 2) appears correct and useful. The positive-edge rule (Theorem 3) is quite strong and thus will rarely fire when constraints are sparse; this limits its practical impact and should be discussed.
- Ordering-constraint inference: The MaxSAT encoding is interesting, but two issues reduce confidence:
  1) Rule 3 (“sufficient condition for acyclicity”) has a symmetric premise but an asymmetric disjunction in the conclusion. Please justify this constraint rigorously (or provide a proof/intuition that it suffices) and explain how it avoids excluding DAGs in G(A).
  2) Rule 4 turns X 6 Y into a soft clause Y < X. This is reasonable for compatibility (a DAG with X 6 Y always admits some topological order with Y < X), but when both X 6 Y and Y 6 X are given, the encoding must avoid selecting both Y < X and X < Y. Since (4) is soft this can be handled by MaxSAT, but it would be useful to explain how you prevent the solver from introducing cycles while maximizing selected orderings.
- Empirical evaluation: The study is thorough on benchmarks and explores ablations (with vs without projected constraints), but the main baseline (GOBNILP) was not designed specifically for this setting, which weakens the claim of superiority. Consider adding greedy/heuristic baselines that can incorporate path constraints (e.g., GES or greedy local search with path constraints), and/or CP-based solvers. Also report results with very sparse constraints (e.g., 1–5% of pairs or a fixed small number of constraints), as this is closer to practice. Finally, quantify the number of projected edge vs. ordering constraints obtained and their respective ablation impact on runtime.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and easy to follow. A few suggestions:
- Be consistent and precise about optimizing minimization vs. maximization (e.g., if scores are additive costs with shortest paths, say “minimize cost” early; line ~90).
- “Omniscient oracle” vs. “oracle”: prefer “oracle for decomposable scores” to avoid suggesting it returns solutions unavailable to the algorithm (line ~30). “Empower the oracle” (line ~38) could be rephrased as “tighten the oracle’s admissible heuristic via projected constraints.”
- Explain the “acyclicity” constraint in the MaxSAT section (line ~267); its current form is not easily recognizable as encoding acyclicity; provide intuition and a reference or proof.
- Theorem statements (esp. 1, 3, 4) would benefit from explicit quantifiers, assumptions (closed/maximal A), and brief proofs or pointers to an appendix.
- Minor: the claim that 10–25% ancestral constraints is realistic (line ~310) seems optimistic; acknowledge that such dense prior knowledge is uncommon and report experiments with much sparser constraints.

Originality
Moderate. Using EC-tree pruning for non-decomposable constraints and projecting them to decomposable constraints to strengthen a decomposable oracle is a neat combination that, to my knowledge, has not been explored for ancestral constraints. Prior work has encoded path constraints for constraint-based or greedy learning, and ILP/CP can represent such constraints, but the specific projection plus EC-tree pruning is novel.

Significance
Mixed. On the positive side, the method yields large computational gains in the tested regime and shows how to leverage background knowledge in exact BN learning. However, the impact may be limited by (i) the practical availability of only a small number of ancestral constraints, (ii) the focus on fully observed DAGs (no latent confounders), where many causal applications would prefer MAG/PAG models, and (iii) the dependence on strong oracles with precomputed scores, which can be restrictive for larger domains.

Pros
- Sound framework for incorporating ancestral constraints into exact score-based BN learning.
- Clever use of projected decomposable constraints to tighten admissible heuristics.
- Strong empirical speedups; clear ablation showing the value of projections.
- Well written; builds logically on recent EC-tree work.

Cons
- Some theoretical components (Theorems 1, 3; MaxSAT Rule 3) need clearer statements/justification.
- Positive edge inference is likely rare under sparse constraints.
- Baseline comparison may be unfair; lacks greedy/CP baselines tailored to path constraints.
- Practical scenarios often have very few constraints; limited discussion/experiments in that regime.
- The MaxSAT encoding is presented tersely; acyclicity constraints are non-obvious.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify Theorem 1 with explicit assumptions and a short proof; add proofs for Theorems 3–4 or move full details to an appendix.
- Provide intuition/proof for the MaxSAT acyclicity constraint; discuss why the premise and conclusion are sufficient and safe.
- Report how many edge vs. ordering constraints are inferred and their separate contributions to speedup.
- Add baselines such as GES or greedy hill-climbing with path constraints and a CP solver; include experiments with very sparse constraints.
- Tone down claims about typical availability of 10–25% constraints and broaden discussion to settings with latent confounding.
- Minor wording edits noted above.

Overall assessment
A solid and well-executed paper that extends EC-tree search to handle non-decomposable ancestral constraints and demonstrates substantial computational benefits. With clearer theoretical exposition and broader empirical baselines—especially under sparse prior knowledge—the contribution would be stronger. I view this as a promising but somewhat niche advance; currently borderline for acceptance.
