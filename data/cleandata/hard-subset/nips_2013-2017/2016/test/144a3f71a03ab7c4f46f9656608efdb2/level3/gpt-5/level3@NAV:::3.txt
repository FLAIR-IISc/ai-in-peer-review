Summary and relation to prior work: The paper tackles optimal Bayesian network (BN) structure learning with background knowledge given as ancestral constraints (reachability and non-reachability). Building on EC-tree search with an oracle for decomposable scores (Chen et al. 2015, 2016), the authors translate non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints to “empower” the oracle. Ordering constraints are extracted via a MaxSAT formulation that makes implied constraints explicit; edge implications are derived via closed-form rules. Empirically, the method is compared to a modified GOBNILP ILP baseline and is reported to be orders of magnitude faster. The work connects to ILP/CP encodings of BN learning (Jaakkola et al. 2010; Cussens 2008) and to path-constraint causal discovery (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos), and extends recent non-decomposable scoring search spaces.

Quality: The pruning strategy on the EC tree is conceptually sound, and the projection idea is well motivated. However, Theorem 1 needs clarification: “P satisfies A iff its descendants satisfy A” seems too strong unless “satisfies” is defined carefully. As stated, the set of constraints A satisfied by a CPDAG P (existential over class(P)) is, in general, a subset of those satisfied by a particular DAG descendant; please restate as a monotonicity property and make quantifiers explicit. Theorem 3 appears weak when ancestral constraints are sparse, so few positive edge implications may arise. The MaxSAT “acyclicity” encoding (line 267) is not obviously sufficient; a short proof or citation would help. Specific rule concerns (lines 257–261): acyclicity rule 3 has symmetric premises but asymmetric conclusion; rule 4 seems to exclude constraints like “X and Y do not cause each other.” Please justify or revise.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written overall. Minor issues: line 30 (use “oracle for decomposable scores,” not “omniscient oracle”), line 38 (“empower the oracle” sounds informal), line 90 (clarify optimizing=minimizing the score), line 310 (10–25% ancestral constraints imply substantial prior knowledge; not typical).

Originality: Moderate. Translating ancestral to ordering constraints via MaxSAT inside an EC-tree search is a neat and novel combination (novelty=3).

Significance: Limited in practical impact (impact=2). In many applications, ancestral knowledge arises with possible unobserved confounding, suggesting broader causal discovery settings (e.g., MAGs/FCI). In practice, few ancestral constraints may be available, limiting search-space reduction.

Empirics: The comparison to GOBNILP is informative but not entirely fair—GOBNILP was not designed for this setting, and the strong gains may reflect this mismatch. Consider additional baselines such as constrained GES/UGES (assuming consistency of ancestral constraints with the optimal model) to assess efficiency and pruning benefit.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear problem formulation and well-written exposition
- Conceptually simple, general projection framework
- Sound EC-tree pruning idea; practical MaxSAT reduction
- Strong empirical speedups on benchmarks
- Relevant to specialists in score-based BN learning

Cons for acceptance:
- Practical impact limited by artificial setting and scarcity of constraints
- Theorem 1 statement likely incorrect/unclear; Theorem 3 weak in sparse regimes
- Acyclicity encoding and inference rules need justification
- Baseline comparison may be mismatched; missing greedy/constraint-aware baselines
- Claims around 10–25% prior constraints seem unrealistic in practice

Overall: Borderline. Constructive revisions addressing theory clarity, rule justifications, and broader baselines would strengthen the paper.
