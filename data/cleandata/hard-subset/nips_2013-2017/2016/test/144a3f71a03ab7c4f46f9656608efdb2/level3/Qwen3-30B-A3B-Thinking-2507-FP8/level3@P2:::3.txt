

# Review for "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"

## Summary
This paper addresses the problem of optimal Bayesian network structure learning under ancestral constraints, which are non-decomposable and challenging for existing approaches that rely on decomposable scores. The authors propose a framework that leverages the EC tree search space introduced in Chen et al. (2016) and empowers an oracle (for decomposable scores) by translating ancestral constraints into decomposable ordering and edge constraints. The key insight is that while ancestral constraints cannot be directly used by decomposable-score-based methods, they can be transformed into equivalent constraints that these methods can handle. The authors evaluate their approach against an ILP-based system (GOBNILP) modified to handle ancestral constraints, demonstrating significant speed improvements.

The work builds on Chen et al. (2015, 2016), which introduced the BN graph and EC tree for learning with non-decomposable scores, and extends it to handle ancestral constraints. This represents a meaningful extension to the literature on optimal structure learning, as prior work (e.g., Koivisto and Sood, 2004; Parviainen and Koivisto, 2013) has largely focused on decomposable constraints.

## Strengths
**Quality**: The paper presents a technically sound approach with a clear theoretical foundation. The transformation of ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints is well-motivated, and the empirical evaluation demonstrates significant efficiency gains (orders of magnitude faster) compared to ILP-based approaches. The authors carefully address the problem of non-decomposability through a novel constraint projection technique.

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explained. The concepts are presented in a logical flow, with good use of figures (Figure 1) to illustrate the EC tree. The technical details are accessible to readers familiar with Bayesian network structure learning. The authors make careful distinctions between different constraint types and their implications.

**Originality**: The work makes a novel contribution by enabling the use of ancestral constraints within the EC tree framework. While prior work (e.g., Chen et al., 2015) has addressed non-decomposable scores, this paper specifically tackles the challenging case of non-decomposable ancestral constraints. The MaxSAT encoding for ordering constraints represents a clever solution to a non-trivial problem.

**Significance**: The results demonstrate clear practical significance for structure learning with background knowledge. The empirical results show that even a small number of ancestral constraints (10-25%) can substantially improve learning efficiency, comparable to increasing training data size by several orders of magnitude. This has important implications for applications where background knowledge is available but data is limited.

## Weaknesses
**Quality**: Theorem 1 contains an inaccuracy: the set of constraints satisfied by a CPDAG P is only a subset of those satisfied by the DAGs in its equivalence class, not equivalent. This requires clarification. The acyclicity constraint (rule 3) in Section 5.2 has a symmetric premise but asymmetric conclusion, which is confusing. Theorem 3 is unlikely to produce many positive edge implications with sparse ancestral constraints, limiting its practical utility.

**Clarity**: The paper could better clarify the relationship between the oracle and "omniscient oracle" (line 30), and the phrase "empower the oracle" (line 38) is somewhat vague. The claim that 10-25% ancestral constraints is typical (line 310) is questionable, as such a high proportion would imply extensive background knowledge.

**Significance**: The practical impact is somewhat limited. Ancestral constraints often arise in causal discovery settings where unobserved confounders are present, but the paper does not address this important extension. The comparison to GOBNILP is not entirely fair, as GOBNILP was not designed for ancestral constraints. A comparison to greedy approaches like GES with ancestral constraints would better demonstrate the value of the optimal approach.

**Originality**: While the constraint translation technique is novel, the overall framework builds heavily on prior work (Chen et al., 2015, 2016), which slightly diminishes the novelty contribution.

## Arguments for Acceptance
- Presents a technically sound solution to a non-trivial problem in Bayesian network structure learning.
- Clear, well-written presentation with strong empirical validation.
- Demonstrates significant practical efficiency gains (orders of magnitude faster than ILP).
- Addresses an important gap in the literature regarding non-decomposable constraints.
- Provides a novel technique for transforming non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones.

## Arguments Against Acceptance
- The practical significance is limited by the artificial nature of the constraint setting (ignores unobserved confounders).
- The comparison to GOBNILP is not entirely fair as it wasn't designed for this problem.
- Theoretical results (Theorems 1 and 3) have limitations that need clarification.
- The claimed impact of 10-25% ancestral constraints is questionable as this implies extensive background knowledge.

## Recommendation
This is a strong technical contribution with clear practical implications for structure learning with background knowledge. The paper makes a meaningful extension to the EC tree framework and demonstrates significant efficiency improvements. While the practical significance could be strengthened by addressing the unobserved confounder issue and providing a more appropriate baseline comparison, the core contribution is valuable and well-executed.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the theoretical inaccuracies (Theorem 1) and clarify the claims about constraint density. The paper makes a solid contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning.
