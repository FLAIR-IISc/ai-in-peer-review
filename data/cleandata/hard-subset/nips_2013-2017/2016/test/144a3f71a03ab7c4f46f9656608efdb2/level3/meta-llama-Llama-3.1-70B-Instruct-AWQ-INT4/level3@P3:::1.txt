This paper presents a novel approach to learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints using branch-and-bound A* search over CPDAGs, symmetry breaking, and a heuristic from URLearning. The authors tackle the problem of learning Bayesian networks optimally, subject to background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints, which are non-decomposable and pose a difficulty for learning approaches based on decomposable scores.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the problem of learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints. They also provide a thorough review of related work, including structure learning approaches based on integer linear programming (ILP) and constraint programming (CP). The authors' approach is based on a recently proposed framework for optimal structure learning based on non-decomposable scores, which is general enough to accommodate ancestral constraints.

The authors propose a sound and complete method for pruning the EC tree, based on ancestral constraints, and show how the employed oracle can be empowered by passing it decomposable constraints inferred from the non-decomposable ancestral constraints. They also provide a detailed explanation of how to infer edge and ordering constraints from ancestral constraints, which is a crucial step in their approach.

The experimental evaluation of the proposed approach is thorough and well-designed. The authors compare their approach to GOBNILP, an ILP-based system, and show that their approach is orders-of-magnitude more efficient for almost all values of n, N, and p that they varied. They also evaluate the impact of introducing ancestral constraints on the efficiency of search and the scalability of their approach.

However, there are some weaknesses in the paper. Lemma 3 is the key result enabling pruning with ancestral constraints, but its proof is unclear and needs better justification. The statement "By the EC tree edge generation rules, Gk also contains edge Z -> W" requires more explanation. The treatment of implied/projected constraints and inferred ordering constraints is poorly explained, and the use of "can infer" is misleading without clarifying that authors add consistent (but not necessarily entailed) ordering constraints.

Additionally, the authors use a MAXSAT approach to control which ordering constraints are added, but the inference procedure and rationale are insufficiently detailed. The comparison to GOBNILP shows large speedups, but it is unclear if GOBNILP's poor performance is due to ancestral constraints specifically or its general weakness with unbounded parent set size.

The paper also compares against a MAXSAT encoding of ancestral constraints (Cussens 2008), which is a poor ILP formulation. A tighter ILP using A(X,Y) and I(Z,U) variables with transitivity and A–I linking constraints would be preferable. Comparing to a weak encoding weakens the empirical claim, and even if the suggested ILP were used, the authors' method may still win, but the current comparison is not fully convincing.

Finally, the work of van Beek and Hoffmann (CP 2015) is not discussed, which often outperforms GOBNILP and could likely be adapted to handle ancestral constraints and should be compared.

In conclusion, the paper presents a novel approach to learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints, and the experimental evaluation is thorough and well-designed. However, there are some weaknesses in the paper that need to be addressed, including the proof of Lemma 3, the explanation of the EC tree edge generation rules, and the treatment of implied/projected constraints and inferred ordering constraints.

* The paper presents a novel approach to learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints.
* The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-designed.
