

# Review of "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"

This paper addresses the problem of optimal Bayesian network structure learning under ancestral constraints, which specify cause-and-effect relationships between variables. The authors build on Chen et al.'s (2015, 2016) work on non-decomposable scores by introducing a method to incorporate ancestral constraints (which are non-decomposable) into the EC tree search space. The key insight is to project these non-decomposable constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints that can be used to prune the search space and improve the heuristic function used in A* search. This approach leverages an oracle (e.g., URLEARNING) that optimizes decomposable scores, which cannot directly handle ancestral constraints.

The paper makes several important contributions: it formally establishes how to infer decomposable constraints from ancestral constraints (Theorems 2 and 3), proposes a MaxSAT-based approach for inferring ordering constraints (Section 5.2), and demonstrates empirically that their method outperforms ILP-based approaches (GOBNILP) by orders of magnitude (Tables 1 and 2). The authors also show that a relatively small number of ancestral constraints can significantly improve learning accuracy, comparable to substantially larger datasets.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with appropriate references to related work. The theoretical foundation is sound, with detailed proofs of the constraint projection methods. The authors effectively relate their work to previous NIPS contributions, particularly Chen et al. (2015, 2016) and Yuan and Malone (2013).

However, the experimental evaluation has significant issues that undermine the paper's claims. The most critical problem is the confusing presentation of results. Table 1 compares "EC + new work" (the authors' approach with constraint projection) against "GOB + new work'" (GOBNILP with projected constraints), but it's unclear what "new work'" refers to or why this comparison is made. This obscures the main contribution of the paper, which is the constraint projection method itself.

More importantly, the paper fails to provide the explicit comparison requested: (EC + new work) versus EC alone. The authors claim their constraint projection "empowers the oracle," but without showing how much improvement this brings over the base EC approach (without constraint projection), it's impossible to assess the value of their key contribution. Table 2 shows some improvement when using projected constraints with ILP, but this doesn't address the main point: how much does constraint projection improve the EC tree method itself?

The authors also fail to clarify the role of Table 3, which reports results for n=16,18,20 variables with time and memory limits. It's unclear why these results are presented separately from Tables 1-2, and it doesn't provide the missing comparison between EC with and without constraint projection.

While it's true that the EC approach is superior to GOBNILP (as shown in Table 1), the paper should focus on demonstrating the value of their constraint projection method for the EC approach, not comparing against ILP approaches. The most relevant comparison is (EC + constraint projection) versus EC alone, which would show whether the constraint projection method is worth implementing. Without this comparison, the paper cannot convincingly demonstrate the significance of their contribution.

The paper's significance is somewhat diminished by this experimental flaw. While incorporating ancestral constraints is valuable, the authors don't clearly show how much their method improves upon existing EC tree approaches. The claim that their method is "orders-of-magnitude more efficient" is supported only by comparisons against ILP approaches, not against the base EC approach.

In summary, the paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution to Bayesian network learning under ancestral constraints. The method of projecting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones is novel and well-motivated. However, the experimental evaluation fails to properly assess the main contribution (constraint projection), making it difficult to evaluate the paper's significance. The paper would be significantly strengthened by adding an explicit comparison between EC with and without constraint projection.

Strengths:
- Solid theoretical foundation with well-proven constraint projection methods
- Clear connection to previous work on EC trees and non-decomposable scores
- Demonstrates practical value of ancestral constraints in improving learning accuracy

Weaknesses:
- Experimental evaluation does not address the core contribution (constraint projection)
- Confusing presentation of results (Table 1 comparison)
- Missing critical comparison (EC + projection vs EC alone)
- Role of Table 3 is unclear

Given these issues, I recommend rejection due to insufficient experimental validation of the main contribution. The paper would be suitable for acceptance if the authors could provide the missing comparison and clarify the experimental results.
