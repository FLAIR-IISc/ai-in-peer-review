- Problem: Learning optimal Bayesian network structures with non-decomposable ancestral constraints (X is/ is not ancestor of Y) which break standard decomposable-score algorithms.  
- Main contribution: Adaptation of an A*-based EC-tree search by empowering the oracle with inferred decomposable edge and ordering constraints from ancestral constraints.  
- Technical novelty: Reduction of inferring ordering constraints to a MaxSAT problem to produce a maximal consistent set of orderings.  
- Empirical claim: Orders-of-magnitude runtime improvements over an ILP-based baseline (Gobnilp).  
- Prior work: Builds directly on EC-tree and BN graph search frameworks by Chen et al. (2015, 2016).  
- Strength — originality: Novel idea to project non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones usable by efficient oracles.  
- Strength — technical choice: Using MaxSAT to infer maximal consistent ordering constraints is novel and appropriate.  
- Strength — clarity: Paper is very well-written, motivated, and organized; decomposable vs non-decomposable distinction is clearly explained.  
- Strength — quality: Theorems provide a sound theoretical basis; experiments are thorough across variables, sample sizes, and amount of background knowledge.  
- Strength — empirical: Substantial empirical advantage over the ILP baseline.  
- Weakness — practical impact: Assumes no latent variables, limiting applicability where unobserved confounders exist; causal methods (e.g., FCI) may be more appropriate in such settings.  
- Weakness — prior density assumption: Experiments assume 10–25% ancestral relations known, which may be unrealistically dense for many real-world domains.  
- Weakness — baseline choice: Comparison only to Gobnilp (ILP) may overstate gains, since heuristic search methods (e.g., GES) often scale better than ILP.  
- Weakness — technical clarity: Theorem 1 statement is imprecise/possibly incorrect as written and needs clarification.  
- Weakness — MaxSAT encoding: Some MaxSAT rules (e.g., the acyclicity sufficient condition and rule 4) are asymmetric or restrictive and lack clear justification.  
- Weakness — Theorem 3 practicality: The condition for inferring positive edge constraints seems very strict and may rarely fire; needs empirical frequency/comment.  
- Acceptance pro: Addresses an important problem of incorporating rich, non-decomposable background knowledge into score-based BN learning.  
- Acceptance pro: The projection technique is elegant and conceptually significant.  
- Acceptance pro: Strong empirical results and clear exposition justify publication.  
- Rejection con: Limited practical significance due to latent variable assumption and need for dense prior knowledge.  
- Rejection con: Missing comparisons to strong heuristic baselines that could challenge claimed runtime benefits.  
- Rejection con: Several technical details (MaxSAT formulation, Theorem 1) lack sufficient clarity or justification.
