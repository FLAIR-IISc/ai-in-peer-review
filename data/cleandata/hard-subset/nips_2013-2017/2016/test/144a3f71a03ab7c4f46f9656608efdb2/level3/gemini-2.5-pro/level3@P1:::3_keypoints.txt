- Addresses learning optimal Bayesian network structures with non-decomposable ancestral constraints by projecting them to decomposable constraints usable by existing solvers.  
- Builds on EC-tree A*-like search and uses an "oracle" solver for decomposable scores to compute admissible heuristics.  
- Core contribution: projecting ancestral constraints to edge and topological ordering constraints, formulated as a MaxSAT problem to improve oracle heuristics and prune search.  
- Empirical claim: orders-of-magnitude speedups versus an ILP baseline (GOBNILP) on benchmark networks.  
- Quality: technically sound, with theoretical justification and thorough empirical evaluation.  
- MaxSAT formulation concerns: Rule 3’s asymmetric disjunction and omission of (Y < Z) appears dubious; Rule 4 may wrongly force an ordering when variables are causally independent.  
- Theorem statements need precision: existential conditions over DAGs in CPDAG classes should be stated explicitly; current iff claims may be stronger than proven or necessary.  
- Theorem 3’s practical utility is limited since its conditions (all intermediate paths forbidden) are unlikely under sparse ancestral constraints.  
- Experimental comparison weakness: GOBNILP wasn’t designed for these constraints, so the large speedups may partly reflect baseline mismatch.  
- Suggested additional baselines: include a constrained greedy search (e.g., GES adapted to constraints) to better contextualize gains.  
- Practical significance concern: assumption of causal sufficiency (no latent confounders) limits applicability in domains where ancestral knowledge is common.  
- Practical relevance concern: experiments assume relatively dense constraint sets (10–25%); real-world priors are often much sparser, possibly reducing speedups.  
- Clarity strengths: paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and makes complex ideas accessible.  
- Minor clarity edits: avoid “omniscient oracle,” prefer simply “oracle”; clarify that “optimizing” the score means minimizing in the shortest-path formulation.  
- Originality: novel and clever combination of EC-tree search, MaxSAT projection, and BN optimization to handle non-decomposable constraints.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel method, significant empirical efficiency over ILP, and high clarity/presentation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited applicability due to no-latent assumption, evaluation should include constrained greedy baselines and sparser constraints, and some technical/maxSAT/theorem details need clarification.  
- Overall: strong, novel, and well-executed contribution, but with important technical clarifications and evaluation extensions needed to fully assess impact.
