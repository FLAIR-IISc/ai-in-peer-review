This paper addresses the important problem of learning Bayesian network structures optimally, subject to background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints. The authors build upon their previous work [Chen et al., 2015, 2016] which introduced a framework for optimal structure learning with non-decomposable scores, a key limitation of many existing approaches. The core contribution lies in demonstrating how to incorporate these non-decomposable ancestral constraints by inferring decomposable constraints that can empower existing oracle-based learning algorithms. They empirically demonstrate significant performance gains—orders of magnitude faster—compared to Integer Linear Programming (ILP) approaches.

The work is well-motivated. Bayesian networks are widely used in various fields, and the ability to incorporate prior knowledge, such as causal relationships expressed as ancestral constraints, is crucial for both improving accuracy and efficiency. The paper clearly articulates the limitations of traditional decomposable-score-based methods when faced with such constraints.  The connection to previous work, including [Darwiche, 2009; Koller and Friedman, 2009; Murphy, 2012] providing background on Bayesian networks, and [Koivisto and Sood, 2004; Parviainen and Koivisto, 2013] discussing structure-modular constraints, is solid. The paper appropriately positions itself within the existing literature.  The recent related work by Borboudakis and Tsamardinos [2012, 2013] on constraint-based learning and path constraints is also well-acknowledged.

The technical approach is sound.  The use of the EC tree and the method for projecting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable forms are clearly explained. The introduction of Theorem 1 (soundness of pruning) and Theorems 2-4 (inferring edge and ordering constraints) adds to the rigor of the work.  Formulating the problem as a MaxSAT instance is a clever way to find a maximal set of inferrable ordering constraints, and the discussion on how this empowers the oracle is compelling.  

The empirical evaluation is a strong point.  The authors present a thorough comparison against a state-of-the-art ILP-based system (GOBNILP) using simulated data from established Bayesian network benchmarks (ALARM, ANDES, etc.). The results consistently show significant speedups achieved by their approach, particularly as the number of variables increases.  The ablation study, comparing performance with and without projected constraints (Tables 1 & 2), convincingly demonstrates the effectiveness of the constraint projection technique. The analysis of the structural Hamming distance (∆) and its relationship to data size and constraint proportion provides valuable insight into the quality of the learned models.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an important problem:** Efficiently incorporating background knowledge into Bayesian network structure learning.
* **Novel approach:** Leveraging decomposable constraints inferred from non-decomposable ancestral constraints.
* **Strong empirical results:**  Orders-of-magnitude speedup compared to ILP.
* **Technical rigor:** Clear explanations, well-defined theorems, and a sound methodology.
* **Thorough evaluation:** Comprehensive experiments using standard benchmarks and ablation studies.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited scalability beyond 20 variables:** While the reported results are promising, the scalability to significantly larger networks remains an open question, although this is acknowledged and is comparable to the state of the art.
* **MaxSAT solver dependency:**  The performance of the MaxSAT solver (EVASOLVER) could potentially become a bottleneck for larger problems. This is not extensively discussed.
* **Oracle reliance:** The approach relies on the quality of the oracle used for decomposable score optimization (URLEARNING in this case). A more detailed analysis of the oracle’s impact would be beneficial.
* **Limited discussion of constraint types:** The focus is primarily on ancestral constraints. Exploring the applicability of this approach to other types of non-decomposable constraints could be valuable.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to a significant problem in Bayesian network structure learning.
* The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate a substantial improvement over existing methods.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details.
* The work is likely to be influential in the field, as it offers a practical solution for incorporating background knowledge into Bayesian network learning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The scalability to very large networks remains a concern, although this is a common limitation in Bayesian network structure learning.
* The dependency on a MaxSAT solver and the oracle introduces potential performance bottlenecks.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. The authors have presented a novel, technically sound, and empirically validated approach for incorporating ancestral constraints. The demonstrated performance improvements are substantial. While some limitations exist (scalability, dependency on external solvers), these are acknowledged and do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.  I **strongly recommend acceptance** of this paper to NIPS 2016.  I have limited expertise in this specific subfield, but the clarity of the presentation and the compelling empirical results lead me to this positive assessment.
