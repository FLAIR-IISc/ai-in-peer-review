This paper addresses the problem of learning Bayesian network structures optimally under ancestral constraints – a challenging task due to the non-decomposability of these constraints. The authors build upon a recent framework based on navigating the space of all network structures using an oracle for learning with decomposable scores, and demonstrate how to incorporate ancestral constraints by projecting them into decomposable constraints that the oracle can handle. The core idea is to prune the search space based on ancestral constraints and empower the oracle with inferred, decomposable constraints to improve search efficiency.

The paper is well-motivated, as ancestral constraints are important in practice and typically hinder performance in standard structure learning algorithms. The approach of leveraging an oracle for decomposable scores alongside constraint projection is a sensible strategy. The authors correctly identify the limitation of existing methods that rely on decomposable scores when dealing with non-decomposable constraints. The framework builds on prior work by Chen et al. (2015, 2016) establishing a solid foundation, and properly references related work in Bayesian network structure learning (Darwiche, 2009; Koller & Friedman, 2009; Murphy, 2012), constraint-based methods (Koivisto & Sood, 2004; Parviainen & Koivisto, 2013), and integer linear programming (Jaakkola et al., 2010; Bartlett & Cussens, 2015).

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The paper presents a novel approach to incorporating ancestral constraints into optimal Bayesian network structure learning, particularly through the projection of non-decomposable constraints into a decomposable form usable by an existing oracle.
* **Empirical Results:** The experiments demonstrate significant performance improvements (orders of magnitude in speed) compared to an ILP-based approach (GOBNILP), indicating a practical benefit.
* **Soundness:** The theoretical basis, particularly the pruning step based on ancestral constraints, is logically sound. Theorem 1 provides a formal guarantee for the pruning process.
* **Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly defines the problem and the limitations of existing approaches, laying a strong foundation for the proposed solution.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Lack of Clarity in Proofs & Explanations:** The justification for Lemma 3, crucial for the pruning process, is insufficiently detailed and needs a more rigorous explanation. The statement concerning the EC tree edge generation rule requires further elaboration.
* **Misleading Terminology:** The use of “can infer” regarding ordering constraints is imprecise. It’s unclear whether the authors are deriving entailed constraints or adding consistent, but not necessarily entailed, constraints. This distinction is vital and requires clarification.
* **Insufficient Detail on Constraint Inference:** The process of projecting ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints (especially the MAXSAT formulation) lacks sufficient detail. The rationale for the MAXSAT formulation and the specific constraints used aren't fully explained.  Why MAXSAT and not a simpler heuristic for ordering constraint selection?
* **Weak Baseline Comparison:** While the experimental comparison demonstrates speedups, the choice of GOBNILP as a baseline is questionable. GOBNILP’s performance might be poor due to inherent limitations with unbounded parent set size, rather than specifically due to the ancestral constraints.  A stronger ILP formulation would be beneficial.
* **Missing Related Work:** The paper doesn’t discuss the work of van Beek and Hoffmann (2015), a constraint programming approach that often outperforms GOBNILP and could be a more relevant comparison point, especially as it’s adaptable to ancestral constraints.  
* **Scalability Limitations:** While the results show scalability up to n=20, it would be helpful to discuss potential limitations with larger networks and more complex ancestral constraints.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant problem in Bayesian network structure learning.
* The proposed approach is novel and theoretically sound.
* The empirical results show substantial performance gains.
* The work represents a valuable contribution to the field.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The lack of clarity in the proofs and explanations weakens the theoretical contribution.
* The choice of baseline and missing related work limit the strength of the empirical evaluation.
* The insufficient detail regarding constraint inference hinders reproducibility.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. The approach of leveraging an oracle and projecting constraints is a clever and effective way to handle ancestral constraints. However, the weaknesses related to clarity, experimental setup, and related work need to be addressed.  

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Provide a more rigorous justification for Lemma 3.
2.  Clarify the distinction between entailed and consistent ordering constraints.
3.  Provide a more detailed explanation of the MAXSAT formulation and the rationale behind its constraints.
4.  Include a comparison to van Beek and Hoffmann (2015) or justify its exclusion.
5.  Discuss potential scalability limitations and future research directions.
