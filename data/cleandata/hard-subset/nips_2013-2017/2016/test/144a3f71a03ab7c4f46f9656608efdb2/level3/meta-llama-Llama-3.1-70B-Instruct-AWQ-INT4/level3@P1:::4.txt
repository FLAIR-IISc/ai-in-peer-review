This paper presents a novel approach to learning Bayesian networks optimally, subject to background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints. The authors propose a method to project non-decomposable ancestral constraints onto decomposable edge and topological-ordering constraints, which can be used to empower an oracle for structure search. The approach is based on a recently proposed framework for optimal structure learning with non-decomposable scores, which is general enough to accommodate ancestral constraints.

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the theory and experiments. The authors provide a thorough review of the problem of Bayesian network structure learning and the challenges posed by non-decomposable constraints. They also provide a detailed description of their approach, including the projection of ancestral constraints onto decomposable constraints and the use of an oracle to navigate the search space.

The experimental results show that the proposed approach is orders-of-magnitude more efficient than alternative frameworks, such as those based on integer linear programming (ILP). The authors also demonstrate that the projection of ancestral constraints onto decomposable constraints has a significant impact on the efficiency of structure learning, often by several orders of magnitude.

However, there are some concerns and suggestions for improvement. Firstly, the results in Table 3 show improvement for EC trees but not ILP, raising doubts about whether the projection generalizes to other learning approaches. The authors should provide evidence that the inferred decomposable constraints improve performance/efficiency of methods that originally cannot handle non-decomposable constraints.

Secondly, Table 2 exhibits an easy-hard-easy pattern as the proportion of ancestral constraints increases. The authors should explain why and whether EC trees are highly sensitive to the amount of domain knowledge compared to ILP. This has implications for the number of ancestral constraints that may need careful tuning to achieve ideal EC tree performance.

Thirdly, Table 2 reports structural Hamming distance only for EC trees. The authors should add a comparison of SHD with the ILP approach to provide a more comprehensive evaluation of their approach.

Finally, the authors may consider dropping Section 4.1 on BN graph and instead expanding explanations of EC trees and the A* algorithm in the EC-tree experimental setting. This would provide a more detailed understanding of the approach and its experimental evaluation.

Overall, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning, and the authors have done a good job in presenting their approach and experimental results. With some additional evidence and explanations, the paper has the potential to make a strong impact in the field.
