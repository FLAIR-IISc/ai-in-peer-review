This paper proposes a novel approach to learning Bayesian networks optimally under ancestral constraints. The key idea is to translate these non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ordering constraints, which can then be leveraged within an efficient EC-tree search framework. The authors demonstrate empirically that their method significantly outperforms an Integer Linear Programming (ILP)-based approach (GOBNILP) in terms of computational efficiency.

The paper is well-written and clearly explained, making the conceptual contributions readily accessible. The problem addressed is relevant – incorporating background knowledge into Bayesian network structure learning is a crucial area – and the proposed solution is conceptually straightforward and well-motivated. The novelty is appreciable (rating: 3/5); while leveraging oracles for BN structure learning isn’t entirely new [Chen et al., 2015, 2016], adapting this framework to handle non-decomposable ancestral constraints via projection to ordering constraints is a significant contribution. However, the practical impact is somewhat limited (rating: 2/5). The experimental setting, focused specifically on the presence of *known* ancestral constraints, feels somewhat artificial. In many real-world scenarios, ancestral constraints would serve as starting points for broader causal discovery, explicitly accounting for potential unobserved confounders, which this work doesn't address.

The technical aspects are generally sound. The use of a MaxSAT solver to infer maximal sets of ordering constraints is a clever technique. However, some aspects warrant closer scrutiny. Specifically, concerns arise regarding the correctness of the acyclicity rule presented in lines 257–261. The asymmetric conclusion of rule 3, and the potential for rule 4 to exclude valid constraints (e.g., mutual independence), should be carefully examined. Additionally, the authors acknowledge that, in practice, only a limited number of ancestral constraints are likely to be available. While the projection to ordering constraints can still be beneficial, it's questionable whether a sparse set of constraints will dramatically reduce the search space.

The empirical evaluation is well-designed and demonstrates a clear advantage over the ILP-based baseline. However, the comparison to GOBNILP is not entirely fair. GOBNILP was not specifically designed for learning with ancestral constraints, and the substantial performance gains observed may, in part, reflect this mismatch. A more compelling comparison would involve a baseline leveraging a greedy algorithm (e.g., GES) adapted to incorporate ancestral constraints as prior knowledge, allowing for a better gauge of the efficiency improvements achieved by the proposed approach.

Several minor points also require attention. The statement of Theorem 1 is potentially misleading. A CPDAG satisfying constraints *A* only implies the existence of *at least one* DAG within its equivalence class that satisfies *A*, not that all DAGs in the class do. Hence the “iff” statement is questionable. Theorem 3 is likely to generate limited positive edge implications when the ancestral constraints are sparse. The term “acyclic” used in line 267 isn't immediately recognizable as capturing acyclicity and could be clarified. Finally, minor wording and clarity issues exist throughout the paper, including the inconsistent use of “oracle” vs. “omniscient oracle” (line 30), the ambiguity of “empower the oracle” (line 38), the need to clarify “optimizing=minimizing” (line 90), and the caveat that the 10-25% ancestral constraint results (line 310) represent a substantial degree of prior knowledge that is not typically available.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to integrating non-decomposable constraints into Bayesian network structure learning.
*   Clear and well-written presentation of the method.
*   Significant empirical improvement over a relevant baseline (GOBNILP).
*   Sound theoretical foundation and justification for the proposed approach.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The experimental setting is somewhat artificial and may not accurately reflect real-world applications.
*   Potential issues with the correctness of the acyclicity rule.
*   The impact of sparse ancestral constraints on search space reduction is unclear.
*   The comparison to GOBNILP is not entirely fair.
*   Minor technical and clarity issues throughout the paper.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some limitations, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. The proposed approach is novel, well-motivated, and demonstrates promising results. I recommend **acceptance** contingent upon addressing the concerns raised regarding the correctness of the acyclicity rule and clarifying the discussion of the experimental setup and results. Specifically, adding a more relevant baseline (e.g., a greedy algorithm with ancestral constraints) would greatly strengthen the evaluation. Addressing the points in Theorem 1 and 3 would also benefit the clarity and soundness of the paper.
