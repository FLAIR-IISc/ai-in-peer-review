This paper addresses optimal Bayesian network structure learning under non-decomposable ancestral constraints, a practically important class of background knowledge for cause–effect relations. The authors build on the BN-graph and EC-tree shortest-path frameworks of Chen et al. (2015; 2016), which can accommodate non-decomposable scores by searching directly in the space of DAGs/equivalence classes while leveraging an oracle that optimizes decomposable scores. The main technical novelty is a “projection” method that infers decomposable constraints—edge presence/absence and pairwise topological orderings—from ancestral constraints. These projected constraints are then fed to the oracle (e.g., URLearning on the order graph), tightening its heuristic and dramatically reducing the EC-tree search. On the modeling side, the paper gives sound pruning rules for CPDAG nodes in the EC tree under ancestral constraints, and on the inference side, it provides: (i) an efficient algorithm to infer all entailed edge constraints via α(·)/β(·) reachability sets (Theorems 2–3), and (ii) a MaxSAT formulation to extract a maximally large, acyclic set of order constraints consistent with the ancestral constraints (Theorem 4). Empirically, on standard BN benchmarks (ALARM, ANDES, CHILD, CPCS, HEPAR2), the EC-tree approach with projected constraints is orders-of-magnitude faster than an ILP baseline (GOBNILP), especially as n grows; the projection is shown to be crucial for EC-tree efficiency. The paper also reports an easy–hard–easy pattern as the proportion of ancestral constraints increases and shows that ancestral knowledge can substantially reduce SHD versus ground truth, sometimes rivaling the effect of large increases in data.

Relation to prior work: Mainstream exact methods with decomposable scores—DP/order-graph search (Koivisto & Sood, 2004; Silander & Myllymäki, 2006), A* (Yuan et al., 2011; Yuan & Malone, 2013)—cannot encode path/ancestry. ILP/CP approaches can encode non-decomposable constraints (Jaakkola et al., 2010; Cussens, 2008; Bartlett & Cussens, 2015; van Beek & Hoffmann, 2015) but often scale poorly without indegree bounds. The paper is also complementary to constraint-based methods exploiting path constraints and priors (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos, 2012; 2013). The idea of using projected constraints to empower a decomposable-score oracle within a non-decomposable search framework appears novel.

Quality:
- Theory: Pruning for ancestral constraints in the EC tree is sound; edge entailment (Thms. 2–3) and MaxSAT encoding (Thm. 4) are well-motivated. However, some details are deferred (e.g., completeness/complexity of α/β-closure; frequency/cost of enumerating class(P) when path tests are inconclusive), and a brief complexity analysis would strengthen the claims.
- Empirics: The speedups are compelling and consistent. Ablations (no projection vs. edge-only vs. order-only) would more cleanly attribute gains to each projection component and quantify the MaxSAT overhead. Reporting expansions/closed nodes in A* would make the easy–hard–easy phenomenon more transparent. Finally, accuracy (SHD) is only reported for EC-tree runs; see below.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally very well written and easy to follow. Section 4 on EC trees and the pruning rules is clear. I would suggest dropping Section 4.1 on BN graphs and use the space to (i) expand the A* + oracle interface for EC trees, including how projected edge/order constraints are injected into URLearning, and (ii) detail the two fast CPDAG tests for positive/negative ancestral constraints to minimize resorting to class(P) enumeration.
- The reference list contains several formatting issues/placeholders that should be fixed.

Originality:
- The central idea of projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints and using them to tighten a decomposable oracle within a non-decomposable search space is, to my knowledge, new. The specific α/β entailment tests and the MaxSAT formulation for extracting a large acyclic subset of orderings entailed by ancestry are also original contributions.

Significance:
- Practically impactful: background causal knowledge is common, and the demonstrated speedups are substantial without indegree bounds. The projection technique is simple and likely adoptable beyond this particular search framework.
- The current experimental scope focuses on EC trees vs. ILP; broader demonstrations that projection helps other learners would increase impact.

Specific concerns and requests:
1) Do projected constraints help competing learners?
- With projected constraints, ILP shows little improvement for small n and some for larger n; the gains are not nearly as dramatic as for EC trees. Please provide evidence that the inferred decomposable constraints improve performance in other settings that “originally cannot handle non-decomposable constraints,” e.g., dynamic programming over the order graph and A* without EC-tree pruning (or with precedence constraints per Parviainen & Koivisto, 2013), CP, or alternative ILP formulations. An ablation that plugs the projected constraints into a strong order-graph/A* baseline would clarify generality.

2) Easy–hard–easy behavior and sensitivity to p:
- The easy–hard–easy pattern as the proportion of ancestral constraints increases deserves a deeper explanation. Please analyze how p affects (i) the number of CPDAG nodes pruned by the cheap path tests vs. by exhaustive enumeration, (ii) heuristic tightness (g+h gap or bound difference), and (iii) branching factor/expansions. Contrast with ILP’s more monotone behavior to understand whether EC-tree search is unusually sensitive to the amount of domain knowledge. This would guide users on how much knowledge is needed and why mid-range p can be hardest.

3) Report ILP accuracy:
- Table 2/3 report SHD only for EC-tree runs. Please add SHD (or at least final score and whether optimality was certified) for ILP runs under the same time/memory limits. If ILP times out, report best-feasible SHD at timeout. This will reassure readers that both exact methods recover the same optimum when possible, and quantify anytime behavior.

4) Robustness to noisy/inconsistent knowledge:
- Real background knowledge can be wrong or inconsistent. Can your projection/MaxSAT approach handle conflicts (e.g., by softening ancestral constraints with penalties) and still guarantee admissibility for the heuristic? A brief discussion and, if possible, a small experiment would broaden applicability.

5) Cost of projection:
- Please report the overheads of edge entailment and MaxSAT (wall-clock and fraction of total time), and provide a small complexity discussion of the α/β closure and Theorems 2–3 (worst-case dependence on n and density of A).

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, well-motivated problem with practical importance.
- Elegant theory: sound pruning in EC trees; novel projection of ancestry to edges and orders (Theorems 2–4).
- Strong empirical speedups, especially as n grows; projection demonstrably crucial.
- Good writing and connection to relevant prior work.

Cons against acceptance:
- Limited evidence that projection broadly helps other learners beyond EC-tree + oracle.
- Sensitivity to the amount of domain knowledge is not fully analyzed.
- Missing accuracy metrics for ILP; incomplete ablations and overhead analysis.
- Some algorithmic details (closure/enumeration costs) are deferred.

Overall, this is a solid and timely contribution. Addressing the above points—especially broader evidence of generality and more detailed analysis of the easy–hard–easy phenomenon and projection overhead—would significantly strengthen the paper.
