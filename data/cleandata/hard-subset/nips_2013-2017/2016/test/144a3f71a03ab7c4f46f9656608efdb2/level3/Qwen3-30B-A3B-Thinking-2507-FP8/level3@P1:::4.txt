

This paper addresses the important problem of learning Bayesian network structures with ancestral constraints, which are non-decomposable and thus challenging for standard optimal structure learning approaches. The authors build on Chen et al. (2015, 2016), who proposed a framework for optimal structure learning with non-decomposable scores by leveraging oracles for decomposable scores. Their key contribution is a novel method to project non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and topological-ordering constraints (Section 5), which can then be used to empower existing oracles. The paper demonstrates that their EC tree approach, when augmented with these projected constraints, achieves orders-of-magnitude speedups over ILP-based methods like GOBNILP.

The paper is well-written and clearly explains both the theoretical framework and experimental results. The technical presentation is precise, with careful definitions of ancestral constraints and their relationship to decomposable constraints. The authors properly contextualize their work within previous NIPS literature, particularly Chen et al. (2015, 2016), Koivisto and Sood (2004), and Parviainen and Koivisto (2013), while clearly distinguishing their contributions.

The main strength of the paper is the elegant solution to a significant problem in Bayesian network structure learning. Ancestral constraints are common in domain knowledge but have been largely ignored in optimal structure learning due to their non-decomposable nature. The authors' projection method enables standard oracles to effectively handle these constraints, which is both theoretically sound and empirically effective. The experimental results (Tables 1-3) are convincing, showing consistent speed improvements across different problem sizes and constraint proportions.

However, there are several concerns that need addressing before acceptance:

First, the paper claims that the projected constraints improve efficiency for methods that originally cannot handle non-decomposable constraints, but Table 2 shows that the ILP approach (GOBNILP) does not benefit from the projected constraints in the same way as the EC tree approach. This raises questions about whether the projection method generalizes to other learning frameworks. The authors should provide additional evidence demonstrating that the projected constraints improve performance for ILP-based methods or explain why the benefit is limited to the EC tree approach.

Second, the easy-hard-easy pattern observed in Table 2 (as constraint proportion increases) requires explanation. This pattern is not intuitive and suggests that EC trees may be highly sensitive to the amount of domain knowledge provided. The authors should discuss why this pattern occurs and whether it represents a fundamental limitation of their approach compared to ILP, which shows more consistent improvements with increasing constraints.

Third, Table 2 reports structural Hamming distance (SHD) only for the EC tree approach, making it difficult to assess whether the speedup comes at the cost of model quality. The authors should include SHD comparisons between EC trees and ILP to demonstrate that the faster approach also produces comparable or better models.

Finally, Section 4.1 on BN graphs appears redundant given the paper's focus on EC trees. The authors should consider removing this section to streamline the presentation and instead expand the explanation of EC trees and the A* algorithm in the experimental section, which would better support their empirical claims.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning by providing a practical solution to incorporating ancestral constraints. The theoretical framework is sound, and the empirical results are compelling. The main issue is the lack of evidence that the constraint projection method generalizes to other learning frameworks, which weakens the claim about the method's broad utility.

Quality: The paper is technically sound with strong theoretical foundations and well-designed experiments. The authors are careful to evaluate both strengths (speedup) and weaknesses (sensitivity to constraint proportion) of their approach.

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written with clear explanations of complex concepts. The experimental setup is well-described, though the SHD comparison would strengthen the evaluation.

Originality: The projection method for converting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones is novel and represents a significant advancement over previous work. The paper clearly differentiates itself from Chen et al. (2015, 2016) and other related work.

Significance: This work has practical importance for domains where causal knowledge is available but has been difficult to incorporate into optimal structure learning. The orders-of-magnitude speedup could enable more complex Bayesian network applications in practice.

Pro: Novel projection method for ancestral constraints, strong empirical results, clear writing, practical significance.

Con: Limited evidence of generalization to other learning frameworks, unexplained easy-hard-easy pattern, missing SHD comparison with ILP, redundant section on BN graphs.

Given the strengths of the paper and the manageable nature of the concerns (which could be addressed in a revision), I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should address the generalization question, explain the easy-hard-easy pattern, and include SHD comparisons with ILP.
