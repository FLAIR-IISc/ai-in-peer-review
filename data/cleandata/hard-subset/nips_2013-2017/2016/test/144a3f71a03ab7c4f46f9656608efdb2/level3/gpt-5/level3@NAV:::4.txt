This paper addresses optimal Bayesian network structure learning under ancestral (path) constraints. Building on EC-tree search with an oracle for decomposable scores, the key contribution is a projection technique (Section 5) that infers decomposable edge and topological-ordering constraints from non-decomposable ancestral constraints. The projected constraints are then fed to the oracle to tighten heuristics during A* search. The authors provide correctness statements for pruning in EC trees (Theorem 1) and for projected constraints (Theorems 2–4), and empirically compare against an ILP baseline (GOBNILP). The work is positioned against prior non-decomposable search (BN/EC graphs; Chen et al.), ILP/CP formulations (Jaakkola et al., Cussens; van Beek & Hoffmann), constraint-based path priors (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos), and order-graph/A* methods (Yuan & Malone).

Quality: The technical core appears sound, with clear statements of entailment conditions and a MaxSAT reduction for ordering inference. The experimental evaluation is systematic over benchmarks, data sizes, and constraint densities. However, Table 3 shows large gains for EC trees but not for ILP, leaving open whether projection helps approaches that natively cannot handle non-decomposable constraints. Please provide evidence that the inferred decomposable constraints improve performance and/or efficiency for other learners (e.g., dynamic programming or order-graph A*, CP), and analyze why ILP benefits little.

Clarity: The paper is well written and the theory/experiments are explained clearly. To improve focus, I recommend dropping Section 4.1 (BN graph) and instead expanding the EC-tree/A* setup and the mechanics of the oracle interface. Adding pseudo-code or more detail for the edge-inference procedure and the MaxSAT encoding would enhance reproducibility.

Originality: The projection of ancestral constraints into edge/order constraints, especially the completeness of the negative-edge test and the MaxSAT construction for orderings, is novel and concentrated in Section 5.

Significance: If the projection generalizes across learners, the impact is high: ancestral knowledge is common and often non-decomposable. Current evidence demonstrates substantial wins for EC-tree search; broader validation would strengthen claims.

Specific concerns and requests:
- Explain the easy–hard–easy pattern in Table 2 and whether EC trees are more sensitive to the amount of domain knowledge than ILP. This suggests p must be tuned; please discuss practical guidance.
- Report SHD for ILP alongside EC trees in Table 2 (and elsewhere) to compare accuracy under time/memory limits.
- Include ablations showing oracle node expansions, heuristic tightness, and runtime with/without projected edge vs ordering constraints across learners.

Pros:
- Clear problem formulation; novel, well-argued projection.
- Strong speedups for EC trees; soundness guarantees.
- Good positioning within related work.

Cons:
- Limited evidence of generalization of projection benefits beyond EC trees.
- Missing SHD comparison for ILP; limited analysis of constraint-density effects.
- Space spent on BN graph could be reallocated to core methods.
