This paper addresses optimal Bayesian network (BN) structure learning under non-decomposable background knowledge in the form of ancestral (path) constraints. Building on the BN-graph/EC-tree framework of Chen et al. (2015, 2016), the authors search over CPDAGs using A* with an admissible heuristic supplied by a decomposable-score oracle (URLearning). The key technical idea is twofold: (i) prune the EC tree using tests that detect whether a CPDAG necessarily violates (or cannot possibly satisfy) a given set of ancestral constraints; and (ii) “project” non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and topological-order constraints to empower the oracle. The paper proposes an algorithm that infers necessary edge-presence/absence constraints entailed by a maximum closure of ancestral constraints (Theorems 2–3), and selects a maximally large, cycle-free set of ordering constraints via a MaxSAT formulation (Theorem 4). Empirically, the approach yields large speedups over an ILP baseline (GOBNILP) on benchmarks, with especially strong gains when projected constraints are supplied to the oracle.

Relation to prior work: The paper is well-positioned relative to order-graph/dynamic programming (Koivisto & Sood 2004; Silander & Myllymäki 2006), heuristic search (Yuan et al. 2011; Yuan & Malone 2013), BN-graph/EC-tree (Chen et al. 2015, 2016), and ILP/CP formulations (Jaakkola et al. 2010; Cussens 2008; Bartlett & Cussens 2015; van Beek & Hoffmann 2015). It contrasts with previous use of path constraints in constraint-based learning and soft priors (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos 2012, 2013) by aiming for exact model selection.

Quality:
- Correctness/pruning. The pruning criterion for CPDAGs under ancestral constraints is central. However, the key lemma/theorem enabling pruning (called Lemma 3 in the draft; Theorem 1 in the provided text) is insufficiently justified and, as stated, seems too strong. Pruning requires a monotonicity property: once a CPDAG necessarily violates A (or cannot possibly satisfy A), all its descendants obtained by adding leaves also violate A. Please restate the exact condition you use (violation monotonicity) and provide a complete proof. The current statement “P satisfies A iff its descendants satisfy A” is not correct as written.
- EC-tree edge generation. The sentence “By the EC tree edge generation rules, Gk also contains edge Z -> W” needs a detailed justification. The EC-tree adds a leaf X with parents U into some representative Gi ∈ class(Pi) subject to a canonical ordering; it is not obvious why this forces a specific orientation Z -> W in Gk. Please expand the rule set (including Meek’s rules if used) and give a concrete argument for the claimed orientation.
- Projected constraints: entailment vs. selection. The exposition sometimes says “we can infer ordering constraints” in a way that suggests logical entailment from A, yet the MaxSAT step actually selects a maximally large set of order constraints that are consistent with A and acyclicity. These constraints need not be entailed in the logical sense (i.e., true in all DAGs satisfying A); rather, they form a safe strengthening that prunes the oracle’s search without eliminating any DAG in G(A). Please separate “entailed” edge constraints (Theorems 2–3) from “selected” ordering constraints, and clarify soundness conditions (Theorem 4) under which the selected order constraints are guaranteed not to remove any DAG in G(A).
- MaxSAT formulation. The rationale and completeness of the MaxSAT encoding need more detail. In particular: (i) explain why clauses (1)–(3) suffice to ensure that the selected orders cannot exclude any DAG satisfying A; (ii) state whether the objective maximizes the number of selected X<Y subject to those hard constraints; (iii) prove Theorem 4 (or give a reference) and justify clause (3), which is nonstandard and hard to parse; and (iv) discuss complexity and solver overhead relative to overall runtime (an ablation would help).
- CPDAG feasibility tests. The paper proposes two efficient tests (presence of a directed path violating X 6 Y; absence of any partially directed path needed for X Y) and then “exhaustively enumerates class(P)” if inconclusive. Full enumeration can be exponential. Please describe a polynomial-time sufficient test or a bounded search procedure for feasibility of a directed path within a CPDAG, and quantify how often full enumeration occurs and its cost in practice.
- Empirical baselines. The comparison to GOBNILP appears to use a path-constraint encoding derived from Cussens (2008), which is known to be relatively weak. A tighter ILP is possible by adding ancestry variables A(X,Y), standard transitivity constraints for A, and linking constraints: if I(Y,U)=1 and X∈U then A(X,Y)=1; prohibit cycles with A(X,Y) and A(Y,X) not both true; encode given ancestral constraints directly over A. Such formulations (or CP variants, van Beek & Hoffmann 2015) are typically stronger and often outperform GOBNILP. Even if your method remains faster, the current baseline weakens the empirical claim. Please include: (i) a tighter ILP/CP baseline; (ii) results with bounded parent-set size to rule out the known weakness of ILP with large parent sets; and (iii) an ablation isolating the effect of ancestral constraints vs. unbounded families on GOBNILP.
- Ablations. To understand where the gains come from, please report: (a) EC-tree pruning only; (b) oracle empowerment only (edge constraints); (c) oracle empowerment with ordering constraints; and (d) full method. Also report the time spent in MaxSAT.

Clarity:
- Tighten definitions and notation for α(·), β(·), “maximum” closure of A, and the precise meaning of “entails” vs. “consistent with.” Provide worked examples illustrating Theorems 2–3 and the candidate sets Z considered.
- Explain the admissible heuristic used by URLearning and how projected constraints are passed to it (edge constraints vs. precedence constraints).
- Fix confusing or unsupported statements (e.g., the EC-tree descendant property; the orientation claim above). Improve figure captions and cross-references.

Originality:
- Using EC trees to handle non-decomposable ancestral constraints with exact pruning is novel and interesting. The edge-projection rules (Theorems 2–3) are a nice contribution. The MaxSAT-based selection of order constraints is a pragmatic idea; the novelty hinges on the soundness guarantees (Theorem 4).

Significance:
- Many applications require incorporating causal/background knowledge that is naturally ancestral. A scalable, exact method is valuable. If the theoretical gaps are addressed and stronger baselines are added, the work could influence subsequent BN learning under rich constraints.

Pros:
- Addresses an important, underexplored constraint type with an exact method.
- Clever integration of EC-tree search with projected decomposable constraints to empower a strong oracle.
- Large empirical speedups on standard benchmarks; useful insights on easy–hard–easy behavior as p increases.

Cons:
- Key pruning lemma/theorem is unclear; some claims seem misstated.
- MaxSAT selection of orderings is underspecified; entailment vs. consistency is conflated.
- Baselines are weaker than necessary; unclear how much gain is due to better encoding vs. the method itself.
- Occasional reliance on exponential enumeration in CPDAG feasibility checks without quantification.

Suggestions for revision:
- Correct and fully prove the pruning property; expand EC-tree generation details.
- Refactor the “projection” section to clearly separate entailed edge constraints from selected ordering constraints; strengthen the MaxSAT exposition and proofs.
- Add stronger ILP/CP baselines and bounded-family experiments; include ablations and runtime breakdowns.
- Quantify and reduce CPDAG enumeration; discuss scalability beyond n≈20 and memory use.

Recommendation: Borderline accept if the theoretical clarifications and stronger baselines can be provided; otherwise borderline reject. The ideas are promising and potentially impactful, but clarity and evaluation need tightening.
