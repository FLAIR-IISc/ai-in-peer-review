This paper presents a novel approach to learning Bayesian networks optimally, subject to background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints. The authors leverage a recently proposed framework for optimal structure learning based on non-decomposable scores, which can accommodate ancestral constraints. The proposed framework exploits oracles for learning structures using decomposable scores, which cannot accommodate ancestral constraints since they are non-decomposable. The authors show how to empower these oracles by passing them decomposable constraints that they can handle, which are inferred from ancestral constraints that they cannot handle.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the problem, the proposed approach, and the experimental results. The technical content is sound, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the strengths and weaknesses of their approach. The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide enough information for the expert reader to reproduce the results.

One of the key strengths of the paper is the authors' ability to leverage existing work on optimal structure learning and adapt it to accommodate ancestral constraints. The authors provide a clear and concise explanation of how they modify the existing framework to handle non-decomposable scores and constraints. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach, showing that it can be orders-of-magnitude more efficient than alternative frameworks, such as those based on integer linear programming.

However, there are some concerns regarding the experimental evaluation. Specifically, the role of Table 3 is unclear, and it appears to compare different aspects of the proposed approach. Additionally, the reviewer requests an explicit comparison of the proposed approach (EC + new work) versus the EC alone. Even if the GOBNILP benefits more from the new work, EC is much better than GOBNILP, so demonstrating (EC + new work) vs EC is most relevant.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel combination of familiar techniques. The authors adapt existing work on optimal structure learning to accommodate ancestral constraints, which is a non-trivial contribution. The paper also provides a thorough analysis of the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed approach, which is a valuable contribution to the field.

Regarding significance, the paper addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research. The authors demonstrate that their approach can be orders-of-magnitude more efficient than alternative frameworks, which is a significant improvement. The paper also provides a unique theoretical contribution, showing how to empower oracles by passing them decomposable constraints that they can handle.

Overall, the paper is well-written, technically sound, and provides a significant contribution to the field. However, the experimental evaluation could be improved by providing a clearer comparison of the proposed approach versus existing work.
