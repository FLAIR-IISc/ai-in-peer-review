This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data when subject to ancestral constraints. This is a well-known challenge, as most high-performance, exact score-based methods (e.g., those based on dynamic programming) require decomposable scores and cannot natively handle non-decomposable constraints like ancestral relationships. The authors build directly upon the recent framework of Chen et al. [2015, 2016], which introduced an A* search over a graph of Markov equivalence classes (the EC tree) to accommodate non-decomposable scores. The primary contribution of this work is to extend this framework to efficiently incorporate ancestral constraints. The core technical idea is a novel method for "projecting" the non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and topological ordering constraints, which can then be passed to the search algorithm's oracle to significantly improve the heuristic and prune the search space. The formulation for inferring a maximal set of consistent ordering constraints via MaxSAT is particularly clever.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance:** The paper tackles an important and practical problem. Incorporating expert background knowledge is often critical for learning accurate models, and the proposed method provides an exact and highly efficient way to do so for a common and powerful constraint type.
*   **Quality:** The claims are well-supported by a strong empirical evaluation. The reported orders-of-magnitude speedup over a state-of-the-art Integer Linear Programming (ILP) system is impressive and provides compelling evidence of a significant advance over alternative exact methods. The experiments are thorough, analyzing performance across varying network sizes, data amounts, and constraint densities.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It clearly motivates the problem, explains the technical details of the approach, and situates the contribution within the existing literature.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The work is an extension of the Chen et al. framework, which could be perceived as an incremental contribution. However, it is a non-trivial and valuable extension that substantially increases the practical applicability of the underlying method.
*   While I have limited expertise with the specific A* on EC-tree search method, the empirical results and comparisons to the well-established ILP baseline are convincing.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper with a clear and significant contribution. The novel projection method is elegant, and the empirical results convincingly demonstrate a new state of the art for this problem.
