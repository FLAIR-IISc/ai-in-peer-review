This paper addresses the important problem of learning Bayesian network structures when incorporating prior knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints. The authors build upon their previous work [Chen et al., 2015, 2016], which introduced a framework for optimal structure learning with non-decomposable scores, and extend it to handle these constraints. The core contribution lies in efficiently incorporating ancestral constraints by projecting them into decomposable constraints that can be utilized by existing oracles for structure learning. The technical approach centers around a MaxSAT formulation to infer topological ordering constraints from the ancestral constraints. Empirically, the authors demonstrate significant – orders of magnitude – runtime improvements compared to integer linear programming (ILP) based approaches.

This work is highly relevant to the NIPS community, building on a strong line of research in probabilistic graphical models and structure learning. The problem of integrating background knowledge into structure learning has been a recurring theme in NIPS and related conferences (e.g., UAI, ICML). Prior approaches often relied on decomposable constraints, limiting their expressiveness. The authors correctly identify this limitation and offer a compelling solution. The connection to prior work on ILP-based structure learning [Jaakkola et al., 2010, Bartlett and Cussens, 2015, van Beek and Hoffmann, 2015] is well-articulated, and the empirical comparison provides valuable insights into the trade-offs between different approaches. The recent work by Borboudakis and Tsamardinos [2012, 2013] on incorporating causal knowledge is also appropriately mentioned.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The approach appears technically sound, leveraging a well-defined framework (EC trees and oracles) and a solid theoretical foundation for constraint projection. The theorems presented add rigor to the work.
*   **Significant Empirical Results:** The reported runtime improvements – orders of magnitude faster than ILP – are compelling. This suggests a substantial practical impact for the proposed method. The comprehensive experimental setup, varying network size, dataset size, and constraint percentage, strengthens the validity of the results.
*   **Novelty:** The incorporation of non-decomposable constraints, specifically ancestral constraints, into an optimal structure learning framework represents a novel contribution. The projection technique to leverage existing decomposable oracles is particularly innovative.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly presented, and the technical details are explained adequately. The use of examples helps to illustrate key concepts.
*   **Completeness:** The paper appears to be a complete piece of work, with a clear problem statement, a detailed technical approach, and thorough empirical evaluation.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Expertise Caveat:** As the authors acknowledge (and as a reviewer), my expertise in this specific area is limited. While I can assess the technical soundness and clarity, a deeper assessment of the subtleties of the constraint projection and MaxSAT formulation requires specialized knowledge.
*   **Scalability Limits:** While the results show scalability up to n=20, it would be beneficial to see experiments with even larger networks, closer to real-world scenarios.
*   **MaxSAT Solver Dependence:** The performance relies on an external MaxSAT solver (EVASOLVER). Further investigation of the sensitivity of the approach to the choice of MaxSAT solver could be valuable.
* **Structural Hamming Distance:** While reporting the structural Hamming distance is helpful, its interpretation is somewhat nuanced (as noted in the paper). A more detailed discussion of the types of errors made by the learned networks would be beneficial.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a challenging and important problem in Bayesian network structure learning.
*   The proposed approach is technically sound and empirically validated with impressive results.
*   The novelty of the constraint projection technique is significant.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presents its contributions.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The reliance on an external MaxSAT solver introduces a potential dependency and requires further investigation.
*   The scalability to very large networks remains an open question.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. The problem is well-motivated, the technical approach is sound, and the empirical results are compelling. Despite the minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh them significantly. I recommend acceptance, with the caveat that further investigation into the scalability and MaxSAT solver dependence would be beneficial. The impressive runtime improvements alone justify acceptance, as they represent a significant step forward in the practical applicability of optimal structure learning with background knowledge.
