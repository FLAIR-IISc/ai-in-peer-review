- Problem & contribution: Addresses learning Bayesian network structures with non-decomposable ancestral constraints by transforming them into decomposable constraints usable by existing oracles.  
- Method: Builds on Chen et al. (2015) and leverages the EC tree search space plus an oracle for decomposable scores.  
- Key insight: Transforming ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints enables efficient pruning and use of existing optimal structure-learning machinery.  
- Novelty: Original extension of prior decomposable-constraint work to handle ancestral (non-decomposable) constraints via EC tree representation.  
- Theoretical claim: EC tree gives a more compact search space and pruning based on ancestral constraints is claimed to be sound.  
- Empirical result: Demonstrates orders-of-magnitude speedups over ILP-based GOBNILP with projected constraints across varied experiments.  
- Evaluation thoroughness: Experiments vary network size, dataset size, and constraint density and consistently show significant speedups.  
- Major theoretical weakness: Lemma 3, central to pruning, lacks a clear proof and the edge-generation claim "Gk also contains edge Z -> W" needs detailed justification.  
- Inferred-constraints issue: The paper’s use of "can infer" for constraints is misleading—does not clarify that inferred constraints are consistent selections, not entailed consequences.  
- Baseline comparison weakness: Comparison to GOBNILP uses a weak ILP encoding for ancestral constraints; a tighter ILP with A(X,Y) and I(Z,U) variables plus transitivity constraints would be fairer.  
- Missing related work: Fails to discuss van Beek & Hoffmann (2015), a constraint-programming approach that often outperforms GOBNILP and could adapt to ancestral constraints.  
- Clarity gaps: MaxSAT-based inference explanation and encoding lack sufficient detail and rationale.  
- Overall assessment & recommendations: Significant and original contribution with strong empirical gains, but needs stronger theoretical justification, clearer inference description, and more comprehensive comparisons to state-of-the-art methods.
