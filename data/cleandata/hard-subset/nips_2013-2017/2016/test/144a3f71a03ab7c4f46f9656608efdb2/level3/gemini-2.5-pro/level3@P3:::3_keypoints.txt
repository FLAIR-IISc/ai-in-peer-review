- Presents an exact algorithm integrating ancestral constraints into EC-tree search by translating global ancestral constraints into local edge/ordering constraints, using MaxSAT for complex cases.  
- Empirical results show orders-of-magnitude speedup over a Gobnilp ILP baseline on chosen benchmarks.  
- Strength: Sound framework leveraging EC-tree with an oracle for decomposable scores.  
- Strength: Principled translation from non-decomposable ancestral constraints to decomposable constraints usable by local search.  
- Strength: Clever use of MaxSAT to infer consistent ordering constraints.  
- Weakness: Practical significance limited because the setting (exact DAG learning, hard ancestral constraints, no hidden confounders) is somewhat niche.  
- Weakness: Fairness of comparison — Gobnilp (general-purpose ILP) may be an unfair baseline; comparison to a strong greedy method (e.g., GES adapted to constraints) is suggested.  
- Weakness: MaxSAT formulation unclear—Rule 3 has symmetric premises but an asymmetric conclusion (needs derivation/clarification).  
- Weakness: MaxSAT Rule 4 ((X ↛ Y) ⇒ (Y < X)) may incorrectly force an ordering when both non-ancestry constraints (X ↛ Y and Y ↛ X) hold, possibly eliminating valid DAGs.  
- Theorem 1 is imprecise: satisfaction by a parent CPDAG vs. its descendant needs qualification (projection onto relevant variable sets).  
- Theorem 3 (inferring positive edge constraints) seems unlikely to trigger except with very dense ancestral constraints; report how often it fired in experiments.  
- Clarity: The MaxSAT encoding claimed to ensure "acyclicity" is not clearly justified—provide explicit reasoning.  
- Writing suggestions: tighten wording (e.g., replace "omniscient oracle", rephrase "empower the oracle", specify "minimizing" the score).  
- Significance: Novel and original combination of techniques with moderate impact; could be influential for projecting global constraints into decomposable forms.  
- Limiting factors: Real-world domains often have latent confounders (favoring MAG/PAG methods), and sparse prior constraints (more common) may limit practical benefit of the proposed complex machinery.
