This paper tackles the challenging problem of learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints, a crucial task for incorporating prior knowledge into structure learning. The authors build upon their prior work utilizing a framework based on navigating the search space with an oracle for decomposable scores, adapting it to handle the non-decomposable nature of ancestral constraints. The core idea is to infer decomposable constraints from the ancestral ones, thereby empowering the oracle and improving efficiency.

The paper is well-motivated; ancestral constraints are common in practice and existing methods struggle to incorporate them efficiently. The approach of leveraging an oracle for decomposable scores, while conceptually complex, is a promising direction. The experimental results demonstrate significant speedups compared to GOBNILP, suggesting a substantial practical benefit. 

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses that hinder its clarity and impact. The most critical is the lack of rigor in justifying Lemma 3, which is foundational to their pruning strategy. The proof sketch is insufficient, leaving the reader unconvinced of its correctness. A more detailed and formal proof is essential. 

Furthermore, the explanation surrounding the generation of edges "Z -> W" in the EC tree (following the statement about EC tree edge generation rules) is vague and requires greater elaboration.  The connection between the constraints and the resulting structure isn't immediately clear.

The treatment of implied/projected constraints is also problematic. The authors state they "can infer" ordering constraints, but this is misleading. They *add* constraints consistent with the ancestral constraints, not necessarily constraints that are logically entailed. This distinction is vital and needs to be explicitly clarified. The use of MAXSAT to select which ordering constraints to add is a clever idea, but the inference procedure and rationale behind the MAXSAT encoding are insufficiently detailed.  Why this specific encoding? What trade-offs are being made?

The experimental evaluation, while promising, has limitations. While the speedups compared to GOBNILP are substantial, it's unclear whether this is due to the ancestral constraints specifically, or a more general weakness of GOBNILP with unbounded parent set sizes. The paper doesn't adequately address this possibility.  

The comparison to Cussens (2008) is also weak. The authors rightly point out that this is a poor ILP formulation, but then don't convincingly demonstrate that their method would still outperform a tighter ILP. A comparison against a more sophisticated ILP encoding (as suggested, using A(X,Y) and I(Z,U) variables with transitivity and A–I linking constraints) would significantly strengthen their empirical claim. Even if their method still wins, acknowledging and addressing the limitations of the comparison is crucial for scientific credibility.

Finally, the work of van Beek and Hoffmann (CP 2015) is a significant omission. This work often outperforms GOBNILP and is directly relevant to structure learning with constraints. It’s highly likely that their approach could be adapted to handle ancestral constraints, and a comparison would provide valuable insight into the relative merits of the proposed method.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to incorporating ancestral constraints into Bayesian network structure learning.
*   Significant empirical speedups compared to GOBNILP.
*   Addresses a practically relevant problem with limited existing solutions.
*   Clever use of an oracle and constraint projection.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Insufficient justification for Lemma 3 (critical flaw).
*   Lack of clarity in several explanations (EC tree edge generation, constraint inference).
*   Potentially misleading use of "infer" regarding ordering constraints.
*   Weakness of the comparative evaluation (GOBNILP, Cussens).
*   Omission of relevant related work (van Beek and Hoffmann).

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a promising approach to a challenging problem. However, the weaknesses, particularly the lack of rigor in the theoretical justification and the limited comparative evaluation, significantly detract from its impact.  I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to address these issues and resubmit. Specifically, the authors *must* provide a complete and rigorous proof of Lemma 3, clarify the explanations surrounding constraint inference and EC tree edge generation, and perform a more comprehensive empirical evaluation, including a comparison to van Beek and Hoffmann (2015) and a stronger ILP baseline. Addressing these points would substantially improve the paper's quality and contribution.
