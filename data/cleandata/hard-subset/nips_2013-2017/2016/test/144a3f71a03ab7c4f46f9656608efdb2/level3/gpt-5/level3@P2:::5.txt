Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses optimal Bayesian network (BN) structure learning when background knowledge is given as ancestral (path) constraints—statements of the form X is/is not an ancestor of Y. While most exact score-based methods rely on decomposable scores and hence support only decomposable constraints (edge presence/absence, parent set size, precedence constraints), ancestral constraints are inherently non-decomposable. Building on Chen et al. (2015, 2016), the authors use the BN graph/EC tree shortest-path view to support non-decomposable constraints directly by pruning nodes (DAGs/CPDAGs) that violate ancestral constraints. To regain efficiency, they “empower” a decomposable-score oracle (URLEARNING) by projecting the non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints that the oracle can exploit. Edge projections are derived via closure over ancestral implications (Theorems 2–3); ordering projections are selected via a MaxSAT formulation designed to maximize safe ordering constraints entailed by ancestral constraints (Theorem 4). Empirically, the EC-tree approach with projected constraints is compared against GOBNILP (ILP), showing orders-of-magnitude speedups under a variety of network sizes, data sizes, and constraint densities, and an easy–hard–easy trend as more ancestral constraints are provided.

This paper connects to a line of exact BN learning with decomposable scores via dynamic programming and A* over order graphs (Koivisto & Sood, 2004; Silander & Myllymäki, 2006; Yuan et al., 2011; Yuan & Malone, 2013), and to ILP/CP approaches (Jaakkola et al., 2010; Cussens, 2008; Bartlett & Cussens, 2015; van Beek & Hoffmann, 2015). Prior work on causal/path knowledge in constraint-based and heuristic score-based learning (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos, 2012, 2013) treated soft priors or guided local search; here the focus is exact model selection with hard ancestral constraints. The use of EC trees with pruning and the projection of path constraints into edge and ordering constraints appear novel.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The EC-tree pruning rule is intuitive and formalized (Theorem 1). Edge projection rules (Theorems 2–3) are plausible; the negative-edge criterion via α/β sets is particularly clear. The MaxSAT formulation for ordering projections is carefully constrained to remain sound (Theorem 4 provides sufficient conditions). However, several details are “omitted for space,” notably (i) the closure algorithm to obtain a maximal set of ancestral constraints, (ii) proof sketches/complexity for Theorems 2–4, and (iii) how often the EC-tree pruning requires enumerating all DAGs in a CPDAG class (potentially exponential).
- Empirical evaluation: The setup is systematic (five benchmarks, varying n, N, and constraint density p), with precomputed local scores and a standard BDeu setting. Comparisons to GOBNILP are appropriate; both methods receive the projected constraints in one table and not in another. Results suggest large speedups and demonstrate the importance of projection for the oracle. That said, experiments cap at n=20 and focus only on ILP; CP-based baselines are mentioned but not evaluated.
- Fairness/reproducibility: Hardware, solver names (URLEARNING, EVASOLVER, GOBNILP), and key settings are given, but ILP parameters (version, cuts, presolving, time limits per instance), the MaxSAT instance sizes, and statistics on EC-tree node expansions and CPDAG enumerations are missing. Code/data availability is not stated.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The EC tree and the notion of satisfying ancestral constraints at the CPDAG level are explained with actionable tests. A few aspects could be clarified:
- Define precisely “maximum set of ancestral constraints” and provide the inference rules and complexity of the closure procedure.
- Elaborate on the “sufficient condition for acyclicity” clause in the MaxSAT encoding and intuition for Theorem 4.
- Quantify how often the CPDAG-level ancestral tests are inconclusive, forcing full enumeration.

There are minor editorial issues (placeholder text in citations, stray non-English characters, and formatting artifacts) that should be corrected.

Originality
The combination of EC-tree search with pruning for non-decomposable constraints plus the projection pipeline (complete edge entailments and MaxSAT-based ordering entailments) is, to the best of my knowledge, new. Prior exact methods either cannot encode ancestral constraints (order-graph) or can but at higher cost (ILP/CP). The formalization of safe ordering projections via MaxSAT is a nice contribution.

Significance
Enabling exact structure learning with hard path knowledge is practically relevant (e.g., causal directionality constraints) and methodologically interesting. The reported speedups over ILP and the gains from projection suggest this could change practice for constrained exact learning on small-to-moderate n. The scope is limited by scalability (n≈20), but this is in line with exact BN learning literature for unbounded indegree and BDeu. The work should stimulate follow-up on richer non-decomposable priors/constraints and more scalable heuristics.

Strengths
- Clear formulation of learning with ancestral constraints in EC trees; sound pruning.
- Novel and useful projection of ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints; MaxSAT encoding is principled.
- Strong empirical gains over ILP; good analysis showing the value of projections and the easy–hard–easy trend.
- Good alignment with existing exact BN learning literature and tools.

Weaknesses and questions
- Missing algorithmic details: closure to “maximum” ancestral set; proof/complexity of Theorems 2–4.
- Potential bottleneck: exhaustive enumeration of class(P) when CPDAG tests are inconclusive; please report frequency and cost, or alternative sufficient tests.
- Baselines: no evaluation of CP-based approach (van Beek & Hoffmann, 2015). Consider including it or discussing why not.
- Fairness of ILP comparison: GOBNILP often benefits from indegree bounds; authors use unbounded families. Please add results with small indegree bounds to test relative behavior under commonly used ILP settings.
- Robustness: How does the method handle noisy or contradictory ancestral constraints? Can the MaxSAT step be extended to select a consistent subset of constraints (soft path knowledge)?
- Scaling: Provide clause/variable counts for the MaxSAT encoding and runtime for projection; report EC-tree node expansions and A* heuristic quality with/without projections.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add an appendix with the closure rules and proofs for Theorems 2–4; include complexity analyses.
- Provide ablations: edge-only projections vs edge+ordering projections; BN graph vs EC tree.
- Include stats on CPDAG enumeration, EC-tree breadth/height, and heuristic accuracy.
- Clean up references and formatting; ensure all citations have full bibliographic info.
- Consider releasing code and scripts for reproducibility.

Broader impact/scope
The topic is squarely within NIPS: probabilistic graphical models, exact combinatorial optimization, and learning with prior knowledge. The approach could be extended to other non-decomposable constraints and priors.

Recommendation
- Overall: Accept (strong) for a methodologically solid and practically impactful contribution.
- Confidence: Moderate (I am not an expert on EC-tree internals, but the empirical results are compelling and the formalization appears sound).
