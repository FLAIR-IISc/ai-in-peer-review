This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data when subject to background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints (e.g., "X is an ancestor of Y" or "X is not an ancestor of Y"). This is a significant problem, as standard score-based learning algorithms typically require the scoring function to be decomposable, a property that is violated by ancestral constraints. The authors build upon the recent framework of Chen et al. [2015, 2016], which performs an A* search over a space of Markov equivalence classes (the "EC tree") and uses an oracle for learning with decomposable scores to compute the heuristic.

The main contribution is a method to effectively incorporate non-decomposable ancestral constraints into this search framework. This is achieved in two ways: first, by directly pruning nodes from the EC tree that are inconsistent with the given constraints; and second, and more cleverly, by "projecting" the non-decomposable ancestral constraints into a set of decomposable edge and ordering constraints. These inferred decomposable constraints can then be passed to the oracle, significantly improving the heuristic's accuracy and pruning power. For the particularly challenging task of inferring ordering constraints, the authors propose an elegant reduction to a Maximum Satisfiability (MaxSAT) problem. The empirical evaluation demonstrates that this approach offers orders-of-magnitude runtime improvements over a state-of-the-art Integer Linear Programming (ILP) based system, GOBNILP, on a variety of benchmark problems.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Important Problem:** The paper tackles a well-motivated and practical problem. Integrating expert knowledge is crucial for learning robust and meaningful graphical models, especially with limited data. Ancestral constraints are a natural and powerful form of such knowledge, but their non-decomposable nature has made them difficult to handle for many optimal learning algorithms.

2.  **Elegant Technical Approach:** The core idea of projecting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones that can be consumed by an existing, powerful oracle is insightful. It provides a principled bridge between the expressive power of non-decomposable background knowledge and the efficiency of algorithms designed for decomposable problems. The use of a MaxSAT solver to find the largest consistent set of ordering constraints is a sophisticated and appropriate solution to a non-trivial inference problem.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments are thorough and convincing. The comparison against a strong baseline (ILP) clearly shows the practical benefit of the proposed approach, with consistent and dramatic speedups. The ablation study (comparing results with and without projected constraints in Tables 1 and 2) effectively isolates and validates the contribution of the constraint projection technique, showing it is essential for performance. The scalability results up to 20 variables are competitive with the state-of-the-art in exact score-based learning.

4.  **Clear Illustration of Value:** The paper also does a good job of showing that a small number of ancestral constraints can have a similar impact on model accuracy (measured by Structural Hamming Distance) as a large increase in dataset size. This provides a compelling argument for the practical significance of incorporating such prior knowledge.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity on the Oracle:** The paper relies heavily on an external "oracle" (URLEARNING) to provide the A* heuristic. While citing the relevant work is appropriate, the paper would be more self-contained and clearer if it included a brief discussion on the properties of this oracle and how the inferred decomposable constraints are formally incorporated into it. For a reader not intimately familiar with this line of work, the oracle's role and its interaction with the projected constraints feel somewhat like a black box.

2.  **Scalability of Constraint Projection:** The MaxSAT formulation for inferring ordering constraints is a key component. However, the runtime of this preprocessing step is not discussed. While likely fast for the problem sizes considered, MaxSAT can itself be computationally expensive. A brief comment on the time taken by the EVASOLVER and whether this inference step could become a bottleneck for larger numbers of variables or constraints would strengthen the empirical analysis.

3.  **Limited Scope of Discussion:** The paper focuses exclusively on ancestral constraints. It would be beneficial to include a short discussion on whether the general "projection" framework could be extended to other classes of global or non-decomposable constraints, which would help position the contribution in a broader context.

**Assessment:**

*   **Quality:** The paper appears technically sound. The claims are well-supported by a strong and well-designed set of experiments. The work is a complete contribution, presenting the theory, the algorithm, and a compelling evaluation.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The problem is well-motivated, and the proposed method is described logically. Minor improvements could be made by providing more intuition for the theorems (especially Theorem 3 and 4) and clarifying the role of the oracle, as noted above.

*   **Originality:** The work is a novel and non-trivial extension of the Chen et al. [2015, 2016] framework. The key original contribution is the method for projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable ones to empower a search heuristic. While ILP methods can theoretically handle such constraints, this search-based approach with a specialized heuristic is new and, as demonstrated, highly effective.

*   **Significance:** The results are highly significant for the field of Bayesian network structure learning. It provides a practical and demonstrably much faster method for exact learning with a very natural and useful type of expert knowledge. The dramatic performance gains over ILP are likely to make this the preferred method for this problem and could inspire similar "projection" techniques for other constrained optimization problems in machine learning.

**Reviewer Caveat:** My expertise in the specifics of A*-based search over EC-trees is limited. However, the logic of the approach is clear, and the empirical comparisons against the well-established ILP method are impressive and speak for themselves.

In summary, this is a strong paper that presents a novel, elegant, and highly efficient solution to an important problem. The contributions are clear and the results are significant. I recommend acceptance.
