- Proposes translating non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable ordering constraints for EC-tree search.  
- Empirically outperforms ILP-based GOBNILP in computational efficiency.  
- Paper is well-written and conceptually clear.  
- Problem is relevant: incorporating background knowledge into BN structure learning.  
- Novelty is moderate: extends oracle-based BN learning to handle ancestral constraints via projection to ordering constraints.  
- Practical impact is limited because experiments assume known ancestral constraints (an artificial setting).  
- Does not address unobserved confounders or broader causal discovery scenarios.  
- Uses MaxSAT cleverly to infer maximal sets of ordering constraints.  
- Concerns about correctness of the acyclicity rule (lines 257–261), including asymmetry of rule 3 and possible exclusion by rule 4.  
- Sparse ancestral constraints may not substantially reduce the search space in practice.  
- Comparison to GOBNILP may be unfair since it was not designed for ancestral constraints.  
- Recommends adding a baseline like a greedy algorithm (e.g., GES) adapted for ancestral constraints for a fairer comparison.  
- Theorem 1 is misleading: a CPDAG satisfying A implies at least one DAG in its class satisfies A, not all (so 'iff' is questionable).  
- Theorem 3 may produce limited positive edge implications when constraints are sparse.  
- Terminology and clarity issues: ambiguous use of “acyclic” (line 267), inconsistent use of “oracle” vs “omniscient oracle,” vague phrases like “empower the oracle,” and formatting/wording issues (e.g., “optimizing=minimizing”).  
- Reported results using 10–25% ancestral constraints reflect substantial prior knowledge that may not be realistic.  
- Recommendation: accept conditionally after addressing acyclicity rule correctness, clarifying theorems and experimental setup, and adding a more relevant baseline.
