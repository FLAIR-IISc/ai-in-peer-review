The paper "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints" proposes a novel approach for learning the structure of Bayesian networks optimally, subject to non-decomposable ancestral constraints. The authors build upon the framework of Chen et al. [2015, 2016] for optimal structure learning with non-decomposable scores, and extend it to accommodate ancestral constraints. The key insight is to empower an oracle that optimizes decomposable scores by passing it decomposable constraints inferred from non-decomposable ancestral constraints.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it easy to follow the authors' line of reasoning. The technical approach is sound, and the empirical evaluation is thorough and convincing. The authors demonstrate significant runtime improvements over ILP-based alternatives, which is a major contribution to the field.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to clearly explain the problem of learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints, and how it differs from traditional structure learning approaches. The authors provide a comprehensive review of the relevant literature, highlighting the limitations of existing methods and the need for a new approach.

The technical approach is based on rephrasing the problem as a MaxSAT formulation with specific constraints. The authors provide a detailed explanation of how to infer decomposable constraints from non-decomposable ancestral constraints, and how to use these constraints to empower the oracle. The MaxSAT formulation is well-suited to the problem, and the authors provide a clear explanation of how it works.

The empirical evaluation is thorough and convincing, demonstrating significant runtime improvements over ILP-based alternatives. The authors evaluate their approach on a range of benchmark datasets, varying the number of variables, dataset size, and proportion of ancestral constraints. The results show that their approach is orders-of-magnitude faster than ILP-based alternatives, even when the number of variables is large.

One potential limitation of the paper is that the authors assume a certain level of expertise with the Chen et al. [2015, 2016] framework, which may not be familiar to all readers. However, the authors provide a clear explanation of the framework and how it is extended to accommodate ancestral constraints.

In terms of the review criteria, the paper scores highly on quality, clarity, originality, and significance. The technical approach is sound, and the empirical evaluation is thorough and convincing. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field, demonstrating a new approach for learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints.
