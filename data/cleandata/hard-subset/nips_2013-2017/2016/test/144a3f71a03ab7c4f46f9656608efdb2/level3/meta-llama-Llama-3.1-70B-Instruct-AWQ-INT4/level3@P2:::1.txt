This paper tackles the problem of learning Bayesian networks optimally, subject to background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints. The authors propose a framework that leverages a recently developed search space for structure learning with non-decomposable scores, called the EC tree, and employs an oracle that optimizes decomposable scores. The approach is based on navigating the search space of all possible DAGs and pruning away those that do not satisfy the given ancestral constraints.

The authors' approach is related to previous work on learning Bayesian networks with non-decomposable priors and scores. However, their method is distinct in that it specifically targets ancestral constraints, which are non-decomposable and pose a particular challenge for learning approaches based on decomposable scores. The authors' use of the EC tree and the oracle is also novel, as it allows them to efficiently prune the search space and find an optimal Bayesian network that satisfies the given constraints.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a good balance between technical details and high-level explanations. The authors provide a thorough review of the relevant literature and clearly explain the contributions of their work.

One of the strengths of the paper is its empirical evaluation, which demonstrates the effectiveness of the authors' approach in learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints. The experiments show that the authors' method is orders-of-magnitude more efficient than alternative frameworks, such as those based on integer linear programming. The authors also provide a detailed analysis of the results, highlighting the impact of ancestral constraints on the efficiency of the learning process.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. One of the main concerns is the lack of clarity in the proof of Lemma 3, which is a key result enabling pruning with ancestral constraints. The authors should provide a more detailed and justified proof of this lemma to strengthen their claims.

Additionally, the treatment of implied/projected constraints and inferred ordering constraints is poorly explained. The authors use the phrase "can infer" without clarifying that they add consistent (but not necessarily entailed) ordering constraints. This lack of clarity makes it difficult to understand the authors' approach and its limitations.

The use of a MAXSAT approach to control which ordering constraints are added is also not fully justified. The authors should provide a more detailed explanation of the inference procedure and the rationale behind their approach.

Furthermore, the experimental comparison to GOBNILP is not fully convincing. While the authors show that their method is orders-of-magnitude more efficient, it is unclear if GOBNILP's poor performance is due to ancestral constraints specifically or its general weakness with unbounded parent set size. A more thorough comparison to alternative methods, including a tighter ILP formulation, would strengthen the authors' claims.

Finally, the authors do not discuss the work of van Beek and Hoffmann (CP 2015), which is a relevant and potentially competing approach. This omission weakens the authors' empirical claim, and they should consider including a comparison to this work in their evaluation.

In conclusion, this paper presents a novel approach to learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints. While it has some limitations, the authors' method shows promise and demonstrates the potential for efficient learning with non-decomposable constraints. With some revisions to address the concerns mentioned above, this paper could make a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian network learning.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper tackles a challenging problem in Bayesian network learning and proposes a novel approach to address it.
* The authors provide a thorough empirical evaluation that demonstrates the effectiveness of their method.
* The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it easy to follow for readers familiar with the field.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The proof of Lemma 3 is unclear and needs better justification.
* The treatment of implied/projected constraints and inferred ordering constraints is poorly explained.
* The experimental comparison to GOBNILP is not fully convincing and should be strengthened with a more thorough comparison to alternative methods.
* The authors do not discuss the work of van Beek and Hoffmann (CP 2015), which is a relevant and potentially competing approach.
