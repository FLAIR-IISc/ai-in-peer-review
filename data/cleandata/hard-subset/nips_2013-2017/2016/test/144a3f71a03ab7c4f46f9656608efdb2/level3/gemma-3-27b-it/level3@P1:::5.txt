This paper addresses the problem of optimal Bayesian network structure learning under ancestral constraints. The authors tackle a significant challenge: standard structure learning approaches rely on decomposable scores and struggle to incorporate the non-decomposable nature of ancestral constraints (which define relationships like "X is an ancestor of Y").  Their core contribution lies in adapting the framework of Chen et al. [2015, 2016], which utilizes oracles for learning structures with decomposable scores, to handle these constraints. The authors demonstrate a method to ‘project’ the non-decomposable ancestral constraints into equivalent decomposable constraints, effectively empowering the oracle and enabling efficient structure search.  The work also includes a MaxSAT formulation for inferring ordering constraints from the ancestral constraints. Empirical evaluation convincingly shows orders-of-magnitude speedups compared to integer linear programming (ILP)-based methods.

The paper is well-structured and logically presented. It begins with a clear motivation, outlining the importance of incorporating background knowledge into Bayesian network learning. The background section adequately summarizes relevant concepts like decomposable scores (e.g., MDL, BDeu) and existing approaches like the K2 algorithm and ILP formulations.  The introduction of ancestral constraints and their incompatibility with decomposable scoring functions is clearly explained. The core technical contribution – projecting ancestral constraints into decomposable ones – is well-motivated and presented. The authors convincingly argue that empowering the oracle with these projected constraints leads to significant performance gains.  The formulation using EC trees (introduced by Chen et al.) appears to be a sound framework for this task.

The strengths of the paper are substantial. Firstly, the problem tackled is genuinely important.  Real-world applications often involve prior knowledge about causal relationships, and the ability to integrate this knowledge into learning algorithms is crucial. Secondly, the proposed approach appears novel and effective. The idea of projecting non-decomposable constraints to empower a decomposable oracle is clever and demonstrably improves performance.  The empirical results are compelling, showing significant speedups compared to existing ILP methods. The extensive experimental setup, varying network size, dataset size, and constraint percentage, adds credibility to the findings.  The thoroughness of the evaluation, including structural Hamming distance as a measure of accuracy, is commendable.  The connection to MaxSAT, while complex, offers a principled way to infer additional constraints.

However, some weaknesses exist. The paper assumes a reasonable level of familiarity with Bayesian networks and structure learning algorithms. While the background section is adequate, readers less familiar with the field might struggle with some of the technical details. The discussion of the MaxSAT formulation, while mathematically sound, feels somewhat rushed and lacks intuition.  A more detailed explanation of the trade-offs involved in this formulation would be beneficial.  Furthermore, the comparison is primarily against ILP. While ILP is a strong baseline, it would be valuable to compare the approach to other state-of-the-art constraint-based or hybrid structure learning algorithms. Although the authors acknowledge Borboudakis and Tsamardinos [2012, 2013], a more comprehensive comparison would strengthen the paper. A discussion of the limitations of the approach—for example, the scalability of the MaxSAT solver as the number of variables increases—would also be beneficial.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Significant performance improvement:** The orders-of-magnitude speedup compared to ILP is a major achievement.
*   **Novel and clever approach:** Projecting non-decomposable constraints to empower a decomposable oracle is a novel and effective technique.
*   **Important problem:** Integrating background knowledge into Bayesian network learning is a crucial task with practical relevance.
*   **Thorough empirical evaluation:**  The experiments are well-designed and demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Complexity:** The technical details can be challenging for readers less familiar with Bayesian network structure learning.
*   **Limited comparison:** The primary comparison is against ILP; comparing to other relevant algorithms would strengthen the results.
*   **MaxSAT formulation:** The discussion of the MaxSAT formulation could be more detailed and intuitive.



Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. The proposed approach is novel, effective, and well-supported by empirical evidence. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper far outweigh them.  I recommend acceptance.
