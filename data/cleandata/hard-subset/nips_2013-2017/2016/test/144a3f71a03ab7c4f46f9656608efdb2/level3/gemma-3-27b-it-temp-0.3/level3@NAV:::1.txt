## Review of "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"

This paper tackles the problem of learning Bayesian networks (BNs) under ancestral constraints, a crucial aspect for incorporating domain knowledge into structure learning. The authors leverage a recent framework based on navigating the search space with oracles for decomposable scores, adapting it to handle the non-decomposable nature of ancestral constraints. The core idea is to infer decomposable constraints from the ancestral ones, effectively empowering the oracle and improving search efficiency. This approach is a valuable contribution as traditional methods struggle with such non-decomposable constraints. The work builds upon prior research in optimal structure learning, particularly Chen et al.’s framework, and addresses a limitation of existing ILP and CP approaches.

The paper’s strength lies in its attempt to bridge the gap between non-decomposable constraints and existing efficient learning algorithms. The proposed method of projecting ancestral constraints into decomposable forms is a clever approach. The experimental results demonstrate significant speedups compared to GOBNILP, suggesting a practical benefit. The exploration of MaxSAT for inferring ordering constraints is also a noteworthy addition. 

However, several weaknesses hinder the paper’s overall impact. The proof of Lemma 3, central to the pruning strategy, is insufficiently detailed and lacks clarity. The explanation of how implied/projected constraints are used, and the role of “can infer” is vague; it’s unclear if these are consistently entailed constraints or merely added for heuristic guidance. The MaxSAT inference procedure is also insufficiently detailed – the rationale behind the encoding and its impact on the quality of inferred constraints needs further elaboration. 

Furthermore, the comparison to Cussens (2008) is unconvincing as it utilizes a weak ILP formulation. A tighter ILP encoding using A(X,Y) and I(Z,U) variables would provide a more robust benchmark. The paper also fails to address the work of van Beek and Hoffmann (2015), which often outperforms GOBNILP and could likely be adapted to handle ancestral constraints, representing a significant omission. Finally, the experimental setup could be strengthened by providing more detailed ablation studies on the impact of constraint projection. It remains unclear if the observed speedups are solely due to ancestral constraints or a general improvement in handling complex constraints.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important problem – learning BNs with background knowledge.
*   Presents a novel approach to handle non-decomposable constraints.
*   Demonstrates significant speedups compared to GOBNILP.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of clarity in key theoretical justifications (Lemma 3).
*   Insufficient detail in the MaxSAT inference procedure.
*   Weak baseline comparison (Cussens 2008) and omission of relevant work (van Beek & Hoffmann 2015).
*   Limited ablation studies to isolate the impact of constraint projection.



**Overall:** While the paper presents a promising approach, the lack of clarity in key aspects and the inadequate comparison to existing methods prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance in its current form. I recommend rejection with encouragement to address the identified weaknesses and resubmit.
