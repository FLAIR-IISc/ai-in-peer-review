This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data when given background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints (e.g., X is an ancestor of Y, or X is not an ancestor of Y). Such constraints are non-decomposable, which means they cannot be handled by standard, efficient dynamic programming or search algorithms that rely on decomposable scores. The authors' main contribution is to adapt a recently proposed A*-based search framework over Equivalence Class (EC) trees, which can handle non-decomposable scores. The key idea is to "empower" the oracle (the heuristic function for A* search) by inferring decomposable edge and ordering constraints from the given non-decomposable ancestral constraints. For inferring ordering constraints, they propose a novel reduction to a MaxSAT problem. The proposed method is empirically evaluated against an Integer Linear Programming (ILP) approach (Gobnilp), demonstrating orders-of-magnitude improvements in runtime.

This work builds directly on the recent EC-tree framework of Chen et al. (2016) and the BN graph search of Chen et al. (2015), extending them to handle a new and important class of non-decomposable constraints. While ILP and constraint programming methods have the flexibility to encode such constraints, the authors correctly note that they have not been empirically evaluated for this specific task.

**Strengths**

*   **Originality:** The paper presents a novel and clever approach to a practical problem. The idea of projecting non-decomposable constraints onto a set of decomposable ones that can be consumed by an efficient oracle for decomposable scores is a significant conceptual contribution. The use of a MaxSAT solver to find a maximal set of consistent ordering constraints is also a novel and appropriate technical choice.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the technical preliminaries are concise, and the proposed approach is explained logically and step-by-step. The distinction between decomposable and non-decomposable constraints, and why this matters, is particularly well-articulated.

*   **Quality:** The proposed method is technically sound. The theorems provided form a solid theoretical basis for the inference of edge and ordering constraints. The experimental evaluation is thorough, exploring the effects of varying the number of variables, dataset size, and the quantity of background knowledge. The empirical results are impressive, showing a substantial performance advantage over the ILP-based baseline.

**Weaknesses**

*   **Significance and Practical Impact:** The practical setting for this work feels somewhat narrow. In many real-world domains where one has strong ancestral (causal) knowledge, there is also a significant concern about unobserved confounders. This work, like most score-based BN learning methods, operates under the assumption of no latent variables. When ancestral constraints are available, it might be more appropriate to use methods from the causal discovery literature (e.g., FCI) that are robust to latent confounders. Furthermore, the experiments assume that a relatively large number (10%-25%) of ancestral relations are known. In practice, such extensive prior knowledge is rare, and the performance benefit might be less pronounced with the sparse constraints that are more typical.

*   **Choice of Baseline:** The comparison is made against Gobnilp, an ILP solver. While ILP is a valid method for exact inference, it is known that for unconstrained BN learning with BDeu scores, heuristic search methods often scale better than ILP. The dramatic speedup reported may, in part, reflect this known architectural difference rather than solely the benefit of the proposed constraint projection method. A comparison with a strong greedy search algorithm (like GES), modified to respect the ancestral constraints, could provide a more complete picture of the practical trade-offs.

*   **Clarity of Technical Details:** Some of the technical descriptions could be improved:
    1.  **Theorem 1:** The statement "a CPDAG P satisfies ancestral constraints A... iff its descendants satisfy A" needs to be more precise. The constraints `A` on the variables of P are a subset of the constraints on the variables of its descendants. The "iff" seems incorrect as stated; presumably, the theorem means that if a path in the EC tree is valid up to node P, it can be extended to a valid full DAG if and only if there is a valid path from P to a leaf. This should be clarified.
    2.  **MaxSAT Encoding:** The constraint labeled "a sufficient condition for acyclicity" (lines 259-261) is not immediately obvious. The premise `(X < Y) ^ (Z < W)` is symmetric with respect to the two pairs of variables, but the conclusion is a disjunction of six specific ancestral constraints that is not symmetric. This makes the rule's correctness hard to verify. Similarly, rule 4 seems to preclude learning from constraints of the form "X and Y are not ancestors of each other".
    3.  **Theorem 3:** This theorem provides a condition for inferring a positive edge constraint. However, the condition seems very strict and is unlikely to be met unless the set of ancestral constraints is extremely dense. It would be helpful to comment on how often this rule actually fires in the experiments.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  The paper addresses an important problem in score-based structure learning: how to incorporate rich, non-decomposable background knowledge.
2.  The proposed technique of projecting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones to guide a search algorithm is novel and elegant.
3.  The empirical results show a significant and impressive performance improvement over a relevant exact baseline.
4.  The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  The practical significance is somewhat limited by the assumption of no latent variables and the potential need for a dense set of constraints to see large gains.
2.  The main baseline (ILP) may not be the most informative one for assessing the method's practical advantage over other heuristic approaches.
3.  Several key technical details, particularly regarding the MaxSAT formulation and Theorem 1, lack sufficient clarity or justification.

In summary, this is a strong paper with a novel contribution to the specialized but important area of exact Bayesian network structure learning. The strengths in originality, clarity, and empirical performance outweigh the weaknesses concerning the practical assumptions and minor technical obscurities. The weaknesses identified are addressable and do not undermine the core contribution.
