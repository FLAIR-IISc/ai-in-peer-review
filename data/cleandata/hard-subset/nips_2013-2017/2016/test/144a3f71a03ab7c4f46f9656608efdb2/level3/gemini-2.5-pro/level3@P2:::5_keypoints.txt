- Problem: Learning optimal Bayesian network structure from data with ancestral (X ancestor of Y) background knowledge, which is non-decomposable and breaks many efficient algorithms.  
- Challenge: Ancestral constraints are non-decomposable, preventing use of decomposable-score oracles in state-of-the-art search methods.  
- Approach: Project non-decomposable ancestral constraints into entailed decomposable edge constraints and topological ordering constraints to empower a decomposable-score oracle.  
- Technical core: Reduce inference of ordering constraints to a MaxSAT formulation, enabling extraction of useful decomposable constraints.  
- Integration: Use the projected constraints to tighten the heuristic in an A* search over equivalence classes (EC tree) guided by a decomposable-score oracle.  
- Theoretical contributions: Theorems for inferring edge constraints and the MaxSAT formulation for ordering constraints are sound and well-motivated.  
- Empirical results: Thorough experiments show orders-of-magnitude speedups over a state-of-the-art ILP solver (GOBNILP) across varying variables, data sizes, and prior knowledge.  
- Ablation: Comparison with and without constraint projection demonstrates the massive benefit of the projection technique.  
- Clarity: Paper is well-written and organized; exposition of the main idea and experiments is clear.  
- Minor clarity issue: Section 5.2 (MaxSAT formulation) is dense; adding an illustrative example for the acyclicity constraints would help.  
- Originality: Novel idea of projecting non-decomposable constraints into the decomposable domain and the MaxSAT reduction is a significant, original contribution.  
- Significance: High practical impact—enables exact learning with ancestral constraints and shows small amounts of background knowledge can substitute for large increases in data.  
- Pros for acceptance: Strong technical novelty, impressive empirical speedups, high significance, and clear presentation.  
- Cons against acceptance: Builds on a very recent framework from the same group (though extension is non-trivial); like all exact methods, scalability is limited to moderate sizes (~20 variables).  
- Overall: Strong paper with sound theory, novel methods, and compelling empirical evidence; reviewer strongly recommends acceptance.
