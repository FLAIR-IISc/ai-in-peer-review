This paper addresses the important problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data when subject to ancestral constraints. Such constraints are non-decomposable, posing a challenge for many state-of-the-art exact structure learners that rely on decomposable scores. The authors propose an elegant solution based on a branch-and-bound A* search over the space of completed partially directed acyclic graphs (CPDAGs), building upon the EC tree framework of Chen et al. (2016). The core contribution is a method to handle ancestral constraints by both pruning the search space and "empowering" the search heuristic (an oracle based on URLearning) by projecting the non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints.

The main strength of this work is its novel approach to integrating global, non-decomposable constraints into a search framework designed for decomposable scores. The idea of inferring simpler, decomposable constraints to tighten the heuristic is clever and demonstrates significant empirical promise, showing orders-of-magnitude speedups over a standard integer linear programming (ILP) solver, GOBNILP.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that undermine its contribution. On the technical side, the clarity and soundness of key results are questionable. The proof for Lemma 3, a central result for pruning, contains an unsubstantiated claim ("By the EC tree edge generation rules, Gk also contains edge Z -> W") that requires more rigorous justification. Furthermore, the treatment of inferred ordering constraints is confusing; the use of "can infer" is misleading, as the authors seem to be adding constraints that are merely consistent with the ancestral knowledge, rather than logically entailed by it. The MAXSAT formulation used for this purpose is insufficiently detailed, making the procedure difficult to reproduce and evaluate.

The experimental evaluation, while showing impressive speedups, is not entirely convincing due to the choice of baselines. It is unclear whether GOBNILP’s poor performance stems from the difficulty of ancestral constraints specifically or its known weakness with unbounded parent set sizes. More critically, the authors compare against a MAXSAT-based ILP encoding for ancestral constraints (Cussens, 2008) that is known to be a relatively weak formulation. A comparison against a tighter ILP encoding would provide a much stronger test of the proposed method's superiority. Finally, the paper omits any discussion of or comparison to highly relevant work from the constraint programming community, such as van Beek and Hoffmann (CP 2015), which often outperforms GOBNILP and could likely be adapted to this problem. While the core idea is promising, these issues of clarity, theoretical justification, and experimental rigor should be addressed.
