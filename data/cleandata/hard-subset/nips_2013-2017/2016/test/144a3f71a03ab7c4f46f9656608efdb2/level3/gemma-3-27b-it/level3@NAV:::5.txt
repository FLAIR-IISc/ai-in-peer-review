## Review of “Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints”

This paper addresses the important problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures under ancestral constraints – a form of background knowledge often present in real-world applications. The core contribution lies in extending the Chen et al. [2015, 2016] framework for optimal structure learning to accommodate these non-decomposable constraints. The authors achieve this by cleverly rephrasing the problem as a MaxSAT formulation, enabling them to infer decomposable constraints from the ancestral ones, which can then be used to guide the search process.

The technical approach is well-motivated and clearly presented. Leveraging the existing work on oracles for decomposable scores is a smart strategy, and the method for projecting ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints appears sound, supported by theorems regarding entailment. The empirical evaluation is a significant strength. The reported orders-of-magnitude runtime improvements over ILP-based methods are impressive and demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed approach. The experiments are well-designed, varying network size, dataset size, and constraint density. The analysis of structural Hamming distance also provides valuable insight into the accuracy of the learned networks.

However, my expertise in this specific area of Bayesian network structure learning is limited. While the paper is logically structured and the technical details seem reasonable, a deeper understanding of the underlying algorithms and their complexities would be needed for a more in-depth critique. It would be beneficial to see a more detailed comparison with other approaches that *can* handle non-decomposable constraints, beyond the ILP baseline.  For example, relating the work to Borboudakis and Tsamardinos [2012, 2013]’s use of path constraints in constraint-based learning would strengthen the positioning of this work. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Significant runtime improvements over existing methods.
*   Clear and well-motivated technical approach.
*   Thorough empirical evaluation.
*   Addresses a relevant and challenging problem.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Reviewer’s limited expertise necessitates reliance on the authors’ claims.
*   Could benefit from a more comprehensive comparison with alternative methods for handling non-decomposable constraints.

**Overall:** Despite my limited background, I find the paper to be a strong contribution. The impressive empirical results, combined with the clear and logical presentation, suggest that this work represents a valuable advancement in the field of Bayesian network structure learning. I recommend acceptance.
