## Review of “Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints”

This paper proposes a novel approach to learning Bayesian networks (BNs) when provided with ancestral constraints – knowledge specifying ancestor/descendant relationships between variables. The core idea is to translate these non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ordering constraints and leverage these within an EC-tree based score-based algorithm. This allows the utilization of efficient oracles designed for decomposable scores. The method is evaluated by comparing its performance against a modified version of GOBNILP, demonstrating significant speedups.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains a conceptually straightforward, yet impactful, contribution. The motivation for addressing ancestral constraints is well-defined, and the approach of mapping them to ordering constraints is intuitively appealing. The novelty (rated 3/3 by the author) is indeed present, offering a new method for incorporating background knowledge into BN structure learning. The authors also demonstrate a good understanding of related work, referencing relevant literature in the field.

However, the significance (rated 2/3 by the author) is somewhat limited. While the performance gains are notable, the setting feels somewhat artificial. Ancestral constraints in many real-world scenarios would necessitate more sophisticated causal discovery methods capable of handling unobserved confounders, rather than simply restricting the search space within a standard BN framework. The reliance on a perfectly known set of ancestral constraints is a strong assumption. 

Several technical points require attention. The mapping from ancestral to ordering constraints via a MaxSAT solver is a smart move, but the specific rules (lines 257-261) warrant closer scrutiny. Rule 3's asymmetry is concerning, and rule 4’s exclusion of constraints like “X and Y do not cause each other” feels restrictive. Furthermore, the theorem statements (1 and 3) require clarification and refinement for mathematical correctness. The authors acknowledge that providing even a small percentage of ancestral constraints implies considerable prior knowledge, which is not always realistic. 

The comparison to GOBNILP, while demonstrating performance improvements, is not entirely fair as GOBNILP wasn’t specifically designed for this problem. A more compelling baseline would be a comparison against a greedy algorithm like GES, configured to utilize the ancestral constraints as guiding principles. This would better illustrate the efficiency gains achieved by the proposed method.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to integrating non-decomposable constraints.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Demonstrated performance improvements over existing methods.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited real-world applicability due to the assumption of known ancestral constraints.
*   Potential issues with the technical correctness of some rules and theorems.
*   Unfair comparison to GOBNILP.



Overall, this is a solid paper with a clear contribution. Addressing the raised technical concerns and broadening the discussion of real-world applicability would significantly strengthen the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
