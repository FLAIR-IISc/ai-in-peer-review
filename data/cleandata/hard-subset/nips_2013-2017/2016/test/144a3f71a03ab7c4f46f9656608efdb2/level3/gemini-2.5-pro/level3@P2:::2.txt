This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data, subject to background knowledge given as ancestral constraints. Such constraints (e.g., X is an ancestor of Y) are non-decomposable, meaning they cannot be verified locally for each variable's parent set. This makes them incompatible with many efficient, exact structure learning algorithms that rely on decomposable scores.

The authors build upon a recent framework [Chen et al., 2015, 2016] that performs an A* search over the space of Markov equivalence classes (an "EC tree") to find an optimal network, even with non-decomposable scores. The heuristic for this A* search is provided by an "oracle"—an efficient optimal learner for decomposable scores. The core contribution of this paper is a method to handle non-decomposable ancestral constraints within this framework. The key idea is to "project" the non-decomposable ancestral constraints into a set of decomposable constraints (specifically, edge presence/absence and variable ordering constraints) that are logically entailed by them. These simpler, decomposable constraints can then be passed to the oracle, effectively "empowering" it. This leads to a tighter, more informed heuristic for the A* search, significantly pruning the search space and speeding up the learning process. The authors provide theorems and algorithms for this projection, including a reduction to MaxSAT for inferring ordering constraints. The proposed approach is compared experimentally to an Integer Linear Programming (ILP) based system, GOBNILP.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a very important and practical problem. Incorporating domain knowledge is critical for learning accurate and meaningful graphical models, and ancestral constraints are a natural way to express causal priors. The proposed method for integrating these non-decomposable constraints into a state-of-the-art exact search algorithm is novel and elegant. The idea of projecting complex constraints into a simpler form that an underlying oracle can handle is a powerful and potentially generalizable concept.
*   **Technical Quality:** The work is technically sound. It builds on a solid foundation of prior work on exact structure learning (EC trees, A* search). The methods for inferring decomposable edge and ordering constraints from ancestral constraints are well-reasoned and supported by theorems. The overall approach provides a principled way to find provably optimal networks under these complex constraints.
*   **Empirical Results:** The experiments demonstrate that the proposed method can be orders of magnitude more efficient than a state-of-the-art ILP-based approach (GOBNILP), which is a strong result. The paper also effectively shows that adding even a small percentage of ancestral constraints can significantly improve the structural accuracy of the learned network, highlighting the value of incorporating such background knowledge.

**Weaknesses:**

The primary weakness of the paper lies in the clarity and organization of the experimental evaluation, which makes it difficult to isolate and appreciate the paper's core contribution.

*   **Clarity of Experiments:** The experimental setup is confusing. The main claim is that projecting ancestral constraints into decomposable ones empowers the oracle and speeds up the search. To validate this, the most crucial comparison is the performance of the EC tree search *with* the projected constraints versus the EC tree search *without* them. While the data for this comparison seems to be present across Tables 1 and 2, it is not presented as the main result.
    *   Table 1 compares your proposed method (EC tree + projection) with an ILP solver (GOBNILP + projection). This compares two different search frameworks, both augmented by your technique. While this shows your framework is superior, it doesn't quantify the benefit of the projection technique itself.
    *   The most important comparison—EC tree performance with and without the empowered oracle—is buried. Comparing the "EC tree" column in Table 2 (without projection) to the "EC tree" column in Table 1 (with projection) shows the dramatic speedup that is the central contribution of your work. This should be the headline result, but it is not presented this way.
*   **Framing of Results:** Because of the above, the main takeaway from the experiments is muddled. A reader might mistakenly believe the contribution is simply a faster algorithm than GOBNILP, rather than the specific technique of constraint projection that *causes* the speedup. Even if the projection technique also benefits GOBNILP, the most relevant demonstration for this paper is its effect on the EC tree framework, which is established as the superior base algorithm.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

I strongly recommend restructuring the experimental section to improve clarity.

1.  Create a single, clear table that directly compares:
    *   Column 1: EC Tree (baseline, without projection)
    *   Column 2: EC Tree + Projection (proposed method)
    This would cleanly and directly demonstrate the performance gain from your contribution. The data for this appears to be in Tables 1 and 2 already.

2.  After establishing the benefit of your projection technique, you can then present the comparison to GOBNILP as a secondary result, showing that your overall system is state-of-the-art. When doing so, please clarify why GOBNILP was also given the projected constraints (e.g., "to ensure a fair comparison of the underlying search algorithms when both are given the same available information").

3.  Explicitly state the purpose of each table in the text. For instance, clearly state that Table 3 is intended to demonstrate the scalability of the approach with the number of variables and to show the positive impact of constraints on the accuracy (structural Hamming distance) of the learned models.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper addresses a significant and practical problem in Bayesian network learning.
*   The proposed method is novel, technically sound, and represents a clear contribution to exact inference for structure learning.
*   The core idea of projecting non-decomposable constraints to empower a decomposable oracle is elegant and powerful.
*   The empirical results, once deciphered, are very strong and demonstrate orders-of-magnitude improvements.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The experimental section is confusingly presented, obscuring the paper's core contribution and making it difficult for a reader to assess the impact of the proposed technique without significant effort.

**Overall Recommendation:**
This is a strong paper with a novel and significant contribution. The technical work is solid and the results appear to be excellent. The main drawback is the presentation of the experiments, which I believe can be easily fixed. I am therefore recommending acceptance, with the strong suggestion that the authors revise the experimental section to improve its clarity and better highlight the paper's core scientific contribution.
