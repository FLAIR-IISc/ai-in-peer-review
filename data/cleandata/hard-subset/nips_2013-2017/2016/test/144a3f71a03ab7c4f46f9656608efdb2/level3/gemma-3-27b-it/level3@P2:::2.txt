This paper proposes a method for learning Bayesian networks optimally under ancestral constraints. The core idea is to leverage a recently proposed framework based on non-decomposable scores and oracles for learning structures with decomposable scores. The key contribution lies in efficiently incorporating ancestral constraints, which are non-decomposable, by inferring decomposable constraints that can empower the oracle. The approach is empirically demonstrated to be significantly more efficient than integer linear programming (ILP)-based methods.

The motivation for this work is well-articulated. Learning Bayesian networks with background knowledge, particularly in the form of causal constraints, is a crucial problem with practical applications. Existing methods often struggle with non-decomposable constraints, limiting their utility. The authors effectively position their work within the context of recent advances in optimal structure learning, specifically building upon the framework of Chen et al. [2015, 2016]. The paper is clearly written and logically organized, with a good breakdown of technical preliminaries and a clear presentation of the proposed approach.

The strengths of this paper are substantial. First, the technical contribution of projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints is clever and appears to be a key enabler for efficiency. The theoretical justifications (Theorems 1-4) lend credibility to the approach. Second, the empirical results demonstrate a significant speedup compared to ILP-based methods, even *with* the inclusion of projected constraints in the ILP formulation. The experiments are reasonably comprehensive, varying network size, dataset size, and constraint percentage.  The observation that even a small percentage of ancestral constraints can significantly improve model accuracy, comparable to increasing training data size, is a compelling result. Finally, the discussion of scalability, reaching networks with 20 variables, is encouraging.

However, the presentation of the experimental results is somewhat confusing and hinders a clear assessment of the contribution. The comparisons in Tables 1 and 2 are structured in a way that makes it difficult to isolate the impact of the proposed method. Specifically, comparing "EC + new work" against "GOB + new work" obscures the core question: how much does the proposed method improve upon the existing EC tree approach *without* the constraint projection? While demonstrating superiority over GOBNILP is valuable, the most relevant comparison is the performance gain achieved by leveraging the inferred decomposable constraints within the EC tree framework.  A dedicated comparison of EC tree with and without the projected constraints is crucial to understand the true impact of the projection step. Table 3, while showing scalability and the effect of constraint percentage, doesn't address this critical question.

Furthermore, while the paper briefly mentions prior work on incorporating causal priors (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos, 2012, 2013), it doesn't fully situate itself within this broader landscape. How does the proposed approach differ in its handling of constraints or its algorithmic efficiency compared to these existing methods? A more detailed discussion would strengthen the novelty claim.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Significant speedup:** The proposed method demonstrably outperforms ILP-based approaches, offering a substantial practical advantage.
*   **Novel approach:** The projection of non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones is a novel and effective technique.
*   **Theoretical grounding:** The theoretical results provide a solid foundation for the approach.
*   **Clear writing:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow.
*   **Practical relevance:** The ability to efficiently learn Bayesian networks with causal constraints has broad applications.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Confusing experimental presentation:** The experimental setup and reporting obscure the primary contribution of the constraint projection.
*   **Limited comparison to related work:** A more thorough discussion of existing methods for incorporating causal priors is needed.
*   **Lack of a direct comparison:**  The paper lacks a focused comparison between the EC tree with and without the proposed constraint projection technique.



**Overall assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant technical contribution. The proposed method offers a practical and efficient solution to a challenging problem. However, the confusing presentation of the experimental results detracts from the overall impact.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The authors should:

1.  **Provide a dedicated comparison** of the EC tree approach with and without the proposed constraint projection technique. This will clearly demonstrate the benefit of their contribution.
2.  **Reorganize Tables 1 and 2** to focus on this comparison, or provide a new table specifically addressing it.
3.  **Expand the discussion of related work** to more thoroughly situate the proposed approach within the existing literature on causal prior incorporation.
