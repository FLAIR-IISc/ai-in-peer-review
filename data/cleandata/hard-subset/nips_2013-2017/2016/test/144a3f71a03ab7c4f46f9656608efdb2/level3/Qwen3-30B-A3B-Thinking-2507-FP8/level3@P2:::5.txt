

# Review of "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"

This paper addresses a significant gap in Bayesian network structure learning: the incorporation of non-decomposable ancestral constraints into optimal structure learning frameworks. The authors build upon Chen et al.'s [2015, 2016] work on non-decomposable scores, which introduced the BN graph and EC tree search spaces. Previous approaches (e.g., K2 algorithm, order graph methods) assume decomposable scores and cannot directly handle ancestral constraints, which specify cause-and-effect relationships between variables (e.g., "X is an ancestor of Y"). While ILP-based methods can theoretically represent such constraints, the authors provide the first empirical evaluation showing their inefficiency compared to the proposed approach.

The main contribution is a framework that enables optimal Bayesian network learning with ancestral constraints by:
1. Using the EC tree search space (an improvement over Chen's BN graph)
2. Pruning the EC tree based on ancestral constraints (Theorem 1)
3. Inferring decomposable constraints (edge and ordering constraints) from ancestral constraints to empower an oracle that optimizes decomposable scores

The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution by formalizing how to project non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable ones (Theorems 2-4), and demonstrates empirically that this approach provides orders-of-magnitude speed improvements over ILP-based alternatives (GOBNILP) across multiple datasets and constraint sizes. Notably, the authors show that even a small percentage of ancestral constraints (10%-25%) can have a similar impact on model quality as increasing training data from 512 to 8192 samples.

**Strengths:**

*Quality:* The paper is technically sound with rigorous theorems supporting the constraint projection approach. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, varying key parameters (number of variables n, dataset size N, constraint percentage p) across 5 benchmark networks (ALARM, ANDES, CHILD, CPCS54, HEPAR2). The authors demonstrate clear efficiency gains (up to orders of magnitude) while maintaining model quality (measured by structural Hamming distance).

*Clarity:* The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with helpful figures (Figures 1a-b) illustrating the BN graph and EC tree. The distinction between ancestral constraints (non-decomposable) and edge/ordering constraints (decomposable) is well-explained. The MaxSAT formulation for ordering constraints (Section 5.2) is presented clearly, though could benefit from more intuitive explanation.

*Originality:* This is a novel approach to handling a specific type of non-decomposable constraint in Bayesian network learning. While constraint-based learning methods (Borboudakis and Tsamardinos [2012, 2013]) use ancestral constraints, they operate under different assumptions (constraint-based vs. score-based learning). The paper's focus on optimal structure learning with non-decomposable constraints is distinct from prior work.

*Significance:* The work has clear practical significance as background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints is common in scientific applications. The demonstration that such constraints can improve model quality as much as increasing training data size is particularly compelling. The approach scales to 20 variables, which is comparable to state-of-the-art score-based methods.

**Weaknesses:**

*The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with other non-decomposable score approaches beyond ILP. While the authors reference constraint programming approaches (van Beek and Hoffmann [2015]), they don't evaluate these directly against their proposed method.

*The evaluation is limited to simulated data from standard benchmark networks. While the authors note they used "random sub-networks" and "simulated datasets," a case study with real-world data would strengthen the significance claim.

*The paper could better elaborate on the computational complexity of the constraint projection step (Section 5), particularly the MaxSAT formulation. The runtime impact of this step on the overall algorithm is not fully analyzed.

**Overall assessment:**

This is a strong contribution to Bayesian network structure learning that addresses a meaningful gap in the literature. The paper presents a technically sound approach with impressive empirical results demonstrating significant efficiency gains. The theoretical foundations are rigorous, and the practical implications of the work are clear. While the evaluation could be strengthened with real-world applications, the paper's core contribution is valuable and well-executed.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Solves a well-identified problem with a technically sound solution
- Demonstrates substantial empirical improvements over alternatives
- Provides clear theoretical justification for the approach
- Has direct practical implications for incorporating background knowledge

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Limited to simulated data (though the methodology is sound)
- Could better distinguish from constraint-based learning approaches
- The MaxSAT formulation could be made more intuitive for readers

Given the paper's technical quality, novelty, and significance, I recommend acceptance. The work represents a meaningful advancement in Bayesian network structure learning that will be valuable to both researchers and practitioners. The authors have clearly demonstrated the value of ancestral constraints in improving learning efficiency and model quality, which is a significant contribution to the field.
