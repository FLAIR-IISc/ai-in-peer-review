- Problem: Learning optimal Bayesian network structures under non-decomposable ancestral constraints by translating them into decomposable ordering and edge constraints.  
- Method: Leverages the EC tree search space (Chen et al.) and an oracle for decomposable scores to handle ancestral constraints via constraint projection.  
- Empirical result: Outperforms a modified ILP solver (GOBNILP) by orders of magnitude in speed.  
- Contribution: Extends EC tree-based optimal structure learning to support ancestral constraints.  
- Key technical idea: Transform non-decomposable ancestral constraints into equivalent decomposable constraints usable by existing oracles.  
- Novel encoding: Uses a MaxSAT encoding for ordering constraints as a clever solution.  
- Strength — quality: Technically sound approach with a clear theoretical foundation and careful treatment of non-decomposability.  
- Strength — clarity: Exceptionally well-written, logical flow, good figures (e.g., EC tree illustration), accessible to the target audience.  
- Strength — originality: Novel enabling of ancestral constraints within the EC tree framework, building on prior non-decomposable-score work.  
- Strength — significance: Shows that modest numbers of ancestral constraints can greatly improve learning efficiency (comparable to much larger data increases).  
- Weakness — theorem accuracy: Theorem 1 is inaccurate about CPDAG constraint satisfaction and needs clarification.  
- Weakness — rule clarity: Acyclicity constraint (rule 3) has a confusing symmetric premise but asymmetric conclusion.  
- Weakness — limited utility of Theorem 3: Likely yields few positive edge implications when constraints are sparse.  
- Weakness — phrasing/clarity: Relationship between the oracle and “omniscient oracle” and wording like “empower the oracle” are vague.  
- Weakness — claim on constraint density: The assertion that 10–25% ancestral constraints is typical is questionable and needs justification.  
- Weakness — practical scope: Does not address unobserved confounders, limiting relevance for causal discovery settings.  
- Weakness — baselines: Comparison to GOBNILP may be unfair; should include greedy baselines (e.g., GES with constraints) for a more relevant comparison.  
- Weakness — originality caveat: Heavy reliance on Chen et al. (2015, 2016) somewhat reduces perceived novelty.  
- Acceptance arguments: Solid technical solution, clear presentation, strong empirical validation, fills a literature gap, and introduces a novel transformation technique.  
- Rejection arguments: Artificial constraint setting, unfair baseline comparison, theoretical issues needing fixes, and questionable real-world prevalence of dense ancestral constraints.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions to fix theoretical inaccuracies (Theorem 1), clarify claims about constraint density, and improve certain explanations.
