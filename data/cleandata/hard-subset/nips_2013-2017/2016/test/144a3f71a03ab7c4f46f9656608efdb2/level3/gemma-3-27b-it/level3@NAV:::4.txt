## Review of "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"

This paper tackles the important problem of learning Bayesian networks under ancestral constraints, which represent valuable domain knowledge often unavailable in purely data-driven approaches. The core idea – projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable forms usable by existing efficient algorithms – is novel and potentially impactful. The approach builds upon the recent framework of optimal structure learning with non-decomposable scores [Chen et al., 2015], leveraging oracles for decomposable scores and cleverly adapting them to handle the new constraints. The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains the theoretical foundations and experimental setup, making it easy to follow the authors’ line of reasoning. 

The strength of the paper lies in its technical soundness and the potential for significant efficiency gains, demonstrated empirically against ILP. The idea of constraint projection is a compelling contribution, bridging the gap between expressiveness and computational tractability. However, the experimental results raise some concerns regarding the generalizability of the improvements. While Table 3 shows significant speedups for EC trees, the results for ILP are less conclusive, suggesting the benefits might be specific to the EC tree framework. 

A key concern is the lack of evidence demonstrating that the inferred decomposable constraints actually *improve* performance or efficiency *for methods that originally couldn't handle the non-decomposable constraints*. The paper shows a speedup *within* the proposed framework, but doesn’t prove that the projection itself is helpful for other learning approaches. Furthermore, the observed “easy-hard-easy” pattern in Table 2, with performance of EC trees fluctuating as the proportion of ancestral constraints increases, is puzzling. The authors should delve deeper into why EC trees appear highly sensitive to the amount of domain knowledge, and how this sensitivity compares to ILP. This sensitivity suggests careful tuning of the number of ancestral constraints may be required – an important implication that warrants further discussion.

Additionally, reporting structural Hamming distance (SHD) only for EC trees in Table 2 makes comparison with the ILP approach difficult. Including SHD for ILP would provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the accuracy of both methods. Finally, while Section 4.1 on BN graphs provides context, it feels less central to the main contribution and detracts from the focus on EC trees. Expanding the explanations of EC trees and the A* algorithm *within* the EC-tree experimental setting would be a more effective use of space.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to handling non-decomposable constraints in Bayesian network learning.
*   Clear and well-written presentation of complex ideas.
*   Strong empirical results demonstrating efficiency gains with EC trees.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Limited evidence of generalizability beyond the EC tree framework.
*   Unexplained “easy-hard-easy” performance pattern.
*   Incomplete comparison of SHD between EC trees and ILP.



Overall, this is a strong paper with a valuable contribution. Addressing the concerns outlined above would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
