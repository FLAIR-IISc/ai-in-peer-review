This paper proposes a novel approach for learning Bayesian networks optimally, subject to ancestral constraints. The authors utilize a search space for structure learning with non-decomposable scores, called the EC tree, and employ an oracle that optimizes decomposable scores. They also propose a sound and complete method for pruning the EC tree, based on ancestral constraints. The authors demonstrate how the employed oracle can be empowered by passing it decomposable constraints inferred from the non-decomposable ancestral constraints.

The main idea of the paper is to translate ancestral constraints into ordering constraints and use them in an EC-tree score-based algorithm to learn optimal Bayesian networks. The authors evaluate their approach against a modified version of GOBNILP and show that it outperforms it. The paper is well-written and clearly explained, making it easy to follow the authors' reasoning.

The concept of translating ancestral constraints into ordering constraints is novel and of interest to specialists in the field. The use of a MaxSAT solver to make implied ancestral constraints explicit is also a clever approach. However, the impact of the paper is limited because the setting is somewhat artificial. Ancestral constraints would often motivate broader causal discovery methods that allow unobserved confounders.

The authors' approach to mapping from ancestral to ordering constraints raises some concerns. The acyclicity rule 3 has a symmetric premise but an asymmetric conclusion, and rule 4 seems to exclude constraints like "X and Y do not cause each other." Additionally, the authors assume that a large number of ancestral constraints are available, which may not be the case in practice. In reality, only a few ancestral constraints are likely to be available, which may not substantially reduce the search space.

The comparison to GOBNILP is not entirely fair, as GOBNILP was not developed for this specific problem. The impressive results may reflect this mismatch. A more suitable baseline for comparison would be greedy algorithms like GES, assuming ancestral constraints align with the optimal model, to better gauge efficiency.

The paper also contains some minor issues. Theorem 1 needs a clearer statement, as the set of constraints A satisfied by the CPDAG P on the EC tree is only a subset of those satisfied by the full DAG G. Theorem 3 is unlikely to produce many positive edge implications when ancestral constraints are sparse. The encoding labeled as "acyclic" is not easily recognizable as capturing acyclicity. Finally, there are some minor wording and clarity issues throughout the paper.

Quality: 7/10
The paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approach. However, the assumptions made about the availability of ancestral constraints and the comparison to GOBNILP are limitations of the paper.

Clarity: 8/10
The paper is well-written and easy to follow. However, there are some minor issues with wording and clarity, as mentioned earlier.

Originality: 7/10
The concept of translating ancestral constraints into ordering constraints is novel and of interest to specialists in the field. However, the approach is not entirely new, as it builds upon existing work on EC trees and MaxSAT solvers.

Significance: 6/10
The paper's impact is limited because the setting is somewhat artificial. Ancestral constraints would often motivate broader causal discovery methods that allow unobserved confounders. However, the paper still makes a contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning.
