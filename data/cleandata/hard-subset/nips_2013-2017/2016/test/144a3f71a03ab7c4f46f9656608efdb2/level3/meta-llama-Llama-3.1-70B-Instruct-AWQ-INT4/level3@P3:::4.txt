This paper presents a novel approach for learning Bayesian networks optimally, subject to background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints. The authors tackle the challenge of incorporating non-decomposable constraints into a structure learning approach, which is a significant contribution to the field.

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the theory and experiments. The authors provide a thorough review of the problem of Bayesian network structure learning and the limitations of existing approaches in handling non-decomposable constraints. They then introduce their approach, which leverages a recently proposed framework for optimal structure learning based on non-decomposable scores. The authors show how to empower an oracle for learning structures using decomposable scores by passing it decomposable constraints inferred from non-decomposable ancestral constraints.

The main contribution of the paper is the method to project non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and topological-ordering constraints, which is presented in Section 5. This is a novel and significant contribution, as it enables the incorporation of non-decomposable constraints into a structure learning approach.

The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach in learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints. The results show that the approach is orders-of-magnitude more efficient than alternative frameworks, such as those based on integer linear programming (ILP). The authors also evaluate the impact of introducing ancestral constraints on the efficiency of search and the scalability of their approach.

However, there are some concerns and suggestions for improvement. Firstly, Table 3 shows improvement for EC trees but not ILP, raising doubts about whether the projection generalizes to other learning approaches. The authors should provide evidence that the inferred decomposable constraints improve the performance/efficiency of methods that originally cannot handle non-decomposable constraints.

Secondly, Table 2 exhibits an easy-hard-easy pattern as the proportion of ancestral constraints increases. The authors should explain why this pattern occurs and whether EC trees are highly sensitive to the amount of domain knowledge compared to ILP. This could have implications for the tuning of the number of ancestral constraints to achieve ideal EC tree performance.

Thirdly, Table 2 reports structural Hamming distance only for EC trees. The authors should add a comparison of SHD with the ILP approach to provide a more comprehensive evaluation.

Lastly, Section 4.1 on BN graph could be dropped, and the explanations of EC trees and the A* algorithm in the EC-tree experimental setting could be expanded. This would provide more clarity on the approach and its implementation.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors are careful and honest about evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of their work.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-written and well-organized. The authors provide enough information for the expert reader to reproduce their results.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel combination of familiar techniques. The approach is significantly different from previous contributions, and the authors adequately reference related work.

In terms of significance, the results are important, and other researchers and practitioners are likely to use these ideas or build on them. The paper addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research and advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. With some revisions to address the concerns and suggestions mentioned above, the paper has the potential to be even stronger.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel approach for learning Bayesian networks optimally, subject to background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints.

* The paper could benefit from more comprehensive evaluation, including a comparison of SHD with the ILP approach.
* The authors should provide more evidence that the inferred decomposable constraints improve the performance/efficiency of methods that originally cannot handle non-decomposable constraints.
* The paper could benefit from more clarity on the approach and its implementation, particularly in the explanations of EC trees and the A* algorithm in the EC-tree experimental setting.
