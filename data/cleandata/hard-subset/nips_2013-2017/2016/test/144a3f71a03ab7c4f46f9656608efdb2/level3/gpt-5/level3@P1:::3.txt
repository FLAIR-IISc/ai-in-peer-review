This paper addresses optimal Bayesian network (BN) structure learning under background knowledge in the form of ancestral (path) constraints. Building on the BN-graph and EC-tree search spaces of Chen et al. (2015; 2016), the authors show how to prune EC-tree nodes that cannot satisfy the given ancestral relations and how to “empower” a decomposable-score oracle by projecting the non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints. Edge implications are derived from closure of ancestral constraints (Theorems 2–3), while ordering implications are selected via a MaxSAT formulation that enforces acyclicity and transitivity (Theorem 4). Empirically, the method is compared to an ILP formulation instantiated in GOBNILP; with projected constraints supplied to both methods, the EC-tree approach is often orders of magnitude faster, especially as n grows. The study also reports an easy–hard–easy trend as the proportion of ancestral constraints increases and shows that moderate amounts of background knowledge can improve structural accuracy comparably to substantial increases in data.

Relation to prior work: The paper situates itself well relative to decomposable-score dynamic programming and A* search in the order graph (Koivisto & Sood; Silander & Myllymäki; Yuan & Malone), ILP/CP methods (Jaakkola et al.; Cussens; van Beek & Hoffmann), and path-belief priors and constraint-based use of ancestral relations (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos). The distinctive contribution is making non-decomposable ancestral constraints usable within a score-based optimality framework by combining EC-tree pruning with safe projection to decomposable constraints.

Quality
- Soundness of pruning: The EC-tree pruning criterion is natural and appears sound; the forward-closure property (Theorem 1) underlies safe pruning of subtrees.
- Projection correctness: The edge-projection rules are intuitive and, if the ancestral constraints are maximally closed, seem correct for entailing negative edges (Theorem 2) and sufficient conditions for positive edges (Theorem 3). However, Theorem 3 is quite strong and likely to fire rarely when constraints are sparse; empirical quantification of how often positive edges are inferred would help.
- MaxSAT encoding: The ordering-projection MaxSAT constraints merit clarification. In particular:
  - The “acyclicity” rule (your Rule 3) has a symmetric premise but an asymmetric disjunction in the conclusion; it is not immediately recognizable as sufficient for preventing cycles in the induced order constraints. A proof sketch or an illustrative example would help.
  - Rule 4 infers Y < X from X 6 Y. This is fine, but it seems to exclude expressing mutually non-ancestral pairs (“X and Y do not cause each other”). If the intention is to avoid introducing both X < Y and Y < X, please clarify how such symmetric negative knowledge is handled.
- Theorem 1 statement: As written, “P satisfies A iff its descendants satisfy A” is confusing. A CPDAG P represents many DAGs; typically, one says that P satisfies A iff class(P) contains at least one DAG satisfying A. The descendants inherit constraints monotonically, but the “iff” seems to need refinement or a more precise definition of “satisfies.”
- Empirical design: The experimental setup is careful and reproducible (benchmarks, BDeu, precomputed family scores, solver named), and the speedups are compelling. However, the fairness of comparing to GOBNILP is debatable since ILP excels with family-size bounds and different constraint structures; the mismatch may partly explain the large gaps. Ablations (EC-tree pruning only vs. pruning + projection; ordering-only vs. edge-only projection) would strengthen the case that projection is the key driver.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written and well organized.
- Minor suggestions:
  - Clarify early that optimizing the score means minimizing in the MDL/BDeu conventions used.
  - “Omniscient oracle” is overstated; “oracle that solves optimal BN learning with decomposable scores” suffices. Similarly, “empower the oracle” could be rephrased as “tighten the heuristic by adding safe decomposable constraints.”
  - The “acyclicity” encoding should be explicitly motivated; a small worked example would aid readers.
  - When claiming “orders of magnitude faster,” indicate the typical n, N, p regimes where this holds.
  - The claim that 10–25% ancestral constraints are typical seems strong; please tone down or cite applications where such density of accurate constraints is realistic.

Originality
- Moderate. The EC-tree framework and oracle-guided A* are known; the novelty lies in integrating non-decomposable ancestral constraints via EC-tree pruning and safe projection, with a MaxSAT formulation for maximal ordering extraction. This is a useful and, to my knowledge, new combination.

Significance
- Potentially useful to practitioners who have trusted path knowledge and want optimal BN structures. Impact may be limited in broader causal discovery settings where latent confounders and MAGs are more appropriate; ancestral constraints then speak to a larger model class than DAGs over observed variables.
- The empirical speedups are promising, but practical benefit depends on availability and accuracy of ancestral constraints. In many domains such constraints are sparse; quantifying performance when p is small is important. Theorem 3’s limited yield in sparse regimes may mean that most gains come from pruning plus negative edge implications.

Pros for acceptance
- Tackles an important practical need: incorporating non-decomposable prior knowledge into score-based optimal BN learning.
- Clear algorithmic framework with theoretically justified pruning and safe projection.
- Strong empirical improvements over a well-known exact baseline.
- Careful use of existing exact solvers (URLearning, MaxSAT) in a principled way.

Cons for acceptance
- Comparison baseline may be mismatched; missing comparisons to strong greedy/equivalence-class search baselines (e.g., GES variants) augmented with path constraints.
- Some theoretical statements (Theorem 1, acyclicity rule) need clarification to remove ambiguity.
- Limited discussion of robustness to sparse or noisy constraints and of frequency/impact of positive edge implications.
- The application scope is narrower than general causal discovery with hidden variables.

Suggestions to improve the paper
- Provide ablations quantifying the contribution of pruning vs. projected edge vs. projected ordering constraints; report how many of each type are inferred as a function of p.
- Add baselines: greedy equivalence search or local search with ancestral constraints (e.g., via penalty terms), and constraint-based methods that accept path constraints (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos), to gauge efficiency and solution quality.
- Clarify the correctness and sufficiency of the MaxSAT constraints for acyclicity and provide examples; specify whether the MaxSAT solver is guaranteed optimal and what happens if it returns a suboptimal set.
- Discuss mis-specified constraints: does the method remain complete/sound if some ancestral constraints are wrong? Can the projection be made robust (e.g., soft constraints)?
- Rephrase minor wording issues noted above and clarify the optimize=minimize convention.

Overall, this is a solid and careful contribution that meaningfully extends optimal score-based BN learning to a useful class of non-decomposable knowledge. With stronger theoretical clarity in the ordering encoding and broader empirical comparisons, it would make a compelling NIPS paper.
