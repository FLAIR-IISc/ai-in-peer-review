This paper presents a method for learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints, a valuable extension to existing structure learning algorithms. The core contribution lies in projecting these non-decomposable constraints into decomposable edge and topological-ordering constraints, enabling the use of efficient oracle-based learning frameworks like those proposed by Chen et al. (2015, 2016). The paper is well-written and clearly explains the theoretical foundations and experimental setup. The authors appropriately position their work within the context of existing approaches, including ILP and constraint programming.

The originality of the work is noteworthy. While ILP can *represent* ancestral constraints, the authors demonstrate empirically that their projection-based approach, leveraging efficient oracles, offers significant speedups. The idea of translating non-decomposable constraints into a form usable by existing decomposable-score based methods is a clever and potentially impactful contribution. Related work on constraint-based learning incorporating path constraints (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos, 2012, 2013) is also correctly referenced, highlighting how this work differs by focusing on score-based approaches and optimal structure learning.

The significance of this work stems from its potential to broaden the applicability of efficient Bayesian network structure learning algorithms. Ancestral constraints are often available in real-world applications, providing valuable domain knowledge. Enabling these constraints to be easily incorporated without resorting to computationally expensive ILP solvers is a tangible advancement.

However, several points require clarification and further investigation. Firstly, Table 3 shows improvement for EC trees but not ILP when using the proposed method. This raises concerns about the generalizability of the projection technique. Does the performance gain hinge on the specific learning approach (EC trees)? The authors need to convincingly demonstrate that the projection improves efficiency *across* different learning algorithms, not just within their chosen framework. Providing evidence that the inferred decomposable constraints demonstrably improve the performance or efficiency of methods which originally cannot handle non-decomposable constraints would strengthen this claim significantly.

Secondly, Table 2 exhibits an unusual "easy-hard-easy" pattern with increasing proportions of ancestral constraints. The authors should explain this phenomenon. Is there a sensitivity threshold in the amount of domain knowledge provided? Are EC trees particularly susceptible to this behavior compared to ILP, and if so, why? This suggests a need for careful tuning of the number of ancestral constraints to achieve optimal performance, a point the authors correctly identify, but requires further explanation.

Thirdly, Table 2 only reports structural Hamming distance (SHD) for EC trees. To facilitate a direct comparison, the authors should include the SHD metric for the ILP approach as well. This would provide a more complete and balanced evaluation of the accuracy of both methods. 

Finally, Section 4.1, discussing BN graphs, feels somewhat tangential to the core contribution. The paper's strength lies in the EC tree adaptation and the constraint projection technique. Dropping Section 4.1 and expanding the explanation of EC trees and the A* algorithm within the EC-tree experimental setting would improve the paper’s focus and clarity. A more detailed explanation of how the inferred constraints are integrated into the A* search process within the EC-tree framework would be particularly helpful.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to incorporating non-decomposable constraints.
*   Significant speedups compared to ILP.
*   Clear presentation of theory and experiments.
*   Addresses a practical problem in Bayesian network learning.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Generalizability of the projection technique is unclear.
*   Unexplained "easy-hard-easy" pattern in experimental results.
*   Lack of SHD comparison for ILP.
*   Section 4.1 feels out of focus and detracts from the core contribution.



**Overall:** This paper presents a promising approach to Bayesian network structure learning with ancestral constraints. Addressing the concerns outlined above would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, contingent on the authors providing a more convincing argument for the generalizability of their method and clarifying the observed experimental patterns.
