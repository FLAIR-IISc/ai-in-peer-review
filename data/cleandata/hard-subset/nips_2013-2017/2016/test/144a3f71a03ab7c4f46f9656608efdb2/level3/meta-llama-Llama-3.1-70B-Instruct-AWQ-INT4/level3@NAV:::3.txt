The paper "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints" proposes a novel approach to learning Bayesian networks with non-decomposable ancestral constraints. The authors introduce a method to translate ancestral constraints into ordering constraints and utilize them in an EC-tree score-based algorithm to learn optimal Bayesian networks. The approach is evaluated against a modified version of the Gobnilp algorithm and demonstrates significant improvements in efficiency.

The paper is well-written and clearly explained, making it easy to follow for readers familiar with Bayesian networks and constraint-based learning. The concept is straightforward, and the authors provide a thorough explanation of the approach, including the theoretical background and experimental results.

The novelty of the paper lies in its ability to incorporate non-decomposable ancestral constraints into the structure learning process, which is a challenging problem in the field of Bayesian networks. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through extensive experiments on various benchmark datasets.

However, the impact of the paper is somewhat limited by the artificial setting of the problem. Ancestral constraints often motivate broader causal discovery methods that allow for unobserved confounders, which are not addressed in this paper. Additionally, the mapping from ancestral to ordering constraints is done using a MaxSAT solver, which may not be efficient for large-scale problems.

The paper raises some concerns about specific rules used in the approach, such as the acyclicity rule and the exclusion of certain constraints. The authors also acknowledge that in practice, only a few ancestral constraints are likely to be available, which may not substantially reduce the search space.

The comparison to Gobnilp is not entirely fair, as Gobnilp was not developed for this specific problem. The authors suggest using greedy algorithms like GES as an alternative baseline to better gauge the efficiency of their approach.

The paper also highlights some minor wording and clarity issues, such as the use of "oracle" and "omniscient oracle," which may be confusing for some readers.

Overall, the paper is a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian networks and constraint-based learning. While it has some limitations, it demonstrates a novel approach to incorporating non-decomposable ancestral constraints into the structure learning process.

Arguments for acceptance:

Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions to address the concerns raised above.
