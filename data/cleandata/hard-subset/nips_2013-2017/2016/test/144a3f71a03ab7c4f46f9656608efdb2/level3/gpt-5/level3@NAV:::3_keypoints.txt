- Tackles optimal BN structure learning with ancestral constraints by translating non-decomposable constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints via MaxSAT and closed-form rules.  
- Builds on EC-tree search with a decomposable-score oracle and reports orders-of-magnitude speedups over a modified GOBNILP baseline.  
- Connects to prior ILP/CP BN learning work and path-constraint causal discovery; extends recent non-decomposable scoring search spaces.  
- Pruning strategy on the EC tree and the projection idea are conceptually sound and well motivated.  
- Theorem 1 is unclear/likely incorrect as stated; needs explicit quantifiers and a monotonicity-style restatement.  
- Theorem 3 appears weak in sparse ancestral-constraint regimes, producing few positive edge implications.  
- The MaxSAT acyclicity encoding (line 267) is not obviously sufficient and requires a short proof or citation.  
- Specific inference rules raise concerns: acyclicity rule 3 has symmetric premises but an asymmetric conclusion, and rule 4 may wrongly exclude mutual non-causation—these need justification or revision.  
- Overall clarity is good, but there are minor wording/phrasing issues (e.g., “omniscient oracle,” “empower the oracle,” clarify optimize=minimize, and overstated prevalence of constraints).  
- Novelty is moderate; combining MaxSAT-derived ordering constraints with EC-tree search is a neat, novel combination.  
- Practical significance is limited since ancestral knowledge is often sparse and can involve unobserved confounding (suggesting MAGs/FCI rather than DAG settings).  
- Empirical comparison to GOBNILP is informative but potentially unfair; additional baselines like constrained GES/UGES should be considered.  
- Pros: clear problem formulation, simple and general projection framework, sound EC-tree pruning, practical MaxSAT reduction, and strong empirical speedups on benchmarks.  
- Cons: limited practical impact, problematic/unclear theoretical statements, insufficient justification for encodings and rules, potentially mismatched baseline comparisons, and unrealistic claims about constraint prevalence.  
- Overall recommendation: Borderline accept; revise to clarify theory, justify inference rules and encodings, and include broader/more appropriate baselines.
