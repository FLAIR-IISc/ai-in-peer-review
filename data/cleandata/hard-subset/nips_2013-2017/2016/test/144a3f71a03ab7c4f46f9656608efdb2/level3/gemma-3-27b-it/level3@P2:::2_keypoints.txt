- Proposes a method to learn Bayesian networks optimally under ancestral (non-decomposable) constraints by inferring decomposable constraints to enable existing oracles.
- Builds on a recent framework for non-decomposable scores and oracles for decomposable-score structure learning.
- Main technical contribution: projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints to empower the oracle.
- Empirical claim: approach is significantly faster than ILP-based methods (GOBNILP), even when ILP includes projected constraints.
- Motivation is strong: learning Bayesian networks with causal/background knowledge is important and existing methods struggle with non-decomposable constraints.
- Paper is clearly written, well-organized, and gives a good presentation of technical preliminaries and the proposed approach.
- Strength: the projection idea is clever and appears key to efficiency gains.
- Strength: theoretical support via Theorems 1–4 lends credibility.
- Strength: experiments vary network size, dataset size, and constraint percentage and show substantial speedups and scalability to ~20 variables.
- Notable empirical finding: a small percentage of ancestral constraints can significantly improve model accuracy, comparable to more training data.
- Weakness: experimental presentation is confusing and comparisons (e.g., "EC + new work" vs "GOB + new work") obscure the specific benefit of the projection step.
- Critical missing comparison: no focused evaluation of EC tree with vs without the projected constraints to isolate impact of the projection.
- Weakness: limited discussion and positioning relative to prior work on causal priors (e.g., Borboudakis & Tsamardinos).
- Overall assessment: strong technical contribution and practical relevance, but experimental clarity issues reduce impact.
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions.
- Required revisions: add a dedicated comparison of EC tree with/without projected constraints, reorganize Tables 1–2 (or add a new table) to highlight that comparison, and expand related-work discussion on causal prior methods.
