The paper "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints" addresses the problem of optimal structure learning in Bayesian networks when subject to background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints. The authors' main contribution is incorporating non-decomposable constraints, particularly ancestral constraints, into the Chen et al. [2015, 2016] structure learning approach. This is a significant extension, as ancestral constraints are important in practice for expressing cause-and-effect relationships between variables.

The authors' technical approach is to utilize a search space for structure learning with non-decomposable scores, called the EC tree, and employ an oracle that optimizes decomposable scores. They propose a sound and complete method for pruning the EC tree based on ancestral constraints and show how the employed oracle can be empowered by passing it decomposable constraints inferred from the non-decomposable ancestral constraints. This is achieved through a MaxSAT formulation with specific constraints.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it easy to follow the authors' reasoning and technical contributions. The authors provide a thorough review of the relevant literature, including previous work on structure learning approaches based on integer linear programming (ILP) and constraint programming (CP). They also provide empirical evidence that their approach is orders-of-magnitude more efficient than ILP-based alternatives.

The empirical evaluation is thorough and convincing, with the authors comparing their approach to the ILP-based system of GOBNILP. The results show that their approach is consistently faster than GOBNILP for almost all values of the number of variables, training dataset size, and proportion of ancestral constraints. The authors also evaluate the impact of introducing ancestral constraints on the efficiency of search and the scalability of their approach.

The paper's strengths lie in its technical contributions, empirical evaluation, and clarity of writing. The authors provide a detailed and well-structured presentation of their approach, making it easy to understand and follow. The empirical results are convincing and demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach.

However, there are some potential weaknesses and areas for improvement. One potential weakness is that the authors' approach relies on the quality of the oracle used to optimize decomposable scores. If the oracle is not effective, the overall approach may not perform well. Additionally, the authors could provide more discussion on the limitations of their approach and potential avenues for future work.

In terms of originality, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. The incorporation of non-decomposable constraints, particularly ancestral constraints, is a novel extension of the Chen et al. [2015, 2016] approach. The use of a MaxSAT formulation to empower the oracle is also a new contribution.

In terms of significance, the paper's results have important implications for the field of Bayesian network structure learning. The authors' approach has the potential to improve the efficiency and scalability of structure learning, making it more practical for real-world applications. The paper's results also highlight the importance of incorporating background knowledge, such as ancestral constraints, into the structure learning process.

Overall, the paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. The authors' approach has the potential to improve the efficiency and scalability of structure learning, making it more practical for real-world applications.
