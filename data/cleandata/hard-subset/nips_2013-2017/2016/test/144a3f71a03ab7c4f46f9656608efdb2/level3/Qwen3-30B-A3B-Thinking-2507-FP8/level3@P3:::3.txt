

# Review: Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints

This paper presents a novel approach to learning optimal Bayesian networks under ancestral constraints, a type of non-decomposable constraint that is difficult to incorporate into mainstream structure learning algorithms. The authors build upon the EC tree framework for optimal structure learning with non-decomposable scores (Chen et al., 2015, 2016), which uses an oracle that optimizes decomposable scores. The key insight is to translate ancestral constraints (which are non-decomposable) into decomposable ordering and edge constraints, which can be passed to the oracle to significantly improve search efficiency.

The paper makes a clear contribution by showing how to infer decomposable constraints from non-decomposable ancestral constraints. The authors develop a MaxSAT-based approach to infer maximal sets of ordering constraints that preserve all DAGs satisfying the ancestral constraints, and they present algorithms for inferring edge constraints. The approach is evaluated against an ILP-based implementation of ancestral constraints (Gobnilp), demonstrating orders-of-magnitude speedups.

**Strengths:**

1. **Quality:** The technical approach is sound and well-motivated. The authors provide clear theoretical foundations for constraint projection and demonstrate empirical efficiency gains. The EC tree search space is well-explained and properly justified.

2. **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The concepts are presented in a logical flow, with good use of examples and figures. The distinction between ancestral constraints and decomposable constraints is particularly well-explained.

3. **Originality:** The core idea of translating ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints is novel for this specific application (novelty=3). While some constraint-based methods exist (e.g., Borboudakis and Tsamardinos, 2012), the authors demonstrate a unique approach to constraint projection that enables significant efficiency gains.

4. **Significance:** The results demonstrate substantial efficiency improvements (orders of magnitude) in structure learning with ancestral constraints, which is significant for applications where such constraints are available. The paper makes a clear contribution to the problem of incorporating background knowledge into Bayesian network structure learning.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Impact:** The impact is somewhat limited as the setting (ancestral constraints) is somewhat artificial. In practice, ancestral constraints would typically motivate broader causal discovery methods that handle unobserved confounders, rather than being the focus of structure learning. The paper would benefit from discussing how this work might integrate with causal discovery frameworks.

2. **Theoretical Concerns:** Theorem 1 has an incorrect "iff" statement - the set of constraints satisfied by a CPDAG P is only a subset of those satisfied by any DAG in its equivalence class, not necessarily all constraints. Theorem 3 is unlikely to yield many positive edge implications when ancestral constraints are sparse (as they typically would be in practice).

3. **Empirical Comparison:** The comparison to Gobnilp is not entirely fair, as Gobnilp wasn't designed for this specific problem. A more appropriate baseline would be a greedy algorithm like GES that can incorporate ancestral constraints, to better gauge the efficiency gains.

4. **Implementation Details:** The encoding labeled "acyclic" (line 267) is unclear and not easily recognizable as capturing acyclicity. The paper should clarify the relationship between the constraints and acyclicity.

5. **Practical Relevance:** The paper overstates the typical availability of ancestral constraints. Line 310 claims that "10-25% ancestral constraints already imply extensive knowledge and are not typical," but this contradicts the paper's own claim that ancestral constraints are sparse. The paper should clarify how many ancestral constraints are typically available in practice.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- Novel approach to translating non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable ones for efficient structure learning
- Clear, well-written paper that makes a concrete technical contribution
- Demonstrates substantial empirical improvements over existing approaches
- Well-motivated and sound theoretical foundation

*Con:*
- Limited practical impact due to artificial nature of the problem setting
- Theoretical concerns with Theorem 1 and Theorem 3
- Comparison to Gobnilp is not entirely fair
- Overstates the typical availability of ancestral constraints
- The paper's significance is somewhat limited by the narrow focus on ancestral constraints rather than broader causal discovery

The paper makes a valuable technical contribution to the specific problem of structure learning with ancestral constraints. While the setting may be somewhat narrow for broader impact, the approach is elegant and the empirical results are compelling. The authors should address the theoretical concerns in Theorem 1, clarify the practical relevance of ancestral constraints, and consider a more appropriate comparison baseline. With these revisions, this paper would be a strong contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
