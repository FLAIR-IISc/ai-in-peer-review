## Review of "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"

This paper tackles the problem of learning Bayesian networks (BNs) when prior knowledge is available in the form of ancestral constraints – relationships asserting that one variable is an ancestor of another. The core idea is to translate these non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ordering constraints, allowing them to be leveraged within an efficient EC-tree based structure learning algorithm. This is a clever approach, building on recent work on optimal structure learning with non-decomposable scores. The paper is well-written and clearly explains the concepts, making it conceptually straightforward to follow. The novelty (rated 3/5) lies in the mapping from ancestral to ordering constraints and its application within the EC-tree framework.

The strength of this work is its technical soundness and the clear presentation of the method. The authors demonstrate a good understanding of existing approaches like ILP-based methods (GOBNILP) and provide a compelling empirical comparison, showcasing significant speedups with their EC-tree approach. The theoretical justification, including Theorems 1-4, attempts to formalize the correctness of the constraint projection and its impact on the search space. 

However, the impact (rated 2/5) feels somewhat limited. The setting, while well-defined, feels somewhat artificial. Ancestral constraints, in many real-world causal discovery scenarios, often motivate the need for methods capable of handling unobserved confounders – a limitation not addressed here. The reliance on complete knowledge of ancestral constraints is also a practical concern; in many applications, only a few such constraints might be available, potentially offering minimal search space reduction. 

Several points require attention. The mapping from ancestral to ordering constraints via MaxSAT is a good idea, but the practical benefit of this step, particularly when constraints are sparse (as Theorem 3 suggests), needs further investigation. The rules for constraint inference (lines 257-261) contain potential issues: rule 3 appears asymmetric, and rule 4 seems overly restrictive. The comparison to GOBNILP isn’t entirely fair, as GOBNILP wasn’t specifically designed for this problem. A more appropriate baseline would be a greedy algorithm like GES, adapted to incorporate the ancestral constraints, to better assess the efficiency gains. Finally, Theorem 1’s statement requires clarification regarding the relationship between constraints satisfied by the CPDAG and the full DAG. Minor clarity issues exist in the wording (oracle vs. omniscient oracle, empowering the oracle, optimizing=minimizing, and the significance of 10-25% constraints).

**Arguments for acceptance:** Technically sound, novel approach to incorporating constraints, significant empirical speedups compared to ILP.
**Arguments against acceptance:** Limited impact due to the artificial setting and reliance on complete ancestral knowledge, potential issues with constraint inference rules, unfair baseline comparison.

Overall, this is a solid technical contribution with a clear presentation. Addressing the concerns regarding impact, baseline comparison, and constraint inference rules would significantly strengthen the paper.
