This paper introduces a novel approach for learning Bayesian network structures when subject to ancestral constraints, which are non-decomposable and thus challenging for many existing algorithms. The core contribution lies in projecting these non-decomposable constraints into decomposable edge and topological ordering constraints, enabling the use of efficient algorithms that rely on decomposable scores. The paper is generally well-written and clearly presents both the theoretical framework and empirical evaluation. It builds upon recent work by Chen et al. (2015, 2016) on optimal structure learning with non-decomposable scores and demonstrates significant performance improvements over Integer Linear Programming (ILP)-based methods. The work addresses an important problem in Bayesian network learning, as incorporating background knowledge can improve efficiency and accuracy.

The authors leverage the EC tree search space, combined with an oracle for learning structures with decomposable scores, and demonstrate how to effectively prune the search space based on the projected ancestral constraints. The method for inferring decomposable constraints is presented with sound theoretical justification, including theorems regarding the soundness of the pruning step and the conditions for inferring both edge and ordering constraints.  The experimental setup is well-defined, using benchmark datasets and varying parameters such as network size, dataset size, and the proportion of ancestral constraints.  The results presented in Tables 1 and 2 consistently show orders-of-magnitude speedups compared to ILP, particularly when utilizing the projected constraints.

However, a crucial point requiring further clarification is the generalization of the benefits of constraint projection. While Table 1 shows significant speedups for both the EC tree and ILP when using projected constraints, Table 3 doesn't demonstrate a similar advantage for ILP as the number of variables increases. This raises the question of whether the projection is primarily beneficial for approaches like the EC tree that are designed around oracle-based search, or whether it can broadly improve the performance of other methods designed to handle non-decomposable constraints. The paper would be strengthened by providing more evidence of the positive impact of projected constraints on methods that cannot inherently handle ancestral constraints, thereby validating the broader applicability of the proposed technique.

Furthermore, the observed "easy-hard-easy" pattern in Table 2, as the proportion of ancestral constraints increases, is intriguing and requires a more detailed explanation. The authors mention this trend, but a deeper understanding of why the EC tree appears sensitive to the amount of domain knowledge compared to ILP would be valuable.  Is this related to the efficiency of the pruning strategy, the specific characteristics of the datasets used, or a limitation of the EC tree representation itself?  The implication is that the optimal performance of the EC tree may require careful tuning of the number of ancestral constraints, and this sensitivity should be discussed in more detail.

The evaluation of model accuracy is also somewhat limited. Table 2 currently reports the Structural Hamming Distance (SHD) only for the EC tree approach.  Including a comparison of the SHD between the EC tree and the ILP approach would provide a more complete picture of the trade-offs between efficiency and accuracy.  Specifically, it would be useful to know if the speed gains achieved by the EC tree come at the cost of slightly higher structural error, and under what conditions.

Finally, while Section 4.1 introducing the BN graph provides helpful context, it feels somewhat tangential to the core contribution of the paper, which focuses on the EC tree and constraint projection. Given space limitations, streamlining the paper by removing Section 4.1 and expanding the explanations of the EC tree structure and the application of the A* algorithm within the EC-tree experimental setting would enhance clarity and focus. Expanding on the discussion of the heuristic function used within A* would also be beneficial.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound approach to handling ancestral constraints in Bayesian network learning.
*   Significant empirical evidence of performance improvements over state-of-the-art ILP methods.
*   Clear and well-written presentation of the methodology and experimental results.
*   Addresses an important problem with practical relevance in many real-world applications.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited evidence of the generalization of the constraint projection technique to other learning approaches beyond the EC tree.
*   Unclear explanation of the “easy-hard-easy” pattern observed in Table 2.
*   Incomplete accuracy evaluation (SHD reported only for EC trees).
*   Section 4.1 (BN graphs) feels somewhat extraneous and could be trimmed.



Overall, this is a strong paper with a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian network learning. Addressing the concerns outlined above, particularly regarding the generalizability of the constraint projection and providing a more comprehensive accuracy comparison, would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact. Despite these minor concerns, I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.
