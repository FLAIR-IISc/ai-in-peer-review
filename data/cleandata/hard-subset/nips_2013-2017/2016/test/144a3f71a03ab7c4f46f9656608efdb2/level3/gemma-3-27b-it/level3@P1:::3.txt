This paper addresses the problem of learning Bayesian network structures optimally, given background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints. The authors build upon a recent framework for optimal structure learning based on non-decomposable scores, which allows incorporating such constraints – something traditional decomposable score-based methods struggle with. Their core idea is to translate the non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable ordering constraints, enabling the use of efficient oracles within the EC-tree search space. The paper demonstrates significant performance improvements compared to an Integer Linear Programming (ILP) based approach (GOBNILP) on simulated datasets.

The work is well-written and clearly explains a relatively complex topic. The conceptual approach of bridging the gap between non-decomposable constraints and decomposable score-based learning is straightforward and logically presented. The novelty is strong; this represents a clear advance in the ability to incorporate complex prior knowledge into Bayesian network structure learning (rating: 3/3). The paper is also thorough in its presentation of related work, referencing key contributions in the field like Darwiche [2009], Koller & Friedman [2009], and Murphy [2012].

However, the significance of the work is somewhat limited (rating: 2/3). While technically sound, the primary application scenario – having precise ancestral constraints known *a priori* – is less common in many practical settings. Often, ancestral constraints arise as hypotheses during causal discovery, and more flexible approaches allowing for unobserved confounders would be more relevant. The impact is therefore somewhat restricted to specialist applications where such constraints are reliably available. 

A key contribution is the method for projecting ancestral constraints into decomposable ordering constraints, utilizing a MaxSAT solver. This is an elegant solution to enable leveraging existing efficient learning algorithms. However, the rules for inferring these constraints (lines 257-261) raise some concerns. Rule 3, concerning acyclicity, appears asymmetric, and rule 4 potentially excludes reasonable constraints (e.g., mutual independence). Further clarification and justification of these rules would strengthen the paper.

In practice, the number of available ancestral constraints is often limited. The paper acknowledges this (line 310), but it’s a crucial point. A small number of constraints might not significantly reduce the search space, diminishing the benefits of the proposed approach. The comparison to GOBNILP, while favorable, may not be entirely fair. GOBNILP wasn’t specifically designed for this constrained learning problem, so the observed performance difference might partially reflect a mismatch in optimization strategies. A more compelling comparison would involve evaluating against greedy algorithms, such as GES, adapted to incorporate ancestral constraints as hard constraints—this would provide a better gauge of the efficiency gains in a more comparable setting.

There are also minor issues with clarity and precision. The use of "oracle" vs. "omniscient oracle" (line 30) could be clarified. The term “empower the oracle” (line 38) feels somewhat imprecise. Line 90 would benefit from explicitly stating that "optimizing" refers to *minimizing* the score. Theorem 1’s statement requires refinement; a CPDAG P satisfying constraints A only guarantees that *at least one* DAG in class(P) satisfies A, not that *all* do, making the ‘iff’ incorrect. Similarly, Theorem 3’s utility may be limited by sparse ancestral constraints, potentially yielding few positive edge implications. The labeling of the acyclicity constraint encoding (line 267) could be made more descriptive. Finally, minor wording suggestions: lines 310 suggests 10-25% constraints is "extensive", while it seems like these are relatively low constraint levels for this type of learning.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and technically sound approach to incorporating ancestral constraints.
*   Significant performance improvements over an ILP-based baseline.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Addresses a relevant problem in Bayesian network structure learning.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited practical applicability due to the need for precise prior knowledge.
*   Potential issues with the inference rules for decomposable constraints.
*   Comparison to a potentially mismatched baseline.
*   Minor clarity and precision issues.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some limitations, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. The proposed approach is novel, technically sound, and demonstrably effective. While the practical relevance may be somewhat limited, the work provides a solid foundation for future research in incorporating complex prior knowledge into Bayesian network models. I recommend **acceptance** contingent on addressing the concerns raised regarding the inference rules and clarification of the theorems. A stronger comparison against more relevant baselines would also be beneficial.
