

This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian networks under ancestral constraints, which are non-decomposable and thus challenging for existing structure learning methods that rely on decomposable scores. The authors propose a novel approach that translates ancestral constraints into ordering constraints using a MaxSAT solver, then employs these in an EC-tree score-based algorithm. The method outperforms a modified Gobnilp implementation by orders of magnitude in empirical evaluations.

The paper is well-written, clearly explaining the technical contributions and demonstrating a conceptually straightforward approach to a non-trivial problem. The novelty is evident, particularly in the constraint translation mechanism, and the work would interest specialists in Bayesian network structure learning. The theoretical foundation is sound, with a clear presentation of the EC tree search space and its adaptation to handle ancestral constraints.

However, the significance is somewhat limited. Ancestral constraints are typically used in causal discovery settings where unobserved confounders are common, but the paper's approach doesn't address this broader context, making the setting somewhat artificial. The comparison to Gobnilp is questionable since Gobnilp wasn't designed for this specific problem, potentially inflating the performance advantage. A more appropriate baseline would be greedy algorithms like GES that incorporate ancestral constraints.

Several theoretical issues require clarification: Theorem 1's "iff" statement is incorrect as the set of constraints satisfied by a CPDAG is a subset of those satisfied by the corresponding DAG. Theorem 3 is unlikely to yield many positive edge implications when constraints are sparse, as noted in the paper. The MaxSAT encoding (line 267) is misleadingly labeled "acyclic," and specific rules (lines 257-261) contain logical inconsistencies that need addressing.

While the empirical results are impressive, the practical relevance is questionable as the paper suggests that only 10-25% of ancestral constraints are typically available, which may not substantially reduce the search space. The minor wording issues (e.g., line 30, 38, 90, 310) further detract from the clarity.

Overall, this is a technically sound paper with a clear contribution to a specific subproblem, but the broader impact is limited by the artificial setting and questionable comparison baseline.
