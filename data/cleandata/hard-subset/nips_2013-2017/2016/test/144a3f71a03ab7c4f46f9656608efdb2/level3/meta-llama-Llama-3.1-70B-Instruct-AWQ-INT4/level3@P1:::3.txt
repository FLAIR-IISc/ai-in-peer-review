This paper proposes a novel approach to learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints, which are non-decomposable and pose a challenge to traditional score-based methods. The authors leverage a recently proposed framework for optimal structure learning based on non-decomposable scores, which can accommodate ancestral constraints. They show how to empower an oracle that optimizes decomposable scores by passing it decomposable constraints inferred from the non-decomposable ancestral constraints. The proposed approach is evaluated against a modified version of GOBNILP, an integer linear programming-based system, and demonstrates orders-of-magnitude improvements in efficiency.

The paper is well-written and clearly explained, making it accessible to specialists in the field. The concept is straightforward, and the novelty of the approach is significant, as it addresses a challenging problem in Bayesian network learning. However, the impact of the paper is limited by the somewhat artificial setting, as ancestral constraints often motivate broader causal discovery methods that allow for unobserved confounders.

One of the key contributions of the paper is the mapping from ancestral to ordering constraints using a MaxSAT solver. This allows for the explicit representation of implied ancestral constraints, which can be used to improve the efficiency of structure learning. However, there are some concerns about specific rules used in the paper, such as the acyclicity rule, which has a symmetric premise but an asymmetric conclusion.

In practice, it is likely that only a few ancestral constraints will be available, which may not substantially reduce the search space. Therefore, it is essential to evaluate the proposed approach against other baselines, such as greedy algorithms like GES, assuming ancestral constraints align with the optimal model.

The paper provides a clear and concise explanation of the proposed approach, and the experiments demonstrate its efficiency and scalability. However, there are some minor wording and clarity issues that need to be addressed. For example, the term "oracle" is sometimes used interchangeably with "omniscient oracle," and the phrase "empower the oracle" could be rephrased for better clarity.

Overall, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian network learning and provides a novel approach to addressing the challenge of ancestral constraints. However, it is essential to consider the limitations of the approach and evaluate it against other baselines to fully assess its impact.
