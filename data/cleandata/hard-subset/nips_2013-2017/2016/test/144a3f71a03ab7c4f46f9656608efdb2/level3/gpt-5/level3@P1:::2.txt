This paper tackles optimal Bayesian network (BN) structure learning under non-decomposable ancestral constraints (X is/is not an ancestor of Y). The authors build on the BN-graph/EC-tree shortest-path perspective (Chen et al., 2015; 2016) to handle non-decomposable constraints by pruning CPDAG nodes in an EC tree and by leveraging an admissible heuristic derived from an “oracle” that solves the decomposable-score problem. The key idea is to “project” non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and topological ordering constraints that the oracle can exploit, thereby tightening the heuristic and reducing search. They give sound pruning rules for CPDAGs, algorithms to infer entailed edge constraints (using ancestor/descendant closures α, β and a negative-edge test; a positive-edge condition that rules out two-step detours), and a MaxSAT formulation to infer a maximal, cycle-free set of ordering constraints entailed by ancestral constraints. Empirically, the method reportedly outperforms ILP (GOBNILP) by orders of magnitude across standard BN benchmarks and shows favorable scalability trends with increasing proportions of ancestral constraints.

Relation to prior work: Most exact score-based methods rely on decomposability (dynamic programming on the order graph, A* on subset lattices, and order-modular constraints), which cannot natively encode ancestral constraints. ILP/CP can encode them but have mixed scalability. Chen et al. (2015; 2016) introduced BN-graph/EC-tree search with oracles for non-decomposable priors (e.g., order-modular), and the present paper extends that framework to non-decomposable constraints, providing theory for pruning and projection plus an empirical comparison, mainly against ILP. The work complements soft path-belief priors (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos, 2012; 2013) and earlier ILP/MaxSAT encodings (Cussens, 2008), by giving a complete search framework with admissible heuristics for the constrained problem.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: clean separation between (i) a non-decomposable search space (EC tree), (ii) admissible heuristics via decomposable-score oracles, and (iii) empowering the oracle with projected, decomposable constraints.
- Soundness of pruning: CPDAG pruning conditions are logically justified and Theorem 1 ensures pruning does not discard any descendant solution.
- Useful projection machinery: Theorems for entailed negative and positive edges are intuitive and efficient to check once α/β closures are computed; MaxSAT encoding for orderings is a practical way to maximize safe ordering information.
- Practical impact: Empirical results suggest large speedups over ILP; constraint projection substantially tightens the heuristic and can change search complexity by orders of magnitude.
- Relevance: Incorporating background causal knowledge is important and can substitute for large amounts of data; experiments illustrate improved accuracy with modest constraint fractions.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Experimental clarity: The presentation of Tables 1–3 is confusing. It appears Table 1 compares “EC tree + projection” vs “GOBNILP + (redundant) projected constraints,” while Table 2 compares both without projection, and Table 3 studies scaling with constraint proportion. Please make this explicit and unify labels. In particular, an ablation directly comparing “EC tree with projection” versus “EC tree without projection” is essential to quantify the contribution of the proposed projection; this is currently missing or hard to extract.
- Baselines: Since CP can also encode ancestral constraints (van Beek & Hoffmann, 2015), a CP baseline, or at least discussion, would strengthen the case. Likewise, a BN-graph baseline (without EC merging) would isolate gains due to EC-tree canonization.
- Cost of CPDAG enumeration: When pruning tests are inconclusive, you enumerate class(P). This can be exponential. Please quantify how often enumeration is needed, its depth, and wall-clock overhead; consider using stronger polynomial tests on partially directed reachability or Meek-rule closures to reduce enumeration.
- MaxSAT entailment: Theorem 4 gives sufficient conditions for orderings to be entailed; the text occasionally suggests finding a “maximally large” set of entailed orderings. Please reconcile: are you maximizing a sufficient—but possibly conservative—set, or computing true logical entailments? Report MaxSAT run times and the fraction of constraints accepted.
- Heuristic properties: The oracle-induced heuristic is admissible; is it also consistent in the EC-tree? If not, please comment on A*’s reopen behavior and its empirical impact.
- Reporting: Provide timeouts/failed runs per setting, memory usage, and dispersion (e.g., medians/IQR). Several references and footnotes appear malformed, and there are stray artifacts in the text; please clean up.

Quality
- Theory appears sound where proofs are sketched; pruning and edge-projection results are well-motivated. Some formal details (completeness of inferred edge sets, exactness of MaxSAT entailment) would benefit from fuller proofs or explicit caveats.
- Empirical methodology is standard; more thorough ablations and clearer tables are needed.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-organized but marred by table confusion and some formatting/reference issues. A figure showing the effect of projection on oracle lower bounds would help.

Originality
- Extends the non-decomposable EC-tree framework to ancestral constraints with novel projection rules and a MaxSAT-based ordering inference. This combination is, to my knowledge, new.

Significance
- Practically significant: strong speedups; shows how to integrate causal background knowledge in exact BN learning. Likely to be adopted by researchers using A*/DP oracles.

Requested additions before final acceptance
- A clear ablation: EC tree + oracle with vs. without constraint projection (same oracle, same datasets), ideally also splitting edge-only vs. ordering-only projections.
- Clarify Tables 1–3 and explicitly state which configurations each compares.
- Report cost and benefit of the MaxSAT step and of CPDAG enumeration.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important limitation (non-decomposable constraints) with a principled, effective method.
- Strong empirical performance and practical value.
- Solid theoretical underpinnings and integration with established exact BN learners.

Cons for acceptance
- Experimental presentation is confusing; missing key ablations.
- Some algorithmic costs (enumeration, MaxSAT) unquantified.
- Limited baseline coverage beyond ILP.

Overall: A strong and timely contribution; I lean toward acceptance provided the authors add the EC ablation and clarify the experimental sections.
