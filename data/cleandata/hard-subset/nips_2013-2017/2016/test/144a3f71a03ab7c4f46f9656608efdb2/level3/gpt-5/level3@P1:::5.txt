This paper addresses optimal Bayesian network structure learning with background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints, which are non-decomposable and therefore not directly supported by mainstream exact score-based methods that rely on decomposable scores. Building on the BN-graph and EC-tree search spaces of Chen et al. (2015, 2016), the authors show how to enforce ancestral constraints by pruning nodes in the EC tree and, crucially, how to “project” these non-decomposable constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints that can be consumed by a decomposable-score oracle (URLEARNING) to provide strong, admissible heuristics within A* search. The projection onto ordering constraints is formulated as a MaxSAT problem; edge constraints are derived via explicit inference rules leveraging closure under ancestral entailment. Empirically, the approach yields orders-of-magnitude speedups over ILP (GOBNILP), with stronger gains as the number of variables grows and when projected constraints are supplied.

Relation to prior work: The paper situates itself against dynamic programming and A* in the order graph (Koivisto and Sood; Silander and Myllymäki; Yuan and Malone), ILP/CP formulations (Jaakkola et al.; Cussens; van Beek and Hoffmann), and causal prior/path-constraint methods in constraint-based or local search settings (Borboudakis and Tsamardinos). Unlike order-graph methods that cannot enforce non-decomposable constraints, the EC tree can prune CPDAG nodes by checking ancestral feasibility. Compared to ILP/CP, the novelty lies in delegating global non-decomposable reasoning to pruning plus a projected set of decomposable constraints that significantly tightens the oracle heuristic while preserving optimality.

Technical contributions:
- EC-tree pruning under ancestral constraints: a CPDAG node is pruned if no DAG in its class satisfies the constraints. The paper gives efficient necessary checks (presence of a directed path violating a negative constraint; absence of any partially directed path for a required positive constraint) and falls back to enumerating the equivalence class when needed. Correctness is justified via Theorem 1.
- Projection to decomposable constraints:
  - Edge constraints: Theorem 2 provides a necessary and sufficient test for entailed negative edges X ↛ Y using ancestor/descendant closures α(X), β(Y). Theorem 3 gives a condition for entailed positive edges X → Y based on forbidding any X–Z–Y mediating paths consistent with A. Ancestral closure rules (transitivity and cycle/conflict pruning) are applied first.
  - Ordering constraints: A MaxSAT encoding maximizes a consistent set O of X < Y implied by negative ancestral relations while enforcing transitivity and acyclicity (hard clauses), with soft clauses to include Y < X for each X 6 Y. Theorem 4 gives sufficient conditions for O to be entailed by A.
- Empirical evaluation: Across ALARM/ANDES/CHILD/CPCS/HEPAR2-derived subgraphs (n up to 20) and BDeu scoring, the EC-tree approach with projected constraints is often orders of magnitude faster than ILP, with an easy–hard–easy pattern as the proportion of constraints increases. Projected constraints substantially tighten the search for EC trees; they help ILP modestly for larger n. Accuracy (SHD) improves with more data and more constraints.

Quality: The approach is technically sound and exact, with clear correctness arguments for pruning and edge-projection, and a reasonable MaxSAT formulation for order projection. Some details are deferred (“details omitted”), and complexity bounds for (i) CPDAG-class enumeration when pruning is inconclusive, (ii) the edge-projection algorithm, and (iii) MaxSAT instances are not analyzed. The experimental setup is careful (shared family scores, same projected constraints passed to both methods), but additional baselines and ablations would strengthen the case.

Clarity: The paper is generally well written and well organized. The separation between non-decomposable pruning and decomposable projection is clear, as is the role of the oracle in A*. However, the projection algorithms would benefit from explicit pseudo-code, complexity analysis, and small running examples illustrating Theorems 2–3. The conditions in Theorem 4 are sufficient but not necessary; making the “maximality” goal precise (and how close the MaxSAT solution gets to it) would help. A brief discussion on how often CPDAG-class enumeration triggers in practice would clarify scalability.

Originality: Integrating ancestral constraints into exact score-based learning via EC-tree pruning and empowering a decomposable oracle using projected constraints is a novel and elegant combination. The reduction of ordering inference to MaxSAT in this context is also new to my knowledge. While ILP/CP can encode non-decomposable constraints, the proposed decomposition/projection strategy is distinct and practically impactful.

Significance: The ability to leverage non-decomposable causal knowledge in exact structure learning is important and timely. The reported speedups over ILP, together with improved accuracy from modest amounts of constraints, suggest this will be useful for practitioners with prior knowledge. The methods (especially projection) could generalize to other non-decomposable priors/constraints.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Scalability: Results top out at n=20 with BDeu. How does the method fare at n≈25–30, and with parent-set size caps? What is the empirical frequency and cost of CPDAG-class enumeration?
- MaxSAT projection: Provide sizes of instances vs n and constraint density; solver sensitivity; whether hard/soft clause design biases toward too few/too many order constraints. Is the resulting O guaranteed maximal under entailment, or just large?
- Baselines: Consider CP-based methods and ILP variants with different cutting planes/cluster constraints. Also compare to order-graph A* with hand-crafted ordering constraints to isolate the gain from projection.
- Ablations: Edge-only vs order-only projection; effect of projection on heuristic accuracy (e.g., node expansions).
- Robustness: Handle inconsistent or noisy constraints (soft ancestral constraints), or partial constraints with uncertainty. Could the MaxSAT machinery already support soft ancestral constraints?
- Reproducibility: Some inference-rule details are omitted. Include full rules, proofs (or appendix), and code/data.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, principled integration of non-decomposable knowledge into exact score-based learning.
- Strong theoretical underpinning; exactness preserved.
- Substantial empirical speedups over a strong ILP baseline.
- Practical technique (projection) likely useful beyond this setting.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited scalability evaluation and missing complexity analysis for key subroutines.
- Dependence on CPDAG-class enumeration in edge cases not well quantified.
- MaxSAT projection’s optimality/size guarantees not fully characterized.
- Experimental scope could be broadened (more baselines, ablations).

Overall: A strong, well-motivated, and practically impactful contribution that extends exact BN structure learning to a valuable class of non-decomposable constraints with convincing empirical benefits. I lean toward acceptance, pending clarifications on scalability and projection details.

Reviewer confidence: moderate (familiar with BN structure learning and ILP/A* literature, less so with the specific EC-tree internals).
