Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles optimal Bayesian network (BN) structure learning in the presence of non-decomposable background knowledge, focusing on ancestral constraints (X is/is not an ancestor of Y). Mainstream exact score-based methods exploit decomposable scores and typically support only decomposable constraints (edge presence/absence, indegree bounds, and order-modular constraints), hence cannot directly encode ancestral constraints. The authors build on the non-decomposable framework of Chen et al. (2015, 2016): they search over DAGs/equivalence classes (BN graphs/EC trees) and use an “oracle” solver for decomposable scores to guide search. Their key idea is to prune the EC tree via tests that are sound with respect to ancestral constraints, and to “project” non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable ones that the oracle can exploit. Projection includes:
- Edge constraints: sound rules (Theorems 2–3) to infer X→Y or X↛Y from a maximally closed set of ancestral constraints.
- Ordering constraints: cast as a MaxSAT problem to select the largest safe set of X<Y constraints entailed by ancestral constraints while preserving all DAGs that satisfy them (hard constraints enforce transitivity and acyclicity; soft constraints prefer orderings implied by negative ancestry).

The approach is compared empirically to an ILP baseline (GOBNILP with linear encodings of ancestral constraints). On standard BN benchmarks (ALARM, ANDES, CHILD, CPCS54, HEPAR2) with synthetic subgraphs and data, the method achieves orders-of-magnitude speedups, especially as n grows, and further gains from projection. The authors also observe an easy–hard–easy trend as the proportion of constraints increases. Related work includes dynamic programming/order-graph methods (Koivisto & Sood; Silander & Myllymäki; Yuan & Malone), ILP/CP approaches (Jaakkola et al.; Bartlett & Cussens; van Beek & Hoffmann), path constraints in constraint-based learning (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos), and MaxSAT encodings (Cussens).

Strengths
- Clear problem motivation: ancestral constraints capture causal/temporal prior knowledge that practitioners care about.
- Elegant integration of non-decomposable constraints into a search space (EC tree) that supports pruning and leverages strong decomposable-score oracles.
- Technically neat projection: simple, sound conditions for edge constraints; a principled MaxSAT formulation to harvest as many safe ordering constraints as possible.
- Strong empirical findings: large runtime improvements over ILP, amplified by projected constraints; informative analysis of constraint density (easy–hard–easy) and structural accuracy (SHD) vs. data size and constraint proportion.
- Good connections to prior frameworks (BN/EC graphs, order-graph DP/A*), and a fair baseline (GOBNILP).

Weaknesses and questions
- Completeness vs. scalability of pruning: when simple path tests on CPDAGs are inconclusive, the method resorts to enumerating class(P), which can be exponential. How frequently does enumeration occur, and how is it mitigated (caching, bounds, or SAT-based feasibility checks)?
- Ordering projection via MaxSAT: the constraints are sufficient, not necessary. How much entailment is left on the table in practice? Ablations quantifying gains from ordering vs. edge projections would help.
- Experimental scope: n up to 20 is reasonable but modest for modern exact solvers; more discussion on memory/runtime limits of the oracle and MaxSAT as n increases would be useful.
- Baseline fairness: ILP is known to struggle without indegree bounds; the authors acknowledge this but the comparison could be complemented with bounded-indegree settings and/or CP baselines (e.g., van Beek & Hoffmann).
- Robustness: all constraints are consistent with ground truth. What happens with misspecified/noisy constraints (common in practice)? Can the framework accommodate soft/penalized violations?
- Generality: results shown only for BDeu and discrete data. Any obstacles to other decomposable scores, mixed/continuous variables, or missing data?
- Theoretical clarity: positive edge entailment (Theorem 3) requires checking all Z; clarify complexity and whether it is complete. For ordering MaxSAT, a brief proof sketch of Theorem 4’s sufficiency would improve confidence.

Quality
- Technical claims are plausible and mostly well supported. The pruning tests and projection theorems are clearly stated; empirical evaluation is careful and demonstrates the main claims. Some omitted details (frequency of CPDAG enumeration; MaxSAT overhead) should be added.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well organized. The EC-tree pruning rules and projection sections are easy to follow. It would help to explicitly outline the overall algorithmic loop, quantify MaxSAT instance sizes, and provide pseudocode for edge projection (details currently omitted).

Originality
- Novel and useful combination: integrating ancestral constraints into the Chen et al. non-decomposable framework, plus the MaxSAT-based ordering projection. Prior ILP/CP can encode such constraints, but systematic evaluation and the oracle-empowerment angle are new.

Significance
- Practical impact is high: many users have causal/temporal priors of the ancestral form. The reported speedups over ILP and the demonstrated accuracy gains from adding constraints suggest the method will be adopted and extended.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, practical limitation of exact BN learning.
- Conceptually clean technique with theoretical backing.
- Strong empirical speedups; clear benefit from projection.
- Connects and advances a promising line of work (EC trees, oracle-guided search).

Cons for acceptance
- Limited scalability evidence beyond n≈20 and lack of CP baselines.
- Handling of inconsistent/noisy constraints not explored.
- Some technical details deferred; risk of exponential CPDAG enumeration not fully quantified.

Suggestions for improvement
- Report frequencies/times for CPDAG enumeration vs. quick tests; consider SAT-based feasibility checks to avoid enumeration.
- Provide ablations for edge vs. ordering projections, and MaxSAT overheads.
- Add experiments with bounded indegree and a CP baseline; include a robustness study with a fraction of incorrect constraints.
- Release code and benchmark scripts; include more details on MaxSAT instance sizes and solver settings.

Recommendation
- Leaning toward accept. The problem is well motivated, the approach is technically sound and original, and the empirical runtime gains are compelling.

Reviewer confidence
- Moderate: I am not deeply versed in this exact approach, but the presentation is convincing and the comparisons are impressive.
