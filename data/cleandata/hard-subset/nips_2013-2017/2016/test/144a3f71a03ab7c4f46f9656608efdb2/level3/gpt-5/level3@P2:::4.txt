Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses optimal Bayesian network (BN) structure learning when background knowledge is provided as ancestral constraints (statements of the form X is/is not an ancestor of Y). Because ancestral constraints are non-decomposable, mainstream exact score-based methods that rely on decomposable scores (dynamic programming over order graphs, A*, etc.) cannot directly incorporate them. The paper builds on the BN graph and EC tree search spaces of Chen et al. (2015; 2016), which support non-decomposable scores via shortest-path/A* search guided by an oracle that solves decomposable subproblems. The main contribution is a method to project non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints of two types: edge presence/absence and topological ordering constraints. Edge constraints are inferred using closure properties of ancestral constraints (Theorems 2–3), and ordering constraints are inferred via a MaxSAT formulation with hard clauses ensuring soundness (Theorem 4), maximizing the set of safe orderings. These projected constraints are then fed to the decomposable-score oracle (URLearning/A* over order graphs), “empowering” it to yield much tighter heuristics.

In addition, the paper extends pruning in the EC tree: nodes (CPDAGs) are pruned if no DAG in the equivalence class satisfies the ancestral constraints. When quick tests are inconclusive, the paper enumerates DAGs in the class. Experiments compare this approach to an ILP method (GOBNILP) that can encode ancestral constraints directly, showing orders-of-magnitude speedups in many settings. The study further explores the effect of the number of constraints (p), dataset size (N), and number of variables (n), and reports accuracy (structural Hamming distance, SHD) for the EC-tree-based method.

The work is related to causal/associative priors (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos), exact BN structure discovery via DP/A* (Koivisto & Sood; Silander & Myllymäki; Yuan & Malone), and ILP/CP formulations (Jaakkola et al.; Bartlett & Cussens; van Beek & Hoffmann). Its novelty is in the principled derivation and use of decomposable projections to make non-decomposable constraints usable by decomposable oracles.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The pruning criterion on EC trees is sound (Theorem 1). Edge projection rules are clearly stated (Theorems 2–3), and the ordering projection is formulated as a MaxSAT instance with sufficient conditions (Theorem 4) guaranteeing safety. One limitation is that the ordering projection is sufficient but not characterized as complete; nonetheless the MaxSAT objective seeks a maximal safe set within those constraints.
- Empirical support: The comparison to ILP is appropriate and suggests large speedups for the proposed method, especially with projected constraints. The accuracy trends (SHD) are sensible as N and p increase. However, some key ablations and fairness checks are missing (see weaknesses).
- Completeness: The approach is end-to-end and includes implementation details (BDeu, URLearning, EVASOLVER), but a few parts need more detail (frequency/cost of EC-tree class enumeration, time spent in MaxSAT vs search).

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and easy to follow. The examples for non-decomposability and theorems are helpful. There are a few presentation issues: some references appear with placeholder tags and there are stray characters in the text; and Section 4.1 on BN graphs seems less critical than a deeper exposition of EC trees and their A* integration in the experimental setting.

Originality
Projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints to empower a decomposable oracle appears novel and well-motivated. The MaxSAT-based derivation of a large safe set of ordering constraints is also original in this context.

Significance
Efficiently incorporating domain knowledge of causal ancestry into exact BN learning is important. The reported speedups over ILP, combined with improved accuracy with modest p, suggest practical value. If the projection methodology can benefit other decomposable-score learners (beyond the particular oracle used), the impact would be broader.

Strengths
- Clear, principled framework that unifies non-decomposable constraints with decomposable-score oracles via projection.
- Sound EC-tree pruning under ancestral constraints; practical A* search instantiation.
- Theorems and algorithms for inferring edge and ordering constraints; MaxSAT formulation is compelling.
- Strong empirical performance vs ILP and informative analysis of how p and N affect runtime and accuracy.

Weaknesses and requests for clarification
- Generality of projection benefits: The evidence that projected constraints help ILP is limited/mixed (mild overhead for small n, some gains for larger n). Please:
  - Report ablations showing the impact of edge-only, ordering-only, and both projections on ILP and on at least one additional decomposable oracle, to demonstrate generality.
  - Clarify whether projection time is included in all reported runtimes for both EC-tree and ILP.
- Easy–hard–easy pattern: Table 2 (and later results) show an easy–hard–easy trend as p varies for EC trees, whereas ILP appears to monotonically improve. Please provide a deeper explanation (e.g., heuristic accuracy vs feasible-space size; conflicts between projected orderings; branching factor in EC tree), and discuss sensitivity to p in practice. If performance is highly sensitive, guidance on “safe” ranges of p or adaptive strategies would be useful.
- Accuracy reporting: SHD is reported only for EC trees. Please add SHD for ILP on the same datasets to rule out accuracy/runtime trade-offs and ensure both methods return comparable optima.
- Cost of CPDAG-class enumeration: When pruning tests are inconclusive, you enumerate class(P). Please quantify how often this occurs, the average class size encountered, and the runtime share of enumeration vs A* search. Any caching or early stopping?
- MaxSAT details: Provide the full encoding, solver parameters, and time breakdown (solver time vs total), and report how often inferred orderings contribute to oracle pruning vs edges. Are the hard clauses in Theorem 4 the only ones used, or are there additional constraints to tighten the formulation?
- Robustness to incorrect/background knowledge noise: Experiments sample only correct constraints from the ground-truth network. Please report sensitivity to a small fraction of incorrect ancestral constraints, or discuss how the framework could accommodate soft/uncertain constraints.
- Presentation: Consider removing or shortening Section 4.1 (BN graph) to make room for a more pedagogical explanation of EC trees, CPDAG properties, and how A* and the oracle interact in practice (including heuristic computation and tie-breaking). Also fix reference placeholders and typographical artifacts.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Novel and well-justified projection of non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones.
- Strong theoretical framing and sound pruning within EC trees.
- Substantial empirical speedups and sensible accuracy gains with modest domain knowledge.
- Topic is timely and relevant to NIPS (exact/optimal structure learning, integration of prior knowledge).

Cons:
- Limited evidence that projection broadly benefits other learning paradigms (e.g., ILP, other oracles).
- Missing SHD comparison for ILP and ablations (edge vs ordering projections).
- Incomplete analysis of easy–hard–easy behavior and sensitivity to p.
- Some implementation and presentation details need clarification/polish.

Overall evaluation
This is a solid and potentially impactful contribution. With additional experiments clarifying the generality of the projection benefits, accuracy comparisons with ILP, and an explanation of the easy–hard–easy trend, the paper would be strengthened further. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on addressing the empirical and clarity issues above.
