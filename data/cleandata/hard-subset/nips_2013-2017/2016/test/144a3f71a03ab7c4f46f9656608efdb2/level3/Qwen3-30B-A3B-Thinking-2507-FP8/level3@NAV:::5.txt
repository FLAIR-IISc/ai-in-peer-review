

This paper addresses the important problem of optimal Bayesian network structure learning with ancestral constraints, which are non-decomposable and thus challenging for most existing approaches. The authors build upon the Chen et al. [2015, 2016] framework for non-decomposable score learning by introducing a method to transform ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints that can be handled by existing oracles. Their key technical contribution is a MaxSAT formulation for inferring decomposable constraints from non-decomposable ancestral constraints, enabling significant runtime improvements.

The paper demonstrates excellent technical quality with sound theoretical foundations. The authors clearly explain how ancestral constraints can be transformed into edge and ordering constraints that can be passed to oracles for decomposable scores, providing formal proofs for their constraint inference methods. The empirical evaluation is thorough, systematically varying problem size, dataset size, and constraint density across multiple benchmark networks. The results consistently show orders-of-magnitude speedups over ILP-based approaches, with the most dramatic improvements occurring with larger networks and higher constraint density.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making complex concepts accessible. The authors effectively relate their work to prior structure learning approaches, particularly distinguishing between decomposable and non-decomposable constraints. The connection to Chen et al.'s framework is well-motivated, and the paper adequately references relevant work on constraint-based learning and ILP approaches.

The contribution represents a meaningful advance in the field, as ancestral constraints are common in practical applications but have been difficult to incorporate into optimal structure learning. The runtime improvements make the approach practically viable for larger networks where ILP-based methods become prohibitive. The paper demonstrates that background knowledge through ancestral constraints can significantly improve both efficiency and accuracy, with even small percentages of constraints (10%-25%) providing benefits comparable to substantially larger datasets.

The paper excels in significance, addressing a practical problem with demonstrable impact. While the core framework builds on Chen et al., the specific application to ancestral constraints and the constraint projection method represent a novel and valuable contribution to the field of structure learning. The empirical results convincingly support the authors' claims, and the paper provides a clear path for practitioners to implement their approach. This is a strong, high-quality contribution that advances the state of the art in Bayesian network structure learning.
