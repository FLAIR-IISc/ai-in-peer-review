## Review of "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"

This paper tackles the challenging problem of learning Bayesian networks (BNs) under ancestral constraints, a crucial aspect for incorporating domain knowledge into structure learning. The authors build upon their prior work utilizing a framework based on navigating the search space of DAGs with a branch-and-bound A* search over CPDAGs, symmetry breaking, and a heuristic derived from URLearning. The key contribution lies in efficiently handling the non-decomposable nature of ancestral constraints by projecting them into decomposable constraints usable by the underlying oracle (URLearning).

The approach is well-motivated, as most existing methods struggle with non-decomposable constraints. The proposed projection technique is a clever way to bridge this gap and leverage existing efficient algorithms. The experimental results, demonstrating orders-of-magnitude speedups compared to GOBNILP, are compelling. The authors also identify a key limitation of the comparison to Cussens (2008), acknowledging that the ILP formulation used is suboptimal. 

However, several aspects require clarification and further justification. Lemma 3, central to the pruning strategy, lacks a clear proof, hindering a complete understanding of its validity. The explanation of how "can infer" ordering constraints is used—specifically, that the authors *add* consistent (but not necessarily entailed) constraints—is unclear and potentially misleading. The MaxSAT approach for inferring ordering constraints is mentioned but lacks sufficient detail regarding its implementation and rationale for specific choices. 

Furthermore, the comparison to GOBNILP could be more nuanced. It’s unclear whether the observed performance gap stems specifically from ancestral constraints or from GOBNILP's inherent weaknesses with unbounded parent sets. The omission of van Beek and Hoffmann (2015), a competitive CP-based approach often outperforming GOBNILP, is a significant oversight. Incorporating this baseline would strengthen the empirical evaluation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant problem in BN structure learning – incorporating non-decomposable constraints.
*   Novel approach to project constraints, enabling the use of efficient existing algorithms.
*   Strong experimental results showing significant speedups compared to GOBNILP.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Proof of Lemma 3 is unclear and needs substantial improvement.
*   Explanation of constraint inference is insufficient and potentially misleading.
*   Incomplete experimental comparison (missing van Beek and Hoffmann, unclear GOBNILP performance attribution).
*   Lack of detail regarding the MaxSAT implementation.



**Overall:** The paper presents a promising approach with strong potential. However, addressing the identified weaknesses, particularly regarding the clarity of the theoretical justification and a more thorough experimental comparison, is crucial for a strong acceptance decision. I recommend acceptance contingent on major revisions addressing these points.
