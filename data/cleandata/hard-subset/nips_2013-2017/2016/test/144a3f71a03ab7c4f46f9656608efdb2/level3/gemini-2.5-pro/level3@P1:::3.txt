This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data when subject to background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints. Such constraints are non-decomposable, which poses a challenge for many state-of-the-art optimal structure learning algorithms that rely on decomposable scores (e.g., BDeu, MDL). The authors build upon the recent EC-tree search framework of Chen et al. (2015, 2016), which uses an A*-like search over equivalence classes of DAGs and relies on an "oracle" (an exact solver for decomposable scores) to compute an admissible heuristic. The core contribution of this work is a method to "project" the non-decomposable ancestral constraints onto decomposable edge and topological ordering constraints. These projected constraints can then be passed to the oracle, significantly improving its heuristic estimate and pruning the search space. The projection of ancestral constraints to ordering constraints is framed as a MaxSAT problem. The proposed method is empirically compared against an Integer Linear Programming (ILP) approach (GOBNILP), demonstrating orders-of-magnitude speedups on benchmark networks.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper presents a complete piece of work, with theoretical justification for its constraint projection methods and a thorough empirical evaluation. The overall approach is technically sound. However, there are a few technical points that require clarification:
*   **MaxSAT Formulation:** The constraints used in the MaxSAT encoding (Section 5.2, lines 257-261) raise some questions. Rule 3, the "sufficient condition for acyclicity," appears to have a symmetric premise `(X < Y) ∧ (Z < W)` but an asymmetric conclusion. Why is `(Y < Z)` not included in the disjunction? This seems to be an important case. Further, Rule 4, `(X ⇎ Y) ⇒ (Y < X)`, seems to imply that if two variables are known not to have an ancestral relationship in one direction, they must have a specific ordering. This might exclude valid DAGs where, for instance, `X` and `Y` are causally independent but part of a structure that allows topological orderings in both directions (e.g., `Z -> X`, `Z -> Y`).
*   **Theorems:** The statement of Theorem 1 could be more precise. A CPDAG P satisfies constraints A if *there exists* a DAG G in `class(P)` satisfying A. A descendant `P'` of P satisfies A if *there exists* a DAG `G'` in `class(P')` satisfying A. The `iff` statement seems to imply a stronger condition than what is proven or necessary for pruning. Similarly, Theorem 3, which infers positive edges, relies on showing that all intermediate paths are forbidden. In a practical setting with sparse ancestral constraints, the conditions for this theorem are unlikely to be met frequently, limiting its utility.
*   **Experimental Comparison:** The comparison to GOBNILP is valuable as it's another exact method. However, since GOBNILP was not specifically designed or optimized for this type of constraint, the dramatic performance difference may partially reflect a mismatch between the problem and the baseline's strengths. It would be beneficial to also compare against a strong greedy search baseline (e.g., GES) adapted to respect the ancestral constraints. This would help contextualize the efficiency gains of the exact search method.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow. The problem is clearly motivated, and the proposed solution is explained in a logical progression from the underlying search framework to the novel constraint projection techniques. The authors do a great job of making a complex topic accessible. A few minor points could further improve clarity:
*   The term "omniscient oracle" (line 30) is a bit strong; simply "oracle" as used later is sufficient. The phrase "empower the oracle" (line 38 and elsewhere) is evocative but could be replaced with more direct language like "improve the oracle's heuristic by providing additional constraints."
*   In Section 2, it would be helpful to explicitly state that "optimizing" the score means minimizing it in the context of the shortest-path formulation (line 90).

**Originality:** The core idea of projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable ones that can be exploited by an existing exact search algorithm is novel. While the components (EC-tree search, MaxSAT solvers, ILP for BNs) are not new, their combination to solve this specific problem is original and clever. This work represents a clear and non-trivial extension of the framework by Chen et al. (2015, 2016) to a new and important class of constraints.

**Significance:** The paper addresses the important problem of incorporating prior knowledge into structure learning. However, the practical impact may be somewhat limited by the problem setting. Ancestral constraints are often derived from causal assumptions. In many such real-world settings (e.g., biology, social sciences), the presence of unobserved confounders is a primary concern. The score-based BN learning framework used here assumes causal sufficiency (no latent confounders), which limits its applicability in those domains. Researchers with strong ancestral knowledge might prefer causal discovery methods that can account for latent variables. Furthermore, the experiments assume a relatively large number of known ancestral relations (e.g., 10-25%). In practice, prior knowledge is often much sparser, consisting of only a handful of high-confidence constraints. In such scenarios, the reduction in the search space and the corresponding speedup might be less pronounced.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  The paper presents a novel and technically interesting method for integrating non-decomposable ancestral constraints into an exact score-based learning framework.
2.  The proposed approach is shown to be significantly more efficient than a general-purpose ILP solver, demonstrating its practical value for the problem as formulated.
3.  The manuscript is extremely clear, well-structured, and provides a good overview of the relevant background, making the contribution easy to understand and appreciate.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  The significance is somewhat constrained by the assumption of no unobserved confounders, which may limit the applicability of the method in domains where ancestral knowledge is most common.
2.  The experimental evaluation could be strengthened by including a constrained greedy search baseline and by exploring performance with sparser sets of constraints, which may be more representative of real-world scenarios.
3.  Some technical details, particularly in the MaxSAT formulation and theorem statements, require clarification to fully assess their correctness and impact.

Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a novel and well-executed contribution to the field of exact Bayesian network structure learning. Despite some concerns about the practical significance of the problem setting, the technical novelty and clarity of the paper make it a solid contribution to the NIPS community.
