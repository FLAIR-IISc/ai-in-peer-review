Summary: The paper tackles optimal Bayesian network structure learning with background knowledge expressed as ancestral constraints. Building on BN graphs and EC trees (Chen et al. 2015, 2016), the authors search in CPDAG space with A* and an admissible heuristic induced by a decomposable-score oracle (e.g., URLearning). The key technical contribution is to leverage non-decomposable ancestral constraints in two ways: (i) sound pruning of the EC tree via constraint satisfaction tests, and (ii) projection of ancestral constraints onto decomposable edge and ordering constraints (the latter via a MaxSAT formulation) to tighten the oracle’s heuristic. Experiments compare against ILP/CP formulations (notably GOBNILP; Cussens 2008; Jaakkola et al. 2010). The setup relates to order-graph dynamic programming and A* search (Koivisto & Sood 2004; Silander & Myllymäki 2006; Yuan & Malone 2013), and to causal priors/path constraints in constraint-based approaches (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos 2012, 2013).

Quality: The theoretical pieces (pruning correctness over EC trees; necessary/sufficient entailment tests for edges; MaxSAT encoding for maximal consistent ordering constraints) are interesting and, if proofs are complete, technically sound. The idea of using inferred decomposable constraints to inform an admissible heuristic for exhaustive search is well-motivated. However, the empirical section is confusing. Table 1 appears to compare (EC + projection/new work) to (GOBNILP + its own projection/new work’), and Table 2 “withheld projections” is not clearly aligned with an EC-only baseline. The role of Table 3 is unclear (is it assessing scaling, constraint tightness, or accuracy trade-offs?), and the experimental narrative does not separate effects of pruning versus projection versus MaxSAT overhead. I strongly request an explicit ablation: (EC + new work) versus EC alone (same oracle) on identical instances. Even if GOB benefits more or less from the projected constraints, EC substantially outperforms GOB overall; hence the most relevant question is whether the new projection machinery significantly improves EC.

Clarity: Writing is generally good, but experimental descriptions need reorganization: define “new work” precisely; state which projected constraints each method receives; report node expansions, heuristic tightness, and MaxSAT time. Clarify proofs’ assumptions (e.g., closure to a “maximum” ancestral set) and complexity.

Originality: High. The projection of ancestral constraints to decomposable edge and ordering constraints (with MaxSAT) to empower an admissible heuristic is novel.

Significance: Promising; speedups over ILP and a principled way to exploit background knowledge. Practical impact would be strengthened by tests with noisy/misstated constraints and larger n.

Pros:
- Conceptually elegant integration of non-decomposable knowledge into admissible heuristic search.
- Sound pruning on EC trees; new entailment tests and MaxSAT reduction.
- Strong potential practical relevance.

Cons:
- Experimental comparisons are unclear; missing EC-vs-EC ablation.
- Limited scale and no robustness to noisy constraints.
- Some proof and complexity details deferred; reproducibility details (code/params) sparse.

Recommendation: Promising contribution; consider acceptance after clarifying and strengthening the experimental evaluation, especially the EC + new work vs EC baseline.
