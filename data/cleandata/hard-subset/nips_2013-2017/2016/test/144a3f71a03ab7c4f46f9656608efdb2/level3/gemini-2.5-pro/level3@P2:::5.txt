**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of learning the optimal structure of a Bayesian network from data, particularly when background knowledge is available in the form of ancestral constraints (e.g., variable X must be an ancestor of Y). Such constraints are powerful for incorporating expert knowledge but are non-decomposable, which prevents their use in many state-of-the-art learning algorithms that rely on decomposable scores for efficiency.

The authors build upon a recent framework for optimal structure learning with non-decomposable scores (Chen et al., 2015, 2016), which performs an A* search over a space of equivalence classes of DAGs (the "EC tree"). This search is guided by a heuristic function, or "oracle," that can efficiently find optimal structures for decomposable scores. The core contribution of this paper is a method to "empower" this oracle by inferring decomposable constraints (which the oracle can handle) from the non-decomposable ancestral constraints (which it cannot). Specifically, the authors show how to project ancestral constraints into a set of entailed edge constraints and topological ordering constraints. The inference of ordering constraints is framed as a novel and interesting reduction to a MaxSAT problem. By passing these inferred decomposable constraints to the oracle, the heuristic becomes much tighter, leading to a significantly more efficient search. The paper provides a strong empirical evaluation, comparing their method to a state-of-the-art Integer Linear Programming (ILP) approach, and demonstrates orders-of-magnitude speedups.

**Quality**

The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The theoretical contributions—the theorems for inferring edge constraints and the MaxSAT formulation for ordering constraints—appear correct and are well-motivated. The central claim that this approach is significantly more efficient than ILP-based methods is convincingly supported by a thorough experimental evaluation. The experiments are well-designed, varying the number of variables, dataset size, and the amount of background knowledge, and the results are averaged over many runs on standard benchmarks. The authors are also careful to evaluate the impact of their "constraint projection" technique by comparing their system's performance with and without it (Tables 1 and 2), clearly demonstrating its massive benefit.

**Clarity**

The paper is clearly written and well-organized. It provides a good introduction to the problem and the necessary background on score-based learning and the EC tree search framework. The central idea of empowering a decomposable-score oracle with projected constraints is communicated effectively. The experimental setup and results are presented in a way that is easy to understand.

A minor suggestion for improvement would be to expand slightly on the MaxSAT formulation in Section 5.2. This section is quite dense, and the motivation for the complex acyclicity constraints (item 3 in the formulation) could be made more intuitive, perhaps with a small example illustrating why simpler conditions are insufficient. However, this is a minor point, and the overall clarity of the paper is high.

**Originality**

The work builds upon the recent EC tree framework, but the contributions are highly original. The primary novelty lies in the idea of "projecting" non-decomposable constraints into the decomposable domain to accelerate a search that relies on a decomposable solver as a subroutine. This is a clever and generalizable idea. The formulation of the ordering constraint inference problem as a MaxSAT instance is particularly novel and serves as a significant technical contribution. The paper is well-situated in the literature, clearly distinguishing its approach from both traditional dynamic programming methods and alternative ILP/CP formulations.

**Significance**

The results presented in this paper are of high significance to the community focused on Bayesian network structure learning. Incorporating expert knowledge is crucial for learning accurate models, especially with limited data, and ancestral relations are a very natural form for this knowledge. The demonstrated orders-of-magnitude speedup over GOBNILP, a prominent ILP-based system, makes exact learning with this important class of constraints much more practical. The empirical result that a relatively small number of ancestral constraints can have an impact on model accuracy equivalent to a massive increase in training data is a powerful finding for practitioners. This work advances the state of the art in exact score-based structure learning and is likely to be built upon by other researchers.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Strong technical contribution:** The paper introduces a novel and effective method for incorporating non-decomposable ancestral constraints into an efficient, oracle-guided search framework. The reduction to MaxSAT for inferring ordering constraints is particularly clever.
*   **Impressive empirical results:** The method is shown to be orders-of-magnitude faster than a state-of-the-art ILP-based competitor, making exact learning with these constraints feasible for larger problems than previously possible.
*   **High significance and impact:** The work addresses a practical and important problem. The findings on the trade-off between background knowledge and data size are valuable for practitioners.
*   **Clarity and completeness:** The paper is well-written, self-contained, and presents a complete research contribution.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The work is an extension of a very recent framework from the same group of authors. However, it is a non-trivial and significant extension that enables an important new capability, which is a valuable form of scientific progress.
*   Like all exact methods for this NP-hard problem, the approach is ultimately limited to networks of a moderate size (around 20 variables in the experiments).

**Overall Impression**

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. The proposed method is novel, the technical details are sound, and the empirical results are impressive. While I have limited personal expertise in the details of MaxSAT solvers, the runtime comparisons against a strong ILP baseline are striking and clearly demonstrate the value of the proposed approach. I strongly recommend acceptance.
