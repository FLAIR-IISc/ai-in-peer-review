This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data when subject to ancestral constraints. The core contribution is a method to translate these non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable ordering and edge constraints. This translation is achieved in part using a MaxSAT solver to make implied constraints explicit. These decomposable constraints are then used to prune the search space of an EC-tree-based algorithm, effectively "empowering" an oracle designed for decomposable scores. This work extends the framework for learning with non-decomposable scores recently proposed by Chen et al.

The paper's main strengths are its clarity and originality. The proposed method is conceptually straightforward and explained very well, making it easy to follow. The approach of projecting ancestral constraints for use in a state-of-the-art optimal structure learning algorithm is novel and represents a valuable contribution for specialists in this area (Originality: Good).

However, the paper has several weaknesses that limit its impact. The primary concern is the significance of the problem setting. The availability of ancestral constraints often motivates causal discovery methods that can handle unobserved confounders, a crucial aspect this score-based framework does not address, making the setting somewhat artificial (Significance: Limited). Furthermore, the experimental evaluation is not fully convincing. The impressive performance gains over the modified Gobnilp baseline may be exaggerated, as Gobnilp was not originally developed for this specific task. A comparison against a greedy search algorithm like GES, modified to incorporate the constraints, would provide a more meaningful benchmark for the efficiency gains. The practical utility is also questionable, as the experiments rely on a large number of known ancestral constraints (10–25%), which is an unrealistic level of prior knowledge for most applications. The benefit with a sparser, more realistic set of constraints is not clearly demonstrated.

Several technical points also require attention (Quality: Fair). The statement of Theorem 1 should be clarified to note that a CPDAG satisfies only a subset of the constraints satisfied by all consistent DAGs. The logic behind the acyclicity rules in the MaxSAT encoding is not immediately clear; for instance, rule 3's conclusion is asymmetric despite a symmetric premise, and rule 4 seems to incorrectly exclude mutual non-causation constraints. Addressing these points and providing a more robust experimental baseline would significantly strengthen the paper.
