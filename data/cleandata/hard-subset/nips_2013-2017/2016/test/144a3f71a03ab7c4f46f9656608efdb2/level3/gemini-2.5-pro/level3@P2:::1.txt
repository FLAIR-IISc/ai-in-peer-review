This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data when given background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints (e.g., X is an ancestor of Y, or X is not an ancestor of Y). Such constraints are non-decomposable, which makes them incompatible with many state-of-the-art optimal structure learning algorithms that rely on decomposable scores and dynamic programming. The authors build upon a recently proposed A*-based search framework over equivalence classes (the EC tree) which can handle non-decomposable scores. The primary contributions are twofold: (1) a method for pruning nodes in the EC tree that are inconsistent with the given ancestral constraints, and (2) a method for "projecting" the non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints. These projected constraints are then passed to the A* search's heuristic function (an oracle that solves a relaxed, decomposable problem), thereby empowering the oracle and speeding up the search. The authors compare their method to an Integer Linear Programming (ILP) baseline (GOBNILP) and report orders-of-magnitude speedups on several benchmark datasets.

**Quality and Clarity**

The paper's core ideas are interesting, but the presentation suffers from a lack of clarity and sufficient technical detail, which makes it difficult to assess the soundness of the approach.

- The theoretical justification for pruning the search space based on ancestral constraints (Theorem 1) is not adequately explained. The proof is omitted for space, but the intuition provided is insufficient to be convincing. A key step in such a proof would involve reasoning about how adding new variables/edges (i.e., moving to a descendant node in the EC tree) preserves or violates ancestral constraints. This logic is central to the paper's correctness and needs to be established more clearly.

- The description of how decomposable constraints are derived from ancestral constraints is particularly confusing. In Section 5.2, the authors use the term "infer" when discussing ordering constraints. However, the example provided (X -> Y <- Z) shows that naively inferring ordering constraints (e.g., Y < Z from Z ⊀ Y) can incorrectly eliminate valid DAGs. The paper then proposes a MaxSAT formulation to find a "maximally large set" of ordering constraints. This is misleading. It seems the authors are not inferring *entailed* constraints, but rather adding a large set of *consistent* ones. This distinction is critical and must be clarified. Is the resulting set of ordering constraints guaranteed to be entailed by the ancestral constraints, or is it just a heuristic addition? The MaxSAT formulation itself is presented with little justification, making it hard to understand its properties or why it is a suitable choice.

**Originality**

The paper builds on the prior work of Chen et al. (2015, 2016), adapting their EC tree search framework to a new type of constraint. The main novelty lies in the idea of projecting non-decomposable constraints to empower a decomposable oracle. This is a clever and potentially generalizable technique for hybrid search algorithms. However, the paper fails to discuss or compare against some highly relevant recent work. Notably, the constraint programming approach of van Beek and Hoffmann (CP 2015) is a powerful method for optimal BN structure learning that has been shown to outperform ILP approaches like GOBNILP. Given that CP systems are highly flexible with respect to global constraints, it seems plausible their method could be adapted to handle ancestral constraints, making it a critical point of comparison that is missing from the evaluation.

**Significance**

The problem of incorporating rich background knowledge into structure learning is of high practical and theoretical importance, and this paper presents a promising approach. The reported empirical results show a massive performance gap between the proposed method and the ILP baseline. However, the significance of these results is undermined by the experimental design.

- The choice of GOBNILP as the primary baseline is questionable. ILP-based methods for BN learning are known to scale poorly when the maximum in-degree (parent set size) is not strictly bounded. The experiments appear to be run in this unbounded setting, which may disadvantage GOBNILP for reasons unrelated to its handling of ancestral constraints. It is therefore unclear if the reported speedup is due to a superior handling of ancestral constraints or simply a reflection of the known scaling limitations of this specific ILP solver in this regime.

- More critically, the ILP encoding used for the ancestral constraints is based on Cussens (2008), which is known to be a relatively weak formulation. A much tighter and more standard ILP formulation for reachability/ancestorship would involve introducing binary variables A(X,Y) for ancestorship and linking them to the parent-selection variables I(Z,U) via transitivity constraints (e.g., A(X,Z) and I(Z, pa(Z)) implies A(X,Y) if Y is in pa(Z)) and linking constraints that enforce the user-provided ancestral knowledge on the A variables. Comparing against a known weak encoding does not provide convincing evidence of superiority. While the proposed method might still be faster than a stronger ILP formulation, the current comparison is not a fair one and weakens the paper's central claim.

**Summary of Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  The paper tackles an important and practical problem: optimal BN structure learning with non-decomposable ancestral constraints.
2.  The core algorithmic idea of using projected decomposable constraints to empower an oracle within a non-decomposable search space is novel and interesting.
3.  The proposed method demonstrates very promising performance, suggesting it is a viable approach for this challenging task.

**Arguments for rejection:**
1.  The paper suffers from significant clarity issues, particularly regarding the theoretical justification for pruning (Theorem 1) and the procedure for deriving ordering constraints via MaxSAT.
2.  The experimental evaluation is not convincing. The large performance gap reported may be an artifact of comparing against a baseline that is ill-suited for the experimental setting (unbounded in-degree) and using a sub-optimal ILP encoding for the ancestral constraints.
3.  The authors omit discussion and comparison with key related work, particularly the constraint programming approach by van Beek and Hoffmann (2015), which is a stronger baseline than GOBNILP in many settings.

In its current form, the paper's claims are not sufficiently supported due to the unclear theoretical explanations and a weak experimental evaluation. While the underlying ideas are promising, the paper requires a major revision to address these issues before it can be accepted.
