- Addresses optimal Bayesian network structure learning with ancestral constraints by translating non-decomposable constraints to decomposable ordering constraints.
- Builds on recent non-decomposable score framework and leverages EC-tree search with efficient oracles.
- Core technical idea: project ancestral constraints into decomposable ordering constraints using a MaxSAT solver.
- Demonstrates significant performance improvements over an ILP-based baseline (GOBNILP) on simulated datasets.
- Well-written, clear exposition of a complex topic with thorough related-work coverage.
- Strong novelty: advances incorporation of complex prior knowledge into BN structure learning.
- Practical significance is limited because precise ancestral constraints known a priori are uncommon in real applications.
- Many real scenarios involve ancestral constraints as hypotheses and unobserved confounders, reducing applicability.
- A small number of available constraints may not sufficiently reduce search space to realize benefits.
- Inference rules for projecting constraints (lines 257–261) raise concerns: Rule 3 seems asymmetric and Rule 4 may exclude reasonable constraints (e.g., mutual independence).
- The MaxSAT projection is elegant but needs clearer justification and possible revision of inference rules.
- Comparison to GOBNILP may be unfair since GOBNILP wasn’t designed for this constrained problem.
- Suggest stronger comparisons to adapted greedy algorithms (e.g., GES with hard ancestral constraints) for a more relevant baseline.
- Theorem 1 is misstated: a CPDAG satisfying constraints A only guarantees at least one DAG in class(P) satisfies A, so the iff is incorrect.
- Theorem 3 may have limited utility under sparse ancestral constraints, yielding few positive edge implications.
- Terminology and clarity issues: clarify "oracle" vs "omniscient oracle", avoid imprecise phrasing like "empower the oracle", and state that "optimizing" means minimizing the score.
- Labeling of the acyclicity constraint encoding could be more descriptive for clarity.
- Line 310’s characterization of 10–25% constraints as "extensive" is misleading; these levels may be relatively low.
- Acceptance arguments: novel, technically sound, substantial empirical gains, clear presentation, and relevance to BN structure learning.
- Rejection concerns: limited practical applicability, potential flaws in inference rules, potentially mismatched baseline comparison, and minor clarity/precision issues.
- Overall recommendation: accept contingent on addressing inference-rule concerns, refining theorems/clarity, and adding stronger baseline comparisons.
