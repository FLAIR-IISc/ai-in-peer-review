This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data when given background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints (e.g., X is an ancestor of Y, or X is not an ancestor of Y). The authors correctly point out that such constraints are non-decomposable, which makes them incompatible with many efficient, modern score-based learning algorithms that rely on the assumption of score decomposability (e.g., dynamic programming or A* search over order graphs).

The proposed approach builds upon the recently introduced EC-tree search framework [Chen et al., 2016], which uses A* search to find an optimal network in a space of Markov equivalence classes. This framework is general enough to handle non-decomposable constraints by pruning nodes (CPDAGs) that violate them. The core technical contribution of this paper is a method to "project" the non-decomposable ancestral constraints into a set of decomposable edge and topological ordering constraints. These inferred decomposable constraints are then used to "empower" the oracle that computes the admissible heuristic for the A* search. By providing the oracle with a more constrained problem, the heuristic becomes tighter, leading to a more efficient search. The paper presents methods for inferring both edge constraints and ordering constraints, the latter via a reduction to MaxSAT. The proposed approach is empirically compared to an Integer Linear Programming (ILP) baseline, GOBNILP, showing orders-of-magnitude speedups.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The problem is well-motivated and significant. Incorporating rich prior knowledge is crucial for learning accurate models, especially with limited data. Ancestral constraints are a natural and powerful form of such knowledge. The proposed method for translating non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones that can be exploited by an underlying exact solver (the oracle) is novel and clever.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed constraint projection techniques are technically sound. The use of a MaxSAT solver to find a maximal set of consistent ordering constraints is an elegant solution to a non-trivial inference problem. The overall framework, which integrates these projected constraints into an A*-based search over EC-trees, is a solid contribution.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly laid out in the introduction, and the technical sections provide sufficient detail on the proposed methods.

4.  **Empirical Performance:** The reported speedups over the ILP-based GOBNILP system are dramatic and compellingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed search framework for this problem.

**Weaknesses and Detailed Comments**

My main concern with the paper lies in the experimental evaluation, which, while showing impressive results against a baseline, fails to clearly isolate and quantify the benefit of the paper's primary technical contribution: the constraint projection method.

1.  **Confounded Experimental Comparison:** Table 1 compares the authors' proposed method (EC-tree + projection) against an ILP baseline that is also supplied with the projected constraints (GOBNILP + projection). While this demonstrates that the EC-tree framework is superior to ILP for this task, it does not answer the most critical question: **How much does the constraint projection actually speed up the EC-tree search?** The main claim of the paper is that empowering the oracle with projected constraints is the key to efficiency. To validate this claim, a direct comparison is needed between:
    a) The proposed system: EC-tree search with a projection-empowered oracle.
    b) An ablation: EC-tree search with a "naive" oracle that is not given the projected constraints (i.e., it solves a less-constrained subproblem to compute the heuristic).

    This ablation study is essential. Without it, the reader cannot disentangle the performance gains from the underlying EC-tree search framework from the gains provided by the novel projection method. Table 2, which withholds the projected constraints from both systems, comes closer but still focuses on the comparison to GOBNILP rather than a direct self-ablation. It is plausible that the EC-tree search is so much better than GOBNILP that it would still be orders-of-magnitude faster even without the projection. A direct comparison of (EC-tree + projection) vs. (EC-tree without projection) would provide a much clearer and more convincing measure of the paper's contribution.

2.  **Clarity of Experimental Narrative:** The role of Table 3 is somewhat unclear in the context of the main claims. It appears to be a scalability and sensitivity analysis, which is valuable. However, the narrative could be strengthened by first establishing the core contribution via the ablation study suggested above, and then using the results in Table 3 to demonstrate how this contribution scales to larger problems and how it behaves under varying levels of constraint. As it stands, the jump to scalability feels premature when the core benefit of the projection has not been explicitly measured against the proper baseline.

In summary, this is a strong paper with a novel technical idea addressing an important problem. The methodology is sound and the results against the ILP baseline are impressive. However, the evaluation is missing a crucial ablation study that would quantify the impact of the main contribution. If the authors can provide a direct comparison of their method with and without the constraint projection, I believe this would make a very solid contribution to the conference.
