## Review of "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"

This paper addresses the important problem of learning Bayesian networks under ancestral constraints, which represent valuable domain knowledge often unavailable in purely data-driven approaches. The core contribution lies in a method to project these non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and topological-ordering constraints, enabling the use of efficient algorithms designed for decomposable scores. This is a clever approach, building on the recent framework of Chen et al. [2015, 2016] that leverages oracles for learning with decomposable scores. The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains the theoretical foundations and experimental setup. The motivation is well-articulated, and the connection to prior work in Bayesian network structure learning (e.g., ILP, constraint programming, and decomposable score-based methods) is thoroughly established.

The strength of this work is the elegant projection method and the demonstration of significant efficiency gains compared to ILP-based approaches (Table 1). The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, utilizing benchmark datasets and varying parameters like network size, data size, and constraint proportion. However, the improvement over ILP is only observed for EC trees (Table 3), raising concerns about the generalizability of the projection to other learning approaches. While the authors acknowledge this, further investigation into why the projection is less effective for ILP would strengthen the paper. 

A key request is for evidence demonstrating that the inferred decomposable constraints actually *improve* the performance or efficiency of methods that originally couldn’t handle non-decomposable constraints. Currently, the experiments primarily show speedups, but not necessarily improved accuracy or the ability to solve problems previously intractable without the projection. The observed easy-hard-easy pattern in Table 2 as the proportion of ancestral constraints increases is also puzzling and requires a more detailed explanation. Are EC trees particularly sensitive to the amount of domain knowledge, compared to ILP? This suggests a need for careful tuning of the number of ancestral constraints.

Furthermore, Table 2 only reports Structural Hamming Distance (SHD) for EC trees. Including a comparison of SHD with the ILP approach would provide a more complete picture of the accuracy differences. Finally, while Section 4.1 on BN graphs provides context, it feels somewhat tangential to the core contribution. Dropping this section and expanding the explanations of EC trees and the A* algorithm within the EC-tree experimental setting, as suggested by the authors, would improve the paper's focus and clarity.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound approach to incorporating non-decomposable constraints.
*   Significant efficiency gains demonstrated compared to ILP for EC trees.
*   Excellent clarity of writing and presentation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited generalizability of the projection method (improvement not seen with ILP).
*   Lack of evidence showing improved accuracy or the ability to solve previously intractable problems.
*   Unexplained easy-hard-easy pattern in experimental results.



Overall, this is a strong paper with a valuable contribution. Addressing the concerns regarding generalizability and providing more evidence of performance improvement would significantly strengthen the work and warrant acceptance.
