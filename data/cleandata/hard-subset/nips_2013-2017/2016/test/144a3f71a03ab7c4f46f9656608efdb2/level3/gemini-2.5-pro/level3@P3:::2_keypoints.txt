- Problem addressed: learning optimal Bayesian network structures with non-decomposable ancestral constraints that break many exact learning algorithms.  
- Main contribution: a method to empower the A*-based EC-tree oracle by projecting ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints.  
- Technical approach: infers decomposable edge constraints (Theorems 2–3) and ordering constraints via a MaxSAT encoding (Theorem 4), and prunes the EC tree using Theorem 1.  
- Key advantage claimed: the projected decomposable constraints tighten the admissible heuristic, yielding a more efficient exact search.  
- Experimental claim: significant performance improvements over an ILP baseline (GOBNILP + projection).  
- Strength — significance and originality: tackles a practical, expressive form of prior knowledge with a novel, potentially generalizable projection idea.  
- Strength — technical quality: sound theoretical grounding with formal theorems and algorithms supporting correctness.  
- Strength — clarity: well-written, logical exposition and clear distinction between decomposable and non-decomposable constraints.  
- Primary weakness: experimental evaluation is confusing and lacks a clear ablation isolating the projection’s benefit.  
- Critical missing experiment: direct comparison of EC tree with projection vs EC tree without projection (oracle unaware of projected constraints).  
- Confusing presentation: Table 1 compares EC tree + projection to GOBNILP + projection but does not isolate the projection’s contribution; referenced Table 2 is absent or unclear.  
- Table 3’s role is unclear: shows scalability and SHD but doesn’t directly support the effectiveness of the projection method.  
- Recommendation: add a central table comparing System A (EC tree using ancestral constraints only for pruning, no projected constraints in oracle) vs System B (EC tree + projection) reporting runtimes to demonstrate the projection’s impact.  
- Overall verdict: strong, novel idea with solid theory but needs a clearer experimental ablation to convincingly attribute efficiency gains to the projection technique.
