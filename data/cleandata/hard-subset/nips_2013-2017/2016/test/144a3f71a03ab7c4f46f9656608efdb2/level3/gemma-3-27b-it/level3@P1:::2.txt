## Review of Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints

This paper presents a novel approach to learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints, addressing a limitation of existing methods which struggle to incorporate non-decomposable constraints. The authors build upon their previous work on optimal structure learning using non-decomposable scores and an oracle for learning structures with decomposable scores. Their key contribution lies in demonstrating how to “empower” this oracle by inferring decomposable constraints from the given ancestral constraints, significantly improving efficiency compared to Integer Linear Programming (ILP)-based methods.

The problem addressed is important. Bayesian networks are widely used, and incorporating background knowledge in the form of constraints is crucial for both improving accuracy and scalability. The limitation of traditional methods in handling non-decomposable constraints, such as ancestral relations, is well-established (citing Darwiche, Koller & Friedman, Murphy). The proposed framework, leveraging the EC tree search space and an oracle, offers a promising solution. The idea of projecting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable forms to enhance the oracle’s performance is particularly clever and forms the core technical contribution.  The theoretical justification for the soundness of the pruning step and the completeness of the constraint projection are valuable additions. The connections drawn to related work in constraint-based learning (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos) and ILP-based approaches are also helpful in situating this work within the broader field.

The paper’s strengths lie in its clear articulation of the problem, its novel approach to solving it, and the thorough empirical evaluation. The empirical results, presented in Tables 1-3, demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms the ILP-based GOBNILP by orders of magnitude. The ablation study, although a bit muddled in presentation (see weakness below), indicates that the projection of constraints has a significant impact on efficiency. The scaling analysis in Table 3 is valuable, showing the method’s applicability to problems with up to 20 variables. The theoretical guarantees regarding constraint satisfaction and projection further strengthen the work.

However, the presentation of the experimental results requires clarification.  The comparison in Table 1 between “EC + new work” and “GOB + new work” is somewhat confusing.  While demonstrating the benefit of applying the constraint projection technique to both methods is valid, the primary interest for the NIPS audience lies in assessing the improvement *specifically* to the authors' own EC tree-based approach.  The authors acknowledge that EC is already superior to GOB, and thus the most compelling evidence would be a direct comparison of EC *with* constraint projection versus EC *without* constraint projection. A similar point applies to Table 3 – while valuable, isolating the impact of the projection technique would offer greater insight.  

Furthermore, the MaxSAT reduction for inferring ordering constraints, while theoretically sound, could benefit from a more detailed discussion of its practical implications. What is the computational cost of this reduction? How does it scale with the number of constraints and variables? Addressing these questions would provide a more complete picture of the overall approach.  The paper could also benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the approach. For instance, what types of ancestral constraints are most effectively handled?  Are there scenarios where the constraint projection technique fails to provide significant benefits? 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of projecting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable forms for use with an oracle is a novel contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning.
*   **Significance:** The ability to efficiently handle ancestral constraints opens up new possibilities for incorporating background knowledge into Bayesian network models, which is crucial for real-world applications.
*   **Soundness:** The theoretical guarantees regarding constraint satisfaction and projection provide confidence in the correctness of the approach.
*   **Empirical Results:** The substantial performance improvements over ILP-based methods demonstrate the practical value of the proposed approach.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Presentation of Experimental Results:** The organization of the experimental results and the lack of a direct comparison between EC with and without constraint projection make it difficult to fully assess the impact of the authors' contribution.
*   **Scalability of MaxSAT Reduction:** The computational cost of the MaxSAT reduction for inferring ordering constraints needs further investigation and discussion.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the minor issues with the presentation of the experimental results, I believe this is a strong paper with significant potential impact. The proposed approach is novel, theoretically sound, and empirically validated. Addressing the reviewer's concerns regarding the comparison of EC with and without the constraint projection technique would significantly strengthen the paper. **I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.**  Specifically, I recommend adding a table demonstrating the performance of the EC tree with and without the projected constraints, clearly isolating the benefit of the proposed technique.
