

# Review of "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"

This paper addresses a significant challenge in Bayesian network structure learning: integrating non-decomposable ancestral constraints into learning frameworks that assume decomposable scores. The authors propose a novel method to project these non-decomposable constraints into decomposable edge and topological-ordering constraints, enabling existing structure learning algorithms to handle ancestral constraints efficiently. This builds on Chen et al.'s (2015, 2016) EC tree approach for learning with non-decomposable scores, extending it to accommodate ancestral constraints through a sound projection method detailed in Section 5.

The paper makes a technically sound contribution with clear theoretical foundations. The authors demonstrate how ancestral constraints (which are non-decomposable) can be transformed into decomposable constraints that can be handled by oracles for decomposable scores. This is a significant advance over prior work, as most structure learning approaches cannot directly incorporate ancestral constraints. The experimental results in Table 1 are compelling, showing orders-of-magnitude speed improvements over ILP-based approaches like GOBNILP when projected constraints are used. The paper also provides valuable insights into the impact of background knowledge on learning efficiency, showing that a small percentage of ancestral constraints (10-25%) can have a similar impact on model accuracy as significantly increasing training data size.

The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear explanations of both theoretical concepts and experimental results. The presentation of the EC tree search space and the projection method in Section 5 is particularly strong, making a complex technical contribution accessible to readers. The experiments are well-designed, using standard benchmarks and varying key parameters to demonstrate scalability and efficiency.

However, there are several concerns that need addressing before acceptance:

1. **Generalization of the projection method**: Table 3 shows significant improvements for EC trees but no comparable gains for ILP. This raises serious questions about whether the projection method truly generalizes to other learning approaches. The authors should provide evidence that the inferred decomposable constraints improve performance/efficiency of methods that originally cannot handle non-decomposable constraints. Specifically, they should demonstrate how the projected constraints benefit ILP approaches beyond the modest improvements seen in Table 2.

2. **Easy-hard-easy pattern**: Table 2 exhibits an easy-hard-easy pattern as the proportion of ancestral constraints increases. The authors should explain this phenomenon and address whether EC trees are highly sensitive to the amount of domain knowledge compared to ILP. This sensitivity has important implications for practical application, as it suggests that the number of ancestral constraints may need careful tuning to achieve ideal performance.

3. **Missing SHD comparison**: Table 2 reports structural Hamming distance (SHD) only for EC trees, making it impossible to compare the quality of solutions between the two approaches. The authors should add SHD comparisons with the ILP approach to demonstrate that the EC tree method achieves both better efficiency and comparable or better accuracy.

4. **Section 4.1 redundancy**: Section 4.1 on BN graphs appears to be redundant given the paper's focus on EC trees. The authors should consider dropping this section and instead expanding explanations of EC trees and the A* algorithm in the EC-tree experimental setting, as this would better support their main contribution.

The paper's main strength lies in its novel projection method (Section 5), which represents a significant technical advance in handling non-decomposable constraints. The work is original, as it addresses a gap in the literature regarding how to incorporate ancestral constraints into structure learning frameworks that assume decomposable scores. The significance is high, as the approach enables more efficient learning with background knowledge, which is crucial for real-world applications where domain knowledge is often available.

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:
- Address the generalization concern by providing evidence that the projection method benefits ILP approaches
- Explain the easy-hard-easy pattern in Table 2 and discuss the sensitivity of EC trees to the amount of domain knowledge
- Add SHD comparisons with ILP in Table 2
- Streamline Section 4.1 and expand the EC tree explanation

With these revisions, the paper would make a strong contribution to the field. As it stands, the concerns about the generalization of the projection method and the missing SHD comparison significantly undermine the claimed advantages of the approach. The paper has strong potential but needs to provide more comprehensive evidence that the projection method delivers benefits across different learning approaches.

Overall, the paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses an important problem. With the suggested revisions addressing the concerns about generalization and completeness of evaluation, it would be a valuable contribution to NIPS. I recommend acceptance with major revisions.
