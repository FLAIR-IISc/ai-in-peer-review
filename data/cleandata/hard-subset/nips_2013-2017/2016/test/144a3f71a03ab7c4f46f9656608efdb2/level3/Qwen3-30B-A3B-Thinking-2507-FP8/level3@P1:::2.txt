

This paper addresses the problem of learning Bayesian network structures under ancestral constraints, which are non-decomposable and thus challenging for standard structure learning approaches. The authors build upon Chen et al. (2015, 2016), who introduced the EC tree search space for optimal structure learning with non-decomposable scores, and extend it to handle ancestral constraints through constraint projection. The key insight is that ancestral constraints (non-decomposable) can be projected into decomposable edge and ordering constraints, which can then be used to empower an oracle (a standard structure learning algorithm for decomposable scores), significantly improving search efficiency.

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. The problem of incorporating non-decomposable constraints has been largely overlooked in previous work, with most approaches (including the K2 algorithm and order graph methods) only supporting decomposable constraints. The authors' approach is theoretically sound, with clear theorems establishing how to infer edge and ordering constraints from ancestral constraints. Their method for projecting constraints (Sections 5.1 and 5.2) is both novel and practical, with the MaxSAT-based approach for ordering constraints being particularly elegant.

The paper demonstrates significant empirical improvements over existing methods. The authors compare their EC tree-based approach (with constraint projection) against GOBNILP (an ILP-based approach) with and without constraint projection. The results in Table 1 show their method is orders-of-magnitude faster than GOBNILP across various problem sizes. However, the experimental design has significant issues that undermine the paper's claims. Specifically, the comparison in Table 1 appears to be between (EC + constraint projection) and (GOBNILP + constraint projection), but it's unclear whether the constraint projection was applied to GOBNILP in the same way it was to the EC tree. The role of Table 3 is also unclear - it reports results with a time limit and memory limit but doesn't provide a clear comparison to the EC tree without constraint projection.

The most serious weakness is the lack of a direct comparison between the EC tree with constraint projection versus the EC tree without constraint projection. As the reviewer notes, even if GOBNILP benefits more from constraint projection, the EC tree approach is already superior to GOBNILP, so demonstrating the impact of constraint projection on the EC tree itself is crucial. Without this comparison, it's impossible to determine whether the constraint projection technique is genuinely beneficial or whether the EC tree would have been efficient even without it.

The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized, making it easy to follow the technical contributions. The theoretical results are well-motivated and the proofs are sound. However, the experimental section needs significant clarification and additional results to support the claims. The authors should provide a direct comparison showing the impact of constraint projection on the EC tree's performance, and they should clarify how constraint projection was applied to GOBNILP.

The significance of this work is substantial. Ancestral constraints are common in real-world applications (e.g., expressing causal relationships), and incorporating them efficiently can dramatically improve structure learning. The paper shows that even a small percentage of ancestral constraints (10-25%) can have a similar impact on model accuracy as significantly increasing the training data size, which is particularly valuable when data collection is expensive.

In terms of quality, the paper's theoretical contributions are strong, but the empirical evaluation is weakened by the experimental design issues. The authors make a clear theoretical contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning, but the experimental results don't fully support the claimed advantages.

For clarity, the paper is well-structured, but the experimental section would benefit from additional explanations about the constraint projection process and the comparison setup. The authors should clarify how the constraint projection was applied to both methods and provide the requested comparison between EC with and without constraint projection.

Originality is strong. While constraint-based learning with ancestral constraints has been explored (e.g., Borboudakis and Tsamardinos, 2012), this is the first work to address how to efficiently incorporate ancestral constraints into optimal structure learning with non-decomposable scores. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work on constraint-based learning (which typically uses a different approach to structure learning) and from the standard decomposable score approaches.

The significance is high. The ability to efficiently incorporate background knowledge (in the form of ancestral constraints) into Bayesian network structure learning has important practical implications for applications where domain knowledge is available. The results showing that a small number of ancestral constraints can improve model accuracy as much as substantially increasing the training data size is particularly compelling.

Overall, this paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to Bayesian network structure learning, but the experimental evaluation needs significant improvement to fully support the claims. The core idea of projecting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones is sound and promising, but the experimental results don't adequately demonstrate its impact on the EC tree approach.

Strengths: Strong theoretical contribution, clear problem statement, novel constraint projection technique, significant empirical improvements over ILP-based approaches.

Weaknesses: Experimental design issues, lack of direct comparison between EC with and without constraint projection, unclear description of how constraint projection was applied to GOBNILP.

Recommendation: Major revision. The authors should provide the requested comparison between EC with and without constraint projection, clarify the experimental setup, and ensure the experimental results fully support the paper's claims. With these improvements, this paper would be a strong contribution to the field.
