Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses optimal Bayesian network structure learning in the presence of ancestral constraints (path constraints expressing ancestor/descendant relations). Most exact algorithms assume decomposable scores and thus cannot directly handle such non-decomposable constraints. Building on the BN-graph/EC-tree shortest-path framework for non-decomposable scores (Chen et al., 2015; Chen et al., 2016), the authors (i) prune the BN/EC search space using ancestral constraints and, crucially, (ii) project non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and topological-ordering constraints to empower strong “oracle” learners that assume decomposability. Section 5 is the central technical contribution: a sound procedure to infer negative/positive edge constraints from ancestral constraints (Theorems 2–3), and a MaxSAT-based approach to infer a maximally large consistent set of ordering constraints (Theorem 4 provides sufficient conditions). Experiments compare the EC-tree approach with GOBNILP (ILP), with and without projected constraints.

This work is well positioned versus prior exact learning over decomposable scores (dynamic programming, A*, order-graph), ILP/CP formulations (Jaakkola et al., 2010; Cussens, 2008; Bartlett & Cussens, 2015), and prior use of path constraints in constraint-based learning (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos). To my knowledge, a general method that projects path constraints to decomposable constraints for reuse inside decomposable oracles is novel and addresses a practical gap.

Strengths
- Clear problem statement and motivation: ancestral constraints are practically important and non-decomposable.
- Solid integration in the EC-tree framework, with sound pruning under path constraints (Theorem 1).
- Main novelty: projection of ancestral constraints to decomposable edge and ordering constraints; theorems are intuitive and the procedure seems implementable.
- Strong empirical evidence that projected constraints substantially tighten the oracle and accelerate EC-tree search; in many settings, orders-of-magnitude improvements over ILP are reported.
- Presentation is clear and organized; theory and experiments are easy to follow.

Weaknesses and specific concerns
1) Evidence that projection generalizes beyond EC-tree/oracle
- Table 3 (and discussion) highlights large gains for EC-trees, but it is less clear that projection robustly helps other learning approaches. ILP shows limited or mixed improvements when projections are added, raising doubts about generality.
- Request: Demonstrate that the inferred decomposable constraints improve methods that originally cannot handle non-decomposable constraints, not just the EC-tree-with-oracle setting. Concretely:
  - ILP: Report runtime, branch-and-cut statistics, and solution counts with/without projected constraints across p and n. Include both small and large instances and ensure GOBNILP is configured with strong cuts.
  - Dynamic programming/order-graph (Silander & Myllymäki; Koivisto & Sood) or precedence-constraint DP (Parviainen & Koivisto): plug in projected edges/orderings and quantify speedups.
  - Heuristic A* over order graph (Yuan & Malone): ablate no-projection vs edge-only vs ordering-only vs both.
  - Provide counts of inferred constraints (per type) and correlate these with runtime reductions across methods.

2) Easy–hard–easy pattern in Table 2 and sensitivity to p
- The EC-tree exhibits an easy–hard–easy trend as p increases; ILP appears more monotone-easy. Please explain the mechanism: e.g., are mid-range constraints pruning enough to degrade heuristic accuracy (less informative oracle scores) but not enough to shrink the EC-tree, creating search plateaus? Or does MaxSAT inference produce fewer/less helpful orderings in mid p?
- Provide diagnostic plots: (i) number of EC nodes expanded, (ii) heuristic error estimates or f=g+h breakdowns, (iii) number of projected constraints vs p, and (iv) fraction of nodes pruned by ancestral tests vs enumeration fallback. Compare to ILP’s cut generation and LP gaps.
- Implication: EC-trees may be sensitive to the amount of domain knowledge; practitioners might need to tune p. Please discuss guidance for selecting the number of constraints and/or adaptive projection strategies that maintain heuristic tightness.

3) Structural Hamming Distance (SHD) only for EC trees
- Table 2 reports SHD for EC trees only. Please add SHD for ILP on the common subset of solved instances (and note timeouts separately). Since both are exact given the same score/constraints, the solutions should match on solved cases; if not, investigate differences.

4) Scope and space usage
- Section 4.1 (BN graph) could be dropped or condensed to a short paragraph with a pointer, freeing space to expand the EC-tree+A* setting: define the heuristic/oracle interface precisely, detail how projected constraints are injected, and report ablations (edge-only vs ordering-only vs both). Also expand the MaxSAT encoding: soft clause weights, solver settings, and complexity.

Additional suggestions
- Robustness to noisy constraints: evaluate when a fraction of ancestral constraints are incorrect. What happens to pruning soundness and to projection? Can MaxSAT or the framework incorporate soft path constraints?
- Completeness/optimality of projection: Theorems 2–3 are sound; please clarify completeness boundaries (e.g., are there entailed edge constraints not captured by the rules?). For MaxSAT, “maximally large set” depends on the soft objective; specify and justify weights and prove that any returned O preserves G(A)⊆G(O).
- Report end-to-end counts: number of inferred edges/orderings, percent of parent sets ruled out, and reduction in candidate orders. Include per-dataset statistics.
- Fairness to ILP: ensure strong GOBNILP settings (family-size constraints if standard, cutting planes/clique cuts on), and report nodes, cuts, and runtime. Consider CP baseline (van Beek & Hoffmann).
- Reproducibility: release code, scripts, and MaxSAT instances.

Quality
Technically sound overall; pruning and projection rules are well motivated. Please strengthen evidence for generality of the projection (Concern 1), add SHD comparisons (Concern 3), and analyze the easy–hard–easy phenomenon (Concern 2).

Clarity
Very well written. Small improvements: condense BN graph; expand EC-tree+A* mechanics; detail MaxSAT encoding and solver configuration.

Originality
Novel and practical idea to project non-decomposable path constraints into decomposable constraints and reuse existing oracles. To my knowledge, new relative to prior NIPS/UAI work.

Significance
High potential impact: enables practitioners to inject causal/domain knowledge in exact structure learning while leveraging mature decomposable-score machinery. Strengthen by showing general applicability across learning paradigms and robustness to imperfect knowledge.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, novel projection technique; solid theory and strong empirical speedups with EC tree.
- Practical importance of ancestral constraints; method integrates well with exact learning.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited evidence that projection consistently benefits other frameworks (ILP, DP).
- Missing SHD comparison with ILP; limited analysis of easy–hard–easy behavior and sensitivity to p.
- Space could be reallocated to deepen the core EC-tree+A* and projection components.

Overall: I lean toward acceptance if the authors address the generality and analysis concerns and add the requested comparisons and diagnostics.
