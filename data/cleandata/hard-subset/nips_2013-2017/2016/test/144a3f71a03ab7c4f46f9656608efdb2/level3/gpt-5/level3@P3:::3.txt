Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles optimal Bayesian network (BN) structure learning when given background knowledge in the form of ancestral (path) constraints. The method operates in the EC-tree search space (Chen et al., 2016), pruning CPDAG nodes that cannot be extended to satisfy the ancestral constraints, and leveraging an “oracle” for decomposable scores to guide A* search. The key idea is to translate non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints that the oracle can exploit: (i) edge implications via sufficient/necessary rules, and (ii) topological ordering constraints obtained by solving a MaxSAT problem that selects a maximally large, acyclic, jointly compatible set of orderings implied by the ancestral constraints. Empirically, the approach is compared against a (modified) ILP baseline (Gobnilp), showing orders-of-magnitude speedups; the gains are especially pronounced when projected constraints are fed to the oracle.

The work builds directly on BN-graph/EC-tree frameworks for exact non-decomposable scoring (Chen et al., 2015; 2016), contrasts with classical order-graph dynamic programming and A*, and relates to ILP/CP approaches (Jaakkola et al., 2010; Bartlett & Cussens, 2015) and constraint-based/path-constraint ideas (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos, 2012; 2013). Using MaxSAT (Li & Manyà, 2009) to extract ordering constraints is a notable design decision, and the empirical section provides a useful look at the effect of constraint projection.

Strengths
- Clear and well-organized presentation; definitions and the EC-tree adaptation are easy to follow.
- Conceptually straightforward: prune EC-tree by feasibility and empower a decomposable-score oracle with projected constraints.
- Solid integration of MaxSAT to turn ancestral knowledge into decomposable order constraints; the rules for edge implications are also well-motivated.
- Strong empirical speedups over an ILP baseline, especially when projected constraints are used; careful analysis of easy–hard–easy behavior w.r.t. constraint density.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Quality
- The mapping from ancestral to ordering constraints via MaxSAT is sound as a way to collect many order implications, but the acyclicity/compatibility encoding is not very transparent. The constraint labeled “acyclic” (around line 267) is difficult to recognize as a sufficient encoding of acyclicity; a clearer derivation or alternative encoding (e.g., standard cycle-prevention over orders) would help.
- Rules in lines 257–261 raise concerns:
  - Acyclicity Rule 3 has a symmetric premise ((X<Y) ∧ (Z<W)) but an asymmetric disjunction on the right; please justify the asymmetry or provide the symmetric closure.
  - Rule 4 appears to exclude constraints like “X and Y do not cause each other” (X 6 Y and Y 6 X) unless both directions are explicitly allowed in the encoding; clarify whether and how mutual non-ancestry is supported without eliminating valid DAGs.
- Theorem 1 needs a sharper statement. As written (“a CPDAG P satisfies A iff its descendants satisfy A”), it risks conflating “P satisfies A” with “some DAG in class(P) satisfies A.” Since a CPDAG abstracts a family of DAGs, what is guaranteed at a node is at best a subset of G that satisfies A; descendants can only further restrict that subset. Please restate to make it explicit that the set of constraints satisfied at P is a subset of those satisfied by a particular DAG G ∈ class(P); otherwise the iff is misleading.
- Theorem 3 (positive edge implications) seems weak in sparse-constraint regimes. When A is small, the quantification over Z will rarely rule out all mediators, so few X→Y edges will be implied. This limits the practical impact of the positive-edge projection in realistic settings with few constraints.
- In practice, only a few ancestral constraints are typically available. It would be useful to quantify how many projected ordering/edge constraints are generated under sparse A (1–5% of pairs), and how much the oracle’s search is actually tightened. Without many constraints, search-space reduction may be modest.

Originality
- The contribution primarily lies in translating ancestral constraints into decomposable ones (especially orders) and integrating that translation into EC-tree search. This is a conceptually simple but useful extension of Chen et al. (2015; 2016). Novelty is moderate but of specialist interest (novelty=3).

Significance
- The target setting is somewhat artificial for many causal discovery applications: when one elicits path constraints from experts, allowance for latent confounding (e.g., MAGs/MAG-equivalents) is often more appropriate than pure DAG learning. This limits broader impact (impact=2).
- Because few ancestral constraints are likely available in practice, the empirical gains reported at 10–25% constraints (line 310) may overstate real-world benefits; that level already implies substantial prior knowledge.

Empirical evaluation and baselines
- The comparison to Gobnilp is informative, but not entirely fair: Gobnilp was not developed with this specific projection/oracle setting in mind, and the impressive wins may partly reflect this mismatch. Please make this limitation explicit.
- Suggested alternative baseline: compare to greedy score-based methods such as GES (or A*-based order-graph solvers) augmented with the same projected constraints. Assuming ancestral constraints align with the optimal model, a greedy or heuristic solver may be competitive and would better isolate the efficiency contribution of the proposed projections/oracle.
- Report the overhead of the MaxSAT stage and an ablation: EC-tree with/without projected constraints, and with only edge vs only ordering projections.

Clarity
- Generally very clear, but some minor wording issues:
  - Line 30: distinguish “oracle” from “omniscient oracle.”
  - Line 38: “empower the oracle” is informal; consider “tighten the heuristic provided by the oracle.”
  - Line 90: explicitly state that “optimize” means “minimize” the given score.
  - Line 310: clarify that 10–25% known ancestral constraints is atypically high in most applications.
- Consider adding intuition and small proofs/sketches for the MaxSAT encoding and edge-rules to improve readability.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Clear, well-executed extension of EC-tree to ancestral constraints.
- Sound pruning at the CPDAG level; neat use of MaxSAT to extract orders.
- Strong empirical speedups versus ILP on benchmarks.

Cons:
- Limited practical impact: ancestral constraints are few in practice and causal discovery often needs to allow hidden confounders.
- Baselines are not fully compelling; Gobnilp mismatch may inflate gains.
- Some technical details (Theorem 1 statement, MaxSAT rules/acyclicity encoding) need clarification; positive-edge inference likely weak in sparse regimes.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper is clearly written and technically sound in its main thrust, with interesting engineering choices and strong reported speedups. However, novelty and expected impact are moderate, some theoretical/encoding statements need tightening, and the experimental comparison does not fully isolate the contribution. With clarified theory/encoding, more realistic sparse-constraint experiments, and stronger baselines (e.g., GES with the same projected constraints), this could be a solid contribution.
