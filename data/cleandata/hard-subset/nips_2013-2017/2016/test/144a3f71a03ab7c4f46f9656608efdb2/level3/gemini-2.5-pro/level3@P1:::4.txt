This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data when given background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints. Such constraints, like "X is an ancestor of Y" or "X is not an ancestor of Y", are non-decomposable, meaning they cannot be evaluated locally for each variable's parent set. This makes them incompatible with many efficient, score-based learning algorithms (e.g., those based on dynamic programming) that rely on score decomposability.

The authors build upon a recent framework for exact structure learning that uses A* search over a space of equivalence classes (the EC tree) [Chen et al., 2016]. This framework can handle non-decomposable scores and constraints. The main contribution of this work is a novel method to "project" the non-decomposable ancestral constraints into a set of decomposable constraints, namely required/forbidden edges and constraints on the topological ordering. These projected constraints are then used to "empower the oracle"—that is, to provide a much tighter heuristic for the A* search, dramatically pruning the search space and improving efficiency. The method for inferring edge constraints is based on logical rules, while the inference of ordering constraints is cleverly formulated as a MaxSAT problem. The proposed approach is empirically compared against an Integer Linear Programming (ILP) baseline (GOBNILP), demonstrating orders-of-magnitude speedups on a variety of benchmark networks.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The core contribution—the projection of non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints (Section 5)—is novel and significant. It provides a principled way to bridge the gap between powerful, non-decomposable domain knowledge and efficient, decomposable search heuristics. This allows practitioners to leverage valuable background knowledge within a state-of-the-art exact learning framework, which is an important problem with practical implications.
*   **Technical Quality:** The proposed projection methods are technically sound. The theorems provided formalize the conditions under which edge and ordering constraints can be safely inferred from a set of ancestral constraints. The use of MaxSAT to find a maximal consistent set of ordering constraints is a clever and appropriate formulation for a non-trivial inference problem.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the technical preliminaries are explained concisely, and the main ideas are presented logically. The description of the method and the experimental setup is detailed enough to understand the work.
*   **Empirical Results:** The experiments are thorough and convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The speedups of several orders of magnitude over a competitive ILP baseline are impressive and clearly establish the practical utility of empowering the A* search oracle with projected constraints.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Generalizability of the Projection Method:** The primary technical novelty lies in the constraint projection technique. However, its benefit is almost exclusively demonstrated within the context of the EC-tree search algorithm. The results in Table 1 vs. Table 2 show that while the projection offers a massive speedup for the EC-tree method, its impact on the ILP solver is much less pronounced (and even introduces a slight overhead for n=12). This raises the question of how generally applicable this projection method is. The contribution would be significantly strengthened if the authors could show that the inferred decomposable constraints also provide a benefit to other types of algorithms, especially those that originally could not handle non-decomposable constraints at all (e.g., standard dynamic programming approaches). This would demonstrate that the projection method is a valuable contribution in its own right, independent of the EC-tree framework.

2.  **Analysis of the "Easy-Hard-Easy" Pattern:** The authors correctly note an "easy-hard-easy" trend in performance as the percentage of ancestral constraints (`p`) increases (Table 3). However, the paper does not offer a detailed explanation for this phenomenon. It would be valuable to discuss why this occurs. Presumably, for small `p`, the problem is close to unconstrained and the oracle is very accurate; for large `p`, the search space is small. The "hard" region in the middle is where the problem is highly constrained in complex ways that might reduce the heuristic's accuracy before the search space is sufficiently small. A deeper analysis of this behavior would be insightful. It also suggests that the efficiency of the EC-tree approach may be highly sensitive to the amount of domain knowledge, potentially requiring careful tuning of the number of constraints, whereas the ILP approach appears to benefit more monotonically.

3.  **Incomplete Experimental Comparison:** The experiments in Table 3 report the structural Hamming distance (SHD) to the ground-truth network for the proposed method, but not for the ILP baseline. While both are exact methods and should find the same optimal network (and thus have the same SHD), explicitly reporting the SHD for the ILP solver would serve as a sanity check and make the empirical comparison more complete.

4.  **Minor Presentation Suggestion:** The paper dedicates Section 4.1 to the BN graph, which serves as a conceptual predecessor to the EC tree. To improve focus, the authors could consider condensing or merging this section into the EC tree discussion (Section 4.2). This would free up space to provide a more detailed explanation of the A* search implementation within the EC-tree setting, which is more central to the paper's experimental methodology.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and practical problem: incorporating non-decomposable background knowledge into optimal BN structure learning.
*   Presents a novel and technically sound method for projecting ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints.
*   The proposed approach demonstrates a clear and dramatic performance improvement over a state-of-the-art baseline on standard benchmarks.
*   The paper is well-written and easy to follow.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The utility of the main technical contribution (the projection method) is primarily demonstrated for one specific learning algorithm, and its general applicability is not fully established.
*   The experimental analysis could be strengthened with a deeper discussion of performance characteristics and a more complete comparison of metrics (i.e., including SHD for the baseline).

**Conclusion:**

Overall, this is a strong paper with a novel contribution, solid technical foundations, and impressive empirical results. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and addressable. The proposed method represents a significant advance in making exact Bayesian network learning more practical, especially in domains where rich background knowledge is available. I recommend acceptance.
