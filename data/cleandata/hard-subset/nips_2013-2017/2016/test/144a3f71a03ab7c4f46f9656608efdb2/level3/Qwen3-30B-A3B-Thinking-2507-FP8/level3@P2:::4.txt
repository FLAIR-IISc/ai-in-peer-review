

# Review for "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints"

This paper addresses an important problem in Bayesian network structure learning: incorporating non-decomposable ancestral constraints (which specify cause-and-effect relationships between variables) into optimal structure learning. The authors build on the EC tree framework proposed by Chen et al. [2015, 2016], which enables optimal structure learning with non-decomposable scores, and introduce a novel method for projecting ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints that can be handled by existing oracles.

The paper makes a significant contribution by providing a theoretically sound method to transform non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints that can be used to improve the efficiency of structure learning. The core innovation is presented in Section 5, where the authors develop algorithms for inferring edge constraints (positive and negative) and ordering constraints from ancestral constraints. This enables the use of oracles designed for decomposable scores in the context of non-decomposable ancestral constraints, which is a substantial practical advancement.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with thorough theoretical development and comprehensive experimental evaluation. The authors demonstrate that their approach is orders-of-magnitude more efficient than ILP-based methods (GOBNILP) across a range of network sizes and constraint proportions, particularly when constraints are projected (Table 1). The results also show that a relatively small number of ancestral constraints (10-25%) can have a similar impact on model accuracy as significantly increasing training data size.

**Strengths:**
- High technical quality: The theoretical framework is sound, with formal proofs for constraint projection (Theorems 2-4) and clear experimental design.
- Significant practical impact: The method demonstrates how domain knowledge (in the form of ancestral constraints) can dramatically improve learning efficiency, potentially reducing the need for large datasets.
- Strong empirical results: The experiments are well-designed and show consistent, substantial improvements in efficiency across multiple benchmark networks.
- Clarity: The paper is well-organized and clearly explains complex concepts, making it accessible to NIPS readers.

**Weaknesses:**
- **Limited generalization evidence:** The paper focuses on the EC tree approach and doesn't provide sufficient evidence that the constraint projection method generalizes to other learning approaches. Table 2 shows that ILP benefits inconsistently from projected constraints, raising questions about whether the projection method is truly broadly applicable.
- **Insufficient explanation of the "easy-hard-easy" pattern:** Table 2 shows an interesting pattern where performance first improves then worsens and then improves again as the proportion of ancestral constraints increases. The authors do not adequately explain this phenomenon or compare it with ILP's behavior.
- **Incomplete comparison:** The structural Hamming distance (SHD) is reported only for the EC tree approach in Table 3, but not compared with the ILP approach, making it difficult to assess the trade-off between efficiency and accuracy.
- **Redundant content:** Section 4.1 on BN graphs is largely redundant given the focus on EC trees, and the paper would benefit from expanding the explanation of EC trees and A* in the experimental context as suggested in the key points.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- The paper addresses an important practical problem in Bayesian network structure learning.
- The constraint projection method is novel, theoretically sound, and demonstrates significant empirical improvements.
- The paper is well-written with strong technical content and thorough evaluation.
- The approach has clear practical significance for incorporating domain knowledge into structure learning.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- The paper does not sufficiently demonstrate that the constraint projection method generalizes beyond the EC tree approach.
- The "easy-hard-easy" pattern in Table 2 is not adequately explained, raising questions about the robustness of the approach.
- The comparison between EC tree and ILP approaches is incomplete without SHD comparisons for both methods.
- The paper could be improved by removing redundant content (Section 4.1) and expanding on the EC tree implementation details.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. The constraint projection method is novel and useful, and the empirical results are compelling. However, the paper would benefit from addressing the weaknesses noted above, particularly by providing stronger evidence for the generalizability of the projection method and better explaining the performance patterns observed in the experiments. The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it presents a technically sound approach with clear practical implications for Bayesian network structure learning.

The paper meets all NIPS quality criteria: it's technically sound with strong theoretical and empirical support, clearly written, original in its constraint projection approach, and significant in demonstrating how domain knowledge can substantially improve learning efficiency. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the concerns about generalizability and completeness of the experimental comparison.
