This paper addresses the important problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data when incorporating non-decomposable ancestral constraints. This is a practical challenge, as many state-of-the-art optimal learning algorithms rely on decomposable scores and cannot natively handle such constraints. The main contribution, concentrated in Section 5, is a novel method for projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints into a set of decomposable edge and topological-ordering constraints. This allows the authors to "empower" oracles designed for decomposable scores within a broader search framework (EC trees), effectively bridging a significant gap between existing methods and practical needs. This work builds upon the EC tree framework previously introduced by Chen et al. (2016) and presents a compelling alternative to general-purpose solvers like Integer Linear Programming (ILP).

The paper's primary strengths are its originality and clarity. The projection method is a clever and significant technical contribution that could be widely applicable. The paper is well-written, clearly explaining the theoretical underpinnings of the projection and providing empirical evidence that the proposed method can be orders of magnitude more efficient than a comparable ILP-based approach.

Despite the strong contribution, there are several concerns that should be addressed to strengthen the paper. First, while the core claim is that the projection method empowers oracles, the experimental results only show this within the EC-tree framework. To fully validate the generalization of this projection, the authors should provide evidence that it directly improves the performance of methods that originally cannot handle non-decomposable constraints (e.g., standalone DP or A* algorithms).

Second, the experimental results in Table 2 reveal an "easy-hard-easy" performance pattern as the proportion of ancestral constraints increases. The authors should provide an explanation for this phenomenon. Does it indicate that the EC tree method's performance is highly sensitive to the quantity of domain knowledge, suggesting that the number of constraints may need careful tuning to achieve optimal results, in contrast to the more monotonic improvements seen with ILP?

Finally, the empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive. Table 2 reports the Structural Hamming Distance (SHD) for EC trees only. Including a comparison of SHD with the ILP baseline would provide a more complete assessment of the trade-offs between the methods in terms of both solution quality and runtime. As a minor suggestion for improving focus, the authors could consider condensing the background on BN graphs (Section 4.1) to expand the explanation of the specific EC tree and A* implementation used in the experiments.
