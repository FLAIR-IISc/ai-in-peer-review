- Paper tackles optimal Bayesian network structure learning with ancestral constraints, searching CPDAG space via A* and an admissible heuristic derived from a decomposable-score oracle.
- Key technical contributions: sound pruning of the EC tree using ancestral constraint satisfaction tests, and projection of non-decomposable ancestral constraints to decomposable edge and ordering constraints (MaxSAT).
- Theoretical results (pruning correctness, edge entailment tests, MaxSAT encoding) are interesting and appear technically sound if proofs are complete.
- Novel idea: using projected decomposable constraints to tighten an admissible heuristic for exhaustive search is original and well-motivated.
- Empirical section is confusing: table comparisons are unclear about which methods receive which projected constraints and what baselines are used.
- Important missing ablation: compare (EC + new work) directly to EC alone (same oracle) on identical instances to isolate the projection/pruning benefit.
- Experimental narrative fails to separate effects of pruning, projection, and MaxSAT overhead; roles of Tables 2 and 3 are not clearly explained.
- Requested experimental metrics not reported: node expansions, heuristic tightness, and MaxSAT runtime.
- Proofs and complexity assumptions need clarification (e.g., closure to a “maximum” ancestral set) and some details are deferred.
- Scalability is limited in experiments and practical impact would be strengthened by tests with noisy/misstated constraints and larger n.
- Reproducibility details (code, parameters) are sparse and should be provided.
- Pros: elegant integration of non-decomposable knowledge into admissible heuristic search, sound EC pruning, useful entailment tests and MaxSAT reduction, strong practical potential.
- Cons: unclear experimental comparisons, missing EC-vs-EC ablation, limited scale and robustness tests, and some deferred proof/complexity/reproducibility details.
- Recommendation: promising contribution likely acceptable after clarifying and strengthening the experimental evaluation, especially the EC + new work vs EC baseline.
