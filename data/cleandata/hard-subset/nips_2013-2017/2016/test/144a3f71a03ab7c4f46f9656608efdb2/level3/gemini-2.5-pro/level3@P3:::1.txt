This paper addresses the important problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures subject to ancestral constraints. Such constraints are non-decomposable, which poses a significant challenge for many state-of-the-art exact structure learning algorithms that rely on decomposable scores. The authors build upon a recent A*-based search framework that navigates a space of Completed Partially Directed Acyclic Graphs (CPDAGs), represented as an EC tree. The core idea is to handle the non-decomposable ancestral constraints in two ways: first, by pruning nodes in the EC tree that provably violate the constraints, and second, by "projecting" the ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints. These projected constraints are then passed to the A* search's heuristic oracle (in this case, URLEARNING), making the heuristic more informed and tightening the search bounds. Empirically, the method is shown to be orders of magnitude faster than GOBNILP, an integer linear programming (ILP) based solver.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance:** The problem is well-motivated and practically relevant. Incorporating rich background knowledge, such as direct or indirect causal relationships expressed as ancestral constraints, is crucial for learning meaningful models from data, especially when data is scarce.
2.  **Novelty:** The high-level approach is elegant. Integrating non-decomposable constraints into a best-first search framework by both pruning the search space and simultaneously empowering the heuristic function is a powerful and novel combination of techniques for this problem.
3.  **Performance:** The reported speedups over the GOBNILP baseline are impressive, suggesting that for this class of problems, this search-based approach is a very promising direction. The "easy-hard-easy" pattern observed as the number of constraints increases provides interesting insight into the problem's complexity.

**Weaknesses and Detailed Comments:**

The paper's primary weaknesses lie in its lack of clarity regarding key technical details and the unconvincing nature of its empirical evaluation.

**Quality and Clarity:**

1.  **Unclear Proof for Pruning:** The key theoretical result that justifies pruning the search space, which appears to be Lemma 3 (referenced through Theorem 1 in the main text), is not clearly proven. The proof sketch states, "By the EC tree edge generation rules, Gk also contains edge Z -> W," but this assertion is non-obvious and requires substantial justification. The EC tree generation rules are complex, and it is not immediately clear why adding a new leaf node would preserve a specific edge from a representative DAG in the parent CPDAG. Without a clear and complete proof for this central lemma, the soundness of the proposed pruning strategy is in question.

2.  **Poorly Explained Constraint Projection:** The section on "Projecting Constraints" is confusing and lacks necessary detail. The use of phrases like "can infer" is misleading, particularly for ordering constraints. The paper implies that the inferred ordering constraints are logically entailed by the ancestral constraints. However, the MaxSAT formulation seems to be designed to find a *maximally large set of consistent* ordering constraints, which is not the same thing. For example, the DAG `X -> Y` and `Z -> W` is consistent with ancestral constraints `X ⇏ Z` and `Z ⇏ X`. From these, one might "infer" ordering constraints `X < Z` or `Z < X`. Adding both is inconsistent, but adding one might be valid, yet it prunes valid DAGs (e.g., adding `X < Z` prunes any valid DAG with a topological sort `Z, ..., X`). This critical distinction between entailment and consistency is not discussed, and the paper should clarify that it is potentially adding constraints that are not strictly entailed but are consistent with all solutions.

3.  **Insufficient Detail on MaxSAT:** The MaxSAT formulation itself is described at too high a level. The clauses are presented abstractly, making it difficult for a reader to reproduce the method or fully grasp the logic. For a core component of the proposed method, the description is insufficient.

**Quality of Evaluation:**

1.  **Weak ILP Baseline:** The experimental comparison to GOBNILP is weakened by the choice of baseline. The performance of GOBNILP is known to degrade significantly when the maximum parent set size is unbounded, which appears to be the case here. It is therefore unclear if the observed speedup is due to the proposed method's superior handling of ancestral constraints, or simply because heuristic A* search (the basis of the oracle) generally outperforms ILP in this unbounded setting. The experiments do not successfully isolate the contribution of the ancestral constraint handling from the underlying strengths/weaknesses of the base algorithms.

2.  **Outdated ILP Encoding for Ancestral Constraints:** The paper compares against an encoding of ancestral constraints from Cussens (2008), which is a straightforward but relatively weak formulation for ILP. A much stronger and tighter ILP formulation would introduce explicit binary variables `A(X,Y)` to represent "X is an ancestor of Y" and link them to the family-selection variables `I(Z,U)` through constraints that enforce transitivity (`A(X,Y) ∧ A(Y,Z) ⇒ A(X,Z)`) and connect ancestry to parentage (e.g., `I(Y, U) with X ∈ U ⇒ A(X,Y)`). Comparing against a known weak encoding overstates the empirical contribution. While the proposed method might still be faster than a state-of-the-art ILP formulation, the current comparison is not convincing enough to support the strong claims made.

**Missing Related Work:**

The paper fails to cite or compare against the highly relevant work of van Beek and Hoffmann (CP 2015), "Machine learning of Bayesian networks using constraint programming." Their CP-based solver is another state-of-the-art exact method that has been shown to outperform GOBNILP on many benchmarks. Constraint programming is naturally suited to handling complex logical constraints, and their framework could likely be adapted to incorporate ancestral constraints. Any serious empirical evaluation of a new exact method in this area should include this system as a baseline.

In summary, this paper presents a promising and elegant idea for a difficult problem. However, the execution is flawed. The lack of clarity in key technical proofs and descriptions, combined with an experimental evaluation that relies on weak and potentially ill-suited baselines, significantly undermines the paper's contribution. The work is not yet a complete or convincing scientific contribution in its current form.
