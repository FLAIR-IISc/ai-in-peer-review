This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data when given background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints (e.g., X is an ancestor of Y). The key challenge is that ancestral constraints are non-decomposable, making them incompatible with many state-of-the-art exact learning algorithms that rely on decomposable scores for efficiency. The authors build upon a recently proposed A*-based search framework over equivalence classes (the EC tree), which can, in principle, handle non-decomposable constraints. The main contribution of this work is a novel method to "empower" the admissible heuristic (the oracle) used in this search. This is achieved by inferring or "projecting" decomposable edge and ordering constraints from the non-decomposable ancestral constraints. These inferred constraints can be directly utilized by the oracle, leading to a tighter heuristic and a more efficient search. The approach is compared experimentally to an Integer Linear Programming (ILP) formulation, showing significant performance improvements.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a significant and practical problem. Incorporating rich prior knowledge is crucial for learning accurate models, especially with limited data. The proposed method for handling ancestral constraints—a particularly expressive form of prior knowledge—is novel and elegant. The core idea of projecting non-decomposable constraints into a decomposable form to tighten a heuristic for an exact search is a clever and potentially generalizable contribution to constrained search.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The work appears technically sound. It provides a formal basis for how ancestral constraints can be used to prune the EC tree search space (Theorem 1) and presents sound algorithms for inferring decomposable edge constraints (Theorems 2 and 3) and ordering constraints (Theorem 4, via a MaxSAT encoding). This theoretical grounding provides confidence in the correctness of the approach.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly structured. The motivation is well-established, the background on existing methods is sufficient, and the proposed approach is explained logically. The distinction between decomposable and non-decomposable constraints, which is central to the paper, is well-articulated.

**Weaknesses:**

The primary weakness of this paper lies in its experimental evaluation, which is confusing and fails to provide a clear ablation study to support the central claim of the paper.

1.  **Missing Baseline Comparison:** The core claim is that projecting ancestral constraints into decomposable ones empowers the oracle and makes the EC tree search more efficient. To validate this claim, the most critical experiment is a direct comparison of the proposed method `(EC tree + constraint projection)` against a baseline of `(EC tree without constraint projection)`. This baseline would involve running the A* search on the EC tree, pruning nodes that violate the ancestral constraints, but using an oracle that is *unaware* of the projected decomposable constraints. Such a comparison would directly measure the performance gain attributable to the proposed projection technique. This experiment is missing from the paper.

2.  **Confusing Experimental Setup:** Table 1 compares `(EC tree + projection)` to `(GOBNILP + projection)`. While this shows that the EC tree framework is superior to ILP even when both are given the same additional information, it does not isolate the contribution of the projection itself. The text mentions a comparison between Table 1 and a (non-existent) Table 2 where projected constraints are withheld. The text states: "When we compare Table 2 to Table 1, we see that for the EC tree, the projection of constraints has a significant impact on the efficiency of learning (often by several orders of magnitude)." This is a crucial result, but it is not presented in a clear, tabular form, making it difficult for the reader to verify and analyze. The experimental section needs to be revised to present its findings clearly.

3.  **Unclear Role of Table 3:** While Table 3 provides interesting results on scalability and the impact of constraints on learning accuracy (structural Hamming distance), its connection to the main contribution feels secondary. It demonstrates the utility of the overall system but does not further substantiate the claim about the effectiveness of the constraint projection method itself.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement:**

The authors argue that their method dramatically improves efficiency. The current experiments show that their overall system (EC tree + projection) is much faster than an alternative system (ILP + projection). However, the experiments must be restructured to show that the *projection technique* is the reason for the efficiency gain *within their chosen framework*.

I strongly recommend the authors revise the experiments to include a clear, central table that compares the following two configurations on the same datasets:
*   **System A (Baseline):** The EC tree search algorithm where ancestral constraints are used only to prune invalid CPDAGs from the search space. The A* heuristic/oracle would be called without any of the inferred decomposable constraints.
*   **System B (Proposed):** The full proposed method, where the oracle is empowered with the decomposable constraints inferred via projection.

Presenting the runtimes of System A vs. System B would provide a direct and convincing measure of the "empowerment" that the projection technique provides. This is the single most important change that would strengthen the paper.

While the GOBNILP comparison is useful for context, the argument that GOBNILP might benefit more or less from the projection is less relevant than demonstrating the absolute improvement on the state-of-the-art framework the authors have chosen to build upon. Since the EC tree approach is shown to be significantly better than GOBNILP, proving that the proposed technique makes the *best* method even better is the most compelling result.

In summary, the paper presents a strong, novel idea for a significant problem. However, the experimental evidence for the effectiveness of this core idea is presented in a confusing and incomplete manner. With a revised experimental section that includes the crucial baseline comparison, this would be a very strong paper.
