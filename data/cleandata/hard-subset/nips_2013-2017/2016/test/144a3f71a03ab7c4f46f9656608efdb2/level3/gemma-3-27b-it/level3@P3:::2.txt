This paper presents a method for learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints, building upon recent work leveraging oracles for decomposable scores. The core idea – utilizing ancestral constraints to infer decomposable constraints that empower these oracles – is a logical and promising approach to address a known limitation of standard structure learning algorithms. The motivation is well-explained: ancestral constraints are practically important but difficult to incorporate directly into methods relying on decomposable scoring functions. The connection to previous work, particularly [Chen et al., 2015, 2016] and related ILP approaches, is clearly established.

However, the experimental evaluation is considerably confusing and detracts from the paper’s impact. Table 1 compares a system combining the EC tree framework (EC) with the proposed constraint projection (“new work”) against GOBNILP combined with a different set of constraints ("new work'"). This makes it difficult to isolate the benefit of the proposed method; is the improvement due to the EC tree, the constraint projection, or both? More crucially, the relevance of comparing against GOBNILP with *different* projected constraints is questionable. The authors acknowledge that EC is superior to GOBNILP in general, and thus a more direct comparison of EC *with* and *without* the proposed projection is essential. This is the core contribution, and its impact needs to be demonstrated clearly. Table 3’s role is also unclear. While it explores scalability and the impact of constraint density, its connection to the central claim – the benefit of projecting ancestral constraints – isn’t sufficiently highlighted. The authors state the "easy-hard-easy" trend, but fail to explain *why* projecting constraints helps in the “easy” regimes and is less beneficial in the “hard” regime.

The authors address reviewer requests for a direct comparison of EC + new work vs. EC alone implicitly by stating the improvements in Table 1 result from the projected constraints, but a dedicated experiment showcasing this directly would be substantially more convincing. Even if GOBNILP benefits more from the "new work," the fact that EC is fundamentally superior to GOBNILP means demonstrating the value added to EC is the most important result.

**Strengths:**

* **Technically Sound:** The approach of projecting ancestral constraints onto decomposable constraints is a sound theoretical idea. The theorems presented (1-4) establish the correctness of the pruning and constraint inference steps.
* **Clear Motivation:** The problem of incorporating non-decomposable constraints into Bayesian network structure learning is well-motivated and relevant.
* **Connection to Prior Work:** The paper builds on a strong foundation of recent work in optimal structure learning ([Chen et al., 2015, 2016]) and appropriately cites relevant literature.
* **Scalability:** The experiments demonstrate that the approach scales reasonably well to networks with up to 20 variables, comparable to other modern structure learning methods.
* **Potential Impact:** Leveraging background knowledge to improve structure learning efficiency is a valuable contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Confusing Experiments:** The experimental setup and comparison in Table 1 are poorly designed and make it difficult to assess the true contribution of the proposed method.  The comparison to GOBNILP with a different constraint set is misleading.
* **Lack of Direct Comparison:**  The paper lacks a direct, controlled comparison of EC with and without the proposed constraint projection.
* **Unclear Role of Table 3:** The relevance of Table 3 to the central claim of the paper is not clearly articulated.
* **MaxSAT Complexity:** The use of MaxSAT for inferring ordering constraints might be computationally expensive for very large networks, although this isn’t empirically demonstrated in the paper.
* **Limited Exploration of Constraint Density:** While Table 3 explores constraint density, a more systematic investigation of the relationship between constraint accuracy/noise and performance would be beneficial.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses an important problem in Bayesian network structure learning.
* The proposed approach is theoretically sound and logically well-motivated.
* The experimental results, despite their flaws, suggest potential performance improvements.
* The work builds upon a strong foundation of recent research.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The confusing experimental setup significantly weakens the paper’s claims.
* The lack of a direct comparison between EC with and without constraint projection is a major flaw.
* The unclear role of Table 3 detracts from the overall clarity of the evaluation.




**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a promising approach for incorporating ancestral constraints into Bayesian network structure learning. However, the confusing experimental evaluation and lack of a direct comparison severely limit its impact.  While the technical contributions are sound, the paper needs substantial revision to clearly demonstrate the benefits of the proposed method. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to resubmit after addressing the experimental concerns. Specifically, the authors *must* include a controlled experiment comparing EC with and without the constraint projection, and clarify the role of Table 3 in demonstrating the effectiveness of the method.
