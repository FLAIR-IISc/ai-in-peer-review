Summary and relation to prior work
This paper addresses optimal Bayesian network (BN) structure learning under background knowledge expressed as ancestral (path) constraints. Mainstream exact score-based methods rely on decomposable scores and cannot directly accommodate such non-decomposable constraints; ILP/CP-based formulations can, but may be slow. Building on Chen et al. (2015, 2016), the authors search in the BN/EC spaces using A* with an oracle that solves decomposable subproblems (e.g., URLearning). They integrate ancestral constraints in two ways: (i) sound pruning of the EC tree by removing CPDAG nodes that cannot realize any DAG consistent with the constraints (Theorem 1), and (ii) “projecting” non-decomposable ancestral constraints to decomposable edge and ordering constraints to empower the oracle. For edges, they propose entailment rules (Theorems 2–3) after closing the ancestral constraints under transitivity and certain inconsistency rules; for orderings, they formulate a MaxSAT problem to infer a maximally large consistent set of X < Y constraints (with sufficient conditions summarized in Theorem 4). Empirically, they compare against GOBNILP (ILP) and report large speedups, and explore the effect of data size, number of variables, and fraction of ancestral constraints.

The work connects to prior BN search spaces (order graph, dynamic programming, A*; Koivisto & Sood 2004; Silander & Myllymäki 2006; Yuan & Malone 2013), ILP (Cussens 2008; Bartlett & Cussens 2015), constraint-based/path constraints (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos 2012/2013), and to the BN/EC search spaces (Chen et al. 2015/2016). The novelty lies in handling non-decomposable ancestral constraints within EC-tree search and in projecting them into decomposable constraints to strengthen the heuristic oracle.

Strengths
- Clear problem motivation: path constraints are practically important and non-decomposable.
- Conceptual advance: integrates non-decomposable constraints with an EC-tree search and an admissible heuristic via constraint projection. The pruning criterion on CPDAGs is simple and sound.
- Technical contributions: edge-constraint entailment rules (necessary and sufficient as stated), MaxSAT formulation to extract a large consistent set of ordering constraints, and the use of these to tighten the oracle and thus A*’s h.
- Empirical promise: substantial runtime gains over ILP on standard BN benchmarks under a range of variable counts and constraint densities; informative easy–hard–easy behavior with respect to constraint density.
- Relevance to NIPS community: contributes to exact structure learning with background knowledge and to combining global search with constraint inference.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Experimental clarity/ablations: The presentation of Tables 1–3 is confusing. It appears Table 1 compares (EC+projection/new) to (GOBNILP+projection/new’), Table 2 compares both without projection, and Table 3 studies scaling. However:
  - The most relevant comparison for isolating this paper’s contribution is an explicit ablation of EC search with pruning but without projection to the oracle (EC alone) versus EC+projection (this work). Please add this comparison across n, N, p to quantify how much the projected constraints improve the oracle/heuristic and total runtime and node expansions.
  - Separate edge-only projection vs ordering-only vs both to show their individual effects.
  - Clarify the role of Table 3 relative to Tables 1–2 (what configuration is used, and are projections enabled?).
- Soundness/completeness details: Theorem 1 relies on deciding whether a CPDAG can realize a path constraint, and the text suggests enumerating class(P) if the simple tests are inconclusive. Enumeration can be exponential; please clarify:
  - How often does enumeration occur in practice? Provide statistics.
  - Can you use polynomial-time PDAG reachability/orientability tests (e.g., via Meek rules and covered edge reversals) to avoid enumeration? Any caching?
- Ordering projection via MaxSAT:
  - Theorem 4 provides sufficient conditions; hence the selected set of orderings may be conservative. Report how many orderings are inferred vs potentially entailed, and the runtime of EVASOLVER relative to total search time.
  - Analyze sensitivity to MaxSAT solver choice and scalability as n grows.
- Assumptions and robustness:
  - The “maximum set” closure procedure for ancestral constraints (rules “omitted for space”) is crucial for Theorems 2–3. Please include the exact rules, complexity, and guarantees (termination, correctness).
  - Investigate noisy/inconsistent constraints (common in practice). How does pruning/projection behave when a small fraction of constraints is wrong? Can the framework be extended to soft constraints?
- Generality and scope: Experiments use BDeu with ESS=1 and no parent-set size limit. Report whether results hold for BIC/MDL and for parent-set caps (which often help ILP).
- Reproducibility/clarity: Several citation and typesetting glitches; ensure consistent notation for constraints and clarify that “closed/maximum” sets are transitive closures with implied negative constraints.

Quality
- Theoretical components are mostly soundly stated; however, key derivations and closure rules are omitted and CPDAG feasibility sometimes falls back to enumeration. Empirical claims are broadly supported but need the EC-vs-EC+projection ablation and clearer reporting (node expansions, memory, time splits for projection/MaxSAT vs search).

Clarity
- The main ideas are well organized, but the experimental section needs clearer baselines, captions, and explanations of Tables 1–3. Include pseudo-code or precise descriptions for the edge-entailment and closure procedures.

Originality
- Novel combination: EC-tree pruning by ancestral constraints plus projection of non-decomposable constraints into decomposable constraints to strengthen a decomposable oracle. Prior work has used path constraints in constraint-based learning and in ILP/CP, but not in this EC+oracle framework.

Significance
- Useful in settings with credible causal background knowledge. The reported speedups over ILP are substantial. While the absolute scale (≈20 variables without parent limits) matches prior exact BN learning, the approach should be impactful and could be adopted by practitioners who already use decomposable-score solvers.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important, under-supported form of background knowledge.
- Solid integration of theory (pruning/projection) with practice (A* over EC, oracle).
- Strong empirical performance relative to ILP; likely to influence exact BN learning workflows.

Arguments against acceptance
- Missing key ablations (EC vs EC+projection) and some methodological details.
- Potential exponential fallback (EC-class enumeration) not quantified.
- Evaluation uses randomly sampled constraints; no tests with noisy or real expert constraints.

Requested changes for camera-ready
- Add EC-alone vs EC+projection ablation (and edge-only vs ordering-only).
- Provide details and proof sketches for the ancestral-closure rules; quantify costs of CPDAG enumeration and MaxSAT.
- Clarify experimental setup/tables and report search statistics.
- Discuss robustness to noisy/inconsistent constraints and other scores.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept (assuming the authors address the ablation and clarity issues). Confidence: High.
