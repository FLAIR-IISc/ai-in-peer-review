- Presents a novel method to learn Bayesian networks optimally under ancestral constraints by projecting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints.  
- Leverages a framework for optimal structure learning with non-decomposable scores and uses an oracle for decomposable scores to improve efficiency.  
- Key technical contribution is the projection method (Section 5) that enables decomposable constraints usable by standard optimizers.  
- Uses EC trees and the A* algorithm in the experimental setup and explains theory and methods clearly.  
- Experiments show orders-of-magnitude efficiency gains over alternative frameworks (e.g., ILP) and that the projection significantly improves EC-tree efficiency.  
- Paper is well-written, with thorough problem review and clear explanations of challenges with non-decomposable constraints.  
- Concern: Table 3 improvements appear for EC trees but not for ILP, casting doubt on whether the projection generalizes to other learning approaches.  
- Suggestion: Provide evidence that inferred decomposable constraints also improve performance/efficiency of methods that originally cannot handle non-decomposable constraints.  
- Observation: Table 2 shows an easy-hard-easy pattern as ancestral-constraint proportion increases; authors should explain this pattern and whether EC trees are highly sensitive to constraint amount.  
- Concern: The sensitivity to number of ancestral constraints may require careful tuning to get ideal EC-tree performance.  
- Missing comparison: SHD is reported only for EC trees; add SHD comparison with ILP for a more complete evaluation.  
- Presentation suggestion: Remove or shorten Section 4.1 on BN graph and expand explanations of EC trees and the A* algorithm in the experimental context.  
- Overall assessment: Technically sound and well-supported by theory and experiments, with originality and significance rated 8/10 and clarity 9/10.  
- Arguments for acceptance: Novel, effective approach targeting an important problem and demonstrating significant empirical improvements.  
- Arguments against acceptance: Questionable generalizability of the projection to other methods, missing comparative SHD results, and some sections need improvement.
