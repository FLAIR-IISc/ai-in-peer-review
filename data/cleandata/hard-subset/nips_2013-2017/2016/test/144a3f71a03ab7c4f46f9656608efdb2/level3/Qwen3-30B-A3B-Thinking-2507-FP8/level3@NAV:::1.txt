

This paper proposes a novel approach for learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints using EC trees (equivalence class trees) with A* search, leveraging an oracle for decomposable scores. The key innovation lies in inferring decomposable edge and ordering constraints from non-decomposable ancestral constraints to empower the oracle. The authors demonstrate orders-of-magnitude speedups over ILP-based approaches like GOBNILP, suggesting that ancestral constraints can significantly improve structure learning efficiency.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the literature on Bayesian network structure learning by addressing a practical problem (incorporating background knowledge) that has been largely neglected in optimal learning frameworks. The EC tree representation offers a more compact search space than the BN graph, and the constraint inference technique is theoretically sound for edge constraints (Theorems 2-3). The empirical results are compelling, showing that ancestral constraints can substantially reduce search space size and improve learning accuracy.

However, several critical weaknesses undermine the paper's impact. First, Lemma 3, which enables pruning with ancestral constraints, lacks a clear proof and requires better justification. The explanation of how EC tree edges are generated (e.g., "Gk also contains edge Z → W") is insufficiently detailed. More seriously, the treatment of inferred ordering constraints is misleading: the paper states "can infer" without clarifying that authors add consistent but not necessarily entailed ordering constraints. The MAXSAT approach for selecting ordering constraints is poorly explained, with inadequate rationale for the chosen formulation.

The experimental comparison is problematic. The authors compare against GOBNILP with ancestral constraints encoded via Cussens (2008), a weak ILP formulation. A tighter ILP formulation with transitivity constraints would be more appropriate. The comparison to this weak encoding weakens the empirical claim, as GOBNILP's poor performance might stem from general weaknesses with unbounded parent sets rather than ancestral constraints specifically. The omission of van Beek and Hoffmann (CP 2015), which often outperforms GOBNILP and could be adapted to ancestral constraints, is a significant oversight.

While the core idea is promising and the empirical results are impressive, the theoretical gaps, unclear explanations, and suboptimal experimental comparisons prevent the paper from making a fully convincing contribution. The work would benefit from addressing these issues before being considered for publication.
