

This paper addresses the problem of learning Bayesian network structures with ancestral constraints, which are non-decomposable and thus challenging for most existing structure learning approaches that rely on decomposable scores. The authors propose a novel framework that leverages the EC tree search space (introduced in Chen et al. 2016) and an oracle for decomposable scores, by translating non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints that can be efficiently handled by the oracle. This approach is evaluated against ILP-based methods (specifically GOBNILP) and demonstrates orders-of-magnitude improvements in efficiency.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it provides a sound method for pruning the EC tree using ancestral constraints, which is theoretically well-founded. Second, it introduces a practical method for inferring decomposable constraints from non-decomposable ancestral constraints, using MaxSAT to efficiently derive ordering constraints. The experimental evaluation on standard benchmark networks (ALARM, ANDES, CHILD, CPCS54, HEPAR2) shows significant performance gains over the ILP baseline, particularly as problem size increases (n=12 to n=20 variables). The authors also demonstrate that even a small percentage (10-25%) of ancestral constraints can substantially improve model accuracy, comparable to increasing training data size from 512 to 8192 samples.

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical contributions. The connection to previous work is appropriately established, particularly referencing Chen et al. 2015, 2016 for the EC tree framework and the use of oracles. The approach builds on recent advances in optimal structure learning with non-decomposable scores, making it relevant to the NIPS community.

The strengths of the paper include its theoretical soundness (with well-proven theorems), clear presentation of the constraint projection method, and impressive empirical results. The framework is conceptually straightforward and represents a significant practical advance for structure learning with background knowledge.

However, several weaknesses should be noted. The significance of the work is somewhat limited by the artificial nature of the problem setting. Ancestral constraints are rarely available in practice in the form assumed by the paper, as they would typically motivate broader causal discovery methods that can handle unobserved confounders. The paper's claim that "a relatively small number of ancestral constraints (say 10%-25%) can have a similar impact" is misleading, as 10-25% of all possible ancestral relations (n(n-1)/2) represents extensive prior knowledge that would be unusual in most applications. The paper would benefit from discussing more realistic scenarios.

The comparison to GOBNILP is not entirely fair, as GOBNILP was not designed specifically for ancestral constraints. The authors should compare against more appropriate baselines, such as greedy algorithms like GES that can incorporate ancestral constraints. The paper's claim of "orders-of-magnitude more efficient" may partly reflect this mismatch rather than the inherent superiority of their approach.

Several technical issues require clarification. Theorem 1's statement that "a CPDAG P satisfies ancestral constraints A iff its descendants satisfy A" is problematic because a CPDAG may satisfy constraints that its descendant DAGs don't (or vice versa). The authors should clarify that they mean "the set of constraints satisfied by the CPDAG is a subset of those satisfied by its descendant DAGs" or similar. The acyclicity rule (line 267) is poorly named and explained, as it doesn't clearly capture acyclicity.

Theorem 3 is unlikely to produce many positive edge implications in practice, as the conditions for inferring X→Y are very restrictive (requiring that A dictates X can reach Y and no path through any other Z exists). This limits the practical utility of the edge constraint inference.

The paper's claim of novelty (3/5) is reasonable within the narrow context of optimal structure learning with ancestral constraints, but the broader relevance to causal discovery is limited. The approach doesn't address the more common problem of discovering causal structure from data with unobserved confounders.

In summary, this is a technically sound paper that makes a valuable contribution to the specific problem of optimal Bayesian network structure learning with ancestral constraints. The method is well-designed and empirically validated, with impressive efficiency gains. However, the practical significance is somewhat limited by the artificial nature of the constraints and the unrealistic expectation of having 10-25% of all possible ancestral relations available. The paper would benefit from more realistic baselines, clearer theoretical statements, and a more nuanced discussion of the practical applicability of the approach. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the theoretical and practical concerns noted above.
