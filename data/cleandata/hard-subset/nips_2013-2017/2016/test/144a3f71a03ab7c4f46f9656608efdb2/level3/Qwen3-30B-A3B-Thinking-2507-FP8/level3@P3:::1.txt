

# Review of "Learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints" (NIPS 2016)

This paper addresses the important problem of optimal Bayesian network structure learning under ancestral constraints, which are non-decomposable and thus challenging for most existing methods. The authors propose a framework that leverages the EC tree search space (introduced in Chen et al. 2016) and an oracle for decomposable scores. Their key insight is to infer decomposable edge and ordering constraints from ancestral constraints, which can then be passed to the oracle to significantly improve search efficiency. The approach is grounded in the recently proposed framework for optimal structure learning with non-decomposable scores (Chen et al. 2015, 2016) and builds upon the observation that ancestral constraints can be efficiently handled through pruning of the EC tree.

## Strengths

The paper makes a significant contribution by addressing a practically important problem (incorporating background knowledge) that is largely overlooked in the literature. The EC tree approach offers a novel way to handle non-decomposable constraints by leveraging the structure of Markov equivalence classes. The empirical results demonstrate substantial speedups (orders of magnitude) over ILP-based approaches like GOBNILP, which is impressive. The paper also provides a clear theoretical foundation for constraint projection and demonstrates how ancestral constraints can be translated into decomposable constraints for the oracle.

## Weaknesses

The paper has several significant weaknesses that undermine the strength of its claims:

1. **Lemma 3 and Pruning Justification**: Lemma 3 is central to the pruning technique but lacks a rigorous proof. The paper states that "a CPDAG P satisfies ancestral constraints A iff its descendants satisfy A" without providing sufficient justification. The proof appears to be incomplete, particularly regarding the case where ancestral constraints are not immediately violated by the CPDAG but may be violated by some structures in its equivalence class. The authors need to provide a more detailed proof or at least clarify the conditions under which this lemma holds.

2. **EC Tree Edge Generation Statement**: The statement "By the EC tree edge generation rules, Gk also contains edge Z → W" (Section 4.3) is presented without explanation. This is critical to understanding how ancestral constraints are enforced during tree construction, but the paper fails to clarify the relationship between the CPDAG edge generation rules and the ancestral constraints. This omission makes it difficult to verify the soundness of the pruning procedure.

3. **Inferred Constraints Treatment**: The paper's treatment of "inferred" ordering constraints is misleading. The authors state they "can infer" ordering constraints from ancestral constraints, but fail to clarify that they are adding *consistent* (not necessarily *entailed*) ordering constraints. This distinction is crucial for understanding the method's theoretical guarantees. The MAXSAT approach for selecting ordering constraints is not sufficiently explained - it's unclear how the MAXSAT formulation ensures that the inferred constraints are both consistent and maximally informative.

4. **Comparison to GOBNILP**: The experimental comparison to GOBNILP is problematic. The paper doesn't establish whether GOBNILP's poor performance is due to ancestral constraints specifically or its general weakness with unbounded parent set size. GOBNILP is known to be less efficient than other approaches (like the one by van Beek and Hoffmann, CP 2015) for large networks with unbounded parent sets. The authors should compare against a more recent and efficient CP/ILP approach that handles ancestral constraints.

5. **Weak ILP Comparison**: The paper compares against Cussens' (2008) MAXSAT encoding of ancestral constraints, which is an outdated and suboptimal ILP formulation. A tighter ILP formulation using A(X,Y) and I(Z,U) variables with transitivity and A-I linking constraints would be a more appropriate baseline. Comparing against a weak encoding weakens the empirical claim that their method is "orders-of-magnitude more efficient."

6. **Missing Related Work**: The work of van Beek and Hoffmann (CP 2015) is not discussed. Their constraint programming approach for structure learning often outperforms GOBNILP and could be adapted to handle ancestral constraints. This omission is significant as it represents a more relevant baseline for comparison.

## Quality, Clarity, Originality, and Significance

**Quality**: The technical content is sound, but the paper lacks sufficient theoretical justification for key claims (Lemma 3, constraint inference). The experimental comparison is not sufficiently rigorous due to the weak baselines used.

**Clarity**: The paper is generally well-written but contains several ambiguous statements (e.g., about "inferred" constraints) and critical gaps in explanation (EC tree edge generation rules, MAXSAT formulation details).

**Originality**: The paper makes a valuable contribution by extending the EC tree framework to handle ancestral constraints. However, the constraint projection technique has some similarities to prior work (e.g., Cussens 2008), and the paper doesn't sufficiently clarify how this work differs from or improves upon those approaches.

**Significance**: The problem addressed is important for practical Bayesian network learning, and the empirical results suggest significant practical improvements. However, the significance is somewhat diminished by the weak comparison baselines and the incomplete theoretical foundation.

## Recommendation

The paper presents an interesting approach to a challenging problem with promising empirical results. However, the theoretical gaps and weak experimental comparisons prevent it from making a fully convincing contribution. To strengthen the paper, the authors should:
1. Provide a rigorous proof for Lemma 3
2. Clarify the EC tree edge generation rules and their relationship to ancestral constraints
3. Better explain the MAXSAT approach and the distinction between "entailed" and "consistent" constraints
4. Compare against van Beek and Hoffmann's CP approach (CP 2015) and a tighter ILP formulation
5. Clarify whether the speedups are due to handling ancestral constraints specifically or general efficiency improvements

With these revisions, the paper would make a strong contribution to the field. As it stands, I recommend rejection due to the significant theoretical gaps and weak empirical comparisons.
