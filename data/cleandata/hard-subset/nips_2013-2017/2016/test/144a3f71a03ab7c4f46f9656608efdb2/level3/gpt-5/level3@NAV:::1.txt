This paper studies optimal Bayesian network learning with ancestral constraints by searching over CPDAGs (EC tree) using branch-and-bound A* with symmetry breaking and a URLearning-derived heuristic. The central idea is to keep the non-decomposable constraints in the EC-tree search while “empowering” a decomposable-score oracle via projected decomposable constraints: (i) entailed edge constraints from ancestry and (ii) a maximized set of compatible ordering constraints obtained via MaxSAT. The approach builds directly on Chen et al. (2015, 2016) for BN/EC graphs and on A* methods (Yuan & Malone, 2011/2013), and is positioned against ILP/MAXSAT encodings (Jaakkola et al., 2010; Cussens, 2008) and CP (van Beek & Hoffmann, 2015). Empirically, the method reports large speedups over GOBNILP.

Quality. The pruning strategy over EC trees is appealing, and the projection of constraints is a useful practical idea; however, some key arguments are underspecified. Lemma 3 is pivotal for pruning with ancestry yet its proof sketch is unclear; please expand the proof and explicitly state the conditions under which the pruning is complete and admissible for A*. The claim “By the EC tree edge generation rules, Gk also contains edge Z -> W” needs a precise reference to the rule and a short derivation (or a figure) showing why the orientation must appear in all completions. The section on implied/projected constraints blurs entailment vs. consistent augmentation. “Can infer” is misleading when the method adds consistent but not logically entailed ordering constraints—state this explicitly and prove that adding such constraints to the oracle cannot exclude any A-feasible DAG during EC-tree search. The MaxSAT formulation (variables, hard/soft clauses, weights, and acyclicity rationale) and its computational overhead need more detail. The experimental comparison is suggestive but not conclusive: GOBNILP’s weakness may stem from unbounded parent sets rather than ancestral constraints per se. Moreover, the baseline encoding follows Cussens (2008), which is known to be loose; a tighter ILP with A(X,Y) and I(X,U) variables plus transitivity and A–I linking constraints would be a fairer test. The omission of van Beek & Hoffmann (CP 2015), which often outperforms GOBNILP and could be adapted to ancestry, is a significant gap.

Clarity. Generally well-written, but proofs and the projection pipeline require more rigor and algorithmic detail to be reproducible.

Originality. A thoughtful synthesis of EC-tree search with constraint projection; the MaxSAT-based selection of compatible orderings is novel in this context.

Significance. Potentially impactful for incorporating causal background knowledge, but claims would be stronger with fairer baselines and ablations (bounded parent sizes; URLearning heuristic alone; with/without projected constraints; MaxSAT overhead).

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear problem and practical importance of ancestral constraints.
- Sound EC-tree pruning framework and integration with A*.
- Useful projection of ancestry to decomposable constraints; strong speedups reported.
- Builds on and extends influential BN search frameworks.

Cons against acceptance:
- Key proof (Lemma 3) and key claim about EC-tree edge generation lack rigor.
- Entailment vs. consistent augmentation of orderings is not cleanly separated.
- Insufficient detail on MaxSAT inference procedure and rationale.
- Empirical comparison weakened by a loose ILP encoding and lack of CP baseline.
- Ambiguity about whether speedups derive from ancestry vs. unbounded parent set effects.
