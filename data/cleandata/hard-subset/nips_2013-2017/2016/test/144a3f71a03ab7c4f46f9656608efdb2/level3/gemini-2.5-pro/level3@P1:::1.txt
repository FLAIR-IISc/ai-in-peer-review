This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data when given background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints (e.g., X is an ancestor of Y, or X is not an ancestor of Y). Such constraints are non-decomposable, which makes them incompatible with many efficient, exact structure learning algorithms that rely on decomposable scores. The authors build upon a recent A*-based search framework over the space of Markov equivalence classes (the EC tree), which uses an oracle for solving subproblems with decomposable scores. The main contributions are twofold: (1) a method for pruning the EC tree search space by checking if a given equivalence class (CPDAG) is compatible with the ancestral constraints, and (2) a method for "projecting" the non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints, which can be passed to the oracle to significantly improve its efficiency and thus tighten the A* heuristic. The authors compare their method against an Integer Linear Programming (ILP) approach (GOBNILP) and demonstrate orders-of-magnitude speedups on a variety of benchmark networks.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The problem of incorporating non-decomposable background knowledge into exact Bayesian network structure learning is important and challenging. Ancestral constraints are a natural and powerful way for experts to encode causal knowledge. The proposed approach of integrating these constraints into a state-of-the-art A* search framework by both pruning the search space and strengthening the heuristic oracle is novel and elegant.

2.  **Performance:** The empirical results are impressive. The proposed method demonstrates significant speedups over the GOBNILP baseline, scaling to problems with 20 variables, which is competitive with state-of-the-art exact learners even without complex constraints. The "easy-hard-easy" pattern observed as the number of constraints increases is interesting and well-explained.

3.  **Constraint Projection Idea:** The idea of inferring decomposable constraints from non-decomposable ones to "empower" an oracle designed for the former is a powerful concept that could be applicable in other constrained optimization settings.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Clarity and Quality of Theoretical Justification:** The paper's technical core relies on the correctness of the pruning procedure for the EC tree. The soundness of this pruning is stated in Theorem 1. However, the proof is omitted and the justification given is insufficient. A key part of such a proof would need to detail how adding a new variable and parents to a CPDAG (an edge in the EC tree) preserves satisfaction of the ancestral constraints. For example, a statement like "By the EC tree edge generation rules, Gk also contains edge Z -> W" would require careful justification, which is currently missing. Without a clear and rigorous proof of the soundness of the pruning step, the technical quality of the paper's main contribution is difficult to assess.

2.  **Clarity of Constraint Inference:** Section 5, which describes the projection of constraints, is particularly unclear. The distinction between constraints that are logically *entailed* by the ancestral constraints and constraints that are merely *consistent* with them is blurred. For ordering constraints (Section 5.2), the paper states the goal is to find a set of ordering constraints "as large as possible, but without eliminating any DAGs satisfying A." This suggests adding consistent, but not necessarily entailed, constraints. The use of "can infer" is misleading in this context. The MaxSAT formulation is presented as a recipe without sufficient rationale. It is not explained why these specific hard and soft clauses correctly capture the problem of finding a maximal set of consistent ordering constraints. This lack of detail makes a core component of the method difficult to understand and verify.

3.  **Quality of the Empirical Comparison:** The experimental evaluation, while showing large speedups, has weaknesses that temper the conclusions.
    *   The primary baseline, GOBNILP, is known to perform poorly on problems with a large or unbounded maximum in-degree (parent set size), as noted by the authors themselves in footnote 9. It is therefore unclear how much of the reported speedup is due to the proposed method's superior handling of ancestral constraints versus GOBNILP's general inefficiency on this class of problems. The comparison would be much stronger if it were made on problems with a bounded in-degree where GOBNILP is more competitive.
    *   The ILP formulation used for ancestral constraints is based on Cussens (2008), which is not the tightest possible encoding. A stronger ILP baseline could be formulated using binary variables `A(X,Y)` for ancestry, linked to the parent-set indicator variables `I(Z,U)` via constraints that enforce transitivity and connect parent relationships to ancestral ones. Comparing against a relatively weak ILP formulation weakens the empirical claim. The proposed method might still be superior, but the gap could be smaller with a stronger baseline.
    *   The paper fails to discuss or compare against other relevant work. Notably, van Beek and Hoffmann (CP 2015) introduced a constraint programming approach that has been shown to outperform GOBNILP on many structure learning tasks. Their framework could likely be adapted to handle ancestral constraints and would serve as a more compelling modern baseline.

**Summary and Recommendation:**

The paper presents a novel and promising approach to a significant problem in Bayesian network learning. The core ideas—pruning the EC tree search and empowering the A* heuristic by projecting constraints—are compelling.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses an important, practical problem.
*   The high-level approach is original and clever.
*   The empirical results show a massive performance improvement over a known baseline.

**Arguments for rejection:**
*   The paper lacks clarity and rigor in its key technical sections, particularly the justification for the pruning method (Theorem 1) and the rationale for the MaxSAT-based constraint inference.
*   The empirical evaluation is weakened by the choice of a baseline (GOBNILP) that has known performance issues on this problem class and by the use of a suboptimal ILP encoding for the constraints.
*   Failure to compare with other relevant state-of-the-art methods (e.g., from the constraint programming community).

Overall, while the ideas are of interest to the NIPS community, the weaknesses in clarity and experimental rigor are significant. The paper feels incomplete in its current form. I lean towards rejection, but would be open to changing my mind if the authors can substantially clarify the theoretical claims and strengthen the experimental evaluation in their rebuttal. A major revision would be required to address these issues for the paper to be acceptable.
