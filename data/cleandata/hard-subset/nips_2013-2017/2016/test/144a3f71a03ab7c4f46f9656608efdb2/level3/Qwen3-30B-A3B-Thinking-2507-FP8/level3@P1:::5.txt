

The paper addresses Bayesian network structure learning with ancestral constraints, a problem that has been largely overlooked in the literature due to the non-decomposable nature of such constraints. The authors build upon Chen et al.'s (2015, 2016) framework for optimal structure learning with non-decomposable scores, which uses a search space called the EC tree and leverages oracles for decomposable scores. Previous work has typically assumed decomposable scores (e.g., BDeu), which cannot accommodate ancestral constraints directly, limiting approaches to only structure-modular or order-modular constraints (Koivisto and Sood, 2004; Parviainen and Koivisto, 2013). While ILP-based approaches (Jaakkola et al., 2010; Bartlett and Cussens, 2015) can theoretically represent ancestral constraints, the authors correctly note that these have not been empirically evaluated for this specific problem.

The main contribution is a sound method for incorporating ancestral constraints into the EC tree framework. The authors show how to infer decomposable edge and ordering constraints from non-decomposable ancestral constraints, which can then be passed to the oracle (an algorithm for decomposable scores). For edge constraints, they provide a polynomial-time algorithm based on ancestral relations. For ordering constraints, they formulate a MaxSAT problem to find the maximal set of compatible constraints. The authors demonstrate that this approach enables the EC tree search to efficiently prune invalid structures while maintaining optimality.

The paper excels in quality: the theoretical claims are well-supported by formal proofs (Theorems 2-4) and the empirical evaluation is comprehensive. The experiments compare against GOBNILP (an ILP-based system) across multiple Bayesian network benchmarks (ALARM, ANDES, etc.), varying problem size (n=12-20 variables), dataset size, and constraint density. The results consistently show orders-of-magnitude speedups for the proposed approach, especially as problem size increases. The authors also provide insightful analysis of how ancestral constraints improve learning accuracy (Table 3 shows that 10-25% constraints can match the effect of increasing dataset size from 512 to 8192 samples).

The clarity is excellent: the paper is well-organized with clear definitions, intuitive examples, and helpful figures (Figure 1). The EC tree concept is explained thoroughly, and the transition from BN graphs to EC trees is well-motivated. The MaxSAT formulation for ordering constraints is presented accessibly, with clear constraints and explanations of their necessity.

The originality is strong. While Chen et al. (2015, 2016) introduced the EC tree framework, this paper is the first to address ancestral constraints within this framework. The approach of projecting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones is novel and effectively solves a long-standing limitation in structure learning. The authors properly contextualize their work against related approaches, noting that constraint programming methods could represent ancestral constraints but haven't been evaluated for this purpose (Bartlett and Cussens, 2015; van Beek and Hoffmann, 2015).

The significance is high. Bayesian network structure learning with background knowledge is crucial for real-world applications where domain experts provide causal relationships. The paper demonstrates that incorporating ancestral constraints can dramatically improve both efficiency (orders-of-magnitude speedup) and accuracy (reduced structural Hamming distance). The scalability to 20 variables (Table 3) is impressive for exact structure learning with non-decomposable constraints.

Weaknesses are minimal but worth noting. The paper could better discuss the limitations of the constraint projection approach (e.g., when constraints are inconsistent or when the inferred constraints are too weak). The MaxSAT formulation for ordering constraints is theoretically sound but may become computationally expensive for extremely large constraint sets, though this isn't evident in the experiments. The paper also doesn't compare against constraint-based methods (like PC algorithm with ancestral constraints), though the authors note these are typically approximate rather than optimal.

Pro-con acceptance arguments:
Pro: 
- Solves a meaningful problem (ancestral constraints) that has been largely ignored in optimal structure learning
- Demonstrates substantial empirical advantages over existing approaches
- Theoretical foundation is solid and well-explained
- Provides practical benefits for real-world applications

Con:
- The MaxSAT formulation for ordering constraints might be less efficient than the edge constraint approach for some constraint sets
- Limited discussion of when the constraint projection approach might fail

The paper makes a significant contribution to Bayesian network structure learning by enabling the incorporation of important background knowledge (ancestral constraints) into optimal search methods. The technical approach is elegant, the theoretical foundation is strong, and the empirical results convincingly demonstrate substantial improvements over alternatives. The paper is clearly written and addresses an important gap in the literature. For NIPS 2016, this represents a high-quality contribution that advances the state of the art in structure learning with practical implications for applications requiring causal knowledge. The authors have successfully demonstrated that non-decomposable constraints can be effectively handled within an optimal search framework, making this a compelling paper for acceptance.
