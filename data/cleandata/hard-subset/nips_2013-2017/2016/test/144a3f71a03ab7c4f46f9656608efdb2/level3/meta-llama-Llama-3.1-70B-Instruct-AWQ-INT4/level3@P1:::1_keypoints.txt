- Proposes a novel branch-and-bound A* search over CPDAGs for learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints.  
- Leverages symmetry breaking and a heuristic from URLearning to improve search efficiency.  
- Main pruning result (Lemma 3) enabling ancestral-constraint pruning requires further clarification and stronger justification.  
- Key contribution: projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints onto decomposable edge and ordering constraints to empower the oracle.  
- Treatment of implied and projected constraints is unclear and insufficiently explained.  
- The inference procedure for ordering constraints is not clearly described.  
- Claiming the method "can infer" ordering constraints is misleading; authors add consistent but not necessarily entailed constraints.  
- The MAXSAT mechanism controlling which ordering constraints are added is under-specified and lacks rationale.  
- Experimental results show large speedups over GOBNILP, but it’s unclear if speedups stem from handling ancestral constraints or GOBNILP’s weakness with unbounded parent sets.  
- Comparison against a weak MAXSAT/ILP encoding undermines empirical claims; a tighter ILP using A(X,Y) and I(Z,U) with transitivity and linking constraints would be a better baseline.  
- Unclear whether the proposed method would still outperform a stronger ILP formulation.  
- Missing discussion and comparison to van Beek & Hoffmann (CP 2015), which often outperforms GOBNILP and could likely handle ancestral constraints.  
- Quality score: 7/10 — novel but needs improvements in exposition and empirical comparison.  
- Clarity score: 6/10 — main idea clear but key technical explanations are lacking.  
- Originality score: 8/10 — approach is original.  
- Significance score: 7/10 — addresses a challenging problem but empirical claims need strengthening.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel method, projection to decomposable constraints, reported large speedups.  
- Arguments against acceptance: unclear treatment of constraints/inference, misleading wording about inference, weak empirical baselines, missing related-work comparison.
