Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses optimal Bayesian network (BN) structure learning in the presence of background knowledge expressed as ancestral (path) constraints. Since such constraints are non-decomposable, they are not directly supported by mainstream exact score-based methods that rely on decomposable scores and dynamic programming over parent sets/orderings. Building on the BN-graph/EC-tree search spaces introduced by Chen et al. (IJCAI GKR’15; AISTATS’16), the authors (i) prune the EC tree using ancestral constraints and (ii) “project” ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints to empower a decomposable-score oracle (URLEARNING), thereby guiding an A* search over CPDAGs. Edge constraints are inferred by efficient rules (with theorems characterizing when negative/positive edges are entailed), while ordering constraints are selected via a MaxSAT formulation that maximizes a consistent set of order relations derived from negative ancestor constraints. Empirically, the proposed method is compared primarily against an ILP/MIP approach (GOBNILP) with a standard path-constraint encoding (Cussens 2008), showing large speedups and good scaling with the number of ancestral constraints.

Prior work on leveraging non-decomposable priors/constraints includes order-modular priors (Chen et al. 2015), constraint-based learning with path constraints and causal/associative priors (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos 2012, 2013), and exact structure learning via ILP/CP (Jaakkola et al. 2010; Bartlett & Cussens 2015; van Beek & Hoffmann 2015). The contribution here is to support non-decomposable ancestral constraints inside an exact A*-over-EC-tree framework and to show substantial empirical gains versus a standard ILP approach.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: ancestral constraints capture useful causal background knowledge not handled by decomposable methods.
- Technically interesting search space: pruning CPDAG nodes in the EC tree is a principled way to integrate non-decomposable constraints with A*.
- Projection of ancestral constraints to decomposable edges/orders to strengthen the oracle is a practical and seemingly impactful idea; theorems for entailed edge constraints are crisp.
- Solid empirical trend: orders-of-magnitude speedups vs. the specific ILP baseline, the easy-hard-easy behavior with more constraints, and improvements in structural accuracy with constraints.
- In scope for NIPS; builds on recent lines of exact BN learning (order graphs, EC trees) and connects to ILP/CP work.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
- Key pruning result proof clarity: The central pruning lemma/theorem (called Lemma 3 in the paper; Theorem 1 here) needs a clearer, self-contained proof. The statement “By the EC tree edge generation rules, Gk also contains edge Z -> W” is non-obvious and should be justified more explicitly, ideally with a figure and a step-by-step argument about how CPDAG extensions enforce the edge in all completions.
- Projection to ordering constraints: The paper alternates between “can infer” and “entailed by A.” For edges, Theorems 2–3 indeed characterize entailment. For orderings, the MaxSAT model appears to select a large consistent set, not necessarily all and only the entailed orderings. Please clearly separate “entailed” (logically follows for all DAGs satisfying A) from “selected consistent,” and give formal guarantees. The current “sufficient condition for acyclicity” clause may be conservative; discuss completeness loss and when MaxSAT may exclude entailed orderings.
- MaxSAT formulation details: The rationale for each hard clause (especially clause (3)) and the overall correctness/soundness should be expanded. Provide a proof sketch that any selected ordering set O satisfies Theorem 4’s requirements, and that passing O to the oracle does not eliminate any DAG in G(A). A small counterexample illustrating why one must avoid naively taking all pairwise orderings Y<X from X 6 Y would be helpful.
- Algorithmic specificity: The edge-inference algorithm is only sketched (“details omitted”); include pseudocode and complexity bounds. For orderings, clarify whether closure under transitivity is enforced eagerly or via MaxSAT clauses, and how ties/conflicts are handled.
- Experimental fairness: The ILP comparison uses an encoding based on Cussens (2008), known to be weaker than more modern/tighter ILP formulations. A stronger baseline would:
  - Introduce explicit ancestor variables A(i,j) with transitivity constraints and link them to I(X,U) (parent-set) variables via linear implications; and/or
  - Use CP (van Beek & Hoffmann 2015), which often outperforms GOBNILP and could be extended with ancestor constraints.
  Without such baselines, the empirical claim “orders of magnitude faster than ILP” is weakened. Even if your approach remains superior, showing results against a tighter ILP/CP model is important.
- Confounding factors: The experiments allow unbounded parent set size, a regime where heuristic A* methods are known to scale better than ILP (Yuan & Malone 2013; Malone et al. 2014). Please control for maximum in-degree and report results under degree bounds to disentangle the effect of ancestral constraints from family-size effects.
- Ablations: Table 1 vs. 2 shows the value of projection as a whole. Please further separate the contributions of (i) EC-tree pruning only, (ii) + projected edge constraints, (iii) + projected ordering constraints (MaxSAT), to quantify each component’s impact.
- Reproducibility: Provide code, scripts, and exact constraint encodings (including MaxSAT templates) to facilitate replication.

Quality
- The approach appears technically sound overall, but the core pruning result and the MaxSAT encoding need clearer proofs/guarantees. The edge-entailment theory is well specified; ordering selection is less rigorous in its current form.
- Experiments are extensive over benchmarks, but stronger baselines and ablations are needed for a definitive empirical case.

Clarity
- Writing is generally clear and well organized. However, some key steps are elided. Please:
  - Expand the proof of the pruning lemma/theorem and the specific CPDAG extension step.
  - Provide explicit pseudocode for edge and ordering projections.
  - Clearly distinguish “entailed” vs. “consistent” ordering constraints throughout.

Originality
- Novel integration of ancestral constraints into EC-tree/A* with projection to empower decomposable oracles. While path constraints have been used in constraint-based methods and ILP/CP can encode them, this exact combination and theory for edge entailment is original.

Significance
- Ancestral knowledge is common in causal discovery; enabling exact BN learning with such constraints is valuable. If the empirical gains hold against stronger ILP/CP baselines, this would be a notable advance in exact BN learning under rich constraints.

Suggestions/questions for the authors
- Provide a formal statement and complete proof of the pruning lemma/theorem; clarify the CPDAG-to-DAG extension step cited.
- Strengthen the ILP/CP baselines (tighter ILP with A(i,j) variables and linking constraints; compare to van Beek & Hoffmann 2015 if possible).
- Add ablations separating pruning, edge projection, and ordering projection contributions.
- Report node expansions and heuristic error (admissible lower bounds) to illuminate search behavior.
- Analyze sensitivity to MaxSAT solver choices and clause weights.
- Evaluate with degree bounds and larger n where feasible.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important gap: exact BN learning with non-decomposable ancestral constraints.
- Elegant use of EC-tree pruning plus projection to empower a decomposable oracle.
- Strong empirical performance on standard benchmarks; clear benefits from projected constraints.

Arguments against acceptance
- Key proof steps and MaxSAT correctness not fully justified; some statements require clearer explanation.
- Empirical comparison relies on a relatively weak ILP encoding; missing CP baseline.
- Limited ablations make it hard to attribute gains to individual components.

Recommendation: Weak accept (borderline). The paper is promising and relevant; with tightened theory and stronger baselines it would be a clear accept. My confidence is medium.
