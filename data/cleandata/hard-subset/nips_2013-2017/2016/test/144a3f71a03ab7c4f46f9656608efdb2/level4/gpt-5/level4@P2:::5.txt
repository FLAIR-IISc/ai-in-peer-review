Summary
The paper addresses optimal Bayesian network (BN) structure learning under non-decomposable background knowledge, focusing on ancestral (path) constraints. Building on the BN-graph and EC-tree search frameworks of Chen et al. (2015, 2016), the authors show how to enforce ancestral constraints by pruning the search space at the level of CPDAGs and by “projecting” non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints that can be handled by a decomposable-score oracle (e.g., order-graph solvers). Edge constraints are derived via closure over ancestral relations, while ordering constraints are obtained by solving a MaxSAT formulation that maximizes a sound set of topological-order constraints implied by the ancestral knowledge. The paper presents theorems that justify pruning and projection rules, and empirically compares the approach against an ILP baseline (GOBNILP), showing substantial runtime gains—often orders of magnitude—particularly when projected constraints are used to strengthen the oracle. Additional experiments explore scaling with number of variables and the effect of constraint density, showing an easy–hard–easy trend and accuracy improvements as more constraints or data are provided.

Relation to prior work
Most exact score-based methods assume decomposable scores and support only structure- or order-modular constraints (e.g., Koivisto & Sood; Silander & Myllymäki; Yuan & Malone), making ancestral constraints difficult to encode. ILP/CP formulations can express non-decomposable constraints (Jaakkola et al.; Cussens; van Beek & Hoffmann), but have not been systematically evaluated for ancestral-constraint learning. The paper is closely related to Chen et al. (2015, 2016), which introduced the BN graph and EC tree for non-decomposable priors, and to work on causal/associative path beliefs for constraint-based or heuristic search (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos). The novelty here lies in exact search with ancestral constraints via EC-tree pruning and the projection of ancestral constraints to decomposable constraints, including a MaxSAT-based method for aggressively, yet safely, tightening the oracle.

Strengths
- Clear problem motivation: ancestral constraints are practically useful and non-decomposable.
- Methodologically solid: EC-tree pruning criteria are well specified; theorems formalize when CPDAGs can be pruned and when edge/order constraints are entailed.
- Practical leverage: projected constraints substantially improve the oracle’s heuristic quality.
- Empirical results: consistent, large speedups over an ILP state-of-the-art baseline; helpful analyses of how constraint density and data size affect runtime and accuracy.
- Significance: enables exact learning under a richer class of background knowledge with notable efficiency gains.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Some technical details are deferred (“details omitted” for closure rules, complexity of enumerating class(P)), making it hard to assess worst-case behavior (e.g., when CPDAG classes are large).
- Terminology: “maximum set of ancestral constraints” appears to mean a transitive/reflexive closure or a maximal closure; clarify precisely and provide the algorithmic complexity.
- Scope of baselines: only ILP is evaluated; CP-based methods are mentioned but not compared. Including a CP baseline, or discussing why it is expected to behave similarly/differently, would strengthen the case.
- Robustness: experiments assume correct constraints. A study of sensitivity to noisy/mistaken ancestral constraints would improve practical relevance.
- Generality: while the approach targets ancestral constraints, clarify how the projection framework extends to other non-decomposable constraints (e.g., path bans over sets, v-structure constraints).
- Editorial issues in the current draft: stray text (e.g., “th Conference...”), footnote markers without text, language artifacts, and some notation inconsistencies (e.g., α/β sets). A careful editorial pass would improve readability.

Evaluation against NIPS criteria
- Quality: The approach appears technically sound, with formal statements supporting pruning and projection; empirical evaluation is careful and uses standard benchmarks with precomputed scores. More detail on closure and enumeration procedures would further substantiate completeness and complexity.
- Clarity: The core ideas are well explained, but the draft contains editorial glitches and a few places where definitions and assumptions (closure, “maximum” vs. “maximal”) should be tightened.
- Originality: Combining EC-tree search with provably safe projection of ancestral constraints to decomposable edge/order constraints, and the MaxSAT formulation for maximizing safe ordering constraints, represents a novel and useful contribution.
- Significance: Results show large runtime gains and improved accuracy with modest background knowledge. The method should be of interest to practitioners needing exact BN learning under causal/path knowledge.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, well-motivated contribution addressing a known limitation of exact BN learning.
- Theoretically justified pruning and projection with clear empirical benefits.
- Demonstrated, often dramatic, speedups over a strong ILP baseline.
- Practical technique (MaxSAT-based ordering inference) that can be reused.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited ablations and missing baselines (no CP comparison; limited analysis of MaxSAT overhead).
- Incomplete presentation of some algorithmic details and complexity considerations.
- No robustness study for misspecified constraints.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers a substantial and original improvement to exact BN structure learning under ancestral constraints, supported by strong empirical evidence. Addressing the clarity issues and expanding on algorithmic details and baselines in the final version would further strengthen the work.
