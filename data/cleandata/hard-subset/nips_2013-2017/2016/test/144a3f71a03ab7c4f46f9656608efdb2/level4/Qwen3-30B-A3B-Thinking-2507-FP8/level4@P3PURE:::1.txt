

This paper addresses constrained optimization for learning Bayesian networks from local scores under ancestral constraints, employing a branch-and-bound search where nodes in the search tree are CPDAGs. The search progresses from CPDAG₁ to CPDAG₂ by selecting parent sets for variables absent in CPDAG₁, with sensible symmetry breaking. The search utilizes the URLearning system to provide a heuristic (details deferred to a prior paper). Ancestral constraints enable pruning, with Lemma 3 (supplementary material) as the key technical result. While I believe Lemma 3 is correct, its proof requires clarification: the phrase "By the EC tree edge generation rules, Gₖ also contains edge Z → W" lacks sufficient explanation.  

The paper distinguishes between "implied constraints" (standard terminology; here termed "projected constraints") and "ordering constraints" derived from ancestral constraints. A set of ancestral constraints may imply edge constraints for pruning, and ordering constraints (e.g., "Y < Z") can be inferred when "Z is not an ancestor of Y." However, the phrasing "we can infer Y < Z from Z not an ancestor of Y" is misleading: in a graph with vertices Y, Z and no edges, Z is not an ancestor of Y yet Y < Z is consistent. The authors clearly intend that such constraints may be *added* (not entailed) to maximize consistent ordering constraints, and they carefully avoid over-inference using a MAXSAT approach. The method for inferring these constraints, however, is inadequately explained.  

Experiments vary the number of variables, data points, and ancestral constraints, comparing the proposed system to GOBNILP. The system demonstrates significant speed improvements over GOBNILP. However, the comparison is problematic: without a parent set cardinality limit (as in the experiments), GOBNILP's poor performance is expected in unconstrained settings (as noted by the authors), making it unclear whether the observed gap stems from GOBNILP's general inefficiency with large parent sets or its specific handling of ancestral constraints. Crucially, the encoding of ancestral constraints (supplementary material) uses a MAXSAT formulation from [Cussens, 2008], rather than the ILP encoding in [Cussens, 2010, Section 3.1.1]. This MAXSAT encoding is suboptimal for ILP solvers. A simpler ILP formulation is possible: introduce binary variables A(X,Y) (X is an ancestor of Y) alongside I(Z,U) (Z is a parent of U), omitting the O(n³) E(X,Y,Z) variables. The required constraints are transitivity on A(X,Y) and:  
A(X,Y) ≥ ∑_{U: X ∈ U} I(Y,U)  
This ensures that if X is a parent of Y, then X is an ancestor of Y. This formulation yields a tighter linear relaxation and superior performance, as demonstrated in [Cussens, 2010] for total orderings (with a minor adjustment for partial orders). The current comparison to GOBNILP using a poor MAXSAT encoding weakens the paper’s claims; even with a sensible ILP formulation, the proposed method would likely outperform GOBNILP.  

The paper omits a relevant comparison to van Beek and Hoffmann (2015), which often outperforms GOBNILP and could be adapted to handle ancestral constraints. This oversight is notable, as the van Beek and Hoffmann approach would provide a more meaningful benchmark.
