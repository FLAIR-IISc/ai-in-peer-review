

This paper addresses the problem of optimal Bayesian network structure learning with ancestral constraints, a significant challenge in the field. The authors build upon Chen et al.'s framework for learning with non-decomposable scores (Chen et al., 2015, 2016), which represents a notable advancement over traditional decomposable score approaches that cannot handle non-decomposable constraints. The paper makes a clear contribution by showing how to incorporate ancestral constraints (a type of non-decomposable constraint) into the EC tree search space, a key component of Chen et al.'s framework. The authors demonstrate that ancestral constraints, which express cause-and-effect relationships between variables, can be efficiently handled by first inferring decomposable edge and ordering constraints from the non-decomposable ancestral constraints, then passing these to an oracle that optimizes decomposable scores.

The paper's main technical contributions include: (1) a sound and complete method for pruning the EC tree based on ancestral constraints, (2) a systematic approach for projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints, and (3) an empirical demonstration that their approach is orders-of-magnitude more efficient than ILP-based methods. The authors provide a clear theoretical foundation for their approach, with formal theorems establishing the correctness of their constraint projection methods. The experimental evaluation is thorough, using multiple standard Bayesian network benchmarks (ALARM, ANDES, CHILD, CPCS54, and HEPAR2) with varying numbers of variables, dataset sizes, and constraint proportions.

The paper's strengths are numerous. First, it addresses a genuine limitation in existing Bayesian network structure learning approaches: the inability to efficiently incorporate non-decomposable constraints like ancestral relationships. This is a significant practical limitation since such constraints are common in real-world applications where domain knowledge about causal relationships is available. Second, the authors provide a well-structured theoretical foundation that demonstrates how to bridge the gap between non-decomposable constraints and existing decomposable score-based oracles. Third, the empirical results are compelling, showing dramatic improvements in efficiency (orders of magnitude faster) compared to ILP-based approaches, even when the ILP approach is provided with the same projected constraints. The scalability results (up to 20 variables) are particularly impressive given the computational complexity of exact Bayesian network structure learning.

The paper also makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of how background knowledge can substitute for additional data. The authors demonstrate that a relatively small number of ancestral constraints (10-25%) can have a similar impact on learning accuracy as increasing the dataset size from 512 to 8192 observations, highlighting the practical value of incorporating domain knowledge.

While the paper is strong overall, there are some minor weaknesses. The presentation of the MaxSAT formulation in Section 5.2 could be clearer for readers unfamiliar with the formalism. The paper would benefit from more explicit discussion of the computational complexity of the constraint projection algorithms. Additionally, while the empirical results are impressive, the paper would be strengthened by a more detailed analysis of when the proposed method is most effective (e.g., with what types of ancestral constraints or network structures).

The paper aligns well with NIPS topics, particularly in the areas of probabilistic graphical models, structure learning, and efficient algorithms. It builds upon relevant NIPS work, including Chen et al.'s EC tree framework (NIPS 2016) and the earlier work on non-decomposable scores (NIPS 2015). The paper demonstrates a clear understanding of the limitations of previous approaches and provides a well-motivated solution that advances the state of the art.

In terms of the NIPS evaluation criteria:
- Quality: The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical foundations and thorough empirical validation. The claims are well-supported by both theoretical analysis and experimental results.
- Clarity: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with sufficient detail for an expert reader to reproduce the results. The figures effectively illustrate the EC tree and BN graph concepts.
- Originality: The paper makes a significant contribution by addressing a gap in the literature regarding non-decomposable constraints in Bayesian network structure learning. The approach represents a novel combination of existing techniques (EC tree search, constraint projection, oracle-based learning).
- Significance: The results demonstrate a substantial practical improvement in efficiency, making exact Bayesian network structure learning with constraints feasible for larger problems. The insight about background knowledge substituting for data has important implications for practitioners.

Pro:
- Addresses a genuine limitation in current Bayesian network structure learning approaches
- Provides a theoretically sound method for handling non-decomposable constraints
- Demonstrates dramatic efficiency improvements over existing approaches
- Shows practical value of background knowledge in reducing data requirements
- Clear theoretical foundation with formal proofs

Con:
- Some technical details of constraint projection could be more accessible
- Limited discussion of computational complexity of constraint projection
- Could provide more insight into when the method is most effective

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to Bayesian network structure learning. The theoretical insights are sound, the empirical results are compelling, and the practical implications are substantial. The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way and is well-suited for publication at NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
