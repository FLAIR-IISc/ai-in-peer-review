This paper presents an efficient method for incorporating ancestral constraints into optimal Bayesian network (BN) learning from data. The core idea is to translate a set of ancestral constraints into a set of ordering constraints suitable for use with a score-based learning algorithm built upon the EC tree framework introduced by Chen et al. (2015). The proposed approach is evaluated against a modified version of the ILP-based GoBNILP algorithm, demonstrating significant performance improvements in the tested settings. Overall, the paper is well-written and clearly explains a conceptually straightforward, yet valuable, contribution. The combination of a novel problem and solution, coupled with positive results, makes this work of definite interest to a specialist audience.

The problem addressed – optimal BN learning under ancestral constraints – is interesting and novel, as there is currently no standard solution available (novelty: 3). However, the practical relevance of this setting deserves consideration. While introducing ancestral knowledge is valuable, the scenario considered here feels somewhat artificial. In many real-world causal discovery problems, the presence of ancestral relations often suggests the need for more general algorithms capable of handling unobserved confounders, rather than solely focusing on constrained structure learning (impact: 2).

The approach cleverly addresses the challenge of integrating hard-to-use ancestral constraints – which don't naturally fit within decomposable score-based algorithms – by transforming them into ordering constraints that *do* align with such algorithms. This mapping is performed using a MaxSAT solver to ensure the explicit representation of all implied ancestral constraints. However, the correctness and completeness of the translation rules (lines 257-261) require further scrutiny. Specifically, the symmetry of the premise in acyclicity rule 3, ((X < Y) and (Z < W) == (Z < W) and (X < Y)), is not reflected in the asymmetric conclusion (Y -/-> Z). Furthermore, rule 4 appears to exclude ancestral constraints indicating mutual independence between variables (e.g., ‘X and Y do not cause each other’).

While the comparison against the modified GoBNILP algorithm is compelling, GoBNILP wasn’t specifically designed to address this type of constrained learning problem. A more informative evaluation would involve assessing performance against a greedy algorithm like GES, assuming the ancestral constraints are consistent with the optimal BN structure. In this scenario, GES—given oracle knowledge of the constraints—should ideally reproduce the same structure. Although greedy algorithms cannot guarantee optimality, this comparison would provide a more meaningful indication of the efficiency gains achieved by the proposed method.

Several minor points warrant attention:

*   Line 30: Clarify the distinction between a “standard” oracle and an “omniscient” oracle.
*   Line 38: Rephrase “empower the oracle” to something more precise, such as “use in combination with the oracle.”
*   Line 90: Explicitly state that “optimizing” refers to “minimizing” the score.
*   Line 164: Theorem 1 should more explicitly state that the set of constraints A satisfied by the CPDAG P on the EC tree is a *subset* of the constraints satisfied by the full DAG G, to ensure the validity of the “if and only if” statement.
*   Lines 225-227: Theorem 3's ability to generate many positive edge implications seems limited, particularly when dealing with sparse sets of ancestral constraints.
*   Line 267: The connection between the rule and the concept of ‘acyclicity’ is not immediately apparent, and the comment regarding rule 3 applies here.
*   Line 310: A constraint set comprising 10%-25% of possible ancestral constraints implies a substantial amount of prior knowledge about the target graph, which is not typical in many practical causal discovery scenarios.



In summary, this paper presents a well-conceived and implemented approach to a novel, though somewhat specialized, problem in BN learning. While the setting may not be universally applicable, the technical contribution is sound, and the results are promising. Addressing the concerns raised regarding the correctness of the translation rules and the evaluation methodology would further strengthen this work.
