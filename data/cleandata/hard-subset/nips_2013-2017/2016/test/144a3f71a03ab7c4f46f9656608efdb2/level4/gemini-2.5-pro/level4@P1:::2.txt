### Review

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data when subject to background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints. Such constraints (e.g., X must be an ancestor of Y, or X cannot be an ancestor of Z) are non-decomposable, which prevents their direct use in most state-of-the-art, score-based learning algorithms that rely on decomposable scores and dynamic programming.

The authors build upon a recent framework that performs an A* search over a space of equivalence classes of DAGs (an "EC tree"). Their main contribution is a method to effectively incorporate the non-decomposable ancestral constraints into this search. The approach has two key components: 1) A sound pruning rule to remove nodes from the EC tree that are inconsistent with the given constraints. 2) A novel technique to "project" the non-decomposable ancestral constraints into a set of decomposable edge and ordering constraints. These inferred decomposable constraints are then passed to the oracle that computes the A* search heuristic, making the heuristic tighter and the overall search significantly more efficient. The method for inferring ordering constraints via a reduction to MaxSAT is particularly noteworthy. Empirically, the proposed approach is shown to be orders of magnitude faster than a state-of-the-art integer linear programming (ILP) baseline.

**Strengths**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a significant and practical problem. Incorporating expert knowledge is crucial for learning reliable models, especially with limited data, and ancestral relations are a natural way to express such knowledge. The proposed method for projecting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones that can empower an existing exact search algorithm is a novel and powerful contribution.
*   **Technical Quality:** The approach is technically sound. The pruning rules for the EC tree search are correct, and the methods for inferring edge and ordering constraints are well-founded. The use of MaxSAT to find a maximal set of consistent ordering constraints is an elegant solution. The algorithm remains an exact method, guaranteeing the optimality of the learned structure.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments are extensive and convincing. The authors demonstrate that their method provides orders-of-magnitude speedups compared to GOBNILP, a competitive ILP-based system. The results also clearly show that the constraint projection technique is the main driver of these performance gains and that incorporating even a small percentage of ancestral constraints can significantly improve model accuracy, rivaling the effect of a large increase in data.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

The main weakness of the paper lies in the clarity of the experimental presentation. While the results themselves are strong, their organization could be improved to more directly highlight the central contributions.

*   The core ablation study, which demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed constraint projection technique, is split between Table 1 (results with projection) and Table 2 (results without projection). This makes it difficult for the reader to make a direct comparison and immediately appreciate the performance impact of this key contribution. It would be much clearer to present a side-by-side comparison of the proposed method (EC tree + projection) against a baseline of the same search algorithm without projection (EC tree alone).
*   My understanding is that Table 1 compares `(EC tree + projection)` versus `(GOBNILP + projection)`, while Table 2 compares `EC tree` versus `GOBNILP`. This setup is somewhat confusing. While the comparison showing that the projection technique also benefits the ILP solver is interesting, the primary claim of the paper is about the new method built on the EC tree. Given that the EC tree approach is substantially superior to GOBNILP with or without the projected constraints, the most important comparison is the ablation on the EC tree method itself. A single table focused on this comparison would strengthen the paper's message considerably.

In summary, the technical contributions are solid and significant, but the experimental section could be restructured for greater clarity and impact.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses an important and practical problem in Bayesian network structure learning.
*   Presents a novel and technically sound method for incorporating non-decomposable constraints into an exact, score-based algorithm.
*   The empirical results show dramatic performance improvements over a state-of-the-art baseline.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The presentation of the experimental results could be clearer, potentially obscuring the impact of the core contribution for some readers.

Overall, this is a strong paper with a novel contribution that is well-supported by theory and experiments. The weakness in presentation is minor and can be easily addressed.
