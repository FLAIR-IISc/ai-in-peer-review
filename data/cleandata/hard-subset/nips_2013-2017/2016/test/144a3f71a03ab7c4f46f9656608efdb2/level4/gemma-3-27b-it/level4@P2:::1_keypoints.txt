- Problem: learning Bayesian networks optimally under ancestral constraints is hard because these constraints are non-decomposable.
- Method: search-based approach using Equivalence Class (EC) trees and A* search guided by a heuristic from the URLearning system.
- Key contribution: pruning the search space using ancestral constraints and “projecting” them into decomposable edge and ordering constraints to empower the heuristic.
- Novelty: projecting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones for heuristic improvement is a novel and potentially impactful idea.
- Motivation: incorporating background knowledge (ancestral constraints) is practically important and well-motivated.
- Reuse of prior work: sensible leverage of an existing framework for non-decomposable scores rather than building a new framework from scratch.
- Clarity issues: explanation of how projected constraints “empower” the heuristic is vague and lacks detailed mechanism for integration with URLearning.
- Technical gap: proof of Lemma 3 (supplement) is insufficiently explained, particularly the justification about edge Z → W.
- Ambiguity: phrasing about “inferring” ordering constraints is imprecise — constraints can be added if consistent, not strictly entailed.
- MAXSAT concern: using MAXSAT to infer ordering constraints is reasonable but its necessity and justification are not fully convincing.
- Related work missing: connection to constraint programming approaches, especially van Beek and Hoffmann (2015), is omitted.
- Evaluation weakness: comparison with GOBNILP is weakened by using a suboptimal ILP encoding for ancestral constraints (based on Cussens 2008).
- Suggested improvement: a tighter ILP formulation (e.g., Cussens 2010 Section 3.1.1) would provide a stronger empirical comparison.
- Empirical strength: significant speedups shown versus GOBNILP and clear demonstration of the benefit of constraint projection on efficiency.
- Practical impact: a small number of ancestral constraints can improve model accuracy comparably to increasing training data, valuable when labeled data are scarce.
- Scalability: method scales to ~20 variables, aligning with state-of-the-art score-based methods.
- Acceptance arguments: novel approach, strong empirical speedups, clear impact of projection, addresses a practical problem — recommend acceptance.
- Rejection concerns: presentation clarity, missing comparison to constraint programming, weakened ILP baseline, and unclear justification for MAXSAT.
- Overall assessment: strong and potentially impactful paper; accept with requested improvements to clarity and additional comparisons.
