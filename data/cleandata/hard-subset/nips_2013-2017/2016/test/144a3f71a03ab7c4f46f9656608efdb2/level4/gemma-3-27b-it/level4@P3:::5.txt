This paper addresses the problem of learning Bayesian network structures optimally, subject to background knowledge expressed as ancestral constraints. The core challenge lies in incorporating these non-decomposable constraints into existing structure learning frameworks, which typically rely on decomposable scores. The authors build upon the recent work of Chen et al. [2015, 2016], leveraging their framework for optimal structure learning with non-decomposable priors. Their key contribution is demonstrating how to infer decomposable constraints from ancestral constraints, enabling the use of efficient oracles for structure learning. The paper includes a thorough empirical evaluation demonstrating significant performance improvements—orders of magnitude faster—compared to integer linear programming (ILP) based approaches.  The problem is clearly motivated, and the authors appropriately contextualize their work within the existing literature on Bayesian network structure learning and constraint handling.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an important problem:**  Integrating background knowledge, particularly causal constraints, into Bayesian network learning is crucial for practical applications and improving model accuracy and interpretability. The paper directly tackles this challenging problem.
* **Novel approach:** The technique of projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints to empower existing oracles is a clever and innovative idea.  This bridges the gap between theoretical optimality and practical efficiency.
* **Strong empirical results:** The reported speedups over ILP-based methods are substantial and compelling. The experiments are well-designed, varying network size, dataset size, and constraint density.
* **Thorough related work:** The paper provides a good overview of relevant work in Bayesian network structure learning, including both score-based and constraint-based methods, and accurately positions their contribution within the field.
* **Clear presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The use of theorems to formalize key properties adds to the rigor.

**Weaknesses:**

* **MaxSAT formulation detail:** While the paper mentions reducing the problem of inferring ordering constraints to MaxSAT, the details of this reduction are somewhat glossed over. A more detailed explanation would be helpful for readers less familiar with MaxSAT. While the reference to Li and Manyà [2009] is provided, a more specific description of the clause construction would increase clarity.
* **Scalability limitations (acknowledged):** The experiments demonstrate scalability to networks with 20 variables. While this is comparable to existing approaches, it would be beneficial to discuss potential limitations and future directions for scaling to larger, more complex networks.
* **Oracle dependence:** The performance heavily relies on the effectiveness of the chosen oracle (URLEARNING). While justified, a sensitivity analysis exploring the impact of different oracle choices might strengthen the results.
* **Structural Hamming Distance Interpretation:** The discussion of the Structural Hamming Distance (∆) could be improved. The authors acknowledge that ∆ can be greater than 0 even with perfect constraints due to Markov equivalence, but a more in-depth explanation of this phenomenon would be helpful, particularly for readers unfamiliar with this metric.




**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to a significant problem in Bayesian network structure learning.
* The empirical results are highly convincing, demonstrating substantial performance improvements over existing methods.
* The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and appropriately contextualized within the relevant literature.
* The theoretical contribution (projecting constraints) is insightful and could have broader implications.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* While the MaxSAT formulation is mentioned, the lack of detail limits understanding.
* Scalability, while demonstrated to 20 variables, remains a potential concern for larger networks.
* The performance is tied to the oracle used, requiring justification for its selection.


**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. The proposed approach is innovative, the empirical results are compelling, and the paper is well-written. While the weaknesses are present, they are not severe enough to warrant rejection. I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance to NIPS 2016.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
