

This paper addresses the challenge of learning optimal Bayesian network structures when incorporating background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints, which are non-decomposable and thus difficult to integrate with standard structure learning approaches. The authors build upon Chen et al.'s (2015) framework for non-decomposable scores, which uses an oracle for decomposable scores to navigate the search space of all DAGs. Their key contribution is a method to project non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and topological ordering constraints, enabling standard structure learning algorithms to effectively incorporate such domain knowledge.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the literature on Bayesian network structure learning. Previous work on incorporating constraints (e.g., Borboudakis & Tsamardinos, 2012; Parviainen & Koivisto, 2013) has focused on structure-modular or order-modular constraints, which are compatible with decomposable scores. The authors' approach represents a novel extension that handles non-decomposable ancestral constraints, which are particularly important for expressing cause-and-effect relationships in real-world applications. Their method leverages the EC tree search space (Chen et al., 2016) and demonstrates how to prune this space using ancestral constraints while maintaining soundness.

The paper excels in quality, presenting rigorous theoretical foundations with well-justified theorems (Theorems 1-4) and sound algorithms for constraint projection. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, using standard benchmarks (ALARM, ANDES, CHILD, CPCS54, HEPAR2) across varying problem sizes, dataset sizes, and constraint proportions. The results consistently show orders-of-magnitude speed improvements over ILP-based approaches (GOBNILP), with the EC tree approach scaling to 20 variables—comparable to state-of-the-art score-based methods.

The clarity of the paper is excellent. The authors clearly explain the BN graph and EC tree search spaces (though Section 4.1 on BN graphs is somewhat redundant given the experimental focus on EC trees), and the constraint projection algorithms are well-motivated with concrete examples. The writing is precise and accessible to experts in the field.

The originality is strong, particularly in Section 5 where the authors develop novel methods for projecting ancestral constraints into decomposable forms. This represents a significant extension of Chen et al.'s framework to handle a broader class of constraints, which previous approaches (like ILP-based methods) could not efficiently incorporate.

The significance of this work is substantial. The paper demonstrates that a relatively small number of ancestral constraints (10-25%) can have a similar impact on learning accuracy as significantly increasing training data size (e.g., from 512 to 2048 samples), highlighting the practical value of incorporating domain knowledge. This has important implications for real-world applications where collecting large datasets is expensive or difficult.

However, I have several concerns that would strengthen the paper:

1. The claim that constraint projection "empowers" the oracle is not fully supported. Table 3 shows that projection significantly improves EC tree performance but has minimal impact on ILP (Table 2). This raises questions about the generalizability of the authors' key innovation. To strengthen their contribution, the authors should demonstrate that their constraint projection method improves performance in other learning frameworks that cannot handle ancestral constraints natively (e.g., A* search with order graphs).

2. The easy-hard-easy pattern in Table 3 (as proportion of constraints increases) requires more explanation. Why does this pattern occur, and how does it compare to the behavior of ILP approaches? This sensitivity to constraint quantity suggests that practitioners would need to carefully tune the number of ancestral constraints to achieve optimal performance, which deserves more discussion.

3. The paper does not compare the structural Hamming distance (SHD) between EC trees and ILP approaches in Table 2. Since the authors claim their method is more efficient, it would be valuable to show whether the accuracy is comparable or superior to ILP approaches.

4. Section 4.1 (BN graphs) is redundant with the experimental focus on EC trees. I recommend moving this to a brief mention in the introduction and expanding on EC tree details and the A* algorithm in the main text.

Overall, this is a strong paper with significant technical contributions that advance the state of the art in Bayesian network structure learning. The method is theoretically sound, empirically validated, and has clear practical implications. The minor concerns raised above are important for strengthening the paper's claims about the generalizability and impact of their constraint projection technique.

Strengths: Novel constraint projection method, strong theoretical foundation, comprehensive empirical evaluation, clear writing, significant efficiency gains over ILP.

Weaknesses: Limited demonstration of generalization to other learning frameworks, insufficient explanation of the easy-hard-easy pattern, missing SHD comparison between EC trees and ILP, redundant BN graph section.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to NIPS and should be accepted with minor revisions addressing these concerns. The authors' approach to handling ancestral constraints represents an important step toward more practical Bayesian network learning that incorporates domain knowledge.
