This paper proposes an exact method for learning optimal Bayesian network structures under ancestral (path) constraints. The approach searches an EC tree over CPDAGs using A*; pruning removes nodes/classes that provably cannot satisfy the given ancestral constraints. To make the A* heuristic stronger while still relying on a decomposable-score oracle, the authors “project” non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints: (i) a rule-based procedure infers entailed edge-presence/absence constraints; and (ii) a MaxSAT formulation extracts a large acyclic set of ordering constraints entailed by the ancestral constraints. Experiments compare against the ILP-based GOBNILP and show large speedups. The work builds on and extends prior frameworks for non-decomposable scores (BN graphs, EC trees; Chen et al. 2015, 2016) and contrasts with ILP/CP formulations that can encode but have not been extensively evaluated for ancestral constraints.

Strengths
- Addresses a practically important class of non-decomposable constraints (ancestral/path constraints) that mainstream decomposable-score methods cannot directly enforce.
- Sound pruning in EC trees and clear conditions for when a CPDAG satisfies ancestral constraints.
- Novel projection of ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints; the MaxSAT reduction is well motivated.
- Strong empirical speedups over ILP on several benchmarks, with consistent benefits from projected constraints.

Weaknesses and clarity issues
- Experimental comparisons are hard to parse. Table 1 appears to compare both methods with projected constraints, while Table 2 removes them; Table 3’s setup is less clear. Please explicitly state, for each table, which components are enabled: ancestral constraints, projected edge constraints, projected orderings, and whether the oracle receives them.
- Missing ablations. A direct EC+projection vs EC (no projection) comparison is essential, as is the overhead breakdown for projection (edge-rule closure, MaxSAT solve) versus search. Similarly, showing GOBNILP with/without projected constraints is useful, but the decisive question is how much projection benefits EC over its own baseline.
- Scalability and coverage. Results top out at n≈20. Please discuss memory/runtime bottlenecks (e.g., CPDAG enumeration for inconclusive pruning, MaxSAT size), and whether the approach benefits similarly for larger n or with family-size bounds.
- Robustness. All constraints are sampled from ground truth; what happens with noisy or inconsistent ancestral constraints? Is detection/repair (e.g., closure to a consistent “maximum set”) guaranteed and what is its cost?
- Minor presentation issues: a few typos, dangling footnotes, and notational slips (e.g., inconsistent symbols for constraints) hinder readability.

Evaluation against NIPS criteria
- Quality: The core algorithms and pruning conditions are sound; the projected-constraint inference is justified (with theorems). The experimental design is reasonable but needs clearer setup and stronger ablations; robustness and overhead analyses are missing.
- Clarity: The method section is generally clear. The experimental section needs a more explicit legend and per-table configuration details. Some editing would improve flow and fix typos.
- Originality: Integrating ancestral constraints via EC-tree pruning and empowering a decomposable-score oracle through projected constraints (especially the MaxSAT-based ordering selection) is original and well positioned relative to prior work.
- Significance: Practical impact is promising: orders-of-magnitude speedups over ILP on several datasets. If confirmed with clearer ablations and broader scaling, this could become a preferred approach for constraint-rich BN learning.

Requested clarifications and suggestions
- For each table, list exactly which components are active: EC tree vs GOBNILP; ancestral constraints; projected edges; projected orderings; time limits; memory limits; and whether family-size bounds are used.
- Add ablations: EC w/ ancestral constraints only vs EC+edge projection vs EC+ordering projection vs EC+both. Report the number of projected constraints found and their effect on oracle runtime.
- Report projection overhead (closure rules, MaxSAT solve time) and total runtime composition.
- Evaluate robustness to noisy/inconsistent ancestral constraints (e.g., inject errors at varying rates) and describe how inconsistencies are detected and handled.
- Clarify when CPDAG pruning requires enumerating class(P) and how often this occurs; report average/maximum enumerations and their cost.
- Discuss scalability limits and memory consumption; consider experiments with larger n and/or bounded parent sets.
- Fix typographical and citation issues, and standardize notation for constraints.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, principled treatment of non-decomposable ancestral constraints with exact optimality.
- Elegant projection that leverages powerful decomposable-score oracles.
- Substantial empirical speedups over a strong ILP baseline.

Cons for acceptance
- Experimental presentation is confusing; key ablations and overhead analyses are missing.
- Limited scalability evaluation; no robustness study to constraint noise.
- Some reliance on potentially expensive CPDAG-class enumeration remains unquantified.

Overall, this is a strong and timely contribution. With clearer experiments and ablations—especially EC+projection vs EC alone—and added robustness/scalability analyses, the paper would be significantly strengthened.
