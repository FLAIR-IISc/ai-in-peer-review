This paper presents an efficient method for learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data when subject to ancestral constraints. The core challenge is that ancestral constraints are non-decomposable, making them incompatible with many state-of-the-art optimal structure learning algorithms that rely on decomposable scores. The authors' approach builds upon the recently proposed EC tree search framework, which uses an oracle for learning with decomposable scores. The main contribution is a method to "project" the non-decomposable ancestral constraints into a set of decomposable edge and ordering constraints that the oracle can handle. This projection is formulated as a MaxSAT problem. The empirical evaluation shows that this approach is orders of magnitude faster than a baseline system based on Integer Linear Programming (ILP).

**Strengths**

*   **Originality:** The paper tackles a novel and interesting problem. While incorporating prior knowledge into Bayesian network learning is a well-studied area, the specific focus on using ancestral constraints within an *optimal* score-based learning framework is new. There is no readily available "standard solution" for this task, making this work a valuable contribution.
*   **Technical Approach:** The proposed solution is elegant. The idea of translating difficult, non-decomposable constraints into a format that existing, powerful oracles can leverage is compelling. The use of a MaxSAT solver to find a maximal set of consistent ordering constraints is a clever way to operationalize this translation.
*   **Empirical Results:** The experiments demonstrate a significant performance improvement over the GOBNILP system, which was adapted to handle ancestral constraints. The orders-of-magnitude speedup provides strong evidence for the efficiency of the proposed method compared to a general-purpose constrained optimization approach like ILP.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and the technical details are explained clearly, making the proposed method easy to understand.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

*   **Problem Framing and Significance:** The practical setting of this problem could be debated. When strong prior knowledge in the form of ancestral relations is available, one might argue that the problem is more closely related to causal discovery. In such scenarios, methods that can also account for unobserved confounders (e.g., FCI) might be more appropriate. A discussion of how this work fits within the broader context of causal inference would strengthen the paper and clarify its target applications.
*   **Experimental Evaluation:**
    *   The comparison against GOBNILP, while showing impressive gains, is against a system not specifically designed or optimized for this type of constraint. To provide a more meaningful benchmark of efficiency, it would be valuable to compare against a strong heuristic algorithm like Greedy Equivalence Search (GES). While GES does not guarantee optimality, one could measure the time it takes to find the optimal network when the ancestral constraints are used to guide its search.
    *   The experimental setup assumes that 10% to 25% of all possible ancestral relations are provided as constraints. This represents a very high degree of prior knowledge, which may not be realistic in many domains. The practical utility of the method would be better demonstrated by showing how performance scales when only a small, sparse set of ancestral constraints is available.
*   **Clarity of MaxSAT Formulation:** The formulation for inferring ordering constraints (Section 5.2) raises some questions that could be clarified in the paper:
    *   Constraint 3, the "sufficient condition for acyclicity," appears to have an asymmetric conclusion. The premise `(X < Y) ∧ (Z < W)` is symmetric with respect to swapping the two pairs of variables, but the conclusion contains the term `(Y 6 Z)`, which is not symmetric. Please clarify this logic or correct it if it is a typo.
    *   Constraint 4 seems to imply that any negative ancestral constraint `X 6 Y` is best translated into a single ordering constraint `Y < X`. This does not seem to capture the symmetric case where both `X 6 Y` and `Y 6 X` hold (i.e., X and Y are not ancestors of each other). It would be helpful to explain how such bidirectional negative constraints are handled by the formulation.

**Minor Questions and Suggestions**

*   Line 30: The term "omniscient oracle" is used. Is there a specific technical distinction between this and a standard "oracle"?
*   Line 38: The phrase "empower the oracle" is used frequently. For clarity, it might be more direct to state that the inferred constraints are passed to the oracle to prune its search space.
*   Theorem 1: The wording could be more precise. As a CPDAG `P` represents an entire equivalence class, it seems to satisfy constraints `A` if *at least one* DAG in its class satisfies `A`. However, a descendant `P'` must also satisfy `A`. This implies a relationship between the constraints satisfied by `P` and the constraints satisfied by all DAGs extending those in `class(P)`. Clarifying this could prevent misinterpretation of the "iff" condition.
*   Theorem 3: Given the strict conditions, how often is this theorem expected to infer a positive edge constraint in practice, particularly when the initial set of ancestral constraints is sparse? Some intuition on its practical impact would be useful.

Overall, this is a solid paper that presents a novel and efficient solution to an interesting problem. The main weaknesses relate to the practical framing of the problem and the scope of the experimental evaluation. Addressing the points above would significantly strengthen the contribution.
