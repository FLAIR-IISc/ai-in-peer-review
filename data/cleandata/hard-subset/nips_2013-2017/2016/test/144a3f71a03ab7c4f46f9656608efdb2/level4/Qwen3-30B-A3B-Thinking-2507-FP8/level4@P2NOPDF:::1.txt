

This paper addresses constrained Bayesian network structure learning under ancestral constraints, using a branch-and-bound search over CPDAGs where nodes represent equivalence classes. The approach employs symmetry-breaking techniques and an A* search guided by the URLearning heuristic (as detailed in prior work). A key theoretical contribution is Lemma 3 (supplementary material), which enables pruning via inferred constraints. While I find the lemma plausible, its proof requires greater clarity; specifically, the justification for "Gk contains edge Z → W" based on EC tree edge rules needs elaboration.  

The paper distinguishes between *ancestral constraints* (explicitly provided) and *implied constraints* (standardly termed "projected constraints" here), which arise when ancestral constraints entail restrictions on edge directions. Similarly, *ordering constraints* (e.g., Y < Z) are inferred from the absence of ancestral relationships (e.g., "Z not an ancestor of Y"). However, the claim that "Z not an ancestor of Y implies Y < Z" is misleading: such an ordering constraint is *not entailed* (e.g., the edgeless graph on {Y,Z} permits Y < Z *or* Z < Y). The authors correctly intend that *consistent* ordering constraints can be *added* to the search space, and they use MAXSAT to avoid over-constraining. This is a sound strategy, but the exposition should clarify that inference is *optional* and *consistent*, not deductive.  

Experiments demonstrate speedups over GOBNILP across varying problem sizes (variables, data points, constraints). However, the comparison is confounded: without ancestral constraints, GOBNILP typically underperforms A*-based methods when parent set cardinality is unconstrained (as in these experiments). Thus, GOBNILP’s poor performance may stem from its general inefficiency with large parent sets rather than a specific weakness with ancestral constraints. The paper further compounds this by encoding ancestral constraints via a MAXSAT formulation from Cussens (2008), rather than the more efficient ILP encoding in Cussens (2010), Section 3.1.1. This choice is problematic: the MAXSAT approach uses cubic E(X,Y,Z) variables, while a direct ILP formulation would suffice with binary A(X,Y) (ancestor indicators) and I(Z,U) (edge indicators), plus transitivity constraints on A and:  
$$A(X,Y) \geq \sum_{U: X \in U} I(Y,U)$$  
This eliminates the need for E(X,Y,Z) variables and yields a tighter linear relaxation, as demonstrated in Cussens (2010). The current encoding thus weakens the comparison, as GOBNILP’s poor performance may reflect the suboptimal constraint encoding, not the inherent difficulty of ancestral constraints.  

The authors should instead compare against a properly encoded ILP baseline (e.g., Cussens 2010) or, more compellingly, against van Beek and Hoffmann’s constraint programming method (CP 2015), which often outperforms GOBNILP and could be adapted to handle ancestral constraints. Crucially, the paper omits this relevant prior work, despite its applicability.  

**Summary of concerns:**  
1. **Theoretical clarity:** Lemma 3’s proof and the distinction between entailed vs. inferred constraints require rigorous explanation.  
2. **Experimental validity:** The GOBNILP comparison is confounded by the poor constraint encoding and GOBNILP’s known limitations with unconstrained parent sets.  
3. **Technical oversight:** The MAXSAT encoding for ancestral constraints is suboptimal; the ILP approach from Cussens (2010) should be used instead.  
4. **Related work:** Van Beek and Hoffmann (2015) is omitted despite its direct relevance to efficient Bayesian network learning.  

**Recommendation:** The core idea is valuable, but the paper’s technical execution and experimental design undermine its contribution. The authors should:  
- Revise Lemma 3’s proof and clarify constraint inference.  
- Replace the MAXSAT encoding with the efficient ILP formulation from Cussens (2010).  
- Compare against van Beek and Hoffmann (2015) and a properly encoded GOBNILP baseline.  
- Explicitly address the distinction between entailed and *chosen* ordering constraints.  

Without these revisions, the claim that ancestral constraints enable significant speedups over GOBNILP remains unsubstantiated due to the flawed comparison. A corrected version would substantially strengthen the paper’s contribution to constrained structure learning.
