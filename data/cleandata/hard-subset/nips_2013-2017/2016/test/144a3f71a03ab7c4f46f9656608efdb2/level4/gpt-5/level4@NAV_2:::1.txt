This paper addresses optimal Bayesian network structure learning under background knowledge expressed as ancestral constraints. The search is formulated over CPDAGs, with nodes in the tree representing equivalence classes, and a downward transition from CPDAG1 to CPDAG2 effected by (roughly) selecting a parent set for a BN variable not yet present in CPDAG1; appropriate symmetry breaking is applied. The search is guided by A* with URLEARNING providing the heuristic (the connection to the heuristic oracle follows earlier work). Given ancestral constraints, the search space can be pruned. The central technical ingredient for pruning is Lemma 3 (supplement), which I believe is correct, though I found the proof unclear. In particular, the step “By the EC tree edge generation rules, Gk also contains edge Z -> W” requires further justification. Beyond direct pruning, the authors derive additional constraints implied by the ancestral constraints (“projected constraints” in the paper; “implied constraints” is the more standard term). These include edge constraints usable by decomposable-score oracles, and ordering constraints on permissible topological orders. The treatment of ordering constraints is underspecified. The paper states that one “can infer Y < Z from Z not an ancestor of Y.” The issue is the word “can”: there are DAGs where Z is not an ancestor of Y yet both Y < Z and Z < Y are compatible (e.g., two isolated nodes). The intended meaning is that when Z is not an ancestor of Y, one may choose to add Y < Z, with the goal of selecting as many consistent ordering constraints as possible (not only those logically entailed). The authors control over-commitment via a MaxSAT formulation.

The experimental evaluation varies the number of variables, data size, and number of ancestral constraints, comparing against GOBNILP. The proposed method is substantially faster than GOBNILP. As the authors note, without ancestral constraints, ILP-based methods such as GOBNILP tend to lag behind A*-based approaches when parent sets are unrestricted. Since the experiments here impose no bound on parent set size, it is hard to disentangle whether GOBNILP’s weaker performance stems from handling ancestral constraints or from the usual difficulty with large parent-set spaces. Moreover, the ILP encoding of ancestral constraints (supplement) is adapted from a MaxSAT formulation [Cussens, 2008], rather than a native ILP encoding as in Section 3.1.1 of [Cussens, 2010]. That MaxSAT-style encoding is not well suited for ILP. A more appropriate ILP formulation would introduce binary variables A(X,Y) alongside I(Z,U), impose transitivity on A, and link ancestry to parent selection via A(X,Y) >= sum_{U: X in U} I(Y,U), i.e., if X is a parent of Y then X is an ancestor of Y. This yields a much tighter LP relaxation and should improve ILP performance. This mirrors the approach in [Cussens, 2010] for total orders, except that here ancestry is a partial order so one drops the totality constraint. I expect the proposed method would still outperform GOBNILP even under a stronger ILP encoding, but the comparison is weakened by using an unfavorable formulation. It would also be valuable to compare with the constraint programming approach of van Beek and Hoffmann (often competitive with or better than GOBNILP), which seems adaptable to accept posted ancestral constraints. That work is not cited by the authors.
