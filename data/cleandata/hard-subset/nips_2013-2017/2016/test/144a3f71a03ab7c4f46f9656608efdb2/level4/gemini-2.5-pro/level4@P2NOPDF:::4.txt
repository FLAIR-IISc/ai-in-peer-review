This paper introduces a novel method for incorporating non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable-score-based structure learning algorithms. The core contribution, detailed in Section 5, is a projection technique that transforms these complex ancestral constraints into a set of simpler, decomposable edge and topological ordering constraints. This transformation enables algorithms that rely on score decomposability to leverage rich domain knowledge, which they otherwise could not, thereby improving learning efficiency and accuracy. The paper is well-written and clearly presented, with both the theoretical framework and the experimental validation being easy to follow.

While the proposed method is promising, I have several concerns and suggestions that could help strengthen the work.

**Major Concerns:**

1.  **Generalizability of the Projection Method:** A key concern arises from the results in Table 3, which show that the proposed projection method improves the performance of the EC tree algorithm but not the ILP approach. This raises a question about the generalizability of the projection technique. Is its effectiveness limited to specific algorithms like EC trees? To substantiate the paper's main claim—that it provides a general method for incorporating non-decomposable constraints—it would be highly beneficial to demonstrate its utility on at least one other structure learning algorithm. Ideally, the authors could show that their method enables an algorithm, which previously could not handle ancestral constraints, to successfully incorporate them and achieve improved performance.

2.  **Non-monotonic Performance with Increasing Constraints:** The results in Table 2 for the EC tree performance exhibit a curious "easy-hard-easy" pattern as the proportion of ancestral constraints increases. The performance first improves, then degrades, then improves again. The authors should provide some intuition or analysis for this non-monotonic behavior. This pattern suggests that the performance of the EC tree approach, when augmented with the projected constraints, might be highly sensitive to the quantity of domain knowledge provided, in contrast to the more stable behavior reported for the ILP approach. Does this imply that practitioners would need to carefully tune the number of ancestral constraints to achieve optimal results? A discussion on this sensitivity would strengthen the paper's practical implications.

3.  **Incomplete Experimental Comparison:** Table 2 presents the Structural Hamming Distance (SHD) for the EC tree method but omits the corresponding results for the ILP approach. For a comprehensive evaluation, a direct comparison of the SHD between the EC tree (with projected constraints) and the ILP (with native constraints) under the same experimental conditions is essential. Including the ILP results in this table would allow readers to better assess the relative accuracy of the two methods.

**Minor Suggestion:**

1.  **Paper Structure and Focus:** Regarding the paper's presentation, while the background on Bayesian Networks (BNs) in Section 4.1 provides context, its necessity could be reconsidered. Given that the experiments focus exclusively on Essential Graph (EC) trees, the paper might be more focused and impactful if Section 4.1 were condensed or removed. The space saved could be used to provide a more detailed explanation of EC trees and the specific implementation of the A* search algorithm used in this context. This would enhance the clarity and self-containedness of the experimental section for readers less familiar with this specific search strategy.
