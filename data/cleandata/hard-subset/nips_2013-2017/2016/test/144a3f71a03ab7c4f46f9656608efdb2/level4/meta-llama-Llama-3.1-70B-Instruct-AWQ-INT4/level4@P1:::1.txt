This paper addresses the problem of learning Bayesian networks optimally, subject to background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints. The authors propose an approach that leverages a recently developed framework for optimal structure learning based on non-decomposable scores, which can accommodate ancestral constraints. The key idea is to empower an oracle for learning structures using decomposable scores by passing it decomposable constraints inferred from ancestral constraints.

The paper is well-motivated, and the problem of learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints is an important one. The authors provide a clear overview of the challenges posed by ancestral constraints and how they can be addressed using their proposed approach. The technical contributions of the paper are sound, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the strengths and weaknesses of their approach.

One of the main strengths of the paper is its ability to efficiently learn Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints. The authors demonstrate that their approach can be orders-of-magnitude more efficient than alternative frameworks, such as those based on integer linear programming. This is a significant contribution, as it enables the learning of Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints in a more scalable and efficient manner.

Another strength of the paper is its thorough evaluation of the proposed approach. The authors conduct a series of experiments that vary the number of variables, datapoints, and ancestral constraints, and compare their approach to the GOBNILP system. The results demonstrate that the proposed approach is significantly faster than GOBNILP, even when the number of variables is large.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. One of the main limitations is the comparison to GOBNILP, which is based on a poor formulation of ancestral constraints. As noted by the authors, GOBNILP performs worse than A* approaches when there is no limit on parent set cardinality, and it is unclear whether the poor performance of GOBNILP is due to its failure to deal with many parent sets or its poor handling of ancestral constraints. A more sensible formulation of ancestral constraints, such as the one proposed in [Cussens, 2010], may provide a more accurate comparison.

Additionally, the paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the implications of the proposed approach. For example, the authors note that a relatively small number of ancestral constraints can have a significant impact on the quality of the learned model, but they do not provide a more detailed analysis of the trade-offs between the number of constraints and the quality of the model.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise overview of the technical contributions. However, there are some areas where the paper could be improved. For example, the authors could provide more detail on the implementation of the proposed approach, particularly with regards to the inference of decomposable constraints from ancestral constraints.

Overall, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian network learning, and the proposed approach has the potential to be widely adopted. However, there are some limitations to the paper that should be addressed in future work.
