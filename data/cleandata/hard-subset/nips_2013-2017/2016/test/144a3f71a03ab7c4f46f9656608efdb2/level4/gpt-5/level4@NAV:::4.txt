Review- This paper presents a method to map non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable ones, allowing decomposable score–based structure learning methods to exploit such domain knowledge and thereby improve learning efficiency. The main novelty lies in Section 5, where the authors propose projecting ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and topological-order constraints. The paper is clearly written and effectively conveys both the theoretical development and the empirical evaluation. I have the following concerns and suggestions: i. Table 3 indicates that projecting ancestral constraints improves EC-tree performance but not the ILP method. Does this imply that the projection is effective primarily for EC trees and generalizes poorly to other learners? Given that the central contribution is deriving decomposable constraints from non-decomposable ancestral constraints, it would be more persuasive to demonstrate that injecting these decomposable constraints boosts the performance and efficiency of a method that originally cannot handle non-decomposable constraints. ii. Table 2 reveals an easy–hard–easy pattern for EC-tree performance as the proportion of ancestral constraints increases. What drives this phenomenon? Does it suggest that pruning the EC-tree search space via ancestral constraints is quite sensitive to the amount of domain knowledge, more so than the ILP approach? If so, does the number of ancestral constraints require careful tuning to achieve strong performance? iii. Table 2 reports structural Hamming distance only for EC trees. How does this compare against the ILP approach under the same settings? iv. A minor suggestion: I recognize the BN graph provides the theoretical basis for EC trees, as EC trees extend from it. However, since the experiments focus solely on EC-tree search spaces with ancestral constraints, I would consider dropping Section 4.1 and reallocating space to clarify EC trees and the A* algorithm within the EC-tree context.
