

This paper addresses the problem of Bayesian network structure learning with ancestral constraints, which are non-decomposable constraints expressing cause-and-effect relationships between variables. The authors build upon Chen et al.'s (2015, 2016) framework for optimal structure learning with non-decomposable scores, extending it to handle ancestral constraints. Previous approaches to Bayesian network structure learning typically assume decomposable scores, which cannot accommodate non-decomposable constraints like ancestral relationships. While ILP and constraint programming approaches can theoretically represent such constraints, the authors provide the first empirical evaluation of these methods for learning with ancestral constraints. The paper's key contribution is a method to project non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints that can be efficiently handled by existing oracles for decomposable scores, leading to significant computational improvements.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The authors provide a clear theoretical foundation for their approach, including soundness proofs for constraint projection (Theorems 1-4) and a detailed algorithmic framework for incorporating ancestral constraints into the EC tree search space. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, using standard Bayesian network benchmarks (ALARM, ANDES, CHILD, CPCS54, HEPAR2) with varying numbers of variables, dataset sizes, and constraint proportions. The results consistently show their approach outperforms ILP-based methods (GOBNILP) by orders of magnitude, with improvements increasing as problem size grows. The authors also provide valuable insights into how ancestral constraints impact learning accuracy and efficiency, demonstrating that even small percentages of constraints (10-25%) can have similar effects on model quality as substantially larger datasets.

The paper excels in clarity. The technical exposition is well-organized, with clear definitions of key concepts (ancestral constraints, EC trees, constraint projection) and well-chosen examples to illustrate complex ideas. The figures (particularly Figures 1a and 1b) effectively visualize the BN graph and EC tree structures. The experimental setup is thoroughly described, including details about dataset generation, constraint simulation, and implementation choices. The authors also provide useful theoretical insights about the relationship between ancestral constraints and their decomposable projections.

The work demonstrates strong originality by addressing a significant limitation in current Bayesian network structure learning approaches. While Chen et al. (2015, 2016) introduced a framework for non-decomposable scores, they did not consider how to incorporate non-decomposable constraints like ancestral relationships. The authors successfully extend this framework to handle such constraints by developing a novel projection method that transforms non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones. This approach represents a meaningful advancement over existing methods that either cannot handle ancestral constraints (like order graph-based approaches) or have not been empirically evaluated for this purpose (like ILP approaches).

The significance of this work is substantial. Ancestral constraints are frequently encountered in real-world applications where domain knowledge about cause-and-effect relationships is available. The authors demonstrate that incorporating even a small number of such constraints can dramatically improve learning efficiency (orders of magnitude faster) and model quality (comparable to using much larger datasets). This has important practical implications for researchers and practitioners who want to incorporate domain knowledge into Bayesian network learning without incurring prohibitive computational costs.

Strengths:
- Novel solution to a significant problem (incorporating non-decomposable ancestral constraints)
- Strong theoretical foundation with sound proofs
- Comprehensive empirical evaluation showing orders-of-magnitude improvements
- Practical insights about the impact of ancestral constraints on learning efficiency and accuracy
- Clear, well-organized presentation with helpful examples and figures

Weaknesses:
- The MaxSAT formulation for ordering constraints could benefit from more discussion of computational complexity
- The paper could more explicitly address how their approach compares to constraint-based learning methods that incorporate ancestral constraints (e.g., Borboudakis and Tsamardinos, 2012)
- The evaluation focuses primarily on runtime, with less emphasis on how the quality of learned networks compares to alternative approaches (beyond the structural Hamming distance in Table 3)

Pro-acceptance arguments:
- Solves a meaningful problem in Bayesian network learning with practical implications
- Demonstrates significant efficiency improvements over existing methods
- Provides a theoretically sound and well-validated approach
- Empirical results are comprehensive and consistently support the main claims
- Clear presentation of technical contributions

Con-acceptance arguments:
- Limited discussion of how the approach might scale to larger networks beyond 20 variables
- Some potential overlap with constraint-based learning methods (though the authors acknowledge this in the related work)
- The MaxSAT approach for ordering constraints, while effective, might be less intuitive for some readers

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. It addresses an important limitation in current approaches, provides a theoretically sound solution, and demonstrates substantial empirical improvements. The work is well-executed, clearly presented, and has clear practical significance for researchers and practitioners working with Bayesian networks. The paper meets all the criteria for a strong NIPS submission and should be accepted.
