

This paper addresses an important challenge in Bayesian network structure learning: incorporating ancestral constraints (non-decomposable constraints specifying cause-effect relationships between variables) into optimal structure learning algorithms. The authors propose a novel approach that translates ancestral constraints into decomposable ordering constraints, enabling the use of efficient oracles designed for decomposable scores. The core contribution lies in demonstrating how to leverage the EC tree search space (introduced by Chen et al., 2015, 2016) with an oracle that optimizes decomposable scores, while still respecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints.

The paper makes a clear connection to previous work, noting that mainstream approaches for optimal structure learning assume decomposable scores, which cannot directly accommodate ancestral constraints. The authors properly contextualize their work relative to ILP-based methods (e.g., GOBNILP) and constraint programming approaches (e.g., Cussens, 2008), which can represent non-decomposable constraints but have not been evaluated for this specific problem. The paper's theoretical foundation builds on Chen et al.'s framework for navigating search spaces with non-decomposable scores, extending it to handle ancestral constraints through constraint projection.

The main strengths of the paper include:
1. A novel and practical solution to a non-trivial problem: translating ancestral constraints into decomposable ordering constraints using MaxSAT, which enables significant efficiency gains.
2. Clear theoretical development of the constraint projection method, with formal theorems establishing soundness and completeness (Theorems 2-4).
3. Comprehensive empirical evaluation showing orders-of-magnitude speed improvements over ILP-based approaches (GOBNILP), with careful analysis of how ancestral constraints affect search efficiency.
4. Demonstrated scalability to 20 variables, which is comparable to state-of-the-art score-based methods for BDeu scores.

The paper excels in technical quality, with rigorous theoretical development and a thorough experimental evaluation. The authors demonstrate that even modest numbers of ancestral constraints (10-25% of possible relations) can significantly improve learning accuracy or reduce data requirements, highlighting the practical value of incorporating background knowledge.

Regarding weaknesses, the paper could benefit from:
1. More detailed discussion of when ancestral constraints are realistically available in practice, as the draft review's claim that "only a few ancestral constraints are given" may be misleading. The paper shows that even sparse constraints can have substantial impact, which is valuable for practitioners.
2. Clarification of the "omniscient oracle" concept (line 30) and the precise meaning of "empowering the oracle" (line 38), which could be made more concrete.
3. A more nuanced discussion of the relationship between the CPDAG constraints (Theorem 1) and the full DAG constraints, to avoid potential confusion.

The paper's significance is strong for the Bayesian network learning community, particularly for applications where causal knowledge is available. The efficiency gains (orders-of-magnitude faster than ILP approaches) make this method highly practical for real-world applications. While the problem of incorporating ancestral constraints may not be the most general causal discovery problem (which often requires handling unobserved confounders), it addresses a well-defined and practically important scenario where domain knowledge about causal relationships is available.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a logical flow from problem formulation to theoretical development to empirical evaluation. The technical details are sufficiently precise for experts to reproduce the results, though some sections (e.g., the acyclicity constraints in Section 5.2) could benefit from slightly more intuitive explanations.

In terms of novelty, the paper addresses a gap in the literature that has not been previously filled by existing methods. While the core idea of translating constraints is conceptually straightforward, the implementation and evaluation are sophisticated and demonstrate significant practical value. The approach represents a meaningful contribution to the state of the art in optimal Bayesian network structure learning.

The empirical results are compelling and well-presented. The authors carefully vary multiple parameters (number of variables, dataset size, constraint density) and provide a thorough analysis of how these factors affect performance. The finding that ancestral constraints can substitute for substantial increases in training data size (Table 3) is particularly noteworthy for practitioners.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that addresses a meaningful problem in Bayesian network structure learning. The authors demonstrate both theoretical soundness and practical value, with clear evidence of efficiency gains over existing approaches. The paper is well-suited for NIPS, as it makes a solid scientific contribution to the field of probabilistic graphical models.

I recommend acceptance for NIPS 2016. The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and makes a valuable contribution to the literature on Bayesian network structure learning. The empirical results are convincing and demonstrate significant practical impact. While the problem is specialized, it addresses an important practical challenge that will be of interest to a substantial portion of the NIPS community working on probabilistic modeling and structure learning.
