The authors present a method that leverages a given set of ancestral constraints to inform the (admissible) heuristic for an exhaustive search algorithm. I found the experiments somewhat difficult to follow. Tables 1 and 3 compare different configurations, and it would benefit from clearer explanation of what each comparison aims to demonstrate. Specifically, Table 1 compares two setups: one employing the authors’ EC tree-based approach with constraint projection, and another utilizing the GOBNILP system also enhanced with the projected constraints. Table 3 then compares the EC tree approach with and without constraint projection, and against GOBNILP. While the comparison involving GOBNILP is informative, the significant performance advantage of the EC tree approach suggests that a direct comparison between the EC tree with and without constraint projection would be particularly valuable.

The paper addresses the important problem of learning Bayesian networks with background knowledge, specifically ancestral constraints. These constraints, representing causal or acausal relationships, are common in practice and can potentially improve learning efficiency by reducing the search space. However, ancestral constraints are non-decomposable, making them difficult to integrate with many existing structure learning algorithms that rely on decomposable scores. The authors build upon a recent framework [Chen et al., 2015, 2016] that uses oracles for learning structures with decomposable scores, and demonstrate how to empower these oracles by inferring decomposable constraints from the non-decomposable ancestral constraints. This allows the oracle to more effectively guide the search process. 

The key contribution of this work is a method for projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints, and then leveraging these constraints to improve the efficiency of structure learning. The theoretical foundations are sound, with clear theorems establishing the correctness of the constraint projection process. The experimental evaluation, while potentially clearer with more focused comparisons, convincingly demonstrates that the proposed approach can be orders of magnitude more efficient than existing methods based on integer linear programming (ILP). The results indicate a significant benefit from projecting the constraints to empower the oracle. Moreover, the authors show that a relatively small number of ancestral constraints can substantially improve the accuracy of the learned network, often providing benefits comparable to increasing the amount of training data.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses an important problem:** Learning Bayesian networks with background knowledge is a crucial area of research.
*   **Novel approach:** The method for projecting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones is a clever and effective technique.
*   **Strong theoretical foundation:** The theorems provide a solid basis for the proposed approach.
*   **Empirically validated:** The experiments demonstrate significant performance improvements over existing methods.
*   **Clear writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, although some sections could benefit from further clarification (as noted above regarding the experiments).

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Experimental presentation:** The experimental setup and presentation could be improved to more clearly highlight the contributions of the different components. The comparison between EC and GOBNILP feels somewhat secondary given the overwhelming advantage of EC.
*   **MaxSAT solver:** The reliance on an external MaxSAT solver for inferring ordering constraints may introduce a performance bottleneck or dependency on external software. While the results are promising, the computational cost of the MaxSAT step should be further investigated.
*   **Scalability limits:** While the authors demonstrate scalability to 20 variables, it would be beneficial to investigate the limitations of the approach for larger networks.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a challenging problem.
*   The experimental results demonstrate a significant improvement in efficiency compared to existing methods.
*   The work has the potential to advance the state-of-the-art in Bayesian network structure learning.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The experimental presentation could be improved to more clearly highlight the contributions of the different components.
*   The reliance on an external MaxSAT solver could introduce a potential bottleneck.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. The proposed approach is novel, theoretically sound, and empirically validated. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract from the overall quality of the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to improve the clarity of the experimental presentation.
