Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses optimal Bayesian network structure learning under non-decomposable background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints. The authors build on the BN graph and EC tree search spaces introduced by Chen et al. [2015, 2016], which allow shortest-path/A* search over DAGs or equivalence classes (CPDAGs) even when the score or prior is not decomposable. The key idea is to prune the EC tree using ancestral constraints and to further “empower” a decomposable-score oracle (URLEARNING) by projecting the non-decomposable ancestral constraints onto decomposable edge and ordering constraints that the oracle can exploit.

Concretely:
- They define when a CPDAG satisfies a set of ancestral constraints and show how to soundly prune CPDAG nodes and their descendants (Theorem 1).
- They propose procedures to infer necessary negative and sufficient positive edge constraints from a closure of ancestral constraints (Theorems 2 and 3).
- They formulate the inference of a maximally large, cycle-free set of ordering constraints from ancestral constraints as a MaxSAT problem with hard clauses ensuring transitivity and acyclicity and soft clauses encouraging inclusion of orderings consistent with negative ancestral relations (Theorem 4).
- Empirically, they compare against an ILP-based method (GOBNILP) with ancestral constraints encoded linearly, and report orders-of-magnitude speedups. They also demonstrate that projected constraints dramatically tighten the oracle’s heuristic and reduce search.

This work is related to order-graph-based exact methods [Koivisto and Sood, 2004; Silander and Myllymäki, 2006; Yuan and Malone, 2011, 2013], ILP/CP formulations [Jaakkola et al., 2010; Cussens, 2008; Bartlett and Cussens, 2015; van Beek and Hoffmann, 2015], and constraint-based approaches that use path constraints [Borboudakis and Tsamardinos, 2012, 2013]. The novelty here is the integration of ancestral constraints with EC-tree search and the projection of non-decomposable path constraints into decomposable constraints that improve an exact decomposable oracle.

Strengths
- Addresses an important and practical class of non-decomposable knowledge (ancestral constraints) with a principled, exact method.
- Sound pruning of the EC tree with clear necessary conditions; leverages equivalence-class search to avoid redundant exploration.
- Clever projection of ancestral constraints into edge and ordering constraints; the MaxSAT formulation is a neat way to select a large, consistent set of orderings to strengthen the heuristic.
- Strong empirical results showing large speedups over an ILP baseline, and informative ablations demonstrating the value of projections.
- The easy–hard–easy pattern vs. proportion of constraints is insightful; the accuracy gains from modest path knowledge are practically relevant.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Proof clarity: The key pruning argument (Lemma 3 in the supplement; also the sentence “By the EC tree edge generation rules, Gk also contains edge Z -> W”) needs more detailed justification. Please expand this step with a concrete application of the edge generation/canonicalization rules and any assumptions about the canonical ordering.
- Entailment vs. selection in ordering constraints: Statements like “we can infer Y < Z from Z not an ancestor of Y” are misleading. The paper does not claim logical entailment; rather, it selects orderings that are safe to impose without excluding any DAG satisfying A. Please state this explicitly and clarify the difference between entailed orderings and MaxSAT-selected orderings.
- MaxSAT formulation: The hard constraints listed are terse. Provide the full set of clauses, the exact variable set, and a brief proof sketch that any MaxSAT solution yields a set of orderings O such that G(A) ⊆ G(O). Complexity/runtime of MaxSAT on the sizes considered would also be useful.
- Baseline fairness: The ILP encoding for ancestral constraints appears to follow a MAX-SAT–style formulation with additional E(X,Y,Z) variables; this can be weak for ILP. A tighter formulation would add binary ancestor variables A(X,Y), enforce transitivity, and link parent selection to ancestry via A(X,Y) ≥ ∑_{U: X∈U} I(Y,U). This yields a much stronger LP relaxation and could substantially improve GOBNILP’s performance. The paper’s empirical claim would be stronger if evaluated against such a formulation (see Cussens, 2010, Sec. 3.1.1, adapted to partial orders).
- Missing CP baseline: The constraint programming approach of van Beek and Hoffmann [2015] is a relevant exact competitor that often outperforms ILP; it seems feasible to extend it to ancestral constraints. A comparison would strengthen the empirical case.
- Practicality of CPDAG feasibility tests: When simple path checks are inconclusive, the paper states it exhaustively enumerates class(P) to test satisfaction. In worst case this is exponential. Please discuss the frequency of such fallbacks in practice, any pruning used, and whether more efficient partial checks are possible.

Quality
Overall technically sound, with correct high-level constructions and empirically validated benefits. However, several proofs and algorithmic details (especially Lemma 3 and the MaxSAT clauses) need more thorough exposition to reach the standard of a reproducible exact method.

Clarity
Generally well written, but some terminology and phrasing should be tightened:
- Use “projected constraints” consistently and relate to “implied constraints.”
- Clearly distinguish logical entailment vs. safe augmentation for ordering constraints.
- Provide pseudocode or step-by-step descriptions for edge-projection and the MaxSAT construction.
- Fix typographical issues: stray text (“th Conference…”, non-English date strings), unify theorem/lemma numbering, and clean up references.

Originality
The combination of EC-tree pruning under ancestral constraints and the projection of non-decomposable path constraints into decomposable constraints for an exact oracle appears novel and non-trivial.

Significance
High practical relevance: ancestral knowledge is common, and the observed speedups and accuracy gains suggest this approach could be adopted in exact BN learning toolkits.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear and useful problem with a principled, exact solution.
- Strong empirical improvements, especially with projected constraints.
- Novel integration of non-decomposable constraints with equivalence-class search and a decomposable oracle.

Cons against acceptance
- Insufficient detail in key proofs and MaxSAT encoding.
- Comparisons rely on a relatively weak ILP formulation; lack of CP baseline.
- Some steps (CPDAG feasibility via enumeration) may not scale without further clarification.

Suggestions for revision
- Expand the proof of Lemma 3 and the EC-tree edge-generation argument.
- Provide full MaxSAT encoding details and a proof that selected orderings are safe (do not exclude any DAG satisfying A).
- Re-implement the ILP baseline with ancestor variables and transitivity constraints, or justify why the chosen encoding is representative.
- Add a CP baseline (or discuss expected performance and feasibility).
- Report how often class(P) must be enumerated and any mitigations.

Overall, this is a strong and promising paper; with the clarifications and stronger baselines suggested above, it would make a valuable contribution.
