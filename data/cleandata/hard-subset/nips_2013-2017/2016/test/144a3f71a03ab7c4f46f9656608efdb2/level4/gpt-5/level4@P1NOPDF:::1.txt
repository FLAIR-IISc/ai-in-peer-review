This paper tackles the constrained optimization problem of learning Bayesian networks from local scores under ancestral constraints. The search is branch-and-bound with CPDAGs as nodes; one descends from CPDAG1 to CPDAG2 by (roughly) fixing the parent set for a BN variable not yet fixed in CPDAG1. Sensible symmetry breaking is applied. The search uses A*, with the URLearning system providing the heuristic (details are deferred to an earlier paper).

Given ancestral constraints, the authors show that pruning is possible; Lemma 3 (in the supplementary material) is central here. I believe the claim is correct, but the proof is unclear. In particular, the statement “By the EC tree edge generation rules, Gk also contains edge Z -> W” needs further justification.

In addition, there are what I would call implied constraints (the paper calls them “projected constraints”): sets of ancestral constraints imply edge constraints that enable further pruning. The paper also infers constraints on permissible topological orders (“ordering constraints”). However, the approach is not explained clearly. The authors state that “we can infer Y < Z from Z not an ancestor of Y.” The issue is the word “can.” There exist DAGs where Z is not an ancestor of Y yet Y < Z is consistent—for example, the graph on vertices Z and Y with no edges. The authors, of course, know this. What they appear to mean is that if “Z is not an ancestor of Y,” then we may choose to add the ordering constraint Y < Z. The goal is to select as many consistent ordering constraints as possible (not only those that are logically entailed). The authors take care not to add too many and use a MAXSAT approach to make these choices.

The experiments vary the number of variables, datapoints, and ancestral constraints, and compare against GOBNILP. The proposed system is shown to be much faster. As the authors note, without ancestral constraints GOBNILP underperforms A* approaches when there is no bound on parent-set cardinality. In the present experiments, there is no such bound, so it is hard to determine whether GOBNILP’s poor performance is due to its known difficulty with many parent sets or specifically to handling ancestral constraints.

Moreover, the encoding of ancestral constraints (given in the supplement) is based on the MAXSAT encoding from Cussens (2008), rather than, for example, the ILP encoding in Section 3.1.1 of Cussens (2010). This MAXSAT-derived encoding is a weak formulation for an ILP solver. Why not introduce A(X,Y) binary variables (X is an ancestor of Y) alongside the I(Z,U) variables (U is the chosen parent set for Z) and omit the cubic number of E(X,Y,Z) variables? Then add transitivity constraints on A(X,Y) and link A and I with
A(X,Y) >= sum over U containing X of I(Y,U).
In other words, if X is a parent of Y, then X is an ancestor of Y. This yields a much tighter linear relaxation and should significantly improve ILP performance. It mirrors the approach in Section 3.1.1 of Cussens (2010) for a total variable ordering, except that one constraint is omitted here because “ancestor” induces a partial, not total, order.

I am confident that even with a stronger, sensible ILP formulation of ancestral constraints, the current system would still outperform GOBNILP. However, the paper’s empirical claims are weakened by comparing against a needlessly poor formulation rather than a competitive one.

It would also be interesting to compare against the method of van Beek and Hoffmann, which often outperforms GOBNILP. I expect their approach could be adapted to accommodate posted ancestral constraints. This related work is not cited in the paper.
