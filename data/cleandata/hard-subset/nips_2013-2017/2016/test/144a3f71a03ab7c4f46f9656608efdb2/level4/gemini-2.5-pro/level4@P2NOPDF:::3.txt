This paper presents an efficient method for incorporating ancestral constraints into score-based optimal Bayesian network (BN) learning. The core idea is to translate a given set of ancestral constraints into a maximal set of ordering constraints. These ordering constraints are then integrated into a search algorithm based on the equivalence class (EC) tree structure introduced by Chen et al. (2015). The proposed method is evaluated against a straightforward modification of the Integer Linear Programming (ILP) based Gobnilp algorithm and demonstrates a significant performance improvement in this specific setting.

The paper is generally well-written, and the proposed solution is clearly explained. The problem it addresses—finding optimal BNs subject to ancestral constraints—is novel, as there is no standard, off-the-shelf solution for this task. The approach of translating ancestral constraints into ordering constraints, which are more amenable to decomposable score-based search, is conceptually elegant. The empirical results, which show a substantial speed-up over the adapted Gobnilp baseline, are impressive at first glance.

Despite these strengths, there are several concerns regarding the problem's framing, the methodological details, and the experimental evaluation.

**Major Comments:**

1.  **Practical Relevance and Impact:** While the problem is novel from a technical standpoint, its practical relevance is somewhat questionable. In scenarios where ancestral relationships are known, it might be more appropriate to employ more general causal discovery algorithms that can also account for latent confounders, a common challenge in real-world settings. The paper's framing feels slightly artificial, which potentially limits the broader impact of the proposed method.

2.  **Methodology and Constraint Translation:** The translation from ancestral to ordering constraints via a MaxSAT solver raises some questions. The formulation of the acyclicity rules (lines 257-261) appears problematic.
    *   In Rule 3, the premise `(X < Y) and (Z < W)` is symmetric with respect to the two pairs of variables, but the conclusion `Y -/-> Z` is not. This asymmetry is not explained and seems inconsistent.
    *   Rule 4 appears to preclude certain useful types of constraints, such as specifying that 'X and Y are not ancestors of each other'.
    Further clarification on these formulations is needed. There is also a concern about the practical utility of this translation: if only a few ancestral constraints are available (as is common in practice), they may not be sufficient to significantly prune the search space, diminishing the method's efficiency gains. Similarly, Theorem 3, which infers required edges, seems unlikely to be very effective with a sparse set of initial constraints.

3.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The empirical comparison is a primary concern. The proposed method is benchmarked against Gobnilp, an ILP-based solver, modified to include the ancestral constraints as additional linear inequalities. While the performance gains are substantial, this comparison may be misleading. Gobnilp was not designed for this specific type of constraint, and the modification, while straightforward, is not necessarily an optimized or fair baseline.

    A more informative benchmark could be established by comparing against a greedy search algorithm like GES. Assuming the provided ancestral constraints are consistent with the ground truth, one could use them as an oracle to guide GES. While greedy methods do not guarantee optimality, such a comparison would provide a more meaningful assessment of the practical efficiency achieved by the proposed exact algorithm relative to a strong heuristic. Additionally, the experimental setup, which assumes that 10% to 25% of all possible ancestral relations are known, seems overly optimistic and may not reflect typical real-world scenarios where prior knowledge is much sparser.

**Minor Comments and Questions:**

*   **Line 30:** Is there a meaningful distinction between a "standard" oracle and an "omniscient" oracle? Please clarify or use consistent terminology.
*   **Line 38:** The phrase "empower the oracle" is slightly awkward. Consider rephrasing to something like "used in conjunction with an oracle" or "to augment the oracle's knowledge."
*   **Line 90:** It would be helpful to explicitly state that "optimizing" the score function means "minimizing" it in this context for clarity.
*   **Line 164 (Theorem 1):** The theorem should be stated more precisely. It needs to be clear that the set of constraints A satisfied by the CPDAG P on the EC tree is a *subset* of the constraints satisfied by the full DAG G. As written, the "if and only if" condition could be misinterpreted.
*   **Line 267:** The logical formulation for acyclicity is not immediately intuitive and connects to the concerns raised above about the rules in lines 257-261.

**Conclusion:**

In summary, this paper introduces a novel and interesting approach to a new problem in BN structure learning. The solution is clever and the paper is clearly presented. However, significant concerns about the practical relevance of the problem setting, the formal correctness of the constraint translation rules, and the fairness of the experimental evaluation temper the overall contribution. While the work is of definite interest to a specialist audience in graphical model learning, its impact may be limited unless these issues are addressed.
