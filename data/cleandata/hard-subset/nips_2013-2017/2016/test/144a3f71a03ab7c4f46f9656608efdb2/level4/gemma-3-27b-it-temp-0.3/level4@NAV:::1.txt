This paper addresses the constrained optimization problem of Bayesian network structure learning from local scores, incorporating ancestral constraints. The proposed approach employs a branch-and-bound search, navigating a space of CPDAGs by iteratively defining parent sets for variables not yet present in the current CPDAG. Effective symmetry breaking is implemented, and the search is guided by an A* algorithm utilizing the URLearning system for heuristic evaluation (details of which are found in a prior publication). The inclusion of ancestral constraints allows for pruning of the search tree, with Lemma 3 (in the supplementary material) representing a key result enabling this pruning. While I believe the lemma to be correct, the provided proof lacks clarity; specifically, the statement “By the EC tree edge generation rules, Gk also contains edge Z - > W” requires further elaboration. 

The paper also deals with “implied constraints” – termed “projected constraints” here – derived from the initial ancestral constraints, and leverages constraints on permissible topological orderings (“ordering constraints”). However, the method for inferring these ordering constraints is not fully explained. The statement regarding inferring Y < Z from Z not being an ancestor of Y is imprecise. While technically correct, it overlooks scenarios where Z is not an ancestor of Y yet Y < Z remains a valid ordering (e.g., a graph with only Z and Y and no edges). The authors’ intention appears to be to proactively *add* the constraint Y < Z when Z is not an ancestor of Y, rather than simply inferring entailed constraints. They appropriately employ a MAXSAT approach to manage the addition of consistent, but not strictly entailed, ordering constraints.

Experimental evaluation varies the number of variables, data points, and ancestral constraints, demonstrating significant speed improvements over the GOBNILP system. The authors acknowledge that GOBNILP’s performance degrades without ancestral constraints compared to A* approaches with unbounded parent set cardinality. Given the absence of a parent set cardinality limit in these experiments, it’s unclear whether GOBNILP’s poor performance stems from its inherent difficulties with large parent sets or specifically from handling ancestral constraints. Furthermore, the ancestral constraint encoding, based on a MAXSAT formulation from [Cussens, 2008], differs from the more efficient ILP encoding presented in Section 3.1.1 of [Cussens, 2010]. This MAXSAT encoding appears suboptimal for an ILP system. A more effective formulation would involve incorporating binary A(X,Y) variables (representing X being a parent of Y) alongside the existing I(Z,U) variables, eliminating the need for the cubic number of E(X,Y,Z) variables.  Specifically, adding transitivity constraints on A(X,Y) and the constraints A(X,Y) ≥ Σ{U:X∈U} I(Y,U) – ensuring that if X is a parent of Y, then X is an ancestor of Y – would yield a tighter linear relaxation and improved performance. This mirrors the approach in Section 3.1.1 of [Cussens, 2010] for total orderings, adapted for the partial order of ancestry. 

I am confident that even with a more sensible ILP formulation of ancestral constraints, the proposed system would outperform GOBNILP. However, the comparison to a suboptimal formulation weakens the paper. A comparison to the constraint programming approach of van Beek and Hoffmann, which often surpasses GOBNILP, would be valuable, particularly given the potential for adapting their method to incorporate ancestral constraints. Notably, the van Beek and Hoffmann paper is not cited by the authors.
