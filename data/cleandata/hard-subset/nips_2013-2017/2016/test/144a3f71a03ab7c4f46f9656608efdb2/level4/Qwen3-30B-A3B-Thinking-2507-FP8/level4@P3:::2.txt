

The paper addresses a significant challenge in Bayesian network structure learning: incorporating ancestral constraints, which are non-decomposable, into optimal learning frameworks. The authors build upon Chen et al.'s EC tree framework [2015, 2016], which enables optimal structure learning with non-decomposable scores by navigating a search space of Markov equivalence classes. Their key contribution is a method to project non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints that can be leveraged by existing oracles (e.g., order graph-based methods) to significantly improve search efficiency.

The work relates directly to previous NIPS contributions. Chen et al. [2015, 2016] established the EC tree framework for optimal structure learning with non-decomposable scores, while Koivisto and Sood [2004], Parviainen and Koivisto [2013], and Yuan and Malone [2013] developed efficient approaches for decomposable scores. The paper also connects to constraint-based methods like those in [Bartlett and Cussens, 2015, van Beek and Hoffmann, 2015] and path-constraint approaches by Borboudakis and Tsamardinos [2012, 2013]. However, it makes a clear distinction by focusing on how to incorporate non-decomposable ancestral constraints into optimal structure learning frameworks.

Quality: The paper demonstrates strong technical soundness. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical foundation for projecting ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints (Theorems 2-4) and prove the soundness of their pruning approach (Theorem 1). The experimental design is well-structured, using standard Bayesian network benchmarks (ALARM, ANDES, etc.) with systematically varied parameters (n, N, p). The results consistently show their approach is orders-of-magnitude faster than ILP-based methods (GOBNILP), with improvements increasing as problem size grows. The paper also provides valuable insights into how ancestral constraints affect learning accuracy (Table 3 shows that 10-25% ancestral constraints can have similar impact on model accuracy as increasing data size from 512 to 8192).

Clarity: The paper is generally well-organized, with clear explanations of the EC tree framework and how ancestral constraints can be incorporated. The theoretical contributions are well-motivated, and the experimental section provides sufficient detail for reproducibility. However, the experimental comparisons in Tables 1-3 could be clarified. The draft review correctly identifies confusion about the experimental setup: Table 1 shows EC tree with projected constraints (EC+proj) versus GOBNILP with projected constraints (GOB+proj), while Table 2 shows EC tree without projected constraints versus GOBNILP without projected constraints. The comparison between EC tree with and without projected constraints (which would better illustrate the value of their projection method) is not directly presented, though Table 2 does show that projected constraints significantly improve both methods (with greater impact on EC tree).

Originality: This work makes a meaningful contribution to the field. While ILP and CP approaches can represent ancestral constraints [Jaakkola et al., 2010, Bartlett and Cussens, 2015], the authors provide the first comprehensive evaluation of these methods for learning with ancestral constraints, demonstrating their inefficiency compared to the EC tree approach. The novel contribution lies in their method for projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints that can be used to empower existing oracles. This is a significant advancement over previous work, as it allows leveraging efficient oracles (which require decomposable constraints) to handle non-decomposable ancestral constraints.

Significance: The paper addresses an important practical challenge in Bayesian network learning where domain knowledge (in the form of causal relationships) is available but cannot be incorporated into most optimal structure learning approaches. The demonstrated efficiency gains (orders-of-magnitude faster) make this method highly practical for real-world applications where learning time is a constraint. The results showing that a small number of ancestral constraints can have similar impact on model accuracy as substantially more data (Table 3) highlight the value of integrating background knowledge, which is particularly relevant for domains where data collection is expensive.

Strengths:
1. Clear theoretical contribution: Sound and complete method for projecting ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints.
2. Strong empirical validation: Comprehensive experiments showing significant efficiency gains over ILP-based approaches.
3. Practical relevance: Demonstrates how to incorporate domain knowledge (ancestral constraints) into optimal structure learning.
4. Clear connection to prior work: Positions the contribution within the broader literature on Bayesian network structure learning.

Weaknesses:
1. Experimental comparison could be more directly framed: The paper compares EC+proj vs GOB+proj (Table 1), but doesn't explicitly show EC vs EC+proj, which would better illustrate the impact of their constraint projection method.
2. Limited discussion of limitations: The paper doesn't explore scenarios where ancestral constraints might be incomplete or conflicting, or how the method scales to extremely large networks.
3. Theoretical connection to MaxSAT: The MaxSAT approach for ordering constraints is promising but could be more thoroughly motivated.

Overall, this paper makes a valuable contribution to Bayesian network structure learning by enabling the incorporation of important domain knowledge (ancestral constraints) into optimal learning frameworks. The technical approach is sound, the experiments are well-designed, and the results demonstrate significant practical benefits. While the experimental comparisons could be clarified, the work is clearly original and addresses an important gap in the literature.

Pro: Strong theoretical contribution, significant efficiency gains, practical relevance, clear connection to prior work.
Con: Experimental comparison could be clearer, limited discussion of limitations.

Recommendation: Accept. The paper makes a substantial contribution to the field with clear theoretical and practical advances. The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and addresses an important challenge in Bayesian network learning. The results demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods, making this an important contribution for both theoretical and practical applications of Bayesian network structure learning.
