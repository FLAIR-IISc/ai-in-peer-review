This paper addresses the constrained optimization problem of learning Bayesian networks from local scores under ancestral constraints. The search employs a branch-and-bound algorithm, where nodes in the search tree represent CPDAGs and progression from CPDAG1 to CPDAG2 involves, effectively, selecting a parent set for a Bayesian network variable not already present in CPDAG1. Symmetry breaking is implemented effectively. The A* search algorithm is utilized, with a heuristic provided by the URLearning system (implementation details are available in a prior publication). Leveraging ancestral constraints allows for pruning of the search tree. Lemma 3 (in the supplementary material) is central to this pruning; while I believe the lemma to be correct, the provided proof lacks clarity. Specifically, the statement “By the EC tree edge generation rules, Gk also contains edge Z -> W” requires further explanation. The paper also considers implied constraints (referred to as “projected constraints” here, though “implied constraints” is standard terminology) and constraints on permissible topological orderings (“ordering constraints”). However, the methodology for deriving these constraints is not fully explained. For instance, the statement “we can infer Y < Z from Z not an ancestor of Y” is imprecise. While it is true that Z not being an ancestor of Y *allows* for the ordering Y < Z, it doesn't *entail* it – a graph with only Z and Y and no edges serves as a counterexample. The authors likely intend to convey that, when Z is not an ancestor of Y, the constraint Y < Z *can be added* to the set of consistent orderings. Their approach aims to infer as many consistent ordering constraints as possible, not just those strictly entailed, and employs MAXSAT to avoid over-constraining the search.

Experiments vary the number of variables, data points, and ancestral constraints. The results demonstrate a significant speedup compared to the GOBNILP system. However, the authors acknowledge that GOBNILP’s performance degrades relative to A* algorithms without limitations on parent set cardinality. Since the experiments do not impose such a limit, it's unclear whether GOBNILP’s observed poor performance is due to inherent limitations in handling large parent sets or specifically to the ancestral constraints. Furthermore, the encoding of ancestral constraints (provided in the supplementary material) utilizes a MAXSAT formulation from [Cussens, 2008] instead of the ILP encoding presented in Section 3.1.1 of [Cussens, 2010]. This MAXSAT encoding is suboptimal for an ILP system. A more efficient formulation would involve incorporating binary A(X,Y) variables to indicate parent-child relationships in addition to the I(Z,U) variables, thus avoiding the cubic number of E(X,Y,Z) variables. Only transitivity constraints on A(X,Y) and the following link between A and I variables would be necessary: A(X,Y) ≥ Σ{U:X∈U} I(Y,U). This ensures that if X is a parent of Y, then X is an ancestor of Y. Such a formulation would yield a tighter linear relaxation and, consequently, improved performance. This approach mirrors Section 3.1.1 of [Cussens, 2010] for total ordering, with the modification to account for ancestor being a partial rather than total order. I anticipate that even with a more sensible ILP formulation of the ancestral constraints, the proposed system will outperform GOBNILP, but the comparison to a suboptimal encoding weakens the paper.

A comparison with the method of van Beek and Hoffmann would be valuable, especially given its frequent superiority to GOBNILP. The authors do not cite this relevant work, and adapting their method to accommodate ancestral constraints shouldn't be overly difficult.
