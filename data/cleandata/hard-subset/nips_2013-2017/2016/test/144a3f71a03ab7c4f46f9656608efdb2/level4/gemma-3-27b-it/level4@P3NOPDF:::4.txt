## Review of NIPS 2016 Submission: [Paper Title]

This paper addresses the important problem of incorporating non-decomposable ancestral constraints into score-based Bayesian network structure learning. The authors propose a novel method for projecting these constraints into equivalent, decomposable forms – specifically, edge and topological ordering constraints – allowing them to be utilized by algorithms that traditionally require decomposable constraints. The paper is well-written and clearly presents both the theoretical foundations of their approach and the empirical evaluation. The motivation is sound, and the technical contribution, particularly in Section 5, is potentially significant. However, I have several concerns regarding the generalizability of the results and the focus of the experimental analysis.

My primary concern relates to the observed performance differences between the EC tree and ILP learning approaches. Table 3 indicates that projecting ancestral constraints improves performance for EC trees but *not* for ILP. This raises the question of whether the benefits of projecting ancestral constraints are specific to the EC tree algorithm, suggesting limited generalization. Given the central claim of this work – that one can effectively infer usable constraints from non-decomposable ancestral knowledge – it would be far more convincing to demonstrate performance improvements (or increased efficiency) in a learning algorithm that *could not* originally handle the non-decomposable ancestral constraints. Showing a positive impact on a previously incompatible algorithm would strongly support the broad applicability of the proposed projection method. 

Secondly, the performance trend observed in Table 2 for EC trees is puzzling. The “easy-hard-easy” pattern as the proportion of ancestral constraints increases warrants further investigation. This suggests that the effectiveness of using ancestral constraints to prune the EC tree search space is highly sensitive to the *amount* of domain knowledge incorporated. If true, it implies careful tuning of the number of ancestral constraints is crucial to achieving optimal performance. The authors should discuss this sensitivity and potentially offer guidance on determining appropriate constraint levels, or investigate why performance degrades with intermediate constraint proportions. Is this related to over-constraining the search space?

Furthermore, the evaluation currently focuses solely on the Structural Hamming Distance (SHD) for EC trees. While informative, a comparison of SHD scores *between* the EC tree and ILP approaches would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the relative benefits of incorporating the projected constraints. This would allow a direct assessment of whether the projection is generally beneficial, even if the gains aren't universally observed across different learning algorithms.

Finally, while I appreciate the theoretical grounding provided by the discussion of Bayesian Networks in Section 4.1, I believe this section could be streamlined. The experiments exclusively focus on the EC tree search space with ancestral constraints applied. As such, a detailed discussion of general Bayesian Networks feels somewhat tangential. I suggest removing Section 4.1 and instead dedicating more space to a more thorough explanation of EC trees, specifically within the context of the A* algorithm used for structure search. A clearer explanation of the A* implementation and how the projected constraints interact with the search process would be valuable. Details on how the heuristic is affected, and the practical impact of reduced search space, are needed.

In summary, this paper presents a promising approach to incorporating valuable domain knowledge into Bayesian network structure learning. The projection method appears sound, and the writing is clear. However, the limited generalizability observed in the experiments and the lack of direct comparison between learning algorithms raise concerns. Addressing these points – by demonstrating applicability to a broader range of algorithms, investigating the sensitivity of performance to constraint proportion, and providing a more comprehensive comparative analysis – would significantly strengthen the paper and its contribution to the field.
