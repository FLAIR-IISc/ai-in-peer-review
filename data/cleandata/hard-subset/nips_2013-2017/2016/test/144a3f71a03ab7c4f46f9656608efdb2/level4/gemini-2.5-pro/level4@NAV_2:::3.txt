This paper presents a novel approach for learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data, specifically addressing the challenge of incorporating background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints. Because ancestral constraints are non-decomposable, they are incompatible with the majority of exact structure learning algorithms that depend on decomposable scores. The authors build upon a recent framework that uses an EC tree search space to handle non-decomposable scores. The central contribution is a technique to infer decomposable edge and ordering constraints from the given non-decomposable ancestral constraints. These inferred constraints are then passed to an oracle—an exact learner for decomposable scores—which prunes the search space and significantly accelerates the learning process. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated through empirical comparisons against an Integer Linear Programming (ILP) formulation, showing orders-of-magnitude improvements in runtime.

**Strengths of the paper:**

*   **Originality:** The paper addresses the novel and important problem of optimal score-based structure learning with ancestral constraints. Standard exact methods cannot handle these constraints, and this work provides a well-founded solution. The approach of projecting non-decomposable constraints into a decomposable form to guide an exact sub-problem solver is an elegant and insightful contribution.
*   **Clarity:** The manuscript is generally well-written and clearly organized. The core concepts, including the use of the EC tree search space and the strategy of "empowering" an oracle with inferred constraints, are communicated effectively.
*   **Quality:** The proposed algorithm is conceptually sound. The experimental results are impressive, demonstrating a substantial performance advantage over an adapted ILP-based system.

**Weaknesses of the paper:**

*   **Significance and Practicality:** The practical impact of the work is debatable for two main reasons. First, the problem setting may be somewhat artificial; in applications where strong ancestral knowledge is available, it might be more natural to employ causal discovery algorithms that can also reason about latent confounders, a capability this score-based framework lacks. Second, the method's efficiency appears to depend on a relatively dense set of prior constraints (10-25% of all possible ancestral relations in the experiments). It is questionable whether such comprehensive background knowledge is available in many real-world scenarios.
*   **Experimental Evaluation:** The comparison to an adapted version of the ILP-based GOBNILP system is a key part of the evaluation. However, since GOBNILP was not specifically designed for this task, the comparison may not be entirely representative of what a specialized ILP approach could achieve. To provide a more meaningful efficiency benchmark, a comparison against a leading greedy search algorithm like GES could have been included. While greedy methods are not exact, they are often fast and can provide a useful baseline for practical performance.
*   **Technical Clarity:** Certain technical details regarding the projection of constraints via MaxSAT are not fully clear. The formulation of the acyclicity constraint (rule 3, line 260) is particularly hard to parse; its premise is symmetric in `(X < Y)` and `(Z < W)`, but its conclusion is not, which warrants further explanation. Additionally, rule 4 seems to only cover one type of negative ancestral constraint (`Y ⊄ X`) and may not address mutual non-ancestry. Clarifying these points is important for verifying the correctness of the method.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   Line 30: The term "omniscient oracle" could be confusing. It would be clearer to use a more standard term like "exact oracle" or to simply define the oracle as an algorithm that optimally solves the subproblem with decomposable scores.
*   Line 38: The phrase "empower the oracle" is used repeatedly. For greater clarity, consider rephrasing this to be more explicit, for example: "supply the oracle with inferred decomposable constraints to prune its search space."
*   Line 90: It would be helpful for the reader if it is explicitly mentioned that "optimizing" the score corresponds to finding the structure with the minimum score.
*   Line 164 (Theorem 1): The theorem's statement that a CPDAG `P` satisfies constraints `A` iff its descendants do needs more precision. Presumably, `A` is the full set of constraints on all `n` variables. It should be clarified that `P` is checked against the subset of `A` relevant only to the variables in `P`, and how this projects to its descendants.
*   Lines 225-227 (Theorem 3): This theorem describes how to infer positive edge constraints. The conditions appear quite strict. A discussion on how frequently these conditions are likely to be met in practice, particularly with a sparse set of initial ancestral constraints, would be valuable.
*   Line 310: The experiments use 10-25% of all possible ancestral relations as constraints. This represents a very high degree of prior knowledge. The authors should comment on the applicability of their method in more realistic settings where only a handful of such constraints might be known.
