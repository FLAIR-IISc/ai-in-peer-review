This paper tackles the constrained optimization task of learning Bayesian networks from local scores under ancestral constraints. The search uses branch-and-bound with CPDAGs as states; one descends from CPDAG1 to CPDAG2 by, roughly, selecting a parent set for a BN variable that is not a node in CPDAG1. Reasonable symmetry breaking is applied. The search is A, with URLearning providing the heuristic (details are not given here but were presented in earlier work). With ancestral constraints, additional pruning of the search tree is possible. Lemma 3 (in the supplementary material) is the central result here. I believe it is correct, but I do not follow the proof; in particular, the sentence “By the EC tree edge generation rules, Gk also contains edge Z -> W” needs further justification. The paper also uses implied constraints (the standard term; here called “projected constraints”). A collection of ancestral constraints implies edge constraints that can be used for pruning. Constraints on allowable topological orders are also derived (“ordering constraints”). The method for doing this is insufficiently explained. The text claims we “can infer Y < Z from Z not an ancestor of Y.” The issue is the word “can.” Clearly there are DAGs with Z not an ancestor of Y for which Y < Z is a consistent order—for example, the graph with vertices Z and Y and no edges. The authors of course know this; what they mean is that given “Z not an ancestor of Y,” we may choose to add the constraint Y < Z. Thus the idea is to “infer” as many consistent ordering constraints as possible (not only those that are logically entailed). The authors take care not to infer too many and use a MAXSAT-based approach for this inference. The experiments vary the number of variables, data points, and ancestral constraints, and compare against GOBNILP; the proposed system is shown to be much faster. As the authors note, without ancestral constraints GOBNILP typically underperforms A-based approaches when there is no cap on parent set size. In the present experiments there is no such cap, so it is unclear whether GOBNILP’s poor performance is its usual difficulty with large parent sets or specifically due to the handling of ancestral constraints. Moreover, the encoding of ancestral constraints (given in the supplement) is based on a MAXSAT encoding from [Cussens, 2008], rather than, for example, the ILP encoding in Section 3.1.1 of [Cussens, 2010]. That MAXSAT-based encoding is a weak formulation for an ILP solver. Why not introduce A(X,Y) binary variables alongside the I(Z,U) variables and omit the (cubic number of) E(X,Y,Z) variables? Then one only needs transitivity constraints over the A(X,Y) variables plus the linkage A(X,Y) >= sum_{U: X in U} I(Y,U)—i.e., if X is a parent of Y then X is an ancestor of Y. This yields a much tighter linear relaxation and should markedly improve performance. This mirrors Section 3.1.1 of [Cussens, 2010] for a total order on variables, with a constraint dropped since ancestry is a partial, not total, order. I am confident that even with a reasonable ILP formulation of ancestral constraints, the proposed method would still outperform GOBNILP; however, the paper is weakened by comparing against a poor formulation rather than a stronger one. It would also be interesting to compare with the method of van Beek and Hoffmann, which often outperforms GOBNILP; adapting it to accept ancestral constraints should not be difficult. The van Beek and Hoffmann paper is not cited. [Cussens, 2010] James Cussens. Maximum likelihood pedigree reconstruction using integer programming. WCB’10. Peter van Beek and Hella-Franziska Hoffmann. Machine learning of Bayesian networks using constraint programming. In CP 2015, Cork, Ireland, August 2015.
