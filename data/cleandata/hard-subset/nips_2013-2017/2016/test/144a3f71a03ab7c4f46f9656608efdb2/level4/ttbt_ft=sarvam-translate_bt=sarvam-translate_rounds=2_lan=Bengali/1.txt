This paper discusses the limited optimization problem of learning a Bayesian network from local scores with genetic constraints. The search is branch-and-bound where the nodes in the search tree are CPDAG and we select a parent set from CPDAG1 to CPDAG2 (roughly). A semantic constraint is violated here. The search is A where a Uದಾರ্নিং system is used to provide a heuristic. (The details of how this happens are not given here, but are found in a previous paper.) Given genetic constraints, some pruning of the search tree is possible. Lemma 3 (complementary element) is the main result here. I believe this is true, but I cannot understand the proof. The sentence "According to the EC tree generation rule, there is an edge Z -> W in Gk" needs further explanation. There are also inherent constraints ("inherent constraints" is the standard terminology, here they are called "forced constraints"). A cluster of genetic constraints means constraints on edges that can be used for pruning. Constraints on the allowed topological ordering are also assumed ("order constraints"). The method of doing this is not explained correctly. We have the statement that we can "infer Z from Y < Z, which is not an ancestor of Y". The problem is the word "can". Clearly there are edges where Z is not an ancestor of Y but Y < Z is a consistent ordering: for example, a graph with Z and Y as vertices and no edges. The authors must know this! What they wanted to say is that if "Z is not an ancestor of Y", then we can add the constraint Y < Z. So the idea is to 'infer' as many consistent ordering constraints as possible (not just the ones involved). The authors are careful not to 'infer' too much and use a MAXSAT method for inferring. Experiments are run with different numbers of variables, datಾಪoints, and genetic constraints. A comparison is made with the GOBNILP system where the current system is shown to be much faster than GOBNILP. As the authors note, without genetic constraints, GOBNILP performs worse than the A method when there is no limit on the cardinality of the parent set. There is no such limit in the current experiment, so it is difficult to say whether the poor performance of GOBNILP here is due to 'normal' failure to work with a much larger parent set or due to the poor handling of genetic constraints. Also, the encoding of genetic constraints (given in the complementary element) is based on an ILP encoding found in [Cussens, 2008], [Cussens, 2010] in section 3.1.1, not on a MAXSAT encoding. And this MAXSAT encoding is a weak formulation for an ILP system. Why not just have A(X,Y) binary variables and I(Z,U) variables, and (dense number!) E(X,Y,Z) variables not be excluded? I think only the following constraints are needed: A(X,Y) \geq \sum{U:X \in U} I(Y,U), which is only a transitive constraint on the A(X,Y) variable and a connecting constraint between the A and I variables. In other words, if X is an ancestor of Y, then X is an ancestor of Y. This formulation provides more rigorous linear relaxations and should provide better performance. This is a method in [Cussens, 2010] in section 3.1.1 for complete ordering on variables but a constraint is excluded because the ancestor is not a partial, complete order. I am confident that the current system will perform better than GOBNILP even with a semantic ILP formulation of genetic constraints, but this paper is undermined by comparing it to a weak formulation, not a semantic one.
