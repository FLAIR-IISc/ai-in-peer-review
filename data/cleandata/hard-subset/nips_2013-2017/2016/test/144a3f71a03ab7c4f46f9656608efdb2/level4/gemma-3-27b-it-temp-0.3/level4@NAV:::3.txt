The paper presents an effective method for integrating ancestral constraints into optimal Bayesian network (BN) learning from data. This is achieved by transforming ancestral constraints into a comprehensive set of ordering constraints, which are then utilized within a score-based learning algorithm built upon the EC tree framework introduced by [Chen et al., 2015]. The proposed approach demonstrates significant performance gains compared to a modified ILP-based algorithm (Gobnilp) in the evaluated setting. The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the methodology, though the core idea is conceptually relatively simple. The work addresses a novel problem with a valuable solution, yielding results that, while not groundbreaking, are certainly of interest to specialists in the field.

The problem of optimally learning BNs subject to ancestral constraints is interesting and novel, as there isn't a readily available standard solution (novelty=3). However, the practical relevance of this specific setup is somewhat limited; when ancestral relationships are known, more general causal discovery algorithms capable of handling unobserved confounders would often be more appropriate (impact=2). The approach cleverly bridges the gap between difficult-to-use ancestral constraints – which are not naturally suited to decomposable score-based algorithms – and ordering constraints, which *are* readily expressed as edge absences and compatible with these scores. The use of a MaxSAT solver ensures that all implied ancestral constraints are explicitly considered. However, some of the rules presented in lines 257-261 appear either unusual or incomplete. For instance, the premise of acyclicity rule 3 exhibits symmetry ((X < Y) and (Z < W) == (Z < W) and (X < Y)), while the conclusion (Y -/-> Z) does not reflect this symmetry. Rule 4 also seems to unnecessarily exclude certain ancestral constraint types, such as those indicating mutual non-causation between variables. Furthermore, it’s likely that in practical scenarios, only a small number of ancestral constraints would be provided, and these may not substantially reduce the search space.

The comparison against Gobnilp with added linear constraints is compelling, but Gobnilp is not specifically designed to address this problem. A more informative performance evaluation would involve assuming the ancestral constraints align with the true underlying model and comparing against a greedy algorithm like GES, which, given oracle input, should ideally reproduce the optimal structure. While greedy algorithms cannot guarantee optimality, this comparison would offer a more meaningful assessment of the method’s efficiency. 

Minor points for consideration:
- Line 30: Clarify the distinction between a (standard) oracle and an omniscient oracle.
- Line 38: Rephrase “empower the oracle” to “use in combination with the oracle” for clarity.
- Line 90: Explicitly state that “optimizing” refers to minimizing.
- Line 164: Theorem 1 should explicitly state that the set of constraints A satisfied by the CPDAG P on the EC tree is a *subset* of the constraints A satisfied by the full DAG G to ensure the validity of the “if and only if” condition.
- Lines 225-227: Theorem 3 may not yield many positive edge implications, particularly when dealing with sparse ancestral constraint sets.
- Line 267: The rule’s connection to ‘acyclicity’ is not immediately apparent (see also comments on lines 257-261).
- Line 310: A prevalence of 10%-25% ancestral constraints would suggest substantial prior knowledge of the target graph, which is not typical in practical applications.
