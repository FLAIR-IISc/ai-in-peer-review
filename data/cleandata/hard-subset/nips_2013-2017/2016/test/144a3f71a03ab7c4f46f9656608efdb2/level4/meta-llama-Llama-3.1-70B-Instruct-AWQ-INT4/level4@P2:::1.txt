This paper addresses the problem of learning Bayesian networks optimally when subject to background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints. The proposed approach is based on a recently developed framework for optimal structure learning with non-decomposable scores, which can accommodate ancestral constraints. The authors demonstrate how to empower an oracle for learning structures using decomposable scores by passing it decomposable constraints inferred from ancestral constraints.

The paper is well-motivated, and the problem of learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints is clearly relevant to the field. The authors provide a thorough review of previous work on Bayesian network structure learning and highlight the limitations of existing approaches in handling non-decomposable constraints.

The technical contribution of the paper is sound. The authors propose a method for pruning the EC tree, a search space for structure learning with non-decomposable scores, based on ancestral constraints. They also show how to infer decomposable constraints from non-decomposable ancestral constraints, which can be used to improve the efficiency of structure learning.

The experimental evaluation is thorough, and the authors provide a comparison with the GOBNILP system, which is a state-of-the-art approach for learning Bayesian networks using integer linear programming. The results show that the proposed approach is orders-of-magnitude faster than GOBNILP, especially when the number of variables is large.

However, there are some limitations to the experimental evaluation. The authors note that GOBNILP performs poorly when there is no limit on parent set cardinality, which is the case in the experiments. Therefore, it is unclear whether the poor performance of GOBNILP is due to its inability to handle ancestral constraints or its general poor performance in this setting.

Additionally, the authors use a MAXSAT encoding from [Cussens, 2008] to encode ancestral constraints for GOBNILP, which may not be the most efficient formulation. A more sensible ILP formulation of ancestral constraints, such as the one proposed in [Cussens, 2010], may provide better performance.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the technical contribution is sound. However, the experimental evaluation could be improved by comparing the proposed approach to other state-of-the-art methods, such as the constraint programming approach of van Beek and Hoffmann, and by using a more efficient ILP formulation of ancestral constraints.
