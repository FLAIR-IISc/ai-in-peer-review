- Paper proposes an efficient method to incorporate ancestral constraints into optimal Bayesian network learning by translating them into ordering constraints used with an EC-tree score-based algorithm.  
- Translation uses a MaxSAT solver to make implied ancestral constraints explicit and produces a (maximal) set of ordering constraints.  
- Empirical results show significant speedups over a modified ILP-based system (GOBNILP) in the evaluated setting.  
- Paper is well-written and explains a straightforward, conceptually clear solution.  
- Problem is interesting and novel because optimal BN learning with ancestral constraints lacks a standard solution (novelty=3).  
- Practical impact may be limited because when ancestral relations are known, more general causal discovery methods that handle confounding might be preferred (impact=2).  
- Some derivation rules for ordering constraints (lines 257–261) appear incomplete or counterintuitive (e.g., symmetric premise but asymmetric conclusion, problematic rule 4).  
- In practice, typically only a limited number of ancestral constraints are available, which may limit search-space reduction benefits.  
- GOBNILP comparison is impressive but may be unfair since GOBNILP wasn’t designed for this problem; stronger baselines are needed.  
- Suggested stronger baseline: compare to a greedy algorithm like GES with oracle input, which should match optimal results in oracle cases and indicate efficiency gains.  
- Theorem 1 needs clarification: constraints A satisfied by a CPDAG P on the EC tree represent a subset of constraints A satisfied by the full DAG G for the iff to hold.  
- Theorem 3 may not produce significant positive edge implications, especially with sparse ancestral constraints.  
- The connection between certain rules and guaranteeing acyclicity (line 267) is not clear and should be clarified.  
- For reasonably sized graphs, requiring 10–25% ancestral constraints implies substantial prior knowledge that is not typical in practice.  
- Minor wording/clarity fixes: distinguish “standard oracle” vs “omniscient oracle” (line 30), rephrase “empower the oracle” (line 38), replace “optimizing” with “minimizing” (line 90).  
- Overall arguments for acceptance: tackles a relatively unexplored relevant problem, offers novel approach with significant speedup, clever use of EC trees and decomposable constraints, and clear exposition.  
- Overall arguments against acceptance: limited practical relevance, incomplete/questionable theoretical derivations for ordering rules, lacking comparison to strong greedy baseline, and unclear real-world impact.
