This paper addresses the problem of learning Bayesian networks optimally under ancestral constraints, which represent background knowledge about relationships between variables. The core idea is to bridge the gap between non-decomposable ancestral constraints and learning algorithms reliant on decomposable scoring functions. The authors achieve this by projecting ancestral constraints into equivalent, decomposable constraints – specifically, edge constraints and topological ordering constraints – which can then be leveraged by existing algorithms. The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed approach, both theoretically and empirically.

The main novelty lies in Section 5, where the authors detail their method for projecting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable forms. This is a significant contribution, as it allows existing decomposable-score-based structure learning algorithms to incorporate valuable domain knowledge that would otherwise be inaccessible. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of this projection, particularly within the EC tree search space. 

However, I have a few concerns and suggestions. First, Table 3 indicates that projecting ancestral constraints improves the performance of EC trees but *not* the ILP approach. This raises the question of whether the benefits of projection are specific to the EC tree framework, or if they generalize to other learning approaches. Given that the primary innovation is the constraint projection itself, it would be more compelling to demonstrate its utility in improving a learning approach that *originally* couldn’t handle the non-decomposable constraints. Exploring this would strengthen the argument for the broader applicability of the method.

Second, Table 2 reveals an "easy-hard-easy" pattern in EC tree performance as the proportion of ancestral constraints increases. This suggests the search space reduction achieved by incorporating ancestral constraints is sensitive to the amount of domain knowledge provided. Understanding *why* this pattern emerges is crucial. Is it due to an over-constrained search space at high proportions, or other factors?  Investigating this phenomenon, and potentially offering guidelines for tuning the number of constraints, would be valuable.  

Third, while Table 2 presents structural Hamming distance results for EC trees, a comparison to the ILP approach would provide a more complete picture of the method’s performance. How does the accuracy of the learned network compare between the two approaches under different constraint levels?

Finally, while the BN graph provides a theoretical foundation for the EC tree, the experimental focus is entirely on EC trees with ancestral constraints. Section 4.1 could be streamlined or removed, allowing for a more detailed explanation of EC trees and the A* algorithm *within* the specific context of ancestral constraint integration.  A deeper dive into the EC tree implementation and its interaction with the projected constraints would enhance the paper’s clarity.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The projection of non-decomposable constraints into decomposable forms is a novel and significant contribution.
*   **Soundness:** The theoretical justification for the projection method appears sound.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate a substantial performance improvement for EC trees.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly presents the method.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The ability to incorporate domain knowledge into Bayesian network learning is highly relevant.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Generalizability:** The benefits of constraint projection seem to be specific to the EC tree framework.
*   **Unexplained Performance Pattern:** The “easy-hard-easy” pattern in Table 2 requires further investigation.
*   **Missing Comparison:** The lack of a direct comparison of structural Hamming distance between EC trees and ILP.
*   **Redundancy:** Section 4.1 feels somewhat extraneous given the experimental focus.



**Overall assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a clear contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. The method proposed is novel and addresses a practical challenge. While the concerns raised above warrant attention, they do not fundamentally undermine the paper’s value. I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance, pending minor revisions to address the points raised.
