This paper addresses the constrained optimization problem of learning Bayesian networks from local scores, subject to ancestral constraints. The proposed method uses a branch-and-bound, A*-style search where nodes in the search tree represent CPDAGs. The search progresses from a parent CPDAG to a child CPDAG by selecting a specific parent set for a variable whose parents were previously unresolved. The search uses a heuristic provided by the URLearning system (details of which are deferred to a prior publication) and incorporates sensible symmetry-breaking techniques.

The ancestral constraints enable additional pruning of the search tree. Lemma 3 (in the supplementary material) is the key result for this pruning. While the lemma itself appears correct, the provided proof is difficult to follow. Specifically, the statement "By the EC tree edge generation rules, Gk also contains edge Z -> W" requires further explanation to be understood.

The paper also discusses "projected constraints," which appear to be what is standardly termed "implied constraints." A set of ancestral constraints can imply additional edge constraints, which are then used for pruning. Similarly, "ordering constraints" on the permissible topological orderings of variables are inferred. However, the method for inferring these ordering constraints is not clearly explained. The paper states that one "can infer Y < Z from Z not an ancestor of Y". The ambiguity lies in the word "can". The constraint "Z is not an ancestor of Y" does not logically entail "Y < Z" in all valid topological sorts (e.g., in a graph with only nodes Y and Z and no edges). It seems the authors' intent is not to infer entailed constraints, but rather to add as many *consistent* ordering constraints as possible to further prune the search. The paper mentions using a MAXSAT approach for this process, presumably to avoid adding a set of mutually inconsistent constraints, but the details of this strategy are lacking.

The experiments evaluate the proposed system against GOBNILP across variations in the number of variables, data points, and ancestral constraints. The results show that the proposed system is significantly faster than GOBNILP. However, this comparison has a potential confound. The authors acknowledge that GOBNILP is known to perform poorly compared to A*-based approaches when parent set cardinality is unbounded, which is the case in these experiments. Consequently, it is difficult to determine whether GOBNILP's poor performance is due to its known weakness with large parent sets or a specific inefficiency in its handling of ancestral constraints.

Furthermore, the Integer Linear Programming (ILP) formulation used for GOBNILP's ancestral constraints appears suboptimal. The supplementary material indicates it is based on a MAXSAT encoding from Cussens (2008), which is known to be a weak formulation for ILP. A much stronger formulation, such as the one in Section 3.1.1 of Cussens (2010), would likely yield better performance. For example, one could use binary variables A(X,Y) for ancestry and I(Z,U) for parent sets, omitting the cubic number of E(X,Y,Z) variables. This would require transitivity constraints on A(X,Y) and linking constraints of the form:
A(X,Y) >= sum_{U: X in U} I(Y,U)
This constraint enforces that a parent is an ancestor. This formulation, adapted from Cussens (2010) for a partial order, provides a much tighter linear relaxation. While I am confident the proposed system would still outperform GOBNILP even with this improved formulation, the paper's experimental claims are weakened by comparing against a known weak baseline.

Finally, a comparison against the constraint programming approach of van Beek and Hoffmann (2015) would be highly relevant. Their method often outperforms GOBNILP and could likely be adapted to handle ancestral constraints. This work is a notable omission from the related work section.
