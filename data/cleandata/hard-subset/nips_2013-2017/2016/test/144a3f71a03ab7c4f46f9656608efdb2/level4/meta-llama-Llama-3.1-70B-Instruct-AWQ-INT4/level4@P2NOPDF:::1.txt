This paper addresses the constrained optimization problem of learning Bayesian networks from local scores with ancestral constraints. The approach utilizes a branch-and-bound search, where the nodes in the search tree are CPDAGs. The search progresses by selecting a parent set for a Bayesian network variable that is not present in the current CPDAG. Symmetry breaking is applied sensibly to reduce the search space. The search is guided by a heuristic provided by the URLearning system, as described in an earlier paper. The incorporation of ancestral constraints enables pruning of the search tree, with Lemma 3 in the supplementary material being a crucial result. Although the proof of this lemma is not entirely clear, particularly the statement "By the EC tree edge generation rules, Gk also contains edge Z -> W," it appears to be correct.

In addition to explicit ancestral constraints, the authors also consider "implied constraints" or "projected constraints," which are standard terminology in this field. These constraints can be used to further prune the search tree. The authors also infer constraints on permissible topological orderings, referred to as "ordering constraints." However, the explanation of this process is not entirely clear. The statement "we can infer Y < Z from Z not an ancestor of Y" is misleading, as it implies that this inference is always possible. A more accurate interpretation is that if "Z is not an ancestor of Y," then the constraint Y < Z can be added. The authors use a MAXSAT approach to infer a maximal set of consistent ordering constraints without over-constraining the problem.

The experimental evaluation involves varying the number of variables, datapoints, and ancestral constraints. The results are compared to the GOBNILP system, demonstrating that the proposed approach is significantly faster. However, as the authors note, GOBNILP's performance is generally poor when dealing with an unlimited number of parent sets, making it difficult to determine whether the proposed approach's superior performance is due to its ability to handle ancestral constraints or its overall efficiency.

The encoding of ancestral constraints used in the comparison is based on a MAXSAT encoding from [Cussens, 2008], which is not well-suited for an ILP system. A more effective approach would be to use A(X,Y) binary variables in addition to I(Z,U) variables, omitting the E(X,Y,Z) variables. This formulation would require only transitivity constraints on the A(X,Y) variables and constraints linking A and I variables, resulting in a tighter linear relaxation. This approach is similar to that described in Section 3.1.1 of [Cussens, 2010] but adapted for a partial ordering.

It is likely that even with a sensible ILP formulation of ancestral constraints, the proposed approach would still outperform GOBNILP. However, the comparison is weakened by the use of a suboptimal formulation. A more interesting comparison would be with the method proposed by van Beek and Hoffmann, which often outperforms GOBNILP and could be adapted to accommodate ancestral constraints. Unfortunately, this paper is not mentioned by the authors.
