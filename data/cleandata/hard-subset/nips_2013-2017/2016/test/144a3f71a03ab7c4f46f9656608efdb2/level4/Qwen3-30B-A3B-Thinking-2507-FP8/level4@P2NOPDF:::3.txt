

The paper presents a method for efficiently incorporating ancestral constraints into optimal Bayesian network (BN) learning from data. The approach translates ancestral constraints into a maximal set of ordering constraints, which are then integrated into a score-based learning algorithm leveraging Chen et al.'s EC tree framework. Experimental results demonstrate significant performance gains over a modified ILP-based Gobnilp algorithm, which is not designed for this specific problem. The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology, though the core idea is conceptually straightforward. The problem addressed—optimal BN learning under ancestral constraints—lacks a standard solution, establishing novelty (score 3). However, the practical impact is limited (score 2) because ancestral constraints typically arise within broader causal discovery contexts where algorithms accommodating unobserved confounders (e.g., FCI) are more appropriate.  

The technical contribution lies in transforming intractable ancestral constraints into decomposable ordering constraints via a MaxSAT solver, ensuring all implied constraints are explicitly captured. While this translation is sound, several concerns arise. First, the constraint rules (lines 257–261) contain inconsistencies: Rule 3’s premise is symmetric ((X < Y) ∧ (Z < W) ≡ (Z < W) ∧ (X < Y)), yet its conclusion (Y ↛ Z) is asymmetric. Rule 4 appears to exclude valid ancestral constraints of the form "X and Y do not cause each other." Second, the assumption that sparse ancestral constraints (e.g., small sets) significantly reduce the search space is questionable; in practice, such constraints would likely imply extensive prior knowledge of the graph structure (e.g., 10–25% constraints would characterize most edges), contradicting the typical use case.  

The experimental comparison against Gobnilp with added linear constraints is misleading, as Gobnilp is not optimized for ancestral constraints. A more meaningful baseline would compare against a greedy algorithm like GES, using oracle input (i.e., ancestral constraints aligned with the true model). While GES cannot guarantee optimality, it would provide a realistic benchmark for efficiency gains, as oracle-guided GES should recover the optimal structure. The current comparison lacks this context, overstating the method’s practical relevance.  

Minor issues require attention:  
- Line 30: Clarify the distinction between "standard" and "omniscient" oracle.  
- Line 38: "Empower the oracle" likely means "use in conjunction with the oracle."  
- Line 90: Specify that optimization minimizes the score.  
- Line 164: Theorem 1 must clarify that the constraint set A derived from the CPDAG is a *subset* of those satisfied by the full DAG, or the iff condition fails.  
- Lines 225–227: Theorem 3’s practical utility for sparse ancestral constraints is dubious, as few positive edge implications would arise.  
- Line 267: The acyclicity condition is unclear and conflicts with earlier points.  
- Line 310: The claim that 10–25% ancestral constraints are "not typical" requires justification, as this would represent highly informative constraints.  

Overall, the paper offers a technically valid solution to a niche problem, but its impact is constrained by the artificiality of the setting and the lack of a meaningful experimental baseline. The method is of interest to specialists in BN structure learning but does not represent a breakthrough. The writing is clear, and the technical exposition is generally sound, though minor revisions to the constraint rules and theoretical claims are needed. The novelty is modest, and the contribution would be strengthened by addressing the baseline comparison and clarifying the practical relevance of sparse ancestral constraints.
