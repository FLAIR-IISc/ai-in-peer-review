

This paper introduces a novel approach for incorporating non-decomposable ancestral constraints into Bayesian network structure learning via constraint projection to decomposable edge and ordering constraints. The authors leverage Chen et al.'s EC tree search space framework, demonstrating that projecting ancestral constraints (which are inherently non-decomposable) into decomposable forms enables significant efficiency gains over ILP-based methods like GOBNILP. The core contribution lies in Sections 5.1–5.2, where the authors formally establish sound and complete methods for inferring edge constraints (Theorems 2–3) and ordering constraints (via MaxSAT reduction), which are then used to empower oracles in the EC tree search. This work directly addresses a gap in the literature: prior approaches (e.g., Koivisto & Sood, 2004; Chen et al., 2015) either cannot handle ancestral constraints or lack empirical validation for such constraints. The paper builds effectively on Chen et al. (2015, 2016) while extending their framework to encompass non-decomposable background knowledge, a critical practical need in causal inference applications.

**Strengths:**  
*Quality:* The theoretical foundation is rigorous, with clear proofs of constraint projection completeness (Theorems 2–4). The experimental design is well-justified, using standard benchmarks (ALARM, CHILD, etc.) with controlled variations in network size, data quantity, and constraint density. The results consistently show orders-of-magnitude speedups for the EC tree approach (Tables 1–2), with structural Hamming distance improvements (Table 3) demonstrating that ancestral constraints significantly enhance model accuracy—equivalent to increasing dataset size by 4×. The empirical validation of the constraint projection method’s efficacy is thorough and convincing.  
*Clarity:* The paper is exceptionally well-written, with precise definitions (Section 3), intuitive examples (e.g., Figure 1), and clear exposition of the EC tree’s advantage over BN graphs (Section 4.2). The algorithm descriptions (Section 5) are sufficiently detailed for reproducibility.  
*Originality:* The constraint projection technique is novel; prior work on ancestral constraints (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos, 2012, 2013) only addressed constraint-based learning, not optimal score-based learning. The MaxSAT reduction for ordering constraints (Section 5.2) is a significant technical contribution not present in related ILP/CP approaches (Jaakkola et al., 2010; van Beek & Hoffmann, 2015).  
*Significance:* The work solves a practically important problem (integrating causal knowledge into structure learning) with demonstrable impact. The efficiency gains (Table 1) enable scalability to 20 variables—comparable to state-of-the-art score-based methods (Yuan & Malone, 2013). The finding that 10–25% ancestral constraints can match data-size improvements (Table 3) is highly valuable for data-scarce domains.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions:**  
1. **Generalizability claim:** The paper asserts that constraint projection "empowers" oracles broadly, yet Table 2 shows no efficiency gain for ILP when using projected constraints. This undermines the generality of the core contribution. *Suggestion:* The authors should clarify that projection is specifically beneficial for oracle-based methods (like EC trees) that rely on decomposable-score oracles, not for ILP/CP systems that directly encode constraints. A brief discussion of why ILP doesn’t benefit (e.g., redundant constraints increase ILP complexity) would strengthen the argument.  
2. **"Easy-hard-easy" pattern:** Table 3 reveals that EC tree performance peaks at intermediate constraint density (p), suggesting sensitivity to constraint quantity. *Suggestion:* The authors should briefly analyze this phenomenon (e.g., sparse constraints fail to prune effectively; dense constraints may over-constrain valid structures). This is critical for practical deployment where constraint density is often unknown.  
3. **Comparative evaluation:** Table 3 omits ILP’s structural Hamming distance, making it unclear whether the EC tree’s speedup comes at the cost of accuracy. *Suggestion:* Include ILP’s Hamming distance in Table 3 to confirm that the EC tree’s efficiency gains are not due to suboptimal solutions.  
4. **Section 4.1 redundancy:** The BN graph explanation (Section 4.1) is largely redundant given the paper’s focus on EC trees (Section 4.2–4.3) and experiments. *Suggestion:* Condense or remove Section 4.1 to improve flow and emphasize the novel EC tree adaptation. The current length dilutes the paper’s core contribution.

**Conclusion:** This is a strong, technically sound paper that makes a meaningful contribution to Bayesian network structure learning. The constraint projection method is novel, well-validated, and practically impactful. While the generalizability claim requires refinement and the "easy-hard-easy" pattern warrants deeper analysis, these are minor points for an otherwise excellent work. The paper advances the state of the art in handling non-decomposable constraints—a persistent challenge in optimal structure learning—and provides a scalable solution with clear empirical advantages. I recommend acceptance.
