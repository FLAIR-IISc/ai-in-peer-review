The authors address the problem of learning Bayesian networks optimally under ancestral constraints, a challenging task due to the non-decomposability of such constraints. They build upon a recent framework for optimal structure learning based on non-decomposable scores, leveraging oracles designed for decomposable scores by intelligently projecting the ancestral constraints into equivalent decomposable constraints. This allows them to empower these oracles and significantly improve efficiency compared to approaches like integer linear programming. The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulating the limitations of existing methods and the benefits of their approach. 

The core idea – projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable forms to enhance the efficiency of structure learning – is novel and potentially impactful. The theoretical framework presented, including Theorems 1-4, provides a solid foundation for their method. The experiments demonstrate significant speedups compared to GOBNILP, particularly as the number of variables increases. The analysis of how constraint projection affects performance is insightful. The connection to MaxSAT for inferring ordering constraints is a clever and promising direction.

A key strength is the thorough empirical evaluation. The experimental setup, varying network size, data size, and constraint percentage, provides a comprehensive assessment of the approach’s performance. The results consistently demonstrate substantial improvements in efficiency over ILP-based methods, especially with the aid of constraint projection. The analysis of structural Hamming distance offers a useful perspective on accuracy.

However, the presentation of the experimental results could be clarified. The comparison in Tables 1 and 2, involving different combinations of methods with and without constraint projection, is somewhat convoluted. It would be beneficial to include a direct comparison between the full proposed approach (EC tree + constraint projection) and the EC tree alone, to isolate the benefit of the projection technique. While the superior performance of EC over GOBNILP is established, focusing on the incremental improvement achieved by the proposed projection method would strengthen the presentation. Furthermore, specifying the equivalent sample size used for the BDeu score would be helpful for reproducibility. 

Arguments for acceptance:
* **Novelty:** The approach of projecting ancestral constraints into decomposable forms is a novel contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning.
* **Efficiency:** The experimental results demonstrate substantial efficiency gains compared to existing methods like GOBNILP, making the approach scalable to larger networks.
* **Soundness:** The theoretical framework, supported by theorems, provides a strong guarantee of correctness.
* **Significance:** Addressing the integration of background knowledge (ancestral constraints) into optimal structure learning has practical relevance in various domains.

Arguments against acceptance:
* **Presentation of Results:** The comparison of different methods in the experimental section could be more straightforward, with a clearer focus on the impact of constraint projection.
* **Reproducibility:** Specifying the equivalent sample size used for the BDeu score would enhance reproducibility.



Overall, this is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field. Despite the minor suggestions for improvement in the experimental presentation, the novelty, soundness, and efficiency of the proposed approach justify acceptance to NIPS.
