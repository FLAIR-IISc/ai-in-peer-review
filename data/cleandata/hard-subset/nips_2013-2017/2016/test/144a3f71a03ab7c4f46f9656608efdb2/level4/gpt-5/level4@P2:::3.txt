Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles optimal Bayesian network (BN) structure learning under background knowledge given as ancestral (reachability) constraints. Because ancestral constraints are non-decomposable, they are not directly compatible with standard score-based methods that rely on decomposable scores. The authors build on the BN-graph/EC-tree framework of Chen et al. (2015, 2016), which searches over (equivalence classes of) DAGs and can incorporate non-decomposable constraints by pruning. They further “empower” an oracle that optimizes decomposable scores (e.g., an order-graph–based solver) by projecting ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints of two kinds: (i) edge presence/absence constraints derived by efficient entailment rules, and (ii) topological ordering constraints obtained via a MaxSAT formulation that selects a maximal set of consistent order constraints entailed by the ancestral constraints. Empirically, they compare to an ILP formulation implemented in GOBNILP (augmented with linear encodings of ancestral constraints) and report orders-of-magnitude speedups, particularly when the set of projected constraints is supplied to the oracle. They also study how the proportion of ancestral constraints affects search hardness and report an easy-hard-easy pattern.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The use of EC trees with pruning is sound, and the projection of ancestral constraints to edge and ordering constraints is well motivated. The edge-constraint entailment rules are clear; the MaxSAT encoding for order constraints is interesting but some rules (see below) appear asymmetric or insufficiently justified.
- Empirical support: Comparisons to ILP show strong runtime gains. The ablation showing the impact of projected constraints on the EC-tree oracle is informative. However, the baseline (ILP) is not tailored to this specific constraint type, and the study does not include greedy or constraint-based baselines that might perform well when ancestral information is available.
- Clarity: The paper is generally well written and easy to follow. Some statements (e.g., Theorem 1, rules in lines 257–261) would benefit from clarification.

Originality
- The problem setting—optimal BN learning with explicit ancestral constraints—has not been exhaustively addressed in prior score-based work. Integrating pruning on EC trees with projected decomposable constraints and a MaxSAT-based selection of order constraints is novel as a combined approach. The conceptual components (pruning in EC trees, ILP encodings, path-constraint use in constraint-based methods) are known, but their synthesis here is new and practically useful.

Significance
- The contribution addresses a niche yet relevant setting: incorporating causal/temporal knowledge of reachability in optimal BN learning. The approach yields substantial speedups over an ILP baseline and demonstrates a practical way to use non-decomposable constraints within a decomposable-score oracle. That said, the setting may be somewhat artificial in practice: when path knowledge is available, many practitioners turn to causal discovery methods that also account for latent confounding (e.g., MAGs/PAGs), which this framework does not handle. Hence, impact may be moderate outside specialist contexts.

Strengths
- Clear formulation of learning with ancestral constraints via EC-tree pruning.
- Effective projection of non-decomposable constraints to decomposable edge and order constraints, with a principled MaxSAT selection.
- Strong empirical gains versus an ILP baseline; useful analysis of constraint density and search difficulty.
- Good reporting of experimental setup and use of publicly available solvers.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Baselines: GOBNILP is not designed specifically for ancestral constraints. Including additional baselines would strengthen the case, e.g., a greedy score-based method such as GES adapted to respect ancestral constraints (or at least evaluated in oracle settings), or CP-based methods. Even if suboptimal, these comparisons would better contextualize speedups.
- Practicality of constraint density: Experiments often assume 10–25% of all pairwise ancestral relations, which may implicitly reveal a large fraction of the target structure. More realistic scenarios likely have few (and possibly noisy) constraints. Please report results with small, noisy sets of constraints and quantify how many edge/order constraints are inferred from them.
- MaxSAT rule set (lines 257–261): 
  - The premise of rule 3 is symmetric, but the conclusion appears asymmetric (involving Y 6 Z). Please clarify symmetry and provide intuition/proof that the set of hard constraints suffices for acyclicity without excluding valid DAGs.
  - Rule 4 seems to exclude mutual non-ancestry constraints (“X and Y do not cause each other”); clarify how such negative relations are treated or extend the encoding.
- Limited positive edge entailment: Theorem 3 may rarely trigger when ancestral information is sparse. Please quantify how often positive edges are inferred, and assess performance when only edge vs. only order constraints are supplied to the oracle (ablation).
- Theorem 1 wording: Specify precisely that a CPDAG node is pruned if no member DAG in its equivalence class satisfies the ancestral constraints; the “iff” should be read with respect to the existence of at least one satisfying DAG in class(P).
- Scope: Discuss limitations regarding latent confounders and whether path constraints in MAGs could be handled within a related framework.

Pros and cons for acceptance
- Pros: Clear problem formulation; sound pruning; clever constraint projection; substantial empirical speedups; good clarity.
- Cons: Baseline choice limits external validity; rule set for MaxSAT needs clarification; experimental regimes may overstate available knowledge; limited discussion of latent confounding and practical deployment.

Minor comments (by line number)
- 30: Clarify whether “omniscient oracle” differs from a standard oracle; if not, use a consistent term.
- 38: “Empower the oracle” could be rephrased as “pass projected decomposable constraints to the oracle.”
- 90: Note explicitly that optimizing the score here means minimizing.
- 164: Rephrase Theorem 1 to state that a CPDAG P is retained iff there exists G ∈ class(P) satisfying A; otherwise P (and descendants) are pruned.
- 225–227: Theorem 3 likely yields few positive edge implications for sparse A; please comment empirically.
- 267: The acyclicity constraint is not immediately recognizable; add intuition or a reference/derivation.
- 310: For moderate-size graphs, 10–25% of pairwise ancestral relations can reveal extensive structure; discuss typical availability in practice.

Overall evaluation
A solid and well-presented contribution with a conceptually straightforward but effective integration of ancestral constraints into optimal BN learning. I view the novelty and technical depth as moderate but the practical speedups as compelling for specialists. Strengthening baselines, clarifying the MaxSAT rules, and adding experiments with sparse/noisy constraints would substantially improve the paper.
