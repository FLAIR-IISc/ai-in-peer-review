This paper addresses the constrained optimization problem of learning Bayesian networks from local scores with ancestral constraints. The search algorithm employed is a branch-and-bound approach, where the nodes in the search tree are CPDAGs, and we 'move down' from CPDAG1 to CPDAG2 by selecting a parent set for a BN variable that is not a node in CPDAG1. Symmetry breaking is effectively implemented. The search is guided by a heuristic provided by the URLearning system, although the details of this process are not presented here, but can be found in an earlier paper. Given ancestral constraints, some pruning of the search tree is possible, with Lemma 3 in the supplementary material being the key result. I believe this lemma to be true, but I do not fully understand the proof, particularly the statement "By the EC tree edge generation rules, Gk also contains edge Z -> W," which requires further explanation.

In addition to explicit constraints, there are also implied constraints, referred to as "projected constraints" in the paper, although the standard terminology is "implied constraints." A set of ancestral constraints can imply constraints on edges that can be used for pruning. The approach also infers constraints on permissible topological orderings, referred to as "ordering constraints." However, the method for inferring these constraints is not properly explained. The statement "we can infer Y < Z from Z not an ancestor of Y" is problematic, as it implies that this inference is always possible. However, there are DAGs where Z is not an ancestor of Y, but Y < Z is a consistent order, such as a graph with vertices Z and Y and no edges. The authors likely mean to say that if "Z not an ancestor of Y" then we can choose to add the constraint Y < Z. The idea is to 'infer' as many consistent ordering constraints as possible, not just those that are entailed. The authors exercise caution not to 'infer' too many constraints and employ a MAXSAT approach to perform the inference.

The experimental evaluation involves varying the number of variables, datapoints, and ancestral constraints. The results are compared to those obtained using the GOBNILP system, which the current system outperforms by a significant margin. As the authors note, GOBNILP's performance is typically worse than A* approaches when there is no limit on parent set cardinality, and the current experiments do not impose such a limit. Therefore, it is difficult to determine whether GOBNILP's poor performance is due to its general inability to handle many parent sets or its specific difficulty in dealing with ancestral constraints. Furthermore, the encoding of ancestral constraints, presented in the supplementary material, is based on a MAXSAT encoding from [Cussens, 2008], rather than the ILP encoding found in Section 3.1.1 of [Cussens, 2010]. However, this MAXSAT encoding is a poor formulation for an ILP system.

A more effective approach would be to include the A(X,Y) binary variables in addition to the I(Z,U) variables and omit the E(X,Y,Z) variables. This would require only the transitivity constraints on the A(X,Y) variables and the following constraints linking A and I variables: A(X,Y) ≥ ∑{U:X ∈ U} I(Y,U). This formulation provides a tighter linear relaxation and should result in improved performance. This approach is similar to the one presented in Section 3.1.1 of [Cussens, 2010] for a total ordering on variables, with the constraint dropped since ancestor is a partial, not total order.

I am confident that even with a sensible ILP formulation of ancestral constraints, the current system will outperform GOBNILP. However, the paper is weakened by comparing to a poor formulation rather than a sensible one. It would be interesting to compare the current method to that of van Beek and Hoffmann, particularly since it often outperforms GOBNILP. Adapting that method to allow ancestral constraints would not be too challenging. Unfortunately, the paper by van Beek and Hoffmann is not mentioned by the current authors.
