Summary
This paper addresses optimal Bayesian network (BN) structure learning under non-decomposable background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints. The authors build on the BN graph and EC tree search spaces (Chen et al., 2015; Chen et al., 2016), which support exact learning with non-decomposable scores, and use an oracle (e.g., URLearning) that optimizes decomposable scores to provide admissible heuristics for A* search. The key technical contribution is a method to project ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and topological ordering constraints that can be fed to the oracle, substantially tightening the heuristic. The paper provides: (i) pruning rules for CPDAG nodes in the EC tree to enforce ancestral constraints; (ii) an algorithm to infer all entailed edge constraints (Theorem 2–3) from a maximally closed set of ancestral constraints; and (iii) a MaxSAT reduction to extract a maximally large, safe set of ordering constraints (Theorem 4). Empirically, the approach is compared against ILP (GOBNILP) on standard BN benchmarks and shows orders-of-magnitude speedups, particularly when projected constraints are used. The authors also observe an easy-hard-easy pattern in EC-tree performance as more ancestral constraints are provided and report improved structural accuracy (SHD) with more data or constraints.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation and motivation: ancestral constraints encode valuable causal knowledge yet are non-decomposable, limiting integration in standard decomposable-score learners.
- Principled search framework: exact EC-tree search augmented with an oracle for decomposable scores, with sound pruning for ancestral constraints.
- Novel projection of ancestral constraints into safe edge and ordering constraints; the MaxSAT formulation for orderings is interesting and practically useful for tightening heuristics.
- Strong empirical performance and scalability compared to ILP on multiple benchmarks; informative analysis of constraint proportion and data size on runtime and SHD.

Weaknesses and questions
- Generalization of projection benefits to other approaches. The projected constraints greatly help EC-tree search but appear to provide at best modest gains for ILP (and sometimes mild overhead at small n). Please:
  - Clarify why projection helps heuristic search more than ILP (e.g., tighter heuristic vs redundant linear constraints).
  - Explore stronger ILP integrations (e.g., using projected constraints as cutting planes, candidate–parent pruning, or order-based formulations) and report whether they close the gap.
- Easy-hard-easy pattern. The paper notes this trend for EC trees as constraint proportion p increases. Please quantify and explain:
  - Report node expansions, oracle calls, effective branching factor, and number of pruned CPDAGs versus p.
  - Provide intuition for why the intermediate region is hardest in this setting and why ILP does not exhibit the same pattern.
- Accuracy comparison. Table 3 reports SHD only for EC trees. Since both methods are exact (subject to time limits), please also report ILP SHD (or best-feasible SHD and optimality gaps when time-limited) to allow a fair comparison of effectiveness, not only efficiency.
- Pruning complexity. The CPDAG pruning procedure may enumerate class(P) when simple path checks are inconclusive. This can be exponential. Please provide:
  - How often enumeration is triggered in practice and with what cost.
  - Any additional sufficient conditions that reduce the need for enumeration, or bounds on the size of class(P) encountered.
- MaxSAT scaling and guarantees. The encoding seems to introduce O(n^2) variables and potentially many clauses. Please report:
  - Variable/constraint counts and solver time as a function of n and p.
  - The practical impact on total runtime (projection overhead vs search savings).
  - Robustness to inconsistent/noisy constraints. If ancestral constraints are noisy, can MaxSAT be used to select a maximal consistent subset of ordering constraints? How would pruning behave under inconsistency?
- Experimental design details. Clarify the sampling of ancestral constraints:
  - Are positive and negative constraints sampled uniformly over ordered pairs? What is the proportion of each?
  - Sensitivity to constraint noise (e.g., a small fraction of flipped constraints) would be valuable.
- Presentation and organization.
  - Section 4.1 (BN graph) could be condensed and the saved space used to better explain EC-tree mechanics and the A* setup in this context (e.g., a small worked example showing pruning and oracle guidance).
  - Minor issues: fix reference placeholders (e.g., “?\Q2015\E”), remove stray text (“th Conference…”, non-English calendar text), and ensure theorem statements/notation (α, β sets; “maximum set of ancestral constraints”) are precisely defined.

Quality
Overall technically sound with useful theoretical characterizations (Theorems 2–4) and a practical exact algorithm. Some details (frequency/cost of class(P) enumeration, MaxSAT scaling, handling inconsistent constraints) should be clarified to fully support the claims and guide practitioners.

Clarity
Generally well written and well organized. The exposition would benefit from trimming BN-graph preliminaries, expanding EC-tree/A* details, and tightening the description of the projection procedures. Fixing the formatting and reference glitches is important.

Originality
The combination of EC-tree search with projected decomposable constraints derived from ancestral constraints is novel and addresses a real limitation of exact BN learners. While ILP/CP can encode such constraints, the projection that empowers decomposable-score oracles is a new angle.

Significance
The method enables exact BN learning with meaningful causal background knowledge and substantially improves efficiency in practice. The observation that modest numbers of ancestral constraints can rival large increases in data size for SHD is impactful.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important limitation with a principled, exact method.
- Strong empirical speedups over a well-known ILP baseline.
- Useful theoretical results and practical projection machinery.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited evidence that projection transfers effectively to non-EC-tree approaches; ILP gains are mixed.
- Missing accuracy comparison for ILP and deeper analysis of the easy-hard-easy phenomenon.
- Some complexity details and presentation issues need tightening.

Suggestions to the authors
- Report ILP SHD/optimality gaps; add breakdowns of search and projection costs; analyze projection benefits for ILP or other learners (e.g., CP).
- Provide statistics on enumeration frequency and MaxSAT overhead.
- Condense Section 4.1 and expand EC-tree/A* explanation.
- Clean up references and stray text.

Overall, this is a promising and well-executed contribution. With the clarifications and additional analyses above, it would make a strong NIPS paper.
