### Review

**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data, subject to user-provided background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints (e.g., variable X must be an ancestor of Y, or X cannot be an ancestor of Y). Such constraints are important for incorporating domain knowledge but pose a significant challenge for most efficient, score-based learning algorithms, as they are non-decomposable and violate the assumptions required by dynamic programming or other methods that rely on decomposable scores.

The authors' main contribution is to adapt the recently proposed search framework of Chen et al. (2015, 2016), which performs an A* search over a space of equivalence classes (the EC tree), to handle these non-decomposable constraints. The core of their approach is a novel and powerful two-pronged strategy:
1.  **Pruning:** They develop a sound method for pruning nodes (CPDAGs) from the EC tree search space if they cannot satisfy the given ancestral constraints.
2.  **Projection:** They introduce a technique to "project" the non-decomposable ancestral constraints into a set of decomposable constraints (i.e., required/forbidden edges and variable ordering constraints). This is the key innovation, as these simpler, decomposable constraints can be passed to the A* search's heuristic oracle (an exact solver for problems with decomposable scores), making the heuristic much more accurate and dramatically speeding up the search. They provide an efficient algorithm for inferring edge constraints and a MaxSAT formulation for inferring a maximal set of consistent ordering constraints.

The paper provides a strong empirical evaluation, comparing their method against a standard Integer Linear Programming (ILP) approach, which can also handle such constraints. The results demonstrate that the proposed method is orders of magnitude faster, scaling to problems that are intractable for the ILP baseline.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical claims, particularly regarding the projection of constraints and the pruning of the search space, appear sound. The claims of performance improvement are very well-supported by a thorough experimental evaluation across several benchmark networks, varying the number of variables, dataset size, and number of constraints. The authors are careful to compare against a relevant and powerful baseline (ILP), which represents the state-of-the-art for exact learning with arbitrary constraints. The work is a complete contribution, presenting a full algorithm and demonstrating its practical effectiveness.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It provides the necessary background on score-based learning, the EC tree search framework, and ancestral constraints before detailing its own contributions. The distinction between decomposable and non-decomposable constraints is made very clear, which is central to the paper's motivation. The proposed "projection" method is explained logically, and the experimental setup and results are presented in a clear and compelling manner.

**Originality:** While the paper builds directly on the EC tree framework from Chen et al. (2015, 2016), the core contribution—the method for empowering the oracle by projecting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones—is highly novel and insightful. This idea of using the constraints to tighten the heuristic in the A* search, rather than just using them as passive checks, is a significant conceptual contribution. The use of a MaxSAT solver to find a maximal set of entailed ordering constraints is also a novel application in this context. The work is well-situated within the literature, clearly distinguishing itself from ILP/CP methods and prior constraint-based learning approaches.

**Significance:** The results presented are highly significant. The ability to incorporate ancestral knowledge is of great practical importance in many scientific domains (e.g., biology, medicine) where causal priors are available. Existing methods that can handle these constraints, like ILP, often do not scale well. By providing a method that is orders of magnitude faster, this work makes optimal learning with this form of background knowledge feasible for a wider range of real-world problems. The empirical result showing that a small number of ancestral constraints can improve model accuracy as much as a large increase in data quantity further underscores the practical importance of this work. Researchers and practitioners working on score-based structure learning are very likely to use these ideas.

**Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The projection of constraints into a decomposable form to empower the oracle is a very elegant idea. A key part of this is the MaxSAT formulation for inferring ordering constraints. Could you provide some insight into the computational overhead of this pre-processing step? How does the runtime of the MaxSAT solver scale with the number of variables and constraints, and how does it compare to the subsequent structure search time?

2.  In Section 4.3, you mention that if the initial efficient checks for constraint satisfaction on a CPDAG are inconclusive, you resort to exhaustively enumerating the structures in its equivalence class. Could this step become a bottleneck for CPDAGs representing very large equivalence classes? Did you observe this being a frequent or time-consuming issue in your experiments?

3.  The performance improvement over the GOBNILP baseline is dramatic. The paper notes that when projected constraints were also provided to the ILP solver, they sometimes led to improvements (for n=14) but also added overhead (for n=12). This suggests the constraints act as helpful redundant constraints for the ILP solver. Is there any intuition as to why the benefit isn't as consistent or pronounced as it is for the A* oracle?

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Pro:** Addresses the important and practical problem of incorporating non-decomposable prior knowledge into optimal Bayesian network learning.
*   **Pro:** The core technical contribution—projecting constraints to empower a heuristic oracle—is novel, clever, and highly effective.
*   **Pro:** The empirical results are outstanding, demonstrating orders-of-magnitude speedup over a strong ILP-based baseline, thereby pushing the state-of-the-art.
*   **Pro:** The paper is extremely well-written, clear, and easy to follow.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   **Con:** The work is an extension of a framework very recently proposed by the same group of authors. However, the extension is non-trivial and solves a fundamentally new and important aspect of the problem, with impressive results.

**Conclusion:**

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant and practical contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. The proposed method is elegant, technically sound, and empirically shown to be highly effective. It solves a long-standing problem in a much more scalable way than previous approaches. I strongly recommend acceptance.
