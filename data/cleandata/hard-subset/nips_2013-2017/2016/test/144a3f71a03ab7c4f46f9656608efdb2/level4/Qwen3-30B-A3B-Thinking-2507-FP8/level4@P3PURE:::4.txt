

This paper proposes a method to project non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints (specifically, edge and topological ordering constraints), enabling decomposable-score-based structure learning approaches to incorporate domain knowledge and improve learning efficiency. The novelty primarily resides in Section 5, where the authors introduce their projection technique. The paper is well-written and clearly presents both theoretical and experimental contributions. I have the following concerns and suggestions:  

i. Table 3 indicates that the projection of ancestral constraints improves performance only for EC trees, not for the ILP approach. Does this imply that the projection technique is limited to EC trees and lacks generalizability to other learning frameworks? Given the paper’s core innovation is inferring decomposable constraints from non-decomposable ancestral constraints, demonstrating broader applicability—specifically, showing that incorporating these decomposable constraints enhances performance and efficiency for a learning approach originally incapable of handling non-decomposable constraints—would significantly strengthen the contribution.  

ii. Table 2 reveals an "easy-hard-easy" pattern in EC tree performance as the proportion of ancestral constraints increases. What explains this behavior? Does it suggest that the impact of reducing the EC tree search space via ancestral constraints is highly sensitive to the quantity of domain knowledge relative to the ILP approach? If so, does this imply that the number of ancestral constraints must be carefully tuned to achieve optimal performance?  

iii. Table 2 reports structural Hamming distance results solely for EC trees. How do these results compare to the ILP approach? A direct comparison between the two methods on this metric is necessary to contextualize the EC tree results.  

iv. While the BN graph underpins EC trees (as EC trees extend BNs), the experiments focus exclusively on EC trees with ancestral constraints applied. Therefore, Section 4.1 (on BN theory) is redundant; I recommend removing it and devoting more attention to clarifying EC trees and the A* algorithm within the EC tree framework.
