This paper presents a method for projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints into a decomposable form, allowing decomposable-score-based structure learning algorithms to incorporate this domain knowledge and improve learning efficiency. The primary novelty of this work lies in Section 5, where the authors detail their approach to projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints into edge and topological ordering constraints, which *are* decomposable. The paper is well-written and clearly explains both the theoretical contributions and experimental results. I have several concerns and suggestions outlined below:

i. Table 3 indicates that projecting ancestral constraints improves the performance of EC trees but not the ILP approach. Does this suggest that the benefits of projecting ancestral constraints to decomposable constraints are specific to EC trees and do not generalize well to other learning methods? Given that the core innovation of this paper is inferring decomposable constraints from non-decomposable ancestral constraints, it would be more compelling if the authors could demonstrate that incorporating these decomposable constraints improves the performance *and* efficiency of a learning approach that originally couldn’t handle the non-decomposable constraints.

ii. Table 2 reveals an unusual easy-hard-easy pattern in the EC tree performance as the proportion of ancestral constraints increases. What explains this behavior? Does this indicate that the effectiveness of reducing the EC tree search space with ancestral constraints is more sensitive to the amount of incorporated domain knowledge compared to the ILP approach? Consequently, does the number of ancestral constraints require careful tuning to achieve optimal performance? 

iii. Table 2 currently reports only the structural Hamming distance for EC trees. How does the ILP approach compare in terms of this metric?

iv. A minor suggestion: While I understand that Bayesian Network (BN) graphs provide the theoretical foundation for EC trees, and EC trees represent an extension of them, the experiments focus solely on the EC tree search space with ancestral constraints applied. Therefore, I recommend removing Section 4.1 to allow for a more in-depth explanation of EC trees and the A* algorithm specifically within the EC tree setting.
