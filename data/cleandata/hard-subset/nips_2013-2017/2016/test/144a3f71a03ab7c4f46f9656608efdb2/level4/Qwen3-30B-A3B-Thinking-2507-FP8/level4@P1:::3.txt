

This paper addresses a significant gap in optimal Bayesian network structure learning by proposing a method to incorporate ancestral constraints—non-decomposable background knowledge specifying cause-effect relationships—into existing score-based frameworks. The authors leverage Chen et al.’s EC tree search space (2015, 2016), which handles non-decomposable scores, and introduce a novel translation mechanism to convert ancestral constraints into decomposable ordering constraints via MaxSAT. This enables the use of standard oracles (e.g., order-graph-based algorithms) for efficient search. Empirically, the approach outperforms ILP-based methods (e.g., GOBNILP) by orders of magnitude across benchmark networks, with the constraint translation providing dramatic efficiency gains. The work directly extends Chen et al.’s framework to handle a practically important class of constraints previously unsupported by optimal structure learning systems.

**Strengths**  
The paper excels in *quality* and *originality*. The core contribution—translating ancestral constraints into enforceable ordering constraints via MaxSAT—is technically sound, complete, and well-motivated. The theoretical foundation (Theorems 2–4) is rigorous, and the empirical evaluation is thorough, demonstrating consistent speedups across varying problem sizes (variables, data, constraint density). The approach cleanly bridges a key limitation in the field: while most optimal structure learners assume decomposable scores (limiting constraint expressiveness), this work enables the use of powerful oracles with non-decomposable constraints. This is a meaningful advancement over prior ILP/CP approaches (e.g., [Cussens, 2008]), which lack empirical validation for ancestral constraints. The *clarity* is excellent, with precise definitions, intuitive examples (e.g., Fig. 1), and a logical flow from problem statement to solution. The connection to Chen et al.’s EC tree is particularly well-executed, showing how pruning and oracle empowerment integrate seamlessly.

**Weaknesses**  
The *significance* of the contribution, while solid, warrants nuanced discussion. The problem setup assumes ancestral constraints are provided as background knowledge, which is practical in specific domains (e.g., biology with known pathways) but less aligned with broader causal discovery pipelines that handle unobserved confounders. As noted in the draft, 10–25% ancestral constraints (as used in experiments) typically encode near-complete knowledge of the target graph (e.g., for 20 variables, 50–125 constraints out of ~190 possible), which may not reflect typical real-world scenarios where constraints are sparse. A more compelling comparison would benchmark against greedy optimal search (e.g., GES with oracle input) to clarify whether the efficiency gains are *only* due to constraint pruning or also stem from the exact-search advantage. While the ILP comparison is valid, it does not address whether the method’s core value lies in constraint handling or exactness.  

Theoretical points require minor clarification. Theorem 1’s statement should explicitly note that ancestral constraints satisfied by a CPDAG *imply* satisfaction by all DAGs in its equivalence class (not vice versa), as the current phrasing risks misinterpretation. Similarly, Theorem 3’s practical utility is limited with sparse constraints (e.g., few positive edge implications), which the authors acknowledge but could emphasize more. The MaxSAT rules (e.g., Rule 3’s asymmetric conclusion) are logically sound but could benefit from a brief justification for the chosen form. The phrase "empower the oracle" is slightly misleading; the constraints are *passed to* the oracle, not used to "empower" it.  

**Overall Assessment**  
This is a high-quality, technically robust contribution that solves a well-defined problem with clear empirical impact. The method is novel (novelty=3/5), as prior work (e.g., [Borboudakis and Tsamardinos, 2012, 2013]) focused on constraint-based learning (not optimal score-based search) with path constraints. While the *significance* is moderate (impact=2/5) due to the niche constraint-setting assumption, the work is highly relevant to practitioners needing optimal structure learning with explicit causal knowledge. The results are compelling: the EC tree with constraint projection consistently outperforms ILP by orders of magnitude, even scaling to 20 variables—a notable achievement for exact methods.  

**Recommendation**  
**Accept**. The paper makes a valuable, well-executed contribution to a specific but important problem in Bayesian network learning. The technical depth, clarity, and empirical rigor align strongly with NIPS standards. The minor theoretical clarifications suggested are easily addressed in revision, and the significance is sufficient for the specialized audience interested in optimal structure learning with constraints. The work advances the state of the art demonstrably, particularly by enabling efficient use of oracles with non-decomposable constraints—a gap previously filled only by inefficient ILP.
