This paper addresses the problem of learning Bayesian networks from local scores under ancestral constraints, employing a branch-and-bound search algorithm. The search space consists of CPDAGs, and the algorithm expands the search by iteratively choosing parent sets for variables not yet present in the current CPDAG. Symmetry breaking techniques are incorporated, and a heuristic derived from the URLearning system guides the search (though details of this integration are found in a prior publication). Ancestral constraints enable pruning of the search tree, with Lemma 3 (in the supplementary material) representing the core theoretical contribution. While I believe the lemma to be correct, the provided proof lacks clarity. Specifically, the statement “By the EC tree edge generation rules, Gk also contains edge Z -> W” requires further elaboration.

The paper also considers “implied constraints” – terminology standard in the field, though referred to here as “projected constraints” – and constraints on permissible topological orderings (“ordering constraints”). The process of inferring these ordering constraints is not fully explained. The statement that “we can infer Y < Z from Z not an ancestor of Y” is imprecise. It's not a strict logical entailment, as a DAG can exist where Z is not an ancestor of Y yet Y precedes Z in a valid ordering (e.g., a graph with only Z and Y and no edges). The authors likely intend to convey that *if* Z is not an ancestor of Y, *then* the constraint Y < Z can be *added* without introducing inconsistency. They employ a MAXSAT approach to cautiously infer such consistent ordering constraints, avoiding over-constraint.

Experimental evaluation varies the number of variables, datapoints, and ancestral constraints. The method is compared to GOBNILP, demonstrably outperforming it in speed. However, the authors acknowledge that GOBNILP’s performance degrades without ancestral constraints when parent set cardinality is unbounded, as is the case in these experiments. This makes it difficult to disentangle whether GOBNILP’s poor performance is intrinsic to handling ancestral constraints or simply a consequence of its known difficulties with large parent sets. 

Furthermore, the encoding of ancestral constraints, detailed in the supplementary material, relies on a MAXSAT encoding from Cussens (2008) rather than the ILP encoding presented in Cussens (2010). This MAXSAT formulation appears suboptimal for integration with an ILP system. A more efficient encoding could be achieved by introducing binary variables A(X,Y) representing parent-child relationships, alongside the existing I(Z,U) variables, and omitting the cubic number of E(X,Y,Z) variables. Adding transitivity constraints on A(X,Y) and linking A and I variables with the constraint A(X,Y) ≥ Σ<sub>U:X∈U</sub> I(Y,U) – representing that if X is a parent of Y, then X is an ancestor of Y – would likely provide a tighter linear relaxation and improved performance. This approach, outlined in Cussens (2010), addresses total ordering but can be adapted to handle the partial ordering inherent in ancestor relationships by removing a single constraint.

I am confident that even a reasonably implemented ILP formulation of the ancestral constraints would result in superior performance compared to GOBNILP. However, the comparison to a less-optimized formulation weakens the paper’s conclusions. A comparison to the constraint programming approach of van Beek and Hoffmann, which often surpasses GOBNILP, would be valuable. Notably, the van Beek and Hoffmann paper is not cited by the authors, and adapting their method to accommodate ancestral constraints appears feasible.



References:

Cussens, J. (2010). Maximum likelihood pedigree reconstruction using integer programming. *WCB'10*.

van Beek, P., & Hoffmann, H.-F. (2015). Machine learning of Bayesian networks using constraint programming. *Proceedings of CP 2015, Cork, Ireland*.
