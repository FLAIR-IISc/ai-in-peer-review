This paper addresses the constrained optimization problem of learning Bayesian networks from local scores under ancestral constraints. The search employs a branch-and-bound algorithm, where nodes in the search tree represent CPDAGs and the algorithm progresses by selecting a parent set for a Bayesian network variable not already present in the current CPDAG. Effective symmetry breaking is incorporated, and the search is guided by a heuristic derived from the URLearning system (details of which are provided in a prior publication). Given ancestral constraints, the search space can be pruned. Lemma 3 (in the supplementary material) is a key result; while I believe it to be correct, I found the proof difficult to follow. Specifically, the statement “By the EC tree edge generation rules, Gk also contains edge Z -> W” requires further clarification. 

The paper also considers implied constraints (typically referred to as “projected constraints”) derived from the ancestral constraints, and infers constraints on permissible topological orderings (“ordering constraints”). The approach to inferring these ordering constraints is not fully explained. The statement that “we can infer Y < Z from Z not an ancestor of Y” is imprecise. While it’s true that Z not being an ancestor of Y *allows* the constraint Y < Z, it doesn't *entail* it—a graph with vertices Z and Y and no edges provides a counterexample. The authors intend to infer a maximal set of *consistent* ordering constraints, not just those that are logically entailed, and appropriately employ a MAXSAT approach to avoid over-constraint.

Experiments vary the number of variables, data points, and ancestral constraints. A comparison with the GOBNILP system demonstrates significantly faster performance for the proposed method. However, the authors acknowledge that without ancestral constraints, GOBNILP performs worse than A* approaches when there is no limit on parent set cardinality. The current experiments lack such a limit, making it difficult to determine whether GOBNILP’s poor performance is due to its inherent difficulty with large parent sets or specifically to handling ancestral constraints.

Furthermore, the encoding of ancestral constraints (detailed in the supplementary material) utilizes a MAXSAT formulation from [Cussens, 2008], rather than the ILP encoding found in Section 3.1.1 of [Cussens, 2010]. This MAXSAT encoding is a suboptimal choice for an ILP system. A more efficient formulation would involve incorporating binary variables A(X,Y) representing parent-child relationships, in addition to the existing I(Z,U) variables, and eliminating the cubic number of E(X,Y,Z) variables. This formulation would then require transitivity constraints on the A(X,Y) variables and linking constraints between A and I variables: A(X,Y) ≥ Σ{U:X ∈ U} I(Y,U). In essence, if X is a parent of Y, then X must be an ancestor of Y. This revised formulation provides a tighter linear relaxation and should lead to improved performance. This is the approach taken in Section 3.1.1 of [Cussens, 2010] for total orderings, adapted here by removing a constraint since ancestor relations define a partial, not total, order.  I am confident that even a sensible ILP formulation of ancestral constraints would result in the proposed system outperforming GOBNILP, but the comparison to a suboptimal encoding weakens the paper. 

It would be beneficial to compare the current method to that of van Beek and Hoffmann, which often outperforms GOBNILP. Adapting their method to incorporate ancestral constraints should be feasible, yet their work is not cited by the authors.

Peter van Beek and Hella-Franziska Hoffmann. “Machine learning of Bayesian networks using constraint programming.” *Proceedings of CP 2015, Cork, Ireland, August 2015*.
