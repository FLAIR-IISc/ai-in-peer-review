

This paper addresses the problem of learning Bayesian network structures under ancestral constraints, which specify cause-and-effect relationships between variables. The authors propose an efficient approach that extends Chen et al.'s EC tree framework for structure learning with non-decomposable scores. The key insight is that while ancestral constraints are non-decomposable, they can be used to infer decomposable edge and ordering constraints that empower an oracle (URLearning) for efficient structure search. The paper demonstrates significant empirical advantages over ILP-based approaches, showing orders-of-magnitude speed improvements.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it provides a sound and complete method for pruning the EC tree using ancestral constraints, which is crucial for handling non-decomposable background knowledge. Second, it develops a systematic approach to infer decomposable edge constraints from ancestral constraints, which can be used to tighten the heuristic function guiding the search. Third, it introduces a MaxSAT-based method for inferring ordering constraints that maximizes the number of consistent constraints without creating cycles. These contributions are well-motivated by the limitations of existing approaches, which typically cannot handle non-decomposable constraints like ancestral relations.

The paper is technically sound, with clear theoretical foundations and thorough empirical validation. The authors demonstrate that their approach can handle larger networks (up to 20 variables) than typical ILP-based methods, and they provide convincing evidence that ancestral constraints significantly improve learning efficiency. The experimental design is robust, with careful control of variables (number of variables, dataset size, and constraint density) and multiple benchmark networks. The results consistently show their method outperforms GOBNILP, with the efficiency advantage growing with problem size. The paper also provides valuable insights into how background knowledge (in the form of ancestral constraints) can compensate for limited training data.

The main strengths of the paper include its novel approach to handling non-decomposable constraints, the thorough theoretical analysis of constraint inference, and the compelling empirical evidence supporting the method's efficiency. The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with sufficient technical detail to enable replication. The connection to previous work is well-established, particularly with Chen et al.'s EC tree framework and the limitations of decomposable-score approaches.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that need addressing. First, the explanation of how ancestral constraints imply edge constraints (Theorems 2 and 3) is somewhat opaque. The phrase "By the EC tree edge generation rules, Gk also contains edge Z → W" requires additional clarification to demonstrate how the inference works. The authors should provide a concrete example illustrating how the constraints are derived from the ancestral relations.

Second, the comparison with GOBNILP is weakened by the use of a suboptimal ILP formulation for ancestral constraints. The paper encodes ancestral constraints using a MAXSAT-based approach from Cussens (2008), rather than the more efficient ILP formulation described in Cussens (2010). Specifically, the authors could have used a simpler ILP formulation with A(X,Y) binary variables (indicating X is an ancestor of Y) along with the standard I(X,U) variables for parent sets, plus transitivity constraints and a linking constraint: A(X,Y) ≥ Σ_{U:X∈U} I(Y,U). This would provide a tighter linear relaxation and better performance, making the comparison fairer. The current ILP formulation appears unnecessarily complex with cubic constraints, which likely contributes to GOBNILP's poor performance rather than demonstrating the inherent limitations of ILP approaches for ancestral constraints.

Third, the paper overlooks relevant work on constraint programming approaches for Bayesian network structure learning, particularly van Beek and Hoffmann (2015). This work often outperforms GOBNILP and could be adapted to handle ancestral constraints. The authors should compare their method with this approach to better establish their method's relative advantages.

The paper would benefit from a more detailed explanation of the constraint inference process, especially for the ordering constraints. The claim that "we can infer Y < Z from Z not an ancestor of Y" is technically correct but requires clarification that this is a choice to add a consistent constraint, not a logical entailment. The authors are careful not to infer inconsistent constraints, but the explanation of this distinction could be clearer.

In terms of significance, the paper makes an important contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning by enabling the use of rich background knowledge (in the form of ancestral constraints) that was previously inaccessible to optimal structure learning methods. The efficiency gains demonstrated could have practical impact in domains where causal knowledge is available but data is limited.

Overall, this paper presents a valuable method for handling ancestral constraints in Bayesian network structure learning. While the technical contributions are sound and well-validated, the comparison with existing methods could be strengthened by using a more appropriate ILP formulation and considering constraint programming approaches. The paper is well-written and makes a significant contribution to the field, though it would be improved with the clarifications and comparisons suggested above. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
