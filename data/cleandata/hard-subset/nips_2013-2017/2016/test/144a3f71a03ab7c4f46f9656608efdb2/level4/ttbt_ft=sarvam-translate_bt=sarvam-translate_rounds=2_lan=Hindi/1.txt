This paper deals with the problem of constraint optimization of Bayesian network learning with local constraints. The search branch-and-bound is where the search tree has CPDAGs as nodes, and we 'go down' from CPDAG1 to CPDAG2 (approximately) by choosing a parent set for a BN variable that is not a node in CPDAG1. Sensible symmetry is broken. Search A is where a URLर्निंग system is used to provide a heuristic. (How this happens is not detailed here, but it is available in the previous paper.) Given the ensemble constraints, some pruning of the search tree is possible. Lemma 3 (complementary material) is the main result here. I believe this is true, but I don't understand the proof. The phrase "by the EC tree generation rules, Z -> W is also an edge in Gk" needs further explanation. There are also implicit constraints ("implicit constraints" is standard terminology, here they are called "projected constraints"). A set of ensemble constraints will indicate the constraints on the edges that can be used for pruning. Constraints on acceptable topological ordering are also implicit ("ordering constraints"). The approach to doing this is not explained properly. We have the statement that we can " अनुमानित Y < Z from Z, not from Y's ancestor". The issue is the word "can". Clearly there are DAGs in which Z is not an ancestor of Y, but Y < Z is a consistent ordering: for example, a graph with vertices Z and Y and no edges. The authors, of course, know this! They mean that if "Z is not an ancestor of Y", then we can choose to add the constraint Y < Z. So the idea is to ' अनुमानित' as many consistent ordering constraints as possible (not just the ones that are implicit). The authors are careful not to ' अनुमानित' too much and to use the MaxSAT approach for estimating. Experiments are used where the number of variables, data points, and ensemble constraints are different. There is a comparison with the GOBNILP system where the current system is shown to perform much faster than GOBNILP. As the authors noted, without ensemble constraints, GOBNILP performs worse than the MaxSAT approach when there is no limit on the parent set cardinality. There is no such limit in the current experiments, so it is difficult to tell whether GOBNILP's poor performance here is due to its 'general' failure to deal with multiple parent sets or specifically due to the poor handling of ensemble constraints. Also, the encoding of ensemble constraints (given in complementary material) is based on MaxSAT encoding [Kacens, 2008], not on the ILP encoding found in section 3.1.1 of [Kacens, 2010]. And this MaxSAT encoding is a poor construction for an ILP system. Why not have A(X,Y) binary variables in addition to the I(Z,U) variable and leave out the (density number!) E(X,Y,Z) variable? I think then only the transit constraints on the A(X,Y) variable and the following constraints connecting the A and I variables are needed: A(X,Y) \geq \sum{U:X \in U} I(Y,U). In other words, if X is an ancestor of Y, then X is an ancestor of Y. This construction provides a much stricter linear छूट and should provide better performance. This is the approach to total ordering on variables from section 3.1.1 of [Kacens, 2010], but one constraint has been omitted because the ancestor is a partial, not a complete, ordering. I am sure that even with a sensible ILP construction of ensemble constraints, the current system will perform better than GOBNILP, but the paper is weakened rather than made sensible compared to a poor construction. It would be interesting to compare the current method to van Beek and Hoffman, especially since it often performs better than GOBNILP.
