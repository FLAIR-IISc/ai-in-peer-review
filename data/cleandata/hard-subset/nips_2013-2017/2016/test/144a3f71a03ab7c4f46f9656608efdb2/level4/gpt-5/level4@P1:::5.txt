Summary
The paper addresses optimal Bayesian network (BN) structure learning in the presence of non-decomposable background knowledge, specifically ancestral constraints (X is/is not an ancestor of Y). The authors build on the BN-graph and EC-tree frameworks of Chen et al. [2015, 2016], which support non-decomposable priors by searching directly over DAGs or equivalence classes while using an oracle that optimizes decomposable scores. The key idea is twofold: (1) prune the EC-tree using tests that determine whether a CPDAG can be extended to a DAG satisfying the ancestral constraints; and (2) “project” non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints (edge presence/absence and topological ordering constraints) that can be passed to a decomposable-score oracle to tighten its heuristic. For edges, the paper provides efficient inference rules with soundness theorems (Theorems 2–3). For orderings, it formulates a MaxSAT problem with hard clauses (transitivity, necessary conditions, acyclicity constraints) and soft clauses that prefer constraints implied by ancestral negatives, with correctness summarized by Theorem 4. Empirically, the approach is compared to an ILP-based method (GOBNILP), showing orders-of-magnitude speedups across multiple benchmarks and settings, with further gains when projected constraints are supplied to the oracle.

Relation to prior work
The work extends Chen et al. [2015, 2016] by enabling non-decomposable constraints (ancestral relations) within their exact search frameworks (BN graph, EC tree). It contrasts with ILP/CP approaches that can encode non-decomposable constraints directly [Jaakkola et al., 2010; Cussens, 2008; Bartlett and Cussens, 2015; van Beek and Hoffmann, 2015]. It complements constraint-based methods that use path constraints [Borboudakis and Tsamardinos, 2012, 2013], but here in the exact score-based setting. The use of projected decomposable constraints to empower order-graph oracles (e.g., URLEARNING) is, to my knowledge, new.

Strengths
- Technical contribution: A clear, principled mechanism to incorporate ancestral constraints into exact score-based structure learning via EC-tree pruning and constraint projection. Theorems for edge and ordering projections provide soundness conditions.
- Practical impact: Substantial empirical speedups over ILP (often orders of magnitude), especially as n grows; further improvements when projected constraints are used.
- Generality: The framework leverages any decomposable-score oracle and is compatible with standard scores (BDeu) and parent-set preprocessing. The MaxSAT formulation for ordering constraints is modular.
- Clarity of the main idea: The separation between non-decomposable constraint handling (EC-tree pruning) and decomposable oracle empowerment (projection) is well explained, with useful intuition.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Scalability and worst-case behavior: Pruning a CPDAG may require enumerating its class(P) when simple path tests are inconclusive. The paper should quantify how often enumeration is triggered in practice and provide complexity bounds or heuristics to avoid blow-ups.
- Experimental scope: Experiments are limited to n ≤ 20 and BDeu; consider reporting on larger n or at least stronger evidence of scalability trends. Include other decomposable scores (e.g., MDL) and constraints on parent-set size to assess generality and fairness vs ILP (ILP typically benefits from parent-size bounds).
- Comparisons: Only GOBNILP is evaluated; a CP baseline (e.g., van Beek and Hoffmann, 2015) would strengthen the case. Also report results without precomputed parent scores to show end-to-end performance.
- Constraint realism and robustness: Ancestral constraints are sampled from ground-truth networks and assumed consistent. Evaluate robustness to noisy or inconsistent background knowledge (how does the projection/MaxSAT machinery behave, and how are conflicts resolved?).
- MaxSAT details: Provide more detail on the encoding size, solver settings, and runtime share. Are results sensitive to the choice of solver? Is the MaxSAT step guaranteed to return a set O that preserves all DAGs satisfying A (theorem statements suggest sufficiency conditions; clarify completeness/optimality of the selected O)?
- Clarity/presentation: There are several typos/artifacts (e.g., “th Conference…”, stray date strings, footnote formatting, inconsistent notation such as “maximum” vs. “maximal” set of constraints). The “sufficient condition for acyclicity” clause in the MaxSAT encoding would benefit from intuition and a proof sketch. Pseudocode for edge projection would help reproducibility.

Quality
The approach appears technically sound, with clear definitions and theorems for pruning and projection. Using an admissible oracle-induced heuristic preserves optimality in A* over the EC tree. However, the reliance on enumerating equivalence classes in some pruning cases and the lack of complexity analysis leave some uncertainty about worst-case behavior. Experimental methodology is careful in giving projected constraints to both methods.

Clarity
Overall well organized, with a logical flow from preliminaries to constraints, pruning, projection, and experiments. The paper would benefit from polishing, consistent notation, and more explicit algorithmic descriptions (particularly the edge-projection algorithm and the CPDAG pruning procedure).

Originality
High: integrating ancestral constraints into an exact score-based framework via EC-tree pruning and decomposable projection (especially the MaxSAT-based ordering inference) is novel relative to prior exact methods.

Significance
High: Many applications require incorporating causal/ancestral knowledge; providing an exact and efficient method with large empirical speedups is valuable. The projection idea should be useful beyond ancestral constraints.

Reasons to accept
- Novel and principled treatment of non-decomposable ancestral constraints in exact BN learning.
- Strong empirical speedups over a state-of-the-art ILP baseline.
- Clean theoretical underpinning for constraint projection that empowers existing oracles.

Reasons to reject
- Limited experimental scope (n, score types, baselines) and lack of robustness tests to noisy/inconsistent constraints.
- Some algorithmic details (CPDAG pruning complexity, MaxSAT encoding/optimality) are under-specified.
- Presentation issues and minor errors detract from clarity.

Recommendation
Lean accept. The technical idea is solid and practically impactful; addressing the clarity and experimental breadth concerns would make this a strong contribution.
