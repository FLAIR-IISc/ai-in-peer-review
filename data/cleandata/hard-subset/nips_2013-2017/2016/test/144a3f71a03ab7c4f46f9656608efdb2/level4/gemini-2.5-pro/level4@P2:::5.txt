This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data, specifically when the learning process is constrained by background knowledge in the form of ancestral relationships. Mainstream optimal structure learning algorithms often rely on decomposable scores, which makes it difficult to incorporate non-decomposable constraints such as "X must be an ancestor of Y". While methods like Integer Linear Programming (ILP) can handle such constraints, they often face scalability challenges.

The core contribution of this work is to integrate ancestral constraints into the efficient A*-based search framework of Chen et al. [2015, 2016], which operates on a search space of equivalence classes (the EC-tree). This framework uses an oracle—an optimal learner for decomposable scores—to compute the A* heuristic. The key innovation is a method to "empower" this oracle, which cannot handle ancestral constraints directly. The authors achieve this by projecting the non-decomposable ancestral constraints onto a set of decomposable constraints (i.e., required/forbidden edges and variable ordering constraints) that the oracle *can* handle. This projection tightens the A* heuristic, leading to significant search space pruning. For inferring edge constraints, a simple and efficient algorithm is proposed. For the more complex task of inferring a consistent set of ordering constraints, the problem is cleverly reduced to a MaxSAT instance.

The authors provide a strong empirical evaluation, comparing their approach against the state-of-the-art ILP solver GOBNILP. The results demonstrate that the proposed method is orders of magnitude faster, particularly as the number of variables increases.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The paper tackles a highly relevant problem: incorporating rich, non-local background knowledge into score-based structure learning. The proposed technique of inferring and projecting decomposable constraints from non-decomposable ones to empower an existing, efficient solver is a novel and powerful idea. It provides a practical bridge between the scalability of decomposable-score methods and the expressiveness of non-decomposable constraints.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The approach is technically sound. The theorems formalize the logic behind constraint projection, and the reduction of ordering constraint inference to MaxSAT is elegant. The use of the EC-tree search space is well-motivated as it builds upon recent advances in exact structure learning.

3.  **Impressive Empirical Results:** The experiments are thorough and the results are compelling. The orders-of-magnitude speedup over a well-established ILP-based system is a significant achievement and clearly demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method. The experiments are well-designed, varying the number of variables, dataset size, and density of constraints. The paper also highlights the practical value of background knowledge, showing that a small percentage of ancestral constraints can improve model accuracy as much as a large increase in training data.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. It provides sufficient background on structure learning, the EC-tree framework, and ancestral constraints before detailing its own contributions. The motivation is clear and the experimental setup is well-described.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against acceptance):**

1.  **Scope of Constraint Projection:** The paper focuses exclusively on ancestral constraints. While this is a very useful class of constraints, a brief discussion on the potential generality of the "projection" approach to other types of non-decomposable constraints would strengthen the paper's contribution and outline future research directions.

2.  **Complexity of Constraint Inference:** The inference of ordering constraints relies on solving a MaxSAT instance, which is an NP-hard problem. While this is likely not the bottleneck for the problem sizes tested, the paper does not analyze the runtime or scalability of this pre-processing step. A short discussion on its computational cost relative to the main search would be beneficial.

**Evaluation Summary:**

*   **Quality:** High. The paper presents a complete and technically sound piece of work. The claims are very well-supported by extensive and convincing experimental results.
*   **Clarity:** High. The manuscript is well-organized and clearly written, making the complex ideas accessible.
*   **Originality:** High. The central idea of empowering a decomposable-score oracle by projecting non-decomposable constraints is novel and distinct from previous work in both the exact learning and ILP communities.
*   **Significance:** High. The work provides a major performance improvement for a practical and important problem. Researchers and practitioners who need to learn Bayesian networks from data with causal or temporal background knowledge are very likely to use or build upon these ideas. The method demonstrably advances the state of the art in exact structure learning under constraints.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that presents a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. The strengths, particularly the novelty of the approach and the striking empirical results, far outweigh the minor weaknesses. I strongly recommend acceptance.
