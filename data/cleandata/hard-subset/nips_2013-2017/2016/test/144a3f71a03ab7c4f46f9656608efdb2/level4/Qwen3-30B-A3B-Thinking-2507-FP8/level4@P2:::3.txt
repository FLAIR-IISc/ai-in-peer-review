

This paper addresses the important problem of learning Bayesian network structures with ancestral constraints, which are non-decomposable and thus incompatible with most mainstream optimal structure learning approaches that rely on decomposable scores. The authors propose a novel method that translates ancestral constraints into a set of decomposable ordering constraints, which can be efficiently handled by existing oracles for decomposable-score-based learning. Their approach leverages the EC tree search space introduced by Chen et al. (2015, 2016), incorporating ancestral constraints through pruning and empowering the oracle with inferred decomposable constraints.

The work builds upon Chen et al.'s framework for optimal structure learning with non-decomposable scores, extending it to handle ancestral constraints that are common in real-world applications. The authors demonstrate that their method outperforms ILP-based approaches like GOBNILP by orders of magnitude in empirical evaluations across multiple benchmark networks. This represents a significant practical contribution, as ancestral constraints are often available in domain knowledge but have been difficult to incorporate into optimal structure learning.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with a clear methodological contribution. The authors provide a formal framework for translating ancestral constraints into decomposable ordering constraints using MaxSAT, and rigorously prove the soundness of their pruning approach (Theorem 1). The experimental evaluation is thorough, comparing against a modified ILP approach and demonstrating significant efficiency gains. The authors properly account for the limitations of their approach, noting that the oracle's effectiveness depends on the quality of the projected constraints.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with appropriate technical detail. The problem statement and approach are well-motivated, and the EC tree representation is effectively explained. The authors make good use of examples to illustrate their key concepts. The main weakness is that some technical details (particularly regarding the MaxSAT constraints) could be clarified for readers unfamiliar with constraint satisfaction techniques.

**Originality:** The paper addresses an important gap in the literature, as optimal Bayesian network structure learning with ancestral constraints has not been adequately addressed in previous work. While the core idea of translating constraints is conceptually straightforward, the specific implementation using EC trees and MaxSAT is novel and well-executed. The authors demonstrate that this approach is significantly different from previous NIPS work on structure learning (e.g., Chen et al. 2015, 2016; Yuan and Malone 2013).

**Significance:** The problem tackled is of practical importance, as ancestral constraints represent valuable background knowledge in many real-world applications. The authors convincingly demonstrate that their method provides substantial efficiency improvements over existing approaches, making optimal structure learning with ancestral constraints feasible for larger networks. The empirical results show that even a small percentage (10-25%) of ancestral constraints can significantly improve learning accuracy, comparable to substantially larger datasets.

**Strengths:**
- Novel solution to a meaningful problem with practical implications
- Strong theoretical foundation with formal proofs
- Comprehensive empirical evaluation demonstrating significant efficiency gains
- Clear connection to previous NIPS work on structure learning
- Well-executed implementation that addresses a real limitation of current methods

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could better address why causal discovery algorithms with unobserved confounders aren't sufficient for this specific problem (the draft review's criticism about "artificial setting" seems misplaced—ancestral constraints are a specific type of background knowledge that may not require full causal discovery)
- Some technical details (particularly in Section 5.2 regarding MaxSAT constraints) could be clarified
- The claim that "10%-25% ancestral constraints would already imply extensive knowledge of the target graph" is questionable; in practice, these constraints may represent specific domain knowledge rather than comprehensive graph information
- The comparison against GES (a greedy algorithm) would provide additional context for the efficiency gains, though the authors' comparison against an ILP-based exact method is valid

**Recommendation:** The paper makes a solid contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning, with clear technical merit and practical significance. While the approach is conceptually straightforward, the implementation is sound, and the empirical results are convincing. The work is well-suited for NIPS, as it addresses an important practical problem with a technically sound solution that advances the state of the art in structure learning. I recommend acceptance.

**Pro:** Significant efficiency gains over existing exact methods, well-executed empirical evaluation, addresses an important gap in the literature, technically sound approach.

**Con:** Conceptually straightforward approach, some technical details could be clarified, the practical impact of the constraint percentage claim needs justification.
