This paper presents a method for projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints into a decomposable form, enabling their incorporation into score-based structure learning algorithms. The core novelty lies in Section 5, where the authors detail their approach to projecting these constraints into equivalent edge and topological ordering constraints. The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains both the theoretical foundations and experimental results. However, I have several concerns and suggestions that could strengthen the work.

First, the results in Table 3 indicate that the performance improvement from projecting ancestral constraints is limited to the EC tree learner, but not observed with the ILP approach. This raises questions about the generalizability of the proposed projection method. Given that the central contribution is the translation of non-decomposable constraints into a decomposable form, it would be more compelling to demonstrate that incorporating these newly derived constraints *improves* the performance of a learning algorithm that is *unable* to directly handle the original, non-decomposable constraints. Showing benefit beyond algorithms already suited to decomposable constraints would more strongly highlight the value of this work.

Second, Table 2 reveals an unexpected “easy-hard-easy” pattern in EC tree performance as the proportion of ancestral constraints increases. This suggests that the effectiveness of using ancestral constraints to reduce the EC tree search space is highly sensitive to the amount of prior knowledge provided, potentially more so than with the ILP approach. It would be beneficial to investigate this phenomenon further and discuss whether careful tuning of the number of ancestral constraints is crucial for achieving optimal performance. Exploring the reasons behind this sensitivity would significantly add to the paper’s insights.

Third, the evaluation currently focuses solely on the structural Hamming distance for EC trees (Table 2). It would be valuable to include a comparison of performance, using the same metric, between the EC tree and ILP approaches. This would provide a more complete picture of the effectiveness of the proposed method across different learning paradigms.

Finally, while I appreciate the connection to Bayesian Networks (BNs) outlined in Section 4.1, the experiments exclusively focus on the EC tree search space with the application of ancestral constraints. Given this experimental scope, Section 4.1 feels somewhat tangential and could be removed. Instead, I suggest dedicating more space to a more detailed explanation of EC trees themselves, and a thorough discussion of the A* algorithm as implemented within the EC tree setting. A clearer presentation of these foundational elements would enhance the paper’s accessibility and improve understanding of the experimental framework.
