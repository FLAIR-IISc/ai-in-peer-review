This paper proposes projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and topological ordering constraints so that decomposable-score-based structure learning methods can exploit domain knowledge and improve efficiency. The key technical novelty is in Section 5, which details the projection from ancestral constraints to decomposable constraints. The paper is clearly written and the theory–experiment connection is generally well presented.

Strengths:
- Clear motivation: enabling methods limited to decomposable constraints to leverage richer domain knowledge.
- Technical contribution: a principled projection of ancestral constraints to edge/topological constraints.
- Presentation: the main ideas are easy to follow, and the empirical section is organized.

Concerns and suggestions:

1) Generalization beyond EC trees (Table 3)
- Table 3 suggests the projected constraints noticeably help EC trees but not the ILP approach. This raises a key question: is the benefit specific to EC trees, or is the integration into ILP suboptimal?
- Please analyze why ILP does not benefit: e.g., do the projected constraints redundantly overlap with ILP’s existing pruning, are they too weak after projection, or does the ILP formulation already encode equivalents?
- To support the main claim—that projecting ancestral constraints enables learners that cannot directly handle non-decomposable constraints—please demonstrate a case where a method originally unable to use ancestral constraints (e.g., ILP with standard family-variable formulation, dynamic programming, or greedy search) gains measurable accuracy and/or runtime improvements when given the projected constraints. An ablation comparing: (a) no constraints, (b) original ancestral constraints (if the method can’t handle them, show infeasibility or no effect), and (c) projected constraints, would be convincing.
- If the effect depends on the strength or number of projected constraints, report how many constraints survive projection and their tightness (e.g., implied edge directions or partial orders) for both EC trees and ILP.

2) Easy–hard–easy pattern for EC trees (Table 2)
- The reported easy–hard–easy pattern as the proportion of ancestral constraints increases is intriguing. Please provide diagnostics to explain the phenomenon, such as:
  - Number of A* node expansions, effective branching factor, and closed-list size versus constraint density.
  - The fraction of constraints that are (i) correct w.r.t. the ground truth, (ii) redundant, and (iii) conflicting; and how each category affects heuristic tightness and pruning.
  - How many projected constraints reduce the admissible heuristic gap or eliminate inconsistent partial orders.
- Does this pattern indicate that EC trees are more sensitive to the quantity/quality of constraints than ILP? If so, discuss whether there is a “sweet spot” in constraint density and whether practitioners need to tune the number of ancestral constraints. A sensitivity curve and variance across datasets would help.

3) Missing comparison of SHD with ILP (Table 2)
- Table 2 reports structural Hamming distance (SHD) only for EC trees. Please include comparable SHD (and, ideally, precision/recall of edges and v-structures) for ILP under the same conditions and datasets to assess accuracy, not just runtime or feasibility.
- If ILP attains similar SHD with or without projected constraints, discuss whether the primary benefit of projection lies in efficiency rather than accuracy.

4) Clarity and focus of the exposition
- Section 4.1 on the BN graph background seems less critical given the empirical focus on EC trees under ancestral constraints. Consider moving 4.1 to an appendix and using the space to:
  - More thoroughly explain EC trees in this specific setting: state space, node representation, and how projected constraints prune expansions or tighten bounds.
  - Clearly define the A* evaluation function, admissibility/consistency of the heuristic, and how projected constraints modify g/h costs and feasibility checks.
  - Provide a small running example showing how a sample ancestral constraint is projected and then enforced during A* search on the EC tree.

Additional suggestions:
- Report both effectiveness and efficiency: wall-clock time, memory usage, number of explored states, and optimality certificates (when applicable) for EC trees and ILP, with and without projected constraints.
- Clarify whether projection ever weakens information (e.g., many-to-one mapping from ancestral to edge/order constraints). Quantify information loss, and discuss when the projection is exact versus conservative.
- If possible, add a robustness check: vary noise levels or mis-specified ancestral constraints and show how performance degrades, since real domain knowledge is often imperfect.
- Improve table/figure captions to specify datasets, scoring function, priors, and constraint counts before and after projection, so readers can interpret the numbers without cross-referencing the text.

Overall assessment:
The projection of ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints is a useful and timely idea with clear practical relevance. The current draft is well written and the theoretical construction is sound. The main weakness is empirical: the benefits appear concentrated on EC trees, with limited gains for ILP, and the easy–hard–easy pattern lacks analysis. Strengthening the cross-method evaluation, adding diagnostic metrics to explain the search dynamics, and sharpening the exposition on EC trees and A* would significantly improve the paper’s impact and credibility.
