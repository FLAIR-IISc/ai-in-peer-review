This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures, specifically focusing on incorporating non-decomposable constraints, such as ancestral constraints. The authors build upon the structure learning framework of Chen et al. [2015, 2016], which handles non-decomposable priors and scores, and demonstrate how to leverage decomposable constraints inferred from ancestral constraints to empower an underlying oracle. Through empirical evaluation against integer linear programming (ILP)-based approaches, they demonstrate significant performance improvements—orders of magnitude faster runtimes. The problem is further addressed by formulating it as a MaxSAT problem with tailored constraints. While my expertise in this specific area is limited, the reported results (runtime comparisons) are compelling. The observed runtime reductions, relative to alternative methods, are substantial.

The paper begins with a clear introduction to the problem of Bayesian network structure learning and motivates the need for handling ancestral constraints, which are often crucial in real-world applications. The authors effectively highlight the limitations of traditional decomposable score-based methods in integrating such constraints. The connection to previous work, including references to Darwiche [2009], Koller and Friedman [2009], and Murphy [2012], provides necessary context. The recent work of Chen et al. [2015, 2016] is appropriately positioned as a foundation for this research.

The technical preliminaries section is well-organized and provides a concise overview of relevant concepts, such as decomposable scores, the K2 algorithm, and integer linear programming. The explanation of how ILP can be used to formulate the problem is particularly helpful. The discussion of ancestral constraints and their non-decomposability is clear and provides a strong justification for the proposed approach. 

The core contribution of the paper – incorporating non-decomposable constraints into the structure learning process – is well-articulated in Section 4. The explanation of the BN graph and EC tree search spaces is easy to follow, and the method for pruning the EC tree based on ancestral constraints is logically sound. Theorems 1, 3, and 4 are crucial, offering a theoretical basis for the proposed methodology. The connection to the oracle approach and the benefit of empowering it with inferred decomposable constraints are convincingly presented.

The projection of constraints section, detailing how to infer edge and ordering constraints, is a key component of the work. The transformation to a MaxSAT problem is a reasonable approach for finding a maximally large set of ordering constraints, and the discussion of the theorems supporting this approach adds to the rigor of the paper. 

The experimental evaluation (Section 6) is comprehensive and well-presented. The use of standard Bayesian network benchmarks, the varying of key parameters (n, N, p), and the comparison against a state-of-the-art ILP-based system provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The results clearly demonstrate the significant performance gains achieved by the authors. The discussion of the impact of ancestral constraints on learning efficiency and model accuracy is insightful.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clear problem statement and motivation:** The paper effectively articulates the importance of handling ancestral constraints in Bayesian network structure learning.
*   **Novel approach:** The proposed method for incorporating non-decomposable constraints into the EC tree search space is novel and well-justified.
*   **Strong empirical results:** The experimental evaluation demonstrates significant performance improvements compared to existing approaches.
*   **Theoretical grounding:** The theorems presented provide a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed methodology.
*   **Well-written and organized:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, with a logical flow of ideas.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited discussion of scalability:** While the experiments show promising results up to n = 20, a more detailed discussion of the scalability limitations of the approach would be beneficial.
*   **MaxSAT solver dependency:** The performance of the MaxSAT solver could be a potential bottleneck for larger problems. It would be helpful to discuss the characteristics of the MaxSAT instance and the potential for optimization.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important problem in Bayesian network structure learning.
*   The proposed approach is novel and technically sound.
*   The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate significant performance gains.
*   The paper is well-written and organized.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The scalability of the approach to very large networks is not fully explored.
*   The dependence on a MaxSAT solver could be a limitation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. The proposed approach is novel, technically sound, and empirically validated. Despite some minor limitations, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
