

This paper presents a novel approach for learning Bayesian network structures under ancestral constraints, which are non-decomposable and pose challenges for traditional structure learning methods that rely on decomposable scores. The authors build upon Chen et al.'s framework for optimal structure learning with non-decomposable scores, introducing a method to project ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and topological ordering constraints that can be efficiently handled by existing oracles. This enables significant efficiency improvements in structure learning while maintaining optimality. The work extends the EC tree search space (introduced in Chen et al., 2016) to incorporate ancestral constraints through sound pruning techniques and constraint projection.

The paper makes a clear contribution to the field by addressing an important practical problem: integrating background knowledge in the form of cause-and-effect relationships into Bayesian network structure learning. Previous work on ancestral constraints has largely focused on constraint-based methods (e.g., Borboudakis and Tsamardinos, 2012, 2013) or used ILP/CP approaches without empirical evaluation of their performance with ancestral constraints (as noted by the authors). The authors' method represents a significant advancement by enabling optimal structure learning with ancestral constraints while leveraging existing decomposable-score-based algorithms.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with a sound theoretical foundation. The constraint projection algorithms (Section 5) are well-motivated, and the authors provide formal proofs for their correctness. The empirical evaluation in Section 6 is comprehensive, showing orders-of-magnitude improvements over ILP-based approaches across multiple benchmark networks and constraint settings. The results are presented clearly with appropriate statistical reporting (averaged over 50 datasets) and meaningful metrics (structural Hamming distance).

The paper is well-written with clear organization, though I have a few suggestions to enhance clarity. The connection between the BN graph (Section 4.1) and EC trees (Section 4.2) could be more explicitly stated, as the EC tree is an extension of the BN graph. The theoretical development in Section 5 is particularly strong, with precise definitions and helpful examples illustrating the constraint projection process.

Regarding the main concerns:

First, the paper demonstrates that constraint projection significantly improves EC tree performance but has limited impact on ILP (Table 2). This is an important observation that merits more discussion. The authors should clarify whether this limitation is inherent to the ILP approach or stems from how constraints are encoded. It would strengthen the paper to provide a more detailed analysis of why projection benefits EC trees more than ILP, perhaps by examining how the constraints interact with each algorithm's search space.

Second, the easy-hard-easy pattern observed in EC tree performance (Table 3) as constraint proportion increases is intriguing but not fully explained. The authors should discuss why this pattern occurs—whether it's due to the search space dynamics or the nature of the constraints themselves. Understanding this would help practitioners determine optimal constraint levels for their specific problems.

Third, while Table 3 reports structural Hamming distances for EC trees, it does not compare these to ILP results. Given the primary claim of efficiency improvements, it would be valuable to see how the quality of learned networks compares between the two approaches. The authors should include this comparison to provide a more complete picture of the trade-offs between efficiency and accuracy.

Fourth, I suggest omitting Section 4.1 (BN graphs) since the experiments focus exclusively on EC trees. The BN graph serves as a theoretical foundation for EC trees, but the paper could better serve readers by spending more time explaining the EC tree structure and A* search in the context of ancestral constraints. This would make the paper more accessible to readers unfamiliar with Chen et al.'s prior work.

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. The authors successfully demonstrate that non-decomposable ancestral constraints can be effectively incorporated into optimal structure learning through constraint projection, yielding substantial efficiency gains. This work is particularly significant given the widespread use of Bayesian networks in practical applications where domain knowledge about causal relationships is often available.

The paper's strengths are its technical rigor, clear presentation, and compelling empirical results. The main weaknesses are the limited discussion of why constraint projection benefits EC trees more than ILP, the unexplained easy-hard-easy pattern, and the absence of comparative quality metrics between EC trees and ILP. Addressing these would strengthen the paper significantly.

In terms of the four review criteria:
- Quality: Strong theoretical foundation and comprehensive empirical evaluation
- Clarity: Well-written with clear organization, though some sections could be tightened
- Originality: Novel constraint projection technique that enables efficient incorporation of ancestral constraints
- Significance: Practical approach that makes optimal Bayesian network learning with domain knowledge feasible for larger networks

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field that advances the state of the art in Bayesian network structure learning. The authors have demonstrated a clear understanding of the problem space, provided a sound solution, and shown compelling empirical results. With minor revisions to address the concerns above, this paper would be an excellent fit for NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
