- Presents an efficient method to incorporate ancestral constraints into optimal Bayesian network discovery by translating them into (maximal) ordering constraints used in an EC-tree score-based algorithm.  
- Uses a MaxSAT solver to map ancestral constraints to ordering constraints, making implied ancestral relations explicit.  
- Experimental results show the proposed method significantly outperforms a modified ILP-based Gobnilp in the tested setting.  
- Paper is well-written and clear, though the solution is conceptually straightforward.  
- Novelty: the problem is interesting and new (novelty score 3), but somewhat artificial since causal discovery methods allowing latent confounders would often be more appropriate (impact score 2).  
- Some acyclicity rules (lines 257–261) appear strange or incomplete: rule 3 has a symmetric premise but asymmetric conclusion, and rule 4 may wrongly exclude constraints like “X and Y do not cause each other.”  
- In practice, a small number of ancestral constraints is likely and may not substantially reduce the search space.  
- Comparison to Gobnilp is impressive but arguably unfair because Gobnilp wasn’t developed for this problem.  
- A more meaningful baseline would be a greedy algorithm like GES under the assumption that ancestral constraints align with the optimal model.  
- Theorem 1 needs to clarify that the constraints A satisfied by the CPDAG on the EC-tree are only a subset of those satisfied by the full DAG G.  
- Theorem 3 likely produces few positive edge implications when ancestral constraints are sparse.  
- Notation and presentation of “acyclic” constraints (e.g., line 267) are unclear and could be improved.  
- Practicality concern: 10–25% ancestral constraints already imply extensive knowledge of the target graph, which is not typical in practice.  
- Minor wording/clarity points: distinctions between “oracle” vs “omniscient oracle” (line 30), phrasing “empower the oracle” (line 38), and explicitly stating “optimizing = minimizing” (line 90).
