- Proposes an effective method to incorporate ancestral constraints into optimal Bayesian network learning by converting them to (maximal) ordering constraints used within an EC tree score-based algorithm.  
- Empirical results show significant performance gains over a modified ILP-based approach (Gobnilp) in the evaluated setting.  
- Paper is well-written and clear, though the underlying solution is conceptually straightforward.  
- Addresses a novel problem with a solution likely of interest to specialists (novelty positive).  
- Practical relevance is limited: when ancestral relations are known, more general causal discovery methods that handle unobserved confounders may be preferable (impact lower).  
- Core technical idea: transform hard-to-use ancestral constraints into ordering constraints representing edge absences so they fit decomposable score-based methods.  
- Uses a MaxSAT solver to ensure completeness by explicitly representing all implied ancestral constraints.  
- Concerns about specific rules (lines 257–261): rule 3’s premise is symmetric while its conclusion is not, and rule 4 may exclude valid constraints such as mutual non-causation.  
- In realistic applications few ancestral constraints are typically available, so the method’s ability to substantially reduce search space is questionable.  
- Comparison to Gobnilp is compelling but Gobnilp isn’t designed for this specific problem; a more informative benchmark would compare against a greedy algorithm like GES given oracle-consistent ancestral constraints.  
- Minor edits and clarifications suggested: clarify “standard” vs “omniscient” oracle (line 30); rephrase “empower the oracle” (line 38); state “optimizing” means “minimizing” (line 90).  
- Theorem/formal clarifications requested: Theorem 1 should state A satisfied by CPDAG P is a subset of those satisfied by full DAG G (line 164).  
- Theorem 3 may produce few positive edge implications, especially with sparse ancestral constraints (lines 225–227).  
- Rule–acyclicity link unclear (line 267) and prevalence claim (10–25% ancestral constraints) overstates typical prior knowledge (line 310).
