This paper presents a method for projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable ones so that structure-learning approaches based on decomposable scores can exploit such domain knowledge and thereby improve efficiency. The main novelty lies in Section 5, where the authors introduce their approach to projecting ancestral constraints into edge and topological ordering constraints. The paper is clearly written and explains the theory and experiments well. I have the following concerns and suggestions:

i. Table 3 suggests that projecting ancestral constraints improves EC-tree performance but not the ILP approach. Does this imply that projection to decomposable constraints benefits EC trees specifically and generalizes poorly to other learning paradigms? Since the core contribution is inferring decomposable constraints from non-decomposable ancestral constraints, it would be more convincing to show that supplying these projected constraints can improve the performance and efficiency of a method that originally cannot accommodate non-decomposable constraints.

ii. Table 2 shows an easy–hard–easy pattern in EC-tree performance as the proportion of ancestral constraints increases. What causes this behavior? Does it indicate that the impact of reducing the EC-tree search space via ancestral constraints is quite sensitive to the amount of prior knowledge, more so than for ILP? If so, does the number of ancestral constraints require careful tuning to achieve good performance?

iii. Table 2 reports structural Hamming distance only for EC trees. How does this compare with the ILP approach?

iv. Minor suggestion: I understand that BN graphs provide the conceptual foundation for EC trees since EC trees extend them. However, because the experiments focus solely on the EC-tree search space with ancestral constraints, I suggest dropping Section 4.1 or shortening it, and using the space to better explain EC trees and the use of A* in the EC-tree setting.
