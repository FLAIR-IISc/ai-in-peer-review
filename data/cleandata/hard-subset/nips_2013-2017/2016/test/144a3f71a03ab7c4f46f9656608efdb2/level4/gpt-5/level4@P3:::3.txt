Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses optimal Bayesian network (BN) structure learning in the presence of background knowledge expressed as ancestral constraints (statements that a variable is or is not an ancestor of another). Because ancestral constraints are non-decomposable, they cannot be directly handled by standard dynamic-programming and A* methods that rely on decomposable scores and local family choices. Building on the BN-graph/EC-tree framework of Chen et al. (2015, 2016), the authors search over CPDAGs (equivalence classes) and prune nodes inconsistent with the constraints. To regain efficiency, they “empower” a decomposable-score oracle (e.g., URLearning) by projecting ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and topological ordering constraints: (i) a sound-and-complete edge-projection procedure based on entailed ancestor/descendant sets, and (ii) a MaxSAT formulation to infer a maximal acyclic set of ordering constraints entailed by the ancestral constraints. Experiments compare the proposed approach to an ILP formulation (GOBNILP) augmented with linear encodings of ancestral relations and show large speedups. The work is complementary to prior ILP/CP encodings that can represent non-decomposable constraints (e.g., Jaakkola et al., 2010; Bartlett & Cussens, 2015) and to constraint-based learning using path constraints (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos, 2012, 2013). It extends Chen et al. by handling ancestral constraints and by leveraging projected constraints to guide decomposable-score oracles.

Strengths:
- Tackles a relevant and underexplored setting: optimal BN learning with ancestral constraints.
- Solid integration of EC-tree search with pruning tests and an oracle guided by projected constraints.
- Clear soundness statements (Theorems 1–4) for pruning and projection; the edge-projection rules are intuitive and appear complete under the closure assumptions.
- Empirical results show consistent, often dramatic, speedups over an ILP baseline; sensible ablations with and without projected constraints.
- The work is practically useful when domain knowledge is available and optimality is desired.

Weaknesses:
- The MaxSAT specification for ordering projection (Sec. 5.2) needs clearer justification and appears asymmetric in places; the sufficiency/necessity of constraints for acyclicity is not fully convincing.
- Positive edge entailment (Theorem 3) may be rare under sparse constraints, limiting the practical strength of edge projection.
- The CPDAG pruning fallback to enumerating all DAGs in a class can be exponential; practical impact and mitigations are not discussed.
- Evaluation relies on constraints sampled from ground truth; typical applications may have far fewer and noisier constraints. The claim that 10–25% constraints is “small” likely overstates typical prior knowledge.
- Comparison is mainly to ILP; additional baselines (e.g., order-graph search with projected constraints alone, or GES as a heuristic point of reference) would better contextualize efficiency/quality trade-offs.

Quality:
- The theoretical components for EC-tree pruning and edge projection are sound as stated. However, the ordering MaxSAT formulation deserves tighter formalization. In particular:
  - The “acyclicity” clause (rule 3 in Sec. 5.2) is symmetric in its premise but not in its consequences; please explain or symmetrize the formulation and justify sufficiency for preventing cycles in implied orderings.
  - Clarify whether the MaxSAT solution is guaranteed to produce a maximal closed set of orderings entailed by A without eliminating any DAG in G(A); Theorem 4 is helpful, but a direct proof that the clause set enforces safety would strengthen the argument.
- The fallback enumeration of class(P) for pruning is potentially expensive. Consider a SAT-based feasibility test or use of orientation rules to decide reachability in CPDAGs without full enumeration.
- Experiments are carefully designed and show strong gains. Still, adding:
  - An ablation where the oracle (order-graph method) runs with projected constraints but without EC-tree pruning would isolate the benefit of the EC-tree layer.
  - A heuristic baseline (e.g., GES) under the given constraints would inform practical efficiency, even though it cannot guarantee optimality.
  - Sensitivity to constraint noise (mispecified ancestries) would increase robustness claims.

Clarity:
- Generally well written. Suggested clarifications:
  - Define “omniscient oracle” vs. “oracle,” and avoid implying access to ground truth beyond decomposable optimization.
  - Replace “empower the oracle” with “supply the oracle with projected edge/order constraints.”
  - State explicitly whether the score is minimized (it is, per Eq. 1 as a cost).
  - Theorem 1: clarify that a CPDAG P “satisfies A” iff some DAG in class(P) satisfies A, and that pruning is safe in the EC tree because descendants cannot reorient to satisfy A.
  - Fix minor formatting issues (stray text like “th Conference...”, non-ASCII characters, placeholder citations with “?” markers), and ensure consistent notation (X→Y vs. X 6→ Y, X≺Y for orders).
- The inference rules in Sec. 5.2 would benefit from an example showing the MaxSAT clauses in a small instance and how they avoid eliminating any DAG in G(A).

Originality:
- The paper combines known ingredients (EC-tree search, CPDAG pruning, SAT/MaxSAT encodings) in a new way to handle ancestral constraints with exact optimality. While incremental relative to Chen et al., the projection to decomposable constraints for guiding an oracle is a neat idea and seems novel.

Significance:
- The method is relevant for domains with credible ancestral knowledge and where optimal structures are required. The reported speedups over ILP are compelling. Impact may be limited in settings with latent confounding or where only a handful of (possibly noisy) constraints are available; connecting to MAGs/PAGs or accommodating uncertainty in constraints would broaden applicability.

Specific questions and suggestions:
- How often did CPDAG enumeration trigger in practice, and what was its cost? Any pruning heuristics before enumeration?
- Can the MaxSAT projection incorporate “non-ancestry in both directions” constraints (X not ancestor of Y and Y not ancestor of X) without inducing cycles?
- Report the size of the projected edge/order sets vs. the original constraints to quantify the benefit.
- Consider noisy constraints: does the search degrade gracefully if some asserted ancestries are wrong?

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear problem formulation and solid, principled approach.
- Strong empirical performance with orders-of-magnitude speedups.
- Useful tool for practitioners with background knowledge.
- Extends a recent line of NIPS-relevant work on exact BN learning.

Cons for acceptance:
- Some theoretical gaps/ambiguities in the ordering MaxSAT formulation and its guarantees.
- Practicality may hinge on having many accurate constraints; evaluation could better reflect sparse/noisy knowledge.
- Limited baselines and ablations make it hard to isolate where the gains come from.

Minor/editorial:
- Clarify “optimize” = minimize the score.
- Tighten the statement of Theorem 1 as noted above.
- Revisit Theorem 3’s practical utility under sparse constraints.
- Clean up references with placeholders and formatting issues.
