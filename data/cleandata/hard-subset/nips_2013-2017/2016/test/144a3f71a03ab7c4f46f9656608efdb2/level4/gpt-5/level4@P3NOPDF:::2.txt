Summary
The paper proposes a method that incorporates a given set of ancestral constraints into an admissible heuristic to guide exhaustive search. The idea of using domain constraints to tighten heuristic bounds is appealing and, if properly validated, could substantially reduce search while preserving optimality.

Strengths
- Clear and relevant motivation: leveraging constraints to improve search efficiency without sacrificing admissibility.
- Theoretical grounding: the use of admissible heuristics suggests completeness and optimality are preserved.
- Potential generality: the approach could apply to multiple search back-ends if the heuristic interface is standard.

Main concerns and suggestions
- Experimental clarity: The current comparisons are difficult to interpret. My reading is that Table 1 compares (EC + your method) to (GOB + your method′), whereas Table 3 compares EC to GOB. If that is correct, these tables mix two axes (base algorithm and heuristic variant) in a way that obscures the incremental contribution of your method.
  - Please explicitly define “new work” versus “new work′.” Are these the same heuristic instantiated differently, or distinct algorithms?
  - State clearly, for each table, which base algorithm (EC or GOB) and which heuristic (baseline vs your method) are being used.

- Missing ablations against the relevant baselines:
  - Show (EC + your method) versus EC alone, on the same datasets and compute budgets. This is the most important comparison if EC is generally stronger than GOB.
  - Show (GOB + your method′) versus GOB alone, for completeness.
  - If EC already dominates GOB by a large margin, please highlight whether your method closes that gap for GOB or, more importantly, how much it advances the state of the art when paired with EC. Even if GOB benefits more than EC from your method, the key question is whether (EC + your method) is the best practical choice.

- Evaluation metrics and reporting:
  - Report wall-clock time, number of node expansions, and memory usage, not just accuracy or optimality, to quantify heuristic effectiveness.
  - Include success rates and timeouts; specify per-instance time limits and identical hardware/software environments for all methods.
  - Provide variability (e.g., across problem instances) with appropriate spread measures.

- Assumptions about constraints:
  - Clarify whether ancestral constraints are guaranteed correct. Does admissibility rely on perfect constraints, or does the heuristic remain admissible under partial/noisy constraints? What is the impact on performance if constraints are incomplete or contain errors? An experiment with perturbed constraints would be informative.
  - Describe the computational overhead to construct and maintain the heuristic from constraints, and whether that cost is amortized during search.

- Scope and generality:
  - Specify which classes of problems/graphs are supported and any restrictions on constraint types.
  - If the method can be combined with multiple search strategies, discuss how it interfaces with EC and GOB and whether any algorithm-specific tuning was used.

Clarity and presentation
- Clearly define EC and GOB at first mention and maintain consistent nomenclature throughout (e.g., EC, EC+Ours; GOB, GOB+Ours).
- Reorganize tables to isolate factors:
  - One table comparing EC vs EC+Ours.
  - One table comparing GOB vs GOB+Ours.
  - Optionally, a summary table comparing the strongest baselines: EC, GOB, and EC+Ours.
- Ensure all tables use the same datasets, metrics, and resource limits; include column/row headers that unambiguously denote base algorithm and heuristic.
- If “new work′” differs in any way (e.g., a variant heuristic), make that explicit in the captions and text.

Overall assessment
The paper presents a promising and principled idea—using ancestral constraints to derive an admissible heuristic that can substantially reduce exhaustive search. However, the current experimental presentation makes it hard to isolate the benefit of the proposed method, especially on the dominant EC baseline. A clearer and more direct comparison of (EC + your method) versus EC is essential. With tightened experimental design, explicit ablations, and clarification of constraints and overheads, the paper would make a stronger empirical case for the proposed contribution.
