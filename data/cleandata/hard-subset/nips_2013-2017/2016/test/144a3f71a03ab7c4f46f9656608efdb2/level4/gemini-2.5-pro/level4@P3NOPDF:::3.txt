This paper presents an efficient method for incorporating ancestral constraints into the task of score-based Bayesian network structure learning. The core contribution is a procedure to translate a given set of ancestral constraints into a maximal set of ordering constraints. These ordering constraints, which are more amenable to decomposable score-based methods, are then integrated into a learning algorithm that leverages the Essential Component (EC) tree structure proposed by Chen et al. (2015). The method's performance is evaluated against a straightforward adaptation of the ILP-based Gobnilp algorithm, demonstrating a significant improvement in efficiency for this specific setting.

**Strengths**

The paper is well-written, and the proposed solution is clearly explained. The problem of finding an optimal Bayesian network subject to ancestral constraints is novel, as there is no readily available standard solution. The approach of translating ancestral constraints into ordering constraints using a MaxSAT solver is a sensible and conceptually clean way to integrate this form of prior knowledge into existing score-based frameworks. The empirical results are strong and convincingly show that the proposed method outperforms the adapted ILP baseline. This work is likely to be of interest to a specialist audience focused on Bayesian network structure learning.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

While the paper presents a novel solution to a well-defined problem, there are several points that temper its overall impact and raise questions about the methodology and evaluation.

1.  **Practicality of the Problem Setting:** A key concern is the practical impact of this problem formulation. While tackling optimal BN learning with ancestral constraints is new, the setting can be viewed as somewhat artificial. In scenarios where ancestral information is available (e.g., from an expert), it is often more natural to employ causal discovery algorithms that can accommodate more general assumptions, such as the presence of unobserved confounders. The restriction to the Bayesian network formalism, which assumes causal sufficiency, may limit the applicability of this work in real-world causal inference tasks.

2.  **Clarity of the MaxSAT Formulation:** The translation from ancestral constraints to ordering constraints via MaxSAT is central to the paper, but some of the rules (lines 257-261) are not immediately clear or seem incomplete.
    *   The acyclicity rule (Rule 3) appears unusual. The premise `(X < Y) and (Z < W)` is symmetric with respect to the two pairs of variables, but the conclusion `Y -/-> Z` is not. Could the authors clarify the logic behind this asymmetric conclusion?
    *   Rule 4 seems to exclude certain types of constraints, such as specifying that two variables, X and Y, do not cause each other in either direction.
    *   The encoding described in line 267 is not easily recognizable as a standard or intuitive way to capture acyclicity. Further explanation would be beneficial.

3.  **Effectiveness with Sparse Constraints:** In practice, one would expect to have only a small number of ancestral constraints from an expert. It is unclear how significantly such a sparse set of constraints would prune the search space. Theorem 3, which concerns the implication of required edges, seems unlikely to be triggered frequently when the initial set of ancestral constraints is small. The experiments, which use 10%-25% of all possible ancestral constraints, may represent an overly optimistic scenario that assumes extensive prior knowledge of the target graph.

4.  **Experimental Baseline:** The empirical comparison against a modified version of the Gobnilp solver is compelling, but it should be noted that Gobnilp was not originally designed for this specific task. A more informative baseline could provide a better sense of the method's practical efficiency. For instance, a comparison against a greedy algorithm like GES would be valuable. If the provided ancestral constraints are consistent with the ground truth, GES (given this oracle input) should recover the same structure. While greedy methods do not guarantee optimality, such a comparison would offer a more meaningful benchmark for the computational gains achieved by the proposed algorithm.

**Minor Details**

*   **L30:** The term "omniscient oracle" is used. Is this distinct from a standard oracle in this context? If so, a brief clarification would be helpful.
*   **L38:** The phrase "empower the oracle" is slightly awkward. A clearer alternative might be "use in combination with the oracle" or "augment the oracle".
*   **L90:** It would be helpful to explicitly state that "optimizing" the score in this context means "minimizing" it.
*   **L164:** Theorem 1 should be stated more precisely. It seems the set of constraints `A` satisfied by the CPDAG `P` on the EC tree is a *subset* of the constraints satisfied by the full DAG `G`. If this is the case, the "if and only if" statement may not hold as written and requires clarification.

Overall, this paper introduces a novel and interesting problem with a technically sound solution. However, its practical impact is somewhat limited by the specificity of the problem setting and questions surrounding the experimental design. The work is a solid contribution for a specialized audience but is not a spectacular breakthrough.
