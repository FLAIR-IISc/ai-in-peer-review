

The paper presents a method for incorporating ancestral constraints into optimal Bayesian network structure learning by translating these non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ordering constraints that can be efficiently handled by existing score-based learning algorithms. The authors build upon the EC tree search space framework introduced by Chen et al. (2015, 2016) and demonstrate how to empower oracles for decomposable scores by passing them constraints inferred from ancestral constraints through a MaxSAT solver. The approach is evaluated against an ILP-based implementation of Gobnilp with linear constraints for ancestral relations, showing orders-of-magnitude efficiency improvements.

Strengths:
- The paper addresses a meaningful gap in the literature, as most optimal structure learning approaches assume decomposable scores and cannot directly incorporate ancestral constraints, which are important for expressing cause-and-effect relationships.
- The translation of ancestral constraints into decomposable ordering constraints is technically sound and well-motivated.
- The empirical evaluation demonstrates clear and substantial efficiency gains over the ILP-based baseline, which was not designed specifically for handling ancestral constraints.
- The paper is well-written with clear explanations of the technical contributions and the relationship to prior work.

Weaknesses:
- The problem setting is somewhat artificial, as ancestral constraints are typically used in conjunction with more comprehensive causal discovery methods that handle unobserved confounders, which the paper does not address.
- The practical impact may be limited, as the paper suggests that only 10%-25% ancestral constraints are needed to achieve significant improvements, which may not reflect typical real-world scenarios where such constraints are sparse.
- The comparison against Gobnilp lacks meaningful baselines; a more relevant comparison would be against greedy algorithms like GES, which would provide context for the efficiency gains in practical settings (though acknowledging that greedy methods cannot guarantee optimality).
- Several technical details require clarification: The distinction between "standard" and "omniscient" oracles (line 30) is unclear; "empower the oracle" (line 38) would benefit from clearer phrasing; the optimization direction should be specified as minimization (line 90); Theorem 1 should clarify that the constraints satisfied by a CPDAG are a subset of those satisfied by the corresponding DAG; Theorem 3 is unlikely to yield many positive edge implications with sparse ancestral constraints; the acyclicity condition in line 267 is not clearly captured by the presented rule.

The paper makes a technically sound contribution to a niche but important problem in Bayesian network learning. While the problem setting has some limitations, the solution is elegant and the empirical results are compelling. The authors would strengthen their contribution by addressing the practical limitations of their approach and providing more meaningful comparisons against relevant baselines.
