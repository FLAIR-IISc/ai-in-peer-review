

This paper presents a method for projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints, enabling decomposable-score-based structure learning approaches to incorporate such domain knowledge and enhance learning efficiency. The primary novelty resides in Section 5, where the authors introduce their technique to transform non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and topological ordering constraints. The paper is well-written and clearly articulates both theoretical contributions and experimental results. I offer the following concerns and suggestions:  
i. Table 3 indicates that constraint projection improves EC tree performance but not the ILP approach. Does this imply the projection method is effective solely for EC trees and lacks generalization to other learning frameworks? Given the paper's core innovation is inferring decomposable constraints from non-decomposable ancestral constraints, it would strengthen the argument if the authors demonstrated that incorporating these decomposable constraints enhances the performance and efficiency of learning approaches previously unable to handle non-decomposable constraints.  
ii. Table 2 reveals an "easy-hard-easy" performance pattern for EC trees as ancestral constraint proportion increases. What explains this phenomenon? Does it suggest that the impact of ancestral constraints on reducing the EC tree search space is highly sensitive to the quantity of incorporated domain knowledge relative to the ILP approach, necessitating careful tuning of constraint numbers for optimal performance?  
iii. Table 2 reports structural Hamming distance results exclusively for EC trees. How do these results compare directly with the ILP approach?  
iv. While the BN graph underpins EC trees theoretically (as EC trees extend from Bayesian networks), the experiments focus exclusively on EC trees with ancestral constraints applied. Thus, Section 4.1 may be omitted to allocate more space to thoroughly explaining EC trees and the A* algorithm within the EC tree framework.
