This paper proposes an efficient method for incorporating ancestral constraints into the search for optimal Bayesian networks (BNs) from data. The key idea is to translate a given set of ancestral constraints into a (maximal) set of ordering constraints that can be seamlessly integrated into a decomposable, score-based learning framework built on the EC tree of Chen et al. (2015). Empirically, the approach is compared to a straightforward modification of the ILP-based Gobnilp method and is shown to outperform it substantially in this setting. The paper is generally clear and well structured, though the core idea is conceptually straightforward. The problem and solution are novel and the results are solid, yielding a contribution that is of interest to a specialist audience, if not spectacular.

Novelty and impact: The problem—optimal BN learning with ancestral constraints—does not have a standard solution, which supports the paper’s novelty (novelty = 3). However, the setting feels somewhat artificial: once ancestral relations are available, more general causal discovery approaches that allow for latent confounding often become more appropriate. This limits the broader impact (impact = 2).

Method and correctness: The approach is to convert hard-to-use ancestral constraints (which do not align naturally with decomposable scores) into ordering constraints that can be expressed as absences of edges and thus do fit decomposable scoring. The mapping is performed via a MaxSAT solver, which ensures that all implied ancestral constraints are made explicit. While the overall strategy is reasonable, several of the rules given on lines 257–261 appear strange or incomplete:
- In acyclicity rule 3, the premise is symmetric—(X < Y) and (Z < W) is equivalent to (Z < W) and (X < Y)—but the conclusion is not (it concludes Y -/-> Z). This asymmetry is puzzling and needs justification or correction.
- Rule 4 appears to exclude plausible ancestral constraints of the form “X and Y do not cause each other,” which seems like an important class to support.

Practicality and evaluation: In practice, one might expect only a small number of ancestral constraints, which are unlikely to substantially prune the search space. The comparison against Gobnilp with added linear constraints is visually compelling, but Gobnilp is not purpose-built for this task. A more telling benchmark would assume the ancestral constraints are consistent with the optimal model and compare against a greedy algorithm such as GES; with oracle input, GES should recover the same model. While greedy methods lack optimality guarantees, such a comparison would better indicate the efficiency gains achieved by the proposed translation and search procedure.

Minor comments and requests for clarification:
- Line 30: Is there a difference intended between a (standard) oracle and an omniscient oracle?
- Line 38: “Empower the oracle” sounds like “use in combination with the oracle.” Please clarify.
- Line 90: Explicitly note that optimizing here means minimizing the score.
- Line 164: Theorem 1 should state clearly that the set of constraints A satisfied by the CPDAG P on the EC tree is only a subset of the constraints A satisfied by the full DAG G; otherwise the “iff” does not hold as written.
- Lines 225–227: Theorem 3 seems unlikely to yield many positive edge implications when only small/sparse sets of ancestral constraints are provided.
- Line 267: The encoding is not easily recognizable as capturing acyclicity (see also the concern above about rule 3).
- Line 310: For reasonably sized graphs, specifying 10%–25% of ancestral constraints would already imply extensive prior knowledge of the target graph, which is not typical in practice.

Overall assessment: A clear and technically sound contribution with a novel angle on incorporating ancestral constraints into optimal BN learning. The main concern is the practicality and evaluation context: the setting may be uncommon in practice, and the most prominent baseline is not tailored for this problem. Addressing the rule inconsistencies, clarifying the theoretical statements, and adding a comparison to a greedy baseline under oracle conditions would strengthen the paper.
