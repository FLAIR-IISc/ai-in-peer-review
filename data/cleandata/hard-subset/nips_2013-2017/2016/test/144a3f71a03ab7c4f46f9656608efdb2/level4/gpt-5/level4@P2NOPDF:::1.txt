This paper addresses the constrained optimization problem of learning Bayesian networks from local scores under ancestral constraints. The search is a branch-and-bound over CPDAGs: a child CPDAG is obtained by fixing a parent set for a variable whose parents are not yet fixed in the current CPDAG. Sensible symmetry breaking is applied. The search uses A* with a heuristic provided by the URLearning system; while details are omitted here, they are available in prior work.

Given ancestral constraints, the authors derive pruning rules. Lemma 3 (in the supplement) is the key result. I believe the lemma is correct, but the proof is hard to follow. In particular, the step “By the EC tree edge generation rules, Gk also contains edge Z -> W” needs a fuller justification.

The paper also discusses constraints implied by ancestral constraints (the standard term is “implied constraints”; here they are called “projected constraints”). Sets of ancestral constraints can imply edge constraints that enable additional pruning. The authors further infer constraints on permissible topological orderings (“ordering constraints”). The exposition here is unclear. The text states that we “can infer Y < Z from Z not an ancestor of Y.” The issue is the word “can”: there are DAGs where Z is not an ancestor of Y but Y < Z is still a consistent order (e.g., a graph with vertices Z and Y and no edges). I think the intended meaning is that given “Z is not an ancestor of Y,” we may choose to add the constraint Y < Z. The goal is to propose as many consistent ordering constraints as possible (not only those strictly entailed). The authors carefully avoid overcommitting and use a MAXSAT approach to select such constraints, but the method needs a clearer explanation.

The experiments vary the number of variables, data points, and ancestral constraints, and compare the proposed method to GOBNILP. The proposed method is substantially faster. However, as the authors note, even without ancestral constraints GOBNILP typically performs worse than A* approaches when there is no bound on parent-set size. Since the experiments also impose no parent-set bound, it is difficult to disentangle whether GOBNILP’s poor performance is mainly due to handling many parent sets or specifically to ancestral constraints.

In addition, the GOBNILP encoding of ancestral constraints (supplement) follows the MAXSAT encoding from [Cussens, 2008], rather than an ILP-native encoding such as Section 3.1.1 of [Cussens, 2010]. The MAXSAT-based formulation is ill-suited for ILP, in part because it introduces a cubic number of E(X,Y,Z) variables. A stronger ILP formulation would introduce binary A(X,Y) variables to represent ancestry, alongside the usual I(Y,U) variables for parent-set selection, and drop the E variables. One would add transitivity constraints on A and the following linking constraints between A and I:
A(X,Y) ≥ Σ_{U: X ∈ U} I(Y,U)
That is, if X is chosen as a parent of Y, then X must be an ancestor of Y. This yields a much tighter linear relaxation and should significantly improve ILP performance. It mirrors Section 3.1.1 of [Cussens, 2010] for a total order, adjusted for the fact that ancestry is a partial order. I am confident that even with such a sensible ILP formulation, the proposed method would still outperform GOBNILP, but the empirical comparison is weakened by using a poor baseline encoding.

It would also be informative to compare against the constraint-programming approach of van Beek and Hoffmann (CP 2015), which often outperforms GOBNILP and seems readily adaptable to accept ancestral constraints. This related work is not cited.

Overall, the paper proposes a promising A*-based framework with sound symmetry breaking and useful pruning from ancestral and inferred constraints. The main weaknesses are (i) gaps in clarity—especially the proof of Lemma 3 and the procedure for inferring projected and ordering constraints—and (ii) the experimental comparison, which relies on an unfavorable ILP encoding and omits a strong CP baseline. Addressing these issues would substantially strengthen the paper. References: [Cussens, 2010] James Cussens. Maximum likelihood pedigree reconstruction using integer programming. WCB’10. Peter van Beek and Hella-Franziska Hoffmann. Machine learning of Bayesian networks using constraint programming. CP 2015.
