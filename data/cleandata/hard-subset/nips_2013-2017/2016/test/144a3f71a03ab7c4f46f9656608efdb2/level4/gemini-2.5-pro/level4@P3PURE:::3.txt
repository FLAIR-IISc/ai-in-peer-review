This paper presents an efficient method for learning optimal Bayesian networks from data subject to a set of ancestral constraints. The core idea is to translate these ancestral constraints into a maximal set of ordering constraints using a MaxSAT solver. These ordering constraints are then integrated into a score-based learning algorithm that utilizes the EC tree structure proposed by Chen et al. (2015). The proposed method is experimentally evaluated against a modified version of the ILP-based Gobnilp algorithm, demonstrating significant performance improvements in this specific setting.

The paper is generally well-written and the proposed solution, while conceptually straightforward, is clearly explained. The primary strength is its novelty; the problem of optimal Bayesian network learning with ancestral constraints does not have a standard, off-the-shelf solution. The proposed method provides a novel approach to this interesting problem, and the results will likely be of interest to a specialist audience.

However, there are several weaknesses and areas that require further clarification:

1.  **Problem Formulation:** A key concern is the practical impact and motivation for the problem setting. The paper argues for the utility of ancestral constraints, but if such causal knowledge is available, it may be more appropriate to employ more general causal discovery algorithms that can also account for latent confounders. This makes the problem setting feel somewhat artificial.

2.  **Technical Details of the SAT Formulation:** The translation from ancestral to ordering constraints is the core technical contribution. However, there are potential issues with the proposed SAT rules (lines 257-261).
    *   In 'acyclicity rule 3', the premise `(X < Y) and (Z < W)` is symmetric with respect to the two pairs, but the conclusion `(Y -/-> Z)` is not.
    *   Rule 4 seems unable to capture constraints of the form 'X and Y are not ancestors of each other'.
    Further clarification on the logic and completeness of these rules is needed.

3.  **Experimental Baseline:** The experimental comparison to a modified Gobnilp is not entirely convincing, as Gobnilp was not designed for this specific task. While the performance gains are impressive, the baseline is not a strong one. A more informative baseline for efficiency would be a comparison against a greedy search algorithm like GES. If the provided ancestral constraints are consistent with the true model (acting as an oracle), GES should find the optimal network. While greedy methods do not guarantee optimality in the general case, this comparison would provide a more meaningful benchmark for the computational efficiency achieved by the proposed algorithm.

4.  **Practicality of Assumptions:** In many real-world scenarios, one would likely have only a sparse set of known ancestral constraints. It is questionable whether such a small set would be sufficient to significantly prune the search space and realize the method's efficiency gains. Furthermore, the experimental setting using 10%-25% of all possible ancestral constraints seems unrealistic. This level of prior knowledge would imply an extensive, almost complete, understanding of the target graph, which is not typical in practical applications.

**Minor Details:**

*   **Line 30:** What is the distinction between a "standard" oracle and an "omniscient oracle"?
*   **Line 38:** The phrase "empower the oracle" is unclear. Does this mean "use in combination with the oracle"?
*   **Line 90:** It should be explicitly stated that "optimizing" the score means "minimizing" it in this context.
*   **Line 164:** Theorem 1 should be rephrased for clarity. It should state that the set of constraints A satisfied by the CPDAG on the EC tree is a subset of the constraints satisfied by the full DAG G; otherwise, the "if and only if" condition may not hold.
*   **Lines 225-227:** Theorem 3 seems unlikely to yield many positive edge implications, especially when the set of initial ancestral constraints is sparse.
*   **Line 267:** The name "acyclic" for the rule is not immediately intuitive, especially given the concerns raised about the rule's logic above.
