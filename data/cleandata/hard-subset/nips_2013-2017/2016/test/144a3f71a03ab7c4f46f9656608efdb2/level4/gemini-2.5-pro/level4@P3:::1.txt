This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data when subject to ancestral constraints. Such constraints, which specify direct or indirect causal relationships, are non-decomposable and thus cannot be handled by many state-of-the-art, score-based learning algorithms that rely on decomposable scores.

The authors' approach extends a recently proposed A*-based search framework that navigates a search space of equivalence classes of DAGs (an "EC tree"). The A* heuristic is provided by an "oracle"—an algorithm like URLearning that can efficiently find optimal structures for decomposable scores. The paper's main contributions are twofold. First, it introduces a sound method for pruning the EC tree search space by checking whether a given equivalence class (CPDAG) can satisfy the set of ancestral constraints. Second, and more significantly, it proposes a method to "project" the non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints (i.e., required/forbidden edges and constraints on the topological ordering). These inferred decomposable constraints are then passed to the oracle, which tightens the A* heuristic and dramatically improves search efficiency.

The proposed method is empirically evaluated against an Integer Linear Programming (ILP) approach (GOBNILP), demonstrating orders-of-magnitude speedups on a variety of benchmark networks.

### Strengths

*   **Significance:** The paper tackles a significant and practical problem. Incorporating complex prior knowledge like ancestral relationships is crucial in many domains, but it poses a major challenge for exact structure learning algorithms. This work provides an effective solution.
*   **Originality:** The approach is novel and clever. The idea of projecting non-decomposable constraints into a set of decomposable constraints that can be leveraged by a fast, decomposable-score oracle is a powerful and elegant contribution. This "empowerment" of the heuristic is key to the method's efficiency.
*   **Quality:** The proposed framework is technically sound. The experiments are well-designed, systematically varying the number of variables, data size, and the number of constraints. The results are impressive, showing a clear and substantial performance advantage over the ILP baseline. The paper also provides an interesting insight into the value of prior knowledge, showing that a small percentage of ancestral constraints can improve model accuracy as much as a significant increase in training data.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

While the paper presents a strong contribution, there are several areas where clarity could be improved and the experimental evaluation could be strengthened.

1.  **Clarity of Constraint Inference:** The explanation of how topological ordering constraints are inferred is somewhat confusing. The paper states, "we can infer Y < Z from Z not an ancestor of Y". This wording is ambiguous because this is not a necessary entailment; a DAG can satisfy "Z is not an ancestor of Y" without requiring Y to precede Z in all of its topological orderings (e.g., a graph with no edge between Y and Z). The authors' method, which uses MaxSAT, appears to be finding a maximal set of *consistent* ordering constraints rather than only those that are strictly entailed. It would improve clarity to explicitly state that the goal is to select a large set of consistent ordering constraints that do not improperly prune valid DAGs from the search space.

2.  **Strength of the ILP Baseline:** The comparison to the GOBNILP system is a core part of the evaluation, but the chosen ILP formulation for ancestral constraints may not be the strongest available. The formulation is based on a 2008 MAX-SAT encoding which introduces a cubic number of auxiliary variables, potentially leading to a weak linear relaxation. A more direct and modern ILP encoding, such as that described in Section 3.1.1 of Cussens (2010, WCB), could provide a much more competitive baseline. This alternative approach involves adding binary variables for ancestral relations and linking them directly to parent-set variables, which should yield a tighter relaxation and better performance. Comparing against a potentially suboptimal formulation weakens the claim of superiority. While the proposed method is still likely to be faster, demonstrating this against a stronger baseline would make the results more conclusive.

3.  **Experimental Conditions:** The experiments are conducted without an upper bound on the parent set size. It is a known characteristic that A*-based search methods tend to outperform ILP-based methods under this condition. Therefore, it is difficult to fully disentangle whether GOBNILP's poor performance is due to its known weakness in handling an unrestricted number of parents or its specific handling of the ancestral constraint encoding. Adding experiments with a bounded in-degree would provide a more nuanced comparison and strengthen the paper's conclusions.

4.  **Missing Related Work:** The paper omits a relevant line of work on Bayesian network structure learning using Constraint Programming (CP), notably van Beek and Hoffmann (CP 2015). This CP-based approach has been shown to be highly competitive and often outperforms GOBNILP. Since ancestral constraints can be naturally expressed in CP, a discussion of this related work is warranted, and a comparison would further solidify the paper's empirical contributions.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper with a novel and significant contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. The proposed method for handling non-decomposable constraints is powerful and effective. The primary weaknesses lie in the clarity of some technical descriptions and the setup of the experimental comparison. Addressing these points, particularly by strengthening the ILP baseline and discussing related CP methods, would make this an even stronger paper.
