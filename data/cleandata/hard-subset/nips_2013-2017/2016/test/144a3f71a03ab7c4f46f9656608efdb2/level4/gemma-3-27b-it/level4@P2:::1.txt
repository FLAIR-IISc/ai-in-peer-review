This paper addresses the problem of learning Bayesian networks optimally under ancestral constraints, a challenging task due to the non-decomposable nature of these constraints. The authors propose a search-based approach leveraging an existing framework for optimal structure learning with non-decomposable scores, building upon their previous work. The search space is based on Equivalence Classes (EC) trees, and an A* search strategy is employed, guided by a heuristic derived from the URLearning system. A key contribution is the ability to prune the search space using the ancestral constraints, and to further improve efficiency by “projecting” these constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints that can empower the heuristic oracle. 

The paper is well-motivated, as incorporating background knowledge is crucial for practical Bayesian network learning, and ancestral constraints represent a common form of such knowledge. The approach of leveraging an existing framework for non-decomposable scores, rather than developing a new one from scratch, is sensible. The idea of projecting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones to improve search efficiency is novel and potentially impactful. 

However, the clarity of the presentation could be improved. The explanation of how the heuristic is “empowered” by the projected constraints is somewhat vague. While the authors mention utilizing the projected constraints as input to the oracle, the specific mechanism for incorporating them into the URLearning system isn't fully detailed. The proof of Lemma 3 (in the supplementary material) also lacks sufficient explanation, particularly the justification for the statement regarding edge Z → W. The discussion of “inferring” ordering constraints requires clarification. The phrasing regarding what constraints “can” be inferred is imprecise; it's more accurate to say constraints *can be added* if consistent with the ancestral constraints, rather than being strictly entailed. The use of MAXSAT for inferring ordering constraints is a reasonable approach, but its necessity isn't fully justified.

Regarding originality, the core idea of combining a search-based approach with constraint projection is novel. However, the paper builds heavily on previous work, particularly [Chen et al., 2015, 2016], and the connection to related work in constraint programming (specifically [van Beek and Hoffmann, 2015]) is missing. While the authors acknowledge ILP approaches, the chosen comparison with GOBNILP, using a suboptimal encoding of ancestral constraints (based on [Cussens, 2008]), weakens the evaluation. A more direct comparison using a tighter ILP formulation (as suggested in [Cussens, 2010], Section 3.1.1) would provide a stronger assessment of the proposed method's performance.

The significance of the work is considerable. The empirical results demonstrate significant speedups compared to GOBNILP, and the impact of constraint projection is clearly shown. The observation that a relatively small number of ancestral constraints can improve model accuracy as much as increasing training data is particularly valuable. This suggests the approach could be highly beneficial in scenarios where obtaining labeled data is expensive or difficult. The scalability of the approach to 20 variables is also a positive result, aligning with the capabilities of state-of-the-art score-based methods.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to incorporating ancestral constraints into Bayesian network learning.
*   Significant empirical speedups compared to existing ILP-based methods.
*   Demonstrated impact of constraint projection on search efficiency and model accuracy.
*   Addresses a practically relevant problem with potential for real-world applications.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Clarity of the presentation could be improved, particularly regarding the heuristic empowerment and constraint projection mechanisms.
*   Lack of comparison with a state-of-the-art constraint programming approach ([van Beek and Hoffmann, 2015]).
*   Comparison to GOBNILP is weakened by the use of a suboptimal ILP encoding.
*   The justification for using MAXSAT for ordering constraint inference is not entirely convincing.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and potentially impactful contribution. While the clarity of the presentation could be improved and the experimental evaluation could be strengthened, the positive aspects outweigh the negatives. I recommend **acceptance**, with the caveat that the authors address the clarity issues and consider including a comparison with the van Beek and Hoffmann method in a future version.
