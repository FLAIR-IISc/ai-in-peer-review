Summary and contributions:
The paper proposes an efficient method for incorporating ancestral constraints into exact score-based Bayesian network (BN) structure learning. The key idea is to translate a given set of ancestral constraints into a maximal set of ordering constraints, computed via a MaxSAT solver, and then exploit these ordering constraints within the EC-tree–based exact search of Chen et al. (2015). Because ordering constraints can be expressed as forbidden edges, they align well with decomposable scores. Experiments compare the proposed approach against a straightforward modification of the ILP-based Gobnilp system augmented with linear constraints, and show substantial improvements under the tested settings.

Strengths:
- Clear and generally well-written exposition with a clean separation between constraint translation and exact search.
- Tidy conceptual move: map hard-to-handle ancestral constraints to order constraints that fit decomposable scores and EC-tree search.
- Use of MaxSAT to compute the closure of implied ancestral relations is a principled way to expose all usable constraints to the EC-tree algorithm.
- Novelty lies in the explicit integration of ancestral constraints into optimal BN learning, for which there is no standard off-the-shelf solution.
- Empirical results against the adapted Gobnilp baseline are favorable.

Limitations and concerns:
- Scope and practical impact: In many realistic causal-discovery scenarios where ancestral information is relevant, latent confounding is plausible; methods that operate over equivalence classes with hidden variables or ADMGs/ancestral graphs may be more appropriate. This somewhat narrows the practical impact of the contribution, despite its technical neatness.
- Strength of pruning: In practice, one often has only a small number of ancestral constraints. It is unclear how much these few constraints reduce the search space. The paper would benefit from a more systematic analysis of pruning as a function of the number and type of constraints.
- Baseline choice: Although the Gobnilp modification is a reasonable first comparison, Gobnilp was not designed for this particular constraint class. Additional baselines would better calibrate the gains. In particular, assuming constraints are consistent with the ground truth, a greedy algorithm such as GES (with oracle scores) should, in principle, return the same equivalence class; comparing runtime and success rates in this oracle setting would give a more meaningful picture of efficiency.
- Soundness/clarity of the rule set (lines 257–261): Some rules look asymmetric or incomplete.
  - For example, the premise of acyclicity rule 3 is symmetric ((X < Y) and (Z < W) is identical to (Z < W) and (X < Y)), but the conclusion is not (Y -/-> Z). Please clarify the intended directionality and whether additional symmetric conclusions are required.
  - Rule 4 appears to exclude constraints of the form “X and Y do not cause each other.” Is this intentional? If not, please refine the rule or state the conditions under which mutual non-ancestry is expressible.
  - Overall, it would help to provide a formal statement of soundness and completeness for the rule system, or to delimit precisely which classes of ancestral constraints are captured.
- Theorem 1 (line 164): As written, the iff statement risks overclaiming. The set of constraints A satisfied by a CPDAG P on the EC tree is, in general, only a subset of those satisfied by the corresponding DAG G. Please restate the theorem to make the subset relationship explicit, or add conditions under which equality holds.
- Theorem 3 (lines 225–227): With sparse ancestral constraints, it seems unlikely that many positive edge implications will be derived. It would be useful to quantify this empirically (e.g., expected number of implied edges vs. number of input constraints) and to discuss when the rule yields substantial benefit.
- Acyclicity encoding (line 267 and the related rules): The current presentation does not make it easy to recognize how acyclicity is enforced. Please add intuition and, ideally, a short correctness argument that the ordering constraints prevent directed cycles without unintentionally forbidding acyclic structures.

Quality of the evaluation:
- The head-to-head with Gobnilp shows impressive speedups; however, because Gobnilp is not tailored for ancestral constraints, this may overstate the advantage. Please add:
  - A greedy baseline (e.g., GES with oracle scores), used in the regime where constraints are known to be consistent with the optimal model.
  - A sensitivity analysis varying the number, type, and noise level of ancestral constraints to evaluate robustness and pruning effectiveness.
  - Discussion of whether constraints derived from realistic sources (e.g., limited domain knowledge) are more likely to be pairwise, chain-like, or otherwise structured, and how that structure affects performance.

Clarity and minor comments:
- Line 30: Please clarify whether “(standard) oracle” and “omniscient oracle” differ. If not, use one term consistently.
- Line 38: “Empower the oracle” reads oddly; consider “use in combination with the oracle” or “augment the oracle with...”.
- Line 90: Note explicitly that optimizing the score here means minimizing it (if using MDL/BIC) or maximizing a negated form; be consistent across the paper.
- Line 164: See Theorem 1 comment above; clarify the CPDAG vs. DAG constraint relationship to justify the iff.
- Lines 225–227: See Theorem 3 comment; consider tempering the claim or adding empirical support.
- Line 267: Not easily recognizable as encoding acyclicity; add intuition or a reference to a standard acyclicity-to-ordering reduction.
- Line 310: Claiming that 10%–25% ancestral constraints are typical seems unrealistic for moderate/large graphs; that would imply substantial prior knowledge. Please justify with data or revise.

Overall assessment:
A solid and clearly executed contribution that makes a conceptually straightforward but practically useful bridge from ancestral constraints to order constraints, enabling effective use of EC-tree–based optimal BN learning. The work addresses a niche but real gap and will be of interest to specialists in exact BN learning and constraint-based causal modeling. The primary weaknesses are the limited practical scope (when latent confounding is plausible), questions about the rule system’s coverage and clarity, and the reliance on a baseline not designed for this setting. Addressing the theoretical clarifications and strengthening the evaluation with more appropriate baselines and sensitivity analyses would significantly enhance the paper.
