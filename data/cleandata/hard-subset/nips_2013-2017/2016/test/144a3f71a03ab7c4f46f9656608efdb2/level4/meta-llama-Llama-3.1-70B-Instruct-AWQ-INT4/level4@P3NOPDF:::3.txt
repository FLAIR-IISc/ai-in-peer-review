This paper presents an efficient method for incorporating ancestral constraints into the process of finding optimal Bayesian networks from data. The approach involves translating a set of ancestral constraints into a maximal set of ordering constraints, which are then utilized in a score-based learning algorithm based on the EC tree introduced by [Chen et al., 2015]. The results are evaluated against a modified version of the ILP-based Gobnilp algorithm, demonstrating significant outperformance in the given setting.

The paper is reasonably well-written and clearly explained, although the solution is conceptually straightforward. The problem addressed in this paper is novel and of interest to a specialist audience. The combination of optimal BN learning and ancestral constraints does not have a readily available standard solution, making this a unique contribution (novelty=3). However, the setting may be somewhat artificial, as ancestral relations often necessitate more general causal discovery algorithms that account for unobserved confounders (hence impact=2).

The approach involves translating hard-to-use ancestral constraints into sets of ordering constraints that can be more easily expressed in terms of absence of edges, which fit decomposable scores. This mapping is achieved through a MaxSAT solver, ensuring that all implied ancestral constraints are made explicit. However, some rules (e.g., 257-261) appear incomplete or incorrect. For instance, the premise in acyclicity rule 3 is symmetric, but the conclusion is not. Additionally, rule 4 seems to exclude ancestral constraints of the type 'X and Y do not cause each other'.

In practice, only a few ancestral constraints are typically given, and these are unlikely to significantly reduce the search space. The comparison against Gobnilp with added linear constraints appears impressive, but Gobnilp is not designed to tackle this specific problem. A more meaningful alternative would be to assume that the ancestral constraints align with the optimal model and compare against a greedy algorithm like GES, which should produce the same output for oracle input. While greedy algorithms cannot guarantee optimality, this comparison would provide a more accurate indication of the method's efficiency.

Minor details and suggestions for improvement:

- Clarify the distinction between a standard oracle and an omniscient oracle (line 30).
- Consider rephrasing 'empower the oracle' to 'use in combination with the oracle' (line 38).
- Specify that optimizing refers to minimizing (line 90).
- Revise Theorem 1 to clearly state that the indicated set of constraints A satisfied by CPDAG P on the EC tree is only a subset of the set of constraints A satisfied by the full DAG G (line 164).
- Theorem 3 may not lead to many positive edge implications given sparse ancestral constraints (lines 225-227).
- The notation used in line 267 may not be easily recognizable as capturing 'acyclic' (see also above).
- For reasonable-sized graphs, 10%-25% ancestral constraints would already imply extensive knowledge of the target graph, which is not typical in practice (line 310).

Overall, the paper presents a novel solution to an interesting problem, but the setting may be somewhat artificial, and the comparison could be strengthened by considering alternative algorithms.
