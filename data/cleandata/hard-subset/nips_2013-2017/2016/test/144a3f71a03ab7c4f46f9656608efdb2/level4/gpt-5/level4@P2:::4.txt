Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses optimal Bayesian network structure learning in the presence of background knowledge expressed as ancestral constraints, which are non-decomposable and therefore not directly supported by mainstream decomposable-score methods. Building on BN graphs and EC trees (Chen et al., 2015; 2016), the authors (1) prune the EC-tree search space by testing whether a CPDAG can still realize a set of ancestral constraints, and (2) “project” non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and topological ordering constraints to empower a decomposable-score oracle (e.g., URLEARNING). Theoretical results justify pruning (Theorem 1) and characterize entailment for inferred negative and positive edge constraints (Theorems 2–3) and sufficient conditions for entailed ordering constraints via a MaxSAT formulation (Theorem 4). Empirically, the approach achieves large speedups over an ILP baseline (GOBNILP), and the authors analyze how the amount of ancestral knowledge affects search difficulty and solution quality.

The work is well aligned with prior literature on optimal BN learning using dynamic programming and heuristic search over order graphs (Koivisto and Sood; Yuan and Malone), EC trees (Chen et al.), and ILP/CP approaches (Jaakkola et al.; Cussens; van Beek and Hoffmann). It complements constraint-based uses of path constraints and priors (Borboudakis and Tsamardinos) by giving an exact, score-based approach that leverages non-decomposable knowledge.

Strengths
- Clear, principled integration of non-decomposable ancestral constraints into an exact search space (EC tree), with sound pruning and an oracle-based heuristic.
- Novel projection of ancestral constraints to decomposable edge and ordering constraints; the MaxSAT reduction is sensible and leverages strong solvers.
- Theoretical statements make the pruning and projection procedures precise; the edge entailment rules are intuitive and complete for the stated closure.
- Strong empirical gains over ILP in runtime, especially as n grows; useful analysis of the easy–hard–easy trend; evidence that constraints can improve accuracy at fixed sample sizes.

Weaknesses and questions
- Impact on non-EC-tree learners. While projected constraints substantially help EC-tree search, the benefits to ILP are mixed for small n and positive for larger n. Please clarify:
  - When do projected constraints introduce overhead for ILP (e.g., extra constraints without much pruning power)? 
  - Beyond ILP, can you show benefits for other decomposable-score exact methods (e.g., DP over order graphs, A* on order graphs), thereby demonstrating broader applicability of the projection?
- Easy–hard–easy pattern. The EC-tree search shows an easy–hard–easy pattern as the proportion of ancestral constraints increases. A deeper explanation would be valuable:
  - Is the “hard” region driven by conflicts between the oracle’s lower bounds and pruning efficacy, or by constraint-induced fragmentation that weakens heuristic accuracy?
  - Can you provide guidance for practitioners on robustness to imperfect or partial knowledge (e.g., how performance degrades with noisy or inconsistent constraints)?
- Accuracy comparisons. Table 3 reports structural Hamming distance (SHD) only for the EC-tree approach. Please include SHD (or other accuracy metrics) for ILP on the same datasets to assess whether speed differences trade off against quality.
- Completeness and computational cost of projection:
  - Edge constraints: you state the algorithm efficiently enumerates all inferrable edge constraints given a “maximum” closure of ancestral constraints. Please quantify the cost of this closure and the subsequent checks in practice.
  - Ordering constraints: Theorem 4 provides sufficient conditions for entailment; is the MaxSAT solution guaranteed to yield a maximal set of entailed orderings, or a maximal set under those sufficient conditions? Clarify optimality and any gaps between entailment and your encoding.
- Scalability and limits:
  - How do performance and memory scale beyond n≈20, especially with bounded parent set size restrictions (common in practice)?
  - What is the runtime breakdown among pruning, MaxSAT, and oracle calls? This would help users decide when projection pays off.
- Robustness to noisy constraints: Real background knowledge can be incorrect. Can the framework accommodate soft/penalized violations (e.g., weighted MaxSAT or soft ancestral priors), and how would that affect guarantees?

Quality
- Technical soundness appears strong: pruning criteria are correct for EC trees, edge entailment rules are justified, and the MaxSAT constraints are reasonable. More detail on the completeness/optimality of the ordering projection would strengthen the claims.
- Experiments are carefully designed and demonstrate substantial gains, though adding accuracy comparisons for ILP and more diverse networks/datasets would improve the empirical case.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written. Suggestions:
  - Define “maximum set of ancestral constraints” earlier and specify the closure procedure (rules and complexity).
  - In Section 4.3, clarify the test complexity for pruning CPDAGs without exhaustive enumeration, and when enumeration is triggered.
  - In Section 5.2, clearly separate hard vs soft clauses and state whether the solution is globally maximal for entailment.
  - Ensure table numbering, settings (with/without projection), and takeaways are unambiguous.

Originality
- The projection of ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints and their use to strengthen a decomposable-score oracle within the EC-tree framework is novel and well motivated relative to ILP/CP and prior EC-tree work.

Significance
- The approach can materially reduce computation and data requirements when domain knowledge is available. Given the prevalence of decomposable-score learners, the projection mechanism has potential impact beyond the specific EC-tree instantiation.

Pros for acceptance
- Solid theoretical framing and integration of non-decomposable constraints into exact search.
- Novel and practical projection method with strong empirical speedups.
- Clear relevance to real-world learning with prior knowledge.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited evidence of generalization of projection benefits to other learners beyond EC trees.
- Missing accuracy comparisons to ILP; limited analysis of projection/MaxSAT overhead and scalability beyond n≈20.
- Some clarity gaps around the maximality and completeness of ordering projections.

Minor organizational suggestion
- Given experiments focus on EC trees, consider shortening the BN graph section and using the space to further explain EC trees, the A* setup, heuristic behavior under constraints, and the MaxSAT encoding.
