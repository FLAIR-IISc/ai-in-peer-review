The paper presents an efficient method for incorporating ancestral constraints into the discovery of optimal Bayesian networks from data. This is achieved by translating a set of ancestral constraints into a (maximal) set of ordering constraints, which are then utilized in a score-based learning algorithm based on the EC tree introduced by [Chen et al., 2015]. The results are evaluated against a modified version of the ILP-based Gobnilp algorithm, and the proposed method is shown to outperform it significantly in the given setting. Overall, the paper is reasonably well-written and clearly explained, although the solution is conceptually straightforward. The combination of a novel problem and solution, along with the nice results, makes this paper of interest to a specialist audience.

The problem tackled in this paper is interesting and new, as optimal Bayesian network learning in combination with ancestral constraints does not have a readily available 'standard solution' (novelty=3). However, part of the reason behind this apparent deficiency is that the situation is somewhat artificial: once ancestral relations come into play, it would make more sense to use more general causal discovery algorithms that also allow for unobserved confounders. (hence impact=2) The approach taken is based on translating hard-to-use ancestral constraints, which do not fit well in decomposable-score-based algorithms, into sets of ordering constraints that are more easily expressed in terms of absence of edges, which do fit decomposable scores. The mapping is done through a MaxSAT solver, which ensures that all implied ancestral constraints are made explicit.

However, some of the rules in 257-261 seem strange or incomplete. For example, the premise in acyclicity rule 3 is symmetric ( (X < Y) and (Z < W) == (Z < W) and (X < Y)), but the conclusion is not (Y -/-> Z). Additionally, rule 4 seems to exclude ancestral constraints of the type 'X and Y do not cause each other'. Furthermore, in practice, one might expect that only a few ancestral constraints are given, and these are unlikely to significantly reduce the search space.

The resulting comparison against Gobnilp with added linear constraints looks impressive, but the latter is not developed to tackle this problem. A reasonable alternative to measure performance would be to assume that the ancestral constraints are in line with the optimal model and compare against a greedy algorithm like GES, which for oracle input should produce the same output. Although greedy algorithms in general cannot guarantee optimality, this comparison would provide at least a more meaningful indication of the efficiency achieved by the method in the paper.

Minor details:

* 30: Is there a difference between a (standard) oracle and an omniscient oracle?
* 38: Does 'empower the oracle' mean 'use in combination with the oracle'?
* 90: It would be helpful to mention here that optimizing refers to minimizing.
* 164: Theorem 1 should state more clearly that the indicated set of constraints A satisfied by CPDAG P on the EC tree is only a subset of the set of constraints A satisfied by the full DAG G (otherwise, the if-and-only-if statement does not apply).
* 225-227: Theorem 3 seems unlikely to lead to many positive edge implications given sparse (small sets of) ancestral constraints.
* 267: The notation used does not easily convey the concept of 'acyclic' (see also above).
* 310: For reasonable-sized graphs, 10%-25% ancestral constraints would already imply extensive knowledge of the target graph (so not typical in practice).
