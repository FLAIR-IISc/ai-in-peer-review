This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data when subject to background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints. The authors recognize that such constraints are non-decomposable, posing a significant challenge for mainstream score-based learning algorithms which rely on decomposable scores for efficiency. The core contribution is to adapt the recently proposed EC-tree search framework [Chen et al., 2015, 2016], which can handle non-decomposable scores, to this problem. The novelty lies in a method to "project" the non-decomposable ancestral constraints into a set of decomposable edge and topological ordering constraints. These inferred constraints are then used to "empower" the oracle (an optimal learner for decomposable scores) that provides the admissible heuristic for the A* search on the EC-tree, dramatically improving search efficiency. The authors formalize the inference of edge constraints and propose a reduction to MaxSAT for inferring a maximal set of consistent ordering constraints. The empirical evaluation demonstrates that this approach is orders of magnitude faster than a state-of-the-art integer linear programming (ILP) based method.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

*   **Significance:** The paper tackles a highly relevant problem. Incorporating expert background knowledge is critical for learning accurate models, especially with limited data. Ancestral constraints are an intuitive and powerful way to express such knowledge (e.g., causal relationships), and this work provides a practical and optimal method for integrating them into score-based learning, which has been a long-standing challenge.
*   **Originality:** The proposed technique of projecting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones to tighten the heuristic for a search-based exact algorithm is novel and elegant. This provides a compelling general strategy that could potentially be applied to other forms of non-decomposable background knowledge beyond ancestral constraints.
*   **Quality:** The technical approach is sound and well-supported. The method for pruning the EC-tree is proven to be sound (Theorem 1), and the inference rules for projecting constraints are formally presented (Theorems 2-4). The experimental methodology is rigorous, comparing against a strong ILP baseline (GOBNILP) on a variety of benchmark networks and showing a very significant performance improvement.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. It provides sufficient technical background, clearly articulates the problem, and explains the proposed solution and its components in a logical manner.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against acceptance / questions for authors):**

*   **Scalability of Constraint Projection:** A key part of the method involves solving a MaxSAT instance to infer topological ordering constraints. The paper does not analyze the computational cost of this pre-processing step. For problems with a large number of variables, could this MaxSAT solving become a bottleneck that limits the overall scalability of the approach? Some discussion on the runtime of this step relative to the main search would be beneficial.
*   **Comparison to ILP:** The paper notes that when parent set sizes are unbounded, heuristic search approaches tend to outperform ILP. However, many real-world applications involve a constraint on the maximum in-degree. It would strengthen the empirical section to include a comparison in a bounded parent set setting, where ILP methods are often very competitive.
*   **Generality of the Approach:** The work is focused exclusively on ancestral constraints. While this is an important class, a brief discussion on the potential applicability of the projection framework to other types of global or non-decomposable constraints would broaden the perceived impact and significance of the contribution.

Overall, this is a strong paper presenting a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. The proposed method is novel, technically sound, and empirically shown to be highly effective. The weaknesses are minor and can be addressed through further clarification and discussion.
