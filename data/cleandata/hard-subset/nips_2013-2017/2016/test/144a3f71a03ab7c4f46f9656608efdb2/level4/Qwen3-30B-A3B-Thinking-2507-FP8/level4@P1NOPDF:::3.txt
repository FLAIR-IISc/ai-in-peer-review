

The paper presents an efficient method for incorporating ancestral constraints into optimal Bayesian network (BN) learning from data. It translates ancestral constraints into a maximal set of ordering constraints, which are then integrated into a score-based learning algorithm leveraging the EC tree framework from Chen et al. (2015). Results demonstrate significant performance gains over a modified ILP-based Gobnilp algorithm, which was not designed for this specific problem. Overall, the paper is well-written and clearly explained, though the solution is conceptually straightforward. The problem is novel (novelty=3), as optimal BN learning with ancestral constraints lacks a standard solution. However, the problem setting is somewhat artificial: once ancestral relations are considered, more general causal discovery methods accommodating unobserved confounders would be preferable (impact=2).  

The core approach—translating ancestral constraints into ordering constraints compatible with decomposable scores via a MaxSAT solver—is sound. However, the constraint translation rules (lines 257–261) contain issues. Rule 3’s premise (e.g., (X < Y) ∧ (Z < W)) is symmetric but the conclusion (Y ↛ Z) is not, creating logical inconsistency. Rule 4 also excludes ancestral constraints like "X and Y do not cause each other," which may be necessary in practice. Furthermore, the assumption that ancestral constraints are sparse (e.g., 10–25% of possible constraints) is questionable: such constraints would likely encode extensive structural knowledge, making them atypical in real-world scenarios (line 310). Consequently, the search space reduction may be minimal, weakening the practical motivation.  

The comparison against Gobnilp with added linear constraints appears impressive but is misleading. Gobnilp was not optimized for ancestral constraints, so its poor performance does not fairly evaluate the proposed method. A more meaningful benchmark would compare against a greedy algorithm like GES, assuming ancestral constraints align with the true model (an oracle setting where GES should recover the optimal solution). While GES cannot guarantee optimality, this comparison would better isolate the efficiency gains of the proposed method.  

Minor points:  
- Line 30: Clarify the distinction between a "standard oracle" and "omniscient oracle."  
- Line 38: "Empower the oracle" likely means "leverage the oracle" or "use in combination with the oracle."  
- Line 90: Specify that optimization is minimization (not just "optimizing").  
- Line 164: Theorem 1 must explicitly state that the constraint set A derived from the CPDAG P on the EC tree is a subset of the constraints satisfied by the full DAG G; otherwise, the iff condition fails.  
- Lines 225–227: Theorem 3’s edge implication potential is likely negligible under sparse ancestral constraints.  
- Line 267: The "acyclic" rule is unclear (see above).  

In summary, the paper addresses a novel problem with a technically sound solution, but the problem’s practical relevance is limited by its artificial nature, and the experimental evaluation lacks a strong baseline. The method is of interest to BN specialists, but the impact is modest due to the narrow scope.
