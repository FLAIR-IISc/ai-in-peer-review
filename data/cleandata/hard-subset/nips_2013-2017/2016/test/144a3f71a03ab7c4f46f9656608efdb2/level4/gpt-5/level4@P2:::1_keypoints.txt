- Summary: tackles optimal BN structure learning with ancestral constraints using A* search over EC-tree/CPDAG nodes, pruning infeasible CPDAGs and projecting ancestral constraints into edge/order constraints to tighten a decomposable-score oracle via MaxSAT-selected orderings.  
- Empirical claim: reports orders-of-magnitude speedups over ILP (GOBNILP) and shows projected constraints greatly improve search efficiency and structure accuracy.

- Relation to prior work: extends shortest-path/order-graph methods to non-decomposable EC-tree search, combining non-decomposable search spaces with decomposable-score oracles; contrasts with ILP/CP encodings that can handle non-decomposable constraints but lacked systematic evaluation for ancestral constraints.

Strengths
- Practical significance: handles important causal/background ancestral knowledge while retaining optimality.  
- Technical novelty: sound EC-tree pruning under ancestral constraints and principled projection to edge and ordering constraints.  
- MaxSAT use: selects a large, cycle-free set of ordering constraints compatible with ancestral constraints.  
- Empirical results: substantial speedups vs. ILP baseline and insightful analysis of constraint-density effects on difficulty.  
- Integration: effectively leverages a decomposable-score oracle (URLEARNING) inside a non-decomposable search framework.

Weaknesses / clarifications needed
- Pruning lemma clarity: Lemma 3’s step that “Gk also contains edge Z→W” is not clearly justified and needs an explicit case analysis.  
- Entailed vs. selected orderings: misleading claim that Y<Z is inferred from “Z not ancestor of Y”; must distinguish logical entailment from MaxSAT-selected ordering constraints and state exact guarantees (soundness, maximality wrt weighting, transitivity closure).  
- MaxSAT encoding: variable sets and clause families are only sketched; need formal encoding and clearer proof/justification of Theorem 4 for reproducibility.  
- Baseline fairness: all experiments use unbounded parent sets, favoring order-graph methods; should include bounded-indegree experiments to isolate effect of ancestral constraints.  
- ILP encoding: GOBNILP baseline uses a weak MAXSAT-derived encoding; a stronger ILP with ancestor variables A(X,Y) enforcing transitivity/antisymmetry and linking to family variables would give a much tighter relaxation and fairer comparison.  
- Missing baseline: omission of the CP approach (van Beek & Hoffmann 2015), which can outperform GOBNILP and extend to ancestral constraints.

Suggestions for improvement
- Expand and detail the proof of the pruning lemma, showing how EC-tree edge generation compels orientations.  
- Explicitly state which projected constraints are logical consequences vs. chosen for heuristic strength and formalize guarantees (soundness, maximality, transitive closure).  
- Provide a formal MaxSAT encoding (variables, clause families) and a brief proof/argument for Theorem 4.  
- Add an ablation study separating pruning, edge projection, ordering projection, and combined effects.  
- Run experiments with bounded indegree, a tighter ILP with A(X,Y) variables, and include the CP baseline where possible.  
- Standardize terminology in the paper (e.g., “projected” vs. “implied”) and ensure all proofs/algorithms are fully included or summarized in the supplement.

Evaluation vs. criteria
- Quality: main ideas sound but some proofs and encodings need clarification and baselines need tightening.  
- Clarity: generally well written but key steps (pruning lemma, MaxSAT formulation, entailment vs. selection) require clearer exposition.  
- Originality: novel combination of EC-tree pruning with projected constraints and MaxSAT-selected orderings.  
- Significance: practically important with strong empirical benefits.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important, underexplored problem (optimal BN learning with ancestral constraints).  
- Solid theoretical-practical integration and large empirical speedups.  
- Clear potential for broader impact by improving use of existing oracles.

Arguments against acceptance
- Incomplete proof exposition and ambiguity around “inference” of ordering constraints.  
- Baseline comparisons may be weakened by suboptimal ILP encoding and unbounded parent sets.  
- Missing comparison to a strong CP baseline.
