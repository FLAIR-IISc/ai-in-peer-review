- Proposes using ancestral constraints to enhance admissible heuristics in exhaustive search.  
- Experimental evaluation is unclear and misses key comparisons needed to validate contributions.  
- Tables 1 and 3 are inconsistent/confusing about whether "new work'" is a variant of the proposed method or a different baseline.  
- Table 3 appears to compare EC vs GOB directly, conflicting with the paper’s narrative that the new method is integrated with both.  
- The paper should directly compare (EC + new method) against plain EC to test the proposed improvement on the strongest baseline.  
- Focusing on (GOB + new method') vs GOB is secondary because EC already outperforms GOB substantially in reported settings.  
- Absolute performance matters: even larger relative gains on GOB would not necessarily beat EC in absolute terms.  
- The paper does not quantify the magnitude of heuristic improvement (e.g., node counts, runtime), so claims about informing the heuristic lack empirical grounding.  
- Preprocessing overhead from integrating constraints is not measured; marginal accuracy gains might not justify extra cost.  
- The claim of preserved admissibility is asserted but lacks formal proof or empirical validation showing constraints do not overestimate costs.  
- The motivation conflates improving heuristic quality and improving exhaustive search efficiency, but experiments measure only accuracy.  
- Recommended fixes: reorganize experiments to compare (EC + new method) vs EC with accuracy and efficiency metrics; clarify table relationships; prove or empirically validate admissibility; report impact on search complexity and computational cost.  
- As presented, the experimental design obscures the method’s contribution and the main claim that it advances the state-of-the-art is unvalidated; results suggest at best marginal gains over a strong baseline.
