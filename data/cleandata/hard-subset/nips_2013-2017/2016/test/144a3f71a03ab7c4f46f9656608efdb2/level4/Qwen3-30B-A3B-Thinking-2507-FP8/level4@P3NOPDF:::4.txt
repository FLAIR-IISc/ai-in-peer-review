

This paper proposes a novel method to project non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and topological ordering constraints, enabling standard decomposable-score-based structure learning approaches (e.g., EC trees) to incorporate domain knowledge. The core innovation lies in Section 5, where the authors formalize this projection mechanism. The paper is well-structured, with clear theoretical exposition and comprehensive experimental validation. However, three key concerns require clarification to strengthen the contribution and implications:

First, Table 3 demonstrates that the projected constraints improve EC tree performance but show no benefit for the ILP approach. This raises a critical question: does the projection method *only* benefit EC trees, implying limited generalizability? Given the paper’s central claim—that the projection enables *any* decomposable-score method to handle ancestral constraints—the lack of ILP improvement undermines the stated generality. The authors should explicitly address this discrepancy (e.g., by analyzing why ILP’s constraint-handling mechanism is incompatible with the projected constraints) or provide additional experiments demonstrating broader applicability. Without this, the method’s scope appears restricted to EC trees, diminishing its impact.

Second, Table 2 reveals an "easy-hard-easy" pattern in EC tree performance as ancestral constraint proportion increases. This non-monotonic behavior suggests the effectiveness of constraint-induced search-space reduction is highly sensitive to constraint density. The authors should investigate *why* this occurs: Is it due to over-constraining the search space (leading to suboptimal solutions) or insufficient constraints (failing to guide the search)? Crucially, this sensitivity implies that constraint quantity must be carefully tuned for optimal results—a practical limitation not discussed. The paper should clarify whether this pattern generalizes to other datasets or structural complexities, and whether it poses a barrier to real-world deployment.

Third, Table 2 reports structural Hamming distance (SHD) *only* for EC trees, omitting direct comparison with ILP. Since ILP is a key baseline (and the method’s intended target), the absence of SHD comparisons between EC trees and ILP in Table 2 is a significant omission. The authors should add this comparison to contextualize EC tree improvements—e.g., "EC trees with constraints achieve SHD = X, outperforming unconstrained ILP (SHD = Y) by Z%." Without this, it remains unclear whether the constraint-driven EC tree gains are meaningful relative to the state-of-the-art.

Finally, Section 4.1 (on BN fundamentals) is tangential to the paper’s core contribution. The experiments exclusively evaluate EC trees with ancestral constraints, making the BN background redundant. Removing this section would streamline the narrative and allow deeper focus on: (1) the EC tree algorithm’s adaptation to the projected constraints, and (2) the A* search mechanics under ancestral constraints. The authors should instead dedicate space to clarifying *how* the projected constraints integrate with EC tree search (e.g., via pruning rules or scoring modifications), which is currently underdeveloped.

In summary, the paper presents a technically sound and novel projection technique with clear potential. However, the limited empirical validation (EC trees only), unexplained performance patterns, and missing ILP comparisons weaken the claim of broad applicability. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper’s contribution and relevance to structure learning. The writing is clear and professional, but the experimental analysis requires deeper scrutiny to support the method’s generality. With the suggested revisions, this work would make a valuable addition to the field.
