This paper addresses the constrained optimization problem of learning Bayesian networks from local scores with ancestral constraints. The proposed approach utilizes a branch-and-bound search, where the nodes in the search tree are CPDAGs, and the transition from one CPDAG to another involves selecting a parent set for a Bayesian network variable not present in the initial CPDAG. Symmetry breaking is effectively applied to reduce the search space. The search is guided by the URLearning system, which provides a heuristic, although the details of this process are not provided in the paper but can be found in an earlier publication. 

Given the ancestral constraints, some pruning of the search tree is possible. The key result underlying this pruning is presented in Lemma 3 of the supplementary material. While I believe the lemma to be true, I find the proof unclear, particularly the statement "By the EC tree edge generation rules, Gk also contains edge Z -> W," which requires further explanation.

The authors also discuss implied constraints, referred to as "projected constraints" in the paper, although the standard terminology is "implied constraints." A set of ancestral constraints can imply additional constraints on edges, which can be used for pruning. Furthermore, constraints on permissible topological orderings, termed "ordering constraints," are inferred. However, the approach to inferring these constraints is not adequately explained. The statement "we can infer Y < Z from Z not an ancestor of Y" is problematic, as it implies that the inference is always possible, which is not the case. A more accurate interpretation is that if "Z is not an ancestor of Y," then the constraint Y < Z can be added. The authors' intention is to infer as many consistent ordering constraints as possible, without entailing too many, and they employ a MAXSAT approach to achieve this.

The experimental evaluation involves varying the number of variables, data points, and ancestral constraints. The results are compared to the GOBNILP system, demonstrating that the proposed approach is significantly faster. However, as the authors note, GOBNILP performs poorly when there is no limit on parent set cardinality, even in the absence of ancestral constraints. Therefore, it is difficult to determine whether GOBNILP's poor performance in the current experiments is due to its general limitations or its specific handling of ancestral constraints. 

Moreover, the encoding of ancestral constraints, provided in the supplementary material, is based on a MAXSAT encoding from [Cussens, 2008], rather than the ILP encoding presented in Section 3.1.1 of [Cussens, 2010]. Unfortunately, the chosen MAXSAT encoding is not suitable for an ILP system. A more effective approach would be to introduce A(X,Y) binary variables, in addition to the I(Z,U) variables, and omit the E(X,Y,Z) variables. This formulation would require only transitivity constraints on the A(X,Y) variables and the following constraints linking A and I variables: A(X,Y) ≥ ∑{U:X ∈ U} I(Y,U). This encoding would provide a tighter linear relaxation and likely lead to improved performance.

In light of this, I am confident that even with a sensible ILP formulation of ancestral constraints, the proposed approach would outperform GOBNILP. However, the comparison to a poor formulation rather than a sensible one weakens the paper. It would be interesting to compare the proposed method to the approach of van Beek and Hoffmann, which often outperforms GOBNILP. Adapting their method to accommodate ancestral constraints does not seem to be a daunting task. Notably, the paper by van Beek and Hoffmann is not mentioned by the authors.
