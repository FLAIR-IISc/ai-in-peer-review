

This paper proposes a method to project non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints, enabling decomposable-score-based structure learning approaches to incorporate domain knowledge and thereby improve learning efficiency. The novelty primarily resides in Section 5, where the authors introduce their approach to project ancestral constraints into edge and topological ordering constraints—both decomposable. The paper is well-written and clearly presents both theoretical and experimental results. I have the following concerns and suggestions:  
i. Table 3 indicates that constraint projection only improves EC tree performance, not ILP. Does this imply the projection method only benefits EC trees and lacks generalizability to other learning approaches? Given the core innovation is inferring decomposable constraints from non-decomposable ancestral constraints, it would be more convincing if the authors demonstrated that incorporating these decomposable constraints enhances performance and efficiency for a learning approach that originally cannot handle non-decomposable constraints.  
ii. Table 2 exhibits an easy-hard-easy pattern in EC tree performance as the proportion of ancestral constraints increases. What explains this phenomenon? Does it suggest that the effect of reducing the EC tree search space via ancestral constraints is highly sensitive to the quantity of domain knowledge relative to the ILP approach, necessitating careful tuning of ancestral constraint counts for optimal performance?  
iii. Table 2 reports structural Hamming distance results solely for EC trees. How does this compare to the ILP approach’s performance?  
iv. A minor suggestion: While BN graphs underpin EC trees theoretically (as EC trees extend BNs), the experiments only evaluate EC trees with ancestral constraints applied. Thus, the authors should consider removing Section 4.1 and dedicating more space to clarifying EC trees and the A* algorithm within the EC tree framework.
