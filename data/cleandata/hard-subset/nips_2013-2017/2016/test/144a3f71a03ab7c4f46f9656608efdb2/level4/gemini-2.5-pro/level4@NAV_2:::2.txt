This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data when given background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints. The central challenge is that ancestral constraints are non-decomposable, which prevents their direct use in most state-of-the-art optimal learning frameworks that rely on decomposable scores. The authors build upon the recent EC tree search framework [Chen et al., 2016], which can handle non-decomposable scores by using an "oracle" for the decomposable parts of the problem. The core contribution of this work is a method to "project" the non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints. These projected constraints can then be passed to the oracle, significantly improving its heuristic estimates and pruning the search space more effectively. The authors provide an algorithm for inferring edge constraints and a MaxSAT-based formulation for inferring a maximal set of consistent ordering constraints. The proposed method is compared empirically to an Integer Linear Programming (ILP) approach, demonstrating orders-of-magnitude improvements in runtime.

### Strengths:
*   **Significance:** The paper tackles a highly relevant and practical problem. Incorporating expert knowledge, especially causal or temporal relationships expressed as ancestral constraints, is a crucial task in many scientific and industrial domains. Providing an exact and efficient method to do so is a significant contribution.
*   **Originality:** The primary technical contribution—projecting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones to "empower" an oracle within a larger search framework—is both novel and elegant. It provides a generalizable strategy for integrating complex global constraints into search algorithms that rely on decomposable sub-problem solvers.
*   **Quality:** The proposed approach is technically sound, providing a method for exact (optimal) structure learning. The theoretical justifications for inferring edge and ordering constraints (Theorems 2, 3, and 4) appear solid. The empirical evaluation is extensive, varying the number of variables, dataset size, and constraint density, and the performance gains shown over the ILP-based GOBNILP system are substantial.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly established, and the technical preliminaries and the proposed approach are explained logically.

### Weaknesses:
*   **Clarity of Experimental Setup:** The experimental evaluation, while showing strong results, could be presented more clearly. The comparison in Table 1 is between (EC tree + projection) and (GOBNILP + projection). Comparing the results in Table 1 and Table 2 allows one to infer the benefit of the projection technique for both methods. However, this setup is slightly confusing and requires careful reading to fully understand. A more direct presentation would be beneficial.
*   **Missing Baseline Comparison:** The most direct and arguably most important baseline is missing from the experiments. To isolate the benefit of the core contribution (empowering the oracle via projection), a comparison should be made between:
    1.  The full proposed method (EC tree search with pruning and with the oracle empowered by projected constraints).
    2.  An ablated version using EC tree search with pruning based on ancestral constraints, but *without* passing the projected constraints to the oracle.
    This comparison would directly quantify the performance gain from the projection technique itself, which is the paper's main novelty. While comparing Tables 1 and 2 gives a sense of this, the ablated baseline would be a much cleaner and more powerful demonstration of the contribution's impact.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
1.  The paper addresses a significant and practical problem in Bayesian network structure learning.
2.  The core technical idea of projecting non-decomposable constraints to empower a decomposable-score oracle is novel and insightful.
3.  The method is exact and demonstrates orders-of-magnitude performance improvements over a relevant state-of-the-art exact learning framework (ILP).
4.  The work demonstrates the substantial value of incorporating background knowledge, showing that a small number of constraints can have a similar impact on model accuracy as a large increase in data.

### Arguments Against Acceptance:
1.  The experimental section lacks clarity and is missing a critical baseline (the ablated version without oracle empowerment), which makes it difficult to precisely gauge the impact of the main technical contribution.

In summary, this is a strong paper with a novel and significant contribution. The primary weakness lies in the experimental evaluation, which could be made clearer and more compelling with the inclusion of a direct ablation study. However, the strengths of the paper—the importance of the problem, the novelty of the solution, and the impressive performance gains—outweigh this weakness. I recommend acceptance, with the suggestion that the authors revise the experimental section to improve its clarity and, if possible, include the suggested baseline comparison.
