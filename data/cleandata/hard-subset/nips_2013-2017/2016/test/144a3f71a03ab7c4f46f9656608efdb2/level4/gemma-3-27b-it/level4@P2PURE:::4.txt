This paper presents a method for projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints into a decomposable form, allowing them to be integrated into decomposable-score-based structure learning algorithms and potentially improving learning efficiency. The primary novelty resides in Section 5, where the authors detail their approach for projecting these constraints into edge and topological ordering constraints, which *are* decomposable. The paper is well-written and clearly explains both the theoretical contributions and experimental results. However, I have several concerns and suggestions:

i. Table 3 indicates that projecting ancestral constraints improves performance for EC trees but not for the ILP approach. This raises the question of whether the effectiveness of projecting ancestral constraints to decomposable constraints is specific to EC trees and does not generalize well to other learning approaches. Given that the core innovation is the inference of decomposable constraints from non-decomposable ancestral ones, it would be more compelling to demonstrate performance *and* efficiency improvements with a learning approach that would otherwise be unable to handle the original, non-decomposable constraints.

ii. Table 2 reveals an easy-hard-easy pattern in EC tree performance as the proportion of ancestral constraints increases. The cause of this pattern is unclear. Does this suggest the reduction of the EC tree search space via ancestral constraints is more sensitive to the amount of incorporated domain knowledge than in the ILP approach? If so, careful tuning of the number of ancestral constraints may be crucial for achieving optimal performance. 

iii. Table 2 currently reports only the structural Hamming distance for EC trees. A comparison of results with the ILP approach using the same metric would be valuable.

iv. While I understand the theoretical foundation of EC trees in Bayesian Networks (BNs), and that EC trees are an extension of BNs, the experiments focus solely on the EC tree search space with applied ancestral constraints. Therefore, I suggest removing Section 4.1 to allow for a more detailed explanation of EC trees and the A* algorithm specifically within the EC tree setting.
