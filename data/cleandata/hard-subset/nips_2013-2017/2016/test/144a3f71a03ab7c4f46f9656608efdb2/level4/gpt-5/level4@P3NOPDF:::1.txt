Summary
The paper addresses learning Bayesian networks from local scores under ancestral constraints. It presents a branch-and-bound search whose nodes are CPDAGs; branching selects a parent set for an undecided variable and updates the CPDAG accordingly. The search uses A* with heuristics from the URLearning system, and includes sensible symmetry breaking. Given ancestral constraints, the paper proposes pruning rules (with Lemma 3 in the supplement as a central result) and infers additional constraints for efficiency: (i) “projected” edge constraints implied by ancestral constraints, and (ii) “ordering constraints,” inferred via a MAXSAT procedure to add as many consistent (not necessarily entailed) topological ordering relations as possible.

Strengths
- Tackles an important and timely problem: incorporating ancestral constraints into exact BN learning.
- Uses a principled A* framework with effective heuristics and symmetry breaking.
- Exploits ancestral constraints both for pruning and to derive additional constraints that appear to substantially reduce search.
- Empirical results show large speedups over GOBNILP on the tested settings.

Clarity and correctness concerns
- Lemma 3 is pivotal, but the proof in the supplement is difficult to follow. In particular, the statement “By the EC tree edge generation rules, Gk also contains edge Z -> W” needs fuller justification. Please restate the EC tree edge-generation rules precisely and show step-by-step why Z -> W must be present in Gk under the lemma’s hypotheses.
- Terminology: “projected constraints” appears to be the standard “implied constraints.” Consider aligning terminology and clearly distinguishing:
  1) logical entailments of the ancestral constraints (sound pruning), and
  2) optional, consistent ordering constraints added to accelerate search (not entailed).
- Ordering constraints: The paper states that “we can infer Y < Z from Z not an ancestor of Y.” As written this is unsound; many DAGs satisfy “Z is not an ancestor of Y” while also allowing Z < Y. I believe the intended meaning is: given “Z is not an ancestor of Y,” one may choose to add Y < Z as an additional consistent ordering constraint to reduce the search space. Please make this explicit and clearly separate entailed from optional constraints. Also, outline the MAXSAT formulation used to select a large set of mutually consistent ordering constraints and the criterion optimized (e.g., maximizing the number or weight of added constraints).
- CPDAG branching step: The description “choose a parent set for a BN variable which is not a node in CPDAG1” is confusing. Every BN variable is a node of the CPDAG. I believe the intent is to choose a parent set for a variable whose parent set is not yet fixed by previous branching/CPDAG orientations; please clarify.

Technical comments and suggestions
- Soundness/completeness: Provide clear statements (and pointers to proofs) that:
  - All pruning rules based on ancestral constraints and implied edge constraints are sound (no optimal solution is cut off).
  - A* with the URLearning heuristic remains admissible and consistent in this constrained setting, or explain any deviations.
- Quantify pruning: Report how many nodes/edges are pruned by each component (implied edges, inferred ordering constraints, Lemma 3 pruning), to show their separate contributions.
- Interaction with symmetry breaking: Briefly explain how ancestral constraints interact with CPDAG symmetry breaking; confirm no contradictions arise.

Comparison to GOBNILP and fairness of evaluation
- The experiments impose no bound on parent set cardinality. It is well known that, without such bounds, GOBNILP can be disadvantaged relative to A* approaches due to the explosion of candidate parent sets, even without ancestral constraints. Thus, it is hard to attribute the observed gaps specifically to the handling of ancestral constraints versus parent-set explosion.
- The supplementary ancestral-constraint encoding for GOBNILP is based on the MAXSAT-style formulation of Cussens (2008), rather than a tighter ILP encoding as in Cussens (2010, Sec. 3.1.1). The chosen encoding is weak for an ILP solver and likely hurts GOBNILP disproportionately.

A stronger ILP encoding for ancestral constraints
- Introduce binary variables A(X,Y) indicating “X is an ancestor of Y,” in addition to the standard family-selection variables I(Y,U).
- Add transitivity constraints on A: if A(X,Y) and A(Y,Z) then A(X,Z). In ILP form, standard linearizations can be used.
- Link parenthood to ancestry via: A(X,Y) ≥ sum over U with X in U of I(Y,U). That is, if X is selected as a parent of Y in any chosen family, X must be an ancestor of Y.
- Omit cubic E(X,Y,Z) variables entirely. This yields a much tighter LP relaxation and typically far better ILP performance (cf. Cussens, 2010). Note that the total-order constraint present in Cussens (2010) is not needed here since ancestry is only a partial order.

I expect that even with a strong ILP encoding, the proposed A* approach will often remain faster, but the comparison would then be more compelling and informative about where the gains come from.

Additional related work
- Please compare with the constraint programming approach of van Beek and Hoffmann (CP 2015), which frequently outperforms GOBNILP and seems adaptable to ancestral constraints. Including or at least discussing this baseline would strengthen the empirical story.

Experimental details and reporting
- Provide parent-set statistics (average/maximum number per node) across settings to contextualize the difficulty for ILP-based methods.
- Report memory usage, timeouts, and anytime bounds where applicable.
- Varying parent-set caps (e.g., k in-degree bounds) would help tease apart the effects of ancestral constraints versus parent-set explosion on the baselines.

Minor comments
- Unify terminology (implied vs projected constraints).
- Ensure all symbols used in the supplement are defined where first introduced.
- Clarify the exact version/settings of URLearning used to generate heuristics.

Overall assessment
The paper presents a promising and well-motivated approach to exact BN learning under ancestral constraints, with strong empirical performance. The main weaknesses are clarity gaps in key proofs and an arguably unfair baseline comparison due to a weak ILP encoding and the absence of degree bounds. Clarifying Lemma 3, precisely distinguishing entailed versus optional constraints, and strengthening the comparative evaluation (ILP encoding and additional baselines) would significantly improve the paper. I lean positive, contingent on addressing these issues. References:
- Cussens, J. (2010). Maximum likelihood pedigree reconstruction using integer programming. WCB’10.
- van Beek, P., & Hoffmann, H.-F. (2015). Machine learning of Bayesian networks using constraint programming. CP 2015.
