This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data when given background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints (e.g., variable X must be an ancestor of Y, or X must not be an ancestor of Y). The authors correctly point out that while such constraints are practically useful, they are non-decomposable, which makes them incompatible with many efficient, state-of-the-art structure learning algorithms that rely on decomposable scores (e.g., dynamic programming or A* search over orderings).

The proposed solution builds upon a recent framework for exact structure learning with non-decomposable scores, which performs an A* search over a space of equivalence classes of DAGs (the "EC tree"). The key technical contribution of this paper is a method to "project" the given non-decomposable ancestral constraints into a set of decomposable constraints (i.e., required/forbidden edges and pairwise topological ordering constraints). These inferred decomposable constraints can then be passed to the "oracle" that computes the admissible heuristic for the A* search, thereby dramatically pruning the search space and accelerating the learning process. The authors propose sound methods for inferring edge constraints and a MaxSAT-based approach for inferring a consistent set of ordering constraints. The approach is empirically compared to an integer linear programming (ILP) baseline, demonstrating significant performance improvements.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a well-motivated and important problem. Incorporating rich background knowledge is critical for learning accurate models, especially with limited data. The core idea of inferring decomposable constraints from non-decomposable ones to "empower" an oracle within a larger search framework is both novel and elegant. It provides a principled way to bridge the gap between the expressiveness of non-decomposable constraints and the efficiency of algorithms designed for decomposable problems.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed methods are technically sound. The theorems for projecting ancestral constraints onto edge constraints are well-defined. The reduction to MaxSAT for finding a maximal set of consistent ordering constraints is a clever way to handle the subtleties of inferring global order from local non-ancestry relations. The overall algorithm correctly leverages the EC-tree search to guarantee optimality.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments show that the proposed approach is orders-of-magnitude faster than the ILP-based alternative (GOBNILP), which is a strong competitor for exact learning with complex constraints. The results clearly demonstrate that the constraint projection technique provides a substantial speedup, highlighting the practical value of the contribution. The paper also shows that incorporating even a small percentage of ancestral constraints can significantly improve model accuracy, rivaling the effect of a large increase in data size.

**Weaknesses (Arguments for Rejection / Areas for Improvement):**

1.  **Clarity of Experimental Evaluation:** The presentation of the experimental results could be improved. The primary contribution is the constraint projection method that accelerates the EC-tree search. However, there is no direct, side-by-side comparison of the EC-tree search *with* projection vs. *without* projection. This comparison can be inferred by looking at the EC-tree results in Table 1 (with projection) and Table 2 (without projection), but this is not ideal. A dedicated ablation study would make the impact of the core contribution much clearer and more forceful.

2.  **Confusing Experimental Narrative:** The draft review's confusion about the tables is understandable. It seems Table 1 compares (EC-tree + projection) vs. (ILP + projection), Table 2 compares (EC-tree) vs. (ILP), and Table 3 evaluates the scalability of the proposed method (EC-tree + projection) on its own. A clearer narrative is needed. For example, the authors could first establish the baseline performance of EC-tree vs. ILP (from Table 2), and then show how their projection method improves both, with a much more dramatic improvement for the EC-tree approach (from Table 1). This would better frame the contribution.

**Detailed Comments:**

*   The core idea presented in this paper is excellent. My main suggestion is to restructure the experimental section to better highlight the specific contribution. A clear ablation study is the most effective way to do this. A table or figure explicitly showing the runtime of the EC-tree algorithm with and without the projected constraints, on the same problem instances, would be highly compelling.

*   While the paper shows that the proposed approach is much faster than GOBNILP, the discussion could benefit from acknowledging why. The text notes that heuristic search approaches have been observed to scale better than ILP when parent set sizes are unlimited. This is a key factor, and framing the contribution as a way to make this superior search paradigm compatible with non-decomposable constraints would strengthen the paper's positioning.

*   The MaxSAT formulation is an interesting component. A brief comment on its computational overhead relative to the main search would be useful. Is it a negligible part of the pre-processing, or does it become a bottleneck for very large numbers of variables?

In summary, this is a strong paper with a novel and significant contribution. The technical approach is sound, and the empirical results are impressive. The main weakness lies in the clarity of the experimental presentation. If the authors can revise the evaluation section to include a direct ablation study showing the benefit of their projection method, it would make an already good paper even better. I recommend acceptance.
