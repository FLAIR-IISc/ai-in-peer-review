- Paper tackles optimal Bayesian network structure learning with ancestral constraints that are non-decomposable and hard for standard DP/A* methods.
- Method: search over CPDAGs with pruning and “empower” a decomposable-score oracle via (i) an edge-projection procedure and (ii) a MaxSAT-based ordering projection.
- Builds on EC-tree/CPDAG framework (Chen et al.) and uses projected constraints to guide an oracle like URLearning.
- Empirical results show consistent, often large speedups over an ILP (GOBNILP) baseline, with sensible ablations.
- Strength: addresses a relevant, underexplored setting for exact BN learning with background knowledge.
- Strength: solid integration of EC-tree search, pruning tests, and oracle guidance by projected constraints.
- Strength: clear soundness theorems (1–4) and intuitive, apparently complete edge-projection rules under closure assumptions.
- Strength: practically useful when domain knowledge is available and optimality is required.
- Weakness: MaxSAT ordering projection needs clearer justification, appears asymmetric, and its sufficiency/necessity for acyclicity is not fully convincing.
- Weakness: positive edge entailment may be rare with sparse constraints, limiting practical impact of edge projection.
- Weakness: CPDAG pruning fallback that enumerates all DAGs can be exponential; practical cost and mitigations are not discussed.
- Weakness: evaluation uses constraints sampled from ground truth; real applications may have fewer or noisy constraints, so claims about typical sizes (10–25%) may be optimistic.
- Weakness: comparisons are mainly to ILP; missing baselines (e.g., order-graph search with projected constraints, GES) to contextualize trade-offs.
- Theoretical critique: the acyclicity clause (rule 3) is symmetric in premise but not in consequences—needs explanation or symmetrization.
- Theoretical critique: clarify whether MaxSAT solution guarantees a maximal closed set of orderings without eliminating DAGs in G(A); strengthen proof of safety.
- Practical suggestion: replace or augment class(P) enumeration with SAT-based feasibility tests or orientation rules to avoid expensive enumeration.
- Experimental suggestion: include ablation where oracle runs with projected constraints without EC-tree pruning to isolate EC-tree benefits.
- Experimental suggestion: add heuristic baseline (e.g., GES) and sensitivity analysis to noisy/misspecified constraints.
- Clarity suggestions: define “omniscient oracle,” use consistent wording (e.g., “supply the oracle with projected constraints”), and state score minimization explicitly.
- Clarity suggestions: tighten Theorem 1 wording (CPDAG satisfies A iff some DAG in class(P) satisfies A) and fix notation/formatting issues and placeholder citations.
- Presentation suggestion: give a small example of MaxSAT clauses in Sec. 5.2 to illustrate inference rules and safety.
- Originality: combines known components (EC-tree, CPDAG pruning, SAT/MaxSAT) in a novel way to handle ancestral constraints for exact learning.
- Significance: relevant and impactful for domains with accurate ancestral knowledge and need for optimality, but applicability limited with latent confounding or sparse/noisy priors.
- Specific questions: how often did CPDAG enumeration trigger and what was its cost; any pruning heuristics used?
- Specific questions: can MaxSAT projection handle mutual non-ancestry constraints without inducing cycles?
- Specific requests: report sizes of projected edge/order sets vs. original constraints and study degradation under noisy constraints.
- Pros for acceptance: clear formulation, principled approach, strong empirical speedups, practical usefulness, and extension of recent exact BN learning work.
- Cons for acceptance: theoretical gaps in ordering MaxSAT guarantees, dependence on many accurate constraints, and limited baselines/ablations.
- Minor/editorial: clarify “optimize”=minimize, revisit Theorem 3’s practical utility, and clean up references/formatting.
