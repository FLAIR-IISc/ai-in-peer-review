

This paper presents a novel approach for learning Bayesian network structures under ancestral constraints, which are non-decomposable constraints expressing cause-and-effect relationships between variables. The authors build upon Chen et al.'s framework for optimal structure learning with non-decomposable scores, introducing an EC tree search space that enables efficient pruning based on ancestral constraints. The key innovation is projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints, which can be effectively utilized by existing oracles for decomposable scores. The authors demonstrate significant efficiency gains over ILP-based approaches like GOBNILP, with improvements of multiple orders of magnitude.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the literature on Bayesian network structure learning. Previous work has largely focused on decomposable scores and constraints (e.g., K2 algorithm, order graph approaches), with few methods handling non-decomposable constraints like ancestral relationships. Chen et al. (2015, 2016) introduced the BN graph and EC tree for non-decomposable scores, but this paper extends their framework to handle ancestral constraints, which are particularly important for incorporating domain knowledge. The approach relates well to recent work on constraint-based learning (e.g., Borboudakis and Tsamardinos, 2012, 2013) and ILP formulations for structure learning (e.g., Cussens, 2008, 2010; Bartlett and Cussens, 2015).

The paper's strengths include:
1. A clear and theoretically sound framework for handling ancestral constraints through constraint projection
2. Effective empirical demonstration of substantial efficiency gains over alternative approaches
3. Well-structured presentation of the EC tree search space and constraint projection methods
4. Comprehensive experimental evaluation across multiple datasets and constraint configurations

The paper is technically strong, with the EC tree providing a more efficient search space than the BN graph by merging Markov-equivalent structures. The authors successfully show how to prune the search space using ancestral constraints, with Theorem 1 providing a sound theoretical basis for this pruning. The approach to inferring decomposable constraints from ancestral constraints is particularly innovative, with the MaxSAT-based method for ordering constraints offering a practical solution to a challenging problem.

However, I have some concerns regarding the experimental comparison to GOBNILP. The authors compare their method against GOBNILP using a MAXSAT encoding for ancestral constraints, but this encoding appears suboptimal for ILP approaches. As noted in Cussens (2010), a more standard ILP formulation would introduce binary variables A(X,Y) for ancestor relationships and add constraints linking these to the parent variables. Specifically, the constraint A(X,Y) ≥ Σ_{U: X ∈ U} I(Y,U) would ensure that if X is a parent of Y, then X is an ancestor of Y. This formulation would provide a tighter linear relaxation and likely result in significantly better performance for GOBNILP. The current comparison uses a poor ILP formulation, which may unfairly disadvantage GOBNILP and overstate the advantage of the proposed method.

The paper also omits comparison to van Beek and Hoffmann's constraint programming approach for Bayesian network learning (CP 2015), which has demonstrated superior performance to GOBNILP in previous work. This omission weakens the evaluation, as it would be valuable to see how the proposed method compares to a state-of-the-art constraint programming approach that handles non-decomposable constraints.

Regarding clarity, the paper generally presents its ideas well, though some technical details require more explanation. For instance, the proof of Lemma 3 in the supplementary material is not fully clear, particularly the statement "By the EC tree edge generation rules, Gk also contains edge Z → W" which needs additional justification. The distinction between "entailed" and "inferred" constraints could also be clarified - the authors mean to say that they can choose to add consistent ordering constraints (like Y < Z when Z is not an ancestor of Y), rather than asserting that these constraints are necessarily entailed.

The paper is well-positioned to advance the field of Bayesian network structure learning by providing a practical solution to incorporating domain knowledge through ancestral constraints. The significant efficiency gains demonstrated (orders of magnitude faster than ILP approaches) make this approach particularly valuable for real-world applications where domain knowledge is available. The method's scalability to 20 variables (Table 3) is impressive and comparable to the state of the art.

In summary, this is a strong paper with significant technical contributions that address an important gap in Bayesian network structure learning. The main weaknesses are the suboptimal comparison to GOBNILP and the omission of comparison to van Beek and Hoffmann's constraint programming approach. I recommend acceptance with the following minor revisions:
1. Provide a more complete explanation of Lemma 3's proof
2. Clarify the distinction between entailed and inferred constraints
3. Compare with a properly formulated ILP approach (Cussens 2010) and van Beek and Hoffmann's constraint programming method
4. Include a more detailed explanation of the MAXSAT encoding for ordering constraints

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field, demonstrates significant practical improvements, and is well-positioned to advance the state of the art in Bayesian network structure learning with domain knowledge.
