This paper addresses the constrained optimization problem of learning Bayesian networks from local scores, incorporating ancestral constraints. The search employs a branch-and-bound algorithm where nodes in the search tree represent CPDAGs. The algorithm progresses by, in essence, selecting a parent set for a Bayesian network variable that is not already present in the current CPDAG. Effective symmetry breaking is implemented, and the A* search algorithm is utilized with the URLearning system providing a heuristic function. Details of this heuristic integration are available in a prior publication. The presence of ancestral constraints allows for pruning of the search tree. Lemma 3 (in the supplementary material) is a crucial result, and while I believe it to be correct, I found the proof difficult to follow. Specifically, the statement “By the EC tree edge generation rules, Gk also contains edge Z -> W” requires further clarification.

Furthermore, the paper deals with implied constraints—standardly termed “implied constraints,” but referred to here as “projected constraints”—derived from the initial ancestral constraints. These implied constraints, relating to permissible edges, are used to prune the search space. The approach also infers constraints on permissible topological orderings (“ordering constraints”). However, the method for doing so is not fully explained. The authors state they “can infer Y < Z from Z not an ancestor of Y,” but the use of "can" is imprecise. There exist DAGs where Z is not an ancestor of Y, yet Y < Z represents a valid ordering (e.g., a graph with vertices Z and Y and no edges). The authors are clearly aware of this nuance; their intention is that if Z is not an ancestor of Y, then the constraint Y < Z *can be chosen* to be added. Therefore, the aim is to infer as many consistent ordering constraints as possible – not solely those that are logically entailed. To avoid over-constraining the search, a MAXSAT approach is used for this inference. 

Experiments vary the number of variables, data points, and ancestral constraints. A comparison to the GOBNILP system demonstrates that the proposed method is significantly faster. The authors rightly point out that, without ancestral constraints, GOBNILP performs worse than A* search algorithms when there is no limit on parent set cardinality.  The current experiments do not impose such a limit, making it challenging to determine whether GOBNILP’s poor performance stems from its general difficulty with large parent sets, or specifically from its handling of ancestral constraints.

Additionally, the encoding of ancestral constraints (provided in the supplementary material) utilizes a MAXSAT encoding from [Cussens, 2008] rather than the ILP encoding presented in Section 3.1.1 of [Cussens, 2010]. This MAXSAT encoding appears to be a suboptimal formulation for an ILP system.  Why not instead incorporate binary variables A(X,Y) representing parent-child relationships, alongside the existing I(Z,U) variables, and eliminate the (cubic number of!) E(X,Y,Z) variables? I believe only transitivity constraints on A(X,Y) and the following constraints linking A and I are then needed: A(X,Y) ≥ Σ{U:X ∈ U} I(Y,U). This expresses that if X is a parent of Y, then X must be an ancestor of Y. This formulation would yield a tighter linear relaxation and likely provide much improved performance. This is the approach taken in Section 3.1.1 of [Cussens, 2010] for total variable ordering, but adapted here by omitting a constraint since ancestor relationships define a partial, rather than total, order. 

I am confident that even with a sensible ILP formulation of the ancestral constraints, the current system would outperform GOBNILP. However, the comparison to a suboptimal formulation weakens the paper. A more compelling evaluation would compare the current method against van Beek and Hoffmann’s approach, particularly given its frequent outperformance of GOBNILP. Adapting their method to incorporate ancestral constraints should not be overly difficult.  The paper notably fails to mention the work of van Beek and Hoffmann.

[Cussens, 2010] James Cussens. “Maximum likelihood pedigree reconstruction using integer programming”. WCB’10.

Peter van Beek and Hella-Franziska Hoffmann. “Machine learning of Bayesian networks using constraint programming.” Proceedings of CP 2015, Cork, Ireland, August, 2015.
