This paper addresses the constrained optimization problem of Bayesian network structure learning from local scores under ancestral constraints, employing a branch-and-bound search strategy. The search space consists of CPDAGs, with exploration progressing by iteratively defining parent sets for variables not yet present in the current CPDAG. Effective symmetry breaking is implemented, and the search is guided by an A* algorithm utilizing the URLearning system to provide a heuristic (details of which are found in a prior publication). The inclusion of ancestral constraints enables pruning of the search tree, with Lemma 3 (in the supplementary material) representing a key contribution. While I believe the lemma to be correct, the provided proof lacks clarity, specifically regarding the statement "By the EC tree edge generation rules, Gk also contains edge Z - > W," which requires further elaboration.

The paper also considers implied constraints – termed "projected constraints" here – derived from the initial ancestral constraints, and leverages constraints on permissible topological orderings ("ordering constraints"). However, the methodology for inferring these ordering constraints isn’t fully explained. The statement regarding inferring Y < Z from Z not being an ancestor of Y is imprecise. While technically correct, it overlooks the possibility of DAGs where Z is not an ancestor of Y yet Y < Z remains a valid ordering (e.g., a graph with only Z and Y and no edges). The authors' intent appears to be the *optional* addition of the constraint Y < Z when Z is not an ancestor of Y, aiming to infer a maximal set of consistent ordering constraints using a MAXSAT approach to avoid over-constraining the search.

The experimental evaluation varies the number of variables, data points, and ancestral constraints, demonstrating a significant speed advantage over the GOBNILP system. However, the authors acknowledge that GOBNILP's performance degrades relative to A* methods when parent set cardinality is unbounded, as it is in these experiments. Consequently, it is unclear whether the observed performance difference stems from GOBNILP's general limitations with large parent sets or its specific difficulty handling ancestral constraints. 

Furthermore, the encoding of ancestral constraints (provided in the supplementary material) utilizes a MAXSAT formulation from Cussens (2008), rather than the more efficient ILP encoding detailed in Cussens (2010). This MAXSAT encoding appears to be a suboptimal choice for an ILP system. A more effective formulation would involve incorporating binary A(X,Y) variables representing the ancestor relationship, alongside the existing I(Z,U) variables, and eliminating the cubic number of E(X,Y,Z) variables. Specifically, transitivity constraints on A(X,Y) and the following constraint linking A and I variables should be used: A(X,Y) ≥ Σ<sub>U:X∈U</sub> I(Y,U). This revised formulation would provide a tighter linear relaxation and potentially yield substantial performance improvements, mirroring the approach in Cussens (2010) but adapted for a partial, rather than total, ordering.

I anticipate that even with a refined ILP formulation of ancestral constraints, the presented system would outperform GOBNILP. However, the comparison to a less-than-optimal encoding weakens the paper. A comparison to the constraint programming approach of van Beek and Hoffmann, which often surpasses GOBNILP’s performance, would be valuable, particularly given the potential for adapting their method to incorporate ancestral constraints. The current authors do not cite this relevant work.
