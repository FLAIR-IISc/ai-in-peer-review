

This paper addresses constrained optimization for learning Bayesian networks from local scores with ancestral constraints, employing a branch-and-bound search over CPDAGs where nodes represent equivalence classes. The search progresses by selecting parent sets for variables not yet constrained in the current CPDAG, incorporating symmetry-breaking techniques. The approach leverages a heuristic from the URLearning system (details referenced in prior work) and utilizes ancestral constraints to prune the search tree, with Lemma 3 (supplementary material) serving as the key theoretical foundation. While the lemma appears correct, its proof requires clarification: the statement "By the EC tree edge generation rules, Gk also contains edge Z → W" lacks sufficient justification, particularly regarding how edge generation rules interact with the constraints.  

The paper distinguishes between "implied constraints" (standard terminology) and "projected constraints," noting that ancestral constraints induce edge-based pruning via inferred ordering constraints. A critical ambiguity arises in the claim that "we can infer Y < Z from Z not an ancestor of Y." This phrasing misrepresents the logic: the absence of a parent-ancestor relationship (Z not an ancestor of Y) does not entail Y < Z; rather, the authors can *choose* to add Y < Z as a *consistent* ordering constraint (e.g., in a graph with no edges between Y and Z). The method aims to infer *all possible consistent* ordering constraints via MAXSAT, avoiding over-constraining the search. However, the description of this inference process is insufficient, and the rationale for the MAXSAT approach over alternative constraint propagation techniques is unclear.  

Experiments demonstrate the system’s speed advantage over GOBNILP across varying problem sizes (variables, data points, constraints). While the authors correctly note that GOBNILP underperforms standard A* approaches without parent set limits, the comparison is undermined by the encoding of ancestral constraints. The paper adopts a MAXSAT formulation from Cussens (2008), which is suboptimal for ILP solvers used in GOBNILP. Specifically, the MAXSAT encoding introduces O(n³) E(X,Y,Z) variables, whereas a tighter ILP formulation (Cussens, 2010, Sec. 3.1.1) would use O(n²) variables:  
- Introduce binary variables A(X,Y) (X ancestor of Y) and I(Z,U) (Z parent of U),  
- Replace cubic constraints with transitivity on A(X,Y) and:  
  A(X,Y) ≥ ∑_{U: X ∈ U} I(Y,U).  
This formulation provides significantly stronger linear relaxations, as it directly encodes the ancestor-parent relationship without redundant variables. The current encoding’s inefficiency likely exacerbates GOBNILP’s poor performance, making it unclear whether the observed speed gap stems from ancestral constraint handling or the weak encoding. Crucially, the paper fails to compare against a *sensible* ILP formulation (e.g., Cussens 2010), weakening the experimental contribution.  

Furthermore, the work omits a relevant comparison to van Beek and Hoffmann (CP 2015), whose constraint programming approach often outperforms GOBNILP and could be adapted for ancestral constraints with minimal effort. The absence of this comparison is notable, as it represents a stronger baseline than GOBNILP for unconstrained settings.  

**Strengths:** The symmetry-breaking techniques and use of ancestral constraints for pruning are well-motivated. The branch-and-bound framework over CPDAGs is a reasonable choice for handling equivalence classes under constraints.  

**Major Concerns:**  
1. **Lemma 3’s proof lacks clarity** on edge generation rules.  
2. **Critical misstatement** regarding ordering constraint inference (confusing "can infer" with logical entailment).  
3. **Suboptimal constraint encoding** (MAXSAT vs. ILP) invalidates the GOBNILP comparison.  
4. **Omission of van Beek & Hoffmann** (2015), a state-of-the-art alternative for unconstrained learning.  

**Recommendation:** The paper presents a promising direction for constrained Bayesian network learning but requires major revisions. The theoretical foundation (Lemma 3) must be clarified, and the constraint inference logic must be rigorously corrected. Most critically, the experimental section must be re-evaluated using a standard ILP encoding for ancestral constraints (e.g., Cussens 2010) and include a comparison to van Beek & Hoffmann (2015). Without these changes, the performance claims remain questionable, as the current encoding choice artificially disadvantages GOBNILP. If the authors implement these revisions, the work could make a meaningful contribution to constrained structure learning. As written, however, the technical shortcomings significantly undermine the paper’s validity.  

**Summary:** The core idea is sound but marred by insufficient theoretical justification, ambiguous terminology, and a flawed experimental setup. Addressing these issues would substantially strengthen the paper. The current version warrants major revision.
