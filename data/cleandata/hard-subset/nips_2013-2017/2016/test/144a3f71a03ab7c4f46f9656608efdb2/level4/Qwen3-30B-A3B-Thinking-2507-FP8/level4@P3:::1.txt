

This paper addresses the important problem of learning Bayesian network structures under ancestral constraints, which specify cause-and-effect relationships between variables (e.g., X is an ancestor of Y). The authors propose an elegant approach that leverages Chen et al.'s EC tree search space, which represents Markov equivalence classes, to incorporate non-decomposable ancestral constraints. Their key insight is to infer decomposable edge and ordering constraints from the non-decomposable ancestral constraints, which can then be used to empower existing oracles for decomposable score learning.

The paper makes a clear and significant contribution to the literature on Bayesian network structure learning. The EC tree framework, introduced by Chen et al. (2016), represents a substantial improvement over previous approaches by merging Markov-equivalent nodes and canonizing the search space. The authors extend this framework to handle ancestral constraints through two main innovations: (1) a sound pruning mechanism for the EC tree based on ancestral constraints, and (2) a method to project non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints that can be used to improve search efficiency.

The paper demonstrates strong empirical results, showing their approach is orders-of-magnitude faster than ILP-based methods like GOBNILP across various network sizes and constraint percentages. The experiments are well-designed, varying the number of variables, dataset size, and constraint density, with results averaged over 50 different datasets. The paper also provides valuable insights into how ancestral constraints affect learning accuracy, showing that even a small percentage (10-25%) of constraints can have similar impact as increasing training data size from 512 to 8192.

Regarding quality, the paper is technically sound with a clear theoretical foundation. The main theoretical contribution is Theorem 1, which establishes the soundness of pruning the EC tree based on ancestral constraints. While the draft review mentioned difficulty understanding Lemma 3, the paper provides sufficient detail for experts to verify the proof. The experimental design is robust, with appropriate baselines and controlled variables. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, noting that ancestral constraints may not uniquely determine the optimal network (as reflected in the structural Hamming distance results).

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with the technical contributions logically progressing from background to methods to experiments. The connection to previous work is well-established, particularly with Chen et al.'s EC tree framework. The references to relevant work on constraint-based learning (Borboudakis and Tsamardinos) and structure learning with order constraints (Koivisto and Sood, Parviainen and Koivisto) are appropriate.

The originality of this work is strong. While ILP approaches can in principle handle ancestral constraints, the paper demonstrates that their practical implementation is inefficient compared to the authors' approach. The key novelty lies in the efficient projection of non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones, which is a clever solution to a problem that has been largely overlooked in the literature. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work by focusing on the practical efficiency of handling ancestral constraints rather than just theoretical feasibility.

The significance of this work is substantial. Bayesian network learning is a core problem in machine learning, and the ability to efficiently incorporate background knowledge (via ancestral constraints) has important practical implications. The empirical results showing that ancestral constraints can significantly improve both learning efficiency and model accuracy (with fewer data points) demonstrate tangible benefits. This work could be valuable for practitioners who have domain knowledge to guide structure learning, particularly in fields where data collection is expensive.

The main weakness I identified is the comparison with GOBNILP. The authors encode ancestral constraints using a MAXSAT approach (based on Cussens, 2008), but this is not the most efficient ILP encoding for the problem. As noted in the draft review, a more natural ILP formulation would use binary variables A(X,Y) for ancestral relationships with transitivity constraints and linking constraints to the parent variables. The paper would be strengthened by comparing against a properly implemented ILP approach that uses this more efficient encoding, as the current comparison may be biased against ILP methods. The authors should also consider comparing with van Beek and Hoffmann's constraint programming approach, which is mentioned in the draft review but not in the paper.

Another minor issue is the clarity of the MAXSAT encoding for ordering constraints. While the paper provides a good high-level description, the technical details of how the MAXSAT solver infers the ordering constraints could be clarified. The draft review's concern about the phrase "can infer Y < Z from Z not an ancestor of Y" is valid, as this could be misinterpreted as entailment rather than a choice of constraint. The paper could more clearly state that the authors are choosing to add consistent ordering constraints (not those that must hold).

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a clear contribution to the field. The technical approach is sound, the experiments are well-designed, and the results are compelling. The minor issues with the comparison to ILP methods and the clarity of the MAXSAT encoding are easily addressable and do not undermine the paper's main contribution. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to clarify the ILP comparison and the MAXSAT encoding details. The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it presents a significant, technically sound advance in a fundamental problem area.
