### Review

**Summary**

This paper addresses the important problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures subject to background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints. Since ancestral constraints are non-decomposable, they cannot be directly handled by many state-of-the-art, score-based learning algorithms that rely on decomposable scores. The main contribution of this work is a method for "projecting" these non-decomposable ancestral constraints into a set of decomposable edge and topological ordering constraints. This projection allows the authors to empower an oracle-based search algorithm (A* search on an EC tree), which uses an optimal learner for decomposable scores as its heuristic. The authors demonstrate empirically that their approach is significantly more efficient, often by orders of magnitude, than a standard Integer Linear Programming (ILP) formulation for the same problem.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The method for inferring decomposable constraints from non-decomposable ancestral constraints (Section 5) is novel and addresses a significant limitation of many efficient structure learning algorithms. This technique could potentially be a valuable contribution to the field beyond the specific search framework presented.
2.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly structured. The authors do a good job of explaining the underlying search framework (BN graphs and EC trees) and detailing the constraint projection methods.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments provide compelling evidence that the proposed approach substantially outperforms a state-of-the-art ILP solver (GOBNILP) in terms of runtime, particularly as the number of variables increases. The results also effectively highlight the practical value of incorporating background knowledge for improving model accuracy.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors**

1.  **Generalizability of Constraint Projection:** The primary innovation of the paper is the constraint projection technique. The experiments (comparing Tables 1 and 2) show that this projection dramatically improves the performance of the EC tree search. However, its benefit for the ILP approach appears mixed, with overhead for smaller problems and moderate improvements for larger ones. This raises a question about the general applicability of the proposed projection. Could the authors comment on whether this technique is primarily suited for heuristic search methods like A*, or if it could be adapted to provide more consistent benefits to other classes of algorithms, such as ILP or constraint programming solvers? Highlighting the conditions under which this projection is most effective would strengthen the paper's contribution.

2.  **Performance Pattern of EC Tree Search:** In Table 3, the runtime for the EC tree search exhibits an "easy-hard-easy" pattern as the percentage of ancestral constraints increases. This is an interesting phenomenon. Could the authors provide some intuition as to why this occurs? Does this imply that the performance of the proposed method is particularly sensitive to the density of the constraints, perhaps requiring careful tuning of the amount of background knowledge provided? This contrasts with the ILP approach, which appears to benefit more monotonically from additional constraints.

3.  **Incomplete Comparison in Table 3:** Table 3 reports the Structural Hamming Distance (SHD) for the networks learned by the proposed method, but not for the ILP baseline. To provide a complete picture of the trade-offs, it would be valuable to see a comparison of the model accuracy for both methods, especially for the larger problem instances where the proposed approach is much faster.

4.  **Suggestion for Presentation:** Section 4.1 provides a helpful introduction to BN graphs as a precursor to EC trees. However, since the experiments exclusively use the EC tree search space, the paper might be improved by condensing the discussion of BN graphs and using the space to provide a more detailed explanation of the EC tree representation and the A* search algorithm in that context. This would make the paper more self-contained and focus the reader's attention on the components most central to the empirical evaluation.
