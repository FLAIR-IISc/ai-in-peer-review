Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper tackles exact Bayesian network structure learning when background knowledge is provided as ancestral constraints (statements of the form X is/is not an ancestor of Y). Since ancestral constraints are non-decomposable, they are not directly supported by standard exact methods that rely on decomposable scores (dynamic programming on the order graph, A* with local family scores, etc.). Building on Chen et al. (2015, 2016), the authors search in the BN/EC spaces that represent full DAGs or equivalence classes and can, in principle, accommodate non-decomposable priors/constraints. Their key contributions are: (i) pruning EC-tree nodes (CPDAGs) that cannot satisfy a given set of ancestral constraints, with conditions for efficient checks and a fallback to enumerating class(P) when necessary; (ii) projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints to “empower the oracle” used inside A* search—specifically, inferring entailed edge-presence/absence constraints and a large safe set of ordering constraints using a MaxSAT formulation; and (iii) empirical comparisons against ILP (GOBNILP) showing orders-of-magnitude speedups.

This advances prior exact learning frameworks: compared to order-graph-based dynamic programming and A* (Koivisto & Sood 2004; Silander & Myllymäki 2006; Yuan & Malone 2011, 2013), the method supports non-decomposable constraints; compared to ILP/CP (Jaakkola et al. 2010; Cussens 2008; Bartlett & Cussens 2015; van Beek & Hoffmann 2015), it achieves substantial empirical gains while still providing exactness; and it complements prior work on path constraints in constraint-based and heuristic search settings (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos 2012, 2013) by providing an exact, score-based approach.

Strengths:
- Addresses a practical and important form of prior knowledge (ancestral/path constraints) in an exact learning setting.
- Sound integration of constraints into EC-tree search; Theorem 1 and the pruning rules are intuitive and appear correct.
- Clever projection of ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints to tighten the oracle’s heuristic while preserving admissibility; negative-edge entailment (Theorem 2) is simple and useful, and the sufficient conditions for positive edges (Theorem 3) are reasonable.
- MaxSAT-based inference of a large, provably safe set of ordering constraints provides an effective and flexible mechanism to boost the oracle without risking suboptimality (Theorem 4).
- Strong empirical results: consistent orders-of-magnitude speedups over ILP, clear demonstration that projection materially improves EC search, and sensible easy–hard–easy behavior as the number of constraints grows. The analysis of SHD vs. data size and constraint proportion is a nice illustration of practical value.

Weaknesses and requests for clarification:
- Experimental clarity: The comparison setup across Tables 1–3 is hard to follow. Please clearly label which configurations use projected constraints (EC+Proj vs. ILP+Proj) and which do not (EC vs. ILP), and whether Table 3 includes projections. An explicit ablation within the EC framework—EC with pruning only vs. pruning+edge projections vs. pruning+ordering projections vs. both—would quantify the contribution of the new projection techniques separately from the base EC-tree approach.
- Fairness and reporting: Include absolute times, node expansions (or A* queue sizes), and timeouts across all tables. For GOBNILP, specify the exact acyclicity and constraint encodings used, solver versions, and parameter settings. For the MaxSAT step, report instance sizes and solve times relative to total runtime.
- Completeness and admissibility: The ordering constraints are stated as sufficient (not necessary) for entailment. Please clarify that only constraints guaranteed to be entailed by the ancestral set are added, ensuring the oracle’s heuristic remains admissible. If the MaxSAT solution is only maximal under the sufficient conditions, note the potential gap to the true maximal entailed set.
- Complexity/scalability: Discuss the worst-case cost of (a) enumerating class(P) when pruning CPDAGs (class(P) can be exponential), including how often this fallback occurred in experiments; and (b) constructing/solving the MaxSAT instance as n grows (number of variables and clauses).
- Robustness to noisy or inconsistent constraints: The method appears to assume constraints are correct and consistent. How does the system behave if the provided ancestral constraints are contradictory or include errors? A brief discussion (and, ideally, a small experiment) would be valuable. If infeasibility detection is performed, please describe it.
- Notational and presentation issues: There are typographical artifacts (e.g., stray text in Section 1, incomplete citations, inconsistent notation X vs. X). Footnotes and figure references should be cleaned up. The definition of “maximum set of ancestral constraints” (closed under transitivity and other inference rules) should be made precise, and the closure algorithm’s complexity should be stated.

Qualitative evaluation:
- Quality: The approach is technically sound, with clear theorems guiding pruning and projection. Providing full proofs (or expanded proof sketches) in the supplement would strengthen confidence. The search remains exact provided the oracle receives only entailed decomposable constraints; this point should be emphasized and justified explicitly.
- Clarity: The core ideas are well-motivated, but the experimental section needs clearer organization and labeling. Tightening notation, improving the explanation of CPDAG pruning tests vs. enumeration, and summarizing the MaxSAT encoding succinctly (variables, number/type of hard/soft clauses) would improve readability.
- Originality: Projecting ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints for exact score-based learning is novel and well-aligned with the EC-tree framework. The MaxSAT formulation for safely inferring a large set of ordering constraints is a nice contribution.
- Significance: The ability to exploit practically important prior knowledge while achieving large speedups over ILP makes this work meaningful. The results suggest the approach will be useful both to researchers and practitioners, especially when domain knowledge is available.

Pros for acceptance:
- Exact method enabling non-decomposable ancestral constraints.
- Strong empirical performance and clear practical relevance.
- Conceptually clean integration of pruning and safe projection into EC-tree search.

Cons against acceptance:
- Experimental comparisons and ablations need clearer structure and reporting.
- Limited discussion of worst-case costs (CPDAG enumeration; MaxSAT sizing).
- No evaluation under noisy or inconsistent constraints.

Suggestions to improve the manuscript:
- Reorganize the experimental section to explicitly compare EC vs. EC+Projections and ILP vs. ILP+Projections, and add an ablation splitting edge vs. ordering projections.
- Report runtime breakdowns and search statistics; clarify solver configurations.
- Add a short subsection on computational complexity and on constraint consistency/noise handling.
- Clean up notation, citations, and minor typographical issues.

Overall recommendation:
This is a solid and timely contribution that extends exact BN structure learning to accommodate ancestral constraints with substantial empirical benefits. Conditional on clarifying and slightly strengthening the experimental presentation and addressing the noted clarity issues, I recommend acceptance.
