This paper addresses the problem of optimal Bayesian network configuration. The authors demonstrate how to incorporate non-decomposable constraints – and specifically ancestral constraints – into the structure learning framework of Chen et al. [2015, 2016]. Empirical evaluation of the proposed approach reveals significant improvements in performance, with orders of magnitude speedups compared to existing Integer Linear Programming (ILP) based methods. The authors formulate the problem as a MaxSAT instance with tailored constraints. While I lack deep expertise with this particular approach, the reported results – particularly the runtime comparisons – are compelling. Specifically, they demonstrate runtime reductions of several orders of magnitude relative to alternative methods.

The core idea of extending the Chen et al. framework to handle non-decomposable constraints is a valuable contribution. The ability to directly incorporate ancestral constraints is particularly noteworthy, as these constraints are often crucial for representing domain knowledge and ensuring meaningful network structures. The MaxSAT formulation appears well-motivated and allows for the efficient exploitation of existing SAT solvers. 

However, the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the approach. For example, the scalability of the MaxSAT solver to very large networks is not explicitly addressed. While the empirical results are promising for the tested network sizes, it would be helpful to understand how performance degrades as the number of variables increases. Further investigation into the impact of different constraint types on runtime could also be beneficial. 

Regarding the experimental setup, it would be useful to include details on the SAT solver used (including version and key parameters) to facilitate reproducibility. Additionally, a discussion of the sensitivity of the results to the choice of SAT solver would strengthen the analysis. While the comparison to ILP methods is convincing, including comparisons to other structure learning algorithms – perhaps those based on score-based search or greedy approaches – could provide a more comprehensive benchmark.

The presentation of the results is generally clear, but the figures could be improved. For instance, the y-axis labels in the runtime comparison plots are somewhat ambiguous. Specifying the units (e.g., seconds, minutes) would enhance clarity.

Minor points: The phrase “orders of magnitude” appears multiple times. While accurate, varying the phrasing (e.g., “significant speedups”, “substantial reductions”) could improve readability.

Overall, this is a strong paper with a clear contribution. The proposed approach is well-motivated, the empirical results are impressive, and the connection to existing work is clearly established. Addressing the limitations discussed above and refining the experimental details would further strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance.
