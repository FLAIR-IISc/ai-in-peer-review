This paper presents an efficient method for learning optimal Bayesian networks (BNs) from data while incorporating ancestral constraints. The core idea is to translate these constraints into a set of ordering constraints suitable for use with a score-based learning algorithm leveraging the EC tree framework introduced by Chen et al. (2015). Experimental results demonstrate significant performance gains over a modified ILP-based algorithm (Gobnilp) when operating under these constraints. The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains a conceptually straightforward, though novel, approach. The combination of a new problem and a viable solution, coupled with solid results, makes this work of interest, particularly to researchers specializing in BN structure learning.

The problem addressed – optimal BN learning subject to ancestral constraints – is interesting and novel, as no readily available standard solution currently exists (novelty: 3). However, the practicality of this specific setting is questionable. When ancestral knowledge is available, more general causal discovery algorithms capable of handling unobserved confounders are often more appropriate. This consideration limits the broader impact of the work (impact: 2).

The paper’s strength lies in its translation of often unwieldy ancestral constraints—which do not easily integrate into decomposable score-based algorithms—into ordering constraints that *can* be efficiently expressed as edge absence restrictions, aligning with the requirements of decomposable scoring functions. This translation is accomplished using a MaxSAT solver, which ensures all implied ancestral constraints are explicitly represented. However, several rules presented in lines 257-261 appear either incomplete or counterintuitive. For instance, the premise of acyclicity rule 3 exhibits symmetry – (X < Y and Z < W) is logically equivalent to (Z < W and X < Y) – yet the conclusion (Y -/-> Z) does not reflect this. Rule 4 also seems to unduly restrict the types of ancestral constraints that can be represented, potentially excluding cases where X and Y are mutually non-causal. Furthermore, it's reasonable to assume that, in typical applications, only a limited number of ancestral constraints would be provided. Consequently, the reduction in search space achieved by these constraints may be marginal.

The comparison against a Gobnilp variant augmented with linear constraints is compelling, but Gobnilp is not inherently designed to address this problem. A more informative evaluation would involve assuming the ancestral constraints align with the true underlying model and comparing against a greedy algorithm such as GES. While GES, like other greedy methods, cannot guarantee optimality, it would offer a more meaningful baseline for assessing the efficiency gains provided by the proposed method.

Several minor points require attention:

*   Line 30: Clarify the distinction between a "standard" oracle and an "omniscient" oracle.
*   Line 38: Rephrase "empower the oracle" to something more precise, such as "use in combination with the oracle."
*   Line 90: Explicitly state that "optimizing" refers to "minimizing" the score.
*   Line 164: Theorem 1 should explicitly acknowledge that the set of constraints *A* satisfied by the CPDAG *P* on the EC tree is a *subset* of the constraints *A* satisfied by the full DAG *G* for the “if and only if” condition to hold.
*   Lines 225-227: Theorem 3’s utility in deriving positive edge implications may be limited, particularly with sparse ancestral constraints.
*   Line 267: The justification for this rule being considered ‘acyclic’ is not immediately clear (see also comments on rules 257-261).
*   Line 310: The statement that 10%-25% ancestral constraints imply “extensive knowledge” may be too strong; in many scenarios, this level of constraint wouldn’t necessarily represent atypical prior knowledge.
