Summary
The paper tackles optimal Bayesian network structure learning under non-decomposable ancestral constraints. The core contribution is to adapt the EC tree search space (nodes are CPDAGs) so that A* search can prune CPDAGs that cannot be extended to any DAG satisfying the constraints. Transitions (edges) correspond to adding a leaf variable with a chosen parent set, with sensible symmetry breaking to avoid duplicate exploration. The heuristic for A* is provided by URLearning, i.e., an oracle for optimal structure learning with decomposable scores; details of how this oracle induces an admissible heuristic were given in prior work.

Beyond pruning, the paper proposes to “project” non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable ones to empower the oracle: (i) infer implied edge presence/absence constraints and (ii) infer topological ordering constraints. The edge constraints are derived by closure and entailment rules (Theorems 2 and 3). Ordering constraints are selected by a MaxSAT formulation that aims to include as many constraints X < Y (suggested by ancestral constraints Y not ancestor of X) as possible without excluding any DAGs consistent with the ancestral constraints (Theorem 4).

Empirically, the approach is compared to GOBNILP (ILP-based) on benchmark networks with varying numbers of variables, data sizes, and percentages of ancestral constraints. The EC-tree-based method is consistently faster—often by orders of magnitude—especially when projected constraints are supplied. The results also show an easy–hard–easy pattern as the proportion of ancestral constraints increases, and they quantify improvements in structural accuracy (Hamming distance) from adding constraints or data. The work relates to prior structure learning with decomposable scores (order graph, dynamic programming, A*), to ILP/CP formulations capable in principle of non-decomposable constraints, and to path constraints in constraint-based learning.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The pruning criterion for CPDAGs under ancestral constraints is central. Lemma 3 (supplementary material) appears to be the key result; I believe it is correct, but the proof is not clear to me. In particular, the step “By the EC tree edge generation rules, Gk also contains edge Z -> W” needs a more detailed justification. Please expand this argument or provide an illustrative example to make the implication explicit.
- Projection of constraints: The distinction between entailed versus selected constraints is important. For ordering constraints, the text says “we can infer Y < Z from Z not an ancestor of Y.” The word “can” is crucial: there exist DAGs where Z is not an ancestor of Y and yet both Y < Z and Z < Y are consistent with different topological orders (e.g., the edgeless graph on {Y, Z}). My understanding is that you intentionally choose a large set of ordering constraints that remains consistent with all DAGs satisfying the ancestral constraints—i.e., you are not claiming logical entailment but performing a maximization subject to soundness. Please state this explicitly when first introducing ordering constraints, and clarify that the MaxSAT step identifies a maximally large, jointly consistent set of ordering constraints, not the set of all entailed ones.
- Oracle empowerment: The idea of feeding projected (decomposable) edge and ordering constraints to the oracle is compelling, and the experimental results convincingly show large practical gains.

Clarity
- The high-level search procedure (EC tree, transition semantics, and how A* leverages URLearning) is clear with the cited prior work, but a brief recap of the heuristic construction would help readers who do not know those details.
- Please standardize terminology: “projected constraints” are commonly called “implied constraints.” Using both or explaining the correspondence would avoid confusion.
- The proof of Lemma 3 needs additional explanation (see above).
- For ordering constraints, clearly separate statements of logical entailment from the MaxSAT selection process and define “closed set” of ordering constraints when used.

Originality
- The combination of EC-tree search with (i) sound pruning for ancestral constraints and (ii) projection of ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints to strengthen a decomposable-score oracle is novel to my knowledge.
- The MaxSAT-based selection of ordering constraints compatible with ancestral constraints is also a novel and useful idea.

Significance
- Practically significant: The approach yields large speedups over ILP on the reported benchmarks and shows how background knowledge can substantially reduce search while improving model accuracy.
- Conceptually significant: It demonstrates a principled way to leverage non-decomposable knowledge within frameworks that rely on decomposability, via sound projection.

Experimental evaluation and comparisons
- The comparison to GOBNILP is valuable, but its interpretation is confounded by the absence of a parent-set size bound. As the authors note, without such bounds, ILP often underperforms A* even without ancestral constraints. Thus, it is hard to isolate how much of GOBNILP’s poor performance stems from ancestral constraints versus large parent-set spaces.
- More importantly, the ILP encoding of ancestral constraints used in the comparison (adapted from a MaxSAT encoding in Cussens, 2008) is not a strong ILP formulation. A more appropriate ILP model would introduce binary variables A(X,Y) for ancestor relations in addition to the family-selection variables I(Z,U), enforce transitivity on A(X,Y), and link them via constraints such as A(X,Y) >= sum_{U: X in U} I(Y,U) (if X is a parent of Y, then X is an ancestor of Y). This mirrors Section 3.1.1 of Cussens (2010) for total orders, dropping the totality constraint to capture partial ancestry. This yields a much tighter linear relaxation and should improve ILP performance. I expect your method would still outperform a strengthened ILP, but comparing against a stronger baseline would make the empirical case more compelling.
- It would also be informative to compare against the constraint programming approach of van Beek and Hoffmann (CP 2015), which often outperforms GOBNILP and seems adaptable to posting ancestral constraints. This related work is not currently cited.

Pros
- Clear problem formulation and relevant non-decomposable constraints.
- Sound pruning on EC trees under ancestral constraints; strong practical speedups.
- Effective projection to edge and ordering constraints; MaxSAT selection is well-motivated.
- Solid empirical evaluation demonstrating both efficiency and accuracy gains.

Cons
- Key proof step (Lemma 3) lacks clarity.
- Ambiguity about “inferring” ordering constraints versus selecting consistent ones.
- Comparative evaluation uses a weak ILP formulation and omits a CP baseline likely to be competitive.

Recommendation and suggestions
- Clarify proofs (particularly Lemma 3) and tighten explanations of ordering-constraint selection.
- Include, if possible, experiments with a stronger ILP encoding (A and I variables with transitivity and linking constraints) and/or a CP baseline (van Beek and Hoffmann, 2015).
- Briefly recap the heuristic-oracle construction and standardize terminology for implied/projected constraints.

Overall, this is a strong and timely contribution. With improved clarity and a fairer baseline comparison, it would make an excellent NIPS paper.
