This paper proposes a method for learning Bayesian networks optimally, subject to background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints. The authors' approach is based on a recently proposed framework for optimal structure learning with non-decomposable scores, which is general enough to accommodate ancestral constraints. The proposed framework exploits oracles for learning structures using decomposable scores, which cannot accommodate ancestral constraints since they are non-decomposable. The authors show how to empower these oracles by passing them decomposable constraints that they can handle, which are inferred from ancestral constraints that they cannot handle.

The paper is well-written and clearly illustrates the authors' work in both the theoretical and experimental sections. The main contribution of this paper lies in Section 5, where the authors introduce their proposed method for projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints onto decomposable edge and topological ordering constraints. This allows decomposable-score-based structure learning approaches to incorporate domain knowledge and improve learning efficiency.

The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their approach, comparing it to integer linear programming (ILP) based systems. The results show that their approach is orders-of-magnitude more efficient than ILP-based systems, especially when the number of variables is large. The authors also demonstrate that their approach can scale to 20 variables, which is comparable to the scalability of modern score-based approaches.

However, there are some concerns and suggestions that need to be addressed. Firstly, the results in Table 3 suggest that projecting ancestral constraints to decomposable constraints only improves the performance of EC trees, but not the ILP approach. This raises questions about the generalization ability of this method to other learning approaches. It would be more convincing if the authors could show that incorporating decomposable constraints can improve the performance and efficiency of certain learning approaches that originally cannot handle non-decomposable constraints.

Secondly, the results in Table 2 show an easy-hard-easy pattern in the EC tree performance as the proportion of ancestral constraints increases. This suggests that the effect of reducing the EC tree search space with ancestral constraints is sensitive to the amount of domain knowledge incorporated, and that the number of ancestral constraints needs to be carefully tuned to achieve ideal performance.

Thirdly, the results in Table 2 only show the structural Hamming distance from the EC trees, but it would be useful to compare this with the ILP approach.

Finally, a minor suggestion is to drop Section 4.1 and spend more time explaining EC trees and the A* algorithm in the EC tree setting, as the experiments only consider the search space of EC trees with ancestral constraints applied to it.

Overall, this paper provides a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning, and the authors' approach shows promise for improving learning efficiency in the presence of non-decomposable constraints. However, further work is needed to address the concerns and suggestions raised above.
