

This paper addresses the problem of learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints, which are non-decomposable and thus challenging for existing structure learning methods that rely on decomposable scores. The authors propose a framework that leverages the EC tree search space [Chen et al., 2016] and an oracle for decomposable scores to efficiently incorporate ancestral constraints. Their key insight is that non-decomposable ancestral constraints can be projected to decomposable edge and ordering constraints, which can then be used to empower the oracle.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the Bayesian network structure learning literature. Previous work on incorporating constraints has focused on structure-modular or order-modular constraints [Koivisto and Sood, 2004; Parviainen and Koivisto, 2013], but not on ancestral constraints. The authors' approach builds on Chen et al. [2015, 2016], which introduced the BN graph and EC tree for non-decomposable scores, but extends it to handle ancestral constraints. The paper also relates to constraint-based approaches like ILP [Cussens, 2008; Jaakkola et al., 2010] and constraint programming [van Beek and Hoffmann, 2015], but provides the first empirical evaluation of these approaches for learning with ancestral constraints.

**Strengths:**
- **Quality**: The paper presents a technically sound approach with a clear theoretical foundation. The authors prove theorems about how to project ancestral constraints to decomposable constraints, and provide a sound pruning method for the EC tree. The empirical evaluation demonstrates orders-of-magnitude improvements over ILP-based approaches, with consistent performance across different network sizes and constraint densities. The experimental design is well-considered, with results averaged over 50 different datasets and varying problem parameters.
- **Originality**: The paper addresses a significant gap in the literature by providing a method to incorporate ancestral constraints, which are non-decomposable. The projection technique for inferring decomposable constraints from non-decomposable ones is novel and well-motivated. The work builds on Chen et al. [2015, 2016] but extends it in a meaningful way to handle a practically important class of constraints.
- **Significance**: The results are highly significant as they demonstrate that incorporating even a small percentage of ancestral constraints (10-25%) can have a similar impact on model accuracy as significantly increasing the dataset size (from 512 to 8192 samples). This is particularly valuable in settings where collecting more data is expensive or difficult. The paper also shows that the approach scales to 20 variables, which is comparable to state-of-the-art score-based approaches.
- **Clarity**: The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The theoretical contributions are explained with appropriate examples, and the experimental setup is detailed enough to be reproducible. The figures illustrating the BN graph and EC tree are particularly helpful for understanding the search space.

**Weaknesses:**
- The experimental comparison in Table 1 is somewhat confusing. The paper compares (EC + new work) to (GOB + new work'), but it's not clear how "new work'" differs from the main contribution. The authors should clarify the exact differences between the projected constraints used in the EC approach versus the ILP approach.
- The paper could better explain why the EC tree approach scales better than ILP for larger networks. While the paper mentions that heuristic search approaches scale better than ILP when family sizes aren't restricted [Yuan and Malone, 2013], this claim could be better supported with specific analysis of the computational complexity.
- The paper doesn't fully explore the limitations of the approach. For example, how does the method perform when the ancestral constraints are noisy or partially incorrect? This is an important consideration for practical applications.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- The paper presents a technically sound method that solves a well-defined problem with significant practical implications.
- The empirical results demonstrate clear and consistent improvements over the state of the art.
- The approach is theoretically grounded with sound proofs and theorems.
- The paper makes a clear contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- The experimental comparison could be clearer and more directly address the core contribution.
- The paper could better explore potential limitations of the approach.
- The paper could provide more detail on how the projection of constraints works in practice.

Overall, this paper makes a strong contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. The technical approach is sound, the experimental results are compelling, and the significance of the work is clear. The minor weaknesses noted are easily addressable with clarifications in the final version. The work advances the state of the art in structure learning with constraints, which is an important area for practical applications. I recommend acceptance.
