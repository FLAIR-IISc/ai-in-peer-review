This paper addresses the constrained optimization problem of learning Bayesian networks from local scores under ancestral constraints. The search uses branch-and-bound with CPDAGs as nodes; moving from a parent CPDAG to a child roughly corresponds to selecting a parent set for a BN variable whose parents are not yet fixed in the parent CPDAG. Reasonable symmetry breaking is applied. The search is A*, with URLearning providing the heuristic (details are omitted here but appear in an earlier paper). Given ancestral constraints, additional pruning is possible. Lemma 3 in the supplementary material is the key result for this pruning. I believe the lemma is correct, but the proof is unclear. In particular, the statement “By the EC tree edge generation rules, Gk also contains edge Z -> W” requires more explanation.

The paper also discusses constraints implied by the ancestral constraints (typically called “implied constraints,” here termed “projected constraints”). A set of ancestral constraints can imply edge constraints that support pruning. The authors further infer constraints on permissible topological orderings (“ordering constraints”), but the procedure is not well explained. They state that “we can infer Y < Z from Z not an ancestor of Y.” The issue is the word “can”: there are DAGs in which Z is not an ancestor of Y while Y < Z is a consistent order—for example, the graph with vertices Y and Z and no edges. The authors surely know this; what they mean is that if “Z is not an ancestor of Y,” one may choose to add the constraint Y < Z. The idea, then, is to infer as many consistent (not necessarily entailed) ordering constraints as possible. The authors are careful not to infer too many such constraints and use a MAXSAT approach to select them.

The experiments vary the number of variables, data points, and ancestral constraints, and compare against GOBNILP. The proposed system is shown to be much faster than GOBNILP. As the authors note, without ancestral constraints, GOBNILP typically performs worse than A*-based approaches when there is no bound on parent-set size. In the current experiments there is no such bound, so it is unclear whether GOBNILP’s poor performance here is due to its usual difficulty with large parent sets or specifically to handling ancestral constraints poorly.

Moreover, the supplementary material encodes ancestral constraints using the MAXSAT formulation from [Cussens, 2008], rather than, for example, the ILP encoding in Section 3.1.1 of [Cussens, 2010]. That MAXSAT-based encoding is a weak formulation for an ILP solver. Why not introduce A(X,Y) binary variables (ancestor indicators) in addition to the I(Y,U) variables (parent-set selection for Y), and omit the cubic number of E(X,Y,Z) variables? Then all that is needed are:
- transitivity constraints on the A(X,Y) variables, and
- linking constraints between A and I:
  A(X,Y) ≥ ∑_{U: X ∈ U} I(Y,U)
In words: if X is chosen as a parent of Y, then X must be an ancestor of Y. This yields a much tighter linear relaxation and should perform substantially better. It mirrors Section 3.1.1 of [Cussens, 2010] for total variable orderings, with the total-order constraint dropped since “ancestor” induces only a partial order.

I am confident that even with a principled ILP formulation of ancestral constraints, the proposed system would still outperform GOBNILP. However, the paper is weakened by comparing against a poor formulation rather than a stronger, more appropriate one. It would also be informative to compare against the method of van Beek and Hoffmann, which often outperforms GOBNILP and could likely be adapted to handle ancestral constraints. This work is not cited by the authors.
