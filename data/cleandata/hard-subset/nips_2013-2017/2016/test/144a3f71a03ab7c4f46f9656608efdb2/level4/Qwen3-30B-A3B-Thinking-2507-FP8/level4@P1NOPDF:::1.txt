

This paper addresses constrained optimization for learning Bayesian networks from local scores under ancestral constraints, using a branch-and-bound approach where nodes in the search tree are CPDAGs. The algorithm "moves down" from one CPDAG to another by selecting parent sets for variables not yet constrained in the current CPDAG, incorporating sensible symmetry breaking. The search employs the URLearning system to provide a heuristic (details are deferred to an earlier paper). Ancestral constraints enable pruning, with Lemma 3 (supplementary material) serving as the key theoretical result. While I believe the lemma is correct, its proof requires clarification: the phrase "By the EC tree edge generation rules, Gk also contains edge Z → W" needs explicit justification.  

The paper uses "projected constraints" (standard term: "implied constraints") to denote constraints on edges derived from ancestral constraints, which can be leveraged for pruning. It also infers "ordering constraints" from these, such as "Y < Z" when Z is not an ancestor of Y. However, the phrasing "can infer Y < Z from Z not an ancestor of Y" is misleading. While it is true that Z not being an ancestor of Y does not entail Y < Z (e.g., the empty graph on {Y,Z} allows both Y < Z and Z < Y), the authors intend to say that such constraints *can be added* as consistent orderings. The approach carefully avoids over-inference by using MAXSAT to maximize consistent ordering constraints. The explanation of this inference process, however, is insufficiently detailed.  

Experiments vary the number of variables, data points, and ancestral constraints, comparing the proposed method to GOBNILP. The results show the current system is significantly faster than GOBNILP. The authors note that without ancestral constraints, GOBNILP underperforms compared to A-based approaches when parent set cardinality is unbounded. Since the experiments here also lack such a limit, it remains unclear whether GOBNILP’s poor performance stems from its general difficulty with large parent sets or specifically from handling ancestral constraints.  

Crucially, the encoding of ancestral constraints (supplementary material) uses a MAXSAT formulation from [Cussens, 2008], rather than the ILP encoding in Section 3.1.1 of [Cussens, 2010]. This choice is suboptimal: the MAXSAT encoding is poorly suited for ILP solvers. A more efficient ILP formulation would retain the A(X,Y) binary variables (indicating ancestor relationships) and I(Z,U) variables (indicating parent sets), while omitting the cubic number of E(X,Y,Z) variables. The necessary constraints would be:  
1. Transitivity on A(X,Y), and  
2. A(X,Y) ≥ ∑_{U: X∈U} I(Y,U) (if X is a parent of Y, then X is an ancestor of Y).  
This approach, adapted from [Cussens, 2010] (with a minor relaxation for partial orders), provides a tighter linear relaxation and superior performance. The paper is weakened by comparing against a poor MAXSAT encoding instead of a sensible ILP formulation. I am confident that even with an optimal ILP encoding, the proposed method would outperform GOBNILP, making the current comparison misleading.  

Furthermore, the paper omits comparison to van Beek and Hoffmann’s constraint programming approach [CP 2015], which often surpasses GOBNILP. Adapting their method to handle ancestral constraints would be straightforward, and such a comparison would strengthen the evaluation. The absence of this benchmark is notable given the paper’s focus on constraint handling.  

In summary, while the core idea of leveraging ancestral constraints for pruning is sound, the paper’s technical execution requires refinement. The unclear proof of Lemma 3, imprecise explanation of constraint inference, and suboptimal comparison to GOBNILP (due to the MAXSAT encoding choice) undermine the evaluation. Addressing these points would significantly improve the contribution.
