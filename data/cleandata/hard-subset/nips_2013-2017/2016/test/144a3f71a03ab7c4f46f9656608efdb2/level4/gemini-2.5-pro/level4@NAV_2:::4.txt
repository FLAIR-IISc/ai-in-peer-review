This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data when presented with background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints. Such constraints are non-decomposable, which makes them incompatible with many efficient, score-based learning algorithms that require decomposable scores. The authors build upon a recent framework [Chen et al., 2015, 2016] that performs A* search over a space of equivalence classes (the EC tree) and uses an oracle—an optimal learner for decomposable scores—to compute an admissible heuristic. The central contribution of this work is a method to infer decomposable edge and topological ordering constraints from the given non-decomposable ancestral constraints. These inferred constraints are then passed to the oracle, significantly improving its heuristic estimates and thereby accelerating the search. The proposed method is empirically compared against an integer linear programming (ILP) approach, showing substantial performance gains.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The core idea of "projecting" non-decomposable constraints into a decomposable form that can be leveraged by a specialized oracle is novel and powerful. It provides an effective bridge between the desire to incorporate rich, non-decomposable background knowledge and the need to use efficient, decomposable-score-based learning machinery. This significantly advances the state-of-the-art for optimal Bayesian network learning under this important class of constraints.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proposed methods for inferring edge and ordering constraints from a set of ancestral constraints (Section 5) are well-founded. The experimental evaluation is thorough, using standard benchmarks and comparing against a strong baseline (GOBNILP). The results convincingly demonstrate that the proposed approach is orders of magnitude faster than the ILP formulation.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. It effectively introduces the necessary background on structure learning and the EC tree search framework before presenting its main contributions. The motivation for the work is clear, and the experimental results are well-presented.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**
1.  The experiments show that providing the inferred decomposable constraints to the oracle for the EC tree search yields a dramatic speedup (comparing Table 1 and Table 2). However, the benefit for the ILP baseline seems less clear-cut, showing consistent improvement for n=14 but a slight overhead for n=12. Could the authors elaborate on why adding these redundant constraints might sometimes slow down the ILP solver? This result raises interesting questions about the general applicability of this projection technique for other types of solvers.
2.  In Table 3, the authors report an "easy-hard-easy" performance pattern as the percentage of ancestral constraints increases. This is an interesting observation. While the authors provide a high-level explanation, further discussion on the practical implications would be valuable. Does this suggest that for problems with a moderate number of constraints, the search could become unexpectedly challenging compared to problems with few or many constraints?
3.  Table 3 reports the structural Hamming distance (SHD) of the learned networks for the proposed method, showing that accuracy improves with more constraints and data. To provide a complete comparison, it would be very informative to also include the SHD results for the ILP baseline in this table. This would confirm that the proposed method's significant speed advantage does not come at the expense of model accuracy relative to the baseline.
4.  A minor suggestion for improving clarity: The paper first introduces the BN graph (Section 4.1) and then the EC tree (Section 4.2). Since the methodology and experiments are exclusively based on the EC tree, the authors might consider streamlining the introduction to the search space by condensing the description of the BN graph and expanding the details of the A* search on the EC tree itself.

In summary, this is a strong paper with a novel and significant contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. It presents a well-executed idea that makes optimal learning with ancestral constraints much more scalable. The weaknesses identified are minor and mostly pertain to requests for further clarification or experimental detail.
