This paper proposes a method for projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints so that decomposable-score-based structure learning approaches can incorporate such domain knowledge and thereby improve learning efficiency. The main novelty lies in Section 5, where the authors project ancestral constraints into edge and topological-ordering constraints, which are decomposable. The paper is well written and clearly presents both the theoretical development and the experiments. I have the following concerns and suggestions:

i. Table 3 shows that projecting ancestral constraints improves the performance of EC trees but not the ILP approach. Does this imply that the projection benefits EC trees only and generalizes poorly to other learning approaches? Given that the core innovation is inferring decomposable constraints from non-decomposable ancestral constraints, it would be more convincing to demonstrate that incorporating these decomposable constraints improves the performance and efficiency of a learning method that originally cannot handle non-decomposable constraints.

ii. Table 2 exhibits an easy–hard–easy pattern in EC-tree performance as the proportion of ancestral constraints increases. Why does this occur? Does it suggest that the effect of reducing the EC-tree search space via ancestral constraints is quite sensitive to the amount of domain knowledge incorporated, especially compared to the ILP approach? If so, does the number of ancestral constraints need to be carefully tuned to achieve strong performance?

iii. Table 2 reports structural Hamming distance only for EC trees. How does this compare with the ILP approach?

iv. Minor suggestion: I understand that BN graphs provide the theoretical foundation for EC trees, as EC trees extend from them. However, since the experiments consider only the EC-tree search space with ancestral constraints, I would drop Section 4.1 and instead devote more space to a clearer explanation of EC trees and the A* algorithm in the EC-tree setting.
