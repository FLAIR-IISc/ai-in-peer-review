This paper presents a method for transforming non-decomposable ancestral constraints into a decomposable form, enabling their use within decomposable-score-based structure learning algorithms and potentially enhancing learning efficiency. The primary contribution of this work resides in Section 5, where the authors detail their novel approach to projecting these constraints into equivalent edge and topological ordering constraints. The paper is well-structured and effectively communicates both the theoretical foundations and experimental results. However, I have several questions and suggestions for improvement:

i. The results in Table 3 indicate that projecting ancestral constraints improves performance for EC trees, but not for the ILP approach. This raises the question of whether the effectiveness of projecting ancestral constraints to decomposable constraints is specific to EC trees, and whether the method possesses limited generalizability to other learning algorithms. Given the core innovation is the translation of non-decomposable to decomposable constraints, strengthening the argument with results demonstrating performance improvements – or the enabling of constraint handling – in a learning approach *unable* to natively process the original non-decomposable constraints would be highly valuable.

ii. Table 2 exhibits an unusual easy-hard-easy pattern in EC tree performance as the proportion of ancestral constraints increases. What explains this observation? Does this suggest that the benefits of reducing the EC tree search space via ancestral constraints are more sensitive to the amount of incorporated domain knowledge compared to the ILP approach? If so, it implies careful tuning of the number of ancestral constraints is required to achieve optimal performance.

iii. Table 2 currently presents only the structural Hamming distance results for EC trees. A comparison of these results with the ILP approach would provide a more complete picture of performance.

iv. While I recognize the theoretical connection between Bayesian Networks and EC trees, and that EC trees can be viewed as an extension of the former, the experiments focus solely on the EC tree search space with ancestral constraints. Therefore, I recommend removing Section 4.1 to allow for a more in-depth explanation of EC trees and the A* algorithm specifically within the EC tree experimental context.
