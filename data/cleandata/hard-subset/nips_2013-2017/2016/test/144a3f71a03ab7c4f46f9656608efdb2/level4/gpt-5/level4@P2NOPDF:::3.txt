This paper proposes an efficient way to incorporate ancestral constraints into optimal Bayesian network (BN) learning. The core idea is to translate a set of ancestral constraints into a (maximal) set of ordering constraints, and then use these within a score-based search over the EC tree of Chen et al. (2015). The approach is evaluated against a simple extension of the ILP-based Gobnilp solver with additional linear constraints, and shows substantial performance gains in the tested scenarios. The paper is generally well written and easy to follow, although the method is conceptually straightforward. The problem is new and interesting, and the solution is likely to interest a specialized audience. I would characterize novelty as moderate (3/5) and likely impact as limited (2/5).

Motivation and scope. Optimal BN learning with ancestral constraints lacks a standard, off-the-shelf solution, so the problem is well posed. That said, the setting feels somewhat artificial: when ancestral relations are available, one often cares about causal structure with potential unobserved confounders, where more general causal discovery frameworks might be more appropriate. This limits the broader impact.

Method. The key technical move is to convert ancestral constraints—awkward for decomposable score-based methods—into ordering constraints that can be naturally expressed as edge-absence relations and exploited by decomposable scores. A MaxSAT solver is used to make all implied ancestral relations explicit and to produce a maximal set of consistent ordering constraints. Conceptually simple, this translation is neat and practically useful.

Correctness and rules. Some aspects of the rule set (lines 257–261) appear unclear or possibly incomplete:
- In the “acyclicity rule 3,” the premise is symmetric ((X < Y) and (Z < W) is equivalent to (Z < W) and (X < Y)), but the conclusion is not (it asserts Y -/-> Z). This asymmetry is puzzling and deserves clarification or correction.
- Rule 4 seems to rule out constraints expressing “X and Y are not ancestors of each other,” which can be meaningful in practice. If excluded by design, please justify; if not, please revise the rule or its statement.
- More broadly, the encoding at line 267 is not readily recognizable as capturing acyclicity; a clearer justification or example would help.

Empirical evaluation. The comparison against Gobnilp augmented with linear constraints makes the proposed method look strong, but Gobnilp is not designed for this task. The search space contraction from a few ancestral constraints may be limited in realistic cases, so a fairer point of reference would help:
- Consider a baseline that assumes the ancestral constraints are consistent with the true optimum and evaluates an efficient greedy algorithm such as GES. Under oracle scoring, GES should recover the same essential graph; while greedy methods do not guarantee optimality, they would provide a more meaningful sense of the practical efficiency achieved here.
- Also discuss how performance scales when only a small number of ancestral constraints is provided. In many realistic settings, 10%–25% of all possible ancestral relations would already imply substantial prior knowledge of the target graph and is not typical.

Clarity and theory. The exposition is largely clear, but a few places need tightening:
- Line 90: Explicitly state that “optimize” here means “minimize” the score.
- Theorem 1 (line 164): It should be stated clearly that the set of constraints A satisfied by the CPDAG P on the EC tree is a subset of the constraints satisfied by some consistent DAG G; otherwise, the “iff” claim is misleading.
- Theorem 3 (lines 225–227): With sparse ancestral constraints, the number of positive edge implications may be very small in practice; please qualify the conditions under which this theorem gives substantial leverage.

Minor comments and wording:
- Line 30: Clarify whether an “omniscient oracle” differs from the standard oracle model typically assumed in BN structure learning.
- Line 38: “Empower the oracle” reads oddly; consider “use in combination with the oracle” or “leverage oracle information.”
- Line 257–261: See comments on rule asymmetry and expressiveness above.
- Line 310: As noted, 10%–25% ancestral constraints likely encode extensive prior knowledge; justify why such densities are representative.

Overall assessment. The paper addresses a novel and niche but relevant problem with a conceptually simple and well-executed idea: translating ancestral constraints into ordering constraints and exploiting them via the EC tree with decomposable scores. The approach is sound and, with clarifications to the rule set and a more appropriate baseline, could provide a compelling contribution for specialists interested in constrained optimal BN learning.
