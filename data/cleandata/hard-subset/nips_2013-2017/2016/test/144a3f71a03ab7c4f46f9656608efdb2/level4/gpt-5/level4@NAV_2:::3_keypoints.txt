- Paper proposes converting ancestral constraints into ordering constraints and using an EC-tree score-based learner to learn optimal Bayesian networks efficiently.  
- Empirical results show substantial speedups compared to adapting ILP-based GOBNILP for the task.  
- Presentation is clear and the idea is straightforward; contribution is novel but not a breakthrough—likely of interest to specialists.  
- The problem formulation is somewhat stylized; in practice one might prefer broader causal discovery methods that handle latent confounding.  
- Method maps non-decomposable ancestral constraints to decomposable-ordering constraints using a MaxSAT solver to make implied ancestral relations explicit.  
- Some MaxSAT translation rules (lines 257–261) appear unusual or incomplete (e.g., acyclicity rule 3 asymmetry and omission in rule 4).  
- With realistically small sets of ancestral constraints, the search space may not shrink substantially, limiting practical gains.  
- Comparison baseline (GOBNILP with added linear constraints) is not ideal since GOBNILP is not designed for this use case.  
- A more informative baseline would be a greedy method like GES with oracle scoring, which could better contextualize efficiency gains.  
- Theorem 1 (line 164) should more explicitly state the relationship between constraints satisfied by a CPDAG and the full DAGs in its Markov equivalence class.  
- Theorem 3 (lines 225–227) is unlikely to produce many edge entailments when ancestral constraints are sparse.  
- The acyclicity enforcement formulation (line 267) is not clearly recognizable as enforcing acyclicity.  
- Minor wording/clarity issues: distinction between “standard” vs “omniscient” oracle (line 30); rephrase “Empower the oracle” (line 38); clarify “optimizing” means minimizing the score (line 90).  
- Specifying 10%–25% of ancestral relations (line 310) conveys substantial prior knowledge and may be atypical in realistic applications.
