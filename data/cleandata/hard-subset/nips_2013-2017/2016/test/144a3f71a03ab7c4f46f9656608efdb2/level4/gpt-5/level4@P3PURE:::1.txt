This paper addresses the constrained optimization problem of learning Bayesian networks from local scores under ancestral constraints. The search uses branch-and-bound where nodes in the search tree are CPDAGs, and we move from CPDAG1 to CPDAG2 by (roughly) choosing a parent set for a BN variable that is not a node in CPDAG1. The paper employs sensible symmetry breaking. The search is A, with the URLearning system providing a heuristic (details are not given here but are available in earlier work).

Given ancestral constraints, the paper shows that additional pruning of the search tree is possible. Lemma 3 (in the supplementary material) is the key result here. I believe the lemma is correct, but the proof is hard to follow. In particular, the step “By the EC tree edge generation rules, Gk also contains edge Z -> W” needs more explanation.

The paper also discusses constraints implied by the ancestral constraints (the standard term is “implied constraints,” whereas the paper uses “projected constraints”). A collection of ancestral constraints will imply edge constraints that can be exploited for pruning. The authors further infer constraints on permissible topological orderings (“ordering constraints”), but the approach is not adequately explained. The paper states that we “can infer Y < Z from Z not an ancestor of Y.” The issue is with “can.” There are DAGs in which Z is not an ancestor of Y and yet Y < Z is a consistent ordering (e.g., the graph with vertices Z and Y and no edges). The authors of course know this. What they mean is that, given “Z not an ancestor of Y,” we may choose to add the constraint Y < Z. The idea, then, is to infer as many consistent ordering constraints as possible (not only those that are logically entailed). The authors are careful not to infer too many and use a MAXSAT approach to guide this inference.

The experimental section varies the number of variables, datapoints, and ancestral constraints, and compares against GOBNILP. The proposed system is shown to be much faster than GOBNILP. As the authors note, without ancestral constraints GOBNILP typically performs worse than A approaches when there is no bound on parent-set cardinality. In the current experiments there is no such bound, so it is hard to determine whether GOBNILP’s poor performance here is due to its usual difficulty with many parent sets or specifically to poor handling of ancestral constraints.

Related to this, the encoding of ancestral constraints (as described in the supplementary material) is based on the MAXSAT encoding from Cussens (2008) rather than, for example, the ILP encoding in Section 3.1.1 of Cussens (2010). The MAXSAT-based encoding is a poor formulation for an ILP solver. Why not introduce A(X,Y) binary variables in addition to the I(Z,U) variables and omit the (cubic number of) E(X,Y,Z) variables? One then needs transitivity constraints on the A(X,Y) variables, plus constraints linking A and I:
A(X,Y) ≥ ∑_{U: X ∈ U} I(Y,U)
i.e., if X is a parent of Y, then X is an ancestor of Y. This formulation yields a much tighter linear relaxation and should perform substantially better. This is essentially the approach of Section 3.1.1 of Cussens (2010) for a total ordering, but with the total-order constraint dropped since “ancestor” induces a partial, not a total, order. I am confident that even with a sensible ILP formulation of ancestral constraints the current system will still outperform GOBNILP, but the comparison is weakened by benchmarking against a poor formulation rather than a sensible one.

It would also be informative to compare against the method of van Beek and Hoffmann (CP 2015), which often outperforms GOBNILP. I do not think it would be difficult to adapt their method to allow ancestral constraints to be posted. This related work is not cited.

References:
- James Cussens (2010). “Maximum likelihood pedigree reconstruction using integer programming.” WCB’10.
- Peter van Beek and Hella-Franziska Hoffmann (2015). “Machine learning of Bayesian networks using constraint programming.” Proceedings of CP 2015, Cork, Ireland, August 2015.
