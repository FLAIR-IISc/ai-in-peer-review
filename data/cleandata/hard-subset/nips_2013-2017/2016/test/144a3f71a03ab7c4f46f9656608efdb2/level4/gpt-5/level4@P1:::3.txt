Summary
This paper addresses optimal Bayesian network (BN) structure learning under background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints. The key challenge is that such constraints are non-decomposable and thus do not fit standard score-based methods that rely on decomposability. The authors build on the BN graph and EC tree frameworks of Chen et al. (2015, 2016), using the EC tree to search over equivalence classes while pruning nodes that cannot satisfy the ancestral constraints. To make search efficient, they “empower” a decomposable-score oracle (e.g., an order-graph-based solver) by projecting ancestral constraints onto decomposable constraints: (i) edge constraints inferred via sound rules, and (ii) topological ordering constraints inferred by solving a MaxSAT problem that seeks a maximally large, cycle-free set of orderings entailed by the ancestral constraints. They provide theoretical conditions for pruning and projection (Theorems 1–4) and empirically compare their approach to an ILP formulation implemented in GOBNILP, showing substantial speedups. They also study how constraint density and data size affect search and structural accuracy.

Relation to prior work
The paper is well-positioned relative to score-based optimal structure learning (dynamic programming, A*, order-graph methods) and ILP/CP formulations (e.g., cluster constraints), and connects to constraint-based work that has used path constraints. It extends the non-decomposable scoring/search perspective of Chen et al. by incorporating non-decomposable constraints via pruning and projection, enabling the reuse of strong decomposable oracles.

Strengths
- Clear problem statement: efficiently handling ancestral constraints during optimal BN learning.
- Sensible use of EC trees to prune infeasible equivalence classes; the pruning is sound and leverages the structure of CPDAGs.
- Practical projection strategy: inferring decomposable edge and ordering constraints to tighten the oracle’s heuristic and reduce search.
- Empirical results show consistent, often orders-of-magnitude speedups over an ILP baseline, especially when projected constraints are used.
- Writing is generally clear and the overall pipeline (constraints → projection → oracle-empowered EC-tree search) is easy to follow.

Weaknesses and questions
- Conceptual simplicity: while the solution is neat, it is relatively straightforward—prune infeasible CPDAGs and project constraints to help a decomposable oracle.
- Practical setting: the use of exact ancestral constraints may be somewhat artificial. In many real applications where causal relations are of interest, methods that can handle latent confounding (e.g., algorithms operating over MAGs/PAGs) may be more appropriate. This limits expected impact in practice.
- Limited effect under sparse constraints: in many realistic scenarios only a few ancestral constraints may be available, which may not dramatically shrink the search space or yield many projected edge/order constraints. This may reduce practical gains relative to the reported benchmarks.
- Evaluation baseline: GOBNILP is not specifically designed for this setting. A complementary baseline would be a greedy algorithm such as GES configured to respect the provided ancestral constraints (assuming they are consistent with the optimal model), to illustrate efficiency when aggressively exploiting such constraints. While GES is not optimal, such a comparison could give a more meaningful sense of the efficiency gains attributable to projection.
- Rule set for MaxSAT (lines 257–261): some rules appear strange or incomplete.
  • In the acyclicity rule (3), the premise is symmetric ((X < Y) ∧ (Z < W) is identical under swapping), but the conclusion is not (e.g., the clause includes Y 6 Z but not its symmetric counterparts consistently), which raises questions about completeness and symmetry.
  • Rule (4) seems to exclude expressing constraints of the form “X and Y are mutually non-ancestral,” i.e., neither X is an ancestor of Y nor Y of X. Clarification on how such mutual non-causation constraints are handled (or whether they are out of scope) would be helpful.
- Theorem 3 (lines 225–227): given sparse ancestral constraints, the conditions for inferring positive edges are stringent and may rarely be satisfied in practice, limiting the impact of edge-presence projections.
- Acyclicity encoding (line 267): the connection between the proposed logical constraints and DAG acyclicity is not immediately transparent; a brief justification or pointer would improve clarity.

Quality
The approach appears sound overall: pruning in the EC tree is justified, and the projection procedures are accompanied by theorems. However, the MaxSAT constraint set and Theorem 1’s statement could be clarified (see below). Empirical methodology is reasonable and demonstrates clear efficiency gains, but the choice of baselines could be broadened.

Clarity
Generally well-written and organized. The paper would benefit from:
- Explicitly stating that the EC-tree pruning test checks whether there exists at least one DAG in the CPDAG’s equivalence class satisfying the constraints; thus “P satisfies A” is existential over class(P) (see Theorem 1 comment below).
- A more intuitive explanation of the MaxSAT hard constraints and why they suffice to avoid cycles, plus a symmetry check of rule (3).

Originality
Moderate. The combination—EC-tree pruning for non-decomposable constraints plus projection to empower a decomposable oracle—is novel and useful, though conceptually straightforward.

Significance
Moderate. The method will interest researchers focused on exact BN learning and constrained structure learning. The practical impact may be limited if, in real applications, only a small number of ancestral constraints are available or if latent confounding is central.

Suggestions and minor comments
- Line 30: clarify whether “omniscient oracle” differs from a standard oracle (e.g., does it return globally optimal completions for partial DAGs?).
- Line 38: “empower the oracle” could be rephrased as “use the oracle in combination with projected decomposable constraints.”
- Line 90: explicitly note that optimizing the score here corresponds to minimizing cost.
- Line 164 (Theorem 1): state explicitly that a CPDAG P satisfies A iff there exists G ∈ class(P) that satisfies A; i.e., the check is existential over the class.
- Lines 225–227 (Theorem 3): acknowledge that with small sets of ancestral constraints, few positive edge implications may be inferred.
- Line 267: consider revising the acyclicity constraint or providing a short proof sketch to show it captures acyclicity; also ensure symmetry of premises/conclusions.
- Line 310: for moderately sized graphs, 10–25% ancestral constraints could already encode substantial knowledge of the target structure; consider discussing typical availability of such constraints in practice.

Pros
- Clear problem formulation and useful contribution to exact BN learning under non-decomposable constraints.
- Sound pruning on EC trees; principled projection to edge and ordering constraints.
- Strong empirical speedups over an ILP baseline; careful study of constraint density and data size.

Cons
- Practical setting may be limited; few constraints may yield modest gains.
- Evaluation lacks a greedy baseline configured to respect constraints (e.g., GES).
- Some MaxSAT rules/conditions lack clarity or symmetry; positive-edge inference may be rare with sparse constraints.

Recommendation
Borderline accept for a specialist audience. The contribution is technically solid and empirically convincing within its scope, though the setting is somewhat narrow. Strengthening the clarity of the MaxSAT formulation and adding a complementary baseline would improve the paper.
