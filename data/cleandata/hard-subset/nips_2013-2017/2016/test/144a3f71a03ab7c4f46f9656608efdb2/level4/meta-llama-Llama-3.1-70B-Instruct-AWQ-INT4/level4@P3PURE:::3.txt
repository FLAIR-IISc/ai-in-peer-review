The paper proposes an efficient method for incorporating ancestral constraints into the process of finding optimal Bayesian networks from data. This is achieved by translating a set of ancestral constraints into a maximal set of ordering constraints, which are then utilized in a score-based learning algorithm based on the EC tree introduced by Chen et al. (2015). The results are evaluated against a straightforward modification of the ILP-based Gobnilp algorithm, and the proposed method is shown to significantly outperform it in the given setting. Overall, the paper is reasonably well-written and clearly explained, although the solution is conceptually straightforward.

The paper tackles an interesting and novel problem, as optimal Bayesian network learning in combination with ancestral constraints does not have a readily available standard solution (novelty=3). However, part of the reason behind this apparent deficiency is that the situation is somewhat artificial, as once ancestral relations come into play, it would make more sense to employ more general causal discovery algorithms that also allow for unobserved confounders (hence impact=2).

The approach taken in the paper involves translating hard-to-use ancestral constraints, which do not fit well in decomposable-score-based algorithms, into sets of ordering constraints that are more easily expressed in terms of absence of edges, which do fit decomposable scores. This mapping is performed using a MaxSAT solver, which ensures that all implied ancestral constraints are made explicit. However, some of the rules in the paper (specifically, rules 257-261) seem strange or incomplete. For example, the premise in acyclicity rule 3 is symmetric, but the conclusion is not. Additionally, rule 4 appears to exclude ancestral constraints of the type 'X and Y do not cause each other'. Furthermore, in practice, it is likely that only a few ancestral constraints are given, and these are unlikely to significantly reduce the search space.

The comparison against Gobnilp with added linear constraints appears impressive, but Gobnilp is not designed to tackle this problem. A more meaningful alternative to measure performance would be to assume that the ancestral constraints are in line with the optimal model and compare against a greedy algorithm like GES, which should produce the same output for oracle input. While greedy algorithms cannot guarantee optimality in general, this comparison would provide a more meaningful indication of the efficiency achieved by the method in the paper.

Minor details that require clarification or correction include:

* Line 30: Is there a difference between a standard oracle and an omniscient oracle?
* Line 38: The phrase 'empower the oracle' could be rephrased to 'use in combination with the oracle' for clarity.
* Line 90: It would be beneficial to mention that optimizing refers to minimizing.
* Line 164: Theorem 1 should explicitly state that the indicated set of constraints A satisfied by CPDAG P on the EC tree is only a subset of the set of constraints A satisfied by the full DAG G, as the current statement does not accurately convey this relationship.
* Lines 225-227: Theorem 3 seems unlikely to lead to many positive edge implications given sparse ancestral constraints.
* Line 267: The notation used does not easily convey the concept of 'acyclic' (see also the comments on rules 257-261).
* Line 310: For reasonably sized graphs, 10%-25% ancestral constraints would already imply extensive knowledge of the target graph, which is not typical in practice.
