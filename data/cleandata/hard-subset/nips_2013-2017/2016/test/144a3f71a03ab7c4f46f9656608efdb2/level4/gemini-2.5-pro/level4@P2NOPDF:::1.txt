This paper addresses the problem of learning Bayesian network structures from local scores under a set of ancestral constraints. The proposed method is a branch-and-bound A* search where nodes in the search tree represent CPDAGs. The search progresses by refining a CPDAG through the selection of a parent set for a variable, incorporating sensible symmetry-breaking rules to improve efficiency. The heuristic for the A* search is derived from the URLearning system, though the details of this integration are cited from a previous publication.

The main contribution of this work lies in leveraging ancestral constraints to enable additional pruning of the search tree. The key theoretical result supporting this pruning is presented as Lemma 3 in the supplementary material. While I believe the lemma is correct, its proof is difficult to follow. Specifically, the statement, "By the EC tree edge generation rules, Gk also contains edge Z -> W," requires further justification to be clear to the reader.

The paper also describes how ancestral constraints can be used to infer additional constraints for pruning. These include "projected constraints"—a term the authors use for what is more commonly known as "implied constraints"—which are logical consequences of the initial set of ancestral constraints. Additionally, the method infers "ordering constraints" on the topological sort of the variables. The explanation for this inference process is underdeveloped. The authors state that they "can infer Y < Z from Z not an ancestor of Y." This phrasing is imprecise, as Z not being an ancestor of Y does not logically entail that Y must precede Z in all valid orderings (e.g., a graph with only nodes Y and Z and no edges). A more accurate description would be that if "Z not an ancestor of Y" is a constraint, one can *choose* to add the consistent ordering constraint Y < Z to further restrict the search space. The paper mentions using a MAXSAT approach to infer a large set of such consistent ordering constraints, but the details of this procedure are not adequately explained.

The experimental evaluation compares the proposed system against GOBNILP, a well-known integer linear programming (ILP) based system. The results demonstrate that the proposed method is significantly faster across experiments that vary the number of variables, data points, and ancestral constraints. However, the design of this comparison weakens the paper's conclusions for two primary reasons:

1.  It is known that A* based approaches often outperform GOBNILP when there is no hard limit on the parent set cardinality. The experiments in this paper are conducted without such a limit. Consequently, it is difficult to determine whether the observed performance gap is due to the novel handling of ancestral constraints or simply reflects the known architectural advantages of A* search in this specific experimental setting.

2.  More critically, the encoding of ancestral constraints used for the GOBNILP baseline appears to be suboptimal. The authors state that they use a MAXSAT encoding from [Cussens, 2008], which is not an efficient formulation for an ILP solver. A much tighter and more standard ILP formulation for ancestry, such as the one described in Section 3.1.1 of [Cussens, 2010], would likely yield far better performance for GOBNILP. For instance, a stronger formulation could introduce binary variables A(X,Y) for ancestry and link them to the parent-indicator variables I(Z,U) with transitivity constraints and the following set of constraints:
    A(X,Y) ≥ Σ_{U: X ∈ U} I(Y,U) for all X,Y.
    This states that if X is a parent of Y, then X must be an ancestor of Y. Such a formulation provides a much tighter linear relaxation and is standard practice for encoding partial orders in ILP. By comparing their method to a GOBNILP baseline handicapped by a weak encoding, the authors have not presented the strongest possible case for their system's superiority. While I suspect the proposed method would still outperform GOBNILP even with an improved formulation, the current comparison is not as compelling as it could be.

Finally, the paper omits any discussion of or comparison to the constraint programming (CP) based approach for Bayesian network learning by van Beek and Hoffmann (CP 2015). Their method is a state-of-the-art competitor that is often faster than GOBNILP, and it seems plausible that their CP framework could be extended to incorporate ancestral constraints. Acknowledging and comparing against this highly relevant work would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution and provide a more complete picture of the current landscape.

In summary, the paper presents a promising idea for accelerating Bayesian network learning with ancestral constraints. However, its contributions are undermined by a lack of clarity in key theoretical and methodological descriptions and, most importantly, by an experimental comparison against a weakly formulated baseline. Addressing these issues would substantially improve the quality and impact of the work.
