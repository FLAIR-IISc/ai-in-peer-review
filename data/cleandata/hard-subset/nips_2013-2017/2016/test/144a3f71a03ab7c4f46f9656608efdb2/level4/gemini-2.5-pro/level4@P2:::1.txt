This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data when subject to ancestral constraints. Such constraints, which specify the presence or absence of a directed path between two variables, represent a form of expert knowledge but are non-decomposable, making them incompatible with many efficient, score-based learning algorithms. The authors extend the A*-based search framework of Chen et al. (2015, 2016), which operates on a search space of Markov equivalence classes (the EC tree). The core contributions are twofold: first, a method to prune nodes in the EC tree that violate the given ancestral constraints; and second, a technique to "project" the non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints. These projected constraints are then used to empower the search heuristic (an oracle that solves the unconstrained problem), thereby significantly improving search efficiency. The proposed method is empirically evaluated against an Integer Linear Programming (ILP) baseline (GOBNILP), demonstrating orders-of-magnitude speedups.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles the important and practical problem of incorporating non-decomposable background knowledge into optimal Bayesian network structure learning. The central idea of inferring simple, decomposable constraints from complex, non-decomposable ones to tighten the heuristic of a state-of-the-art A* search algorithm is both novel and insightful.

2.  **Technical Approach:** The method is technically sound, building upon a solid foundation of recent work in exact structure learning. The proposed constraint projection techniques for both edges and orderings are clever ways to bridge the gap between what the search algorithm can handle (non-decomposable constraints) and what its heuristic oracle can handle (decomposable constraints).

3.  **Empirical Performance:** The experimental results are impressive, showing that the proposed approach is substantially faster than the GOBNILP baseline. The paper also provides a valuable demonstration of how a relatively small amount of expert knowledge can significantly improve the accuracy of the learned model, often having an impact comparable to a large increase in training data.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity of Technical Explanations:**
    *   The explanation for inferring ordering constraints is potentially misleading. The paper states that one "can infer Y < Z from Z not an ancestor of Y". This is not a logical entailment, as a DAG with no edge between Y and Z is consistent with the ancestral constraint but allows for topological orderings where Z < Y. It seems the authors' method is to find a maximal set of *consistent* ordering constraints that do not eliminate any valid DAGs, rather than inferring *entailed* constraints. This is a subtle but important distinction that should be clarified. A more precise description of the goal of the MaxSAT formulation would greatly improve the paper's clarity.
    *   The proof of Theorem 1 (Lemma 3 in the supplementary material) is difficult to follow. Specifically, the line "By the EC tree edge generation rules, Gk also contains edge Z -> W" is not immediately obvious and requires more detailed justification.

2.  **Weakness of the Experimental Baseline:** The comparison with GOBNILP, while showing dramatic speedups, may not be entirely fair and could be strengthened.
    *   It is known that A*-based search methods tend to outperform ILP-based approaches like GOBNILP in settings with no hard limit on parent set cardinality, which is the case in these experiments. It is therefore difficult to disentangle how much of the performance gain is due to the novel handling of ancestral constraints versus the known architectural advantages of A* search in this specific setting. Including experiments with a bounded in-degree might provide a more direct comparison.
    *   More importantly, the ILP formulation used to encode ancestral constraints in GOBNILP appears to be suboptimal. The formulation, based on a MAX-SAT encoding from Cussens (2008), introduces a cubic number of variables and is not ideal for an ILP solver. A much tighter and more standard ILP formulation exists (e.g., Section 3.1.1 of Cussens, 2010, "Maximum likelihood pedigree reconstruction using integer programming"), which links ancestor variables `A(X,Y)` directly to parent-indicator variables `I(Y,U)` via constraints like `A(X,Y) >= sum_{U: X in U} I(Y,U)`. Using this stronger formulation would likely result in significantly better performance for the ILP baseline and provide a more compelling comparison. Comparing against a known weak formulation slightly undermines the strength of the empirical claims.

3.  **Missing Related Work:** The paper overlooks the relevant work of van Beek and Hoffmann (2015), "Machine learning of Bayesian networks using constraint programming," which presents a constraint programming (CP) approach to this problem. The CP approach has been shown to be very competitive with, and in some cases superior to, GOBNILP. Given that CP is a natural framework for handling complex constraints, it would be a highly relevant point of comparison, and its inclusion would provide a more complete picture of the state of the art.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro-acceptance:** The paper introduces a novel and elegant method for a significant problem in structure learning. The core idea of projecting non-decomposable constraints to empower an existing search oracle is a valuable contribution. The method is shown to be highly effective empirically, yielding substantial performance improvements.
*   **Con-acceptance:** The clarity of some technical details could be improved. The experimental evaluation is weakened by the use of a suboptimal formulation for the ILP baseline, which may exaggerate the performance gap between the proposed method and existing approaches. Key related work in constraint programming is not discussed.

Overall, this is a strong paper with novel ideas and impressive results. The weaknesses, particularly regarding the experimental baseline, are addressable. I would recommend acceptance, but I strongly encourage the authors to address the points above in the final version to strengthen the paper, especially by clarifying the technical explanations and acknowledging the limitations of their comparison to GOBNILP.
