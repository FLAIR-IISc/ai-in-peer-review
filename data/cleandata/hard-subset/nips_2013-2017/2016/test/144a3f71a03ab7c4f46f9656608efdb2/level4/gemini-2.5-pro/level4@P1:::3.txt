This paper presents an efficient method for learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data when subject to ancestral constraints. The core idea is to translate a given set of non-decomposable ancestral constraints into a maximal set of decomposable ordering constraints. These ordering constraints are then used to prune the search space of a score-based learning algorithm that builds on the EC tree framework. The authors evaluate their approach against an Integer Linear Programming (ILP) baseline (Gobnilp) modified to handle these constraints, demonstrating significant performance improvements in their experimental setting.

Overall, the paper is well-written and the proposed approach is clearly explained. It tackles an interesting and novel problem, and the results are promising. The work is a solid contribution that will be of interest to the specialized community working on optimal structure learning.

**Strengths**

*   **Novelty:** The problem of finding optimal Bayesian networks under ancestral constraints is both interesting and, as the authors point out, lacks a standard, efficient solution. The paper addresses a clear gap in the literature.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and the proposed solution is conceptually straightforward and easy to follow.
*   **Results:** The empirical results are compelling, showing that the proposed method significantly outperforms the ILP-based alternative on the chosen tasks.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors**

1.  **Problem Motivation and Scope:** While novel, the problem setting feels somewhat specialized. When ancestral relationships are part of the available background knowledge, one might naturally turn to causal discovery algorithms, which are designed to reason about such relationships and can often handle more complex scenarios, such as the presence of unobserved confounders. It would be helpful if the authors could better motivate the specific scenarios where one would seek an optimal BN score under these constraints, rather than using a causal discovery framework.

2.  **Evaluation Baseline:** The performance comparison against Gobnilp is impressive. However, since Gobnilp was not specifically designed for this task, the comparison may not fully capture the efficiency gains relative to other relevant methods. A useful point of comparison would be a greedy search algorithm like GES. While greedy methods do not guarantee optimality, comparing against GES (assuming the provided constraints are consistent with the optimal model) would provide a more practical benchmark and a better indication of the proposed method's efficiency.

3.  **Practicality of Experimental Setup:** In practice, one would likely have only a small number of ancestral constraints from domain experts. The experiments report results using 10% to 25% of all possible ancestral relations as constraints. For even moderately sized graphs, this represents a very large amount of prior knowledge about the target structure, which may not be a typical use case. It would be interesting to see how the method performs with a much sparser set of constraints.

4.  **MaxSAT Formulation:** There are some points in the MaxSAT formulation for inferring ordering constraints that could be clarified or appear problematic:
    *   The acyclicity condition (Rule 3, lines 259-260) has a premise that is symmetric with respect to the pairs (X, Y) and (Z, W), but its conclusion is not. For instance, the conclusion includes `Y ⇹ Z` but not the symmetric `W ⇹ X`. Please clarify the logic behind this formulation.
    *   Rule 4 appears to infer `Y < X` only from `X ⇹ Y`. This seems to preclude specifying that two variables are non-ancestors of each other in both directions. How would a constraint like "X and Y are not causally related" be handled?
    *   It seems unlikely that Theorem 3 would be able to infer many positive edge constraints (`X -> Y`) in practice, especially with a sparse set of initial ancestral constraints. Do the authors have any empirical data on how often this rule fires?

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement**

*   The term "omniscient oracle" (line 30) is used. Is this different from a standard "oracle" in this context? Using consistent terminology would improve clarity.
*   The phrase "empower the oracle" (e.g., line 38) is a bit vague. It would be clearer to state more directly that the inferred decomposable constraints are "passed to the oracle" or "used to prune the oracle's search space."
*   In Section 2, when introducing the score (line 90), it would be helpful to explicitly state that the goal is to find the DAG with the *minimum* score, as this is not the universal convention in score-based learning literature.
*   The statement of Theorem 1 (line 164) could be made more precise. For the "if and only if" condition to hold, it seems the set of constraints A applied to the CPDAG `P` (which is over a subset of variables) must be a projection of the full set of constraints A applied to the final DAG `G`. Please clarify the relationship between these sets of constraints.
