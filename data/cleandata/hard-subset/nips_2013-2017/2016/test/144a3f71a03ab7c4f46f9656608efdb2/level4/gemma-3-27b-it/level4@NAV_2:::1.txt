This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning Bayesian networks optimally under ancestral constraints. The authors build upon a recent framework for structure learning with non-decomposable scores, leveraging oracles for decomposable scores by projecting the non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable forms. The core idea is to empower these oracles with constraints they *can* handle, derived from those they cannot. The approach utilizes the EC tree search space and incorporates A* search with a heuristic derived from the URLearning system. A key contribution is the ability to infer both edge and ordering constraints from the ancestral constraints, significantly improving search efficiency.

The paper begins by providing necessary technical background on Bayesian network structure learning and ancestral constraints, clearly outlining the limitations of existing decomposable-score-based methods. The proposed approach is well-motivated, and the connection to prior work, particularly [Chen et al., 2015, 2016], is clearly established. The authors correctly identify the intractability of directly incorporating non-decomposable constraints and their novel solution of projection is a significant contribution. The theorem establishing the soundness of pruning the EC tree based on ancestral constraints (Theorem 1) is valuable.

The strengths of this work lie in its technical soundness and its potential for significant performance gains. The experimental results demonstrate orders-of-magnitude speedups compared to the ILP-based GOBNILP system. The analysis of how introducing ancestral constraints affects learning efficiency, and the scalability observations, are insightful. The investigation into projecting constraints, and the use of a MaxSAT solver to infer ordering constraints, showcases a thoughtful approach to maximizing the benefits of the available background knowledge.

However, there are some weaknesses. The explanation surrounding Lemma 3 in the supplementary material could be improved for clarity; the reasoning behind the statement regarding edge generation rules is not immediately obvious. While the authors acknowledge the ability to infer ordering constraints, the initial explanation regarding the meaning of "can" infer is potentially misleading, requiring careful reading to understand the nuanced approach of inferring *consistent* constraints, rather than solely entailed ones.

A significant concern relates to the comparison with GOBNILP. The authors acknowledge that without ancestral constraints, GOBNILP struggles with unlimited parent set cardinality. However, the comparison is made with a suboptimal implementation of ancestral constraints within GOBNILP, using a MAXSAT encoding from [Cussens, 2008] rather than a more efficient ILP formulation as described in [Cussens, 2010]. A more direct comparison with a well-formulated ILP approach, or with the constraint programming approach of van Beek and Hoffmann [2015] (which is not cited), would strengthen the claims of performance superiority. Adapting van Beek and Hoffmann’s method to handle ancestral constraints seems a natural extension and a compelling area for future work.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of projecting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones is a novel and effective technique for tackling a challenging problem.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical foundation is strong, and the soundness of the pruning strategy is formally established.
*   **Empirical Results:** The experiments demonstrate substantial performance improvements compared to existing methods.
*   **Significance:** The work addresses a practical and important problem – incorporating background knowledge into Bayesian network structure learning – and offers a scalable solution.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Clarity:** Some explanations, particularly regarding Lemma 3 and the inference of ordering constraints, could be more clear.
*   **Completeness of Comparison:** The comparison with GOBNILP is weakened by the use of a suboptimal constraint encoding. The lack of comparison to van Beek and Hoffmann [2015] is a missed opportunity.

**Overall:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and effective approach to learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints. While some clarifications and a more comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art ILP and CP methods would further strengthen the work, the technical soundness, empirical results, and potential impact warrant acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the clarity concerns and to expand the related work discussion.
