This paper addresses the problem of optimal Bayesian network structure learning under ancestral constraints, which are non-decomposable and thus challenging for traditional methods. The authors build upon the framework introduced by Chen et al. [2015, 2016], adapting it to handle these constraints by inferring decomposable constraints that can empower existing oracles. They present a method for projecting ancestral constraints into edge and ordering constraints, and evaluate their approach empirically, demonstrating significant performance gains – orders of magnitude faster – compared to integer linear programming (ILP) based methods. They formulate constraint inference as a MaxSAT problem.

The core idea – leveraging decomposable constraints derived from non-decomposable ancestral constraints to enhance the efficiency of structure learning – is well-motivated. The problem of integrating background knowledge into Bayesian network structure learning is important, and ancestral constraints represent a valuable form of such knowledge. Previous approaches, as highlighted by the authors, often struggle with non-decomposable constraints. The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing methods and positions their work as a valuable contribution. The empirical results are compelling, showing substantial speedups over ILP methods on benchmark datasets. The formulation using MaxSAT is a sensible approach to constraint projection and further demonstrates the authors’ technical depth.

A key strength of the paper is its thoroughness in relating the work to existing literature. The authors carefully cite relevant work in Bayesian network structure learning, integer linear programming, and constraint programming. They also appropriately position their contributions relative to recent NIPS publications by Chen et al. [2015, 2016] and others. The presentation of the theoretical preliminaries is clear and concise, providing necessary background without overwhelming the reader. The empirical evaluation is well-designed, varying key parameters like network size, dataset size, and constraint percentage to provide a comprehensive assessment of the approach.

However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the MaxSAT approach to constraint projection. While the authors demonstrate its effectiveness, a discussion of potential scalability issues or scenarios where the projection might become computationally expensive would strengthen the paper. Additionally, a more intuitive explanation of *why* the projected constraints lead to such substantial performance improvements would be beneficial. Is it solely due to pruning the search space, or does it also impact the effectiveness of the oracle? Providing further insight into this mechanism would enhance the understanding of the approach. The connection to Borboudakis and Tsamardinos [2012, 2013] feels somewhat brief, especially given their work on path constraints in constraint-based learning. A more detailed comparison would be helpful.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important problem in Bayesian network structure learning.
*   Presents a novel and well-motivated approach to handling non-decomposable constraints.
*   Demonstrates significant empirical improvements over existing methods.
*   Clear and concise writing with thorough references to related work.
*   Sound theoretical foundation, with a reasonable formulation using MaxSAT.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion of the limitations of the MaxSAT approach for constraint projection.
*   Could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the mechanisms behind the performance gains.
*   Connection to Borboudakis and Tsamardinos could be elaborated.

**Overall:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. The proposed approach is well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically validated. While there are minor areas for improvement, the overall quality of the paper is high. I recommend acceptance.
