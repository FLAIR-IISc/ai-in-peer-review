This paper discusses the limited optimization problem of learning Bayesian networks from local scores with genetic constraints. The search is branch-and-bound where the nodes in the search tree are CPDAGs and we select a parent set from CPDAG1 to CPDAG2 (roughly) for a BN variable that is not a node in CPDAG1. Here, a semantic constraint is violated. The search is A where a URLearning system is used to provide a heuristic. (Details of how this happens are not given here, but are found in a previous paper.) Given genetic constraints, some pruning of the search tree is possible. Lemma 3 (complementary element) is the key result here. I believe this is true, but I cannot understand the proof. The sentence "According to the EC tree edge generation rule, there is also an edge Z -> W in Gk" needs further explanation. There are also inherent constraints ("inherent constraints" is the standard terminology, here they are called "forced constraints"). A cluster of genetic constraints will mean constraints on edges that can be used for pruning. Constraints on the allowed topological ordering are also assumed ("ordering constraints"). The method of doing this is not properly explained. We have the statement that we can "infer Z from Y < Z which is not an ancestor of Y". The problem is the word "can". Clearly there are DAGs where Z is not an ancestor of Y but Y < Z is a consistent ordering: for example, a graph with Z and Y as vertices and no edges. The authors must know this! What they meant to say is that if "Z is not an ancestor of Y", then we can add the constraint Y < Z. So the idea is to 'infer' as many consistent ordering constraints as possible (not just the ones that are involved). The authors are careful not to 'infer' too much and use a MAXSAT method for inferring. Experiments are run with different numbers of variables, datapoints, and genetic constraints. A comparison is made with the GOBNILP system where the current system is shown to be much faster than GOBNILP. As the authors note, without genetic constraints, GOBNILP performs worse than the A method when there is no limit on the cardinality of the parent set. There is no such limit in the current experiment, so it is difficult to say whether the poor performance of GOBNILP here is due to 'normal' failure to work with a much larger parent set or due to the weak handling of genetic constraints. Also, the encoding of genetic constraints (given in the complementary element) is based on a MAXSAT encoding [Cussens, 2008], not on the ILP encoding found in section 3.1.1 of [Cussens, 2010]. And this MAXSAT encoding is a weak formulation for an ILP system. Why not just have A(X,Y) binary variables as well as I(Z,U) variables, and exclude the (dense number!) E(X,Y,Z) variables? I think only the following constraints are needed: A(X,Y) \geq \sum{U:X \in U} I(Y,U), which is only a transitive constraint on the A(X,Y) variable and a connecting constraint between the A and I variables. In other words, if X is a parent of Y, then X is an ancestor of Y. This formulation provides more rigorous linear relaxation and should provide better performance. This is a method from section 3.1.1 of [Cussens, 2010] for a complete ordering over variables but a constraint is excluded because the ancestor is not a partial, complete order. I am confident that the current system will perform better than GOBNILP even with a semantic ILP formulation of genetic constraints, but this paper is weakened by comparing it with a weak formulation, not by comparing it with a semantic formulation. I think it would not be too difficult to make that method suitable for allowing genetic constraints to be posted. Van বিক and Hoffman's paper is not mentioned by the current authors.
