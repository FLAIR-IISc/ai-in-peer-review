This paper addresses the constrained optimization problem of learning Bayesian networks from local scores with ancestral constraints. The proposed approach utilizes a branch-and-bound search, where the nodes in the search tree are CPDAGs, and the transition from CPDAG1 to CPDAG2 involves selecting a parent set for a BN variable not present in CPDAG1. Symmetry breaking is effectively implemented. The search is A*, leveraging the URLearning system to provide a heuristic, although the details of this process are not provided in the paper and can be found in an earlier publication. The incorporation of ancestral constraints enables pruning of the search tree, with Lemma 3 in the supplementary material being the key result. While I believe the lemma to be true, I found the proof to be unclear, specifically the statement "By the EC tree edge generation rules, Gk also contains edge Z -> W," which requires further explanation.

The authors also discuss implied constraints, referred to as "projected constraints," which are a standard concept in this field. A set of ancestral constraints can imply additional constraints on edges, allowing for further pruning. Furthermore, constraints on permissible topological orderings, denoted as "ordering constraints," are inferred. However, the approach to inferring these constraints is not adequately explained. The statement "we can infer Y < Z from Z not an ancestor of Y" is misleading, as it implies that this inference is always possible. A more accurate representation would be that if "Z is not an ancestor of Y," then we can choose to add the constraint Y < Z. The authors aim to infer as many consistent ordering constraints as possible without over-constraining the problem, employing a MAXSAT approach for this purpose.

Experiments were conducted to evaluate the performance of the proposed method, varying the number of variables, datapoints, and ancestral constraints. A comparison with the GOBNILP system demonstrated that the proposed method is significantly faster. However, as the authors note, GOBNILP's performance is generally worse than A* approaches when there is no limit on parent set cardinality, and it is unclear whether its poor performance in these experiments is due to its inherent limitations or its inability to handle ancestral constraints effectively. Moreover, the encoding of ancestral constraints used in the experiments is based on a MAXSAT encoding from [Cussens, 2008], which is not suitable for an ILP system.

A more effective formulation would involve incorporating A(X,Y) binary variables in addition to the I(Z,U) variables, omitting the E(X,Y,Z) variables, and adding transitivity constraints on the A(X,Y) variables, as well as constraints linking A and I variables: A(X,Y) ≥ ∑{U:X ∈ U} I(Y,U). This formulation would provide a tighter linear relaxation and potentially improve performance. This approach is similar to the one presented in Section 3.1.1 of [Cussens, 2010] for a total ordering on variables, with the constraint dropped since ancestor is a partial, not total order.

I am confident that even with a sensible ILP formulation of ancestral constraints, the proposed method would outperform GOBNILP. However, the comparison to a poorly formulated ILP system weakens the paper. A comparison to the method proposed by van Beek and Hoffmann, which often outperforms GOBNILP, would be more informative. Adapting their method to accommodate ancestral constraints does not seem to be a significant challenge. The paper by van Beek and Hoffmann is not mentioned by the authors, and the reference [Cussens, 2010] is James Cussens. Maximum likelihood pedigree reconstruction using integer programming. WCB'10. Peter van Beek and Hella-Franziska Hoffmann. Machine learning of Bayesian networks using constraint programming. Proceedings of CP 2015, Cork, Ireland, August, 2015.
