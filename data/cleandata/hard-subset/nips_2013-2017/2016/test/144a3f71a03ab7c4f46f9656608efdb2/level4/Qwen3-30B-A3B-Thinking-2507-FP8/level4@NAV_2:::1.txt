

This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures under ancestral constraints, which are non-decomposable and thus challenging for standard approaches that rely on decomposable scores. The authors build upon Chen et al.'s EC tree framework, which provides a search space for non-decomposable scoring functions, and extend it to handle ancestral constraints through a novel projection method that infers decomposable edge and ordering constraints from the non-decomposable ancestral constraints. The approach leverages an oracle (such as URLEARNING) for decomposable scores, which is empowered by the inferred constraints to guide the search more efficiently. The work relates to recent NIPS contributions by Chen et al. (2015, 2016) on EC trees and non-decomposable scores, as well as prior work on ancestral constraints by Borboudakis and Tsamardinos (2012, 2013), but extends these by providing a systematic method for handling non-decomposable constraints in structure learning.

The paper demonstrates significant empirical improvements over ILP-based approaches (specifically GOBNILP), showing orders-of-magnitude speedups when ancestral constraints are present. The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation across multiple benchmark networks and varying problem sizes, showing that their approach scales well to 20 variables while maintaining accuracy. The technical contribution is sound, with a clear theoretical foundation for pruning the EC tree based on ancestral constraints, and a practical method for inferring decomposable constraints that can be used to improve the oracle's heuristic.

Strengths:
- High technical quality: The paper presents a theoretically sound approach for handling non-decomposable ancestral constraints, with a clear proof of the pruning method's correctness (Theorem 1).
- Significant empirical results: The approach consistently outperforms ILP-based methods by orders of magnitude, with improvements increasing as problem size grows.
- Practical significance: The work demonstrates how background knowledge (ancestral constraints) can substantially improve learning efficiency, with 10-25% constraints having an impact comparable to increasing training data size by 4-16x.
- Clear presentation of the EC tree framework and its adaptation to handle ancestral constraints.

Weaknesses:
- The proof of Lemma 3 in the supplementary material requires more explanation; specifically, the statement "By the EC tree edge generation rules, Gk also contains edge Z → W" needs further clarification to establish the logical connection.
- The discussion of inferred ordering constraints could be more precise; the phrase "we can infer Y < Z from Z not an ancestor of Y" is potentially misleading, as there exist DAGs where Z is not an ancestor of Y but Y < Z is inconsistent with some topological orderings. The authors correctly use MAXSAT to infer only consistent constraints, but this nuance should be more clearly articulated.
- The comparison with ILP-based methods uses a suboptimal encoding for ancestral constraints (based on [Cussens, 2008] rather than the more efficient formulation in [Cussens, 2010]), which may understate ILP's potential performance.

Quality: The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The theoretical framework is complete and the experimental evaluation is thorough. The authors carefully address the strengths and weaknesses of their approach.

Clarity: The paper is well-written and well-organized, with clear explanations of the EC tree framework and the constraint projection method. Some technical details (particularly in the proof of Lemma 3) could benefit from additional clarification.

Originality: The paper makes a significant contribution by providing a systematic method to incorporate non-decomposable ancestral constraints into Bayesian network structure learning, building on the EC tree framework. This differs substantially from previous work that only supported decomposable constraints (e.g., edge presence/absence constraints).

Significance: The results demonstrate substantial practical improvements in learning efficiency with background knowledge, which is important for real-world applications where causal relationships are known. The approach advances the state of the art in structure learning with non-decomposable constraints, with clear implications for practitioners who have access to ancestral knowledge.

Pro acceptance: The paper presents a novel, theoretically sound approach to a significant problem in Bayesian network structure learning. The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate clear practical advantages. The work extends recent NIPS contributions in a meaningful way.

Con acceptance: The paper would be strengthened by providing a clearer explanation of the proof for Lemma 3 and by addressing the potential confusion around inferred ordering constraints. The ILP comparison would be more convincing with a more efficient encoding of ancestral constraints.
