This paper addresses the problem of learning Bayesian networks optimally under ancestral constraints, a challenging task due to the non-decomposability of these constraints. The authors build upon their previous work introducing a framework for optimal structure learning with non-decomposable scores, and demonstrate how to incorporate ancestral constraints by projecting them into decomposable constraints usable by an existing oracle. The core idea—empowering a decomposable-score learning oracle with constraints inferred from non-decomposable ones—is a clever and potentially impactful contribution.

The paper is well-structured and begins with a clear introduction to the problem and its challenges, relating it to existing work in Bayesian network structure learning (e.g., Darwiche, 2009; Koller & Friedman, 2009; Murphy, 2012). It correctly identifies the limitations of traditional approaches that rely on decomposable scores and highlights the potential benefits of incorporating background knowledge. The authors effectively position their work within the broader context of structure learning, referencing relevant techniques like integer linear programming (Jaakkola et al., 2010; Bartlett & Cussens, 2015; van Beek & Hoffmann, 2015) and constraint programming. The technical preliminaries section provides necessary background on score-based learning and the K2 algorithm (Cooper & Herskovits, 1992). The introduction of BN graphs and EC trees (Chen et al., 2015, 2016) is clear and concise, setting the stage for the proposed approach. The theoretical justification for constraint projection (Theorems 1-4) is a valuable addition, and the empirical evaluation appears thorough.

A major strength of this paper is its empirical evaluation. The experimental setup, involving simulations across several benchmark networks with varying dataset sizes and constraint levels, allows for a robust assessment of the proposed approach. The consistent orders-of-magnitude speed improvements over GOBNILP (Bartlett & Cussens, 2015) are significant and demonstrate the practical benefits of the constraint projection technique. The analysis of the impact of constraint projection on both EC tree search and ILP performance provides valuable insights. Furthermore, the exploration of the easy-hard-easy trend with varying constraint percentages and the investigation of structural Hamming distance offer a comprehensive understanding of the algorithm’s behavior. The connection drawn between ancestral constraints, data size, and model accuracy is insightful.

However, the presentation of experimental results could be clarified. The reviewer's point about the comparison setup in Tables 1 and 3 is valid and creates some confusion. Specifically, it is not entirely clear what “new work’” refers to within the GOBNILP comparisons. Explicitly stating what modifications were applied to GOBNILP alongside the proposed approach (EC tree with constraint projection) would improve readability. A direct comparison between the EC tree with and without constraint projection would also be beneficial, as the reviewer suggests, to isolate the impact of the projection technique. While the authors demonstrate that their approach is faster than GOBNILP, a more detailed analysis of the specific scenarios where the benefits are most pronounced would be valuable. Finally, the discussion of the MaxSAT solver (EVASOLVER) could be expanded slightly to provide more context for readers unfamiliar with the tool.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints to empower a learning oracle is a novel and insightful idea.
*   **Significance:** The demonstrated orders-of-magnitude speed improvements over existing methods like GOBNILP are significant and address a critical bottleneck in Bayesian network structure learning.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical framework, including the theorems regarding constraint projection, is well-developed and justifies the proposed approach.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The thorough experimental evaluation across multiple datasets and varying conditions provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   **Clarity of Results Presentation:** The comparison setup in the experiments could be clarified to better highlight the contribution of the proposed approach.
*   **Limited Discussion of MaxSAT:** The role and impact of the MaxSAT solver could be discussed in more detail.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. The proposed approach is novel, technically sound, and empirically validated. While there is room for improvement in the clarity of the experimental results presentation, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the clarity concerns raised above. The paper is likely to be of interest to researchers and practitioners working on Bayesian networks, causal inference, and structure learning.
