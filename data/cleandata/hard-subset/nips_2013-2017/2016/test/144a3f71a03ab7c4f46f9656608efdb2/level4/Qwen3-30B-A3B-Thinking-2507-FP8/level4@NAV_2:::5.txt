

This paper addresses the problem of optimal Bayesian network structure learning under ancestral constraints, which are non-decomposable and cannot be directly handled by most existing structure learning approaches that rely on decomposable scores. The authors build upon the framework proposed by Chen et al. (2015, 2016) for optimal structure learning with non-decomposable scores, extending it to handle ancestral constraints through a novel constraint projection technique. They demonstrate that ancestral constraints can be converted into decomposable edge and ordering constraints that can be efficiently handled by existing oracles, significantly improving search efficiency. The paper provides theoretical guarantees through well-structured theorems and comprehensive empirical validation across multiple benchmark networks.

Quality: The paper presents a technically sound contribution with a clear theoretical framework. The authors provide complete solutions with both theoretical guarantees (Theorems 1-4) and thorough empirical validation. The experimental design is robust, varying multiple parameters (number of variables, dataset size, constraint density) across standard benchmark networks (ALARM, ANDES, CHILD, CPCS54, HEPAR2). The results consistently show dramatic efficiency improvements (orders-of-magnitude faster) without compromising solution quality, as evidenced by structural Hamming distance metrics. The paper carefully addresses the limitations of existing approaches, particularly those based on integer linear programming that can theoretically handle ancestral constraints but have not been empirically evaluated for this purpose.

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The presentation of the BN graph and EC tree search spaces is clear and effectively supported by Figures 1a and 1b. The exposition of the MaxSAT formulation for ordering constraints is particularly accessible. The authors provide sufficient detail for experts to reproduce their results, including implementation specifics (EVASOLVER for MaxSAT, BDeu scores with equivalent sample size 1, and the use of URLEARNING as an oracle). The paper clearly distinguishes the proposed approach from related work, particularly the ILP-based GOBNILP system.

Originality: The work represents a significant extension of the Chen et al. framework to handle ancestral constraints, which is a notable contribution as most existing approaches cannot directly incorporate such non-decomposable constraints. The projection technique from non-decomposable to decomposable constraints is novel and provides a general solution to a problem that has been largely overlooked in the literature. The paper carefully distinguishes its approach from previous work, particularly the constraint-based methods of Borboudakis and Tsamardinos (2012, 2013) that use ancestral constraints for constraint-based learning rather than score-based structure learning.

Significance: This work has substantial significance for the Bayesian network learning community. The ability to efficiently incorporate background knowledge (in the form of ancestral constraints) is practically valuable, as such constraints are commonly available in real-world applications. The demonstrated orders-of-magnitude speedup makes the approach highly practical for larger networks, and the scalability to 20 variables is impressive for exact structure learning. The findings also highlight the substantial impact that background knowledge can have on learning quality, potentially reducing the need for large datasets (a relatively small number of ancestral constraints can have similar impact as increasing dataset size from 512 to 2048).

Strengths:
- Novel projection technique for converting non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable ones
- Theoretical guarantees with well-structured theorems
- Comprehensive empirical evaluation across multiple benchmarks and parameter settings
- Orders-of-magnitude speedup over ILP-based approaches
- Demonstrated scalability to 20 variables, comparable to state-of-the-art

Weaknesses:
- The MaxSAT formulation for ordering constraints, while well-explained, could benefit from more detailed discussion of the trade-offs between constraint density and search efficiency
- Limited exploration of potential limitations in handling complex ancestral constraint patterns

Pro-acceptance arguments:
- Solves a meaningful problem that has been largely overlooked in the literature
- Demonstrates substantial practical improvements over existing methods
- Theoretical framework is sound and well-supported
- Empirical results are thorough, convincing, and consistently show dramatic improvements
- Contributes to the growing body of work on efficient Bayesian network structure learning
- Provides a general technique that could be extended to other non-decomposable constraints

Con-acceptance arguments:
- The approach is specialized to ancestral constraints, though the projection technique has broader applicability
- The paper could better contextualize the scalability results relative to other recent state-of-the-art approaches

Overall, this is a strong contribution that makes a meaningful advancement in Bayesian network structure learning. The paper is technically sound, clearly presented, and demonstrates significant practical improvements over existing methods. I recommend acceptance.
