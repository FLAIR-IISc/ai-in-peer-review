- Problem: learning optimal Bayesian network structures with user-provided ancestral (non-decomposable) constraints is challenging for score-based methods.

- Main contribution: adapt EC-tree A* search to handle ancestral constraints via pruning and projection.

- Pruning: sound method to eliminate CPDAG nodes that cannot satisfy ancestral constraints.

- Projection: convert non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable required/forbidden edges and ordering constraints for the oracle.

- Edge inference: provide an efficient algorithm to infer implied edge constraints from ancestral constraints.

- Ordering inference: novel MaxSAT formulation to infer a maximal set of consistent ordering constraints.

- Heuristic empowerment: projected constraints tighten the A* heuristic, dramatically speeding up search.

- Theoretical claims: projection and pruning approaches appear sound and well-justified.

- Empirical evaluation: thorough experiments across benchmarks show orders-of-magnitude speedups over ILP (GOBNILP).

- Baseline comparison: ILP sometimes benefits from projected constraints but less consistently and with overhead.

- Scalability/significance: enables optimal learning with ancestral priors for larger, real-world problems; small numbers of constraints can substantially improve accuracy.

- Clarity: paper is exceptionally well-written, organized, and explains decomposable vs non-decomposable constraints clearly.

- Originality: novel conceptual use of constraint projection to improve heuristics and novel MaxSAT application in this context.

- Question: need insight on MaxSAT preprocessing runtime and its scaling relative to overall search time.

- Question: ask whether exhaustive enumeration of large CPDAG equivalence classes can become a bottleneck in practice.

- Question: request intuition why projected constraints help A* more consistently than ILP.

- Pro arguments: addresses important problem, novel and effective technique, strong empirical support, clear presentation.

- Con argument: extension of a very recent framework by same authors, though non-trivial and impactful.

- Conclusion: reviewer strongly recommends acceptance.
