This paper addresses the challenging problem of optimal Bayesian network structure learning, specifically focusing on incorporating non-decomposable constraints – and particularly ancestral constraints – into the framework established by Chen et al. [2015, 2016]. The authors demonstrate a compelling approach by reformulating the problem as a MaxSAT instance with constraints designed to enforce the desired network structure. 

The key contribution lies in effectively bridging the gap between complex, non-decomposable constraints and a tractable computational framework. Previous approaches, often reliant on Integer Linear Programming (ILP), have suffered from significant scalability issues. This work presents a substantial improvement in this regard, evidenced by empirical evaluations demonstrating runtime reductions of *orders of magnitude* compared to existing ILP-based methods. 

The experimental results are particularly striking. While my expertise in MaxSAT solvers is limited, the reported performance gains are highly encouraging and suggest a significant advancement in the field. The authors provide a clear comparison against relevant baselines, convincingly showcasing the efficiency of their MaxSAT-based formulation. The magnitude of the runtime reduction observed warrants further investigation and could unlock the possibility of learning optimal Bayesian networks for problems previously considered intractable.

Several aspects of the paper are well-executed. The problem definition is clear and the motivation for addressing non-decomposable constraints is well-articulated. The connection to the prior work of Chen et al. is clearly established, building upon their foundation and extending its capabilities. The translation of the Bayesian network structure learning problem into a MaxSAT formulation appears sound, and the authors provide sufficient detail to understand the mapping between the two representations.

However, a few points could be strengthened. While the runtime improvements are impressive, the paper would benefit from a more detailed analysis of the types of networks and constraints for which the proposed approach exhibits the greatest advantage. Are there specific network topologies or constraint patterns where the performance gains are most pronounced? Understanding these nuances would provide valuable insights for practitioners.

Furthermore, a discussion of the limitations of the approach would be beneficial. For example, the performance of MaxSAT solvers can be sensitive to the specific structure of the generated SAT instance. Do certain constraint configurations lead to particularly challenging instances for the solver? Addressing these potential weaknesses would provide a more balanced and nuanced assessment of the method.

Finally, while the paper demonstrates substantial improvements over ILP, a comparison against other state-of-the-art structure learning algorithms (beyond ILP) – perhaps constraint-based or score-based methods – could further contextualize the contribution. This would help to establish the relative performance of the proposed approach across a broader landscape of existing techniques.

Overall, this is a strong paper presenting a novel and effective solution to a challenging problem. The significant runtime improvements demonstrated empirically are particularly noteworthy. Addressing the suggested points regarding analysis of performance characteristics, limitations, and broader comparisons would further strengthen the manuscript. I recommend acceptance.
