This paper addresses the constrained optimization problem of learning Bayesian networks from local scores with ancestral constraints using a branch-and-bound search. The search tree consists of CPDAGs, and the transition from one CPDAG to another involves selecting a parent set for a BN variable not present in the initial CPDAG. Symmetry breaking is effectively implemented, and the search is guided by a heuristic provided by the URLearning system. Ancestral constraints enable the pruning of the search tree, with Lemma 3 being a crucial result in this context. However, the proof of Lemma 3 is unclear, particularly the statement "By the EC tree edge generation rules, Gk also contains edge Z -> W," which requires further explanation. Additionally, the authors utilize implied constraints, referred to as "projected constraints," to infer constraints on edges and permissible topological orderings. The approach to inferring these constraints is not thoroughly explained, and the statement "we can infer Y < Z from Z not an ancestor of Y" is misleading, as it implies a certainty that does not exist. The authors' intention is to infer consistent ordering constraints, and they employ a MAXSAT approach to achieve this. The experimental evaluation involves varying the number of variables, datapoints, and ancestral constraints. The results show that the proposed system significantly outperforms the GOBNILP system. However, this comparison is compromised by the use of a suboptimal MAXSAT encoding for ancestral constraints, which is based on an earlier formulation rather than a more efficient ILP encoding. A revised encoding, incorporating A(X,Y) binary variables and omitting E(X,Y,Z) variables, would provide a tighter linear relaxation and potentially improve performance. Furthermore, the paper would benefit from a comparison with the method proposed by van Beek and Hoffmann, which often outperforms GOBNILP and could be adapted to accommodate ancestral constraints.
