This paper addresses the constrained optimization task of learning Bayesian networks using local scores in the presence of ancestral constraints. The authors employ an A* branch-and-bound search where nodes in the search tree represent CPDAGs. The search progresses by refining a CPDAG through the selection of a parent set for a variable that is not a parent set in the current CPDAG. The method incorporates effective symmetry-breaking techniques. The A* heuristic is derived from the URLearning system, though the specifics of this integration are detailed in a previous publication.

The inclusion of ancestral constraints enables additional pruning of the search tree. Lemma 3, located in the supplementary material, is the central result underpinning this pruning. While I believe the lemma to be correct, its proof is not sufficiently clear. Specifically, the statement "By the EC tree edge generation rules, Gk also contains edge Z -> W" requires further elaboration.

The paper also leverages implied constraints—referred to here as "projected constraints," though "implied constraints" is the standard term—for further pruning. A set of ancestral constraints can imply edge constraints that narrow the search space. Similarly, "ordering constraints" on the topological sort are inferred. The methodology for this inference is not adequately explained. For instance, the paper states that one "can infer Y < Z from Z not an ancestor of Y." The ambiguity lies in the word "can," as a graph with only nodes Z and Y demonstrates a case where Z is not an ancestor of Y, but Y < Z is not a required ordering. The authors likely mean that if Z is not an ancestor of Y, one can optionally add the consistent constraint Y < Z. The goal seems to be to infer a maximal set of consistent ordering constraints, not just those that are logically entailed. To prevent over-constraining the problem, this inference is prudently managed using a MAXSAT approach.

The experimental evaluation varies the number of variables, data points, and ancestral constraints, comparing the proposed system against GOBNILP. The results show the authors' method to be substantially faster. However, this comparison has limitations. As the authors acknowledge, GOBNILP is known to perform poorly compared to A* approaches when parent set cardinality is unlimited, which is the case in these experiments. Consequently, it is difficult to determine whether GOBNILP's inferior performance stems from its known inefficiency with large parent sets or a specific difficulty in handling ancestral constraints.

Furthermore, the encoding of ancestral constraints for GOBNILP (provided in the supplement) is based on a MAXSAT encoding from [Cussens, 2008], which is a suboptimal formulation for an ILP system. A more suitable ILP encoding, such as the one in Section 3.1.1 of [Cussens, 2010], would be more appropriate. For instance, why not use A(X,Y) binary variables alongside the I(Z,U) variables and eliminate the cubic number of E(X,Y,Z) variables? This would only require transitivity constraints on the A(X,Y) variables and linking constraints between A and I variables, such as: A(X,Y) ≥ Σ_{U:X ∈ U} I(Y,U), which enforces that a parent is an ancestor. This formulation, adapted from the total ordering in [Cussens, 2010] to a partial order, would provide a significantly tighter linear relaxation and likely yield much better performance.

While I am confident the proposed system would still outperform GOBNILP even with a more sensible ILP formulation, the paper's contribution is weakened by comparing against an inefficient baseline. It would also be highly informative to compare the proposed method to the constraint programming approach of van Beek and Hoffmann, which often outperforms GOBNILP and could likely be adapted to handle ancestral constraints. The work by van Beek and Hoffmann is notably absent from the paper's citations.
