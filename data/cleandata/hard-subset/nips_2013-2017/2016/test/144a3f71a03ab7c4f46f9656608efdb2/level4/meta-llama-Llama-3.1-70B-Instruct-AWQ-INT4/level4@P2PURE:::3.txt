The paper presents an efficient method for incorporating ancestral constraints into the discovery of optimal Bayesian networks from data. This is achieved by translating a set of ancestral constraints into a maximal set of ordering constraints, which are then utilized in a score-based learning algorithm based on the EC tree introduced by [Chen et al., 2015]. The results are evaluated against a modified version of the ILP-based Gobnilp algorithm, demonstrating significant outperformance in the given setting. Overall, the paper is reasonably well-written and clearly explained, although the proposed solution is conceptually straightforward. The combination of a novel problem and solution yields a nice result, which, while not spectacular, is certainly of interest to a specialist audience.

The problem tackled in this paper is interesting and new, as optimal Bayesian network learning in combination with ancestral constraints lacks a readily available 'standard solution' (novelty=3). However, part of the reason behind this apparent deficiency is that the situation is somewhat artificial, as ancestral relations often imply the need for more general causal discovery algorithms that also account for unobserved confounders (hence impact=2). The approach taken involves translating hard-to-use ancestral constraints, which do not fit well in decomposable-score-based algorithms, into sets of ordering constraints that are more easily expressed in terms of absence of edges, compatible with decomposable scores. This mapping is performed using a MaxSAT solver, ensuring that all implied ancestral constraints are made explicit.

However, some of the rules presented in the paper (e.g., 257-261) seem unusual or incomplete. For instance, the premise in acyclicity rule 3 is symmetric ((X < Y) and (Z < W) == (Z < W) and (X < Y)), but the conclusion is not (Y -/-> Z). Additionally, rule 4 appears to exclude ancestral constraints of the type 'X and Y do not cause each other'. Furthermore, in practice, it is likely that only a few ancestral constraints are given, which may not significantly reduce the search space. The resulting comparison against Gobnilp with added linear constraints appears impressive, but Gobnilp is not designed to tackle this problem. A reasonable alternative to measure performance would be to assume that the ancestral constraints are in line with the optimal model and compare against a greedy algorithm like GES, which should produce the same output for oracle input. Although greedy algorithms cannot guarantee optimality, this comparison would provide a more meaningful indication of the efficiency achieved by the proposed method.

Minor details and suggestions for improvement include:

- Clarifying the difference between a standard oracle and an omniscient oracle (line 30).
- Replacing 'empower the oracle' with 'use in combination with the oracle' for better clarity (line 38).
- Specifying that optimizing refers to minimizing (line 90).
- Revising Theorem 1 to clearly state that the indicated set of constraints A satisfied by CPDAG P on the EC tree is only a subset of the set of constraints A satisfied by the full DAG G (line 164).
- Noting that Theorem 3 may not lead to many positive edge implications given sparse ancestral constraints (lines 225-227).
- Improving the presentation of the acyclicity rule to make it more recognizable (line 267).
- Acknowledging that for reasonable-sized graphs, 10%-25% ancestral constraints would already imply extensive knowledge of the target graph, which is not typical in practice (line 310).
