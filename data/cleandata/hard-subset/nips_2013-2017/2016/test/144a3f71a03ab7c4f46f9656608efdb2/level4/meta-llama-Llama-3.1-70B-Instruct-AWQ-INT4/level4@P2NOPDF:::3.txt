This paper presents an efficient approach to incorporating ancestral constraints in learning optimal Bayesian networks from data. It achieves this by translating a set of ancestral constraints into a maximal set of ordering constraints, which are then used in a score-based learning algorithm based on the EC tree introduced by Chen et al. (2015). The results are evaluated against a modified version of the ILP-based Gobnilp algorithm and demonstrate significant outperformance in the given setting. The paper is reasonably well-written and clearly explained, although the solution is conceptually straightforward.

The paper tackles an interesting and novel problem, as optimal Bayesian network learning with ancestral constraints does not have a readily available standard solution. However, it is worth noting that this problem setting is somewhat artificial, as ancestral relations often imply the need for more general causal discovery algorithms that can handle unobserved confounders. As a result, the impact of this work may be limited to specialist audiences.

The approach taken in the paper involves translating hard-to-use ancestral constraints into sets of ordering constraints that can be easily expressed in terms of absent edges, which fit well with decomposable scores. This mapping is achieved using a MaxSAT solver, ensuring that all implied ancestral constraints are made explicit. However, some of the rules presented (e.g., 257-261) appear incomplete or incorrect. For instance, the acyclicity rule 3 has a symmetric premise but an asymmetric conclusion, and rule 4 seems to exclude certain types of ancestral constraints.

Furthermore, in practice, only a few ancestral constraints are typically given, which may not significantly reduce the search space. The comparison against Gobnilp with added linear constraints appears impressive, but Gobnilp is not designed to tackle this problem. A more meaningful evaluation would be to compare the proposed method against a greedy algorithm like GES, assuming the ancestral constraints are in line with the optimal model. While greedy algorithms cannot guarantee optimality, this comparison would provide a more informative indication of the efficiency achieved by the proposed method.

Minor comments:

* On page 30, it is unclear whether there is a difference between a standard oracle and an omniscient oracle.
* On page 38, the phrase "empower the oracle" could be rephrased as "use in combination with the oracle."
* On page 90, it would be helpful to explicitly state that optimizing refers to minimizing.
* Theorem 1 (page 164) should clarify that the indicated set of constraints A satisfied by CPDAG P on the EC tree is a subset of the set of constraints A satisfied by the full DAG G.
* Theorem 3 (pages 225-227) may not lead to many positive edge implications, especially with sparse ancestral constraints.
* The notation on page 267 does not clearly capture the concept of acyclicity.
* On page 310, it is noted that even 10%-25% ancestral constraints would imply extensive knowledge of the target graph, which is not typical in practice.

Overall, the paper presents a novel solution to an interesting problem, but its impact may be limited due to the artificial nature of the problem setting. The approach is conceptually straightforward, and the evaluation could be strengthened by comparing against more suitable baselines.
