- Paper adapts the EC tree search (CPDAG nodes) so A* can prune CPDAGs that cannot extend to any DAG obeying ancestral constraints.
- Transitions add leaf variables with chosen parent sets and include symmetry breaking to avoid duplicate exploration.
- Heuristic for A* is provided by URLearning (oracle for decomposable scores), building on prior work to ensure admissibility.
- Main novel idea: project non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints to empower the oracle.
- Edge constraints are derived via closure and entailment rules (Theorems 2 and 3).
- Ordering constraints are selected by a MaxSAT formulation to include many X<Y relations while remaining consistent with ancestral constraints (Theorem 4).
- Empirically, the EC-tree method is consistently faster than GOBNILP, often by orders of magnitude, especially when using projected constraints.
- Results show an easy–hard–easy pattern as the fraction of ancestral constraints varies and quantify Hamming-distance improvements from constraints/data.
- Central pruning lemma (Lemma 3) appears correct but its proof is unclear; a key step needs more justification or an illustrative example.
- Clarify the distinction between entailed ordering constraints and the MaxSAT-selected consistent set; the method finds a large jointly consistent set, not all logically entailed constraints.
- Recommend explicitly stating that ordering constraints are selected (maximized) subject to soundness rather than claimed as entailments.
- Suggest adding a brief recap of the heuristic/oracle construction for readers unfamiliar with prior work.
- Standardize terminology: use either “projected constraints” or “implied constraints” and explain correspondence.
- The MaxSAT-based selection of ordering constraints is novel and useful.
- Practically significant: approach yields large speedups and improves model accuracy by leveraging background knowledge.
- Conceptually significant: provides a principled way to incorporate non-decomposable knowledge into decomposable-score frameworks via sound projection.
- Experimental comparison to GOBNILP is valuable but confounded by absence of parent-set size bounds affecting ILP performance.
- The ILP encoding used is weak; recommend comparing to a stronger ILP with ancestor variables A(X,Y), family variables I(Z,U), transitivity, and linking constraints for a tighter relaxation.
- Also recommend comparing to the CP approach of van Beek and Hoffmann (CP 2015), which is omitted and may be competitive.
- Pros: clear problem, sound pruning, effective projection, MaxSAT selection, and strong empirical gains.
- Cons: unclear proof step (Lemma 3), ambiguity about “inferring” vs selecting ordering constraints, and a weak/omitted comparative baseline.
- Suggested revisions: clarify proofs (Lemma 3), tighten ordering-constraint explanations, standardize terms, and run stronger ILP/CP baselines.
- Overall assessment: strong, novel, and practically important work; with clarity improvements and fairer baselines it is publication-ready.
