Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses optimal Bayesian network structure learning under non-decomposable background knowledge, specifically ancestral constraints. Building on the BN-graph and EC-tree frameworks of Chen et al. (2015, 2016), the authors (i) prune the EC tree using tests on CPDAGs to enforce ancestral constraints, and (ii) “empower” a decomposable-score oracle by projecting ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints. For edges, they derive necessary and sufficient conditions (Theorems 2–3) based on ancestor/descendant closures α(·), β(·). For orderings, they reduce the inference problem to MaxSAT, with hard constraints ensuring transitivity, necessity (Y 6 X needed for X < Y), and acyclicity, and soft constraints to select as many orderings as possible (Theorem 4 provides sufficient conditions for entailment). They compare their approach against ILP (GOBNILP) which can also encode ancestral constraints.

This work complements order-graph, DP, and A* approaches that assume decomposable scores (Koivisto & Sood; Silander & Myllymäki; Yuan & Malone), and relates to ILP/CP formulations (Jaakkola et al.; Cussens; van Beek & Hoffmann). It is also distinct from constraint-based use of path constraints (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos), focusing instead on exact score-based learning with non-decomposable constraints.

Quality:
- Theory: The EC-tree pruning strategy is sound (Theorem 1). The edge-projection theorems are clear and intuitively justified. The MaxSAT formulation for orderings uses sufficient (not necessary) conditions; this maintains admissibility/safety but may miss some inferable constraints. Details on computing the transitive closure to obtain a “maximum” set of ancestral constraints are omitted (“details omitted for space”), and the worst-case cost of enumerating all DAGs in a CPDAG (when quick tests are inconclusive) can be exponential; some guidance on how often this fallback is needed in practice would strengthen the paper.
- Heuristic admissibility: Passing only entailed decomposable constraints to the oracle should preserve admissibility of the heuristic used in A* on the EC tree, but an explicit statement/argument confirming this would be helpful.
- Experiments: The evaluation covers several benchmarks (ALARM, ANDES, CHILD, CPCS54, HEPAR2) with varying n, data size, and proportion p of ancestral constraints. Orders-of-magnitude speedups over ILP are reported, particularly when projected constraints are supplied. The easy–hard–easy trend with increasing p for the EC-tree search is reasonable, and accuracy gains (structural Hamming distance) from constraints are informative. It would be valuable to quantify solver overheads (e.g., MaxSAT runtime), the number of inferred edge/ordering constraints, and the frequency of CPDAG enumeration.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written, but I found aspects of the experimental comparison confusing:
- Table 1 appears to compare (EC tree + pruning + projections via your new methods) against (GOBNILP + the same projected constraints), i.e., both methods benefit from projected constraints.
- Table 2 withholds projections from both, showing the impact of projections.
- Table 3 then varies n and p with time/memory limits. It is not fully clear whether these results use projected constraints for EC and/or GOBNILP. Please clarify exactly which components are active in each table.
An explicit ablation for EC—baseline EC vs EC + pruning only vs EC + pruning + edge projection vs EC + pruning + edge + ordering projection—would make the contribution of each component unambiguous. Similarly, GOBNILP with and without the projected constraints is interesting but, given EC’s strong baseline, the key question for readers is how much your projections help EC itself.

Originality:
Incorporating ancestral constraints into EC-tree search via principled pruning and, especially, projecting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints to power a decomposable-score oracle is novel and well motivated. The MaxSAT reduction for extracting a maximally consistent set of ordering constraints under sufficient conditions is also a nice contribution.

Significance:
Learning with realistic causal/ancestral background knowledge is important. The reported orders-of-magnitude gains and improved model accuracy as constraints increase suggest practical utility. Although scalability is currently in the ~20-variable range (without family-size limits), this is comparable to prior exact methods using BDeu and should be valuable for moderate-scale domains or as a component in pipelines.

Strengths:
- Clear integration of ancestral constraints into EC-tree search with sound pruning.
- Effective projection of non-decomposable constraints to decomposable ones, preserving heuristic admissibility while dramatically improving search efficiency.
- Comprehensive comparisons to ILP under multiple settings; insightful easy–hard–easy behavior.
- Demonstrated accuracy gains from background knowledge.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Experimental clarity: precisely specify which components are on/off in each table; include an ablation for EC as noted above.
- Report the computational overhead of MaxSAT and the number of inferred constraints, to show cost–benefit.
- Discuss the frequency and cost of enumerating class(P) when CPDAG tests are inconclusive.
- Evaluate sensitivity to noisy or incorrect ancestral constraints, which is likely in practice.
- Consider including a CP baseline (if feasible) or explain why not.
- Provide more details (or an appendix) on computing the maximal closure of ancestral constraints and proofs/sketches for Theorems 2–4.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel and sound methodology for handling non-decomposable ancestral constraints in exact BN learning.
- Strong empirical performance with large speedups and improved model quality.
- Clear theoretical underpinnings and practical implementation details.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Some ambiguity in experimental design and lack of ablations makes it hard to isolate the contribution of each component.
- Limited scalability and lack of evaluation under noisy constraints.
- Some technical details deferred (closure computation, enumeration impacts, MaxSAT overhead).

Overall, this is a solid and timely contribution. Clarifying the empirical comparisons and adding an EC-focused ablation would significantly strengthen the paper.
