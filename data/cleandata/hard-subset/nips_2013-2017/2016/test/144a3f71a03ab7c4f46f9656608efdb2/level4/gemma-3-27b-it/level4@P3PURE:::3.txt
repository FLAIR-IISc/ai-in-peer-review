The paper presents an efficient method for incorporating ancestral constraints into optimal Bayesian network (BN) learning from data. It achieves this by translating a set of ancestral constraints into a (maximal) set of ordering constraints, which are then used within a score-based learning algorithm based on the EC tree introduced by [Chen et al., 2015]. Experimental results demonstrate significant performance improvements compared to a straightforward modification of the ILP-based Gobnilp algorithm in the tested setting. The paper is overall well-written and clearly explained, though the solution is conceptually straightforward. The combination of a novel problem and solution, along with promising results, makes this work of definite interest to a specialist audience.

The problem addressed – optimal BN learning combined with ancestral constraints – is interesting and novel, as there is currently no readily available standard solution (novelty=3). However, the perceived need for this approach stems from a somewhat artificial setting. When ancestral relations are present, it often makes more sense to employ more general causal discovery algorithms capable of handling unobserved confounders (hence impact=2). The core idea of the paper is to translate difficult-to-use ancestral constraints, which are ill-suited for decomposable-score-based algorithms, into sets of ordering constraints that *are* readily expressed as the absence of edges, thereby fitting within the framework of decomposable scores. This mapping is performed using a MaxSAT solver to ensure all implied ancestral constraints are explicitly represented. 

However, some of the rules presented in lines 257-261 appear either strange or incomplete. For example, the premise in acyclicity rule 3 is symmetric – ((X < Y) and (Z < W) is equivalent to (Z < W) and (X < Y)) – yet the conclusion is not (Y -/-> Z). Furthermore, rule 4 seems to exclude ancestral constraints stating that 'X and Y do not cause each other'. Moreover, in practice, only a limited number of ancestral constraints are likely to be provided, and these may not substantially reduce the search space. While the comparison against Gobnilp with added linear constraints is impressive, the latter was not specifically designed to address this problem. A more appropriate performance benchmark would be to assume the ancestral constraints are consistent with the optimal model and compare against a greedy algorithm such as GES, which should produce the same output given oracle input. Although greedy algorithms cannot guarantee optimality, this comparison would offer a more meaningful indication of the method’s efficiency.

Minor points:
- Line 30: Is there a distinction intended between a (standard) oracle and an omniscient oracle?
- Line 38: Does “empower the oracle” mean “use in combination with the oracle”?
- Line 90: Clarify that “optimizing” refers to minimizing.
- Line 164: Theorem 1 should explicitly state that the set of constraints A satisfied by CPDAG P on the EC tree is only a *subset* of the constraints A satisfied by the full DAG G; otherwise, the “iff” condition is not valid.
- Lines 225-227: Theorem 3 appears unlikely to yield many positive edge implications given sparse (small sets of) ancestral constraints.
- Line 267: The relationship to ‘acyclic’ is not immediately clear (see also the comments on lines 257-261).
- Line 310: For reasonably sized graphs, having 10%-25% ancestral constraints would already imply a significant prior knowledge of the target graph, which is not typical in many practical scenarios.
