This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures subject to ancestral constraints. This is a challenging constrained optimization problem because ancestral constraints are non-decomposable, which prevents the use of standard dynamic programming or heuristic search approaches that rely on decomposable scores. The authors' proposed method extends a recent framework based on A* search over a state space of equivalence classes (the "EC tree"). The core contributions are twofold: first, a method for pruning the search space based on checking the satisfiability of ancestral constraints for each equivalence class (CPDAG), and second, a technique to "empower" the A* heuristic by inferring, or "projecting," decomposable edge and ordering constraints from the non-decomposable ancestral constraints. This allows the oracle used for the heuristic (URLearning) to return tighter bounds, speeding up the search. The paper provides an empirical comparison against an integer linear programming (ILP) approach (GOBNILP), demonstrating significant speed improvements.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance:** The paper tackles an important problem. Incorporating background knowledge, such as causal or temporal information expressed as ancestral constraints, is critical for learning accurate models in many real-world domains. Providing an optimal and efficient method to do so is a valuable contribution.
*   **Originality:** The proposed approach of integrating non-decomposable constraints into an A*-based search over equivalence classes by both pruning and empowering the heuristic is novel and elegant. The method for inferring a maximal set of consistent ordering constraints using MaxSAT is particularly clever.
*   **Quality:** The core methodology appears sound. The experiments show that the proposed system is orders of magnitude faster than the GOBNILP baseline on the tested problems, demonstrating the practical effectiveness of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Clarity:** Certain key technical details could be explained more clearly.
    *   The proof of Lemma 3 in the supplementary material, which is central to the pruning strategy, is difficult to follow. The statement "By the EC tree edge generation rules, Gk also contains edge Z -> W" requires more justification for the reader to verify its correctness.
    *   The explanation of inferring ordering constraints is potentially confusing. The paper states one can infer Y < Z from the ancestral constraint "Z is not an ancestor of Y". This is not a logical entailment, as a graph with no path between Y and Z is consistent with both Y < Z and Z < Y. The method appears to be finding a maximal set of *consistent* ordering constraints to add, rather than inferring entailed ones. Clarifying this distinction would strengthen the paper.

*   **Evaluation:** The experimental comparison to GOBNILP, while showing impressive results, has some limitations that weaken the conclusion.
    *   The experiments were run without a limit on parent set cardinality. It is known that A*-based approaches tend to outperform ILP-based ones like GOBNILP in this setting, even for unconstrained problems. It is therefore difficult to disentangle how much of the performance gain comes from the novel handling of ancestral constraints versus the baseline advantage of the search strategy in this specific experimental setting.
    *   The ILP formulation used to encode ancestral constraints for GOBNILP seems suboptimal. The authors cite a MAXSAT encoding from Cussens (2008), which can be inefficient when translated to ILP. A tighter and more direct ILP formulation, for instance, based on the approach in Section 3.1.1 of Cussens (2010, "Maximum likelihood pedigree reconstruction using integer programming"), would likely provide a much stronger baseline. This formulation would link ancestor variables directly to parent-set indicator variables, yielding a tighter linear relaxation. Comparing against a potentially weak formulation makes the claimed speed-up less convincing.

*   **Related Work:** The paper is missing a key reference. The work of van Beek and Hoffmann (2015, "Machine learning of Bayesian networks using constraint programming") presents a constraint programming approach that is a strong competitor to both ILP and A* methods and could likely be adapted to handle ancestral constraints. A discussion of this work would provide a more complete picture of the state of the art.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  The paper presents a novel and technically sound method for an important and difficult problem in structure learning.
2.  The idea of empowering an oracle for decomposable scores with constraints inferred from non-decomposable ones is a powerful concept.
3.  The empirical results, despite the issues with the baseline, demonstrate a substantial performance improvement, indicating the approach is highly effective in practice.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  The experimental comparison is weakened by the choice of a suboptimal ILP formulation for the baseline and an experimental setting known to favor the A* search strategy, making the magnitude of the contribution harder to assess.
2.  Key explanations, particularly in the proofs and the description of constraint inference, lack the clarity required for full reproducibility and verification.
3.  The paper omits relevant and important related work in constraint-based structure learning.

In summary, this is a strong paper with a novel contribution to an important problem. However, its claims would be significantly strengthened by addressing the issues in the experimental comparison and improving the clarity of key technical descriptions.
