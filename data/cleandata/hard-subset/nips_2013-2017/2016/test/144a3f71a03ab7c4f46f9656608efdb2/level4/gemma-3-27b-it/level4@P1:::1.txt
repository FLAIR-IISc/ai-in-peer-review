This paper concerns the constrained optimization problem of learning Bayesian networks from local scores with ancestral constraints. The search is branch-and-bound, where the nodes in the search tree are CPDAGs, and the algorithm progresses by adding a parent set for a Bayesian network variable to a given CPDAG. Sensible symmetry breaking is employed. The search utilizes A* search, with the URLearning system providing a heuristic function. Given ancestral constraints, pruning of the search tree becomes possible. Lemma 3 (in the supplementary material) is a key result, and while I believe it to be true, I find the proof difficult to follow. Specifically, the phrase "By the EC tree edge generation rules, Gk also contains edge Z → W" requires further clarification. In addition to direct pruning, the paper explores “implied constraints” (standard terminology) – here termed “projected constraints” – which can be inferred from the ancestral constraints. These projected constraints can then be used for further pruning. Constraints on permissible topological orderings are also inferred (“ordering constraints”). The approach to this inference is not fully explained. The authors state that they “can infer Y < Z from Z not an ancestor of Y,” which is potentially misleading. Clearly, DAGs exist where Z is not an ancestor of Y, yet Y < Z is a consistent ordering (e.g., a graph with vertices Z and Y and no edges). The authors correctly intend to convey that, given “Z is not an ancestor of Y,” one *can choose* to add the constraint Y < Z. Therefore, the goal is to infer as many consistent ordering constraints as possible, rather than solely those which are logically entailed. To avoid inferring too many constraints, the authors utilize a MaxSAT approach. Experiments vary the number of variables, data points, and ancestral constraints. A comparison with the GOBNILP system demonstrates that the presented approach is significantly faster. As the authors note, without ancestral constraints, GOBNILP often underperforms A* search approaches when there is no limit on parent set cardinality. In the current experiments, there is no such limit, making it difficult to determine whether GOBNILP’s poor performance is inherent to dealing with ancestral constraints or a consequence of its general difficulties handling large parent sets. Furthermore, the encoding of ancestral constraints (presented in the supplementary material) is based on a MAXSAT encoding from [Cussens, 2008], rather than the ILP encoding found in Section 3.1.1 of [Cussens, 2010]. This MAXSAT encoding represents a suboptimal formulation for an ILP system. A more effective approach would be to introduce binary variables A(X,Y) representing ancestral relations, in addition to the existing I(Z,U) variables, and to avoid the cubic number of E(X,Y,Z) variables. The necessary constraints would include transitivity constraints on the A(X,Y) variables and the following linking constraints between A and I variables: A(X,Y) ≥ ∑{U:X ∈ U} I(Y,U). This states that if X is a parent of Y, then X is an ancestor of Y. This formulation provides a tighter linear relaxation and should significantly improve performance. This is the approach used in Section 3.1.1 of [Cussens, 2010] for total orderings, but it can be adapted for partial orderings, such as ancestral relations. I am confident that even with a sensible ILP formulation of ancestral constraints, the current system would outperform GOBNILP. However, the paper is somewhat weakened by comparing to a suboptimal formulation rather than a well-designed one. It would also be interesting to compare the current method with that of van Beek and Hoffmann, particularly since it often outperforms GOBNILP. The authors do not mention the work of van Beek and Hoffmann.
