This paper addresses the problem of learning Bayesian network structures from data, subject to a set of ancestral constraints. The authors propose a branch-and-bound A* search algorithm that operates in the space of Completed Partially Directed Acyclic Graphs (CPDAGs). The search progresses by refining a CPDAG through the selection of parent sets for variables. The algorithm incorporates sensible symmetry-breaking techniques and utilizes the URLearning system to provide an admissible heuristic, though the details of this heuristic are referenced from a previous publication.

The main contribution of this work lies in leveraging ancestral constraints to enable significant pruning of the search space. This is achieved by deriving "projected constraints" (more commonly known as implied constraints) and "ordering constraints" from the initial user-specified constraints. The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed system is substantially faster than the GOBNILP system on the tested benchmarks.

While the core idea is sound and the performance gains appear impressive, the paper has several weaknesses in its current form, primarily related to the clarity of its technical descriptions and the fairness of its experimental comparison.

**Major Concerns:**

1.  **Clarity of Technical Contributions:**
    *   **Proof of Lemma 3:** The proof for Lemma 3, presented in the supplementary material, is a key result underpinning the pruning strategy. However, the proof is difficult to follow. For instance, the statement "By the EC tree edge generation rules, Gk also contains edge Z -> W" is not self-evident and requires a more detailed explanation to make the argument fully comprehensible.
    *   **Inference of Ordering Constraints:** The process for inferring ordering constraints is not adequately explained. The paper states that "we can infer Y < Z from Z not an ancestor of Y". This statement is imprecise; this is not a logical entailment but rather the imposition of a consistent, but not necessarily entailed, ordering. The authors seem to be selecting a maximal set of consistent ordering constraints using a MAXSAT approach. The rationale and methodology for this inference process—how one ensures a large but consistent set of constraints is chosen—needs to be formally described.

2.  **Experimental Baseline and Comparison:**
    The comparison with the GOBNILP system is a central component of the paper's evaluation, but it suffers from two significant issues that may overstate the performance gap:
    *   **Confounding Factors:** As the authors note, A*-based approaches are known to outperform GOBNILP when there is no hard limit on parent set cardinality, which is the setting for these experiments. It is therefore difficult to determine how much of the reported speedup is due to the novel handling of ancestral constraints versus the inherent advantage of the A* search strategy in this specific experimental setting.
    *   **Suboptimal GOBNILP Formulation:** The Integer Linear Programming (ILP) formulation used to encode ancestral constraints for GOBNILP is based on a MAXSAT encoding from [Cussens, 2008]. This is known to be a poor formulation for ILP solvers. A much stronger and more standard ILP formulation exists, for example, in Section 3.1.1 of [Cussens, 2010]. A tighter formulation could be achieved by using binary variables A(X,Y) for ancestry and I(Z,U) for parentage, linked by transitivity constraints on A(X,Y) and the constraint that if X is a parent of Y, then X is an ancestor of Y (e.g., A(X,Y) >= I(Y, {X} U S) for all parent sets S). This approach yields a much tighter linear relaxation and would likely lead to significantly improved performance from GOBNILP. By comparing against a suboptimal formulation, the paper weakens the credibility of its experimental claims. While the proposed method is still likely to be faster, the comparison should be made against a reasonably optimized baseline.

3.  **Missing Related Work:**
    The paper fails to cite or compare against the highly relevant work of van Beek and Hoffmann ("Machine learning of Bayesian networks using constraint programming," CP 2015). Their constraint programming approach is a strong competitor in this domain, often outperforming GOBNILP, and it is plausible that their method could be adapted to incorporate ancestral constraints. A discussion of this work is necessary to properly position the paper's contribution within the current state of the art.

**Minor Suggestions:**

*   The term "projected constraints" is used for what is standardly referred to as "implied constraints" in the literature. Adopting the standard terminology would improve clarity.

**Conclusion:**

This paper presents a promising A*-based approach for learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints. The core algorithm is well-motivated, and the performance results are encouraging. However, the paper must be revised to address the significant concerns regarding the clarity of its technical proofs, the methodology for inferring ordering constraints, and the fairness of the experimental comparison with GOBNILP. Furthermore, the omission of key related work must be rectified. I believe the paper could be a valuable contribution if these issues are thoroughly addressed.
