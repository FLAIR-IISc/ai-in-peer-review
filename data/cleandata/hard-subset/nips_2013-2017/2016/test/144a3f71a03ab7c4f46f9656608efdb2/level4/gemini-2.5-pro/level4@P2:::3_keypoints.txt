- Novelty: Integrating ancestral constraints into optimal score-based Bayesian network learning is a new and valuable contribution.  
- Method: Projects non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints using the EC tree search and an oracle.  
- MaxSAT-use: Formulates the projection as a MaxSAT problem to find maximal consistent ordering constraints — a clever operationalization.  
- Empirical speedups: Shows orders-of-magnitude runtime improvements over a GOBNILP (ILP) baseline.  
- Clarity: Paper is generally well-written with clear technical exposition.  
- Framing concern: When strong ancestral priors exist, the work should be situated with causal discovery methods (e.g., handling unobserved confounders, FCI).  
- Baseline choice: GOBNILP is not specialized for these constraints; adding comparisons to heuristics like GES would give a stronger efficiency benchmark.  
- Prior density in experiments: Using 10–25% of possible ancestral relations may be unrealistically dense; evaluate performance with sparse constraint sets.  
- MaxSAT clarity — Constraint 3: The asymmetric conclusion (Y 6 Z) vs symmetric premise ((X < Y) ∧ (Z < W)) needs clarification or correction.  
- MaxSAT clarity — Constraint 4: Translating X 6 Y to Y < X may not capture bidirectional negatives (X 6 Y and Y 6 X); explain how such cases are handled.  
- Terminology: Clarify "omniscient oracle" vs standard "oracle" and rephrase "empower the oracle" for precision.  
- Theorem 1 wording: Clarify how a CPDAG satisfies constraints (whether at least one DAG in the class must satisfy A) and the intended "iff" semantics.  
- Theorem 3 practical impact: Provide intuition or empirical evidence on how often positive edge constraints are inferred in practice, especially when constraints are sparse.  
- Overall assessment: Solid, efficient, and original work; main weaknesses are practical framing and the breadth/realism of experimental evaluation.
