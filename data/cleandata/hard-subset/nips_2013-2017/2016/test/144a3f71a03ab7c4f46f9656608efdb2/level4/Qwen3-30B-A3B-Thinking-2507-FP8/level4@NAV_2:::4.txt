

This paper addresses the problem of learning Bayesian network structures with ancestral constraints, which are non-decomposable and thus challenging for existing approaches that assume decomposable scores. The authors build upon the recently proposed framework for optimal structure learning based on non-decomposable scores [Chen et al., 2015, 2016], introducing a method to project non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints that can be handled by standard oracles. This approach enables the incorporation of background knowledge about causal relationships without sacrificing optimality, as demonstrated through empirical comparisons with ILP-based methods. The work relates to prior NIPS contributions on Bayesian network structure learning, particularly the EC tree search space [Chen et al., 2016] and constraint-based learning methods [Borboudakis and Tsamardinos, 2012, 2013], while extending the capabilities of the framework to handle non-decomposable constraints.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality, with a clear theoretical foundation for constraint projection (Section 5) and thorough empirical validation (Section 6). The authors provide a sound and complete method for pruning the EC tree search space based on ancestral constraints, and their approach to empowering oracles with decomposable constraints is both novel and effective. The empirical results (Tables 1-3) consistently show orders-of-magnitude improvements in efficiency compared to ILP-based approaches, particularly as the number of variables increases. The paper also makes a significant contribution to understanding how background knowledge can substantially improve learning accuracy with minimal data (Table 3), which speaks to the significance of the work.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with appropriate technical depth in both the theoretical and experimental sections. The writing effectively guides readers through the complex concepts of ancestral constraints, EC trees, and constraint projection. The authors have thoroughly addressed related work, including the limitations of existing approaches that assume decomposable scores [Koivisto and Sood, 2004, Parviainen and Koivisto, 2013] and the potential of ILP/CP approaches [Jaakkola et al., 2010, Bartlett and Cussens, 2015].

Strengths:
- Novel theoretical contribution in Section 5 on projecting non-decomposable constraints to decomposable ones
- Strong empirical validation showing orders-of-magnitude efficiency improvements
- Clear demonstration of how background knowledge can reduce data requirements
- Well-organized presentation of complex concepts
- Comprehensive comparison with ILP-based approaches
- Sound theoretical foundation for constraint projection

Weaknesses:
- The discussion of why projected constraints benefit EC trees more than ILP approaches (Table 2) could be expanded to better address the generalizability of the constraint projection method
- The easy-hard-easy pattern in Table 3 (as the proportion of ancestral constraints increases) requires more thorough explanation regarding its implications for practical application
- The structural Hamming distance comparison between EC trees and ILP approaches is not provided (only for EC trees in Table 2), making it difficult to assess relative accuracy
- Section 4.1 (BN graphs) is somewhat tangential to the main contribution (EC trees), and could be condensed or removed to focus more on the EC tree implementation

Arguments for acceptance:
- The paper makes a significant contribution to handling non-decomposable constraints in Bayesian network structure learning
- The proposed constraint projection method is novel and well-justified
- The empirical results demonstrate clear efficiency advantages over existing approaches
- The work has clear practical implications for incorporating domain knowledge
- The paper is technically sound and well-written

Arguments against acceptance:
- The paper does not sufficiently demonstrate that the constraint projection method generalizes to other learning approaches beyond EC trees
- The explanation of the easy-hard-easy pattern in Table 3 is insufficient
- The comparison of accuracy between EC trees and ILP approaches is missing
- The paper could be strengthened by focusing more on the EC tree approach rather than including extensive background on BN graphs

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that addresses an important gap in Bayesian network structure learning. The work is technically sound, well-executed, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field. With minor revisions addressing the weaknesses noted above, this paper would be an excellent fit for NIPS.
