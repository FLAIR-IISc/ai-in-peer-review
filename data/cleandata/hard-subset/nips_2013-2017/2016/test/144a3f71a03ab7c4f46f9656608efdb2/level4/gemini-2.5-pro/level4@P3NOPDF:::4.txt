This paper presents a novel method for projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints into a set of decomposable constraints, namely edge and topological ordering constraints. This projection enables score-decomposable structure learning algorithms, which cannot natively handle such complex prior knowledge, to incorporate it effectively, thereby improving learning efficiency. The primary contribution lies in the projection technique detailed in Section 5. The paper is well-written and clearly articulates its theoretical and experimental contributions. However, I have several concerns and suggestions that the authors should address.

**Major Concerns:**

1.  **Generalizability of the Projection Method:** A primary concern arises from the results in Table 3, which show that the proposed projection method improves the performance of EC trees but not the ILP approach. This raises a crucial question about the generalizability of the technique. Since the paper's core innovation is the projection of non-decomposable constraints, its value would be significantly strengthened if the authors could demonstrate its effectiveness on at least one other score-decomposable learning algorithm. Demonstrating that the inferred decomposable constraints can enhance the performance and efficiency of another algorithm that cannot originally handle non-decomposable constraints would make a much more compelling case for the method's broad utility.

2.  **Performance Sensitivity and Explanation:** The results for EC trees in Table 2 exhibit a non-monotonic "easy-hard-easy" performance pattern as the proportion of ancestral constraints increases. The paper lacks an explanation for this phenomenon. Does this behavior suggest that the effectiveness of pruning the EC tree search space is highly sensitive to the quantity of domain knowledge supplied? This could imply that the number of ancestral constraints must be carefully tuned to achieve ideal performance, which would be a practical limitation. A discussion of why this pattern occurs and its implications would be valuable.

3.  **Incomplete Experimental Comparison:** Table 2 presents the Structural Hamming Distance (SHD) for the EC tree method but omits the corresponding results for the ILP approach under the same experimental conditions. To allow for a direct and fair comparison between the two methods, it is essential to include the ILP performance metrics in this table.

**Minor Suggestion:**

Regarding the paper's structure, Section 4.1 provides background on general Bayesian network (BN) structure learning. While BNs are foundational to EC trees, the experiments focus exclusively on the search space of EC trees. The paper's focus could be sharpened by reallocating the space from Section 4.1 to a more detailed explanation of EC trees and the specifics of the A* search algorithm as applied in this context. This would better align the background material with the paper's empirical contributions and make the paper more self-contained for readers focused on this specific problem domain.
