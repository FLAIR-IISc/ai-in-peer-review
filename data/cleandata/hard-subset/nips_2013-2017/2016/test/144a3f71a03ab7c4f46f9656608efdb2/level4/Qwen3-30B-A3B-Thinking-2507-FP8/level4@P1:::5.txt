

This paper addresses an important challenge in Bayesian network structure learning: incorporating non-decomposable ancestral constraints into optimal structure learning frameworks. The authors build upon Chen et al.'s recent framework for learning with non-decomposable scores [2015, 2016], which leverages oracles for decomposable scores to navigate the intractable search space of all DAGs. Specifically, they propose a method to convert ancestral constraints (which are non-decomposable) into decomposable constraints that can be handled by existing oracles, enabling the use of the EC tree search space [Chen et al., 2016] for efficient structure learning.

The paper makes a valuable contribution by providing a systematic method to project non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints. The authors develop theoretically sound algorithms for inferring these constraints, proving key theorems (Theorems 2-4) that establish the completeness and soundness of their approach. They then demonstrate through extensive empirical evaluation that this method leads to orders-of-magnitude improvements in runtime compared to ILP-based approaches (GOBNILP), particularly as problem size increases.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The technical preliminaries (Section 2) provide a solid foundation for understanding Bayesian network structure learning, and the explanations of ancestral constraints (Section 3) and the EC tree (Section 4) are particularly accessible. The authors effectively connect their work to previous research, properly citing relevant papers by Borboudakis and Tsamardinos [2012, 2013], Cussens [2008], and others who have explored constraint-based approaches to Bayesian network learning.

The empirical evaluation (Section 6) is thorough and well-designed. The authors simulate learning problems from standard benchmarks (ALARM, ANDES, etc.) with varying numbers of variables, dataset sizes, and constraint proportions. Their results consistently show that their approach outperforms ILP-based methods, with the runtime advantage increasing significantly as the number of variables grows. The analysis of structural Hamming distance (Table 3) further demonstrates that ancestral constraints can substantially improve learning accuracy, with even modest constraint sets (10-25%) having an impact comparable to substantially larger training datasets.

The paper makes a strong case for the significance of this work. Ancestral constraints are a natural way to incorporate domain knowledge into structure learning, and the authors convincingly demonstrate that their approach makes this practical by dramatically improving computational efficiency. This addresses a real limitation in current Bayesian network learning systems, which typically cannot effectively incorporate such constraints. The scalability to 20 variables (Table 3) is particularly impressive given the computational complexity of optimal structure learning.

The paper has only minor weaknesses. While the theoretical development is sound, the connection to the broader literature on constraint-based learning (e.g., PC algorithm) could be strengthened. The authors note that Borboudakis and Tsamardinos [2012] use ancestral constraints in constraint-based learning, but don't fully explain how their approach differs from and complements this line of work. Additionally, the MaxSAT formulation for ordering constraints (Section 5.2) is technically sound but could benefit from more intuitive explanation for readers unfamiliar with MaxSAT.

In terms of the NIPS review criteria:
- Quality: The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical results and comprehensive empirical validation. The authors carefully address both strengths (significant runtime improvements) and limitations (theoretical complexity of constraint projection).
- Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The EC tree concept is explained clearly with helpful figures (Figure 1), and the constraint projection method is logically presented.
- Originality: The key contribution—projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable ones—is novel and represents a meaningful extension of Chen et al.'s work. The paper properly distinguishes itself from prior approaches.
- Significance: The work addresses an important practical limitation in Bayesian network learning, with demonstrated efficiency gains that could enable real-world applications where domain knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints is available.

The paper's main strengths are:
1. A theoretically sound method for converting non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable ones
2. Clear exposition of the EC tree approach and constraint projection
3. Comprehensive empirical evaluation showing orders-of-magnitude runtime improvements
4. Demonstrated practical significance through scalability to 20 variables and meaningful accuracy gains

The main weaknesses are:
1. Limited discussion of how this approach relates to constraint-based learning methods
2. Some technical depth in the MaxSAT formulation could be made more accessible
3. The paper doesn't explore the trade-offs between constraint projection complexity and runtime benefits

Overall, this is a strong contribution that advances the state of the art in Bayesian network structure learning. The authors successfully address a significant practical limitation in the field, and their approach provides a valuable tool for researchers and practitioners who have background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints. The empirical results are compelling and the theoretical foundation is solid. The paper meets all the criteria for a high-quality NIPS submission and represents a meaningful advance in the field.

Pro-con acceptance list:
- Pro: Novel approach to handle ancestral constraints (non-decomposable) with significant efficiency gains

- Pro: Comprehensive empirical evaluation showing orders-of-magnitude improvements

The paper is clearly within the scope of NIPS, as it makes a significant technical contribution to a fundamental problem in machine learning. I recommend acceptance.
