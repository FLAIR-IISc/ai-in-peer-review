

This paper addresses constrained optimization for learning Bayesian networks from local scores under ancestral constraints. The search employs a branch-and-bound approach where nodes in the search tree are CPDAGs, progressing from CPDAG₁ to CPDAG₂ by approximately selecting a parent set for a Bayesian network variable absent in CPDAG₁. Effective symmetry breaking is implemented. The search utilizes system A, which leverages the URLearning system to provide a heuristic (details are referenced to an earlier paper). Ancestral constraints enable pruning of the search tree, with Lemma 3 (supplementary material) serving as the key theoretical result. While I believe Lemma 3 to be true, I do not fully comprehend its proof; specifically, the phrase "By the EC tree edge generation rules, Gₖ also contains edge Z → W" requires further elaboration. The paper uses "projected constraints" for constraints implied by ancestral constraints (standard terminology is "implied constraints"). These implied constraints, along with inferred ordering constraints (e.g., constraints on permissible topological orderings), facilitate pruning. However, the approach to inferring ordering constraints is inadequately explained. The claim that "we can infer Y < Z from Z not an ancestor of Y" is problematic: while there exist DAGs where Z is not an ancestor of Y but Y < Z is a valid ordering (e.g., a graph with only vertices Z and Y and no edges), the authors clearly intend to state that if Z is not an ancestor of Y, the constraint Y < Z may be added. The method thus aims to infer as many *consistent* ordering constraints as possible (not merely entailed ones), avoiding over-inference through a MAXSAT approach. Experiments vary the number of variables, data points, and ancestral constraints, comparing the proposed system to GOBNILP and demonstrating significantly faster performance. As noted by the authors, GOBNILP underperforms relative to A-based approaches without ancestral constraints when parent set cardinality is unbounded. Since the experiments here also lack such a bound, it is unclear whether GOBNILP's poor performance stems from its inherent limitations in handling large parent sets or specifically from poor handling of ancestral constraints. Additionally, the encoding of ancestral constraints (supplementary material) adopts a MAXSAT formulation from [Cussens, 2008] instead of the ILP encoding in Section 3.1.1 of [Cussens, 2010]. This MAXSAT encoding is suboptimal for an ILP system. A superior alternative would use A(X,Y) binary variables alongside I(Z,U) variables, omitting the cubic number of E(X,Y,Z) variables. Only transitivity constraints on A(X,Y) and the linking constraints A(X,Y) ≥ ∑_{U: X ∈ U} I(Y,U) would be needed (ensuring X parent of Y implies X ancestor of Y). This formulation yields a tighter linear relaxation and should significantly improve performance, as outlined in Section 3.1.1 of [Cussens, 2010] (adapted for partial, not total, ordering). Even with a sensible ILP formulation, the proposed system would likely outperform GOBNILP; the paper is weakened by comparing to a poor MAXSAT encoding rather than a well-established ILP approach. It would be valuable to compare against van Beek and Hoffmann (CP 2015), which frequently outperforms GOBNILP; adapting their method to accommodate ancestral constraints would be straightforward. The current paper omits this comparison.
