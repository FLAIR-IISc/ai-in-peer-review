This paper addresses the constrained optimization problem of learning Bayesian networks from local scores, incorporating ancestral constraints. The approach employs a branch-and-bound search, where nodes in the search tree represent CPDAGs. The search progresses by iteratively choosing a parent set for a variable not yet present in the current CPDAG. Symmetry breaking techniques are utilized, and the search is guided by a heuristic derived from the URLearning system (details of which are provided in a prior publication). Leveraging ancestral constraints allows for pruning of the search tree. The core theoretical result appears to be Lemma 3 (in the supplementary material), which I believe to be correct, though the provided proof lacks sufficient clarity. Specifically, the statement “By the EC tree edge generation rules, Gk also contains edge Z -> W” requires further elaboration to be fully understood. 

The paper also addresses *implied constraints*—constraints on edges that logically follow from the given ancestral constraints—and *ordering constraints*, derived from permissible topological orderings. The methodology for inferring these constraints is not sufficiently detailed. The statement that “we can infer Y < Z from Z not an ancestor of Y” is potentially misleading. While true that Z not being an ancestor of Y *allows* for the constraint Y < Z, it does not *entail* it; a DAG exists with no edge between Y and Z where Y precedes Z in a consistent ordering. The authors acknowledge this nuance and employ a MAXSAT approach to prudently infer only consistent ordering constraints, avoiding over-constraining the search.

Experimental evaluation varies the number of variables, data points, and ancestral constraints. The method is compared to GOBNILP, demonstrating significantly faster performance. However, the authors rightly point out that GOBNILP’s performance degrades without ancestral constraints when there’s no limit on parent set cardinality—a condition present in these experiments. Consequently, it’s unclear whether the observed performance gap stems from GOBNILP’s inherent difficulty with large parent sets or its specific challenges with ancestral constraints.

Furthermore, the encoding of ancestral constraints within the MAXSAT framework (based on Cussens, 2008) seems suboptimal. The ILP encoding presented in Cussens, 2010 (Section 3.1.1) offers a potentially more efficient alternative. The current MAXSAT encoding introduces a cubic number of variables (E(X,Y,Z)) that appear unnecessary. A tighter formulation could be achieved by incorporating binary variables A(X,Y) to represent ancestral relationships alongside the I(Z,U) variables, and omitting the E variables altogether. The necessary constraints would include transitivity constraints on A(X,Y) and linking constraints: A(X,Y) ≥ ∑{U:X ∈ U} I(Y,U). This mirrors the approach in Cussens, 2010, adapted for partial rather than total orderings, and should yield a superior linear relaxation and improved performance.

I am confident that even with a refined ILP formulation of the ancestral constraints, the proposed system would outperform GOBNILP. However, the comparison to a less-optimized encoding weakens the paper's claims. A more compelling evaluation would involve a comparison with the constraint programming approach of van Beek and Hoffmann (2015), which often surpasses GOBNILP’s performance. The authors do not cite this related work, which is a notable omission given its relevance.

In summary, the paper presents a promising approach to Bayesian network learning with ancestral constraints, demonstrating improved speed compared to GOBNILP. However, the clarity of the theoretical justification could be enhanced, and the experimental evaluation would benefit from a comparison to more competitive constraint-based methods, particularly van Beek and Hoffmann’s work, and from a more efficient encoding of ancestral constraints based on existing ILP formulations.
