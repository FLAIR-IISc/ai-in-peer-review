This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures subject to background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints. As the authors correctly point out, ancestral constraints are non-decomposable, which prevents their direct use in many efficient, score-based learning algorithms that rely on the assumption of decomposable scores.

The main contribution is a method to integrate these non-decomposable constraints into a recently proposed search framework based on Equivalence Class (EC) trees. The core idea is to "project" the non-decomposable ancestral constraints onto a set of decomposable constraints—specifically, edge constraints and topological ordering constraints. These inferred decomposable constraints can then be passed to the A* search oracle, which is typically a fast learner designed for decomposable scores. This "empowers" the oracle, allowing it to prune the search space more effectively and dramatically improve the efficiency of finding the optimal network structure that satisfies the original ancestral constraints. The authors formulate the inference of edge constraints as a direct logical derivation and the more complex inference of ordering constraints as a MaxSAT problem. Empirically, the paper demonstrates that this approach is orders of magnitude faster than a standard Integer Linear Programming (ILP) formulation for the same problem.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a significant and practical problem. Incorporating domain knowledge is crucial for learning accurate models, especially with limited data, and ancestral relationships are a natural way to express such knowledge. The proposed method of projecting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones that an efficient sub-routine (the oracle) can handle is a novel and clever contribution. It provides a bridge between the expressiveness of non-decomposable constraints and the efficiency of algorithms built for decomposable scores.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed projection methods are technically sound. The theorems provided for inferring edge constraints are clear, and the reduction to MaxSAT for finding a consistent set of ordering constraints is a well-founded approach. The overall framework, which uses an A* search over EC trees with an empowered oracle, is a complete and powerful system for exact structure learning under these constraints.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of providing the necessary background on score-based learning, EC trees, and ancestral constraints before detailing their own contributions in Section 5. The experimental setup and results are presented clearly.

4.  **Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are thorough and convincing. The comparison against a strong ILP-based baseline (GOBNILP) effectively highlights the performance benefits of the proposed approach. The study of performance across varying numbers of variables, dataset sizes, and constraint percentages provides a comprehensive view of the method's behavior. The results showing orders-of-magnitude speedup are impressive.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

While the paper is strong, there are a few areas that could be clarified or strengthened:

1.  **Generality of the Constraint Projection:** The results in Table 1 and Table 2 show that providing the projected constraints leads to massive speedups for the EC tree method but only modest improvements for the ILP approach (and sometimes a small overhead for n=12). The paper's core innovation is the projection method. To bolster its significance, it would be valuable for the authors to discuss *why* this projection has such a differential impact. Is the A* oracle uniquely suited to exploit these inferred edge/ordering constraints compared to how a modern ILP solver uses them as redundant constraints? This would help clarify the conditions under which this projection technique is most beneficial.

2.  **Analysis of the "Easy-Hard-Easy" Pattern:** The experiments in Table 3 reveal an interesting "easy-hard-easy" runtime pattern for the EC tree approach as the percentage of ancestral constraints increases. The paper briefly explains this as a transition from a nearly unconstrained problem to a highly constrained one. This is a plausible explanation, but a more detailed discussion would be welcome. Does this sensitivity imply that there is a "sweet spot" of constraint density that is particularly challenging, and are there practical implications for users of this method?

3.  **Comparison of Solution Quality:** Table 3 reports the structural Hamming distance (SHD) for the learned networks from the proposed method, which is a great inclusion. However, a direct comparison of the SHD for the networks learned by the ILP baseline is missing. Since both are exact methods, they should find the same optimal (and thus equally accurate) network. Confirming this would strengthen the claim that the proposed method achieves its speedup with no loss in solution quality.

4.  **Presentation Suggestion:** Section 4.1 on BN graphs serves as a useful conceptual introduction to EC trees. However, since the experiments and the core method rely exclusively on the EC tree framework, the authors could consider slightly condensing this section. The saved space could be used to further elaborate on the A* algorithm's implementation details within the EC tree search, which is central to the paper's experimental success.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper presenting a novel, significant, and well-evaluated contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. The weaknesses are minor and primarily relate to opportunities for deeper analysis and small presentation tweaks. The paper is a clear candidate for acceptance.
