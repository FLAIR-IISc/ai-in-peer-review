This paper addresses the problem of learning Bayesian networks from data using local scores, subject to a set of ancestral constraints. The proposed method employs a branch-and-bound A* search over the space of CPDAGs. The search proceeds by refining a CPDAG by selecting a parent set for a variable. The algorithm incorporates symmetry-breaking techniques and utilizes a heuristic derived from the URLearning system, with details provided in a prior publication.

The core of the contribution involves pruning the search space using the given ancestral constraints. This is formalized in Lemma 3 (supplementary material), which appears to be the key theoretical result. While the lemma itself seems correct, its proof is difficult to follow. Specifically, the statement "By the EC tree edge generation rules, Gk also contains edge Z -> W" requires further justification and explanation.

The method also leverages what the authors term "projected constraints"—more commonly known as implied constraints—to enable further pruning. These are additional edge and ordering constraints inferred from the initial set of ancestral constraints. However, the procedure for inferring these "ordering constraints" is not adequately explained. For instance, the statement that one "can infer Y < Z from Z not an ancestor of Y" is ambiguous. The word "can" suggests a possibility rather than a logical entailment; for example, if Z is not an ancestor of Y, the ordering Y < Z is consistent but not required (e.g., in a graph with no edge between Z and Y). The authors' intended meaning appears to be that they selectively add consistent ordering constraints to restrict the search space, using a MAXSAT approach to find a large set of such constraints without introducing contradictions. This crucial part of the methodology should be clarified.

The experimental evaluation demonstrates that the proposed system is significantly faster than the GOBNILP system on tasks with a varied number of variables, data points, and ancestral constraints. However, this comparison may be confounded by a known characteristic of the baseline. As the authors themselves note, GOBNILP is often outperformed by A*-based approaches in settings with an unlimited parent set cardinality, which is the setting used here. Consequently, it is unclear whether the observed performance gap is attributable to the proposed method's superior handling of ancestral constraints, or simply to GOBNILP's known inefficiency in this unbounded parent set scenario.

Furthermore, the comparison to GOBNILP is weakened by the choice of encoding for the ancestral constraints. The authors implemented a MAXSAT-based encoding from [Cussens, 2008], which is a poor formulation for an ILP solver. A much stronger ILP formulation, such as the one in [Cussens, 2010, Section 3.1.1], would likely yield significantly better performance for GOBNILP. This alternative formulation avoids a cubic number of `E(X,Y,Z)` variables and provides a much tighter linear relaxation by directly linking parent indicators `I(Y,U)` to ancestor indicators `A(X,Y)` via transitivity and constraints of the form `A(X,Y) >= Σ_{U: X ∈ U} I(Y,U)`. While the proposed system would likely still outperform GOBNILP even with this improved formulation, the current evaluation's strength is diminished by its comparison against a suboptimal baseline implementation.

Finally, the paper omits a comparison to the relevant work of van Beek and Hoffmann [CP 2015], whose constraint programming approach has also been shown to outperform GOBNILP. Given that their method could likely be adapted to incorporate ancestral constraints, a comparison would provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed system's performance. The absence of this citation and comparison is a notable omission.
