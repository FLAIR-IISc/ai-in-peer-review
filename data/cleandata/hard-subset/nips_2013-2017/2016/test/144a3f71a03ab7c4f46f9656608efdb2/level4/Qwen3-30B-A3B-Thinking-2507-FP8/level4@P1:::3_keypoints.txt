- Problem: Introduces incorporation of ancestral (non-decomposable) constraints into optimal Bayesian network structure learning.  
- Method: Leverages Chen et al.’s EC tree and translates ancestral constraints into decomposable ordering constraints via MaxSAT.  
- Oracle usage: Enables use of standard order-graph-based oracles by passing translated constraints to them.  
- Empirical result: Outperforms ILP-based methods (e.g., GOBNILP) by orders of magnitude across benchmark networks.  
- Efficiency: Constraint translation and projection into the EC tree provide dramatic pruning and speedups.  
- Theoretical rigor: Theorems 2–4 are rigorous, and the translation mechanism is technically sound and complete.  
- Clarity: Paper is well-written with precise definitions, intuitive examples, and a clear logical flow.  
- Novelty: Extends prior EC-tree work to handle a practically important class of constraints previously unsupported (novelty moderate).  
- Significance: Impact is moderate because the approach assumes availability of ancestral constraints, a niche but useful setting.  
- Realism concern: Experiments use 10–25% of possible ancestral constraints, which can encode near-complete graph knowledge and may not reflect typical sparse constraints.  
- Comparative gap: Lacks comparison to greedy optimal/search-with-oracle baselines (e.g., GES with oracle input) to separate benefits of constraint handling from exact search.  
- Theoretical clarifications needed: Theorem 1 should state that CPDAG satisfaction implies DAG satisfaction (directional clarification); Theorem 3’s practical limits with sparse constraints should be emphasized.  
- MaxSAT detail: Some MaxSAT rule forms (e.g., Rule 3’s asymmetric conclusion) need brief justification.  
- Wording: Phrase “empower the oracle” is misleading; better to say constraints are passed to or enforced for the oracle.  
- Recommendation: Accept — high-quality, technically robust contribution with minor clarifications needed.
