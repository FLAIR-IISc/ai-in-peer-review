### Summary

This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data when given background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints (e.g., variable X must be an ancestor of Y, or must not be). The core challenge is that such constraints are non-decomposable, making them incompatible with many efficient, mainstream structure learning algorithms that rely on decomposable scores.

The authors build upon a recent A*-based search framework over the space of equivalence classes (the "EC tree"), which can handle non-decomposable scores. The key contribution of this work is a method to "project" the non-decomposable ancestral constraints into a set of decomposable constraints (i.e., required/forbidden edges and pairwise variable orderings). These inferred decomposable constraints are then used to "empower" the oracle that computes the A* search heuristic, resulting in a much tighter lower bound and a significantly more efficient search. The authors propose a sound algorithm for inferring edge constraints and a novel reduction to MaxSAT for inferring a maximal set of consistent ordering constraints.

The proposed approach is empirically compared against an integer linear programming (ILP) based method (GOBNILP), demonstrating speedups of several orders of magnitude.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Elegant Contribution:** The central idea of projecting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones to tighten the heuristic for a search algorithm is both clever and powerful. It provides a principled way to bridge the gap between frameworks designed for non-decomposable problems and the highly efficient solvers that exist for decomposable subproblems.

2.  **Technically Sound:** The methods for inferring edge and ordering constraints are well-motivated and technically sound. The use of MaxSAT to find a globally consistent set of ordering constraints is a particularly novel and appropriate formulation for a complex inference problem.

3.  **Significant Empirical Improvement:** The experiments demonstrate dramatic performance gains. The proposed method consistently outperforms the ILP-based baseline by orders of magnitude, pushing the boundary of what is feasible for exact structure learning with this class of constraints. The paper shows scalability up to 20 variables, which is competitive for exact score-based methods.

4.  **Practical Relevance:** Ancestral constraints represent a natural and common form of expert knowledge (e.g., causal pathways). This work makes it significantly more practical to incorporate such knowledge into the learning process, which can improve both the accuracy of the learned model and the efficiency of the search, as shown in the analysis of the structural Hamming distance in Table 3.

### Weaknesses / Suggestions for Improvement

The primary weakness of the paper lies in the clarity of its experimental evaluation. While the results are strong, their presentation makes it difficult to isolate and appreciate the core contribution.

1.  **Ablation Study is Indirect:** The most important comparison for evaluating this paper's contribution is the performance of the EC tree search *with* the proposed constraint projection versus *without* it. This comparison is present, but it is split across Table 1 (with projection) and Table 2 (without projection). The reader must manually compare the "EC tree" columns across two separate tables to see the impact of the "empowerment" step. A single, unified table presenting the runtimes for `EC tree`, `EC tree + projection`, `GOBNILP`, and `GOBNILP + projection` would have made the contribution's impact much clearer and more direct.

2.  **Unclear Comparison in Table 3:** Table 3 presents an important scalability analysis for the proposed method. However, its relationship to the previous tables is not immediately obvious. The text suggests that this table evaluates the proposed method, which implies it is `EC tree + projection`. It would be helpful to explicitly state this in the table caption or the main text to avoid ambiguity.

3.  **Framing of the GOBNILP Comparison:** The paper demonstrates that `EC tree + projection` is much faster than `GOBNILP + projection`. This is a valuable result. However, the core claim is about the benefit of the projection method itself. The comparison of `GOBNILP` vs. `GOBNILP + projection` (deduced from Tables 1 and 2) shows that projection also helps the ILP solver, though not as dramatically. Given that the EC tree approach is already superior, the focus should be on how much the new work improves the *best existing method* for this problem class (the EC tree search), rather than primarily framing it as a win over a slower alternative.

### Detailed Comments

*   **Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed algorithms are sound and the claims are well-supported by extensive experiments. The theoretical results (Theorems 1-4) provide a solid foundation for the proposed methodology.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction and background sections clearly motivate the problem and review the necessary concepts. The main point of confusion, as noted above, is in the presentation of the experimental results. A clearer table structure and more direct discussion of the ablation would significantly improve the manuscript.

*   **Originality:** The work is highly original. While it builds upon the EC tree framework from Chen et al. [2016], the core contribution—a method for systematically inferring decomposable constraints from non-decomposable ancestral constraints to accelerate the search—is novel. The MaxSAT formulation for ordering constraints is also a new and significant contribution in this context.

*   **Significance:** The results are significant. The paper presents a method that makes exact Bayesian network learning with an important class of prior knowledge substantially more scalable. This is likely to be a useful technique for practitioners and a foundation for further research into incorporating other types of non-decomposable background knowledge into structure learning algorithms. The finding that a small amount of ancestral knowledge can have a similar effect on model accuracy as a large increase in data size is a compelling demonstration of the work's practical value.
