The paper describes an efficient method to incorporate ancestral constraints into optimal Bayesian network learning. It achieves this by translating a set of ancestral constraints into a (maximal) set of ordering constraints, which are then used within a score-based learning algorithm based on the EC tree introduced by [Chen et al., 2015]. Empirical evaluation against a modified ILP-based system, GOBNILP, demonstrates significant performance improvements in the given setting. Overall, the paper is reasonably well-written and clearly explains a conceptually straightforward solution. The problem addressed is interesting and novel, as optimal Bayesian network learning combined with ancestral constraints lacks a standard solution (novelty=3). However, the setting is somewhat artificial; when ancestral relations are known, more general causal discovery algorithms that accommodate unobserved confounders might be more appropriate (impact=2). 

The approach translates challenging-to-use ancestral constraints, which don’t fit well with decomposable-score-based algorithms, into sets of ordering constraints more easily expressed as edge absence constraints, which *do* fit decomposable scores. This mapping utilizes a MaxSAT solver to ensure all implied ancestral constraints are explicit. However, some of the rules (lines 257-261) appear incomplete or counterintuitive. For example, the acyclicity rule has a symmetric premise but an asymmetric conclusion; also, rule 4 seems to preclude ancestral constraints indicating the absence of a causal relationship between two variables. Furthermore, in practice, only a limited number of ancestral constraints are typically available, potentially limiting their impact on reducing the search space. The performance comparison with GOBNILP, augmented with linear constraints, is impressive, but the latter wasn’t specifically designed to tackle this problem. A more meaningful performance benchmark would involve ensuring the ancestral constraints align with the optimal model and comparing against a greedy algorithm like GES, which should produce identical results for oracle input. While greedy algorithms don’t guarantee optimality, this comparison would provide a more relevant indication of the method's efficiency gains.

Minor points: 
- Line 30: Is there a distinction between a standard oracle and an ‘omniscient’ oracle?
- Line 38: “Empower the oracle” could be rephrased as “use in combination with the oracle” for clarity.
- Line 90: Replace “optimizing” with “minimizing” for accuracy.
- Line 164: Theorem 1 should clarify that the set of constraints A satisfied by a CPDAG P on the EC tree represents a subset of the constraints A satisfied by the full DAG G; otherwise, the ‘if and only if’ condition doesn't hold.
- Lines 225-227: Theorem 3 might not yield significant positive edge implications, particularly with sparse ancestral constraints.
- Line 267: The connection to ensuring ‘acyclicity’ is not immediately obvious and could be clarified.
- Line 310: For reasonably sized graphs, 10-25% ancestral constraints suggest substantial prior knowledge about the target graph, which is not always typical in practice.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles a relevant and relatively unexplored problem: optimal Bayesian network learning with ancestral constraints.
*   The proposed approach is novel and offers a significant speedup compared to existing ILP-based methods.
*   The use of EC trees and the projection of constraints onto decomposable constraints are clever ideas.
*   The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the technical details.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The practical relevance of the setting might be limited; more general causal discovery algorithms might be preferred when ancestral knowledge is available.
*   Some of the theoretical justifications (specifically the rules for deriving ordering constraints) appear incomplete or questionable.
*   The experimental evaluation lacks a comparison against a strong baseline (e.g., a greedy algorithm with oracle input).
*   The impact of the proposed method on real-world problems isn’t clearly demonstrated.
