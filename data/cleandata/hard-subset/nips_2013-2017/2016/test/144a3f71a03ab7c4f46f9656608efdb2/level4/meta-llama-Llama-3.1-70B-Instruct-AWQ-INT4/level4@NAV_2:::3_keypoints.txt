- Paper proposes translating ancestral constraints into (maximal) ordering constraints and using them in a score-based EC-tree BN learning algorithm via a MaxSAT solver.  
- Empirical results show the method outperforms a straightforward ILP-modified Gobnilp baseline in the evaluated setting.  
- The solution is conceptually straightforward, well written, and of interest to a specialist audience (novel but not spectacular).  
- Novelty: addresses optimal BN learning with ancestral constraints where no standard solution exists (novelty score 3).  
- Impact concern: the setting is somewhat artificial — real causal problems with ancestral relations often require methods allowing hidden confounders (impact=2).  
- The MaxSAT mapping makes implied ancestral constraints explicit but some translation rules appear strange or incomplete (lines 257–261).  
- Specific rule issues: acyclicity rule 3 has a symmetric premise but an asymmetric conclusion; rule 4 may wrongly exclude constraints like “X and Y do not cause each other.”  
- Practical concern: typical use will provide few ancestral constraints, which may not meaningfully reduce the search space.  
- Baseline concern: comparing to Gobnilp with added linear constraints may be unfair because Gobnilp is not designed for this problem.  
- Suggested alternative baseline: compare against greedy algorithms like GES under the assumption ancestral constraints align with the optimal model to better gauge efficiency.  
- Theorem 1 needs clearer wording: constraints A satisfied by a CPDAG on the EC tree are only a subset of those satisfied by the full DAG G.  
- Theorem 3 likely yields few positive edge implications when ancestral constraints are sparse.  
- Line 267: the rule is not easily recognizable as encoding acyclicity (see rule concerns above).  
- Line 310: noting that 10–25% ancestral constraints would already imply extensive knowledge of the target graph, so that density is not typical.  
- Minor editorial queries: clarify “oracle” vs “omniscient oracle”, rephrase “empower the oracle”, and replace “optimizing” with “minimizing” at line 90.  
- Strengths summarized: novel problem + solution, solid theoretical basis, clear exposition, and useful specialist interest.  
- Weaknesses summarized: artificial setting, problematic translation rules, potentially unfair baseline comparison, and several minor technical/clarity issues.  
- Reviewer scores: Quality 7/10, Clarity 8/10, Originality 7/10, Significance 6/10.
