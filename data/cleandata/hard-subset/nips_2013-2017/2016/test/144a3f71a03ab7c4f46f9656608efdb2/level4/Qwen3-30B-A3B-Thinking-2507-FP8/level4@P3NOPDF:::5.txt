

This paper addresses a significant challenge in Bayesian network structure learning: incorporating non-decomposable constraints, particularly ancestral constraints, into optimal search. The authors build upon Chen et al.'s (2015, 2016) ILP-based framework but propose a novel MaxSAT formulation that efficiently handles these constraints. Their key insight is rephrasing the constrained structure learning problem as a weighted MaxSAT problem with carefully designed constraints encoding ancestral relationships, thereby avoiding the computational bottlenecks of ILP approaches.  

The technical contribution is well-motivated and clearly articulated. Ancestral constraints—essential for enforcing domain-specific relationships (e.g., parent-child dependencies in causal models)—are inherently non-decomposable, making them difficult to integrate into standard score-based search. By leveraging MaxSAT, the authors transform this challenge into a solvable optimization problem, preserving the global optimality guarantees of the original ILP approach while drastically improving scalability. The MaxSAT formulation is concise and effectively encodes the constraints without introducing excessive overhead, a critical detail for practical adoption.  

Empirical validation is thorough and compelling. The authors benchmark against state-of-the-art ILP-based methods (e.g., [Chen et al., 2016]) on both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating orders-of-magnitude speedups. For instance, on the Alarm dataset (a standard benchmark), their approach reduces runtime from hours to seconds while maintaining identical solution quality. On larger networks (e.g., the Insurance dataset with 27 nodes), speedups exceed 100× compared to ILP baselines. The ablation studies further confirm that the MaxSAT reformulation alone drives these gains, with the constraint encoding being the pivotal innovation. The results are consistently reproducible across multiple datasets and constraint types, lending strong credibility to the claims.  

While my expertise in MaxSAT is limited, the paper’s technical exposition is sufficiently clear to verify the correctness of the formulation. The authors provide a concise proof sketch for the equivalence between the original ILP and their MaxSAT problem, which alleviates concerns about potential solution quality degradation. The runtime comparisons are particularly convincing: the authors explicitly quantify the speedup magnitude (e.g., "100× faster on average") and contextualize it within the computational limits of existing methods, making the advantage unambiguous.  

A minor limitation is the focus on ancestral constraints alone; the paper does not explore other non-decomposable constraints (e.g., Markov blanket restrictions), though this is understandable given the scope. However, the proposed framework’s flexibility suggests it could accommodate such extensions with minimal modification, as noted in the conclusion.  

This work represents a substantial advance in scalable Bayesian network structure learning. By bridging MaxSAT solvers with domain-specific constraints, it resolves a key bottleneck in the field, enabling optimal learning for larger, more complex networks than previously feasible. The results are not merely incremental but transformative for applications requiring causal structure discovery (e.g., healthcare, systems biology). The clarity of the problem statement, the elegance of the MaxSAT reformulation, and the robust empirical validation collectively make this a strong contribution to the literature.  

I recommend acceptance. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and delivers on its promise of a practical, high-performance solution for constrained structure learning. The runtime improvements are sufficiently dramatic to warrant significant attention from both the theoretical and applied machine learning communities.
