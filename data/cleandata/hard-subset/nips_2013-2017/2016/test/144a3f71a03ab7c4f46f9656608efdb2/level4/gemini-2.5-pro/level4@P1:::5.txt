This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures when background knowledge is available in the form of ancestral constraints. These constraints are non-decomposable, which makes them incompatible with most standard, efficient score-based learning algorithms that rely on score decomposability.

The authors' approach extends the recent search framework of Chen et al. [2015, 2016], which navigates a search space of equivalence classes (the EC-tree) using an A*-like search guided by an oracle that can solve the simpler, decomposable version of the problem. The core contribution is a method for handling non-decomposable ancestral constraints within this framework. This is achieved by "projecting" the non-decomposable constraints into a set of decomposable edge and ordering constraints that can be handled by the oracle. This projection effectively empowers the oracle, allowing it to provide a much tighter heuristic and prune the search space more aggressively. A particularly novel aspect of this projection is the use of a MaxSAT solver to infer a maximal set of consistent ordering constraints.

**Strengths:**
The main strength of this paper is the impressive empirical results. The proposed method is shown to be orders of magnitude faster than a state-of-the-art Integer Linear Programming (ILP) based approach, which is one of the few other exact methods capable of handling such constraints. The experiments clearly demonstrate that the constraint projection technique is critical to achieving this performance leap. The runtime improvements make optimal structure learning with ancestral constraints practical for problems of a size that was previously infeasible.

The technical approach is elegant and well-motivated. The idea of translating non-decomposable global constraints into local, decomposable ones that an existing powerful solver (the oracle) can leverage is a strong and generalizable concept. The specific formulation for inferring ordering constraints as a MaxSAT problem is clever and appears to be highly effective.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper is well-executed and the claims are well-supported. While my expertise with MaxSAT solvers is limited, the results stemming from their use are compelling. The overall presentation is clear and the contribution is significant for the field of structure learning.

In summary, this is a strong paper that presents a novel and highly efficient algorithm for a challenging and important problem. The empirical evaluation is thorough and convincingly demonstrates the superiority of the proposed method over existing alternatives.
