### **Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data, subject to background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints (e.g., variable X must be an ancestor of Y). Such constraints are non-decomposable, which prevents their direct use in many efficient, state-of-the-art structure learning algorithms that rely on decomposable scores. The authors build upon a recent framework [Chen et al., 2015, 2016] that uses A* search on a space of equivalence classes (the "EC tree") and can handle non-decomposable scores. The core contribution of this work is a novel method to "empower" the oracle (heuristic) used in this A* search. The authors propose techniques to infer or "project" decomposable constraints (specifically, edge and topological ordering constraints) from the given non-decomposable ancestral constraints. These simpler, inferred constraints can be processed by the oracle, leading to a much tighter search heuristic and, as shown empirically, dramatic improvements in runtime.

### **Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a significant and practical problem. Incorporating expert domain knowledge is crucial for learning meaningful models, especially with limited data, and ancestral relations are an intuitive way to express such knowledge. The main technical contribution—a method to project non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints—is novel and clever. This idea of empowering a fast but limited oracle within a more general but slower search framework is an elegant and powerful approach.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The work is technically sound. The proposed methods for inferring edge constraints (Theorems 2 and 3) and ordering constraints via a reduction to MaxSAT (Theorem 4) are well-defined. The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The authors compare their method against a strong Integer Linear Programming (ILP) baseline, which is another standard approach for exact structure learning with complex constraints. The results demonstrate that the proposed method is orders of magnitude faster, clearly validating the effectiveness of the constraint projection technique.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. It effectively builds from the necessary background on score-based learning and the EC tree search space to the core technical contributions in Section 5. The experimental setup and results are presented clearly and support the paper's main claims.

### **Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors (Areas for Improvement)**

While this is a strong paper, I have a few questions and suggestions that could further improve the manuscript:

1.  **The "Easy-Hard-Easy" Pattern:** In Table 3, the authors observe an "easy-hard-easy" pattern in runtime for their method as the percentage of ancestral constraints increases. The paper provides an intuitive explanation for this. It would be beneficial for the reader if the authors could elaborate on this phenomenon. For instance, is this behavior characteristic of A*-based searches in this domain, where a moderately constrained problem is harder to prune effectively than either a nearly unconstrained or a heavily constrained one? This contrasts with the ILP approach, which appears to benefit more monotonically from additional constraints. A deeper discussion could provide valuable insights for practitioners.

2.  **Comparison of Solution Quality:** Table 3 reports the Structural Hamming Distance (SHD) for the proposed EC tree method. As both the proposed method and the ILP baseline are exact, they should theoretically return the same optimal structure (and thus have the same SHD) if they both run to completion. It would be helpful to state this explicitly for clarity. A relevant point to mention would be whether the ILP baseline timed out on instances where the proposed method succeeded, which would make the SHD comparison particularly relevant in practice.

3.  **Generalizability of Constraint Projection:** The speedup from projecting constraints is dramatic for the proposed EC-tree method, but the benefit for the ILP baseline is less consistent (Tables 1 and 2 show a slight overhead for n=12 but improvement for n=14). Could the authors comment on why adding these redundant constraints to the ILP formulation has a less pronounced positive impact compared to their effect on the A* oracle? This might suggest that the primary power of this projection technique lies specifically in tightening A* heuristics, which is an interesting finding in its own right.

4.  **Presentation Suggestion:** Section 4.1 provides a good introduction to the BN graph, which is the conceptual precursor to the EC tree. However, since the methodology and experiments focus exclusively on the EC tree, the authors could consider slightly condensing this section. The saved space could be used to provide a little more detail on the A* algorithm's operation within the EC tree framework, which is central to the paper's implementation.

### **Recommendation**

This is a high-quality paper with a novel and significant contribution to Bayesian network structure learning. The proposed method for handling non-decomposable ancestral constraints is elegant, well-supported by theory, and shown to be highly effective in a strong empirical evaluation. The weaknesses identified are minor and primarily relate to opportunities for deeper discussion and clarification. I recommend acceptance.
