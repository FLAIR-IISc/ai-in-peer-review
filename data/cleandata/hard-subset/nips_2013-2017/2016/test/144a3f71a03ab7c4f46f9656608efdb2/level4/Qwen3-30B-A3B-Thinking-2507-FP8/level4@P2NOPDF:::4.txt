

This paper introduces a method to project non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and topological ordering constraints, enabling existing decomposable-score-based structure learning approaches to incorporate domain knowledge and improve efficiency. The core innovation lies in Section 5, where the authors formalize this projection framework. The paper is well-written, with clear theoretical exposition and experimental validation. However, three concerns require clarification to strengthen the contribution:  

i. **Generalizability to diverse learning approaches**: Table 3 indicates that projected constraints only enhance EC tree performance, not ILP. This raises questions about the method’s scope: does the projection technique apply exclusively to EC trees, or does it lack generalizability for other structure learning frameworks? Given the paper’s central claim—that converting non-decomposable constraints to decomposable forms enables broader applicability—the results would be more compelling if the authors demonstrated performance gains across multiple approaches (e.g., ILP, greedy search) that inherently cannot handle ancestral constraints. Without such evidence, the practical utility of the projection method remains limited to EC trees.  

ii. **Sensitivity of constraint quantity**: Table 2 reveals an "easy-hard-easy" performance pattern for EC trees as ancestral constraint proportion increases. Why does this occur? Is the search-space reduction from ancestral constraints highly sensitive to the *amount* of domain knowledge incorporated? If so, this suggests that optimal performance requires careful tuning of constraint quantities, which may undermine the method’s practicality. The authors should investigate whether this pattern stems from constraint conflicts, over-constraining the search space, or other factors, and clarify whether the ILP approach exhibits similar sensitivity.  

iii. **Comparative performance metrics**: Table 2 reports structural Hamming distance (SHD) *only* for EC trees under ancestral constraints but omits direct comparison with ILP. How do EC tree results (with projected constraints) stack against ILP (with the same constraints)? Without this comparison, it is unclear whether the EC tree’s improved SHD represents a genuine advantage or merely reflects the inherent limitations of ILP under the same constraints. Including ILP SHD in Table 2 would clarify whether the projection method’s benefits are specific to EC trees or applicable more broadly.  

iv. **Streamlined presentation**: Section 4.1 discusses Bayesian network (BN) theory as the foundation for EC trees. However, since the experiments exclusively evaluate EC trees with ancestral constraints (not general BNs), this section appears redundant. The authors should either integrate BN background into the EC tree explanation or omit Section 4.1 to focus on refining the EC tree and A* algorithm details. This would improve conciseness and align the theory with the experimental scope.  

In summary, the paper addresses a meaningful gap in constraint-based structure learning, but the current evaluation does not sufficiently demonstrate the projection method’s general utility beyond EC trees. Addressing the sensitivity of constraint quantities, providing cross-method comparisons, and streamlining the theoretical exposition would significantly strengthen the contribution. The core idea is promising, but the current results lack the breadth needed to establish its broader impact.
