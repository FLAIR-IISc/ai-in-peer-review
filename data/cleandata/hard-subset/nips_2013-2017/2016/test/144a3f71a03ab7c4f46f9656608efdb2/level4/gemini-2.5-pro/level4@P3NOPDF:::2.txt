This paper introduces a novel method for improving exhaustive search algorithms by incorporating ancestral constraints into the admissible heuristic. The core idea is to leverage this side-information to prune the search space more effectively, thereby accelerating the discovery of optimal solutions.

While the proposed method is interesting, I found the experimental evaluation to be a source of confusion, which weakened the paper's overall impact. My primary concern lies with the structure of the comparisons and the clarity of the results presented.

My understanding of the experiments, particularly Table 1, is that it compares the performance of two existing baseline algorithms (EC and GOB) *after* each has been augmented with the proposed method. That is, the comparison is between (EC + Proposed Method) and (GOB + Proposed Method). Conversely, Table 3 appears to present a direct comparison between the original EC and GOB baselines, without the proposed enhancements. I would appreciate it if the authors could confirm whether this interpretation is correct, as it is central to assessing the contribution.

Assuming my interpretation is accurate, the current evaluation is missing a crucial component. To clearly demonstrate the value of the proposed contribution, a direct ablation study is essential. The most informative comparison would be to evaluate the performance of the state-of-the-art baseline both with and without the proposed enhancement. Specifically, the paper would be significantly strengthened by an explicit comparison between (EC + Proposed Method) and the original EC.

While the comparison between an augmented GOB and the original GOB is also of interest, the results in Table 3 suggest that EC is a significantly stronger baseline. Therefore, even if the proposed method provides a substantial relative improvement to GOB, the community is most interested in whether it can advance the state-of-the-art, which is represented by EC. Establishing the absolute performance gain over the strongest baseline is critical for understanding the practical impact of this work. Without this direct comparison, it is difficult to isolate the benefit of the proposed heuristic from the underlying performance of the search algorithm it is applied to.

In summary, the paper presents a promising idea, but its empirical validation is not yet convincing due to the experimental design. I would strongly recommend that the authors restructure their experiments to include a clear ablation study that directly compares the strongest baseline (EC) with and without the proposed method. This would allow for a much clearer and more compelling demonstration of the work's contribution.
