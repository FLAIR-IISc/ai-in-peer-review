The paper presents an efficient method to incorporate ancestral constraints into optimal Bayesian network (BN) learning from data. It converts a given set of ancestral constraints into a maximal set of ordering constraints, which are then enforced within a score-based learning algorithm built on the EC tree of Chen et al. (2015). The method is evaluated against a straightforward modification of the ILP-based Gobnilp system and shows clear performance gains in this setting. The paper is generally well written and the ideas are clearly explained, although the core idea is conceptually straightforward. Novel problem plus solution; a nice result—not spectacular, but of definite interest to a specialist audience.

Novelty and impact: The problem is interesting and new in that optimal BN learning under ancestral constraints lacks a standard, readily available solution (novelty = 3). However, part of the reason for this gap is that the setting is somewhat artificial: once ancestral relations are introduced, it often makes more sense to use more general causal discovery methods that can accommodate unobserved confounders (impact = 2).

Approach: The key idea is to translate hard-to-use ancestral constraints—which do not fit neatly into decomposable score-based frameworks—into sets of ordering constraints that are easier to express in terms of edge absences and are therefore compatible with decomposable scores. The mapping is obtained via a MaxSAT solver that makes all implied ancestral constraints explicit.

Concerns about the rule system (lines 257–261): Some of the stated rules seem odd or incomplete. For example, the premise in the acyclicity rule 3 is symmetric—(X < Y) and (Z < W) is equivalent to (Z < W) and (X < Y)—but the conclusion is not (Y -/-> Z). Rule 4 also appears to exclude ancestral constraints of the form “X and Y do not cause each other.” These deserve clarification or correction.

Practicality and evaluation: In typical applications, one would expect only a small number of ancestral constraints, which are unlikely to shrink the search space dramatically. The comparison against Gobnilp augmented with linear constraints looks strong, but Gobnilp was not designed for this class of constraints, so this may not be the most informative baseline. A more meaningful efficiency assessment would assume that the ancestral constraints are consistent with the optimal model and compare against a greedy method such as GES; under oracle input, GES should produce the same output. While greedy methods cannot guarantee optimality, such a comparison would better indicate the practical efficiency gains of the proposed approach.

Minor comments:
- Line 30: Is there a distinction between a standard “oracle” and an “omniscient oracle”?
- Line 38: “empower the oracle” reads awkwardly—do you mean “use in combination with the oracle”?
- Line 90: Please note explicitly that “optimizing” here means “minimizing.”
- Line 164: Theorem 1 should state more clearly that the set of constraints A satisfied by the CPDAG P on the EC tree is a subset of the set of constraints A satisfied by the full DAG G; otherwise the “iff” claim does not hold.
- Lines 225–227: Theorem 3 seems unlikely to yield many positive edge implications when ancestral constraints are sparse (small sets).
- Line 267: The stated rule is not easily recognizable as encoding acyclicity (see also the concern above).
- Line 310: For realistic graph sizes, specifying 10%–25% of all ancestral constraints would already encode extensive knowledge of the target graph; this is not typical in practice.

Overall: A clear, well-executed paper addressing a novel but somewhat specialized problem. The main idea—compiling ancestral constraints into ordering constraints via MaxSAT and leveraging EC-tree search—is sound and practically useful in the stated setting, though the evaluation could be strengthened with more appropriate baselines and the rule set requires clarification.
