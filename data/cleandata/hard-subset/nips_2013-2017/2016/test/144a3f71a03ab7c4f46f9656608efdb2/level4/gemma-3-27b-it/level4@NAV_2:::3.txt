The paper addresses the problem of learning Bayesian networks optimally under ancestral constraints, a valuable contribution given the frequent need to incorporate background knowledge in practical applications. The core idea is to translate non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable ordering constraints, leveraging an existing EC tree-based structure learning framework [Chen et al., 2015] and empowering its oracle with these derived constraints. This allows for significant efficiency gains compared to alternative approaches like integer linear programming (ILP). 

The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the concepts and methodology. It effectively builds upon the previous work of Chen et al. [2015, 2016] and demonstrates a sound understanding of the challenges associated with non-decomposable constraints in Bayesian network structure learning. The empirical evaluation, comparing against GOBNILP, convincingly showcases the computational advantages of the proposed approach.

The originality of the work lies in the specific combination of techniques—using MaxSAT to project ancestral constraints into ordering constraints within the EC tree framework—to achieve efficient optimal learning. While the individual components (EC trees, MaxSAT, ancestral constraints) are not novel in isolation, their integration for this specific problem is a worthwhile contribution (Originality: 3/5). The significance stems from the potential to handle problems with background knowledge that are intractable for existing methods, although the practical impact might be limited by the assumptions about the prevalence of strong ancestral constraints (Significance: 2/5).

The technical soundness of the paper appears strong. However, some aspects of the constraint projection method warrant closer scrutiny. The rules presented in lines 257-261, used for inferring constraints, seem potentially incomplete or asymmetric, and the justification for their correctness could be strengthened. Specifically, the acyclicity rule's symmetry is questionable, and the exclusion of mutual non-causality in rule 4 might be overly restrictive. Furthermore, the effectiveness of this approach hinges on the assumption that a reasonable number of ancestral constraints are available, which may not always hold in practice. The discussion of this assumption (lines 310-312) could be expanded.

Regarding clarity, the paper is generally well-written, though some phrasing could be improved (e.g., line 38: "empower the oracle" could be replaced with "use in conjunction with the oracle"). Theorem 1 would benefit from a clarification that the constraints satisfied by a CPDAG on the EC tree represent a subset of the constraints satisfied by any DAG within its equivalence class. Minor issues are noted throughout the review.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important problem: optimal Bayesian network learning with background knowledge.
*   Presents a novel and technically sound solution based on a well-defined framework.
*   Demonstrates significant empirical improvements over an existing ILP-based approach.
*   Clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The assumption of sufficient ancestral constraints may limit practical applicability.
*   The rules for projecting ancestral constraints require further justification and analysis.
*   The empirical evaluation could be strengthened by comparison against a more relevant baseline, such as a greedy algorithm (e.g., GES) with oracle input.
*   The potential impact may be limited by the specific setting and the strength of the constraints assumed.



**Overall Assessment:**  I recommend acceptance, contingent upon the authors addressing the concerns raised regarding the completeness and justification of the constraint projection rules, and potentially including an additional baseline comparison in the experimental evaluation. The work represents a solid contribution to the field, offering a practical and efficient solution to a challenging problem.
