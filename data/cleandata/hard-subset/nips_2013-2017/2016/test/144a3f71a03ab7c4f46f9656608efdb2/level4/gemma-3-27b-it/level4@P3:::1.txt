This paper addresses the problem of learning Bayesian networks optimally under ancestral constraints, a challenging task due to the non-decomposability of these constraints. The authors build upon a recent framework utilizing an oracle for decomposable scores, cleverly adapting it to handle ancestral constraints by inferring and applying decomposable constraints. The approach involves searching a space of CPDAGs using A* search, guided by a heuristic leveraging the oracle (URLearning). Key contributions include a pruning strategy for the search space based on ancestral constraints and a method for “projecting” these constraints into decomposable edge and ordering constraints to further empower the oracle.

The paper is well-motivated, as incorporating background knowledge is crucial for practical Bayesian network learning, and ancestral constraints are a natural way to represent such knowledge. The core idea of decomposing non-decomposable constraints is sound and potentially impactful. The authors correctly identify the limitations of traditional approaches that rely on decomposable scores and highlight the efficiency gains achievable by their method. The use of MaxSAT for inferring ordering constraints is a pragmatic approach to balancing constraint tightness and feasibility.

However, the paper suffers from some clarity issues and a potentially incomplete comparison to existing work. The explanation of how the oracle is “empowered” with the projected constraints lacks detail; it would benefit from a more explicit description of how these constraints are passed to and utilized by URLearning. The proof of Lemma 3, central to the pruning strategy, is referenced but not fully elucidated, hindering full understanding. The discussion surrounding the inference of ordering constraints is initially confusing. While the authors acknowledge the nuanced meaning of “can infer” and clarify their intent to find *consistent* constraints, this point could be presented more directly and less indirectly.

A significant weakness lies in the experimental comparison. While the authors demonstrate substantial speedups compared to GOBNILP, they acknowledge a suboptimal ILP encoding was used.  The comparison would be significantly strengthened by using a more competitive ILP formulation, such as the one outlined in [Cussens, 2010], which utilizes a tighter linear relaxation and directly incorporates ancestral constraints via binary variables.  Furthermore, the paper fails to consider van Beek and Hoffmann’s constraint programming approach [van Beek and Hoffmann, 2015], which is frequently competitive with, or outperforms, GOBNILP and could offer a more direct comparison. The lack of comparison to this method is a notable omission.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Approach:** The method of decomposing ancestral constraints to leverage a decomposable-score oracle is a novel and promising strategy.
* **Efficiency Gains:** Empirical results demonstrate significant performance improvements over the compared ILP-based system.
* **Soundness:** The theoretical foundation for pruning the search space based on ancestral constraints appears solid (though the Lemma 3 proof needs more explanation).
* **Practical Relevance:** Addressing ancestral constraints is important for real-world Bayesian network learning.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity:**  Some explanations are unclear, particularly regarding the oracle empowerment and ordering constraint inference.
* **Incomplete Comparison:** The comparison to ILP uses a suboptimal encoding, and van Beek and Hoffmann's approach is not considered.
* **Missing Details:**  More detail is needed regarding how the projected constraints are used within the URLearning oracle.
* **Lemma Proof:** The proof of Lemma 3, crucial for understanding the pruning strategy, is not provided in full.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to a challenging problem.
* The empirical results, even with the limitations of the comparison, demonstrate significant efficiency gains.
* The theoretical contributions, particularly regarding constraint decomposition, are valuable.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The lack of a thorough comparison to state-of-the-art ILP formulations and constraint programming approaches weakens the claims of superiority.
* Clarity issues and missing details hinder full understanding and reproducibility.
* The incomplete proof of Lemma 3 raises concerns about the rigor of the theoretical foundation.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a worthwhile contribution to the field, but it falls short of being a strong accept. The core ideas are compelling, and the results are promising, but the lack of a comprehensive comparison and clarity issues prevent a confident recommendation.  I recommend **weak reject** with the suggestion that the authors address the concerns raised above, particularly by incorporating a more competitive ILP baseline and comparing to van Beek and Hoffmann’s approach.  Addressing the clarity issues and providing a complete proof for Lemma 3 would also significantly strengthen the paper.
