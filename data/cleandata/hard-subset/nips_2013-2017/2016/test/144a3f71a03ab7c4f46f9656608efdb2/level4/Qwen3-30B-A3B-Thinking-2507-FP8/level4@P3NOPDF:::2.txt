

The paper proposes a method that leverages ancestral constraints to enhance the heuristic for an exhaustive search algorithm, with the goal of improving search efficiency under domain-specific constraints. While the core idea is theoretically sound and aligns with prior work on constraint-based search, the experimental evaluation suffers from critical ambiguities that undermine the validity of the claimed contributions. Specifically, the presentation of results does not clearly demonstrate the incremental value of the proposed method, raising significant concerns about the paper’s central claim.  

The primary confusion arises from the experimental comparisons presented in Tables 1 and 3. Table 1 compares (EC + proposed method) against (GOB + proposed method'), where "EC" and "GOB" denote baseline search algorithms, and "proposed method'" appears to be a modified or alternative version of the authors’ approach applied to GOB. However, Table 3’s description—stating it compares EC to GOB—creates ambiguity: does Table 3 report the *base* performance of EC versus GOB (without the authors’ method), or does it inadvertently include the proposed method in one or both comparisons? The paper does not explicitly clarify this, leaving readers to infer whether Table 3 reflects the original baselines or the modified approaches. This lack of transparency is problematic because it obscures the true contribution of the proposed method.  

More critically, the authors fail to present a direct comparison between EC (the stronger baseline) and (EC + proposed method). As the paper states, EC already outperforms GOB significantly in the results (e.g., Table 3 suggests EC achieves substantially higher accuracy or efficiency), yet the evaluation focuses on comparing the *combined* approaches (EC + method vs. GOB + method') rather than isolating the method’s impact on the dominant baseline. This is a fundamental oversight. For instance, if EC achieves 90% accuracy and GOB 60% (as implied by typical results in such settings), a 5% improvement from the proposed method on EC (to 95%) would be more valuable than a 20% improvement on GOB (to 72%), even if the relative gain is larger for GOB. The paper’s current design does not establish whether the method delivers meaningful absolute gains on EC, which is the most relevant baseline for claiming practical utility.  

The absence of this comparison is especially troubling given the paper’s emphasis on "admissible heuristics" and "exhaustive search"—domains where small improvements on strong baselines are often the most impactful. Without demonstrating that the proposed method *adds value to EC*, the work risks overstating its significance. The authors’ focus on (EC + method) vs. (GOB + method') inadvertently shifts attention from the method’s true potential to a relative comparison between two baselines, which is secondary to the core contribution. This design choice also conflates the method’s effect with the inherent superiority of EC, making it impossible to attribute performance gains solely to the proposed technique.  

To address this, the authors should revise the experimental section to include a clear comparison between EC (without the method) and (EC + proposed method). This would directly test whether the method provides a measurable improvement over the strongest baseline, which is essential for validating its utility. Additionally, the paper should explicitly state whether Table 3 reports base results (EC vs. GOB) or includes the proposed method, and if the latter, clarify the exact setup. For example, if Table 3 is meant to show base performance, it should be labeled as "Baseline Comparison (EC vs. GOB)" and separated from tables reporting method-enhanced results.  

The current experimental narrative also lacks depth in explaining *why* the proposed method works better on EC than GOB, or whether the gains are consistent across different problem instances. The paper mentions that ancestral constraints are leveraged, but it does not analyze how these constraints interact with EC’s structure versus GOB’s, nor does it quantify the sensitivity of the method to constraint quality. Without such analysis, the reader cannot assess the method’s robustness or generalizability.  

In summary, while the theoretical foundation is promising, the experimental evaluation is insufficient to support the paper’s claims. The confusion between baseline and method-enhanced comparisons, coupled with the missing (EC + method) vs. EC test, prevents a clear assessment of the method’s value. To strengthen the submission, the authors must: (1) clarify the experimental setup in Tables 1 and 3; (2) add a direct comparison of EC with and without the proposed method; and (3) provide analysis on how the method’s performance varies with constraint characteristics. Without these revisions, the work’s contribution remains ambiguous, and its practical relevance is difficult to justify. The paper has potential but requires significant refinement to meet the standards of NIPS.
