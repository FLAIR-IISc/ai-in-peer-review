

The authors propose a method that incorporates ancestral constraints to refine the admissible heuristic for an exhaustive search algorithm. While the core idea is interesting, the experimental evaluation lacks clarity in its presentation. Specifically, Table 1 compares (EC + new work) against (GOB + new work'), yet Table 3 appears to compare EC directly against GOB. This creates ambiguity: does Table 3 supersede Table 1’s comparison, or are these complementary analyses? The relationship between these tables is unclear, making it difficult to assess the method’s contribution relative to prior work.  

A more direct and meaningful comparison would explicitly evaluate (EC + new work) against EC alone. This is critical because EC already substantially outperforms GOB in the baseline (as shown in Table 3), rendering the comparison between (GOB + new work') and GOB less central to the paper’s main claim. Even if (GOB + new work') yielded significantly larger gains over GOB than (EC + new work) did over EC, the absolute performance of (EC + new work) would still dominate due to EC’s strong baseline advantage. Thus, the primary validation should focus on whether the new work enhances EC, not whether it benefits GOB more than EC.  

The current experimental design risks obscuring the method’s true value. For instance, if (EC + new work) improves only marginally over EC but (GOB + new work') shows large gains over GOB, the paper might inadvertently suggest the new work is more impactful for GOB—a conclusion that misrepresents the relative significance of the contributions. To strengthen the argument, the authors should replace or supplement Table 1 with a clear comparison of (EC + new work) versus EC, alongside the existing GOB-related results. This would directly address the paper’s central claim about leveraging ancestral constraints to improve an existing strong baseline (EC), rather than shifting focus to GOB.  

Additionally, the description of "new work'" in Table 1 (implied to be a modified version of the proposed method) is confusing. If "new work'" is identical to the method presented in the paper, the notation should be consistent (e.g., "new work" for both). If it represents a variant, this must be explicitly defined to avoid misinterpretation. Without this clarity, the reader cannot determine whether the comparison in Table 1 is fair or whether the results reflect the proposed approach.  

In summary, the paper’s experimental section requires restructuring to prioritize the most relevant comparison: (EC + new work) versus EC. The current presentation risks overemphasizing GOB-related results, which are less indicative of the method’s primary contribution. Clarifying the relationship between Tables 1 and 3, and refocusing the evaluation on EC’s enhancement, would significantly strengthen the paper’s technical argument.
