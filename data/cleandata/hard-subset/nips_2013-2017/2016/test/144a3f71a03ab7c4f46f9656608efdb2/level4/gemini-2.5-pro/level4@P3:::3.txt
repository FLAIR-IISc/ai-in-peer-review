### Summary

This paper presents a novel and efficient algorithm for learning the optimal structure of a Bayesian network from data, subject to a set of user-provided ancestral constraints. The core challenge is that ancestral constraints (e.g., "X is an ancestor of Y") are non-decomposable, making them incompatible with many standard, efficient score-based learning algorithms that rely on decomposable scores.

The authors' approach builds upon the recently developed EC tree search framework, which navigates a search space of equivalence classes of DAGs and uses an "oracle" (an exact solver for problems with decomposable scores) to compute heuristic estimates. The main contribution is a method to "project" the non-decomposable ancestral constraints into a set of decomposable edge and topological ordering constraints. These inferred decomposable constraints can then be passed to the oracle, significantly improving its heuristic estimates and pruning the search space. The inference of topological ordering constraints is particularly novel, framed as a MaxSAT problem. The authors empirically demonstrate that their approach is orders of magnitude faster than a baseline system based on Integer Linear Programming (ILP) that is adapted to handle these constraints.

### Strengths and Arguments for Acceptance

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles an important and practical problem. Incorporating background knowledge is crucial for learning accurate models, especially with limited data, and ancestral relationships are a natural form for this knowledge to take. The proposed method for "projecting" non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones that can be leveraged by existing efficient solvers is a novel and valuable contribution. This work provides a principled and effective solution to a problem for which no standard, efficient optimal method previously existed.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed method is technically sound and well-motivated. The use of the EC tree search framework is appropriate, and the reduction of the ordering constraint inference problem to MaxSAT is clever. The theoretical results (Theorems 1-4) formally connect the ancestral constraints to the inferred decomposable ones. The empirical evaluation is solid, comparing against a relevant exact method (ILP) and showing substantial performance improvements across various datasets and experimental conditions.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The authors effectively explain the key challenge posed by non-decomposable constraints and lucidly describe how their proposed solution overcomes it. The distinction between the main search algorithm and the oracle, and how they interact, is well-articulated.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Problem Positioning:** The practical motivation could be situated more broadly. Knowledge of ancestral relations often arises in causal discovery contexts. In such settings, researchers are also frequently concerned with the possibility of unobserved confounders, which standard Bayesian network models cannot represent. A brief discussion on how this work relates to the broader field of causal discovery, which uses different models (e.g., Maximal Ancestral Graphs) and algorithms, would strengthen the paper's positioning and clarify the specific set of assumptions under which this method is applicable.

2.  **Clarity of the MaxSAT Formulation:** The MaxSAT formulation for inferring ordering constraints in Section 5.2 is a key contribution but could benefit from further clarification.
    *   The third hard constraint, intended to ensure acyclicity, appears asymmetric. The premise `(X < Y) ∧ (Z < W)` is symmetric with respect to the pairs `(X,Y)` and `(Z,W)`, but the conclusion includes `Y 6 Z` without a corresponding symmetric term like `W 6 X`. Please clarify the logic behind this rule and why it is sufficient.
    *   The fourth soft constraint, `(X 6 Y) ⇒ (Y < X)`, seems to pose a challenge when mutual non-ancestry constraints (i.e., both `X 6 Y` and `Y 6 X`) are provided. This would lead to the contradictory inference that `Y < X` and `X < Y`. How does the MaxSAT solver handle this common and important type of constraint?

3.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The comparison to GOBNILP is appropriate as a comparison between two optimal methods. However, to better contextualize the practical efficiency of the proposed algorithm, a comparison against a strong heuristic baseline would be valuable. For instance, how does the method's runtime and solution quality compare to a state-of-the-art greedy algorithm like GES, modified to respect the given ancestral constraints? While GES is not optimal, it is widely used, and such a comparison would provide a more complete picture of the performance landscape.

### Minor Comments and Questions for Clarification

*   Throughout the paper, the terms "oracle" and "omniscient oracle" are used. Is there a technical distinction, or could the terminology be made consistent for clarity?
*   L90: A minor but helpful addition would be to state explicitly that optimizing the score corresponds to minimizing it in your setting.
*   L164 (Theorem 1): The theorem's statement could be more precise. Does it mean that if a CPDAG `P` satisfies a set of constraints `A` over its variables `X`, its descendants will satisfy the same set of constraints `A`? This seems non-trivial as descendants involve a superset of variables. Please clarify the exact scope of the constraints being satisfied by the descendants.
*   L225 (Theorem 3): This theorem seems to require very strong conditions to infer a positive edge constraint, as it involves checking conditions for *all* other variables `Z`. Could you comment on the practical utility of this theorem, particularly when the initial set of ancestral constraints is sparse?
*   The experiments with 10%–25% of all possible ancestral relations known represent a scenario with substantial prior knowledge. It would be interesting to see results for smaller percentages to understand how the method's performance scales with the amount of available background knowledge.
