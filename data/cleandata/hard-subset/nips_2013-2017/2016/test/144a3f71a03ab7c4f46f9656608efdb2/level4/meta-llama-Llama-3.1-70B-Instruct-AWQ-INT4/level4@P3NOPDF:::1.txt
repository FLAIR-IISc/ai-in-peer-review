This paper addresses the constrained optimization problem of learning Bayesian networks from local scores with ancestral constraints. The approach employs a branch-and-bound search, where the nodes in the search tree are CPDAGs (completed partially directed acyclic graphs). The search progresses by selecting a parent set for a Bayesian network variable not present in the current CPDAG. Symmetry breaking is applied to reduce the search space. The URLearning system is utilized to provide a heuristic for the A* search. Although the details of this integration are not provided in the paper, they can be found in an earlier publication.

The incorporation of ancestral constraints enables pruning of the search tree, with Lemma 3 in the supplementary material being a crucial result. While the reviewer believes the lemma to be true, the proof is not entirely clear, particularly the statement "By the EC tree edge generation rules, Gk also contains edge Z -> W," which requires further explanation.

In addition to explicit ancestral constraints, the authors also consider implied constraints, referred to as "projected constraints" in the paper. These constraints can be used to further prune the search tree. The authors also infer constraints on permissible topological orderings, denoted as "ordering constraints." However, the approach to inferring these constraints is not fully explained. The statement "we can infer Y < Z from Z not an ancestor of Y" is misleading, as it implies that the inference is always possible. A more accurate interpretation is that if "Z not an ancestor of Y," then the constraint Y < Z can be added. The authors use a MAXSAT approach to infer a maximal set of consistent ordering constraints.

The experimental evaluation involves varying the number of variables, datapoints, and ancestral constraints. The results are compared to the GOBNILP system, demonstrating that the proposed approach is significantly faster. However, as noted by the authors, GOBNILP's performance is generally poor when there is no limit on parent set cardinality, which is the case in the experiments. Therefore, it is unclear whether GOBNILP's poor performance is due to its inability to handle many parent sets or its specific handling of ancestral constraints.

Furthermore, the encoding of ancestral constraints in the supplementary material is based on a MAXSAT encoding from [Cussens, 2008], rather than the ILP encoding presented in [Cussens, 2010]. The chosen MAXSAT encoding is not well-suited for an ILP system. A more efficient formulation would be to include the A(X,Y) binary variables in addition to the I(Z,U) variables and omit the E(X,Y,Z) variables. This approach would require only the transitivity constraints on the A(X,Y) variables and the following constraints linking A and I variables: A(X,Y) >= sum{U:X in U} I(Y,U). This formulation provides a tighter linear relaxation and is likely to result in better performance.

The comparison to GOBNILP is weakened by the use of a suboptimal formulation for ancestral constraints. It would be more informative to compare the proposed approach to a sensible ILP formulation, as described above. Additionally, it would be interesting to compare the proposed method to the approach by van Beek and Hoffmann, which is not mentioned in the paper. Adapting their method to accommodate ancestral constraints may not be overly challenging.

Overall, the paper presents a promising approach to learning Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints. However, the comparison to GOBNILP could be strengthened by using a more efficient formulation for ancestral constraints. Furthermore, considering alternative approaches, such as the one by van Beek and Hoffmann, may provide a more comprehensive understanding of the proposed method's performance.
