Summary
This paper addresses optimal Bayesian network (BN) structure learning under non-decomposable background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints. The authors build on the BN graph and EC tree search spaces introduced by Chen et al. (2015, 2016), which allow searching over DAGs/CPDAGs while leveraging an oracle that optimally solves decomposable-score subproblems. They show how to enforce ancestral constraints by pruning CPDAGs in the EC tree and, crucially, how to project non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge and topological ordering constraints that can be passed to the oracle to tighten heuristics and accelerate search.

The paper makes three technical contributions. First, it gives a sound pruning strategy for EC trees under ancestral constraints: a CPDAG is pruned if no DAG in its equivalence class satisfies the constraints (with efficient tests plus fallback enumeration), ensuring correctness by Theorem 1. Second, it presents entailment conditions for inferring negative and positive edge constraints X ↛ Y and X → Y from the transitive closure of ancestral constraints via ancestor/descendant sets α(·) and β(·) (Theorems 2–3). Third, it formulates the inference of maximally many consistent ordering constraints X < Y from ancestral constraints as a MaxSAT problem with hard clauses enforcing transitivity, a necessary condition (Y ≮ X) for X < Y, and sufficient acyclicity conditions, along with soft clauses that encourage taking orderings implied by negative ancestries (Theorem 4).

Empirically, the approach is compared to the ILP-based GOBNILP (encoding ancestral constraints linearly). With projected constraints, the EC-tree-based method is often orders of magnitude faster across datasets and settings, and projection substantially tightens the search. The paper also reports an easy–hard–easy pattern for EC trees as the proportion of ancestral constraints increases; accuracy improves with data size and with more constraints.

Relation to prior work
Most exact score-based BN learning methods assume decomposable scores and cannot directly handle non-decomposable constraints (order-graph, dynamic programming, A* approaches by Koivisto & Sood; Silander & Myllymäki; Yuan & Malone). ILP/CP methods can in principle encode such constraints (Jaakkola et al.; Cussens; van Beek & Hoffmann), but have not been evaluated specifically for ancestral constraints. The present work extends Chen et al.’s non-decomposable search framework (BN graph/EC tree with oracle guidance) by adding mechanisms to enforce ancestral constraints and by projecting them to decomposable constraints for the oracle. It is complementary to causal priors/path constraints used in constraint-based or local-search methods (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos).

Strengths
- Clear motivation: ancestral constraints are practically important and non-decomposable.
- Solid theory: sound pruning on EC trees and precise entailment criteria for edge/order projections.
- Practical impact: “empowering the oracle” with projected constraints yields large speedups.
- Careful empirical setup across multiple benchmarks, varying n, sample size, and constraint density.
- Good discussion of trends (e.g., easy–hard–easy behavior) and of accuracy (SHD) as data/constraints grow.

Weaknesses and questions
1) Generalization of projected constraints beyond EC trees
From the reported tables, the benefits of projecting ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints appear to accrue primarily to the EC-tree framework, with limited or no gains for the ILP approach in some settings. Does this suggest that projection mainly helps heuristic search guided by an oracle, with poor transfer to ILP? Since the core novelty is the projection itself, it would strengthen the paper to demonstrate performance improvements in at least one additional learning framework that originally could not handle non-decomposable constraints but can benefit from the projected ones (e.g., order-graph dynamic programming/A* variants, CP, or alternative ILP formulations).

2) Easy–hard–easy pattern for EC trees
Table 2 shows an easy–hard–easy trend as the proportion of ancestral constraints increases. Please provide a deeper analysis: for example, how do pruning rates, oracle calls, and heuristic accuracy vary with the constraint density? Does this indicate that EC-tree search is particularly sensitive to the amount of background knowledge (compared to ILP), implying that the number of ancestral constraints may need tuning to avoid the hard middle regime? Any guidance on predicting or mitigating the hard region would be valuable.

3) Accuracy comparison with ILP
Table 2 reports structural Hamming distance (SHD) only for EC trees. Please include the corresponding SHD for ILP on the same datasets to confirm that both methods recover models of comparable quality under identical scoring and constraints, especially when projected constraints are used or withheld.

4) Presentation
While the BN graph provides useful background, the experiments rely on EC trees. Consider trimming Section 4.1 and using the space to deepen the EC-tree exposition: e.g., a more detailed description of A* in the EC-tree setting, heuristic construction from the oracle, and the practical implementation of pruning with ancestral constraints (including the frequency and cost of equivalence-class enumeration when the quick tests are inconclusive).

Additional comments and suggestions
- Complexity of CPDAG checks: When the quick path tests are inconclusive, you fall back on enumerating class(P). Please quantify how often this occurs and its cost; if rare, say so; if not, consider additional sufficient tests to avoid enumeration.
- MaxSAT overhead: Report the time spent inferring ordering constraints and its impact on end-to-end runtime. Are the inferred orderings stable across random seeds or solver choices?
- Terminology: You use “maximum set of ancestral constraints.” If you mean “closure under your inference rules,” consider clarifying (maximal vs. transitive closure) to avoid ambiguity.
- Reproducibility: You precompute parent-set scores and use EVASOLVER and URLEARNING. Provide command lines, parameter settings (e.g., BDeu ESS), and any limits on parent set size.

Overall, this is a clear and worthwhile contribution that extends exact BN learning to incorporate ancestral constraints effectively and demonstrates substantial practical benefits. Addressing the questions above—especially accuracy comparisons with ILP and the generality of projected constraints—would further strengthen the paper.
