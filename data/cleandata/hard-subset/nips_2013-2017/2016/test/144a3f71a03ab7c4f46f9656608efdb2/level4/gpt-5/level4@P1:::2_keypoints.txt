- Proposes handling non-decomposable ancestral constraints in exact BN structure learning via EC-tree pruning and projection into decomposable edge/order constraints.  
- Derives necessary and sufficient edge-projection conditions (Theorems 2–3) using ancestor/descendant closures α(·), β(·).  
- Reduces ordering inference to MaxSAT with hard transitivity/necessity/acyclicity constraints and soft preferences; provides sufficient (not necessary) entailment conditions (Theorem 4).  
- Builds on BN-graph and EC-tree frameworks (Chen et al.) and complements decomposable-score DP/A* approaches and ILP/CP formulations.  
- EC-tree pruning strategy is sound (Theorem 1) and edge-projection theorems are clear and intuitive.  
- MaxSAT formulation is conservative (sufficient only), preserving admissibility but potentially missing inferable constraints.  
- Paper omits details on computing maximal transitive closure of ancestral constraints and the worst-case exponential cost of enumerating all DAGs in a CPDAG.  
- Would benefit from empirical guidance on how often CPDAG enumeration fallback is needed in practice.  
- Passing only entailed decomposable constraints to the oracle should preserve heuristic admissibility; an explicit argument is requested.  
- Experiments show orders-of-magnitude speedups over ILP (GOBNILP), especially when projected constraints are supplied.  
- Benchmarks include ALARM, ANDES, CHILD, CPCS54, HEPAR2 with varying n, data size, and constraint proportion p.  
- Reports an easy–hard–easy trend with increasing p and improved SHD accuracy with more constraints.  
- Experimental reporting lacks solver overhead metrics (e.g., MaxSAT runtime), counts of inferred constraints, and CPDAG enumeration frequency.  
- Presentation of tables is confusing about which components (projections/pruning) are active in each result table.  
- Recommends an explicit EC ablation: baseline EC vs EC+pruning vs EC+pruning+edge projection vs EC+pruning+edge+ordering projection.  
- Novelty: principled projection of non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable ones and MaxSAT reduction for ordering constraints is a novel contribution.  
- Significance: useful for learning with realistic causal/ancestral knowledge; practical utility for moderate-scale domains (~20 variables).  
- Strengths: sound integration of ancestral constraints, effective projection improving search efficiency, comprehensive ILP comparisons, and demonstrated accuracy gains.  
- Weaknesses: ambiguous experimental design, missing ablations, lack of analysis on noisy/incorrect constraints, and deferred technical details (closure computation, proofs).  
- Suggested additions: clarify tables, include EC ablation, report MaxSAT overhead and inferred-constraint counts, discuss enumeration costs, evaluate robustness to noisy constraints, and add appendix details/proofs.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, sound methodology with strong empirical speedups and theoretical grounding.  
- Arguments against acceptance: unclear experimental isolation of contributions, limited scalability, missing robustness evaluation, and omitted technical details.
