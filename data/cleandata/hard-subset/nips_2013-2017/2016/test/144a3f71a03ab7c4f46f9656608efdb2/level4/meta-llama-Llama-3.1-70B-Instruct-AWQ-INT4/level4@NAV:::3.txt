The paper proposes an efficient method for incorporating ancestral constraints in the discovery of optimal Bayesian networks from data. This is achieved by translating a set of ancestral constraints into a maximal set of ordering constraints, which are then utilized in a score-based learning algorithm based on the EC tree introduced by [Chen et al., 2015]. The results are compared to a modified version of the ILP-based Gobnilp algorithm, demonstrating significant outperformance in the given setting. Overall, the paper is reasonably well-written and clearly explained, although the solution is conceptually straightforward. The problem addressed in this paper is novel and of interest to a specialist audience, as optimal BN learning in combination with ancestral constraints lacks a readily available standard solution (novelty=3). However, the scenario is somewhat artificial, as ancestral relations would typically necessitate more general causal discovery algorithms that account for unobserved confounders (hence impact=2). The approach involves translating hard-to-use ancestral constraints into sets of ordering constraints that are more easily expressed in terms of absence of edges, which fit decomposable scores. This mapping is accomplished through a MaxSAT solver, ensuring that all implied ancestral constraints are made explicit. Nevertheless, some rules (e.g., 257-261) seem unusual or incomplete, such as the acyclicity rule 3, which has a symmetric premise but an asymmetric conclusion, and rule 4, which appears to exclude certain ancestral constraints. Furthermore, in practice, only a few ancestral constraints are likely to be given, which may not significantly reduce the search space. The comparison against Gobnilp with added linear constraints appears impressive, but Gobnilp is not designed to tackle this problem. A more meaningful alternative would be to compare against a greedy algorithm like GES, assuming ancestral constraints are in line with the optimal model. Minor details include: - Clarifying the distinction between a standard oracle and an omniscient oracle (line 30). - Revising the phrase 'empower the oracle' to 'use in combination with the oracle' (line 38). - Specifying that optimizing refers to minimizing (line 90). - Revising Theorem 1 to clearly state that the indicated set of constraints A satisfied by CPDAG P on the EC tree is a subset of the set of constraints A satisfied by the full DAG G (line 164). - Noting that Theorem 3 may not lead to many positive edge implications given sparse ancestral constraints (lines 225-227). - Revising the notation in line 267 to better capture the concept of 'acyclic'. - Acknowledging that 10%-25% ancestral constraints would imply extensive knowledge of the target graph, which is not typical in practice (line 310).
