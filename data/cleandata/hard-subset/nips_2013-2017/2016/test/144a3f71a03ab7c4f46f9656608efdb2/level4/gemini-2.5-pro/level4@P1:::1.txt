This paper addresses the problem of learning optimal Bayesian network structures from data, subject to background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints. Since ancestral constraints are non-decomposable, they cannot be handled by standard, efficient learning algorithms that rely on decomposable scores. The authors' approach builds on a recently proposed A*-based search framework that navigates a search space of CPDAGs (the EC tree) and can accommodate non-decomposable scores.

The core contribution is a method for incorporating ancestral constraints into this framework. This is achieved in two main ways: first, by pruning nodes (CPDAGs) from the search space that are inconsistent with the given constraints; and second, by inferring decomposable "projected" constraints (edge and ordering constraints) from the non-decomposable ancestral constraints. These simpler, projected constraints are then passed to the A* heuristic oracle (the URLEarning system), significantly improving its accuracy and dramatically speeding up the search. The paper presents an empirical comparison against the ILP-based GOBNILP system, demonstrating that the proposed approach is orders of magnitude faster on the tested problems.

**Strengths**

*   The paper tackles an important and practical problem. Incorporating expert knowledge, such as cause-and-effect relationships expressed as ancestral constraints, is crucial for learning meaningful models, especially from limited data.
*   The proposed method is novel and well-motivated. Extending the EC tree search framework to handle ancestral constraints is a logical and powerful next step for this line of work.
*   The idea of "projecting" non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones to empower the search heuristic is a key insight. The experimental results clearly show that this technique has a massive positive impact on performance.
*   The empirical evaluation demonstrates that the proposed method is highly efficient, scaling to problems with 20 variables and showing a significant performance advantage over the chosen ILP baseline.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Clarity of Technical Details:**
    *   The justification for inferring ordering constraints from ancestral constraints is not explained precisely. The paper states that one "can infer Y < Z from Z not an ancestor of Y". This wording is ambiguous. While `Y < Z` is a consistent ordering constraint to add in this case (e.g., in a graph with no edge between Y and Z), it is not necessarily entailed by the ancestral constraint. It appears the goal is to find a maximally large set of *consistent* ordering constraints, not just those that are logically entailed. This crucial distinction should be made explicit.
    *   The proof of Lemma 3 in the supplementary material is difficult to follow. In particular, the statement "By the EC tree edge generation rules, Gk also contains edge Z -> W" requires further justification.

2.  **Experimental Comparison with ILP:**
    *   The primary weakness of the paper lies in its experimental comparison with GOBNILP. As the authors themselves note in a different context, A*-based approaches tend to outperform ILP-based ones when there is no limit on parent set cardinality. Since the experiments were run without such a limit, it is difficult to determine whether GOBNILP's poor performance is due to its known inefficiency in this setting or a specific difficulty in handling the ancestral constraint encoding. This potential confound makes it hard to isolate and evaluate the paper's primary contribution.
    *   The ILP formulation used for ancestral constraints appears to be sub-optimal for an ILP solver. The encoding is based on a formulation from [Cussens, 2008] designed for MAX-SAT. A tighter, more direct ILP formulation exists, for example in Section 3.1.1 of [Cussens, 2010], which should provide a much stronger baseline. A better approach would be to use binary variables `A(X,Y)` for ancestry and `I(Z,U)` for parent sets, linking them with constraints of the form `A(X,Y) >= I(Y,U)` for all U containing X, along with transitivity constraints on `A`. This formulation provides a far tighter linear relaxation. By comparing against a weaker formulation, the paper's empirical claims are less convincing than they could be.

3.  **Missing Related Work:** The paper does not cite or discuss the work of van Beek and Hoffmann (CP 2015), which uses constraint programming for Bayesian network learning and has been shown to be a very strong competitor to ILP-based methods like GOBNILP. Given that constraint programming is a natural fit for this type of problem, a comparison or at least a discussion would be highly relevant.

In summary, the paper presents a promising and very fast algorithm for an important problem. However, the experimental evaluation is weakened by the use of a potentially confounded comparison and a sub-optimal encoding for the baseline ILP method. While I am confident the proposed system would still outperform GOBNILP even with a better ILP formulation, the current evaluation undermines the strength of the paper's empirical claims.
