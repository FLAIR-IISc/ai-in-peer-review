This paper addresses the important problem of learning Bayesian network structure when incorporating background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints. The authors build upon a recent framework for optimal structure learning with non-decomposable scores, adapting it to handle these constraints. Their core idea is to project the non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints that can empower existing oracle-based search algorithms. 

The paper is well-motivated, clearly outlining the limitations of traditional approaches that rely on decomposable scores. The authors effectively position their work within the existing literature on Bayesian network structure learning, referencing key papers in the field (e.g., Darwiche, Koller & Friedman, Murphy) and related work on integer linear programming (ILP) approaches (e.g., Jaakkola et al., Bartlett & Cussens). The introduction does a good job of explaining why ancestral constraints are challenging for conventional methods and how the proposed framework overcomes these limitations. The use of EC trees, building on their previous work (Chen et al., 2015, 2016), is a strong foundation. 

The primary strength of this work lies in its technical soundness and the potential for significant efficiency gains. The theoretical argument for projecting non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones is compelling. The theorems presented (Theorems 1-4) provide a solid basis for the proposed approach. The experimental evaluation, while somewhat complex, demonstrates that the proposed method substantially outperforms an ILP-based approach (GOBNILP), particularly when leveraging the projected constraints. The scaling experiments (Table 3) suggest the approach can handle networks with a reasonable number of variables, comparable to state-of-the-art methods. The observation that even a small percentage of ancestral constraints can improve model accuracy is noteworthy.

However, the presentation of the experimental results could be clearer. The comparison structure, as noted in the original draft review, is somewhat convoluted. Separating the impact of the projection step from the baseline EC tree performance would provide a more direct assessment of the contribution. While the authors acknowledge the superior performance of EC over GOBNILP, a more focused comparison of (EC + projection) versus EC would strengthen the claims.  Further clarification on the choice of the BDeu score and the equivalent sample size would be beneficial. The reliance on EVASOLVER for MaxSAT may also be a limitation worth discussing, as solver performance can vary. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The approach is theoretically well-grounded and supported by theorems.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments demonstrate significant performance improvements over existing methods, particularly ILP.
*   **Practical Relevance:**  The ability to incorporate background knowledge (ancestral constraints) is valuable for real-world applications.
*   **Novelty:** The projection of non-decomposable constraints into decomposable ones is a novel contribution.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Experimental Clarity:** The presentation of experimental results is somewhat complex and could be more focused.
*   **Solver Dependence:** The reliance on a specific MaxSAT solver might limit reproducibility and generalizability.



**Overall:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. While the experimental presentation could be improved, the technical soundness, empirical results, and practical relevance of the work are strong. I recommend acceptance, with minor revisions to enhance the clarity of the experimental section and address the solver dependence.
