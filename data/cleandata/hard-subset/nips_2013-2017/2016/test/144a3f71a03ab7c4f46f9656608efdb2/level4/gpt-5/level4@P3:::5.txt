Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses optimal Bayesian network structure learning in the presence of non-decomposable background knowledge, specifically ancestral (path) constraints. Building on the BN-graph and EC-tree search spaces of Chen et al. (2015, 2016), the authors show how to enforce such constraints by pruning in the EC tree and, crucially, how to “project” non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints that can be handled by powerful order-graph oracles (e.g., URLEARNING). They propose (i) an algorithm to infer entailed positive/negative edge constraints from a closed set of ancestral constraints, with correctness characterized by Theorems 2 and 3, and (ii) a MaxSAT reduction to infer a large, cycle-free set of ordering constraints entailed by the ancestral constraints, with sufficient conditions formalized in Theorem 4. These projected constraints are then supplied to a decomposable-score oracle to tighten A* heuristics in the EC-tree search.

The pruning criterion for ancestral constraints within CPDAG nodes is described and justified (Theorem 1), with efficient tests for immediate accept/reject and fallback to enumerating DAGs in the equivalence class when necessary. Empirically, the method is compared to ILP (GOBNILP) on benchmarks (ALARM, ANDES, CHILD, CPCS54, HEPAR2), using synthetic datasets and synthetic ancestral constraints drawn from ground truth. The results show orders-of-magnitude runtime improvements over ILP in most settings, and significant gains from the projection step itself. The study also examines accuracy (structural Hamming distance), showing that modest amounts of background knowledge can substitute for substantial increases in sample size.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The approach leverages established search spaces (BN-graph/EC-tree) and provides principled conditions for inferring edge/order constraints from ancestral constraints. The pruning conditions for CPDAGs and the inference theorems are plausible and useful. However, some aspects are underspecified: the construction of the “maximum” closure of ancestral constraints is only sketched, and the fallback to exhaustive enumeration of DAGs in a CPDAG could be expensive; a discussion of frequency and mitigation would help. The MaxSAT formulation is outlined with hard/soft clauses; more detail on correctness and completeness, and on the computational cost of the MaxSAT step, would strengthen the paper.
- Empirical evaluation: The experimental setup is careful (same BDeu scores, precomputed local scores, multiple datasets per setting) and includes ablations with/without projection. The comparisons to ILP are fair in spirit, though ILP/CP can be more competitive when limiting parent set sizes; the paper notes that no parent bounds are used. Reporting variance and solver statistics (nodes expanded, MaxSAT solve times, size of inferred constraint sets) would provide deeper insight.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well organized, with a clear narrative from problem to approach to experiments. Nonetheless, there are several presentation issues that should be addressed:
- Clean up typographical artifacts (e.g., stray characters, truncated headings, footnote numbering).
- Define “maximum set of ancestral constraints” explicitly as the transitive and logically closed set under their inference rules.
- Clarify “closed set” of ordering constraints in Theorem 4.
- Provide a brief complexity discussion and practical checks to avoid CPDAG enumeration, or cite algorithms for testing existence of oriented paths in CPDAGs.
- Include pseudocode or succinct procedural descriptions for the edge-inference algorithm and the MaxSAT encoding.

Originality:
The key novelty lies in projecting non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge/order constraints to empower decomposable-score oracles within an EC-tree search. While prior work has incorporated path beliefs in constraint-based or local-search methods (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos), and ILP/CP can encode non-decomposable constraints, this paper delivers a principled and effective integration within exact score-based search using EC trees and oracles. This combination and the supporting inference theorems appear original.

Significance:
The problem is important: many applications come with causal/ancestral background knowledge that standard decomposable-score pipelines cannot directly exploit. The reported speedups over ILP are substantial, and the projection step’s impact is practically meaningful. The demonstrated scale (up to ~20 variables without parent bounds) is in line with exact methods on BDeu but still modest; nonetheless, the ideas are likely to be adopted in exact and possibly approximate BN learners, and the results provide evidence that background knowledge can reduce both computational cost and data requirements.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear and relevant problem with strong motivation.
- Novel and well-justified projection of ancestral constraints to decomposable constraints.
- Sound pruning of EC-tree nodes and effective oracle integration.
- Empirical results showing significant runtime gains and accuracy benefits.
- Connects and extends multiple strands of prior work (EC trees, order-graph oracles, MaxSAT).

Cons and suggestions:
- Scalability beyond ~20 variables is not demonstrated; discuss limits and potential extensions (e.g., bounds on indegree, incremental constraint propagation).
- Fallback to CPDAG enumeration needs either practical bounds or more efficient checks.
- Comparisons are limited to ILP; adding a CP baseline or reporting on different ILP configurations would strengthen claims.
- The MaxSAT step’s runtime and impact should be quantified.
- Minor clarity/formatting issues and a few imprecise statements (e.g., Theorem 1 wording) should be corrected.

Recommendation:
Lean accept. The paper provides a principled and practically effective way to handle ancestral constraints in optimal BN learning, with strong empirical support and a clear path to adoption. Addressing the clarity issues and adding a bit more detail on the inference procedures and MaxSAT costs would further solidify the contribution.
