

This paper addresses constrained optimization for learning Bayesian networks from local scores under ancestral constraints, employing a branch-and-bound search where nodes in the search tree are CPDAGs. The search progresses from CPDAG₁ to CPDAG₂ by selecting parent sets for variables absent in CPDAG₁, with sensible symmetry breaking applied. The search utilizes the URLearning system for heuristic guidance (details referenced in an earlier paper). Ancestral constraints enable pruning, with Lemma 3 (supplementary material) as the pivotal result. While I believe Lemma 3 is correct, its proof lacks clarity; specifically, the assertion "By the EC tree edge generation rules, Gₖ also contains edge Z → W" requires elaboration. The terminology "implied constraints" (standardly termed "projected constraints" here) refers to constraints on edges derived from ancestral constraints, enabling pruning. Similarly, "ordering constraints" (e.g., inferred from "Z not an ancestor of Y") are used for pruning. The phrase "can infer Y < Z from Z not an ancestor of Y" is misleading: while it is possible to add Y < Z as a consistent constraint (e.g., in an edgeless graph on {Y,Z}), the authors intend to infer *all* consistent ordering constraints via MAXSAT, avoiding over-inference. The experimental comparison against GOBNILP demonstrates significant speed improvements. However, GOBNILP’s poor performance here is ambiguous: without parent set limits, GOBNILP typically underperforms A-based methods generally, making it unclear whether the deficit stems from ancestral constraint handling or inherent limitations in its approach. The encoding of ancestral constraints (supplementary material) adopts a suboptimal MAXSAT formulation from [Cussens, 2008] instead of the superior ILP encoding in [Cussens, 2010], Section 3.1.1. Specifically, the current MAXSAT uses cubic E(X,Y,Z) variables unnecessarily; a tighter ILP formulation would use binary A(X,Y) (ancestor) and I(Z,U) (parent) variables with constraints A(X,Y) ≥ Σ_{U: X∈U} I(Y,U) (ensuring parents are ancestors), plus transitivity on A(X,Y). This reformulation would yield a better linear relaxation and performance, yet the paper compares against a poor encoding rather than a sensible one, weakening its claims. A comparison with van Beek and Hoffmann’s method (which often outperforms GOBNILP) would be valuable, especially as adapting it to ancestral constraints appears feasible—though the current paper omits this work.  
[Cussens, 2010] James Cussens. Maximum likelihood pedigree reconstruction using integer programming. WCB'10.  
[van Beek and Hoffmann] Peter van Beek and Hella-Franziska Hoffmann. Machine learning of Bayesian networks using constraint programming. Proceedings of CP 2015, Cork, Ireland, August 2015.
