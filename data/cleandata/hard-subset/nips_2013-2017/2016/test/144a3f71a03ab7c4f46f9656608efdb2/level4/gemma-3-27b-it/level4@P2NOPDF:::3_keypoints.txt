- Proposes an efficient method to learn optimal Bayesian networks with ancestral constraints by translating them into ordering constraints using the EC tree framework.

- Uses a MaxSAT solver to convert ancestral constraints into explicit edge-absence restrictions compatible with decomposable scoring functions.

- Experimental results show significant performance gains over a modified ILP-based Gobnilp when ancestral constraints are present.

- Paper is well-written, conceptually straightforward, novel, and of interest to BN structure-learning researchers.

- Novelty is noted but practicality is questioned because general causal discovery methods that handle unobserved confounders may be more appropriate when ancestral knowledge exists (impact limited).

- Several translation rules (lines 257–261) appear incomplete or counterintuitive; e.g., acyclicity rule 3’s conclusion does not reflect the premise’s symmetry.

- Rule 4 may unduly restrict the kinds of ancestral constraints representable, potentially excluding mutually non-causal cases.

- Likely only a small number of ancestral constraints are provided in practice, so search-space reduction from constraints may be marginal.

- Comparison against Gobnilp is useful but not ideal; a baseline like GES (assuming constraints match the true model) would be a more informative efficiency comparison.

- Minor wording edits requested: distinguish “standard” vs “omniscient” oracle (line 30); rephrase “empower the oracle” (line 38); clarify “optimizing” means “minimizing” (line 90).

- Theorem 1 needs to explicitly acknowledge that constraints A satisfied by the CPDAG P on the EC tree are a subset of those satisfied by the full DAG G for the iff to hold (line 164).

- Theorem 3 may have limited utility for deriving positive edge implications when ancestral constraints are sparse (lines 225–227).

- Justification for labeling the rule at line 267 as ‘acyclic’ is unclear (see also comments on lines 257–261).

- The claim that 10–25% ancestral constraints constitute “extensive knowledge” may be too strong and not generally true (line 310).
