- Paper proposes translating ancestral constraints into maximal ordering constraints and integrating them into an EC-tree score-based BN search, showing large speedups over a modified Gobnilp baseline.  
- The writing and exposition are clear, and the translation-to-ordering idea is conceptually elegant and novel.  
- Practical relevance is questionable: real-world causal problems often involve latent confounders and more general causal discovery methods, making the framed setting somewhat artificial.  
- The MaxSAT formulation for translating constraints, especially the acyclicity rules, is unclear and possibly incorrect.  
- Rule 3’s premise/conclusion asymmetry is unexplained and appears inconsistent.  
- Rule 4 may forbid useful constraints (e.g., specifying that two nodes are not ancestors of each other).  
- Sparse sets of ancestral constraints (typical in practice) may not sufficiently prune the search, limiting the method’s practical gains; Theorem 3 may be ineffective under sparsity.  
- Experimental comparison to a straightforwardly modified Gobnilp may be unfair since Gobnilp wasn’t designed for these constraints.  
- A more informative baseline would be a constraint-guided greedy algorithm (e.g., GES) to assess practical efficiency versus strong heuristics.  
- The experimental assumption that 10–25% of ancestral relations are known is overly optimistic and may not reflect realistic prior knowledge.  
- Minor issues: clarify distinction between “standard” and “omniscient” oracle (Line 30) and rephrase “empower the oracle” (Line 38).  
- Minor clarity fixes: state that “optimizing” means minimizing the score (Line 90), and make Theorem 1’s statement precise about constraint subsets (Line 164).  
- The logical acyclicity formulation (Line 267) is not intuitive and ties back to concerns about rules in lines 257–261.  
- Overall: novel and interesting for specialists but impact is limited unless practical relevance, correctness of translation, and experimental fairness are addressed.
