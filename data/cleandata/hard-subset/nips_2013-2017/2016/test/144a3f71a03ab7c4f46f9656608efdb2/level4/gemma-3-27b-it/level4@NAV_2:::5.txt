This paper addresses the problem of learning Bayesian network structures optimally, under the constraint of ancestral knowledge. The authors build upon the recent framework introduced by Chen et al. [2015, 2016] for optimal structure learning with non-decomposable scores, adapting it to accommodate ancestral constraints. Their approach centers on inferring decomposable constraints from the non-decomposable ancestral constraints, effectively enabling the use of oracles designed for decomposable scores. The authors demonstrate empirically that their method achieves significant speedups—orders of magnitude—compared to alternative formulations based on integer linear programming (ILP). A key contribution involves formulating constraint projection as a MaxSAT problem, allowing for the efficient identification of additional, helpful decomposable constraints.

The paper is well-structured and clearly motivated. The introduction effectively explains the limitations of existing approaches that rely on decomposable scores and the importance of incorporating background knowledge in the form of ancestral constraints. The technical preliminaries are concise and provide sufficient context for understanding the proposed method. The description of the learning approach, incorporating EC trees and constraint projection, is thorough and logical.  The theoretical foundation, particularly Theorems 1-4, provides a solid justification for the proposed approach and demonstrates a clear understanding of the underlying principles. 

A key strength of this work is its practical impact. The empirical evaluation convincingly demonstrates the significant performance improvements over state-of-the-art ILP-based methods. The comprehensive experimentation, varying network size, dataset size, and constraint percentage, strengthens the conclusions. The analysis of structural Hamming distance provides valuable insight into the accuracy of the learned models. The observation that even a small number of ancestral constraints can substantially improve model accuracy, comparable to increasing dataset size, is a significant finding. The connection to MaxSAT is a clever and well-executed idea.

However, there are a few areas where the paper could be strengthened. While the authors acknowledge related work in ILP and CP, further discussion of recent advances in these areas would be beneficial.  The empirical evaluation focuses on simulated data. While this allows for controlled comparisons, evaluating the approach on real-world datasets would provide further evidence of its practical utility.  The discussion around the MaxSAT solver (EVASOLVER) is brief. Some details about the solver’s configuration and performance could be useful. Additionally, while the authors claim scalability to n=20, a more detailed discussion of memory usage and computational limitations as network size increases would be valuable.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Significant Performance Improvement:** The empirical results demonstrate a substantial and consistent speedup over existing methods.
*   **Novel Approach:** The combination of non-decomposable score learning with ancestral constraints and the use of constraint projection is a novel and effective technique.
*   **Solid Theoretical Foundation:** The paper is grounded in a strong theoretical understanding of Bayesian network structure learning.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The ability to efficiently incorporate background knowledge has significant practical implications.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Real-World Evaluation:** The lack of evaluation on real-world datasets.
*   **Brief Discussion of MaxSAT Solver:** Limited details about the MaxSAT solver configuration.
*   **Scalability Details:** Could benefit from a more detailed analysis of scalability limitations.

**Overall:** This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian network structure learning. The combination of theoretical rigor and practical impact makes it a worthy addition to NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above.
