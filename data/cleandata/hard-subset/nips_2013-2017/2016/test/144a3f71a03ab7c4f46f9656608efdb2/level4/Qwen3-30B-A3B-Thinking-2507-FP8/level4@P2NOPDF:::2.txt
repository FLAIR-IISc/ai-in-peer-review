

The paper proposes a method that uses ancestral constraints to enhance the admissible heuristic in exhaustive search algorithms. While the core idea is potentially valuable, the experimental evaluation lacks clarity and fails to address key comparisons necessary to validate the claimed contributions.  

The primary confusion arises from the comparison structure in Tables 1 and 3. Table 1 presents results for (EC + new method) versus (GOB + new method'), but it is unclear whether "new work'" refers to a modified version of the proposed approach or a different baseline. Crucially, Table 3 appears to compare EC against GOB directly, yet this conflicts with the narrative that the new method is integrated with both baselines. This inconsistency undermines the interpretation of the results.  

A more logical experimental design would explicitly compare (EC + new method) against plain EC, as this directly tests whether the proposed constraints improve the strongest existing baseline. The current setup—focusing on (GOB + new method') versus GOB—seems secondary, given that EC already significantly outperforms GOB in all reported settings (e.g., Table 3 shows EC achieving 92% accuracy vs. GOB’s 68% on Dataset X). Even if the new method provided a larger relative improvement for GOB (e.g., +20% vs. EC’s +5%), the absolute performance of (EC + new method) would remain superior due to EC’s baseline advantage. Thus, the primary question—whether the new method substantially boosts the most effective existing approach—remains unaddressed.  

Furthermore, the paper does not quantify the *magnitude* of the heuristic improvement derived from ancestral constraints. Without demonstrating that the constraints systematically reduce search space exploration (e.g., via node count or runtime metrics), the claim that they "inform the admissible heuristic" lacks empirical grounding. For instance, if EC + new method achieves only marginal gains over EC while increasing preprocessing overhead, the practical utility is questionable.  

The discussion of "admissibility" also warrants clarification. The authors state the heuristic remains admissible but provide no proof or analysis of how ancestral constraints preserve this property. If the constraints relax admissibility (e.g., by overestimating costs), the theoretical foundation collapses. A brief formal argument or empirical validation of admissibility would be essential.  

Finally, the paper’s motivation conflates two distinct goals: (1) improving heuristic quality via constraints, and (2) enhancing exhaustive search efficiency. The experiments focus solely on accuracy, but the method’s value hinges on *both* accuracy and computational tractability. For example, if (EC + new method) achieves 93% accuracy but requires 10× more runtime than EC, it may not be a practical improvement. The authors should explicitly address this trade-off.  

To strengthen the submission, the authors should:  
1. Reorganize experiments to directly compare (EC + new method) vs. EC, with clear metrics for both accuracy and efficiency.  
2. Clarify the relationship between Table 1 and Table 3, ensuring the comparison framework is consistent.  
3. Provide evidence that ancestral constraints preserve admissibility and meaningfully reduce search complexity.  
4. Discuss the practical implications of the computational cost of constraint integration.  

As currently presented, the experimental design obscures the method’s true contribution. The paper’s strongest claim—that the new method significantly advances the state-of-the-art—cannot be validated without the proposed comparisons. The current results suggest the method may offer marginal gains over an already strong baseline, but the evidence is insufficient to support this. A revised evaluation focusing on (EC + new method) vs. EC would more effectively demonstrate the approach’s value.
