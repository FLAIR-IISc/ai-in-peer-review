- Main contribution: projects non‑decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable edge/topological constraints so decomposable-score learners can use richer domain knowledge.
- Technical novelty: Section 5 details the principled projection from ancestral to decomposable constraints.
- Presentation: paper is clearly written and the theory–experiment connection is generally well presented.
- Strength: clear motivation—enabling methods limited to decomposable constraints to leverage richer domain knowledge.
- Strength: principled technical contribution for projecting ancestral constraints to edge/topological constraints.
- Strength: empirical section and main ideas are easy to follow and well organized.
- Concern: Table 3 shows projected constraints help EC trees but not ILP, raising whether benefits are EC‑specific or ILP integration is suboptimal.
- Request: analyze why ILP does not benefit—redundancy with ILP pruning, projection weakness, or ILP already encoding equivalents.
- Request: demonstrate a case where a method that originally cannot use ancestral constraints gains accuracy/runtime from projected constraints, with an ablation (no constraints / original ancestral / projected).
- Request: report how many constraints survive projection and their tightness (e.g., implied edges/partial orders) for EC trees and ILP.
- Concern: the easy–hard–easy pattern (Table 2) needs diagnostics to explain the phenomenon rather than just reporting it.
- Request: provide A* diagnostics (node expansions, effective branching factor, closed‑list size) vs constraint density to explain easy–hard–easy behavior.
- Request: report fraction of constraints that are correct, redundant, or conflicting and how each category affects heuristic tightness and pruning.
- Request: quantify how many projected constraints reduce the admissible heuristic gap or eliminate inconsistent partial orders.
- Question: discuss whether EC trees are more sensitive to constraint quantity/quality than ILP and whether there is a “sweet spot” in constraint density; provide sensitivity curves and variance across datasets.
- Missing metric: Table 2 omits SHD for ILP—include SHD and ideally precision/recall of edges and v‑structures for ILP under the same conditions.
- If ILP’s SHD is unchanged, discuss whether projection’s primary benefit is efficiency rather than accuracy.
- Exposition: consider moving BN background (Section 4.1) to an appendix and use space to better explain EC trees in this setting (state space, node representation, pruning effects).
- Exposition: clearly define the A* evaluation function, admissibility/consistency of the heuristic, and how projected constraints modify g/h costs and feasibility checks.
- Request: provide a small running example showing projection of an ancestral constraint and its enforcement during A* on an EC tree.
- Additional metrics: report wall‑clock time, memory usage, number of explored states, and optimality certificates for EC trees and ILP, with and without projected constraints.
- Clarify information loss: quantify whether projection weakens information, when it is exact versus conservative, and the extent of many‑to‑one mapping loss.
- Robustness check: vary noise or mis‑specified ancestral constraints to show performance degradation under realistic imperfect domain knowledge.
- Presentation detail: improve table/figure captions to state datasets, scoring function, priors, and constraint counts before and after projection.
- Overall assessment: useful and timely idea with sound theory, but empirical weaknesses—benefits concentrated on EC trees and lacking analysis of easy–hard–easy pattern—need stronger cross‑method evaluation, diagnostics, and clearer exposition.
