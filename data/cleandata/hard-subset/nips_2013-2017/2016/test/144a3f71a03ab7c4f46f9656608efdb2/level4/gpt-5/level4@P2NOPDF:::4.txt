This paper proposes a method to project non-decomposable ancestral constraints into decomposable constraints (on edges and topological orders), enabling score-decomposable structure learners to incorporate such domain knowledge and potentially improve efficiency. The central novelty lies in Section 5, where the projection to edge and order constraints is introduced. The paper is clearly written, and the theoretical and empirical components are well presented. I have several concerns and suggestions:

i. Generalization beyond EC trees. Table 3 indicates that projecting ancestral constraints improves EC-tree performance but not ILP. Does this imply that the projection primarily benefits EC trees and does not transfer well to other learning paradigms? Since the key contribution is inferring decomposable constraints from non-decomposable ancestral constraints, it would strengthen the paper to demonstrate gains for at least one prominent approach that cannot natively encode ancestral constraints (e.g., a standard score-based search with decomposable scores), both in accuracy and runtime. Clarifying when and why ILP fails to benefit (e.g., due to already tight formulations or conflicting constraints) would also help.

ii. Easy–hard–easy pattern in Table 2. The EC-tree results exhibit an easy–hard–easy trend as the proportion of ancestral constraints increases. What mechanism drives this non-monotonicity? Does the effectiveness of search-space pruning depend sensitively on the quantity or consistency of constraints? How does this sensitivity compare with ILP? If performance hinges on the number of ancestral constraints, guidance on selecting or validating the constraint set (e.g., thresholds, conflict resolution, or robustness to noisy constraints) would be valuable. An analysis separating constraint quantity, consistency, and strength, or a controlled ablation varying constraint quality, could clarify this behavior.

iii. Missing SHD comparison with ILP. Table 2 reports structural Hamming distance (SHD) only for EC trees. Please include SHD for ILP under the same settings, with and without projected constraints, to enable a direct comparison of accuracy, not just runtime or feasibility.

iv. Presentation suggestions. While the BN graph discussion provides theoretical background for EC trees, the experiments focus exclusively on EC-tree search with ancestral constraints. Consider streamlining or removing Section 4.1 and reallocating space to:
- A clearer, self-contained explanation of EC trees in this setting.
- Details of the A* search (heuristics, admissibility, tie-breaking).
- How projected constraints affect branching, pruning, and heuristic tightness.
- Complexity or empirical profiling that links the theory to observed speedups.

Overall, the idea of projecting ancestral constraints into decomposable forms is interesting and well motivated, and the exposition is strong. Addressing the questions above—especially demonstrating benefits beyond EC trees and explaining the easy–hard–easy trend—would substantially improve the clarity, generality, and empirical persuasiveness of the contribution.
