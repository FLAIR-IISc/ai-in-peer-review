The paper proposes an efficient method for incorporating ancestral constraints into the optimal learning of Bayesian networks from data. This is achieved by translating a set of ancestral constraints into a maximal set of ordering constraints, which are then utilized in a score-based learning algorithm based on the EC tree introduced by Chen et al. (2015). The results are evaluated against a straightforward modification of the ILP-based Gobnilp algorithm and demonstrate significant outperformance in the given setting.

The paper tackles an interesting and novel problem, as optimal Bayesian network learning in combination with ancestral constraints lacks a readily available standard solution. However, it is worth noting that once ancestral relations come into play, it might be more sensible to employ more general causal discovery algorithms that also account for unobserved confounders.

The approach taken in this paper is based on translating hard-to-use ancestral constraints, which do not fit well in decomposable-score-based algorithms, into sets of ordering constraints that are more easily expressed in terms of absence of edges, which do fit decomposable scores. The mapping is done through a MaxSAT solver, which ensures that all implied ancestral constraints are made explicit.

However, some of the rules in the MaxSAT formulation seem strange or incomplete. For instance, the premise in the acyclicity rule is symmetric, but the conclusion is not. Additionally, one of the rules appears to exclude ancestral constraints of the type 'X and Y do not cause each other'. Furthermore, in practice, only a few ancestral constraints are typically given, and these are unlikely to significantly reduce the search space.

The comparison against Gobnilp with added linear constraints looks impressive, but the latter is not developed to tackle this problem. A more reasonable alternative to measure performance would be to assume that the ancestral constraints are in line with the optimal model and compare against a greedy algorithm like GES, which should produce the same output for oracle input. Although greedy algorithms cannot guarantee optimality in general, this would provide at least a more meaningful indication of the efficiency achieved by the method in the paper.

Minor details include the clarification of the difference between a standard oracle and an omniscient oracle, as well as the notation of 'empowering the oracle', which seems to mean 'using in combination with the oracle'. Additionally, Theorem 1 should state more clearly that the indicated set of constraints A satisfied by CPDAG P on the EC tree is only a subset of the set of constraints A satisfied by the full DAG G. Furthermore, Theorem 3 seems unlikely to lead to many positive edge implications given sparse ancestral constraints, and the formulation of the acyclicity rule in the MaxSAT solver is not easily recognizable as capturing 'acyclic'.

Lastly, it is worth noting that for reasonable-sized graphs, 10%-25% ancestral constraints would already imply extensive knowledge of the target graph, which is not typical in practice.

Strengths:

* The paper tackles a novel and interesting problem.
* The approach is based on a clear and logical translation of ancestral constraints into ordering constraints.
* The results demonstrate significant outperformance against a straightforward modification of the ILP-based Gobnilp algorithm.

Weaknesses:

* The situation tackled in the paper is somewhat artificial, as ancestral relations might be better addressed by more general causal discovery algorithms.
* Some of the rules in the MaxSAT formulation seem strange or incomplete.
* The comparison against Gobnilp is not entirely fair, and a more reasonable alternative would be to compare against a greedy algorithm like GES.
* Minor details, such as notation and theorem formulations, could be improved.
