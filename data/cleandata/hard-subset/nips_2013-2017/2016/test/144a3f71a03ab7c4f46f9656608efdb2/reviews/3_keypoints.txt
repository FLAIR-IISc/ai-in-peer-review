- Paper proposes translating ancestral constraints into ordering constraints and using them in an EC-tree score-based algorithm to learn optimal Bayesian networks; evaluated vs. modified Gobnilp and outperforms it.  
- Well written and clearly explained; conceptually straightforward; novel and of interest to specialists (novelty=3).  
- Impact is limited because the setting is somewhat artificial—ancestral constraints would often motivate broader causal discovery methods that allow unobserved confounders (impact=2).  
- Mapping from ancestral to ordering constraints is done with a MaxSAT solver to make implied ancestral constraints explicit.  
- Concerns about specific rules (lines 257–261): acyclicity rule 3 has a symmetric premise but an asymmetric conclusion, and rule 4 seems to exclude constraints like “X and Y do not cause each other.”  
- In practice, only a few ancestral constraints are likely to be available, so they may not substantially reduce the search space.  
- Comparison to Gobnilp is not entirely fair since Gobnilp wasn’t developed for this problem; the impressive results may reflect that mismatch.  
- Suggested alternative baseline: compare against greedy algorithms like GES assuming ancestral constraints align with the optimal model to better gauge efficiency.  
- Theorem 1 needs a clearer statement: the set of constraints A satisfied by the CPDAG P on the EC tree is only a subset of those satisfied by the full DAG G (otherwise the iff is incorrect).  
- Theorem 3 is unlikely to produce many positive edge implications when ancestral constraints are sparse.  
- Encoding labeled as “acyclic” (line 267) is not easily recognizable as capturing acyclicity.  
- Minor wording and clarity issues: line 30 (oracle vs omniscient oracle), line 38 (“empower the oracle”), line 90 (clarify optimizing=minimizing), and line 310 (10–25% ancestral constraints already imply extensive knowledge and are not typical).
