This paper presents a novel and valuable framework for providing theoretical guarantees for graph clustering without relying on the standard assumption that the data is generated from a specific block model. The core idea is to establish the stability of a given clustering: if a clustering C and its associated block-model parameters fit the observed graph G well, then any other clustering C' that also fits G well must be a small perturbation of C. This shifts the paradigm from generative model validation to a more practical, data-dependent notion of correctness. The authors instantiate this framework for the Preference Frame Model (PFM) and the Stochastic Block Model (SBM), connecting their model-free approach to the extensive existing literature on model-based recovery (e.g., Rohe et al. 2011, Wan & Meila 2015).

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper’s main contribution is its conceptual shift towards model-free validation. This addresses a critical and often-overlooked limitation in the block-model literature, where generative assumptions are difficult to verify in practice. The framework to "turn around" existing model-based theorems is clever and likely to inspire follow-up work on making theoretical guarantees more applicable.
*   **Quality:** The work is technically sound, building its proofs on established matrix perturbation results like the Davis-Kahan theorem. A significant strength is that all quantities in the derived bounds (e.g., goodness-of-fit ε, eigengaps) are computable from the data, making the guarantees verifiable and not reliant on unknown constants. The experimental section provides a clear validation of the PFM-based theorem.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The "Generic Theorem" blueprint introduced in Section 3 provides an excellent high-level overview, which is then effectively instantiated for PFM and SBM, making the methodology easy to follow.

**Weaknesses (Arguments for Improvement):**
*   **Practicality of Bounds:** While the PFM results are promising, the experimental section reveals that the derived bounds for the SBM (Theorem 9) are too loose to be informative, even for data designed to fit the model well (LFR benchmark). The authors acknowledge this, but the paper would be stronger with a deeper discussion on why these specific bounds are not tight and potential avenues for improving them.
*   **Clarity of Alternative Results:** Section 4.2, which connects the framework to existing recovery theorems, could be structured more clearly. A summary table comparing the assumptions (e.g., spectral vs. Frobenius norm) and resulting bounds from Theorems 4, 9, 11, and 12 would greatly aid the reader in understanding the trade-offs between the different approaches.

Overall, this is a strong paper with a significant and original contribution to the theory of graph clustering. The weaknesses identified are minor and primarily relate to the tightness of one specific result and opportunities for improved presentation. The proposed framework is an important step towards bridging the gap between theory and practice in community detection.
