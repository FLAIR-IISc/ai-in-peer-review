- Introduces a framework for graph clustering that provides theoretical guarantees without assuming a specific generative block model.  
- Core idea: guarantees are based on how well a clustering and its model parameters fit observed data; any alternative good fit is a small perturbation.  
- Framework instantiated for PFM and SBM, showing links between model-free and model-based recovery results.  
- Well-positioned within existing literature on spectral clustering, perturbation bounds, and model-based recovery with appropriate citations.  
- Strength — Novelty: expands theoretical guarantees beyond model-based limitations.  
- Strength — Technical soundness: rigorous theorems and proofs (supplement) using perturbation theory and spectral results.  
- Strength — Generality: generic theorem provides a template for other block models and algorithms.  
- Strength — Practical relevance: enables validation of cluster quality without knowing the generative model; computationally feasible quantities.  
- Strength — Connections: demonstrates mutual reinforcement between model-free and model-based results.  
- Strength — Experiments: empirical evidence supports theoretical stability under perturbations.  
- Weakness — Assumptions: still relies on conditions (eigengap, goodness-of-fit) that may be strong or hard to verify in practice.  
- Weakness — Conservativeness: bounds can be loose; SBM bounds often not satisfied in experiments, suggesting room for tighter analysis.  
- Weakness — Clarity: many separate assumptions (PFM, SBM) make it hard to quickly grasp overall applicability; could use consolidation.  
- Weakness — Comparison: related work on stability/robustness is discussed but needs more explicit comparative analysis of pros/cons.  
- Weakness — Complexity: lengthy and technical proofs in the supplement may hinder accessibility and independent verification.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses a fundamental challenge, is novel and technically strong, connects to existing work, and is empirically supported.  
- Arguments against acceptance: practical restrictiveness of assumptions, conservative bounds, and technical complexity may limit impact.  
- Overall assessment: strong, valuable contribution that justifies acceptance despite noted limitations.  
- Recommendation: Accept.
