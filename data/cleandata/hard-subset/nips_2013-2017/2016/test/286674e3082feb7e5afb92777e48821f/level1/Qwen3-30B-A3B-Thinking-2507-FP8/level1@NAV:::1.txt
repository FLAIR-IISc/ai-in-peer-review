

This paper proposes a novel framework for graph clustering that provides theoretical guarantees without assuming the data is generated from a specific model (e.g., SBM, DC-SBM, or PFM). Instead, the authors establish guarantees based on whether a model fits the observed graph well, with all quantities being computable from the data. The core contribution is a "Generic Theorem" showing that if a clustering C fits the graph well under a model M, then any other clustering C' that also fits well must be close to C. The authors instantiate this theorem for SBM and PFM, demonstrating how their model-free guarantees connect to and strengthen existing model-based results. This work relates to recent NIPS papers on community detection (e.g., Rohe et al., 2011; Abbe & Sandon, 2015) but offers a fundamentally different approach by eliminating the model assumption.

Strengths: The paper is technically sound with clear theoretical contributions. The authors provide precise bounds for both SBM and PFM that are computable from data, addressing a significant limitation in the literature. The proof techniques are elegant and simpler than previous approaches. The connection between model-free and model-based results is particularly insightful, showing how their framework can improve existing guarantees (e.g., improving bounds from [20] by a factor of 128). The experimental validation demonstrates the practical applicability of the framework across multiple datasets.

Weaknesses: The significance of the results is somewhat limited by the fact that the model-free guarantees for SBM are much harder to satisfy than for PFM (as shown in experiments), suggesting the framework may not be broadly applicable to all common clustering scenarios. The paper could better address why the SBM bounds are weaker than PFM bounds. The discussion of "sanity checks" (Section 4.3) is somewhat superficial, and the paper doesn't sufficiently explore the practical trade-offs between model assumptions and guarantee strength.

Arguments for acceptance: High technical quality, novel framework addressing a significant limitation in the field, clear connection to existing work, and practical experimental validation. The work represents an important step toward more robust community detection methods.

Arguments against acceptance: The framework's applicability is somewhat limited to specific models (PFM works well, SBM less so), and the significance is somewhat constrained by this limitation. The paper could better contextualize how their approach compares to other model-free methods like those in [6] or [11].

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution with clear potential impact on community detection research. The framework is well-motivated, technically sound, and offers new perspectives on model-based clustering. I recommend acceptance.
