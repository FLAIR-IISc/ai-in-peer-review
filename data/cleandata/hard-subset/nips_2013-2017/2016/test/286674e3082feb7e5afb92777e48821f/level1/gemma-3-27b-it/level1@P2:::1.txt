This paper proposes a framework for clustering graphs with theoretical guarantees that do *not* rely on the assumption that the data is generated from a specific block model (SBM, DC-SBM, PFM). The core idea is to establish guarantees based on how well a chosen clustering *fits* the observed data, rather than assuming the data originates from a model. This is a significant and potentially impactful direction. The authors demonstrate this framework by deriving model-free guarantees for PFM and SBM, and also show connections between the model-free and model-based recovery results.

The paper is well-motivated and addresses an important limitation of existing work in graph clustering. The assumption of a specific generative model is often unrealistic in practice, and having guarantees that hold without this assumption is highly desirable. The authors clearly articulate this motivation in the introduction. The background section provides a concise review of relevant concepts (graphs, clusterings, block models) and sets the stage for the main contribution. The technical presentation is generally clear, although some parts could be more streamlined (see Clarity below). The theorems are stated precisely, and the authors attempt to connect their work to existing results (e.g., in Section 4.2 and 4.3). The experimental evaluation provides some initial support for the theoretical findings.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theorems appear to be correctly stated and the proofs (partially presented, with promises of full proofs in the supplement) seem reasonable, given the complexity of the subject. The connection between model-free and model-based guarantees is particularly interesting and well-justified. However, the reliance on the supplement for core proofs is a weakness; full visibility is crucial for a thorough assessment.  The experimental section, while present, feels somewhat cursory. The claims about the applicability of the theorems and the computational tractability of the quantities involved are not fully substantiated with compelling empirical evidence. A deeper experimental analysis, perhaps with more diverse datasets and a more systematic exploration of the parameter space, would strengthen the paper significantly.  The paper does not shy away from stating limitations, which is appreciated.

**Clarity:** The paper is mostly clearly written, but there are areas for improvement.  The notation is fairly dense, and it takes some effort to keep track of all the symbols. A more gradual introduction of notation would be helpful.  Some of the explanations are a bit terse. For instance, the explanation of Proposition 10 (improving the bound by using Y) could be expanded to clarify its practical implications. The connection between the theorems and the existing model-based results could be made more explicit. The structure of the proofs, even in the main text, could be improved with more signposting and intermediate steps clearly explained.  The organization within Section 4 is a bit disjointed; it feels like a collection of observations rather than a cohesive discussion.

**Originality:** The approach of providing model-free guarantees for graph clustering is novel. While stability-based approaches exist in clustering, applying them to the context of block models and connecting them back to the model-based literature is a significant contribution. The specific theorems and the techniques used to derive them appear to be original. The authors adequately reference related work, although a more thorough discussion of existing stability results for clustering (beyond the cited papers) would be beneficial. It's good to see comparisons to existing bounds, for example, noting the differences between the derived bounds and those in [16] and [14].

**Significance:** The potential impact of this work is significant.  Providing guarantees for graph clustering without model assumptions broadens the applicability of these techniques to real-world datasets where the underlying data generating process is unknown. The connection between model-free and model-based results is also important, as it allows one to leverage existing theoretical tools in a more general setting.  The framework presented could inspire further research on developing more robust and flexible clustering algorithms. The demonstrated connection to resilience and clusterability are promising. However, the practical significance is somewhat tempered by the restrictive nature of the conditions required for the guarantees to hold (as indicated by the experiments).  Making the framework more applicable to a wider range of datasets and scenarios is an important direction for future work.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and significant contribution to the field of graph clustering.
*   Technically sound and well-motivated.
*   Clear articulation of the problem and the proposed solution.
*   Connections to existing model-based results are insightful.
*   Potential to broaden the applicability of graph clustering techniques.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Reliance on the supplement for key proofs hinders thorough evaluation.
*   Experimental evaluation is limited and does not fully demonstrate the practical relevance of the results.
*   Conditions for the guarantees to hold may be restrictive, limiting the applicability in practice.
*   Some parts of the presentation could be clearer and more streamlined.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of graph clustering. The core idea of providing model-free guarantees is novel and potentially impactful. While the paper has some weaknesses (primarily related to the reliance on the supplement and the limited experimental evaluation), the strengths outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with the expectation that the authors will address the reviewer's comments, particularly by providing a more comprehensive experimental analysis and ensuring the full proofs are readily accessible. A clearer discussion of the practical implications and limitations of the theorems would also be beneficial.
