

# Peer Review: Graph Clustering: Block-models and model free results

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper introduces a novel framework for providing theoretical guarantees in graph clustering without assuming that the data follows a specific probabilistic model (e.g., Stochastic Block Model, Degree-Corrected SBM, or Preference Frame Model). The authors propose a "Generic Theorem" stating that if a clustering C fits the observed graph G well according to a model-based goodness-of-fit measure, then any other clustering C' that also fits G well must be close to C in terms of weighted misclassification error. The authors instantiate this framework for two specific models (SBM and PFM), providing concrete guarantees that depend on computable quantities from the data.

This work builds upon and extends the extensive literature on model-based community detection at NIPS, including seminal works on SBM by Abbe & Sandon (2015), Rohe et al. (2011), and Qin & Rohe (2013). The paper also relates to stability results in clustering (e.g., Meilà, 2005; Awasthi, 2015) but distinguishes itself by providing a framework that explicitly connects model-based and model-free guarantees. While some stability results exist for spectral clustering (e.g., Peng et al., 2015; Meilà & Shortreed, 2013), this paper uniquely demonstrates how to use these results to validate clusterings without model assumptions.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Framework**: The core contribution - a framework for model-free validation of clustering results - is highly original and addresses a significant limitation in the current literature. The idea of "turning around" model-based recovery theorems to be used in a model-free context is particularly insightful.

2. **Technical Soundness**: The paper presents rigorous theoretical results with clear proofs. The Generic Theorem and its instantiations for SBM and PFM are well-justified, and the authors demonstrate how to compute all necessary quantities from the data (unlike many distribution-free results that contain undetermined constants).

3. **Practical Relevance**: The experimental section demonstrates the practical utility of the framework on both synthetic and real-world datasets (including Political Blogs), showing that the bounds can be computed and used to validate clustering results. The authors also show how their framework can improve upon existing model-based results (e.g., by improving the error bound in [20] by a factor of 128).

4. **Clarity and Organization**: The paper is exceptionally well-organized, with clear definitions, logical flow, and helpful examples. The notation is consistent and well-motivated. The authors provide sufficient detail for experts to reproduce their results.

5. **Thorough Treatment of Limitations**: The authors acknowledge the limitations of their approach, particularly for SBM compared to PFM, and suggest directions for future work (e.g., tighter bounds for SBM, studying non-spectral thresholds).

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Scope of Model-Free Guarantees**: While the framework is theoretically sound, the authors acknowledge that the SBM bounds are much harder to satisfy than the PFM bounds. For the SBM, the paper notes that "all δ values from Theorem 9 are above 1, and not shown," which limits the practical applicability of their model-free guarantees for SBM.

2. **Experimental Validation**: The experiments primarily validate the theoretical framework rather than demonstrating practical improvements over existing clustering algorithms. While the authors show that their framework can be used to validate clusterings, they don't compare their method against other model-free validation techniques (e.g., bootstrap methods in [11]).

3. **Computational Complexity**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational cost of computing the goodness-of-fit measures and the resulting bounds. For large graphs, the spectral decomposition required for the PFM framework could be computationally intensive, which might limit practical applicability.

4. **Comparison to Alternative Validation Methods**: While the authors reference related work on stability and clusterability, they don't provide a thorough comparison of their framework with existing model-free validation techniques (e.g., stability-based validation in [6], [11]).

## Arguments for and against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- The framework is highly original and addresses a significant limitation in current graph clustering literature.
- The technical contributions are sound and well-justified with rigorous proofs.
- The results are immediately applicable to practitioners who want to validate clustering results without model assumptions.
- The paper demonstrates how to connect model-based and model-free approaches, advancing the state of the art.
- The clarity and organization of the paper make it accessible to the NIPS audience.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- The practical applicability of the model-free guarantees for SBM is limited (as the bounds are not informative).
- The experimental section could have better demonstrated the practical advantages of the framework over existing validation techniques.
- The paper doesn't address the computational cost of implementing the framework for large-scale graphs.

## Final Recommendation

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of graph clustering by providing a novel framework for obtaining theoretical guarantees without model assumptions. The technical contributions are sound, the writing is clear, and the results have clear practical implications. While the practical applicability of the SBM guarantees is limited, the PFM results are strong and the framework's ability to improve upon existing model-based results (e.g., improving error bounds by a factor of 128) is particularly noteworthy.

The paper addresses a fundamental limitation in current clustering theory and provides a bridge between model-based and model-free approaches. The authors have carefully considered the limitations of their framework and have provided clear directions for future work. This paper is well-suited for NIPS 2016 as it advances the state of the art in a meaningful way and has the potential to influence both theoretical and practical approaches to graph clustering.

I recommend acceptance.
