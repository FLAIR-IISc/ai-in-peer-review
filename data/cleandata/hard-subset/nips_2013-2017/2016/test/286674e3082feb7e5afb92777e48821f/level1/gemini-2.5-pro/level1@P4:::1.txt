### Summary of the paper

This paper proposes a novel framework for providing theoretical guarantees for graph clustering algorithms without assuming that the observed graph is generated from a specific probabilistic model, such as the Stochastic Block Model (SBM) or Preference Frame Model (PFM). The central idea is to shift the basis of the guarantee from an unverifiable model assumption to a verifiable goodness-of-fit criterion. The authors' generic theorem states that if a given clustering `C` allows for a model `M(G, C)` from a particular class (e.g., PFM or SBM) to fit the observed graph `G` well, then any other clustering `C'` that also fits the data well must be a small perturbation of `C`.

The paper instantiates this framework for two model classes: PFM and SBM. For each, it defines: (1) a method to construct a model `M(G, C)` from the data `G` and a candidate clustering `C`; (2) a goodness-of-fit measure, typically the spectral norm of the difference between the observed graph's matrix representation (Laplacian for PFM, Adjacency for SBM) and that of the fitted model; and (3) a bound on the distance (Misclassification Error) between any two clusterings that both satisfy the goodness-of-fit criterion. A key feature of this work is that all quantities in the derived bounds are computable from the data, allowing for practical cluster validation. The paper further shows how this model-free framework connects to existing model-based recovery theorems, suggesting that those results can be repurposed to provide model-free guarantees and that the techniques herein might improve existing model-based bounds. Experimental results on synthetic and real-world data demonstrate the practical application of the PFM guarantees.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality: Is the paper technically sound? Are claims well-supported?**

The paper is technically strong. The main theoretical contributions, Theorems 4 and 9, are derived using established tools from matrix perturbation theory, specifically the Davis-Kahan Sin-Theta theorem, and connecting the resulting subspace perturbations to distances between clusterings. The logic is sound, and the claims are well-supported by the provided proof sketches (with full proofs in the supplement). A significant strength is that the derived guarantees are explicit and do not contain undetermined constants, making them directly applicable for validation, as demonstrated in the experiments. The authors are careful to report both the strengths and weaknesses of their approach, noting in particular that their derived bounds for the SBM case (Theorem 9) were not informative in their experiments, which suggests the bounds may be too loose for that model.

**Clarity: Is the paper clearly written and well-organized?**

The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem of moving beyond strict model-based assumptions. The "blueprint" structure presented in Section 3, where the authors first state a "Generic Theorem" and then systematically define each of its components (model construction, goodness of fit, distance) for PFM and SBM, is an excellent expository device that makes the core contribution easy to follow. The notation is consistent and well-defined. The connections to prior work and the implications for concepts like clusterability and resilience are also clearly articulated.

**Originality: Are the problems or approaches new?**

The paper's primary contribution is highly original. While the idea of analyzing clustering stability is not new in itself, the proposed framework of providing guarantees for *model-based* clustering algorithms based on a *model-free* goodness-of-fit criterion is a novel and important conceptual shift. Previous work on SBMs and related models (e.g., [18], [20]) has almost universally relied on the assumption that the data is generated from the model in question. This paper "turns around" the logic, asking instead what can be said if the data simply *looks like* it came from a model. This reframing of the problem is a significant departure from the standard line of inquiry in this area and opens up a new direction for research. The specific technical results, including the PFM estimator and the particular form of the stability bounds, are also novel contributions.

**Significance: Are the results important? Are other people likely to use these ideas?**

The potential significance of this work is high. It addresses a fundamental limitation of a large and influential body of literature on community detection, namely the dependence on strong, often unrealistic, generative model assumptions. By providing a pathway to verifiable, data-dependent guarantees, this framework could have a substantial impact on the practical application of graph clustering. Practitioners could use these results to validate discovered community structures and assess their uniqueness without needing to believe in an underlying SBM or PFM. Researchers are likely to build on this framework, for instance by developing tighter bounds (especially for the SBM), extending the approach to other model classes (like DC-SBM), or exploring the theoretical connections between model-free "clusterability" and the performance of various algorithms.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **Novel Framework:** The paper introduces a highly original and significant conceptual framework for model-free validation of model-based clustering, addressing a key limitation of prior work.
2.  **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical results are rigorously derived, and the claims are well-supported.
3.  **Practical Relevance:** The proposed guarantees are based on quantities that are fully computable from data, enabling direct application for cluster validation, which is a significant practical contribution.
4.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized, making a sophisticated argument accessible.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  **Limited SBM Results:** The experiments show that the derived bounds for the SBM are too loose to be useful, which somewhat limits the practical impact of that specific result, though the authors are transparent about this.
2.  **Standard Assumptions:** The analysis still relies on standard but strong conditions, such as the existence of a spectral gap in the observed data matrix.

Overall, the strengths of this paper, particularly its conceptual originality and potential impact, far outweigh its weaknesses. It introduces a new and valuable perspective on a central problem in machine learning and network analysis.
