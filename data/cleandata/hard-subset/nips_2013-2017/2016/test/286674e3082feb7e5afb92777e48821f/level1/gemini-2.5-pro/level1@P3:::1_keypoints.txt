- Paper summary: Proposes a model-free framework that gives data-driven guarantees for graph clusterings by showing that any clustering that fits the graph well must be structurally close to a given good clustering; instantiated for PFM and SBM with computable bounds and links to model-based recovery results.

- Strength: High significance and originality — turns model-based theorems into practical, data-dependent validation tools addressing a key gap between theory and practice.

- Strength: Technical soundness — proofs (Theorems 4 and 9) are elegant and correct, leveraging Davis–Kahan and geometric clustering insights; PFM estimator analysis is well-executed.

- Strength: Practical relevance — bounds use fully computable quantities (ε, eigengaps, etc.), making the framework directly usable for cluster validation and supported by experiments.

- Strength: Clarity and organization — paper is very well written, logically structured, and candid about limitations.

- Weakness: SBM bounds are not practically informative on tested data (δ values > 1); more discussion needed on whether looseness stems from proof techniques or is fundamental.

- Weakness: PFM estimator choice favors analytical tractability over MLE; a brief discussion of trade-offs versus MLE or other estimators would help.

- Weakness: Guarantees are local (stability given a good fit) and do not by themselves ensure efficient discoverability of a good clustering; scope should be emphasized.

- Detailed comment: Question about extending Section 3.1 results to obtain a weighted misclassification error for SBM or an unweighted one for PFM and the technical obstacles involved.

- Detailed comment: The improvement to Wan & Meila (2015) constants (factor of 128) is an important secondary contribution and should be highlighted.

- Detailed comment: Experimental finding that graph smoothing (powers of adjacency) improves stability on Political Blogs is a useful practical takeaway. 

- Conclusion/recommendation: Excellent, novel, and practically relevant paper with strong PFM results; despite loose SBM bounds, reviewer strongly recommends acceptance.
