## Review of "Graph Clustering: Block-models and model free results"

This paper presents a novel framework for graph clustering that aims to provide correctness guarantees *without* assuming the data originates from a specific block model (like SBM or PFM). The core idea is to establish stability: if a clustering 'C' fits the data well, any other clustering that also fits well must be a small perturbation of C. The authors instantiate this framework for both PFM and SBM, demonstrating connections between model-free and model-based recovery guarantees, and even strengthening existing results.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a significant limitation of prior work in graph clustering – the reliance on model assumptions. It builds upon the well-established literature of block models (references [1, 17, 20] are relevant) and spectral clustering ([15]) while offering a distinctly different perspective.  The concept of “goodness of fit” and the subsequent stability guarantees are presented in a rigorous manner.  The theoretical contribution, specifically Theorems 4 and 9, is a clear step towards a more practical and robust approach to graph clustering.  The connection drawn between model-based and model-free approaches is insightful, as exemplified by using results from [20] to improve the model-free PFM guarantees (Theorem 11).

However, the paper suffers from a degree of technical density, making it challenging to follow the proofs without significant background in spectral graph theory and matrix perturbation theory. While the authors mention computability of quantities like ε and δ, a more concrete discussion of the practical implications of these bounds (e.g., what values of ε are typically observed in real-world graphs) would be valuable.  The experimental section, while showing stability in some cases, feels somewhat limited in scope.  The comparison between SBM and PFM bounds (Section 6) highlights that the SBM guarantees are very difficult to satisfy, which raises questions about the practical applicability of the SBM results. The discussion of related work ([6, 16, 14, 5]) is thorough but could be more integrated into the narrative, explicitly contrasting the proposed approach with these existing methods.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** Presents a significant shift in perspective on graph clustering, moving beyond strict model assumptions.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** Rigorous mathematical framework and clear theorems with provable guarantees.
*   **Connection to Existing Work:**  Successfully bridges the gap between model-based and model-free recovery, offering potential improvements to existing algorithms.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Technical Density:** The paper is difficult to grasp without significant prior knowledge.
*   **Limited Practicality (SBM):** The SBM results appear highly restrictive and may have limited practical value.
*   **Experimental Scope:** The experimental evaluation could be more extensive and insightful.



**Overall:** Despite its technical challenges, this paper presents a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of graph clustering. The model-free approach and the connections to existing work are compelling.  I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**, focusing on improving clarity, expanding the experimental evaluation, and discussing the practical limitations of the SBM results.
