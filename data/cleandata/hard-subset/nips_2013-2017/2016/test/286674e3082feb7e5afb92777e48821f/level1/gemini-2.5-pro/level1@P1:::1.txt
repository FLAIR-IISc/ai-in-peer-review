### Reviewer Comments

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a novel framework for providing theoretical guarantees for graph clustering without making the common, and often unrealistic, assumption that the observed graph is generated from a specific random graph model. The central idea is a form of post-hoc validation: if a given clustering `C` provides a good fit to the observed graph `G` under a specific model class (e.g., SBM or PFM), then any other clustering `C'` that also fits the data well must be a small perturbation of `C`. This shifts the burden of proof from an unverifiable generative assumption to a computable "goodness-of-fit" criterion.

The authors instantiate this "Generic Theorem" for two prominent block models: the Preference Frame Model (PFM) and the Stochastic Block Model (SBM). For each model, they define: 1) a procedure to construct model parameters from a graph and a clustering, 2) a goodness-of-fit measure based on the spectral or Frobenius norm difference between the graph's matrix representation (Laplacian for PFM, Adjacency for SBM) and the fitted model's matrix, and 3) a formal theorem bounding the distance between two "good" clusterings as a function of their fit quality and the spectral properties (i.e., eigengap) of the observed graph.

The work is firmly rooted in the context of recent advances in the theoretical understanding of block models and spectral clustering (e.g., Rohe et al., 2011; Wan & Meila, 2015), but it fundamentally challenges the reliance of these works on model-based assumptions. The authors also demonstrate that their framework is not only new but also connected to prior work, showing how existing model-based recovery theorems can be leveraged to derive model-free guarantees and how their techniques can, in turn, tighten existing model-based bounds.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical claims are well-supported by proofs that leverage established tools from matrix perturbation theory, notably the Davis-Kahan theorem. The development is rigorous, and the authors are careful to state their assumptions clearly (e.g., Assumptions 1-4). A major strength is that all quantities in the derived bounds (`ε`, `δ`, eigengaps) are computable from the observed data alone, making the framework practically applicable for cluster validation, which is a significant step forward. The experimental section, while not exhaustive, serves as a solid proof of concept for the theoretical results.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The introduction of the "Generic Theorem" in Section 3 provides an excellent high-level blueprint that makes the subsequent, more technical sections for PFM and SBM easier to follow. The distinction between the model-based and the proposed model-free paradigm is clearly articulated. The notation is dense but consistent. The experimental results in Figure 1 are presented clearly and effectively illustrate the core claim: the stability guarantee holds for small perturbations and breaks down as the clustering deviates further from the one that best fits the data.

**Originality:** The core contribution—providing model-free stability guarantees for model-based clustering algorithms—is highly original. To my knowledge, this is the first work to formalize this "goodness-of-fit implies uniqueness" idea in the context of SBMs and related models. While the concept of clustering stability is not new, this paper's approach of tying it directly to the fit of a generative model is a novel and powerful perspective. It cleverly "turns around" the logic of existing consistency proofs to create a practical validation tool.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is substantial. It addresses a critical and widely acknowledged gap between the theory and practice of community detection. Researchers and practitioners are often hesitant to rely on theoretical guarantees that hinge on strong, untestable modeling assumptions. This paper provides a concrete, computable alternative that could increase confidence in clustering results on real-world networks. The framework is general and could potentially be extended to other models and clustering methods. Furthermore, the insight that model-based theorems can be adapted for model-free validation is an important meta-contribution that could inspire similar work in other areas of machine learning.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Practical Tightness of Bounds:** The derived bounds depend inversely on the eigengap of the graph's Laplacian or adjacency matrix. In many real-world sparse graphs, this gap can be quite small, which may lead to loose or uninformative bounds in practice. The experiments show the bounds are useful in the tested settings, but a more thorough investigation on a wider range of graphs with varying spectral properties would strengthen the paper. A discussion of this potential limitation would be welcome.

2.  **SBM Results:** The experiments show that the SBM-based guarantees (Theorem 9) are much harder to satisfy than the PFM-based ones, even on data generated by an SBM-like LFR model. The paper notes this but could benefit from a deeper discussion. Is this due to looseness in the theoretical analysis for the SBM case, or does it reflect a fundamental rigidity of the SBM that makes it a poor fit for even slightly perturbed data? Clarifying this would be valuable.

3.  **Clarity of PFM Construction:** The PFM model construction in Section 3.1 is defined primarily to facilitate the proof via subspace alignment. While technically sound, a more intuitive explanation for this specific construction choice would improve readability for a broader audience.

4.  **Scope of Experiments:** The experiments effectively validate the theorems but could be expanded. For instance, obtaining the initial clustering `C0` from methods other than spectral clustering (e.g., modularity maximization) and then applying the PFM/SBM validation framework would be an interesting test of the framework's generality.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Novelty and Significance:** Proposes a highly original and significant model-free framework for validating graph clustering, addressing a major limitation of prior theoretical work.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The work is theoretically rigorous, with well-supported claims and computable bounds.
*   **Practical Relevance:** Provides a tool that can be directly applied to real-world data to assess the stability and meaningfulness of a given clustering.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-structured and clearly communicates its core ideas and contributions.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The practical utility of the bounds may be limited by their dependence on the eigengap, an aspect that could be explored more deeply.
*   The experimental validation, while a good proof of concept, could be more comprehensive.

**Conclusion:**

This is an excellent paper that makes a foundational contribution to the theory of graph clustering. The proposed model-free validation framework is novel, elegant, and addresses a critical need in the field. The technical execution is strong, and the paper is written with clarity. Despite minor weaknesses related to the scope of the experimental evaluation, the strengths in originality and significance are overwhelming. This paper is a clear candidate for acceptance and is likely to inspire a new line of research in the reliable application of model-based methods to real-world data.
