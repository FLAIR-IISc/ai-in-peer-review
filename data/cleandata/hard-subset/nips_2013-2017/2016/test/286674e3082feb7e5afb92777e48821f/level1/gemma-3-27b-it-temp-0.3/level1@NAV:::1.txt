## Review of "Graph Clustering: Block-models and model free results"

This paper presents a novel framework for graph clustering that provides theoretical guarantees without assuming the data originates from a specific block model (like SBM, DC-SBM, or PFM). The core idea is to establish stability: if a clustering fits the data well according to a chosen model, then any other clustering that also fits well must be a small perturbation of the original. This allows leveraging existing model-based recovery results while relaxing the strong assumption of model fidelity. The authors instantiate this framework for PFM and SBM, demonstrating connections between model-free and model-based guarantees and even strengthening existing results.

The paper is a significant contribution to the field of graph clustering. The problem addressed – providing guarantees without model assumptions – is a crucial one, as real-world networks rarely perfectly conform to theoretical models. The proposed framework is conceptually elegant and offers a promising path towards more robust clustering methods. The technical results, particularly Theorems 4 and 9, are well-defined and provide concrete bounds on clustering stability. The connection drawn between model-free and model-based recovery is insightful and potentially unlocks new avenues for algorithm design and analysis. The experimental validation, while limited, demonstrates the applicability of the theoretical results.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. The notation is dense and can be challenging to follow, particularly for readers less familiar with spectral graph theory. While the authors claim simplicity in their proofs, the technical details are still quite involved. The experimental section feels somewhat cursory; more extensive evaluation on diverse datasets, and a comparison to existing model-free clustering techniques, would strengthen the claims. Furthermore, the discussion of the limitations of the SBM bounds and potential for tighter results feels underdeveloped. The paper also lacks a detailed discussion of the computational cost of verifying the “goodness of fit” conditions, which could be a practical bottleneck.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and important problem: Addressing the limitations of model-based clustering.
*   Elegant framework: Providing a general approach to model-free guarantees.
*   Strong theoretical results: Theorems 4 and 9 offer concrete stability bounds.
*   Connections to existing work: Leveraging and strengthening existing model-based recovery theorems.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Dense notation and technical complexity.
*   Limited experimental evaluation.
*   Underdeveloped discussion of limitations and computational cost.



**Overall:** Despite some weaknesses, the paper presents a significant theoretical advance in graph clustering. The novelty of the approach, the strength of the theoretical results, and the potential impact on the field outweigh the drawbacks. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to improve clarity and expand the experimental evaluation.
