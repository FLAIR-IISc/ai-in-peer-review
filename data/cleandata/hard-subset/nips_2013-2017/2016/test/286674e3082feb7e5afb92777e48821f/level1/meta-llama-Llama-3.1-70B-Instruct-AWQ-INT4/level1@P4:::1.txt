The paper "Graph Clustering: Block-models and model free results" proposes a framework for clustering graphs without assuming the data comes from a model. The authors provide a generic theorem that shows if a clustering C fits the data well with respect to a model class M, then any other clustering C' that also fits the data well is close to C. The paper instantiates this theorem for the Stochastic Block Model (SBM) and the Preference Frame Model (PFM).

The main ideas of the paper are related to previous work on model-based clustering in networks, particularly the SBM, Degree-Corrected SBM, and PFM. However, the authors' approach is distinct in that it does not assume the data comes from a model. Instead, the framework relies on the statistics of the data that can be checked.

The paper's strengths include:

* The proposed framework is novel and provides a new perspective on graph clustering.
* The authors provide a generic theorem that can be instantiated for different model classes.
* The paper includes a thorough analysis of the PFM and SBM models, including the development of new algorithms and theoretical guarantees.

The paper's weaknesses include:

* The framework's applicability is limited to graphs that can be well-represented by a block model.
* The authors' use of the spectral norm in their assumptions may be too restrictive, and using the Frobenius norm could simplify some of the proofs.
* The paper's experimental evaluation is limited to a few datasets, and more extensive experiments would be beneficial.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel framework for graph clustering that does not rely on model assumptions.
* The authors provide a thorough analysis of the PFM and SBM models, including new algorithms and theoretical guarantees.
* The paper's results have implications for cluster validation, existing model-based recovery theorems, and the stability of clusterings to perturbations of the graph.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The framework's applicability is limited to graphs that can be well-represented by a block model.
* The authors' use of the spectral norm in their assumptions may be too restrictive.
* The paper's experimental evaluation is limited, and more extensive experiments would be beneficial.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and thorough presentation of their ideas. The proposed framework is novel and has the potential to contribute significantly to the field of graph clustering. However, the paper's limitations, such as the restrictive assumptions and limited experimental evaluation, should be carefully considered.

Rating: 8/10
