- Introduces a novel framework for graph clustering that gives model-free guarantees based on how well a clustering fits observed data, not on block-model generation assumptions.

- Instantiates the framework for PFM and SBM and shows connections to and strengthening of existing model-based recovery results.

- Technically sound with rigorous mathematical derivations; theorems are clearly stated and assumptions well-defined.

- Cleverly leverages existing model-based theorems to obtain model-free guarantees; results progress logically from a generic theorem to specific models.

- Discussion of limitations (e.g., tightness of bounds) is insufficient; reasons for weaker SBM bounds need more detail.

- The claim that all required quantities are computable seems optimistic; a concrete computational example is needed.

- Heavy reliance on perturbation bounds (e.g., Davis–Kahan) raises concerns about applicability and sensitivity that are not fully explored.

- Perturbation bounds are acknowledged as sometimes uninformative, but the implications are not deeply analyzed.

- Well-written and organized overall but dense; heavy notation can be overwhelming for readers.

- Proofs are logically structured but would benefit from more intuitive explanations alongside formal derivations.

- Experimental setup is described adequately, but the presentation of results could be clearer and more informative.

- Original and high-impact idea: validating model-based clustering without assuming the model is novel and valuable.

- Positions the work well relative to prior literature and provides alternative proofs using existing results to highlight originality.

- Significant potential: enables theoretical guarantees for clustering on a broader class of real-world graphs without model assumptions.

- Experimental evaluation is limited—mostly reports whether assumptions hold rather than demonstrating algorithmic performance guided by the guarantees.

- Would benefit from visualizations of perturbations and resulting clustering changes, and a deeper comparison to existing stability results.

- The observed difficulty achieving stability under SBM is an important finding that warrants further investigation.

- Arguments for acceptance: novelty, technical rigor, connections to prior work, and potential impact.

- Arguments against acceptance: limited experiments, practical complexity and clarity issues, and insufficient discussion of bound tightness and perturbation sensitivity.

- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions, especially strengthening experimental validation and elaborating limitations.
