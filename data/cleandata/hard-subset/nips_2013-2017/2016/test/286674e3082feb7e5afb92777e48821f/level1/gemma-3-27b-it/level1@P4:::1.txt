This paper introduces a framework for graph clustering that aims to provide theoretical guarantees without assuming the data originates from a specific block model (like SBM, DC-SBM, or PFM). The core idea is to establish guarantees based on how well a clustering and its associated model parameters fit the observed data, demonstrating that any alternative well-fitting clustering will be a small perturbation of the original. The authors instantiate this framework for PFM and SBM, and show connections between model-free and model-based recovery results.

The paper builds upon a substantial body of work in graph clustering and stochastic block models. The motivation for moving beyond model assumptions is well-articulated, as the assumption of a specific generative model often limits the applicability of existing theoretical results. The work directly relates to spectral clustering ([15]), perturbation bounds for clustering ([13, 14, 16]), and recent advances in model-based recovery guarantees ([17, 20]).  The authors appropriately cite relevant literature and position their work within the broader context of the field.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The framework for obtaining correctness guarantees without model assumptions is a significant contribution. It expands the scope of theoretical guarantees for graph clustering beyond the limitations of model-based approaches.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical development is rigorous and well-structured.  The theorems are clearly stated, and the proofs (with details in the supplement) appear sound. The use of perturbation theory and connections to existing spectral clustering results are well-justified.
*   **Generality:**  The generic theorem provides a blueprint for extending the approach to other block models or clustering algorithms.
*   **Practical Relevance:**  The framework offers a way to validate the quality of a clustering *without* needing to know the underlying generative model. The computational feasibility of the quantities involved is highlighted.
*   **Connections to Existing Work:** The paper effectively demonstrates how model-free results can strengthen and be strengthened by model-based results, highlighting a beneficial interplay between these approaches.
*   **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental section provides evidence supporting the theoretical findings, demonstrating the stability of the clustering under perturbations.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Assumptions:** While the goal is to avoid model assumptions, the theorems still rely on conditions like the eigengap condition (Assumption 1) and goodness-of-fit measures (Assumptions 2, 3, 4, 5).  The strength of these assumptions, particularly in real-world scenarios, could be a limitation.  The conditions could be difficult to verify in practice, diminishing the practical impact.
*   **Conservativeness:** The theorems can be conservative; the experimental results show that the SBM bounds are often not satisfied, suggesting that tighter bounds might be possible.  This is acknowledged by the authors.
*   **Clarity of Assumptions:**  While technically correct, the numerous assumptions (e.g., for PFM and SBM separately) can make it challenging to quickly grasp the overall conditions for applying the theorems.  A more consolidated presentation of the required conditions might improve clarity.
*   **Comparison to Related Work:** The discussion of related work, particularly on stability and robustness ([5, 16]), is thorough but could be more comparative. Explicitly contrasting the approach and results with these existing works, highlighting the advantages and disadvantages, would strengthen the paper.
*   **Complexity of Proofs:** While the authors state the proofs are in the supplement, the sheer length and technicality may make it difficult for some readers to fully assess the validity of the results.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a fundamental challenge in graph clustering: providing theoretical guarantees without strong model assumptions.
*   The framework is novel, technically sound, and has the potential to be widely applicable.
*   The results connect to and strengthen existing work in the field.
*   The experimental validation provides supporting evidence for the theoretical findings.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The assumptions required for the theorems to hold may be restrictive in practice.
*   The results can be conservative, and tighter bounds might be possible.
*   The complexity of the technical details might hinder accessibility.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of graph clustering. The proposed framework for model-free guarantees is innovative and well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. While the assumptions and conservativeness of the theorems are potential limitations, the overall quality of the work justifies acceptance. The paper is a significant step towards developing more robust and broadly applicable methods for graph clustering.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
