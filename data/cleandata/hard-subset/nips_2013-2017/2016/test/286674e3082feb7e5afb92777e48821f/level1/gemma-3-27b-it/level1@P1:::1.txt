This paper proposes a novel framework for graph clustering that provides theoretical guarantees without assuming the data originates from a specific block model (like SBM, DC-SBM, or PFM). The core idea revolves around demonstrating that if a clustering *fits* the data well (according to some metric), then any other clustering that also fits well must be a small perturbation of the original. This allows leveraging existing model-based recovery results and strengthening them, offering a more flexible approach to clustering validation and robustness guarantees.

The paper is well-structured, starting with a clear introduction of the problem and the proposed framework. It provides thorough background on graph theory, clustering concepts, and relevant block models. The main theorem (Generic Theorem 1) is stated clearly, and the instantiation for PFM and SBM are well-developed, accompanied by propositions and theorems detailing model construction, goodness-of-fit measures, and distance metrics. The discussion section effectively contextualizes the work within the broader field and highlights future research directions.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The model-free guarantee approach is a significant contribution. Existing theoretical guarantees for graph clustering typically rely on strong model assumptions. This work offers a way to obtain guarantees even when the underlying data generation process is unknown.
* **Generality:** The Generic Theorem 1 provides a general blueprint applicable to various block models, demonstrating the framework’s flexibility.
* **Connections to Existing Work:** The paper skillfully connects the model-free framework with the existing model-based literature, showing how results can be transferred and improved.
* **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical derivations appear rigorous, and the proofs (though some relegated to the supplement) seem logically sound. The use of perturbation theory and spectral analysis is appropriate.
* **Practical Implications:** The paper emphasizes the computability of the quantities needed for the guarantees, making the framework potentially useful for practical applications. The experimental evaluation supports this claim.
* **Thoroughness:** The paper covers related work comprehensively, placing the contribution in a strong context.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:** The mathematical notation and derivations can be dense and challenging to follow, even for readers familiar with the field. While thorough, the presentation could benefit from more intuitive explanations of some concepts.
* **Conservatism:** As acknowledged by the authors, the guarantees may be conservative, particularly for the SBM. The experimental results demonstrate this, showing that the SBM conditions are harder to satisfy.  Further investigation into tighter bounds for SBM would be valuable.
* **Sensitivity to Parameters:** The success of the framework relies on accurately quantifying the "goodness of fit" (e.g., using ε) and the eigengap. Small errors in these estimates could invalidate the guarantees.
* **Limited Experimental Scope:** While the experimental evaluation is a good start, it could be expanded to include more diverse datasets and clustering algorithms. The comparison to other validation methods would be helpful.
* **Supplement Reliance:**  A significant portion of the proofs are placed in the supplement. While common, it makes it harder for a reviewer to fully assess the technical correctness without dedicated time to thoroughly examine the supplemental material.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to graph clustering validation that relaxes the strong assumptions of traditional model-based methods.
* The generality of the framework and its connection to existing work are significant strengths.
* The results have the potential to impact both theoretical understanding and practical applications of graph clustering.
* The experimental results provide preliminary support for the effectiveness of the approach.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The technical complexity and potential conservatism of the guarantees might limit the practical impact of the work.
* The heavy reliance on the supplement for proofs hinders a thorough review.
* The experimental evaluation, while promising, could be more comprehensive.


**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field of graph clustering. The model-free guarantee framework is a novel and valuable addition to the existing literature.  While the paper has some weaknesses, particularly regarding technical complexity and potential conservatism, the strengths outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.  

**Suggestions for Revision:**

* **Improve Clarity:**  Provide more intuitive explanations of key concepts and derivations, potentially with illustrative examples.
* **Investigate SBM Bounds:** Explore tighter bounds for the SBM, addressing the conservatism observed in the experiments.
* **Expand Experiments:** Include a wider range of datasets and clustering algorithms in the experimental evaluation.
* **Consider a brief discussion of computational complexity:** Adding a brief discussion about the computational cost of verifying the goodness-of-fit and computing the relevant parameters (ε, δ) would be valuable.
* **Briefly address potential limitations regarding the choice of distance metric:** A short discussion on how the choice of distance metric might affect the results could be beneficial.
