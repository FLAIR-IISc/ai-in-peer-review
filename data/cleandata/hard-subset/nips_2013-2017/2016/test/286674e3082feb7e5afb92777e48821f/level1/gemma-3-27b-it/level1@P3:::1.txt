This paper presents a novel framework for graph clustering with theoretical guarantees that do *not* rely on the assumption that the data is generated from a specific block model (SBM, DC-SBM, PFM). The core idea is to establish guarantees based on how well a clustering *fits* the observed data, rather than assuming the data originates from a given model. This is a significant and potentially impactful direction, as model assumptions are often limiting in real-world applications. The authors successfully demonstrate this framework for PFM and SBM, and show connections to existing model-based recovery results, even strengthening some of them.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the mathematical derivations appear rigorous, although a deeper dive into the proofs would be required for complete confirmation (especially given the length and density of the proofs relegated to the supplement). The theorems are clearly stated, and the assumptions are well-defined. The approach of leveraging existing model-based theorems to derive model-free guarantees is clever and well-executed.  The results build logically, starting from a generic theorem and instantiating it for specific models. However, the discussion of the limitations (e.g., tightness of bounds) could be more detailed. It's not always clear *why* the SBM bounds are weaker, beyond a general statement about the model being less flexible.  The statement that all quantities are computable is a strong claim and feels slightly optimistic given the complexity of some calculations; a practical example demonstrating the computation of these quantities would be helpful.  A slight concern is the reliance on perturbation bounds (like Davis-Kahan). While standard, their applicability and sensitivity to parameter choices aren't thoroughly discussed. The fact that the perturbation bounds are not always informative is acknowledged in the discussion, but the implications of this are not fully explored.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, though dense. The notation is standard, but the sheer volume of it can be overwhelming at times. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and outlines the proposed framework.  The background section provides sufficient context for readers familiar with graph clustering and block models.  The presentation of the theorems is clear, and the proofs (in the supplement) appear logically structured.  However, some sections (particularly the proofs) could benefit from more intuitive explanations alongside the formal mathematical derivations. The experimental setup is described reasonably well, but the presentation of results could be improved (see "Significance" below).

**Originality:**  The main contribution – providing model-free guarantees for clustering – is highly original. While stability results for clustering exist, applying this to *validate* model-based clustering without assuming the model is correct is a novel perspective.  The connection between model-based and model-free results is also a valuable contribution. The authors clearly position their work relative to existing literature (e.g., [6], [16], [14]), and correctly identify the unique aspects of their approach. The alternative proofs using existing results (e.g., from [20], [18]) further highlight the originality of the framework.

**Significance:** The potential significance of this work is considerable.  Removing the model assumption opens up the possibility of applying clustering algorithms with theoretical guarantees to a wider range of real-world graphs.  The framework provides a principled way to validate the results of model-based clustering algorithms, even when the underlying model is unknown. The experimental evaluation, however, is somewhat limited. While the authors demonstrate the applicability of their framework on synthetic and real datasets, the results are presented primarily as statements about whether the assumptions hold or fail.  More compelling would be a comparison of the performance of clustering algorithms *without* relying on model assumptions, guided by the guarantees provided in the paper. Visualizations of the perturbations and the resulting clustering changes would also be valuable.  The comparison to existing stability results ([16], [14]) is helpful, but a more detailed discussion of the trade-offs and advantages of their approach would be beneficial.  The observed difficulty in achieving stability under the SBM framework is a noteworthy finding, and further investigation into the conditions under which the SBM is amenable to model-free guarantees would be valuable.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and original framework for model-free graph clustering guarantees.
*   Technically sound and mathematically rigorous.
*   Connections to and strengthening of existing model-based results.
*   Potential for significant impact on the application of clustering algorithms in real-world scenarios.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Experimental evaluation is somewhat limited and could be more compelling.
*   The complexity of the results makes practical application challenging and requires further clarification.
*   The discussion of limitations (e.g., tightness of bounds) could be more detailed.
*   Dependence on perturbation bounds without a thorough discussion of their sensitivity.



**Overall Assessment:**  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper presents a significant theoretical contribution to the field of graph clustering. The originality and potential impact of the work outweigh the limitations in the experimental evaluation. Addressing the suggested revisions, particularly strengthening the experimental validation and providing more detailed discussion of the limitations, would further enhance the quality and impact of the paper.
