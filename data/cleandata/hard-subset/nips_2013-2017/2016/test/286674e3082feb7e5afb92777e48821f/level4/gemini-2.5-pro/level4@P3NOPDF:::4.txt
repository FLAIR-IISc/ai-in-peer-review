This paper introduces a general framework for providing theoretical guarantees on the stability of a given clustering. The proposed stability measure quantifies the distance between a clustering C and any other clustering C' that also fits the data well, according to a specified goodness-of-fit measure. A key strength of this framework is that the resulting guarantees are expressed in terms of quantities computable directly from the data. The authors demonstrate this 'template' by applying it to two specific block models, the Preferential Friendship Model (PFM) and the Stochastic Block Model (SBM), and use the stability results to derive exact recovery guarantees. While the goal of providing model-agnostic, data-dependent guarantees is highly valuable, the current instantiation of the framework and the supporting simulations raise significant concerns about its practical utility.

**Strengths**

The primary contribution of this work is the development of a model-free template for analyzing clustering stability. The ambition to provide theoretical guarantees that depend only on the data and the clustering output, rather than on unverifiable assumptions about a data-generating process, is a significant and commendable research direction. If successful, such a framework would be of great value to both theoreticians and practitioners.

**Major Concerns**

1.  **Inconsistent Treatment of Related Models:** The paper provides separate analyses for the PFM and SBM. Given that PFM is a generalization of the Degree-Corrected SBM (DC-SBM), which itself generalizes the SBM, the motivation for treating these models with different estimators and analyses is unclear. Specifically, why is the graph Laplacian used for the PFM estimator, while the adjacency matrix is used for the SBM? Furthermore, the theoretical results for PFM are in terms of weighted Misclassification Error (ME), whereas for SBM they are in terms of standard ME, making a direct comparison difficult. A unified treatment, or at least a clear justification for the divergence, would significantly strengthen the paper. The omission of results for the DC-SBM, an important intermediate model, is also a notable gap.

2.  **Unconvincing Empirical Results:** The simulations raise serious questions about the practical utility of the derived guarantees, which is a central claim of the paper.
    *   For the PFM, the guarantees appear to hold only for extremely small perturbations. The authors note that a large eigengap causes the model fit to degrade rapidly, but this is not sufficiently explained. Paradoxically, for the political blog data, the graph is manipulated in a way that exaggerates the eigengap (raising the adjacency matrix to a power), which seems to improve stability. This apparent contradiction needs clarification. More importantly, if the goodness-of-fit measure is so sensitive that tiny perturbations invalidate the guarantee, its practical value is diminished.
    *   The admission that the conditions for the SBM guarantee are "very hard to satisfy" (Lines 248-250) is concerning and undermines the paper's claims of providing useful, data-dependent guarantees.

3.  **Limited Scope of the Framework:** The paper's framework appears to be constrained in two significant ways.
    *   First, the analysis is restricted to comparing clusterings with the same number of clusters (K). This overlooks practical scenarios where different algorithms or parameter choices might suggest slightly different numbers of clusters (e.g., K and K+1), where one cluster is split. A robust stability notion should be able to handle such near-agreement.
    *   Second, while the template is presented as generic, its application is only demonstrated on disjoint clusters from block models. It is not clear how or if the techniques would extend to other structures where spectral clustering is known to be effective, such as hierarchical or overlapping clusters (e.g., as analyzed in Balakrishnan et al., "Noise Thresholds for Spectral Clustering", 2011). A discussion on the potential for such extensions would be valuable.

4.  **Insufficient Comparison to Related Work:** The related work section could be substantially improved by discussing other model-free or perturbation-based stability notions. For instance, works on "clustering with spectral norm" (Kumar and Kannan, 2010) or "perturbation resilience/approximation stability" (Balcan et al., 2012, 2013) offer alternative perspectives on stability. The paper would be stronger if it explicitly contrasted its proposed stability measure with these existing concepts, particularly explaining how it relates to stability defined by the perturbation of cluster centers.

**Questions and Suggestions for Clarification**

*   In Proposition 10, the notation `< M', M >_F` is used without definition. Please define this inner product.
*   In Assumption 5, is the term `||L_hat^2 - L^2||_F` or `||L_hat - L||_F^2`? If it is the former, what is the intuition behind squaring the Laplacian matrices before taking the norm?
*   The caption for Figure 1 states, "For SBM, delta is always greater than delta0". It is unclear which subplot this refers to, as SBM results do not appear to be plotted. Please clarify.
*   There is a mismatch between theory and experiments for the PFM. The theorems provide guarantees for weighted ME, but the experiments evaluate performance using standard ME. Please address this discrepancy.

**Minor Presentation Issues**

*   Figure 1 is missing axis labels.
*   (Line 44) The description of block models should clarify that `A_ij` for `i > j` are conditionally independent given the cluster assignments.
*   Several notations (B, C, Y, lambda) are redefined between the PFM and SBM sections. This is confusing. Using distinct notation (e.g., `Y_perp`, `C_D`, `lambda^L`, `lambda^A`) would greatly improve readability. For example, the expression YZ in the SBM section could be written out directly in Proposition 8 to avoid confusion.
*   Section 4.4 seems more appropriate as a subsection within the Related Work section.
