

The authors present novel theoretical results for graph clustering that operate without assuming a specific generative model for the observed graph (e.g., no assumption of stochastic block model generation). The core contribution establishes that if a clustering method identifies a reasonable fit to the graph (under a defined criterion), then any other clustering achieving a comparable fit must be close to the first. This work addresses an important problem by providing distribution-free guarantees, making it highly relevant. The paper is well-written and accessible without requiring supplementary material, though some content was moved there due to page constraints. The notation using "hats" (e.g., $\hat{L}$ for the observed Laplacian) is somewhat confusing, but this is a minor issue. The results are interesting, well-explained, and supported by illustrative examples. However, the framework has notable limitations. First, the main result for the Poisson Flow Model (PFM) employs a non-standard distance measure between clusterings; while the authors argue this measure may be more natural than classical alternatives, this claim is debatable. Second, the theoretical approach relies on a "proxy" model: from a clustering, a model (e.g., a PFM) is reconstructed and compared to the original graph (rather than the underlying generative process). Though this is natural within the authors' framework, it significantly constrains the definition of a "good" clustering for a given graph (requiring the graph to be well-approximated in the normalized Laplacian sense). While necessary for the results, this constraint raises questions about the framework's applicability, particularly given its limitations on real-world data—where graph smoothing is often required to recover structure (per the authors' theoretical framework), whereas classical algorithms frequently recover ground truth without such preprocessing.
