This paper presents a general framework for obtaining theoretical guarantees for graph clustering algorithms, circumventing the typical assumption that data originates from a specific generative model like the Stochastic Block Model (SBM). The authors demonstrate that if a clustering fits the data well, any other well-fitting clustering will be a small perturbation of the original. They instantiate this framework for the SBM and Preference Frame Model (PFM), and show connections between model-free and model-based recovery results. This work is a significant contribution to the field of community detection, offering a novel perspective on establishing clustering validity and robustness without relying on strict model assumptions.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a key limitation of existing theoretical results that hinge on the often-unrealistic assumption of model adherence. The proposed framework is conceptually sound and the authors demonstrate its applicability through concrete results for both the SBM and PFM. The connection made between model-free and model-based approaches is particularly insightful, allowing for the leveraging of existing recovery theorems. The writing is clear and the exposition is well-organized, making the complex ideas accessible. The authors thoughtfully explain their choices regarding error metrics and assumptions, and acknowledge limitations.  The experimental validation, while limited, provides a preliminary assessment of the theoretical results.

A major strength of this work lies in its generality and the fact that all quantities required for applying the theorems are computable from the data. This addresses a common critique of theoretical results in this area, which often rely on parameters difficult to estimate in practice. Furthermore, the authors highlight potential avenues for future research, such as tightening bounds for the SBM and exploring negative guarantees.

However, a significant weakness, acknowledged by the authors, is the reliance on spectral algorithms, whose performance is known to be suboptimal in sparse graph settings. This limits the practical applicability of the guarantees, especially for many real-world networks. The theoretical bounds, while impressive in their generality, may also be loose in certain scenarios, as indicated by the experimental section.  The related work section, while comprehensive, could benefit from discussing more recent advances in spectral clustering and related stability results beyond those cited. Furthermore, the discussion of alternative goodness-of-fit measures or distances between clusterings beyond the Misclassification Error and Weighted ME could enrich the analysis.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The paper introduces a novel framework for model-free clustering guarantees, a significant departure from the traditional model-based approach.
*   **Generality:** The framework is broadly applicable to various block models and offers a unified perspective on clustering validation.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The theorems are rigorously proven and well-supported by mathematical analysis.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow, despite the technical complexity.
*   **Potential Impact:** The work has the potential to influence future research in community detection and graph clustering.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Dependence on Spectral Clustering:** The reliance on spectral algorithms limits the applicability of the results to dense graphs.
*   **Potential Looseness of Bounds:** The theoretical guarantees may be conservative in practice.
*   **Limited Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental section is relatively small and could be expanded to demonstrate the effectiveness of the framework on a wider range of datasets.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the limitations related to the spectral clustering dependency, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of community detection. The novelty, generality, and clarity of the work outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with a minor revision request to expand the experimental evaluation and address the comments regarding related work. Specifically, the authors should explore if the framework can be extended to other clustering algorithms less dependent on spectral methods.
