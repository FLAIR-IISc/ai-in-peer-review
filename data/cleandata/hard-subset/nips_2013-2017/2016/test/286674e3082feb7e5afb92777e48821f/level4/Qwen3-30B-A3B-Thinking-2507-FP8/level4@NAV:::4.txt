

The paper introduces a generic template for deriving theoretical stability guarantees for clustering solutions \(C\) that fit the data well, measured by the distance between \(C\) and any other \(C'\) fitting the data equally well. It applies this template to two block models (PFM and SBM), demonstrating that all guarantee quantities can be explicitly computed from the data. The stability results are further leveraged to establish exact recovery guarantees for these models. However, simulation results indicate that either the goodness-of-fit measure or the theorem conditions render the guarantees practically limited.  

1) The paper addresses the important problem of model-free theoretical guarantees for clustering stability, where quantities depend solely on the data and clustering outputs. This approach holds significant theoretical and practical value.  
2) Given that PFM encompasses DC-SBM (which includes SBM), it is unclear why PFM and SBM are treated as distinct cases. Since spectral clustering performs well across all specified models, why not use a unified estimator? Specifically, the use of the Laplacian for PFM versus the adjacency matrix for SBM lacks justification. Additionally, PFM results are presented for weighted misclassification error (ME), while SBM results use standard ME—how do these compare? The absence of DC-SBM results is also notable.  
3) The definition of \(\langle M', M \rangle_F\) in Proposition 10 requires clarification.  
4) In Assumption 5, is the norm \(\| \hat{L}^2 - L^2 \|_F\) or \(\| \hat{L} - L \|_F^2\)? If the former, what is the rationale for squaring the Laplacians?  
5) The focus on disjoint clusters limits applicability. Can the template extend to non-block models where spectral clustering succeeds, such as hierarchical clustering (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2011)?  
6) The framework assumes \(C\) and \(C'\) have identical cluster counts. However, algorithms may yield differing cluster numbers (e.g., \(K\) vs. \(K+1\) clusters), where \(C'\) might split one cluster of \(C\)—a scenario not captured by the current metric.  
7) The Related Work section omits key model-free clustering results (Kumar & Kannan, 2010; Balcan et al., 2012, 2013). How does the paper’s stability notion relate to perturbation resilience or approximation stability? Specifically, how does it connect to stability measured via center perturbations?  
8) Simulations show PFM tolerates only minuscule perturbations (lines 252–253), but the claim that "large eigengap causes \( \text{PFM}(G, C) \) to differ from \( \text{PFM}(G, C_0) \) under very small perturbations" lacks qualification. Conversely, smoothing political blog data via \(A^k\) exacerbates eigenvalue gaps yet improves stability—this contradiction needs explanation. If small perturbations undermine the guarantees, their practical utility (a key selling point) is questionable. Lines 248–250 note SBM conditions are hard to satisfy, which is unfortunate.  
9) Figure 1 caption states "\(\delta\) is always greater than \(\delta_0\) for SBM," but SBM is not plotted in the figure; this must be clarified.  
10) Theorems for PFM use weighted ME, yet experiments perturb standard ME—this inconsistency requires addressing.  

**Minor Points:**  
1) Figure 1 axes lack labels.  
2) Line 44: For block models, \(A_{ij}\) for \(i > j\) are independent given cluster assignments of \(i\) and \(j\).  
3) Notation inconsistencies: \(B\), \(C\), and \(Y\) are redefined for PFM vs. SBM (Section 3.2). To avoid confusion, consider using \(Y_{\perp}\) and \(C_D\) for PFM instead of \(B\) and \(C\), and directly substitute \(YZ\) in Proposition 8 (SBM estimator). Distinguish eigenvalues via \(\lambda^L\) (PFM) and \(\lambda^B\) (SBM).  
4) Section 4.4 would be more appropriately placed in Related Work.
