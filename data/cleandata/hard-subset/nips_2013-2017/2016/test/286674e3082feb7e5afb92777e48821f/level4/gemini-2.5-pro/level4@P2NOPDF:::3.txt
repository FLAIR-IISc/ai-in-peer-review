This manuscript proposes a collection of algorithms for graph clustering and for evaluating the similarity between two cluster assignments.

A central weakness of the paper is the lack of a clear, unifying motivation for the proposed methods. While the technical descriptions of the individual algorithms are presented with clarity, the manuscript does not adequately explain the rationale for presenting this specific set of algorithms together. As a result, the work reads more like a disconnected catalog of techniques rather than a cohesive contribution driven by a singular research question or principle.

Furthermore, the novelty of the work appears limited. The clustering algorithms are largely presented as incremental modifications of existing, well-established methods. The primary novel contribution seems to be concentrated in the specific constraints introduced toward the end of the paper.

In summary, for the paper to be more impactful, the authors should significantly improve the narrative that connects these different algorithmic contributions. A stronger justification for why these particular modifications are important and a clearer articulation of the paper's core scientific contribution, beyond the final set of constraints, would be necessary.
