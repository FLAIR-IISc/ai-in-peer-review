This paper proposes a novel framework for graph clustering that provides theoretical guarantees without assuming a specific data generating process. The authors aim to bridge the gap between model-based clustering and model-free approaches. The paper's main contribution is the development of a generic theorem that relates the goodness of fit of a model to the closeness of two clusterings.

The paper starts by introducing the Stochastic Block Model (SBM), Degree-Corrected SBM (DC-SBM), and Preference Frame Model (PFM) as common models for graph clustering. The authors then propose a framework for clustering without assuming a specific model. They define a model class M and a model M(G, C) that is compatible with clustering C and fitted to graph G. The generic theorem states that if M(G, C) is close to G, then any other clustering C' that also fits G well is close to C.

The authors then instantiate this generic theorem for the PFM and SBM models. They propose a PFM estimation algorithm and prove that it is consistent under standard recovery conditions. They also derive a main result for PFM, which bounds the distance between two clusterings in terms of the eigengap of the graph Laplacian.

Similarly, the authors propose an SBM estimation algorithm and derive a main result for SBM, which bounds the distance between two clusterings in terms of the eigengap of the graph incidence matrix.

The paper also discusses the implications of the results, including cluster validation, using existing model-based recovery theorems to prove model-free guarantees, and sanity checks and extensions.

The experimental evaluation is conducted on simulated datasets and real-world data, including the Political Blogs dataset. The results show that the proposed framework can provide model-free guarantees for clustering and that the bounds are computable from the data.

Overall, the paper presents an interesting approach to graph clustering that combines model-based and model-free methods. The results are promising, but the paper could benefit from more detailed explanations and justifications of the theoretical framework.

Strengths:

* The paper proposes a novel framework for graph clustering that combines model-based and model-free methods.
* The generic theorem provides a theoretical foundation for the framework.
* The instantiation of the generic theorem for PFM and SBM models is well done.
* The experimental evaluation is thorough and provides insights into the performance of the proposed framework.

Weaknesses:

* The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations and justifications of the theoretical framework.
* Some of the proofs and derivations are relegated to the supplement, which may make it difficult for readers to follow the paper.
* The paper could benefit from more comparisons with existing methods and more discussions on the implications of the results.
