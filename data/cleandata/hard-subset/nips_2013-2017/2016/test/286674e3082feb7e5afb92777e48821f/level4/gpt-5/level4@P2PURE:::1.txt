The authors present new theoretical results for graph clustering that avoid assumptions about the graph’s generative process (i.e., the observed graph is not assumed to follow a specific model such as the stochastic block model). The paper’s central idea is that if a clustering method finds a partition that fits the graph reasonably well (under a precise definition), then any other partition that fits the graph equally well must be close to the first. This is an important and interesting contribution, providing distribution-free guarantees for graph clustering.

Although some content is deferred to the supplementary material due to page limits, the main paper is clear and largely self-contained. I am not convinced that placing “hats” on observed quantities (e.g., using ĤL for the observed Laplacian) is a good notational choice, but aside from this, the exposition is generally clear. The results are well explained and illustrated.

That said, the work has limitations. Empirically, for some real-world datasets, the graph must be smoothed for structure to be recoverable in the authors’ theoretical sense, whereas many classical algorithms recover the ground-truth structure without such preprocessing.

More importantly, two conceptual limitations arise:
1) The main result for PFM relies on a nonstandard distance between clusterings. The authors argue this metric may be more natural than classical alternatives, but this claim is debatable.
2) The analysis is based on a “proxy” model: given a clustering, one reconstructs a model (e.g., a PFM) and compares that model to the original graph, rather than to the model that produced the clustering. While natural in the authors’ framework, this choice imposes a strong notion of what constitutes a good clustering (namely, that the graph is well approximated in the normalized Laplacian sense). This restriction is understandable to obtain rigorous results, but it raises concerns about its practical impact, especially in light of the framework’s limitations on some real-world examples.
