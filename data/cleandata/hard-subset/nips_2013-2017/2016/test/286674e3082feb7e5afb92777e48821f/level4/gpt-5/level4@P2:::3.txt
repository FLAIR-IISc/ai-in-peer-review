Summary
The paper proposes a model-free framework for validating graph clusterings by leveraging fitted block-models as certificates of stability. Given a graph G, a clustering C, and a model class M (SBM, DC-SBM, or PFM), the authors fit model parameters M(G, C) compatible with C. If the fitted model approximates the data well (measured by spectral norm discrepancies in the adjacency or Laplacian), then any other clustering that fits equally well must be close to C in misclassification error (ME) or degree-weighted ME. This yields computable, data-dependent guarantees without assuming the data were generated by a block model. The framework is instantiated for PFM and SBM. For PFM, they design a spectral estimator that aligns subspaces and prove bounds via Davis–Kahan perturbation, leading to a stability guarantee that depends on the Laplacian eigengap and a goodness-of-fit parameter ε. For SBM, they use the MLE estimator and a similar goodness-of-fit condition on the adjacency matrix. They also show how existing model-based recovery results (e.g., Qin & Rohe; Rohe, Chatterjee & Yu; Wan & Meilă) can be repurposed to yield alternative model-free bounds. Experiments on synthetic PFM, perturbed PFM, LFR graphs, and the Political Blogs network compute these quantities empirically: PFM-based guarantees are often informative, while SBM-based bounds tend to be looser.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on spectral recovery results for SBM/DC-SBM/PFM and perturbation theory (Davis–Kahan), as well as notions of clusterability and resilience. It differs from model-based works by removing the generative assumption and replacing it with measurable goodness-of-fit conditions; it also contrasts with objective-based stability analyses (e.g., Cheeger/NCut bounds) by using model fit as the proxy for cluster quality. The connections to [18], [17], and [20] are appropriate; the comparison to stability/clusterability literature is useful.

Strengths
- Clear high-level idea: convert model-based recovery conditions into model-free, computable guarantees using data-driven fit metrics and eigengaps.
- Concrete instantiations for both PFM and SBM, with explicit estimators and bounds involving only observable quantities.
- Use of standard perturbation tools (Davis–Kahan) yields relatively transparent proofs and interpretable dependencies on eigengaps and fit errors.
- Experiments demonstrate feasibility of computing bounds on real and synthetic graphs and highlight where guarantees are and are not informative.

Weaknesses
- Motivation and presentation: while the algorithms/estimators are clearly described, the rationale for presenting multiple estimation steps in “algorithm list” form is underdeveloped. Several estimators are adaptations of well-known procedures; the central novelty is the accompanying stability guarantees, not the estimators themselves. Clarify this to avoid the impression of algorithmic novelty claims.
- Clarity and notation: there are numerous typos and notational inconsistencies (e.g., calling L̂ a normalized Laplacian when it is the symmetrically normalized adjacency; “Rigorously speaking, the normalized graph Laplacian is I − L̂” should be stated upfront to avoid confusion). Some assumptions (e.g., λ̂1 = 1, ordering by |λ|) and symbols (δ, δ0, ε) could be summarized in a notation table.
- Practical informativeness: SBM bounds are often vacuous in experiments (δ > 1), even on LFR graphs where SBM is expected to fit; this limits practical significance unless tighter bounds are developed.
- Strength of assumptions: several results hinge on spectral norm closeness and nontrivial eigengaps; guidance on how to estimate or ensure these in practice (e.g., via regularization) would improve applicability.
- Experimental depth: results largely report whether δ ≤ δ0 holds; adding sensitivity analyses (varying K, unbalanced clusters, degree heterogeneity) and comparisons to objective-based stability measures would strengthen the empirical case.

Quality
Technically sound overall. The use of Davis–Kahan, subspace alignment, and ME/wME distances is appropriate. Proof sketches are reasonable given space; full proofs reportedly in the supplement. Some assumptions (e.g., Assumption 5) are quite strong; the paper acknowledges this and provides alternatives.

Clarity
Moderately clear but needs editing for grammar, typos, and notation. Key definitions (normalized adjacency vs. Laplacian, distance measures, fit metrics) should be consolidated and made consistent. The motivation should more clearly emphasize that the contribution lies in guarantees rather than algorithmic novelty.

Originality
Moderate. The idea of using model fit to certify clustering stability without a generative assumption is a useful reframing, and its instantiation for PFM/SBM with fully computable bounds is novel to my knowledge. The estimators themselves are largely standard.

Significance
Potentially useful for practitioners seeking post hoc validation of clusterings when model assumptions are dubious. The PFM results appear more promising; the current SBM bounds seem too loose to be broadly impactful without further tightening.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually clean framework with computable, data-dependent guarantees.
- Bridges model-based theory and model-free validation; applicable to multiple block-model families.
- Accessible proofs and explicit dependence on eigengaps and fit error.

Cons for acceptance
- Presentation and motivation need polishing; algorithmic listings can distract from the central theoretical contribution.
- Empirical utility is mixed; SBM guarantees often non-informative.
- Several notational and editorial issues reduce readability.

Suggestions for improvement
- Reframe the “algorithms” sections as “estimators used to define fit” and focus the narrative on the stability guarantees.
- Standardize notation and clarify the role of L̂ versus I − L̂; add a notation/assumption summary table.
- Provide practical guidance for computing ε, choosing K, and handling small eigengaps (e.g., via regularization).
- Explore tighter SBM bounds or regimes where they become informative; consider degree-regularization or concentration-based ε estimates.
- Expand experiments with sensitivity analyses and comparison to alternative validation metrics.

Overall evaluation
Interesting and potentially useful contribution with clear theoretical underpinnings, but the writing and empirical informativeness (especially for SBM) need strengthening. I lean borderline accept if the authors substantially improve clarity and better position the contribution as a validation framework rather than new algorithms; otherwise, borderline reject.
