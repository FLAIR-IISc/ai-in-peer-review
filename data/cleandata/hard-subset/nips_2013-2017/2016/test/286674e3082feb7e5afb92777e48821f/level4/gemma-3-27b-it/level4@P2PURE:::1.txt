The authors present novel theoretical results for graph clustering that avoid assumptions about the graph’s generative process – the method does not require the observed graph to be generated by a specific model like the stochastic block model. The central idea is that if a clustering method identifies a reasonable clustering for a graph, any other clustering with comparable fit will be structurally similar to the first. This work is compelling and addresses an important problem by providing distribution-free results for graph clustering. Despite some content being moved to supplementary materials due to page limits, the paper remains clear and self-contained. While I question the notational choice of using “hats” to denote observed quantities (e.g., \hat{L} for the observed Laplacian), this is a minor point. The results are interesting, well-explained, and effectively illustrated. However, they do have limitations. The experimental results demonstrate that, for some real-world data, graph smoothing is necessary to enable structure recovery within the authors’ theoretical framework, whereas many established algorithms can directly recover the ground truth. Furthermore, two key limitations exist: 1) The primary result for the probabilistic framework model (PFM) employs a non-standard distance metric between clusterings. While the authors argue this metric is more natural, this claim is debatable. 2) The results rely on a “proxy” model approach; a model (such as a PFM) is reconstructed from a clustering and then compared to the original graph, rather than directly evaluating the model used to generate the clustering. This approach is natural given the authors’ context, but it also tightly constrains the definition of a “good” clustering – in this case, meaning the graph is well-approximated in the normalized Laplacian sense. While necessary for obtaining results, the impact of these constraints, particularly given the framework’s limitations on certain real-world examples, warrants consideration.
