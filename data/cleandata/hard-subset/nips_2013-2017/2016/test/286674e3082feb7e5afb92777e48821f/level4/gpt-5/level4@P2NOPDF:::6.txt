This paper aims to develop a model-free theoretical framework for graph clustering. In its current form, the contribution is unpersuasive because key claims are stated informally and without clearly specified assumptions. Several central results rely on vague phrases and undefined terms, making it difficult to assess correctness or novelty.

For example, Theorem 1 (Generic Theorem) states that any clustering “which also fits G well is close to C,” but neither “fits G well” nor “close” is defined. What objective, metric, or probabilistic guarantee is intended? Likewise, Proposition 3 (Informal) invokes “standard recovery conditions” without specifying the underlying graph model, separability assumptions, noise regime, or sample complexity that would make these conditions precise.

Because these foundational statements are left imprecise, the theory reads as largely heuristic rather than rigorous. The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
- Providing precise definitions of the graph setting, the notion of “fit,” the distance between clusterings, and any regularity or identifiability conditions.
- Stating all theorems formally with explicit assumptions and quantifiers (including the probability model, norms/metrics, constants, and rates).
- Supplying complete proofs or detailed proof sketches that make clear which established results are being used.
- Clarifying the scope and novelty relative to existing model-free or robust graph clustering theory, and specifying exactly how the proposed framework advances the state of the art.

As written, the results read more like restatements of intuitive desiderata than established guarantees. Without formalization and rigorous analysis, the claims are not credible, and the contribution remains unclear. I cannot recommend acceptance in the paper’s current form.
