The paper proposes a generic template for providing theoretical guarantees on the stability of a clustering C that fits the data well. The stability is measured in terms of the distance between C and any other clustering C' that fits the same data well. The authors demonstrate the application of this template to two block models, namely the planted factor model (PFM) and the stochastic block model (SBM), and show that all the quantities involved in the guarantees can be computed explicitly from the data. Furthermore, they utilize the stability results to establish exact recovery guarantees for these models.

However, the simulations suggest that either the goodness-of-fit measure used or the conditions of the theorems provided render the results not too useful in practice. The paper addresses an important problem of providing model-free theoretical guarantees for clustering, which is crucial for both theoretical and practical purposes.

There are several concerns and questions raised by the paper. Firstly, it is not clear why the authors treat PFM and SBM differently, given that PFM includes DC-SBM as a special case, which in turn includes SBM. Spectral Clustering works well for all the specified models, so it is unclear why different model estimators are used for PFM and SBM. Specifically, the Laplacian is used for PFM, while the adjacency matrix is used for SBM. Additionally, the results for PFM are in terms of weighted maximum entropy (ME), whereas the results for SBM are in terms of ME. It is also unclear how these results compare with each other.

The paper lacks clarity on several notations and definitions. For instance, the definition of <M', M>F in Proposition 10 is not provided. In Assumption 5, it is unclear whether the norm is ||L^2 - L^2||F or ||L - L||F^2. If the former, what is the intuition behind squaring the Laplacians?

The paper primarily focuses on disjoint clusters, but it is unclear whether the techniques used in this paper extend beyond block models to other models for which Spectral Clustering works. For example, Spectral clustering is known to work well for hierarchical clustering, and there are theoretical guarantees available. How would the template work for this case?

The evaluation metric considered, as well as the estimators used in the cases of PFM and SBM, are limited to C and C' having the same number of clusters. However, different clustering algorithms can potentially give different numbers of clusters. For instance, the eigenvalues of A might suggest K clusters for C, while the eigenvalues of the Laplacian of A might suggest K' = K + 1 clusters for C' such that all the clusters in C and C' are the same except for one cluster that splits into two. C' is still "close" to C, but it cannot be captured by the given framework.

The related work section does not reflect a lot of model-free results that exist. For example, "Clustering with Spectral Norm and the k-means Algorithm" by Kumar and Kannan (2010), "Clustering under Perturbation Resilience" by Balcan et al. (2012), and "Clustering under Approximation Stability" by Balcan et al. (2013) are not mentioned. How does the notion of stability considered in this work relate to those in these works? In particular, how is the stability in this paper related to the stability measured for perturbation of centers for two clusterings?

The simulations for PFM have some issues. The perturbations tolerated are too small, and the statement "large eigengap makes PFM(G, C) differ from PFM(G, C0) even for very small perturbations" is unclear. On the other hand, the political blog data is smoothed out by raising the power of A, which would further exaggerate the gaps in eigenvalues, but in this case, it seems to help stability. However, if such small perturbations can affect the model fit, then it renders the guarantee not of much use for practical purposes.

There are some minor points that need to be addressed. The labels for the axes in Figure 1 are missing. The notation B, C, and Y have been used with different definitions for PFM and SBM, which can be confusing. It is better to maintain consistency throughout the paper. For example, using Y and CD instead of B and C for PFM would be clearer. Additionally, the estimator of SBM can be simplified by using the expression of YZ directly in Proposition 8.

The paper has some strengths, but it also has several weaknesses that need to be addressed. The authors should clarify the notations and definitions, provide more insights into the simulations, and discuss the limitations of the current framework.
