This paper establishes a relationship between different distance metrics used to compare clusterings of network data. Focusing on network models such as the Preference Frame Model (PFM) and Stochastic Block Model (SBM), the authors demonstrate a locally convex-like property for clustering solutions. Specifically, they prove that clusterings which fit the observed network data equally well—in terms of model parameter estimation—are also closely related according to various misclassification-based distance measures. This result highlights a form of equivalence between different clustering comparison metrics within the context of network data.

The authors support their theoretical findings with both experimental validation and illustrative examples. The core result—demonstrating the consistency of clustering distance measures—is novel and offers a valuable insight into the landscape of possible clusterings for network data. However, it's important to acknowledge related work in this area. Papers such as Peng, Sun, and Zanetti (COLT, 2015) and Chao, Lu, Ma, and Zhou (arXiv, 2015) explore similar themes concerning the properties of clustering in network settings. Furthermore, the theoretical contributions, while sound, build upon established results and represent incremental progress rather than a radical departure. 

The potential application of this work to assessing “clusterability” – determining whether underlying cluster structure exists in a network – is mentioned, but remains largely unexplored. While the authors suggest this as a future direction, the paper lacks formal results or concrete methodologies for utilizing the established properties in this specific application. Providing even a preliminary investigation into clusterability detection would strengthen the paper considerably.

Overall, the paper is well-written and clearly explains its central ideas. The presentation is lucid, making the theoretical arguments accessible. The combination of theoretical analysis and experimental support provides reasonable confidence in the validity of the findings. 

**Strengths:**

*   Novel and insightful connection between different clustering distance metrics.
*   Clear and well-structured presentation.
*   Support for theoretical claims through experimental validation.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Theoretical results are extensions of existing work, lacking substantial novelty in methodology.
*   Limited exploration of the potential application to clusterability detection.
*   Related work (Peng et al., Chao et al.) highlights a growing body of literature addressing similar concepts.



**Recommendation:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the understanding of clustering in network data. While the theoretical novelty is moderate and the application to clusterability remains undeveloped, the clarity of the presentation and the demonstrated equivalence of distance metrics are valuable. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Expand the discussion of related work to more thoroughly position their contribution within the broader literature.
2.  Include a brief preliminary exploration or discussion of how the established properties could be used to address the clusterability problem, even if only to outline the challenges and potential approaches.
3.  Consider adding a sentence or two to the conclusion acknowledging the incremental nature of the theoretical contribution.
