This paper investigates the relationship between different distance measures used to evaluate clusterings of network data. Specifically, the authors demonstrate a locally convex-like property for clusterings obtained from network models such as the Preference Frame Model (PFM) and the Stochastic Block Model (SBM). The core result establishes that clusterings which provide equally good fits to the observed network data – meaning they estimate model parameters with similar accuracy – are also close to each other according to various misclassification-based distance metrics. 

This is a valuable property for understanding clusterings in network data, as it suggests a degree of robustness in the resulting cluster assignments. The paper clearly states and rigorously proves the equivalence between these different clustering distance measures in the context of network data. Furthermore, the authors support their theoretical findings with experimental validation. 

The results presented are novel and represent a useful contribution to the field. However, it’s important to acknowledge related work. Recent publications, such as Peng, Sun, and Zanetti (COLT, 2015) and Chao, Lu, Ma, and Zhou (arXiv, 2015), explore similar themes in network analysis. While this work doesn’t diminish the paper’s value, it places the contribution within a broader research context. Additionally, the theoretical developments build upon well-established results, representing a logical and accessible extension of existing knowledge.

The paper touches upon the potential application of these findings to the problem of detecting clusterability in networks—determining whether underlying cluster structure exists in the data. However, the paper lacks formal results or a detailed exploration of this application. Expanding on this aspect would strengthen the practical implications of the work. 

Overall, the paper is well-written and effectively communicates the central ideas. The authors present a clear and lucid explanation of the core concepts and mathematical arguments.
