

This paper presents a novel framework for graph clustering that provides theoretical guarantees without assuming the data is generated from a specific stochastic block model (SBM, DC-SBM, or PFM). The authors propose that instead of requiring the data to conform to a model, one can check whether a fitted model "fits the data well" and derive guarantees based on that fit. This approach bridges the gap between model-based and model-free clustering by showing how existing model-based recovery results can be strengthened and applied in a model-free setting.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it introduces a generic theorem showing that if a clustering C fits the data well with respect to a model class M, then any other clustering C' that also fits the data well must be close to C. The authors instantiate this theorem for both PFM and SBM, providing concrete conditions under which clustering stability can be guaranteed. The framework is well-motivated and addresses a genuine limitation in the existing literature, where most theoretical guarantees rely on the restrictive assumption that data is generated from a specific model.

The paper's technical quality is strong. The proofs of the main theorems are rigorous and well-structured, with clear definitions of the key concepts (goodness of fit, clustering distance, etc.). The connection between the model-based and model-free approaches is particularly elegant - for instance, the authors show how their model-free results can improve upon existing model-based guarantees (e.g., improving the error bound in [20] by a factor of 128). The experimental section demonstrates the practical applicability of the framework, showing how the conditions of the theorems can be checked in practice and how the guarantees hold as long as the data fits the model well.

The paper is well-written and well-organized. The mathematical notation is consistent and clearly explained. The authors provide a helpful background on graph clustering and block models, making the paper accessible to readers familiar with machine learning but not necessarily graph theory. The presentation of the main theorems and their instantiations for PFM and SBM is particularly clear.

The originality of this work is significant. While spectral clustering has been extensively studied under model assumptions, this paper's "turning around" of model-based recovery theorems to work in a model-free framework is novel. The authors properly situate their work within the literature, noting that while there are some related works (e.g., [6]), none provide the model-free guarantees they present. The paper also offers a valuable perspective on how to validate clusterings without assuming a generative model.

Regarding significance, the framework has the potential to be widely applicable in practice. Many practitioners apply clustering algorithms without verifying whether their data fits the model assumptions, so this framework provides a principled way to validate clusterings. The paper demonstrates this through experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets (PFM, perturbed PFM, LFR, and Political Blogs), showing that the conditions of the theorems can be checked and that the guarantees hold when the data fits the model well.

However, there are some limitations worth noting. The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited in scope - it primarily demonstrates that the conditions of the theorems hold when the data fits the model, but doesn't fully explore scenarios where the data doesn't fit the model well. The paper could benefit from more thorough experiments comparing the model-free approach to existing methods, especially in cases where the data doesn't conform to the model assumptions. Additionally, while the framework provides a way to validate clusterings, it doesn't directly address how to find good clusterings in the first place (the paper assumes a clustering is already available).

The paper's main weakness is that it doesn't fully articulate why the model-free approach is superior to simply using the model-based approach with a goodness-of-fit check. The connection between the two approaches is clear, but the paper doesn't strongly argue why the model-free framework is necessary or provides additional value beyond the existing model-based methods. This is particularly evident in the SBM results, where the model-free guarantees are much harder to satisfy than the model-based ones.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that makes an important step toward more robust graph clustering methods. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses a genuine limitation in the current literature. While the experimental validation could be more comprehensive, the core theoretical contribution is novel and significant. The framework provides a valuable tool for practitioners to validate clusterings without relying on potentially incorrect model assumptions.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to better articulate the practical advantages of the model-free framework and to expand the experimental evaluation to demonstrate the framework's utility in more challenging scenarios.
