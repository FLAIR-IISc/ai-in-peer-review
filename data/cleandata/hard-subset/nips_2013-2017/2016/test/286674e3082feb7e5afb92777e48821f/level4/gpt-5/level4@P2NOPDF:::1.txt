This paper develops new theoretical guarantees for graph clustering that do not rely on a specific generative model (e.g., stochastic block models). The core insight is that if a method finds a clustering that fits the observed graph well (under a precise notion of fit), then any other clustering that fits the graph equally well must be close to the first. This yields distribution-free stability results for graph clustering, which tackle an important problem.

Despite some material being moved to the supplement due to space limits, the paper remains largely self-contained and readable. The exposition is generally clear, the results are well explained and illustrated, and the technical contributions are interesting. One minor stylistic issue is the use of hats for observed quantities (e.g., \hat{L} for the observed Laplacian), which is unconventional and may be confusing, though this does not materially hinder comprehension.

The theoretical contributions are strong and carefully motivated. The framework provides a principled way to reason about when a clustering is uniquely determined by its quality on the observed graph, without invoking distributional assumptions. The commentary around the results and their empirical illustrations is helpful.

However, there are notable limitations and questions:

- Empirically, some real-world graphs require smoothing to reveal structure in the authors’ framework, whereas standard algorithms can recover ground truth without such preprocessing. This suggests a gap between the theory’s notion of “fit” and practical performance on noisy or heterogeneous graphs.

- Two conceptual issues are more substantial. First, the main result for PFM relies on a nonstandard distance between clusterings. The authors argue this metric may be more natural than the classical one, but this claim is debatable and warrants further justification or comparison. Second, the analysis uses a “proxy” model: starting from a clustering, a model (e.g., a PFM) is reconstructed and compared to the original graph, rather than to the model that produced the clustering. While natural within the chosen setup, this effectively defines “good clustering” via approximation quality in the normalized Laplacian sense. That restriction is understandable to obtain formal results, but it narrows the scope of what counts as a valid clustering and may not align with alternative objectives or data characteristics. The real-world examples where smoothing is required underscore potential practical consequences of this modeling choice.

Overall, the paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution by establishing distribution-free stability properties for graph clustering and presenting clear, insightful results. The work is well written and thoughtfully executed. At the same time, the reliance on a nonstandard clustering distance and on a specific proxy-model notion of fit, along with the empirical need for smoothing in some cases where classical methods succeed, raises questions about general applicability. Clarifying these trade-offs—especially the implications of the chosen distance and fit criterion, and the conditions under which smoothing is necessary—would strengthen the paper and help position its theory relative to common practice.
