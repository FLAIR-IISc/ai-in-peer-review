

This paper presents a framework for providing theoretical guarantees of clustering stability without assuming the data is generated from a specific model. The authors propose a generic theorem stating that if a clustering C fits the data well with respect to a model class M, then any other clustering C' that also fits the data well must be close to C. They instantiate this framework for two block models (PFM and SBM), demonstrating that all quantities required for the guarantees can be computed directly from the data. The paper also shows how model-free guarantees can be derived from existing model-based recovery results.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it establishes a novel paradigm for validating clusterings without model assumptions, which is particularly valuable for practical applications where the data-generating process is unknown. Second, the authors successfully bridge model-based and model-free approaches, showing that existing theoretical results for block models can be repurposed for model-free guarantees. Third, the framework provides computable stability bounds that can be directly applied to real data, making the theoretical results practically useful. The technical development is sound, with clear definitions and proofs for the main theorems.

The paper's strengths are substantial. The framework itself is elegant and generalizable, and the authors effectively demonstrate its application to two important block models (PFM and SBM). The connection between model-based and model-free results is particularly insightful, showing how existing theoretical results can be repurposed. The experimental section provides convincing evidence that the framework works as intended, with stability guarantees holding when the data fits the model well. The proofs are technically sound and the framework is well-motivated.

However, the paper has several important limitations that reduce its practical impact. First, the authors treat PFM and SBM separately despite PFM encompassing DC-SBM, which in turn includes SBM. This creates unnecessary complexity without clear justification. The choice of using the Laplacian for PFM and adjacency matrix for SBM appears arbitrary, and the difference between weighted ME and standard ME metrics for the two models makes direct comparison difficult.

Second, the conditions required for the guarantees to hold are often too restrictive for practical applications. The experimental section demonstrates that for the SBM, the conditions are almost always violated (δ > δ₀), rendering the guarantees ineffective. This is a significant limitation given the paper's emphasis on practical applicability. The claim that "large eigengap makes PFM(G, C) differ from PFM(G, C₀) even for very small perturbations" is not sufficiently supported, and the paper fails to address why such sensitivity to perturbations would be acceptable for practical clustering applications.

Third, the related work section is notably incomplete. The paper overlooks several key model-free clustering results, including Balcan et al.'s work on perturbation resilience (2012) and approximation stability (2013), as well as Kumar and Kannan's "Clustering with Spectral Norm" (2010). These works directly address the same problem of validating clusterings without model assumptions, and the paper's framework should be positioned relative to these contributions.

Fourth, the framework assumes fixed cluster counts, which limits its applicability to real-world scenarios where clustering algorithms may suggest different numbers of clusters. The paper does not address how the framework would handle cases where C and C' have different numbers of clusters, which is common in practice.

The paper also has some technical inconsistencies. For instance, Proposition 10 refers to ⟨M', M⟩F without defining the notation, and Assumption 5's condition ||L̂² - L²||F is ambiguous (is it ||L̂ - L||²F or ||L̂² - L²||F?). These ambiguities reduce the paper's technical credibility.

Despite these limitations, the paper makes an important theoretical contribution by formalizing a model-free validation approach for clustering. The framework is elegant and the connection to existing model-based results is insightful. However, the restrictive conditions for the guarantees to hold and the incomplete treatment of related work significantly limit the paper's practical impact. The authors might consider addressing the SBM case more thoroughly, as the current results for SBM appear uninformative in practice.

In summary, this paper offers a theoretically interesting framework for validating clusterings without model assumptions, with clear connections to existing block-model literature. The technical development is sound, but the restrictive conditions required for the guarantees to hold and the limited practical applicability of the SBM results reduce its impact. The related work is incomplete, and the paper would benefit from addressing the issues with cluster count flexibility and clarifying the technical inconsistencies. The work represents a valuable step toward model-free clustering guarantees, but the current formulation is too restrictive for broad practical adoption.
