

The authors present novel theoretical results for graph clustering that operate without assuming a specific generative model for the observed graph (e.g., not assuming the graph was generated via the stochastic block model). The core contribution establishes that if a clustering method identifies a reasonable fit to the graph (under their defined criterion), any other clustering achieving a similar fit must be close to the first. This work addresses an important problem by providing distribution-free guarantees for graph clustering. The paper is well-written and accessible without requiring the supplementary materials (which contain some content due to page limits). While I question the use of "hat" notation for observed quantities (e.g., \(\hat{L}\) for the observed Laplacian), the paper remains reasonably clear otherwise. The results are compelling, well-explained, and effectively illustrated. However, they face notable limitations. First, in the experimental section, real-world graphs often require smoothing to reveal structure recoverable under the authors' theoretical framework, whereas classical algorithms frequently recover the ground truth without such preprocessing. Second, two critical limitations arise: (1) the main result for the Probabilistic Finite Mixture (PFM) model employs a non-standard distance measure between clusterings. The authors argue this measure is more natural than conventional alternatives, but this claim is debatable. (2) The results rely on a "proxy" model approach: a clustering induces a model (e.g., a PFM), which is then compared to the original graph (rather than the model used to generate the clustering). While this is natural within the authors' framework, it significantly constrains the definition of a "good" clustering for a given graph (specifically, requiring the graph to be well-approximated in the normalized Laplacian sense). This constraint is necessary to derive the results, but its impact—particularly given the framework's limitations on real-world data—warrants deeper consideration.
