### **Summary**

This paper introduces a novel theoretical framework for providing guarantees on graph clustering results without assuming the data is generated from a specific probabilistic model, such as the Stochastic Block Model (SBM) or Preference Frame Model (PFM). The central idea is to shift from model-based assumptions to data-dependent, verifiable conditions. The authors propose a "Generic Theorem" which states that if a given clustering `C` provides a good fit to the observed graph `G` (under a chosen model class like PFM or SBM), then any other clustering `C'` that also fits the graph well must be a small perturbation of `C`.

The paper instantiates this framework for two important model classes: the Preference Frame Model (PFM) and the Stochastic Block Model (SBM). For each, it defines:
1.  A procedure to construct a model `M(G, C)` compatible with a given clustering `C`.
2.  A goodness-of-fit measure `ε` quantifying the distance between the observed graph and the constructed model.
3.  A theoretical guarantee (Theorems 4 and 9) that bounds the distance between two "good" clusterings as a function of `ε` and the spectral properties of the graph.

The authors demonstrate that this framework not only provides model-free validation but also connects to and can strengthen existing model-based recovery results. The experimental section validates the approach on synthetic and real-world data, showing that the derived stability conditions are computable and informative, particularly for the PFM.

### **Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **High Originality and Significance:** The core contribution—providing clustering guarantees based on computable, data-dependent goodness-of-fit rather than unverifiable model assumptions—is a significant and novel conceptual advance. It addresses a fundamental gap between the theoretical analysis of block models and their practical application, where the true data-generating process is unknown. This "model-free" validation paradigm is likely to be influential and built upon by other researchers.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proofs leverage established results from matrix perturbation theory (specifically, the Davis-Kahan Sinus Theorem) in a clever way to connect the goodness-of-fit metric to the geometric distance between subspaces associated with different clusterings. The derivation is clear and the results are presented as explicit, computable bounds without hidden constants, which is a major strength.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The "Generic Theorem" in Section 3 serves as an excellent blueprint, which is then carefully instantiated for PFM and SBM. This structure makes the paper's core logic easy to follow. The authors are also careful to define their terms, such as the model construction procedures and the distance measures between clusterings.

4.  **Practical Relevance:** The framework is directly applicable. A practitioner can take a clustering result from any algorithm (e.g., spectral clustering), compute the goodness-of-fit `ε` and the relevant spectral gap from the data, and use the theorems to assess the stability and uniqueness of their solution. This provides a principled tool for cluster validation, which is a crucial and often-overlooked step in the analysis pipeline.

### **Weaknesses (Arguments against Acceptance)**

1.  **Limited Efficacy of SBM Bounds:** As the authors honestly note in the experiments, the derived bounds for the SBM case (Theorem 9) are much looser than for the PFM and were not informative on the tested datasets. While the PFM results are strong, the paper would be improved by a more detailed discussion of why the SBM bounds are less tight. Is this an artifact of the proof technique, the choice of norm, or a fundamental property reflecting the SBM's lower flexibility compared to the PFM?

2.  **Clarity on Model Fitting vs. Clustering:** The presentation could cause a minor confusion between algorithms that *find* a clustering and the paper's procedures for *evaluating* a clustering. The "PFM Estimation Algorithm" and "SBM Estimation Algorithm" do not find a clustering; they fit a model to a *given* clustering. A more explicit statement in the introduction or Section 3 could prevent readers from misinterpreting the paper as proposing new clustering algorithms.

3.  **Experimental Scope:** The experiments provide a good proof-of-concept. However, demonstrating the framework on a larger and more challenging real-world network where the ground truth is ambiguous or unknown would be more compelling. Such an experiment could showcase how the framework behaves in a truly "model-free" setting—for instance, does it correctly indicate low stability (large `δ`) when a graph lacks a clear block structure?

### **Detailed Comments**

The paper's approach of "turning around" model-based recovery theorems into a model-free validation tool is elegant and powerful. The connection established in Section 4.2, showing how existing theorems can be used within this framework and how this framework can, in turn, tighten their bounds, is particularly insightful.

The choice of the weighted misclassification error (wME) is well-justified and natural for graphs with heterogeneous degree distributions, which are common in practice.

The discussion in Section 4.4 on "Clusterability and resilience" is also valuable, as it correctly frames the paper's contribution within the broader literature on the stability of clustering.

In summary, this is a strong paper with a novel and important contribution. The weaknesses identified are minor and primarily relate to the scope of the results and potential improvements in presentation, rather than any fundamental flaws in the methodology or claims. The work provides a new and valuable perspective on the validation of community detection and graph clustering.
