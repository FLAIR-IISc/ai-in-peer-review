

This paper introduces a suite of algorithms for graph clustering and for evaluating the similarity between cluster assignments. While the technical descriptions of the algorithms are clear and well-structured, the manuscript fails to adequately justify the rationale for presenting these methods as a discrete list rather than as a cohesive framework. The motivation for why these specific modifications are necessary or beneficial over existing approaches remains underdeveloped, leaving readers uncertain about the conceptual contribution of the work.  

The proposed algorithms are largely incremental adaptations of established techniques, with no substantial conceptual innovation in the core clustering or similarity-testing mechanisms. The primary novelty is confined to the constraints introduced in the final section, which, while potentially useful in specific contexts, do not fundamentally alter the methodological foundation. This marginal contribution—limited to constraint specifications—appears insufficient to warrant the presentation as a distinct set of algorithms, especially given the absence of a compelling narrative linking these modifications to broader theoretical or practical gaps in the literature.  

The paper would benefit from a clearer articulation of the problem space it addresses, including a more rigorous comparison against prior work to demonstrate where the proposed methods offer tangible advantages. Without this, the reader is left to question why these particular adaptations merit separate algorithmic treatment rather than being treated as minor extensions within a unified approach. The lack of empirical validation or comparative analysis further weakens the case for the proposed framework, as it remains unclear whether the constraints significantly improve performance or applicability relative to standard methods.  

In summary, while the technical execution is sound, the work’s conceptual contribution is limited. The algorithms do not represent a novel paradigm but rather a collection of constrained variations of existing tools. To strengthen the submission, the authors should either (1) reframe the contribution around the constraints as a meaningful theoretical advance with broader implications, supported by rigorous analysis and comparison, or (2) integrate these modifications into a more unified methodological framework, clearly justifying the necessity of the discrete algorithmic presentation. As currently structured, the paper’s novelty and significance fall short of the standards expected for publication in a venue such as NeurIPS.
