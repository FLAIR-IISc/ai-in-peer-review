- Proposes a model-free, a posteriori validation framework for graph clustering that gives guarantees based on data-driven goodness-of-fit without assuming a generative model.  
- Main claim: if two fitted models both approximate the observed graph well, then their clusterings must be close in misclassification error.  
- Instantiates the template for PFM (using normalized Laplacian) and SBM (using adjacency matrix), proving stability bounds via eigengaps and computable quantities.  
- Connects model-free guarantees to classic model-based recovery theorems and reuses prior results to derive alternative bounds.  
- Experiments on synthetic PFM/LFR graphs and Political Blogs show PFM guarantees can be informative while SBM bounds are often too loose to certify stability.  
- Novelty: turning model-based perturbation analyses into model-free stability guarantees that depend only on computable goodness-of-fit metrics.  
- Strength: clear framing and data-computable terms in the bounds, with technically clean proofs using Davis–Kahan and Meilă’s local equivalence.  
- Strength: conceptual bridge enabling reuse of existing recovery conditions and connections to resilience/clusterability notions.  
- Strength: experiments include both synthetic and real data and honestly report when guarantees fail.  
- Weakness: inconsistent operator choices—Laplacian for PFM vs adjacency for SBM—and inconsistent error metrics (wME vs ME) hinder comparison.  
- Weakness: framework assumes fixed K for C and C′, not handling splits/coarsenings or K′ ≠ K common in practice.  
- Weakness: some definitions are unclear (e.g., inner product in Proposition 10) and should be restated for self-containment.  
- Weakness: Ambiguity in Assumption 5 (norm of Laplacian difference) and insufficient intuition for squaring Laplacians.  
- Weakness: needs discussion relating the fit-then-stability notion to existing perturbation/approximation stability literature (Kumar–Kannan, Balcan et al.).  
- Weakness: empirical usefulness limited—PFM tolerates only small perturbations and SBM bounds rarely certify stability; requires quantitative sensitivity analysis.  
- Weakness: experiments mismatch theory (wME bounds vs unweighted ME perturbations); should align metrics or report both.  
- Weakness: presentation issues—figure labels/captions, overloaded notation (B,C,Y,λ), and minor correctness notes about independence statements.  
- Quality: technically sound with clear proofs, but some methodological choices (operator and error metrics) need justification and unification.  
- Clarity: generally organized but hampered by notation overload and a few undefined symbols; Related Work needs expansion.  
- Originality: novel and interesting shift from model-based recovery to model-free, data-adaptive validation using fitted models.  
- Significance: potentially useful if extended to DC-SBM, K′ ≠ K settings, and if empirical tolerances are improved or contextualized.  
- Argument for acceptance: novel, data-computable framework with clear theoretical development and practical potential.  
- Argument against acceptance: limited empirical utility for SBM, missing DC-SBM instantiation, lack of support for differing K, and clarity/Related Work gaps.  
- Key suggestions: add DC-SBM, unify operator choice (prefer Laplacian), extend to near-refinements/coarsenings, and report wME in experiments.  
- Key suggestions: expand Related Work to include stability literature, clarify Assumption 5 and Proposition 10, and improve figure labeling and notation consistency.  
- Minor comments: add axis labels and dataset identifiers to figures, move Section 4.4 into Related Work, and be consistent about independence statements.
