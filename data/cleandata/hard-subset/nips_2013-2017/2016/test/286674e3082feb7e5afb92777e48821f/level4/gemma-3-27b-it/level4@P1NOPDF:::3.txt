The manuscript presents a set of algorithms for clustering on graphs, and for evaluating the similarity between two cluster assignments. While the algorithms themselves are clearly described, their overall motivation and the rationale for presenting them as a collection of distinct methods isn’t well established. The algorithms largely consist of modifications to existing approaches, and the primary novelty appears to reside in the constraints introduced towards the end of the paper.

The core contribution focuses on relaxing the assumption of balanced cluster sizes, a common limitation in spectral clustering. This is a valuable direction, as imbalanced clusters are frequent in real-world graph data. However, the paper doesn't sufficiently contextualize *why* existing methods struggle specifically with imbalance, beyond stating it as a known issue. A more detailed discussion of the shortcomings of standard spectral clustering in imbalanced scenarios would strengthen the motivation. 

The theoretical analysis provides bounds on the error of the proposed algorithms. However, these bounds appear loose and heavily dependent on parameters that are difficult to estimate in practice (e.g., the graph’s expansion properties). It would be beneficial if the authors could provide some guidance on how these parameters might be approximated or relate the bounds to more readily observable graph characteristics. Furthermore, the practical implications of these bounds—i.e., what level of error can realistically be expected—are not clearly articulated.

The experimental evaluation is conducted on both synthetic and real-world datasets. The results demonstrate that the proposed algorithms can outperform baseline methods, particularly when dealing with imbalanced cluster sizes. However, the comparison with existing state-of-the-art graph clustering algorithms is limited. Including comparisons with more recent and competitive methods (e.g., those leveraging node embeddings or deep learning techniques) would provide a stronger assessment of the proposed approach’s strengths and weaknesses. Additionally, the sensitivity analysis with respect to the key hyperparameters is relatively shallow. A more thorough investigation into the influence of these parameters on performance is needed.

Regarding the constraints introduced, they represent an interesting attempt to incorporate prior knowledge or domain-specific information into the clustering process. However, the paper lacks a clear discussion of how to effectively *choose* or *learn* these constraints in practice. Providing guidelines or suggesting methods for constraint elicitation would increase the practicality of this contribution.

Specific comments:

*   The notation is generally clear, but some definitions could be more explicit (e.g., the precise meaning of 'similarity' between cluster assignments).
*   Figure 1 could benefit from a more detailed caption explaining the different algorithms and their relationships.
*   In several places, the writing is somewhat repetitive. Streamlining the text would improve readability.
*   The conclusion reiterates the benefits of the approach but doesn’t discuss potential future work or limitations.

Overall, this manuscript presents a technically sound contribution to graph clustering with a focus on addressing the issue of imbalanced cluster sizes. However, the motivation could be strengthened, the theoretical results made more practical, and the experimental evaluation broadened. Addressing these points would significantly improve the impact of this work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points outlined above.
