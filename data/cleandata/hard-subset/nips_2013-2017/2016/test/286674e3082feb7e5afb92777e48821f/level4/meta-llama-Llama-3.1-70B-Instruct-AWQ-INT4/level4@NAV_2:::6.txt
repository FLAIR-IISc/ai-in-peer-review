This paper proposes a framework for graph clustering that provides theoretical guarantees without assuming the data comes from a specific model. The authors develop a generic theorem that establishes a connection between model-based and model-free clustering results. They then instantiate this theorem for the Stochastic Block Model (SBM) and the Preference Frame Model (PFM), obtaining model-free guarantees for these models.

The main idea of the paper is to define a framework in which clustering algorithms can be evaluated without making assumptions about the data generating process. The authors propose a goodness of fit measure between the model and the data, as well as a distance between clusterings. They then show that if a clustering algorithm fits the data well, then any other clustering algorithm that also fits the data well will be close to the original clustering.

The authors provide several technical contributions, including the development of a PFM estimation algorithm and the proof of several propositions that establish the connection between model-based and model-free clustering results. They also provide experimental results that demonstrate the effectiveness of their framework.

The paper is well-written and well-organized, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their framework and results. The technical contributions are significant, and the experimental results are convincing.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. The authors' framework is based on a generic theorem that is not specific to any particular model or clustering algorithm. While this provides a general framework for evaluating clustering algorithms, it may not be directly applicable to specific problems or datasets. Additionally, the authors' results are based on several assumptions, including the assumption that the data is well-represented by a block model. While these assumptions may be reasonable in some cases, they may not always hold in practice.
