## Review of “Graph Clustering: Block-models and model free results”

This paper addresses the important problem of graph clustering, focusing on providing theoretical guarantees for clustering results without assuming the data originates from a specific generative model like the Stochastic Block Model (SBM). The authors propose a framework based on the “goodness of fit” between a clustering and the observed graph, aiming to establish stability – that is, if a clustering fits the data well, any other clustering that also fits well must be similar. They instantiate this framework for the SBM and Preference Frame Model (PFM), demonstrating connections to existing model-based recovery results and, in some cases, strengthening them.

The paper builds upon a strong foundation of previous work in spectral clustering and block models (e.g., [18, 17, 20, 15]). The core idea of assessing clustering quality based on data fit, rather than model assumptions, is a valuable contribution and attempts to bridge a significant gap in the field. The authors correctly identify the limitations of purely model-based approaches and strive for broader applicability. The connection drawn between model-free and model-based recovery is insightful and potentially allows for leveraging existing results in a more flexible manner.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty of Approach:** The proposed framework for model-free clustering guarantees is a significant departure from traditional approaches and addresses a relevant limitation in the field.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper employs a rigorous mathematical treatment, providing theorems and proofs for the proposed framework and its instantiations.
* **Connections to Existing Work:** The authors effectively connect their work to existing literature on block models and spectral clustering, demonstrating an understanding of the field and positioning their contribution within it. The ability to strengthen existing results is also a positive aspect.
* **Computational Feasibility:** The authors emphasize the computability of the quantities involved in their theorems, making the results potentially practical.
* **Exploration of Multiple Models:** The instantiation of the framework for both SBM and PFM demonstrates its generality and adaptability.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity of Motivation:** While the problem statement is clear, the motivation for presenting the work as a set of algorithms, rather than a unified theoretical framework, is not fully explained. The narrative feels somewhat fragmented.
* **Assumptions & Restrictions:** The theorems rely on specific assumptions (e.g., eigengaps, goodness of fit measures) which, while mathematically convenient, may be restrictive in practice. The practical implications of these assumptions are not thoroughly discussed.
* **Complexity of Proofs:** While rigorous, the proofs are quite dense and may be difficult for readers unfamiliar with the specific mathematical tools employed. More intuitive explanations of the key proof steps would be beneficial.
* **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation, while present, feels somewhat limited in scope. More diverse datasets and a more thorough comparison with existing methods would strengthen the empirical validation of the proposed approach. The discussion of the results is also somewhat brief. The observation that SBM guarantees are much harder to satisfy warrants further investigation and discussion.
* **Relationship to Related Work:** While related work is cited, a deeper engagement with recent advances in robust clustering and stability analysis (e.g., [16, 14, 5]) would enhance the paper’s position within the broader literature.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a technically sound and potentially impactful contribution to the field of graph clustering. The authors address a significant limitation of existing methods by proposing a model-free framework for obtaining clustering guarantees. However, the clarity of the presentation could be improved, and the experimental evaluation expanded. The assumptions required for the theorems to hold, and their practical implications, warrant further discussion.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically well-grounded approach to model-free graph clustering.
* The connection between model-free and model-based recovery is insightful and potentially valuable.
* The results contribute to a deeper understanding of the stability and robustness of clustering algorithms.
* The computational feasibility of the proposed approach is a strength.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The clarity of the presentation and motivation could be improved.
* The assumptions required for the theorems may be restrictive.
* The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited.
* The engagement with recent related work could be more thorough.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The authors should address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly by clarifying the motivation for the algorithmic presentation, expanding the experimental evaluation, and providing more intuitive explanations of the key theoretical results. Addressing the discussion around the SBM guarantees would also be valuable. While the paper is dense and requires careful reading, the novelty and potential impact of the work warrant its inclusion in NIPS.
