The paper presents novel theoretical findings for graph clustering that do not rely on assumptions about how the observed graph was generated (i.e., it is not presumed to come from a specific generative model such as the stochastic block model). The central idea is that if a graph clustering method identifies a clustering that fits the graph reasonably well (under a particular definition of fit), then any other clustering that achieves a comparable fit should be close to the first. I find the work very interesting, addressing an important problem by providing distribution-free results for graph clustering. Although some material has been moved to the supplementary due to page limits, the paper remains clear and can be read without consulting the additional content. I am not convinced that using “hats” for observed quantities (e.g., \hat{L} for the observed Laplacian) is a good choice, but aside from this detail, the exposition is reasonably clear. The results themselves are quite interesting, well explained, and well illustrated. They are, however, not without limitations. As shown in the experiments, for some real-world datasets the graph must be smoothed for structure to become recoverable (in the sense of the authors’ theoretical framework), whereas many classical algorithms are able to recover the ground-truth structure. In addition, and perhaps more importantly, two limitations arise: 1) the main result for PFM relies on a nonstandard distance between clusterings. The authors argue that this metric may be more natural than the classical one, but this is debatable. 2) the results are based on a “proxy” model: from a clustering, a model (e.g., a PFM) is reconstructed and this model is compared to the original graph (rather than whatever model produced the clustering). This is very natural in the authors’ context, but it strongly constrains what qualifies as a good clustering for a given graph (here, that the graph is well approximated in the normalized Laplacian sense). While such restrictions are arguably necessary to obtain concrete results, one may question their impact, especially given the framework’s limitations on some real-world examples.
