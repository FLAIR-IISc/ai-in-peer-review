- Introduces a general, model-free framework showing that if clustering C fits graph G well then any other good-fitting clustering C' must be close to C.  
- Demonstrates the framework on PFM and SBM with guarantees whose quantities are computable from data.  
- Shows model-free results can strengthen existing model-based recovery guarantees.  
- Experiments indicate the theoretical conditions are often not met, limiting practical utility.  
- Unclear why PFM, DC-SBM, and SBM are treated separately despite nesting relationships; DC-SBM results are missing.  
- Uses Laplacian for PFM but adjacency for SBM, and weighted ME for PFM versus standard ME for SBM—these methodological differences lack justification.  
- Proposition 10 references "< M', M >F" without definition; notation is sometimes undefined or inconsistent.  
- Assumption 5's expression ||L̂² - L²||F is ambiguous (squared Laplacians vs square of norm) and the reason for squaring Laplacians is unexplained.  
- Framework restricts to disjoint clusters with fixed K and cannot handle differing cluster counts or refinement relationships between clusterings.  
- Related work omits several model-free clustering results (e.g., Kumar & Kannan 2010; Balcan et al. 2012, 2013) and does not relate the new stability notion to these prior notions.  
- Experiments show PFM tolerates extremely small perturbations before stability breaks, undermining practical relevance.  
- Claim that large eigengap causes sensitivity lacks justification; smoothing results on political blog data contradict sensitivity claims.  
- SBM statements (e.g., "very hard to satisfy") are unclear and figure captions (e.g., "δ is always greater than δ0") do not clarify which plots show SBM.  
- Theorems use weighted ME but experiments report standard ME—this inconsistency is not explained.  
- Notation (e.g., B, C, Y) is inconsistently defined across sections, hurting clarity.  
- Suggests Section 4.4 on clusterability/resilience belongs in related work; overall technical contributions are interesting but presentation issues and limited empirical relevance reduce impact.
