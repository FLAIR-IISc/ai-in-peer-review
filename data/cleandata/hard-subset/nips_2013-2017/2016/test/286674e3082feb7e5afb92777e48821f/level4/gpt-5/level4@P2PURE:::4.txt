This paper proposes a generic template that provides theoretical guarantees for the stability of a clustering C that fits the data well, where stability is measured by the distance between C and any other clustering C' that also fits the data well. The paper instantiates the template for two block models, PFM and SBM, with all quantities in the guarantees explicitly computable from the data. It further leverages these stability results to derive exact recovery guarantees for the models. However, the simulations suggest that either the chosen goodness-of-fit criterion or the stated theorem conditions make the results of limited practical use.

1) The paper tackles the important problem of developing a model-free template for theoretical clustering guarantees, without assuming the data arises from a specific generative model. The stated goal—guarantees expressed in terms of quantities computable from the data and from the outputs of the clustering algorithms being compared—is valuable for both theory and practice.

2) Since PFM includes DC-SBM as a special case, which in turn includes SBM, it is unclear why PFM and SBM are treated differently. If Spectral Clustering performs well across the specified models, why not use a unified estimator? Specifically, why is the Laplacian used for PFM while the adjacency matrix is used for SBM? Moreover, the PFM results are stated in terms of weighted ME, whereas the SBM results use ME. How do these compare? Also, why are there no results for DC-SBM?

3) In Proposition 10, what is the definition of ⟨M', M⟩_F?

4) Assumption 5: Is the quantity ||L̂^2 − L^2||_F or ||L̂ − L||_F^2? If it is the former, what is the intuition for squaring the Laplacians?

5) The paper focuses primarily on disjoint clusters. Do the techniques/recipe extend beyond block models to other settings where Spectral Clustering is known to work? For instance, spectral methods perform well for hierarchical clustering, and there are theoretical guarantees (e.g., “Noise Thresholds for Spectral Clustering,” S. Balakrishnan et al., 2011). How would your template apply in that case?

6) The evaluation metric and the estimators for both PFM and SBM assume C and C' have the same number of clusters. Different algorithms can output different numbers of clusters. For example, the eigenvalues of A may suggest K clusters for C, while those of the Laplacian of A suggest K' = K + 1 for C', with all clusters matching except one that splits into two. C' is still “close” to C, but the current framework cannot capture this.

7) The related work omits several model-free results, e.g., “Clustering with Spectral Norm and the k-means Algorithm,” Kumar and Kannan (2010); “Clustering under Perturbation Resilience,” Balcan et al. (2012); and “Clustering under Approximation Stability,” Balcan et al. (2013). How does the notion of stability here relate to those? In particular, how does your stability compare to stability under perturbations of centers for two clusterings?

8) The perturbations tolerated in the PFM simulations are very small. Lines 252–253 state that “large eigengap makes PFM(G, C) differ from PFM(G, C0) even for very small perturbations,” but this is not qualified and remains unclear. By contrast, the political blog data are smoothed by raising the power of A, which should further enlarge eigengaps, yet this seems to improve stability. If such small perturbations can substantially affect model fit, the guarantees are of limited practical use (despite the selling point that the quantities are data-computable). Lines 248–250 also note that the SBM guarantee conditions appear very hard to satisfy, which is unfortunate.

9) The Figure 1 caption states, “For SBM, δ is always greater than δ0.” Which subplot(s) does this refer to? If SBM is not plotted, the caption should make that clear.

10) The PFM theorems provide guarantees for weighted ME, whereas the experiments perturb ME. Please reconcile this mismatch.

Similarly, for eigenvalues in PFM vs. SBM, notations like λ^L and λ^B would disambiguate which spectrum is being referenced.
