This paper presents a novel framework for graph clustering with theoretical guarantees that do *not* rely on assumptions about the data generation process, a significant departure from the prevalent model-based approaches like the Stochastic Block Model (SBM) and its extensions. The core idea—establishing stability of a clustering based on its goodness-of-fit to the data—is compelling and addresses an important limitation of existing work. If a clustering fits the data well, any other clustering that fits equally well must be “close” to the original, providing a pathway to model-free guarantees. The authors instantiate this framework for both the PFM and SBM, demonstrating connections to existing model-based recovery results and even strengthening some of them. 

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The notation, while initially dense, is consistently used throughout. The authors effectively relate their work to previous NIPS contributions (e.g., [1, 15, 17, 20]) and the broader literature on spectral clustering and community detection. The inclusion of detailed experimental validation is valuable, though the discussion of limitations is appreciated. The choice to move some content to the supplement is understandable given page constraints, and the authors assure the core ideas remain accessible without it. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The model-free guarantee framework is a significant contribution, addressing a key limitation of existing graph clustering theory.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The theorems are clearly stated and appear well-justified, building on established results in spectral graph theory and perturbation bounds.
* **Connections to Existing Work:** The authors skillfully demonstrate the relationship between their model-free approach and existing model-based methods, showcasing the potential to leverage advancements in both areas.
* **Experimental Validation:** The experimental section provides empirical support for the theoretical results and highlights the practical implications of the framework.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and understandable, despite the technical complexity of the subject matter.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Distance Metric (PFM):** The use of a weighted Misclassification Error (wME) distance for the PFM case may be less intuitive and widely accepted than the standard Misclassification Error. While the authors argue for its naturalness, the choice warrants further justification or comparison to results using the standard metric.
* **“Proxy” Model Approach:** Reconstructing a model from a clustering (e.g., fitting a PFM to a given clustering) introduces a constraint on the definition of a “good” clustering. This reliance on a proxy model, while necessary for obtaining theoretical results, may limit the applicability of the framework to scenarios where the true data generating process is significantly different from the chosen model class.  The paper acknowledges this, but it’s a crucial point to consider.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The experimental results reveal that the stability guarantees are sensitive to graph smoothing, indicating that the framework may require pre-processing steps in real-world applications. This practical limitation should be emphasized.
* **SBM Results:** The experimental results show the SBM bounds are much more restrictive, and the theorems are not often satisfiable, which suggests that the SBM formulation may need refinement or that the theoretical bounds are currently too loose.
* **Notations:** The use of “hats” on observed quantities (e.g.,  \hat{L}) is unconventional and adds a slight cognitive load for the reader.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a truly novel and theoretically sound framework for model-free graph clustering.
* It addresses a significant limitation of existing work, offering the potential to broaden the applicability of theoretical guarantees to real-world datasets.
* The experimental validation, while not exhaustive, provides initial evidence of the framework’s effectiveness.
* The clarity of the writing and organization facilitates understanding of a complex topic.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The reliance on a “proxy” model and the potentially restrictive nature of the goodness-of-fit assumptions could limit the practical impact of the framework.
* The SBM results are not as compelling as the PFM results.
* The unconventional notation may slightly hinder readability.
* The distance metric choice for PFM may be contentious.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of graph clustering. While some limitations exist, the novelty and theoretical rigor of the proposed framework outweigh these concerns. I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance to NIPS 2016, with a recommendation that the authors address the concerns regarding the distance metric for the PFM case and further investigate the parameter sensitivity observed in the experiments. Minor revisions to improve readability by reconsidering the “hat” notation would also be beneficial.
