This paper introduces a general framework for establishing theoretical guarantees for clustering stability without relying on strict model assumptions like those inherent in Stochastic Block Models (SBM), Degree-Corrected SBM (DC-SBM), and Preference Frame Models (PFM). The core idea is to demonstrate that if a clustering *C* fits the data well, any other clustering *C'* that also fits the data well must be a small perturbation of *C*. This is achieved by defining a “goodness of fit” measure and bounding the distance between competing clusterings. The framework is instantiated for both PFM and SBM, and the authors show connections to existing model-based recovery results, even strengthening some. While the concept is promising, the empirical results suggest the conditions for these guarantees may be difficult to satisfy in practice.

The paper addresses an important problem: providing theoretical foundations for clustering algorithms when the underlying data generating process is unknown. The approach of focusing on data fit rather than model adherence is a valuable contribution. The explicit computation of quantities needed for the guarantees is also a strength. However, several points require clarification and potential strengthening. 

Firstly, given the hierarchical relationship between PFM, DC-SBM, and SBM (PFM generalizes DC-SBM, which generalizes SBM), the rationale for treating PFM and SBM separately isn’t fully convincing. While different estimators are used, a more unified treatment, perhaps highlighting the varying degrees of model assumption required, would be beneficial. The choice of using the Laplacian for PFM and the adjacency matrix for SBM feels somewhat arbitrary without further justification. Furthermore, the use of weighted ME for PFM and ME for SBM makes comparison challenging. The exclusion of results for DC-SBM is also a point of concern, given its position within the model hierarchy.

Specific technical questions arise. In Proposition 10, the definition of <M', M><sub>F</sub> is missing. In Assumption 5, the notation ||L̂<sup>2</sup> - L<sup>2</sup>||<sub>F</sub> is unclear – is it the square of the Frobenius norm of the difference, or the Frobenius norm of the squared difference? The latter would require justification. The paper primarily focuses on disjoint clusters; extending the framework to handle overlapping or hierarchical clustering scenarios (like those addressed by Balakrishnan et al., 2011) would broaden its applicability.

The evaluation highlights a potential limitation: the conditions for the guarantees appear restrictive. The statement regarding the sensitivity of PFM to small perturbations (lines 252-253) lacks quantification and needs elaboration. While smoothing the political blog data improves stability, the underlying reason isn’t fully explained. The fact that δ > δ<sub>0</sub> for SBM (as noted in the Figure 1 caption) is a significant concern.

The related work section feels incomplete. Key papers on model-free clustering and stability (Kumar & Kannan, 2010; Balcan et al., 2012, 2013) are missing. A discussion of how the stability notion presented here relates to those in existing literature, particularly regarding perturbation of cluster centers, would enrich the paper.

Minor points: Figure 1 lacks axis labels. Line 44 states edge probabilities are independent *given* cluster assignments, which is standard; this could be slightly rephrased for clarity. The reuse of symbols like B, C, and Y with different meanings across sections could lead to confusion. Maintaining consistent notation (e.g., using Y<sub>orthogonal</sub> for the PFM case) would improve readability. The estimator definition for SBM could be simplified by directly substituting the expression for YZ. The use of distinct notation for eigenvalues in PFM and SBM (e.g., λ<sup>L</sup> and λ<sup>B</sup>) would enhance clarity. Finally, Section 4.4 feels more suited as part of the related work discussion.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel framework for model-free clustering guarantees.
*   Explicitly computable quantities for assessing stability.
*   Connections to existing model-based recovery results.
*   Potential to strengthen existing results (as demonstrated for PFM).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Restrictive conditions for guarantees, limiting practical applicability.
*   Limited empirical validation and potentially overly strong assumptions.
*   Incomplete related work discussion.
*   Lack of clarity in certain technical details (e.g., Assumption 5).
*   Uneven treatment of related models (PFM vs. SBM).



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution. However, the practical utility of the guarantees, as suggested by the simulations, is questionable. The paper would benefit from a more thorough exploration of the conditions required for the theorems to hold, a more comprehensive related work section, and clarification of some technical details. I recommend **weak reject**, with encouragement to address the raised concerns and potentially explore the conditions under which the proposed framework is most effective.
