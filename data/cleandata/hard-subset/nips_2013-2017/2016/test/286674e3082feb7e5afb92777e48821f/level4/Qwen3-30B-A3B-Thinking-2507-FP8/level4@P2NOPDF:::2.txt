

This paper establishes a relationship between different distance metrics for clustering in network data, focusing on Preference Frame Models (PFM) and Stochastic Block Models (SBM). The authors prove a locally convex-like property: for any two clusterings that yield equally good parameter estimates for the network data, these clusterings are close under misclassification distance. This demonstrates the equivalence of various clustering distance measures in the context of network models, supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical validation. The core insight is valuable, particularly for understanding consistency in network clustering. However, the novelty is limited by recent related work, including Peng et al. (COLT 2015) and Chao et al. (ArXiv 2015), which explore similar themes of clustering equivalence in networks. The theoretical contributions largely extend well-known results rather than introducing novel mathematical frameworks. While the paper briefly suggests applications in clusterability detection, it fails to provide formal results or rigorous development in this direction. The manuscript is well-written and clearly articulates its central ideas, with the theoretical exposition being accessible. The experimental section effectively complements the theory but does not significantly advance beyond the stated claims. The primary limitation lies in the incremental nature of the theoretical advances relative to existing literature, which may reduce the paper’s impact for a venue like NIPS. A stronger case for novelty could have been made by more explicitly contrasting the current work with prior results or by addressing gaps in the cited studies. The absence of a formal application to clusterability detection, despite its mention, leaves an important practical dimension underdeveloped. Overall, the paper presents a coherent and technically sound argument but offers modest theoretical innovation. The clarity of presentation and solid validation provide merit, yet the work’s contribution appears incremental rather than transformative for the field. For NIPS, where novelty and conceptual advance are prioritized, this may warrant a borderline acceptance, contingent on the program committee’s assessment of the incremental value relative to the cited literature.
