

This paper introduces a novel framework for providing theoretical guarantees about the stability of graph clustering without assuming the data comes from a specific model. The authors propose a generic template where, if a clustering C fits the data well according to some goodness-of-fit measure, then any other clustering C' that also fits the data well must be close to C. The authors instantiate this framework for two block models (PFM and SBM), demonstrating that all quantities involved in the guarantees can be computed directly from the data. The work connects model-free guarantees to existing model-based results, showing how the former can strengthen the latter.

**Strengths:**
The paper makes an important contribution by addressing a significant gap in the literature: providing model-free theoretical guarantees for clustering. This is particularly valuable for practitioners who lack strong assumptions about data generation. The framework is elegant and generalizable, with the authors successfully demonstrating its application to two important block models. The paper's technical approach is sound, with clear definitions of key concepts (goodness-of-fit, clustering distance) and rigorous proofs. The experimental evaluation provides concrete evidence of the framework's computational feasibility, and the authors thoughtfully address practical limitations through their experiments.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Inconsistent treatment of models:** The paper treats PFM and SBM separately despite PFM encompassing SBM as a special case (with DC-SBM as an intermediate class). The authors use different model estimators (Laplacian for PFM, adjacency matrix for SBM) without justification, which undermines the claimed generality of their framework. The use of weighted ME distance for PFM versus standard ME for SBM creates unnecessary confusion when comparing results.

2. **Technical ambiguities:** Several technical points require clarification. Proposition 10 references "<M', M>F" without definition. Assumption 5's use of "||L̂² - L²||F" is unclear—should this be ||L̂ - L||²_F? The paper would benefit from more precise mathematical notation throughout.

3. **Limited scope:** The framework assumes fixed cluster counts and disjoint clusters, which excludes many practical clustering scenarios where algorithms may suggest different numbers of clusters. The paper does not address how to handle cases where one clustering is a refinement of another (e.g., one cluster splitting into two).

4. **Insufficient related work:** The related work section fails to cite key model-free clustering literature, including Balcan et al.'s "Clustering under Perturbation Resilience" (2012) and "Clustering under Approximation Stability" (2013), as well as Kumar and Kannan's "Clustering with Spectral Norm" (2010). It would strengthen the paper to clarify how their stability notion relates to these existing frameworks.

5. **Practical limitations:** The experimental results suggest the framework's conditions are often too restrictive for practical use. For SBM, the paper notes "all δ values from Theorem 9 are above 1" (Line 250), making the guarantees uninformative. The paper's claim that "the quantities can be actually calculated from the data" is undermined by the small perturbations tolerated (Line 252-253), which would be impractical in real-world applications.

6. **Notational inconsistencies:** The paper uses inconsistent notation (B, C, Y) across the PFM and SBM sections, creating confusion for readers. The authors could improve clarity by standardizing notation or explicitly defining differences between model-specific notations.

7. **Experimental presentation:** Figure 1's caption states "For SBM, δ is always greater than δ0" without specifying which subplot this refers to, and the paper fails to explain why SBM results aren't included in the main figure. The missing axis labels in Figure 1 also diminish the paper's presentation quality.

**Recommendation:**
The paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution to model-free clustering guarantees, but the practical limitations and technical ambiguities significantly undermine its impact. While the framework is conceptually sound and the paper is well-written, the conditions for meaningful guarantees appear too restrictive for most real-world applications. The authors would need to substantially strengthen the practical relevance of their results and clarify the technical details to justify acceptance.

**Pro (for acceptance):** Novel framework for model-free clustering guarantees with computable quantities; strong theoretical foundation; clear connection to existing model-based results.

**Con (against acceptance):** Practical guarantees are too restrictive (SBM conditions rarely satisfied); inconsistent treatment of models; insufficient related work; technical ambiguities; limited scope (fixed cluster counts).

This paper presents an interesting theoretical approach but requires significant revisions to demonstrate practical utility and address the identified weaknesses. While the core idea is promising, the current results do not sufficiently support the claim that the framework provides meaningful guarantees for real-world clustering problems.
