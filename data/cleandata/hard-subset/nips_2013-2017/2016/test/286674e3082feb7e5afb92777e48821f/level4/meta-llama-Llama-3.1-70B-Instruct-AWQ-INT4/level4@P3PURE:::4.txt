This paper proposes a generic template for providing theoretical guarantees on the stability of a clustering C that fits the data well. The stability is measured in terms of the distance between C and any other clustering C' that fits the same data well. The authors demonstrate the applicability of this template by considering two specific block models, namely the planted factor model (PFM) and the stochastic block model (SBM), and show that all the quantities involved in the guarantees can be computed explicitly from the data. Furthermore, the authors leverage the stability results to establish exact recovery guarantees for these models. However, the simulation results suggest that either the goodness-of-fit measure used or the conditions of the theorems provided render the results not particularly useful in practice.

The paper addresses the problem of providing generic, model-free theoretical guarantees for clustering, which is of importance for both theoretical and practical purposes. However, several concerns and questions arise from the presentation. Firstly, given that PFM includes the degree-corrected stochastic block model (DC-SBM) as a special case, which in turn includes SBM, it is unclear why the authors treat these two cases differently. Moreover, since spectral clustering works well for all the models specified, it is not evident why the authors do not use the same model estimator for both cases. Specifically, it is unclear why the Laplacian is used for PFM while the adjacency matrix is used for SBM. Additionally, the results for PFM are in terms of weighted mean squared error (ME), whereas the results for SBM are in terms of ME. A comparison between these two measures would be helpful. Furthermore, it is surprising that the authors do not provide results for DC-SBM.

Several technical questions also arise from the presentation. For instance, in Proposition 10, the definition of < M', M >F is not provided. In Assumption 5, it is unclear whether the norm is ||L^{hat}^2 - L^2||F or ||L^{hat} - L||F^2. If the former is correct, the intuition behind squaring the Laplacians is not evident. The paper primarily focuses on disjoint clusters, and it is unclear whether the techniques used in this paper can be extended beyond block models to other models for which spectral clustering works. For example, spectral clustering is known to work well for hierarchical clustering, and there are theoretical guarantees available (e.g., "Noise Thresholds for Spectral Clustering" by S. Balakrishnan et al., 2011). It would be helpful to discuss how the template would work for this case.

The evaluation metric considered, as well as the estimators used for both PFM and SBM, are limited to the case where C and C' have the same number of clusters. However, different clustering algorithms can potentially yield different numbers of clusters. For instance, the eigenvalues of the adjacency matrix A might suggest K clusters for C, while the eigenvalues of the Laplacian of A might suggest K' = K + 1 clusters for C' such that all the clusters in C and C' are the same except for one cluster that splits into two. In this case, C' is still "close" to C, but it cannot be captured by the given framework.

The related work section does not reflect several model-free results that exist, such as "Clustering with Spectral Norm and the k-means Algorithm" by Kumar and Kannan (2010), "Clustering under Perturbation Resilience" by Balcan et al. (2012), and "Clustering under Approximation Stability" by Balcan et al. (2013). It would be helpful to discuss how the notion of stability considered in this work relates to those in these works, particularly in terms of the stability measured for perturbations of centers for two clusterings.

The simulations suggest that the perturbations tolerated by the results for PFM are too small. The statement on lines 252-253 that "large eigengap makes PFM(G, C) differ from PFM(G, C0) even for very small perturbations" is not adequately qualified. It is unclear why this is the case. On the other hand, the political blog data is smoothed out by raising the power of A, which would further exaggerate the gaps in eigenvalues, but in this case, it seems to help stability. However, if such small perturbations can affect the model fit, then it renders the guarantee not particularly useful for practical purposes. The conditions for the SBM guarantee seem to be very hard to satisfy, which is unfortunate.

Several minor points also need to be addressed. For instance, the labels for the axes in Figure 1 are missing. The caption of Figure 1 mentions that "For SBM, \delta is always greater than \delta0," but it is unclear which subplot this refers to. If SBM is not plotted, this should be made clear in the caption. The guarantees in the theorems for PFM are in terms of weighted ME, whereas in the experiment, the perturbation is done for ME. The notation used in the paper is sometimes inconsistent, which can be confusing. For example, B, C, and Y are used with different definitions for PFM and SBM. It would be better to maintain consistency throughout the paper. Finally, Section 4.4 would be better suited as a subsection in the related work section.
