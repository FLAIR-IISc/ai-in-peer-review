This paper proposes a general template for certifying the stability of a clustering C that “fits the data well,” where stability is quantified by the distance between C and any other clustering C′ that also fits well. The authors instantiate the template for two block models (PFM and SBM), show how all quantities in the guarantees are data-computable, and leverage these stability bounds to derive exact-recovery conditions. While the problem is important and the framework is appealing, the experiments suggest that either the fit criterion or the theorem conditions may limit practical usefulness.

1) Scope and significance
- The goal of model-agnostic, data-computable stability guarantees for clustering is valuable both theoretically and practically. A template that does not assume a specific generative model yet yields verifiable bounds from observed data and algorithm outputs would be a notable contribution.

2) Choice of estimators and model hierarchy
- Since PFM includes DC-SBM and DC-SBM includes SBM, it is unclear why PFM and SBM are treated differently. If spectral clustering is effective across these models, why not use a unified estimator?
- Specifically, why use the Laplacian for PFM and the adjacency matrix for SBM? Please justify these choices in terms of the fit functional and the stability analysis.
- The PFM guarantees are stated in weighted misclassification error (weighted ME), whereas the SBM guarantees use ME. How do these measures compare, and what are the implications for interpreting the bounds?
- Given the centrality of DC-SBM in this hierarchy, why are no results reported for DC-SBM?

3) Proposition 10
- Please define ⟨M′, M⟩_F precisely (presumably the Frobenius inner product ∑_ij M′_ij M_ij).

4) Assumption 5
- Is the intended quantity ∥L̂^2 − L^2∥_F or ∥L̂ − L∥_F^2? If it is the former, please explain the motivation for squaring the Laplacians and how this aids the stability analysis.

5) Beyond disjoint block models
- The paper focuses on disjoint clusters in block models. Do your techniques extend to other settings where spectral clustering has guarantees, such as hierarchical clustering (e.g., “Noise Thresholds for Spectral Clustering,” Balakrishnan et al., 2011)? How would the template specialize in that case, and what would be the computable stability certificate?

6) Mismatched numbers of clusters
- The evaluation metrics and estimators assume C and C′ have the same number of clusters. In practice, different algorithms (or different spectral embeddings) can yield different K. For example, A’s spectrum may suggest K clusters for C, while the Laplacian suggests K′ = K + 1 for C′ with one cluster split into two; in that scenario, C′ is arguably close to C, yet your framework cannot capture this.
- Can the template be extended to handle merges/splits (e.g., via a distance that allows optimal merging/splitting, or via refinement/quotient mappings) so that stability is meaningful across different K?

7) Relation to prior “model-free” guarantees
- The related work should better situate this paper relative to model-agnostic results such as:
  - Kumar and Kannan (2010), “Clustering with Spectral Norm and the k-means Algorithm”
  - Balcan et al. (2012), “Clustering under Perturbation Resilience”
  - Balcan et al. (2013), “Clustering under Approximation Stability”
- How does your stability notion compare to perturbation resilience and approximation stability? In particular, how does your distance between C and C′ relate to stability measured via perturbations of centers or cluster assignments under metric perturbations?

8) Experimental thresholds and interpretability
- For PFM, the tolerated perturbations in simulation are extremely small. Lines 252–253 claim that a large eigengap makes PFM(G, C) differ from PFM(G, C0) even under very small perturbations; please quantify this effect and explain the mechanism.
- Conversely, for the political blogs data, powering A (which typically increases eigengaps and accentuates dominant structure) seems to improve stability. Please reconcile these observations.
- If such small perturbations suffice to break the fit condition, the practical utility of the guarantees is limited despite their data-computability. Likewise, Lines 248–250 indicate the SBM conditions are very stringent; please discuss whether these are proof artifacts, whether constants can be improved, or whether the phenomena are inherent.

9) Figure 1 caption
- The caption states “For SBM, δ is always greater than δ0.” Which subplot(s) show this? If SBM is not plotted, the caption should say so explicitly.

10) Metric mismatch between theory and experiments
- Theorems for PFM use weighted ME, but the experiments perturb ME. Please align the metric used in experiments with the theoretical guarantees, or justify why the conclusions transfer.

Minor points
1) Figure 1 is missing axis labels.
2) Line 44: For block models, A_ij for i > j are independent conditioned on the cluster labels of i and j.
3) Notation reuse is confusing. B, C, and Y are overloaded across PFM and SBM. Given the already heavy notation, please enforce consistency (e.g., use Y_⊥ and C_D for PFM instead of B and C; in the SBM estimator, write the expression directly rather than introducing YZ). Similarly, distinguish eigenvalues by source, e.g., λ^L vs. λ^B.
4) Section 4.4 reads more like related work and would fit better as a subsection of that section.

Overall assessment
- Strengths: a clear attempt at a model-agnostic, data-computable stability template; instantiations for PFM and SBM; connections to exact recovery.
- Weaknesses: lack of a unified estimator across the model hierarchy; unclear treatment of DC-SBM; stringent or fragile conditions in practice; gaps between theory and experiments (metrics, parameter regimes); limited discussion of related model-agnostic stability notions; inability to compare clusterings with different K.

Addressing the above issues—particularly unifying the estimators, clarifying definitions/assumptions, extending to mismatched K, tightening experimental alignment with theory, and broadening the related-work comparison—would significantly strengthen the paper.
