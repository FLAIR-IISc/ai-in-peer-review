This paper presents novel theoretical findings in graph clustering, notably without relying on assumptions about the underlying graph generation process – a significant advantage over methods tied to specific models like the stochastic block model. The core contribution lies in demonstrating that if a clustering adequately fits a given graph, any alternative equally well-fitting clustering must be structurally similar to the first. I find this work compelling and believe it tackles a crucial problem by offering distribution-free results for graph clustering. Despite some content being relegated to supplementary materials due to page limits, the paper remains accessible and self-contained.

My main stylistic concern is the notation using “hats” to denote observed quantities (e.g., \hat{L} for the observed Laplacian), which I find somewhat unconventional. However, this is a minor point. The results are presented clearly, are well-supported by illustrative examples, and are thoroughly discussed.

It’s important to acknowledge certain limitations. The experimental results demonstrate that, for some real-world graphs, smoothing is required to reveal structure within the authors’ theoretical framework, whereas established algorithms can often recover the true underlying structure directly. More fundamentally, two key limitations exist: First, the primary result concerning the Partition Function Metric (PFM) employs a non-standard distance metric for comparing clusterings; while the authors posit its naturalness, this claim remains debatable. Second, the analysis relies on a “proxy” model approach, reconstructing a model (like a PFM) *from* a clustering and then comparing this model to the original graph, rather than directly relating the clustering to the original generative process. This approach, while logical within the paper’s context, inherently constrains the definition of a “good” clustering to one that provides a strong approximation of the graph in the normalized Laplacian sense. While necessary for achieving theoretical results, this restriction raises questions about the broader applicability of the framework, especially given the limitations observed with real-world data. Further investigation into the impact of these constraints would be valuable.
