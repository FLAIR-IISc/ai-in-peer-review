This paper proposes a novel framework for graph clustering that provides theoretical guarantees without assuming the data comes from a specific model. The authors aim to bridge the gap between model-based and model-free clustering approaches. The main contribution of the paper is the development of a generic theorem that establishes a connection between the quality of a clustering and its robustness to perturbations.

The paper starts by introducing the concept of block models, including Stochastic Block Models (SBM), Degree-Corrected SBM (DC-SBM), and Preference Frame Models (PFM). The authors then present a framework for clustering with guarantees without model assumptions. The framework is based on the idea that if a clustering fits the data well, then any other clustering that also fits the data well will be a small perturbation of the original clustering.

The authors instantiate their generic theorem for the cases of PFM and SBM. They provide a PFM estimation algorithm and prove that it is consistent under standard recovery conditions. They also show that the algorithm can be used to obtain model-free guarantees for clustering. Similarly, they present an SBM estimation algorithm and prove that it provides model-free guarantees for clustering.

The paper also discusses the relationship between model-based and model-free clustering approaches. The authors show that existing model-based recovery theorems can be used to prove model-free guarantees for clustering. They also provide alternative model-free guarantees for PFM and SBM using different assumptions.

The experimental evaluation of the paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed framework. The authors show that their theorems can provide model-free guarantees for clustering on various datasets, including simulated and real-world data.

Overall, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field of graph clustering. The proposed framework provides a new perspective on clustering that bridges the gap between model-based and model-free approaches. The technical results are sound, and the experimental evaluation demonstrates the effectiveness of the approach.

However, there are some areas that could be improved. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the proposed framework. Additionally, the authors could provide more insights into the relationship between model-based and model-free clustering approaches.
