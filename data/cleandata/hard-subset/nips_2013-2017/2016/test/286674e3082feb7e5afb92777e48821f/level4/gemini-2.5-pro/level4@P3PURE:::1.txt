This paper presents novel theoretical results for graph clustering that are distribution-free, meaning they do not rely on assumptions about the graph's generative process, such as the stochastic block model. The central thesis is that if a clustering algorithm finds a partition that fits the observed graph well (according to a specific metric), then any other partition that fits the graph equally well must be structurally similar to the first.

This work is very interesting and addresses the important problem of providing theoretical guarantees for graph clustering in a distribution-free setting. The paper is well-written and clear; despite page limitations requiring some content to be moved to the supplementary material, the main paper is self-contained and can be understood without it. The theoretical results are compelling and are well-supported by commentary and illustrations. Aside from a minor notational choice (the use of "hats" for observed quantities like `\hat{L}`, which could be clearer), the presentation is of high quality.

However, the framework has some notable limitations.

First, the experiments reveal a practical limitation: on some real-world datasets, the graph requires smoothing for the proposed framework to recover structure, whereas standard clustering algorithms can recover the ground-truth structure without this preprocessing.

Second, and perhaps more importantly, there are two theoretical limitations:
1.  The main result for the Planted Forest Model (PFM) relies on a non-standard distance measure between clusterings. While the authors argue that this measure may be more natural than classical ones, this claim is questionable.
2.  The results are based on a "proxy" model framework. A clustering's quality is judged by how well a model reconstructed from it (e.g., a PFM) approximates the original graph, specifically in the sense of the normalized Laplacian. While this approach is a natural and necessary choice to derive the results, it imposes a strong and specific definition of what constitutes a "good" clustering for a given graph. One might wonder about the impact of these restrictions, especially considering the framework's difficulties on certain real-world examples.
