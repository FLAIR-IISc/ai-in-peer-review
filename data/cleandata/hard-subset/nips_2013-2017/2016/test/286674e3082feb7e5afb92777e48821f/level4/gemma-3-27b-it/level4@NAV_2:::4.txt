The paper presents a generic framework for theoretical guarantees on the stability of a clustering C when it fits the data well, defining stability as insensitivity to alternative clusterings C' that also fit the data well. This is a valuable contribution, offering a path towards model-free clustering validation. The authors instantiate this framework for the Stochastic Block Model (SBM) and Preference Frame Model (PFM), providing explicit, computable guarantees. While the approach is promising, the experimental results suggest the derived bounds may be difficult to satisfy in practice, limiting immediate applicability.

The core idea – providing guarantees without strict model assumptions – is a significant advance. Existing model-based approaches are limited by the validity of their assumptions. This work attempts to bridge that gap by focusing on data fit rather than generative process. The connection established between model-free and model-based recovery is also noteworthy, potentially allowing for leveraging existing results.

However, several points require clarification and could strengthen the paper. First, given that PFM encompasses both DC-SBM and SBM, the differing treatment and estimators for these models (using the Laplacian for PFM but the adjacency matrix for SBM, differing distance metrics) are not fully justified. A clearer rationale for these choices is needed. Why isn’t a consistent estimator (e.g., spectral clustering) employed across all models? Furthermore, the absence of results for DC-SBM, given its intermediate position, is a gap.

Specific technical questions arise: In Proposition 10, the definition of <M', M>F is missing. In Assumption 5, is it ||L̂<sup>2</sup> - L<sup>2</sup>||<sub>F</sub> or ||L̂ - L||<sub>F</sub><sup>2</sup>? The rationale for squaring the Laplacians, if the latter, should be explained. The framework currently assumes disjoint clusters; exploring extensions to overlapping or hierarchical structures (e.g., relating to work on hierarchical spectral clustering and associated stability guarantees) would broaden its applicability. Currently, the framework is limited to clusterings with the same number of clusters; addressing scenarios where algorithms might produce differing numbers of clusters (e.g., a split in one cluster) would be valuable.

The related work section lacks comprehensive coverage of existing model-free stability results.  Relevant papers like Kumar & Kannan (2010), Balcan et al. (2012, 2013) should be discussed and the relationship between the proposed stability measure and existing notions of perturbation resilience and approximation stability should be clarified. 

The simulations raise concerns about the practicality of the bounds. The claim that the PFM model is sensitive to even small perturbations, due to the eigengap, needs further elaboration.  The smoothing operation applied to the political blog data seems counterintuitive – exacerbating eigenvalue gaps yet improving stability – and warrants a more detailed explanation.  The consistently unsatisfied conditions for the SBM (stated in lines 248-250) are discouraging. Figure 1's caption needs clarification regarding the SBM results (specifically, the statement about δ > δ<sub>0</sub> and whether SBM data is actually plotted). Axis labels are missing from Figure 1.

Minor points include inconsistent notation (B, C, and Y are redefined for SBM), which should be addressed for clarity. The use of subscripts (e.g., λ<sup>L</sup>, λ<sup>B</sup>) to distinguish eigenvalues could improve readability. Subsection 4.4 would be better placed within the related work section. Additionally, line 44 should clarify that A<sub>ij</sub> for i > j are independent *given* the cluster assignments.

Arguments for acceptance: The paper addresses an important problem – model-free clustering validation – with a novel and potentially impactful framework. The theoretical results are well-defined and provide a path towards quantifiable guarantees. The connection between model-free and model-based recovery is a strong contribution.

Arguments against acceptance: The derived bounds appear overly conservative and may not be practically useful, as indicated by the simulations. The lack of thorough comparison with existing model-free stability results weakens the presentation. Several technical details require clarification and justification. The restricted scope (disjoint clusters, limited model coverage) limits the immediate impact.



Overall assessment: Reject with encouragement to revise. The paper presents a promising theoretical framework but requires significant revision to address the concerns raised above. Strengthening the theoretical justification, expanding the empirical evaluation, and thoroughly situating the work within the existing literature are crucial steps towards publication.
