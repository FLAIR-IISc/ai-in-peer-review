This paper presents a novel and ambitious framework for providing theoretical guarantees for graph clustering *without* assuming the data originates from a specific block model. The core idea is to establish stability: if a clustering C fits the data well, then any other clustering C' that also fits the data well must be a small perturbation of C. This framework is instantiated for both the PFM and SBM, with all quantities required for the guarantees being computable from the data. While the theoretical contributions are significant, the experimental results suggest the conditions for these guarantees may be restrictive in practice.

The paper addresses an important problem in network analysis: providing rigorous guarantees for clustering algorithms beyond the limitations of model-based assumptions. The proposed framework offers a potentially powerful approach to validating clustering results and understanding the inherent structure of graphs, even when the underlying generative process is unknown. The emphasis on computable quantities is particularly valuable, as it bridges the gap between theoretical results and practical applications.

However, several aspects of the paper could be strengthened. The treatment of PFM and SBM feels somewhat disjointed, given that PFM encompasses both DC-SBM and SBM. A clearer justification for the different estimators used (Laplacian for PFM, adjacency matrix for SBM) and the differing distance metrics (weighted ME vs. ME) would be beneficial. A natural extension would be to explicitly derive results for DC-SBM as an intermediate step. Furthermore, the paper largely focuses on the case of disjoint clusters. Exploring the framework’s applicability to scenarios where clusterings may differ in the number of clusters (e.g., a split-merge scenario) would broaden its scope. 

Specific concerns and questions include: (1) In Proposition 10, the definition of <M', M>F is missing. (2) Assumption 5: Clarification is needed regarding the squaring of the Laplacians – what is the intuition behind using ||L̂² - L²||F instead of ||L̂ - L||F²? (3) The paper’s connection to existing model-free results is underdeveloped. Key references like Kumar & Kannan (2010), Balcan et al. (2012, 2013) should be discussed, and the relationship between the stability concepts explored here and those in prior work needs to be explicitly addressed. (4) The simulations seem to indicate that satisfying the goodness-of-fit conditions is challenging. The statement regarding the sensitivity of PFM to small perturbations (lines 252-253) requires further elaboration. (5) Figure 1’s caption should clarify which subplots relate to the SBM, given that δ is reportedly always greater than δ0 for SBM. The axes in Figure 1 are also missing labels. (6) Throughout the paper, consistent notation is lacking (e.g., B, C, and Y are redefined). Using subscripts (e.g., Y<sub>PFM</sub>, Y<sub>SBM</sub>) or more descriptive names (e.g., Y<sub>orthogonal</sub>, C<sub>D</sub>) would improve clarity.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The framework for model-free clustering guarantees is a significant contribution.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The mathematical derivations appear rigorous and well-presented.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The emphasis on computable quantities increases the potential for real-world applications.
*   **Connections to Existing Work:** Demonstrates how model-free results can strengthen existing model-based recovery theorems.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Restrictive Conditions:** The experimental results suggest the conditions for the guarantees may be difficult to satisfy in practice.
*   **Limited Scope:** Primarily focuses on disjoint clusters and does not address scenarios with varying cluster numbers.
*   **Incomplete Literature Review:** Does not fully acknowledge or integrate with relevant prior work on model-free clustering and stability.
*   **Clarity Issues:** Notation inconsistencies and lack of explanations in certain areas hinder understanding.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable contribution to the field of graph clustering. However, the restrictive conditions, limited scope, and minor clarity issues raise concerns. I would rate this paper as a **weak accept**. Addressing the concerns outlined above – particularly expanding the literature review, clarifying the practical implications of the conditions, and exploring extensions to more general clustering scenarios – would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact. A stronger emphasis on discussing the limitations of the current approach would also be beneficial.
