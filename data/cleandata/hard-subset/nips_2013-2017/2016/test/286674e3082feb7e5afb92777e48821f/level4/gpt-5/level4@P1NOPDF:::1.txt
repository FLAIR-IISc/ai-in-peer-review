The authors present new theoretical results on graph clustering that do not assume a specific generative process for the observed graph (e.g., no reliance on the stochastic block model). The central idea is that if a clustering method finds a partition that fits the graph reasonably well (under a precise, formal notion of fit), then any other clustering that fits the graph equally well must be close to the first one. This is an interesting and important contribution, providing distribution-free guarantees for graph clustering.

Despite some material being moved to the supplementary section due to conference page limits, the paper remains clear and largely self-contained. I am not convinced by the choice of notation that places “hats” on observed quantities (e.g., \hat{L} for the observed Laplacian), but aside from this minor point, the exposition is generally clear. The results are well-motivated, carefully discussed, and supported by illustrative examples.

That said, the work has notable limitations. As shown in the experiments, for some real-world datasets the graph must be smoothed before any structure is recoverable in the authors’ theoretical sense, whereas many classical algorithms recover the ground-truth structure without such preprocessing. This raises questions about the practical scope of the framework.

More importantly, I see two substantive limitations:

1) The main result for PFM relies on a nonstandard distance measure between clusterings. Although the authors argue that this metric may be more natural than the classical one, this claim is debatable and not fully compelling. Since conclusions depend on this choice, more justification or comparison would be helpful.

2) The analysis proceeds via a “proxy” model: given a clustering, one reconstructs a model (e.g., a PFM) and compares this model to the original graph, rather than comparing the model actually used to obtain the clustering. While this is natural in the authors’ chosen framework, it strongly constrains what counts as a good clustering—for example, requiring that the graph be well approximated in the normalized Laplacian sense. This restriction is understandable as a means to obtain rigorous results, but it raises concerns about how broadly the conclusions apply, especially in light of the empirical cases where the framework struggles.

Overall, this is a thought-provoking and clearly written paper that tackles an important problem with distribution-free guarantees. However, the reliance on a nonstandard clustering distance and on a proxy-model comparison, along with the need for smoothing on some real data where classical methods succeed, temper the practical impact of the theoretical results.
