This paper presents a generic template for theoretical guarantees of clustering stability, measured as the distance between clusterings that fit the data well. The authors demonstrate the template’s application to two block models – the Planted Function Model (PFM) and the Stochastic Block Model (SBM) – with explicitly computable quantities. They then leverage these stability results to derive exact recovery guarantees. While the approach of providing model-agnostic guarantees with data-driven parameters is valuable, the practical utility of the results appears limited based on the reported simulations and certain modeling choices.

The core contribution – a general stability framework for clustering – is a worthwhile pursuit. Providing theoretical guarantees without strong model assumptions is important for both theoretical progress and practical application. However, several aspects of the paper’s presentation and results raise concerns.

First, the treatment of PFM and SBM feels inconsistent. Given that PFM encompasses the Disconnected-SBM (DC-SBM), which in turn includes SBM, it is unclear why the two are handled so distinctly. Both Spectral Clustering and the proposed framework appear applicable to all three models; therefore, a unified treatment with a consistent estimator seems preferable. Specifically, the use of the Laplacian for PFM versus the adjacency matrix for SBM is not well justified. Furthermore, the stability results for PFM are expressed in terms of weighted Mutual Information (ME), while those for SBM use unweighted ME, making direct comparison challenging. The absence of results for DC-SBM is also a missed opportunity.

Several technical points require clarification. In Proposition 10, the definition of <M’, M><sub>F</sub> is unclear. In Assumption 5, is the condition ||L̂<sup>2</sup> - L<sup>2</sup>||<sub>F</sub> or ||L̂ - L||<sub>F</sub><sup>2</sup>? The rationale for squaring the Laplacians in the former case should be elaborated.

The scope of the framework also warrants discussion. The paper primarily focuses on disjoint clusters. Does the proposed methodology extend to other clustering scenarios where Spectral Clustering performs well, such as hierarchical clustering? Existing theoretical work on hierarchical clustering (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2011) could serve as a valuable comparison point.

The framework's reliance on the same number of clusters in C and C’ is restrictive. In practice, different algorithms may suggest differing numbers of clusters, even if the resulting partitions are reasonably close (e.g., a split cluster). This scenario is not captured by the current framework.

The related work section is incomplete. It lacks discussion of relevant model-free clustering results, such as Kumar & Kannan (2010), Balcan et al. (2012, 2013). The paper should explicitly relate its notion of stability to those presented in these works, particularly regarding perturbation of cluster centers.

The simulation results are concerning. The perturbations tolerated in the PFM simulations are very small, and the statement about eigengaps affecting model fit (lines 252-253) lacks sufficient justification. The smoothing operation applied to the political blog data, while seemingly helpful for stability, exacerbates eigenvalue gaps. If small perturbations significantly impact model fit, the practical relevance of the guarantees diminishes, contradicting one of the paper’s central claims. Similarly, the authors acknowledge that the SBM guarantee conditions are difficult to satisfy (lines 248-250).

Specific points regarding presentation and clarity: Figure 1’s caption is unclear regarding the mention of “δ > δ<sub>0</sub>”, and if SBM data is not plotted, this should be stated. The figure also lacks axis labels. There are inconsistencies in notation: B, C, and Y are redefined between sections 3.1 and 3.2, leading to confusion. Employing consistent notation (e.g., Y<sub>⊥</sub>, C<sub>D</sub>) would improve readability. Additionally, the expression for YZ in Proposition 8 could be simplified. Finally, distinguishing between Laplacian and block model eigenvalues with notations like λ<sup>L</sup> and λ<sup>B</sup> would be helpful. Section 4.4 would be better placed within the Related Work section. Minor points include a clarification in line 44 regarding the independence of A<sub>ij</sub> for i > j, and ensuring consistent notation throughout.



Overall, the paper presents an interesting theoretical framework. However, the limitations in practical applicability, coupled with inconsistencies in presentation and analysis, prevent it from being highly impactful in its current form. Further clarification, a more comprehensive comparison with existing work, and a more thorough investigation of the conditions required for the guarantees to hold are needed.
