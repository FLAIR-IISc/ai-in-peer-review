The authors propose new theoretical results on graph clustering that do not make assumptions on the generation of the graph under study. The main idea of the paper is that if a graph clustering method is able to discover a clustering that fits the graph reasonably, then any other clustering that fits as well the graph should be close to the first one. This paper addresses an important problem by providing distribution-free results for graph clustering.

The paper is well-written and clear, despite some content being moved to the supplementary materials due to page number restrictions. However, the use of "hats" on actual observations (e.g., \hat{L} for the observed Laplacian) may not be the best choice. Nevertheless, the results themselves are interesting and well-commented, and the authors provide a good discussion of their implications.

The experimental evaluation is helpful in understanding the applicability of the theoretical framework. However, the results also highlight some limitations. For instance, the need to smooth the graph in order to recover structure using the proposed framework, whereas many classical algorithms can recover the ground truth structure without this preprocessing step. Two significant limitations of the paper are: (1) the main result for PFM uses a non-standard distance measure between clusterings, which may not be widely accepted as a natural choice; and (2) the results rely on a "proxy" model, where a model (e.g., a PFM) is reconstructed from a clustering and compared to the original graph. While this approach is natural in the context of the paper, it constrains the notion of what constitutes a good clustering for a given graph.

The paper's contributions are significant, as it provides a new perspective on graph clustering by offering distribution-free results. The authors' use of a stability paradigm, where the focus is on the perturbation of a clustering rather than the clustering itself, is an interesting approach. However, the limitations of the framework, particularly with regards to the need for smoothing and the use of a proxy model, should be carefully considered.

To further improve the paper, the authors could provide more discussion on the implications of their results, particularly in relation to the limitations mentioned above. Additionally, exploring ways to address these limitations, such as developing more robust distance measures or relaxing the assumptions of the proxy model, could strengthen the paper's contributions.

In terms of the NIPS evaluation criteria, the paper scores well on originality, as it presents a new approach to graph clustering. The paper's significance is also high, as it addresses an important problem in the field. The quality of the paper is good, with clear writing and well-organized structure. However, the clarity could be improved by addressing the limitations mentioned above and providing more discussion on the implications of the results.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a new and interesting approach to graph clustering.
* The results are well-commented and easy to follow.
* The paper addresses an important problem in the field.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The need for smoothing the graph in order to recover structure using the proposed framework.
* The use of a non-standard distance measure between clusterings.
* The reliance on a "proxy" model, which constrains the notion of what constitutes a good clustering for a given graph.

Overall, I believe that the paper's contributions outweigh its limitations, and it has the potential to make a significant impact in the field. Therefore, I recommend acceptance.
