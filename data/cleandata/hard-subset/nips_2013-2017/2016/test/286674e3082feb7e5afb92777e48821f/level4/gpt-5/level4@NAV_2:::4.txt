The paper proposes a general template for providing theoretical guarantees on the stability of a clustering C that matches the observed graph well. Stability is quantified via the distance between C and any alternative clustering C' that also fits the data well. The template is instantiated for two block-model families, PFM and SBM, with all quantities entering the guarantees computable directly from the data. The paper also leverages the resulting stability bounds to derive exact-recovery style implications for these models. That said, the empirical results suggest that either the chosen goodness-of-fit measures or the stated conditions make the guarantees of limited practical utility.

1) The paper addresses the problem of furnishing model-free theoretical guarantees for clustering, avoiding assumptions about the data-generating mechanism. The aim is to produce guarantees in terms of quantities that can be estimated from the observed graph and the clusterings under comparison. Such model-agnostic guarantees are valuable for both theoretical understanding and practical deployment.

2) Since PFM strictly contains DC-SBM, which in turn contains SBM, it is unclear why PFM and SBM are treated asymmetrically. Given that spectral clustering applies to all these models, why not adopt a uniform estimator? In particular, why is the Laplacian used for PFM while the adjacency matrix is used for SBM? Moreover, the PFM results are stated in weighted ME, whereas the SBM results use unweighted ME; how should these be compared? Finally, why are there no results for DC-SBM?

3) In Proposition 10, please define the notation < M', M >_F precisely.

4) Assumption 5: Is the intended quantity ||L_hat^2 − L^2||_F or (||L_hat − L||_F)^2? If it is the former, what is the intuition for squaring the Laplacians?

5) The work concentrates on disjoint partitions. Do the techniques/recipe extend beyond block models to other settings where spectral methods succeed? For instance, spectral clustering is known to perform well for hierarchical clustering with supporting theory (e.g., “Noise Thresholds for Spectral Clustering,” S. Balakrishnan et al., 2011). How would the proposed template apply there?

6) Both the evaluation metric and the proposed estimators for PFM and SBM assume C and C' have the same number of clusters. In practice, different algorithms may select different K. For example, the spectrum of A might suggest K for C while the spectrum of the Laplacian suggests K' = K + 1 for C', where C' only splits one cluster of C into two. In such a case, C' is arguably close to C, yet the current framework cannot capture this relationship.

7) The Related Work section overlooks several relevant model-free lines: “Clustering with Spectral Norm and the k-means Algorithm,” Kumar and Kannan (2010); “Clustering under Perturbation Resilience,” Balcan et al. (2012); “Clustering under Approximation Stability,” Balcan et al. (2013). How does the stability notion here relate to these works? In particular, how does the paper’s stability compare to stability under perturbations of centers (or resilience/approximate stability notions)?

8) The tolerable perturbations in the PFM simulations appear very small. Lines 252–253 state that a large eigengap causes PFM(G, C) to deviate from PFM(G, C0) even for tiny perturbations; this claim needs clarification. Conversely, for the political blogs data, smoothing by powering A increases spectral gaps yet seems to improve stability—how do these observations reconcile? If minute perturbations already break the guarantees, their practical usefulness (one of the paper’s central motivations, since everything is data-computable) is questionable. Also, lines 248–250 note that the SBM conditions are very hard to meet, which is discouraging.

9) The caption of Figure 1 says “For SBM, delta is always greater than delta0.” Which panel(s) is this referring to? If the SBM curves are omitted, the caption should state that explicitly.

10) The PFM theorems are for weighted ME, whereas the experimental perturbations are performed in unweighted ME.
