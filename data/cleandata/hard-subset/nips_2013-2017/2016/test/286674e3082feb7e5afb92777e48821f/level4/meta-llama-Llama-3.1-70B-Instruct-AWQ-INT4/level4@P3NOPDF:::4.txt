The paper proposes a generic template for providing theoretical guarantees on the stability of a clustering C that fits the data well. The stability is measured in terms of the distance between C and any other clustering C' that also fits the data well. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this template by applying it to two block models, namely the planted model (PFM) and the stochastic block model (SBM), and show that all the quantities involved in the guarantees can be computed explicitly from the data. Furthermore, the authors use the stability results to establish exact recovery guarantees for these models.

However, the simulation results suggest that either the goodness-of-fit measure used or the conditions of the theorems provided render the results not too useful in practice. This is a significant concern, as the paper's main selling point is that the quantities involved in the guarantees can be computed from the data, making the results potentially useful for practical purposes.

The paper addresses an important problem of providing model-free theoretical guarantees for clustering, which is crucial for both theoretical and practical purposes. However, there are several issues with the paper that need to be addressed.

Firstly, it is not clear why the authors treat PFM and SBM differently, given that PFM includes DC-SBM as a special case, which in turn includes SBM. Spectral clustering works well for all these models, so it would be more consistent to use the same model estimator for all of them. In particular, it is unclear why the Laplacian is used for PFM, while the adjacency matrix is used for SBM. Additionally, the results for PFM are in terms of weighted mean squared error (ME), whereas the results for SBM are in terms of ME. A comparison between these two measures would be helpful.

Secondly, some of the notations and definitions used in the paper are unclear or inconsistent. For example, the definition of <M', M>F in Proposition 10 is not provided. In Assumption 5, it is unclear whether the norm is ||L^2 - L^2||F or ||L^ - L||F^2. If the former, it would be helpful to provide some intuition behind squaring the Laplacians.

Thirdly, the paper focuses mainly on disjoint clusters, but it is unclear whether the techniques used in this paper can be extended to other models for which spectral clustering works. For example, spectral clustering is known to work well for hierarchical clustering, and there are theoretical guarantees available for this case. It would be helpful to discuss how the template would work for this case.

Fourthly, the evaluation metric considered in the paper, as well as the estimators used for PFM and SBM, are limited to the case where C and C' have the same number of clusters. However, different clustering algorithms can potentially give different numbers of clusters. For example, the eigenvalues of the adjacency matrix might suggest K clusters for C, while the eigenvalues of the Laplacian might suggest K' = K + 1 clusters for C'. In this case, C' is still "close" to C, but it cannot be captured by the given framework.

Fifthly, the related work section does not reflect a lot of model-free results that exist in the literature. For example, the papers "Clustering with Spectral Norm and the k-means Algorithm" by Kumar and Kannan (2010), "Clustering under Perturbation Resilience" by Balcan et al. (2012), and "Clustering under Approximation Stability" by Balcan et al. (2013) are not mentioned. It would be helpful to discuss how the notion of stability considered in this work relates to those in these papers.

Sixthly, the perturbations tolerated by the simulations for PFM are too small. The statement in lines 252-253 that "large eigengap makes PFM(G, C) differ from PFM(G, C0) even for very small perturbations" is unclear and needs to be qualified. Additionally, the political blog data is smoothed out by raising the power of the adjacency matrix, which further exaggerates the gaps in eigenvalues, but it seems to help stability. However, if such small perturbations can affect the model fit, then it renders the guarantee not of much use for practical purposes.

Seventhly, there are some minor points that need to be addressed. The labels for the axes in Figure 1 are missing. The notation used for block models in line 44 is unclear. The use of different notations for PFM and SBM, such as B, C, and Y, can be confusing. It would be better to maintain consistency throughout the paper.

In conclusion, while the paper proposes an interesting template for providing theoretical guarantees on the stability of clustering, there are several issues that need to be addressed. The authors need to clarify the notations and definitions used in the paper, provide more consistent results for PFM and SBM, and discuss the limitations of the current framework. Additionally, the authors need to address the minor points mentioned above to improve the overall quality of the paper.
