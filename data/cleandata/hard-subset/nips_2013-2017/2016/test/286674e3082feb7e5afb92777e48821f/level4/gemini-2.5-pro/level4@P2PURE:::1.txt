The authors present novel theoretical results for graph clustering that are model-agnostic, meaning they do not rely on assumptions about the graph's generative process (e.g., the stochastic block model). The central premise is that if a clustering algorithm finds a partition that fits the graph well (by a specific definition), then any other partition that fits the graph equally well must be structurally similar to the first one.

This paper addresses the important problem of providing distribution-free guarantees for graph clustering, making it a valuable contribution. The manuscript is well-written and clear. While some details are deferred to the supplementary material due to space constraints, the main text is self-contained and can be understood on its own. A minor notational point is the use of "hats" on observed quantities (e.g., \hat{L} for the observed Laplacian), which is somewhat unconventional, but this does not detract from the paper's overall clarity. The theoretical results themselves are compelling and are well-supported by commentary and illustrations.

However, the framework is not without limitations. First, as shown in the experiments, some real-world graphs require smoothing for the proposed framework to recover structure, whereas many classical algorithms can recover the ground-truth structure on the original graphs.

Furthermore, two more significant limitations exist:

1.  The main result for the Planted Partition Model (PFM) relies on a non-standard distance metric between clusterings. The authors' argument that this metric is more natural than classical ones is debatable.

2.  The results are based on a "proxy" model evaluation. Specifically, a model (e.g., a PFM) is reconstructed from a given clustering, and this proxy is then compared to the observed graph. While this approach is natural within the authors' context, it strongly constrains the notion of a "good" clustering to one whose corresponding model closely approximates the graph in the normalized Laplacian sense.

This constraint is necessary to obtain the theoretical results, but it raises questions about the practical impact of such restrictions, especially given the framework's aforementioned difficulties with some real-world examples.
