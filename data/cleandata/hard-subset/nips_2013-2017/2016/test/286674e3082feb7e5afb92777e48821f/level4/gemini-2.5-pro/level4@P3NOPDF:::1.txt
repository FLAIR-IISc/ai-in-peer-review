This paper presents novel theoretical results for graph clustering under a distribution-free framework. A key strength of this work is its departure from common assumptions that the observed graph is generated from a specific model, such as the stochastic block model. The central thesis is a form of stability or uniqueness: if a clustering algorithm identifies a partition that provides a 'good fit' to the graph (according to a defined metric), then any other partition that fits the graph equally well must be structurally close to the first. This is a compelling and important direction for understanding the reliability of clustering algorithms in settings where the true generative process is unknown.

The paper is well-written and generally clear. Despite moving some derivations to the supplementary material to meet page limits, the core arguments remain accessible in the main text. The theoretical results are significant, well-motivated, and effectively illustrated, providing valuable insights into the behavior of graph clustering.

On a minor note concerning presentation, the use of "hats" for observed quantities (e.g., \hat{L} for the observed Laplacian) is somewhat unconventional, as hats typically denote estimated quantities. However, this is a small stylistic point that does not detract from the paper's clarity.

While the results are interesting, the framework has some notable limitations that warrant discussion.

First, the experimental results reveal a potential gap between the theory and practice. On some real-world datasets, the observed graph must be "smoothed" for its structure to be recoverable under the paper's theoretical lens. This is in contrast to several classical algorithms that can successfully recover the ground-truth clustering on the original, unsmoothed graph. This suggests that the conditions required by the theory may not hold for certain raw, real-world graphs.

Second, and more importantly, the theoretical guarantees rely on specific, and in one case non-standard, assumptions:

1.  The main result for the Planted Forest Model (PFM) employs a non-standard distance measure between clusterings. While the authors argue that this measure may be more natural in this context, this claim is not fully substantiated and remains debatable. The use of a non-standard metric can make it difficult to compare these guarantees with existing results in the literature.

2.  The framework is based on a "proxy" model approach. It evaluates a given clustering by first reconstructing a model (e.g., a PFM) from the clustering itself, and then comparing this reconstructed model to the observed graph. This approach is logical within the chosen distribution-free setting, but it implicitly imposes a strong constraint on what constitutes a 'good' clustering. Specifically, a good clustering is defined as one from which the original graph can be well-approximated in the normalized Laplacian sense. While this simplification is necessary to make the analysis tractable, it raises questions about the broader applicability of the results. One might wonder about the impact of these restrictions, particularly in light of the framework's limitations on some real-world examples. The paper could be strengthened by a more thorough discussion of how this modeling choice might restrict the types of cluster structures for which the theory holds.

In summary, this is a very interesting paper that addresses an important problem with a novel, distribution-free approach. The theoretical contributions are significant. However, the practical applicability of the framework is tempered by the need for data smoothing in some cases and by foundational modeling choices that may limit its scope.
