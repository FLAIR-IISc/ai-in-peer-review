This paper presents a framework for providing theoretical guarantees for graph clustering without making assumptions about the data's generative process. This is a departure from much of the existing literature on block models (SBM, DC-SBM, PFM), which typically provides recovery guarantees under the assumption that the observed graph is sampled from a specific model. The core idea is to establish the stability of a given clustering `C`: if `C` and its associated model parameters fit the data well, then any other clustering `C'` that also provides a good fit must be a small perturbation of `C`. The authors instantiate this general framework for the PFM and SBM, deriving explicit, computable bounds on the distance between `C` and `C'`.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

*   **Significance and Novelty:** The paper addresses the important and practical problem of cluster validation when the ground-truth generative model is unknown. Shifting the focus from model-based recovery to model-free stability is a valuable contribution. The approach of using model-based analysis techniques to derive guarantees that do not depend on model assumptions is novel and could inspire further work in this direction.
*   **Practicality of Guarantees:** A major strength of the proposed framework is that all quantities involved in the theoretical guarantees (e.g., `ε`, `δ`) are computable from the observed graph and a given clustering. This makes the results verifiable and potentially useful for practitioners seeking to validate their clustering results.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against acceptance):**

*   **Clarity of Methodology:** The paper presents separate treatments for PFM and SBM using different objects of analysis (normalized Laplacian vs. adjacency matrix) and different error metrics (weighted ME vs. standard ME). The motivation for these distinct approaches is not well-explained, and it is unclear why a more unified framework could not be used, especially since spectral methods are effective for the entire family of block models. Furthermore, an analysis for the widely used Degree-Corrected SBM (DC-SBM) is notably absent.
*   **Limited Scope and Comparison to Related Work:**
    *   The framework is restricted to comparing partitions with an equal number of clusters, K. This limits its applicability, as comparing clusterings with different numbers of communities is a common practical problem. The paper should discuss if or how the framework could be extended to this setting.
    *   The related work section overlooks several highly relevant papers on model-free clustering analysis and stability. The manuscript would be significantly strengthened by discussing and comparing its notion of stability to concepts like perturbation resilience and approximation stability from prior work (e.g., Kumar and Kannan, 2010; Balcan et al., 2012, 2013).
*   **Concerning Experimental Results:** The empirical evaluation raises significant questions about the practical utility of the derived bounds.
    *   For the PFM, the guarantees appear to be effective only for extremely small perturbations of the initial clustering. The paper's brief explanation for this—that a large eigengap causes the fitted model to change rapidly—is not fully convincing and seems at odds with the observation that "smoothing" the graph (which increases the eigengap) improved stability for the political blogs dataset.
    *   More critically, the conditions for the SBM guarantee were reportedly never satisfied in the experiments. If the bounds are too loose to be informative even on synthetic data designed to fit the model (LFR benchmark), their practical value is questionable.
*   **Presentation Issues:**
    *   The reuse of notation (e.g., B, C, Y, λ) for different quantities in the PFM and SBM sections is confusing and hinders readability.
    *   Figure 1 is missing axis labels, making it difficult to interpret. The caption should also be more explicit that SBM results are not plotted because the stability condition was never met.
    *   There is a mismatch between the PFM theory (which bounds weighted ME) and the experiment (which perturbs standard ME).

**Questions and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  Could the authors provide a clearer rationale for using the Laplacian for PFM and the adjacency matrix for SBM? How do the resulting bounds (wME vs. ME) compare?
2.  In Proposition 10, the inner product notation `<M̂, M>F` should be explicitly defined.
3.  In Assumption 5, what is the intuition behind bounding the norm of the difference of the *squared* Laplacians, `||L̂² - L²||F`?
4.  How does the stability concept proposed here relate to the center-based stability notions in the work of Balcan et al.?
5.  Could the techniques be extended to other models where spectral clustering is known to work, such as in hierarchical clustering (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2011)?
6.  The statement on lines 252-253 ("large eigengap makes PFM(G, C) differ...") needs further qualification, especially in light of the seemingly contradictory result on the political blogs data. Can a more detailed analysis be provided?

In summary, while the paper introduces a novel and promising direction for model-free cluster validation, the current execution has several weaknesses. The methodological choices lack clear justification, the connection to important related work is missing, and the experimental results suggest the derived bounds may be too loose for practical use.
