The authors introduce novel theoretical findings on graph clustering, distinct from existing methods in that they do not rely on specific generative models for the observed graph. The core concept presented is that if a graph clustering method successfully identifies a clustering that reasonably fits the graph (based on a particular definition), then any alternative clustering that fits the graph equally well should be similar to the initial one. This paper tackles a significant problem in the field and provides valuable, distribution-free results for graph clustering. Despite some content being relegated to supplementary materials due to page limitations, the paper remains clear and comprehensible without requiring reference to this additional content. One minor criticism is the use of "hats" to denote actual observations (e.g., \hat{L} for the observed Laplacian), which may not be the most effective choice. Nevertheless, the paper is generally well-written, and the results are interesting, well-commented, and effectively illustrated. However, these results also have some limitations. As demonstrated in the experimental section, some real-world data requires graph smoothing to recover certain structures (as defined by the authors' theoretical framework), whereas many classical algorithms can recover the ground truth structure without such preprocessing. Two primary limitations of the work are: 1) the main result for PFM relies on a non-standard distance measure between clusterings, which the authors argue may be more natural than the classical measure, although this is debatable. 2) The results are based on a "proxy" model, where a clustering is used to reconstruct a model (e.g., a PFM), which is then compared to the original graph (rather than the model used to obtain the clustering). While this approach is natural in the authors' context, it also restricts the notion of a good clustering for a given graph, implying that the graph should be well-approximated in the normalized Laplacian sense. Although this restriction is necessary to obtain concrete results, it is essential to consider the impact of these constraints, particularly in light of the limitations observed in some real-world examples.
