- Presents distribution-free theoretical guarantees for graph clustering: if one partition fits the graph well, any other equally good partition must be close to it.  
- Central idea and results are interesting, important, and provide novel guarantees without assuming a generative model (e.g., SBM).  
- Paper is clear and largely self-contained despite some material being moved to the supplement.  
- Minor notational concern: placing “hats” on observed quantities (e.g., \hat{L}) is unconvincing.  
- Results are well-motivated, carefully discussed, and supported by illustrative examples.  
- Empirical limitation: some real-world graphs require smoothing before structure is recoverable under the theory, while classical algorithms can recover structure without smoothing.  
- Major limitation 1: main theorem uses a nonstandard clustering distance that is debatable and needs stronger justification or comparison to classical metrics.  
- Major limitation 2: analysis uses a proxy-model approach (reconstructing a model from a clustering) rather than comparing the model that produced the clustering, which restricts what counts as a “good” clustering and hinges on normalized Laplacian approximation.  
- Overall: thought-provoking and clearly written with important distribution-free theory, but practical impact is tempered by the nonstandard distance, proxy-model restriction, and empirical smoothing requirements.
