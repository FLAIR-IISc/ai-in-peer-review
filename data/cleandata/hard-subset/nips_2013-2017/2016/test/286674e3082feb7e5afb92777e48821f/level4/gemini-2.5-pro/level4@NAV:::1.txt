The authors present novel theoretical findings for graph clustering that are distribution-free; that is, they do not rely on assumptions about the graph's generative process, such as the stochastic block model. The central premise of the work is that if a clustering method discovers a partition that fits the observed graph well (by a certain definition), any other partition that achieves a similarly good fit must be structurally close to the initial one. This is a very compelling paper that tackles a crucial problem by providing model-agnostic results for graph clustering.

The paper is well-written and largely self-contained, remaining comprehensible even though some material has been deferred to the supplementary section to meet page limits. A minor point on presentation: the use of "hat" notation for observed quantities, such as \hat{L} for the empirical Laplacian, is a slightly unconventional choice, but this does not detract from the paper's overall clarity.

The theoretical results themselves are significant and insightful, and they are effectively supported by commentary and illustrations. The framework, however, is subject to some caveats. The experimental section shows that on certain real-world datasets, the graph must be smoothed before a recoverable structure (in the sense defined by the authors' theory) can be found, whereas many standard algorithms can identify the ground-truth structure on the raw data.

Furthermore, and perhaps more critically, two other limitations are apparent. First, the main result concerning the PFM model employs a non-standard distance for comparing clusterings. The authors' argument that this measure is more natural than traditional ones is debatable. Second, the theoretical results are founded on a form of "proxy" model. Specifically, from a given clustering, a model (e.g., a PFM) is reconstructed, and this reconstructed model is then compared to the original graph, rather than comparing the clustering to an underlying ground-truth model. While this approach is a natural consequence of the distribution-free setting, it also strongly constrains the notion of a "good" clustering to one where the graph is well-approximated in the normalized Laplacian sense. Such restrictions are necessary to obtain theoretical guarantees, but they raise questions about the broader impact and applicability of the framework, especially considering its performance limitations on some real-world examples.
