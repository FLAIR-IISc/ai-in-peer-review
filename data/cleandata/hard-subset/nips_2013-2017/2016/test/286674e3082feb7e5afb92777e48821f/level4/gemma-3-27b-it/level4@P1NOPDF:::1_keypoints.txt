- Paper presents new distribution-free theoretical results for graph clustering without assuming a generative model.  
- Core claim: if a clustering fits the observed graph well by a defined criterion, any other equally good clustering must be similar.  
- The work is interesting, addresses an important problem, and is clearly written and mostly self-contained despite moved supplementary content.  
- Minor stylistic complaint: using “hats” to denote observed quantities (e.g., \hat{L}) is slightly odd but not substantive.  
- Results are well-explained with illustrative examples.  
- Experiments show limitations: some real-world datasets require graph smoothing to recover structure under the theory, while classical algorithms can sometimes recover ground truth directly.  
- Limitation 1: the main PFM result uses a non-standard distance between clusterings, which the authors justify but that choice is debatable.  
- Limitation 2: the approach uses a “proxy” model reconstructed from a clustering and compared to the graph, rather than comparing the graph to the true generative model.  
- This proxy requirement effectively forces the definition of a “good” clustering to mean the graph is well-approximated in the normalized Laplacian sense, which may constrain applicability and interpretation.  
- It would be valuable to better understand how these restrictions impact broader applicability and real-world performance.
