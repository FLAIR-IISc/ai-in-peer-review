

The paper proposes a generic framework for deriving theoretical stability guarantees for clustering solutions that fit data well, measured by the distance between a clustering \(C\) and any other clustering \(C'\) that also fits the data. It demonstrates the template's application to two block models (PFM and SBM), with all guarantee quantities computable directly from data. It further leverages these stability results to establish exact recovery guarantees. However, simulations indicate the practical utility of these guarantees is limited, primarily due to the goodness-of-fit measure and theorem conditions.  

1.  The goal of providing model-free theoretical guarantees for clustering—where quantities depend solely on data and clustering outputs—is theoretically and practically significant. This is well-motivated.  
2.  PFM includes DC-SBM as a special case, which itself includes SBM. It is unclear why PFM and SBM are treated as distinct cases. Since spectral clustering performs well across all models, why not use a unified estimator? Specifically: (a) Why does PFM use the Laplacian while SBM uses the adjacency matrix? (b) Why are PFM results presented in terms of *weighted* modularity error (ME) while SBM uses standard ME? How do these error metrics compare? (c) Why are results for DC-SBM omitted?  
3.  Proposition 10 references \(\langle M', M \rangle_F\). The definition of this inner product is unclear.  
4.  Assumption 5 states \(\| \hat{L}^2 - L^2 \|_F\) or \(\| \hat{L} - L \|_F^2\)? If the former, what is the intuition for squaring the Laplacians?  
5.  The framework focuses solely on disjoint clusters. Can the techniques extend beyond block models to other settings where spectral clustering succeeds, such as hierarchical clustering (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2011)? How would the template apply here?  
6.  The evaluation assumes \(C\) and \(C'\) have identical cluster counts. However, different estimators (e.g., based on eigenvalues of \(A\) vs. \(L(A)\)) may suggest different numbers of clusters (\(K\) vs. \(K+1\)), where \(C'\) splits one cluster of \(C\). Such \(C'\) remains close to \(C\) but falls outside the current framework.  
7.  The related work section omits key model-free clustering results: Kumar & Kannan (2010) on spectral norms and \(k\)-means, and Balcan et al. (2012, 2013) on perturbation and approximation stability. How does the stability notion here relate to these works, particularly regarding perturbation of cluster centers?  
8.  Simulations show PFM tolerates only minuscule perturbations (lines 252–253: "large eigengap makes PFM(G, C) differ from PFM(G, C₀) even for very small perturbations"). The claim lacks qualification. Conversely, smoothing the political blog data via \(A^p\) exaggerates eigenvalue gaps but *improves* stability—why? If small perturbations disrupt model fit, the guarantees lose practical relevance (a key selling point). Lines 248–250 note SBM conditions are "very hard to satisfy," which is problematic.  
9.  Figure 1 caption states "For SBM, \(\delta\) is always greater than \(\delta_0\)"—but SBM is not plotted in the figure. The caption must clarify this.  
10. Theorems for PFM use *weighted* ME, yet experiments perturb *standard* ME. This disconnect needs addressing.  

**Minor Points:**  
1.  Figure 1 axes lack labels.  
2.  Line 44: For block models, \(A_{ij}\) for \(i > j\) are independent given cluster assignments of \(i\) and \(j\).  
3.  Notation inconsistency: \(B\), \(C\), and \(Y\) have different definitions for PFM (Section 3.1) vs. SBM (Section 3.2). To avoid confusion: (a) Use \(Y^{\perp}\) and \(C_D\) instead of \(B\) and \(C\) for PFM; (b) For SBM, substitute \(YZ\) directly in Proposition 8; (c) Distinguish eigenvalues via \(\lambda^L\) (Laplacian) and \(\lambda^B\) (adjacency) in PFM/SBM.  
4.  Section 4.4 would better fit under "Related Work."
