

The authors present novel theoretical contributions to graph clustering that operate without assuming a specific generative model for the observed graph (e.g., not requiring the stochastic block model). The core insight establishes that if a clustering method identifies a reasonably fitting partition (under a defined criterion), then any other equally fitting clustering must be close to it. This work addresses a significant problem by delivering distribution-free results for graph clustering. Despite some content being deferred to supplementary materials due to page constraints, the paper remains accessible and well-written without requiring supplementary reading. The notation using "hats" (e.g., \hat{L} for the observed Laplacian) is somewhat unconventional but does not substantially hinder comprehension. The results are compelling, well-explained, and effectively illustrated. However, notable limitations exist:  
1) On real-world datasets, graph smoothing is necessary to recover structure within the authors' theoretical framework—whereas standard algorithms often recover ground truth directly.  
2) The primary result for PFM relies on a non-standard distance metric between clusterings; while the authors argue this metric is more natural, this claim is debatable.  
3) The framework employs a "proxy" model approach: a generative model (e.g., PFM) is reconstructed from the clustering and compared to the observed graph (rather than the original generative process). This significantly constrains the definition of a "good" clustering (requiring the graph to be well-approximated in the normalized Laplacian sense), which is essential for the results but raises questions about the framework's applicability given its limitations on real data.
