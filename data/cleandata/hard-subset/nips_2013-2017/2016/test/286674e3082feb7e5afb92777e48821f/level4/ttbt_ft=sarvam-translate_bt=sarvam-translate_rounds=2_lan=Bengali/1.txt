The authors have proposed new theoretical results on graph clustering that do not assume any origin of the graph under study (it is not assumed that the observed graph is generated according to a specific generative model, such as the Stochastic Block Model). The main idea of this paper is that if a graph clustering method can reasonably find a clustering that matches the graph (for a specific definition), then any other clustering that matches the graph well should be close to the first one. In my opinion, this paper is very interesting and discusses a very important problem by providing distribution-free results for graph clustering. Due to the limitation of the conference's page count, some content has been moved to supplementary materials, but the paper is quite clear and can be read without mentioning this additional content. I am not sure whether using "hats" in the actual observation (e.g., \hat{L} for the observed Laplacian) is a good choice, but apart from this detail, the paper is reasonably clear. The results themselves are very interesting and well commented on and illustrated. However, they have some limitations. As shown in the experimental section, in some real-world data, the graph needs to be smoothed to recover some structure (in the sense of the theoretical structure created by the authors), whereas many classical algorithms are capable of recovering the ground-truth structure. Also, and perhaps more importantly, two limitations are observed: 1) The main result of the PFM uses a non-standard distance measure within the clustering. The authors argue that this measure can be seen as more natural than the classical measure, but this is questionable. 2) The results are based on a "proxy" model, in the sense that from a clustering, a model (such as a PFM) is reconstructed and this model is compared to the original graph (instead of the model used for the clustering). This is very natural in the context chosen by the authors, but it also has the effect of strongly limiting the idea of what is a good clustering for a specific graph (here, this means that the graph is well approximated in the sense of a normal Laplacian). This is certainly necessary to get the actual results, but one might wonder what the impact of these limitations is, especially considering the structure limitations on some real-world examples.
