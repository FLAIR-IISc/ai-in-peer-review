The authors propose novel theoretical results on graph clustering that eschew assumptions regarding the generation of the graph under study, as the observed graph is not assumed to have been generated according to a specific generative model, such as the stochastic block model. The central idea of the paper is that if a graph clustering method can discover a clustering that reasonably fits the graph (according to a certain definition), then any other clustering that fits the graph equally well should be close to the first one. This paper addresses a crucial problem in my opinion, providing distribution-free results for graph clustering, and is very interesting. Although some content has been relegated to the supplementary materials due to the conference's page number restrictions, the paper is quite clear and can be read without referring to this additional content. I am not convinced, however, that the use of "hats" to denote actual observations (e.g., \hat{L} for the observed Laplacian) is a good choice, but apart from this detail, the paper is reasonably clear. The results themselves are very interesting, well commented, and illustrated. Nevertheless, they are not without limitations. As demonstrated in the experimental section, on some real-world data, the graph must be smoothed in order for some structure to be recoverable (in the sense of the theoretical framework developed by the authors), whereas many classical algorithms can recover the ground truth structure. Furthermore, two limitations are apparent: 1) the main result for PFM employs a non-standard distance measure between clusterings, which the authors argue might be seen as more natural than the classical one, although this is debatable. 2) The results are based on a "proxy" model, where a clustering is used to reconstruct a model (e.g., a PFM), which is then compared to the original graph (rather than the model used to obtain the clustering). While this approach is natural in the context chosen by the authors, it also has the effect of strongly constraining the notion of what constitutes a good clustering for a given graph (i.e., the graph being well approximated in the normalized Laplacian sense). Although this is necessary to obtain actual results, one may wonder about the impact of these restrictions, particularly considering the limitations faced by the framework on some real-world examples.
