The authors propose novel theoretical results on graph clustering that eschew assumptions regarding the generation of the graph under study. Unlike existing methods, the observed graph is not assumed to have been generated according to a specific generative model, such as the stochastic block model. The primary contribution of the paper is the idea that if a graph clustering method can discover a clustering that reasonably fits the graph (according to a specific definition), then any other clustering that fits the graph equally well should be close to the first one. This paper addresses a crucial problem by providing distribution-free results for graph clustering, making it a very interesting read.

The authors have successfully adapted their content to fit the conference's page number restriction by moving some sections to the supplementary materials. Nevertheless, the paper remains clear and can be understood without referring to the additional content. One minor point of contention is the use of "hats" to denote actual observations (e.g., \hat{L} for the observed Laplacian), which may not be the most effective notation choice. However, apart from this detail, the paper is reasonably clear.

The results presented in the paper are intriguing, well-commented, and effectively illustrated. Nevertheless, they are not without limitations. As demonstrated in the experimental section, some real-world data requires graph smoothing for structure recovery (in the sense of the theoretical framework developed by the authors), whereas many classical algorithms can recover the ground truth structure. Two primary limitations are evident: 

Firstly, the main result for PFM employs a non-standard distance measure between clusterings. Although the authors argue that this measure might be more natural than the classical one, this assertion is debatable. Secondly, the results rely on a "proxy" model, where a clustering is used to reconstruct a model (e.g., a PFM), which is then compared to the original graph (rather than the model used to obtain the clustering). While this approach is natural in the context chosen by the authors, it also constrains the notion of what constitutes a good clustering for a given graph. Specifically, this means that the graph is well-approximated in the normalized Laplacian sense. Although this constraint is necessary to obtain meaningful results, it raises questions about the impact of these restrictions, particularly in light of the limitations faced by the framework on some real-world examples.

In conclusion, the authors have made significant contributions to the field of graph clustering by providing novel theoretical results that do not rely on specific generative models. While the paper is well-written and the results are intriguing, the limitations and constraints of the framework should be carefully considered, especially when evaluating its performance on real-world data.
