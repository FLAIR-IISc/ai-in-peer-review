The paper presents a generic template for theoretical guarantees of the stability of a clustering C that fits the data well. The stability is measured in terms of the distance between C and any other C' that fit the same data well. The paper also provides specific examples of using the template for two block models, PFM and SBM, and all the quantities involved in the guarantees can be computed explicitly from the data. Furthermore, it also uses the stability results thus obtained to show exact recovery guarantees for the models. However, the simulations suggest that either the goodness of fit used or the conditions of the theorems provided render the results not too useful.

The paper considers the problem of providing a generic template for theoretical guarantees for clustering without assuming that the data is from a specific model. The goal is to obtain such guarantees with quantities that can be computed from the data and the output of the clustering algorithms being compared. Providing such model-free theoretical guarantees for clustering is of importance for both theoretical and practical purposes.

Given that PFM includes DC-SBM as a special class, which in turn includes SBM, it is not clear why the two cases (PFM and SBM) are treated differently. Spectral Clustering works well for all the models specified, so it is unclear why the Laplacian is used for PFM while the adjacency matrix is used for SBM. Moreover, the results for PFM are in terms of weighted ME, whereas for SBM, they are in terms of ME. A comparison between these two measures would be beneficial. Additionally, the results for DC-SBM are not provided, which would have been a valuable addition.

Several clarifications are needed in the paper. For instance, in Proposition 10, the definition of < M', M >F is not provided. In Assumption 5, it is unclear whether the norm is ||L^{hat}^2 - L^2||F or ||L^{hat} - L||F^2. If the former is the case, the intuition behind squaring the Laplacians is not evident.

The paper primarily focuses on disjoint clusters. It is unclear whether the techniques used in this paper extend beyond block models to other models for which Spectral Clustering works. For example, Spectral clustering is known to work well for hierarchical clustering, and there are theoretical guarantees available ("Noise Thresholds for Spectral Clustering", S. Balakrishnan et al., 2011). It would be beneficial to discuss how the template would work for this case.

The evaluation metric considered, as well as the estimators considered in both the cases of PFM and SBM, are limited to C and C' having the same number of clusters. However, different clustering algorithms can potentially give different numbers of clusters. For instance, the eigenvalues of A might suggest K clusters for C, while the eigenvalues of the Laplacian of A might suggest K' = K + 1 clusters for C', such that all the clusters in C and C' are the same except for one cluster that splits into two. C' is still "close" to C, but it cannot be captured by the given framework.

The related work section does not reflect a lot of model-free results that exist. For example, "Clustering with Spectral Norm and the k-means Algorithm" (Kumar and Kannan, 2010), "Clustering under Perturbation Resilience" (Balcan et al., 2012), and "Clustering under Approximation Stability" (Balcan et al., 2013) are not mentioned. It would be beneficial to discuss how the notion of stability considered in this work relates to those in these works. In particular, how is the stability in this paper related to the stability measured for perturbation of centers for two clusterings?

The perturbations tolerated by simulations for PFM are too small. The statement in lines 252-253 that "large eigengap makes PFM(G, C) differ from PFM(G, C0) even for very small perturbations" is not adequately qualified. It is unclear why this is the case. On the other hand, the political blog data is smoothed out by raising the power of A, which would further exaggerate the gaps in eigenvalues, but in this case, it seems to help stability. However, if such small perturbations can affect the model fit, then it renders the guarantee not of much use for practical purposes, which is one of the main selling points since the quantities can be actually calculated from the data. Furthermore, the conditions for SBM guarantees seem to be very hard to satisfy, which is unfortunate.

There are several minor points that need to be addressed. The caption for Figure 1 mentions "For SBM, \delta is always greater than \delta0". However, it is unclear which subplot this refers to. If SBM is not plotted, then it should be made clear in the caption. The guarantees in the theorems for PFM are for weighted ME, whereas in the experiment, the perturbation is done for ME. Labels for axes in Figure 1 are missing. The statement in line 44 that "For block models, A{ij} for i > j are independent given the cluster assignments for i and j" could be rephrased for clarity. The use of different notations for the same variables (e.g., B, C, and Y) in different sections of the paper can be confusing. It would be better to maintain consistency throughout the paper.
