This paper presents a general theoretical framework for guaranteeing the stability of a clustering C that provides a good fit to the data. Stability is measured by the distance between C and any other clustering C' that also fits the data well. The authors apply this framework to two specific block models, the Preferential Friendship Model (PFM) and the Stochastic Block Model (SBM), demonstrating that the quantities in the theoretical guarantees can be computed directly from the data. These stability results are then used to derive guarantees for exact recovery.

The primary contribution of this work is the development of a model-agnostic template for clustering guarantees, with quantities that are empirically computable. This is a valuable goal for both theoretical and practical purposes. However, the experimental results and some theoretical choices raise concerns about the practical utility of the proposed framework.

**Major Concerns:**

1.  **Inconsistent Treatment of PFM and SBM:**
    Given that SBM is a special case of DC-SBM, which is in turn a special case of PFM, the separate and different treatment of PFM and SBM is confusing.
    *   **Choice of Estimator:** Why is the graph Laplacian used for the PFM analysis, while the adjacency matrix is used for the SBM analysis, especially when spectral clustering is effective for both? A unified approach would be more compelling.
    *   **Error Metric:** The theoretical results for PFM are for the weighted Misclassification Error (ME), whereas for SBM, they are for the standard ME. The authors should clarify how these two metrics compare and justify the different choices.
    *   **Missing Results:** Why are results for the more general DC-SBM not provided?

2.  **Practical Utility and Simulation Results:**
    The simulations suggest that the conditions required by the theorems may be too restrictive for practical application, which undermines one of the paper's key selling points.
    *   The perturbations tolerated by the PFM model in the simulations are very small. The paper states that a "large eigengap makes PFM(G, C) differ from PFM(G, C0) even for very small perturbations" but does not provide a clear explanation for this sensitivity. This fragility could render the guarantee impractical.
    *   Conversely, for the political blog data, the adjacency matrix is raised to a power, which would typically exaggerate the eigengap. In this case, it appears to improve stability. This seems contradictory to the previous point and requires further explanation.
    *   The paper acknowledges that the conditions for the SBM guarantee "seem to be very hard to satisfy" (Lines 248-250), which is a significant weakness.

3.  **Scope and Limitations of the Framework:**
    *   **Fixed Number of Clusters:** The proposed framework and evaluation metrics are limited to comparing clusterings C and C' that have the same number of clusters. This is a significant limitation, as different algorithms or parameter choices (e.g., inspecting the eigenvalues of the Laplacian vs. the adjacency matrix) can yield different numbers of clusters. For instance, a clustering C' might be very close to C, differing only by splitting one cluster into two. The current framework cannot capture this notion of similarity.
    *   **Extension to Other Models:** The paper focuses on block models. It would be beneficial to discuss whether the proposed template can be extended to other structures where spectral clustering is known to be effective, such as in hierarchical clustering (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2011).

4.  **Comparison to Related Work:**
    The related work section does not sufficiently engage with existing literature on model-free clustering stability. For example, works like "Clustering with Spectral Norm and the k-means Algorithm" (Kumar and Kannan, 2010), "Clustering under Perturbation Resilience" (Balcan et al., 2012), and "Clustering under Approximation Stability" (Balcan et al., 2013) provide relevant notions of stability. The authors should explicitly compare their definition of stability to the concepts in these papers, particularly how it relates to stability under perturbations of cluster centers.

**Questions and Points for Clarification:**

1.  **Notation and Assumptions:**
    *   In Proposition 10, the definition of the inner product `<M', M>F` is not provided.
    *   In Assumption 5, is the term `||L̂² - L²||F` or `||L̂ - L||F²`? If it is the former, the authors should provide some intuition for squaring the Laplacians before taking the difference.

2.  **Experiments and Figures:**
    *   There is a mismatch between the theory for PFM, which provides guarantees for weighted ME, and the experiments, which measure perturbation in terms of standard ME.
    *   The caption for Figure 1 mentions, "For SBM, δ is always greater than δ0." It is unclear which subplot this refers to, or if SBM results are plotted at all. This should be clarified.
    *   The axes in Figure 1 are not labeled.

**Minor Points:**

*   **(Line 44):** The description of block models should clarify that `Aij` for `i > j` are conditionally independent given the cluster assignments of nodes `i` and `j`.
*   **Notation Consistency:** The symbols B, C, Y, and λ are used with different meanings for PFM and SBM, which can be confusing. To improve clarity, consider using distinct notation, such as `Y⊥` and `CD` for PFM, and `λ^L` and `λ^A` to distinguish eigenvalues of the Laplacian and adjacency matrix, respectively. In Proposition 8, the expression for YZ could be stated directly to avoid introducing unnecessary notation.
*   **Structure:** Section 4.4, which compares the framework to other approaches, would be better integrated into the main Related Work section.
