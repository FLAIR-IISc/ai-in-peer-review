- Paper provides distribution-free, stability-style guarantees: if a clustering fits the observed graph well, any other good-fitting clustering must be close, yielding identifiability from one graph.
- Results developed for specific models (e.g., PFM) with empirical evaluation; paper is largely self-contained and readable.
- Strength: fills gap between model-based theory and practice by offering distribution-free guarantees for graph clustering.
- Strength: clear conceptual framing—“good fit implies uniqueness up to small perturbations.”
- Strength: technically substantial, well-motivated results with illustrative examples.
- Strength: overall clear exposition aside from a few notational choices.
- Concern: main PFM guarantee uses a nonstandard clustering distance, complicating comparisons to prior work.
- Request: relate the proposed distance to standard metrics (misclassification error, VI, ARI) and show examples where it differs usefully.
- Concern: analysis uses a proxy-model fit (e.g., reconstructed PFM via normalized Laplacian), which tightly constrains what counts as “good” clustering.
- Questions: practical scope of the proxy for graphs with degree heterogeneity, hubs, heavy tails; sensitivity to misspecification; whether other fit notions (modularity/cut-based) could fit the framework.
- Concern: empirical gap—some real graphs require smoothing for the theory to detect structure while standard algorithms do not.
- Request: clarify when/why smoothing is necessary, how parameters are chosen, sensitivity analyses, and ablations over smoothing/regularization/sparsity.
- Presentation issues: unconventional use of hats for observed quantities, need clearer pointers to supplement lemmas, and an early, precise definition of “fits the graph reasonably well.”
- Requests for further analysis: formal/theoretical and empirical comparisons of the distance metric; practical checks for the sufficient fit conditions on single graphs; robustness analysis across sparsity and degree heterogeneity.
- Overall: valuable and timely theoretical contribution emphasizing uniqueness of well-fitting clusterings, but requires stronger justification for the distance choice, clearer scope for the proxy fit, and more empirical diagnostics—reviewer leans toward acceptance if addressed.
