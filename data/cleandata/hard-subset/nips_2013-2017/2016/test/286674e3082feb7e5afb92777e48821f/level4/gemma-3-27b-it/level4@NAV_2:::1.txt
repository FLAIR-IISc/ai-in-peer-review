The authors propose new theoretical results on graph clustering that do not require assumptions about the graph’s generative model. The central idea is that if a clustering method finds a clustering that fits the graph well (according to a defined criterion), then any other clustering that fits equally well must be close to the first. This paper addresses an important problem by providing distribution-free results for graph clustering and is, in my opinion, very interesting. While some content has been moved to supplementary materials due to page limits, the paper remains clear and accessible without reference to those materials. I am not entirely convinced by the use of “hats” to denote observed quantities (e.g., \hat{L} for the observed Laplacian), but this is a minor clarity issue. The results are well-explained and illustrated. 

However, the results are not without limitations. As demonstrated in the experimental section, recovering structure within the theoretical framework often requires smoothing the graph, whereas many classical algorithms can recover ground truth structure directly. Furthermore, two limitations are apparent: 1) the main result for the Preference Frame Model (PFM) utilizes a non-standard distance measure between clusterings. While the authors argue its potential as a more natural measure, this remains debatable. 2) The results rely on a “proxy” model approach: a model (e.g., a PFM) is reconstructed from a clustering and then compared to the original graph, rather than comparing directly to the true generative model. This is a natural choice within the authors’ context but strongly constrains the definition of a “good” clustering – here, requiring a close approximation in the normalized Laplacian sense. This is necessary to derive results, but the impact of such restrictions, especially considering limitations observed on real-world examples, warrants consideration.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novelty: The work provides a novel framework for model-free clustering guarantees, a significant departure from traditional model-based approaches.
*   Theoretical Soundness: The theorems are mathematically well-defined and supported by technical proofs (some in the supplement).
*   Significance: The problem addressed—obtaining guarantees without model assumptions—is fundamental and practically relevant.
*   Clarity: The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the core ideas and results.
*   Connections to Existing Work: The authors effectively connect their model-free framework to existing model-based results, strengthening both.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Practicality: The applicability of the framework, particularly the smoothness requirement observed in experiments, is questionable for some real-world datasets.
*   Distance Measure: The use of a non-standard distance measure for PFM may limit broader adoption and comparison.
*   Proxy Model Assumption: The reliance on reconstructing a model from a clustering introduces a restrictive assumption that may not always hold.
*   Conservatism: The theorems may be overly conservative, requiring strong conditions for guarantees.



**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, although the theoretical results come with some caveats regarding their practical applicability. The authors demonstrate careful consideration of both strengths and weaknesses.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally clearly written and well-organized, though the notation can be dense at times.

**Originality:** The approach of obtaining model-free clustering guarantees is novel and represents a significant contribution.

**Significance:** The results have the potential to advance the field of graph clustering by providing a framework for validating clustering results without relying on strong model assumptions. However, the practical impact remains to be seen.
