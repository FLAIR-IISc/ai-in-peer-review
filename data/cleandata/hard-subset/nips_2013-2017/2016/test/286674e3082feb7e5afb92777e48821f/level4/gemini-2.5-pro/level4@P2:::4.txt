This paper introduces a framework for providing model-free theoretical guarantees for the stability of graph clustering. The core idea is that if a given clustering `C` provides a good fit to the observed graph data under a specific model class (e.g., PFM or SBM), then any other clustering `C'` that also fits the data well must be structurally close to `C`. The authors provide specific instantiations of this general theorem for the Preference Frame Model (PFM) and the Stochastic Block Model (SBM). A key contribution is that the quantities involved in these theoretical guarantees are fully computable from the data, without requiring knowledge of a true generative process or unknown constants.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Importance:** The problem of providing model-free validation for clustering is of significant practical and theoretical importance. Moving beyond the common assumption that the data is perfectly generated by a specific model makes theoretical guarantees much more relevant to real-world applications. The proposed stability-based framework is a novel and valuable approach to this problem within the context of block models.

2.  **Computable Guarantees:** A major strength of this work is that the derived bounds are entirely data-dependent and computable. This is a significant advantage over many theoretical results that rely on unknown parameters of a data-generating distribution or contain unspecified constants, making them difficult to apply in practice.

3.  **Elegant Framework:** The conceptual framework is intuitive and elegant. It cleverly repurposes the logic of model-based recovery theorems to serve a model-free validation purpose, centering the guarantees on a verifiable goodness-of-fit criterion rather than an unverifiable model assumption.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

1.  **Practical Utility of the Bounds:** A major concern is that the experimental results seem to undermine the practical utility of the derived guarantees. The conditions for the SBM guarantee were reportedly never satisfied in the experiments, even for data generated from a similar model (LFR benchmark). For the PFM, the guarantees of stability only hold for extremely small perturbations from the initial clustering. This suggests that the derived bounds may be too loose to be informative in many practical scenarios, which conflicts with the paper's primary goal of providing useful, data-dependent validation. The authors' explanation that a "large eigengap" is responsible for sensitivity to small perturbations seems counter-intuitive, as a large eigengap typically implies a more stable cluster structure.

2.  **Clarity and Technical Justification:** The paper's clarity could be improved with better justification for certain technical choices.
    *   The PFM and SBM cases are treated differently without a clear rationale. The analysis for PFM is based on the graph Laplacian and weighted Misclassification Error (wME), while the SBM analysis uses the adjacency matrix and standard ME. Given that SBM is a special case of PFM, it is unclear why a unified approach was not used or why these specific choices were made for each model.
    *   The motivation for some assumptions, like using the squared Laplacians `||L̂² - L²||F` in Assumption 5, is not explained.
    *   The reuse of notation (e.g., matrices B, C, Y) for different purposes across the PFM and SBM sections creates unnecessary confusion. Consistent and distinct notation would significantly improve readability.

3.  **Limited Scope of the Framework:** The framework's applicability is limited by its core assumptions.
    *   It requires comparing clusterings `C` and `C'` that have the same number of clusters, K. This is a significant practical limitation, as a common task is to compare clustering results with different numbers of clusters or to assess solutions where one cluster is a refinement (e.g., a split) of another.
    *   The paper focuses exclusively on block models. It would be beneficial to discuss whether the general framework could be extended to other models for which spectral clustering is effective, such as those with hierarchical structure (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2011).

4.  **Insufficient Engagement with Related Work:** The related work section would be substantially stronger if it situated the paper's notion of stability within the broader literature on this topic. The manuscript overlooks several key papers on clustering stability and model-free analysis. A discussion comparing the proposed stability notion to concepts like perturbation resilience (Balcan et al., 2012), approximation stability (Balcan et al., 2013), and spectral norm-based clustering (Kumar and Kannan, 2010) would better highlight the specific contributions and novelty of this work.

**Questions and Minor Points**

*   In Proposition 10, the notation `<M̂, M>F` appears to be the Frobenius inner product, but this should be explicitly defined.
*   The figures are difficult to interpret. Figure 1 is missing axis labels. The caption states that for SBM, `δ > δ0` always, but it is not clear if the SBM results are plotted or simply omitted. This should be clarified.
*   In the PFM experiments, the theoretical guarantees are derived for the weighted ME, but the perturbations are applied and measured using the standard (unweighted) ME. This mismatch should be addressed or justified.
*   Section 4.4 ("Clusterability and resilience") seems more appropriate as a subsection within the introduction or related work, as it discusses the conceptual implications of the framework rather than presenting new results.
