Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a model-free framework for validating graph clusterings. The main idea is: given a model class M (e.g., SBM, DC-SBM, PFM) and a clustering C of an observed graph G, construct a model M(G, C) consistent with C and check how well it fits G via a computable goodness-of-fit measure. If C fits G well, the paper proves that any other clustering C′ that also fits G well must be close to C (in misclassification error). This provides distribution-free “uniqueness up to perturbations” guarantees that depend only on observable quantities (e.g., spectral norms and eigengaps).

The framework is instantiated for PFM (the main result) and for SBM, with explicit algorithms to construct M(G, C), concrete goodness-of-fit conditions (e.g., ||L̂ − L|| ≤ ε for PFM, ||Â − A|| ≤ ε for SBM), and resulting bounds of the form dist(C, C′) ≤ f(ε, eigengap, cluster balance). The proofs rely on spectral perturbation tools (Davis–Kahan angles, subspace alignment), and the results are connected to model-based recovery guarantees in prior work (e.g., Rohe–Chatterjee–Yu 2011; Qin–Rohe 2013; Wan–Meilă 2016). Experiments on synthetic PFM, perturbed PFM, LFR graphs, and a political blogs network illustrate when the conditions are informative; for some real data, smoothing improves fit and yields guarantees.

Strengths
- Clear, principled framework for model-free validation: if a fitted model explains the data, the induced clustering is locally unique among all clusterings that explain the data equally well.
- Instantiations for PFM and SBM with fully computable quantities (no hidden constants), making the results actionable.
- Elegant and relatively simple proof strategy via subspace perturbation (Davis–Kahan), with a clean link from closeness of fitted models to closeness of clusterings.
- Meaningful perspective: turns existing model-based recovery results into model-free guarantees; connects to stability/clusterability literature.
- Experimental evidence that the bounds can be informative and that smoothing can stabilize structure.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Dependence on a proxy model and metric: the definition of “fit” is tied to the chosen model class and norm (e.g., spectral norm of Laplacians for PFM). This restricts what counts as a “good” clustering and may miss structure that standard algorithms recover under different criteria.
- Distance between clusterings: the main PFM theorem uses a degree-weighted misclassification error. While the authors motivate it (natural under degree heterogeneity, earth-mover interpretation), it is less standard than unweighted ME; implications for practitioners and conversion between the two could be clarified.
- Sensitivity to eigengap and cluster balance: guarantees require nontrivial eigengaps and balanced cluster volumes (δ ≤ δ0), which may fail for real graphs. The SBM bounds in particular are often too loose to be informative in experiments.
- Empirical scope: while synthetic and benchmark datasets are helpful, the need to smooth the political blogs network to obtain guarantees raises questions about how broadly the goodness-of-fit conditions hold in practice.
- DC-SBM instantiation is not fully developed in the main text (PFM subsumes it, but an explicit DC-SBM theorem would help bridge between SBM and PFM).

Quality
The technical development appears sound. The estimator for PFM is carefully constructed via subspace alignment and comes with consistency intuition; the perturbation bounds are standard and well applied. Assumptions and constants are explicit and quantities are data-computable. Some proofs are deferred to the supplement but the main ideas are sufficiently presented.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. Minor notation choices (e.g., placing hats on observed quantities) may be slightly unconventional; consider simplifying notation and emphasizing at first use what is observable versus fitted. It would help to summarize the main theorems with their key dependencies (ε, eigengap, balance) in a compact box or figure.

Originality
The framing of model-free validation via fitted proxy models and spectral perturbation is original for block models (SBM/DC-SBM/PFM). The explicit link that repurposes model-based recovery proofs for model-free guarantees is novel and insightful.

Significance
The work advances our ability to certify the stability/uniqueness of a clustering without assuming a generative model, a frequent pain point in applications. While current bounds can be conservative (especially for SBM), the framework is likely to inspire further refinements and could become a standard tool for cluster validation in networks.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a clear discussion of when degree-weighted ME and unweighted ME are interchangeable, and include experimental curves for both (or bounds translating between them under degree regularity assumptions).
- Quantify sensitivity to eigengap and cluster imbalance; e.g., report how δ scales as a function of the empirical gap and balance, and provide diagnostics for practitioners.
- Add a DC-SBM corollary in the main text to better position the results between SBM and PFM.
- Clarify the role and effect of smoothing: when is smoothing justified, and how does it change the goodness-of-fit quantities and the interpretability of the guarantee?
- Discuss robustness to K mis-specification and whether the framework can flag over/under-clustering.
- Expand on negative guarantees (when no clustering can be stable under given ε) and on extensions to Frobenius-norm conditions where appropriate.

Arguments for acceptance
- Provides a practical, data-driven framework for model-free validation with computable, interpretable quantities.
- Technically sound, leverages standard spectral perturbation tools effectively.
- Conceptually bridges model-based recovery and model-free guarantees; likely to influence future work.

Arguments against acceptance
- Guarantees can be weak in common settings (e.g., SBM on real networks), limiting practical impact.
- Relies on a specific notion of fit tied to a proxy model; may not align with successful heuristic methods.
- Experimental evidence, while suggestive, is limited in scope and requires smoothing to obtain guarantees on a real dataset.

Overall evaluation
A thoughtful and technically solid contribution that opens a promising direction in model-free validation for graph clustering. Despite some limitations in empirical tightness and reliance on weighted misclassification, the framework and results are novel and useful. I lean toward acceptance, with revisions addressing the clarity and empirical scope concerns.
