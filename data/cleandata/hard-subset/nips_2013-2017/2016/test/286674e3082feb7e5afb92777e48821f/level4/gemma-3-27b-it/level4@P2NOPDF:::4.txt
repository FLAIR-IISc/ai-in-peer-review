This paper presents a general template for providing theoretical stability guarantees for clustering, measured as the distance between clusterings that both well-fit the data. The framework aims to provide data-driven guarantees, computing necessary quantities directly from data and algorithm outputs. The authors demonstrate the template’s application to two block models – the Planted Feature Model (PFM) and the Stochastic Block Model (SBM) – deriving explicit recovery guarantees. While the approach is conceptually appealing, the practical utility of the resulting guarantees is questionable, as suggested by the simulations.

The motivation for a model-agnostic stability framework for clustering is strong, with potential benefits for both theoretical understanding and practical applications. However, several aspects of the paper’s execution raise concerns. The treatment of PFM and SBM feels inconsistent, given that PFM encompasses both DC-SBM and SBM. The differing model estimators employed – the Laplacian for PFM versus the adjacency matrix for SBM – is not adequately justified. Furthermore, the guarantees for PFM are expressed in terms of weighted mutual information (ME), while those for SBM are in terms of unweighted ME; a comparison of these measures is absent. The lack of results for the DC-SBM model is also a notable omission.

Several technical points require clarification. In Proposition 10, the definition of <M’, M><sub>F</sub> is unclear. In Assumption 5, it is ambiguous whether the condition is ||L̂<sup>2</sup> - L<sup>2</sup>||<sub>F</sub> or ||L̂ - L||<sub>F</sub><sup>2</sup>, and the rationale for squaring the Laplacians in the former case should be explained. The current framework focuses exclusively on disjoint clusters. It would be valuable to explore the extensibility of the techniques to other clustering scenarios where Spectral Clustering performs well, such as hierarchical clustering, and to relate the template to existing theoretical guarantees (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2011).

A significant limitation of the approach is the restrictive constraint that compared clusterings must have the same number of clusters. Real-world scenarios often involve algorithms producing different numbers of clusters – for example, one algorithm suggesting *K* clusters while another proposes *K+1*, with only a single split occurring. This nuance is not captured by the current framework. 

The related work section is incomplete, failing to acknowledge relevant model-free clustering results such as Kumar & Kannan (2010), Balcan et al. (2012, 2013). A discussion of how the stability notion employed in this work relates to these existing definitions—particularly in the context of perturbation of cluster centers—is needed. 

The simulation results raise further doubts about the practical relevance of the guarantees. The perturbations tested for PFM appear to be unrealistically small, and the observation that “large eigengaps make PFM(G, C) differ from PFM(G, C<sub>0</sub>) even for very small perturbations” lacks sufficient justification. The smoothing applied to the political blog data, while seemingly improving stability, might artificially inflate eigenvalue gaps. Crucially, if small perturbations can significantly impact model fit, the resulting guarantees lose much of their practical value, undermining one of the paper's key selling points. The authors also acknowledge the difficulty in satisfying the SBM guarantee conditions. 

Specific details regarding figures and notation also need addressing. Figure 1’s caption requires clarification regarding the statement “For SBM, δ is always greater than δ<sub>0</sub>” – either by identifying the relevant subplots or removing the statement if SBM is not plotted. Axis labels are missing from Figure 1. Throughout the paper, the inconsistent use of notation (B, C, and Y) between the PFM and SBM sections is confusing and should be addressed by adopting more consistent variable names (e.g., Y<sub>⊥</sub> and C<sub>D</sub>). Furthermore, unnecessary variables like YZ in the SBM estimator can be simplified. Distinguishing between eigenvalues using superscripts (λ<sup>L</sup> and λ<sup>B</sup>) would improve clarity. Finally, Section 4.4 would be more appropriately positioned within the Related Work section.
