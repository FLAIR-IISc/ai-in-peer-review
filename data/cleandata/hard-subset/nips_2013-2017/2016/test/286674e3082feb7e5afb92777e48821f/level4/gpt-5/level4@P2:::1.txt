This paper proposes a framework for model-free guarantees in graph clustering. The core idea is to fit a model to the observed graph and the algorithm’s output clustering C, and then certify that any other clustering C′ that fits the data equally well must be close to C. The framework is instantiated for two model classes: PFM and SBM. For PFM, the goodness-of-fit is measured by the spectral norm between the observed and fitted Laplacians; for SBM, by the spectral norm between the observed and fitted adjacency matrices. The authors leverage perturbation results (Davis–Kahan) to derive bounds on the distance between clusterings, using a weighted misclassification error. They also show how their model-free guarantees connect to and, in some cases, sharpen existing model-based recovery results. Experiments illustrate that the quantities required by the bounds are computable from data, and that the PFM-based guarantees are more often applicable than the SBM-based ones; smoothing can help on real data.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on extensive literature on spectral clustering and block models (SBM, DC-SBM, PFM), and on classical subspace perturbation theory. Rather than assuming a generative model, it inverts the logic of recovery theorems to provide data-dependent, model-free stability guarantees. This perspective complements model-based recovery results for spectral clustering and connects to stability/clusterability lines of work.

Strengths
- Novel perspective: Provides distribution-free, data-dependent guarantees for clustering, avoiding assumptions on the data-generating process.
- General and computable: The criteria (e.g., eigengaps, spectral deviations) are directly computed from the observed graph and the output clustering.
- Theoretical contributions: Clear blueprint (Generic Theorem) instantiated for PFM and SBM; ties to existing results and improved constants in some cases.
- Exposition: Despite relegating proofs to the supplement, the main paper is readable and the technical choices are well motivated.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Distance measure: The main PFM result bounds a weighted misclassification error rather than the standard one. While the authors argue this is more natural when degrees vary, the choice is nonstandard and may complicate comparisons with prior work. Reporting parallel results (or conversion bounds) for the unweighted metric would strengthen the case.
- Proxy-model notion of fit: The guarantees hinge on constructing a fitted model M(G, C) from the observed graph and clustering, and declaring C good if M(G, C) is close to G (e.g., in normalized Laplacian). This is natural within the framework but imposes a specific notion of “good clustering” that may exclude structure recoverable by widely used algorithms under different criteria.
- Practical applicability: On some real data, meaningful guarantees require smoothing the graph, even when standard algorithms can recover accepted ground truth without smoothing. Clarifying when smoothing is justified and how it affects the semantics of communities would be helpful.
- Tightness for SBM: The SBM-based bounds appear loose in practice (often not informative), even on data believed to be block-structured. More discussion on whether this is inherent (model rigidity) or technical (proof slack) would be useful.
- Minor presentation detail: The pervasive use of “hats” for observed quantities (e.g., L̂) is unconventional and arguably distracting.

Quality
- The theoretical results appear sound and carefully stated, with appropriate use of perturbation theory and precise, computable conditions. The experimental evaluation is illustrative, though limited in showing when guarantees fail versus when they are informative.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized and clear. The supplement contains many proofs, but the main thread is understandable without it. A brief justification for the weighted misclassification distance and a short discussion comparing it to the standard metric would improve clarity.

Originality
- The shift to model-free certification via fitted proxy models is original and timely. The connection back to (and reuse of) model-based theorems to obtain distribution-free guarantees is a valuable conceptual contribution.

Significance
- If adopted, the framework provides practitioners with a way to certify the stability of a found clustering directly from data. While the PFM results seem practically useful, the limited strength of the SBM guarantees may restrict impact unless tightened.

Suggestions for improvement
- Report both weighted and unweighted misclassification bounds when possible, or provide interpretable relationships between them.
- Expand empirical evaluation on unsmoothed real graphs and characterize when smoothing is necessary; include sensitivity to eigengap and noise.
- Discuss conditions under which the guarantees are expected to be informative for SBM, or provide refined bounds tailored to sparse/heterogeneous regimes.
- Clarify the interpretability of “goodness-of-fit” via Laplacian/adjacency norms and how it aligns (or not) with commonly used clustering objectives.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important and underexplored question: certifying clustering quality without generative assumptions.
- Conceptually clean framework with computable, data-dependent guarantees and clear links to existing theory.
- Solid technical development and insightful discussion of limitations.

Arguments against acceptance
- Reliance on a nonstandard distance for the main PFM result and potentially restrictive notion of fit.
- Empirical evidence suggests limited applicability of the SBM guarantees and dependence on smoothing for real data.
- Some readers may find the proxy-model perspective less aligned with common evaluation practices.

Overall, this is a thoughtful and technically solid paper that advances the theory of cluster validation in networks from a model-free perspective. Despite limitations—particularly for SBM—I find the contribution significant and the exposition clear.
