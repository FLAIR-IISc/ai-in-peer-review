- Proposes a model-free validation framework: if a fitted block-model M(G,C) fits observed graph G well, then any other clustering C′ that also fits well must be close to C.  
- Instantiates framework for PFM and SBM with recovery guarantees (weighted misclassification for PFM, misclassification for SBM) using spectral perturbation (Davis–Kahan).  
- Bounds depend on data-driven quantities (eigengaps, spectral distances) and convert model-based theorems into model-free stability certificates.  
- Experiments on synthetic PFM, LFR benchmarks, and political blogs show when certificates certify stability and when they fail.  
- Novelty: first systematic “model-fit implies uniqueness” certification in block-model settings, linking model-based recovery to model-free guarantees.  
- Strength: clear, unifying conceptual framework with computable certificates.  
- Strength: technically clean spectral perturbation analysis with explicit eigengap/norm dependence and some improved bounds.  
- Strength: practical emphasis on estimable quantities and exploration of Laplacian smoothing for robustness.  
- Strength: broad applicability in principle to multiple block-model families.  
- Weakness: inconsistent treatments—PFM uses Laplacian and wME while SBM uses adjacency and ME; unclear if a single estimator/metric could unify results.  
- Weakness: missing explicit DC-SBM instantiation despite its importance and intermediate role.  
- Weakness: Proposition 10 lacks a precise definition of the Frobenius inner product ⟨M̂, M⟩F.  
- Weakness: Assumption 5 is unclear—whether it bounds ||L̂2−L2||F or ||L̂−L||2F—and why Laplacian squaring is used.  
- Weakness: unclear extension of techniques beyond block models (e.g., hierarchical clustering); asks for a sketch of instantiation in other spectral-guaranteed settings.  
- Weakness: framework assumes same K for C and C′; asks extension to merges/splits and metrics for unequal K (VI, earth-mover, refinement-aware).  
- Weakness: requests explicit comparison/connection to model-free stability literature (Kumar–Kannan k-means, perturbation resilience/approximation stability).  
- Weakness: practical tightness—PFM tolerates tiny perturbations and SBM conditions rarely hold; asks quantification of sensitivity to eigengaps and potential sharpening to reduce conservativeness.  
- Weakness: experimental inconsistency—PFM theorems use weighted ME but perturbations are measured in unweighted ME; needs alignment or justification.  
- Weakness: Figure 1 caption and plots confusing—missing SBM curves clarification and axis labels.  
- Theory quality: main arguments appear sound and tied to Davis–Kahan, but metric/estimator choices and some definitions need clearer justification and unification.  
- Experiments quality: informative but limited by tight/conservative conditions and metric mismatches; clarify figure labeling and metrics.  
- Clarity issues: notation overload and reuse across models (B, C, Y, λ); recommend model-specific symbols and precise definitions for inner products/norms and Assumption 5.  
- Originality: model-free certification approach for block models is original and connects model-based recovery to stability in a new way.  
- Significance: potentially significant as a validation tool if bounds can be relaxed and extended to DC-SBM and varying K; practical impact currently limited by stringent conditions.  
- Pros for acceptance: novel principled framework with computable certificates, clean spectral analysis, and potential to stimulate further work.  
- Cons for acceptance: conservative bounds limiting practicality, missing DC-SBM instantiation, metric/estimator inconsistencies, and clarity issues.  
- Minor: add axis labels and clearer Figure 1 caption; state independence of Aij given labels; avoid reusing symbols across models; consider moving Section 4.4 to Related Work.
