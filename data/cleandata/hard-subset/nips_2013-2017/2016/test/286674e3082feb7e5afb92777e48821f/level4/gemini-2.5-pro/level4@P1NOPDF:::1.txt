This paper proposes new, distribution-free theoretical results for graph clustering. This is an important contribution, as the framework does not assume the observed graph was generated by a specific probabilistic model, such as the stochastic block model. The core idea is that if a clustering method finds a partition that provides a good fit for the graph (according to a specific metric), then any other partition that fits the graph equally well must be structurally similar to the first.

The paper is well-written and clear. Despite space constraints requiring some content to be moved to the supplementary material, the main text is largely self-contained and understandable on its own. The theoretical results are compelling, well-motivated, and effectively illustrated. A minor point of criticism is the notational choice of using "hats" for observed quantities (e.g., \hat{L} for the observed Laplacian), which is unconventional and could be confusing.

However, the framework has some notable limitations. As shown in the experiments, on certain real-world datasets, the graph must first be smoothed for its structure to become recoverable within the authors' theoretical framework. In contrast, many classical algorithms can recover the ground-truth structure on the original, unsmoothed graph.

Furthermore, two more significant limitations should be considered:

1.  The main result for Planted Frequency Models (PFM) relies on a non-standard distance measure between clusterings. While the authors argue this measure is more natural than conventional ones, this claim is debatable and not fully substantiated.

2.  The results are based on a "proxy" model. Specifically, from a given clustering, a model (e.g., a PFM) is reconstructed, and this reconstructed model is then compared to the observed graph. This approach evaluates the clustering's fit to the graph, rather than its fidelity to any underlying generative model that might have produced it. While this is a natural consequence of the distribution-free setting, it strongly constrains the definition of a "good" clustering. In this framework, a good clustering is one for which the graph is well-approximated by the proxy model, specifically in the sense of the normalized Laplacian.

This constraint is undoubtedly necessary to derive concrete theoretical guarantees. However, it raises questions about the practical implications of this restriction, particularly in light of the framework's empirical limitations on the real-world examples presented.
