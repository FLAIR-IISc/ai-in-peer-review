This work introduces a general framework for establishing theoretical guarantees on the stability of a given clustering C, under the condition that C provides a good fit to the data. Stability is quantified by the distance between C and any alternative clustering C' that also demonstrates a comparably good fit. The authors demonstrate the application of this framework to two specific instances of block models, the Preferential Friendship Model (PFM) and the Stochastic Block Model (SBM), highlighting that all quantities in the theoretical bounds are empirically computable. Moreover, these stability results are leveraged to derive conditions for exact recovery within these models. Despite these contributions, the empirical simulations raise concerns about the practical applicability of the results, suggesting that the proposed goodness-of-fit measure is overly sensitive or that the theorem conditions are too restrictive.

1) The paper addresses the important problem of developing a general template for theoretical clustering guarantees that does not rely on specific generative model assumptions. The goal of deriving such model-agnostic guarantees, with bounds computable from the data and clustering outputs, is a valuable contribution to both theory and practice.

2) Since SBM is a special case of DC-SBM, which is itself encompassed by PFM, the motivation for treating PFM and SBM as distinct cases is unclear. As spectral clustering is effective for all these models, the rationale for employing different model estimators is not well-justified. Specifically, the paper should clarify why the Laplacian matrix is chosen for PFM while the adjacency matrix is used for SBM. Furthermore, the theoretical results are presented in terms of weighted Misclassification Error (ME) for PFM but standard ME for SBM. A discussion on how these two metrics relate is needed. It is also worth noting the absence of explicit results for the DC-SBM case.

3) The definition of the Frobenius inner product `< M', M >_F` in Proposition 10 should be explicitly stated.

4) Assumption 5 requires clarification: is the term `||L̂² - L²||_F` or `||L̂ - L||_F²`? If it is the former, the authors should provide an intuition for why the difference of the squared Laplacians is considered.

5) The analysis is primarily focused on disjoint clusters. It is not clear whether the techniques and the general recipe presented can be extended beyond block models to other settings where spectral clustering is known to be effective. For instance, spectral clustering has proven successful for hierarchical clustering, with associated theoretical guarantees (e.g., "Noise Thresholds for Spectral Clustering," Balakrishnan et al., 2011). It would be insightful to understand how the proposed template would apply in such a context.

6) The evaluation metrics and estimators for both PFM and SBM are constrained to scenarios where the clusterings C and C' have an identical number of clusters. However, different clustering algorithms may yield varying numbers of clusters. For example, the eigenvalues of the adjacency matrix A might suggest K clusters for C, while the eigenvalues of its Laplacian might suggest K' = K + 1 clusters for C'. If C' is identical to C except for one cluster that has been split, C' is intuitively "close" to C, yet this relationship cannot be captured by the current framework.

7) The related work section overlooks several existing model-free results, such as "Clustering with Spectral Norm and the k-means Algorithm" (Kumar and Kannan, 2010), "Clustering under Perturbation Resilience" (Balcan et al., 2012), and "Clustering under Approximation Stability" (Balcan et al., 2013). The paper should discuss how its notion of stability relates to the concepts presented in these works, particularly in comparison to stability defined by the perturbation of cluster centers.

8) The simulations indicate that the method tolerates only very small perturbations for PFM. The statement in lines 252-253 that a "large eigengap makes PFM(G, C) differ from PFM(G, C0) even for very small perturbations" is presented without sufficient justification. The reason for this high sensitivity is unclear. Conversely, for the political blog data, the adjacency matrix is raised to a power, which would further amplify the eigenvalue gaps, yet this appears to improve stability. This apparent contradiction needs to be addressed. If the model fit is indeed this sensitive to minor perturbations, the practical utility of the guarantees is diminished, which undermines a key motivation of the work. Furthermore, the admission in lines 248-250 that the SBM guarantee conditions are difficult to satisfy is concerning.

9) The caption for Figure 1 states, "For SBM, δ is always greater than δ0." It is not specified which subplot this refers to. If SBM results are not plotted, the caption should be clarified accordingly.

10) There is an inconsistency between the theory and experiments for PFM: the theorems provide guarantees for weighted ME, while the experiments measure perturbations in terms of standard ME.

**Minor Points:**

1) The axes in Figure 1 are not labeled.

2) Line 44: For block models, A_ij for i > j are conditionally independent given the cluster assignments of nodes i and j.

3) The symbols B, C, and Y are reused with different meanings for PFM and SBM (Section 3.2), which can cause confusion. Given the notational density of the paper, maintaining consistency is crucial. The authors might consider using more distinct notation, such as Y_⊥ and C_D for PFM. Additionally, the term YZ is not necessary for the SBM estimator and could be replaced by its direct expression in Proposition 8. Similarly, distinguishing the eigenvalues for the Laplacian and the adjacency matrix with notation like λ^L and λ^A would improve clarity.

4) Section 4.4 would be more appropriately placed as a subsection within the related work section.
