This paper presents a novel and valuable framework for providing model-free theoretical guarantees for the stability of graph clustering. The central idea is that if a clustering C provides a good fit to the observed graph G under a given model class (e.g., PFM or SBM), then any other clustering C' that also fits the data well must be structurally close to C. The authors instantiate this general principle for the Preference Frame Model (PFM) and the Stochastic Block Model (SBM), deriving explicit stability bounds. A key strength of this approach is that all quantities involved in the guarantees are computable from the data, making the framework potentially useful for cluster validation without assuming the data was generated from a specific model.

### Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The main contribution is a conceptual shift from model-based recovery to model-free validation. Formulating a stability guarantee that does not depend on a generative model assumption, but rather on how well a model *describes* the data, is an important and original contribution. This paradigm of "turning around" recovery theorems for validation is clever and could have a significant impact on how clustering results are evaluated.

2.  **Practical Potential:** A major advantage of the proposed framework is that the theoretical bounds are expressed in terms of quantities (ε, δ, eigengaps) that can be computed directly from the input graph and a given clustering. This distinguishes the work from many theoretical results that contain unknown constants, and it opens the door for practical applications in cluster validation.

3.  **Technical Contribution:** The paper successfully connects existing tools from matrix perturbation theory (specifically, the Davis-Kahan theorem) with model-based recovery literature to build its model-free framework. The proofs are elegant and demonstrate a strong technical command of the area.

### Weaknesses (Areas for Improvement)

1.  **Clarity and Justification of Methods:** The paper presents separate and notably different treatments for PFM and SBM. Since SBM is a special case of PFM, the motivation for using different estimators (Laplacian-based for PFM, adjacency-based for SBM), different goodness-of-fit criteria, and different error metrics (weighted ME for PFM, standard ME for SBM) is unclear. A unified framework or a clear discussion justifying these divergent choices would significantly strengthen the paper's coherence. The omission of results for the Degree-Corrected SBM (DC-SBM) is also a missed opportunity.

2.  **Unconvincing Empirical Validation:** While the theoretical framework is promising, the experimental results raise questions about its practical utility.
    *   The guarantees for PFM appear to hold only for extremely small perturbations of the initial clustering, which may limit their usefulness.
    *   The conditions for the SBM guarantee (Theorem 9) are reported to be too strict to be satisfied even for synthetic LFR data, which is designed to fit block models well.
    *   This discrepancy between the promising theory and the limited practical results undermines one of the paper's main selling points. The discussion of these limitations is brief and would benefit from a more in-depth analysis.

3.  **Insufficient Comparison to Related Work:** The paper is missing a discussion of several highly relevant works on model-free clustering guarantees and stability, such as "Clustering with Spectral Norm and the k-means Algorithm" (Kumar and Kannan, 2010) and the perturbation resilience frameworks of Balcan et al. ("Clustering under Perturbation Resilience," 2012; "Clustering under Approximation Stability," 2013). Positioning the current work with respect to these alternative notions of stability is essential for readers to understand its unique contributions.

4.  **Limited Scope of the Framework:** The current analysis is restricted to comparing clusterings C and C' that have the same number of clusters, K. This is a significant limitation, as a common practical challenge is determining the correct K or comparing partitions where one is a refinement of the other (e.g., one cluster splits into two).

### Detailed Questions and Suggestions

*   **PFM vs. SBM Treatment:** Could the authors elaborate on the rationale for the different methodological choices for PFM and SBM? For instance, could the Laplacian-based PFM framework be applied to SBM-generated data, and how would the results compare to the adjacency-based SBM analysis?
*   **Clarification on Assumption 5:** Is the norm in Assumption 5 `||L̂² - L²||_F` or `||L̂ - L||_F²`? If it is the former, what is the technical or intuitive reason for comparing the squares of the Laplacians?
*   **Experimental Puzzles:** The paper states that a large eigengap makes the PFM model fit very sensitive to small perturbations. However, in the political blogs experiment, increasing the eigengap by taking powers of the adjacency matrix appears to *improve* stability. Could you please clarify this apparent contradiction?
*   **Scope Extension:** Do the authors have any thoughts on how the framework could be extended to compare clusterings with a different number of clusters? This would greatly increase the practical impact of the work.

### Minor Points

*   **Figure 1:** The axes in the plots in Figure 1 are not labeled, making them difficult to interpret. The caption should also clarify if the SBM results are omitted from the plots because the condition δ ≤ δ₀ was never met.
*   **Notation:** The reuse of symbols such as B, C, Y, and λ for different concepts in the PFM and SBM sections can be confusing. Using more distinct notation (e.g., C_norm vs. C_counts, λ_L vs. λ_B) would improve readability. The definition of the Frobenius inner product `<M̂,M>_F` in Proposition 10 should also be stated for completeness.
*   **Metrics in Experiments:** The theory for PFM provides bounds for the *weighted* ME distance, but the x-axis of the plots seems to represent perturbations in the standard (unweighted) ME. Please clarify this.
*   **Organization:** Section 4.4 ("Clusterability and resilience") seems more aligned with a discussion of related concepts and could potentially be integrated into the Related Work section.
