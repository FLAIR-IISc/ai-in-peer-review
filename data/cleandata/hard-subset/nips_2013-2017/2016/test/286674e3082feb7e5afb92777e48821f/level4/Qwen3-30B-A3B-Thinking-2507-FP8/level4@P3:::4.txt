

This paper introduces a novel framework for providing theoretical guarantees for graph clustering without assuming the data comes from a specific model (e.g., SBM, DC-SBM, or PFM). The core idea is to establish "model-free" guarantees by demonstrating that any clustering that fits the data well must be close to a given clustering C, where "fits well" is quantified by a computable goodness-of-fit measure. The authors instantiate this framework for PFM and SBM, showing that their model-free results are intimately connected with existing model-based guarantees, and even strengthen some of the latter. The paper makes a significant contribution by shifting the perspective from "data comes from a model" to "model fits the data," with all quantities in the guarantees being computable from the data.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with rigorous proofs for their main theorems, particularly for PFM where they leverage spectral perturbation theory. The results for PFM are well-motivated and technically sound, with the weighted misclassification error (wME) distance appropriately accounting for node degree heterogeneity. The connection between model-free and model-based guarantees is particularly compelling, as shown in Theorem 11 where they improve bounds from [20] by a factor of 128.

The paper excels in originality by addressing a fundamental limitation of existing model-based approaches. While model-based guarantees require the assumption that data comes from a specific model, this work provides guarantees based on observable data properties. This approach is novel in the context of block models and represents a significant conceptual contribution to the field of network clustering.

However, the paper has several significant weaknesses that limit its significance. First, the practical utility of the guarantees appears questionable, as the experimental results (Figure 1) show that the conditions required for the guarantees to hold are very restrictive. For SBM, the authors note that "all δ values from Theorem 9 are above 1" and the bounds are not informative, which undermines the main selling point of the paper—providing practically applicable guarantees.

The treatment of PFM and SBM lacks consistency. The paper states that SBM is a subclass of DC-SBM, which is a subclass of PFM, yet they treat PFM and SBM as distinct cases with different estimators (Laplacian for PFM vs. adjacency matrix for SBM) and different distance measures (wME for PFM vs. ME for SBM). The rationale for these differences is not clearly explained, and the omission of DC-SBM results is particularly puzzling given its intermediate position in the model hierarchy.

The related work section is inadequate, failing to acknowledge key papers on model-free clustering under stability assumptions (e.g., Balcan et al. 2012, 2013) and spectral clustering under perturbation resilience (Kumar and Kannan 2010). The paper should clarify how its notion of stability relates to these existing works.

The experimental evaluation has several limitations. The perturbations tolerated by the PFM framework are extremely small (as noted in lines 252-253), making the guarantees practically uninformative for real-world applications. The authors claim "large eigengap makes PFM(G, C) differ from PFM(G, C0) even for very small perturbations" without quantifying what constitutes "small" or providing intuition for this phenomenon. The political blogs experiment uses graph smoothing (raising adjacency matrix to the third power), which appears to improve stability, but this is not adequately explained.

The framework's limitation to fixed cluster counts (K) is also problematic. The paper assumes C and C' have the same number of clusters, which is restrictive since different clustering algorithms or perturbations might yield different cluster counts (e.g., one cluster splitting into two). This limitation prevents the framework from capturing natural cluster perturbations.

Minor issues include inconsistent notation (e.g., using B and C for different purposes in PFM vs. SBM sections), missing axis labels in Figure 1, and unclear definitions (e.g., the inner product <M', M>_F in Proposition 10). The paper would benefit from a clearer explanation of why the SBM results are so much more restrictive than the PFM results, especially given the hierarchical relationship between the models.

Overall, while the paper presents an interesting conceptual framework with technically sound results for PFM, the practical limitations of the guarantees, the lack of clarity in the SBM treatment, and the limited experimental validation reduce its significance. The results for SBM are particularly uninformative, and the framework's applicability to real-world clustering problems is questionable given the stringent conditions required for the guarantees to hold.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of clustering stability, but it falls short of providing practically useful guarantees. The authors should either strengthen the conditions for the SBM results or acknowledge more clearly the limitations of their framework. The connection to existing stability literature (e.g., Balcan et al.) should be expanded to better position their work. With these improvements, the paper could make a more significant contribution to the field.
