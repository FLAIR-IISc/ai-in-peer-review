- Proposes new theoretical, distribution-free results for graph clustering without assuming a specific generative model.
- Core claim: if a clustering fits a graph well (by their definition), any other clustering that fits equally well must be close to it.
- Addresses a crucial problem and provides novel, interesting theoretical insights.
- Paper is clear and readable even with some content in the supplementary material.
- Minor clarity issue: use of “hats” to denote observations (e.g., \hat{L}) is questionable.
- Results are well-commented and accompanied by suitable illustrations.
- Empirical finding: some real-world graphs require smoothing for the theory’s structures to be recoverable, while classical algorithms can sometimes recover ground truth without smoothing.
- Limitation 1: main result for PFM uses a non-standard distance between clusterings, which is debatable.
- Limitation 2: relies on a “proxy” model approach (reconstruct clustering → model → compare to original), constraining the definition of a good clustering.
- Constraint: the framework requires the graph to be well-approximated in the normalized Laplacian sense, which may limit applicability.
- Open concern: the practical impact of these methodological restrictions on real-world examples is unclear.
