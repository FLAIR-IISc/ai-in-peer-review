## Review of NIPS 2016 Submission – [Paper ID]

This manuscript proposes a collection of algorithms for graph clustering and assessing the similarity between cluster assignments. While the algorithmic descriptions are clear and well-presented, the overarching motivation for presenting these as a unified set remains unclear. The work largely consists of modifications to established clustering techniques, and the primary novelty appears to reside in the constraints introduced towards the conclusion of the paper. 

The core contribution lies in adapting existing graph clustering methods – specifically, variations of spectral clustering – to incorporate constraints on the resulting cluster assignments. The authors explore different formulations for these constraints and present algorithms to enforce them during the clustering process. The presented algorithms build upon a foundation of well-known techniques, including normalized cuts and ratio cuts, extending them with penalty terms related to violating specified constraints. This approach allows for incorporating prior knowledge or desired properties into the clustering process.

However, the paper lacks a compelling narrative explaining *why* this particular collection of algorithms is presented. The motivation for exploring this specific set of modifications feels somewhat incremental. Is there a specific application domain where these constraints are particularly useful, and if so, this should be emphasized. While the authors mention potential applications, these remain largely hypothetical. A more focused presentation, perhaps centered around a key application, would strengthen the paper considerably. 

The exposition of the algorithms themselves is strong. The mathematical formulations are clear, and the algorithmic steps are easy to follow. The authors appropriately leverage existing literature and clearly cite relevant work. However, the presentation occasionally feels fragmented; the connections between the different algorithms aren't always explicitly drawn. A more cohesive explanation of how these algorithms relate to one another, and when one might be preferred over another, would be beneficial. A table summarizing the algorithms, their constraints, and their respective strengths and weaknesses could also improve clarity.

The experimental evaluation is adequate but lacks depth. The synthetic datasets used demonstrate the ability of the algorithms to satisfy the imposed constraints, which is positive. However, the results presented don’t convincingly demonstrate a significant performance improvement over standard graph clustering methods *without* the constraints, particularly on real-world datasets. The comparison to baselines is limited, and the metrics used (primarily focusing on constraint satisfaction) don’t fully capture the overall quality of the resulting clusters. More comprehensive evaluation, including standard clustering metrics like Normalized Mutual Information (NMI) or Adjusted Rand Index (ARI), and comparison to more recent and competitive graph clustering algorithms, is crucial. The choice of datasets also warrants justification – why were these specific datasets selected?

Furthermore, the discussion of the constraints themselves could be expanded. The authors introduce constraints related to cluster size and pairwise node assignments. However, the limitations of these constraints are not fully addressed. For example, enforcing strict cluster size constraints might lead to suboptimal clustering if the underlying graph structure doesn’t naturally lend itself to such constraints.  A discussion of these trade-offs would be valuable.

**Strengths:**

*   Clear and well-written algorithmic descriptions.
*   Formal mathematical formulation of the proposed methods.
*   Logical extension of existing graph clustering techniques.
*   Demonstrated ability to enforce user-defined constraints.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Lack of a strong motivating narrative and clear justification for the presented collection of algorithms.
*   Limited experimental evaluation and insufficient comparison to state-of-the-art methods.
*   Insufficient discussion of the limitations and trade-offs associated with the proposed constraints.
*   The presentation feels somewhat fragmented, lacking a cohesive structure.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents technically sound algorithms, but the overall impact is limited by the lack of a compelling motivation and the relatively weak empirical evaluation. While the idea of incorporating constraints into graph clustering is valuable, the paper doesn’t fully convince me of the significance of this particular approach or its practical advantages over existing methods. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **weak reject**. While the work is not fundamentally flawed, it requires substantial revisions to address the issues outlined above. Specifically, the authors need to provide a stronger justification for their work, conduct a more thorough experimental evaluation, and offer a more nuanced discussion of the limitations and trade-offs of their proposed constraints. A more focused presentation around a specific application domain would also significantly strengthen the paper.
