The manuscript proposes a framework for obtaining correctness guarantees for graph clustering algorithms without assuming the data originates from a specific generative model, such as the Stochastic Block Model (SBM). This is achieved by focusing on the “goodness of fit” between a clustering and the observed data, and establishing that any alternative clustering with comparable fit must be structurally similar. The authors instantiate this framework for the SBM and Preference Frame Model (PFM), demonstrating connections between model-free and model-based recovery guarantees, and even strengthening existing results in the model-based setting.

The paper is well-structured, beginning with a clear introduction of the problem and the proposed framework. The background section provides necessary definitions and context for graphs, clusterings, and block models. The main theorems for PFM and SBM are presented with accompanying proofs outlined (with details deferred to the supplement). The discussion section correctly situates the work within the broader literature and highlights potential extensions. 

A significant strength of this work lies in its theoretical rigor. The authors provide precise definitions, assumptions, and theorems, with clearly stated conditions under which guarantees hold. The exploration of the relationship between model-free and model-based approaches is a valuable contribution, offering potential avenues for leveraging existing results. The emphasis on computable quantities, avoiding reliance on undetermined constants, increases the practical relevance of the framework. The experiments provide some validation of the theoretical results, though limited in scope.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. While the motivation for a model-free approach is clear, the presentation could benefit from a more compelling narrative explaining *why* this framework is a substantial advance. The connection between the “generic theorem” and the specific instantiations for PFM and SBM isn’t always immediately apparent, and a more intuitive explanation of how the goodness-of-fit measure ensures clustering stability would be helpful. The experimental evaluation is relatively limited. While the authors acknowledge this, more extensive testing on diverse datasets, and a comparison with existing state-of-the-art methods would strengthen the empirical validation. The exposition is dense and can be challenging to follow, even for readers familiar with the relevant background.  More illustrative examples could improve clarity. Finally, the related work section is comprehensive but could benefit from a more critical assessment of how this work differs from and builds upon previous research. For example, the discussion of [16] feels somewhat dismissive.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper presents rigorous theoretical guarantees for clustering without model assumptions, a significant contribution to the field.
*   **Novel Framework:** The proposed framework provides a new perspective on clustering validation and recovery, connecting model-free and model-based approaches.
*   **Clear Definitions & Computable Quantities:** The definitions and conditions are precise and allow for practical application of the results.
*   **Potential for Impact:** The framework could be extended to other clustering algorithms and datasets, potentially leading to more robust and reliable clustering solutions.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Experimental Validation:** The experimental results are limited and do not provide strong evidence of the framework's performance on real-world datasets.
*   **Dense Exposition:** The paper is challenging to read and could benefit from more intuitive explanations and illustrative examples.
*   **Incremental Contribution:** While technically sound, the practical impact relative to existing methods remains uncertain. The gains, if any, are not demonstrated convincingly.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the theoretical understanding of graph clustering. The framework is novel and the results are rigorously established. However, the limited experimental evaluation and dense exposition temper my enthusiasm. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should: 1) expand the experimental evaluation with more diverse datasets and comparisons to state-of-the-art methods; 2) revise the exposition to improve clarity and readability, potentially adding more illustrative examples; and 3) provide a more nuanced discussion of the related work, acknowledging the contributions of existing methods and clearly articulating the unique advantages of their approach.
