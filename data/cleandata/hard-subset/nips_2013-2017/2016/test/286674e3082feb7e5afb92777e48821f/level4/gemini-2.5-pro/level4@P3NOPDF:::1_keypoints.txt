- Novel theoretical results for graph clustering in a distribution-free framework, departing from model-based assumptions like the SBM.  
- Central thesis: a stability/uniqueness result—if a clustering fits the graph well, any other equally good clustering must be structurally close.  
- Important direction for understanding clustering reliability when the generative process is unknown.  
- Paper is well-written and generally clear; main arguments remain accessible despite moving some derivations to the supplement.  
- Theoretical results are significant, well-motivated, and effectively illustrated.  
- Minor stylistic point: using hats for observed quantities (e.g., \hat{L}) is unconventional but not harmful.  
- Experimental gap: some real-world graphs must be “smoothed” for the theory to recover structure, whereas classical algorithms can recover clustering on unsmoothed graphs.  
- Main PFM result relies on a non-standard clustering distance; its claimed naturalness is not fully justified and hinders comparison to prior work.  
- Framework uses a proxy-model approach (reconstructing a model from a clustering, then comparing to the observed graph), which implicitly constrains what counts as a “good” clustering.  
- “Good” clustering is defined via approximation in the normalized Laplacian sense, a simplification that may limit applicability to certain cluster structures.  
- The paper would benefit from a deeper discussion of how the modeling choices restrict the types of cluster structures covered and affect real-world performance.  
- Summary: significant and novel theoretical contributions, but practical applicability is tempered by smoothing needs and foundational modeling assumptions.
