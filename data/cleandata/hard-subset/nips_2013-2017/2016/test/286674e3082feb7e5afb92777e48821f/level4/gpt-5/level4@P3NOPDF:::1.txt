Summary
The paper presents new theoretical results for graph clustering that do not depend on a particular generative model (e.g., stochastic block model). The central insight is a stability-type guarantee: if a clustering algorithm finds a partition that fits the observed graph well (under a precise notion of fit), then any other partition that also fits the graph well must be close to the first. This yields distribution-free guarantees and a principled notion of identifiability from a single observed graph. The authors develop these results for specific models (e.g., PFM) and evaluate them empirically. Despite some content being deferred to the supplementary material, the paper is largely self-contained and readable.

Strengths
- Novelty and significance: Distribution-free guarantees for graph clustering address an important gap between model-based theory and practice on real graphs where generative assumptions are dubious.
- Conceptual clarity: Framing the results as “good fit implies uniqueness up to small perturbations” is compelling and helps interpret when a clustering is meaningful.
- Technical depth: The results are well-motivated, nontrivial, and accompanied by thoughtful commentary and illustrative examples.
- Exposition: With the exception of a few notational choices (see below), the writing is clear, and the main arguments can be followed without constant reference to the supplement.

Limitations and concerns
1) Nonstandard distance for PFM. The main PFM guarantee uses a clustering distance that is not standard in the literature. While the authors argue it is more natural in their framework, this remains debatable and complicates comparison to prior work. At minimum, it would help to:
- Relate this distance to classical metrics (e.g., misclassification error after optimal relabeling, variation of information, adjusted Rand index), including bounds or equivalences under mild conditions.
- Provide intuition or examples where the proposed metric yields materially different judgments than standard choices, and why those differences are desirable.

2) Proxy-model comparison and induced notion of fit. The analysis reconstructs a model from a candidate clustering (e.g., a PFM) and compares that model to the observed graph (rather than comparing the original clustering algorithm’s objective or implicit model). This is natural within the chosen framework, but it tightly constrains what counts as a “good” clustering—specifically, goodness is judged by approximation quality in the normalized Laplacian sense. This raises questions:
- Practical scope: On which classes of graphs does this proxy accurately reflect meaningful community structure, especially with degree heterogeneity, hubs, or heavy-tailed degree distributions?
- Misspecification: How sensitive are the guarantees to cases where the “best” model within the proxy family fits poorly, yet other reasonable clusterings (by other objectives) capture semantics of interest?
- Alternatives: Could the framework accommodate other notions of fit (e.g., modularity-like or cut-based criteria) without losing the key uniqueness guarantees?

3) Empirical gap on real data. Some real-world graphs require smoothing for the theory to detect recoverable structure, whereas standard algorithms recover ground truth without smoothing. This suggests the fit criterion (via the proxy model and normalized Laplacian) may be too restrictive or brittle in practice.
- Please clarify when and why smoothing is necessary, how its parameters are chosen, and how sensitive the conclusions are to this choice.
- It would strengthen the paper to include ablations showing performance and “fit” measures as a function of smoothing, degree regularization, and sparsity.

Clarity and presentation
- Notation: Using hats to denote observed quantities (e.g., L-hat for the observed Laplacian) is unconventional and potentially confusing, since hats often denote estimators of unobserved targets. Consider using a more standard convention (e.g., plain L for observed, or subscripts) and reserve hats for estimated population quantities if needed.
- Self-containment: While the main text is readable, pointing more explicitly to key lemmas in the supplement when invoked would improve flow.
- Definitions: The precise definition of “fits the graph reasonably well” should be stated early, with an immediate interpretation and, if possible, a simple diagnostic test practitioners could apply.

Requests for clarification and additional analysis
- Distance metric:
  - Provide a formal comparison to standard clustering distances, including theoretical inequalities and a brief empirical check showing correlations between distances on benchmark clusterings.
  - Explain whether your proofs critically rely on properties unique to your metric (e.g., convexity, decomposability), or if analogous statements could hold under standard distances with different constants.
- Checkability of conditions:
  - Offer practical guidance for verifying the sufficient fit conditions on a single observed graph. Are there computable proxies or bounds that indicate when the uniqueness guarantee is likely to hold?
- Robustness:
  - Discuss how graph sparsity, degree heterogeneity, and noise affect the fit criterion and the resulting guarantees. Are there regimes where guarantees are vacuous even though semantically meaningful communities exist?
- Empirical comparisons:
  - When smoothing is required for your method to recover structure, report how baseline methods’ performance changes under the same smoothing. This helps isolate whether the need for smoothing is a property of the data or of your fit criterion.
  - Include datasets where the proxy model is plausibly misspecified and analyze how the theory’s diagnostic (fit quality) correlates with downstream clustering quality.

Overall assessment
This is an interesting and timely paper that advances the theoretical understanding of graph clustering without relying on generative assumptions. The main message—uniqueness of well-fitting clusterings—adds conceptual clarity and, with further development, could inform practical diagnostics for when a clustering is trustworthy. The work is technically solid and clearly written, with thoughtful empirical illustrations.

However, the reliance on a nonstandard clustering distance (for PFM) and on a proxy-model notion of fit limits interpretability and comparability to existing literature. The empirical need for smoothing on some real graphs, in contrast to the success of classical methods without smoothing, further underscores a potential mismatch between the theoretical fit criterion and practical notions of meaningful structure.

With additional justification for the distance choice, a clearer account of when the proxy-model fit is appropriate, and more robust empirical diagnostics (especially around smoothing and misspecification), the paper would be considerably stronger. As it stands, I view it as a valuable theoretical contribution with some practical limitations, and I lean positive on acceptance if the authors can address the concerns above.
