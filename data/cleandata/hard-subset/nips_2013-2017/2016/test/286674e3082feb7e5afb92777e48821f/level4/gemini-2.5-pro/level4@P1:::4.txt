This paper introduces a novel framework for providing theoretical guarantees for graph clustering without assuming that the data is generated from a specific model. The core idea is to establish the stability of a given clustering, C: if C fits the graph data well according to a specific model class (e.g., SBM or PFM), then any other clustering C' that also fits the data well must be a small perturbation of C. The authors instantiate this framework for the Stochastic Block Model (SBM) and the Preference Frame Model (PFM), deriving explicit, computable bounds. A key contribution is that all quantities in these theoretical guarantees can be calculated directly from the data, which is a significant departure from model-based analyses that depend on unknown ground-truth parameters. The paper also demonstrates how this model-free perspective can be used to strengthen existing model-based recovery results.

**Strengths**

The main strength of this paper lies in its originality and potential significance. The proposal of a model-free framework for validating clustering results is an important and timely contribution. Shifting the focus from recovering a latent "true" clustering to guaranteeing the stability of a "good" clustering is a powerful paradigm. The fact that the proposed guarantees are expressed in terms of quantities computable from the data makes the framework potentially very useful for practitioners who want to assess the reliability of a clustering result without making strong generative assumptions. This work could open up a new direction in the theoretical analysis of community detection algorithms.

**Weaknesses and Major Concerns**

1.  **Disparate Treatment of PFM and SBM:** Given that the SBM is a special case of the PFM, the rationale for treating the two models so differently is unclear. The analysis for PFM is based on the graph Laplacian and results in a bound on the weighted Misclassification Error (wME), while the SBM analysis uses the adjacency matrix and bounds the standard ME. The paper would be strengthened by a unified treatment or, at a minimum, a detailed discussion justifying these different choices. Furthermore, a comparison of the wME and ME metrics would be helpful to understand how the two guarantees relate to one another. The absence of results for the Degree-Corrected SBM, which lies between SBM and PFM in generality, is also a missed opportunity to present a more complete picture.

2.  **Practical Utility of the Guarantees:** The experimental results suggest that the derived bounds may be too loose to be useful in practice. For the PFM, the guarantees only hold for extremely small perturbations from the initial clustering. The authors argue this is due to the large eigengap, but this explanation is not fully elaborated and seems counterintuitive, as a large eigengap typically implies stability. For the SBM, the conditions for the guarantee were never met in the experiments, even on data generated from a block model (LFR). While model-free results are expected to be weaker than model-based ones, if the conditions are nearly impossible to satisfy, the practical utility of the framework—a key selling point—is diminished.

3.  **Limited Scope of the Framework:** The current framework is limited to comparing clusterings C and C' that have the same number of clusters, K. This is a significant restriction, as different algorithms or model selection criteria could plausibly suggest different numbers of clusters for the same data. For instance, one clustering might be a refinement of another (e.g., one cluster splits into two). In such a case, the two clusterings could be considered structurally similar, but the current framework, which relies on a permutation-based distance, cannot capture this.

4.  **Discussion of Related Work:** The paper overlooks several highly relevant lines of work on model-free clustering analysis and stability. There is a rich literature on axiomatically-defined notions of stability, such as perturbation resilience (Balcan et al., 2012) and approximation stability (Balcan et al., 2013). Additionally, work on spectral methods like Kumar and Kannan (2010) provides model-free analyses. A discussion comparing the notion of stability proposed here with these existing concepts would be crucial for contextualizing the paper's contribution.

**Questions and Suggestions for Improvement**

*   Could the authors elaborate on the choice to analyze the squared Laplacians in Assumption 5 (i.e., `||L̂² - L²||_F`)? What is the intuition behind this choice compared to, for example, `||L̂ - L||_F`?
*   In Proposition 10, the notation `<M̂, M>_F` is used without definition. Please define it explicitly (presumably the Frobenius inner product).
*   The experiments measure the stability of clusterings by applying perturbations in terms of the unweighted ME. However, the theoretical guarantee for PFM is given for the weighted ME. This mismatch should be addressed or justified.
*   Does the proposed framework extend beyond block models? Spectral clustering is effective for other structures, some with theoretical guarantees (e.g., the work on hierarchical clustering by Balakrishnan et al., 2011). A discussion on the potential applicability of this "stability" template to other spectral methods would be valuable.

**Minor Comments**

*   The plots in Figure 1 are missing axis labels. The caption should also clarify why the SBM results are not shown (presumably because δ > δ₀ in all cases).
*   The notation could be improved for clarity. Several symbols (B, C, Y, λ) are redefined for the SBM analysis after being used for the PFM. Using distinct notation (e.g., λᴸ for Laplacian eigenvalues and λᴬ for adjacency eigenvalues) would prevent confusion.
*   Line 44: The statement that edges are sampled independently under block models should be clarified to state that they are conditionally independent given the node partitions.
*   Section 4.4 ("Clusterability and resilience") seems more appropriate as a subsection within the discussion of related work or significance, as it connects the paper's concepts to existing terminology in the field.
