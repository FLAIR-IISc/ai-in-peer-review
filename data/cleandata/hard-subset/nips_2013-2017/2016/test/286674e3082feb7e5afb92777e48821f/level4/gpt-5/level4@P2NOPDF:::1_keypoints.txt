- Presents distribution-free theoretical guarantees for graph clustering that don’t assume a generative model (e.g., SBM).  
- Core insight: any clustering that fits the observed graph well implies any other equally good clustering must be close to it.  
- Yields principled, model-free stability results addressing an important problem.  
- Paper is largely self-contained and readable despite some material moved to the supplement.  
- Exposition is clear; results are well explained and empirically illustrated.  
- Minor stylistic issue: unconventional use of hats for observed quantities may confuse readers.  
- Theoretical contributions are strong and carefully motivated within the chosen framework.  
- Empirically, some real-world graphs need smoothing to reveal structure under the authors’ fit notion, while standard algorithms can recover ground truth without it.  
- Main result for PFM depends on a nonstandard distance between clusterings; the choice needs more justification or comparison to classical metrics.  
- Analysis uses a “proxy” model (reconstructing a model from a clustering) so “good clustering” is defined by approximation quality in the normalized Laplacian sense, which narrows the notion of validity.  
- The proxy-model and distance choices may limit applicability and misalign with alternative objectives or heterogeneous/noisy data.  
- Clarifying trade-offs—implications of the chosen distance and fit criterion and when smoothing is necessary—would strengthen the paper.
