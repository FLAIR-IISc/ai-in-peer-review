This paper presents novel theoretical results on graph clustering, with a key strength being its distribution-free nature. The analysis does not assume the graph was generated from a specific probabilistic model, such as the stochastic block model. The central thesis is a stability guarantee: if a clustering method discovers a partition that provides a good fit for the observed graph, then any other partition offering a similarly good fit must be structurally close to the first. This is a compelling paper that addresses a fundamental and important problem. The manuscript is well-written and largely self-contained, remaining clear even with some technical details deferred to the supplementary materials.

The results are interesting, well-commented, and effectively illustrated. However, the theoretical framework has several limitations that warrant discussion. A minor presentational point is the unconventional use of "hat" notation (e.g., `\hat{L}`) for observed quantities, but this does not detract from the overall clarity.

More significant limitations emerge from both the theoretical and practical standpoints. First, the experiments show that on some real-world data, the graph requires smoothing before its structure becomes recoverable under the authors' framework. In contrast, many classical algorithms can recover the ground-truth structure on the raw graph, suggesting a potential gap between the theory's requirements and its direct applicability.

Second, and perhaps more critically, are two constraints on the theoretical development:
1.  The main result concerning the Planted Partition Model (PFM) employs a non-standard distance measure for comparing clusterings. The authors provide a justification for this choice, but its advantages over more established metrics are not fully convincing.
2.  The analysis is predicated on what might be called a "reconstruction" approach. A clustering is evaluated based on how well a model reconstructed from it (e.g., a PFM) approximates the original graph's normalized Laplacian. While this is a natural and necessary device for achieving distribution-free results, it also strongly constrains the definition of a "good" clustering to one that performs well under this specific approximation criterion.

This latter point is a crucial trade-off. The framework's power comes from this specific lens, but it raises questions about the broader impact of this restriction, especially when considering the practical challenges the method faces on certain real-world examples where other notions of cluster quality may be more pertinent.
