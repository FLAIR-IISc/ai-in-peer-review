This paper presents new theoretical results on graph clustering that make no assumptions about how the observed graph was generated (e.g., no stochastic block model or other generative assumptions). The central idea is that if a clustering method finds a partition that fits the graph reasonably well (under a precise notion of fit), then any other clustering that fits the graph equally well must be close to the first. This is an important and compelling contribution: the results are distribution-free and address a fundamental question in graph clustering.

Despite some content being moved to the supplementary material due to page limits, the main paper remains clear and self-contained. The results are interesting, well explained, and supported by illustrations. A minor stylistic concern is the use of “hats” to denote actual observations (e.g., using \hat{L} for the observed Laplacian), which I find confusing; however, this does not materially affect readability.

The empirical section highlights a notable limitation: on some real-world datasets, the graph must be smoothed before the framework can recover meaningful structure, whereas many classical algorithms recover the ground truth without such preprocessing. This gap raises questions about the practical robustness of the proposed guarantees.

Two more substantive concerns:

- The main result for PFM relies on a non-standard distance between clusterings. Although the authors argue this metric may be more natural than classical ones, that claim is debatable, and the departure from standard measures complicates interpretation and comparison.

- The guarantees hinge on a “proxy” modeling step: given a clustering, a model (e.g., a PFM) is reconstructed and compared to the observed graph, rather than analyzing the model that was actually used to obtain the clustering. This is natural within the authors’ setup, but it tightly constrains what counts as a “good” clustering to those that approximate the graph well in the normalized Laplacian sense. While such structure is necessary to obtain rigorous results, it also narrows the scope and may limit applicability—especially in light of the empirical cases where the framework struggles without smoothing.

Overall, this is a well-written and thought-provoking paper that tackles an important problem with distribution-free theoretical guarantees. The results are clearly presented and insightful, but the reliance on a non-standard clustering distance and on a proxy-model comparison in the normalized Laplacian sense, together with the need for smoothing in some real-world settings, temper my enthusiasm about the breadth of practical impact.
