The authors propose novel theoretical results on graph clustering that do not rely on specific assumptions about the generation of the graph under study, diverging from traditional approaches that assume the observed graph was generated according to a particular generative model, such as the stochastic block model. The core idea of the paper is that if a graph clustering method can discover a clustering that reasonably fits the graph (based on a certain definition), then any other clustering that fits the graph equally well should be close to the first one. This paper addresses a crucial problem by providing distribution-free results for graph clustering, making it a very interesting and impactful contribution.

The paper is well-written and clear, allowing readers to grasp the main ideas without necessarily referring to the supplementary materials, which were added due to the conference's page limit restrictions. One minor point of contention is the use of "hats" to denote actual observations (e.g., \hat{L} for the observed Laplacian), which may not be the most intuitive choice. Nevertheless, the paper is generally well-organized and easy to follow.

The results presented in the paper are intriguing, well-commented, and effectively illustrated. However, they are not without limitations. The experimental section demonstrates that, on some real-world data, the graph needs to be smoothed for certain structures to be recoverable within the authors' theoretical framework. In contrast, many classical algorithms can recover the ground truth structure without such preprocessing. Furthermore, two significant limitations are apparent. Firstly, the primary result for PFM employs a non-standard distance measure between clusterings, which the authors argue may be more natural than the classical measure, although this is debatable. Secondly, the results rely on a "proxy" model, where a clustering is used to reconstruct a model (e.g., a PFM), and this model is compared to the original graph rather than the model used to obtain the clustering. While this approach is natural in the context chosen by the authors, it also constrains the notion of a good clustering for a given graph, implying that the graph is well-approximated in the normalized Laplacian sense. This constraint is necessary to obtain concrete results but raises questions about the impact of these restrictions, particularly in light of the limitations faced by the framework on some real-world examples.
