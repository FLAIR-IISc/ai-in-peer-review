The authors propose new theoretical results on graph clustering that do not assume any assumptions about the construction of the graphs under study (the observed graphs are not considered to be generated according to a specific generative model, such as a stochastic block model). The main idea of the paper is that if a graph clustering method is able to find a clustering that appropriately fits the graph (for a specific definition), then any other clustering that fits the graph well should be close to the first clustering. This paper is very interesting and, in my opinion, solves a very important problem by providing a distribution-free result for graph clustering. While some material has been moved to supplementary material due to conference page number restrictions, the paper is quite clear and can be read without referring to this additional material. I am not sure if using a "hat" on the actual observation (for example, \hat{L} for the observed Laplacian) is a good option, but apart from this detail, the paper is quite clear. The results themselves are very interesting and well-commented and illustrated. However, they are not without limitations. As shown in the experimental part, on some real-world data, the graph will have to be smoothed to recover some structure (in the sense of the theoretical framework developed by the authors), while many classical algorithms are capable of recovering the ground-truth structure. Furthermore, and perhaps more importantly, two limitations appear: 1) The main result for PFM uses a non-standard distance measure between the clusters. The authors argue that this measure can be considered more natural than the classical measure, but this is questionable. 2) The results are based on a form of "proxy" model, in the sense that from a clustering, a model (such as PFM) is reconstructed and this model is compared to the original graph (instead of the model used to obtain the clustering). This is very natural in the context chosen by the authors, but it also strongly limits what constitutes a good clustering for a given graph (here, this means that the graph is well- अनुमानित in the sense of a generalized Laplacian). This is clearly necessary to obtain real results, but one might wonder what effect those limitations have, especially given the limitations faced by the framework on some real-world examples.
