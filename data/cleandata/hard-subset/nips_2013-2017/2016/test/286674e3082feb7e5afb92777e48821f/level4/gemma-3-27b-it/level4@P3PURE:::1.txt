The authors present novel theoretical results for graph clustering that avoid assumptions about the graph’s generative process – a significant advantage over methods relying on models like the stochastic block model. The core idea is that if a clustering method finds a reasonable clustering for a given graph, then any other clustering that also fits the graph well should be structurally similar to the first. This paper addresses an important problem by providing distribution-free results for graph clustering and is, overall, very interesting. Despite content being moved to supplementary materials due to page limits, the paper remains clear and accessible without needing to consult the additional content. While I find the use of “hats” to denote observed quantities (e.g., \hat{L} for the observed Laplacian) somewhat unconventional, this is a minor point, and the paper is generally well-written and the results are thoroughly explained and illustrated. 

However, the results are not without limitations. The experimental results demonstrate that, for some real-world data, graph smoothing is required to recover structure within the authors’ theoretical framework, whereas more traditional algorithms can often recover the ground truth directly. Furthermore, two key limitations deserve consideration. First, the main result concerning the Partition Function Metric (PFM) employs a non-standard distance measure between clusterings. Although the authors argue for its naturalness, this claim is debatable. Second, the results rely on a “proxy” model approach: a model (e.g., a PFM) is reconstructed from a clustering and then compared to the original graph, rather than comparing to the generative model (if known) used to create the clustering. While natural in this context, this approach inherently constrains the definition of a “good” clustering – here, it implies a strong approximation in the normalized Laplacian sense. This constraint is necessary to achieve theoretical results, but its impact is worth considering, especially given the limitations observed on some real-world examples. It remains an open question how strongly these restrictions influence the broader applicability of the framework.
