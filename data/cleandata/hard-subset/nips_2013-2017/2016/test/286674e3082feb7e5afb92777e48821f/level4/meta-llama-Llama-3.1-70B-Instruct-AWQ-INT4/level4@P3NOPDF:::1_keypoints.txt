- Main contribution: novel, distribution-free theoretical results for graph clustering that avoid assuming a specific generative model.
- Key idea: if a clustering fits the observed graph well (by the paper’s definition), any other clustering that fits equally well must be close to it.
- Important distinction: does not assume graphs come from models like the stochastic block model.
- Paper fits conference page limits by moving material to supplements yet remains understandable without them.
- Minor stylistic issue: using “hats” to denote observed quantities (e.g., \hat{L}) is an awkward notation choice.
- Results are intriguing, well-commented, and effectively illustrated.
- Empirical limitation: some real-world graphs require smoothing for structure recovery, whereas classical algorithms can recover ground truth without it.
- Primary theoretical limitation 1: the main PFM result uses a non-standard distance between clusterings, which may be debatable in naturalness.
- Primary theoretical limitation 2: reliance on a “proxy” model (reconstruct clustering to a model and compare to the graph) constrains the notion of a good clustering and ties results to normalized Laplacian approximation.
- Overall: significant and interesting theoretical contribution, but framework constraints and real-world limitations warrant careful consideration.
