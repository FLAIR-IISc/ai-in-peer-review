- Proposes a model-free framework that certifies clustering stability by fitting a model M(G,C) to the data and showing any other good fit C′ must be close to C in misclassification error.  
- Provides a generic theorem turning model-based recovery results into model-free uniqueness/stability guarantees.  
- Instantiates the framework for PFM: computable estimator via aligning top-K eigenspaces, Davis–Kahan bounds, and weighted misclassification error guarantees depending on ε, eigengap, and imbalance.  
- Instantiates the framework for SBM: ML estimator, spectral-norm goodness-of-fit ||Â − A|| ≤ ε, and unweighted misclassification error bounds.  
- Leverages prior model-based recovery results to obtain alternative model-free guarantees and sometimes tighten constants.  
- All certificate quantities (ε, eigengaps, cluster volumes) are computable from data; extensions to weighted graphs and perturbation resilience discussed.  
- Technically sound approach using standard tools (Davis–Kahan, spectral perturbation, Meilă local equivalence); assumptions are explicit.  
- Honest discussion of limitations (loose SBM bounds, weak spectral-norm concentration in sparse graphs); proofs mostly in supplement but strategy convincing.  
- Empirics show PFM-based certificates informative while SBM-based ones are often conservative; evaluation is limited (mostly synthetic, one heavily-smoothed real network).  
- Clear exposition and motivation for chosen error metrics; minor notational/terminology typos and need to discuss DC-SBM explicitly.  
- Original and impactful idea: novel model-free stability certificates for SBM/DC-SBM/PFM literature.  
- Potentially significant in practice as a computable certificate of uniqueness, but real-world impact depends on spectral-norm goodness in sparse/heterogeneous networks.  
- Strength: clear, computable model-free framework with technically clean proofs and links to prior results.  
- Weakness: reliance on spectral-norm closeness and eigengaps may fail in sparse/noisy graphs; SBM bounds often uninformative; DC-SBM lacks a dedicated theorem.  
- Weakness: assumes known K and lacks guidance on model selection or robustness to K misspecification.  
- Weakness: limited empirical evaluation and no comparisons to bootstrap/confidence-set validation methods.  
- Risk of optimistic operator-norm estimates when fitting and evaluating on the same data; suggests need for holdout or cross-validation.  
- Suggested improvements: add DC-SBM instantiation, robust estimation/upper-bounding of operator norms (holdout/CV), more real datasets, comparisons to bootstrap baselines, and discussion of K selection.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, general, computable framework; technically sound; bridges and strengthens prior results; clear exposition.  
- Arguments against acceptance: bounds may be too loose in realistic sparse settings; missing DC-SBM theorem; limited empirical comparisons and reliance on strong spectral assumptions.  
- Overall recommendation: lean toward acceptance contingent on stronger empirical evaluation and adding a DC-SBM instantiation.
