The authors propose new theoretical results on graph clustering that do not rely on specific assumptions about the generation of the graph under study. Unlike existing methods, their approach does not assume that the observed graph was generated according to a particular generative model, such as the stochastic block model. The core idea of the paper is that if a graph clustering method can discover a clustering that reasonably fits the graph (according to a certain definition), then any other clustering that fits the graph equally well should be close to the first one. This paper is very interesting and, in my opinion, addresses a crucial problem by providing distribution-free results for graph clustering.

While some content has been relegated to the supplementary materials due to the conference's page number restrictions, the paper remains clear and can be read without referencing the additional content. I do question the use of "hats" to denote actual observations (e.g., \hat{L} for the observed Laplacian), but apart from this minor detail, the paper is reasonably clear. The results themselves are fascinating and well-commented upon, with suitable illustrations. However, they are not without limitations.

As demonstrated in the experimental section, on some real-world data, the graph must be smoothed in order for certain structures to be recoverable (in the sense of the theoretical framework developed by the authors), whereas many classical algorithms can recover the ground truth structure. Furthermore, two significant limitations arise: 1) the main result for PFM employs a non-standard distance measure between clusterings. The authors argue that this measure might be more natural than the classical one, but this is debatable. 2) The results rely on a "proxy" model, where a clustering is used to reconstruct a model (e.g., a PFM), which is then compared to the original graph (rather than the model used to obtain the clustering). While this approach is natural in the context chosen by the authors, it also constrains the notion of what constitutes a good clustering for a given graph (specifically, that the graph is well-approximated in the normalized Laplacian sense). This constraint is necessary to obtain meaningful results, but one may wonder about the impact of these restrictions, particularly in light of the limitations faced by the framework on some real-world examples.
