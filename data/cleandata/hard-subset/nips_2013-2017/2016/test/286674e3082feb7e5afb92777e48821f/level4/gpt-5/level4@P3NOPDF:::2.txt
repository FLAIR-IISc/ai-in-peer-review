This paper establishes a relationship between parameter-fit quality and partition similarity in network clustering, focusing on the Preference Frame Model (PFM) and the Stochastic Block Model (SBM). The central claim is a locally convex-like property: among clusterings that fit the data/model parameters equally well, the resulting partitions must be close under standard misclassification-type distances. Equivalences among several natural clustering distance measures are formalized and proved, and the claims are supported with both theory and experiments.

Strengths:
- Clear problem formulation and lucid exposition. The paper is easy to follow and the core ideas are well motivated.
- Conceptual contribution: showing that near-optimal parameter fits imply proximity in partition space provides a useful geometric view of the clustering landscape, which can help explain stability and identifiability phenomena in network models.
- Breadth: results are presented for both PFM and SBM, suggesting the phenomenon is not model-specific.
- Theoretical and empirical validation: proofs are complemented by experiments that are aligned with the claims.

Limitations and concerns:
- Novelty relative to recent literature is not fully established. There are closely related works (e.g., Peng, Sun, and Zanetti, COLT 2015; Chao, Lu, Ma, Zhou, arXiv 2015) with a similar flavor regarding equivalences of metrics or stability of clusterings under network models. The paper would benefit from a sharper positioning: what assumptions or regimes are new here, what technical hurdles are overcome, and what insights cannot be obtained from prior results?
- Theoretical contributions appear to be relatively direct extensions of known techniques. While the unification is useful, the paper should clarify where the nontrivial steps lie (e.g., which parts go beyond standard concentration, coupling, or perturbation arguments).
- Applications to detecting “clusterability” are mentioned as a motivation, but no formal results are provided. Without a concrete detection or certification criterion (with guarantees and experiments), the practical impact of this angle remains unclear.
- Scope and assumptions could be discussed more explicitly. For example, what regimes of sparsity/density, block separation, or degree heterogeneity are required? Are the equivalence results asymptotic only, or do they yield nonasymptotic bounds with explicit constants? How robust are they to model misspecification or overlapping communities?
- Experimental evaluation, while supportive, would be more compelling with comparisons across multiple distance measures and with stress tests (e.g., varying noise levels, number of blocks, degree variability) to illustrate the boundaries of the theory.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Clarify the precise novelty versus Peng–Sun–Zanetti (2015) and Chao–Lu–Ma–Zhou (2015). A side-by-side comparison of assumptions, metrics, and conclusions would help, along with a discussion of how your proofs differ or generalize prior arguments.
- Make the locally convex-like statement as tight and transparent as possible: specify the exact conditions under which equivalence of fit implies small misclassification error, and provide finite-sample bounds. If asymptotic, indicate the rate, dependence on key parameters (e.g., n, number of blocks K, separation), and whether constants are uniform over the model class.
- If feasible, include at least one formal result on clusterability detection (e.g., a computable criterion that certifies when all near-optimal fits are close in partition distance, with guarantees), or otherwise temper that claim in the abstract and conclusions.
- Expand the empirical section to:
  - Compare multiple clustering distance measures side-by-side, illustrating the claimed equivalences quantitatively.
  - Vary network regimes (sparse vs. moderately dense, balanced vs. unbalanced blocks, degree-corrected scenarios if relevant) to show robustness.
  - Include negative or boundary cases where the equivalence weakens, to delineate the theory’s applicability.
- Discuss generality: could the results extend beyond PFM/SBM (e.g., to degree-corrected SBMs, mixed-membership models), or is there an obstacle? Even a brief discussion would enhance impact.
- Provide intuition connecting your results to algorithmic practice. For example, do they explain why different community detection methods with similar likelihood scores often yield nearly identical partitions, or why consensus clustering is effective?

Overall assessment:
The paper presents a clean and potentially useful property relating model fit to partition proximity in network clustering, supported by both proofs and experiments. The writing is strong and the core message is clear. However, the theoretical contributions seem close to existing lines of work, and the paper currently underplays the distinctions and new insights relative to contemporaneous literature. Strengthening the positioning, tightening the formal statements (with explicit regimes and bounds), and delivering at least one concrete, theoretically justified application to clusterability detection would substantially improve the paper’s significance. As it stands, I view the contribution as solid but incremental; with the suggested clarifications and extensions, it could be a valuable addition to the theory of network clustering.
