- Proposes a model-free framework that validates graph clusterings by fitting block-models as certificates of stability and checking spectral-norm fit.
- Given (G, C, M), fits model parameters compatible with C and shows good fit implies any equally good clustering is close to C in ME or degree-weighted ME.
- Instantiates the framework for PFM (spectral estimator, subspace alignment, Davis–Kahan bounds) and SBM (MLE-based bound on adjacency).
- Leverages existing model-based recovery results to derive alternative model-free bounds.
- Experiments on synthetic PFM, perturbed PFM, LFR, and Political Blogs show PFM-based guarantees are often informative while SBM bounds are typically looser.
- Builds on spectral recovery and perturbation theory and differs by replacing generative assumptions with measurable goodness-of-fit conditions.
- Contrasts with objective-based stability analyses by using model fit as the proxy for cluster quality.
- Clear high-level idea converting model-based recovery into computable, data-dependent guarantees.
- Concrete, observable estimators and bounds for PFM and SBM with transparent dependence on eigengaps and fit error.
- Uses standard perturbation tools yielding interpretable proofs.
- Experiments demonstrate feasibility of computing the proposed bounds on real and synthetic graphs.
- Presentation/motivation underdeveloped and may give the false impression of algorithmic novelty when novelty is in the guarantees.
- Numerous typos and notational inconsistencies (e.g., confusion about L̂ vs. I − L̂ and symbols like δ, ε).
- SBM bounds are often vacuous in practice (δ > 1), limiting practical impact without tighter bounds.
- Several results rely on strong assumptions (spectral-norm closeness, nontrivial eigengaps) with limited practical guidance.
- Experimental depth is limited—needs sensitivity analyses (K variation, imbalance, degree heterogeneity) and comparisons to other stability metrics.
- Technically sound overall, but some assumptions (e.g., Assumption 5) are strong; full proofs are in the supplement.
- Moderately clear but requires editing, consolidated definitions, and emphasis that contribution is in guarantees not algorithms.
- Moderate originality: useful reframing of model fit for validation; estimators themselves are largely standard.
- Potentially useful for practitioners seeking post hoc validation; PFM results are more promising than SBM.
- Pros for acceptance: clean, computable framework bridging model-based theory and model-free validation with accessible proofs.
- Cons for acceptance: presentation and editorial issues, mixed empirical utility (especially SBM), and notation problems.
- Suggestions: reframe algorithm sections as estimator descriptions, standardize notation, provide practical guidance for ε/K and small eigengaps, tighten SBM bounds (e.g., regularization), and expand experiments.
- Overall: interesting and potentially useful but needs clearer writing and stronger empirical evidence; reviewer leans borderline accept conditional on substantial improvements.
