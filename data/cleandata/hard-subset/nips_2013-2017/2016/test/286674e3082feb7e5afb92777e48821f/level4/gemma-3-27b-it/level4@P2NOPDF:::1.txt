This paper presents novel theoretical results for graph clustering that avoid strong assumptions about the graph’s underlying generative process – a significant departure from methods reliant on models like the stochastic block model. The core idea is elegantly stated: if a clustering method finds a good fit for a graph, any other equally well-fitting clustering must be structurally similar. This is a compelling and important contribution, offering distribution-free guarantees for graph clustering. Despite page limitations necessitating some content be moved to supplementary materials, the paper remains remarkably clear and accessible without requiring reference to those materials. 

My only minor stylistic concern relates to the notation; the use of “hats” to denote observed quantities (e.g., \hat{L} for the observed Laplacian) feels somewhat unconventional, but doesn’t significantly detract from the overall clarity. The presented results are interesting, thoughtfully explained, and effectively illustrated. However, they are not without limitations.

The experimental evaluation reveals that, for some real-world graphs, significant smoothing is required to reveal structure consistent with the theoretical framework, while more traditional algorithms can directly recover the underlying ground truth. More critically, two key limitations deserve attention. First, the primary result concerning the Partition Function Metric (PFM) employs a non-standard distance measure between clusterings. While the authors argue for its naturalness, this claim is debatable, and the use of a less conventional metric may limit broader adoption and comparison. Second, the analysis relies on a “proxy” model approach. A clustering is first obtained, then a model (e.g., a PFM) is *reconstructed* from that clustering and compared to the original graph, rather than directly evaluating the generative model presumed to have created the clustering. 

This reconstruction approach, while natural within the paper's chosen context, imposes a strong constraint on the definition of a “good” clustering – specifically, that the graph is well-approximated in the normalized Laplacian sense. While such constraints are necessary to achieve theoretical results, it is important to consider their implications, especially given the framework's performance on some real-world examples where substantial smoothing is required. Understanding the sensitivity of these results to the chosen proxy model and Laplacian approximation would be valuable. Overall, this is a strong paper with a significant theoretical contribution, but these limitations should be carefully considered.
