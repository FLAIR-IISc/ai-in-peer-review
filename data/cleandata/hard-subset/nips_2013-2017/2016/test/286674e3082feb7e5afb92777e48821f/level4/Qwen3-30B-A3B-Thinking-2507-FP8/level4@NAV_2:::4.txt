

This paper introduces a general framework for providing theoretical guarantees on clustering stability without assuming that data is generated from a specific model. The core idea is that if a clustering C fits the observed graph G well according to a model-based estimator, then any other clustering C' that also fits G well must be close to C. The authors demonstrate this framework by deriving model-free guarantees for the Preference Frame Model (PFM) and Stochastic Block Model (SBM), with all quantities in the guarantees computable directly from the data. The paper further shows how these model-free results can strengthen existing model-based recovery guarantees. However, the experimental results suggest that the practical utility of these guarantees is limited, as the conditions required for the theorems to hold are often not satisfied in practice.

The paper's main contribution is a valuable theoretical framework for model-free clustering validation, which is important for both theoretical and practical applications. However, several concerns arise regarding the presentation and scope of the work. First, since PFM encompasses DC-SBM, which in turn includes SBM, it is unclear why these models are treated as distinct cases. The authors use the Laplacian for PFM but the adjacency matrix for SBM, and they report weighted ME for PFM but standard ME for SBM—these differences should be justified. The absence of results for DC-SBM is also notable, especially given its intermediate position between SBM and PFM.

Regarding technical details, Proposition 10 references "< M', M >F" without defining this notation. Assumption 5's formulation of ||L̂² - L²||F is ambiguous—does it mean the Frobenius norm of the squared Laplacians or the square of the Frobenius norm of the difference? The rationale for squaring the Laplacians needs clarification.

The framework's focus on disjoint clusters with fixed cluster counts is restrictive. The paper does not address cases where different clustering algorithms produce different numbers of clusters (e.g., one algorithm suggesting K clusters while another suggests K+1). The current framework cannot capture such cases where one clustering is a refinement of another.

The related work section omits several relevant model-free clustering results, including "Clustering with Spectral Norm and the k-means Algorithm" (Kumar and Kannan, 2010), "Clustering under Perturbation Resilience" (Balcan et al., 2012), and "Clustering under Approximation Stability" (Balcan et al., 2013). The paper should clarify how its stability notion relates to these prior works.

The experimental results reveal that PFM tolerates extremely small perturbations before the stability conditions fail, which undermines the practical utility of the guarantees. The claim that "large eigengap makes PFM(G, C) differ from PFM(G, C0) even for very small perturbations" lacks proper justification. Furthermore, the political blog dataset shows that smoothing the graph (by raising adjacency matrix to a power) improves stability, yet this contradicts the paper's assertion about the sensitivity to perturbations.

The paper claims that SBM conditions are "very hard to satisfy" (lines 248-250), yet Figure 1's caption states "For SBM, δ is always greater than δ0" without clarifying which subplot this refers to. If SBM results aren't plotted, this should be explicitly stated.

The theorems for PFM use weighted ME distance while the experiments measure standard ME—this inconsistency needs explanation. Additionally, the paper's extensive notation (B, C, Y) is inconsistently defined across sections, which could confuse readers. For example, B and C are used with different meanings in PFM (Section 3.1) and SBM (Section 3.2) contexts. Adopting more consistent notation would improve clarity.

Finally, Section 4.4 on "Clusterability and resilience" would be better positioned as a subsection within the related work, rather than as a standalone section. The paper's technical contributions are interesting, but the practical limitations and presentation issues significantly reduce its impact.
