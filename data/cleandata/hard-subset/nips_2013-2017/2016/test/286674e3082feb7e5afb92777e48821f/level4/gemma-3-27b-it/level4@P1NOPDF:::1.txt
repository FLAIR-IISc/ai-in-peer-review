The authors present new theoretical results on graph clustering that make no assumptions about the graph’s generative process – the observed graph is not assumed to be generated by a specific model like the stochastic block model. The core idea is that if a clustering method finds a clustering that adequately fits the graph (according to a defined criterion), then any other clustering that fits equally well should be similar to the first. This is a very interesting paper that addresses an important problem by providing distribution-free results for graph clustering. Despite some content being moved to the supplementary materials due to conference page limits, the paper remains clear and self-contained, requiring no reference to the additional material for understanding.

I am not entirely convinced by the authors’ choice to use “hats” to denote observed quantities (e.g., \hat{L} for the observed Laplacian), but this is a minor point. Overall, the paper is reasonably clear, and the results are both interesting and well-explained with illustrative examples. However, the work does have limitations. The experimental results demonstrate that, for some real-world data, graph smoothing is required to recover structure within the theoretical framework, whereas many classical algorithms can recover the ground truth directly. 

Furthermore, two key limitations are apparent. First, the main result for the Probabilistic Finite Mixture (PFM) model employs a non-standard distance measure between clusterings. While the authors argue this measure may be more natural than classical alternatives, this claim is debatable. Second, the results rely on a “proxy” model approach; a model (such as a PFM) is reconstructed *from* a clustering and then compared to the original graph, rather than comparing the graph to the model used to *generate* the clustering. This approach is natural given the authors’ context, but it also strongly constrains the definition of a “good” clustering for a given graph – in this case, requiring that the graph be well-approximated in the normalized Laplacian sense. This constraint is necessary to achieve concrete results, but one might question its impact, particularly considering the framework's limitations on some real-world examples. It would be valuable to understand how these restrictions affect the broader applicability and interpretation of the results.
