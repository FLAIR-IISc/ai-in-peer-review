The authors propose novel theoretical results on graph clustering that circumvent the need to assume a specific generative model for the graph. The central idea is that if a clustering method finds a clustering that adequately fits the observed graph (according to a defined criterion), then any other clustering that fits equally well must be close to the original. This paper addresses a significant problem by providing distribution-free guarantees for graph clustering, and is, in my opinion, very interesting. Despite some content being moved to supplementary materials due to page limits, the paper remains clear and accessible without constant reference to those materials. While the use of “hats” to denote observed quantities (e.g.,  𝐿̂ for the observed Laplacian) might not be ideal, the paper is generally well-written and the results are thoroughly explained and illustrated.

The results, however, are not without limitations. The experimental section demonstrates that, on some real-world datasets, the graph requires smoothing to satisfy the theoretical framework’s conditions for recoverability, whereas many conventional algorithms can recover the underlying structure directly. Furthermore, two key limitations are apparent: 1) the main result for the Preference Frame Model (PFM) relies on a non-standard distance measure between clusterings. Although the authors argue for its naturalness, this claim is debatable. 2) The results are based on a “proxy” model approach: given a clustering, a model (e.g., a PFM) is reconstructed and then compared to the original graph, rather than comparing it to the (unknown) model used to generate the clustering. This is a natural approach within the authors' chosen context, but it strongly constrains the definition of a “good” clustering – here, it requires that the graph is well-approximated in terms of its normalized Laplacian. This is necessary to obtain theoretical results, but raises questions regarding the impact of these constraints, especially given the framework’s limitations on some real-world examples.

**Summary of the Paper:**

The paper introduces a framework for providing theoretical guarantees for graph clustering algorithms without assuming the data originates from a specific model like the Stochastic Block Model (SBM). The core idea is to demonstrate that if a clustering *C* fits a graph *G* well, any other clustering *C'* that also fits *G* well must be close to *C*. The authors instantiate this framework for the SBM and PFM models, and also show a connection between model-free and model-based recovery results.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The work presents a novel approach to clustering validation by offering distribution-free guarantees, a significant departure from traditional model-based methods.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The theorems are clearly stated and appear to be mathematically sound.
*   **Connection to Existing Work:** The authors effectively demonstrate how their framework relates to and strengthens existing results in model-based recovery.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and accessible, despite the technical complexity of the topic.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experiments, despite limitations discussed below, provide some evidence of the framework’s applicability.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Distance Measure:** The use of the weighted Misclassification Error (wME) distance for the PFM result is unconventional and its justification is not entirely convincing.
*   **Proxy Model:** The reconstruction of a model from a clustering (the “proxy” model approach) might be overly restrictive, limiting the applicability of the results to scenarios where a model can closely approximate the observed graph.
*   **Practicality:** The assumptions required for the theorems to hold may be difficult to verify in practice.  The need for graph smoothing in the experimental section highlights this challenge.
*   **Sensitivity to Eigengap:** The results appear sensitive to the eigengap of the Laplacian, which may not be large enough in many real-world graphs to guarantee stability.
*   **SBM Result Limitations:** The SBM results appear less powerful and more restrictive than the PFM results.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important and open problem in graph clustering.
*   The theoretical framework is novel and provides valuable insights into the stability of clustering solutions.
*   The connection to existing model-based results is a significant contribution.
*   The paper is generally well-written and clear.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The practical applicability of the results is limited by the strong assumptions required and the use of a non-standard distance measure.
*   The proxy model approach may be overly restrictive.
*   The SBM results are less compelling than the PFM results.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid theoretical paper that addresses an important problem. While the practical limitations are a concern, the novelty and theoretical rigor of the work are commendable. I believe the paper would be a valuable contribution to the NIPS conference, but would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations and potential extensions of the framework. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address these concerns.
