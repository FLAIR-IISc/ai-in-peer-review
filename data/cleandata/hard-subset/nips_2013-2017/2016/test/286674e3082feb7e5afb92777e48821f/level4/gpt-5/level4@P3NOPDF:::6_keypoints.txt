- Paper aims for a model-free theoretical framework for graph clustering but is vague and lacks precision to support its claims.
- Strength: ambitious and potentially impactful goal of unifying model-free guarantees for graph clustering.
- Strength: intends to provide general theorems that could apply beyond specific generative models.
- Strength: if formalized, could bridge empirical practice and theory.
- Key weakness: central statements (e.g., “fits G well”, “close to C”) are informal and undefined, making results unclear.
- Key weakness: several claims risk being vacuous without formal definitions, metrics, or assumptions.
- Key weakness: “model-free” positioning is unsupported—hidden or informal assumptions appear to underlie results.
- Key weakness: problem setup is imprecise (graph class, directed/undirected, weighted, sparsity, noise, output type, performance metric).
- Key weakness: novelty relative to spectral clustering, modularity, SDPs, stability frameworks, and distribution-free bounds is not convincingly established.
- Key weakness: theoretical claims lack clear proofs, tight conditions, necessity examples, and rely on informal “standard” conditions.
- Key weakness: no discussion of empirical validation; absence of experiments weakens practical relevance.
- Requested clarification: precisely define input/output, performance metrics, and distance notions used in theorems.
- Requested clarification: enumerate explicit assumptions (degree distributions, sparsity regimes, cluster size, separation, robustness) and operationalize “model-free.”
- Requested clarification: formalize Theorem 1 and Proposition 3 with explicit loss/objective, metrics, bounds, and listed recovery conditions.
- Requested work: compare and position results against existing literature and state whether results generalize or differ from known regimes.
- Requested work: provide full proofs or rigorous sketches, and instantiate generic theorems into concrete corollaries for common algorithms.
- Requested work: state computational tractability versus existential guarantees and clarify algorithmic implications.
- Requested work: include experiments on synthetic and real graphs with standard metrics and baselines to support claims.
- Requested work: improve clarity, consistent notation, eliminate informal qualifiers, and present theorems with fully specified conditions.
- Overall assessment: important problem but current form is too informal and unconvincing; recommendation to reject with encouragement to resubmit after major formalization and validation.
