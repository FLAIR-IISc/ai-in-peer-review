This paper introduces a novel and valuable theoretical framework for providing performance guarantees for graph clustering in a distribution-free setting. In contrast to the extensive literature on Stochastic Block Models (SBM) and their variants, which provides recovery guarantees under the assumption that the observed graph is generated from a specific model, this work develops stability guarantees that depend only on checkable statistics of the data itself. The core idea is to show that if a given clustering provides a "good fit" to the graph, then any other clustering that also fits well must be structurally close to the first. This approach cleverly bridges the gap between model-free and model-based analysis.

The primary strength of this paper is its originality and significance. Addressing the problem of guarantees without a generative model is a crucial step toward making theoretical results for clustering more applicable to real-world data. The theoretical contributions for both the Preference Frame Model (PFM) and SBM are interesting, appear technically sound, and are a welcome addition to the field. The paper is also well-written and clear, effectively communicating a complex theoretical argument. The empirical illustrations are helpful in demonstrating how the derived bounds behave in practice.

However, the work has several limitations that may affect its practical impact. First, the framework's definition of a "good fit" is quite restrictive. It relies on a proxy-model approach where a clustering is considered good if its reconstructed model's Laplacian closely approximates the observed graph's Laplacian. This specific criterion strongly constrains the notion of a good clustering and may not align with other clustering objectives. Second, the main result for the PFM model relies on a non-standard, degree-weighted misclassification error. The naturalness of this distance metric is debatable, and its use seems driven more by the proof technique than by fundamental clustering principles. Finally, the experiments reveal a practical limitation: some real-world graphs require a "smoothing" step for the theoretical conditions to hold, whereas classical algorithms can often recover meaningful structure without this preprocessing.

In summary, this is a strong paper presenting a novel and important theoretical direction. While its practical applicability is constrained by its restrictive modeling assumptions and the need for data smoothing in some cases, the originality and quality of the theoretical contribution are significant. I recommend acceptance, but encourage the authors to more explicitly discuss the implications of their framework's limitations. A minor stylistic point is that using "hats" on observed quantities is unconventional, as they typically denote estimators.
