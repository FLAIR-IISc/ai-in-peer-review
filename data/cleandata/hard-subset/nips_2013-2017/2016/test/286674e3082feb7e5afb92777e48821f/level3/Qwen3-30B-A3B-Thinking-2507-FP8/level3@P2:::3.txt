

**Review for "Graph Clustering: Block-models and model free results"**

This paper introduces a framework for graph clustering that provides theoretical guarantees without assuming the data comes from a specified model. The authors propose a "model-free" approach that builds on existing model-based clustering methods (SBM, DC-SBM, PFM), showing how to obtain guarantees based on goodness-of-fit measures rather than model assumptions. The main contribution is a generic theorem (Theorem 1) stating that if a clustering fits the data well under a given model, then any other clustering that fits the data well must be close to the original clustering. The authors instantiate this theorem for PFM and SBM, providing specific bounds for these cases.

The paper relates well to previous work in the field. The Stochastic Block Model and its extensions have been extensively studied at NIPS (e.g., [1, 17, 18, 20]), and the authors build on spectral clustering methods (e.g., [15, 17, 18]). The work also connects to recent literature on clusterability and stability (e.g., [13, 14, 16]).

**Strengths:**

1. **Quality:** The technical content is sound and the theorems are rigorously proven. The authors provide clear definitions of goodness-of-fit measures and clustering distances, and their proofs follow standard perturbation theory techniques. The experimental evaluation supports their theoretical claims.

2. **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and the algorithms are clearly presented. The notation is consistent and the mathematical derivations are easy to follow for readers familiar with spectral clustering.

3. **Significance:** The framework provides a valuable bridge between model-based and model-free approaches to clustering. The ability to validate clusterings without model assumptions is important for practical applications where model assumptions are often questionable.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Originality:** The core contribution appears to be a rephrasing of existing model-based guarantees rather than a fundamentally new approach. The "model-free" guarantees are essentially a reinterpretation of existing results, rather than a novel theoretical framework. Similar stability results have been explored in the literature (e.g., [13, 14, 16]), and the authors don't sufficiently differentiate their approach.

2. **Motivation:** The paper fails to adequately explain why this model-free framework is necessary or what problem it solves that existing methods don't address. The motivation section is weak and doesn't clearly articulate the gap in the literature. The paper presents a series of algorithms without connecting them to a clear problem statement.

3. **Significance:** While the framework is theoretically interesting, the practical impact is limited. The conditions required for the guarantees (e.g., small δ relative to δ0) are often difficult to satisfy in practice, as shown in the experiments where the SBM guarantees were not informative. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't explore how to make the conditions more practical.

4. **Presentation:** The paper reads like a list of algorithms rather than a cohesive contribution. The connection to the broader literature could be stronger, and the authors don't clearly explain why their framework is superior to existing approaches for practical clustering tasks.

**Pro and Con Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- The paper provides a clear theoretical framework that connects model-based and model-free clustering.
- The technical content is sound and the proofs are rigorous.
- The experimental results support the theoretical claims.

*Con:*
- The contribution appears incremental rather than novel.
- The motivation for the work is weak and the problem statement is unclear.
- The practical utility of the framework is limited by stringent conditions for the guarantees to hold.
- The paper doesn't clearly differentiate itself from existing work on clustering stability.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a technically sound but incremental contribution to the literature on graph clustering. While the framework they propose is theoretically interesting and connects existing model-based results to a model-free setting, the paper fails to clearly articulate the motivation for this work or demonstrate its superiority over existing methods. The technical content is solid, but the paper reads more like a compilation of known results rather than a novel contribution.

The paper would be significantly stronger if the authors could provide a clearer motivation, better differentiate their approach from existing work, and demonstrate more compelling practical applications of their framework. As it stands, the paper's contribution is limited and the significance of the work is not sufficiently established.

I recommend rejection due to limited novelty and insufficient motivation. The paper would benefit from a major revision to clarify the contribution and its significance in the context of existing work.
