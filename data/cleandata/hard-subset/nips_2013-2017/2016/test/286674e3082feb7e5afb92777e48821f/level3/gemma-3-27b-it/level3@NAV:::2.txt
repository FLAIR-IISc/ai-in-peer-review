## Review of "Graph Clustering: Block-models and model free results"

This paper presents a novel framework for establishing theoretical guarantees for graph clustering without relying on the assumption that the data originates from a specific block model. The authors demonstrate a “locally convex-like” property for clustering under the Stochastic Block Model (PFM) and SBM, proving that clusterings that equally well fit model parameters will be close in misclassification distance. A significant contribution is demonstrating the equivalence of multiple clustering distance measures for networks. The work is well-motivated, building on recent progress in model-based clustering while addressing its key limitation – the model assumption. 

The paper’s strength lies in its elegant theoretical development. The authors successfully extend existing results to derive model-free guarantees, and importantly, highlight the connection between model-based and model-free approaches, offering potential for strengthening existing recovery theorems. The experimental validation, while relatively simple, provides initial support for the theoretical findings, showcasing the applicability of the proposed framework. The clarity of the writing is commendable; the core ideas are clearly explained, and the notation is consistently used.

However, the paper’s significance is somewhat tempered by the relatively simple extension of known results and the lack of substantial practical application. While the authors mention potential applications such as detecting clusterability, these remain largely unexplored. The experimental section, while helpful, is limited in scope and does not provide a comprehensive evaluation of the method's performance on diverse datasets. The derived bounds, particularly for the SBM, appear quite restrictive, limiting their immediate practical utility. The comparison to related work is solid, acknowledging relevant papers (e.g., Peng et al. 2015, Chao et al. 2015) but could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of the trade-offs between their approach and existing methods. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel framework providing model-free guarantees for graph clustering.
*   Sound theoretical foundation with clear proofs and connections to existing work.
*   Clear and well-written presentation of complex ideas.
*   Demonstrates equivalence between multiple clustering distance measures.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Theoretical results are, in some sense, extensions of existing work rather than entirely groundbreaking.
*   Limited exploration of practical applications beyond mentioning potential use cases.
*   Experimental validation is limited in scope and does not demonstrate substantial practical advantage.
*   Derived bounds may be too restrictive for practical application in some cases.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of graph clustering. While not a breakthrough, the paper's novelty and clear presentation make it a worthwhile addition to the literature. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, primarily focusing on expanding the discussion of practical implications and potentially conducting more extensive experimental evaluation.
