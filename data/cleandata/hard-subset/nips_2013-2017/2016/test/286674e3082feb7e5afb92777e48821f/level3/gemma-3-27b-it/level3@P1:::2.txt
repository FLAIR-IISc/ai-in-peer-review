This paper presents a novel framework for clustering graphs, providing theoretical guarantees without assuming the data originates from a specific model like the Stochastic Block Model (SBM) or Preference Frame Model (PFM). Instead, the guarantees are based on how well a clustering *fits* the observed data, establishing a connection between model-free and model-based recovery approaches. The core idea is that if a clustering fits the data well, any other clustering that also fits well will be a small perturbation of the original, effectively capturing the underlying data structure. This is demonstrated through theorems for both the PFM and SBM, complemented by experimental validation.

The work builds upon a rich body of literature in model-based clustering, notably the advancements in understanding SBM, Degree-Corrected SBM (DC-SBM), and PFM, as highlighted in references [1, 17, 20]. The authors acknowledge the limitations of relying on model assumptions and aim to bridge the gap by offering a more generalizable framework.  Related work in stability-based clustering [16, 14, 5] is discussed, pointing out how this paper’s approach differs by focusing on model-based clustering rather than cost-based methods, and by utilizing a novel perspective of “turning around” existing recovery theorems.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The proposed framework for model-free clustering with guarantees is a significant contribution. Existing work primarily focuses on model-based recovery, and this paper offers a compelling alternative.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The theorems are well-defined and supported by rigorous proofs (detailed in the supplement). The approach of leveraging existing model-based recovery results is particularly clever.
* **Generality:** The framework is applicable to multiple block models (PFM and SBM demonstrated) and lays the groundwork for extension to others. The adaptability to weighted graphs is a welcome addition.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and explains the core concepts clearly. The notation is well-defined, and the logical flow of the arguments is easy to follow.
* **Experimental Validation:** The experiments, although not extensive, provide evidence supporting the theoretical results. The comparison of δ values between PFM and SBM is insightful.
* **Practical Implications:** The framework allows for validation of clustering results without prior knowledge of the underlying data-generating process, which is valuable in real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Conservatism of Bounds:** The theoretical guarantees, especially for the SBM, appear quite restrictive. The experimental results confirm this, as the conditions for stability are often not met for the SBM. This is noted by the authors themselves.
* **Limited Exploration of Applications:** While the paper mentions potential applications like assessing clusterability, these remain largely unexplored.  Formal results demonstrating these applications would strengthen the contribution.
* **Dependency on Eigengap:** The assumption of a non-zero eigengap, crucial for the PFM analysis, may not always hold in practice. This limits the applicability of the results.
* **Comparison to Existing Stability Results:** The comparison to related stability results [16, 14] could be more in-depth.  A more detailed discussion of the trade-offs between different approaches would be beneficial.
* **Supplement Reliance:** While reasonable, a significant portion of the technical details and proofs resides in the supplement. This can hinder accessibility for readers.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper tackles a significant problem in network clustering – providing guarantees without model assumptions.
* The theoretical framework is novel, rigorously proven, and demonstrably connects model-free and model-based approaches.
* The experimental results, while limited, support the theoretical findings.
* The writing is clear and the presentation is well-organized.
* The paper offers a valuable contribution to the theoretical foundations of network clustering.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The practical applicability of the results may be limited by the restrictive conditions required to satisfy the theoretical guarantees, especially for the SBM.
* The exploration of potential applications is superficial.
* The dependence on the eigengap assumption might restrict applicability in certain scenarios.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and theoretically sound contribution to the field of network clustering. While the practical limitations of the results should be acknowledged, the framework itself is valuable and opens up new avenues for research. The clear writing and well-organized presentation further enhance the paper's quality.  I believe the strengths significantly outweigh the weaknesses, and **recommend acceptance**.  Future work could focus on tightening the bounds for the SBM, exploring the applications of the framework in greater detail, and investigating extensions to other network models.
