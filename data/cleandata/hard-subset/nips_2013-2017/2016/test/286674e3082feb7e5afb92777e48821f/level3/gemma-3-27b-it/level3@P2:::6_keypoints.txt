- Proposes a framework for clustering graphs with guarantees that do not assume a specific data-generating model (focus on SBM, DC-SBM, PFM).  
- Central idea: derive guarantees based on how well a chosen clustering fits the observed graph rather than assuming a generative model.  
- Provides theorems connecting model-free fit-based guarantees to model-based recovery results for PFM and SBM.  
- Builds on extensive prior work in spectral and graph clustering (e.g., Ng et al., Rohe et al., Karrer et al.).  
- Motivation is natural and valuable: offering guarantees without strong model assumptions.  
- Contribution feels incremental — more a reinterpretation of existing model-based results than a fundamentally new approach.  
- Theorem 1 (core claim) is stated vaguely; “fits G well” is not defined concretely until later and remains arbitrary.  
- Proposition 3 is informal and relies on unspecified “standard recovery conditions,” weakening its value.  
- Distances between clusterings are defined, but their link to meaningful statistical properties is not always clear.  
- Proofs are sketched and defer heavily to the supplement, lacking sufficient detail in the main text for verification.  
- Claim of simplifying existing proof techniques is not convincingly demonstrated.  
- Experimental validation is weak: lacks comparative analysis and fails to show practical benefit over existing methods.  
- Clarity issues: background is lengthy/repetitive, many new definitions introduced without enough motivation.  
- Connection between theoretical results and experiments is weak; it's unclear how theorems validate empirical clustering beyond checking conditions.  
- Originality is limited; related work in robust/stability clustering addresses similar themes and the paper reuses existing recovery theorems.  
- Significance is limited by restrictive conditions (e.g., strong eigengap assumptions) that reduce practical applicability.  
- Provides computable bounds and a potential bridge between model-free and model-based techniques (argument for acceptance).  
- Addresses an important problem and offers a framework to validate clustering results (argument for acceptance).  
- Major reasons against acceptance: lack of technical rigor, vague definitions, incremental novelty, restrictive assumptions, and weak experiments.  
- Overall recommendation: reject — paper needs stronger theoretical rigor, clearer definitions, and more convincing experimental evidence.
