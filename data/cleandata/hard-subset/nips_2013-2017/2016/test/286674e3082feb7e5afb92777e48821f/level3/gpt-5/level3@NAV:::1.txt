This paper proposes distribution-free guarantees for graph clustering by showing that, within a specified model class, if one clustering fits the observed graph well, then any other equally well-fitting clustering must be close. The framework is instantiated for PFM and SBM via computable goodness-of-fit conditions (spectral-norm closeness to a fitted model; eigengap assumptions) and distance between clusterings. The analysis uses Davis–Kahan-type perturbation bounds and leverages prior model-based results (e.g., Ng–Jordan–Weiss; Rohe–Chatterjee–Yu; Qin–Rohe; Abbe–Sandon; Meilă’s local equivalence). Experiments on synthetic PFM, perturbed PFM, LFR, and Political Blogs illustrate when guarantees are informative and how smoothing affects stability.

Quality: The theoretical development is technically sound and novel. Proofs appear careful (though many details are deferred), and the constants are explicit and data-computable, which is valuable. However, the main PFM guarantee relies on a non-standard metric (degree-weighted misclassification), whose naturalness is debatable; a more extensive justification or robustness analysis to alternative distances would strengthen the contribution. The proxy-model approach (fitting PFM(G, C) or SBM(G, C) and measuring normalized Laplacian/adjacency approximation) narrows what counts as a “good” clustering and likely explains why SBM bounds are often noninformative.

Clarity: The paper is generally clear and readable despite the supplemental proofs. A minor stylistic issue: placing hats on observed quantities (e.g., Ĥ for the observed Laplacian) is unconventional and can be confusing; consider reserving hats for estimates and using plain symbols for data.

Originality: The distribution-free stability perspective for block-model-based clustering, together with the blueprint that reuses model-based techniques, is original and interesting relative to prior cost-based stability and model-based recovery literatures.

Significance: The framework addresses an important problem and may stimulate follow-on work. That said, the strong modeling restrictions (normalized Laplacian approximation; eigengap; specific distances) and the empirical need for smoothing on some real graphs limit immediate real-world impact.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel distribution-free theory with computable bounds; bridges model-based and model-free views.
- Clear connection to and strengthening of prior results; rigorous, well-commented proofs and experiments.
- Practical diagnostic for cluster stability when assumptions hold.

Cons for acceptance:
- Reliance on degree-weighted ME distance; unclear robustness to alternative metrics.
- Proxy-model fit around normalized Laplacian/adjacency strongly constrains applicability; SBM bounds often uninformative.
- Empirical limitations (e.g., need for smoothing where classical methods recover without it).

Suggestions: justify or relax the clustering distance; assess sensitivity to norm choices and model classes (including DC-SBM); compare guarantees with/without smoothing; discuss negative guarantees and broader clusterability notions.
