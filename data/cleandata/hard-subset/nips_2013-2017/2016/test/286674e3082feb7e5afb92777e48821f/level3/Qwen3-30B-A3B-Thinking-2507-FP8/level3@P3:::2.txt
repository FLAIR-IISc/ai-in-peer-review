

# Review of "Graph Clustering: Block-models and model free results"

This paper introduces a novel framework for providing theoretical guarantees for graph clustering without assuming the data comes from a specific model (such as SBM or PFM). The core insight is that if two clusterings fit the observed graph data equally well under a given model, then they must be close in misclassification distance. This approach bridges the gap between model-based and model-free clustering guarantees.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it establishes relationships between different clustering distance measures for network data, showing that weighted and unweighted misclassification distances are equivalent under appropriate conditions. Second, it analyzes both the Preference Frame Model (PFM) and Stochastic Block Model (SBM), proving a "locally convex-like" property for clustering under these models. The main theoretical result (Theorem 4 for PFM and Theorem 9 for SBM) demonstrates that if two clusterings fit the data equally well, they must be close in misclassification distance. The authors support their claims with both theoretical proofs and experimental validation across multiple datasets.

The paper relates well to previous work, particularly the recent NIPS 2015 papers by Peng et al. (on spectral clustering stability) and Chao et al. (on clustering guarantees), while making a distinct contribution by establishing a framework that provides model-free guarantees. The theoretical arguments are relatively simple extensions of known results, making the paper accessible to the broader NIPS audience.

Regarding the review criteria:

Quality: The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The authors provide both rigorous theoretical proofs and experimental validation. The assumptions are clearly stated and the results are carefully bounded. The paper acknowledges limitations, such as the more restrictive nature of SBM guarantees compared to PFM, and notes where tighter bounds might be possible.

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written. The core ideas are explained clearly with appropriate mathematical notation. The organization is logical, moving from problem statement to background, main results, and experimental validation. The authors provide sufficient detail for an expert to reproduce their results.

Originality: The framework for model-free clustering guarantees represents a significant conceptual contribution. While the technical arguments are extensions of known results, the specific application to network clustering with the equivalence between model-based and model-free guarantees is novel. The paper clearly distinguishes itself from related work, particularly Peng et al. 2015 and Chao et al. 2015, by focusing on the relationship between model-based recovery and model-free validation.

Significance: The results provide a new perspective on clustering guarantees that could be widely applicable to practitioners. The framework enables practitioners to validate clusterings without assuming the data comes from a specific model, which is a significant practical advantage. The paper notes potential applications in detecting clusterability, though formal results in this direction are lacking. The results advance the state of the art by strengthening existing model-based recovery results and providing a more general framework for clustering guarantees.

Strengths:
- Clear and well-organized presentation
- Strong theoretical foundation with practical computational implications
- Demonstrates equivalence between multiple clustering distance measures
- Provides a bridge between model-based and model-free clustering
- Experimental validation across multiple datasets

Weaknesses:
- Lacks formal results on clusterability (noted as a potential application)
- SBM guarantees are more restrictive than PFM guarantees, which may limit practical utility
- Comparison to related work could be more extensive (though the paper does cite relevant recent work)

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of network clustering. The framework provides a valuable new perspective on clustering guarantees that could have broad applicability. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel framework for model-free clustering guarantees
- Clear connections between model-based and model-free results
- Strong theoretical foundation with practical implications
- Well-written and well-organized
- Provides a useful tool for practitioners to validate clusterings

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited formal results on clusterability (though this is noted as a future direction)
- SBM guarantees are more restrictive than PFM, which might limit some applications

The paper is clearly within the scope of NIPS, addressing a significant problem in network analysis with a theoretically grounded approach that advances the field. I recommend acceptance.
