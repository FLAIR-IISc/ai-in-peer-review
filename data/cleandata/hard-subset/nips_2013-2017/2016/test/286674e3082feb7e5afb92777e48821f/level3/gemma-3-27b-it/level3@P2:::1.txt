This paper presents a novel framework for graph clustering that provides theoretical guarantees without assuming the data originates from a stochastic block model (SBM) or its extensions. The authors demonstrate that if a clustering fits the observed graph well according to a defined goodness-of-fit measure, any other clustering that also fits well must be “close” to the original. This framework is instantiated for the PFM and SBM, and connections to existing model-based recovery theorems are explored, offering both model-free guarantees and improvements to existing model-based results.

The problem addressed is significant. While model-based clustering has seen substantial progress, its reliance on generative model assumptions limits its applicability. Providing guarantees without such assumptions is a valuable contribution, potentially broadening the scope of theoretical results to real-world networks where model fidelity is questionable. The core idea of focusing on goodness-of-fit and clustering stability is both insightful and well-motivated. This work builds upon and connects to a substantial body of research in spectral clustering, stochastic block models (e.g., [1, 17, 20]), and clustering validation (e.g., [11, 14, 16]).

The paper is generally well-written and organized. The authors clearly articulate their approach and provide sufficient detail for the reader to follow the main arguments. While some proofs and details are relegated to the supplement, this is reasonable given the technical nature of the work. The experimental section provides illustrative examples demonstrating the applicability of the theoretical results.

However, there are several weaknesses. First, the use of “hats” (e.g., Ĥ) to denote observed quantities feels somewhat arbitrary and stylistically jarring. While not a major issue, it could be simplified. More substantially, the main result for the PFM relies on a weighted Misclassification Error (wME) distance (3), which while justified by the authors, feels less natural than the standard Misclassification Error. The motivation relating this to the earth-mover’s distance is helpful but may not fully convince all readers of its appropriateness.

A more significant limitation lies in the proxy-model approach. The framework essentially reconstructs a model *from* a clustering and then assesses how well this reconstructed model approximates the observed graph. This inevitably constrains what constitutes a “good” clustering, heavily favoring those well-approximated by the chosen models (PFM or SBM). While the authors acknowledge this, it's a fundamental limitation impacting the broader applicability of the results. The focus on approximating the normalized Laplacian, in particular, might exclude valid clusterings that don't align with this particular representation.  This constraint is necessary to achieve analytical tractability, but it’s important to recognize the trade-off.

The empirical evaluation highlights another practical consideration. The authors note that some real-world graphs require smoothing (e.g., taking powers of the adjacency matrix) before the proposed framework yields meaningful guarantees.  This smoothing step itself introduces a potential distortion of the original graph structure and contrasts with the ability of traditional clustering algorithms to operate directly on the observed data.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novelty: The paper presents a genuinely novel framework for model-free graph clustering guarantees.
*   Theoretical Soundness: The technical arguments appear rigorous and well-supported.
*   Connections to Existing Work: The paper thoughtfully connects to and builds upon a substantial body of existing research.
*   Potential Impact: The results could broaden the applicability of theoretical graph clustering results to real-world scenarios.
*   Clear Presentation: The paper is generally well-written and organized.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Modeling Restrictions: The reliance on a proxy-model approach significantly constrains the types of clusterings that can be guaranteed.
*   Practical Limitations: The need for smoothing in some real-world scenarios reduces the immediate practical impact.
*   Distance Metric (PFM): The choice of the wME distance for the PFM result may be debatable.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and theoretically sound contribution. While the modeling restrictions and practical limitations are significant concerns, they are acknowledged by the authors.  The potential for broadening the applicability of graph clustering theory makes this work valuable.  I believe the paper is of sufficient quality for acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the reviewer's concerns in a revision. Specifically, a more thorough discussion of the limitations imposed by the proxy-model approach and a justification for the choice of the wME distance would be beneficial.  Perhaps exploring alternative distance metrics could be considered.

**Recommendation:** Accept with minor revisions.
