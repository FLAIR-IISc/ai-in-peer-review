

This paper introduces a novel framework for obtaining theoretical guarantees in community detection without assuming the data comes from a specific generative model, addressing a significant limitation in current model-based approaches. The authors propose a "model-free" stability paradigm where clustering guarantees are derived based on how well a model fits the observed data, rather than assuming data originates from a model. This approach connects existing model-based results with model-free guarantees, demonstrating how stability results can be "turned around" to work in the model-free setting.

The paper's main strength lies in its general theoretical framework, which provides concrete, computable guarantees applicable to popular models like SBM and PFM. The authors prove their results with mathematical rigor, demonstrating how the framework can strengthen existing model-based results (e.g., improving error bounds in [20] by a factor of 128). The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear explanations of technical concepts and thoughtful motivation for their error metrics and assumptions. The experimental validation further demonstrates the practical applicability of their framework.

The primary weakness concerns the reliance on spectral algorithms with non-tight guarantees, particularly problematic for very sparse graphs where spectral methods become computationally challenging. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, the paper doesn't provide alternative approaches for sparse settings, which represents a significant gap given the prevalence of sparse networks in practice.

The paper excels in all four evaluation criteria: it's technically sound (Quality), exceptionally clear (Clarity), offers a genuinely novel perspective on community detection stability (Originality), and addresses a fundamental problem with practical implications (Significance). The framework opens new avenues for stability analysis in community detection, making it a valuable contribution to the field.

Pro: Novel framework, rigorous proofs, strong connection to existing literature, excellent writing, practical validation. Con: Spectral method limitations in sparse graphs, lack of alternative approaches for sparse settings.

Overall, this is a strong contribution that advances the theoretical understanding of community detection stability. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to better address the sparse graph limitations.
