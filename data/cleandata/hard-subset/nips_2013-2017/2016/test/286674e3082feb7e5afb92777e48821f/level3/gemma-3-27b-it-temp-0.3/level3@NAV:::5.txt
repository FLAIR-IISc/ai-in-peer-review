## Review of “Graph Clustering: Block-models and model free results”

This paper presents a novel framework for providing correctness guarantees for community detection via spectral clustering, crucially *without* assuming the data originates from a specific generative model like the Stochastic Block Model (SBM). This is a significant contribution, as model assumptions often limit the applicability of theoretical results. The authors demonstrate their approach by deriving model-free guarantees for both the PFM and SBM, and importantly, show connections between model-free and model-based recovery results, even strengthening existing bounds in the latter case. 

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly motivated. The authors carefully explain their error definitions and assumptions, making the work accessible. The progression of ideas is logical, building from a general framework (Theorem 1) to specific instantiations for popular graphical models. The connection to existing work, particularly in spectral clustering and related models (SBM, DC-SBM, PFM), is well-established and appropriately referenced. The inclusion of experimental validation, even if preliminary, strengthens the presentation.

A key strength lies in the generality of the results and the authors’ ability to leverage existing spectral clustering theory. The demonstration that model-free guarantees can, in some cases, improve upon model-based recovery is particularly noteworthy. The paper successfully addresses the important question of stability in community detection solutions, a crucial aspect for real-world applications.

However, the primary weakness lies in the reliance on spectral algorithms and results that, while theoretically sound, can be computationally intractable for very sparse graphs. The authors acknowledge this limitation, and it’s a valid concern. The tightness of the spectral bounds used impacts the practical applicability of the guarantees. While the theoretical framework is strong, the conditions required for the guarantees to hold may be difficult to verify in practice, especially for large, sparse networks. Furthermore, the experimental section, while helpful, could benefit from a more thorough exploration of the sensitivity of the results to parameter choices and different graph structures.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and significant contribution to the field of community detection.
*   Strong theoretical foundation and clear, well-written presentation.
*   Addresses an important and relevant problem (stability of clustering solutions).
*   Demonstrates connections between model-free and model-based approaches.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Computational limitations of the underlying spectral algorithms for sparse graphs.
*   Practical verification of conditions for guarantees may be challenging.



**Overall:** This is a strong paper with a promising start to establishing stability results for community detection. Despite the limitations regarding computational complexity, the theoretical contributions are substantial and warrant acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to further discuss the practical implications of the computational constraints and potentially explore avenues for mitigating them.
