- Proposes a distribution-free framework: if clustering C fits observed graph G under model class M, any other clustering that also fits must be close to C.  
- Avoids assuming G is sampled from a generative model by fitting a proxy model M(G, C) to observed data and measuring fit via spectral or Frobenius norms.  
- Instantiates framework for PFM: aligns leading K-dimensional eigenspace of normalized Laplacian L̂ with subspace implied by C; stability via Davis–Kahan and local clustering-distance equivalence.  
- Instantiates framework for SBM: uses MLE B for inter-block probabilities; stability derived from eigenstructure of A and an eigengap condition.  
- Connects to classical model-based recovery results, reinterpreting their assumptions as data-driven fit conditions to obtain model-free guarantees.  
- Experiments on synthetic PFM, perturbed PFM, LFR, and Political Blogs show computability of ε, δ and compare tightness of PFM vs SBM bounds.  
- Theorems (PFM Theorem 4, SBM Theorem 9) are technically sound, using appropriate perturbation tools with data-computable constants.  
- PFM estimator is consistent under standard recovery conditions and tailored for sharp eigenspace comparison; SBM estimator is MLE.  
- Proofs use local equivalence of clustering distances (Meilă 2007) rather than combinatorial counting, which is an elegant approach.  
- Explicit dependence on eigengap is natural for spectral methods and goodness-of-fit assumptions are verifiable from data.  
- Reliance on proxy-model fit under spectral/Frobenius norms narrows what counts as a “good fit,” potentially excluding clusterings good under other criteria (e.g., cut-based or likelihood).  
- SBM bounds are often weak in practice and may fail to certify stability on benchmarks like LFR.  
- Empirics demonstrate qualitative behavior and computability but sometimes require smoothing (e.g., Political Blogs) to certify stability.  
- Paper is generally clear and well organized; PFM(G, C) construction and alignment steps are explained cleanly, though many proofs are in the supplement.  
- Notation could be improved by reducing pervasive use of hats and clarifying normalized Laplacian convention.  
- Contribution is novel: distribution-free stability guarantees and turning model-based recovery into model-free validation are fresh ideas.  
- Practical significance is mixed: provides a useful validation tool conceptually, but spectral-norm proxy focus and lack of degree-correction limit real-world applicability.  
- Major weakness: use of degree-weighted misclassification error (wME) is non-standard; need parallel guarantees in unweighted ME or quantification of wME vs ME under degree heterogeneity.  
- Major weakness: defining good fit via L̂ or Â closeness biases toward normalized Laplacian alignment and excludes other natural fit criteria (e.g., modularity, DC-SBM).  
- Missing an explicit DC-SBM instantiation/theorem, limiting applicability to degree-heterogeneous networks.  
- Suggested improvements: add DC-SBM case, discuss selection of K and δ sensitivity, relate eigengap to δ tightness and smoothing advice, compare with other confidence/stability approaches, report scatter of certified δ vs actual error, and clarify computational costs.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel distribution-free framework, technically clean with explicit constants, bridges model-based recovery to model-free validation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: practical utility limited by wME and spectral-proxy fit, SBM guarantees often non-informative, empirical evaluation thin and reliant on smoothing.  
- Overall: interesting theoretical contribution with careful analysis and potential impact, but needs stronger empirical work and broader fit criteria (or DC-SBM) to increase practical relevance.
