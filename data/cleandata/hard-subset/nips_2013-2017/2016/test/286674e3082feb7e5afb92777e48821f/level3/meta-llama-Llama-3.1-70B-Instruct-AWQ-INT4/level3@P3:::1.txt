This paper proposes a novel framework for graph clustering that provides distribution-free theoretical results without assuming a generative model for the observed graph. The core idea is that if a clustering fits the graph well, any other clustering that fits equally well must be close to it. This approach is particularly interesting because it ties in with existing model-based frameworks and can exploit results in model-based recovery.

The problem addressed in this paper is important, and the theoretical contributions are novel and interesting. The authors provide a clear and well-organized paper, despite some content being moved to the supplement. The use of "hats" on observed quantities, such as Ĥ for the observed Laplacian, is questionable from a stylistic perspective, but it does not affect the overall clarity of the paper.

The results are well-commented and illustrated empirically, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the limitations of their approach. One of the main limitations is that the framework relies on a non-standard distance between clusterings, whose naturalness is debatable. Additionally, the analysis uses a proxy-model approach, which strongly constrains what counts as a "good" clustering. These modeling restrictions are necessary for the results but may limit the applicability and impact of the framework on real-world examples.

The authors also discuss the relationship between their model-free guarantees and existing model-based recovery theorems. They show that their framework can be used to prove model-free guarantees for the Stochastic Block Model (SBM) and Preference Frame Model (PFM), and that these guarantees are closely related to the existing model-based recovery theorems.

The experimental evaluation of the paper is thorough and well-designed. The authors test their framework on several datasets, including simulated and real-world data, and provide a detailed analysis of the results. They also compare their framework to existing approaches and discuss the implications of their findings.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a new approach to graph clustering that is distinct from existing methods. The use of distribution-free theoretical results is a significant contribution, and the authors' ability to tie their framework to existing model-based approaches is a major strength.

In terms of significance, the paper addresses an important problem in graph clustering, and the results have the potential to impact a wide range of applications. The authors' framework provides a new perspective on graph clustering, and their results can be used to improve the accuracy and robustness of clustering algorithms.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes significant contributions to the field of graph clustering. The authors' framework is novel and well-motivated, and the results are thorough and well-supported by empirical evidence. While there are some limitations to the approach, the authors provide a clear and honest discussion of these limitations, and the paper is well-written and easy to follow.
