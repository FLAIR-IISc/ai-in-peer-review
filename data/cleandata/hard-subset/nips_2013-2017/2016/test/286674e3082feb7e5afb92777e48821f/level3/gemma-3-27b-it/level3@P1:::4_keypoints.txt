- Proposes a novel model-free framework that gives theoretical clustering guarantees by showing any other good-fitting clustering must be close to a reference clustering.  
- Framework instantiated for Preference Frame Model (PFM) and Stochastic Block Model (SBM), yielding new theoretical results.  
- Addresses limitation of model-based clustering by focusing on data statistics rather than assuming data comes from the model.  
- Connects to and leverages existing recovery theorems, spectral clustering, graph Laplacian analysis, and perturbation theory.  
- Rigorous mathematical formulation with clear definitions of goodness-of-fit and distances between clusterings.  
- Detailed, careful proofs (with supplementary material) demonstrating technical soundness.  
- All quantities required by the theorems are computable from data, emphasizing practical applicability.  
- Novelty: model-free guarantee framework is a significant and original contribution.  
- Generality: framework can be applied across different block models, showing broader potential.  
- Constructive: concrete guarantees derived for PFM and SBM, though currently restrictive.  
- Simulations indicate derived stability conditions are often very restrictive, especially for SBM, raising practical concerns.  
- Uneven treatment of PFM vs. SBM: different estimators (Laplacian vs. adjacency) and distance metrics (weighted ME vs. ME) lack full justification.  
- Omission of Degree-Corrected SBM (DC-SBM) is notable given it bridges PFM and SBM.  
- Framework assumes candidate clusterings have the same number of clusters, excluding splits/merges common in practice.  
- Related work section is incomplete; misses model-free stability results (e.g., Kumar & Kannan 2010, Balcan et al.).  
- Need clearer comparison between this paper’s stability notion and existing notions (perturbation, approximation, center-perturbation).  
- Minor issues: inconsistent notation, ambiguous definition of Frobenius inner product in Proposition 10, unclear Laplacian norm in Assumption 5.  
- Mismatch between theoretical metric (weighted ME) and experimental metric (ME) complicates interpretation.  
- For acceptance: tackles important problem, introduces novel framework, rigorous math, and informative experiments despite limitations.  
- Against acceptance: restrictive practical conditions, inconsistencies across models, limited stability scope, and incomplete related work weaken the contribution.  
- Overall assessment: interesting and technically sound but practical relevance and presentation gaps warrant rejection with strong encouragement to revise and resubmit.  
- Recommended revisions: relax stability conditions, reconcile PFM/SBM approach (include DC-SBM), extend to splits/merges, and expand related work discussion.
