The paper "Graph Clustering: Block-models and model free results" proposes a novel framework for graph clustering that does not rely on a generative model for the observed graph. The core idea is that if a clustering fits the graph well, as defined by the paper, then any other clustering that fits equally well must be close to it. This idea is formalized in the Generic Theorem, which is then instantiated for the Stochastic Block Model (SBM) and Preference Frame Model (PFM).

The paper's main contribution is the development of a distribution-free theoretical framework for graph clustering. This is an important problem, as many existing methods rely on strong assumptions about the generative process of the graph. The paper's approach is novel and interesting, and the theoretical results are well-presented and clearly explained.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, despite some content being relegated to the supplement. The use of "hats" on observed quantities, such as Ĥ for the observed Laplacian, is a minor stylistic concern. However, the notation is generally clear and consistent throughout the paper.

The empirical results are well-commented and illustrated, and provide a good understanding of the paper's theoretical contributions. However, there are some limitations to the empirical evaluation. In particular, some real-world graphs must be smoothed for structure to be recoverable under the proposed framework, whereas classical algorithms can recover ground truth without smoothing.

There are two major limitations to the paper's approach. Firstly, the main result for PFM relies on a non-standard distance between clusterings, whose naturalness is debatable. Secondly, the analysis uses a proxy-model approach, reconstructing a model from a clustering and comparing it to the observed graph, which strongly constrains what counts as a "good" clustering. This approach is focused on normalized Laplacian approximation, which may limit the applicability and impact of the results on real-world examples.

Despite these limitations, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of graph clustering. The results are well-presented and clearly explained, and the empirical evaluation provides a good understanding of the paper's theoretical contributions. The paper's approach is novel and interesting, and the distribution-free framework is an important contribution to the field.
