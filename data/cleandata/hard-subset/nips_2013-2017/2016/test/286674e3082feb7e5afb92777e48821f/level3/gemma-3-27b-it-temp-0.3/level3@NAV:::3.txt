## Review of "Graph Clustering: Block-models and model free results"

This paper presents a framework for graph clustering that aims to provide theoretical guarantees without relying on the assumption that the data originates from a specific block model (SBM, DC-SBM, PFM). The core idea is to establish stability: if a clustering fits the data well according to a chosen model, then any other clustering that also fits well must be a small perturbation of the original. The authors instantiate this framework for the PFM and SBM, demonstrating connections between model-free and model-based recovery results.

The algorithms presented – primarily estimators for model parameters given a clustering – are clearly described and mathematically sound. The proofs appear rigorous, leveraging existing results like the Sinus Theorem and adapting them to this novel context. The paper builds upon a substantial body of work in spectral clustering and stochastic block models, referencing key papers in the field (e.g., [1, 18, 20]). The connection drawn between model-based and model-free guarantees is a potentially valuable contribution, allowing for strengthening of existing results.

However, the paper suffers from a lack of clear motivation. While the problem of model-free guarantees is stated, the *why* behind pursuing this direction isn't adequately explained. Why is relaxing the model assumption crucial? What practical scenarios benefit from this approach? The presentation feels like a collection of algorithms and theorems rather than a cohesive argument addressing a compelling problem. Furthermore, the contribution feels largely incremental. The algorithms are modifications of existing methods, and the primary novelty lies in the constraints introduced to establish stability. 

The experimental evaluation, while present, feels somewhat limited. The focus on demonstrating the applicability of the theorems is good, but a more thorough comparison with existing model-based approaches on real-world datasets would strengthen the claims. The observation that the SBM bounds are less informative is acknowledged but not deeply investigated.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Technically sound and mathematically rigorous.
*   Clear presentation of algorithms and proofs.
*   Establishes a connection between model-free and model-based recovery.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lacks a compelling motivation for the work.
*   Incremental contribution – primarily modifications of existing algorithms.
*   Limited experimental evaluation.



**Overall:** While the paper presents solid technical work, its lack of clear motivation and incremental nature raise concerns. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and address the motivational aspects, and to provide a more comprehensive experimental evaluation demonstrating the practical benefits of the model-free approach. A clearer articulation of the problem being solved and the advantages of this framework over existing methods is crucial for elevating the paper's significance.
