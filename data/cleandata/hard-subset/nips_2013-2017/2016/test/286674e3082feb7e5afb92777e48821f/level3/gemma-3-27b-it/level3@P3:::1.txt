This paper presents a novel framework for graph clustering that provides theoretical guarantees without assuming the observed graph originates from a specific generative model like the Stochastic Block Model (SBM) or Preference Frame Model (PFM). The core idea is elegantly simple: if a clustering fits the data well, any other equally well-fitting clustering must be close to it. This approach is a significant contribution, addressing a crucial limitation of existing model-based methods. The work is technically sound, and the results are interesting and, in principle, impactful.

The paper is largely well-written and clear, although some details were moved to the supplement, which slightly hinders accessibility. The empirical evaluation is well-commented and effectively illustrates the theoretical findings. The authors are commendably thorough in exploring the implications of their results, including connections to existing model-based recovery theorems and the concept of cluster stability.

However, several limitations exist. The use of "hats" to denote observed quantities (e.g., Ĥ) feels unconventional and slightly distracting. While not a major flaw, it’s a stylistic choice that could be reconsidered for clarity. More substantially, the main result for the PFM relies on a non-standard distance metric between clusterings – the weighted Misclassification Error (wME) – whose intuitive appeal is debatable. While the authors argue for its naturalness, it’s not immediately obvious why weighting by degree should be preferred, and the potential consequences of this choice aren’t fully explored.

A more significant limitation is the proxy-model approach used in the analysis. By reconstructing a model (PFM or SBM) from a clustering and comparing it to the observed graph, the framework inherently focuses on how well a clustering *approximates* a model, specifically through the normalized Laplacian. This emphasis strongly constrains what constitutes a “good” clustering. In practical scenarios, the true underlying structure of a graph might not align perfectly with any standard block model, and this approach could miss valuable clusterings that don’t lend themselves to easy model reconstruction.  The authors acknowledge this, noting the need for smoothing in real-world graphs where classical algorithms can recover ground truth directly. This smoothing step effectively forces the data to conform to the assumptions inherent in their framework, limiting its applicability.

The empirical results highlight this limitation. While the theorems provide guarantees under certain conditions, the need for smoothing in some real-world graphs demonstrates that the framework isn’t universally applicable and might struggle with graphs exhibiting structures not easily captured by the considered models. The experiments also reveal the SBM bounds are often not satisfiable, suggesting a discrepancy between theoretical guarantees and practical application for that model.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* **Novelty:** The model-free approach to graph clustering is genuinely novel and addresses a significant gap in the existing literature.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The theoretical results are rigorously derived and appear technically correct.
* **Significance:** The problem addressed is important, and the results have the potential to impact the field of community detection and graph clustering.
* **Connections to Existing Work:**  The paper thoughtfully connects its findings to existing model-based results, strengthening both.
* **Clear Presentation:** Despite some technical complexity, the paper is generally well-written and accessible.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* **Restricted Applicability:** The reliance on model reconstruction and the resulting focus on Laplacian approximation limit the framework's applicability to real-world graphs.
* **Questionable Distance Metric:** The wME distance used for PFM is not intuitively justified and may influence the results.
* **SBM Results:** The SBM bounds appear overly restrictive and are rarely satisfied in the experiments.
* **Proxy-Model Approach:** The inherent constraints introduced by the proxy-model approach restrict the definition of a "good" clustering.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant theoretical contribution. The model-free framework is a valuable addition to the field, and the authors have clearly articulated the strengths and limitations of their approach. However, the practical limitations – particularly the reliance on model reconstruction and the restrictive nature of the SBM results – temper the overall impact.  While not a perfect paper, the novelty and theoretical rigor warrant acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, focusing on clarifying the justification for the wME distance and further discussion on the limitations of the proxy-model approach and potential extensions to address them.  Further elaboration on strategies to overcome the need for smoothing in real-world datasets would also strengthen the paper.
