This paper proposes a model-free framework for guaranteeing clustering stability, applicable to Stochastic Block Models (SBM) and Preference Frame Models (PFM). The core idea is to establish correctness guarantees based on how well a clustering and its associated model parameters fit the data, rather than assuming the data originates from a specific model. While a valuable theoretical contribution, the practical relevance is questionable based on the provided simulations.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty & Significance:** Providing model-free guarantees for clustering is a significant advancement, bridging the gap between theory and practice. The framework’s potential to leverage existing model-based results and strengthen them is also noteworthy. It addresses a critical limitation of existing methods that rely heavily on model assumptions.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The framework appears mathematically rigorous, with clear definitions of goodness-of-fit and distance metrics. The use of perturbation theory (Davis-Kahan) is a sensible approach.
* **Generality:** The “Generic Theorem” provides a flexible template adaptable to different models and estimators.
* **Computability:** The authors emphasize the computability of quantities used in the guarantees, which is crucial for practical application.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Inconsistent Treatment of PFM & SBM:** The paper's approach to PFM and SBM feels disjointed. The choice of Laplacian estimator for PFM versus adjacency for SBM, and the differing distance metrics (weighted ME vs. ME) are not adequately justified. The lack of DC-SBM results, given its relation to both SBM and PFM, is a significant omission.  Why are different estimators and metrics chosen, and how do these choices impact the theoretical guarantees and practical applicability? A comparative analysis is needed.
* **Ambiguity & Clarification Needed:** Proposition 10’s definition of <M', M>_F should explicitly state it's the Frobenius inner product. Assumption 5’s notation (||Ĺ^2 - L^2||_F or ||Ĺ - L||_F^2) needs clarification, and the intuition behind squaring the Laplacians should be explained.
* **Limited Generalizability:** The framework currently requires the same number of clusters (K) in both clusterings, restricting its applicability to scenarios with cluster splits or merges.  Exploring extensions to handle such cases would enhance the framework’s utility.
* **Related Work Gaps:** The related work section is incomplete, omitting relevant model-free stability results (e.g., Kumar & Kannan 2010, Balcan et al. 2012/2013).  A clearer comparison of this paper’s notion of stability (goodness-of-fit based) to other types (perturbation, approximation, center-perturbation) is needed.
* **Simulation Concerns:** The simulations show that PFM is sensitive to only extremely small perturbations, casting doubt on its practical robustness. The claim about the large eigengap making PFM sensitive contradicts the observation that power iteration (smoothing) is often used to improve stability.  The SBM conditions also appear difficult to satisfy, diminishing practical relevance.
* **Figure Clarity:** Figure 1’s caption is unclear about whether SBM results are plotted and the meaning of “δ is always greater than δ0”.  The axes labels are missing, hindering interpretation.
* **Theory-Experiment Mismatch:** The theoretical guarantees for PFM are expressed in terms of weighted ME, while the experiments perturb using ME. This inconsistency needs to be addressed and justified.
* **Minor Issues:** Notation is inconsistent across sections (B, C, Y used differently), and line 44’s wording regarding the independence of A_{ij} for i>j should be corrected to state independence *given* cluster assignments.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a genuinely novel theoretical framework with the potential to impact the field of community detection.
* The mathematical analysis appears sound and provides concrete, computable guarantees.
* The framework’s generality is appealing and opens avenues for future research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The practical relevance of the results is questionable, based on the simulations.
* The inconsistent treatment of PFM and SBM raises concerns about the framework’s robustness.
* Several minor issues (clarification needs, notation inconsistencies) detract from the overall quality.
* The incomplete related work section suggests a lack of awareness of existing results.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper tackles an important problem with a novel and theoretically sound approach. However, the lack of practical validation and the inconsistencies in treatment of different models raise significant concerns. While the contribution is valuable, the paper requires substantial revision to address the identified weaknesses before it can be considered for acceptance.  I recommend **rejection with request for major revisions.** The authors should focus on justifying the methodological choices, strengthening the empirical evaluation, and addressing the gaps in the related work. Specifically, demonstrating the applicability of the framework to more realistic datasets and scenarios would be crucial for improving its impact.
