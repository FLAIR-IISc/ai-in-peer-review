## Review of "Graph Clustering: Block-models and model free results"

This paper presents a novel framework for graph clustering that provides theoretical guarantees without assuming the data originates from a specific generative model like the Stochastic Block Model (SBM) or Preference Frame Model (PFM). The core idea is compelling: if a clustering well-fits the observed graph, then any other equally well-fitting clustering must be “close” to it. This work addresses a significant problem in the field, as many existing clustering guarantees rely on strong model assumptions, which are often unrealistic in practice. The theoretical contributions are interesting and represent a step towards more robust clustering methodologies.

The paper is largely clear and well-written, with the proofs of several key propositions relegated to the supplement, which is reasonable. The empirical evaluation is well-commented and illustrates the applicability of the derived theorems, showcasing how to practically assess clustering stability. However, the use of “hats” (e.g., Ĥ) to denote observed quantities feels stylistically questionable and could be clarified. 

A major limitation lies in the reliance on a non-standard distance metric (the weighted Misclassification Error) for the main result concerning the PFM. While the authors attempt to justify its naturalness, its adoption may hinder broader acceptance and comparison with existing work. More significantly, the entire analysis utilizes a proxy-model approach – reconstructing a model *from* a clustering and comparing it to the observed graph. This inherently constrains the definition of a “good” clustering to those that can be well-approximated by the normalized Laplacian, potentially overlooking valid clusterings that do not align with this modeling restriction. While necessary for obtaining theoretical results, this constraint limits the framework’s applicability to real-world graphs where the underlying structure might not be neatly captured by the chosen models.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound framework for model-free clustering guarantees.
*   Addresses a critical limitation of existing work relying on strong model assumptions.
*   Clear exposition of the core idea and well-supported theoretical results.
*   Informative empirical validation showcasing practical implications.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The choice of distance metric for PFM lacks strong justification.
*   The proxy-model approach imposes significant constraints on what constitutes a “good” clustering.
*   Empirical limitations, needing graph smoothing in some instances where classical algorithms suffice.



**Overall:** This is a promising paper with novel theoretical contributions. While the modeling restrictions and choice of distance metric pose limitations, the framework presented offers a valuable new perspective on graph clustering and warrants acceptance, pending minor revisions to address stylistic concerns and a more thorough discussion of the impact of the proxy-model approach.
