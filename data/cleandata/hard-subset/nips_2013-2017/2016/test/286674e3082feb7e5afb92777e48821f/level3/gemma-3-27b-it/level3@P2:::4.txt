This paper introduces a novel framework for providing clustering guarantees without assuming the data originates from a specific generative model like the Stochastic Block Model (SBM) or Preference Frame Model (PFM). The core idea is to establish stability: if a clustering *C* fits the data well, any other clustering *C'* that also fits well must be “close” to *C*. The authors instantiate this framework for PFM and SBM, relating model-free and model-based recovery results, and demonstrating the computability of all required quantities.

The paper addresses an important and valuable problem. While model-based clustering has seen significant theoretical progress, its applicability is limited by the model assumption. A model-free approach, providing guarantees based on observed data statistics, expands the scope of reliable clustering. The proposed framework is a worthwhile contribution, and the technical results, particularly for the PFM, are interesting.

However, the paper has several weaknesses. The treatment of PFM and SBM feels somewhat disjointed. Given that PFM generalizes both DC-SBM and SBM, a more unified treatment would be expected. The differing estimator choices—using the Laplacian for PFM and the adjacency matrix for SBM—and the corresponding use of weighted versus standard Misclassification Error (ME) need strong justification. The absence of results for the DC-SBM is a significant omission, given its position in the model hierarchy. 

The definition of <M', M>_F in Proposition 10 lacks clarity. It’s assumed to be the Frobenius inner product, but this should be explicitly stated. Assumption 5 is ambiguously worded; specifying whether it refers to ||Ĺ^2 - L^2||_F or ||Ĺ - L||_F^2 is crucial, and a rationale for squaring the Laplacians would strengthen the argument. While the authors aim for computability, the conditions required for stability appear quite stringent, particularly for the SBM, potentially limiting practical applicability.

The framework inherently requires a fixed number of clusters *K*. This limitation prevents handling splits or merges (K vs K+1), a common scenario in realistic clustering tasks where clusterings differ by a small number of nodes. The related work section, while covering relevant literature, overlooks several prior model-free stability results (e.g., Kumar & Kannan 2010, Balcan et al. 2012/2013). A discussion of how this work’s stability notion compares to concepts like perturbation/approximation/center-perturbation stability would be beneficial.

The experimental evaluation raises concerns. The PFM simulations tolerate only tiny perturbations, which seems overly sensitive. The claim that a large eigengap causes sensitivity contradicts the observation that smoothing the adjacency matrix improves stability. The SBM conditions appear exceedingly difficult to satisfy, further undermining practical relevance. The inconsistency between the theoretical guarantees (weighted ME) and the experimental perturbation (ME) needs to be addressed. Minor issues include unclear axis labels on Figure 1 and some inconsistencies in notation across sections. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The model-free stability framework is a new perspective on clustering validation.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The technical results, particularly for the PFM, appear rigorous.
*   **Computability:** All required quantities are, in principle, computable from the data.
*   **Potential Impact:** If the conditions for stability can be relaxed, the framework could have significant practical implications.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Practicality:** The stringent stability conditions for the SBM diminish practical relevance.
*   **Disjointed Treatment:** The separate handling of PFM and SBM feels ununified.
*   **Missing DC-SBM Results:** The lack of results for the DC-SBM is a significant gap.
*   **Experimental Concerns:** The simulations and inconsistencies between theory and experiment raise doubts about the practical usefulness of the framework.
*   **Limited Scope:** The fixed-*K* assumption restricts the applicability to more complex clustering scenarios.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable theoretical contribution, but its practical impact is currently limited by stringent conditions and inconsistencies. While the model-free framework is a novel and potentially impactful idea, significant improvements are needed to address the weaknesses identified above. I recommend **weak reject**. The authors should consider relaxing the stability conditions, providing results for the DC-SBM, clarifying the relationship between PFM and SBM treatments, and addressing the concerns raised by the experimental evaluation. A more unified and practical framework would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution.
