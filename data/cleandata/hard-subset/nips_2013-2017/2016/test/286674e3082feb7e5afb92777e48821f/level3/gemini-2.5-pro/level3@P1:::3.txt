This paper proposes a framework for providing theoretical guarantees for graph clustering without making assumptions about the data generating process. The central idea is one of stability: if a given clustering `C` provides a "good fit" to an observed graph `G` under a specific block model (e.g., SBM, PFM), then any other clustering `C'` that also provides a good fit must be a small perturbation of `C`. This shifts the focus from model-based recovery (which assumes the data comes from the model) to model-free validation (which only uses the model as a descriptive tool). The authors instantiate this general framework for the Preference Frame Model (PFM) and the Stochastic Block Model (SBM). For each, they define a procedure for fitting the model to a given graph and clustering, a goodness-of-fit measure (typically the spectral norm of the difference between the observed and fitted graph Laplacians or adjacency matrices), and derive a theorem that bounds the distance between two well-fitting clusterings. This work builds upon the extensive literature on spectral clustering for block models (e.g., Rohe et al. 2011, Qin & Rohe 2013, Wan & Meila 2015) but addresses a key limitation of that work—the reliance on a true underlying generative model.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical claims are well-supported by proofs that leverage established results from matrix perturbation theory, notably the Davis-Kahan Sin-Theta theorem. The work appears complete; the proposed framework is self-contained, and the derived bounds are fully expressed in terms of quantities that are computable from the data (e.g., the goodness-of-fit `ε` and the eigengap of the observed graph's Laplacian). This is a significant strength, as it makes the framework practically applicable for cluster validation, which the authors demonstrate in the experimental section. The authors are also careful to discuss the limitations and connections to other work, for instance, by showing how their framework can leverage existing model-based theorems.

**Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The core idea is introduced well in the abstract and introduction. The procedures for fitting a PFM or SBM to a given graph-clustering pair are presented clearly as explicit algorithms, which aids reproducibility. The notation is standard and defined appropriately. However, the motivation could be more strongly woven throughout the manuscript. While the introduction clearly states the goal of moving beyond model-based assumptions, the main body of the paper quickly delves into dense technical derivations. The connection between the high-level "Generic Theorem 1" and its specific instantiations could be made smoother to help guide the reader through the technical details without losing sight of the main conceptual contribution.

**Originality:** The primary contribution of this work is the novel problem formulation. While block models and spectral methods are well-studied, the idea of using them in a purely descriptive, model-free validation framework is new and insightful. Turning model-based recovery theorems "on their head" to provide model-free guarantees is a creative and valuable contribution. However, the technical machinery used to achieve this is largely standard. The methods for fitting the models are not fundamentally new (MLE for SBM, a spectral projection method for PFM), and the proofs rely on classic spectral perturbation bounds. Thus, the originality lies more in the conceptual framework and its application rather than the development of new mathematical tools or algorithms. This is not necessarily a weakness, as the novel application of existing tools to a new and important problem is a valid contribution.

**Significance:** The work is potentially very significant. It addresses a fundamental problem faced by any practitioner using graph clustering: how can one trust the output of a clustering algorithm without knowing if the data conforms to the algorithm's implicit assumptions? This paper provides a concrete, computable answer based on the principle of stability. Researchers and practitioners could use this framework to validate clusters found on real-world networks, providing a measure of confidence that is not dependent on unverifiable generative assumptions. The demonstration that the framework can improve upon existing model-based bounds is also a valuable contribution to that line of research. A potential limitation on its immediate impact is the tightness of the derived bounds. As the authors note and the experiments show, the conditions for the SBM-based theorem, in particular, are quite restrictive and may not be met in practice, even for data that seems well-suited to the model.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Novel Framework:** Proposes a novel and important conceptual framework for model-free validation of graph clustering, addressing a key limitation of prior work.
*   **Practical Relevance:** All quantities in the derived theoretical guarantees are computable from the data, making the method directly applicable for validating clustering results on real-world graphs.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The paper is technically solid, with clear theorems and proofs based on established matrix analysis.
*   **Clarity:** The proposed procedures are presented clearly and the overall paper is well-structured.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   **Limited Practicality of Bounds:** The derived bounds, especially for the SBM, appear to be quite loose. The experiments show that the conditions of the theorems are only met for very small perturbations of an initial "good" clustering, which may limit the method's practical utility.
*   **Originality of Tools:** While the framework is novel, the underlying technical tools (spectral perturbation theory) and model-fitting procedures are standard.

In summary, this is a strong paper that introduces a novel and significant idea for validating graph clustering. The conceptual contribution of a computable, model-free guarantee is valuable. While the originality of the technical tools is moderate and the tightness of the current bounds could be improved, the work provides a solid foundation and a new perspective on an important problem.
