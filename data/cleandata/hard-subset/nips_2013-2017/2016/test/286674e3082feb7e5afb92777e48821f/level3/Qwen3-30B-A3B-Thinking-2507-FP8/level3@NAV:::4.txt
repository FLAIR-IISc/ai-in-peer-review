

This paper proposes a model-free framework for clustering stability guarantees, which is a valuable contribution to the field. The core idea is to provide "correctness" guarantees for clustering algorithms without assuming the data comes from a model, instead relying on computable statistics. The authors apply this framework to PFM and SBM, deriving exact recovery results and demonstrating how model-free guarantees connect with existing model-based results. The framework is theoretically sound, with computable quantities (ε, δ, δ₀) that can be verified from data, and the proofs are relatively elementary compared to existing perturbation bounds.

However, several significant weaknesses undermine the paper's impact. The treatment of PFM and SBM is inconsistent: PFM uses Laplacian-based estimation with weighted ME distance, while SBM uses adjacency matrices with standard ME, without justification. The omission of DC-SBM results (which is a special case of PFM) is particularly problematic. The paper also fails to clarify key technical points, such as the definition of <M', M>_F in Proposition 10 and the ambiguous Assumption 5 (whether it's ||Ĺ² - L²||_F or ||Ĺ - L||_F²). 

The framework has important limitations: it cannot handle cluster splits/merges (K vs K+1), and the SBM conditions appear excessively restrictive in simulations (all δ values >1). Crucially, there's a mismatch between theoretical guarantees (weighted ME for PFM) and experimental evaluation (ME perturbations), and the simulations for PFM tolerate only tiny perturbations—contradicting the claim that large eigengaps make PFM sensitive. The paper also omits key related work on model-free stability (Kumar & Kannan 2010; Balcan et al. 2012/2013), failing to clarify how their stability notion relates to existing perturbation stability concepts.

While the core idea is valuable, the inconsistencies, omissions, and practical limitations reduce the paper's significance. The framework requires substantial refinement to be useful beyond theoretical interest. I recommend rejection due to the unresolved technical issues and lack of practical relevance demonstrated in the simulations.
