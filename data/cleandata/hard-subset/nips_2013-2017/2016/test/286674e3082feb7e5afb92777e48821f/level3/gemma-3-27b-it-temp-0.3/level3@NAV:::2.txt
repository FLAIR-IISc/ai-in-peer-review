## Review of “Graph Clustering: Block-models and model free results”

This paper presents a novel framework for analyzing graph clustering, aiming to provide theoretical guarantees without assuming data originates from a specific block model. The core idea is to establish a “locally convex-like” property: if a clustering fits the data well according to a model, any other clustering that also fits well must be “close” in terms of misclassification distance. This is demonstrated for both the Preference Frame Model (PFM) and Stochastic Block Model (SBM), with proofs building upon existing results in the field. The authors also demonstrate the equivalence of multiple clustering distance measures.

The work is well-motivated, addressing a significant limitation of existing block model-based clustering guarantees – the reliance on model assumptions. The approach of focusing on data statistics rather than model origin is a valuable contribution. The paper is clearly written and logically structured, effectively explaining the core ideas and mathematical derivations. The inclusion of experimental validation, though limited in scope, strengthens the claims. The authors appropriately acknowledge related work, including recent contributions from Peng et al. (2015) and Chao et al. (2015), and correctly position their work as an extension of existing theory. 

However, while the theoretical arguments are presented as “simple extensions,” they still require a significant level of mathematical sophistication, and the proofs, while seemingly correct, are dense and may be challenging for some readers to fully grasp. The experimental section, while helpful, feels somewhat cursory. The authors mention potential applications in detecting clusterability but don't provide concrete results in that direction, representing a missed opportunity. Furthermore, the SBM results appear less compelling than the PFM results, with the authors themselves acknowledging the difficulty in satisfying the SBM-specific conditions. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel framework for model-free clustering guarantees.
*   Solid theoretical foundation with rigorous proofs.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation.
*   Demonstrated equivalence of clustering distance measures.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited experimental validation.
*   Lack of concrete results on applications like clusterability detection.
*   SBM results appear less strong than PFM results.
*   Theoretical arguments, while presented as simple, are mathematically demanding.



**Overall:** This is a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of graph clustering. While the experimental validation could be more extensive and the application to clusterability detection is only hinted at, the novelty and rigor of the work justify acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper, with a minor revision request to expand the experimental section and provide a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the SBM results.
