## Review of “Graph Clustering: Block-models and model free results”

This paper presents a framework for graph clustering that aims to provide theoretical guarantees without assuming the data originates from a specific generative model like the Stochastic Block Model (SBM) or its variations. The core idea revolves around establishing stability: if a clustering fits the data well, then any other clustering that also fits the data well must be “close” to the original. The authors instantiate this framework for the Preference Frame Model (PFM) and SBM, demonstrating connections between model-based and model-free recovery results and strengthening existing bounds in some cases.

The paper builds upon a substantial body of work in spectral clustering and block model analysis. The authors correctly position their work within the existing literature, referencing key papers on SBM ([18]), DC-SBM ([17]), PFM ([20]), spectral clustering ([15]), and related stability results ([6, 16, 14]). The background section provides a concise overview of necessary concepts, including graph definitions, clusterings, and the mentioned block models. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty of Approach:** The central idea of a model-free framework for clustering guarantees is a valuable contribution. Existing guarantees are heavily reliant on model assumptions, limiting their applicability in real-world scenarios. This work attempts to bridge that gap.
* **Technical Soundness:** The theorems and propositions appear to be technically sound, supported by detailed proofs (with some relegated to the supplement). The use of perturbation theory (specifically, the Sinus Theorem) is a clever and appropriate technique for establishing stability bounds.
* **Clear Presentation of Algorithms:** The PFM and SBM estimation algorithms are clearly described, making them readily implementable.
* **Connections to Existing Work:** The paper successfully demonstrates the link between model-based and model-free recovery, highlighting how results from one can be leveraged to improve the other. The exploration of alternative bounds using existing theorems ([20, 18]) is a positive aspect.
* **Computability:** A significant strength is that the quantities needed for applying the theorems are computable from the data and the clustering itself, enhancing practical applicability.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Motivation & Framing:** The paper lacks a compelling overarching motivation. It feels somewhat like a collection of algorithms and theorems rather than a unified research narrative. Why is this framework important *now*? What specific real-world problems does it address that existing methods cannot? A more focused introduction would address this. The authors state the "impact of the above results is limited by the assumption that the observed data comes from the model," but do not adequately elaborate on scenarios where this assumption frequently fails.
* **Limited Originality beyond the Framework:** While the model-free framework is novel, the algorithms themselves are largely modifications of existing methods (spectral clustering, maximum likelihood estimation). The primary contribution appears to be the stability constraints introduced in the theorems, rather than entirely new algorithmic techniques.
* **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental section is descriptive but lacks depth. It demonstrates that the theorems *can* be applied and that the assumptions sometimes hold, but it doesn't convincingly show that the model-free approach outperforms existing methods in practice. The comparisons are often limited to showing which scenarios satisfy the theoretical guarantees. More rigorous comparisons against baseline algorithms on real-world datasets would strengthen the paper. The claim regarding the Political Blogs dataset being smoothed, and therefore improved stability, is under-explained.
* **Conservatism of Bounds:** The paper acknowledges that the SBM bounds are restrictive and that tighter bounds may be possible. This suggests the current results are not yet sufficiently practical for broad adoption.
* **Clarity of notation:** The frequent use of hats (e.g. Â, d̂) and the multiple definitions of similar concepts (dist vs distd̂) require careful attention and could be simplified.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound framework for model-free graph clustering with guarantees.
* The connections between model-based and model-free recovery are insightful.
* The theoretical results are rigorously proven and the required quantities are computable.
* The paper addresses an important limitation of existing clustering methods.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The motivation is weak and the paper lacks a clear narrative.
* The algorithmic contributions are incremental, primarily focusing on modifying existing methods.
* The experimental evaluation is limited and does not demonstrate a clear practical advantage.
* The SBM bounds are currently too restrictive to be widely applicable.




**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of graph clustering. The model-free framework is a significant step towards more robust and applicable clustering methods. However, the lack of a strong motivation, the incremental nature of the algorithmic contributions, and the limited experimental evaluation prevent me from strongly recommending acceptance in its current form. I believe the paper has the potential to be a significant contribution with further refinement, particularly regarding the motivation, experimental validation, and exploration of tighter bounds.

**Recommendation:** Weak Reject. I encourage the authors to address the weaknesses outlined above and resubmit to a future conference. Focusing on a specific application area and demonstrating a clear practical advantage would significantly strengthen the paper.
