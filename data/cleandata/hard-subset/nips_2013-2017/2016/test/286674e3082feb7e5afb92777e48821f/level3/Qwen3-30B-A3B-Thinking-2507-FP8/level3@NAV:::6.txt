

This paper proposes a "model-free" framework for graph clustering guarantees, claiming to bypass the standard assumption that data follows a block model (SBM/DC-SBM/PFM) by instead verifying data fit to a model via computable statistics. The core idea is that if a clustering *C* fits the graph *G* well under a model *M*, then any other clustering *C'* that fits *G* well must be close to *C*. The authors instantiate this for PFM and SBM, deriving theorems (Theorems 4, 9) with bounds on clustering distance.

**Strengths**: The background on block models and spectral clustering is well-organized. The experimental section demonstrates the computability of their bounds (e.g., δ, δ₀) on synthetic and real data, and correctly notes that PFM bounds are more applicable than SBM bounds for non-model data.

**Critical weaknesses** undermine the paper’s contribution. The framework’s central claim—*model-free guarantees*—is misleading because it still implicitly assumes the data is *close to* a model (via "standard recovery conditions" in Prop. 3 and Assumptions 2,4), failing to truly eliminate model dependence. Theorems lack rigor:  
- **Theorem 1** is vague (e.g., "which also fits G well is close to C" lacks mathematical precision).  
- **Proposition 3** relies on "standard recovery conditions" without specifying them or citing the necessary assumptions (e.g., eigengap bounds from [20]).  
- **Theorem 4**’s condition δ ≤ δ₀ is never satisfied for SBM (δ > 1 in experiments), making the guarantee vacuous. The proof (e.g., Prop. 7) cites prior work [13] but provides no novel insights, merely repackaging existing perturbation theory.  

The paper’s "model-free" label is incorrect; it merely shifts the model assumption to a *computable fit measure*, which is no less restrictive than the original model-based approach. The claimed "strengthening" of [20] (Theorem 11) is weaker (by factor 128), not stronger. Crucially, the framework offers no new theoretical tools—its bounds are weaker than established model-based results (e.g., [18, 20]) and lack practical utility due to restrictive conditions.

**Originality and Significance**: The idea of using model fit for validation is not novel (see [6, 11]). The "model-free" framing is misleading, and the results provide no meaningful advance over existing model-based guarantees. The paper fails to distinguish its contribution from prior work on stability (e.g., [9, 14]).

**Verdict**: The paper’s technical claims are unsubstantiated, its core theory is poorly defined, and its "model-free" claim is incorrect. While the presentation is clear, the quality is low, and the significance is negligible. **Reject**.
