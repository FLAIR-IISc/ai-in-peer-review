This paper proposes a framework for model-free graph clustering, which the authors claim provides a new perspective on the field. The main idea is to obtain "correctness" guarantees without assuming the data comes from a specific model. However, upon closer inspection, the results appear naive and unconvincing.

The paper starts by introducing a framework for clustering with guarantees without model assumptions. The authors define a model-based algorithm that clusters a graph G and outputs a clustering C and parameters M(G, C). They then propose a generic theorem that states if M(G, C) fits the data G well, then any other clustering C' that also fits G well will be a small perturbation of C. However, this theorem is vague and lacks precision, with statements like "which also fits G well is close to C" that are not rigorously defined.

The authors then instantiate this generic theorem for the case of the Preference Frame Model (PFM) and the Stochastic Block Model (SBM). They propose a PFM estimation algorithm and prove a main result for PFM, which provides a bound on the distance between two clusterings C and C' in terms of the eigengap of the graph Laplacian. However, the proof relies on several technical assumptions, including a non-zero eigengap, which may not always hold in practice.

The authors also propose an SBM estimation algorithm and prove a main result for SBM, which provides a bound on the distance between two clusterings C and C' in terms of the eigengap of the graph adjacency matrix. However, this result is less general than the PFM result, as it requires a non-singular edge probability matrix.

One of the main issues with this paper is the lack of formal justification for the claims made. Proposition 3, for example, is informal and relies on underspecified "standard recovery conditions" that are not clearly defined. The authors also fail to provide sufficient evidence for their claims, with many of the proofs relegated to the supplement.

In terms of related work, the authors claim that their results are novel and provide a new perspective on the field. However, they fail to engage with existing work on model-based clustering, such as the stochastic block model and its variants. They also fail to discuss the limitations of their approach and how it compares to existing methods.

In conclusion, while the authors propose an interesting framework for model-free graph clustering, the results are naive and unconvincing. The lack of formal justification, insufficient evidence, and failure to engage with existing work make it difficult to take the claims made in this paper seriously.
