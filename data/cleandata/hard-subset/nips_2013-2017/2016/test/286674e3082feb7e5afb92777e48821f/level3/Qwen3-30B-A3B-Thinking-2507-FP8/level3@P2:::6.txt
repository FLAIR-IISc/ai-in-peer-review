

# Review for "Graph Clustering: Block-models and model free results"

This paper proposes a framework for graph clustering that claims to provide theoretical guarantees without assuming the data comes from a specific model (like SBM, DC-SBM, or PFM). The authors argue that their approach can provide "model-free" guarantees by checking whether a fitted model fits the data well, rather than assuming the data comes from a model. The main contribution is Theorem 1, which states that if a clustering fits the data well with respect to a model, then any other clustering that also fits well must be close to the original clustering. The paper then instantiates this theorem for PFM and SBM models.

The work relates to previous NIPS contributions on stochastic block models (e.g., Rohe et al. 2011, Qin & Rohe 2013, Abbe & Sandon 2015), spectral clustering (Ng et al. 2002), and stability-based clustering (Meilà 2003, Awasthi 2015). However, it fails to clearly distinguish its approach from existing stability arguments in the literature.

**Quality:** The paper's main theoretical claims lack rigor. Theorem 1 is vague and unquantified, with key terms like "fits G well" and "small perturbation" left undefined. Proposition 3 is described as "informal" and relies on "standard recovery conditions" that are not specified, making it impossible to verify. The claimed improvement of Theorem 11 (by a factor of 128) actually weakens the bound, contradicting the paper's claim of strengthening existing results. The experimental section shows that the SBM bounds (Theorem 9) are not satisfied even for the LFR dataset, suggesting the guarantees are not practically useful. The paper does not sufficiently address the limitations of the proposed framework.

**Clarity:** While the paper is generally well-organized and uses standard notation for graph clustering, key definitions are imprecise. The phrase "fits G well" appears throughout but is never formally defined. The relationship between model-based and model-free guarantees is not clearly articulated, and the proof techniques are not sufficiently explained. The connection between the theoretical results and the experimental validation is weak.

**Originality:** The core idea of model-free guarantees for clustering is not novel. Previous work (e.g., Meilà 2003, Awasthi 2015) has explored similar stability-based approaches. The paper does not clearly distinguish its framework from existing work, and the claimed "new" approach appears to be a rephrasing of standard stability arguments rather than a genuinely novel contribution. The connection to existing model-based recovery theorems is not sufficiently innovative.

**Significance:** The significance of the results is limited. The guarantees provided are not practically useful, as shown in the experiments where the SBM bounds are not satisfied even for data expected to fit the model. The paper does not provide new insights or tools that would be valuable to practitioners or researchers. The results are not generalizable beyond the specific theoretical framework presented, and the paper fails to demonstrate how these results advance the state of the art in a demonstrable way.

**Arguments for acceptance:** The paper is well-written, presents a clear framework, and has a reasonable experimental evaluation. The connection between model-based and model-free approaches is conceptually interesting, even if the execution is flawed.

**Arguments against acceptance:** The theoretical results lack rigor, the claims are unsubstantiated, the novelty is questionable, and the practical significance is limited. The paper's main theorem is vague, and the supporting propositions are insufficiently justified. The experimental results contradict the paper's claims about the usefulness of the model-free guarantees.

The paper's key weakness is that it presents a framework that appears to be a rephrasing of existing stability arguments without providing new theoretical insights or practical advantages. The purported "model-free guarantees" are not substantially different from standard stability results in the literature, and the paper fails to provide convincing evidence that its approach offers any meaningful improvement over existing methods.

While the paper makes a valiant attempt to bridge model-based and model-free clustering, the theoretical foundation is too weak to support the claims made. The results are neither novel nor practically useful, and the presentation lacks the precision required for a high-quality NIPS paper. The authors should either substantially strengthen the theoretical arguments or significantly revise the claims to better align with the actual contributions.

I recommend rejection, as the paper does not meet the quality standards expected for NIPS. The theoretical contributions are insufficiently rigorous and do not advance the field in a meaningful way.
