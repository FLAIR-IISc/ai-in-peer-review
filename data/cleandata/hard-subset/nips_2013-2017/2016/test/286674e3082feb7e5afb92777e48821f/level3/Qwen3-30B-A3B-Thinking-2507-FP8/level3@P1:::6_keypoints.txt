- Proposes a graph-clustering framework claiming theoretical guarantees without assuming a specific generative model.
- Core idea: if a fitted model M(G, C) fits data G well, then any other clustering C' that also fits G well must be close to C.
- Authors instantiate the framework for SBM and PFM and provide specific theorems with error bounds.
- The “model-free” claim is misleading: guarantees still critically depend on specific model classes (SBM, PFM) and tailored goodness-of-fit measures.
- Theorem 1 is stated generically but is too vague and lacks rigorous definitions for “fits well” and “small perturbation.”
- Specific assumptions (e.g., Assumption 2 for PFM) and the formalization of key terms are underspecified and unclear.
- Proposition 3’s consistency claim is informal, citing “standard recovery conditions” from [20] without specifying which conditions are used.
- Overall lack of technical rigor: vague theorem statements, hand-wavy arguments, and proofs pushed to the supplement without sufficient detail.
- Experimental evaluation is superficial and limited to synthetic data from SBM/PFM, so it does not test the framework on non-model data as claimed.
- Quality issues: technical claims are not properly supported, proofs are incomplete, and assumptions are not clearly defined.
- Clarity: paper is organized but core theoretical contributions are obscured by vague statements and poorly defined concepts.
- Originality is limited: framework essentially reframes existing model-based results rather than delivering a genuinely model-free approach.
- Significance is limited: the framework doesn’t eliminate model assumptions and its practical utility is unclear because “good fit” conditions aren’t verifiable.
- Strengths: identifies an important literature gap and draws an interesting (but underdeveloped) connection between model-based and model-free perspectives.
- Recommended improvements: provide precise definitions, rigorous proofs, and experiments on data not generated from the assumed models.
