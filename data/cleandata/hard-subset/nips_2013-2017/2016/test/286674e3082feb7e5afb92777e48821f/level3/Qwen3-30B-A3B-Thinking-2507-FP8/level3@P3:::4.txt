

# Review of "Graph Clustering: Block-models and model free results"

This paper proposes a novel framework for obtaining model-free guarantees for clustering stability in graphs. The authors argue that instead of assuming data comes from a specific model (like SBM or PFM), their approach provides guarantees based on computable quantities that can be verified from the data itself. The framework establishes that if a clustering fits the data well (measured by a goodness-of-fit criterion), then any other clustering that also fits the data well must be close to the original clustering. The authors instantiate this framework for PFM and SBM, deriving theoretical guarantees that connect to existing model-based results.

**Strengths:**
The paper's main strength lies in its conceptual contribution: providing a bridge between model-based and model-free clustering guarantees. The framework is theoretically sound, with guarantees that are fully computable from the data (unlike many distribution-free results). The proof techniques are indeed simpler and more direct than previous approaches. The connection to existing model-based results is well-executed, as shown in Theorems 11 and 12. The paper also thoughtfully addresses the practical aspect of cluster validation, making it valuable for both theoretical and applied work.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper's main weakness is the inconsistency in its treatment of PFM and SBM. For PFM, the authors use the Laplacian as the estimator and weighted ME distance, while for SBM they use adjacency matrix and standard ME distance. This inconsistency requires justification, especially since PFM includes DC-SBM/SBM as special cases. The omission of DC-SBM results is particularly problematic given the authors' claim that "SBM is a subclass of DC-SBM."

Several technical ambiguities undermine the paper's quality. Assumption 5 is unclear: is it ||Ĺ² - L²||_F or ||Ĺ - L||_F²? The former would require explaining the intuition for squaring Laplacians. The definition of <M', M>_F in Proposition 10 is not specified (presumably the Frobenius inner product, but this should be explicitly stated). The framework also cannot handle cluster splits or merges (K vs K+1), which is a significant limitation for practical applications.

The experimental evaluation has serious issues. The simulations for PFM tolerate only tiny perturbations, contradicting the authors' claim that "large eigengap makes PFM(G,C) sensitive." The SBM conditions appear very hard to satisfy (all δ values exceed 1), undermining practical relevance. There's a mismatch between theoretical guarantees (weighted ME) and experiments (ME), which must be addressed. Figure 1's caption is unclear, and the axes lack labels.

The related work section is inadequate, omitting key model-free stability results by Kumar & Kannan (2010), Balcan et al. (2012/2013), and others. The paper doesn't clarify how its notion of stability relates to these existing concepts.

**Originality:**
The paper's core idea—using model-based recovery theorems in a model-free framework—is novel. However, the specific techniques for PFM and SBM are not entirely new, as the authors acknowledge they're "different from existing perturbation bounds." The paper's main contribution is the framework itself, which is a significant conceptual advance.

**Significance:**
The paper makes an important theoretical contribution to understanding clustering stability without model assumptions. However, the practical significance is limited by the restrictive conditions required for the guarantees to hold. The simulations suggest these conditions are rarely satisfied in practice, and the framework's inability to handle cluster splits/merges further reduces its utility.

**Recommendation:**
The paper presents an interesting theoretical framework with potential value for both theory and practice. However, significant revisions are needed to address the technical ambiguities, inconsistencies, and experimental shortcomings. Specifically, the authors should:

1. Clarify all ambiguous technical points (Assumption 5, <M', M>_F definition)
2. Justify the inconsistent treatment of PFM and SBM
3. Extend the framework to handle cluster splits/merges
4. Address the mismatch between theoretical guarantees and experimental evaluation
5. Expand the related work to include key model-free stability results

With these revisions, the paper would make a strong contribution to the field. As it stands, the practical relevance is questionable, and the framework's limitations need clearer acknowledgment. I recommend rejection with encouragement to revise and resubmit after addressing these issues.
