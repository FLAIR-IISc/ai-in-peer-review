This paper introduces a novel and valuable framework for providing model-free concentration guarantees for community detection. The central idea is to shift from the standard assumption that a graph is generated by a specific model (e.g., SBM, PFM) to a more practical setting. The authors prove that if a given clustering provides a good fit to the observed data under a model's structure, then any other clustering that also fits the data well must be a small perturbation of the original one. This work connects to the extensive literature on model-based recovery guarantees but cleverly reframes the problem as one of stability and uniqueness of a good clustering solution, making the results dependent on checkable statistics of the data rather than unverifiable model assumptions.

The paper has several significant strengths.
**Pros:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** It addresses the highly relevant question of how to validate a clustering result without knowing the true generative process. This is a fundamental problem and the proposed framework is an elegant and important step forward.
*   **Technical Quality:** The theoretical results are stated with great generality and then carefully instantiated for popular graphical models like the PFM and SBM. The analysis appears sound and builds upon established spectral perturbation theory.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clear, and enjoyable to read. The authors do an excellent job motivating their approach, their choice of error metrics, and the underlying assumptions, making a complex topic accessible.

The main weakness lies in the technical tools used to derive the bounds.
**Cons:**
*   **Limitations of Bounds:** The proofs rely on classical spectral concentration results (e.g., Davis-Kahan theorem), which are known to be loose, particularly in the sparse graph regime where community detection is often most challenging. This can result in conditions that are too conservative to be satisfied in practice, as hinted at in the experiments where the SBM-based guarantees were not met. This reliance on non-tight bounds may limit the immediate practical impact of the guarantees for very sparse networks.

Overall, this is a strong paper that introduces an important new direction for research in community detection. While the technical bounds could be tightened in future work, the conceptual contribution of establishing a model-free stability framework is significant. The work is a strong start that is likely to inspire considerable follow-up research. I recommend acceptance.
