This paper presents a general framework for providing model-free stability guarantees for graph clustering. The core idea is to show that if a clustering C and its corresponding fitted model M(G, C) provide a good fit to an observed graph G, then any other clustering C' that also fits the data well must be a small perturbation of C. This shifts the focus from assuming the data is generated by a model to verifying, post-hoc, that a given clustering solution is stable because it explains the data well. The authors instantiate this framework for the Preference Frame Model (PFM) and the Stochastic Block Model (SBM), deriving explicit, computable bounds on the distance between two such "good" clusterings.

### Strengths

The primary strength of this work is its novel and important goal: to develop a framework for clustering validation that does not rely on unverifiable assumptions about the data-generating process. This is a significant conceptual contribution, as most theoretical guarantees for block model clustering require assuming the graph is sampled from the model in question. By providing guarantees that depend only on computable statistics of the data, this work offers a path toward more practical and trustworthy validation of clustering results.

The approach of "turning around" model-based recovery theorems to provide model-free guarantees is clever and could inspire similar work in other areas. The fact that the derived bounds are free of unknown constants and depend only on quantities like the goodness-of-fit error (ε) and spectral gaps makes them, in principle, directly applicable.

### Weaknesses and Major Concerns

Despite the promising direction, the paper has several significant weaknesses in its current form that undermine its impact and clarity.

1.  **Disjoint Treatment of PFM and SBM:** The paper presents PFM and SBM as related instances of the framework, yet their treatments are inconsistent and lack justification.
    *   **Estimator Choice:** For PFM, a spectral-based model estimator is constructed from the top K eigenvectors of the graph Laplacian. For SBM, the Maximum Likelihood estimator is used. Given that SBM is a subclass of PFM, it is unclear why different estimation strategies are used. A unified approach or a clear justification for the difference is needed.
    *   **Metrics:** The PFM guarantee is for the weighted Misclassification Error (wME), while the SBM guarantee is for the standard ME. The experiments then use unweighted perturbations to test the PFM theory. This inconsistency between models and between theory and experiment is confusing and needs to be addressed.
    *   **Missing DC-SBM:** The Degree-Corrected SBM is mentioned as a key model linking SBM and PFM, but no specific results are derived for it. This is a noticeable omission.

2.  **Questionable Practical Utility from Experiments:** The experimental results raise serious doubts about the tightness and practical applicability of the derived bounds.
    *   For PFM, the stability condition (δ ≤ δ0) is only met for extremely small perturbations of the initial clustering. The claim that this is due to the large eigengap making the model sensitive is not fully convincing and seems to contradict the observation that smoothing (powering the adjacency matrix) induces stability.
    *   More critically, for SBM, the conditions of Theorem 9 were apparently never satisfied in any experiment, even for the LFR benchmark graph which is explicitly designed to have SBM-like structure. If the guarantee cannot be met even in idealized settings, its value for real-world data is questionable.

3.  **Incomplete Related Work:** The paper misses several key references on model-free clustering stability. The work of Kumar & Kannan (2010) on perturbation resilience and Balcan et al. (2012, 2013) on various forms of stability (e.g., center-perturbation, approximation-stability) are highly relevant. The paper should position its contribution more clearly with respect to these established notions of stability. The current notion appears to be a form of uniqueness guarantee for a well-fitting model, which is different from, for instance, stability under data perturbation.

4.  **Limited Scope of the Framework:** The current framework is restricted to comparing clusterings C and C' that have the same number of clusters, K. This prevents its use for validating solutions against alternatives with K-1 or K+1 clusters (i.e., merges or splits), which is a common and important practical problem in cluster validation.

### Detailed Comments and Questions for Clarification

*   **Proposition 10:** The notation `<M̂, M>F` should be explicitly defined, presumably as the Frobenius inner product.
*   **Assumption 5:** The expression `||L̂^2 - L^2||_F` is ambiguous. Does it refer to the norm of the difference of the squared matrices, `||(L̂^2) - (L^2)||_F`, or the squared Frobenius norm of the difference, `(||L̂ - L||_F)^2`? If the former, the intuition for squaring the Laplacians before taking the difference is not clear and should be explained.
*   **Generalizability:** Could the authors discuss whether this framework can be extended beyond block models? For instance, how would the template apply to other settings where spectral clustering is known to work, such as the hierarchical stochastic block models studied by Balakrishnan et al. (2011)?
*   **Notation:** The notation is inconsistent across sections. The symbols Y, B, and C are used differently in the PFM and SBM sections. A more consistent notation (e.g., distinguishing between graph-based and model-based quantities) would significantly improve readability. For example, the use of `YZ` in the SBM estimator seems unnecessary.
*   **Figure 1:** The figure is difficult to read. The axes are unlabeled. The caption is also unclear; for instance, it is not specified which subplot demonstrates that "For SBM, δ is always greater than δ0." It appears the SBM results are not plotted at all, which should be stated.
*   **Minor Wording:** The statement on line 44 that `Â_ij` are sampled independently is only true conditional on the cluster assignments.
*   **Organization:** Section 4.4 ("Clusterability and resilience") feels more like a discussion of connections to other concepts and would perhaps fit better as a subsection of Related Work (Section 5).

In summary, the paper introduces a promising and valuable idea for model-free cluster validation. However, the execution suffers from inconsistencies, experiments that undermine the practical relevance of the results, and an incomplete discussion of related work. Addressing these major issues is crucial for this work to realize its potential contribution.
