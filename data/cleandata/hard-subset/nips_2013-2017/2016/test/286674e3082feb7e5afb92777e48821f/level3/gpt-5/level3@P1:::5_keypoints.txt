- Proposes a model-free validation framework for graph clustering: a clustering C is "stable" if some fitted model in a class M both closely fits the data (operator-norm goodness-of-fit) and forces any other good-fit clustering to be close in misclassification error.

- Instantiates the framework for PFM: constructs a consistent Laplacian estimator aligned with the top-K eigenspace and proves weighted-ME stability bounds scaling like O((K−1)ε^2/gap^2) with dependence on cluster volume imbalance.

- Instantiates the framework for SBM: uses ML block-probability estimates to get a rank-K reconstruction and proves ME stability bounds depending on the adjacency singular-value gap and ε = ||Â−A||, but these bounds are often loose in practice.

- Connects framework to prior model-based recovery results (e.g., [18,20]), deriving alternative model-free bounds and improving constants from prior work.

- Highlights resilience to small graph perturbations and provides computable cluster-validation diagnostics that can be evaluated from data.

- Uses standard spectral-perturbation tools (Davis–Kahan, projection-matrix perturbation, Frobenius control) and a geometric link between subspace distance and clustering error.

- Theorems are precise with verifiable conditions and explicit constants; required quantities (ε, gaps) are computable from data.

- Choice of distance metrics (ME and weighted ME) is well motivated; dependence on eigengap and cluster-volume imbalance is transparent.

- Experiments (synthetic PFM, perturbed PFM, LFR, smoothed political blogs) show PFM-based guarantees are informative while SBM bounds are often pessimistic, consistent with theory.

- Paper is well organized and readable; PFM estimator is described algorithmically and clearly.

- Suggests adding a brief main-text summary linking Proposition 7 to the final clustering error bound and a short practical recipe for computing/interpreting ε, δ, δ0.

- Claims novelty in turning model-based recovery results into model-free stability/validation guarantees with computable diagnostics; PFM instantiation and improved constants are new.

- Significant conceptually: enables practitioners to validate clustering without committing to a generative model and unifies model-based and model-free perspectives.

- Main weakness: reliance on spectral gaps and operator-norm concentration limits applicability in very sparse graphs where eigenvector localization occurs.

- SBM bounds are often loose and may pessimistically depend on observed singular-value gaps and ε; tighter model-free detectability analogs would help.

- Balance assumptions penalize unbalanced clusters (δ ≤ δ0); it's unclear if degree-weighted volumes or alternative distances reduce this penalty.

- The framework assumes known K; lacks guidance for selecting K (e.g., eigengap heuristics plus δ robustness).

- Overfitting risk: PFM flexibility can produce small residuals for poor clusterings; recommends holdout edge validation or split-sample fitting.

- DC-SBM is discussed but not instantiated; adding DC-SBM would address degree heterogeneity common in applications.

- Empirics could be strengthened with more real sparse networks and comparisons to regularized spectral methods (nonbacktracking/Bethe-Hessian).

- Questions for authors: can nonbacktracking or Bethe-Hessian embeddings be accommodated for sparse graphs; how sensitive are guarantees to K misspecification; can goodness-of-fit be adapted to cut or max-norms for robustness?

- Pros: novel, practical model-free validation framework; clean spectral perturbation analysis with explicit bounds; strengthens links to model-based theory; well written with sensible experiments.

- Cons: limited in very sparse regimes; SBM bounds often uninformative; needs DC-SBM treatment and K-selection guidance; imbalance and overfitting issues not fully addressed.

- Recommendation: Accept — a well-executed, timely contribution with clear theoretical and practical value, though extensions for sparsity and DC-SBM are needed.
