## Review of “Graph Clustering: Block-models and model free results”

This paper proposes a framework for graph clustering that aims to provide theoretical guarantees without assuming the data originates from a specific block model (SBM, DC-SBM, PFM). The core idea is to establish stability: if a clustering *C* fits the data well, any other clustering *C'* that also fits well must be “close” to *C*. The authors instantiate this framework for PFM and SBM, attempting to connect model-free results to existing model-based recovery guarantees. 

While the motivation of moving beyond strict model assumptions is valuable, this paper falls short of delivering a convincing contribution. The purported “new” theory feels largely like a restatement of existing ideas, lacking significant novelty or rigorous development. Theorem 1, the central claim, is extremely vague – the notion of “close” is ill-defined and hinders concrete application. Subsequent propositions suffer similarly; Proposition 3, for example, relies on unspecified “standard recovery conditions,” weakening its impact. 

The technical execution is also problematic. The proofs, while present, lack clarity and often rely on handwaving arguments instead of precise mathematical justification. The experimental section, while attempting to validate the theory, doesn’t convincingly demonstrate practical benefits or address the limitations inherent in the theoretical results. The connection between the proposed framework and existing recovery theorems (as shown in Sections 4.1 and 4.2) is more a recitation of known results than a synergistic advancement. Several references are cited (e.g. [6, 16]) but not adequately engaged with regarding the differences between the existing work and this proposal.

**Quality:** The paper lacks technical soundness. Claims are often unsubstantiated and the theoretical framework lacks precision. It feels incomplete, resembling a position paper more than a rigorous scientific contribution. The authors do acknowledge limitations, but the extent of these limitations severely undermines the core argument.
**Clarity:** The paper is difficult to follow due to the lack of precise definitions and rigorous derivations. The organization is reasonable, but the informal tone of many arguments hinders understanding.
**Originality:** The core idea is not novel. The attempt to generalize model-based results is interesting but executed weakly.
**Significance:** The results are unlikely to be broadly impactful due to the lack of concrete guarantees and the imprecision of the theoretical framework.



**Arguments for rejection:**

*   Lack of rigorous theory and vague definitions.
*   Unconvincing and unsubstantiated claims.
*   Limited novelty and incremental contribution.
*   Weak experimental validation.



**Arguments for acceptance:** (Very limited)

*   The motivation for moving beyond model assumptions is relevant.
*   Attempts to connect model-free and model-based recovery frameworks.




**Overall Assessment:** I strongly recommend rejecting this paper. While the authors identify an interesting direction, the work presented is insufficient in terms of technical rigor and practical impact to warrant acceptance at a competitive conference like NIPS. A significantly revised version, with substantial strengthening of the theoretical framework and more compelling experimental evidence, would be necessary for reconsideration.
