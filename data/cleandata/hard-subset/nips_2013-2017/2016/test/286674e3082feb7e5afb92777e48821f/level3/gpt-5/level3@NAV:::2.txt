Summary and relation to prior work: This paper proposes a model-free validation framework for graph clustering that formalizes a locally convex–like property: among clusterings that fit a network equally well under a given model class, any two must be close in misclassification distance. The authors instantiate this idea for the Preference Frame Model (PFM) and the Stochastic Block Model (SBM). Using Davis–Kahan perturbation bounds and Meilă’s local equivalence between clustering distances, they show that goodness-of-fit (spectral or operator-norm) implies proximity in weighted or unweighted misclassification error, and they prove an equivalence between several clustering distance measures in the local regime. They connect their “model-free” guarantees to classical model-based recovery results (e.g., Rohe–Chatterjee–Yu; Qin–Rohe; Peng et al. 2015) and provide experiments on synthetic PFM/SBM data, LFR benchmarks, and Political Blogs, computing fully data-driven bounds.

Quality: The paper is technically sound. The proofs are careful and rely on standard but appropriate tools (Davis–Kahan; spectral decompositions; local equivalence). Claims are supported by theorems with computable quantities and by empirical validations that probe the tightness/breaking points of the bounds. Some arguments are relatively simple extensions of known results, and constants/eigengap conditions can be conservative, especially for SBM.

Clarity: The manuscript is well written and well organized. Core ideas, assumptions, and algorithms (PFM/SMB estimators, fit metrics, distances) are clearly defined, with proofs in the supplement. Reproducibility is facilitated by explicit, computable criteria.

Originality: The unifying model-free perspective—showing that equal fit implies closeness and tying together multiple clustering distances—is novel and timely, though technically built from established perturbation theory and prior SBM recovery analyses (cf. Peng et al. 2015; related stability and clusterability works).

Significance: The results offer a practical pathway to post hoc validation of clusterings in networks and help clarify when model-based fits are meaningfully unique. Potential applications (e.g., detecting clusterability or resilience) are compelling, but the paper lacks formal results there; SBM bounds appear loose in practice.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, useful framework with computable, data-driven guarantees
- Conceptual bridge between model-based recovery and model-free validation
- Local equivalence of clustering distances clarified and exploited
- Solid, readable exposition with informative experiments

Cons for acceptance:
- Theoretical novelty modest; extensions of known tools
- Strong eigengap/balance assumptions; SBM bounds often uninformative
- Applications (clusterability detection) discussed but not formalized
- Limited empirical breadth beyond spectral methods and undirected settings

Overall: In scope for NeurIPS; a solid, thoughtful contribution with moderate novelty and practical value. I lean toward acceptance if the committee values principled validation frameworks; otherwise, a borderline accept.
