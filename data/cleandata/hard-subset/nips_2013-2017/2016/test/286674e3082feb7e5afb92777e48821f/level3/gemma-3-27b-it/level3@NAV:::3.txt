## Review of "Graph Clustering: Block-models and model free results"

This paper introduces a framework for graph clustering that aims to provide theoretical guarantees without assuming the data originates from a specific block model (SBM, DC-SBM, PFM). The core idea is to demonstrate that if a clustering fits the data well according to a given model, any other clustering that also fits well must be a small perturbation of the original. This framework is then instantiated for PFM and SBM, and connections to existing model-based recovery results are explored.

The presentation is very clear and mathematically rigorous. The algorithms and theorems are well-defined, and the notation is consistent throughout. The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of the relevant literature in spectral clustering and stochastic block models, referencing key papers in the field (e.g., [18, 20, 15]). The paper builds upon prior work by attempting to bridge the gap between model-based and model-agnostic approaches to clustering, and the proofs appear sound, although detailed verification is beyond the scope of this review.

However, the primary weakness lies in the motivation and overall presentation. While the technical aspects are strong, the paper feels like a collection of algorithms and theorems without a central, compelling narrative. The motivation for studying this model-free approach isn't clearly articulated – why is it important to have guarantees that *don't* rely on model assumptions? The paper states this is a limitation of existing results but doesn’t fully expand on practical scenarios where this would be crucial.  Furthermore, the work largely consists of adapting existing algorithms with additional constraints.  The truly novel contribution seems to be the constraints introduced at the end, but their impact isn't fully explored beyond the theoretical bounds.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical framework and proofs appear rigorous.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow.
*   **Potential for Future Work:** The framework opens possibilities for extending model-based recovery guarantees to broader contexts.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Lack of Strong Motivation:** The need for a model-free approach isn’t convincingly justified.
*   **Incremental Contribution:** The work largely modifies existing algorithms; the novelty is limited.
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** While an experimental section is included, it is not central and doesn’t fully demonstrate the practical benefits of the framework.



**Overall:** This paper presents a solid technical contribution, but its lack of a strong motivating narrative and incremental nature make it a borderline submission. While the results are interesting and mathematically well-supported, the paper needs to better articulate the significance of its contribution to the field. I recommend a **weak accept** contingent on the authors strengthening the introduction to better motivate the problem and discuss the practical implications of their work.
