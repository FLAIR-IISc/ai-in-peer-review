Summary and relation to prior work: The paper proposes a model-free validation framework for graph clustering that provides stability-style guarantees without assuming a generative model. Given a graph G and a clustering C, the authors define fitted models M(G, C) within block-model families (PFM and SBM) and show that if M(G, C) fits G well, any other clustering that also fits well must be close to C (in misclassification error, including a degree-weighted variant). They instantiate this through explicit algorithms: a PFM estimator that aligns eigenspaces via Davis–Kahan perturbation bounds and an MLE-style SBM estimator. Theorems bound dist(C, C′) by functions of spectral gaps and fit errors, with experiments on synthetic PFM, LFR graphs, and Political Blogs. The work connects to spectral clustering and SBM theory (Ng et al. 2002; Rohe, Chatterjee, Yu 2011; Qin & Rohe 2013; Abbe & Sandon 2015; Karrer & Newman 2011), stability/clusterability (Bilu & Linial 2010; Awasthi 2016), and geometric relations between clustering distances (Meilă 2007). The idea is close in spirit to confidence-set/robustness perspectives (Airoldi et al. 2011).

Strengths:
- Clear algorithmic presentation and notation; quantities in bounds are computable from data.
- Useful unification: relates model-based recovery results to model-free stability guarantees.
- Sound reliance on classical perturbation tools (Davis–Kahan) and careful distance definitions (weighted ME).
- Experiments illustrate how guarantees degrade with perturbations and sampling.

Weaknesses:
- Motivation is underdeveloped: why present as a sequence of algorithms rather than a cohesive testing procedure for comparing clusterings? The practical decision process (when to accept/reject similarity) is not articulated.
- Many methods are modifications of existing algorithms; the novelty seems concentrated in the end-stage constraints (δ ≤ δ0) and packaging of known perturbation arguments.
- SBM bounds are often vacuous in practice (as noted by the authors), limiting significance; conditions require strong eigengaps.
- Limited empirical breadth and no statistical calibration for “testing similarity” (e.g., Type I/II error control).

Quality: Technically careful, with appropriate citations and reasonable proofs (though several are deferred). Assumptions (spectral gap, spectral-norm closeness) may be strong.

Clarity: Generally well written and organized; algorithms are easy to follow. The paper would benefit from a clearer problem statement and end-to-end workflow for “testing similarity.”

Originality: Incremental; the core ideas draw on established spectral perturbation and SBM estimation. The unifying, computable constraints are the main new element.

Significance: Potentially useful for practitioners seeking post-hoc validation, but current bounds’ looseness (especially for SBM) reduces impact.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, technically sound instantiation of a model-free validation viewpoint.
- Computable, data-dependent guarantees.

Cons against acceptance:
- Limited novelty beyond re-packaging known tools; unclear motivation and testing protocol.
- Practical utility constrained by strong conditions and loose SBM bounds.

Suggestions: Strengthen the motivation with a formal test for clustering similarity (including error control), tighten constants, expand empirical evaluation (more real datasets and baselines), and compare to bootstrap-based robustness and confidence-set approaches.
