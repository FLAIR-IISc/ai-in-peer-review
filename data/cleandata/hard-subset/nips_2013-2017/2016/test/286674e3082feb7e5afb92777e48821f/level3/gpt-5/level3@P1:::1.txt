This paper proposes a distribution-free framework for validating graph clusterings: if a clustering C “fits” the observed graph G well with respect to a model class M (e.g., PFM, SBM), then any other clustering C’ that also fits G well must be close to C. The key move is to avoid assuming G was sampled from a generative model; instead, the authors fit a proxy model M(G, C) to the observed graph given C and quantify goodness-of-fit via spectral (or Frobenius) norms that are computable from data. Instantiations are provided for the Preference Frame Model (PFM) and the Stochastic Block Model (SBM): (i) for PFM, the proxy model is built by aligning the leading K-dimensional eigenspace of the normalized Laplacian L̂ with the subspace implied by C, and stability follows from Davis–Kahan perturbation bounds combined with local equivalence of clustering distances; (ii) for SBM, the proxy is the MLE B for inter-block probabilities and stability follows from eigen-structure of A and an eigengap condition. The framework also connects to classical model-based results (Rohe et al. 2011; Qin & Rohe 2013; Wan & Meilă 2016), allowing those recovery theorems to yield model-free guarantees when their assumptions are reinterpreted as data-driven fit conditions. Experiments on synthetic PFM, perturbed PFM, LFR, and Political Blogs illustrate the computability of the quantities and the relative tightness of PFM vs. SBM bounds.

Quality
- Technical soundness: Theorems 4 (PFM) and 9 (SBM) are derived from standard and appropriate tools (Davis–Kahan, subspace alignment, spectral perturbation), with precise, data-computable constants. The PFM estimator is not MLE but is consistent under standard PFM recovery conditions and tailored to enable a sharp eigenspace comparison; the SBM estimator is the MLE. The proofs leverage local equivalence of clustering distances (Meilă 2007) rather than combinatorial counting, which is elegant.
- Assumptions: Dependence on the eigengap (for L̂ or Â) is explicit and natural for spectral methods, and the goodness-of-fit assumptions are verifiable. However, the reliance on a proxy-model fit under a specific norm (||L̂ − L|| or ||Â − A||) narrows what qualifies as “good fit” and may exclude clusterings that are good under cut-based or likelihood criteria yet misaligned in spectral norm. For SBM, the bounds are relatively weak in practice (as acknowledged), often failing to certify stability even on LFR.
- Empirics: The experiments illustrate both the computability of ε, δ and the qualitative behavior of the bounds. A limitation is that some real graphs (Political Blogs) require smoothing to exhibit stability under the proposed criteria, whereas classical community detection recovers the labels without smoothing.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well organized. The construction of PFM(G, C) and the role of the alignment step (YZ projection and SVD) are explained cleanly. Many proofs are deferred to the supplement, but the blueprint is understandable.
- Minor notation/style: the pervasive use of hats for observed quantities (Â, D̂, L̂) is standard in some communities but arguably heavy here; since all objects are empirical, dropping hats except where needed to contrast with proxy models would improve readability. Clarify at first use that the normalized Laplacian convention is I − D−1/2 A D−1/2, since the text alternates between L̂ and I − L̂.

Originality
- The distribution-free stability guarantee—“any two good fits must be close”—for graph clustering, instantiated for PFM and SBM with fully data-driven quantities, is novel and interesting. The idea of “turning around” model-based recovery results to obtain model-free guarantees is also fresh.

Significance
- Conceptually, this provides a practical validation tool: given any candidate clustering, one can compute a proxy-model fit and certify uniqueness up to small perturbations without assuming the data were generated by that model. This responds to a common gap in community detection practice.
- Practically, impact is mixed: the PFM results are informative on several datasets, but the SBM bounds are often too loose; the framework’s focus on spectral-norm fit to a proxy model may limit applicability on heterogeneous, sparse, or heavy-tailed networks where degree correction and regularization are critical.

Major weaknesses
- Distance metric: The main PFM result uses the degree-weighted misclassification error (wME). While defensible (degree as a natural node measure; equivalence to earth mover’s distance on weighted nodes), it is non-standard in the community detection literature. The paper should (a) provide parallel guarantees in unweighted ME, or (b) at least quantify the relationship between wME and ME under degree heterogeneity, to facilitate comparison with prior work and practical interpretation.
- Proxy-model restriction: Defining “good fit” through closeness of L̂ (or Â) to a fitted model compatible with C is a strong modeling choice that favors normalized Laplacian alignment. This excludes alternative, arguably natural criteria (e.g., modularity, conductance, likelihood under DC-SBM with regularization). The authors partially mitigate this by showing alternative instantiations, but the core guarantees remain tied to the spectral norm and the constructed proxies, which may limit real-world utility.

Minor comments and suggestions
- Provide an explicit DC-SBM instantiation (model estimator and theorem), not just PFM and SBM; many real networks require degree correction.
- Discuss selection of K and sensitivity of δ to K; can the framework produce a model-free certificate for K via, e.g., gaps in δ as K varies?
- Quantify the relationship between eigengap and the tightness of δ; include guidance on when smoothing (Â^p) is advisable.
- Compare more directly with confidence-set approaches for networks (Airoldi et al. 2011) and with stability under loss-based clustering (Peng–Sun–Zanetti).
- Report empirical scatter of certified δ versus actual misclassification error to show calibration of the bounds.
- Clarify computational cost of computing ε and δ and the SVD steps for large graphs.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel, distribution-free stability framework with computable guarantees.
- Technically clean, leveraging classical perturbation tools with explicit constants.
- Bridges model-based recovery to model-free validation; contributes new proof techniques and tighter constants for PFM-related results.

Arguments against acceptance
- Practical utility is limited by reliance on wME and proxy-model spectral fit; SBM guarantees often non-informative.
- Empirical evaluation on real data is thin and requires smoothing to produce certificates.
- Missing an explicit DC-SBM theorem, which is crucial for applicability.

Overall, this is an interesting theoretical contribution with a clear idea, careful analysis, and potential to influence how we validate clusterings in networks. Strengthening the empirical section and broadening the set of fit criteria (or adding DC-SBM guarantees) would substantially increase impact.
