## Review of “Graph Clustering: Block-models and model free results”

This paper proposes a framework for graph clustering that aims to provide theoretical guarantees without assuming the data originates from a specific block model (SBM, DC-SBM, PFM). The core idea revolves around establishing stability: if a clustering *C* fits the data well, any other clustering *C’* that also fits well must be “close” to *C*. The authors instantiate this framework for the PFM and SBM, attempting to connect model-free and model-based recovery results.

The paper builds upon a substantial body of work in spectral clustering and block model analysis, referencing key papers from NIPS and related venues (e.g., Abbe et al., Rohe et al., Karrer et al.). The motivation – moving beyond model assumptions – is relevant, as real-world graphs often deviate from idealized block model structures. The attempt to bridge model-free and model-based guarantees is also a potentially valuable direction.

However, the paper suffers from significant weaknesses. The proposed “theory” feels largely handwaving, lacking the rigor expected of a theoretical NIPS submission. Theorem 1 is particularly vague; the notion of “close” is ill-defined and lacks precision. Propositions 3 (for PFM) relies on underspecified “standard recovery conditions” without sufficient elaboration. While the authors attempt to instantiate the framework with concrete theorems (4 & 9), the conditions for these theorems appear restrictive and the experimental results, while presented, don’t convincingly demonstrate the practical utility or robustness of the approach. The connection to existing model-based results, while mentioned, feels superficial – simply stating that results can be “exploited” isn’t a substantial contribution. 

The presentation also contributes to the issues. Claims are often made without sufficient justification, and the proofs, while present, are complex and lack clarity. The experimental section feels somewhat disconnected from the theoretical development, and the discussion doesn’t adequately address the limitations of the proposed framework. The numerous references are appreciated, but don’t compensate for the lack of novelty and precision in the core theoretical contributions.

**Arguments for rejection:**

*   Lack of theoretical rigor and novelty.
*   Vague definitions and unsubstantiated claims.
*   Restrictive conditions and limited practical impact.
*   Weak connection to existing work beyond citation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a relevant problem (model-free clustering).
*   Attempts to bridge model-based and model-free approaches.



**Overall, I recommend rejection.** The paper presents an interesting idea but falls short of delivering a technically sound and novel contribution suitable for NIPS. Significant revisions addressing the issues of rigor, clarity, and justification would be necessary for reconsideration.
