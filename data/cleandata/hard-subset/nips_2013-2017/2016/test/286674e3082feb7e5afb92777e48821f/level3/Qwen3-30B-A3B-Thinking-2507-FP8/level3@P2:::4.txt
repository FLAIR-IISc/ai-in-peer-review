

# Review of "Graph Clustering: Block-models and model free results"

This paper proposes a framework for obtaining theoretical guarantees for graph clustering without assuming the data is generated from a specific block model. The authors develop a "model-free" approach that provides stability guarantees for clusterings by showing that if a clustering fits the data well according to a model-based estimator, then any other clustering that fits the data well must be close to the original clustering. The framework connects model-based and model-free results, allowing existing model-based recovery theorems to be repurposed in a model-free setting. The authors instantiate this framework for the PFM and SBM models, deriving computable guarantees that depend on statistics of the data rather than model assumptions.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the literature on community detection by addressing a fundamental limitation of model-based approaches. While previous work has focused on recovery guarantees under specific generative models (e.g., [18, 20]), this paper provides a way to validate clusterings without model assumptions, which is important for practical applications where the true data-generating process is unknown. The framework's computability is a significant strength, as all quantities in the theorems can be computed directly from the data.

The paper demonstrates several technical strengths: the proofs use elementary techniques that are simpler than those in previous work, and the framework successfully ties together model-based and model-free perspectives. The authors show that model-free guarantees can be derived from existing model-based theorems, and even improve them (e.g., by a factor of 128 for PFM).

However, the paper has several significant weaknesses. First, the treatment of PFM and SBM is inconsistent despite PFM encompassing SBM as a special case. The authors use different estimators (Laplacian for PFM vs. adjacency for SBM) and different metrics (weighted ME vs. ME), without justification. This inconsistency undermines the paper's central claim of providing a unified framework. The omission of DC-SBM results (which is a special case of PFM) is particularly problematic.

The experimental section reveals practical limitations. The PFM framework tolerates only tiny perturbations before the stability guarantee fails, contradicting the authors' claim that "large eigengap makes PFM(G,C) differ from PFM(G,C0) even for very small perturbations" - this should be qualified. The SBM conditions are so restrictive that the paper states "for the SBM model class, the C cannot be proved stable even for the LFR data," undermining the practical relevance of the approach.

Theoretical and experimental mismatch is another major issue: the theoretical guarantees for PFM are stated in weighted ME distance, but the experiments use ME distance. This inconsistency is not addressed, making it difficult to evaluate the practical applicability of the theorems.

The related work section is incomplete, omitting several key papers on model-free stability (Kumar & Kannan 2010; Balcan et al. 2012/2013), and the connection to these works is not clarified. The paper also fails to address whether the framework can extend beyond block models to other settings where spectral clustering is used (e.g., hierarchical clustering).

The paper contains several notational inconsistencies (e.g., different use of B, C, Y across sections) and ambiguous assumptions (e.g., Assumption 5 appears to use ||L̂² - L²||_F rather than ||L̂ - L||_F²). The claim that the framework handles splits/merges (K vs K+1 clusters) is incorrect, as the theorems require clusterings to have the same number of clusters.

While the paper's core idea is valuable and the framework is technically sound, the practical limitations revealed in the experiments and the inconsistent treatment of the models significantly reduce its impact. The theoretical guarantees for SBM are too restrictive to be useful, and the practical utility of the PFM results is limited by the sensitivity to perturbations. The paper would benefit from addressing these limitations, clarifying the relationship between the PFM and SBM results, and demonstrating the framework's applicability to a broader range of clustering settings.

Overall, the paper makes an important conceptual contribution by bridging model-based and model-free approaches to clustering validation. However, the practical limitations and theoretical inconsistencies prevent it from making a strong contribution to the NIPS community. I recommend rejection, as the paper's limitations outweigh its contributions, particularly given the high bar for acceptance at NIPS.
