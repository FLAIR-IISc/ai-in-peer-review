This paper presents a novel framework for providing theoretical guarantees for graph clustering, specifically focusing on spectral clustering approaches, *without* assuming the data originates from a specific generative model like the Stochastic Block Model (SBM) or its variations. The authors demonstrate that if a clustering fits the data well according to a given model, then any other clustering that also fits well must be close to the original. This allows them to leverage existing model-based recovery theorems to derive model-free guarantees, and also to strengthen results within the model-based framework. The work is well-motivated, clearly written, and addresses a significant challenge in the field of community detection.

The paper builds upon a strong foundation of prior work in spectral clustering (Ng et al., 2002; von Luxburg, 2007), stochastic block models (Holland et al., 1983; Abbe & Sandon, 2015), and related areas like degree-corrected SBMs (Karrer & Newman, 2011; Qin & Rohe, 2013) and the Preference Frame Model (Wan & Meila, 2016). The authors skillfully connect their approach to these existing lines of research, demonstrating a thorough understanding of the landscape.  Notably, they acknowledge related work on stability in clustering (Bilu & Linial, 2009; Meilă, 2007) and contrast their approach, focusing on the model-based to model-free connection. The exploration of how model-based recovery theorems can be 'turned around' to provide model-free guarantees is particularly insightful.

The strengths of this paper are numerous. The technical contributions are significant, offering a unified framework and concrete guarantees for spectral clustering that are independent of specific model assumptions. The theorems are clearly stated and the proofs, while detailed, appear rigorous (though a complete verification is beyond the scope of this review). The authors carefully define relevant quantities (e.g., goodness of fit, distance between clusterings) and justify their choices.  The experimental evaluation, while relatively limited in scope, provides empirical support for the theoretical results and highlights the practical implications of the framework. The inclusion of both synthetic and real-world datasets is appreciated. The discussion section effectively outlines the limitations of the work and suggests avenues for future research. 

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. The core reliance on spectral algorithms, and particularly the sensitivity of the bounds to the eigengap, represents a potential limitation. As the authors acknowledge, these algorithms can be less effective for very sparse graphs, and the resulting bounds may become intractable in such settings. The comparison to related work, particularly the bounds presented in Peng et al. (2016), suggests that the proposed bounds may not always be the tightest possible. While the authors demonstrate the applicability of their theorems to the SBM and PFM, exploring other generative models, or providing more general conditions under which the framework applies, would further strengthen the contribution. The experimental section would benefit from a more extensive comparison to state-of-the-art community detection algorithms, alongside a more thorough analysis of the parameter sensitivity of the proposed approach.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound framework for model-free graph clustering guarantees.
*   Clear and rigorous presentation of the mathematical results.
*   Strong connection to and leverage of existing work in spectral clustering and generative models.
*   Provides a useful perspective on the interplay between model-based and model-free approaches.
*   Well-written and enjoyable to read.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The reliance on spectral algorithms and eigengap conditions could limit applicability to sparse graphs.
*   The proposed bounds might not be the tightest possible, given existing related work.
*   The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant contributions to the field of community detection. The proposed framework is both theoretically elegant and practically relevant, offering a valuable tool for validating and understanding clustering results without relying on strong model assumptions. While there are some limitations, the strengths of the paper clearly outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically requesting that the authors address the comments regarding the limitations of the spectral approach and the potential for tightening the bounds.  A more comprehensive experimental evaluation, including comparisons to other state-of-the-art algorithms, would also be beneficial.

**Recommendation:** Accept with minor revisions.
