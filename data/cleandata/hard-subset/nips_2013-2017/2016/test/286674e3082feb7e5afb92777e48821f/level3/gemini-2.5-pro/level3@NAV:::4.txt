This paper presents a framework for providing model-free stability guarantees for graph clustering algorithms. The central idea is to show that if a given clustering and its associated block model (e.g., PFM or SBM) fit the graph data well, then any other clustering that also provides a good fit must be close to the original one. This is a valuable contribution, as it aims to validate clustering results based on computable, data-dependent quantities without assuming the data originates from a specific generative model, a common limitation in prior theoretical work on block models.

The primary strength of this work is its originality and potential significance. The proposed model-free template is an elegant and important step towards certifying the quality of clusterings on real-world networks where model assumptions may not hold. The ability to "turn around" model-based recovery theorems into model-free guarantees is a novel and powerful concept.

However, the paper suffers from several major weaknesses that limit its impact.

**Quality and Clarity:** The technical execution and presentation lack rigor. The separate and inconsistent treatment of PFM and SBM is a central flaw; the authors use different estimators (Laplacian-based vs. adjacency-based) and distance metrics (weighted ME vs. ME) without justification, even though PFM generalizes SBM. Several key definitions are ambiguous, such as the inner product in Proposition 10 and the norm in Assumption 5. Furthermore, the experiments for PFM are critically flawed, as the theory is developed for the weighted misclassification error, while perturbations are evaluated using the standard (unweighted) ME. This mismatch invalidates the experimental validation of the PFM theory.

**Originality and Related Work:** The submission fails to adequately connect with significant prior art. It omits several key papers on model-free clustering stability (e.g., Kumar & Kannan, 2010; Balcan et al., 2013) and does not explain how its proposed notion of stability relates to established concepts like perturbation or approximation stability.

**Significance:** The practical relevance of the derived bounds is questionable. The simulations show that the stability guarantees for PFM hold only for extremely small perturbations, while the conditions for SBM are reported to be very difficult to satisfy. This suggests the current bounds may be too loose for practical use.

In summary, while the paper proposes a promising and important research direction, it requires a major revision to address the technical inconsistencies, ambiguous definitions, incomplete literature review, and the significant disconnect between theory and experimental validation.
