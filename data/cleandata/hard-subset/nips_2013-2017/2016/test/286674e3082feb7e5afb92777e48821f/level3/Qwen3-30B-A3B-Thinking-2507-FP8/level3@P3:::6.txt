

# Review of "Graph Clustering: Block-models and model free results"

This paper proposes a framework for graph clustering that claims to provide theoretical guarantees without assuming data comes from a specific model (SBM, DC-SBM, or PFM). The core idea is that if a clustering C fits the data well (i.e., the model M(G,C) fitted to C is close to the observed graph G), then any other clustering C' that also fits the data well must be close to C. The authors instantiate this framework for PFM (Theorem 4) and SBM (Theorem 9), claiming to connect model-based and model-free approaches.

The paper builds on the well-established literature of block models for graph clustering, including seminal work by Snijders & Nowicki (2001), Bickel & Chen (2009), and Rohe et al. (2011). It also relates to recent advances in spectral clustering for community detection (e.g., Abbe & Sandon, 2015; Qin & Rohe, 2013). While the connection between model-based and model-free approaches is conceptually interesting, the paper fails to demonstrate a meaningful advance over existing work.

**Quality:** The paper's main theoretical claims are not technically sound. Theorem 1 is fundamentally vague, stating "if M(G,C) fits G well, then any other clustering C' that also fits G well will be close to C" without defining "fits well" or "close" in precise mathematical terms. The authors' attempt to formalize this in Theorems 4 and 9 relies on underspecified assumptions (e.g., Assumption 2 for PFM states ||L̂− L|| ≤ ε without explaining how to compute or interpret ε in practice). Proposition 3 is informal and references "standard recovery conditions" without specifying what these conditions are or providing citations. The proofs of Theorems 4 and 9 rely on standard perturbation bounds (Davis-Kahan theorem) but fail to demonstrate how their approach offers meaningful improvements over existing techniques.

**Clarity:** The paper lacks precision in defining key concepts. The "goodness of fit" measure (||L̂− L|| ≤ ε) is not properly motivated or justified, and it's unclear how this relates to practical graph analysis. The distance measures (dist and distd̂) are well-defined, but the theorems don't provide actionable guidance on how to choose appropriate thresholds for ε. The experimental section is superficial and doesn't demonstrate the practical utility of the framework.

**Originality:** The paper's core idea is not novel. The connection between model-based and model-free approaches has been explored in other contexts, and the framework largely rephrases existing concepts without significant new contributions. The paper fails to clearly articulate how it differs from previous work, such as the "clusterability" framework by Meilă (2007) or the model-free validation approaches by Balcan et al. (2013). The claimed "model-free guarantees" are largely equivalent to existing stability results for spectral clustering.

**Significance:** The results have limited significance. The theorems provide conditions that are likely to be too restrictive for practical application (as demonstrated by the experimental results where SBM bounds are "not informative" and never satisfied). The paper doesn't provide new insights that would advance the field of graph clustering or offer practical tools for practitioners. The claim that "model-free and model-based results are intimately connected" is not substantiated with meaningful theoretical or empirical evidence.

**Arguments for acceptance:** The paper identifies an interesting problem (model-free clustering guarantees) and presents a framework that conceptually connects model-based and model-free approaches. The experimental results provide some empirical validation of the framework.

**Arguments against acceptance:** The main theorems are vague, lack mathematical precision, and rely on underspecified assumptions. The proofs do not provide meaningful theoretical advances over existing work. The framework does not offer practical advantages over established model-based approaches. The paper contains unsubstantiated claims and fails to demonstrate the value of its "model-free" guarantees in real-world applications.

The paper's main weakness is its failure to provide rigorous, precise mathematical statements that would make its claims meaningful. The framework is presented as a novel contribution but is essentially a rephrasing of existing concepts without substantive new insights. The experimental results fail to convincingly demonstrate the practical utility of the approach, and the theorems are too restrictive to be useful in practice.

I recommend rejection due to the lack of technical soundness, precision, and meaningful contribution to the field. The paper's framework is neither novel nor rigorous, and its claims are unsubstantiated. The authors would need to substantially revise the paper to provide precise mathematical definitions, rigorous proofs, and meaningful comparisons to existing work to make a compelling contribution.
