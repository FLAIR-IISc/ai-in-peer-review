This paper proposes a framework for providing theoretical guarantees for graph clustering algorithms without assuming the data is generated from a specific probabilistic model, such as the Stochastic Block Model (SBM) or Preference Frame Model (PFM). The central idea is to establish stability: if a given clustering fits the data well according to a model's structure, then any other clustering that also fits the data well must be a small perturbation of the first. This approach shifts the burden of proof from unverifiable model assumptions to a computable goodness-of-fit criterion. The authors instantiate this framework for both PFM and SBM, deriving explicit bounds on the distance between two well-fitting clusterings. This work relates to the extensive literature on SBM recovery (e.g., Rohe et al., 2011; Abbe & Sandon, 2015) but takes a fundamentally different, model-free validation perspective, which is a valuable direction.

**Strengths:**
The primary strength of this work lies in its significant conceptual contribution. Addressing the limitation of strong model-based assumptions is an important and practical problem in network analysis. The proposed framework is elegant, and its results—tying clustering stability to computable goodness-of-fit metrics—are potentially very useful for cluster validation. The paper is also very clearly written; the algorithms for constructing model parameters from a given clustering are well-defined, and the theoretical results are presented with precision. The technical quality appears sound, leveraging established matrix perturbation theory.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper's main weakness is its limited technical originality and underdeveloped motivation. The methods presented are largely modifications and applications of existing spectral analysis techniques and perturbation bounds (e.g., Davis-Kahan). While the application is novel, the core machinery is not. More importantly, the paper fails to build a strong narrative for its approach. It reads more as a list of algorithms and theorems rather than a cohesive paper explaining *why* this specific framework is the right way to approach model-free validation. The connection between the different theorems and their implications could be better motivated. The experimental results also raise concerns about the practical significance; while the PFM bounds seem informative, the authors note the SBM bounds are too loose to be useful even on data generated for it, which significantly limits the impact of that result.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Addresses a fundamental and important problem in model-based clustering.
- The proposed model-free validation framework is novel and conceptually appealing.
- The paper is technically sound and clearly presented.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- The technical contributions are incremental, relying heavily on existing methods.
- The motivation is weak, making the work feel like a collection of results rather than a unified contribution.
- The practical utility of the SBM results is shown to be limited, questioning the overall significance.
