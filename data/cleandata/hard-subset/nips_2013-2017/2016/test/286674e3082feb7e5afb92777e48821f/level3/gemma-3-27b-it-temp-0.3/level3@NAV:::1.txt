## Review of "Graph Clustering: Block-models and model free results"

This paper presents a novel framework for graph clustering that provides theoretical guarantees without assuming the data originates from a specific generative model like the Stochastic Block Model (SBM) or Preference Frame Model (PFM). The core idea is compelling: if a clustering well-fits the observed graph, any other equally well-fitting clustering must be “close” to it. This work addresses an important problem – the limitations of model-based clustering in real-world scenarios – and the theoretical contributions are genuinely interesting, offering a potential bridge between model-based and model-free approaches. 

The paper is generally well-written and clear, though the movement of some key details to the supplement does impact readability slightly. The empirical results are well-commented and illustrated, demonstrating the applicability of the theorems. However, the use of “hats” on observed quantities (e.g., Ĥ) feels unconventional and could be clarified. 

Despite its strengths, the paper faces significant limitations. A major concern is the reliance on a non-standard distance metric between clusterings (the weighted Misclassification Error) for the PFM result, raising questions about its naturalness and interpretability. More fundamentally, the analysis utilizes a proxy-model approach – reconstructing a model from a clustering and comparing it to the observed graph – which inherently constrains what constitutes a “good” clustering, focusing heavily on normalized Laplacian approximation. While necessary for obtaining theoretical results, this modeling restriction limits the potential applicability and impact on real-world graphs, as demonstrated by the need for smoothing in some empirical evaluations where classical algorithms perform well without it.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound framework for model-free graph clustering.
*   Addresses a significant limitation of existing model-based approaches.
*   Clear presentation and well-supported empirical evaluation.
*   Potential to strengthen existing model-based recovery results.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Reliance on a potentially unnatural distance metric for the PFM result.
*   Proxy-model approach restricts the scope of “good” clusterings.
*   Limited applicability to real-world graphs without pre-processing.
*   Theoretical results may be overly restrictive in practice.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of graph clustering. While the limitations are substantial and may hinder immediate practical impact, the theoretical framework is innovative and opens up new avenues for research. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically addressing the concerns regarding the distance metric and clearly articulating the limitations of the proxy-model approach. The authors should also discuss how their framework might be extended to accommodate more flexible notions of “goodness of fit” beyond Laplacian approximation.
