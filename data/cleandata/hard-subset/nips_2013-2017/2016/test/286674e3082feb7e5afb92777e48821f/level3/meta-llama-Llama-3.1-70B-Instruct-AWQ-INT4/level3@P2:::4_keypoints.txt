- Proposes a novel model-free framework for graph clustering guarantees, instantiated for SBM and PFM.  
- Provides a generic theorem linking model goodness-of-fit to distance between two clusterings.  
- Derives exact recovery results for SBM and PFM using the proposed template.  
- Paper is well-written, clearly organized, and notation is well-defined.  
- Good balance between theoretical development and experiments.  
- Strength: general theorem offers a unified framework for model-free clustering guarantees.  
- Weakness: inconsistent treatment of PFM and SBM—different estimators and metrics used without justification or direct comparison.  
- Missing results for DC-SBM, despite PFM purportedly including DC-SBM/SBM, is a significant omission.  
- Definition of the Frobenius inner product in Proposition 10 needs clarification.  
- Assumption 5 is ambiguous and requires clarification.  
- Authors should explain the intuition and justification for squaring Laplacians.  
- Generality concerns: need demonstration of applicability to other spectral-clustering settings (e.g., hierarchical clustering).  
- Framework requires C and C' to have same number of clusters, so it cannot handle splits/merges (K vs K+1) where clusterings may still be close.  
- Related work section is incomplete; several model-free stability results and connections to perturbation/approximation/center-perturbation stability are omitted.  
- PFM simulations tolerate only tiny perturbations, raising practical relevance concerns.  
- Claim that large eigengap makes PFM(G,C) sensitive is unqualified and seems contradictory to other observations.  
- SBM conditions appear very strong/hard to satisfy, undermining practical applicability.  
- Minor issues: unclear caption and missing axis labels in Figure 1, and mismatch between theory and experiments.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel framework, useful exact recovery results, and clear presentation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: inconsistent treatments, missing DC-SBM results, incomplete related work, and insufficiently convincing simulations.
