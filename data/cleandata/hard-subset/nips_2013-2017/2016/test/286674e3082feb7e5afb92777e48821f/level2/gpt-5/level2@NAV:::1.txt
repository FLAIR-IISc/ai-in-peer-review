Summary and relation to prior work: The paper proposes a model-free validation framework for graph clustering that provides “correctness” guarantees without assuming a generative block model. The central claim is a generic stability theorem: if a clustering C yields model parameters that fit the observed graph well (measured by spectral norm closeness), then any other well-fitting clustering C′ must be close to C in (weighted) misclassification error. The authors instantiate this for PFM and SBM via concrete, computable estimators (PFM(G, C) and the SBM MLE), deriving bounds that depend on eigengaps and data-driven error ε. They connect the analysis to Davis–Kahan and Meilă’s local equivalence results, and show how existing model-based guarantees (Rohe–Chatterjee–Yu; Qin–Rohe; Wan–Meilă) can be “turned around” to yield model-free guarantees, sometimes tightening constants. Experiments on synthetic PFM, perturbed PFM, LFR, and Political Blogs suggest the PFM bounds are informative while the SBM bounds are often loose. This relates to NIPS work on spectral clustering under (DC-)SBM, Cheeger-based partitioning, and cluster stability/clusterability (e.g., Rohe et al., Qin & Rohe, Peng–Sun–Zanetti, Abbe–Sandon, Meilă).

Quality: The theoretical development is careful and technically sound, with clear assumptions (eigengaps, spectral closeness). The use of Davis–Kahan is appropriate, and the bounds are computable from data. However, the goodness-of-fit assumptions (spectral norm closeness of L̂ to the fitted L or Â to A) can be strong; for SBM, the resulting bounds are often not informative in practice. Empirics are indicative but limited in real-world diversity; some proofs are deferred.

Clarity: Generally well-organized, though notation is heavy and some typos remain. Providing more intuition for the PFM estimator, clarifying δ/δ0, and grouping assumptions early would improve readability.

Originality: High. To my knowledge, model-free stability guarantees tailored to block-model families with computable bounds are novel, as is the systematic reuse of model-based results in a model-free setting.

Significance: Potentially impactful for practitioners seeking cluster validation beyond likelihood-based or resampling heuristics. Current SBM tightness limits applicability; still, the framework opens a valuable direction.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel, general framework with computable, data-driven guarantees
- Solid theory leveraging classical perturbation tools; connects multiple literatures
- Clear practical angle (cluster validation, resilience)

Cons for acceptance:
- SBM bounds often too loose; DC-SBM instantiation is not fully developed
- Strong assumptions on spectral closeness; limited real-data evaluation
- Exposition could be streamlined

Constructive suggestions:
- Provide DC-SBM instantiation and empirical evaluation
- Develop data-driven procedures to certify ε (e.g., bootstrap, concentration-based upper bounds), and discuss sensitivity
- Explore tighter SBM analyses (e.g., leveraging non-backtracking/Bethe Hessian spectra)
- Add more real datasets and report runtime/complexity; release code
- Clarify constants and give practical guidance on interpreting δ and δ0
