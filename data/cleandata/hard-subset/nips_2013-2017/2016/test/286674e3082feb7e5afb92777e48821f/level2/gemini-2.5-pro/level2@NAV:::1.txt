This paper introduces a novel and valuable framework for providing theoretical guarantees for graph clustering without assuming the data is generated from a specific model, such as the Stochastic Block Model (SBM) or Preference Frame Model (PFM). The central claim is that if a clustering `C` yields a model `M(G, C)` that fits the observed graph `G` well (measured by a goodness-of-fit metric ε), then any other clustering `C'` that also fits the data well must be a small perturbation of `C`. This effectively repurposes model-based recovery theorems, which are prevalent in the literature (e.g., Rohe et al., 2011; Wan & Meila, 2016), into model-free validation tools. The authors instantiate this framework for both PFM and SBM, deriving explicit, computable bounds on the distance between two such "good" clusterings.

**Strengths and Weaknesses:**

The primary strength of this work is its high originality and significance. It directly addresses a critical gap between theory and practice in network clustering, where generative model assumptions are rarely verifiable. By providing guarantees based on a checkable goodness-of-fit criterion, the paper makes the rich theory of block models more applicable to real-world data analysis. The technical approach, leveraging matrix perturbation theory (Davis-Kahan theorem), is sound and leads to clear, constant-free bounds, which is a significant advantage for practical application. The connection drawn between their model-free framework and existing model-based results, showing how each can inform and strengthen the other, is particularly insightful.

The main weakness lies in the experimental evaluation. While it successfully demonstrates the computability of the proposed bounds, it is not extensive enough. The derived bounds for the SBM are acknowledged to be too loose to be informative on the tested datasets, which undermines the practical utility of that specific result. The paper would be strengthened by a more thorough discussion of this limitation, potentially exploring the conditions (e.g., graph size, cluster separation) under which the SBM bounds might become tighter. A deeper analysis comparing the theoretical "breaking point" of the guarantee with the empirical degradation of clustering quality would also add significant value.

**Arguments for/against acceptance:**
*   **Pro:** Highly original and significant contribution that bridges the gap between model-based theory and model-free application. Provides a technically sound and potentially practical framework for cluster validation. The derived bounds are explicit and computable.
*   **Con:** Experimental validation is limited, and the derived bounds for the SBM are not shown to be practically informative, raising questions about the scope of the method's applicability.

Overall, this is a strong paper presenting an important idea. Despite the limitations in the SBM experiments, the novelty of the model-free framework and its potential impact on the field are substantial. I recommend acceptance.
