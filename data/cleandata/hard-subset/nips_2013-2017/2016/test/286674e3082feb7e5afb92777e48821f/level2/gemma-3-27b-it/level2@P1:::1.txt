This paper presents a novel framework for clustering graphs with theoretical guarantees that do *not* rely on the assumption that the data is generated from a specific block model (SBM, DC-SBM, PFM). The core idea is to establish guarantees based on how well a clustering *fits* the data, rather than assuming the data *is* a block model. The authors show connections between their model-free framework and existing model-based results, and provide both theoretical and experimental validation.

The paper is well-motivated. The limitations of model-based approaches are clearly articulated, and the need for model-free guarantees is compelling. The framework itself is elegantly stated (Theorem 1) and the instantiation of this framework for the PFM and SBM models is a significant contribution. The proofs appear rigorous, though detailed examination is difficult within the review timeframe. The experimental validation, while perhaps not extensive, provides encouraging evidence that the theoretical results are meaningful in practice. The connections to existing work (e.g., using results from [20] to strengthen guarantees) are also valuable.  The discussion section thoughtfully acknowledges the limitations and potential avenues for future work.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The model-free clustering guarantee is a significant departure from the existing literature.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The framework and its instantiations appear rigorously developed, with clear mathematical formulations.
*   **Connections to Existing Work:** The paper demonstrates a good understanding of related work and cleverly leverages existing results to strengthen its own contributions.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The framework provides a potential path towards more robust clustering algorithms, less susceptible to model misspecification.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the core ideas accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Complexity:** While the quantities required for verification (ε, δ, δ0) are computable, their computation may be non-trivial for large graphs. The paper does not explicitly address the computational cost of these calculations.
*   **Conservatism of Bounds:** As noted in the discussion, the bounds obtained may be conservative, especially for the SBM.  The experimental results suggest this is the case, as the SBM guarantees are rarely met.  Improving the tightness of these bounds would significantly enhance the practical impact of the work.
*   **Limited Experimental Evaluation:**  The experimental section, while helpful, could be expanded. A more thorough comparison with existing clustering algorithms (beyond checking the stability bounds) would be beneficial. Exploring a wider range of graph datasets would also strengthen the conclusions.
*  **Notation:**  The use of both `Â` and `A` to represent matrices (with and without hats) can be confusing at times.  A more consistent notation might improve clarity.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a fundamental limitation of existing clustering methods: the reliance on model assumptions.
*   The proposed framework offers a principled way to obtain clustering guarantees without such assumptions.
*   The theoretical results are well-supported and the experimental validation is encouraging.
*   The connections to existing work are insightful and demonstrate a deep understanding of the field.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The practical applicability of the results may be limited by the computational complexity of verifying the guarantees and the conservatism of the bounds.
*   The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper with significant potential impact. The model-free clustering guarantees are a novel and valuable contribution to the field.  While there are some limitations, particularly regarding computational cost and bound tightness, these do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Discuss the computational complexity of verifying the goodness-of-fit measures (ε, δ, δ0) for large graphs.
2.  Acknowledge the conservatism of the SBM bounds more explicitly and discuss potential avenues for tightening them.
3.  Consider expanding the experimental evaluation to include a more comprehensive comparison with existing clustering algorithms and a wider range of datasets.
4. Ensure consistent notation throughout the paper.
