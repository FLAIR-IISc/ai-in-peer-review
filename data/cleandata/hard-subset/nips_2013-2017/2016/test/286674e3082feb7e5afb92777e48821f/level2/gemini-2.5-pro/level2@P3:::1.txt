**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a novel framework for providing theoretical guarantees for graph clustering without making assumptions about the data generating process. The central idea is to shift from a "model-based" to a "model-free" paradigm. Instead of assuming a graph is sampled from a Stochastic Block Model (SBM) or a Preference Frame Model (PFM), the work proposes a stability-based guarantee: if a given clustering `C` fits the observed graph data `G` well (as measured by the proximity of `G` to a model `M(G,C)` compatible with `C`), then any other clustering `C'` that also provides a good fit must be a small perturbation of `C`.

This general principle is instantiated for both the PFM and SBM. For each model, the paper defines a procedure to construct the model parameters from the data, a goodness-of-fit measure, and derives a theorem that bounds the distance between two well-fitting clusterings. A key contribution is that all quantities in these bounds are computable from the data, enabling practical validation of a given clustering. The work demonstrates connections to the existing model-based recovery literature, showing how its techniques can be used to derive model-free guarantees from model-based theorems and potentially improve existing results. The approach is evaluated on synthetic and real-world datasets.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound and complete piece of work. The theoretical claims are well-supported by proofs that build on established matrix perturbation theory (e.g., the Davis-Kahan theorem), but apply it in a novel context. The framework is rigorous, and the derived bounds for both PFM and SBM are explicit, with no hidden constants, which is a significant strength. The authors are honest about the limitations of their results, noting in the experiments that the derived bounds for the SBM are not informative in practice, which reflects careful evaluation.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The "Generic Theorem" in Section 3 serves as an excellent blueprint, which is then methodically instantiated for PFM and SBM, making the overall argument easy to follow. The notation is consistent, and the background section provides the necessary context. The PFM model construction in Section 3.1 is quite technical; a bit more intuition behind the spectral projection choices could further improve readability, but the procedural description is clear.

**Originality:** The core contribution—re-framing clustering guarantees in a model-free, data-dependent manner—is highly original and significant. While stability has been studied as a criterion for clustering, its application to validate results from model-based algorithms like SBM/PFM without the generative assumption is a novel and important conceptual shift. This work effectively "turns around" existing recovery theorems, using them not to prove an algorithm's correctness under model assumptions, but to certify the uniqueness of a clustering given its fit to the data. This provides a new and valuable perspective on the problem of community detection.

**Significance:** The results are important and likely to be influential. The proposed framework addresses a major limitation of most theoretical work on community detection: the reliance on untestable assumptions about the data's origin. By providing computable, data-driven guarantees, this work builds a bridge between theory and practice. Practitioners can use these tools to assess the stability and meaningfulness of a clustering found on a real-world network. Researchers are likely to build on this framework, for instance, by deriving tighter bounds or extending it to other models. The demonstration that the framework can also be used to improve existing model-based bounds further highlights its significance.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

The paper is strong, but could be improved in a few areas:
1.  **Analysis of SBM Bounds:** The experiments compellingly show that the SBM bounds (Theorem 9) are too loose to be practical. The paper acknowledges this, which is commendable. A deeper discussion on *why* this occurs would be a valuable addition. Is the looseness due to the choice of the spectral norm for the goodness-of-fit (Assumption 4), the specific proof technique, or a fundamental difference in the rigidity of the SBM class compared to PFM? Clarifying this would provide greater insight and guide future work on obtaining tighter, more practical bounds for SBM.
2.  **Comparison with Related Work:** The comparison in Section 5 is helpful. It could be sharpened by further highlighting the conceptual differences between this work and stability results for specific algorithms like in [14, 16]. For instance, a key advantage of the present framework is that its guarantees apply to *any* clustering `C`, regardless of how it was obtained, whereas previous results are often tied to the output of a particular algorithm (e.g., spectral clustering). Emphasizing this point would better position the paper's contribution.
3.  **Demonstrating Failure Modes:** The experiments successfully show the framework certifying stable clusterings. A valuable addition, if space permits, would be a demonstration of a "negative" result. For example, showing that on a graph known to be poorly-clusterable (e.g., a random graph), the conditions of the theorems (`δ ≤ δ0`) are not met for any reasonable clustering, thus correctly signaling the absence of a stable community structure. This would further validate the utility of the proposed criteria.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a highly novel and significant "model-free" framework for validating graph clusterings.
*   Addresses a critical gap between theory (which assumes a model) and practice (where the model is unknown).
*   Provides rigorous, technically sound theorems with fully computable, data-dependent bounds.
*   The proposed ideas are likely to be influential and spur further research in this direction.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The practical utility of the SBM results is limited due to the looseness of the derived bounds, as shown in the paper's own experiments.

**Conclusion:**

This is an excellent paper that makes a strong conceptual and technical contribution to the field of graph clustering. The proposed model-free validation framework is novel, significant, and addresses a crucial limitation of prior work. While the derived bounds for the SBM are not yet practically useful, the PFM results demonstrate the viability of the approach, and the overall framework is a major step forward. The weaknesses identified are minor and primarily relate to areas for future exploration. The paper is well-written, the theory is sound, and the potential impact is high. It is a clear candidate for acceptance.
