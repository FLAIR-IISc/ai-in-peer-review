- Introduces a model-free, stability-based framework: if clustering C fits graph G well, any other well-fitting clustering must be a small perturbation of C.  
- Instantiates the framework for PFM and SBM, giving procedures to construct model parameters, goodness-of-fit measures, and computable bounds.  
- Theoretical results are rigorous, leveraging matrix perturbation theory (e.g., Davis–Kahan), with explicit bounds and no hidden constants.  
- Authors transparently acknowledge limitations; experiments show SBM bounds are not informative in practice.  
- Paper is well-organized and clearly written; the Generic Theorem provides a clear blueprint for instantiations.  
- PFM construction is technical but clear; would benefit from more intuition around spectral projection choices.  
- Highly original: reframes recovery theorems to certify clustering uniqueness without assuming a generative model.  
- Significant: bridges theory and practice by providing data-dependent, computable guarantees that practitioners can use.  
- Experiments validate PFM results on real/synthetic data but reveal SBM bounds are too loose to be practical.  
- Suggestion: analyze why SBM bounds are loose (choice of norm, proof technique, or SBM rigidity) to guide tighter results.  
- Suggestion: strengthen comparison with prior stability work by emphasizing applicability to any clustering, not algorithm-specific outputs.  
- Suggestion: include negative/failure-mode experiments (e.g., random graphs) to show the method correctly flags lack of stable structure.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, fills a gap between model-based theory and practice, technically sound, and likely influential.  
- Argument against acceptance: current SBM bounds have limited practical utility due to looseness.  
- Conclusion: strong, mostly minor weaknesses, and a clear candidate for acceptance.
