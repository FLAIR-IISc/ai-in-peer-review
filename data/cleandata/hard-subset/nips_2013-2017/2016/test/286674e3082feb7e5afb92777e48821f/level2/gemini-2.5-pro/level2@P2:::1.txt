### Summary of the paper

This paper introduces a novel framework for providing theoretical guarantees on the quality of a graph clustering without assuming the graph was generated from a specific probabilistic model (e.g., a Stochastic Block Model). The central idea is one of stability: if a given clustering C, when used to fit a model from a specific class (like PFM or SBM), yields a model that explains the observed graph data well, then any other clustering C' that also explains the data well must be structurally close to C. This shifts the focus from model-based recovery (assuming a ground truth) to model-free validation (checking consistency against the data).

The authors formalize this "Generic Theorem" and provide concrete instantiations for the Preference Frame Model (PFM) and the Stochastic Block Model (SBM). For each, they define a procedure to construct a model from a given clustering, specify a goodness-of-fit measure (typically a spectral or Frobenius norm difference between the observed and model-generated matrices), and derive a bound on the distance between two "good" clusterings. A key aspect is that all quantities in these bounds are computable from the data, making the framework practically applicable. The authors also demonstrate how this framework can leverage and even improve upon existing model-based recovery theorems from the literature. The approach is validated on synthetic and real-world datasets.

### Quality

The paper presents a high-quality, technically sound, and complete piece of work.

**Strengths:**
1.  The theoretical development is rigorous. The main proof strategy, which leverages the Davis-Kahan theorem for subspace perturbation and a result from Meilă (2012) connecting subspace distance to clustering error, is elegant and powerful. This provides a fresh and more direct alternative to the counting arguments often used in SBM literature.
2.  The derived bounds (Theorems 4 and 9) are explicit and depend only on computable quantities like the goodness-of-fit error `ε`, the observed graph's spectral properties (eigengaps), and cluster sizes. This is a significant achievement, as it makes the theory directly usable for validating clustering results on real data, a long-standing challenge.
3.  The authors are careful and honest about the limitations of their results. For instance, they openly state in Section 6 that the derived bounds for the SBM case were not informative in their experiments. This transparency strengthens the credibility of the work.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  While the PFM results are strong, the weakness of the SBM result (Theorem 9) is a notable limitation. The paper would benefit from a deeper discussion as to *why* this bound is so loose. Is it an artifact of the proof technique (e.g., the use of `||Â−A||`), or does it reflect the SBM's relative lack of flexibility compared to the PFM in fitting arbitrary graph structures? Providing some intuition here would be valuable.
2.  The connection to existing work is a major strength, but the claim in Section 4.2 that "the error bound in [20] can be improved by a factor of 128" could be slightly better substantiated in the main text. A brief sentence explaining the source of this significant improvement (e.g., "our geometric approach avoids loose constants inherent in prior combinatorial arguments") would make the claim more impactful.

### Clarity

The paper is very well-written and organized.

**Strengths:**
1.  The structure is logical and easy to follow. The introduction of the "Generic Theorem" in Section 3 provides a clear blueprint, which is then methodically instantiated for PFM and SBM. This makes the core ideas accessible.
2.  The notation is standard and clearly defined. The step-by-step presentation of the PFM and SBM estimation algorithms is helpful for understanding how the models are constructed.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  The experimental setup section could be more detailed. The process of generating perturbed clusterings `C` by "perturbing C0 with gradually increasing noise" is vague. The type of noise and the mechanism for controlling its level should be specified to ensure reproducibility.
2.  While the proofs are relegated to the supplement (which is standard practice), a bit more intuition behind the key steps in the main text could further improve readability. For example, a brief intuitive explanation of why the sinus of the principal angles between subspaces (Proposition 5) is the right quantity to control would be helpful for readers less familiar with matrix perturbation theory.

### Originality

The work is highly original and makes a significant conceptual contribution.

**Strengths:**
1.  The primary contribution—a framework for model-free guarantees—is a novel and important departure from the standard model-based recovery paradigm that dominates the literature on block models. Instead of asking "can we recover the true communities?", this paper asks "is the found community structure stable and uniquely supported by the data?". This is a more practical and arguably more fundamental question for real-world applications where a ground-truth model is unknown.
2.  The idea of re-purposing model-based recovery theorems for model-free validation is clever and insightful, creating a bridge between two traditionally separate lines of inquiry.
3.  The proof techniques, drawing on matrix perturbation theory and the geometric analysis of clustering distances, are original in the context of SBM analysis and offer a new set of tools to the community.

### Significance

The paper's contributions are very significant and likely to have a lasting impact on the field.

**Strengths:**
1.  This work provides a principled and practical tool for cluster validation in graphs. Researchers and practitioners who use spectral clustering on network data now have a method to quantify the stability and reliability of their results, which was previously lacking.
2.  The paper opens up several promising avenues for future research. As the authors note, this includes deriving tighter bounds (especially for SBM), exploring sharp thresholds from a model-free perspective, and developing negative guarantees (i.e., proofs of non-uniqueness).
3.  The framework itself is general and could potentially be extended to other clustering models and data types beyond graphs, broadening its impact. The connection between goodness-of-fit and solution stability is a fundamental concept in unsupervised learning.

**Conclusion:**

This is an excellent paper that introduces a novel, significant, and technically strong framework for the model-free validation of graph clusterings. It addresses a critical gap in the existing literature by shifting the focus from model-based recovery to data-driven stability. The theoretical results are elegant and the framework is demonstrated to be practical. Despite minor weaknesses in the experimental details and the looseness of the SBM bound, the overall contribution is outstanding. This paper should be accepted.
