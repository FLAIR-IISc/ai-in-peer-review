## Review of "Graph Clustering: Block-models and model free results"

This paper presents a novel framework for clustering graphs with theoretical guarantees that do *not* rely on assumptions about the underlying data generating process (e.g., adherence to a Stochastic Block Model). The core idea is to establish guarantees based on how well a proposed clustering *fits* the observed data, rather than requiring the data to originate from a specific model. The authors demonstrate this framework by deriving model-free guarantees for the Stochastic Block Model (SBM) and Preference Frame Model (PFM), and importantly, show connections between model-free and model-based results, even strengthening existing bounds. 

The work is technically sound and addresses a significant limitation of existing graph clustering research – the often unrealistic assumption of model adherence. The approach of focusing on “goodness of fit” is conceptually appealing and allows for broader applicability. The theorems presented (4 & 9) are clearly stated and build upon established results in spectral graph theory and perturbation bounds.  The experimental section, though limited in scope, provides initial validation and demonstrates the practical implications of the developed framework, highlighting the differing stability profiles of the SBM and PFM under perturbations. 

A major strength is the explicit computation of all parameters used in the theorems, avoiding reliance on hidden constants and enhancing the practical value of the results. The authors also engage with related work effectively, acknowledging prior efforts in stability and community detection. The connection drawn between model-free and model-based results is particularly insightful and offers a promising avenue for future research.

However, the paper suffers from some clarity issues, particularly in the mathematical notation and the flow of proofs. While the authors state their intention for brevity, some derivations feel abrupt and would benefit from more detailed explanation. The experimental section, while demonstrating the theoretical results, is somewhat limited in its exploration of different graph structures and parameter settings. The comparison to existing bounds (e.g., δRCY) are noted to be uninformative, leaving the reader wanting more concrete comparisons.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel framework offering guarantees without model assumptions.
*   Strong theoretical foundation based on well-established concepts.
*   Clear connection between model-free and model-based approaches.
*   Explicitly computable bounds.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Clarity of mathematical presentation could be improved.
*   Limited scope of experimental validation.
*   Some theoretical results lack compelling empirical support (δRCY).



**Overall:** This is a valuable contribution to the field of graph clustering. The core idea is innovative, and the theoretical results are promising. While improvements in clarity and experimental validation would strengthen the paper, it represents a significant step towards more robust and generalizable graph clustering techniques. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions focusing on improving the readability and expanding the experimental evaluation.
