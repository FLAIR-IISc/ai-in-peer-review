This paper proposes a framework for clustering graphs with theoretical guarantees that do *not* rely on the assumption that the data comes from a specific block model (SBM, DC-SBM, PFM). Instead, guarantees are based on statistics of the data itself – specifically, how well a fitted model explains the data. The authors demonstrate this framework by deriving model-free guarantees for both the PFM and SBM, and also show a connection between model-free and model-based recovery results.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a significant limitation of existing block model-based clustering guarantees. The central idea – to focus on goodness-of-fit rather than model assumption – is compelling and offers a potentially powerful generalization. The technical development, particularly Theorems 4 and 9, appear solid and the connection to existing model-based results (Theorems 11 and 12) is valuable. The experiments, while somewhat limited, provide a reasonable demonstration of the framework’s applicability.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The model-free approach to clustering guarantees is a significant departure from the standard literature and addresses a key practical limitation.
* **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical development appears rigorous, and the theorems are clearly stated. The use of perturbation bounds (e.g., Sinus Theorem) is appropriate.
* **Generality:** The framework is presented generally (Theorem 1) and then instantiated for both PFM and SBM, showing broad applicability.
* **Connections to Existing Work:** The authors effectively link their model-free framework to established model-based results, demonstrating the potential for synergistic improvements.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized.  The notation is standard and consistently used.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Conservatism of Bounds:**  The experimental results (Section 6) highlight a significant practical issue: the derived bounds, especially for the SBM (Theorem 9), are often very conservative and difficult to satisfy in practice. While the authors acknowledge this, it’s a crucial limitation.  The comparison to other bounds (e.g., δRCY) suggests their approach may not be competitive in terms of tightness.
* **Assumptions:** The assumptions (e.g., Assumption 1 for PFM, Assumption 3 for SBM) are stated but their practical implications and potential limitations aren't fully discussed. For example, the non-zero eigengap assumption (Assumption 1) may not always hold in real-world networks.
* **Computational Cost:** While the quantities needed for the guarantees are computable, the cost of computing them may be substantial, potentially negating some of the practical benefits. This isn't explicitly addressed.
* **Limited Experimentation:** The experimental evaluation, while present, feels somewhat cursory.  More extensive testing on diverse datasets and comparison to state-of-the-art clustering algorithms would strengthen the claims.  The focus on perturbing existing clusterings isn't particularly insightful.  Demonstrating the usefulness of the framework on real-world datasets *without* prior knowledge of the underlying structure would be more convincing.
* **Relationship to Stability Results:** The connection to stability results (Section 4.4) is mentioned, but not fully explored. It would be useful to explicitly compare the framework to existing stability-based clustering approaches.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a genuinely novel and theoretically sound approach to clustering guarantees.
* The framework is general and applicable to multiple block model variants.
* The connections to existing model-based results are valuable and potentially lead to improvements.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The derived bounds are often very conservative and may limit the practical applicability of the framework.
* The experimental evaluation is limited and doesn't fully demonstrate the benefits of the approach.
* The assumptions required for the guarantees may not hold in all real-world scenarios.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a solid contribution to the field, presenting a theoretically interesting and novel approach to clustering with guarantees. However, the conservatism of the bounds and the limited experimental evaluation raise concerns about its practical impact.  I lean towards **accept** with minor revisions.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

* **Address the Conservatism:**  Investigate ways to tighten the bounds, perhaps by exploring different goodness-of-fit measures or relaxation techniques.
* **Expand the Experimental Evaluation:**  Conduct more extensive experiments on diverse datasets, including real-world networks, and compare the performance to state-of-the-art clustering algorithms.
* **Discuss Assumptions:** Provide a more thorough discussion of the assumptions and their potential limitations.
* **Analyze Computational Cost:** Briefly analyze the computational cost of computing the required quantities.
* **Explore Stability Connections:**  Explicitly compare the framework to existing stability-based clustering approaches.
* **Clarify Practical Implications:**  Discuss scenarios where the model-free guarantees are most likely to be useful, given the limitations.
