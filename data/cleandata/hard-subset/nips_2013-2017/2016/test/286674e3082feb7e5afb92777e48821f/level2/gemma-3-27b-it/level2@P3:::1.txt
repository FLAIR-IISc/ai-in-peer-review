This paper proposes a framework for clustering graphs with theoretical guarantees that do not rely on the assumption that the data is generated from a specific block model. The core idea is to establish stability: if a clustering fits the data well, then any other clustering that also fits the data well must be close to the original. The paper instantiates this framework for the Stochastic Block Model (SBM) and the Preference Frame Model (PFM), and demonstrates connections between model-free and model-based recovery results.

The paper addresses an important problem: the limitations of model-based clustering due to its reliance on strong assumptions. The proposed framework offers a valuable perspective by shifting the focus from model correctness to data fit. The theorems presented are well-defined and provide concrete guarantees, albeit under specific conditions. The connection established between model-free and model-based recovery is insightful and potentially useful for leveraging existing results. The experimental evaluation, while limited, provides some support for the theoretical findings. The clarity of the presentation is generally good, though some sections, particularly the proofs, could benefit from further simplification and explanation.

Strengths:
* **Novelty:** The model-free clustering guarantee is a significant contribution, as it expands the applicability of theoretical results beyond strictly model-generated data.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The theorems are mathematically well-formulated and appear to be rigorously proven (although a detailed verification of all proofs is beyond the scope of this review).
* **Generality:** The proposed framework is general enough to be applicable to different block models.
* **Practical Relevance:** The computable nature of the bounds (ε, δ, etc.) makes the theorems potentially useful in practice.
* **Connections to Existing Work:** Linking model-free and model-based results provides a unified perspective.

Weaknesses:
* **Assumptions:** The theorems rely on conditions such as a non-zero eigengap and specific relationships between eigenvalues, which may not always hold in real-world graphs. The strength of these assumptions and their impact on the applicability of the results are not fully discussed.
* **Conservatism:** The experimental results suggest the conditions for stability can be quite restrictive, especially for the SBM. The bounds derived may be overly conservative, limiting their practical utility.
* **Limited Experimental Validation:** The experiments are relatively limited in scope and do not cover a wide range of graph types or parameter settings. The results do not offer strong evidence of the practical effectiveness of the approach.
* **Proof Complexity:** While the core ideas are clear, some of the proofs are dense and difficult to follow. More detailed explanations or intermediate steps would enhance readability.
* **Comparison to Existing Stability Results:** The paper mentions existing stability results, but the comparison is not thorough. A more comprehensive discussion of how the proposed approach differs from and improves upon previous work would be beneficial.

Arguments for acceptance:
* The paper addresses an important and challenging problem in graph clustering.
* The proposed framework provides a novel and theoretically sound approach to model-free clustering.
* The results have the potential to advance the state of the art in community detection.
* The work is well-written and clearly presented (despite some complexity in the proofs).

Arguments for rejection:
* The assumptions underlying the theorems may be too restrictive for practical application.
* The experimental results provide limited evidence of the practical effectiveness of the approach.
* The proofs are complex and may hinder understanding and verification.



Overall, this is a strong paper with a novel and potentially impactful contribution. The main concern is the practicality of the derived bounds and the limited experimental validation. However, the theoretical soundness and the general framework are compelling. 

Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions.

Specifically, the authors should:
1.  Provide a more detailed discussion of the assumptions and their implications for real-world graphs.
2.  Expand the experimental evaluation to include a wider range of graph types and parameter settings.
3.  Provide more detailed explanations or intermediate steps in the proofs to enhance readability.
4.  Offer a more comprehensive comparison to existing stability results in the literature.
5. Explore the potential for tightening the bounds, particularly for the SBM.
