This paper presents a novel framework for providing theoretical guarantees for clustering algorithms *without* making assumptions about the underlying data generating process. This is a bold and ambitious goal, and the authors successfully demonstrate the feasibility of this approach by instantiating it for both the SBM and PFM models. The core idea – proving that any other clustering that fits the data equally well must be a small perturbation of the original clustering – is elegant and insightful. The connection drawn between model-free and model-based results is also a valuable contribution.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The model-free guarantee framework is genuinely novel. Existing work relies heavily on model assumptions, and this paper breaks that mold.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The theorems are stated clearly and appear to be rigorously proven (though a deeper dive into the supplementary material would be ideal). The use of perturbation theory (Davis-Kahan) is appropriate.
*   **Generality:** The framework is general enough to be applied to different block models, and the authors demonstrate this with SBM and PFM.
*   **Potential Impact:** If successful, this approach could significantly broaden the applicability of clustering guarantees to real-world datasets where model assumptions are difficult to verify.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is, for the most part, clearly written and well-organized.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Assumptions:** While striving for model-free guarantees, the theorems still rely on assumptions (e.g., eigengap condition, goodness of fit measures). It’s not immediately clear how robust these assumptions are in practice.
*   **Conservatism:** The experimental results (while encouraging) suggest the bounds are quite conservative, especially for the SBM. The SBM guarantees often aren’t met, even for data generated from the model itself.
*   **Limited Experimental Validation:** The experimental section, while present, feels somewhat limited in scope. It primarily serves to demonstrate the computability of the quantities involved, rather than a thorough evaluation of the framework's practical utility.  More diverse datasets and comparisons with existing methods would strengthen the paper.
*   **Connection to Existing Work:** While the related work section is reasonably comprehensive, a more detailed discussion of how this framework complements (or contrasts with) existing stability-based clustering methods (e.g., those based on the normalized cut) would be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a genuinely new and promising framework for clustering validation.
*   The theoretical results are solid and well-motivated.
*   The work has the potential to significantly impact the field of network clustering.
*   The authors demonstrate the feasibility of the approach with two important block models.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical assumptions, while stated, may be overly restrictive.
*   The experimental results suggest the bounds are conservative and may not be practically useful in many scenarios.
*   The limited experimental validation prevents a full assessment of the framework’s real-world applicability.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant step forward in the theory of graph clustering. The authors have tackled a difficult problem with a novel and elegant approach. While the theoretical assumptions and conservative bounds are weaknesses, the originality and potential impact of the work are substantial. I am willing to overlook these flaws, given the boldness of the idea and the clear potential for future research. I lean towards **Accept**, with the caveat that the authors should address the limitations identified above in future work. A more extensive experimental evaluation, exploring the robustness of the assumptions and investigating techniques for tightening the bounds, would greatly strengthen the contribution.
