

# Peer Review: "Graph Clustering: Block-models and model free results"

## Summary

This paper introduces a novel framework for providing theoretical guarantees for graph clustering algorithms without assuming the data comes from a specific generative model (like SBM, DC-SBM, or PFM). The core insight is that if a model-based clustering algorithm produces a clustering that fits the observed graph well according to a defined goodness-of-fit measure, then any other clustering that also fits the data well must be close to the original clustering. The authors formalize this as a "Generic Theorem" and instantiate it for two specific models: the Preference Frame Model (PFM) and the Stochastic Block Model (SBM). The paper builds on the extensive literature on model-based clustering (e.g., SBM, DC-SBM, PFM) which has focused on recovery guarantees under model assumptions, and makes a significant conceptual shift toward model-free guarantees.

The work connects to previous NIPS contributions on spectral clustering (e.g., Rohe et al., "Spectral clustering and the high-dimensional stochastic blockmodel," NIPS 2011) and clusterability (e.g., Awasthi, "Clustering under stability assumptions," Encyclopedia of Algorithms, 2014), while offering a fresh perspective on how model-based recovery theorems can be repurposed for model-free validation.

## Strengths

1. **Conceptual Novelty and Contribution**: The paper's main contribution is the framework for model-free guarantees, which represents a significant conceptual shift from the standard model-based approach. The authors successfully "turn around" model-based recovery theorems to provide guarantees without model assumptions, a perspective that is novel in the community detection literature.

2. **Technical Soundness**: The theoretical results are rigorously developed with careful definitions and proofs. The use of Davis-Kahan perturbation bounds (Proposition 5) and the connection to the existing literature (e.g., [13]) is particularly strong. The authors demonstrate how their approach can improve existing results (e.g., Theorem 11 improves the bound in [20] by a factor of 128).

3. **Computability**: A major strength is that all quantities in the theorems (ε, δ, δ0) are computable from the data and clustering, making the guarantees practically applicable. This distinguishes the paper from many theoretical results that depend on unknown parameters.

4. **Experimental Validation**: The experiments on synthetic and real datasets (PFM, perturbed PFM, LFR, and Political Blogs) provide strong empirical support for the theoretical claims. The results show that PFM-based guarantees are more permissive than SBM-based ones, as predicted by the theory, and demonstrate how smoothing (e.g., taking powers of adjacency matrices) can induce stability.

5. **Clear Connections to Existing Work**: The paper provides excellent context by showing how their model-free results relate to existing model-based recovery theorems (e.g., [18, 20]) and demonstrating how their framework can be used to improve these results.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Scope of the Framework**: The framework is restricted to model-based clustering algorithms that output a clustering and a model (like SBM, DC-SBM, PFM). It's unclear how this framework would apply to other popular clustering approaches (e.g., modularity optimization, hierarchical clustering, or density-based methods), limiting its general applicability.

2. **Practical Constraints of δ ≤ δ0**: The key condition δ ≤ δ0 (where δ0 = mink Ckk/maxk Ckk) can be very restrictive for real-world data with imbalanced clusters. The paper doesn't adequately address how to handle cases where this condition is violated, though the experiments do show that for well-clustered data, δ0 is sufficiently large.

3. **Limited Discussion of Computational Complexity**: While the paper mentions that quantities are computable, it doesn't discuss the computational complexity of applying the framework to large-scale graphs. The PFM estimation algorithm involves spectral decompositions, which could be expensive for very large graphs.

4. **Comparison with Alternative Stability Results**: The paper cites related work on clustering stability ([16], [14], [5]) but doesn't provide a detailed comparison of the relative strengths of their bounds with these alternatives. A more thorough comparison would help contextualize the contribution.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of graph clustering by introducing a novel framework for model-free guarantees. The framework is technically sound, well-motivated, and has clear connections to existing work. The results are interesting and could have implications for both theoretical understanding and practical application of clustering algorithms.

The main limitations are the restricted scope of the framework and the practical constraints imposed by the δ ≤ δ0 condition. However, these limitations are reasonable given the scope of the work and are acknowledged by the authors. The paper represents a meaningful step forward in understanding the stability of clusterings without model assumptions.

## Pro/Con for Acceptance

*Pros:*
- Novel framework for model-free clustering guarantees
- Strong theoretical results with rigorous proofs
- All quantities computable from data
- Clear connection to existing model-based results
- Thorough experimental validation
- Significant conceptual contribution to the field

*Cons:*
- Limited to model-based clustering algorithms
- Practical constraints from δ ≤ δ0 condition
- Limited discussion of computational complexity
- Not clear how to extend to non-spectral clustering methods

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The theoretical contribution is significant and well-executed, and the paper makes a clear step toward understanding the stability of clusterings without assuming a specific data-generating process. The limitations noted are reasonable given the scope of the work, and the paper is well-written and clearly presented.

The paper addresses a fundamental question in clustering: what guarantees can we provide for a clustering without assuming a specific generative model? The framework introduced here provides a new perspective on this question and opens up several interesting directions for future work, such as extending the framework to other clustering algorithms or exploring tighter bounds for the SBM case.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. In the experimental section, include more detailed analysis of cases where δ ≤ δ0 is not satisfied, and how the guarantees fail in those cases.

2. Briefly discuss how the framework might be extended to handle non-spectral clustering algorithms, even if only in a speculative manner.

3. Provide a more detailed comparison with existing stability results (e.g., [16], [14]) to better contextualize the relative strengths of the derived bounds.

4. Address the computational complexity of applying the framework to large graphs, particularly for the PFM estimation algorithm.

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of graph clustering and would be a strong addition to NIPS.
