- Overall assessment: Novel, theoretically strong framework for model-free graph clustering; recommend acceptance despite limited experiments.  
- Main idea: Proposes a stability-based "blueprint" where good fit implies other good fits are close, yielding model-free guarantees.  
- Contributions: Instantiates framework for PFM and SBM and links model-based recovery to model-free results.  
- Related work: Appropriately situates paper among block-model and stability literature, citing key prior works.  
- Strength — Novelty: Establishing stability as a proxy for model-free guarantees is original and significant.  
- Strength — Rigor: Mathematical development and proofs are sound and use appropriate tools (e.g., Davis-Kahan).  
- Strength — Connection: Clever demonstration of how model-based theorems yield model-free guarantees.  
- Strength — Framework: Presents a clear, unifying "Generic Theorem" for clustering validation.  
- Strength — Computability: Focuses on quantities that can be computed from data, enhancing practical relevance.  
- Strength — Coverage: Addresses both PFM and SBM, showing generality.  
- Weakness — Experiments: Empirical validation is limited to a few datasets and mostly checks theorem conditions rather than algorithm performance.  
- Weakness — Assumptions: Results depend on assumptions (e.g., non-zero eigengap) and lack sensitivity analysis to violations.  
- Weakness — Complexity: Dense, technical proofs may hinder accessibility; could use more intuition or streamlining.  
- Weakness — Integration: Section on alternative results (4.2) feels fragmented and could be better integrated.  
- Originality: Framework and connection between model-based and model-free recovery are highly original.  
- Significance: Potentially impactful foundation for robustness and stability research in graph clustering.  
- Clarity: Generally well-written but heavy notation sections need more intuitive explanation.  
- Quality: High technical quality; rigorous analysis and clear presentation.  
- Arguments for acceptance: Novel, rigorous framework that bridges model-based and model-free approaches with potential broad impact.  
- Arguments against acceptance: Limited empirical validation and potentially inaccessible mathematical complexity.  
- Final recommendation: Accept, with suggestion to strengthen experimental validation in future work.
