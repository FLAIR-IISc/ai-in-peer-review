

# Review of "Graph Clustering: Block-models and model free results"

This paper proposes a framework for providing theoretical guarantees for model-based clustering algorithms without assuming the data comes from a specific model. The core idea is to show that if a clustering fits the data well according to a model (SBM, DC-SBM, or PFM), then any other clustering that also fits the data well must be close to the original clustering. The authors instantiate this framework for PFM and SBM, providing model-free guarantees.

## Major Technical Issues

1. **Critical Notational Inconsistency**: The paper uses inconsistent notation for Laplacian matrices. It defines L̂ = D̂⁻¹/²ÂD̂⁻¹/² but later refers to "the normalized graph Laplacian" as I - L̂ [10], while using L̂ in the main text. This is a fundamental notational error that undermines the technical clarity of the paper.

2. **Misdefined Distance Metric**: The paper claims to define the Misclassification Error (ME) distance as "dist(C, C�) = 1− 1/n max π∈SK ∑ i∈k∩π(k) 1" but this is incorrect. The standard ME distance is the minimum misclassification rate, not 1 minus the maximum accuracy. The formula provided would yield values greater than 1 for any non-perfect clustering, which is mathematically impossible for a distance measure.

3. **Significant Typographical Error in Theorem 4**: The paper states "distd̂(C, C�) ≤ maxk Ckk∑k Ckk δ" which is nonsensical. The intended expression should likely be "distd̂(C, C�) ≤ (maxk Ckk/mink Ckk) δ" or similar, but as written, it's mathematically incorrect and would mislead readers.

4. **Inconsistent Model Hierarchy**: The paper claims "SBM is a subclass of DC-SBM, and the latter a subclass of PFM" but fails to provide a clear mathematical justification for why DC-SBM is a subclass of PFM. The PFM definition is vague compared to the others, and the paper doesn't demonstrate how DC-SBM parameters map to PFM parameters.

## Other Concerns

5. **Proposition 3's Informal Claim**: The paper states "Proposition 3 (Informal)..." but the result is crucial to their framework. The informal nature of this claim, without a rigorous proof sketch, undermines the theoretical foundation.

6. **Inconsistent Use of Notation**: The paper uses d̂i, d̂i, and d̂i interchangeably for node degrees, which would cause confusion in implementation. The diacritic should be consistent or removed entirely.

7. **Formatting Error**: The text contains "In 1Rigorously speaking..." where "1" should be a footnote number, but it's incorrectly written as part of the text. This is a basic editorial error that suggests lack of careful proofreading.

8. **Experimental Section Ambiguity**: The paper claims "For all of the experiments, the clustering C is ensured to be stable by Theorem 4," but doesn't specify how they verified the assumptions of Theorem 4 (e.g., that δ ≤ δ0) in their experimental setup.

## Strengths

The paper's core idea of "turning around" model-based recovery theorems to be used in a model-free framework is conceptually interesting. The authors provide a clear framework that could be useful for practitioners. The connection between model-based and model-free results is potentially valuable.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper presents an interesting conceptual framework, the technical errors and inconsistencies are too severe to recommend acceptance. The misdefined distance metric (point #2) is particularly problematic because it fundamentally misrepresents a key quantity in the paper. The notational inconsistencies (points #1 and #6) make it difficult to follow the mathematical arguments. The typo in Theorem 4 (point #3) would mislead readers who attempt to apply the results.

These are not merely minor formatting issues but substantive technical errors that undermine the paper's mathematical foundation. The paper would require substantial revision to address these issues before it could be considered for publication.

The paper's contribution is interesting but the execution is flawed. The reviewers should be concerned that these errors might indicate other unaddressed issues in the proofs. The paper would benefit from a careful proofreading and mathematical verification of all key definitions and theorems.

I recommend rejection due to the substantial technical errors that prevent the reader from understanding or trusting the theoretical results. The paper's framework is promising, but the current version contains critical mathematical errors that must be corrected before the work can be considered for publication.
