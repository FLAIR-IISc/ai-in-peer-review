This paper proposes a novel framework for providing theoretical guarantees for graph clustering without making generative model assumptions. The central idea is to establish the stability of a clustering: if a given clustering `C` allows for a good fit of the data `G` by a model `M(G, C)` from a specific class (e.g., SBM, PFM), then any other clustering `C'` that also provides a good fit must be close to `C`. This shifts the focus from recovering a "true" clustering under a generative model to validating a given clustering based on its ability to explain the data through a model-based lens. The authors instantiate this general framework for the Preference Frame Model (PFM) and the Stochastic Block Model (SBM). For each, they define a procedure to fit the model given a clustering, a goodness-of-fit metric based on the norm of the difference between the data and the fitted model, and derive a theorem that bounds the distance between any two "good" clusterings. The proofs rely on matrix perturbation theory, particularly the Davis-Kahan theorem, to relate the closeness of fitted models to the closeness of the corresponding clusterings. This work connects to the extensive literature on model-based community detection (e.g., Rohe et al. 2011, Wan & Meila 2015), but critically departs by removing the assumption that the data is sampled from the model. It also relates to the broader literature on clustering stability (e.g., Awasthi et al., 2012), but frames stability in terms of model-fit rather than cost-function minimization.

**Quality**

The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical claims are well-supported by detailed proofs that leverage standard but powerful tools from matrix perturbation theory in a novel context. The framework is carefully constructed, with clear definitions for model fitting, goodness-of-fit, and clustering distance. The authors demonstrate technical depth by not only deriving their main theorems (4 and 9) but also by showing how their framework can reinterpret and even improve upon existing model-based recovery results (Theorems 11 and 12). The honesty about the limitations is commendable; for instance, the experimental section openly reports that the derived bounds for the SBM were not informative for the tested datasets, which points to avenues for future work. The work is a complete contribution, presenting a general framework, specific instantiations, theoretical proofs, and experimental validation.

**Clarity**

The paper is well-written and logically structured. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and outlines the proposed framework. The "blueprint" approach in Section 3, where a generic theorem is stated and then instantiated for PFM and SBM, is an effective way to communicate the main ideas. The notation, while necessarily dense, is consistently defined. The experimental section is also clearly described.

However, certain parts could be improved for clarity:
*   The PFM Estimation Algorithm in Section 3.1 is presented as a sequence of matrix operations. While formally correct, more intuition behind the steps would be beneficial. For example, an explanation of why the projection `F = YZ^T Y_hat` is the key step to "align" the subspace of the clustering with the principal subspace of the data would help the reader.
*   The connection between the main results and existing theorems in Section 4.2 is insightful but presented very concisely. A slightly more detailed walkthrough of how Theorem 3 of [20] is used to prove Theorem 11 would strengthen this section.

**Originality**

The paper's primary contribution is highly original. While model-based clustering and stability analysis are established fields, the synthesis of these two ideas into a "model-free" validation framework for model-based methods is novel. The idea of "turning around" a generative model's recovery theorem into a data-dependent stability guarantee without the generative assumption is a significant conceptual advance. To my knowledge, this specific approach has not been proposed before in the context of SBMs or PFMs. The paper adequately references related work and clearly distinguishes its contributions.

**Significance**

The significance of this work is substantial. It addresses a fundamental gap between the theory and practice of community detection: theoretical guarantees almost always rely on strong, often unrealistic, generative assumptions, while practitioners need tools to assess the quality of clusterings on real-world data. This paper provides such a tool. A key strength is that all quantities in the derived bounds (e.g., the goodness-of-fit `ε`, the spectral gap, cluster sizes) are computable from the observed graph and a proposed clustering. This makes the theoretical guarantees directly applicable and verifiable in practice, a feature missing from many theoretical analyses. Researchers can use this framework to validate clustering results, and practitioners can gain confidence in the discovered community structure. The framework is also extensible and could likely be applied to other model classes beyond PFM and SBM, paving the way for future research.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Novel Framework:** The paper introduces a highly original and significant model-free framework for validating results from model-based clustering algorithms, addressing a major limitation of prior work.
2.  **Practical Relevance:** The derived stability guarantees are based on quantities that are fully computable from the data, making the theory directly applicable for practitioners to validate clustering results.
3.  **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical results are rigorously proven using established matrix analysis techniques applied in a novel way.
4.  **Insightful Connections:** The paper skillfully connects its model-free framework back to existing model-based recovery theorems, showing how they can be re-purposed and even improved.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  **Practical Tightness of Bounds:** The experiments show that the bound for the SBM (Theorem 9) is too loose to be informative on the tested datasets. While the PFM bound is useful, the weakness of the SBM result raises questions about the practical tightness of the framework for less flexible models. More discussion on why this might be the case (e.g., choice of norm, proof technique) would strengthen the paper.
2.  **Dependence on Spectral Gap:** The bounds are inversely proportional to the spectral gap of the graph's Laplacian/adjacency matrix. In many real networks, this gap can be small, which would render the bounds vacuous. A more explicit discussion of this potential limitation is warranted.
3.  **Clarity in Technical Sections:** Some of the technical descriptions, particularly the PFM estimation algorithm, are dense and could benefit from more intuition to make the paper more accessible.

In summary, the paper's strengths, particularly the novelty and significance of its model-free framework, substantially outweigh its weaknesses. The work represents a valuable and conceptually important advance in the theory of graph clustering.
