

# Peer Review: "Graph Clustering: Block-models and model free results"

## Summary and Context

This paper introduces a novel framework for providing theoretical guarantees for graph clustering results without assuming the data comes from a specific generative model (like SBM, DC-SBM, or PFM). The core idea is that if a model-based clustering algorithm outputs a clustering C and parameters M(G,C) that fit the observed graph G well, then any other clustering C' that fits G well must be close to C. The authors formalize this as a "Generic Theorem" and instantiate it for PFM and SBM models, providing model-free guarantees that connect to existing model-based recovery theorems. This approach "turns around" traditional model-based recovery results to be used in a model-free validation context.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Innovation**: The paper makes a significant conceptual contribution by establishing a framework for model-free validation of clustering results. This is a meaningful extension of the literature on community detection that moves beyond model assumptions.

2. **Technical Soundness**: The authors provide rigorous proofs for their main theorems, using standard matrix perturbation theory (Davis-Kahan theorem) in a clever way. The proof techniques are simpler and more direct than those in previous works.

3. **Computability**: A key strength is that all quantities in the theorems (ε, δ, δ0) are computable from the data and clustering, making the results practically applicable. This addresses a major limitation in much of the model-based clustering literature.

4. **Connection to Existing Work**: The paper successfully demonstrates how their model-free results connect to and improve upon existing model-based theorems (e.g., improving the error bound in [20] by a factor of 128 for PFM).

5. **Experimental Validation**: The experimental section on simulated and real data (Political Blogs) demonstrates that the theoretical guarantees hold in practice, with the model-free guarantees being satisfied when the clustering is stable.

## Weaknesses

1. **Practical Restrictiveness**: The bounds are highly dependent on the eigengap condition (|λ̂K| - |λ̂K+1| > 0), which may not hold for many real-world graphs. For SBM, the authors acknowledge that "all δ values from Theorem 9 are above 1, and not shown," making the results practically uninformative for this model.

2. **Limited Scope**: The paper focuses only on PFM and SBM, while the framework could potentially be extended to other models. The authors briefly mention this in Section 4.3 but don't explore it in depth.

3. **Experimental Details**: The experimental section lacks sufficient detail about how the "gradually increasing noise" was implemented to perturb the clustering C0, making it difficult to fully reproduce the results.

4. **Assumption Limitations**: The framework relies on the non-zero K-th eigengap assumption (Assumption 1), which is not always satisfied in practice. The paper doesn't explore how to handle cases where this assumption fails.

5. **Comparative Analysis**: While the paper compares the bounds for PFM and SBM, it doesn't provide a comprehensive comparison with existing stability results for spectral clustering (e.g., [14, 16]) in terms of practical utility.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by introducing a framework for model-free validation of graph clustering results. The theoretical results are sound and the connection to existing model-based theorems is insightful. However, the practical utility of the framework is limited by the restrictive assumptions required for the bounds to be informative, particularly for SBM.

The paper's main contribution is conceptual rather than technical—it provides a new perspective on how to validate clustering results without model assumptions. This is important because practitioners often want to know if their clustering results are meaningful without assuming the data follows a specific model. The authors successfully demonstrate that their framework can be used to validate clustering results in practice.

## Recommendation

**Accept**

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution that addresses an important gap in the literature. While the practical applicability of the SBM bounds is limited, the PFM results are promising, and the framework itself is novel and well-developed. The paper is clearly written, technically sound, and provides a valuable new perspective on graph clustering validation. The authors have successfully demonstrated the connection between model-based and model-free results, which is a key insight.

The main limitation is that the bounds are restrictive for some models, but this is a common challenge in theoretical work. The authors acknowledge this limitation and suggest directions for future work (e.g., tighter bounds for SBM). The experimental results support the theoretical claims, and the framework is computable from the data, which is a major practical advantage over many existing approaches.

This paper advances the state of the art in graph clustering by providing a new way to validate clustering results without model assumptions, and it is well-suited for publication at NIPS.
