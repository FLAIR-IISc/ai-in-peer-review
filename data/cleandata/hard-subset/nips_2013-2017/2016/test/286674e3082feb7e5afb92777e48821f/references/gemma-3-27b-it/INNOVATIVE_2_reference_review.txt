This paper presents a compelling framework for analyzing graph clustering without strong model assumptions. The core idea – establishing stability as a proxy for meaningful structure – is genuinely novel and represents a significant departure from the typical model-based approach. I highly appreciate the authors' ambition to provide theoretical guarantees independent of a specific generative process, and the connection they draw between model-free and model-based results is insightful. The theorems themselves, while technically dense, are clearly stated and the proofs (as presented, with details relegated to the supplement) appear sound. The experimental validation, though relatively basic, supports the theoretical claims and demonstrates the practical applicability of the framework.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The model-free guarantee framework is a genuinely new direction in graph clustering research. It circumvents the limitations of relying on often-unrealistic model assumptions.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The theorems are well-defined and appear mathematically sound. The use of perturbation theory (Davis-Kahan) and spectral analysis is appropriate.
*   **Conceptual Clarity:** Despite the mathematical complexity, the central idea of stability-based validation is clearly articulated.
*   **Connection to Existing Work:** The paper nicely bridges the gap between model-based and model-free approaches, demonstrating how insights from one can inform the other.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The computable bounds (ε, δ, δ0) offer a pathway for validating clustering results in real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Assumptions:** While aiming for model-free guarantees, the theorems still rely on assumptions (e.g., eigengap condition, goodness of fit measures). These assumptions, while reasonable, could be a limiting factor in practice.
*   **Conservatism:** The bounds derived appear to be fairly conservative, as evidenced by the experimental results (particularly for the SBM). The conditions for stability (δ ≤ δ0) may not always be met even when the clustering is visually sensible.
*   **Experimental Scope:** The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited. While it provides initial validation, a more extensive and diverse set of experiments would strengthen the claims. The comparison to other validation techniques (e.g., modularity maximization) is missing.
*   **PFM Focus:** The PFM results are more compelling than the SBM results. The SBM section feels less developed and the derived bounds are significantly harder to satisfy.
*   **Limited Discussion of Negative Guarantees:** The authors briefly mention the potential for negative guarantees (i.e., demonstrating non-uniqueness), but don't explore this direction further. This could be a valuable area for future research.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a fundamentally new perspective on graph clustering validation.
*   The theoretical framework is well-developed and mathematically rigorous.
*   The work has the potential to significantly impact the field by providing a more robust and flexible approach to clustering analysis.
*   The originality and boldness of the approach outweigh the current limitations.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The derived bounds are potentially too conservative to be practically useful in many scenarios.
*   The experimental validation is limited in scope.
*   The SBM results are less convincing than the PFM results.
*   The reliance on certain assumptions, even in a model-free setting, may restrict the applicability of the framework.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some weaknesses, I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The novelty and ambition of the work are exceptional. The authors have identified a significant problem and proposed a creative and theoretically sound solution. While the practical utility of the derived bounds may be limited in some cases, the framework itself is a valuable contribution to the field and opens up exciting avenues for future research. I believe the potential impact of this work far outweighs its current limitations. The paper pushes the boundaries of the field and represents a significant step towards more robust and reliable graph clustering methodologies. I would suggest minor revisions to strengthen the experimental evaluation and discuss the limitations of the derived bounds more explicitly.
