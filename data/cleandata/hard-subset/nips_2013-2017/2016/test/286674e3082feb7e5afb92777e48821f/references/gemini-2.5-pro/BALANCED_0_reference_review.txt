### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a novel framework for providing theoretical guarantees for graph clustering without making assumptions about the data-generating process. The central idea is to shift from "model-based recovery" to "model-free validation." Instead of assuming a graph `G` is drawn from a model (like a Stochastic Block Model), the authors propose the following: if a given clustering `C` allows for the construction of a model `M(G, C)` that fits the observed graph `G` well, then any other clustering `C'` that also fits `G` well must be a small perturbation of `C`. This provides a notion of stability or uniqueness for the clustering `C` based solely on the observed data.

The authors instantiate this general framework for two prominent block models: the Preference Frame Model (PFM) and the Stochastic Block Model (SBM). For each, they define a procedure for constructing a model from the data and a clustering, specify a "goodness-of-fit" measure (based on the spectral norm of the difference between the observed and model Laplacians/adjacency matrices), and derive an explicit, computable bound on the distance between any two clusterings that both fit the data well. The theoretical results rely on matrix perturbation theory, particularly the Davis-Kahan Sin-Theta theorem. The paper demonstrates the utility of these bounds through experiments on synthetic and real-world datasets, showing they can be used to validate the stability of a found clustering.

### Strengths

1.  **High Significance and Novelty:** The paper's main contribution窶廃roviding model-free guarantees for model-based clustering窶琶s highly significant and timely. Much of the recent theoretical progress in community detection has been under the strong, and often unrealistic, assumption that the data comes from a specific random graph model. This work directly addresses this limitation by providing a framework to assess the quality and stability of a clustering on any given graph. This shifts the focus from recovering a latent "truth" to verifying the robustness of a structure found in the data, which is a much more practical and widely applicable problem.

2.  **Technical Soundness and Clarity:** The paper is technically solid and well-executed. The general framework is first laid out clearly and then carefully instantiated for PFM and SBM. The use of matrix perturbation bounds is elegant and leads to concrete, computable theorems (Theorem 4 and 9) without hidden constants. The connection drawn between their model-free framework and existing model-based recovery results (Section 4.2) is insightful, showing that their techniques can also be used to improve existing bounds. The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making the sophisticated technical arguments accessible.

3.  **Computable and Practical Guarantees:** A major strength of the derived theorems is that all quantities involved窶杯he goodness-of-fit `ﾎｵ`, the eigengaps, and the resulting stability `ﾎｴ`窶蚤re computable from the observed graph and the clustering in question. This makes the framework immediately applicable for cluster validation. The experimental section, while brief, effectively demonstrates this by showing how the stability of a clustering can be tracked as it is perturbed away from an initial solution found by spectral clustering.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Restrictiveness of the SBM Bound:** The experiments reveal that the derived guarantee for the SBM (Theorem 9) is "much harder to satisfy" and not informative for the tested datasets, even for the LFR benchmark which is designed to be SBM-like. The authors are commendably transparent about this limitation. However, given the prominence of the SBM in the literature, the weakness of this particular result tempers the overall practical impact of the paper. It suggests that the current analysis technique, while effective for PFM, might not be the right one for the less flexible SBM.

2.  **Interpretation of the Goodness-of-Fit Parameter `ﾎｵ`:** The entire framework hinges on the notion of a "good fit," which is quantified by `||L_hat - L|| 竕､ ﾎｵ` or `||A_hat - A|| 竕､ ﾎｵ`. While these are natural definitions, the paper provides little intuition or practical guidance on how to interpret `ﾎｵ` or choose a meaningful threshold for it. Is a value of `ﾎｵ=0.1` good or bad? How does it scale with graph size or density? Without this context, it is difficult for a practitioner to judge whether their clustering truly "fits the data well" according to the premises of the theorems.

3.  **Limited Experimental Scope:** The experiments serve as a good proof of concept, but their scope is limited. It would have been more compelling to see a visualization of the non-informative SBM bounds to substantiate that claim directly. Furthermore, a comparison against alternative validation methods, such as the bootstrap-based approaches mentioned in the related work, would have helped to better position the practical pros and cons of this new framework (e.g., in terms of computational cost vs. the tightness of the guarantee).

### Constructive Suggestions for Improvement

*   To address the issue of interpreting `ﾎｵ`, the authors could add a brief discussion on normalizing this value, for instance by the norm of `L_hat` or `A_hat`, or relating it to graph properties like average degree. This would help make the criterion more transferable across different datasets.
*   Regarding the weak SBM bound, expanding the discussion in Section 7 would be beneficial. The authors could elaborate on why they believe the current spectral approach is insufficient for SBM and offer more concrete hypotheses on how non-spectral techniques (e.g., those from [1]) might lead to tighter model-free bounds. This would frame the negative result as a clear and interesting direction for future work.
*   In Theorem 9, the bound depends on `ﾎｻ_hat_AK`, the K-th singular value of `A_hat`. A brief justification for using singular values here, as opposed to the eigenvalues used elsewhere, would improve clarity.
*   A minor point on presentation: in Figure 1, the x-axis is "Unweighted error", which seems to correspond to `dist(C, C0)`. Explicitly labeling it as such would make the plot easier to interpret.

### Decision

This is a strong paper that introduces a novel and important research direction. The problem of model-free validation for clustering is of great interest to both theorists and practitioners. The proposed framework is elegant, the technical results for PFM are solid, and the paper is written with high clarity. While the derived bound for SBM is shown to be weak, the authors are upfront about this, and the overall contribution remains significant. The work clearly meets the high standards of NeurIPS for originality, quality, and potential impact.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
