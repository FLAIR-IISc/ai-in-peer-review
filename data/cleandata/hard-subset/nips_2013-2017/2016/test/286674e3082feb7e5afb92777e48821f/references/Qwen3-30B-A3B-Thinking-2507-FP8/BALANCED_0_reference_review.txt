

# Review of "Graph Clustering: Block-models and model free results"

This paper proposes a novel framework for providing theoretical guarantees for graph clustering without assuming the data comes from a specific generative model. The authors introduce a "Generic Theorem" stating that if a model fitted to a clustering (M(G, C)) fits the data well, then any other clustering that also fits well must be close to the original clustering. They instantiate this framework for two specific models: the Stochastic Block Model (SBM) and the Preference Frame Model (PFM), proving stability theorems that bound the perturbation of a clustering by the perturbation of a model.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The authors provide a thorough background on graph clustering, block models, and spectral clustering, which is essential for understanding their contribution. The main theorems are technically sound, building on established perturbation theory (Davis-Kahan theorem) and providing concrete bounds on clustering stability. The experimental section demonstrates the practical applicability of their framework on simulated and real-world datasets, showing that their model-free guarantees can be computed and applied in practice.

**Strengths:**

1. **Originality:** The paper addresses a significant gap in the literature by providing a framework for model-free validation of clustering results. While stability of clustering has been studied before (e.g., [14, 16]), the authors' approach of "turning around" model-based recovery theorems to create model-free guarantees is novel and insightful.

2. **Technical Contribution:** The paper provides concrete, computable bounds for clustering stability. Unlike many existing results that depend on unknown constants or model parameters, the authors' bounds (Theorems 4 and 9) are fully computable from the data and clustering, making them practically applicable.

3. **Theoretical Connection:** The paper successfully bridges model-based and model-free clustering by showing how existing recovery theorems (e.g., from [20]) can be repurposed for model-free guarantees. The authors demonstrate this by improving the error bound in [20] by a factor of 128.

4. **Experimental Validation:** The experiments on simulated and real-world datasets (including Political Blogs) effectively illustrate the practical utility of their framework, showing how their bounds can be used to validate clustering results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Practical Threshold Selection:** The paper doesn't adequately address how to choose the goodness-of-fit threshold ε in practice. This is a critical parameter that determines whether the stability guarantee holds, but the paper lacks guidance on selecting an appropriate ε value for real-world applications.

2. **Comparison with Existing Methods:** While the paper mentions related work (e.g., bootstrap validation [11]), it doesn't clearly articulate how their approach compares to or improves upon existing clustering validation techniques. A direct comparison with established methods would strengthen the paper's significance.

3. **Limited Experimental Scope:** The experimental section is somewhat limited, focusing only on spectral clustering and a few datasets. More comprehensive experiments comparing their method against other validation techniques would better demonstrate its practical value.

4. **SBM vs. PFM Discussion:** The paper notes that SBM bounds are more restrictive than PFM bounds (Theorem 9 vs. Theorem 4), but doesn't deeply explore why this is the case or provide guidance on when to prefer one model over the other in practice.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of graph clustering by providing a framework for model-free validation. The technical results are sound, well-justified, and potentially valuable for practitioners who want to validate clustering results without assuming specific generative models. The paper advances the state of the art by connecting model-based and model-free approaches in a novel way.

The main limitations are practical: the paper doesn't sufficiently address how to choose the critical ε parameter, and it doesn't fully demonstrate the practical advantages of their approach compared to existing methods. However, these are important directions for future work rather than fundamental flaws in the current contribution.

**Recommendation:**

This paper is of high quality and makes a meaningful contribution to the field. While the practical implications could be better articulated, the theoretical results are sound and the framework is well-developed. I recommend acceptance with the following suggestions for improvement:

1. Include a more detailed discussion of how to choose the goodness-of-fit threshold ε in practice, potentially with a small empirical study.

2. Add a comparison section that directly evaluates their framework against existing clustering validation techniques (e.g., bootstrap, stability-based methods).

3. Expand the discussion on when to prefer PFM over SBM, or vice versa, based on the data characteristics.

4. Consider adding a brief discussion of computational complexity for computing the bounds in practice, as this would be important for real-world applications.

The paper's contributions are significant enough to warrant acceptance at NIPS, and the suggested improvements would further strengthen its impact.
