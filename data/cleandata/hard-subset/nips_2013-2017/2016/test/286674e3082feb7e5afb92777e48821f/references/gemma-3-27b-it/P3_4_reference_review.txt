## NIPS 2016 Review: Graph Clustering: Block-models and model free results

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel framework for providing theoretical guarantees for graph clustering algorithms *without* assuming the data originates from a specific block model (like SBM, DC-SBM, or PFM). The core idea revolves around demonstrating that if a clustering *C* fits the data well (according to some model *M*), then any other clustering *C'* that also fits the data well must be "close" to *C*.  This establishes *C* as a meaningful representation of the underlying graph structure. The authors instantiate this framework for the PFM and SBM, connecting model-free and model-based recovery guarantees, and providing concrete bounds on clustering stability.

**Related Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the rapidly growing literature on block model-based clustering.  It acknowledges key advancements in understanding recovery conditions (e.g., [1, 17, 20]) and spectral clustering [15]. The authors accurately identify a gap: the strong model assumption limiting the applicability of existing results.  The related work section also touches on stability results from other areas (e.g., [5, 16]) but rightly points out differences – primarily, their reliance on specific clustering criteria or difficulty in computing necessary parameters. The connection to the broader topic of community detection and clusterability [3, 7] is also appropriate.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty of the Framework:** The “model-free” guarantee approach is genuinely innovative. It cleverly leverages the strengths of model-based analysis while removing the restrictive assumption of model fidelity. This is a significant conceptual contribution.
* **Generality:** The “Generic Theorem” provides a powerful, abstract framework applicable beyond the specific models explored in the paper.
* **Concrete Results:**  The instantiation of the framework for PFM and SBM yields specific, computable guarantees for clustering stability, a valuable outcome for practitioners.
* **Clear Presentation:**  The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is standard and the definitions are clear.
* **Thorough Experimental Validation:** The experimental section provides a strong empirical justification for the theoretical results. Demonstrating the bounds hold in practice across different data sets, and on both the original graph and its model representation, is convincing.
* **Connection to Existing Work:** The paper effectively demonstrates how existing model-based recovery theorems can be repurposed to derive model-free guarantees. 
* **Detailed Analysis:** The proofs appear rigorous and well-structured (though a full verification is beyond the scope of a review).

**Weaknesses:**

* **Conservatism of Bounds:** While the guarantees are valuable, the derived bounds (particularly for the SBM, as acknowledged by the authors) appear conservative.  The experimental results illustrate that the conditions for stability are often not met in practice for the SBM.  This suggests the potential for tightening the theoretical analysis.
* **Complexity:** While generally clear, the mathematical details (especially the PFM section) could benefit from further streamlining for readability. The multiple definitions and propositions contribute to a somewhat dense presentation.
* **Limited Discussion of Computational Cost:** The paper doesn't explicitly address the computational cost of verifying the goodness-of-fit conditions (e.g., calculating the eigengap or performing the SVD).  This is a practical consideration for applying the framework.
* **Assumption Dependence:**  The results still rely on certain assumptions (e.g., the non-zero eigengap in the PFM case). While reasonable, the sensitivity of the results to these assumptions is not thoroughly explored.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* **Significant Conceptual Advance:** The model-free clustering framework is a substantial contribution to the field.
* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides rigorous mathematical guarantees for clustering stability.
* **Practical Relevance:**  The framework offers a means of validating clustering results without relying on potentially incorrect model assumptions.
* **Thorough Evaluation:** The experimental section provides convincing evidence of the framework's effectiveness.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* **Conservative Bounds:** The practical utility of the results may be limited by the conservativeness of the bounds, especially for the SBM. This is a key area for improvement.
* **Complexity of Presentation:** The density of the mathematical presentation could hinder accessibility for some readers.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the theory of graph clustering.  The authors present a novel and conceptually elegant framework for providing model-free guarantees.  While the bounds could be tighter and the presentation more streamlined, the theoretical soundness, practical relevance, and thorough evaluation justify acceptance.

**Recommendation:**  **Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the following minor points:

1. **Discussion of Bound Tightness:** Briefly discuss the limitations of the current bounds and potential directions for improvement, particularly for the SBM.
2. **Computational Cost:** Include a brief discussion of the computational complexity of verifying the goodness-of-fit conditions.
3. **Clarify Assumptions:**  Provide a more detailed discussion of the sensitivity of the results to the underlying assumptions.
