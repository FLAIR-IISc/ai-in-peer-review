This paper proposes a framework for providing theoretical guarantees for model-based clustering algorithms *without* making assumptions about the data generating process. The core idea is to establish stability: if a clustering C fits the data well, any other clustering C' that also fits well must be a small perturbation of C. This is demonstrated for the SBM and PFM models, and connections to existing model-based results are explored.

While the motivation for a model-free approach is understandable – real-world data rarely perfectly conforms to theoretical models – the presented work feels highly theoretical and lacks a convincing demonstration of practical impact. The framework itself, while conceptually neat, relies heavily on defining “fitting well” and “small perturbation,” which are ultimately tied to specific model choices (SBM, PFM) even within the “model-free” context. This undermines the claim of true model independence.  The paper essentially *transports* model-based guarantees into a slightly different framework, rather than achieving true model-agnostic clustering validation. 

The technical execution is rigorous, and the proofs appear sound (though verifying them all is beyond the scope of a review). However, the numerous assumptions (e.g., Assumption 1 regarding the Laplacian eigenvalues, and the need for a non-zero eigengap) feel restrictive and potentially limit the applicability of the theorems. The instantiation for the SBM (Theorem 9) yields a particularly weak result, with conditions rarely met in practice.  The extensive use of perturbation theory, while mathematically sound, doesn't automatically translate to practical insights. The paper leans heavily on existing recovery theorems (e.g., [20, 18]) to “prove” model-free guarantees, which feels circular – the guarantees are ultimately dependent on the underlying model assumptions used in those theorems. 

Clarity is reasonable, though the notation is dense and requires careful attention. The organization is logical, progressing from background to the main theorem and then specific instantiations. However, the connection between the general framework and the specific results for SBM/PFM could be made more explicit. The experimental section, while attempting to validate the framework, feels underdeveloped. The choice of datasets and the analysis presented are insufficient to demonstrate the practical utility of the theorems. The comparison to existing spectral clustering stability results (e.g., [16, 14]) is helpful, but further analysis of the tightness of the bounds is needed. 

The originality lies in the attempt to create a model-free validation framework, and in linking it back to model-based recovery guarantees. However, the core idea – stability of clustering – is not novel. The significance is questionable. While the theoretical results are interesting, it's unclear how they will be used in practice. The paper doesn't offer a new clustering algorithm or a novel way to assess clustering quality *without* relying on some underlying model assumptions.  The connection to real-world applications remains tenuous.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* Rigorous mathematical treatment of a challenging problem.
* Interesting connection between model-based and model-free approaches.
* Potential for future work building on the established framework.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* Limited practical impact – the theorems are difficult to satisfy and offer little guidance in real-world scenarios.
* Heavily reliant on existing model-based results, undermining the claim of model independence.
* Restrictive assumptions that limit applicability.
* Underdeveloped experimental evaluation.
* The framework feels more like a reinterpretation of existing results than a truly novel contribution.



**Overall Assessment:** Reject.

The paper presents a technically sound, but ultimately incremental, contribution. While the idea of a model-free validation framework is appealing, the current implementation is too heavily reliant on model-specific assumptions and yields results that are difficult to apply in practice. The theoretical rigor is commendable, but the lack of demonstrated practical impact and the reliance on existing model-based theorems prevent me from recommending acceptance at this time.  I encourage the authors to focus on developing more practical applications of their framework or to explore genuinely model-agnostic approaches to clustering validation.
