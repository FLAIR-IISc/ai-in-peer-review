This paper presents a framework for clustering graphs with theoretical guarantees without making assumptions about the data generating process. The core idea is to demonstrate that if a clustering *C* fits the data well (according to some metric), then any other clustering *C'* that also fits the data well must be a small perturbation of *C*. This is instantiated for the Stochastic Block Model (SBM) and Preference Frame Model (PFM) with novel, model-free guarantees. The paper also highlights the connection between model-free and model-based recovery results.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a significant limitation of existing block model based clustering techniques – the reliance on model assumptions. The proposed framework is conceptually sound and represents a valuable contribution towards more robust clustering methods. The technical approach, based on perturbation bounds and spectral analysis, is appropriate for the problem and clearly explained. The connection established between model-free and model-based results is insightful. The experimental validation, although limited, helps to illustrate the practical implications of the theoretical results. 

However, the paper also has several weaknesses.  The notation is dense and occasionally obscures the main ideas. While the core concept of the framework is clear, the specific details of the instantiation for PFM and SBM are quite involved and require careful reading to follow. The proof techniques, although described as simpler than previous work, still rely on complex mathematical machinery (e.g., Sinus Theorem) and could benefit from more intuitive explanations. The experimental evaluation is relatively weak.  The choice of metrics and the range of parameters explored are not comprehensively justified.  The comparison between SBM and PFM results is somewhat superficial; the reasons for the difficulty in achieving stable SBM clustering are not fully explained.  Furthermore, the link to existing related work is sometimes lacking nuance; for example, the comparison to [16] focuses on the bounds but overlooks differences in the objectives and assumptions. The presentation of Theorems 4 and 9, while mathematically sound, could be improved by emphasizing the practical implications of the conditions δ ≤ δ0.  The discussion section acknowledges some limitations, but a more detailed exploration of potential extensions and future research directions would be beneficial.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The model-free clustering framework and the associated theoretical guarantees are a significant contribution to the field.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical analysis appears rigorous and the proofs, while complex, seem well-constructed (based on the provided information and citations to established results).
*   **Significance:** The work addresses a fundamental challenge in network clustering – the need for methods that are robust to model misspecification.
*   **Clarity:** Despite the technical density, the central ideas are well-articulated, and the organization is logical.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Clarity:** The presentation is sometimes overly technical, making it difficult for readers not deeply familiar with the specific mathematical tools employed to fully grasp the details.
*   **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental section is limited in scope and depth, hindering a thorough assessment of the practical relevance of the theoretical results.
*   **Completeness:**  A more in-depth discussion of the limitations of the framework and potential avenues for future research would strengthen the paper.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of graph clustering. The proposed framework is novel, technically sound, and addresses a significant practical challenge. While the presentation could be improved in terms of clarity and the experimental evaluation is somewhat limited, the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to improve clarity and expand the discussion of the experimental results. Specifically, the authors should consider providing more intuitive explanations of the mathematical derivations and expanding the experimental section to include a wider range of datasets and parameter settings. Addressing the points raised in the related work section (e.g. providing a more thorough comparison with [16]) would also strengthen the paper.
