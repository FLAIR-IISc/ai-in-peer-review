This paper proposes a framework for establishing clustering guarantees without making strong model assumptions, building upon the recent advances in model-based clustering (SBM, DC-SBM, PFM). The core idea is to demonstrate that if a clustering *fits* the data well according to some measure, any other clustering that also fits the data well must be a small perturbation of the original. This is a conceptually appealing approach, and the authors instantiate it for PFM and SBM, connecting model-free and model-based recovery results.

However, despite the interesting idea, I find the paper ultimately unconvincing in its current form. My primary concern stems from the lack of concrete, demonstrable advantage over existing model-based approaches, and the relatively weak practical implications of the derived guarantees. While theoretically appealing, the conditions under which these guarantees hold seem quite restrictive, and the experimental validation, while present, doesn't strongly support broader applicability. 

The technical soundness is generally good, and the authors demonstrate a good grasp of the relevant literature (spectral graph theory, block models). However, the "goodness of fit" measures (specifically, closeness to a fitted model) are somewhat ad-hoc and lack clear justification beyond being mathematically tractable. The choice of the weighted ME distance, while potentially reasonable, isn’t thoroughly motivated in relation to other distance metrics. The proofs appear correct, though the reliance on existing perturbation bounds (e.g., Davis-Kahan) means the novelty lies more in the application of these tools rather than developing new theoretical machinery.

Clarity is reasonable, but the presentation feels dense at times. The notation is heavy, and the conceptual leap from the "Generic Theorem" to the specific instantiations for PFM and SBM requires careful reading. The connection to existing recovery theorems in Section 4.2 is valuable, but could be more clearly articulated; it feels somewhat tacked on rather than integrated into the core argument. 

The originality of the framework is moderate. The idea of using stability to obtain model-free guarantees is plausible, but doesn't represent a radical departure from existing thinking in statistical learning. The key contribution is, perhaps, demonstrating that one *can* translate existing model-based results into a model-free context. However, the conditions and resulting bounds are often weaker, diminishing the practical impact. 

Significance is the biggest weakness. The derived guarantees are often difficult to satisfy in practice, as evidenced by the experimental section. The comparison to existing results (specifically, referencing [16] and its superior bounds) highlights this issue. While the authors acknowledge this, the lack of a compelling case for when the model-free approach *outperforms* established methods limits the paper’s significance. The "sanity checks" and discussion of clusterability/resilience are promising, but not fully developed.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Presents a novel framework for model-free clustering guarantees.
*   Successfully connects model-free and model-based recovery results.
*   Technically sound with rigorous proofs.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Guarantees are often weak and difficult to satisfy in practice.
*   Experimental validation doesn't convincingly demonstrate an advantage over existing methods.
*   The framework's practical impact is limited.
*   Relatively incremental contribution to the field.

**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards rejection. The paper presents an interesting theoretical idea, but its practical relevance and significance are questionable. The conditions for the guarantees to hold are often too restrictive, and the experimental results don't provide strong evidence that this approach offers a significant improvement over established model-based techniques. While the work is technically sound, it feels more like a proof-of-concept than a major advancement in the field. I would be more receptive to a revised version that addresses these concerns, perhaps by focusing on specific scenarios where the model-free approach is demonstrably superior, or by tightening the theoretical bounds.
