### Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper introduces a novel framework for providing theoretical guarantees for graph clustering without relying on the common assumption that the graph is generated from a specific probabilistic model, such as a Stochastic Block Model (SBM) or Preference Frame Model (PFM). The core idea is to establish the stability of a given clustering `C`. The authors propose that if a block model `M(G, C)` fitted to a clustering `C` of a graph `G` explains the data well (i.e., is "close" to `G`), then any other clustering `C'` that also fits the data well must be a small perturbation of `C`. This provides a "model-free" form of validation, shifting the focus from recovering a ground-truth partition to verifying that a found clustering captures a stable structure in the data.

The paper instantiates this general framework for two important model classes: the Preference Frame Model (PFM) and the Stochastic Block Model (SBM). For each, it defines a procedure for fitting the model to a given clustering, specifies a goodness-of-fit measure (typically based on the spectral norm difference between the graph's Laplacian/adjacency matrix and the model's), and derives a theorem that bounds the distance between two "good" clusterings. All quantities in the bounds are computable from the data, making the framework practically applicable.

This work stands in contrast to the majority of theoretical literature on block models (e.g., Rohe et al., 2011; Wan & Meila, 2015; Abbe & Sandon, 2015), which provides recovery guarantees under the strong assumption that the graph is sampled from the model. The proposed framework is more aligned with the literature on clustering stability and "clusterability" (e.g., Ben-David, 2008; Awasthi et al., 2014), but it is, to my knowledge, the first to explicitly connect the stability concept to modern block models like PFM and SBM in such a direct and computable manner. It cleverly "repurposes" tools from model-based analysis, like matrix perturbation theory (Davis-Kahan theorem), for a model-agnostic setting.

### Strengths

1.  **High Significance and Originality:** The central contribution—providing model-free guarantees for model-based clustering algorithms—is highly significant. It addresses a fundamental and often criticized gap between theory and practice, where generative model assumptions are rarely verifiable. This work provides a principled way to assess the quality and uniqueness of a clustering on any given graph, which could have a substantial impact on how clustering results are validated and interpreted.
2.  **Technical Soundness:** The paper is technically solid. The application of matrix perturbation bounds to relate the goodness-of-fit to the distance between clustering subspaces is elegant and rigorously executed. The derivations for both PFM (Theorem 4) and SBM (Theorem 9) are clear and build upon a logical sequence of propositions. The connection made to existing model-based theorems ([20], [18]) in Section 4.2 further strengthens the work by showing its consistency with and ability to improve upon prior results.
3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The "Generic Theorem" in Section 3 serves as an excellent blueprint, which is then methodically instantiated. The distinction between model construction, goodness of fit, and distance measure is clearly laid out. The proof sketches and propositions guide the reader effectively through the main technical arguments.
4.  **Practical Relevance:** A major strength is that the derived bounds are fully computable from the observed graph and a given clustering. The experiments, while preliminary, successfully demonstrate this by calculating the stability criterion `δ` for various datasets. This moves the work beyond a purely theoretical exercise and provides a tangible tool for researchers and practitioners.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Implicit Structural Assumptions:** While the framework does not assume a generative model, it is not entirely assumption-free. It relies on structural properties of the observed graph `G`, most notably the eigengap condition in Assumption 1 (`|λ̂_K| > |λ̂_{K+1}|`). This is a strong "clusterability" assumption on the data itself. If a graph does not possess a clean spectral separation, the bounds become vacuous as the denominator `(|λ̂_K| - |λ̂_{K+1}|)` approaches zero. The paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of this limitation.
2.  **Practicality of the Bounds:** The experiments reveal that the derived bounds can be loose, particularly for the SBM case (Theorem 9), which were found to be uninformative (`δ > 1`) across all experiments. The authors acknowledge this but could provide more intuition as to why. Is it an artifact of using the spectral norm, or does it reflect the SBM's rigidity as a model? For the PFM, the bounds are informative, but stability is only guaranteed for clusterings that are very close to the initial one. This might limit the utility in cases where one wants to compare two substantially different but plausible clusterings.
3.  **Guidance on Goodness-of-Fit:** The framework hinges on the goodness-of-fit parameter `ε`. However, the paper provides no guidance on how to interpret its magnitude. For a practitioner who computes `ε` for their clustering, it is unclear what constitutes a "small enough" value for the theory to be meaningful. A discussion on normalizing `ε` or establishing a baseline (e.g., based on random graphs) would significantly improve the practical applicability.

### Constructive Suggestions for Improvement

*   **Discussion on Eigengap:** In Section 4 or 7, please add a paragraph discussing the role of the eigengap assumption. Acknowledge that this is a critical, data-dependent assumption and that the framework's applicability is limited to graphs that exhibit this spectral property.
*   **Analysis of SBM Bound Looseness:** The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed discussion on why the SBM bounds in Theorem 9 appear loose. Could it be that the spectral norm `||Â - A||` is too strong a requirement for the unnormalized adjacency matrix? Would a different norm or a result based on the Laplacian (as in Theorem 12, despite its own issues) offer a path forward?
*   **Interpreting `ε`:** To aid practitioners, consider adding a brief discussion on interpreting `ε`. For example, could one compare `||L̂ - L||` to the norm of a random perturbation matrix to gauge its significance? Any heuristic or guidance here would be valuable.
*   **Experimental Scope:** To make the experimental section more compelling, consider an experiment where you compare two different, plausible clusterings `C` and `C'` (e.g., from two different algorithms like spectral clustering and Louvain) and use your framework to check if they are provably close. This would be a more realistic use case than comparing to slightly perturbed versions of the same clustering.

### Conclusion

This is an excellent paper that introduces a novel and important perspective on validating graph clustering. The proposed model-free stability framework is a significant conceptual advance over standard model-based recovery guarantees. The technical contributions are sound, and the paper is written with exceptional clarity. While the practical utility of the derived bounds requires further investigation and potential tightening, the work lays a strong foundation and opens up a promising new direction of research. The strengths far outweigh the weaknesses, and I believe this paper will be of great interest to the NeurIPS community.
