- Introduces a framework that gives theoretical guarantees for model-based clustering without assuming the observed graph was generated by the model (Generic Theorem: good model fit implies other good fits are small perturbations).

- Instantiates the framework for PFM and SBM, yielding computable model-free guarantees directly from data.

- Demonstrates the framework on synthetic (PFM, perturbed PFM, LFR) and real (political blogs) datasets, validating theoretical claims.

- Advances prior block-model literature by removing the requirement that data comes from the model, distinguishing it from related works that rely on model assumptions or uncomputable quantities.

- Technically rigorous: clear definitions, complete proofs, and novel use of spectral perturbation tools (e.g., Davis–Kahan) for clustering guarantees.

- Connects model-based recovery theorems to model-free validation, showing existing recovery results can be repurposed for model-free guarantees.

- Well-written and organized with consistent notation; weighted ME distance and earth-mover intuition are clearly explained.

- Minor clarity gap: could provide more guidance on how to choose the initial clustering C in practice.

- Original contribution: novel “turn-around” of recovery theorems and use of Laplacian perturbation to avoid counting arguments in stability bounds.

- Significant practical impact: computable guarantees increase trustworthiness of model-based algorithms on real networks; graph “smoothing” idea may be practically useful.

- Strengths: novel framework, rigorous and computable guarantees, strong experimental validation, clear connection between model-based and model-free, honest discussion of limitations.

- Weaknesses: SBM bounds are less informative than PFM bounds; limited discussion of selecting initial clustering; lacks analysis of computational complexity and broader comparisons to other validation methods.

- Overall assessment: technically sound, novel, and practically relevant work that merits acceptance.
