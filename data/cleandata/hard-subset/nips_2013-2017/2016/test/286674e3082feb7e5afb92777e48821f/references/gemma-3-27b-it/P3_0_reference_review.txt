## NIPS 2016 Review: Graph Clustering: Block-models and model free results

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a compelling and technically sound framework for analyzing graph clustering algorithms without relying on strict model assumptions. The core idea – establishing stability based on goodness-of-fit – is both elegant and potentially very useful. While the theorems and proofs are complex, the authors demonstrate the practical applicability of their approach through experiments. However, the results are more readily applicable to certain models (PFM) than others (SBM), and the practical implications, while promising, require further exploration. I recommend **Accept** with minor revisions, leaning towards a slightly higher confidence score if the authors can address some of the concerns detailed below.

**1. Summary and Related Work:**

The paper addresses a significant limitation of current graph clustering research: the reliance on strong model assumptions (SBM, DC-SBM, PFM). While these models have driven theoretical progress, their applicability is limited in real-world scenarios. The authors propose a “model-free” framework that guarantees clustering stability: if a clustering *fits* the data well (according to some measure), then any other clustering that *also* fits well must be “close” to the original. This is achieved by demonstrating that the perturbation bounds derived from model-specific analyses can be generalized.

The related work section is reasonably comprehensive, covering relevant literature on spectral clustering, block models, and perturbation bounds. The connection to existing work on stability (Bilu & Linial, Peng et al.) is also noted, highlighting the novelty of this work in the context of model-based clustering. 

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The central idea of using a stability framework to validate clustering *without* assuming a specific generative model is a significant contribution. It bridges the gap between theoretical guarantees based on idealized models and the practical challenges of real-world graph data.
* **Technical Soundness:** The theorems are rigorously stated and appear to be supported by careful proofs (although full verification requires a deep dive into the supplementary material). The use of perturbation theory (Davis-Kahan) and spectral graph theory is appropriate.
* **Generality:** The framework is presented in a general form (Theorem 1) and then instantiated for specific models (PFM, SBM), demonstrating its adaptability.  The connection drawn between model-free and model-based results is insightful.
* **Practical Relevance:** The authors demonstrate the applicability of their framework through experiments on both synthetic and real-world data, showing that the conditions for stability can be computed from the data. The comparison with existing methods in the experiments is helpful.
* **Clear Definitions:** The definitions of goodness-of-fit, distance between clusterings (both ME and wME), and model construction are clearly presented.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:** The theoretical results, particularly Theorems 4 and 9, are quite complex and rely on several assumptions (eigengap, goodness-of-fit).  This complexity may limit their direct applicability for practitioners.  Simplifying the conditions or providing more intuitive interpretations would be beneficial.
* **Model Dependence of Applicability:** The experiments demonstrate that the framework is more readily applicable to PFM than to SBM.  The conditions for SBM stability seem stricter.  The authors acknowledge this, but it raises the question of whether the framework is inherently better suited for certain models than others. Further investigation into why this difference exists would be valuable.
* **Conservatism of Bounds:** The experimental results suggest that the bounds are relatively tight, but also somewhat conservative.  While theoretical guarantees are important, it would be helpful to explore whether the bounds could be relaxed without sacrificing theoretical validity.
* **Limited Scope of Experimental Validation:** While the experiments are a good starting point, they could be expanded to include a wider range of datasets and clustering algorithms.  Investigating the performance of the framework on graphs with more complex structures would be valuable.
* **Lack of Discussion of Computational Cost:** Constructing the PFM and SBM estimators likely has a computational cost. A discussion of this cost would be useful.



**4. Detailed Comments and Questions:**

* **Assumption 1 (PFM):** The assumption about the non-zero eigengap is important. How robust is the framework to violations of this assumption?  Can it be relaxed with a slight modification of the theorems?
* **Weighted ME Distance:** The authors argue that the weighted ME distance (distd̂) is more natural. While this is a reasonable point, it’s not immediately obvious why. A more detailed explanation of the intuition behind this choice would be helpful.
* **Section 4.2 (Using existing recovery theorems):**  The discussion of using results from [20] to improve the bound is excellent.
* **Experiments:** The explanation of the perturbation process used in the experiments could be more detailed. How exactly was the principal subspace perturbed?
* **Future Work:** The authors mention extending the framework to cover Frobenius norm perturbations. This would be a valuable extension, and it would be helpful to provide a more detailed outline of how this could be achieved.




**5. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound framework for model-free graph clustering validation.
* The theorems are rigorously stated and demonstrably applicable through experiments.
* The connection between model-free and model-based results is insightful.
* The paper addresses a significant limitation of current graph clustering research.

**Against Acceptance:**

* The theoretical results are complex and may be difficult for practitioners to apply.
* The framework is more readily applicable to certain models (PFM) than others (SBM).
* The bounds may be conservative.




**Overall Recommendation:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. I believe that this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of graph clustering, and that its potential impact justifies acceptance to NIPS. I encourage the authors to address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly by simplifying the theoretical results and exploring the applicability of the framework to a wider range of models and datasets.  The potential to provide truly model-free validation for clustering algorithms is highly valuable.
