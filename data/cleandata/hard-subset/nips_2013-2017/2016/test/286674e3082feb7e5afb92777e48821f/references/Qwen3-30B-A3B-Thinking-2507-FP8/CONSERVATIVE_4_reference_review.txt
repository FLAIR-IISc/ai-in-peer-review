

# Review of "Graph Clustering: Block-models and model free results"

This paper proposes a framework for providing theoretical guarantees for model-based clustering algorithms without assuming the data comes from the model. The core idea is that if a clustering C fits the graph G well according to a model-specific goodness-of-fit measure, then any other clustering C' that fits G well must be close to C. The authors instantiate this "Generic Theorem" for PFM and SBM models, providing bounds on the weighted misclassification error between clusterings.

The paper is well-structured and technically sound within its theoretical framework. The authors correctly reference relevant literature (e.g., [20], [18]) and provide detailed proofs of their theoretical results. The connection between model-based and model-free guarantees is conceptually interesting, and the proof techniques based on matrix perturbation theory are valid.

However, I have significant concerns about the paper's practical relevance and contribution to the field. The main issue is that the framework's conditions for providing guarantees are extremely restrictive in practice. As demonstrated in the experimental section, the SBM bounds are "much harder to satisfy" (all δ values > 1), rendering the guarantees practically useless. The authors themselves note that "the C cannot be proved stable even for the LFR data" using SBM bounds. This undermines the entire premise of "model-free guarantees" since the conditions for the guarantees to hold are essentially never satisfied in practice.

The paper also fails to address a critical limitation: the framework assumes a clustering C is already given, which is precisely what clustering algorithms aim to find. The authors don't provide any method for selecting C in the first place, making the framework purely theoretical rather than a practical tool. In contrast, existing clustering algorithms (like spectral clustering) directly produce a clustering, which is what practitioners need.

The experimental evaluation is limited and doesn't convincingly demonstrate the framework's advantages. The authors show that the clustering C0 is "always stable" in the PFM framework, but this is trivially true since C0 is the output of spectral clustering. The paper doesn't compare against established clustering algorithms or demonstrate that the proposed framework leads to better clustering results. The only comparison is between the PFM and SBM bounds, which shows PFM's advantages but doesn't prove the framework's practical utility.

The paper claims to provide "model-free guarantees," but this is misleading. The framework still relies on a specific model (PFM or SBM) for the goodness-of-fit measure, and the guarantees only apply when the graph approximately fits that model. The authors acknowledge this limitation in Section 4.4, noting that "the fit is good, but not good enough to satisfy the conditions of the theorems."

I'm also concerned about the paper's positioning within the literature. The authors claim their approach is "not entirely new" but that "using it explicitly with model-based clustering (instead of cost-based) is" novel. However, the connection between model-based recovery theorems and model-free guarantees has been explored in prior work (e.g., [6], [11]). The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its contribution from these existing works.

While the technical results are correct, the paper doesn't advance the field in a meaningful way. The theoretical results are essentially rephrasing existing stability results in a different framework, and the practical conditions required for these results to be useful are prohibitively restrictive. The authors note that "the results are not always informative" and "sometimes this should be so, e.g when the data does not fit the model well" - but this is precisely when the guarantees would be most needed, making the framework largely impractical.

In summary, the paper presents technically sound but largely impractical theoretical results. It doesn't provide a useful tool for practitioners, and the claimed advantages over existing methods aren't demonstrated. The framework is too restrictive to be of practical value, and the paper doesn't convincingly show that it offers significant theoretical advantages over existing approaches.

**Strengths:**
- Well-written and technically sound theoretical results
- Correctly references relevant literature
- Clear exposition of the connection between model-based and model-free guarantees

**Weaknesses:**
- Extremely restrictive conditions for practical utility (demonstrated in experiments)
- Doesn't address how to select the clustering C in practice
- Limited experimental evaluation that doesn't demonstrate practical advantages
- Misleading claims about "model-free" guarantees
- Fails to convincingly distinguish from prior work

**Recommendation:** Reject. The paper makes a limited theoretical contribution with minimal practical relevance, and the conditions required for the guarantees to hold are so restrictive that they undermine the paper's main claim. While the technical results are sound, they don't advance the field in a meaningful way for either practitioners or theorists. The paper would be significantly improved by demonstrating practical utility through extensive comparisons with established clustering methods on real-world datasets, but it currently lacks this crucial element.
