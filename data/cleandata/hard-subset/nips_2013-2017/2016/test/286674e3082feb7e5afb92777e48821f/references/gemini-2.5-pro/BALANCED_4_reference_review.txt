### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a novel and valuable framework for providing theoretical guarantees for model-based graph clustering algorithms without making the strong assumption that the data was generated from the model. The central idea is one of stability: if a given clustering `C` and its corresponding fitted model `M(G, C)` (e.g., a PFM or SBM) provide a good fit to an observed graph `G`, then any other clustering `C'` that also fits the graph well must be a small perturbation of `C`. This shifts the paradigm from proving recovery under model assumptions to validating a clustering's uniqueness based on its goodness-of-fit to the observed data.

The authors instantiate this general framework for two important model classes: the Preference Frame Model (PFM) and the Stochastic Block Model (SBM). For each, they define a specific model-fitting procedure, a goodness-of-fit measure, and derive a theorem that bounds the distance between any two "good" clusterings. The proofs rely on matrix perturbation theory, particularly the Davis-Kahan Sinus Theorem. A key feature of the proposed framework is that all quantities involved in the bounds are computable from the data, allowing for practical cluster validation. The paper demonstrates this with experiments on synthetic and real-world graphs, showing when the conditions of the theorems are met and a clustering can be certified as stable.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary contribution—providing model-free guarantees for model-based clustering—is highly original and significant. It directly addresses a fundamental weakness in the vast literature on block models, where theoretical results almost invariably depend on the assumption that the graph is sampled from the model. This work provides a path to apply the rigor of model-based analysis to real-world data where the true generative process is unknown, substantially increasing the relevance and trustworthiness of these methods. The idea of "turning around" model-based recovery theorems for model-free validation is powerful.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The application of matrix perturbation theory to establish the link between goodness-of-fit (a small `||L_hat - L||`) and the closeness of the underlying eigenspaces is elegant and appropriate. The main result for PFM (Theorem 4) is well-derived and provides a concrete instantiation of the paper's general framework. The authors are careful to state their assumptions (e.g., the eigengap) clearly.

3.  **Practical Relevance and Computability:** A major strength is that the proposed bounds are fully computable from the observed graph and a candidate clustering. This is not always the case for theoretical bounds in machine learning. The authors demonstrate this utility in the experimental section, showing how their framework can be used to check the stability of a clustering found by a standard algorithm like spectral clustering. This transforms the work from a purely theoretical contribution into a potentially practical tool for cluster validation.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The "blueprint" of the generic theorem in Section 3, followed by its specific instantiations for PFM and SBM, makes the overall argument easy to follow. The connections drawn between their framework and existing model-based results in Section 4 are insightful and strengthen the paper's contributions.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Practicality of the SBM Result:** The authors commendably report that their main result for SBM (Theorem 9) does not provide meaningful guarantees in their experiments ("All δ values from Theorem 9 are above 1"). While this honesty is appreciated, it raises questions about the utility of this part of the contribution. Is the bound for SBM inherently loose, or is the SBM with its goodness-of-fit measure (`||A_hat - A||`) simply too restrictive a model class for this framework to be effective on the chosen datasets? A more in-depth discussion on why these guarantees fail would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Experimental Detail:** The experimental section, while demonstrating the core idea, could benefit from more detail to ensure reproducibility. For instance, the process of obtaining `C` by "perturbing C0 with gradually increasing noise" is not specified. The details of how the principal subspace was perturbed for the "Perturbed PFM Datasets" are also relegated to the supplement. Including these details in the main text would make the experimental validation more transparent and convincing.

3.  **Dependence on Eigengap:** The derived bounds have a quadratic dependence on the inverse of the eigengap (`1/(|λ_K| - |λ_{K+1}|)^2`). While this is standard for spectral perturbation results, it means the guarantees can become vacuous if the eigengap is small, a common occurrence in real-world networks. This limitation, while inherent to the spectral approach, could be highlighted more explicitly.

### Constructive Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Discuss the SBM Bound Failure:** It would be very insightful if the authors could provide more analysis on the SBM result. For example, is the spectral norm of `A_hat - A` (Assumption 4) too stringent? Would an alternative, perhaps Laplacian-based, goodness-of-fit measure yield more practical bounds for the SBM case? This would add significant value to the paper.

2.  **Elaborate on Experimental Methods:** Please add a more detailed description of the experimental protocol in Section 6. Specifically, describing the noise model used to perturb the initial clustering `C0` would allow other researchers to replicate and build upon these results.

3.  **Refine Comparison to Related Work:** The comparison to loss-based clustering stability results ([16], [14]) in Section 5 is interesting. To make it more concrete, it would be helpful to discuss the relative stringency of the conditions. For instance, on a given graph, is the condition on the Cheeger constant in [16] likely to be harder or easier to satisfy than this paper's goodness-of-fit assumption?

4.  **Title:** The current title is somewhat generic. A more descriptive title, such as "Model-Free Stability Guarantees for Block Model Graph Clustering" or "Validating Graph Clusters without Generative Assumptions," might better reflect the paper's specific and novel contribution.

### Conclusion

This is a strong paper that introduces an important and novel perspective on the validation of graph clustering. The model-free framework is a significant conceptual advance over standard model-based analyses. The technical results for the PFM are solid and the practical potential of computable stability guarantees is high. While the SBM results are less practically compelling as presented, and the experimental description could be more detailed, these weaknesses do not detract from the importance of the core contribution. This work is likely to inspire follow-up research on model-free validation for a wider range of models and algorithms. I recommend acceptance.
