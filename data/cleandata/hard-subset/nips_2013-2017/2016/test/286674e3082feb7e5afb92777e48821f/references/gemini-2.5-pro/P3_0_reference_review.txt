### Summary

This paper introduces a novel and important framework for providing theoretical guarantees for model-based graph clustering algorithms without making the strong assumption that the observed graph was actually generated from the model. The central idea is one of stability or uniqueness: if a given clustering `C` and its associated model parameters `M(G, C)` provide a good fit to the observed graph `G`, then any other clustering `C'` that also fits `G` well must be a small perturbation of `C`. This provides a form of "model-free" validation for the clustering `C`.

The authors instantiate this general framework for two popular block models: the Preference Frame Model (PFM) and the Stochastic Block Model (SBM). For each, they define a procedure to fit the model given a clustering, a goodness-of-fit measure (typically a matrix norm difference between the observed graph and the fitted model), and derive a bound on the distance between two "good" clusterings. The technical core of the proofs relies on matrix perturbation theory, specifically the Davis-Kahan Sin-Theta theorem, to relate the goodness-of-fit to the distance between the principal subspaces associated with the clusterings. This subspace distance is then linked to a standard clustering dissimilarity measure. A key strength of the proposed framework is that all quantities in the final bounds are computable from the data, making the results practically applicable for cluster validation.

### Quality

The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The theoretical development for the PFM case (Theorem 4) is particularly elegant and well-executed. The chain of reasoning—from the model fit `||L̂ - L|| ≤ ε` to the subspace distance via Davis-Kahan, and finally to the clustering distance `dist_d(C, C')`—is clear and rigorously established. The authors are careful and honest about the limitations of their work, most notably in their discussion of the SBM results. They openly state that their bound for the SBM (Theorem 9) is far more restrictive and often not informative in practice, as confirmed by their experiments. This transparency strengthens the credibility of the paper. The experimental validation, while not extensive, effectively demonstrates the utility of the PFM framework and corroborates the theoretical findings regarding the weakness of the SBM bound.

### Clarity

The paper is very well-written and organized. The introduction of the "Generic Theorem" in Section 3 provides an excellent high-level blueprint that makes the subsequent, more technical instantiations for PFM and SBM easy to follow. The notation is standard for the field, and the definitions of model fitting, goodness-of-fit, and clustering distance are laid out clearly. The experimental setup and results are also presented in a clear and understandable manner. The paper provides enough detail for an expert to grasp the main proof ideas, with full proofs rightly deferred to the supplement.

One minor point for improvement would be a more intuitive discussion of the goodness-of-fit parameter `ε`. While its role in the bounds is mathematically clear, some guidance on what constitutes a "small" `ε` in practical scenarios, or how it relates to concepts like signal-to-noise ratio, would be beneficial for practitioners seeking to apply these results.

### Originality

The paper's main contribution is highly original. While stability analysis for clustering is not new (e.g., [4, 9]), this paper is, to my knowledge, the first to formalize it in this specific way for modern block models like PFM and SBM. It represents a significant conceptual shift from the vast body of literature on block model recovery, which almost universally relies on the assumption that the data is generated from the model in question. By "turning around" model-based recovery theorems to provide model-free guarantees, the authors open up a new and promising research direction. The use of matrix perturbation theory in this context is also novel and provides a more direct proof technique than the counting arguments often used in SBM literature. The paper does an excellent job of positioning its contributions with respect to related work in spectral clustering stability and model-based recovery.

### Significance

The significance of this work is substantial. The strong generative model assumption has long been a major barrier between the beautiful theory of block models and its practical application. This paper provides a principled way to bridge this gap. The ability to compute a validation score for a given clustering—essentially answering "is this clustering uniquely good according to the PFM model structure?" without believing the data actually came from a PFM—is a powerful tool for practitioners. This work is likely to be highly influential and could inspire a new line of research on model-free validation for a wide range of generative models beyond graph clustering. The connections drawn between the model-free and model-based settings are also insightful, showing how this new perspective can even lead to improvements in existing model-based bounds.

### Detailed Comments and Questions

**Strengths:**
*   **Novel Framework:** The model-free stability concept is a powerful and timely idea that addresses a critical limitation of existing block model theory.
*   **Practicality:** The bounds are fully computable from data, transforming a theoretical concept into a practical tool for cluster validation.
*   **Technical Elegance:** The proof for the PFM result is clean, direct, and provides a nice template for similar analyses.
*   **Honesty:** The candid discussion of the SBM result's weakness is commendable.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   **The SBM Result:** The paper convincingly shows that the SBM guarantee is too loose. It would be valuable to include more discussion on *why* this might be the case. Is it an artifact of using the spectral norm `||Â - A||`, which might be too strict for sparse graphs? Or does it reflect an inherent lack of "robustness" in the SBM structure compared to the degree-corrected PFM? Exploring alternative norms (e.g., Frobenius, as mentioned) or different SBM fitting procedures might lead to more useful bounds.
*   **PFM Model Fitting:** The `PFM(G, C)` construction is spectrally motivated and convenient for the proof, but it is not a standard estimator like Maximum Likelihood. How sensitive are the results to this specific choice? Could a different fitting procedure lead to a smaller `ε` and thus a tighter guarantee, even if the proof becomes more complex?
*   **Alternative Result (Theorem 11):** It is stated that the techniques in this paper can improve the error bound in [20] by a factor of 128. This is a strong claim and an excellent demonstration of the power of the new proof techniques. It might be worth highlighting this result more prominently.

In summary, this is an excellent paper that introduces a novel, significant, and practical framework for graph clustering. Its strengths far outweigh its weaknesses, and it is likely to have a lasting impact on the field. I strongly recommend acceptance.
