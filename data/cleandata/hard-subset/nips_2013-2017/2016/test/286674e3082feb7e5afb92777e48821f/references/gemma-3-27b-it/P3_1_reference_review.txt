## NIPS 2016 Paper Review: Graph Clustering: Block-models and model free results

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a compelling and theoretically grounded framework for analyzing graph clustering algorithms *without* relying on the assumption that the data originates from a specific block model. The core idea – establishing stability based on goodness-of-fit – is insightful and elegantly executed, providing model-free guarantees for both SBM and PFM. While the technical details are dense and require careful scrutiny, the paper is, on the whole, well-written and offers a valuable contribution to the field of network clustering. I believe this paper is a strong submission and should be accepted.

**1. Summary and Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper tackles a significant limitation of existing work in graph clustering: the reliance on specific generative models like SBM, DC-SBM, and PFM. While substantial progress has been made in understanding recovery guarantees *under* these models, their applicability is constrained by real-world data rarely perfectly adhering to these assumptions. The authors propose a "generic theorem" – if a clustering *fits* the data well (as measured by a model-specific goodness-of-fit metric), then any other clustering that also fits well must be a small perturbation of the original. This allows for establishing stability and, consequently, recovery guarantees without explicitly assuming a model. 

This work builds upon the established theoretical foundations of spectral clustering and block model analysis (references [1, 15, 17, 18, 20] are key). It differentiates itself by *removing* the model assumption.  Related work in stability of clustering (e.g., [16, 14]) is acknowledged, but the authors rightfully point out that their approach differs fundamentally by focusing on model-based fitting as a criterion for establishing stability, rather than directly comparing clustering solutions. The connection between model-based and model-free guarantees is a novel and particularly valuable contribution.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novel Framework:** The "generic theorem" provides a powerful and generalizable approach to validating graph clustering results, moving beyond the limitations of model-specific assumptions.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper is mathematically sound, with clear definitions of goodness-of-fit, distance metrics, and a rigorous proof outline for the PFM case. The SBM extension, while less restrictive, is still a solid contribution.
* **Practical Relevance:** The authors emphasize the computability of the key quantities (ε, δ, δ0) from the data, making the framework potentially applicable to real-world scenarios. The experimental evaluation demonstrates this.
* **Connection to Existing Work:**  The paper skillfully connects the model-free results to existing model-based recovery theorems, demonstrating their interdependence and potential for mutual reinforcement.
* **Clear Writing (Despite Complexity):** While the technical details are challenging, the authors present the ideas in a logical and well-structured manner.
* **Comprehensive Experimental Validation:** The experiments with PFM, LFR and the political blog data are well-designed and illustrate the practicality and limits of the proposed framework.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Technical Density:**  The mathematical notation and proofs are demanding, potentially limiting accessibility for researchers less familiar with spectral graph theory and statistical learning. The proofs are largely relegated to the supplement, which isn’t ideal for a comprehensive understanding.
* **Conservatism of Bounds:** As acknowledged by the authors, the derived bounds can be conservative. The SBM results, in particular, are less informative than the PFM results due to the stricter conditions required.  
* **Limited Discussion of Practical Implications:** While the computability of parameters is mentioned, a deeper discussion of the practical considerations for applying this framework to large-scale graphs would be beneficial.  For example, the computational cost of estimating the Laplacian and performing the SVD.
* **Dependence on Eigengap:** The performance of the framework, especially for PFM, relies on a clear eigengap. This assumption might not always hold in real-world networks, and a discussion of how to handle cases with weak eigengaps would be valuable.

**4. Detailed Evaluation Based on NIPS Criteria:**

* **Quality:** Excellent. The paper is technically sound, the proofs appear rigorous (based on my understanding of the field), and the analysis is careful.  The authors acknowledge the limitations of their approach.
* **Clarity:** Good. The writing is generally clear, though the technical details require focused attention.  The organization is logical.
* **Originality:** Very good.  The framework itself is a novel and significant contribution.  Connecting model-based and model-free recovery is a clever and insightful idea.
* **Significance:** Excellent. This work has the potential to significantly impact the field of network clustering by providing a more robust and generally applicable validation framework. It opens up new avenues for research in both theoretical and practical aspects of clustering.



**5. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound framework for model-free graph clustering validation.
* The results are potentially applicable to a wide range of real-world networks.
* The connection to existing model-based recovery theorems is a significant contribution.
* The experimental evaluation provides supporting evidence for the effectiveness of the framework.

**Against Acceptance:**

* The technical density may limit accessibility.
* The bounds can be conservative.
* The framework relies on assumptions (e.g., clear eigengap) that may not always hold in practice.



**Overall Recommendation:**  I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The strengths significantly outweigh the weaknesses. This is a valuable contribution to the field that has the potential to advance our understanding of graph clustering and its applications. I would suggest encouraging the authors to consider expanding the discussion of practical considerations and exploring ways to mitigate the conservatism of the bounds in future work.
