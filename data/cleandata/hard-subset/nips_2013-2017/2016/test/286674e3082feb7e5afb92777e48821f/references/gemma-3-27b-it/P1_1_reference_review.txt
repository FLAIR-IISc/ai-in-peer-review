## NIPS 2016 Paper Review: Graph Clustering: Block-models and model free results

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel framework for analyzing graph clustering algorithms *without* assuming the data originates from a specific block model (SBM, DC-SBM, PFM). The core idea is to demonstrate that if a clustering *fits* the data well (according to some measure), then any other clustering that also fits the data well must be “close” to the original.  This establishes a form of stability, implying the initial clustering captures meaningful structure. The authors instantiate this framework for the PFM and SBM models, showing connections between model-based and model-free guarantees. They also present experimental validation supporting their theoretical results.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon the substantial body of work on spectral clustering and block model recovery (e.g., Abbe & Sandon, Rohe et al., Karrer et al.).  It differentiates itself by moving beyond the reliance on generative models – a key limitation of existing theoretical results. The authors correctly position their work alongside related stability results for clustering (e.g., Bilu & Linial, Peng et al.) and acknowledge the differences in their approach (e.g., focusing on model-free guarantees, leveraging perturbation bounds).  The connection drawn to existing model-based recovery theorems and their “reversal” to obtain model-free results is a particularly interesting contribution.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Framework:** The model-free approach is a significant contribution, broadening the applicability of theoretical guarantees for clustering.  It addresses a critical limitation of prior work.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper presents a mathematically sound framework with clear definitions of “fitting” and “closeness” between clusterings. The theorems are well-stated and, while complex, appear rigorously derived (though detailed verification requires significant effort).
* **Connections to Existing Work:** The paper doesn’t discard model-based results; rather, it shows how they can be leveraged to obtain model-free guarantees. This creates a valuable link between the two areas of research.
* **Computational Tractability:** The authors emphasize that the quantities needed for verification (ε, δ, δ0) are computable from the data, making the results potentially practical.
* **Experimental Validation:** The experimental section, while limited in scope, provides initial evidence supporting the theoretical findings.  The analysis of the LFR graph, PFM datasets, and the political blog dataset is insightful.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions:** While the framework is model-free in spirit, the theorems still rely on assumptions (e.g., eigengap conditions, goodness of fit measures). These assumptions can be restrictive in practice and their impact on the applicability of the results isn’t fully explored. The assumption that the PFM eigenvectors are uniquely defined may be too strong.
* **Tightness of Bounds:** The bounds presented, particularly for the SBM (Theorem 9), appear quite loose and may not be practically useful. The authors acknowledge this but offer limited discussion on potential improvements.
* **Clarity of Perturbation Analysis:** The detailed proof steps are relegated to the supplement, making it challenging to fully assess the validity of the argument without significant effort. A more concise explanation of the key perturbation bounds (e.g., Sinus Theorem) within the main body would improve clarity.
* **Limited Experimental Scope:** The experiments only cover a limited range of datasets and parameter settings. A more comprehensive evaluation, including comparisons to other clustering algorithms and a sensitivity analysis of the key parameters, would strengthen the empirical validation.
* **Distance Metric:** While the Weighted ME (wME) distance is arguably more natural, the paper doesn’t convincingly demonstrate its advantages over the standard ME distance in all scenarios.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant open problem in graph clustering: providing theoretical guarantees without relying on strong model assumptions.
* The proposed framework is novel and mathematically sound.
* The connection between model-based and model-free results is a valuable contribution.
* The experimental validation, while limited, is encouraging.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The theoretical bounds, particularly for the SBM, are loose and potentially impractical.
* The assumptions required for the theorems to hold may be restrictive.
* The detailed proofs are relegated to the supplement, hindering thorough evaluation.
* The experimental evaluation is limited in scope.




**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a significant and original contribution to the field of graph clustering.  The proposed model-free framework is a promising step towards developing more robust and broadly applicable theoretical guarantees. However, the loose bounds and restrictive assumptions limit the immediate practical impact of the results.  Despite these weaknesses, the paper’s novelty, theoretical rigor, and potential for future development make it a worthy contribution to NIPS. 

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper, contingent upon the authors addressing the following minor revisions:

1. **Discussion of Assumptions:** Provide a more thorough discussion of the assumptions required for the theorems to hold and their potential limitations in real-world applications.
2. **Improve Bound Tightness (if possible):** Explore potential avenues for tightening the bounds, particularly for the SBM.
3. **Clarify Perturbation Analysis:** Provide a more concise and intuitive explanation of the key perturbation bounds used in the proofs within the main body of the paper.
4. **Expand Experimental Evaluation:**  Include additional experiments with a wider range of datasets and parameter settings, and perform a sensitivity analysis of the key parameters. Consider comparing the performance to other clustering algorithms and demonstrating the stability in the face of adversarial perturbations.
