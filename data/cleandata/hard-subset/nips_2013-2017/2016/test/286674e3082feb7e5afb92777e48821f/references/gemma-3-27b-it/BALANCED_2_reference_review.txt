This paper introduces a novel framework for analyzing the stability of clustering solutions without relying on assumptions about the data generating process. The core idea is to establish conditions under which any alternative clustering that fits the data as well as a given clustering must be "close" to it, providing model-free guarantees. The authors instantiate this framework for the Stochastic Block Model (SBM) and Preference Frame Model (PFM), and demonstrate a connection between model-free and model-based results.

The strengths of this paper are considerable. The central idea – focusing on stability as a means to validate clustering without model assumptions – is genuinely novel and potentially impactful. The theoretical results, particularly Theorem 4 for the PFM, appear sound and offer a concrete way to assess the reliability of clustering solutions. The connection established between model-free and model-based approaches is insightful. The paper is generally well-written, and the authors make a clear effort to explain complex concepts. The experimental evaluation, while not exhaustive, demonstrates the practical relevance of the theoretical findings and provides a useful comparison between the PFM and SBM. The inclusion of comparisons with existing bounds and a discussion of limitations strengthens the presentation.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. The notation is dense, and while the authors provide definitions, following the derivations requires careful attention. The proofs, while likely correct, are relegated to the supplement, which makes it difficult for reviewers to fully assess their validity. While the authors acknowledge the limitations of the SBM results (Theorem 9), the comparatively weaker performance compared to the PFM warrants more in-depth discussion.  The experimental section could be strengthened by a more rigorous evaluation against baseline methods and a wider range of datasets. The discussion of related work, while covering important contributions, feels somewhat fragmented and could benefit from a more cohesive narrative. Specifically, the relation to work on clusterability and resilience could be more clearly articulated. It is not immediately obvious why certain distances are preferred (e.g. weighted ME). The scaling of the constants in the bounds remains unclear - are they achievable in practice?

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The model-free stability framework is a significant contribution to the field of clustering.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The theorems appear rigorously derived and provide meaningful guarantees.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The experimental results demonstrate the applicability of the framework to real-world data.
*   **Impact:** The work has the potential to influence future research on clustering validation and algorithm design.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Complexity:** The paper is technically challenging and requires a strong background in graph theory and statistics.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive.
*   **Weak SBM Results:** The performance of the SBM theorem is less compelling than that of the PFM theorem.
*   **Dense Notation:** The dense notation may hinder accessibility for some readers.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of clustering. The novelty of the model-free stability framework, the theoretical soundness of the results, and the potential for practical impact outweigh the weaknesses. However, the paper could be significantly improved by addressing the concerns raised above. Specifically, providing more detailed proofs (perhaps in an appendix), expanding the experimental evaluation, and offering a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the SBM results would strengthen the paper considerably. 

**Recommendation:**

Reject with encouragement to revise and resubmit. The authors should address the weaknesses outlined above to increase the clarity, accessibility, and impact of their work. Addressing the scaling of the constants in the bounds will be particularly important.
