

# Review of "Graph Clustering: Block-models and model free results"

This paper proposes a framework to provide theoretical guarantees for model-based clustering algorithms without assuming the data comes from a specific generative model. The core idea is that if a model fitted to a clustering "fits the data well," then any other clustering that also "fits the data well" must be close to the original clustering. The authors instantiate this framework for PFM and SBM, providing theorems that bound the distance between clusterings in terms of goodness-of-fit measures.

**Strengths:** The paper is technically sound and well-structured. The theoretical development is rigorous, with careful attention to the mathematical details of the perturbation bounds. The authors successfully connect model-based and model-free results, which is a valuable conceptual contribution. The experimental section demonstrates that the framework can be applied to real data, and the computational aspects of the framework are clearly explained.

**Weaknesses:** The paper's main claim of "model-free guarantees" is misleading. The framework still relies on model-based clustering algorithms (spectral clustering) and requires model-specific goodness-of-fit assumptions (e.g., ||L̂−L|| ≤ ε for PFM). The theorems are not truly model-free, as they still depend on properties of the underlying model (e.g., eigengaps, model parameters). This is a significant conceptual flaw that undermines the paper's main contribution.

The novelty claim is overstated. The approach of using perturbation theory to analyze clustering stability has been explored in prior work (e.g., [14, 16] cited in the paper), though the authors' specific implementation may offer some refinements. The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its contributions from existing stability analysis in clustering literature. The "model-free" framework essentially rephrases existing model-based guarantees rather than providing a fundamentally new theoretical perspective.

The experimental validation is insufficient. The authors test on simulated PFM and SBM data, and one real-world dataset, but fail to compare against established clustering validation methods (e.g., normalized mutual information, modularity, or external validation with ground truth). The claim that "all quantities in our theorems are computable from the data" is true, but the paper doesn't demonstrate how these quantities provide practical advantages over existing methods. The experiments don't show how the proposed framework improves upon standard clustering validation procedures.

The paper also doesn't adequately address the "sanity checks" mentioned in Section 4.3. It's unclear why the SBM bounds are "much harder to satisfy" than PFM bounds, and the authors don't provide evidence that their framework would outperform existing model-based methods in practical applications.

**Technical Assessment:** The mathematical derivations are correct, but the framework's utility is questionable. The theorems require assumptions (e.g., δ ≤ δ0) that are likely to be violated in practice, as shown in the experiments where "all δ values from Theorem 9 are above 1, and not shown." This undermines the practical applicability of the proposed guarantees.

**Significance:** The paper makes a marginal contribution to the literature. While the connection between model-based and model-free results is interesting, the framework doesn't provide new insights that would be valuable to practitioners. The claims of "model-free guarantees" are overstated, and the paper doesn't demonstrate clear advantages over existing clustering validation methods.

**Overall Assessment:** This paper falls short of NIPS standards for significance and novelty. The core contribution is a rephrasing of existing model-based guarantees rather than a truly novel framework. The "model-free" claim is misleading, and the experimental validation is insufficient to demonstrate practical utility. The paper would benefit from a more critical assessment of its limitations and a clearer distinction from existing work on clustering stability.

**Recommendation:** Reject. While the paper is technically sound, it fails to provide a significant contribution to the field. The novelty claim is overstated, the framework is not truly model-free as claimed, and the experimental validation does not adequately support the paper's claims. The work would be better suited for a more specialized statistics or applied mathematics venue rather than NIPS, where the bar for significant contributions to machine learning is higher. The authors should substantially revise their claims and provide more rigorous comparisons to established methods before resubmitting to a venue like NIPS.
