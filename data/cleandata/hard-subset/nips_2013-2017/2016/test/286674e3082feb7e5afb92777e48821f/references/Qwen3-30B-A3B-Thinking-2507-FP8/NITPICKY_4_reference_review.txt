

# Review of "Graph Clustering: Block-models and model free results"

This paper proposes a framework for providing theoretical guarantees for model-based clustering algorithms without assuming the data comes from a specific model. The authors define a "model-free" guarantee as one where if a clustering C fits the data well with respect to a model class M, then any other clustering C' that also fits the data well must be close to C. The framework is instantiated for SBM and PFM models, with theorems showing that under certain conditions, the clustering is stable to perturbations.

**Strengths:**
- The framework conceptually bridges model-based and model-free clustering approaches.
- The paper makes a clear technical contribution by showing how to "turn around" existing model-based recovery theorems to be used in a model-free context.
- Theorems 4 and 9 provide explicit error bounds that are computable from the data.
- The experimental section demonstrates the framework's applicability to real-world networks.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Circular Dependency in Theorem 4:** The bound in Theorem 4 depends on δ0 = mink Ckk/maxk Ckk where Ckk = ∑i∈k d̂i (the volume of cluster k). However, Ckk depends on the clustering C itself, creating a circular dependency that makes the bound difficult to verify in practice. The authors claim "all quantities in our theorems are computable from the data and the clustering C," but this condition is self-referential.

2. **Inconsistent Notation and Typographical Errors:**
- Inconsistent spacing in mathematical expressions (e.g., "d̂i = �n j=1 Âij" with a stray character before "n")
- Inconsistent use of notation (e.g., "L̂ = D̂−1/2ÂD̂−1/2" vs "L = D̂1/2LD̂1/2")
- The paper claims "SBM is a subclass of DC-SBM" but this is technically incorrect; DC-SBM is a more general model that includes SBM as a special case when all degree parameters are equal.

3. **Misleading Claims About "Model-Free" Guarantees:**
- The framework still depends on a specific model class (SBM, PFM) for the goodness-of-fit measure, which contradicts the paper's main claim of being "model-free."
- The conditions required for the theorems (e.g., eigengap conditions) are similar to those required for model-based guarantees, making the model-free claim somewhat misleading.

4. **Technical Ambiguities:**
- Proposition 3 states "under standard recovery conditions for PFM" but doesn't specify what these conditions are or how they relate to the model-free framework.
- In the PFM definition, the paper states "PFM : A satisfies D = diag(A1), D−1AZ = ZR" where D = diag(A1), but this creates a circular definition since D depends on A, which is the parameter being estimated.

5. **Formatting Issues:**
- The references section contains incomplete citations (e.g., "Community detection in general stochastic block models: fundamental limits and efficient recovery algorithms, Emmanuel Abbe, Colin Sandon, arXiv preprint arXiv:1503.00609,, , ,").
- The paper inconsistently capitalizes "block-models" and "block-models" throughout.

6. **Experimental Limitations:**
- The experiments are limited to simulated datasets and a single real-world network (Political Blogs), with no comparison to other model-free clustering validation methods.
- The paper claims the PFM framework can validate clusterings but doesn't show how this compares to existing cluster validation techniques.

**Conclusion:**

While the paper presents an interesting conceptual framework that bridges model-based and model-free clustering, it suffers from multiple technical inconsistencies, circular dependencies, and misleading claims about the nature of the guarantees provided. The paper's claim of "model-free guarantees" is undermined by the framework's continued dependence on specific model classes for the goodness-of-fit measure. The technical ambiguities and formatting errors are significant enough to cast doubt on the paper's validity and reproducibility.

The contribution is incremental rather than groundbreaking, as it essentially repurposes existing model-based recovery theorems. The experimental evaluation is limited and doesn't convincingly demonstrate the practical utility of the framework.

Despite the potential value of the conceptual framework, the technical flaws and misleading claims make the paper unsuitable for publication at NIPS. I recommend rejection.
