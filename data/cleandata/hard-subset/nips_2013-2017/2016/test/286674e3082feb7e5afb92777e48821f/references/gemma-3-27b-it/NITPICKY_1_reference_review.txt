This paper proposes a framework for analyzing the stability of clusterings obtained via model-based algorithms, aiming to provide theoretical guarantees without strict model assumptions. The core idea is that if a clustering fits the data well, any other clustering that also fits well must be a small perturbation of the original. This is instantiated for the SBM and PFM, and connections to existing model-based results are explored.

While the high-level idea is interesting and potentially valuable—providing model-agnostic guarantees is a laudable goal—the paper suffers from a multitude of, albeit minor, issues that collectively detract from its impact and clarity. The presentation is frequently imprecise, and the technical details, while seemingly correct, are often glossed over, making it difficult to fully assess the validity of the results. I am leaning towards rejection due to the sheer number of these issues.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty of Approach:** The attempt to bridge model-based and model-free clustering analysis is a worthwhile endeavor.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The framework, while needing refinement in presentation, provides a potential pathway for deriving guarantees without strong assumptions.
*   **Connections to Existing Work:** Highlighting the link between model-free and model-based results is insightful.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Notational Inconsistencies:** The definition of the Laplacian (page 2) is immediately followed by an incorrect statement.  The normalization used is not consistently applied throughout.
*   **Lack of Rigor in Definitions:** Terms like “close to” (in the generic theorem) and “fits well” are not rigorously defined until much later, hindering initial comprehension. This makes the initial presentation of the framework feel vague.
*   **Omitted Proofs:** While relegating proofs to the supplement is acceptable, the lack of even a sketch of the key steps in the main body makes it difficult to assess the complexity and validity of the arguments.  “Relegated to the Supplement” appears repeatedly, and the supplement is not available for review.
*   **Imprecise Language:**  Phrases like "under standard recovery conditions" (page 3) are too vague. Specific conditions should be stated or referenced.
*   **Redundancy & Verbosity:** The text contains repetitive explanations and unnecessary phrasing. Section 4, in particular, feels like a disorganized collection of observations rather than a cohesive discussion.
*   **Questionable Utility of Theorems 9 and 4:** The conditions for applying the theorems seem restrictive, and the experimental section hints that these conditions are often not met in practice. If the bounds are rarely useful, the theorems’ practical value is diminished.
*   **Experimental Section Issues:** The experimental section is poorly described. The details of the perturbations applied to the PFM data are insufficient. Why are only some results shown? The claim about δ values for the SBM being above 1 without showing them is odd.
*   **Typographical Errors:** Numerous minor typos and grammatical errors throughout the paper detract from its professionalism (e.g., “intact” instead of “in fact” on page 1).
* **Inconsistent use of notation:** A and Â are used interchangeably without clear distinction. This is confusing.

**Specific Concerns & Questions:**

*   **Theorem 1:** What constitutes “other technical conditions” in Theorem 1?
*   **Equation (3):** Why is the weighted ME distance considered more “natural”? The justification is weak.
*   **Proposition 3:** The claim that PFM(G, C) is “consistent” needs a more precise definition and justification.
*   **Section 4.2:** The improvement factor of 128 over existing bounds seems suspiciously large. A more detailed comparison is needed.
*   **Section 4.4:** What does “resilient” mean in the context of clustering? This needs clarification.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important problem: providing model-agnostic guarantees for clustering.
*   The framework, despite its presentation flaws, has the potential to be a useful tool for analyzing clustering stability.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The paper is riddled with minor flaws (notational inconsistencies, imprecise language, omitted proofs, typos).
*   The practical utility of the theorems is questionable due to restrictive conditions.
*   The experimental section is inadequate and lacks sufficient detail.
*   The overall presentation is unclear and difficult to follow.



**Overall Assessment:**

I find the ideas presented in this paper interesting, but the execution is significantly flawed. The cumulative effect of the numerous minor issues severely hinders the clarity and impact of the work. While the theoretical framework has potential, its current presentation and the lack of a convincing demonstration of its practical applicability lead me to recommend **rejection**. Substantial revisions, addressing the points raised above, are needed before this paper could be considered for acceptance. The authors need to prioritize clarity, rigor, and a more thorough evaluation of their results.
