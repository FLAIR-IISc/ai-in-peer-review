This paper presents a novel framework for providing theoretical guarantees for clustering algorithms without making assumptions about the underlying data generating process. The core idea is to demonstrate that if a clustering and its associated model parameters fit the data well, then any other clustering that also fits the data well must be a small perturbation of the original. This is instantiated for the Stochastic Block Model (SBM) and Preference Frame Model (PFM), and connections between model-free and model-based results are explored.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a significant limitation of existing block model literature – its reliance on model assumptions. The proposed framework is a clever and potentially impactful approach to achieving model-agnostic clustering guarantees. The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of the relevant literature, including recent work on spectral clustering and block models. The theorems are stated clearly, and the proofs, while detailed and largely relegated to the supplement, appear rigorous. The experimental evaluation, though relatively simple, offers initial validation of the theoretical results, and demonstrates the practical applicability of the approach.  The inclusion of comparisons to existing bounds and discussion of limitations are also commendable.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. First, the notation is dense and can be difficult to follow, particularly in the early sections. While standard for the field, more explanation of key definitions would be beneficial for broader accessibility. Second, the connection between the "generic theorem" and its instantiations isn't always immediately obvious. The steps involved in defining model construction, goodness-of-fit measures, and distances are clearly laid out, but the leap from the abstract theorem to the specific results could be smoother. Third, while the experimental evaluation demonstrates stability under perturbation, the results for the SBM are discouraging – the conditions for stability are rarely met. The authors acknowledge this, but further investigation into why the SBM bounds are weaker would be valuable. Fourth, the claims about simplicity of the proofs, while potentially true, are undercut by the extensive reliance on supplementary material. A more concise presentation of the core ideas within the main body of the paper would enhance readability. Finally, the discussion section, while insightful, feels somewhat disjointed. A more focused summary of the key contributions and future research directions would strengthen the conclusion.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The model-free clustering guarantee framework is a significant contribution.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The theorems appear rigorously proven and offer potentially valuable insights.
*   **Relevance:** Addresses a core limitation of existing block model literature.
*   **Technical Strength:** Demonstrates a strong command of spectral graph theory and related fields.
*   **Potential Impact:** Could enable the application of clustering algorithms in scenarios where model assumptions are unreliable.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   **Complexity:** Dense notation and complex proofs hinder readability.
*   **Limited SBM Results:**  Weak stability guarantees for the SBM model reduce practical applicability.
*   **Proof Accessibility:** Over-reliance on supplementary material obscures core ideas.
*   **Disjointed Discussion:** The conclusion lacks a focused summary of contributions and future work.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and potentially impactful contribution. While the complexity and limited SBM results are concerns, the theoretical soundness and relevance of the work outweigh these weaknesses. I believe the paper warrants acceptance, but with a strong recommendation for the authors to address the clarity and accessibility issues in a revised version. Specifically, simplifying the notation, providing more intuitive explanations of the framework, and streamlining the presentation of the proofs would significantly enhance the paper's impact.



**Recommendation:** Accept with minor revisions.



**Specific Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Notation:** Provide more intuitive explanations of key variables and symbols. Consider using a table of notation.
*   **Clarity:**  Expand on the connection between the generic theorem and its instantiations. Provide more detailed explanations of the model construction and goodness-of-fit measures.
*   **SBM Analysis:** Investigate why the stability guarantees for the SBM are weaker than those for the PFM. Explore potential avenues for tightening the bounds.
*   **Proof Presentation:**  Move key proof steps from the supplement to the main body of the paper. Focus on the core ideas and intuition behind the proofs.
*   **Discussion:**  Provide a more focused and concise summary of the key contributions, limitations, and future research directions.
*   **Experimentation:** Expand the experimental evaluation to include a wider range of datasets and parameters.
