

# Review of "Graph Clustering: Block-models and model free results"

## Summary
This paper proposes a framework for providing theoretical guarantees for model-based clustering algorithms without assuming the data comes from a specific generative model. The core idea is that if a model fitted to a clustering C fits the data well, then any other clustering C' that fits the data well must be a small perturbation of C. The authors instantiate this framework for the Preference Frame Model (PFM) and Stochastic Block Model (SBM), proving stability bounds that connect model-based and model-free clustering results. The paper builds on existing work in network clustering (e.g., [15, 17, 18, 20]) but shifts the perspective from model-based recovery to model-free validation, which is a novel contribution to the field.

## Strengths

**Technical Soundness and Clarity:** The paper is technically rigorous with well-defined concepts and clear theorems. The Generic Theorem (Theorem 1) is properly motivated and instantiated for PFM and SBM. The authors provide precise definitions of goodness-of-fit measures (Assumptions 2 and 4) and clustering distances (weighted ME distance), which are crucial for the framework's applicability. The proofs leverage standard matrix perturbation theory (Davis-Kahan theorem) in a clear and direct manner, with the supplemental material providing necessary technical details. The experimental section validates the theoretical results on both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating the framework's practical utility.

**Novelty:** The paper presents a genuinely novel framework for model-free validation of clustering results. While spectral clustering and model-based clustering have been extensively studied, the approach of "turning around" model-based recovery theorems to provide model-free guarantees is original. The authors correctly note that this is the first work of its type for SBM, DC-SBM, and PFM. The connection between model-based recovery and model-free guarantees is a significant theoretical contribution that advances the understanding of clustering stability.

**Significance:** The framework has immediate practical value for validating clustering results without assuming a specific generative model, addressing a common limitation in the literature. The paper demonstrates how existing model-based recovery theorems can be repurposed for model-free guarantees, which is a valuable insight for both theoretical and applied researchers. The experimental results confirm that the framework can be applied to real-world datasets (Political Blogs) and synthetic data (LFR benchmark), showing that the stability conditions are meaningful in practice.

## Weaknesses

**Limited Scope of Instantiations:** While the framework is general, the paper only provides detailed instantiations for PFM and SBM. The connection to DC-SBM, which is mentioned as a subclass of PFM, is not fully developed. A more comprehensive discussion of how the framework could be applied to other clustering models (beyond the three explicitly mentioned) would strengthen the paper's broader significance.

**SBM Results Are Less Practical:** As the authors acknowledge, the SBM results (Theorem 9) are significantly more restrictive than the PFM results (Theorem 4). The SBM bounds require conditions that are rarely satisfied in practice (δ values above 1 in experiments), making them less useful for practitioners. While the authors note this limitation, they don't explore why the SBM bounds are more restrictive or propose potential improvements.

**Comparison to Existing Model-Free Validation Techniques:** The paper claims no prior work of this type exists, but the connection to existing work on clusterability and stability (e.g., [4, 7, 9]) could be more thoroughly discussed. The relationship between the authors' framework and existing stability results (e.g., [14, 16]) would benefit from clearer comparison, particularly regarding the computational tractability of the bounds.

## Specific Comments

- The authors should clarify why the PFM framework yields more practical bounds than SBM, especially given that SBM is a subclass of PFM. This would help readers understand the trade-offs between model flexibility and theoretical guarantees.

- The experimental section would be strengthened by including a comparison to other model-free validation techniques (e.g., bootstrap methods [11] or stability-based approaches [3]), to demonstrate the relative advantages of the proposed framework.

- While the paper states that "all quantities in our theorems are computable from the data," the practical computation of the eigengap (|λ̂K| - |λ̂K+1|) in high-dimensional settings could be discussed in more detail.

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a novel and technically sound framework for model-free validation of clustering results, with clear theoretical contributions and practical implications. The main strength lies in the elegant connection between model-based recovery theorems and model-free guarantees, which advances the theoretical understanding of clustering stability. While the SBM results are less practical than the PFM results, the framework itself is general and could be extended to other models. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a significant contribution to the field of network clustering.

**Pro Acceptance Arguments:**
- Novel framework bridging model-based and model-free clustering
- Rigorous theoretical results with clear proofs
- Practical validation through experiments on synthetic and real-world data
- The framework is general and could be extended to other clustering models
- All quantities in the theorems are computable from data

**Con Acceptance Arguments:**
- SBM results are less practical than PFM results
- Limited scope of model instantiations (only PFM and SBM)
- Insufficient comparison to existing model-free validation techniques

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by providing a theoretical foundation for validating clustering results without model assumptions, which addresses an important limitation in current network clustering research. The technical soundness, clarity, and novelty of the work are strong, and the significance of the results is well-demonstrated. I recommend acceptance.
