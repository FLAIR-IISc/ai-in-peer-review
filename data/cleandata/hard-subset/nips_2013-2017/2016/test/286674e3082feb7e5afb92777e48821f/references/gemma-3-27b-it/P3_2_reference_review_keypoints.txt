- Overall assessment: novel, theoretically strong paper; recommend accept with minor revisions.  
- Core idea: establish model-free clustering stability via goodness-of-fit—if a clustering fits well, other good fits must be close.  
- Relation to prior work: builds on spectral clustering and block models, positioned as complementary to model-based approaches.  
- Related work gap: related stability-based methods (e.g., [16]) need deeper contextualization.  
- Novelty: original framework connecting goodness-of-fit to clustering stability without assuming a specific generative model.  
- Theoretical rigor: mathematically rigorous with clear definitions and proofs; uses perturbation bounds effectively.  
- Bridge between approaches: shows how model-based recovery theorems can yield model-free guarantees.  
- Methodology: well-defined components (model construction, goodness-of-fit measure, clustering distance) enabling broad applicability.  
- Concrete results: provides instantiations and specific guarantees for PFM and SBM.  
- Computability: emphasizes that the bounds are computable, increasing practical potential.  
- Experiments: validation is limited and dataset choices narrow (LFR only); lacks broad empirical evaluation.  
- Missing comparisons: does not compare with existing stability measures like [16].  
- Conservatism of bounds: bounds—especially for SBM—appear conservative; scaling with n and parameters is unclear.  
- Assumption sensitivity: results rely on conditions like an eigengap; sensitivity to violations is insufficiently explored.  
- Notation clarity: occasional dense notation and distracting use of hats; some definitions could be more intuitive.  
- Practical implications: limited discussion on how theoretical guarantees translate to algorithm choice or validation in practice.  
- Significance: potential to substantially impact graph clustering by providing a model-free validation framework.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, rigorous, bridges model-based and model-free views, and gives computable bounds.  
- Arguments against acceptance: experimental limitations, conservative bounds, and potential sensitivity to assumptions.  
- Revision recommendations: expand experiments and datasets, compare to other stability measures, investigate tightening bounds, analyze assumption sensitivity, streamline notation, and elaborate practical guidance.
