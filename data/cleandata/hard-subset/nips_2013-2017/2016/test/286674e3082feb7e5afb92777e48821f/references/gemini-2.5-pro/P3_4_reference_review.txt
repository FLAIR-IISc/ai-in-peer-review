### Summary

This paper introduces a novel and important framework for providing theoretical guarantees for graph clustering algorithms without making assumptions about the data's generative process. The core idea is based on stability: if a clustering `C` provides a good fit to the observed graph `G` under a given model class (e.g., SBM, PFM), then any other clustering `C'` that also fits the data well must be a small perturbation of `C`. This provides a form of model-free validation for the clustering `C`. The authors instantiate this general framework for two prominent block models: the Preference Frame Model (PFM) and the Stochastic Block Model (SBM). For each, they define a procedure to fit a model to a given clustering, a goodness-of-fit measure, and derive a theorem that bounds the distance between any two "good" clusterings. The theoretical results rely on matrix perturbation theory (specifically, the Davis-Kahan theorem) to relate the goodness-of-fit to the stability of the underlying spectral subspaces, which is a departure from common proof techniques in the SBM literature. The authors demonstrate the utility of their framework with experiments on synthetic and real-world data, showing that the proposed bounds are computable and can be used to validate a given clustering.

### Quality

The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed framework is theoretically sound, and the main results (Theorems 4 and 9) are well-supported by detailed proofs that leverage established results in matrix analysis. The connection between the goodness-of-fit `ε` and the clustering distance via subspace perturbation is elegant and convincing.

A significant strength is that the authors are careful to make their framework constructive. The quantities in their bounds (`ε`, eigengaps, etc.) are all computable from the observed graph and a candidate clustering, making the results practically applicable for cluster validation. The experiments, though limited, effectively demonstrate this point.

The paper is also honest about its limitations. The authors acknowledge that the SBM bounds are much more restrictive than the PFM bounds and are often not informative in practice. This is an important observation, highlighting the trade-off between model flexibility and the tightness of the resulting guarantees.

One minor point for improvement concerns the PFM estimation algorithm. It is a novel construction, and while its properties are stated, more intuition on its design choices would be beneficial. It seems designed specifically to facilitate the proof via spectral perturbation, and clarifying this would strengthen the reader's understanding.

### Clarity

The paper is very well-written and organized. The "Generic Theorem" concept in Section 3 serves as an excellent blueprint, making the subsequent instantiations for PFM and SBM easy to follow. The notation is standard and clearly defined. The logical flow from the high-level idea to the specific theorems and their proofs (outlined in the main text) is clear.

The background section provides the necessary context on block models and spectral graph theory. The discussion in Sections 4 and 5 effectively situates the work within the broader literature, connecting it to concepts of clusterability, resilience, and existing stability results for spectral clustering.

The only minor suggestion for improving clarity would be to provide a slightly more detailed discussion on why the SBM bound (Theorem 9) is so much weaker than the PFM bound (Theorem 4). The paper notes that SBM is "less flexible," but a more technical explanation—perhaps relating to the structure of the respective model matrices `A` vs. Laplacians `L`—would be insightful.

### Originality

The work is highly original. To my knowledge, this is the first paper to formalize this type of model-free stability guarantee for block models. The conventional approach in the SBM/DC-SBM literature is to assume the graph is sampled from the model and then prove recovery of the ground-truth clustering. This paper cleverly inverts the problem: it does not assume a ground truth but instead provides confidence in a computed clustering based on its fit to the data. This is a significant conceptual contribution.

The proof technique, relying on the Sinus Theorem to bound the distance between subspaces associated with different clusterings, is also novel in this specific context and offers a more direct, geometric alternative to the often complex combinatorial arguments used in related work. Furthermore, showing how existing model-based recovery theorems can be re-purposed to provide model-free guarantees is a clever and valuable insight.

### Significance

The significance of this work is substantial. It addresses a critical and long-standing gap between the strong theory of model-based clustering and its practical application, where generative assumptions rarely hold exactly. The proposed framework has the potential to make the theoretical results for block models much more relevant to practitioners. The ability to compute a "stability certificate" for a clustering on a real-world graph is a powerful tool.

This paper is likely to inspire follow-up work. Researchers may be motivated to:
1.  Develop tighter bounds, particularly for the SBM.
2.  Extend this model-free framework to other models (e.g., DC-SBM) or other types of data.
3.  Explore the connection between this stability notion and the generalization properties of graph-based learning algorithms.

The work advances the state of the art by providing a new paradigm for thinking about and validating graph clustering results.

### Arguments for and against acceptance:

**For:**
*   **Novelty and Significance:** Proposes a new, important, and highly relevant framework for model-free guarantees in graph clustering.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical results are well-supported, and the proof technique is elegant and novel for this problem.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The framework is constructive, with all quantities in the bounds being computable from data, enabling practical cluster validation.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow.

**Against:**
*   **Looseness of SBM Bounds:** The experiments show that the SBM guarantees are too loose to be useful in practice, which somewhat limits the immediate impact for one of the most popular block models.
*   **Minor Clarity Issues:** Some design choices (e.g., the PFM estimator) could benefit from more intuition.

### Conclusion

The arguments for acceptance strongly outweigh the arguments against. This is an excellent paper that introduces a novel, significant, and technically sound framework that bridges a critical gap in the literature on graph clustering. It is well-written, thoughtful, and likely to have a lasting impact on the field. I strongly recommend acceptance.
