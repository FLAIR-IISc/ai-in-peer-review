## Review of "Graph Clustering: Block-models and model free results"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel framework for providing theoretical guarantees for graph clustering algorithms *without* assuming the data originates from a specific block model (SBM, DC-SBM, PFM). The core idea revolves around demonstrating that if a clustering *fits* the data well (as measured by a goodness-of-fit criterion), then any other clustering that also fits well must be a small perturbation of the original.  The authors instantiate this framework for PFM and SBM, deriving model-free guarantees and highlighting the connection between model-based and model-free recovery.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The model-free approach to clustering validation is a significant contribution. Existing theoretical guarantees for graph clustering are heavily reliant on model assumptions, limiting their applicability in real-world scenarios. This paper tackles a crucial limitation.
* **Generality:** The proposed framework is general and can, in principle, be applied to various clustering algorithms and goodness-of-fit measures.
* **Theoretical Rigor:**  The paper employs sound mathematical reasoning, leveraging existing perturbation bounds (Davis-Kahan) and carefully defining distances between clusterings (Misclassification Error, weighted ME). The proofs, while relegated to the supplement, appear well-structured.
* **Connection to Existing Work:** The paper explicitly connects model-free results to established model-based recovery theorems, providing a valuable bridge between the two perspectives.  The discussion of how to improve existing bounds using their framework is insightful.
* **Experimental Validation:**  The experimental section, while brief, provides some evidence that the theoretical guarantees hold in practice, and demonstrates the applicability of the theorems.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions:** While the goal is model-free guarantees, the theorems still rely on assumptions such as a non-zero eigengap (Assumption 1) and specific goodness-of-fit conditions (Assumptions 2 & 4). The practical impact of these assumptions needs careful consideration.
* **Conservatism:** The derived bounds (particularly for the SBM) appear conservative.  The experiments show that in many cases, the conditions for the guarantees to hold are not met.  This suggests the current framework may not be sufficiently tight.
* **Complexity:** The mathematical exposition can be dense, especially in sections concerning the PFM.  Greater clarity in defining the various matrices and norms would be beneficial.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experimental evaluation could be more extensive.  Testing on a wider variety of datasets and comparing the bounds with those obtained from model-based approaches would strengthen the claims. The fact that SBM guarantees were not informative (δ > 1) is concerning and should be addressed more thoroughly.



**Addressing the Review Criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with a clear mathematical framework and careful analysis. However, the conservatism of the bounds and the dependence on certain assumptions raise concerns about the practicality of the results.  It's a complete piece of work demonstrating a proof of concept rather than a fully polished, immediately applicable algorithm.
* **Clarity:** The paper is reasonably well-written, but the mathematical notation can be overwhelming at times.  More intuitive explanations of the concepts would improve accessibility.
* **Originality:** The model-free approach is highly original and represents a significant departure from existing work in graph clustering. The connection between model-based and model-free results is a novel perspective.
* **Significance:**  The potential impact is considerable.  A framework for validating clustering results without model assumptions could have broad applications in various domains.  However, the practical significance depends on the tightness of the bounds and the applicability of the assumptions.

**Arguments Pro and Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Presents a novel and theoretically sound framework for model-free clustering validation.
* Bridges the gap between model-based and model-free approaches.
* Demonstrates the feasibility of providing guarantees without strong model assumptions.

**Con:**

* The derived bounds appear conservative and may not be practically useful in many cases.
* The assumptions required for the guarantees to hold may be restrictive.
* The experimental evaluation is limited.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of graph clustering. While the current bounds may be conservative, the proposed framework is novel, theoretically sound, and has the potential to significantly impact the way we approach clustering validation. The originality of the approach and the potential for future work outweigh the current limitations.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**  I recommend acceptance with the following revisions:

1. **Address the Conservatism:** Investigate the reasons for the conservative bounds, particularly for the SBM.  Explore potential avenues for tightening the bounds.
2. **Clarify Assumptions:**  Discuss the practical implications of the assumptions (eigengap, goodness-of-fit) in more detail.  Provide guidance on how to assess whether these assumptions are likely to hold in real-world datasets.
3. **Expand Experimental Evaluation:**  Conduct more extensive experiments on a wider range of datasets.  Compare the derived bounds with those obtained from model-based approaches. Investigate the impact of smoothing the graphs (as observed in the experiments).
4. **Improve Clarity:** Simplify the mathematical notation and provide more intuitive explanations of the key concepts.
