This paper introduces a novel framework for clustering graphs that provides theoretical guarantees *without* assuming the data originates from a specific block model. The core idea is elegant: if a clustering C fits the data well according to some model M, then any other clustering C’ that also fits the data well must be “close” to C. This is demonstrated for both the Stochastic Block Model (SBM) and Preference Frame Model (PFM), and the paper highlights a connection between model-free and model-based recovery results. The authors provide concrete theorems and clearly outline the required assumptions for their guarantees to hold.

The strengths of this work are substantial. First, the problem addressed is important. While model-based clustering has seen significant progress, the real-world applicability is limited by the often-unrealistic assumption of a known generating model. This work offers a path towards more robust and generally applicable clustering guarantees. Second, the technical approach is innovative and well-developed. The “generic theorem” provides a clear blueprint for extending these results to other models. The proofs, while detailed and relegated to the supplement, appear sound (although a thorough verification would require substantial effort). Third, the connection between model-free and model-based results is a valuable insight. The demonstration of how existing model-based theorems can be leveraged to derive model-free guarantees is particularly compelling. Finally, the experimental validation, while not extensive, provides some initial evidence for the practicality of the approach. The discussion section is also well-written, acknowledging limitations and outlining potential future directions.

However, the paper also has weaknesses. The notion of "closeness" between clusterings (using the Misclassification Error) is standard, but the practical implications of the bounds derived are not always clear. The conditions required to satisfy the theorems (particularly regarding the eigengap and the goodness of fit) might be restrictive in practice.  The experimental section, while helpful, could be significantly expanded. The choice of datasets feels somewhat limited, and more thorough comparisons to existing clustering algorithms (beyond just demonstrating stability) would be beneficial.  The notation is dense and could be improved for readability. For example, the numerous subscripts and superscripts can be challenging to follow.  While the authors mention potential improvements and extensions (e.g., Frobenius norm, non-spectral thresholds), these are only briefly discussed. A deeper exploration of these avenues would strengthen the paper. Moreover, the paper extensively cites recent work, but lacks context for seminal papers in spectral clustering and graph perturbation theory.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and important problem: provides a framework for model-free clustering guarantees.
*   Innovative technical approach: the “generic theorem” and its instantiations for SBM and PFM are well-developed.
*   Clear theoretical results: theorems are clearly stated and accompanied by concrete conditions.
*   Connection to existing work: demonstrates how model-based theorems can be used to derive model-free guarantees.
*   Initial experimental validation: supports the feasibility of the approach.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   Restrictive conditions: the assumptions required for the theorems to hold may be limiting in practice.
*   Limited experimental evaluation: the experiments are not extensive enough to fully assess the practical impact.
*   Dense notation: the notation can be difficult to follow.
*   Lack of thorough exploration of extensions: potential improvements are only briefly discussed.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and important contribution. The theoretical framework is well-developed and the results are compelling. While the restrictive conditions and limited experimental evaluation are concerns, the potential impact of this work is significant. I believe the paper is currently borderline. I lean towards **reject** with a strong encouragement to revise and resubmit. The authors should focus on: 1) expanding the experimental evaluation with more diverse datasets and comparisons to existing algorithms, 2) exploring the practical implications of the derived bounds, 3) providing a more detailed discussion of potential extensions and limitations, and 4) improving the readability of the notation. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its likelihood of acceptance.
