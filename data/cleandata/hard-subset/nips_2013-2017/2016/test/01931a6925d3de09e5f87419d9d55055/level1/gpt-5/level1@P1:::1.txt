Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), a parametric density on RD that generalizes the multivariate normal by replacing the Euclidean (Mahalanobis) distance with a geodesic distance induced by a learned, smoothly varying Riemannian metric. The metric is data-adaptive: at each x the metric tensor is the inverse of a locally weighted (diagonal) covariance (Gaussian kernel with bandwidth σ and regularizer ρ), which makes geodesics “follow” high-density regions (Hauberg et al., NIPS’12). Given this metric, the authors adopt the Riemannian normal distribution (Pennec, 2006), which is maximum entropy for fixed mean/covariance on a fixed manifold, and derive a maximum-likelihood procedure for the mean and covariance. A key algorithmic contribution is a Monte Carlo approximation of the normalization constant by integrating over the tangent space at the mean using samples from N(0, Σ) and evaluating Expµ(v) via geodesic ODEs. They further extend LAND to mixtures and provide an EM algorithm. Experiments on synthetic nonlinear manifolds and on EEG sleep-stage features show improved density modeling and clustering compared to Gaussian mixtures and to mixtures based on intrinsic least-squares (Karcher) estimators. Related work on statistics on known manifolds (e.g., Fletcher & Zhang, NIPS’13; Simo-Serra et al., BMVC’12) is discussed, as well as manifold learning (Isomap, LLE, Laplacian eigenmaps).

Strengths
- Conceptually clear and appealing link between manifold learning (via a learned Riemannian metric) and Riemannian statistics; the resulting model is a natural “manifold-aware Gaussian.”
- The maximum-likelihood estimation is nontrivial due to the normalization constant; the tangent-space Monte Carlo scheme with Exp maps is computationally sensible (IVPs rather than BVPs).
- Extension to mixtures with a consistent probabilistic EM (including normalization constants per component) addresses a gap in some prior Riemannian mixture works.
- Empirical results indicate LAND captures non-elliptical structure that GMMs miss; the synthetic examples are illustrative; the EEG clustering application is relevant and shows measurable gains.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Change-of-variables/measure in the normalization integral: The derivation C(µ, Σ) = ∫Tµ √|M(Expµ(v))| exp(−1/2 vTΣ−1v) dv appears to omit the determinant of the Jacobian of Expµ. In normal coordinates, dvol = √|g(v)| dv where g(v) is the metric in those coordinates; in general √|g(v)| = |det d Expµ(v)| √|M(Expµ(v))|. As written, the estimator uses m(µ, v) = √|M(Expµ(v))| and drops |det d Expµ(v)|, which is not generally 1 except in flat spaces. This is a significant theoretical issue affecting the correctness of the normalization constant, gradients, and hence ML estimates. If an approximation is intended (e.g., local flatness), it should be stated and justified; otherwise the Jacobian should be included or approximated.
- Gradient of µ: Equation (12) for ∇µφ includes a term proportional to E[m(µ, v) v], but v ~ N(0, Σ) does not depend on µ. Without the Jacobian term or explicit differentiation of m(µ, v) through Expµ and M(·), it is unclear how the v term arises. A careful derivation is needed; as presented, the gradient seems inconsistent with the stated form of C(µ, Σ).
- Computational cost and scalability: Each evaluation requires S geodesic IVPs (for Expµ) and multiple Logµ(xn) evaluations (BVPs) per iteration. The paper notes the Log map is a bottleneck, but runtime, scaling with N, D, and S, and practical wall-clock comparisons to GMMs are not reported. The diagonal metric alleviates some costs but limits expressiveness.
- Sensitivity and selection of σ, ρ: Performance depends critically on σ. For EEG, the paper reports both a fixed σ and a “best over a grid,” but the selection protocol (training/validation split) is not clearly described; using the best over a range risks optimistic bias if test labels are used. A principled selection procedure would strengthen conclusions.
- Invariance and robustness: The learned metric is not invariant to linear transformations; consequences in practice are not analyzed. Full (non-diagonal) local covariances or metric mixtures (as noted by the authors) might mitigate this but are not explored.
- Comparisons: Baselines are limited to GMMs and LS-based Riemannian mixtures. Given the density-estimation goal in RD, stronger baselines circa 2015–2016 (e.g., kernel density estimators with adaptive bandwidths; normalizing flows) would better position the contribution. For clustering, spectral methods that exploit manifold structure are relevant comparisons.

Quality
- The overall idea and algorithm are interesting, and synthetic/real results are promising. However, the theoretical derivation of the normalization constant and µ-gradient appears incomplete/incorrect unless an approximation is explicitly assumed. Experimental methodology lacks runtime reporting and more rigorous hyperparameter selection. These issues need clarification.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written and organized. Key geometric notions are introduced succinctly. Important algorithmic details are deferred to the supplement (e.g., EM details, step sizes, initialization, convergence), and some equations in the main text are terse; adding a brief complexity discussion and a clearer statement about the measure transformation would improve clarity.

Originality
- Novel combination: learned Riemannian metric + Riemannian normal with ML estimation and mixtures. While each building block exists (Pennec; Hauberg et al.; Riemannian mixtures on known manifolds), applying this to a learned metric with a practical estimator for C(µ, Σ) is original for NIPS 2016.

Significance
- If the theoretical issues are resolved, LAND could be a useful primitive for manifold-aware generative modeling and clustering, with potential to extend many Gaussian-based methods to learned geometries. Practical impact depends on computational efficiency and robust hyperparameter selection.

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptually elegant, bridges two active areas (manifold learning and Riemannian statistics).
- Provides a general-purpose distribution and an ML/EM inference procedure.
- Demonstrates empirical benefits on nonlinear structures and real EEG data.

Arguments against acceptance
- Apparent omission of the Exp-map Jacobian in the normalization integral; unclear µ-gradient derivation.
- Limited baselines and lack of runtime/scalability analysis.
- Sensitivity to σ and potential optimistic evaluation in EEG if σ is chosen post hoc.
- Restriction to diagonal local covariances and lack of invariance analysis.

Questions for the authors
1. Please clarify the measure change from M to Tµ. Does your estimator include or approximate |det d Expµ(v)|? If not, under what assumptions is it negligible?
2. Provide a full derivation of Eq. (12). How does the term E[m(µ, v) v] arise when v ~ N(0, Σ) is independent of µ?
3. What are the computational costs per iteration (number of IVPs/BVPs), and wall-clock times vs. GMMs? How do costs scale with D and N?
4. How were σ and ρ selected in the EEG experiments? Was a validation set used without peeking at labels?
5. How sensitive are results to S (number of Monte Carlo samples), and can variance reduction (common random numbers, control variates) help?

Suggestions
- Fix or justify the normalization constant estimation (include Jacobian term or provide an approximation analysis), and revise gradients accordingly.
- Report ablations: effect of σ, S, and diagonal vs. fuller local metrics; provide runtime/scaling.
- Add stronger baselines (e.g., adaptive KDE; a simple flow) and a manifold-aware clustering baseline.
- Discuss/experiment with metric mixtures or learned σ.

Overall: Interesting and potentially impactful idea with promising results, but current theoretical and experimental gaps need addressing for a strong NIPS paper.
