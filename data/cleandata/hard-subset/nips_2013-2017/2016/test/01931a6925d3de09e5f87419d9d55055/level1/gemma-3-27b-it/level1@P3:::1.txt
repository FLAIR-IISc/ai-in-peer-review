This paper introduces a Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND) by replacing the Euclidean metric of the standard normal distribution with a locally adaptive, smoothly changing (Riemannian) metric. The authors motivate this by arguing that the standard normal's linearity is inadequate for data lying near nonlinear manifolds. They present a maximum likelihood estimation algorithm based on gradient descent and Monte Carlo integration, extending it to mixture models with an EM algorithm, and demonstrate its effectiveness on synthetic and EEG data.

The core idea is well-motivated and builds upon established concepts in Riemannian geometry and manifold learning. The authors correctly identify the limitations of the standard normal distribution when applied to data residing on manifolds and propose a principled approach to address this by adapting the underlying metric. Bridging Riemannian statistics with manifold learning is a valuable contribution. The use of a smoothly changing metric, as opposed to discrete geodesic distances (like in Isomap), is crucial for a generative model.  The Monte Carlo integration scheme for normalization and the gradient-based MLE algorithm appear well-considered, addressing a key computational challenge. The extension to mixtures further enhances the model’s applicability.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The LAND represents a novel generalization of the normal distribution, incorporating a learned Riemannian metric. While Riemannian normal distributions exist (Pennec, 2015), applying this within a manifold learning framework and developing a practical estimation procedure is new.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The work is firmly grounded in Riemannian geometry, providing a strong mathematical foundation. The connection to maximum entropy distributions is a significant theoretical point.
* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed algorithm appears technically sound, combining gradient descent with Monte Carlo integration to address the non-trivial normalization constant calculation. The derivation of the gradient updates, although briefly mentioned, seems correct.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on both synthetic and real (EEG) data demonstrate the effectiveness of the LAND compared to standard GMMs. The improvements observed in the sleep stage classification task are particularly encouraging.
* **Clarity of Presentation:** While dense, the paper is generally well-written and clearly presents the mathematical concepts and algorithmic details. Figures are helpful, though some (e.g., Fig. 2) could benefit from more detailed explanation.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** The computational cost of solving the ODEs to compute geodesics (and therefore the log map) remains a significant concern, especially for high-dimensional data. The paper acknowledges this and mentions using IVPs for speed, but a more detailed analysis of computational scaling would be beneficial.  The choice of σ impacts performance, and an automated method for its selection would be valuable.
* **Metric Learning Details:** The chosen metric (inverse local covariance with a Gaussian kernel) is somewhat arbitrary. While justified by simplicity and computational efficiency, the paper doesn't thoroughly explore alternative metric learning approaches or discuss their potential impact.  The metric's lack of invariance to linear transformations is mentioned but not fully addressed.
* **Limited Comparison:**  The comparison to existing manifold learning techniques (e.g., Isomap, LLE) is limited. Showing how LAND performs relative to these methods, particularly in terms of downstream task performance, would strengthen the paper.  Comparing against more recent advancements in Riemannian machine learning would also be helpful.
* **Sensitivity to Initialization:** The performance of gradient-based optimization algorithms can be sensitive to initialization. The paper mentions initialization in the supplements but lacks a detailed discussion of its impact and best practices.
* **Overstated Claims:** The phrasing “LAND learns faster the underlying true distribution” (related to Fig. 4) is a strong claim that isn't fully substantiated by the presented results. It would be more accurate to say LAND achieves better likelihood.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The LAND introduces a genuinely novel and theoretically sound generalization of the normal distribution.
* The proposed estimation algorithm is technically sophisticated and addresses a challenging problem.
* The empirical results demonstrate the potential of the LAND for modeling complex data distributions.
* The work bridges two important fields (Riemannian geometry and manifold learning).

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The computational complexity remains a significant limitation, potentially hindering scalability to high-dimensional datasets.
* The choice of metric is somewhat arbitrary, and a more thorough exploration of alternative metrics could be beneficial.
* The comparison to other manifold learning and Riemannian machine learning techniques is limited.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field. The idea of adapting the normal distribution to a learned Riemannian manifold is insightful and well-executed. While there are some limitations, the strengths of the work outweigh the weaknesses. The paper is of high quality, clarity, and originality, and the results are significant.

**Recommendation:** Accept.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should:

1.  Provide a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity and scaling of the algorithm.
2.  Consider exploring alternative metric learning approaches and discussing their potential impact.
3.  Expand the comparison to include more relevant manifold learning and Riemannian machine learning techniques.
4.  Elaborate on the initialization procedure and its impact on performance.
5.  Tone down some of the stronger claims made in the results section.
