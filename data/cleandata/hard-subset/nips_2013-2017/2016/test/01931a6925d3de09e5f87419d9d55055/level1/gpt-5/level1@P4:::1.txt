Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), obtained by replacing the Euclidean Mahalanobis distance in a multivariate normal with a geodesic distance induced by a learned, smoothly varying Riemannian metric M(x). The metric is constructed nonparametrically from data via local (diagonal) variances weighted by a Gaussian kernel, making M(x) large in low-density regions and small near data, so geodesics favor high-density regions (cf. Hauberg et al. on metric learning). Given this manifold, the paper adopts Pennec’s Riemannian normal [15] p(x|µ,Σ) ∝ exp(−1/2 ⟨Logµ(x), Σ−1 Logµ(x)⟩), which is maximum entropy for fixed mean/covariance on a fixed manifold. The key contributions are: (i) estimating the normalization constant C(µ,Σ) by Monte Carlo over the tangent space using samples v ∼ N(0,Σ) and evaluating Expµ(v); (ii) gradient-based maximum likelihood for µ and Σ (with Σ−1 factorized as AᵀA); and (iii) an EM algorithm for mixtures of LANDs that includes component-dependent normalization constants (addressing an inconsistency in some prior manifold mixture works). Experiments on synthetic curved data and EEG-derived features show improved density modeling and clustering relative to GMMs and Riemannian least-squares estimates. The work bridges manifold learning (e.g., Isomap, LLE, Laplacian Eigenmaps) and Riemannian statistics (e.g., Pennec [15], Zhang & Fletcher [23]) by learning the metric rather than assuming a known manifold.

Quality (technical soundness):
- Positive: The construction of a nonparametric Riemannian metric that biases geodesics toward high-density regions is reasonable, and the use of the Riemannian normal on the learned manifold is principled. The mixture extension with proper normalization is sound in formulation. The proposed MC estimator for C(µ,Σ) is computationally plausible because it relies on exponential maps (IVPs) rather than logarithm maps (BVPs), and the block coordinate descent with steepest descent direction for µ is consistent with standard manifold optimization heuristics.
- Concerns:
  1) Change-of-variables and normalization constant: The paper rewrites the normalization constant as an integral over TµM with integrand m(µ,v) = sqrt|M(Expµ(v))|, i.e., C = ∫ m(µ,v) exp(−1/2 vᵀΣ−1v) dv. In general, when pulling back the Riemannian volume form via Expµ, the volume element in normal coordinates involves the pullback metric g(v) = (DExpµ(v))ᵀ M(Expµ(v)) DExpµ(v), so the determinant includes both sqrt|M(Expµ(v))| and |det DExpµ(v)|. The presented expression appears to omit the Jacobian factor associated with DExpµ(v). Unless justified (e.g., an approximation, or a proof that the determinant of DExp is 1 for the chosen metric—which does not generally hold), the estimator for C may be biased. This is a central step and requires clarification and/or correction.
  2) Gradient w.r.t. µ of log C: Eq. (12) yields an expectation term proportional to E[m(µ,v) v] under v ∼ N(0,Σ). Since the sampling distribution does not depend on µ, ∂µ E[m(µ,v)] = E[∂µ m(µ,v)], not E[m v]. The derivation leading to the “vs” term is non-obvious and is deferred to the supplement. A clear, rigorous derivation is needed; otherwise, the gradient estimator may be incorrect. Relatedly, the dependence of m(µ,v) on µ via Expµ(v) is nontrivial.
  3) Metric learning restricts M(x) to be diagonal and is not invariant to linear transformations; while acknowledged, this can limit modeling fidelity and introduces sensitivity to preprocessing.
  4) Computational complexity and scalability: Each evaluation of m requires solving a geodesic IVP; the method uses S = 3000 samples per iteration and per component. The paper lacks quantitative runtime/memory analysis, complexity in terms of N, D, S, and convergence diagnostics. The approach may be demanding in higher D, as the authors note.
  5) Hyperparameter selection: The kernel bandwidth σ critically affects M(x). The paper sets σ heuristically or by grid search (per-subject best), which can bias comparisons. No automatic selection or principled criterion is provided.
  6) Theoretical properties: No guarantees are provided on consistency of MLE under the learned metric, bias from MC approximation, or robustness to metric misspecification.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written and organized, with a clear statement of goals, background on Riemannian geometry, and algorithmic steps. However:
- The change-of-variables argument and the exact form of the volume element in tangent-space integration require a careful derivation in the main text or a precise pointer to the supplement; omitting the DExpµ Jacobian needs explicit justification.
- The derivations of gradients (Eq. 12–13) are central; a concise sketch in the main paper would help verify correctness.
- Provide details on ODE solver settings, step sizes αµ, αA selection, stopping criteria, and variance/bias of the MC estimate.
- Explicitly discuss computational cost and scaling.

Originality:
The idea of Riemannian normals is known [15, 23], and manifold embeddings are well studied. The main novelty lies in learning a nonparametric Riemannian metric from data and performing maximum likelihood estimation of a parametric distribution (and its mixture) on the resulting manifold, including estimation of normalization constants via tangent-space MC. Addressing normalization in the mixture setting is also a useful contribution versus prior work (e.g., [19]). Within NeurIPS, this appears to be a novel synthesis.

Significance:
If technically sound, the approach offers a flexible, principled alternative to GMMs for data concentrated near nonlinear structures, with potential impact on generative modeling, clustering, and extensions of classical models (e.g., LDA, PCA) to learned manifolds. The empirical results are promising, though limited in scope. The reliance on heavy computation and hyperparameter tuning may hinder immediate adoption without further engineering.

Pros:
- Principled use of Riemannian normal on a learned metric; addresses mean-in-low-density issue.
- Proper treatment of normalization in mixtures.
- Empirically captures non-ellipsoidal structure better than GMMs.
- Clear algorithmic framework combining geodesics with MC integration.

Cons:
- Apparent omission of the Exp map Jacobian in the normalization integral; needs correction/justification.
- Gradient expression for µ appears insufficiently justified.
- Metric restricted to diagonal and not invariant; σ selection ad hoc.
- Computational cost and scaling not quantified; reliance on large S and ODE solves.
- Limited baselines and ablation analyses.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide a rigorous derivation of the tangent-space integral, explicitly including or justifying the treatment of |det DExpµ(v)|.
- Derive and validate the gradients for µ and A; consider variance-reduction (e.g., control variates) and report estimator variance.
- Add runtime/scaling experiments and sensitivity to S, σ, and ρ; consider automatic σ selection (e.g., based on local k-NN distances or cross-validation).
- Evaluate against additional baselines (e.g., diffusion-distance GMMs, GTM, kernel density on graphs) and include ablations (full vs diagonal local metrics).
- Report robustness to linear preprocessing and provide reproducibility details.

Overall, the paper presents an interesting and potentially impactful idea, but key technical justifications for the normalization and gradients should be strengthened to ensure correctness.
