**Summary of the Main Ideas**

The paper introduces a locally adaptive normal distribution (LAND) that generalizes the normal distribution to the "manifold" setting, where data is assumed to lie near a potentially low-dimensional manifold embedded in R^D. The LAND is parametric, depending only on a mean and a covariance, and is the maximum entropy distribution under a locally adaptive Riemannian metric. The authors develop a maximum likelihood algorithm to infer the distribution parameters, which relies on a combination of gradient descent and Monte Carlo integration. They also extend the LAND to mixture models and provide the corresponding EM algorithm.

**Relation to Previous Work**

The paper builds upon previous work on Riemannian normal distributions, such as Pennec [15] and Zhang and Fletcher [23]. However, the authors adapt the distribution to the "manifold learning" setting by constructing a Riemannian metric that adapts to the data. This is a novel contribution that distinguishes their work from previous approaches.

**Quality**

The paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a thorough derivation of the LAND and its properties. The maximum likelihood algorithm is well-motivated, and the use of Monte Carlo integration is a good choice for estimating the normalization constant. The experiments on synthetic and real data demonstrate the effectiveness of the LAND in capturing non-linear structures and outperforming standard Gaussian mixture models.

**Clarity**

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the technical details. The notation is consistent, and the figures are helpful in illustrating the concepts. However, some of the sections, such as the derivation of the intrinsic least squares estimators, could be improved with more explanations and examples.

**Originality**

The paper presents a novel contribution to the field of machine learning and statistics. The idea of adapting the normal distribution to the "manifold" setting is original and has the potential to impact various applications, such as clustering, dimensionality reduction, and generative modeling.

**Significance**
