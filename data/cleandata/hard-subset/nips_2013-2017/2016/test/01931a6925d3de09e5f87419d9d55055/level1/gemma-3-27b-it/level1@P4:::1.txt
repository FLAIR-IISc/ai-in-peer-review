The paper proposes a locally adaptive normal distribution (LAND) by replacing the Euclidean metric of the standard normal distribution with a locally adaptive, smoothly changing Riemannian metric. The metric is learned from data, favoring regions of high local density. The authors develop a maximum likelihood estimation algorithm, extend the LAND to mixture models, and demonstrate its performance on synthetic and EEG data.

This work builds upon Riemannian geometry and manifold learning. It connects statistics on Riemannian manifolds (Pennec, 2013) with manifold learning techniques (Isomap, Roweis & Saul, 2000; Hauberg et al., 2017).  It also relates to recent work on Riemannian mixture models (Simo-Serra et al., 2014). The key novelty lies in combining a learned, non-parametric Riemannian metric with a parametric normal distribution to create a flexible, locally adaptive model.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of a learned Riemannian metric with a normal distribution is a novel approach. The idea of adapting the metric to the data density is intuitive and promising.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The work is grounded in Riemannian geometry, providing a strong mathematical framework. The authors correctly identify the LAND as the maximum entropy distribution under the given metric.
*   **Algorithmic Contribution:** The development of a maximum likelihood estimation algorithm, incorporating Monte Carlo integration and gradient descent, is a significant contribution. The EM algorithm for mixtures of LANDs is also valuable.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on both synthetic and real (EEG) data demonstrate the effectiveness of the LAND compared to standard Gaussian mixture models. The results suggest the LAND can better capture non-ellipsoidal data distributions.
*   **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the underlying concepts and methodology. The notation is mostly consistent, and the explanations of Riemannian geometry are accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Cost:** The reliance on solving ordinary differential equations (ODEs) to compute geodesics and the use of Monte Carlo integration can be computationally expensive, potentially limiting scalability to high-dimensional data. While the authors mention optimizations, a more thorough analysis of computational complexity is needed.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance of the LAND depends on the choice of the kernel bandwidth parameter (σ). The paper does not provide a systematic method for selecting this parameter and relies on manual tuning in the experiments.  The sensitivity analysis in the EEG experiments is limited.
*   **Metric Invariance:** The proposed metric is not invariant to linear transformations, which could be a limitation in certain applications. This is acknowledged by the authors, but the implications are not fully explored.
*   **Comparison to Existing Methods:** The comparison to other manifold learning methods (e.g., UMAP, t-SNE) is missing.  These methods are often used for visualization and dimensionality reduction and could provide a more comprehensive benchmark.
*   **Limited Theoretical Analysis of Convergence:** The paper lacks a rigorous theoretical analysis of the convergence properties of the maximum likelihood estimation algorithm.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to density estimation.
*   The proposed LAND model offers a flexible alternative to the standard normal distribution, particularly for data lying on nonlinear manifolds.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the LAND on both synthetic and real datasets.
*   The work has the potential to impact various machine learning applications that rely on density estimation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The computational cost of the LAND algorithm could be a barrier to its adoption.
*   The sensitivity to the kernel bandwidth parameter requires careful tuning and may limit its robustness.
*   The lack of comparison to state-of-the-art manifold learning methods weakens the empirical evaluation.
*   The absence of a theoretical analysis of the algorithm's convergence properties is a concern.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of density estimation. The LAND model is a novel and promising approach that has the potential to address limitations of the standard normal distribution. While the computational cost and parameter sensitivity are concerns, the strengths of the paper outweigh these weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The authors should address the following points:

1.  Provide a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of the LAND algorithm.
2.  Investigate methods for automatically selecting the kernel bandwidth parameter (σ).
3.  Compare the LAND to other relevant manifold learning methods (e.g., UMAP, t-SNE).
4.  Discuss potential extensions of the work, such as exploring different metric learning techniques or incorporating prior knowledge about the manifold structure.
