**Summary of the Main Ideas**

The paper proposes a Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND) that generalizes the normal distribution to the "manifold" setting. The LAND is a parametric distribution that depends on a mean and a covariance, and is the maximum entropy distribution under a given metric. The underlying metric is non-parametric and is learned from the data. The authors develop a maximum likelihood algorithm to infer the distribution parameters, which relies on a combination of gradient descent and Monte Carlo integration. They also extend the LAND to mixture models and provide the corresponding EM algorithm.

**Relation to Previous Work**

The paper builds on previous work on Riemannian normal distributions, manifold learning, and probabilistic PCA. The authors adapt the Riemannian normal distribution to the "manifold learning" setting by constructing a Riemannian metric that adapts to the data. This is their overarching contribution. The paper also relates to previous work on dimensionality reduction, clustering, and semi-supervised learning.

**Strengths**

* The paper proposes a novel and interesting distribution that adapts to the local properties of the data.
* The authors develop a maximum likelihood algorithm to infer the distribution parameters, which is a significant contribution.
* The paper provides a thorough evaluation of the LAND on both synthetic and real data, including EEG measurements of human sleep.
* The authors demonstrate that the LAND outperforms a standard Gaussian mixture model in capturing the local properties of the data.

**Weaknesses**

* The paper assumes that the data lies near a low-dimensional manifold embedded in high-dimensional space, which may not always be the case.
* The authors use a local covariance matrix to represent the local structure of the data, which may not be the best choice for all datasets.
* The paper does not provide a thorough analysis of the computational complexity of the algorithm, which may be a concern for large datasets.
* The authors do not discuss the limitations of the LAND in terms of its ability to capture complex distributions.

**Originality**

* The paper proposes a novel distribution that adapts to the local properties of the data, which is a significant contribution to the field of machine learning.
* The authors develop a maximum likelihood algorithm to infer the distribution parameters, which is a new contribution.

**Significance**
