This paper proposes the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), which generalizes the multivariate normal by replacing the Euclidean/Mahalanobis metric with a smoothly varying, data-adaptive Riemannian metric. The metric is constructed nonparametrically from local (diagonal) kernel-weighted covariances, making geodesics prefer high-density regions. Building on Pennec’s Riemannian normal [15], the authors develop maximum-likelihood (ML) estimation for the mean and covariance by: (i) estimating the intractable normalization constant via Monte Carlo on the tangent space using exponential maps; (ii) block-coordinate updates with gradients in µ and a factorization for Σ; and (iii) an EM algorithm for mixtures that correctly accounts for component-wise normalization constants. Experiments on curved synthetic mixtures and EEG sleep-stage features show improved fit and clustering over GMMs and over least-squares Riemannian estimators. The work connects statistics on known manifolds [15, 23] with learned probabilistic geometries/manifolds [9, 22] and addresses deficiencies in prior mixture models on manifolds that ignored normalization [19].

Quality: The formulation is technically sound and the ML procedure is principled. The tangent-space Monte Carlo estimator for C(µ, Σ) is elegant and computationally motivated (exponential maps via IVPs rather than logarithm maps via BVPs). However, several choices limit robustness: the diagonal local metric (no local anisotropy), lack of affine invariance, sensitivity to the kernel bandwidth σ and to the number of Monte Carlo samples S, and nonconvex optimization without convergence guarantees. Computational complexity and wall-clock scaling (in N, D, S, K) are not analyzed; reproducibility would benefit from this and from code. Real-data evaluation is confined to one application without held-out likelihoods.

Clarity: The paper is generally well organized and readable; derivations and algorithmic details are deferred to the supplement appropriately. Please add guidance for σ selection, complexity per iteration, and ablations (σ, S).

Originality: The combination of a learned Riemannian metric with a maximum-entropy normal and a tractable ML/EM scheme appears novel relative to [15, 19, 23], and the normalization strategy is a neat contribution.

Significance: The approach is broadly relevant for generative modeling and clustering on curved data. Impact may be constrained by scalability and hyperparameter sensitivity, but the idea is promising and extensible.

Pros for acceptance:
- Principled, novel bridge between manifold learning and generative modeling.
- Correct treatment of normalization; solid ML and EM procedures.
- Empirical gains on nonlinear densities; interpretable geometry.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited scalability analysis; diagonal metric and σ sensitivity.
- Modest real-data validation; missing held-out likelihoods.
- No comparison to stronger baselines (e.g., manifold GMMs with learned metrics [22]).
