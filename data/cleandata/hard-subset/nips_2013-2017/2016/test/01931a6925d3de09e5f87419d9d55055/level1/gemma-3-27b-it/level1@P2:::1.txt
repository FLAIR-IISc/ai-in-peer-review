The paper proposes a Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND) by replacing the Euclidean metric of the standard normal distribution with a locally adaptive Riemannian metric learned from the data. This allows the distribution to better model data lying on potentially nonlinear manifolds. The authors develop a maximum likelihood estimation algorithm, extend it to mixture models, and demonstrate its effectiveness on synthetic and EEG data.

The core idea is novel and addresses a significant limitation of the standard normal distribution – its inability to adequately model data residing on nonlinear manifolds. The connection between Riemannian geometry and manifold learning is well-motivated and clearly articulated. The approach of learning a metric that favors regions of high density is sensible and aligned with prior work (Hauberg et al. [9]). The development of the maximum likelihood algorithm, including the Monte Carlo integration scheme and the EM algorithm for mixtures, represents a substantial technical contribution. The experimental results on both synthetic and real data (EEG measurements) provide compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the LAND compared to standard Gaussian Mixture Models. The comparison to a least-squares-based LAND variant further clarifies the benefits of the maximum likelihood approach. The clarity of the paper is generally good, with a good balance between mathematical formalism and intuitive explanations. The notation is relatively standard and well-defined.

However, there are several weaknesses. The computational complexity of evaluating geodesics (solving ODEs) is a potential bottleneck, especially in higher dimensions. While the authors address this with Monte Carlo integration and tangent space calculations, the scalability to very high-dimensional data is not fully demonstrated. The choice of the Gaussian kernel for defining locality (parameter σ) seems somewhat arbitrary. While explored in the sleep stage experiment, a more systematic exploration of different kernel choices or adaptive bandwidth selection methods could be beneficial. The paper acknowledges the non-invariance of the metric to linear transformations, which is a limitation. This isn't necessarily a fatal flaw, but it should be clearly stated when interpreting results. While the paper references related work, it doesn’t deeply engage with recent advances in normalizing flows or other flexible density estimation techniques. Briefly discussing how the LAND compares to these modern methods in terms of expressiveness or computational cost could strengthen the contribution. There is a brief discussion of related work but is not extensive and some relevant papers are missed. For example, the connections to optimal transport could be stronger.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of adapting the metric of the normal distribution to the data manifold is a novel and valuable contribution.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The derivation of the maximum likelihood estimation algorithm and the extension to mixture models are technically sound and well-justified.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on both synthetic and real data demonstrate the effectiveness of the LAND.
*   **Theoretical Grounding:** The connection to Riemannian geometry and maximum entropy principles provides a strong theoretical foundation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Scalability:** The computational cost of geodesic calculations could limit the scalability to high-dimensional data.
*   **Kernel Choice:** The choice of the Gaussian kernel is somewhat arbitrary and could benefit from further investigation.
*   **Limited Comparison:** The comparison to other density estimation techniques could be more comprehensive.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field. The novelty of the approach, the technical soundness of the method, and the compelling empirical results outweigh the identified weaknesses. The paper is well-written and clearly presents a novel and valuable idea. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, I suggest the authors elaborate on the scalability limitations, explore alternative kernel choices, and briefly discuss the relationship to more recent density estimation techniques like normalizing flows.
