Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), which replaces the Euclidean/Mahalanobis distance in a Gaussian with a geodesic distance induced by a learned, smoothly varying (Riemannian) metric. The metric is nonparametric and diagonal: at each point it is the inverse of a local covariance estimated via Gaussian kernel weights (with regularization ρ). Given this metric, the authors instantiate the Riemannian normal distribution of Pennec [15] on the learned manifold M, and develop practical maximum-likelihood estimation for mean and covariance, along with a mixture model and EM-like procedure. A key technical component is estimating the normalization constant C(µ,Σ) by Monte Carlo in the tangent space TµM using exponential maps and the induced measure √|M(Expµ(v))|. Optimization proceeds by alternating updates for µ (via steepest descent on the manifold) and Σ (via a factorization Σ−1 = AᵀA). Experiments show improved density modeling and clustering on synthetic data and on EEG sleep features when compared to GMMs and to least-squares intrinsic estimators.

This work bridges two existing lines: (i) Riemannian statistics on known manifolds (e.g., Pennec’s normal [15], Riemannian PPCA [23], Riemannian mixtures [19, 20]), and (ii) manifold learning/metric learning (Isomap [21], Hauberg et al. [9], Tosi et al. [22]). The novelty is to learn a data-adaptive Riemannian metric and use it to define a normalized, maximum-entropy (conditional on the metric) parametric distribution and mixture model, with an ML training algorithm that accounts for normalization constants across components.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The construction of the metric, geodesics, and the induced density are consistent with Riemannian geometry. The change-of-variables to integrate in TµM and the MC estimator C(µ,Σ) = Z EN(0,Σ)[m(µ,v)] are correct and computationally attractive (exponential maps via IVPs vs logarithm maps via BVPs). The gradient expressions for µ and A are plausible (details delegated to supplements). Treating Σ via AᵀA is standard.
- Assumptions/limitations: The metric is diagonal and not invariant under linear transforms; this can bias modeling unless data is prewhitened. The kernel width σ and regularizer ρ critically affect smoothness and curvature; selection is ad hoc (“as small as possible... geodesics can be computed”) and tuned per subject in EEG, which risks overfitting. There is no theoretical analysis of consistency of ML estimates, convergence of the alternating optimization, or variance of the MC estimator as D grows. The scalability in high D is unclear since each MC sample requires an Exp map and determinant of M, and S=3000 may be inadequate in higher dimensions.
- Empirics: Synthetic experiments convincingly show that LAND captures curved manifolds with fewer components than GMMs and outperforms LS intrinsic estimators that overestimate covariance. The EEG clustering results show meaningful gains in F-measure, and the σ sweep improves performance further, suggesting the model captures the nonlinear structure. However, baselines are limited: no comparison to graph-geodesic models (e.g., Isomap-based distances in a GMM), spectral clustering, kernel density estimators, or other adaptive generative models. Runtime and computational cost are not reported; ablations (e.g., effect of diagonal vs full local covariance, sensitivity to S) would strengthen the claims.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and self-contained on the geometry needed (definitions of Exp/Log, measures, and geodesic ODE). The optimization algorithm is clearly stated, and the role of C(µ,Σ) is well explained. Some choices are under-specified: how σ and ρ are set systematically; numerical details and stability for solving ODEs; stopping criteria; and the computational complexity per iteration. Moving key derivations to supplements is acceptable but a brief sketch would improve readability.

Originality
The core idea—use the Riemannian normal on a learned manifold—is novel in the combination and in the practical ML algorithm with a tractable estimator of the normalization constant. Prior works either assume a known manifold [15, 19, 20, 23] or focus on learning metrics for distances/embeddings [9, 21, 22] without normalized generative models. The diagonal, kernel-smoothed metric is a pragmatic design. The mixture extension that correctly includes component-specific normalization differs from some earlier Riemannian mixture formulations [19].

Significance
If made practical and robust, LAND could be a useful drop-in replacement for Gaussians in manifold-structured data, potentially reducing the number of mixture components and improving modeling of curved clusters. The demonstrated applications (synthetic, 5D EEG features) are encouraging but limited. Broader empirical validation and scalability analysis would better support impact claims. Still, the method contributes a principled pathway for manifold-aware generative modeling that program committee members and practitioners may build upon.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually clean bridge between learned Riemannian geometry and normalized generative modeling.
- Practical ML algorithm with a clever tangent-space MC estimator and mixture EM that respects normalization.
- Clear empirical benefit in modeling curved densities and clustering vs GMMs with fewer components.
- Relevance to many NIPS topics (probabilistic modeling, manifold learning, geometry in ML).

Cons for acceptance
- Heavy reliance on a diagonal, kernel-based metric with ad hoc hyperparameter selection; lack of invariance.
- Limited baselines and domains; no runtime or scalability analysis; no theory on estimator properties or convergence.
- Sensitivity to σ and S not thoroughly quantified; reproducibility details (ODE solvers, stability) are sparse.

Suggestions and questions
- Provide a principled procedure for choosing σ (e.g., cross-validation, plug-in rules), and analyze sensitivity to σ and ρ.
- Report computational cost vs GMMs and vs graph-distance alternatives; include ablations on S and ODE tolerances.
- Consider preprocessing (whitening) to mitigate lack of invariance; discuss extension to non-diagonal local metrics.
- Compare to spectral clustering and to GMMs on geodesic distances from neighborhood graphs to strengthen claims.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers a principled and practically implementable approach to manifold-aware generative modeling with clear benefits on curved data. While empirical scope and hyperparameter handling could be stronger, the contribution is novel and relevant, and the methodology is likely to stimulate follow-up work.
