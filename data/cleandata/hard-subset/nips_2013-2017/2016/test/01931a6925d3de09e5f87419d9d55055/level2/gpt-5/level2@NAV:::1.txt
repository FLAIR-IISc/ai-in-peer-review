Summary and relation to prior work: The paper proposes the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), which replaces the Euclidean metric in a multivariate normal with a smoothly varying, data-adaptive Riemannian metric that favors high-density regions. The distribution remains parametric in mean and covariance, but is defined over a nonparametric metric; it is the maximum-entropy distribution under that metric (cf. Pennec). The authors give: (i) a tangent-space Monte Carlo estimator of the normalization constant that requires only exponential maps (IVPs) rather than logarithm maps (BVPs), (ii) gradient-based MLE for mean and covariance with a stable steepest-descent direction, and (iii) an EM algorithm for mixtures. This bridges statistics on known manifolds (e.g., Pennec; Zhang & Fletcher) with manifold learning via learned metrics (e.g., Hauberg; Isomap). Experiments on synthetic manifolds and EEG sleep features show improved fit and clustering over GMMs and LS-based Riemannian estimators.

Strengths:
- Conceptually clean generalization of Gaussians to learned Riemannian geometries; clear distinction between parametric density and nonparametric metric.
- Careful treatment of normalization under a learned metric via tangent-space integration; computationally sensible choice to use Exp maps.
- Well-motivated MLE with explicit gradients; mixture extension that correctly accounts for component-dependent normalizers (addressing gaps in prior work).
- Empirical evidence that LAND avoids intrinsic-mean pathologies and captures non-ellipsoidal structure; promising EEG clustering improvements.

Weaknesses and limitations:
- The learned metric is diagonal and not invariant to linear transforms; potential mis-specification in correlated, high-D settings.
- The metric is estimated from the same data used for density fitting; risk of overfitting is not quantified (no held-out log-likelihoods, calibration, or permutation tests).
- Theoretical assumptions behind using TµM ≅ RD in the integral (cut loci/injectivity radius) are not discussed; conditions ensuring finiteness and bias of the Monte Carlo estimator are unclear.
- Sensitivity to σ is substantial; σ is hand-selected (or grid-searched) rather than learned, and there is no model selection criterion integrated in training.
- Computational complexity and scalability are not rigorously profiled; per-iteration costs scale with S Exp-map solves and N Log-map evaluations, which may be limiting.

Quality: Generally sound, with reasonable algorithmic choices. Providing variance/bias analyses or control variates for the normalization estimator would strengthen it.

Clarity: Well written and organized; some reference entries are malformed. Include more implementation details (geodesic solvers, stopping criteria, initialization) in the main text.

Originality: Novel combination of a learned Riemannian geometry with a properly normalized Riemannian Gaussian and EM mixtures; distinct from prior work assuming known manifolds or ignoring normalization.

Significance: Useful for generative modeling on nonlinear structures; likely to influence manifold-aware density modeling and clustering.

Constructive suggestions:
- Learn σ (and potentially a full local metric) via marginal likelihood or bilevel optimization; consider mixtures of metrics.
- Report held-out log-likelihoods and timing; compare to alternative flexible densities (e.g., manifold GP-LVMs, diffusion-based distances, normalizing flows on manifolds).
- Add variance-reduction (common random numbers, control variates) and study estimator convergence vs. S.
- Discuss geometric conditions (cut loci) under which the tangent-space integral is valid.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear, principled model and algorithms; solid empirical gains; addresses a timely gap.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited theoretical and empirical analysis of estimator variance/consistency and scalability; reliance on hand-tuned σ and diagonal metrics.
