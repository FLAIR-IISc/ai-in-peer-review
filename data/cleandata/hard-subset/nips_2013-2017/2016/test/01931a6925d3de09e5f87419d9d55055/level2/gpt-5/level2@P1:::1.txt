Summary
The paper proposes the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), a parametric distribution that generalizes the multivariate normal to a learned Riemannian manifold on RD. The key idea is to retain mean and covariance as parameters, but replace the Euclidean/Mahalanobis distance in the Gaussian exponent with geodesic distance induced by a smoothly varying (nonparametric) metric M(x) constructed from data. The metric is the inverse of a local (diagonal) covariance estimated by Gaussian kernel smoothing, so geodesics tend to pass through regions of high data density. The resulting density is the Riemannian normal (Pennec, 2006) on the learned manifold, which is also maximum-entropy for a fixed mean and covariance on that manifold.

Because the normalization constant C(µ, Σ) depends on µ, Σ, and the metric curvature, the authors define a scalable Monte Carlo estimator by transforming the manifold integral to an expectation over the tangent space TµM, requiring only exponential map evaluations (IVPs) rather than more expensive logarithm maps (BVPs). They derive gradients for maximum likelihood estimation of µ and Σ and present a block coordinate descent scheme with preconditioning. They also extend LAND to mixtures and derive an EM algorithm that accounts for different normalization constants across components.

Experiments on synthetic curved data show better fit and clustering than Gaussian mixtures and than mixtures estimated via intrinsic least squares (which often overestimate covariance). On EEG sleep data (5D features from NMF), LAND mixtures align more closely with sleep stages (F-measure), and tuning the kernel width σ further improves performance.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: a clean bridge between manifold learning (learned metric) and Riemannian statistics (normal distribution with geodesic distance and induced measure). Using the tangent-space Monte Carlo for normalization is elegant and practically important.
- Sound modeling: the use of the Riemannian normal preserves maximum-entropy properties for a fixed manifold, and the objective correctly includes the normalization constant, unlike some prior manifold mixture approaches.
- Derivation and algorithm: gradients for µ and Σ are derived, with attention to conditioning and practical optimization (steepest descent in µ, factorized Σ). The tangent-space estimator avoids costly Log maps.
- Empirical evidence: clear synthetic examples where LAND contours adapt to nonlinear structure; improved likelihood and clustering; a real EEG application with sensible gains over GMMs.
- Relevance to NIPS: builds on and extends prior Riemannian statistics work (Pennec; Zhang & Fletcher) to the learned-manifold setting; relates to manifold learning (Isomap, LLE) while enabling generative modeling.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Dependence on metric design: the metric uses a diagonal local covariance with isotropic kernel. This is not invariant to linear transformations and may underfit anisotropic local structure. Some discussion appears, but empirical impact and possible remedies (e.g., local full covariances with regularization, or mixtures of local metrics) would strengthen the work.
- Hyperparameter sensitivity and selection: performance depends on σ (and ρ). In real data, σ is tuned by a grid per subject. A more systematic selection strategy (likelihood-based or cross-validation), or an approach to learn σ, would improve practicality.
- Computational cost and scalability: each evaluation of C(µ, Σ) and gradients requires S exponential maps; with S=3000 this could be expensive for larger D or K. Runtimes and scaling plots versus D, N, K, and S would clarify feasibility. The EEG features are 5D; it is unclear how well the method scales beyond ~20–50D.
- Gradient of log normalization: the gradient for µ (Eq. 12) contains a term proportional to EN[m(µ, v) v]. Since m depends on Expµ(v), a brief justification or empirical check (e.g., finite-difference validation) of the gradient estimator’s correctness and variance would be helpful. As written, the connection between ∂m/∂µ and the Monte Carlo moment m·v is not obvious.
- Evaluation breadth: additional baselines would be informative, e.g., mixtures of factor analyzers, probabilistic PCA mixtures, GPLVM-based generative models, or graph-geodesic-based models (e.g., GMM in an embedding with out-of-sample mapping). For clustering, spectral clustering or manifold regularization baselines could contextualize gains.
- Statistical interpretation: since the metric is learned from the same data used to fit the density, the resulting model is data-adaptive in both geometry and parameters. The maximum-entropy claim holds conditioned on the learned manifold, but the joint procedure could overfit. A discussion of sample-splitting or regularization to control this (and any effect on uncertainty quantification) would be valuable.

Quality
Overall technically sound with a coherent objective and plausible algorithms. The Monte Carlo estimator and gradient-based optimization are reasonable; some derivational details (especially for ∇µ log C) merit clearer justification. Experiments support key claims, though broader baselines and runtime analysis would strengthen conclusions.

Clarity
Well organized and mostly clear. The geometric preliminaries are concise and useful. It would help to:
- Expand the derivation or intuition for Eq. 12/13 (possibly move key steps from the supplement).
- Provide algorithmic complexity and runtime numbers.
- Clarify initialization and convergence behavior.

Originality
The main novelty is adapting the Riemannian normal to a learned (data-driven) manifold metric and addressing the normalization/learning challenges. Prior work considered known manifolds; here the metric is learned. The tangent-space normalization approach and mixture EM with proper normalization are valuable.

Significance
The approach is a promising step toward manifold-aware generative models that remain parametric and interpretable. If computational concerns are addressed, LAND could be a useful drop-in for Gaussian-based models in settings with curved data. The EEG application is a nice proof of concept.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide validation (e.g., finite-difference checks) of the gradient of log C with respect to µ? How sensitive is optimization to S, and do you use common random numbers across iterations to reduce variance?
- What are typical runtimes per iteration for D=5 and for the synthetic datasets, and how do they scale with K and S?
- Have you tried local full covariances (with shrinkage) for M(x)? Does this improve performance materially?
- How do results change if the metric is estimated on a separate subset (to reduce “double dipping”)?

Suggestions
- Add ablations on S and σ, including likelihood and runtime trade-offs.
- Include at least one additional baseline (e.g., MFA or spectral clustering) and report standard errors.
- Provide a brief invariance discussion and possibly a simple linear-whitening pre-processing to mitigate sensitivity to rotations.
- Consider adaptive or learned bandwidth σ (e.g., local bandwidths based on kNN distances).

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper offers a neat and practically useful extension of Gaussians to learned manifolds with solid theoretical grounding and promising empirical results. Addressing the gradient justification, runtime/scaling, and metric design sensitivity would significantly strengthen the work.
