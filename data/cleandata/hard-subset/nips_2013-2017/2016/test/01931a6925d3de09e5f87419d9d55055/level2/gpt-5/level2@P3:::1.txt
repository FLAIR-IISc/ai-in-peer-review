Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), a parametric density that generalizes the multivariate normal to a data-adaptive Riemannian metric on RD. The key idea is to replace the Euclidean metric underlying the Gaussian with a smoothly varying, learned Riemannian metric that favors regions of high data density, thereby aligning geodesics with the data manifold. The resulting density is of the Riemannian normal form pM(x|µ,Σ) ∝ exp(−1/2⟨Logµ(x),Σ−1Logµ(x)⟩) with respect to the volume form induced by the metric, following the framework of Pennec [15], but with the important distinction that the manifold is learned non-parametrically from data, rather than known a priori as in [15, 23].

Contributions include: (1) a specific locally defined diagonal metric based on kernel-weighted local covariance (Eq. 7) that encourages geodesics to remain near high-density regions [9]; (2) a Monte Carlo estimator for the normalization constant via integration over the tangent space using exponential maps and the induced measure (Eq. 9–10), which avoids repeated evaluation of expensive logarithm maps; (3) a maximum-likelihood procedure for µ and Σ using block coordinate descent with derived gradients (Eq. 12–13) and a mixture model extension with an EM algorithm; (4) empirical demonstrations on synthetic non-linear densities and EEG sleep-stage clustering, where LAND captures non-ellipsoidal structures better than GMMs and improves alignment with sleep stages.

This bridges Riemannian statistics on known manifolds [15, 23] with manifold learning methods such as Isomap [21], and contrasts with prior mixtures on manifolds that ignore proper normalization across components [19]. The work is in scope for NIPS 2016.

Strengths
- Clear and principled construction grounded in Riemannian geometry, yielding a maximum-entropy model with respect to the learned metric [15].
- Practical and scalable normalization scheme via tangent-space Monte Carlo, leveraging Exp maps (IVPs) rather than Log maps (BVPs).
- Derivation of gradients for maximum likelihood and mixture EM with responsibilities that include per-component normalization constants.
- Empirical evidence that LAND captures complex, curved densities with fewer components than GMMs and improves clustering of EEG sleep stages.
- Honest discussion of limitations (choice of σ, computational costs, high-dimensional scaling).

Weaknesses and concerns
- The change-of-variables formula for the normalization constant in Eq. 9 appears to rely on expressing the volume element in exponential coordinates. Clarifying whether the determinant of the differential of Expµ (the Jacobian of the exponential map) is fully accounted for in m(µ,v) would strengthen the derivation; in general, the Riemannian volume in normal coordinates involves both the local metric determinant and the distortion induced by Exp (see standard differential geometry references or [15]).
- The learned metric is diagonal and not invariant to linear transformations, which can bias performance depending on the choice of coordinates. Some discussion or experiments on the impact of this design (versus full or low-rank local covariances) would be helpful.
- Computational complexity and runtime are not reported. Each evaluation of C(µ,Σ) requires S exponential maps per iteration and per mixture component; inference also requires repeated Logµ(xn). Reporting empirical runtimes and scaling with D, N, K, and S would improve the assessment of practicality.
- Sensitivity analyses for σ (beyond per-subject tuning) and for the number of Monte Carlo samples S would be valuable; variance reduction strategies (e.g., antithetic sampling, control variates) could be considered.
- Baselines are limited. Comparisons to alternative non-linear density/clustering models (e.g., kernel density estimation with variable bandwidths, mixtures of t-distributions, spectral clustering [13], or models using graph geodesics) would provide a broader context.
- The mixture EM details are deferred to the supplement; convergence behavior, initialization robustness, and local optima issues merit brief discussion in the main text.

Quality
The methodology is technically sound overall, with sensible use of Riemannian tools and Monte Carlo estimation. The gradient expressions and optimization scheme are plausible, and the empirical results support the claims. Clarifications around the volume element under exponential coordinates and additional complexity reporting would improve rigor and reproducibility.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The motivation, geometric preliminaries, and algorithmic details are accessible. Figures effectively illustrate the advantages over GMMs. Some derivations (normalization via Eq. 9, mixture EM) would benefit from more detail or explicit citations in the main text.

Originality
Adapting the Riemannian normal to a learned, data-driven metric and providing an MLE procedure with a tractable normalization estimator is novel relative to prior work on known manifolds [15, 23] and geometric mixtures [19]. The tangent-space Monte Carlo approach is a practical idea that differentiates this work from graph-based manifold methods.

Significance
The proposed model advances generative modeling on data manifolds and could influence a range of methods that rely on Gaussian assumptions. The demonstrated improvements in representing non-linear structures and clustering EEG data suggest practical utility. Broader comparisons and runtime analysis would help establish impact.

Suggestions for improvement
Essential:
- Provide a precise derivation (or citation) for Eq. 9 clarifying the role of the exponential map’s Jacobian in the volume element.
- Report computational complexity and empirical runtimes across datasets, including scaling with S and K, and the relative costs of Exp vs. Log evaluations.
- Include sensitivity analyses for σ and S, and discuss variance reduction for Monte Carlo.

Nice-to-have:
- Evaluate robustness to linear transformations or provide a pre-whitening step; consider discussing or testing non-diagonal local metrics.
- Add baselines beyond GMMs (e.g., KDE with adaptive bandwidths, mixtures of t, spectral clustering) to contextualize gains.
- Summarize EM convergence behavior and initialization strategies in the main text.

Arguments for acceptance
- Principled and novel generalization of Gaussians to learned Riemannian metrics with a practical inference algorithm.
- Strong qualitative and quantitative evidence that LAND captures non-linear structures better than GMMs.
- Clear potential for broader impact in manifold-aware generative modeling.

Arguments against acceptance
- Some ambiguity in the normalization derivation and lack of complexity/runtime reporting.
- Limited baselines and sensitivity analyses, making it harder to assess competitiveness and robustness.
- Dependence on a diagonal, coordinate-sensitive metric without ablation.
