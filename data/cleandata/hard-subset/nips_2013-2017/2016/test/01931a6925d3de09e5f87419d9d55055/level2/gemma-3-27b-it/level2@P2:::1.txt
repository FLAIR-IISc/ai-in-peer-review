This paper introduces a Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), aiming to generalize the standard normal distribution to data lying near nonlinear manifolds. The core idea is to replace the Euclidean metric used in the Mahalanobis distance with a locally adaptive Riemannian metric learned from the data. The authors provide a maximum likelihood estimation algorithm based on gradient descent and Monte Carlo integration, extend the LAND to mixture models with an EM algorithm, and demonstrate its effectiveness on synthetic data and EEG measurements.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a clear limitation of the standard normal distribution when applied to nonlinear data structures. The connection to Riemannian geometry provides a strong theoretical foundation, and the authors correctly position their work within the broader context of manifold learning and statistical modeling on manifolds. The contribution of adapting the Riemannian normal distribution for a *learned* manifold, rather than a pre-defined one, is significant. The proposed Monte Carlo integration scheme appears to be a clever way to address the computational challenges of normalizing the density. The experiments, while relatively limited, demonstrate the potential of the LAND to outperform a standard Gaussian mixture model.

However, several weaknesses need to be addressed. Firstly, while the paper provides intuition behind the Riemannian metric learning approach, the choice of a diagonal covariance matrix in Eq. (7) feels somewhat arbitrary and potentially limiting. The authors acknowledge that the metric isn’t invariant to linear transformations, but don’t fully explore the implications or potential alternatives. A more detailed discussion on the tradeoffs of this design choice would be helpful.

Secondly, the parameter sensitivity, particularly the Gaussian kernel width σ, is not adequately discussed. While the authors mention adjusting σ in the EEG experiments, the sensitivity analysis appears limited. The effect of σ on the resulting manifold and the performance of the LAND should be thoroughly investigated, and guidelines for selecting an appropriate value should be provided.

Thirdly, the scalability of the proposed approach is a concern. The authors mention the computational cost of evaluating the logarithm maps and hint at potential optimizations. A more detailed analysis of the computational complexity, especially concerning high-dimensional data, would be valuable.

Fourthly, the comparison with other relevant manifold learning techniques is limited. While the authors compare against a GMM and a least-squares based LAND, they do not benchmark against more recent, sophisticated manifold learning methods (e.g., UMAP, t-SNE) or directly integrate these into a probabilistic framework. 

Fifthly, the presentation of the mathematical derivations (especially in Algorithm 1 and related equations) could be improved for clarity. More intermediate steps and explanations would make the algorithm more accessible.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to combining Riemannian geometry with manifold learning for density estimation.
*   Strong theoretical foundation and a well-motivated problem.
*   The proposed algorithm appears to be a practical solution for maximum likelihood estimation.
*   Demonstrated improvement over GMM in both synthetic and real-world datasets.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited scalability and computational concerns.
*   Sensitivity to parameter choices (e.g., σ) without sufficient analysis.
*   Lack of thorough comparison with state-of-the-art manifold learning methods.
*   The somewhat arbitrary choice of a diagonal covariance matrix.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field, with the potential to advance the state-of-the-art in probabilistic modeling on manifolds. However, the concerns regarding scalability, parameter sensitivity, and comparison with existing methods need to be addressed. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, contingent upon the authors providing a more thorough discussion of the limitations and potential improvements of their approach. Specifically, I would like to see a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity, a more extensive sensitivity analysis of the key parameters, and a more comprehensive comparison with alternative manifold learning and density estimation techniques.
