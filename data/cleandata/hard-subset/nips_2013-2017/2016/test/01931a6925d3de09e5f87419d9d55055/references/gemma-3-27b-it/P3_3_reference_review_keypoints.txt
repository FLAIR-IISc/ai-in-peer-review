- Overall: Novel, mathematically grounded approach adapting the normal distribution to non-linear manifolds; promising results but needs minor revisions on comparisons, scalability, and clarity.  
- Core idea: Replace Euclidean distance with a learned Riemannian metric to compute geodesic distances and adapt the normal distribution to manifold curvature.  
- Positioning: Well connected to Riemannian statistics and manifold learning; builds on prior work (e.g., Hauberg et al., Pennec).  
- Novelty: Explicitly constructing a Riemannian metric for density estimation is original and theoretically strong.  
- Mathematical rigor: Solid use of Riemannian geometry and clear derivation of the Riemannian normal and gradient-based estimation.  
- Practical algorithm: Provides a concrete estimation algorithm with a Monte Carlo scheme for normalization.  
- Empirical validation: Shows promising results on synthetic and EEG data, outperforming GMM in some cases and improving over least-squares Riemannian estimators.  
- Clear motivation: Problem statement and goals are well articulated.  
- Limited comparisons: Lacks evaluation against non-parametric methods (KDE, GP), leaving performance and cost trade-offs unclear.  
- Scalability concerns: Needs more analysis of computational complexity and memory, especially ODE/geodesic solving and Monte Carlo costs as dimension grows.  
- Parameter sensitivity: Kernel bandwidth (σ) choice and initialization strategies are insufficiently analyzed; impact on convergence not explored.  
- Algorithmic clarity: Some steps in Algorithm 1 (step sizes, steepest descent for µ) and other implementation details are under-specified.  
- Overstated language: Claims like "surprisingly simple" optimization are too strong given non-convexity and numerical solver needs.  
- Significance: If scalability issues are addressed, LAND could be a valuable tool for anomaly detection, classification, and generative modeling.  
- Acceptance pros: Strong theory, novel approach, promising experiments, and clear writing.  
- Acceptance cons: Missing non-parametric comparisons, scalability and parameter-sensitivity issues, and unclear algorithmic details.  
- Recommended revisions: Add KDE/GP comparisons; detail computational complexity; discuss parameter tuning and initialization; clarify Algorithm 1; moderate claims about simplicity.
