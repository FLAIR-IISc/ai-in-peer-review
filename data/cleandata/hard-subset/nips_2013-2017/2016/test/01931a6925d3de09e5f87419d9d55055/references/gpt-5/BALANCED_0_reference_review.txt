Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), a Riemannian generalization of the multivariate normal where the Euclidean metric is replaced by a smoothly varying, data-adaptive metric. The metric M(x) is constructed as the inverse of a locally weighted (diagonal) covariance using a Gaussian kernel, thereby making geodesics prefer high-density regions and capturing nonlinear structure in RD without lowering dimensionality. On this learned manifold, the authors adopt the Riemannian normal distribution of Pennec (maximum entropy with fixed mean and covariance). Because the normalization constant C(µ, Σ) depends on µ and on the manifold geometry, they estimate it via Monte Carlo in the tangent space at µ: C = Z Ev~N(0,Σ)[√|M(Expµ(v))|], which avoids costly boundary-value problems (BVPs) for Log and requires only initial-value geodesics (Exp). They derive gradients for maximum likelihood estimation of µ and Σ (via a factorization Σ−1 = AᵀA), propose a block coordinate descent algorithm, and extend the model to mixtures with an EM-like procedure that properly accounts for component-wise normalization constants.

Empirically, LAND is shown on: (i) synthetic data sampled along a nonlinear curve, where LAND fits the generative distribution with fewer components than a GMM and outperforms a mixture based on intrinsic least-squares (LS) estimates; and (ii) EEG-derived features for sleep stage clustering (REM, awake, non-REM), where LAND generally improves F-measure alignment over GMM, with additional gains when tuning the kernel bandwidth σ.

The work connects statistics on Riemannian manifolds (e.g., Pennec; Zhang & Fletcher) with manifold learning (e.g., Isomap, LLE), and addresses deficiencies in prior Riemannian mixture work that ignored varying normalization constants across components.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean bridge between manifold learning and Riemannian statistics: a familiar normal distribution reinterpreted on a learned metric space.
- Sound formulation of the normalization constant and a practical tangent-space Monte Carlo estimator; the identity relating ∇µ log C to an expectation over v is neat and avoids differentiating through ODE solvers.
- Maximum-likelihood learning (as opposed to LS estimators common in Riemannian statistics), including a derivation for Σ via AᵀA and a mixture/EM extension that is probabilistically consistent.
- The model mitigates the known issue of intrinsic means drifting into low-density regions, yielding intuitive fits on nonlinear manifolds.
- Clear qualitative demonstrations with geodesics and contour plots; improved clustering on EEG features is encouraging.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality
- Scalability and computational cost are under-characterized. Each iteration requires Logµ(xn) for all data (BVPs) plus many Expµ(v) (IVPs) for Monte Carlo; complexity vs. N, D, and S is not reported. Runtimes, caching strategies, or approximate geodesic/log maps would be helpful.
- Estimation quality of the normalization constant is not quantified. There is no variance/bias analysis of the Monte Carlo estimator, no adaptive S, and no variance reduction (e.g., control variates, antithetics, or importance sampling). S = 3000 is chosen but not justified beyond a supplement mention.
- The learned metric uses diagonal local covariances and is not invariant to linear transforms; in practice, whitening or learning full local covariances could be important. The regularization ρ and bandwidth σ are critical but not learned.
- Potential statistical circularity: the metric is estimated from the same data used for density learning. It is not fully clear how generalization is assessed; no held-out likelihoods or predictive evaluations are reported.
- For the EEG application, σ is selected from a grid “for best result,” which risks using label information if not strictly unsupervised. More principled selection (CV or marginal likelihood) is needed.

Clarity
- Several key practical details are deferred: how ∂vec M/∂x is computed for the geodesic ODE; numerical tolerances for ODE/BVP; step-size schedules; initialization sensitivity; and how often C is re-estimated. A complexity table and ablation of algorithmic choices would improve reproducibility.
- Some citations are incomplete; a clearer positioning against related density models that warp geometry (e.g., diffeomorphic flows) would help the reader.

Originality
- The Riemannian normal is not new, but adapting it to a learned metric with a workable normalization and ML estimation is novel and nontrivial. The mixture with proper constants is also a useful contribution relative to prior approximations.

Significance
- The idea is potentially impactful, but current empirical scope is limited. Beyond 2D synthetic and a single real dataset, stronger baselines (e.g., kernel density estimators, manifold GMMs using graph geodesics, spectral clustering, or density models via learned diffeomorphisms) and quantitative held-out likelihoods would better establish advantages and practical utility.
- The paper itself acknowledges scaling issues to high D; demonstrating strategies to mitigate this (dimension reduction, sparse kernels, or mixtures of local metrics) would increase significance.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Elegant formulation tying Riemannian statistics to learned geometry.
- Correct treatment of normalization; practical tangent-space estimator.
- ML estimation and mixture model derivations are useful.
- Compelling qualitative behavior on nonlinear manifolds; improved clustering on EEG.

Cons:
- Limited experimental breadth and no held-out likelihoods; missing stronger baselines.
- Scalability and runtime not substantiated; reliance on expensive BVPs.
- Sensitivity to σ/ρ and non-invariance; σ tuning partially ad hoc.
- No analysis of Monte Carlo error or convergence.

Suggestions for improvement
- Report computational complexity and wall-clock times; compare against GMMs and graph-geodesic alternatives. Include scaling with N, D, and S.
- Provide held-out log-likelihoods and predictive density evaluations; add baselines (kernel density, graph-based manifold mixtures, spectral clustering, and possibly density flows).
- Learn σ (and ρ) by cross-validation or an empirical Bayes/likelihood criterion; avoid label-informed tuning in unsupervised settings.
- Explore variance reduction and adaptive S for the normalization estimator.
- Pre-whiten data or discuss invariance; consider richer local metrics (low-rank or full, possibly with sparsity).
- Clarify numerical details of geodesic/log computations; consider caching and approximate Log maps.
- Add an ablation showing the effect of the normalization term and comparisons to LS estimators under identical settings.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper offers a thoughtful and potentially useful contribution, but the current empirical evidence and scalability analysis are not yet at the level expected for NeurIPS. Strengthening experiments, providing rigorous computational characterization, and addressing sensitivity to metric hyperparameters would substantially improve the case for acceptance.
