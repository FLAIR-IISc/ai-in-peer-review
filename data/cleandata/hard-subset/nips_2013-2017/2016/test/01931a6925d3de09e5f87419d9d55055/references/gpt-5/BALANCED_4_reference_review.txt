Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), a generative model that replaces the Euclidean metric in a Gaussian with a data-adaptive Riemannian metric. Specifically, the authors (i) learn a smoothly varying metric from data via a kernel-weighted local (diagonal) covariance, thus turning RD into a Riemannian manifold where geodesics follow regions of high data density; (ii) define a Riemannian normal distribution pM(x | µ, Σ) ∝ exp(−0.5 ⟨Logµ(x), Σ−1 Logµ(x)⟩) with respect to the manifold’s measure; (iii) approximate the normalization constant via Monte Carlo integration over the tangent space using the exponential map; (iv) derive gradients for maximum likelihood estimation of µ and Σ, including a reparametrization Σ−1 = AᵀA and a mixture extension with an EM-like algorithm. Experiments on synthetic curves and EEG sleep-stage features show improved fit and clustering over GMMs and intrinsic least-squares estimators.

This work builds on Riemannian statistics (e.g., Pennec’s Riemannian normal and intrinsic means) and on learned metrics/manifold learning (Isomap, LLE, Hauberg et al.’s locally varying metrics). Prior mixture models on known manifolds (e.g., Simo-Serra et al.; Straub et al.) do not handle learned manifolds or component-wise normalization constants. The key novelty is combining a learned metric with a Riemannian normal and providing a practical ML/EM estimation scheme using tangent-space Monte Carlo.

Strengths
- Conceptual bridge: Elegant connection between manifold learning (via a learned metric) and Riemannian statistics, yielding a generative model that captures local nonlinearity while retaining parametric structure.
- Sound construction: The change of variables to the tangent space with the volume element √|M(Expµ(v))| is appropriate; the MC estimator for the normalization constant is natural and scalable compared to BVPs for Log.
- Practical algorithms: Derivation of gradients for µ and Σ (with reparameterization) and a mixture/EM formulation that accounts for component-specific normalizers.
- Empirical behavior: LAND avoids low-density means better than intrinsic least squares. Contours and clustering on synthetic data and the EEG example qualitatively show the benefit on non-elliptical structures.

Weaknesses and detailed comments by review criteria

Quality
- Evaluation flaw on synthetic data: The paper reports “mean negative log-likelihood of the true generative distribution using samples from each fitted model.” This estimates E_{p_model}[−log p_true], not the desired E_{p_true}[−log p_model]. This choice can favor models that concentrate mass on high-density regions of p_true regardless of coverage and is not a standard generalization metric. Please re-evaluate on held-out data drawn from the true generator (or directly from the known mixture) to estimate E_{p_true}[−log p_model]. Also report per-model test NLLs with uncertainty.
- Computational complexity and scalability: The approach requires repeated evaluations of Expµ(v) inside the MC estimator and repeated Logµ(xn) evaluations in optimization. With S = 3000 samples, per-iteration cost can be substantial, as computing M(x) along a geodesic involves kernel weights to all N data points (O(ND) per evaluation) times ODE steps. No timing, complexity analysis, or accelerations (e.g., neighbor search, Nystrom/inducing points) are reported.
- Stability and convergence: The use of steepest descent in µ to mitigate conditioning is ad hoc; step-size selection, stopping criteria, and convergence diagnostics are largely deferred to the supplement. For mixtures, EM with MC inside the M-step may not be monotonic; this should be discussed and empirically validated (e.g., tracking an approximate lower bound).
- Metric limitations: The learned metric uses only diagonal local covariances and is not affine-invariant; performance may depend strongly on preprocessing. There is no ablation comparing diagonal vs full (with shrinkage) or whitened data. The claim that D(µ) = RD and that geodesics/Log are well-defined globally for the learned metric lacks conditions; cut loci and non-uniqueness are not discussed.
- Variance reduction: The MC estimator for C(µ, Σ) could benefit from control variates, common random numbers across iterations, or quasi-MC; none are explored. A Laplace approximation could serve as a sanity check and initializer.

Clarity
- The exposition is generally clear, but several crucial derivations and algorithmic details are deferred to the supplement (e.g., gradients, step sizes, EM details). Some notational/formatting issues and incomplete references remain. Please add complexity discussion and practical optimization guidance in the main text.

Originality
- While individual components are known (Riemannian normal; learned metrics), the synthesis—learned metric + Riemannian normal + practical ML/EM with tangent-space MC normalization—is novel and timely.

Significance
- Potentially impactful as a drop-in replacement for Gaussians in mixture models and other generative methods, especially where data live near nonlinear manifolds. However, the current empirical evaluation is limited. Missing baselines include mixtures of factor analyzers, kernel GMMs, clustering in an embedded space (e.g., Isomap/LLE followed by GMM), nonparametric density estimators, and modern deep density estimators (at least flows circa 2015). Robustness to σ and dimensionality is insufficiently explored; the EEG σ selection appears tuned post hoc across a grid, and it is unclear whether labels were used to select σ.

Suggestions for improvement
- Correct the synthetic evaluation to use held-out data from the ground-truth generator; report test NLLs and KLs as appropriate.
- Provide runtime and scaling analyses; incorporate accelerations: neighbor truncation for wn(x), caching along geodesics, inducing point summaries of the metric, quasi-MC/control variates, and common random numbers to stabilize gradients.
- Study invariance and preprocessing: report results with whitening, and consider richer local structure (full covariance with shrinkage or local PCA) and mixtures of metrics.
- Robustness: show sensitivity to σ and S; if σ is tuned, do so via cross-validation without peeking at labels. Report variance across random seeds.
- EM/optimization: discuss convergence guarantees or empirical monotonicity; consider SAEM or variational surrogates for the normalization constant.
- Theory: clarify conditions under which Exp/Log are globally well-defined and unique; provide a local approximation (e.g., Laplace) of C(µ, Σ) and compare to MC.

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptually appealing and principled integration of manifold learning with Riemannian statistics.
- Addresses a known deficiency of Euclidean Gaussians on curved data and shows promising qualitative and quantitative gains.
- Practical algorithmic contributions (tangent-space normalization; mixture treatment with proper constants).

Arguments against acceptance
- Flawed synthetic evaluation protocol; limited and potentially biased empirical evidence on real data.
- Missing scalability analysis; unclear practicality on larger N, D.
- Metric design and invariance issues unaddressed; no ablations or stronger baselines.
- Convergence and stability details are thin.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The idea is strong and could be impactful, but the experimental methodology and scalability analysis need strengthening, and several technical clarifications are required. Addressing the evaluation issue, adding stronger baselines and ablations, and providing computational evidence would substantially improve the paper.
