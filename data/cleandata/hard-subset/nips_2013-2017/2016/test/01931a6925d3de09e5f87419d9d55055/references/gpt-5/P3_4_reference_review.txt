Summary
The paper proposes the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND): a Riemannian normal distribution defined on a learned Riemannian manifold whose metric adapts to data density. The metric is constructed as a smoothly varying diagonal precision matrix given by the inverse of a kernel-weighted local covariance (plus regularization), which encourages geodesics to flow through high-density regions. Building on the Riemannian normal pM(x|µ,Σ) ∝ exp(−1/2⟨Logµ(x), Σ−1 Logµ(x)⟩), the authors (i) approximate the normalization constant C(µ,Σ) by Monte Carlo integration performed in the tangent space at µ, sampling v ∼ N(0,Σ) and evaluating m(µ,v) = sqrt|M(Expµ(v))|, (ii) develop gradients for maximum likelihood estimation of µ and Σ (with Σ−1 = AᵀA), and (iii) extend to mixtures with an EM-like algorithm that includes the normalization constant for each component. Experiments on synthetic curved clusters and EEG sleep stage features show improved fit and clustering alignment versus GMMs and least-squares-based Riemannian estimators.

Relation to prior work
This bridges two lines: Riemannian statistics with known geometry (e.g., Pennec’s Riemannian normal; Fletcher and Zhang’s PPGA) and manifold learning (Isomap, LLE, Laplacian Eigenmaps) that typically yields embeddings not suited for generative modeling. It is closely related to Hauberg et al. on learning data-adaptive metrics/geodesics and to Tosi et al. on probabilistic geometries; and to mixtures on known manifolds (Simo-Serra; Straub). The key novelty is treating the learned metric as the base geometry for a tractable, normalized generative model.

Strengths
- Conceptually appealing: a principled generative model with a distance that follows data geometry; avoids the “straight-line” bias of Gaussians.
- Methodologically nontrivial: normalizing a Riemannian normal over a learned manifold and differentiating through Exp/Log with Monte Carlo is interesting and potentially useful.
- Clear link between the normalization constant and regularization: C(µ,Σ) penalizes means placed in low-density regions and large covariances that mass such regions, counteracting the well-known bias of intrinsic means.
- Mixture extension accounts for per-component C(µ,Σ), addressing a common inconsistency in earlier manifold mixture works.
- Empirical results show compelling qualitative fits on curved synthetic data and improvements in clustering alignment on EEG features.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Potentially serious theoretical issue in the tangent-space normalization: Eq. (9) uses C(µ,Σ) = ∫ sqrt|M(Expµ(v))| exp(−1/2⟨v,Σ−1v⟩) dv. For the change of variables x = Expµ(v), the Riemannian volume element in normal coordinates is sqrt|G(v)| dv, where G(v) = (d Expµ(v))ᵀ M(Expµ(v)) d Expµ(v) is the pullback metric. This contributes a |det d Expµ(v)| factor. The proposed estimator appears to omit |det d Expµ(v)|, which is 1 only at v = 0, and generally not negligible for larger v. If not included (or justified by an approximation), both C(µ,Σ) and its gradients are biased. This is central to correctness; please clarify the derivation or justify an approximation regime.
- Metric design is ad hoc and fragile: diagonal, kernel-smoothed local covariance is not invariant under linear transforms or rotations and ignores local cross-correlations. This can strongly affect results (and is acknowledged), but there is no mitigation (e.g., whitening, local PCA, or learned anisotropy).
- Computational scalability is unclear: each iteration requires N evaluations of Logµ(xn) (a BVP), S evaluations of Expµ(v) (IVPs), and repeated metric and derivative evaluations that naively scale as O(ND) per ODE step. No complexity analysis, kNN approximations, or timing ablations are provided. S = 3000 per step per component will be prohibitive for larger N, D, or K.
- Gradient correctness and stability: the µ-gradient relies on ∂/∂µ ⟨Logµ(x), Σ−1 Logµ(x)⟩ = −2 Σ−1 Logµ(x). On general manifolds, gradµ(1/2 dist^2) = −Logµ(x); the extra factor arises from the missing 1/2 in the objective, but details matter in learned geometries. Derivations are deferred to the supplement.
- Experimental scope: comparisons are limited to GMMs and LS-based Riemannian estimators. Stronger baselines (e.g., mixtures of factor analyzers, spectral clustering, kernel GMMs, principal curves, HMMs for sleep staging) are absent. For EEG, σ is sometimes tuned per subject to best F-measure; this risks optimistic reporting without a proper validation protocol. Report absolute numbers and statistical significance.
- Reproducibility: performance depends on ODE solvers and hyperparameters; details and robustness checks are missing from the main paper.

Quality
Technically ambitious, but the missing Jacobian in the normalization appears problematic. The optimization algorithms are plausible but nonconvex and expensive; no convergence or consistency guarantees are given. Empirical evidence is positive but not exhaustive.

Clarity
Generally well-written and organized, with helpful intuition. However, crucial derivations (normalization change of variables, gradients) and computational details are in the supplement; the main text should at least state the full formula including the role of d Exp and any approximations.

Originality
Combining a data-learned metric with a normalized Riemannian normal and providing ML/EM procedures is novel and differentiates from prior manifold mixture works that ignore normalization constants. The metric choice itself is not new, but its use within a generative model is.

Significance
If the normalization and learning machinery are made correct and scalable, LAND could be a useful drop-in replacement for Gaussians in curved-data settings. As is, significance is tempered by theoretical and practical concerns.

Questions for the authors
1) Please clarify Eq. (9): does your m(µ,v) include |det d Expµ(v)|? If not, why is it valid to omit it? What is the empirical impact?
2) How are Log maps computed (solver, boundary conditions, tolerances)? What are per-iteration costs and wall-clock times versus GMMs? Any kNN truncation when evaluating M and its derivatives?
3) How sensitive are results to σ, ρ, S? Can σ be learned (e.g., by maximizing the marginal likelihood) or adapted spatially?
4) Why restrict to diagonal local covariance? Have you tried local PCA or low-rank anisotropy?
5) For EEG, provide absolute F-measures, standard deviations, and a principled σ selection protocol.

Minor comments
- Step 9 in Algorithm 1 inverts AᵀA to form Σ; keeping Σ−1 = AᵀA avoids inversion.
- Discuss numerical stability of det M and of m(µ,v) for large v.
- Some references lack years/pages.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually strong and timely idea; clean integration of manifold learning and generative modeling.
- Inclusion of normalization constant in mixtures is important.
- Promising empirical results and visualizations.

Cons for acceptance
- Potentially incorrect normalization (missing Jacobian), which undermines likelihood, gradients, and EM.
- Limited baselines and analysis; unclear scalability and robustness.
- Heavy dependence on user-chosen σ and diagonal metric.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea is compelling and could be impactful, but the theoretical correctness of the normalization step and the practical scalability need to be addressed. With a corrected derivation (or an explicit, justified approximation) and stronger empirical validation, this would be much stronger. Confidence: high.
