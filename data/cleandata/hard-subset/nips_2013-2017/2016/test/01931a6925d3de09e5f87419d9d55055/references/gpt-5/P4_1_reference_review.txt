Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND) that replaces the Euclidean distance in a Gaussian density with a geodesic distance induced by a learned Riemannian metric on RD. The metric is constructed from data via a smoothly varying, diagonal, kernel-weighted local covariance (with regularization), yielding an anisotropic metric tensor M(x) that is small in high-density regions and large in low-density regions. The density is the Riemannian normal (Pennec), pM(x|µ,Σ) ∝ exp(−1/2⟨Logµ(x), Σ−1 Logµ(x)⟩), with a normalization constant that depends on µ, Σ, and the geometry induced by M.

The key algorithmic contributions are:
- A Monte Carlo estimator of the normalization constant by integrating in the tangent space TµM: C(µ,Σ) = EN(0,Σ)[√|M(Expµ(v))|], avoiding costly BVPs for Log by using IVPs for Exp.
- A block coordinate descent maximum likelihood procedure for µ and Σ, with gradients (or steepest descent directions) that incorporate the Monte Carlo estimate of C. Σ is parameterized via Σ−1 = AᵀA.
- An EM algorithm for mixtures of LANDs, computing responsibilities using the properly normalized component densities.

Empirically, LAND fits nonlinear data manifolds better than standard GMMs and mixtures built from intrinsic least-squares (LS) estimators. On synthetic data (a half-ellipsoidal curve), LAND achieves better likelihood with fewer components. On EEG sleep-stage features (5D), LAND clustering yields higher F-measure alignment with REM/awake/non-REM labels than GMMs, with further gains when σ (the kernel width) is tuned.

The work bridges Riemannian statistics (e.g., Pennec’s Riemannian normal; Zhang & Fletcher’s PPGA) with manifold learning (e.g., Isomap, LLE, Laplacian Eigenmaps), but differs by learning a smooth metric over RD rather than embedding to a lower-dimensional space, enabling a generative model defined everywhere.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: casting a locally adaptive Gaussian as a Riemannian normal on a learned manifold is natural and principled, linking maximum-entropy modeling on manifolds with data-driven metrics.
- Practical algorithm: the tangent-space Monte Carlo estimator is simple, scalable relative to alternatives that would require repeated BVPs, and integrates cleanly into gradient-based ML.
- Addresses a known failure mode: unlike intrinsic LS means, ML estimates in LAND tend to avoid low-density regions, empirically improving fit.
- Mixture extension with proper normalization constants per component addresses a common inconsistency in prior manifold mixture work.
- Empirical evidence shows improved modeling of nonlinear clusters and better sleep-stage clustering alignment than GMMs.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Technical assumptions: The change-of-variables to tangent space and the claim D(µ) = RD implicitly assume favorable geometric properties (e.g., geodesic completeness, injectivity radius ensuring a well-defined Exp and one-to-one coverage) for the learned metric on RD. These conditions are not stated or justified; cut loci or non-uniqueness could, in principle, affect both C(µ,Σ) and inference.
- Gradient of log normalization: The provided µ-gradient (Eq. 12) depends on Monte Carlo averages of m(µ, v) v without explicitly differentiating through m(µ, v) = √|M(Expµ(v))| with respect to µ. The derivation is delegated to the supplement; without it, it is unclear under what conditions the estimator is unbiased and why v (rather than ∂m/∂µ) suffices. Given that m depends on µ via Exp and M, more justification is needed.
- Metric construction limitations: Restricting M(x) to diagonal entries improves robustness but may underfit anisotropic local structure; the metric is not invariant to linear transforms. Discussion acknowledges this but does not quantify impact.
- Hyperparameter selection: σ critically shapes the geometry. In the sleep experiments, best-σ selection is based on grid search “best result,” potentially using test labels for tuning (risk of optimistic bias). A proper validation procedure or unsupervised criterion would strengthen claims.
- Computational cost and scaling: The approach requires S evaluations of Exp per C(µ,Σ) estimate, per component, per iteration. While IVPs are faster than BVPs, overall complexity and wall-clock times are not reported; sensitivity to S and variance of the estimator are only mentioned in the supplement.
- Baselines: Comparisons are mainly to GMM and LS-based mixtures. Stronger baselines could include kernel density models, mixtures using geodesic distances over neighborhood graphs, or modern flexible densities (e.g., flows) for nonlinear structure, acknowledging contemporaneous work.
- Theoretical guarantees: No convergence guarantees for the nonconvex block coordinate scheme; no bounds on Monte Carlo error or its effect on parameter estimates.

Quality
- The model is well-motivated and mostly technically sound, assuming the geometric conditions hold. The MC estimator and optimization scheme are coherent. However, clarity on the µ-gradient derivation and manifold regularity assumptions is necessary for full confidence.
- Experiments are illustrative and support claims, but more rigorous tuning, runtime reporting, and broader baselines would improve the evaluation.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well-organized. Notation is standard. Some claims (e.g., domain D(µ)=RD; gradient of log C) rely on the supplement; brief statements of assumptions in the main text would help. Practical choices (ρ, σ selection strategy, S sensitivity, runtime) should be summarized in the main paper.

Originality
- Novel integration of learned Riemannian metrics with properly normalized Riemannian normals and ML/EM inference. Prior works either assumed known manifolds or neglected normalization. The tangent-space MC normalization and gradient-based ML in this setting appear original.

Significance
- Provides a principled, extensible replacement for Gaussians in generative models on data with nonlinear local structure. Potential to influence modeling in areas where Euclidean Gaussians underperform. Practical impact may be tempered by computational cost, sensitivity to σ, and challenges in high dimensions.

Suggestions for improvement
- State and justify geometric assumptions ensuring global well-posedness of Exp/Log and validity of the tangent-space integral; discuss behavior near cut loci.
- Include a concise derivation or intuition for Eq. 12 in the main text, or at least outline why gradients reduce to m(µ, v) v terms without differentiating m.
- Report runtime and scaling with S, N, D, and K; provide sensitivity plots for S and σ. Use a validation protocol for σ selection that does not use labels.
- Add baselines that reflect nonlinear structure (e.g., spectral clustering with GMM in embedded space, kernel density estimates, or learned flow models).
- Discuss and, if possible, mitigate lack of linear invariance (e.g., prewhitening) and the impact of diagonal metric assumption.

Arguments for acceptance
- Principled and novel formulation with clear links to Riemannian statistics and manifold learning.
- Practical algorithmic contributions (tangent-space MC; ML/EM) with demonstrated benefits over GMMs.
- Addresses a relevant modeling gap and is likely to inspire follow-up work.

Arguments against acceptance
- Incomplete theoretical conditions for the learned manifold and the gradient estimator of log-normalizer.
- Limited experimental baselines and potential tuning bias; no runtime/scaling analysis.
- Restricted metric class (diagonal) and lack of linear invariance may limit applicability.

Scope
- Fully in scope for NeurIPS: probabilistic modeling, geometry in ML, generative modeling.
