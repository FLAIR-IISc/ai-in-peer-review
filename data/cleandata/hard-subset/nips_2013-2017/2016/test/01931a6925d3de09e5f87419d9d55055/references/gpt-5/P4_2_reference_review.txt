Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a locally adaptive normal distribution (LAND) that replaces the Euclidean metric in the Gaussian density with a data-adaptive Riemannian metric, yielding a Riemannian normal distribution defined on a learned manifold M in R^D. The metric is constructed from locally weighted (diagonal) covariances via a Gaussian kernel, producing a smoothly varying metric tensor M(x) whose inverse scales with local variance. This induces geodesics that prefer high-density regions and penalize excursions into low-density areas.

The resulting density pM(x|µ,Σ) ∝ exp(-1/2 ⟨Logµ(x), Σ^-1 Logµ(x)⟩), normalized w.r.t. the Riemannian volume measure, follows the maximum-entropy characterization of Pennec (2006) for known manifolds. The paper adapts this to a learned manifold by: (i) estimating the normalization constant via Monte Carlo on the tangent space at µ by transforming the manifold integral into an expectation over v ~ N(0,Σ) of a weight m(µ,v) = sqrt|M(Expµ(v))|; (ii) deriving gradients for maximum likelihood estimation of µ and Σ via block coordinate descent, including a factorization Σ^-1 = A^T A; and (iii) extending to mixtures with an EM algorithm that accounts for component-specific normalization constants.

Experiments show that LAND better captures nonlinear structure than GMMs and LS-based Riemannian estimators on synthetic data and improves clustering alignment with REM/awake/non-REM labels in EEG-derived 5D features. The work connects Riemannian statistics (e.g., Pennec; Zhang & Fletcher) with learned geometries from manifold learning (e.g., Isomap; Hauberg et al.), and addresses a known issue that intrinsic means on manifolds can lie in low-density regions.

Quality (technical soundness)
- Metric construction: The diagonal, kernel-smoothed covariance inverse is a reasonable, smooth metric capturing local anisotropy while avoiding overfitting. Non-invariance to linear transforms is acknowledged.
- Normalization constant: The key step rewrites the manifold integral as an expectation over the tangent space (Eq. 9) with integrand sqrt|M(Expµ(v))|. For a general manifold, the correct change of variables should involve the determinant of the pullback metric G(v) = (dExpµ(v))^T M(Expµ(v)) (dExpµ(v)), i.e., sqrt|G(v)|, which includes the Jacobian of the exponential map. The paper appears to omit the (dExp) terms, which is exact only infinitesimally at v≈0 or under restrictive conditions; otherwise this is an approximation. This omission can bias C(µ,Σ) and its gradients, especially for larger Σ or highly curved regions. The paper should clarify whether this is intended as an approximation, provide conditions under which it is accurate, or incorporate the Jacobian factor.
- Gradients: The gradients for µ and A (with Σ^-1 = A^T A) are plausible given the sampling scheme: they reduce to differences between empirical moments and MC expectations under N(0,Σ) weighted by m(µ,v). The use of a “steepest descent direction” dµ = -Σ ∇µ appears as a preconditioning choice; calling it steepest descent depends on the chosen inner product on TµM and should be justified.
- Computational aspects: The approach requires repeated evaluations of Logµ(xn) (a BVP) for data-fitting terms and of Expµ(v) (an IVP) for MC normalization, making complexity significant. The paper acknowledges this and identifies computing logarithm maps as the bottleneck. A more explicit complexity analysis (per iteration in N, D, S, ODE solver calls) would improve reproducibility and assessment of scalability.
- Mixture model and EM: Responsibilities include the component-specific normalizers, addressing a known inconsistency in some prior work. Convergence properties and initialization are moved to the supplement; a brief summary would help.
- Experiments: Synthetic examples are appropriate; reporting AIC/BIC is helpful. On EEG, the fixed-σ results are informative. However, selecting σ by choosing the best F-measure across a grid risks using label information for hyperparameter tuning in an unsupervised task; the protocol should be clarified (e.g., cross-validation or unsupervised selection) to avoid optimistic bias.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The background on Riemannian geometry is concise and helpful. Key algorithms are summarized, and notation is consistent. The transformation for normalization and its assumptions should be explained more carefully, including the role of the exponential map’s Jacobian. More detail on ODE solver settings, convergence diagnostics, and runtime would enhance clarity.

Originality
- Novel combination of learned Riemannian metrics with properly normalized Riemannian normal distributions and ML estimation on a learned manifold.
- The MC normalization on the tangent space and gradient-based MLE for µ and Σ in this setting appear novel at the time, as does the mixture extension that respects normalization differences across components.

Significance
- Conceptually significant: provides a way to plug a Gaussian-like model into a learned nonlinear geometry, potentially enabling “manifold-aware” versions of many generative methods.
- Practical impact depends on computational tractability and robustness of the manifold construction (σ choice, diagonal assumption, high-dimensional scaling). The diagonal metric and need for ODE solvers may limit large-scale applicability. Nonetheless, the approach could influence research on generative modeling with learned geometries.

Pros
- Clear, principled attempt to bridge manifold learning and Riemannian statistics.
- Incorporates normalization constants properly in mixtures.
- Empirical results demonstrate advantages over GMMs on nonlinear data and a real EEG application.
- Addresses the low-density mean issue of intrinsic LS estimators.

Cons
- Potentially incorrect/incomplete change of variables for normalization (missing exponential map Jacobian), or at least insufficiently justified approximation.
- Heavy computational requirements; limited discussion of scalability and runtime.
- Metric is diagonal and not invariant to linear transforms; implications not thoroughly examined.
- Hyperparameter selection for σ in EEG may use label-dependent selection; protocol unclear.
- No ablation on the effect of S (MC samples) on accuracy/cost beyond a brief note; sensitivity analyses would be valuable.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify the measure transformation in Eq. 9 and either include the full pullback metric determinant or justify the approximation (e.g., small Σ regime) with empirical validation showing negligible bias.
- Provide complexity and runtime measurements, and sensitivity to S, σ, and ρ; include higher-dimensional tests.
- Specify an unsupervised protocol for σ selection (e.g., likelihood on held-out data) for the EEG experiment.
- Discuss or test robustness to affine transformations; consider learning full local covariances or mixtures of metrics.
- Summarize initialization, convergence behavior, and EM details in the main text.

Overall assessment
The paper presents an interesting and potentially impactful approach with solid ideas and promising results. The main technical concern is the treatment of the normalization constant via tangent-space integration without the exponential map Jacobian; clarifying or correcting this is important. With that addressed and clearer experimental protocols, the contribution would be strong.
