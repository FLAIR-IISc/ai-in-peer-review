Summary
The paper proposes a Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND) obtained by replacing the Euclidean metric in the Gaussian density with a data-driven Riemannian metric on RD. The metric is learned nonparametrically via a local diagonal covariance estimate smoothed by a Gaussian kernel; geodesics under this metric are then used to define the Riemannian normal pM(x|µ,Σ) ∝ exp(−1/2 ⟨Logµ(x), Σ−1 Logµ(x)⟩). Since the normalization constant depends on the manifold geometry and µ, it is estimated by Monte Carlo integration in the tangent space using the exponential map. The authors derive gradient-based maximum likelihood (ML) updates for µ and Σ (with Σ−1 factored as A⊤A), and extend to mixtures with an EM-like procedure. Experiments on synthetic curved data and EEG sleep-stage features suggest LAND better captures nonlinearity than GMMs and avoids intrinsic-mean pathologies.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: bridges Riemannian statistics and manifold learning by using a learned Riemannian metric (rather than a fixed manifold) to define a proper generative density with a principled ML estimator.
- Methodological clarity: the construction (metric → geodesics → Riemannian normal → MC normalization → gradients) is well motivated; the tangent-space Monte Carlo is computationally sensible compared to directly using Log maps.
- Practical relevance: the ability to fit non-ellipsoidal clusters and to avoid placing means in low-density regions is compelling, and the mixture extension is natural.
- Empirical evidence: synthetic examples convincingly illustrate local adaptation; the EEG experiment gives preliminary evidence that LAND can be beneficial in a realistic setting.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
1) Change of variables and normalization: The normalization constant is rewritten as an integral over TµM: C(µ,Σ) = ∫ m(µ,v) exp(−1/2⟨v,Σ−1v⟩) dv with m(µ,v) = √|M(Expµ(v))|. This appears to omit the Jacobian determinant |det d(Expµ)v| associated with the exponential map. In general, the Riemannian volume form in normal coordinates involves a volume distortion factor (the Jacobian of Exp), which is not equal to √|M(x)| alone. Unless the manifold is flat (or under a small-v approximation), ignoring |det d(Expµ)v| biases C(µ,Σ) and thus ML estimates. The paper should either: (a) include the correct Jacobian factor; (b) justify an approximation regime where it is negligible, and quantify the error; or (c) show empirically that including it does not change results materially.

2) Gradients with respect to µ: The gradient in Eq. (12) replaces ∇µ log C(µ,Σ) by (Z/C) E[m(µ,v) v], which presumes a specific identity for ∇µ m(µ,v) or a pathwise derivative through Exp that reduces to v. This is not obvious: m depends on µ through x = Expµ(v) and through M(x), and differentiating requires ∂Exp/∂µ and ∂M/∂x. The paper should provide a rigorous derivation (or explicit approximation) of this step; otherwise the update may be incorrect or unstable.

3) Manifold assumptions: The paper states “for the manifolds we consider, the domain D(µ̂) is RD” and uses Log and Exp globally. For a general learned metric on RD, cut loci and multiple geodesics can appear, making Log multi-valued and Exp non-invertible globally. The method implicitly assumes existence/uniqueness of geodesics and well-behaved maps. Please clarify the conditions on σ, ρ, and M under which the geodesic IVPs/BVPs are guaranteed to be solvable and the maps are single-valued over the regions of interest.

4) Metric design and invariance: The metric is diagonal and not invariant to rotations or affine transforms. This can be problematic when features are correlated. While the diagonal choice controls complexity, the implications for performance (and the need to standardize or whiten inputs) should be discussed. A comparison to a full (or locally low-rank) metric would help.

5) Evaluation protocol: On synthetic data, the reported metric is the expected negative log-likelihood under the true generative distribution estimated by sampling from each fitted model. This estimates KL(pθ || ptrue), not held-out data likelihood. A more standard evaluation would report test log-likelihood on held-out data under each fitted model (with MC for C(µ,Σ) at the test points). Without this, the likelihood comparisons are hard to interpret.

6) Computational cost and scaling: Each MC estimate requires S exponential maps (ODE solves). With S=3000 and mixtures, this may be expensive. There is little discussion of runtime, sensitivity to S, or solver settings. A timing study, convergence diagnostics, and variance reduction (e.g., common random numbers, control variates) would strengthen the case.

7) Hyperparameter selection: σ is critical. In the EEG experiment, showing both a fixed σ and per-subject tuning is informative, but also raises concerns about overfitting. A principled selection (e.g., cross-validation, marginal likelihood, or joint learning of σ) would be preferable.

8) Missing or brief details: The EM updates are deferred; numerical details (stepsizes, line search, stopping criteria, ODE solver tolerances) are sparse; sensitivity to ρ is not explored.

Quality
The core idea is sound and promising, and the empirical results are encouraging. However, there are significant technical gaps around the normalization change-of-variables and the gradient with respect to µ that need to be addressed to ensure correctness. Experimental evaluation could be more rigorous (held-out likelihood, runtime).

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. The exposition of Riemannian preliminaries is concise. The key mathematical steps (Eqs. 9–13) need clearer derivations and assumptions. More implementation detail would aid reproducibility.

Originality
The paper advances prior work on Riemannian normals by learning the metric from data and by proposing a practical ML procedure with MC normalization. To my knowledge (as of the cited literature), this is a novel combination in a generative modeling context.

Significance
If the technical issues are fixed, LAND could influence how Gaussian-based models are adapted to nonlinear structure and could be a useful drop-in replacement in mixture modeling and beyond. The approach opens a path to generative modeling on learned Riemannian geometries.

Suggestions and questions
- Correct the normalization integral to include the Jacobian of Exp, or justify the approximation and quantify its effect.
- Provide a rigorous derivation for Eq. (12) or clearly state and test the approximation used.
- Report held-out log-likelihoods and runtimes; include variance of MC estimates and sensitivity to S.
- Discuss preprocessing (scaling/whitening) and the impact of diagonal metric; consider an ablation with a full or locally low-rank metric.
- Explore automated selection of σ (CV or joint learning).
- Clarify conditions ensuring unique geodesics in the regions used and how violations are handled numerically.

Pros
- Elegant idea connecting learned geometry and probabilistic modeling.
- Practical ML estimator with MC normalization.
- Clear empirical advantages on nonlinear clusters and a real dataset.

Cons
- Potentially incorrect or unjustified treatment of the volume element and µ-gradient.
- Heavy computational burden; limited discussion of scalability.
- Evaluation metrics and hyperparameter selection could be more rigorous.
