Summary
The paper proposes the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND): a Riemannian normal distribution built on a learned Riemannian metric over RD. The metric is defined via a locally weighted diagonal covariance (Gaussian kernel with bandwidth σ), leading to a smoothly varying metric tensor M(x) whose geodesics are attracted to high-density regions. The density is p(x|µ,Σ) ∝ exp(−1/2 ⟨Logµ(x), Σ−1 Logµ(x)⟩) with respect to the Riemannian volume. Because the normalizing constant C(µ,Σ) is intractable, the authors estimate it via Monte Carlo on the tangent space at µ by sampling v ∼ N(0,Σ) and using the exponential map x = Expµ(v); they then develop gradients for maximum likelihood estimation of (µ,Σ) and extend to mixtures with an EM-like algorithm. Experiments include synthetic nonlinear manifolds and an EEG sleep-stage clustering task, where LAND outperforms GMMs and least-squares (intrinsic mean/covariance) estimators.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The core idea—Riemannian normal on a learned metric—is sound and grounded in prior work (Pennec; Hauberg et al.). The learning of M(x) is reasonable and guarantees positive definiteness; geodesics/log/exp are well-defined numerically. However, I have a potentially serious concern with the derivation of the normalization constant in Eq. (9): the change of variables x = Expµ(v) should, in general, introduce a Jacobian term |det DExpµ(v)| in addition to the factor √|M(Expµ(v))| when pulling back the Riemannian volume to TµM. The manuscript uses m(µ,v) = √|M(Expµ(v))| only, apparently omitting |det DExpµ(v)|. Unless there is a specific argument (e.g., special coordinates or properties of the learned metric) that makes |det DExp| ≡ 1, the estimator for C(µ,Σ) (and the gradients that depend on it) may be biased. This needs to be clarified or corrected.
- Gradient derivations: The gradient w.r.t. µ (Eq. 12) takes a moment-matching form. Without the supplement, it is hard to verify the steps, and the result seems to rely on identities that exchange derivatives through Exp/Log and the Monte Carlo expectation. Given the potential omission above, the correctness of these gradients is uncertain. Please include a clear derivation for ∂ log C/∂µ and justify the use of v-weighted expectations.
- Computational aspects: Each evaluation of C requires S exponential map evaluations (solving IVPs), repeated throughout optimization and for every mixture component. M(x) also requires sums over all data to compute local variances. There is no complexity or timing analysis, and no discussion of approximations (e.g., neighbor truncation, caching, low-discrepancy sampling, or fast geodesic approximations). Scalability to larger N, D, and K is unclear.
- Experiments: The synthetic examples convincingly illustrate the ability to capture nonlinear cluster shapes with few components. The EEG experiment is a promising application, with improvements in F-measure. However, the evaluation is relatively limited: one real dataset with 10 subjects, no statistical significance analysis, and no baselines beyond GMM and LS (e.g., spectral clustering, kernel GMMs, normalizing flows, density on graphs, or manifold mixtures on known manifolds). Also, the choice of σ is either fixed or chosen by grid search per subject, which risks optimistic tuning and highlights sensitivity to this hyperparameter.

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable and well organized, with a useful background section. However, several key derivations are deferred to the supplement (especially the measure change and gradients), and the correctness hinges on them. The relationship between the Riemannian volume form, the change of variables under Exp, and the Monte Carlo estimator should be explained in the main text. A brief complexity discussion would help readers assess practicality.
- The metric is diagonal and not invariant to affine transforms; this is acknowledged. It would be helpful to state conditions (e.g., geodesic completeness, smoothness with ρ) under which Exp/Log are numerically stable.

Originality
- The main novelty is to bring the Riemannian normal distribution into a learned-manifold setting, including (i) a practical Monte Carlo scheme for the normalization constant via tangent-space sampling, and (ii) ML estimation (and a mixture model) on learned metrics. Prior works either assume known manifolds or ignore normalization across components. The metric-learning idea follows Hauberg et al., but the integration and ML estimation for a Riemannian normal on such learned metrics is a meaningful contribution.
- That said, the diagonal local covariance metric and kernel smoothing are fairly standard choices; the conceptual advance is more in the coupling of this learned geometry with a normalized generative model.

Significance
- If technically correct, LAND offers a principled way to replace Gaussians by locally geometry-aware components across a range of models (mixtures, LDA, etc.). The empirical results suggest benefits in modeling nonlinear structures. However, practical significance depends on addressing computational scalability and hyperparameter selection, and on clarifying the normalization/gradient correctness. The current experimental scope is not yet strong enough to demonstrate broad impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, well-motivated bridging of Riemannian statistics and manifold learning.
- Principled maximum-likelihood treatment (including normalization) and extension to mixtures.
- Illustrative synthetic and a real application showing gains over GMMs.
- Potentially useful building block for manifold-aware generative modeling.

Cons for acceptance
- Possible error/omission in the change-of-variables for the normalization constant (missing |det DExp|), which could invalidate estimation and gradients if not justified.
- Limited empirical evaluation (few baselines, no timing/scaling studies, modest dataset size).
- Heavy computation (many Exp evaluations, kernel sums), with no complexity analysis or accelerations.
- Sensitivity to σ; no scheme to learn it; diagonal metric may be too restrictive.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify and, if necessary, correct the normalization formula: explicitly derive the pullback of the Riemannian volume under Exp and include the Jacobian determinant term. Update gradients accordingly and assess empirical impact.
- Provide derivations in the main text (or a concise appendix) for the gradients w.r.t. µ and Σ, and discuss conditions for existence/uniqueness.
- Add complexity/timing results and ablations on S, σ, and ODE solver settings; consider neighbor truncation or caching for M(x).
- Strengthen baselines (e.g., spectral clustering, kernel GMMs, manifold mixtures on known manifolds, or modern density models) and add statistical tests on the EEG task.
- Discuss or attempt learning σ (e.g., via cross-validation, marginal likelihood, or bilevel optimization) and consider including off-diagonal local covariances with regularization.

Overall
A promising and potentially impactful idea with solid motivation, but I am concerned about the correctness of the normalization/gradient derivation and the limited empirical scope. With clarification and stronger experiments, this could be a strong contribution. As it stands, I am on the fence leaning negative unless the measure-change issue is resolved.
