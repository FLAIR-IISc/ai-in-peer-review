Summary
The paper proposes the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), which replaces the Euclidean/Mahalanobis metric in a Gaussian with a data-driven Riemannian metric learned from local (diagonal) covariances smoothed via a Gaussian kernel. With this metric, the density is defined as a Riemannian normal (Pennec) whose normalization constant depends on both the covariance and the manifold geometry. The authors (i) approximate the normalization constant via Monte Carlo integration in the tangent space at the mean using the exponential map, (ii) give gradient-based maximum likelihood procedures for the mean and covariance, and (iii) extend to mixtures with an EM scheme. Experiments on synthetic curved manifolds and EEG sleep-stage clustering suggest LAND models the nonlinear structure better than GMMs and intrinsic least-squares baselines.

Relation to prior work
The work bridges manifold learning (e.g., Isomap) and statistics on manifolds (Pennec’s Riemannian Gaussian), extending the latter to the “learned manifold” setting where the geometry is estimated from data. The metric follows ideas from Hauberg et al. on learned Riemannian geometries. Mixtures of Riemannian Gaussians on known manifolds have been explored (e.g., Simo-Serra et al., Straub et al.), but the present contribution is to learn the geometry and then build a generative density that accounts for normalization. This is a sensible direction; however, it overlaps with established nonparametric density/clustering approaches on manifolds and metric-learning work, so careful empirical validation against such baselines is essential.

Strengths
- Conceptual link between manifold learning and Riemannian statistics is interesting and timely.
- Tangent-space Monte Carlo for the normalizer is a reasonable computational choice (avoids expensive BVPs for logarithm maps).
- The MLE procedure is reasonably simple to implement given Exp/Log solvers.
- Synthetic experiments illustrate the advantage of geodesic-aware densities over Euclidean Gaussians for curved supports.
- The observation that MLE LAND means avoid low-density regions, unlike intrinsic least-squares means, is useful.

Weaknesses and concerns
Methodological soundness
- Derivations for gradients, particularly the dependence of the normalization constant on the mean, are under-specified. In the tangent-space reparameterization, the µ-dependence enters only through the volume term m(µ, v). The paper’s mean update direction appears to involve sample averages of m(µ, v) v, but the link between ∂ log C/∂µ and this estimator is not convincingly derived in the main text. A rigorous derivation and discussion of estimator bias/variance are needed.
- The learned metric is diagonal and not affine-invariant. This discards local anisotropy off the coordinate axes, which is at odds with the stated goal of capturing local structure. The justification (“to prevent overfitting”) is weak without empirical ablations showing the trade-off.
- The approach relies on a global, data-derived metric during training and evaluation. Using the same data to define the geometry and to fit/evaluate the density risks optimistic fits and complicates generalization. There is no discussion of how to estimate/test with a metric learned on training data only, or of potential leakage when tuning σ on the same subject.
- No guarantees are provided on geodesic completeness, uniqueness of Exp/Log, or numerical stability under the learned metric; the paper notes that σ is chosen to make geodesics “computable,” which is ad hoc. The claim that domains D(µ̂) equal RD is nontrivial under learned metrics and should be justified.

Empirical validation
- Comparisons are limited. Beyond GMMs and a non-MLE Riemannian baseline, there are no strong baselines: e.g., kernel density estimation with geodesic distances, mixtures of factor analyzers, spectral clustering or graph-based clustering for manifold data, graph-Laplacian regularized models, or modern flexible densities (at least flows/MAF/RNVP were contemporaneous or near contemporaneous). The EEG study uses only clustering F-measure; there is no likelihood-based evaluation or held-out likelihood for any real dataset.
- Model selection for σ is loosely handled: fixed σ=1, then “best σ” per subject. This risks overfitting; no cross-validation, calibration curves, or sensitivity analyses are provided. Error bars are absent.
- Computational claims are not substantiated. Each evaluation of C(µ, Σ) requires thousands of Exp solves per component per iteration. There is no wall-clock, scaling with N, D, K, or S, and no comparison to GMM runtimes. Calling this scalable without quantitative evidence is premature.

Clarity
- Overall readable, but key derivations are relegated to the supplement. Important details (gradient of the normalizer, EM updates, conditions on the metric for IVP/BVP solvability) should be in the main text. A few statements (e.g., domain equals RD) need justification.

Originality and significance
- The idea of using a learned Riemannian geometry with a Riemannian Gaussian is sensible, but the novelty is incremental relative to existing Riemannian statistics on known manifolds and metric-learning approaches. The significance hinges on empirical improvements and practical feasibility; given the limited validation and heavy computational burden, impact is uncertain.

Questions for the authors
1. Please provide a complete derivation of the mean gradient (or steepest descent direction) for log C(µ, Σ), and discuss bias/variance of the Monte Carlo gradient estimator.
2. How is σ selected without using test labels or test data? Can you provide cross-validated results and sensitivity plots?
3. What are the computational costs (per iteration, per component) and scaling with D, N, K, S? Provide runtimes versus GMM.
4. Does using a metric learned from the full dataset during evaluation cause leakage? How would you handle training/validation splits?
5. Can you compare against stronger baselines (e.g., spectral clustering, MFA, KDE on graphs) and report held-out likelihood on at least one real dataset?

Pros
- Clear, principled formulation; interesting bridge between areas.
- Demonstrates modeling advantages on curved synthetic manifolds.
- Accounts for normalization across mixture components.

Cons
- Key derivations incomplete/unclear; potential methodological issues.
- Strong dependence on ad hoc hyperparameters; diagonal metric and lack of invariance.
- Limited, potentially overfitted empirical validation; missing strong baselines and runtime analysis.
- Scalability and robustness unproven.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The idea is appealing, but the paper lacks sufficient theoretical clarity on the optimization, provides limited and potentially optimistic empirical evidence, and does not convincingly demonstrate scalability or superiority over stronger, established baselines. Strengthening derivations, adding rigorous and broader experiments with proper model selection, and providing runtime/scaling evidence would significantly improve the case for acceptance.
