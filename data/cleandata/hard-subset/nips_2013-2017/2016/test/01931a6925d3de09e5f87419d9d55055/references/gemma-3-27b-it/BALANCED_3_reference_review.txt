This paper introduces a Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND) by replacing the Euclidean distance in a standard normal distribution with a learned Riemannian distance. The core idea, bridging Riemannian geometry and manifold learning, is to model data residing on nonlinear manifolds using a distribution adapted to the intrinsic geometry of the data. The authors provide a scalable Monte Carlo integration scheme for normalization and a gradient-based algorithm for parameter estimation, extending this to a mixture model. The method is demonstrated on both synthetic data and EEG measurements of human sleep stages.

The paper is well-motivated. The limitations of the standard normal distribution when applied to data on nonlinear manifolds are clearly articulated, and the connection to existing work in manifold learning (e.g., Isomap) and Riemannian statistics is effectively established. The use of a Riemannian metric to capture the local structure of the data is a natural and promising approach. The proposed Monte Carlo integration scheme for normalization appears crucial for practical application and is a significant contribution. The extension to a mixture model further enhances the model's flexibility. The experimental results, particularly the application to EEG data, demonstrate the potential of the LAND.

However, there are several weaknesses. The paper lacks a thorough comparative analysis against more recent and established manifold learning and density estimation techniques. While GMM is used as a baseline, it would be valuable to compare against, for example, kernel density estimation (KDE), t-SNE, or UMAP, particularly in the context of the sleep stage data. The sensitivity of the method to the choice of the Gaussian kernel width (σ) is only briefly addressed. A more systematic investigation of this parameter and its impact on performance is necessary. The regularization parameter (ρ) is mentioned, but its impact isn't discussed. The derivations, while present, could be more clearly explained and the connections between different equations could be made more explicit. The implementation details, such as the initialization procedures, are relegated to the supplementary material, which makes it harder to assess reproducibility. The claim that the LAND “adapts” to the data is slightly overstated; the metric is *learned* from the data, and demonstrating true adaptation (e.g., online learning) would strengthen this claim. The authors mention that the metric is not invariant to linear transformations, but do not discuss potential implications or mitigation strategies. The discussion section feels somewhat disjointed and lists many future directions without prioritizing them.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach: The LAND represents a novel combination of Riemannian geometry and manifold learning, offering a statistically sound framework for density estimation on nonlinear manifolds.
*   Theoretical grounding: The method is based on a strong theoretical foundation in Riemannian statistics, providing a principled approach to the problem.
*   Scalable implementation: The proposed Monte Carlo integration scheme and gradient-based optimization algorithm make the method practically applicable.
*   Promising results: The experimental results demonstrate the potential of the LAND, particularly in the context of EEG data analysis.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited comparison: The lack of a comprehensive comparison against other state-of-the-art methods weakens the claim of superiority.
*   Parameter sensitivity: The sensitivity to the choice of the Gaussian kernel width and other hyperparameters needs further investigation.
*   Clarity of derivations: Some derivations could be presented more clearly and with better explanations.
*   Overstated claims: The claim of “adaptation” is a bit strong without demonstrating online learning capabilities.



**Overall assessment:**

I believe this paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable contribution to the field. However, the weaknesses outlined above prevent me from recommending acceptance in its current form. I recommend **rejection** but encourage the authors to address the concerns raised in the review, particularly the need for a more thorough comparative analysis and a more systematic investigation of the parameter sensitivity. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact. Specifically, a clear table comparing the performance of LAND against KDE, t-SNE, UMAP and GMM on both the synthetic and the real datasets would be crucial. A dedicated section on parameter tuning would also be highly beneficial.
