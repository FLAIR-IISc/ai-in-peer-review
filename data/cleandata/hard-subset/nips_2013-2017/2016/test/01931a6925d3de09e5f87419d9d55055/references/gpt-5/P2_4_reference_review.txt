Summary
The paper proposes the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), a Riemannian normal distribution defined on a learned Riemannian manifold in the ambient space. The manifold is induced by a smoothly varying metric M(x), constructed as the inverse of a local (diagonal) covariance estimated via Gaussian kernel smoothing. Given this geometry, the distribution pM(x|µ, Σ) uses geodesic distances via Log and Exp maps. The key algorithmic contributions are: (i) a Monte Carlo estimator for the normalization constant C(µ, Σ) using samples in the tangent space TµM and evaluating m(µ, v) = sqrt(|M(Expµ(v))|); (ii) a block coordinate descent MLE with gradients for µ (with a preconditioned/steepest descent step) and Σ (via Σ−1 = AᵀA); and (iii) an EM algorithm for mixtures of LANDs. Experiments on synthetic nonlinear data and EEG sleep-stage features show improved modeling/clustering over GMMs and intrinsic least-squares estimators.

Relation to prior work: The paper connects Riemannian statistics (e.g., Pennec’s Riemannian normal, intrinsic means/covariances) with manifold learning via learned metrics (Hauberg et al., Tosi et al.). Prior mixtures on known manifolds (Simo-Serra et al.) are discussed; the novelty here is using a learned manifold and explicitly normalizing each component via Monte Carlo.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and principled grounding: Replacing Euclidean distance with geodesic distance on a learned manifold yields a coherent generative model, consistent with maximum-entropy characterizations of Riemannian normals.
- Sound algorithmic design: The tangent-space Monte Carlo for C(µ, Σ) is computationally attractive compared to manifold BVPs, and the gradients for µ and Σ are derived with reasonable implementation details.
- Practical extensions: Mixtures and an EM-like procedure are provided (with normalization constants per component), addressing a common gap in manifold mixture models.
- Empirical evidence: On synthetic nonlinear data, LAND fits complex shapes with fewer components than GMMs; on EEG clustering, LAND outperforms GMM baselines in F-measure, suggesting the approach captures meaningful nonlinear structure.
- Honest discussion of limitations: The paper acknowledges non-invariance to linear transforms, the diagonal-metric restriction, and computational bottlenecks.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Change-of-variables/measure: The integral for C(µ, Σ) is rewritten as an integral over TµM with weight sqrt(|M(Expµ(v))|). In general, integrating over the manifold via normal coordinates requires the appropriate volume element in those coordinates; this often involves the pullback metric under Exp or a Jacobian term of Exp. The paper cites [15] but does not provide a derivation. Please clarify and justify that using m(µ, v) = sqrt(|M(Expµ(v))|) is correct (i.e., that the Jacobian of Exp is appropriately accounted for), or state the approximation being made and its implications.
- Metric learning and invariance: The learned metric uses only diagonal local covariance and is not invariant to linear transformations; this can be problematic in rotated/whitened coordinates. Empirically, prewhitening or using a shrunk full local covariance (or diagonal+low-rank) could improve robustness; the paper should discuss this trade-off and provide sensitivity analyses or a mitigation (e.g., pre-whitening).
- Bandwidth selection: σ is central. The current practice (“as small as possible while allowing geodesic computation”; per-subject tuning for EEG) is ad hoc and may favor LAND over baselines. Provide a principled selection strategy (e.g., cross-validated likelihood/F-measure), ensure comparable tuning for GMMs, and report sensitivity curves.
- Monte Carlo variance and complexity: S = 3000 is fixed without variance analysis. The paper should quantify the bias/variance of Ĉ and gradients as a function of S, and report wall-clock time and scaling (N, D, K) relative to GMMs. The per-iteration cost is dominated by many Exp evaluations; empirical timing would help assess practicality.
- Optimization details: Step-size choices, convergence criteria, and stopping conditions are under-specified (Algorithm 1 is truncated). Provide full details and robustness analysis (e.g., conditioning of Σ, effect of preconditioning, line search vs fixed steps).
- Experimental evaluation: 
  - Synthetic evaluation metric is described ambiguously (“mean negative log-likelihood of the true generative distribution using samples from the fitted model”); a more standard evaluation is test log-likelihood of held-out data under the fitted model or KL-estimates in a common direction. Please clarify and, ideally, add held-out likelihoods.
  - Baselines for EEG clustering are limited. Including manifold-learning baselines (e.g., spectral clustering on Laplacian embeddings, GMM on Isomap/LLE/Kernel PCA embeddings) would strengthen the case. Report per-subject variability and statistical significance (means ± std).
  - The mixture LAND EM is relegated to the supplement; at least a concise description and key updates should be in the main text.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with clear derivations for gradients and a reasonable algorithm; however, the measure/change-of-variable point is crucial and needs clarification. Empirical results are promising but would benefit from stronger baselines, clearer metrics, and computational profiling.

Clarity
Generally well written and well organized, with helpful intuition and references. Some notational density and missing details (Algorithm 1 termination, step sizes, ρ, ODE solver/tolerances) hinder full reproducibility. Please expand these in the final version.

Originality
Moderate to high. The distribution itself is known on fixed manifolds, but using a learned Riemannian metric to define a normalized, trainable generative model (and mixture) is a novel and useful synthesis for the NeurIPS community.

Significance
Moderate. The approach could impact generative modeling and clustering on data with nonlinear structure, and it opens a path to “Riemannianizing” standard models. Adoption may be tempered by computational cost and hyperparameter sensitivity unless those are further addressed.

Pros for acceptance
- Principled, well-motivated extension of Gaussians to learned Riemannian geometries
- Handles normalization properly and provides MLE and mixture training
- Demonstrates clear gains on nonlinear synthetic data and real EEG features
- Bridges two active areas (manifold learning and Riemannian statistics)

Cons for acceptance
- Unclear/insufficiently justified change-of-variables for normalization integral
- Sensitivity to σ and lack of principled selection; limited invariance due to diagonal metric
- Limited baselines and evaluation details; no timing/scaling analysis
- Some missing optimization and implementation details

Questions for authors
- Please provide a derivation or citation that the tangent-space integral with m(µ, v) = sqrt(|M(Expµ(v))|) is exact, or quantify the approximation.
- How sensitive are results to S (Monte Carlo samples), σ, and initialization? Any adaptive schemes?
- What is the computational cost per iteration and per data point compared to GMM? Provide timings.
- Would prewhitening or using a shrunk full local covariance improve performance/invariance?
