Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), which replaces the Euclidean Mahalanobis distance in a Gaussian with a geodesic distance induced by a learned Riemannian metric on RD. The metric is constructed from data as the inverse of a local diagonal covariance estimated by Gaussian kernel smoothing, yielding a smoothly varying metric tensor M(x). Using Pennec’s Riemannian normal as the model, the authors tackle two core obstacles: (i) the normalization constant depends on the metric and varies with mean and covariance; they approximate it with Monte Carlo integration over the tangent space at the mean using samples v ~ N(0, Σ) and weighting by sqrt|M(Expµ(v))|; (ii) maximum-likelihood estimation of mean and covariance, for which they derive gradients and propose a block coordinate descent scheme, using exponential/logarithm maps computed by solving ODEs. They also extend to mixtures (EM with component-specific normalizers). Empirically, LAND beats GMMs and least-squares “intrinsic” estimators on nonlinear synthetic data and improves F-measure in clustering of EEG sleep-stage features.

This connects Riemannian statistics (Pennec; Zhang & Fletcher) to manifold learning (Isomap/LLE; Hauberg et al.’s learned metrics), but differs by using a learned metric (not a known, fixed manifold) and by addressing the intractable normalizer with a scalable tangent-space Monte Carlo estimator.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: a clean, principled way to make Gaussians locally adaptive by operating on a learned Riemannian manifold, preserving a maximum-entropy characterization conditioned on mean and covariance.
- Sound geometric formulation: the use of Exp/Log, the tangent-space change of variables in the normalizer, and the explicit dependence of C(µ, Σ) on curvature are all correct and well-justified.
- Practical algorithm: block coordinate descent with gradients; mixture extension with EM; avoids prohibitively expensive manifold integration by sampling in TµM and using IVPs rather than BVPs where possible.
- Empirical evidence that LAND avoids the “intrinsic mean in low-density regions” pathology and captures non-elliptical clusters better than GMMs.
- Clear articulation of limitations and future directions (e.g., learning σ; computational cost of Log maps).

Weaknesses
- Metric design is restrictive and not invariant: only diagonal local covariances with an isotropic kernel. This makes results sensitive to feature scaling and linear transforms; performance could degrade without careful pre-whitening.
- Computational scalability: although the normalizer uses IVPs, the objective and gradients require Logµ(xn) for all data at each iteration, i.e., many BVP solves. The paper acknowledges this, but does not provide runtime, complexity analysis, or engineering tricks (e.g., nearest-neighbor truncation for M, warm-started shooting, caching, or stochastic subsampling).
- Hyperparameter selection: σ is tuned per subject by picking the best performing value for clustering, which favors LAND. An unsupervised, likelihood-based selection or cross-validation would be fairer; joint learning of σ would be better.
- Limited baselines and metrics: for clustering, comparison to manifold-aware clustering (e.g., spectral clustering, diffusion K-means) or density models (e.g., KDE on graph geodesics) is missing. No train/test likelihood evaluation on real data.
- The Monte Carlo estimator: no analysis of variance, bias, or convergence vs. S; reuse of common random numbers across iterations, control variates, or Laplace approximations could reduce variance but are not explored.

Quality
- Technical correctness: formulations are consistent with Riemannian theory. The tangent-space integration for C(µ, Σ) is appropriate, and the gradients are plausible; details deferred to supplements.
- Empirical support: synthetic experiments convincingly show advantages where nonlinearity matters. Real-data experiment is suggestive but limited; reliance on F-measure and post-hoc σ tuning weakens the claim.
- Missing details: complexity and runtime; robustness to σ and ρ; numerical stability (condition number of Σ and choice of steepest descent) beyond brief comments.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized. The geometric background is succinct but sufficient for readers familiar with Riemannian methods.
- Helpful algorithm description; however, reproducibility suffers from missing step-size choices, stopping criteria, and implementation details for geodesic solvers. The EM details are in the supplement; a brief outline in the main text would aid readability.

Originality
- The specific combination—learned Riemannian metric + Riemannian normal + tractable normalizer via tangent-space Monte Carlo + ML estimation and mixture EM—is, to my knowledge, novel relative to prior work that assumed known manifolds. The idea is simple but nontrivial and practically useful.

Significance
- If computational hurdles are addressed, this could impact a range of generative models by swapping Euclidean Gaussians with LANDs. The work highlights a principled path to “manifold-aware” generative modeling without embeddings. Current significance is moderate: compelling concept and early results, but broader adoption will depend on efficiency, invariance, and robust hyperparameter learning.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Provide runtime and complexity: number of IVP/BVP solves per iteration, wall-clock for datasets, and scalability with N, D, K, and S.
- Invariance and preprocessing: do you recommend whitening? How sensitive is performance to arbitrary linear transforms?
- Normalizer variance: report ablations for S and variance reduction techniques (fixed random seeds across iterations, control variates, quasi-MC). Consider a Laplace approximation around v=0 as a baseline.
- Learning σ (and ρ): can you differentiate through M and m(µ, v) to optimize σ by ML?
- Approximations for Logµ(x): can shooting with autodiff, cached geodesics, or local second-order expansions reduce BVP cost?
- Stronger baselines on real data and held-out likelihoods would strengthen claims.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, principled contribution at the intersection of Riemannian statistics and manifold learning.
- Novel, implementable estimator for the intractable normalizer.
- Solid synthetic results demonstrating clear advantages.

Cons for acceptance
- Scalability and practical efficiency not convincingly addressed; reliance on expensive Log maps.
- Sensitivity to σ and lack of invariant metric design.
- Limited baselines and somewhat favorable hyperparameter selection in real-data experiments.

Overall recommendation
Leaning toward weak accept. The paper presents a neat and principled idea with credible technical development and promising evidence. Addressing efficiency and hyperparameter selection more rigorously, plus adding stronger baselines, would make this a stronger accept.
