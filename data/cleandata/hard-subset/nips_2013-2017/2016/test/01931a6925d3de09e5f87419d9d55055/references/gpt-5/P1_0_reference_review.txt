Summary
This paper proposes the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), a generative model that replaces the Euclidean metric in the multivariate normal with a data-driven Riemannian metric learned from the data. Concretely: (i) a smoothly varying metric tensor M(x) is defined as the inverse of a local diagonal covariance estimated via Gaussian kernel weights; (ii) the normal distribution on the learned manifold is taken to be the Riemannian normal pM(x|µ,Σ) ∝ exp(−1/2⟨Logµ(x), Σ−1 Logµ(x)⟩) with respect to the induced volume element; (iii) the intractable normalization constant C(µ,Σ) is approximated by Monte Carlo over the tangent space using samples v ∼ N(0,Σ) and weights m(µ,v) = √|M(Expµ(v))|; (iv) ML estimates of µ and Σ are obtained via gradient-based block coordinate descent, with gradients derived for both µ and a factorization Σ−1 = AᵀA; (v) a mixture of LANDs and an EM algorithm are described (supplement). Experiments on synthetic curved clusters and on EEG sleep-stage features show improved fit/cluster alignment versus GMMs and Riemannian least-squares estimators.

Relation to prior work: The Riemannian normal distribution and intrinsic means are classical (Pennec; Karcher). Prior Riemannian mixtures typically assume a known manifold and often ignore normalization constants. This work adapts the manifold to the data via a learned metric (as in Hauberg et al.) and couples it with ML estimation of a Riemannian normal, including a scalable treatment of normalization.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual bridge between manifold learning and Riemannian statistics, yielding a generative model that adapts to nonlinear structure without embedding.
- Practical ML estimation scheme: avoiding BVPs by integrating on the tangent space and using IVPs for Exp maps is computationally sensible.
- Well-motivated gradients: the µ update compares empirical tangent means to model moments under the MC estimate; the Σ update is stabilized via Σ−1 = AᵀA.
- Mixture extension is natural and useful.
- Experiments show the method can capture non-elliptical clusters and improve clustering of EEG sleep stages.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
1) Normalization via tangent-space change of variables: Eq. (9) asserts C(µ,Σ) = ∫TµM √|M(Expµ(v))| exp(−1/2⟨v,Σ−1v⟩) dv. In general, the change-of-variables x = Expµ(v) requires the Jacobian determinant of the exponential map |det d Expµ(v)| (the volume density), not just √|M(·)|. The Riemannian volume form in local coordinates is √|M(x)| dx, but when switching variables from x to v, the full factor is θµ(v) = √|M(Expµ(v))| · |det d Expµ(v)| relative to a fixed basis at µ. Many references (e.g., Pennec 2006; standard Riemannian geometry texts) discuss this volume density and its curvature dependence. Please clarify whether you are (a) implicitly using coordinates where |det d Expµ| ≡ 1 (which generally does not hold beyond the origin), (b) approximating the Jacobian by 1, or (c) missing this factor. This affects both C and its gradients.

2) Global integration over TµM: The Exp map is not globally injective; integrating over all of TµM can double-count beyond the injectivity radius/cut locus. You state D(µ) = RD for “the manifolds we consider,” but here M is learned over RD and may have nontrivial curvature. Please justify this assumption or bound the mass outside the injectivity radius (e.g., for small Σ).

3) Metric design limitations and invariance: The metric is diagonal, kernel-smoothed, and not invariant to linear transformations. This can yield geodesics sensitive to scaling/rotation and miss important local correlations. At minimum, recommend data whitening, discuss the tradeoff, and, if possible, provide results with a low-rank or locally full metric (even on small D).

4) Choice of σ and model selection: In EEG experiments, reporting the best result over σ ∈ [0.5,1.5] without validation risks optimistic bias. A principled selection (held-out likelihood, stability across subjects, or joint optimization of σ) would strengthen the claims. The paper also suggests σ as small as possible “while geodesics can be computed,” which needs a more operational definition.

5) Computational characterization: The complexity depends on S IVP solves per gradient step and per component. There is no runtime comparison versus GMMs or ablation on S. A wall-clock/runtime scaling vs N, D, S, and K would be helpful, as would discussion of variance reduction (e.g., control variates or quasi-MC) and adaptive S.

6) Experimental clarity: The synthetic evaluation states “we compute the mean negative log-likelihood of the true generative distribution using these samples,” which reads like estimating E_{p_model}[−log p_true], rather than the more standard E_{p_true}[−log p_model]. Please clarify the evaluation protocol. Also, some figures/tables (e.g., Table 1) are referred to but not shown here.

Quality
- The overall approach is technically sound and well-motivated. The gradient expressions are plausible and consistent with moment-matching intuition.
- However, the normalization constant derivation appears to omit the Jacobian of Exp, which is a substantive theoretical issue that should be addressed or justified as an approximation, with an empirical assessment of its impact.
- Experimental evidence is promising but limited in scope and missing some details on evaluation and computational cost.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized. The geometric preliminaries are concise and useful.
- Some typos/formatting (e.g., “.1 Synthetic Data Experiments”), missing figure/table details in the main text, and a few informal choices (σ selection) could be improved.
- Please add a brief complexity discussion and a more explicit algorithmic description of ODE solvers and step-size selection.

Originality
- The combination of data-driven Riemannian metric learning with ML estimation of a Riemannian normal and a practical MC normalization is novel relative to prior work that assumes known manifolds or ignores normalization.
- The mixture extension is natural but not conceptually new; the contribution lies in making it workable on learned manifolds.

Significance
- Conceptually significant: offers a path to bring many Gaussian-based generative tools to learned manifolds.
- Practically promising on low- to moderate-dimensional problems; scalability to high-D is limited by the metric design and ODE costs.

Questions and suggestions
- Clarify the role of |det d Expµ(v)| in Eq. (9); if approximated away, provide rationale and experiments quantifying the bias.
- Discuss injectivity radius/cut locus and any heuristics to restrict integration to regions where Exp is well-behaved.
- Provide runtime comparisons and sensitivity to S.
- Consider learning σ (and possibly metric class) via marginal likelihood or bilevel optimization; or at least cross-validate σ.
- Recommend preprocessing to mitigate lack of invariance (e.g., whitening), and consider testing a low-rank local metric.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and principled link between manifold learning and Riemannian generative modeling.
- Clear algorithm with encouraging results on nontrivial data.
- Potentially useful building block for manifold-aware versions of classic models.

Cons for acceptance
- Potentially incorrect/incomplete normalization due to missing Jacobian of Exp.
- Limited analysis of computational cost, scalability, and sensitivity to hyperparameters.
- Some evaluation choices and clarity issues.

Recommendation
Borderline accept if the authors can clarify/correct the normalization constant derivation (or justify the approximation) and add a brief computational analysis; otherwise borderline reject. The conceptual contribution is strong, but the theoretical point about the volume element needs resolution.
