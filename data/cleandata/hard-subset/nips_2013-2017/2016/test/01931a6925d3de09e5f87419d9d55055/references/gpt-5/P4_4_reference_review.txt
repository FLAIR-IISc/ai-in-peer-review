Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), which replaces the Euclidean metric in the Gaussian density with a geodesic distance induced by a learned Riemannian metric on RD. The metric is constructed as a smoothly varying, diagonal tensor defined as the inverse of a local, kernel-weighted covariance estimate (with Gaussian kernel bandwidth σ and regularization ρ). This yields geodesics that are attracted to high-density regions, thereby modeling nonlinear data structure without changing dimensionality.

The probability density is the Riemannian normal distribution on the learned manifold: pM(x | µ, Σ) ∝ exp(−1/2 ⟨Logµ(x), Σ−1 Logµ(x)⟩). Unlike prior work where the manifold is known a priori (e.g., Pennec’s Riemannian normal, Riemannian PPCA), this paper learns the metric from data in the spirit of manifold learning (e.g., Isomap, Hauberg et al.). A key technical contribution is an efficient Monte Carlo approximation of the normalization constant C(µ, Σ) by integrating over the tangent space at µ with proposal N(0, Σ), using the exponential map to account for the induced measure. The paper derives gradients for maximum-likelihood estimation of µ and Σ (with Σ−1 = AᵀA), proposes a block coordinate descent method, and extends to mixtures with an EM-style algorithm (details in the supplement).

Experiments include synthetic nonlinear densities (where LAND outperforms GMMs and Riemannian least-squares estimators) and EEG sleep-stage clustering in a 5D feature space, where LAND improves F-measure over GMMs and benefits from tuning σ.

Quality
- Technical correctness: The use of a learned Riemannian metric to define a normal distribution follows established differential geometric foundations. The normalization constant is correctly reformulated as an expectation under N(0, Σ) with importance weight m(µ, v) = √|M(Expµ(v))|. Gradients for µ and A appear consistent with standard results (gradient of squared Riemannian distance equals −2 Logµ(x), subject to injectivity assumptions). Using steepest-descent direction dµ = −Σ∇µ addresses conditioning.
- Completeness: The paper provides a practical MLE algorithm with all needed components (geodesics, Exp/Log maps, normalization approximation, gradients), plus a mixture extension. However, key details (mixture EM derivation, initialization, complexity, stability) are deferred to the supplement.
- Empirical support: Synthetic tests illustrate advantages in modeling curved manifolds. Real-data results on EEG (10 subjects; 5D features) show improved clustering alignment over GMMs and sensitivity to σ. The empirical study is informative but limited in scope and baselines.
- Limitations: The learned metric is diagonal and not affine invariant; performance depends on σ and feature scaling. Computational cost may be high: each likelihood/gradient evaluation requires S exponential maps (ODE IVPs), repeated multiple times per iteration and component; no runtime or scaling analysis is provided. The variance and bias of the Monte Carlo estimator for C and its gradients are not quantified; reuse of samples and variance reduction are not discussed. The assumption that D(µ) = RD (global Log map) may not hold in general; injectivity radius and cut-locus issues are not addressed.

Clarity
The manuscript is generally clear: background on Riemannian geometry, definition of the metric, change-of-variables for C(µ, Σ), and optimization steps are well explained. Some notation and presentation could be improved:
- Explicitly state computational complexity per iteration in terms of S, N, K, and the ODE solver cost.
- Provide guidance on choosing σ and ρ and discuss feature scaling.
- Clarify numerical stability and convergence behavior (e.g., sample reuse across iterations, variance reduction for C and ∇ log C).
- Minor editorial issues in references and formatting.

Originality
The Riemannian normal distribution is known (Pennec), and mixture models on known manifolds exist. The novelty here lies in:
- Learning a smooth, data-adaptive metric to bridge manifold learning with Riemannian statistics for generative modeling.
- A practical normalization strategy via tangent-space Monte Carlo for learned manifolds.
This constitutes an incremental but meaningful contribution that enables maximum-likelihood training of Riemannian Gaussians on learned geometries.

Significance
The approach provides a principled way to make Gaussian models locally adaptive along data manifolds, with potential impact on density modeling and clustering. The empirical evidence suggests advantages over GMMs on curved structures. Broader significance would be strengthened by:
- Demonstrations on higher-dimensional data and more varied domains.
- Comparisons to additional baselines (e.g., kernel density estimators with geodesic distances, metric-learning-based GMMs, probabilistic manifold models).
- Runtime/scalability analysis and variance control for the Monte Carlo step.

Pros
- Conceptually clean bridge between manifold learning and Riemannian statistics.
- Proper treatment of the normalization constant on a learned manifold.
- Efficient tangent-space importance sampling avoids expensive Log-map BVPs.
- Derivation of gradients and practical MLE algorithm; extension to mixtures.
- Empirical gains on nonlinear synthetic data and EEG clustering.

Cons
- Dependence on a hand-crafted, diagonal, bandwidth-controlled metric; lack of affine invariance and sensitivity to σ.
- Computational cost and scalability left unquantified; many ODE solves per iteration.
- Limited theoretical analysis (e.g., identifiability, consistency, injectivity/cut-locus issues).
- Modest empirical scope and baselines; limited ablations (S, σ, ρ, diagonal vs. full metric).
- Some details deferred to the supplement; minor clarity and referencing issues.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide complexity and runtime benchmarks, and investigate variance reduction (e.g., control variates, quasi-MC, sample reuse).
- Analyze sensitivity to σ and propose data-driven selection or learning of σ; consider more expressive metrics (e.g., low-rank or full, mixture of metrics).
- Discuss injectivity radius and practical handling of multi-valued Log, if relevant.
- Expand baselines and datasets; include ablations and robustness studies.
- Improve clarity on implementation details and release code for reproducibility.

Overall, the paper offers a technically sound and useful extension of Gaussian modeling to learned Riemannian manifolds, with clear potential and some practical limitations that should be addressed to strengthen impact.
