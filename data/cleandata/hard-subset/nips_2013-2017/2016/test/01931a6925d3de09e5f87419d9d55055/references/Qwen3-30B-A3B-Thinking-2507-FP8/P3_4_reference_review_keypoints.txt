- Summary: Proposes the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), replacing Euclidean distance with a data-driven Riemannian metric to model nonlinear manifolds, with Monte Carlo normalization, gradient MLE, and an EM mixture model.

- Technical novelty: Learns a continuous Riemannian metric from local covariances (Gaussian kernel) and defines a manifold normal distribution adaptable to data geometry.

- Normalization trick: Derives a clever tangent-space Monte Carlo integration (Eq.9) to compute the normalization constant without solving boundary value problems.

- Estimation: Provides a well-motivated, gradient-based maximum likelihood procedure (Algorithm 1) and an EM algorithm for mixtures.

- Empirical validation: Shows clear advantages over Gaussian mixtures and Riemannian least-squares on synthetic nonlinear manifolds and EEG sleep-stage data.

- Theoretical limitation: Claims about maximum-entropy property are incomplete—true only for fixed known manifolds, not clearly for data-adaptive metrics.

- Computational concern: Geodesic computations (ODE solves) are acknowledged but scalability and complexity for high dimensions/larger datasets are not quantified.

- Clarity: Paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear exposition, precise algorithmic details, and effective visualizations facilitating reproduction.

- Originality: Novel adaptation of Riemannian normal distributions to a data-driven manifold learning setting; metric design via local covariances is creative.

- Practical contribution: Monte Carlo normalization scheme and continuous metric definition are useful, practical advances.

- Significance: Extends the foundational multivariate normal to nonlinear structures, with potential to generalize many classical algorithms (Naive Bayes, LDA, PCA) to manifolds.

- Strength 1: Strong theoretical foundation bridging Riemannian statistics and manifold learning.

- Strength 2: Practical, scalable normalization via tangent-space Monte Carlo integration.

- Strength 3: Convincing empirical demonstrations showing advantages over baselines.

- Strength 4: Addresses limitations of linear normal distributions with a well-justified approach.

- Strength 5: Thorough discussion of limitations and future work.

- Weakness 1: Incomplete treatment of how a data-adaptive metric affects the maximum-entropy characterization.

- Weakness 2: Limited discussion and analysis of scalability to high-dimensional data where local covariance estimation may fail.

- Weakness 3: Kernel bandwidth (σ) choice appears ad-hoc with no systematic selection method presented.

- Weakness 4: Computational cost and complexity of geodesic calculations are not quantified.

- Weakness 5: Limited theoretical analysis of parameter estimation convergence.

- Recommendation: Accept — significant, well-executed contribution with high technical quality and convincing empirical results despite noted limitations.

- Minor suggestion 1: Clarify the maximum-entropy relationship for data-adaptive metrics in Section 3.3.

- Minor suggestion 2: Expand discussion of scalability to higher dimensions, referencing probabilistic manifold learning approaches.

- Minor suggestion 3: Provide more detail on σ selection in the EEG experiments.
