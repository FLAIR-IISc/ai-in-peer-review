Summary and relation to prior work
The paper introduces the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), a generative model obtained by replacing the Euclidean metric in the multivariate normal with a data-adaptive Riemannian metric. The authors construct a smoothly varying diagonal metric tensor M(x) from local kernel-weighted covariances, which induces a Riemannian manifold on RD. They then model data via the Riemannian normal distribution pM(x|µ,Σ) ∝ exp(−1/2 ⟨Logµ(x), Σ−1 Logµ(x)⟩), where distances and measure are defined by the learned metric. A key technical contribution is a scalable Monte Carlo estimator for the normalization constant C(µ,Σ), expressed as an expectation over the tangent space at µ using the exponential map: C = ∫Tµ √|M(Expµ(v))| exp(−1/2 v⊤Σ−1v) dv ≈ Z · S−1 ∑s √|M(Expµ(vs))| with vs ∼ N(0,Σ). Maximum likelihood estimation is performed via block coordinate descent: µ is updated along geodesics using a steepest descent direction, and Σ is updated via a factorization Σ−1 = A⊤A with a derived gradient. A mixture of LANDs with an EM algorithm is also presented. Experiments on synthetic data and EEG sleep-stage clustering show improved likelihood and clustering alignment compared to Gaussian mixtures and least-squares (intrinsic) Riemannian estimators.

This work builds on: Riemannian normal distributions and intrinsic statistics (Pennec), Riemannian probabilistic modeling (e.g., probabilistic principal geodesic analysis by Zhang & Fletcher), and manifold learning (Isomap, LLE, Laplacian Eigenmaps). It is closely related to learned probabilistic geometries and metric learning on manifolds (Hauberg et al., Tosi et al.) and to mixture models on known manifolds (Simo-Serra et al., Straub et al.). The main novelty is to use a learned, continuous Riemannian geometry in RD to define a properly normalized generative density, together with tractable estimation via Monte Carlo over the tangent space.

Strengths
- Conceptual advance: A principled way to generalize Gaussians to data lying near nonlinear structures by learning a Riemannian geometry and using the Riemannian normal, which is maximum entropy for fixed mean and covariance.
- Technical contributions: (i) Rewriting the normalization constant as a tangent-space integral enables efficient Monte Carlo using only exponential maps (IVPs), avoiding repeated logarithm maps (BVPs). (ii) Derivations of gradients for µ and Σ and a practical block-coordinate scheme. (iii) An EM formulation for mixtures that accounts for component-wise normalization constants.
- Empirical evidence: On synthetic manifolds, LAND captures non-ellipsoidal structure with fewer components than GMMs and outperforms intrinsic least-squares estimators that overestimate spread. On EEG sleep-stage features, LAND yields higher F-measure alignment than GMMs across subjects, with further gains when σ is tuned.
- Addresses known pitfalls: Unlike intrinsic means that can lie in low-density regions, the ML estimator penalizes low-density placements via the normalization term, empirically avoiding such issues.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Change-of-variables and integration domain: The expression C = ∫Tµ √|M(Expµ(v))| exp(−1/2 v⊤Σ−1v) dv assumes normal coordinates cover the domain of integration without addressing the cut locus/injectivity radius. A discussion or lemma clarifying validity conditions (e.g., that contributions outside the normal neighborhood are negligible due to the Gaussian weight, or how multi-valued Log is handled) would strengthen the theory.
- Computational scalability: Each evaluation of C requires S exponential maps; each gradient step also needs Logµ(xn) for all n. Complexity and wall-clock runtime as functions of N, D, S, and ODE tolerances are not reported. Guidance on practical scalability and amortization strategies (e.g., sample reuse, control variates, mini-batching) would be valuable.
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: The learned metric depends on σ and ρ. The paper fixes S=3000 and heuristically sets σ; results improve when σ is tuned. A more systematic treatment (e.g., ML over σ, cross-validation, or Bayesian treatment) and sensitivity analysis to S and σ would improve robustness.
- Metric design: The diagonal local covariance is computationally convenient but not invariant to linear transformations and may miss important local anisotropies. Discussion of when this simplification suffices, and results with full or low-rank local covariances or pre-whitening, would be informative.
- Optimization details: The objective is nonconvex and the algorithm uses hand-tuned step sizes. There is no convergence analysis or empirical ablation (e.g., effect of steepest-descent preconditioning, number of Monte Carlo samples on stability).
- Baselines and breadth of evaluation: Comparisons are primarily against GMMs and LS estimators. Additional baselines such as kernel density estimates, mixtures of factor analyzers, or manifold-aware clustering/densities (where applicable) would contextualize gains. The real-data evaluation is limited to a single application.
- Presentation: Notational overloading of M for both manifold and metric can confuse readers. Some references are incomplete or contain errors.

Quality
The modeling framework is sound and grounded in Riemannian statistics. The Monte Carlo approach for normalization is plausible and practically motivated. However, formal justification of the tangent-space integral over the entire TµM (cut locus issues) and a more systematic analysis of estimator variance and computational complexity are missing. Experiments support the claims but are somewhat limited in scope and baselines.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and readable, with clear algorithms and intuition. Clarity would improve by: separating symbols for manifold and metric; explicitly stating assumptions for the integral transformation; detailing numerical ODE settings; and providing more guidance on hyperparameter selection.

Originality
Using a learned Riemannian metric to define a globally normalized generative model and estimating its normalizer via tangent-space Monte Carlo is, to my knowledge, novel. The connection between manifold learning and Riemannian normal models is original and well motivated relative to prior work on known manifolds and metric learning.

Significance
The approach has the potential to impact generative modeling and clustering when data concentrate near nonlinear structures. The idea is broadly applicable (mixtures, extensions to other models) and could stimulate further work at the intersection of manifold learning and Riemannian statistics. Demonstrated gains are encouraging, though more comprehensive evaluation would better establish practical significance.

Pros for acceptance
- Principled, general framework bridging manifold learning and Riemannian statistics.
- Practical algorithmic contributions (normalizer estimation via exponential map; gradient derivations).
- Empirical improvements on synthetic and real data.
- Potential for broad applicability (mixtures; extensions to common models).

Cons for acceptance
- Theoretical gaps regarding integration domain and measure change.
- Limited analysis of computational complexity, scalability, and estimator variance.
- Sensitivity to metric hyperparameters; lack of automated selection.
- Restricted baselines and application breadth; some clarity and referencing issues.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide conditions and discussion on the validity of the tangent-space integral (injectivity/normal neighborhood, cut locus) and practical handling.
- Report runtime, scaling with N/D/S, and ODE solver settings; consider variance reduction and sample reuse.
- Explore learning σ (and ρ) or provide robust selection guidelines; include sensitivity plots for S and σ.
- Consider more expressive local metrics or preprocessing to address linear invariance.
- Expand baselines and datasets; include ablations on mixture EM and component-wise normalizers.
- Clean up notation and references; consider releasing code to aid reproducibility.

Scope
The work is in scope for NeurIPS: it addresses probabilistic modeling, manifold learning, and optimization with applications in real data analysis.
