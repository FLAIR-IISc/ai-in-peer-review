Summary
The paper proposes a Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND) by replacing the Euclidean Mahalanobis distance in a Gaussian with a geodesic distance induced by a learned Riemannian metric. The metric is constructed from data via a kernel-weighted local diagonal covariance (with regularization), yielding a smoothly varying SPD tensor. Densities are defined as Riemannian normals p(x|µ,Σ) ∝ exp(-0.5⟨Logµ(x), Σ^{-1} Logµ(x)⟩) with normalization constant computed by Monte Carlo integration over the tangent space using the volume element √|M(Expµ(v))|. Maximum likelihood estimation for µ and Σ is performed via block coordinate descent with gradients that involve expectations under a Gaussian proposal reweighted by m(µ,v)=√|M(Expµ(v))|. A mixture version and an EM-like scheme are sketched. Experiments on synthetic data and an EEG sleep-stage clustering task suggest improvements over GMMs and over least-squares (intrinsic mean/covariance) estimators.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: Euclidean Gaussians are ill-suited for data near nonlinear manifolds; Riemannian geodesics can better respect local structure.
- Bridges Riemannian statistics (Riemannian normals) with learned metrics from manifold learning; computing C by integrating on the tangent space is natural and computationally preferable to BVPs.
- The approach accounts for normalization constants per component (often glossed over in related work).
- Synthetic examples and the REM/awake/non-REM clustering show promise.

Weaknesses
- The learned metric: Only diagonal local covariances are used, making the metric highly coordinate-dependent and not affine-invariant. This is a serious limitation in practice; local correlations are ignored. The paper acknowledges this but does not quantify the impact or provide mitigations (e.g., preprocessing, whitening, low-rank local covariances).
- Theoretical guarantees are thin. There is no analysis of existence/uniqueness of geodesics, completeness, or the domain of the log map for the learned metric. Earlier the paper asserts D(µ)=R^D for the manifolds considered, but this is not justified for a generic data-adapted metric. Convergence guarantees for the proposed optimization are absent.
- Normalization and gradients: C(µ,Σ) is estimated by MC with S=3000 samples, yet there is no variance analysis, no control variates/variance-reduction, and no error bars in experiments. The gradient contains a ratio Z/C times an empirical moment; the resulting self-normalized estimator is biased, but this is not discussed. Sensitivity to S and to Σ’s conditioning is only briefly mentioned. The switch to a “steepest descent” direction dµ = -Σ∇µφ is ad hoc and lacks justification or line-search details.
- Scalability: Each evaluation of M(Expµ(v)) requires summing kernel weights over all data points. There is no complexity analysis, no acceleration (e.g., anchors/inducing points, kNN truncation), and no runtime comparisons. Claiming scalability seems premature.
- Experimental validation is limited and not entirely fair:
  - Synthetic: Only compares to GMM and LS estimators. Missing baselines such as kernel density estimators with adaptive bandwidths, mixtures in warped spaces (e.g., GPLVM-based metrics), manifold-aware mixtures, or normalizing flows. Sensitivity to σ and S is relegated to supplements; no ablations in main.
  - EEG: The metric bandwidth σ is fixed to 1 in one setting, but then the paper reports “best σ” per subject over a grid, which effectively tunes to labels for the LAND but not for GMM, confounding the comparison. The number of mixture components K and model selection are not systematically controlled across methods. No statistical significance or confidence intervals are reported.
- Practical details are missing: step sizes, stopping criteria, initialization specifics, ODE solvers and tolerances, and mixture EM details are mostly in the supplement. Reproducibility is uncertain.
- Evaluation metric choice: For clustering, only F-measure to labels is reported; other standard baselines for sleep-stage clustering (HMMs, spectral clustering, mixture of factor analyzers) are absent.
- The method learns the metric and fits the density on the same data, raising concerns about double-dipping/overfitting; no held-out likelihoods are reported.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide conditions under which your learned metric ensures geodesic completeness and a well-defined Log map with D(µ)=R^D? If not, how often do numerical failures occur?
- What is the computational complexity per iteration (as a function of N, D, K, S)? Any acceleration strategies (kNN kernels, inducing points, or low-rank updates)?
- How sensitive are results to S and to σ? Please include ablation plots with error bars and runtime-accuracy trade-offs.
- For EEG, how were K and σ selected for GMMs? Did you tune GMMs similarly (e.g., covariance types, regularization)? Please avoid using label information when selecting σ.
- Have you validated the C(µ,Σ) estimator by comparing to the Euclidean case (flat metric) where C=Z?
- Can you incorporate non-diagonal local covariances (possibly low-rank) or perform a data whitening step to reduce coordinate dependence?

Assessment w.r.t. NeurIPS criteria
- Quality: The construction is reasonable and builds on established Riemannian tools, but the lack of theoretical guarantees, convergence analysis, and comprehensive empirical validation weakens the technical soundness. Key implementation details and ablations are missing from the main paper.
- Clarity: Generally readable, but several crucial details are deferred; some notational and algorithmic choices (e.g., “steepest descent” preconditioning) are not well motivated.
- Originality: Moderate. Riemannian normals are known; learning metrics from data is also known. The main novelty is combining a learned metric with ML estimation of a Riemannian normal via tangent-space MC normalization and applying it as a drop-in Gaussian replacement. This is an incremental but interesting combination.
- Significance: Potentially useful as a conceptual bridge, but evidence is limited. Without stronger baselines, scalability, and robustness analyses, it is hard to conclude impact beyond small to medium datasets.

Pros
- Interesting idea that could be a practical building block.
- Proper accounting for normalization constants in mixtures.
- Uses established geometry; conceptually clean.

Cons
- Weak theoretical guarantees and convergence analysis.
- Strong coordinate dependence due to diagonal metric; not affine-invariant.
- Scalability and variance issues not addressed.
- Experimental comparisons and tuning not fully fair or comprehensive.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper presents an appealing idea, but the current version lacks the rigor, scalability analysis, and thorough empirical validation (with fair baselines and ablations) needed to support its claims. Strengthening theory (or at least diagnostics and failure modes), providing efficient implementations/accelerations, and conducting more comprehensive experiments would significantly improve the case for acceptance.

Confidence
Medium.
