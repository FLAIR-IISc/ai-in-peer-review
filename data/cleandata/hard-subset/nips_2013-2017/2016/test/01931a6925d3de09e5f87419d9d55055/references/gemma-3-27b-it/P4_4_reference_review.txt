This paper introduces a Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND) which aims to model data lying on nonlinear manifolds by replacing the Euclidean distance with a learned Riemannian metric. The core idea is to leverage Riemannian geometry to define a distribution that adapts to the underlying data structure, providing a more accurate representation than a standard normal distribution when applied to nonlinear data. The authors provide a scalable Monte Carlo integration scheme for normalization and a gradient-based algorithm for parameter estimation, including an extension to mixtures of LANDs. The method is validated on both synthetic data and EEG measurements of human sleep stages.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly identifying the limitations of standard normal distributions when dealing with data residing on nonlinear manifolds. The connection to Riemannian geometry provides a strong theoretical foundation. The proposed approach bridges manifold learning and Riemannian statistics, which is a valuable contribution. The methodology is presented in a logical order, starting with the necessary background in Riemannian geometry, then detailing the metric construction, normalization, parameter estimation, and finally, the extension to mixtures. The experimental validation on both synthetic and real-world data provides evidence of the LAND's effectiveness, particularly in capturing the underlying structure of the sleep stage data. The inclusion of comparisons to GMMs and least-squares LAND estimators strengthens the analysis.

However, several weaknesses need addressing. Firstly, while the authors acknowledge the computational cost of evaluating the logarithm maps, a more in-depth discussion of scalability limitations with respect to data dimensionality and sample size is required. The reliance on a diagonal covariance matrix for computational efficiency is a significant simplification and its impact on model expressiveness should be discussed. The choice of the Gaussian kernel for defining locality is also a potential limitation and a justification for this choice (compared to other kernels) is missing. The sensitivity of the results to the bandwidth parameter σ in the Gaussian kernel needs further investigation and a more robust method for its selection should be proposed. The algorithm's initialization is briefly mentioned, and a more detailed description and justification of the initialization procedure is warranted. Furthermore, while the authors mention improvements possible through probabilistic solvers, a more detailed discussion of the potential benefits and drawbacks of such approaches would be valuable. The experiments, while demonstrating improvement over GMMs, could be expanded to include comparisons with more recent and sophisticated manifold learning techniques (e.g., UMAP, t-SNE). Finally, the paper lacks a thorough discussion of the theoretical properties of the proposed estimator.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The LAND model offers a novel approach to distribution estimation on manifolds by integrating Riemannian geometry and manifold learning.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The methodology is mathematically well-grounded and the proposed algorithms are logically derived.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the LAND model on both synthetic and real-world data.
*   **Potential Significance:** The LAND model has the potential to improve performance in various machine learning tasks that rely on accurate density estimation in nonlinear spaces.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Scalability Concerns:** The computational cost associated with evaluating logarithm maps and the simplification of the covariance matrix raise concerns about scalability.
*   **Limited Comparison:** The comparison with existing manifold learning techniques is limited.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance of the model appears sensitive to the choice of the bandwidth parameter σ, and a more robust selection method is needed.
*   **Theoretical Gaps:** A more thorough discussion of the theoretical properties of the estimator is needed.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to distribution estimation on nonlinear manifolds. While some weaknesses related to scalability, parameter sensitivity, and theoretical analysis exist, the potential significance of the work and the positive experimental results warrant consideration for acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above. Specifically, the authors should provide a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity, the impact of the diagonal covariance assumption, and the parameter selection strategies. A more comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art manifold learning techniques would also strengthen the paper.
