Summary
The paper proposes the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), a parametric density obtained by replacing the Euclidean metric in a multivariate normal with a smoothly varying Riemannian metric learned from data. The metric is defined as the inverse of a local (diagonal) covariance estimated with Gaussian kernels, thereby yielding a Riemannian manifold where geodesics are attracted to high-density regions. The density takes the form of a Riemannian normal pM(x|µ,Σ) ∝ exp(−1/2 〈Logµ(x), Σ−1 Logµ(x)〉), but crucially the normalization constant depends on the mean and on curvature. The authors derive a scalable approximation by integrating over the tangent space at µ and using Monte Carlo with samples v ∼ N(0,Σ) to estimate C(µ,Σ) = Z E[m(µ,v)], with m(µ,v) = √|M(Expµ(v))|. They provide gradients for maximum-likelihood (ML) estimation of µ and Σ (using a factorization Σ−1 = AᵀA), a block-coordinate descent solver, and extend the model to mixtures with an EM algorithm. Experiments on synthetic non-linear manifolds and EEG sleep-stage features demonstrate improved likelihood and clustering F-measure over GMMs and over LS-based Riemannian estimators.

Relation to prior work
- Riemannian normals and intrinsic statistics (Pennec) have considered known manifolds; probabilistic geodesic models (Zhang & Fletcher) and mixtures on known manifolds (Simo-Serra et al.) are related. The novelty here is to learn the metric/manifold from data (in the spirit of Isomap and Hauberg et al.’s probabilistic geometries) and to develop an ML estimator accounting for the (µ,Σ)-dependent normalization constant.
- The learned metric (local kernel-smoothed covariance) ties to manifold learning and metric learning (e.g., Hauberg et al., Tosi et al.). The paper bridges these areas with Riemannian statistics.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean and well-motivated: a maximum-entropy Riemannian normal on a learned manifold that adapts locally to data geometry.
- Technical contribution: tangent-space Monte Carlo for the normalization constant avoids expensive Log maps inside the integral and yields practical gradients for ML estimation; clean derivation of gradients for both µ and Σ.
- Practical algorithm: block coordinate descent, mixture extension with EM.
- Empirical evidence that ML LAND avoids “intrinsic mean in low-density regions” pathology of LS estimators and captures non-ellipsoidal clusters better than GMMs.
- Clear exposition, good figures, and discussion of limitations.

Weaknesses and questions
- Scalability and computational cost: Each evaluation of m(µ,v) requires solving an ODE for Expµ(v) and computing the learned metric M at multiple points, which in turn sums kernel weights over N data points. With S=3000 samples per iteration/component and BVPs for Logµ(xn), this may be costly for large N, D, or K. Empirical runtime and scaling plots are missing.
- Metric choice limitations: Using a diagonal local covariance loses local correlations and is not invariant to linear transforms. This may reduce modeling power on correlated data. Some ablation (diagonal vs. full/shrunk covariance) or preprocessing requirements (whitening) would clarify.
- Choice of kernel bandwidth σ: In real data, σ is selected via a grid and the “best” result is reported. It is unclear whether labeled information (F-measure) influenced this choice; if so, this biases the comparison. A principled unsupervised selection (e.g., likelihood/AIC/BIC, plug-in rules, or learning σ by ML) would strengthen claims.
- Gradient estimation w.r.t. Σ: Since C(µ,Σ) = Z E_{N(0,Σ)}[m], the sampling distribution depends on Σ. The provided gradient (Eq. 13) appears to account for both the determinant term and the expectation; however, the paper delegates derivations to the supplement. Please clarify whether the estimator is unbiased and whether you use common random numbers across iterations/components to reduce variance.
- Convergence/optimization: The objective is non-convex in both blocks. There is no convergence analysis or empirical study of sensitivity to initialization, step sizes, or S. How robust is the solver? Do you reuse Monte Carlo samples across iterations to stabilize gradients?
- Baselines: Comparisons are limited to GMMs and LS Riemannian estimators. Including manifold-aware clustering (e.g., spectral clustering, Laplacian mixtures), kernel GMMs, mixtures of factor analyzers, or probabilistic geometries (Tosi et al.) would better position the contribution.
- Evaluation breadth: Real-data results are on 10 subjects with 5D features; improvements are promising but modest. Additional datasets and a likelihood-based evaluation (held-out log-likelihood) would enhance significance.

Quality
Technically sound overall and carefully motivated. The tangent-space normalization and gradients are plausible; however, key derivations are in the supplement, and the computational burden is not quantified. Experimental evidence supports the claims but could be more comprehensive and controlled (σ selection, stronger baselines).

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized. The Riemannian preliminaries and algorithm are accessible. A few suggestions:
- Explicitly state computational complexity per iteration (in N, D, K, S) and provide runtimes.
- Clarify how Log maps are computed in practice (shooting vs. BVP solver) and how often.
- Detail σ selection and whether labels were used; recommend reporting unsupervised selection results.
- Minor typos and grammar can be polished.

Originality
The idea of placing a Riemannian normal on a learned, data-adaptive manifold and providing a practical ML estimator with normalization via tangent-space Monte Carlo is, to my knowledge, new and nontrivial. The mixture extension is natural but useful. The work differentiates clearly from prior Riemannian-statistics papers on known manifolds and from embedding-based manifold learning.

Significance
If computational challenges are addressed, LAND could serve as a drop-in replacement for Gaussians in generative models, enabling nonlinear local geometry modeling. The paper advances the interface between manifold learning and probabilistic modeling, with potential impact on clustering and density estimation in moderate dimensions.

Constructive suggestions
- Explore scalable approximations: local neighborhoods for M(x), inducing points, precomputation on a grid, or Nystrom/RFF approximations; reuse CRNs for MC; control variates using small-v expansions of m(µ,v).
- Consider learning σ by maximizing the data likelihood (possibly with a prior) or via EB; analyze sensitivity to ρ.
- Investigate low-rank local covariances or shrinkage to capture correlations without overfitting.
- Add stronger baselines and held-out likelihood evaluations; report wall-clock times.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and well-motivated probabilistic model on learned manifolds.
- Solid algorithmic contribution with practical implementation details.
- Empirical gains on nonlinear synthetic data and a real EEG task.
- Bridges two active areas (Riemannian statistics and manifold learning).

Cons for acceptance
- Computational scalability and lack of runtime analysis.
- Limited baselines and potential tuning bias for σ.
- Some key derivations and algorithmic details deferred to the supplement.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The conceptual contribution is strong and relevant to NIPS, and the method is promising. Strengthening the experimental validation (scaling, baselines, σ selection) and clarifying gradient derivations would make the case more compelling.
