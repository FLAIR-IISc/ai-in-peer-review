Paper summary:
The paper proposes a Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), obtained by replacing the Euclidean distance in a Gaussian with a geodesic distance induced by a smoothly varying, data-driven Riemannian metric. The metric M(x) is learned nonparametrically as the inverse of a local diagonal covariance estimated via a Gaussian kernel. This induces a Riemannian manifold structure on RD on which geodesics, Exp/Log maps, and a Riemannian normal (Pennec, 2006) are defined. The main technical challenge—the normalization constant C(µ, Σ)—is addressed by rewriting the integral over the manifold as an expectation over the tangent space at µ, using Expµ(v) and a weight m(µ, v) = sqrt|M(Expµ(v))|, and estimating it by Monte Carlo with samples v ~ N(0, Σ). The authors derive gradients for maximum likelihood estimation of µ (via updates along Expµ) and Σ (via a factor A with Σ−1 = AᵀA), and extend the model to mixtures with an EM-like procedure. Experiments on synthetic data and an EEG sleep-stage clustering task show improved likelihood/clustering relative to GMMs and least-squares intrinsic estimators on manifolds.

Relation to prior work:
The work bridges Riemannian statistics (e.g., Pennec’s Riemannian normal and intrinsic means) with manifold learning and metric learning (Isomap; Hauberg et al.’s learned metrics). Unlike prior mixtures on known manifolds, the manifold here is learned from data and the authors attempt to handle full normalization consistently across components. This is an ambitious direction: a parametric generative model over a nonparametric, learned geometric structure.

Strengths:
- Novelty and conceptual boldness: A principled attempt to generalize the Gaussian to data-adaptive, learned geometries, with a practical recipe for both normalization and MLE.
- Technically interesting: The tangent-space Monte Carlo scheme, combined with Exp map IVPs, is elegant and relatively scalable compared to BVPs for Log. The block coordinate descent with Riemannian updates is practical.
- Empirical evidence: Synthetic experiments show the model captures nonlinear structures with fewer components than GMMs; the EEG clustering results indicate improved alignment with sleep stages, especially after tuning σ.
- Clear potential impact: If robust, LAND could be a drop-in replacement for Gaussians in many generative models.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Change-of-variables/measure issue in Eq. (9): When transforming the integral from x to v via x = Expµ(v), the Riemannian volume element in v-coordinates should involve the pullback metric, i.e., sqrt|JExpµ(v)ᵀ M(Expµ(v)) JExpµ(v)| dv = sqrt|M(Expµ(v))| |det JExpµ(v)| dv. The derivation and proposed estimator seem to omit |det JExpµ(v)|. If not accounted for by a coordinate choice (e.g., true normal coordinates with the correct metric expression), this is a serious theoretical gap: C(µ, Σ), gradients, and the EM rnk would be biased. Please clarify rigorously how Eq. (9) is obtained and whether the Jacobian of Exp is absorbed elsewhere. This point is central.
- Metric design limitations: The metric uses a diagonal local covariance and is not invariant to linear transforms; this can distort geometry substantially. No ablation vs full/low-rank metrics or invariance considerations. Sensitivity to σ is acknowledged but not learned.
- Computational scalability: Each Monte Carlo sample requires an Exp map solve (IVP). With S=3000 per evaluation, per component, per iteration, this could be heavy for large N, D, K. No complexity/timing analysis or discussion of caching/reusing samples across iterations.
- Incomplete comparisons: Baselines are primarily GMMs and LS manifold estimates. Comparisons to more flexible density models (e.g., mixtures of factor analyzers), graph-geodesic-based methods, or (for 2016) early flow-based models would strengthen claims. Also, no likelihood calibration via AIS/bridge sampling to check MC variance/bias.
- Theoretical properties: No consistency or identifiability results; no bounds on the Monte Carlo estimator’s variance or bias; convergence guarantees for the EM-like mixture procedure are unclear given approximate normalization.
- Practicalities: Handling of cut loci and Log-domain issues is not discussed (though they say for their manifolds D(µ̂)=RD). The role of ρ, the regularizer in M, and numerical stability of geodesic solvers are not analyzed.

Quality:
Technically sound in many parts, but the measure transformation/normalization derivation needs clarification. Empirical support is promising but limited in scope and lacks strong baselines and scalability evidence.

Clarity:
Well-written and organized; algorithms are clear. However, key derivations (Eq. 9; gradients) are deferred or sketched. Please expand the measure/normalization derivation and include computational complexity and sensitivity analyses.

Originality:
High. Learning a metric and building a properly normalized Riemannian normal/mix on top is a fresh and ambitious contribution beyond standard manifold learning and beyond Riemannian stats on known manifolds.

Significance:
Potentially high. If the normalization and optimization are correct and scalable, LAND could influence generative modeling and clustering in settings with curved data geometry.

Questions for the authors:
1) Please provide a rigorous derivation of Eq. (9). How is the Jacobian of Exp handled? Is m(µ, v) intended to be sqrt|JExpµ(v)ᵀ M(Expµ(v)) JExpµ(v)|? If not, why is omitting |det JExp| justified?
2) Do you reuse Monte Carlo samples v across µ/Σ updates to reduce variance? Any control variates or stratification to stabilize gradients?
3) What is the computational cost per iteration (N, D, K, S)? Wall-clock timings and scaling plots would help.
4) How sensitive are results to σ and ρ? Any procedure to learn σ (e.g., by maximizing marginal likelihood)?
5) How often do geodesic IVPs/BVPs fail numerically? Any safeguards (step-size control, trust regions)?
6) Can you compare against mixtures of factor analyzers and one additional nonlinear density baseline?

Pros for acceptance:
- Bold, original idea with a coherent probabilistic formulation.
- Elegant tangent-space Monte Carlo normalization.
- Practical MLE and mixture algorithm with promising empirical gains.
- Bridges two communities (manifold learning and Riemannian statistics).

Cons for acceptance:
- Potentially serious omission of |det JExp| in normalization (requires clarification).
- Limited baselines and scalability analysis.
- Metric design is simplistic and non-invariant; sensitivity not fully addressed.

Recommendation: Weak Accept (assuming the normalization derivation is corrected/clarified). The conceptual novelty outweighs the current methodological imperfections, and the approach could inspire substantial follow-up work.
