Summary
This paper proposes the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), obtained by replacing the Euclidean/Mahalanobis distance in a Gaussian with a geodesic distance induced by a smoothly varying, data-driven Riemannian metric on RD. The metric is constructed from local (diagonal) covariances via a Gaussian kernel, yielding geodesics that prefer high-density regions. The resulting density is a Riemannian normal pM(x|µ,Σ) ∝ exp(−1/2 ⟨Logµ(x), Σ−1 Logµ(x)⟩) defined on the learned manifold. Because the normalization constant depends on µ, Σ, and curvature, the authors estimate it via Monte Carlo in the tangent space at µ: draw v ∼ N(0,Σ), map to x = Expµ(v), and weight by m(µ, v) = sqrt|M(Expµ(v))|. They derive gradients for maximum likelihood estimation of µ and Σ and extend to mixtures with an EM-like scheme. Experiments on synthetic manifolds and EEG sleep-stage features show improved likelihood and clustering alignment over GMMs and least-squares (intrinsic) estimators.

Relation to prior work
The work bridges Riemannian statistics (e.g., Pennec’s Riemannian normal; mixtures on known manifolds) with manifold learning. Unlike classical manifold learning (Isomap/LLE/LE), which yields embeddings and is not directly generative, the learned metric enables continuous geodesics in the ambient space, allowing a generative density. Compared to prior mixtures on known manifolds, the novelty here is the learned, data-adaptive metric and an ML training procedure that accounts for normalization.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: a clear, bold idea—swap the linear metric in a Gaussian for a learned Riemannian metric to get a generative, locally adaptive density. This nicely connects manifold learning with probabilistic modeling.
- Practical algorithm: the tangent-space Monte Carlo trick to avoid solving BVPs (only IVPs via exponential maps) is elegant and scalable relative to naive manifold integration.
- ML estimation with gradients for µ and Σ, plus a mixture model and EM; addresses the well-known issue that intrinsic means can fall in low-density regions.
- Empirical indications that LAND captures non-ellipsoidal structure better than GMMs and avoids covariance overestimation by LS estimators; real-data clustering benefit on EEG sleep stages.
- Potentially broad applicability: could replace Gaussians in many classical models.

Weaknesses and concerns
1) Change-of-variables and volume element: When rewriting the normalization integral from manifold to tangent space, dM(x) = sqrt|M(x)| dx, the substitution x = Expµ(v) typically gives dM(x) = sqrt|M(Expµ(v))| |det dExpµ(v)| dv. The presented estimator uses only m(µ, v) = sqrt|M(Expµ(v))| and appears to omit |det dExpµ(v)| (the Jacobian of the exponential map / volume density given by Jacobi fields). Unless there is an argument that |det dExpµ(v)| = 1 in this setting or that it is somehow absorbed, this seems like a significant omission that would bias the normalization and gradients. Please clarify and, if needed, correct.

2) Metric construction is ad hoc and non-invariant: a diagonal, kernel-smoothed local variance inverse. While computationally convenient, it is coordinate-dependent and may be brittle in high dimensions. There is no principled selection of σ; on EEG, σ is tuned per subject. This raises concerns about sensitivity and fairness vs. baselines. Learning σ (e.g., by marginal likelihood or cross-validation) or adopting a probabilistic metric (e.g., [Tosi et al.]) would strengthen the work.

3) Computational scaling: Each Exp evaluation requires evaluating M(x) and its derivatives, which are sums over all data points. With S=3000 samples per C(µ,Σ) estimate and repeated per iteration/component, the cost could be high. No complexity analysis, acceleration (e.g., neighbor pruning, KD-trees, Nyström/inducing points), or runtime/memory numbers are reported.

4) Monte Carlo estimation details: No analysis of variance/bias, sample efficiency, or sensitivity to S. Gradients with respect to Σ are derived, but the implementation uses sampling from N(0,Σ) that changes during optimization; reparameterization (v = Lε) and common random numbers could reduce variance but are not discussed.

5) Evaluation breadth: Comparisons are limited to GMM and LS estimators. It would be informative to compare against flexible alternatives such as normalizing flows (as a strong density baseline), kernel density estimators with adaptive bandwidths, or mixtures with local metrics. For clustering, more baselines (spectral clustering, DP-GMM) and ablations (effect of diagonal vs. full M, σ, ρ) would help.

6) Claims about maximum entropy: While the Riemannian normal has a max-entropy characterization for a fixed manifold, here the manifold is learned from data; thus the ME claim is not directly applicable to the full procedure. This nuance should be stated more clearly.

7) Reproducibility and clarity: Some key details (initialization, EM for mixtures, ODE settings, geodesic solver tolerances, handling of non-uniqueness of Log) are deferred to the supplement. The notation overload of M for manifold and metric can be confusing.

Questions for the authors
- Please clarify the change-of-variables formula and whether |det dExpµ(v)| is included or can be shown to be 1 in your setting. If not, how large is its impact?
- How is σ selected in practice without labels? Can it be learned jointly by ML or via a criterion on held-out likelihood?
- What is the computational complexity per iteration/component, and what are typical runtimes for your datasets? Can you prune kernels to local neighborhoods without degrading performance?
- Do you use reparameterization and common random numbers to stabilize gradient estimates for Σ?
- How do you ensure out-of-sample validity? Is the metric learned only from training data and then fixed for test evaluation?

Pros
- Bold, novel idea connecting manifold learning with generative modeling via Riemannian normals.
- Elegant tangent-space Monte Carlo scheme; practical gradient-based ML; mixture extension.
- Empirical gains on synthetic and EEG clustering; avoids intrinsic mean pitfalls.

Cons
- Potentially serious issue with missing Jacobian of Exp in normalization.
- Sensitivity to and ad hoc choice of σ; lack of invariance.
- Limited scalability analysis and limited breadth of baselines.
- Some theoretical claims (ME) overstated given learned manifold.

Recommendation
Lean accept (7/10), contingent on clarifying/correcting the volume element in the normalization and providing more guidance on σ selection and computational scaling. The core idea is fresh and potentially impactful, and I am willing to overlook some methodological incompleteness if the normalization issue is addressed.

Confidence: 0.6
