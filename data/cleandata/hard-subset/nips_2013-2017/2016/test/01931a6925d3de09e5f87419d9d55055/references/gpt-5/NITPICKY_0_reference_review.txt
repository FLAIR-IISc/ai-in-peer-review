Summary
The paper proposes a Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND): a Riemannian normal distribution defined on RD endowed with a data-driven Riemannian metric. The metric M(x) is constructed as the inverse of a local diagonal covariance estimated via Gaussian kernel weights. The normalizing constant of the Riemannian normal is approximated by Monte Carlo integration in the tangent space TμM using the exponential map, and maximum-likelihood estimators for μ and Σ are derived via gradient-based optimization. A mixture of LANDs and an EM scheme are briefly outlined. Experiments on synthetic data and an EEG sleep-stage dataset aim to demonstrate improved modeling and clustering over GMMs and least-squares (intrinsic) estimators on manifolds.

Relation to prior work
The work connects Riemannian statistics (e.g., Pennec’s Riemannian normal and Zhang & Fletcher’s Riemannian PPCA) with manifold learning by learning the metric from data rather than assuming a known manifold. It is thematically related to metric learning in probabilistic geometries and to mixtures on known manifolds (e.g., Simo-Serra et al., Straub et al.), with the key claimed novelty being the estimation/inference framework on a learned (nonparametric) metric.

Strengths
- Conceptually appealing: bringing a principled Riemannian normal to a learned, data-adaptive metric and attempting ML estimation (including mixtures).
- Practical trick: Monte Carlo over the tangent space using exponential maps (IVPs) instead of logarithm maps (BVPs) is computationally motivated.
- The qualitative behavior (contours following nonlinear structures) is compelling on synthetic examples.
- The application to EEG sleep stages is interesting and potentially useful.

Major technical concerns
1) Normalization constant and change-of-variables. The rewrite of C(μ, Σ) as an integral over TμM (Eq. 9) appears to omit the Jacobian determinant of the exponential map. In coordinates, dM(x) = √|M(x)| dx; under x = Expμ(v), dx = |JExpμ(v)| dv. Thus C should involve √|M(Expμ(v))| · |JExpμ(v)|. The omission biases the estimator. Additionally, Expμ is not globally injective; integrating over the entire TμM risks double-counting beyond the injectivity radius. No assumptions (completeness, cut locus avoidance, decay due to Σ) are stated to justify this step.

2) Metric construction inconsistency. Mdd(x) is defined as (∑n wn(x)(xnd − xd)^2 + ρ)−1. As x moves far from the data, the sum grows and thus Mdd decreases; yet the text claims “the metric tensor is large” outside the support. This is contradictory. Moreover, weights are not normalized, making M sensitive to sample size; adding ρ without scaling further distorts units. This undermines the key intuition that geodesics are “pulled” toward data via large metric away from data.

3) Gradients and ML derivations. The gradients in Eqs. 12–13 are insufficiently justified. For ∇μ log C, since C = Z E_{N(0,Σ)}[m(μ, v)], the derivative depends on ∂m/∂μ which involves both derivatives of M and of Expμ(v) w.r.t. μ, not simply m(μ, v) v. For ∇Aφ, C depends on Σ via both Z and the expectation measure; the given expression contains only E[m vv^T] and seems to ignore ∂m/∂Σ via v’s reparameterization. Without rigorous derivations (or clear approximations), correctness is doubtful.

4) Use of the Riemannian volume element. The paper sets m(μ, v) = √|M(Expμ(v))| and argues it is “large in low density areas,” which depends on the (incorrect/contradictory) metric behavior. If the earlier inconsistency stands, the regularization effect via C may be reversed.

5) Geometric assumptions. The paper assumes Logμ is globally defined and smooth (using “D(μ) is RD”), which is generally false due to the cut locus. No conditions on the learned metric ensuring completeness, injectivity radius, or curvature bounds are provided. This affects both the distance and the integral manipulations.

Experimental and reproducibility issues
- The synthetic evaluation computes E_{x~p_model}[−log p_true(x)], which is an unusual and potentially misleading direction of cross-entropy; it does not measure model fit to the true distribution in the standard sense (should use E_{x~p_true}[−log p_model(x)] or held-out likelihood).
- On EEG, σ is chosen either fixed or by sweeping σ and reporting the best F-measure per subject—apparently using labels for model selection—yielding optimistic results. Baseline hyperparameters (e.g., GMM covariance types, selection/tuning) are not comparably tuned, making comparisons potentially unfair.
- Runtime and scalability are not reported. The cost of S=3000 Exp solves per C evaluation per iteration per component is significant (and each IVP involves O(ND) operations due to the kernel metric).
- Algorithm 1 termination condition is truncated; figures and table references are incomplete; many references are incomplete.

Clarity and presentation
There are numerous typos, broken sentences, and formatting issues (e.g., section “.1 Synthetic,” stray “th Conference…” line, incomplete Algorithm 1, missing figure/table content). Several citations are malformed or missing years/pages. Key derivations are deferred to supplements but the paper relies on them; even the presented equations are suspect (see above).

Originality
Moderate. The idea to place a Riemannian normal on a learned metric is interesting and, to my knowledge, not previously worked out with ML estimation and mixtures. However, the core steps (metric from kernel smoothing, Exp/Log geodesics, MC integration) are combinations of known tools.

Significance
Currently limited by questionable correctness and evaluation. If the derivations were made correct (and efficient), this could be a useful primitive for generative modeling on learned geometries. As written, it is not ready.

Questions for the authors
- Please provide a rigorous derivation of Eq. 9 including the Jacobian of Exp, and specify geometric assumptions ensuring injectivity/absence of double-counting.
- Clarify/correct the metric construction (Eq. 7) vis-à-vis the “large outside support” claim, and justify not normalizing kernel weights. How sensitive are results to sample size and scaling of x?
- Provide full derivations of Eqs. 12–13. If approximations are used (e.g., ignoring ∂Exp/∂μ or reparameterization), state and justify them. Can you use a reparameterization trick to differentiate C w.r.t. Σ properly?
- Report runtimes and scaling with N, D, K, S; ablate S; compare to baselines with matched hyperparameter tuning protocols; use a proper evaluation metric (held-out likelihood or E_{true}[log p_model]).
- What guarantees do you have that Logμ(x) is well-defined for all x encountered during training/inference?

Pros
- Interesting and potentially impactful idea.
- Intuitive geometry-driven modeling; qualitative results are promising.
- Attempts to address normalization and ML estimation rather than resorting to LS.

Cons
- Serious theoretical issues (normalization change-of-variables, gradients).
- Inconsistency in the core metric definition/intuitions.
- Weak/biased empirical evaluation; no scalability analysis.
- Poor clarity/formatting; missing derivations and details.

Recommendation
Reject. While the idea is appealing, the paper in its current form has substantial technical and methodological flaws, and significant clarity/presentation problems. A revised version addressing the change-of-variables, gradient derivations, metric construction, and fair/rigorous evaluation could be much stronger.
