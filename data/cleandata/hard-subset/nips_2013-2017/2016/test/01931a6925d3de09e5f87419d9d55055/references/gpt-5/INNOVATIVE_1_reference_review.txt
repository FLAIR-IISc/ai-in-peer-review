Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), which replaces the Euclidean/Mahalanobis distance in a Gaussian with a geodesic distance induced by a learned Riemannian metric on RD. The metric is constructed from data via a kernel-smoothed, diagonal local covariance (plus regularization), yielding a smoothly varying SPD field M(x) that “pulls” geodesics toward high-density regions. The density is the Riemannian normal pM(x | µ, Σ) ∝ exp(−1/2 ⟨Logµ(x), Σ−1 Logµ(x)⟩), but crucially the normalization constant C(µ, Σ) is not available in closed form for a learned manifold. The authors derive a Monte Carlo estimator by transforming the integral to the tangent space TµM and sampling v ∼ N(0, Σ), with weights involving the metric determinant at Expµ(v). They then provide gradients for maximum likelihood estimation of µ and Σ via block coordinate descent, and extend to mixtures with an EM algorithm. Experiments on synthetic non-linear clusters and EEG sleep-stage features indicate that LANDs (and their mixtures) can model non-ellipsoidal data more faithfully than GMMs and outperform intrinsic least-squares estimators on a learned manifold.

This work bridges Riemannian statistics (e.g., Pennec’s Riemannian normal; Zhang & Fletcher’s PPGA) with manifold learning that does not assume a known manifold a priori. It relates to graph-based geodesic methods (Isomap) but uses a continuous, learned metric. The EM for mixtures resembles prior work on manifold mixtures (e.g., Simo-Serra et al.), but here the normalization constants are accounted for.

Strengths:
- Conceptual novelty: a principled generative density on a learned manifold, not just an embedding. This is a bold and timely idea with potential to generalize many Gaussian-based models to non-linear settings.
- Technical contribution: a practical and scalable scheme for estimating the normalization constant via sampling in TµM and solving IVPs for Exp rather than BVPs for Log. Gradient derivations for µ and Σ and a mixture/EM extension make the method usable.
- Insightful link between maximum entropy properties of Riemannian normals and data-adaptive geometry.
- Empirical evidence that LAND better fits non-elliptical structure than GMMs and avoids the pitfalls of intrinsic LS estimators (e.g., means in low-density regions). The EEG application shows promising alignment with sleep stages.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Change-of-variables/measure: The transformation C(µ, Σ) = ∫TµM √|M(Expµ(v))| exp(−1/2 ⟨v, Σ−1v⟩) dv appears to omit the Jacobian |det DExpµ(v)|. In general, in coordinates u with chart φ, dvol = √|det g(u)| du where g is the metric in those coordinates; using x = Expµ(v), g in v-coordinates is the pullback Exp∗g, whose matrix equals J(v)T M(x) J(v) (J = DExp). Hence the volume element becomes √|M(x)| |det J(v)| dv. The paper uses only √|M(Expµ(v))|. Please clarify whether you are implicitly working in normal coordinates where √|det g(u)| already includes the Jacobian, or justify why |det DExp| cancels. This is a critical technical point for correctness of C(µ, Σ).
- Metric design: restricting to diagonal local covariances is computationally convenient but not invariant (as noted) and may limit expressivity. Sensitivity to σ is acknowledged but not learned; selection via a grid per subject raises practical questions.
- Geometric assumptions: claims like D(µ) = RD and reliance on global Log maps can fail (cut loci, lack of geodesic completeness) for general learned metrics. The paper would benefit from stating conditions on M(x) that ensure existence/uniqueness of shortest geodesics and well-defined Log/Exp in the regions used for learning.
- Scalability: evaluating M(x) requires O(ND) kernel sums per query; estimating C(µ, Σ) requires S evaluations of Exp and |M| per iteration, per component. There is limited discussion of wall-clock time, memory, or approximations (e.g., inducing points, neighbor truncation, KD-trees). How does complexity scale with N, D, K, and S? The EEG experiments are 5D; behavior in higher D is unclear.
- Optimization: both subproblems are nonconvex; no convergence or consistency guarantees are discussed. The Monte Carlo noise in C(µ, Σ) propagates into gradients and EM responsibilities; stability and variance reduction strategies are not explored.
- Empirics: while the synthetic and EEG results are encouraging, comparisons are limited. It would strengthen the case to include baselines such as geodesic GMMs on graphs, density-ridge methods, or modern manifold-aware flows (acknowledging the NIPS 2016 timeframe). Also report variability across runs and computational costs.

Quality:
The approach is technically plausible and mostly careful. The potential omission/ambiguity around the Jacobian is the most serious concern to address. Other issues (σ selection, scalability) are acknowledged as limitations.

Clarity:
Generally well-written and organized. Some references are incomplete; a clearer explanation of the measure change and geometric assumptions would help. Runtime and complexity discussion would improve transparency.

Originality:
High. The combination of learned Riemannian geometry with a properly normalized Riemannian normal (and mixture) estimated by ML appears novel and impactful.

Significance:
High potential. A “drop-in” manifold-aware Gaussian for generative modeling could influence clustering, classification, and dimensionality reduction pipelines, provided computational issues are addressed.

Questions for the authors:
- Please rigorously justify the expression for C(µ, Σ) in TµM with respect to the Riemannian volume element and the exponential map’s Jacobian.
- What are the computational costs (per iteration, per component) on your datasets? Any approximations (neighbor truncation, precomputation) used?
- How sensitive are results to S and σ? Can σ be learned (e.g., via ML or cross-validation) and can you share an ablation?
- What conditions on M(x) ensure geodesic completeness and well-defined Log maps in your setting?

Pros:
- Bold, novel idea enabling generative modeling on learned manifolds.
- Practical algorithm with gradients and mixture/EM.
- Empirical gains on non-linear structures; avoids LS pitfalls.

Cons:
- Potential technical gap in measure change/Jacobian.
- Scalability and parameter selection not fully addressed.
- Limited baselines and higher-D validation.

Recommendation: Weak Accept (leaning accept for conceptual novelty and potential impact), assuming the measure/Jacobian issue is clarified or corrected.

Confidence: Moderate.
