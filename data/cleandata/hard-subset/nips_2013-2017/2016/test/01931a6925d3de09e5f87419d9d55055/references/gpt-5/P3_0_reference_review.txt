Summary and contributions:
The paper proposes a Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND) that replaces the Euclidean metric in a Gaussian with a learned, smoothly varying Riemannian metric over RD. The metric is constructed from a kernel-weighted local (diagonal) covariance at each x, yielding a data-adaptive geodesic distance. On this learned manifold, the authors adopt the Riemannian normal distribution pM(x | µ, Σ) ∝ exp(−0.5⟨Logµ(x), Σ−1 Logµ(x)⟩). The key technical pieces are: (i) a Monte Carlo estimator of the normalization constant C(µ, Σ) that integrates over the tangent space TµM by sampling v ∼ N(0, Σ) and evaluating m(µ, v) = √|M(Expµ(v))|; (ii) gradient-based ML for µ and Σ, and an EM-like algorithm for mixtures. Experiments on synthetic data and EEG sleep-stage features suggest advantages over GMMs and least-squares manifold estimators.

Relation to prior work:
The paper connects Riemannian statistics (e.g., Pennec’s Riemannian normal; intrinsic mean/covariance) with manifold learning (e.g., Isomap, kernel methods), and with learned metrics (Hauberg et al.; Tosi et al.). Mixtures of distributions on known manifolds have been considered (e.g., Simo-Serra et al.; Straub et al.). The key novelty here is to learn a Riemannian geometry from data and then perform generative modeling using the Riemannian normal with ML estimation.

Strengths:
- Conceptually appealing bridge: a principled density on a learned geometry rather than an embedding; geodesics follow high-density regions.
- Practical algorithmic ideas: estimating C by sampling in TµM avoids solving boundary value problems for Log repeatedly; the optimization framework is simple to implement once Exp/Log can be numerically computed.
- Empirical evidence that LAND avoids the intrinsic-mean pitfall of drifting to low-density regions; good qualitative fits to nonlinear structures.
- For EEG clustering, fixed-σ results show promising alignment improvements over GMM.

Weaknesses and technical concerns:
1) Change-of-variables and volume element: The transformation C(µ, Σ) = ∫TµM √|M(Expµ(v))| exp(−0.5 v⊤Σ−1v) dv appears incorrect in general. When pulling back the Riemannian volume via x = Expµ(v), the correct density in normal coordinates is √det G(v) dv, where G(v) = (DExpµ(v))⊤ M(Expµ(v)) DExpµ(v) is the pullback metric. Using √|M(Expµ(v))| alone omits the Jacobian |det DExpµ(v)|, which depends on curvature via Jacobi fields. This omission affects both the value of C and its gradients and is not merely a constant factor. Integrating over all v ∈ TµM further ignores injectivity radius/cut-locus issues (Exp need not be globally injective). The paper should either correct this derivation, provide conditions under which the simplification is valid, or quantify the approximation error.

2) Gradient w.r.t. µ of log C: Eq. (12) expresses ∇µφ using a term proportional to E[m(µ, v) v]. Since m depends on µ only through Expµ(v) and the metric at that point, ∂µ log C requires differentiating through Expµ(v) (Jacobi fields) and M; the v term does not arise without additional identities. As stated, the µ-gradient of C seems to ignore ∂µ m(µ, v) and thus is likely biased. This is critical since µ is updated with Expµ(α d), and the descent direction is built upon this gradient.

3) Metric construction limitations: Restricting to diagonal local covariances makes the learned metric coordinate-dependent and non-invariant to linear transforms (acknowledged by the authors) and may substantially distort geodesics. This choice appears driven by computational expediency; an analysis of the trade-offs and empirical sensitivity to rotations/rescalings is needed.

4) Scalability and complexity: Each evaluation of m requires solving an ODE (Exp) per Monte Carlo sample (S=3000), and evaluating M(x) entails summation over all N data points per ODE step. There is no complexity analysis or runtime report. Practicality beyond very low D and modest N is unclear; approximate kernel sums (e.g., inducing points/trees), mini-batch Monte Carlo, or caching would be needed.

5) Experimental evaluation:
- Synthetic evaluation computes the mean NLL of the true generator using samples from the learned model, which is unusual; standard practice would use held-out likelihood under the learned model. Provide both directions of KL or test likelihoods on held-out data for a fair comparison.
- EEG clustering: reporting the best F-measure over a grid of σ per subject uses labels to select hyperparameters and biases comparisons. The fixed-σ results are more informative; please avoid label-informed tuning or report nested cross-validation.
- Baselines: it would be important to compare with methods tailored to nonlinear structure (e.g., spectral clustering with geodesic distances, kernel density estimators, mixtures of factor analyzers). At minimum, include ablations on S, σ, and diagonal vs. full local covariances.

6) Theoretical properties: No guarantees on existence/uniqueness of Exp/Log for the learned metric, geodesic completeness, identifiability, or consistency. Mixture EM with approximate C is not shown to be monotonic. Some discussion or bounds would strengthen the work.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written and accessible. However, the derivation around the normalization constant and its gradients needs to be made precise (proper volume element in normal coordinates; handling of injectivity). Provide more details on numerical tolerances for ODEs, step sizes, convergence criteria, and computational budgets.

Originality:
The high-level idea—Riemannian normal on a learned manifold—is novel and timely. The diagonal-kernel metric is not new per se, but using it to define a generative model with ML estimation is a fresh angle.

Significance:
If the volume/gradient issues are corrected and scalability addressed, LAND could be a useful primitive for manifold-aware generative modeling and clustering. As it stands, the theoretical gaps cast doubt on the correctness of the likelihood and gradients, limiting impact.

Pros for acceptance:
- Interesting and potentially impactful direction: principled densities on learned geometries.
- Simple, implementable algorithms with appealing qualitative results.
- Bridges two active areas (manifold learning and Riemannian statistics).

Cons for acceptance:
- Likely incorrect change-of-variables for C and suspect µ-gradient; needs correction.
- Scalability concerns not addressed empirically or theoretically.
- Some experimental practices (hyperparameter tuning with labels; unusual synthetic metric) weaken evidence.

Questions for the authors:
- Can you derive the correct pullback volume factor √det((DExp)⊤ M DExp) and incorporate it (or justify your approximation)?
- How do you differentiate C w.r.t. µ without Jacobi fields? Could you use adjoint sensitivity of the geodesic IVP?
- What are the computational costs (wall-time) per iteration for your experiments? How does performance scale with D, N, and S?
- How sensitive are results to rotations/rescalings of the data?

Recommendation:
Borderline reject unless the authors can fix the volume/gradient derivations and provide principled experiments without label-informed hyperparameter tuning. The idea is promising, but the current formulation appears mathematically and empirically insufficient for acceptance. Confidence: high.
