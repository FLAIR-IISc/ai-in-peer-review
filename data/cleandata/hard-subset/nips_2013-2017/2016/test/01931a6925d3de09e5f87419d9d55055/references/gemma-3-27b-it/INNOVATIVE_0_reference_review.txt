This paper introduces a locally adaptive normal distribution (LAND) by replacing the Euclidean distance with a Riemannian metric learned from the data. This is a compelling idea, bridging manifold learning with Riemannian statistics, and offering a principled way to model distributions on non-linear manifolds. The authors demonstrate a scalable Monte Carlo scheme for normalization and a gradient-based estimation algorithm, validating the approach on both synthetic and real (EEG) data.

The core novelty is significant. While Riemannian normal distributions exist (Pennec, 2015), applying them requires *a priori* knowledge of the manifold. This work cleverly learns the metric *from* the data, making the Riemannian framework applicable to scenarios where the underlying manifold is unknown. The connection to Hauberg et al. (2009) in learning a smooth metric is well-articulated, and the proposed method offers a tangible advantage over discrete geodesic-based approaches like Isomap. The locally adaptive nature of the metric addresses a key limitation of standard Riemannian approaches that might struggle with complex data distributions.

The paper’s strengths lie in its ambitious scope and theoretical grounding. The mathematical formulation is solid, and the authors demonstrate a clear understanding of Riemannian geometry. The Monte Carlo integration scheme is a practical solution to a challenging problem (normalization), and the algorithmic details (gradient descent with optimization strategies) are well-defined.  The experimental results, while not overwhelmingly conclusive, provide encouraging evidence that LAND outperforms a standard Gaussian Mixture Model, particularly in capturing non-ellipsoidal clusters and modeling sleep stages. The comparison to least-squares Riemannian estimators is also valuable.

However, several weaknesses require attention. The choice of a diagonal covariance matrix for the metric, while computationally expedient, is a strong assumption and limits the expressiveness of the model. The sensitivity of the results to the bandwidth parameter σ is evident in the EEG experiments, and the method for choosing this parameter could benefit from more rigorous justification or automation. While the authors acknowledge this, the current approach feels somewhat ad-hoc.  Furthermore, the paper lacks a thorough comparison to other non-parametric density estimation methods, such as kernel density estimation, particularly in the context of manifold learning. It’s unclear if the benefits of LAND justify its increased complexity compared to simpler alternatives. The scalability claims, while plausible, are not backed by strong empirical evidence, especially for high-dimensional data.  The discussion of related work, while adequate, could benefit from a more detailed engagement with recent advances in metric learning and manifold regularization.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of Riemannian statistics and data-driven metric learning is genuinely novel and addresses a significant gap in the literature.
*   **Theoretical soundness:** The mathematical foundations are solid, and the proposed method is well-motivated.
*   **Potential impact:** If successful, this approach could lead to more accurate and robust generative models for data lying on non-linear manifolds.
*   **Clear presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the core concepts.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Strong assumptions:** The diagonal covariance assumption limits the model’s expressiveness.
*   **Parameter sensitivity:**  The performance is sensitive to the choice of bandwidth parameter σ.
*   **Limited empirical evaluation:**  The comparison to other density estimation methods is lacking.
*   **Scalability concerns:** The scalability claims are not strongly supported by empirical evidence.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the weaknesses, I lean towards **acceptance**. The paper presents a truly novel and ambitious idea with a strong theoretical foundation. While further refinement and more comprehensive empirical validation are needed, the potential impact of this work is significant. The authors have successfully bridged two important areas of machine learning (manifold learning and Riemannian statistics), and the proposed LAND model offers a promising new direction for modeling data on non-linear manifolds. I would encourage the authors to address the weaknesses identified above in future work. Minor revisions addressing parameter selection and including comparisons to kernel density estimation would significantly strengthen the paper.
