Summary
The paper proposes a Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND): a Riemannian normal distribution defined on a learned Riemannian manifold in the ambient data space. The manifold is induced by a smoothly varying metric M(x) built from a local, diagonal covariance estimate via Gaussian kernels, so that geodesics are attracted to high-density regions. The resulting density is pM(x|µ,Σ) ∝ exp(−0.5⟨Logµ(x), Σ−1 Logµ(x)⟩) with a normalization constant C(µ,Σ) that depends on the manifold geometry. The main algorithmic contribution is a Monte Carlo estimation of C(µ,Σ) by integrating over the tangent space at µ using samples v ∼ N(0,Σ) and evaluating m(µ,v) = √|M(Expµ(v))|; coupled with gradients for µ and Σ (via a factorization Σ−1 = AᵀA) this enables ML estimation. The paper also derives a mixture model and an EM algorithm. Experiments on synthetic data and EEG sleep-stage features show improved likelihoods/clustering performance over GMMs and over least-squares “intrinsic” estimators.

Relation to prior work
The work connects Pennec’s Riemannian normal distribution (known on manifolds specified a priori) with learned Riemannian metrics (e.g., Hauberg et al.). It contrasts with graph-geodesic methods (Isomap) by providing a continuous metric and a generative density. It is distinct from mixtures on known manifolds (e.g., Simo-Serra; Straub) by learning the metric and by accounting for the component-specific normalization constants.

Evaluation against NeurIPS criteria

Quality
- Positive: The ML objective and gradients are derived; the Monte Carlo estimator over tangent space is a sensible way to avoid repeated Log-map BVPs; the block-coordinate scheme is reasonable in practice. The experiments demonstrate that the LAND avoids low-density means that plague intrinsic least-squares estimators and can capture non-elliptical clusters.
- Major technical concern: The change of variables used to rewrite C(µ,Σ) as an integral over TµM appears to omit the Jacobian determinant of the exponential map. In general, for x = Expµ(v), the pullback of the Riemannian volume form satisfies dM(x) = θµ(v) dv with θµ(v) = √|M(x)| det(d Expµ(v)). The paper sets m(µ,v) = √|M(Expµ(v))| and integrates dv, effectively assuming det(d Expµ(v)) ≡ 1. This is not generally true; the volume density in normal coordinates equals 1 only at v = 0 up to second-order corrections. If the Jacobian is omitted, the normalization constant (and gradients) are biased and, in principle, the model may not define a properly normalized density. This needs explicit justification, a proof for the special metric class used, or an amended estimator including det(d Expµ(v)).
- Additional technical limitations: The metric is restricted to diagonal entries and is not invariant under linear transforms; the choice of kernel bandwidth σ is manual; there is no analysis of estimator variance for C(µ,Σ) or of convergence; no complexity/runtime analysis; and no discussion of numerical stability of ODE solvers or step-size selection.

Clarity
- Generally well written, with a clear high-level exposition and helpful algorithm box. However:
  - The measure transformation in Section 3.2 is oversimplified and should carefully state conditions and include Jacobians or justify their omission.
  - More implementation details should be given or moved from the supplement: ODE solver, tolerances, how S=3000 was chosen, reuse of Monte Carlo samples across iterations, line search/step sizes, and stopping criteria.
  - Limitations (non-invariance, diagonal metric) are acknowledged; it would help to emphasize practical implications.

Originality
- The idea of a normalized, parametric density on a learned Riemannian manifold, with MC normalization via tangent-space sampling, is novel and interesting. Prior works consider known manifolds; this paper operationalizes Riemannian normals over learned metrics and provides ML training and mixtures. While the metric learning itself is simple, the overall combination is fresh.

Significance
- If correct and practical, LAND could be a useful drop-in replacement for Gaussians in mixture models and other generative methods when data follow curved structures. The synthetic demos are compelling, and the EEG example suggests potential for clustering. However, significance is tempered by scalability concerns (repeated Exp-map solves and S=3000 per iteration) and by limited empirical breadth: few baselines, low-dimensional data, and no comparisons to contemporary flexible density models (e.g., flows), nor to alternative manifold density estimators.

Pros
- Conceptually clean bridge between manifold learning and Riemannian statistics; principled density with a well-defined normalizer.
- Tangent-space Monte Carlo normalization is a practical idea that avoids expensive Log maps.
- Derivation of ML gradients and mixture EM that account for component-specific normalizers.
- Empirical results show clear advantages over GMMs and Riemannian LS estimators on curved data.

Cons
- Potentially incorrect or incomplete change-of-variables formula for C(µ,Σ) (missing det dExp), which impacts correctness.
- Heavily engineered metric (diagonal, kernel bandwidth) with manual tuning and lack of invariances.
- Limited empirical evaluation: modest datasets, minimal baselines, no runtime or sensitivity analyses beyond σ and S in supplement.
- Scalability to higher dimensions and large N is unclear.

Questions for the authors
1. Please justify Eq. (9): does your m(µ,v) include det(d Expµ(v))? If not, under what assumptions is it valid to omit it? Can you quantify the induced bias?
2. Do you reuse the same Monte Carlo samples across iterations to reduce variance, and do you use control variates or antithetic sampling?
3. What are typical wall-clock times per iteration and per data set? How do cost and accuracy scale with D, N, and S?
4. How sensitive are results to σ and to the diagonal-metric restriction? Could a low-rank or full local covariance be used?
5. How do you ensure numerical stability for geodesic IVPs (solver, tolerances) and for the optimization (step sizes, stopping)?

Minor comments
- The paper notes non-invariance; a simple whitening pre-processing could mitigate this and is worth discussing.
- Clarify initialization (µ, Σ) and mixture EM details (e.g., responsibilities use normalized densities with C(µk,Σk) included).
- Typographical/notation nits: ensure consistent use of bold vectors and inner products, and define Z when first introduced.

Recommendation
Borderline reject, primarily due to the unresolved measure-theoretic issue in the normalization constant and limited empirical validation. If the authors can rigorously address the Jacobian term (with theory or a corrected estimator) and add evidence on computational cost and robustness, this would become a strong and impactful contribution. As it stands, the technical gap undermines confidence in the correctness of the model and the reported likelihoods.
