This paper introduces a “Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution” (LAND) by replacing the Euclidean distance in the standard normal distribution with a Riemannian distance learned from the data. This is a conceptually bold and interesting idea, aiming to bridge Riemannian statistics and manifold learning. The motivation – that standard normals struggle on nonlinear manifolds – is well-established, and the authors correctly draw a parallel to geodesic distance methods like Isomap. The core contribution lies in defining a tractable Riemannian metric and developing a Monte Carlo scheme for normalizing the resulting density, alongside a gradient-based optimization procedure. 

The paper is clearly written and logically organized, with a good review of Riemannian geometry provided. The mathematical formalism is sound, and the authors demonstrate a good understanding of the underlying concepts. The proposed Monte Carlo integration for normalization is a clever way to avoid intractable calculations, and the overall algorithmic approach appears reasonable. The application to EEG data, though brief, is a nice demonstration of potential real-world utility.

However, while the *idea* is strong, the paper feels somewhat underdeveloped in its experimental validation. The synthetic experiment demonstrates improvement over GMM, but the differences aren’t overwhelming, and the analysis is relatively shallow. The choice of parameters (e.g., σ) feels somewhat arbitrary, and a more thorough sensitivity analysis would be beneficial. The EEG experiment is even less convincing – the improvements over GMM are stated but not visually demonstrated, and the justification for the chosen features isn't fully clear. The comparison to a least-squares LAND variant is a good addition, but more thorough exploration of different Riemannian metric learning techniques would be valuable. 

A significant weakness is the limited discussion of scalability. The authors acknowledge potential issues with high-dimensional data, but offer only brief suggestions for improvement. The computational cost of solving ODEs for geodesics, even with the tangent space integration trick, could be a major bottleneck for large datasets, and this needs to be addressed more directly. Further, the paper leans heavily on diagonal covariance matrices for computational efficiency. While pragmatic, this limits the expressiveness of the model and potentially undermines the benefits of the Riemannian adaptation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The concept of a LAND is genuinely novel and offers a compelling way to extend the normal distribution to nonlinear data manifolds.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The mathematical foundation is strong, and the authors demonstrate a solid understanding of Riemannian geometry.
*   **Potential Significance:** If scalable and robust, this approach could have a significant impact on various machine learning applications where nonlinear data structures are prevalent.
*   **Bridging Fields:** The work successfully bridges Riemannian statistics and manifold learning.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are not conclusive and lack thorough analysis.
*   **Scalability Concerns:** The computational cost and limitations of the diagonal covariance assumption are not fully addressed.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The choice of key parameters (e.g., σ) feels somewhat arbitrary and requires more careful investigation.
*   **Incremental Improvement:** While the LAND shows some improvement over GMM, the gains are not dramatic.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite its weaknesses, I lean towards **acceptance**. The novelty and theoretical elegance of the LAND are compelling. The paper presents a significant idea with a solid mathematical foundation. While the experimental validation is limited, the potential impact of this work is substantial. I believe that the authors have identified a promising research direction, and I am willing to overlook the current limitations in exchange for the significant conceptual contribution. I strongly encourage the authors to address the scalability concerns and provide more extensive empirical validation in future work. A more detailed sensitivity analysis and justification for parameter choices would also be helpful. Minor revisions are requested.
