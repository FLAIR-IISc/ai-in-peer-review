### Summary

This paper introduces a novel generative model, the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), designed for data that lies on or near a non-linear manifold. The core idea is to generalize the multivariate normal distribution by replacing the standard Mahalanobis distance with a geodesic distance computed on a Riemannian manifold. Crucially, the geometry of this manifold is not assumed a priori but is learned directly from the data. The paper makes several key contributions:
1.  It constructs a data-dependent Riemannian metric based on local, kernel-weighted covariance estimates, effectively creating a non-parametric space upon which a parametric model is defined.
2.  It proposes a scalable Monte Carlo integration scheme to compute the intractable normalization constant. This is cleverly done by integrating over the tangent space at the mean, which requires solving computationally cheaper initial value problems (IVPs) for the exponential map instead of more expensive boundary value problems (BVPs) for the logarithm map.
3.  It derives a maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) procedure for the distribution's parameters (mean and covariance) using gradient-based optimization, improving upon standard, but potentially biased, intrinsic least squares estimators.
4.  The framework is extended to a mixture of LANDs, with a corresponding EM algorithm.

The work effectively bridges the gap between statistics on Riemannian manifolds, where the manifold is typically known (e.g., Pennec, 2006), and manifold learning, which has traditionally focused on dimensionality reduction rather than generative modeling (e.g., Tenenbaum et al., 2000). The proposed model is evaluated on synthetic data and a real-world EEG dataset, demonstrating its ability to capture non-linear data structures more effectively than a standard Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM).

### Strengths

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper presents a highly original and elegant synthesis of manifold learning and Riemannian statistics. By learning the manifold from the data, it overcomes a major limitation of previous work on Riemannian normal distributions, making the framework broadly applicable. This provides a principled and powerful new tool for generative modeling of complex, non-linear data structures in their native space, without resorting to potentially distortive low-dimensional embeddings. The idea of building a parametric model over a non-parametrically defined space is compelling and likely to inspire future research.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The formulation is well-grounded in differential geometry. The proposed solution for handling the normalization constant by integrating over the tangent space is a clever and critical contribution that makes the approach computationally feasible. The derivation of the MLE procedure and its gradients appears correct and is a significant improvement over intrinsic least squares methods, as convincingly argued and demonstrated by the authors.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The authors provide a concise but helpful introduction to Riemannian geometry, making the work accessible to a broader audience. The motivation is clear, the methodology is explained step-by-step, and the figures (especially Figs. 5 and 6) are highly effective at illustrating the model's behavior and advantages over a GMM.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

While this is a strong paper, there are several areas that could be improved:

1.  **Computational Complexity and Scalability:** The primary concern is the computational cost, which is not formally analyzed. The metric construction in Eq. (7) involves a sum over the entire dataset `N` for each point `x` at which the metric is evaluated. More importantly, the MLE procedure requires repeatedly solving ODEs to compute logarithm maps (for the `N` data points) and exponential maps (for the `S` Monte Carlo samples) within each gradient step. This suggests the method may not scale well to large datasets (large `N`) or high dimensions (large `D`).
    *   **Suggestion:** The authors should include a discussion on the computational complexity of their algorithm. Providing runtime comparisons against the GMM baseline would give readers a practical sense of the trade-offs. It would also be beneficial to discuss potential avenues for scaling up the method, such as using approximations for the metric computation (e.g., Nystrom-style methods) or faster, perhaps probabilistic, ODE solvers.

2.  **Hyperparameter Selection for the Metric:** The kernel bandwidth `σ` is a critical hyperparameter that controls the smoothness of the learned manifold. In the experiments, it appears to be manually selected. The sleep stage experiment reports the *best* result over a range of `σ` values, which is not a rigorous evaluation protocol as it amounts to tuning on the test set. The model's performance is likely sensitive to this choice.
    *   **Suggestion:** The authors should discuss more principled approaches for selecting `σ`, such as cross-validation or optimizing a marginal likelihood objective. For the sleep experiment, results should be reported for a single `σ` chosen via a validation set, or at least the sensitivity to `σ` should be more thoroughly analyzed and reported. Acknowledging this as a current limitation and a direction for future work is good, but a more robust experimental evaluation would strengthen the paper's claims.

3.  **Limited Experimental Baselines:** The experiments primarily compare LAND against GMMs. While this is an important and logical baseline, a GMM is fundamentally a mixture of linear models. It is not surprising that a purpose-built non-linear model outperforms it on non-linear data.
    *   **Suggestion:** To better position the work within the broader field of generative modeling, a comparison against other powerful non-linear generative models would be valuable. Candidates could include Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) or Normalizing Flows, which are also capable of modeling complex densities. Even a comparison to a sophisticated Kernel Density Estimator (KDE) could provide additional context.

4.  **Choice of Metric:** The paper uses a specific metric based on the inverse of a local *diagonal* covariance matrix. While computationally convenient, the diagonal assumption is restrictive and the metric is not invariant to linear transformations.
    *   **Suggestion:** A brief discussion justifying this choice over alternatives (e.g., full local covariance) and acknowledging its limitations would be helpful for the reader.

### Decision

**Accept.**

This paper introduces a novel, theoretically elegant, and potentially impactful approach to generative modeling for data with non-linear structure. The core idea of defining a Riemannian normal distribution on a data-driven manifold is a significant contribution that successfully marries two distinct fields. The technical execution, particularly the method for handling the normalization constant and the MLE formulation, is solid. While there are valid concerns regarding computational scalability and hyperparameter selection, these limitations do not detract from the importance of the core contribution and can be addressed in future work. The paper is clear, well-motivated, and the experiments convincingly demonstrate the promise of the proposed LAND model. It represents a high-quality scientific contribution that is well-suited for NeurIPS.
