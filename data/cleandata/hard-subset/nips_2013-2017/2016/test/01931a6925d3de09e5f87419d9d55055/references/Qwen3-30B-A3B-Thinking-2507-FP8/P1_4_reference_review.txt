

# Peer Review: A Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution

## Summary

This paper introduces the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), a novel generative model that extends the standard multivariate normal distribution by replacing the Euclidean distance with a locally adaptive Riemannian distance. The authors build upon Pennec's Riemannian normal distribution by learning a Riemannian metric from data, rather than assuming a known manifold as in previous work. The metric is constructed using a local covariance matrix with a Gaussian kernel, creating a smoothly varying metric that adapts to the data's nonlinear structure. The paper provides a complete framework including a Monte Carlo integration scheme for normalization, a gradient-based maximum likelihood estimation algorithm, and an extension to mixtures of LANDs. The approach bridges Riemannian statistics with manifold learning, addressing a gap between these two fields that has been noted in recent literature (e.g., Hauberg et al. [9] and Pennec [15]).

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Soundness and Novelty**: The paper makes a significant contribution by extending Riemannian normal distributions to the case where the manifold is learned from data. This is a clear and important step beyond prior work that assumes the manifold is known a priori. The authors properly contextualize their work within the literature on Riemannian statistics and manifold learning, distinguishing their approach from related work by Simo-Serra et al. [19] and Straub et al. [20].

2. **Technical Rigor**: The paper provides a complete mathematical formulation of the LAND model, including clear derivations of the normalization constant, gradient computations for optimization, and the EM algorithm for mixtures. The authors demonstrate careful consideration of both theoretical and practical aspects, such as the computational cost of geodesic computations and the need for Monte Carlo integration.

3. **Empirical Validation**: The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The synthetic data experiments clearly demonstrate LAND's ability to capture nonlinear manifolds better than standard GMMs or least-squares Riemannian approaches. The real-world sleep stage classification task provides strong evidence of practical utility, with LAND significantly outperforming GMMs in capturing the underlying sleep patterns.

4. **Clarity and Organization**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, with clear mathematical notation and effective visualizations. The supplementary materials provide sufficient detail for reproducibility. The presentation of Algorithm 1 is particularly clear and would be straightforward for researchers to implement.

## Weaknesses

1. **Computational Scalability**: While the authors acknowledge that evaluating exponential/logarithmic maps is computationally expensive, they don't provide a thorough analysis of how this limitation affects scalability to large datasets or high-dimensional input spaces. The paper mentions high-dimensional cases might require different approaches (e.g., probabilistic manifold learning [22]), but doesn't discuss how the current approach would perform in such settings.

2. **Parameter Sensitivity**: The choice of σ (the Gaussian kernel bandwidth) appears to be a critical parameter that significantly affects performance (as shown in the sleep stage experiments). The paper doesn't propose an automated method for selecting σ, which would be important for practical applications. While the authors note this is "less straightforward to select" due to intersubject variability, a more principled approach would strengthen the work.

3. **Limited Comparison with Alternative Manifold Learning Approaches**: The paper compares LAND primarily against GMM and least-squares Riemannian methods, but doesn't compare against more recent manifold learning approaches like Isomap, LLE, or t-SNE. While the focus is on generative modeling rather than dimensionality reduction, a brief comparison would strengthen the case for LAND's advantages.

4. **Theoretical Properties**: The paper mentions LAND is the maximum entropy distribution for fixed mean and covariance, but doesn't explore other theoretical properties that might be important for statistical inference or model comparison. A deeper theoretical analysis would provide additional value.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field by bridging Riemannian statistics with manifold learning. The technical content is sound, the writing is clear, and the empirical results convincingly demonstrate the advantages of the proposed approach. While there are some limitations regarding computational scalability and parameter selection, these are acknowledged by the authors and represent natural directions for future work rather than fundamental flaws in the current approach.

The paper meets all the criteria for a NeurIPS publication: it is of high quality, clear, original, and significant. The LAND model has clear potential for application in many machine learning problems that currently rely on Gaussian distributions. The authors have successfully demonstrated the approach on both synthetic and real-world data, providing a compelling case for its utility.

## Recommendation

I strongly recommend acceptance of this paper. The work represents an important step toward developing more flexible generative models for data lying on nonlinear manifolds. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and provides a valuable contribution to the field. The authors have carefully considered both the theoretical and practical aspects of their approach, and the experimental results support their claims. The minor limitations noted are common in papers introducing new frameworks and represent opportunities for future work rather than shortcomings in the current contribution.

The paper is particularly timely given the increasing interest in manifold learning and geometric approaches to machine learning at NeurIPS. It provides a clear pathway for extending many standard Gaussian-based models to the manifold setting, which should be of broad interest to the NeurIPS community.
