This paper introduces a Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND) by replacing the Euclidean distance in the standard normal distribution with a Riemannian distance learned from the data. The core idea is well-motivated – standard normal distributions struggle with nonlinear data manifolds, and leveraging Riemannian geometry offers a principled way to address this. The authors bridge Riemannian statistics and manifold learning, proposing a scalable Monte Carlo integration scheme for normalization and a gradient-based algorithm for parameter estimation. The model is tested on synthetic data and EEG measurements, demonstrating promising results.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty & Theoretical Foundation:** The paper presents a genuinely novel approach by adapting the well-established normal distribution to a Riemannian setting. The connection to maximum entropy principles strengthens the theoretical grounding.
* **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical formulation is generally sound, and the proposed Monte Carlo integration for normalization is a clever solution to a challenging problem. The gradient-based optimization approach is also reasonable.
* **Clear Problem Statement:** The limitations of the standard normal distribution on nonlinear manifolds are clearly articulated.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on both synthetic and real data provide evidence for the effectiveness of the LAND. The results on EEG data are particularly encouraging.
* **Detailed Derivations & Algorithm:** The paper includes detailed derivations for the gradient calculations and a clear algorithmic description, enhancing reproducibility.
* **Related Work Discussion:** The authors demonstrate a good awareness of related work in Riemannian statistics and manifold learning.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** While the authors address the normalization challenge with Monte Carlo integration, the overall computational cost remains a concern, particularly for high-dimensional data. The dependence on solving ODEs for geodesics and exponential/logarithm maps is potentially expensive. The authors acknowledge this but could expand on potential solutions (e.g., approximations, specialized solvers).
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance appears sensitive to the choice of the smoothing parameter σ. A more systematic exploration of parameter tuning strategies would be beneficial. The authors address this partially with the sleep data experiments, but a more thorough sensitivity analysis is needed.
* **Limited Comparison:** The comparison to other manifold learning techniques (e.g., UMAP, t-SNE) is limited. While the GMM comparison is relevant, evaluating the LAND against more modern dimensionality reduction methods would provide a more comprehensive assessment of its performance.
* **Scaling Issues:** The paper focuses on relatively low-dimensional data (5D features from EEG). The scalability to high-dimensional datasets is not thoroughly investigated.
* **Clarity of Notation:** While generally clear, some of the notation (e.g., vec[·], ⊗) could benefit from more explicit explanations for readers unfamiliar with matrix calculus.
* **Overstated Claims:** The claim of "bridging" Riemannian statistics and manifold learning feels somewhat strong. Both fields have been actively researched for decades, and this work primarily *applies* Riemannian geometry to manifold learning rather than fundamentally uniting the fields.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to adapting the normal distribution for nonlinear data.
* The technical contributions (Monte Carlo integration, gradient-based optimization) are significant.
* The empirical results are promising and demonstrate the potential of the LAND.
* The paper is well-written and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The computational complexity and parameter sensitivity raise concerns about the practical applicability of the method.
* The limited comparison to other manifold learning techniques leaves open the question of whether the LAND offers substantial advantages over existing approaches.
* The scaling to high-dimensional data is not demonstrated.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and well-motivated contribution. While there are some weaknesses related to computational complexity, parameter sensitivity, and the scope of empirical evaluation, the strengths of the paper outweigh these concerns. The LAND presents a promising approach to modeling data on nonlinear manifolds, and the authors have made a significant technical contribution in developing a scalable and efficient estimation procedure.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend acceptance, contingent upon the authors addressing the following points:

1. **Expand the discussion of computational complexity:** Elaborate on strategies for mitigating the computational cost, particularly for high-dimensional data.
2. **Perform a more thorough parameter sensitivity analysis:** Investigate the impact of different choices of σ on performance.
3. **Include a comparison to more modern dimensionality reduction techniques:** Evaluate the LAND against methods like UMAP and t-SNE.
4. **Address the scalability issue:** Discuss the limitations of the approach in high dimensions and potential avenues for improvement.
5. **Moderate the claims:** Rephrase the “bridging” statement to reflect the more nuanced relationship between Riemannian statistics and manifold learning.
