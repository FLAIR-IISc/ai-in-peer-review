Summary
The paper proposes the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), a generative model that replaces the linear Euclidean metric in the multivariate normal with a learned, smoothly varying Riemannian metric over RD. The metric M(x) is built from local (diagonal) covariance estimates via Gaussian kernels, inducing a manifold on which geodesics, exponential/logarithm maps, and Mahalanobis distances are defined. The resulting density is the Riemannian normal pM(x|µ,Σ) ∝ exp(−1/2 ⟨Logµ(x), Σ−1 Logµ(x)⟩), with a normalization constant that depends on curvature and µ. A key technical contribution is a scalable Monte Carlo estimate of the normalization constant via integration over the tangent space TµM: C = Z Ev∼N(0,Σ)[√|M(Expµ(v))|], which avoids expensive boundary-value problems for Log and only requires solving initial-value geodesic ODEs (Exp). The authors derive gradients for maximum likelihood estimation with block coordinate descent: update µ along the steepest descent direction on the manifold and update Σ using a factorization Σ−1 = AᵀA. They also develop a mixture of LANDs with an EM-like algorithm. Experiments on synthetic nonlinear manifolds and EEG sleep-stage clustering show that LANDs fit curved structures better than Gaussian mixtures and outperform least-squares (intrinsic) Riemannian estimators; they also show improved F-measure for clustering sleep stages.

Relation to prior work
Riemannian normal distributions and intrinsic statistics have been studied (e.g., Pennec; Zhang & Fletcher for PPGA), typically on known manifolds. The paper’s novelty is to bring these models into a “manifold learning” setting by constructing a data-adaptive metric (building on Hauberg et al.; Tosi et al.), and to tackle the major practical obstacle—the normalization constant—via a convenient tangent-space Monte Carlo estimator. Prior Riemannian mixtures that ignore component-wise normalization constants (e.g., Simo-Serra et al.) are contrasted as probabilistically inconsistent.

Strengths
- Conceptual bridge between manifold learning and Riemannian statistics, yielding a principled, flexible generative model that adapts to local geometry.
- Clever normalization strategy via tangent-space Monte Carlo that reduces the need for expensive BVPs; the gradient formulas for µ and Σ are clear and implementable.
- The maximum-likelihood formulation mitigates the known pitfall of intrinsic means drifting to low-density regions.
- Mixture extension is natural; experiments convincingly show advantages over GMMs in capturing curved clusters and over LS intrinsic estimators.
- The approach is likely of broad interest at NeurIPS given the many Gaussian-based models that could be “manifoldized.”

Weaknesses and concerns
- Metric design: Using only diagonal local covariances is not affine invariant and can be brittle under rotations; correlation structure is ignored. The choice of σ (kernel width) is critical yet treated as a fixed hyperparameter; in the sleep experiments it is tuned per subject, risking overfitting. No procedure to learn σ or assess sensitivity beyond a brief sweep.
- Validity and geometry: The change-of-variables underlying C(µ,Σ) implicitly assumes well-behaved Exp/Log maps (injectivity radius). The paper asserts D(µ̂)=RD for “the manifolds we consider,” but learned metrics can still yield cut loci; some discussion of when the integral over TµM is valid or how violations affect MC estimates is needed.
- Computational cost: Each µ-update needs N logarithm maps (BVPs), which are acknowledged as the bottleneck; the paper provides no runtime/complexity numbers, scaling studies, or strategies (e.g., retractions, approximations, minibatches). Mixtures will exacerbate this. The MC normalization uses S=3000 exponential maps per evaluation—feasible in low D but unclear in higher dimensions.
- Baselines and evaluation: Comparisons are mostly to GMMs with small K and to LS Riemannian estimators. Missing stronger baselines such as mixtures of PPCA/factor analyzers, kernel density, warped mixtures, normalizing flows, or manifold regularization-based generative models. On synthetic data, evaluating “mean NLL of the true generative distribution” via samples is unusual; reporting test log-likelihood on held-out data would be more standard. No ablations on ρ, σ, S, or stability of the optimization.
- Theoretical guarantees: No consistency or identifiability results for MLE; no analysis of the MC estimator’s variance/bias or variance-reduction techniques.
- Clarity/minor: Some derivations are deferred to the supplement; figures are crucial to understanding but not all are described quantitatively; references have formatting issues.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with reasonable derivations of gradients and a practical normalization scheme. However, assumptions behind the tangent-space integration and computational complexity should be discussed more rigorously, and empirical validation lacks runtime/scaling analysis and stronger baselines.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized; the Riemannian preliminaries and algorithm are clear. Please add more details on convergence criteria, step-size selection, and initialization. Include runtime statistics, dimensionalities, and sensitivity plots (σ, S). Clean up reference formatting.

Originality
Moderately original: Riemannian normals and learned metrics exist, but combining them into a tractable maximum-likelihood generative model with a workable normalization and mixture EM is a meaningful and nontrivial contribution.

Significance
Potentially impactful as a drop-in replacement for Gaussians in manifold-aware generative models; however, practicality depends on scalability and robust hyperparameter selection. Broader adoption would benefit from stronger empirical comparisons and runtime analysis.

Questions for the authors
- What are the runtimes per iteration and total for synthetic and EEG experiments? How do costs scale with N, D, K, and S?
- How sensitive are results to σ and ρ? Can σ be learned (e.g., by maximizing held-out likelihood or via a hierarchical prior)?
- Do you use any variance-reduction for estimating C (e.g., control variates, quasi-MC, reusing samples across iterations)?
- How do you handle numerical issues with Exp/Log (step sizes, solver tolerances)? Any fallback retractions/approximations?
- In mixtures, is the metric shared across components and computed from all data? Could this blur cluster boundaries? Have you tried component-specific metrics?

Pros for acceptance
- Principled, well-motivated model; elegant normalization trick; clear algorithm.
- Empirical evidence of improved modeling of nonlinear structure.
- Broad relevance to many Gaussian-based methods.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited baselines and evaluation depth; no scalability study.
- Metric design is simplistic and heavily hyperparameter-dependent.
- Theoretical assumptions behind normalization not fully justified.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The idea is timely and interesting, with a practical algorithm and promising results, but the paper should strengthen empirical comparisons, provide runtimes/scaling, and better justify the geometric and computational assumptions.
