Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), which replaces the Euclidean Mahalanobis distance in a Gaussian with a distance induced by a learned Riemannian metric on RD. The metric is constructed from a locally smoothed (diagonal) covariance estimate via a Gaussian kernel, yielding a smoothly varying SPD field M(x) whose geodesics are attracted to regions of higher data density. On this learned manifold, the authors adopt the Riemannian normal distribution pM(x|µ,Σ) ∝ exp(−1/2⟨Logµ(x), Σ−1Logµ(x)⟩). They address the intractable normalization constant C(µ,Σ) by rewriting the integral over the manifold as an expectation over the tangent space at µ, approximated via Monte Carlo using samples v ∼ N(0,Σ) and m(µ, v) = √|M(Expµ(v))|. They derive gradients for µ (using steepest descent in the tangent space) and for Σ (via a factorization Σ−1 = AᵀA), provide a block-coordinate descent procedure, and extend to mixtures with an EM-like scheme. Experiments on synthetic curved data and on EEG sleep-stage features suggest LAND captures nonlinear structure better than GMMs and intrinsic least-squares Riemannian estimators.

This sits at the intersection of manifold learning (e.g., Isomap, LLE, Laplacian Eigenmaps) and statistics on Riemannian manifolds (e.g., Pennec’s Riemannian normal, Karcher mean, probabilistic geodesic analysis). Prior work typically assumes a known manifold; here the manifold (metric) is learned from data and then used for generative modeling, with careful attention to normalization across components (contrasting Simo-Serra et al.). The MC normalization via tangent space avoids expensive BVPs for Log during integration.

Quality
- Technical correctness: The construction of M(x) and the geodesic machinery are standard. The maximum-likelihood optimization and mixture extension are plausible, and gradients are derived carefully (details deferred to the supplement). The main technical concern is the change-of-variables used for C(µ,Σ). Writing C as an integral over v in TµM with volume element dM(x) = √|M(Expµ(v))| dv appears to omit the Jacobian determinant of the exponential map, i.e., the volume distortion (Jacobi fields) induced by Expµ. In normal coordinates, the volume form is √det g(v) dv where g(v) is the pullback metric (D Expµ(v))ᵀ M(Expµ(v)) (D Expµ(v)); its determinant is not generally equal to det M(Expµ(v)). If the Jacobian is neglected, the MC estimator is biased in curved regions and may depend on curvature in an uncontrolled way. The paper does not justify an approximation regime (e.g., small v, weak curvature) or quantify the error. This is the most serious issue to clarify.
- Algorithms: The optimization requires repeated evaluations of Logµ(xn) (BVPs) and Expµ(v) (IVPs), plus MC with S samples per step. The authors acknowledge the computational burden but do not provide runtimes or scaling behavior; the method may be impractical beyond low D and moderate N. Stability concerns (dependence on Σ’s condition number) are addressed by steepest descent, which is reasonable.
- Empirical evidence: Synthetic demos convincingly show that LAND captures curved clusters with fewer components than GMMs. The EEG study is suggestive but limited: σ is selected by sweep per subject (potentially optimistic), and baselines are not tuned equivalently; comparisons to more flexible density/clustering baselines (e.g., mixtures of factor analyzers, spectral clustering, kernel density models) are missing. No ablations quantify the effect of S, σ, diagonal vs. full M(x), or the cost/accuracy trade-off of the normalization.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized with a clear exposition of Riemannian preliminaries and the learning algorithm. However:
- The key measure transformation for C(µ,Σ) needs a precise derivation; explicitly state any approximations (e.g., ignoring the differential of Exp) and their implications.
- Provide algorithmic details for ODE solvers, step sizes, initialization, and convergence behavior; add complexity analysis and wall-clock times.
- Some notation/typos and figure references could be polished, but these are minor.

Originality
Moderate to good. Using a learned, data-adaptive Riemannian metric to define and fit a Riemannian normal (and mixtures) is a novel combination. The tangent-space MC normalization and end-to-end ML estimation on a learned manifold are new in this setting. The metric choice (diagonal, kernel-smoothed) is simple but pragmatic.

Significance
Potentially impactful as a template to “manifoldize” Gaussian-based generative models with principled normalization and ML fitting. Could stimulate further work at the interface of manifold learning and probabilistic modeling. However, the unaddressed measure-theoretic issue and limited scalability/experimentation weaken the immediate practical significance.

Pros
- Clear, appealing idea; principled attempt to normalize and learn parameters.
- Bridges manifold learning and Riemannian statistics; addresses a real limitation of GMMs on curved data.
- Derives usable gradients and an algorithm; mixture extension accounts for normalization.

Cons
- Potentially incorrect or uncontrolled approximation in the normalization constant (missing Exp Jacobian).
- Computationally heavy; no runtime/scaling analysis; relies on diagonal M and low dimensions.
- Experimental evaluation is limited; baselines and tuning fairness could be improved; no uncertainty quantification or sensitivity analyses.

Questions for authors
1) Is the Jacobian of the exponential map accounted for in Eq. (9)? If not, under what assumptions is it acceptable to drop it, and how does this affect bias/variance of the MC estimator? Can you empirically assess the impact?
2) What are runtimes and scaling with N, D, S, and K? How many Log/Exp solves per iteration?
3) How sensitive are results to σ and S? Any automated selection of σ?
4) Could you compare against stronger baselines (e.g., mixtures of factor analyzers, spectral clustering, kernel density) and report ablations?

Recommendation
Borderline. The idea and empirical promise merit attention, but the treatment of the normalization constant requires clarification/correction, and the experimental evaluation is not yet strong enough. If the authors can justify the measure transformation (or provide a corrected, tractable estimator) and strengthen empirical comparisons with runtime reporting, I would lean toward acceptance. As is, I would give a weak reject.
