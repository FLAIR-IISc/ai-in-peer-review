This paper introduces a Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND) by replacing the Euclidean distance in a standard normal distribution with a Riemannian distance learned from the data. The core idea, bridging Riemannian geometry with manifold learning, is to construct a smoothly changing metric that reflects the data’s structure and utilize this metric for density estimation and statistical inference. The authors propose a Monte Carlo integration scheme for normalization and a gradient-based algorithm for maximum likelihood estimation, extending this to a mixture model with an EM algorithm. The approach is evaluated on synthetic data and EEG measurements of sleep stages, demonstrating its effectiveness compared to standard Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs).

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of Riemannian geometry and manifold learning to define a locally adaptive distribution is a genuinely novel contribution. It offers a principled approach to modeling data residing on nonlinear manifolds, going beyond traditional methods like GMMs that rely on linear assumptions.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper is grounded in a solid mathematical framework, leveraging concepts from Riemannian geometry to provide a rigorous foundation for the proposed LAND.  The connection to maximum entropy principles is also a noteworthy strength.
* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed Monte Carlo integration scheme for normalization appears well-motivated and addresses a key challenge in applying Riemannian statistics to learned manifolds. The gradient-based estimation algorithm is a reasonable approach for parameter inference.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on both synthetic and real data (EEG measurements) provide evidence supporting the effectiveness of the LAND.  The comparison with GMMs and LS-based Riemannian normal distributions showcases the benefits of the maximum likelihood estimation.
* **Clarity of Presentation:** Despite the mathematical complexity, the paper is generally well-written and organized. Key concepts are explained clearly, and the notation is consistent.  Figure 2 visually clarifies the exponential and logarithm maps, enhancing understanding.
* **Practical Relevance:**  The application to EEG sleep stage analysis highlights the potential of the LAND for practical problems where data exhibits nonlinear structure.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** The reliance on numerical solution of ODEs to compute geodesics (and hence the exponential/logarithm maps) is computationally expensive.  While the authors address this with tangent space integration, this remains a potential bottleneck, particularly for high-dimensional data. More discussion of the computational complexity relative to other manifold learning approaches would be helpful.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance of the LAND appears sensitive to the choice of the kernel bandwidth parameter σ. While the authors investigate this parameter in the sleep stage experiments, a more systematic exploration and discussion of methods for adaptive bandwidth selection would strengthen the paper.
* **Metric Invariance:** The authors acknowledge that the proposed metric is not invariant to linear transformations. This limitation should be more prominently discussed, as it may restrict the applicability of the LAND in scenarios where such invariance is desirable.
* **Scalability:**  Although improvements are made with tangent space integration, scalability to very large datasets is not thoroughly addressed.  The authors mention probabilistic solvers as a potential improvement, but a more detailed discussion of scalability challenges and potential solutions would be beneficial.
* **Comparison Depth:** While comparisons with GMMs and LS estimators are provided, a more comprehensive comparison with other recent manifold learning and density estimation techniques (e.g., normalizing flows, diffusion models) would further contextualize the contribution of this work.
* **Missing Details:** The initialization procedure for the optimization algorithm is briefly mentioned in the supplements, but more detail in the main body would be beneficial for reproducibility.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to density estimation on nonlinear manifolds.
* The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the LAND compared to existing methods.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains complex concepts.
* The work has the potential to impact areas where data exhibits nonlinear structure.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The computational cost of the LAND remains a concern, potentially limiting its scalability.
* The sensitivity to the bandwidth parameter σ requires further investigation.
* The lack of a comprehensive comparison with other advanced manifold learning and density estimation techniques.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a compelling contribution to the field. The authors propose a theoretically grounded and practically relevant approach for modeling data on nonlinear manifolds. While the computational cost and parameter sensitivity are legitimate concerns, the novelty and potential impact of the work outweigh these limitations.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. 

**Suggested Revisions:**

*   Expand the discussion of computational complexity and scalability, potentially including a more detailed analysis of the cost of geodesic computation.
*   Provide a more systematic exploration of bandwidth selection methods for the kernel.
*   Include a more comprehensive comparison with recent and relevant manifold learning and density estimation techniques.
*   Elaborate on the initialization procedure for the optimization algorithm in the main paper.
*   Explicitly discuss the limitations due to the lack of metric invariance.
