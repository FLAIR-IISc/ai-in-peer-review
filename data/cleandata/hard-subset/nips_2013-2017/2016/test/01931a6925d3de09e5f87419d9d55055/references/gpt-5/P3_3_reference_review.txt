Summary
The paper proposes the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), which replaces the Euclidean metric underlying a Gaussian with a learned, smoothly varying Riemannian metric on RD. Specifically: (i) a diagonal, data-adaptive metric M(x) is defined as the inverse of a kernel-weighted local diagonal covariance (Eq. 7), encouraging geodesics to stay near high-density regions; (ii) the Riemannian normal distribution pM(x|µ,Σ) is used with this metric (Pennec 2006), and the normalizing constant C(µ,Σ) is approximated via Monte Carlo integration in the tangent space at µ by sampling v∼N(0,Σ) and evaluating m(µ,v)=√|M(Expµ(v))|; (iii) maximum-likelihood estimation is done with gradients for µ and Σ and geodesic updates for µ; (iv) a mixture model and EM are sketched. Experiments on synthetic manifolds and EEG sleep-stage features indicate improved fit and clustering versus GMMs and least-squares Riemannian estimators.

Relation to prior work
The work bridges Riemannian statistics (Pennec; Zhang & Fletcher) with manifold learning by learning the metric rather than assuming a known manifold (e.g., spheres, SPD matrices). It builds on Hauberg et al.’s data-adaptive metrics and geodesic computation, and contrasts with prior mixtures on known manifolds (Simo-Serra et al., Straub et al.). The emphasis on estimating the normalizing constants per component is an important distinction from some earlier works that dropped them.

Strengths
- Conceptually appealing: a simple route to “nonlinear Gaussians” via a learned Riemannian metric; the model inherits maximum-entropy properties conditioned on a fixed manifold.
- Practical algorithm: sampling-based approximation for C(µ,Σ) that avoids expensive BVPs by working in the tangent space; use of geodesic IVPs for Exp.
- Empirical results show LAND captures non-elliptical clusters and can outperform GMMs on synthetic data and EEG clustering.
- The mixture extension is natural and potentially useful.

Main concerns and weaknesses
1) Change-of-variables and normalization constant. Eq. (9) appears to omit the Jacobian of the exponential map. In general, for x=Expµ(v), the Riemannian volume transforms as dM(x)=√|M(x)| |det DExpµ(v)| dv, i.e., the pullback volume density in normal coordinates is √|G(v)| with G(v)=DExpµ(v)ᵀ M(x) DExpµ(v). The paper uses m(µ,v)=√|M(Expµ(v))| only, effectively setting |det DExpµ(v)|=1. This is typically false except in flat spaces or infinitesimal neighborhoods. This omission biases C(µ,Σ) and its gradients. If the omission is intentional as an approximation, it should be stated, justified (e.g., small curvature regime), and its impact assessed. Otherwise, the MLE interpretation and likelihood comparisons are questionable.

2) Gradient derivations. Eq. (12) for ∇µφ features E[m(µ,v) v], which would not arise from ∇µ log C without accounting for how m depends on µ via Expµ(v). The derivation is not provided, and given point (1), the expression seems to neglect ∇µ of the Jacobian and of M along Expµ(v). Similarly, the “steepest descent direction” dµφ=−Σ ∇µφ uses Σ as a preconditioner rather than the actual Riemannian metric M(µ); this is ad hoc and needs justification (is this natural gradient under a chosen inner product, or a heuristic for conditioning?).

3) Metric design. The metric is diagonal and based on raw second moments around x (not centered by the local mean), hence not invariant to affine transforms and unable to capture local anisotropy/coupling. This can distort geodesics and density in oblique directions. A discussion or ablation (e.g., local PCA-based full metrics, or whitened input) would strengthen the case.

4) Global geometry. The approach assumes Logµ(x) is globally defined for all x (Sec. 2.1 claims D(µ̂)=RD). On learned metrics with nontrivial curvature, cut loci and non-unique geodesics are plausible. The paper does not state conditions ensuring geodesic completeness or uniqueness. This affects the well-definedness of the model and optimization.

5) Computational scalability. Computing C(µ,Σ) requires S evaluations of Exp per iteration; with S=3000 this is expensive. No timing, scaling with D, N, or S, or variance-reduction strategies (e.g., common random numbers across iterations, control variates) are reported. Mixture EM could be very costly.

6) Evaluation methodology. In the EEG experiment, σ is also selected per subject by choosing the best F-measure over a grid, which uses labels for model selection in an unsupervised task. This risks optimistic results. A label-free selection (e.g., held-out likelihood, AIC/BIC or stability) or a fixed σ determined on training data only should be used. Baselines are limited; comparisons to manifold-aware clustering (spectral clustering), GMMs after nonlinear embeddings (Isomap/LLE/kPCA), or kernel density estimators with variable bandwidth would be informative. Held-out likelihoods (with unbiased estimates) would help.

7) Theoretical positioning. The paper invokes the maximum-entropy characterization of the Riemannian normal on fixed manifolds; since the manifold is learned from data and not fixed, the practical implication is weaker. This nuance is acknowledged but could be emphasized more.

Quality
There is a potentially serious technical flaw (or undocumented approximation) around the normalization constant and its gradient. The empirical results are promising but not decisive given selection issues and limited baselines. The method description is generally clear but key derivations are deferred or missing.

Clarity
Well-written and organized. However, Eq. (9) and the gradients need a precise derivation or an explicit approximation statement. Choices of σ and ρ, their impact, and computational costs should be detailed. The mixture EM should be summarized beyond “see supplement.”

Originality
Combining a learned Riemannian metric with a Riemannian normal and estimating its normalizer per component is a neat, original combination. Related work is cited appropriately for 2016.

Significance
If the normalization and gradient issues are resolved, LAND could be a useful tool for generative modeling on data manifolds and inspire extensions. As is, the theoretical gap and evaluation limitations reduce confidence.

Questions for the authors
- Please derive Eq. (9) including the Jacobian of Exp, or justify its omission and quantify the approximation error.
- Provide the full derivation of Eq. (12); how do terms from ∂µ Expµ(v) and ∂µ M(x) enter?
- How are ρ and σ selected in a label-free manner? Sensitivity?
- What are the computational costs per iteration and per mixture component? Any variance-reduction used for Monte Carlo?
- Do you observe non-uniqueness of Log/Exp in practice? Any safeguards?
- How robust are results to using a centered local covariance or a full (non-diagonal) local metric?

Pros
- Clear, compelling idea; elegant unification of manifold learning and Riemannian statistics.
- Accounts for per-component normalizers (unlike some prior work).
- Demonstrates ability to model non-elliptical clusters.

Cons
- Likely incorrect/incomplete change of variables for the normalizer; gradients may be inconsistent.
- Metric design is simplistic (diagonal, non-invariant).
- Computational scalability unclear; no timing/variance analysis.
- Evaluation includes label-informed hyperparameter selection; limited baselines.

Recommendation
Weak reject unless the normalization/gradient derivations are corrected or justified as approximations with empirical validation, and the evaluation protocol is tightened. The idea is promising and could be impactful with these issues addressed.
