Summary
The paper introduces the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), a parametric density on a learned Riemannian manifold that replaces Euclidean distances in the Gaussian with geodesic distances under a data-adaptive metric. The manifold is constructed via a smoothly varying diagonal metric tensor M(x), defined as the inverse of a Gaussian-kernel-smoothed local (unnormalized) diagonal covariance with a small ridge ρ. This makes geodesics prefer high-density regions. The authors adopt Pennec’s Riemannian normal pM(x|µ,Σ) ∝ exp(−1/2 ⟨Logµ(x), Σ−1 Logµ(x)⟩) and address the key challenge of the normalization constant C(µ,Σ), which depends on the manifold geometry and µ. They propose a scalable Monte Carlo approximation by pushing the integral onto the tangent space TµM: C = ∫ √|M(Expµ(v))| exp(−1/2 v⊤Σ−1v) dv, estimated via samples v ∼ N(0,Σ) and only requiring exponential maps (IVPs), avoiding expensive logarithm maps (BVPs). They give gradients for maximum-likelihood estimation: a steepest-descent direction for µ and a factorization Σ−1 = A⊤A with gradient for A. They also sketch a mixture of LANDs with an EM-like procedure. Experiments on synthetic curved manifolds and EEG sleep-stage clustering show improved fit/clustering over Gaussian mixtures and over least-squares Riemannian estimators.

Relation to prior work
The work connects Riemannian statistics (Pennec’s Riemannian normal; intrinsic mean/covariance; Riemannian PCA variants) with manifold learning (Isomap, LLE, Laplacian eigenmaps). Unlike embedding-based methods, the learned metric yields continuous geodesics in ambient RD. Compared to Simo-Serra et al. on finite mixtures on known manifolds, the paper accounts for normalization constants and learns the manifold metric from data. The approach is also related to probabilistic geometries and learned metrics, but here the metric is constructed directly from local covariances.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual bridge: a maximum-entropy Riemannian normal instantiated on a learned, smoothly varying metric that favors high-density regions.
- Practical normalization scheme: tangent-space Monte Carlo using Exp maps is elegant and likely more efficient than manifold-space integration requiring Log maps.
- Full ML treatment: gradients for both µ and Σ and an algorithmic recipe; extension to mixtures (with normalized components) is valuable.
- Empirical benefits: LAND captures non-linear cluster structure with fewer components than GMMs; avoids intrinsic-mean pitfalls that drift to low-density regions.
- Generally well motivated, with sensible discussion of limitations and future directions.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Metric design limitations: using only diagonal local covariances yields non-invariance to linear transforms and cannot capture local anisotropy beyond axis-aligned directions. A local PCA (full SPD) variant or mixtures of metrics would likely improve expressiveness; at least an ablation with full SPD in 2D would be informative.
- Dependence on σ: performance hinges on kernel width. In EEG, σ is tuned per subject by selection over a grid, which risks optimistic reporting. Please describe a principled selection (e.g., cross-validation, likelihood on held-out data) or learn σ by ML (with automatic differentiation through the ODE solver).
- Computational complexity: Each evaluation of C(µ,Σ) needs S Exponential map solves; each gradient step for the data term needs N Log maps. There are no runtime/memory reports, scaling analysis (in N, D, S), or sensitivity to S (beyond a single S=3000 choice). Including wall-clock times, convergence plots, and accuracy vs. S would help assess practicality.
- Gradient correctness/derivations: The µ-gradient in Eq. 12 involves E[m(µ,v) v], which is not obviously the derivative of log C; the dependence of m on µ is through Expµ and the metric, so ∂µ log C involves ∂µ m, not m·v, unless additional identities are used. Please provide the full derivation (currently deferred to supplements) and check for missing factors or assumptions (e.g., reparameterization/transport identities). Similarly, clarify factors of 1/2 between Eqs. 11–14 for consistency.
- Geometric well-posedness: The paper assumes D(µ)=RD and easy evaluation of Log maps, but learned metrics can be incomplete or have cut loci where Log is multivalued. Please discuss conditions (on σ, ρ) ensuring existence/uniqueness of Exp/Log used in optimization, and how failures are handled numerically.
- Evaluation breadth: Experiments are low-dimensional (2D synthetic, 5D EEG features) with modest N. No comparisons to kernel density estimation, diffusion distances, or probabilistic manifold models (e.g., GP-LVM variants) that could serve as strong baselines for nonlinear density modeling. Adding at least one higher-D benchmark and a nonparametric density baseline would strengthen the empirical case.
- Mixture EM details: EM updates and how C(µk,Σk) are estimated per component, how responsibilities are stabilized, and initialization are in supplements; some of this should appear in the main text. Also clarify whether samples v are resampled when Σ changes (to avoid bias) and whether variance-reduction techniques were used.

Quality
The approach is technically plausible and well motivated. However, several key derivations are deferred; the µ-gradient for the normalization term needs clearer justification. The algorithmic complexity and stability are underexplored experimentally. The empirical results are promising but limited in scope.

Clarity
The paper reads well and provides helpful geometric context. Some notational choices could be tightened (consistent factors of 1/2, domains, and explicit assumptions for Exp/Log existence). Including pseudo-code for mixture EM and a brief complexity section would improve clarity. Figures effectively illustrate geodesics and contours.

Originality
Using a learned Riemannian metric to instantiate a normalized Riemannian normal and fit it by ML on a data-adaptive manifold is a novel and interesting combination. The tangent-space Monte Carlo normalization is a neat contribution. Related works on known manifolds and on manifold learning are appropriately cited.

Significance
If scalable and robustly implementable, LAND could be a useful drop-in for generative modeling on curved data and a foundation for manifold-aware extensions of standard models (GMMs, LDA, PCA). The idea is likely to stimulate follow-up work on learning metrics and improving computational methods.

Questions for authors
- Provide a full derivation of Eq. 12. Under what assumptions does the E[m(µ,v) v] term arise? Are there missing factors?
- How do you ensure numerical stability/uniqueness of Logµ(x) during optimization? What happens near cut loci?
- What are typical runtimes per iteration and per dataset; how do costs scale with N, D, and S? Any variance-reduction for Monte Carlo?
- Do you resample v when Σ changes in Algorithm 1? If not, what bias is introduced?
- How sensitive are results to ρ and to data standardization? Is the metric computed on normalized features?

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually appealing and novel bridge between Riemannian statistics and manifold learning.
- Practical normalization strategy; ML estimation with mixtures.
- Empirical gains on nonlinear data; clear qualitative improvements.

Cons for acceptance
- Key gradient derivations and theoretical guarantees are unclear or deferred.
- Limited experimental scope and no scalability analysis; σ tuning may overstate gains.
- Metric restrictiveness (diagonal) and lack of invariance.

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. The idea is strong and promising; if the authors clarify derivations (especially Eq. 12), add complexity/scaling evidence, and temper σ selection with a principled procedure, this would be a solid contribution to NIPS.
