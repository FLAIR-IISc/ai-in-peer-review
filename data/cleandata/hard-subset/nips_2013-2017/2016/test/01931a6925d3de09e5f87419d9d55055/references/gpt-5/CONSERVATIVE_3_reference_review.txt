Summary
The paper proposes a “locally adaptive normal distribution” (LAND) obtained by replacing the Euclidean metric in a Gaussian with a learned, smoothly varying Riemannian metric over RD. The metric is defined via diagonal kernel-smoothed local covariances (Eq. 7), producing geodesics that preferentially follow regions of high data density. The authors adopt Pennec’s Riemannian normal form (Eq. 4) and address the intractable normalization constant by a Monte Carlo estimate over the tangent space at the mean (Eq. 9–10), leveraging repeated evaluations of the exponential map. They derive gradients for maximum likelihood estimation of mean and covariance (Eqs. 12–13) and extend to mixtures with an EM-like procedure. Experiments on synthetic data and an EEG sleep-stage clustering task show visual and F-measure improvements over GMMs and over least-squares intrinsic estimators.

Relation to prior work: Riemannian normal distributions on known manifolds are well established (Pennec, Zhang & Fletcher). Learning a metric from data for manifold-conforming geodesics is also known (e.g., Hauberg et al., Tosi et al.). The main novelty here is combining a learned Riemannian metric with a tractable approximation of the normalization constant and an ML training procedure, then packaging it as a mixture model.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: Euclidean Gaussians struggle on data concentrated around nonlinear manifolds; the Riemannian construction is principled and connects to maximum entropy results on manifolds.
- Tangent-space Monte Carlo for the normalizer is a sensible idea that avoids expensive BVPs for the log map inside the integral.
- The framework is modular: given exp/log maps, it could be reused with other learned metrics.
- Synthetic plots suggest the model follows curved clusters more faithfully than GMMs; the LAND mean avoids low-density regions, aligning with known issues of intrinsic means.
- Application to EEG clustering is interesting and relevant, with some positive results.

Weaknesses and concerns
Methodological soundness
1) Derivation of gradients, especially Eq. 12: The derivative of log C(μ, Σ) with respect to μ should involve ∂m(μ, v)/∂μ, which depends on the differential of the exponential map and the spatial variation of the metric determinant. The presented expression that reduces to a weighted average of v is not obviously justified. If this relies on approximations (e.g., first-order expansions, neglecting certain Jacobians), they should be stated and validated. Without a clear, correct derivation (or error bounds), the ML procedure’s validity is uncertain.

2) Monte Carlo normalization: S = 3000 is chosen empirically, but there is no analysis of variance, bias, or stability across iterations, nor use of variance reduction (common random numbers, control variates, importance sampling). The mixture E-step depends on approximate normalizers for each component; monotonicity of EM is not guaranteed under MC noise. Empirical diagnostics (e.g., likelihood traces with/without shared seeds) and sensitivity to S are missing.

3) Computational feasibility: Each evaluation of m(μ, v) requires an exponential map (IVP ODE), and the metric M(x) itself sums kernel weights over all data points (Eq. 7). Training requires repeated log maps Logμ(xn) (BVP) per iteration for all data points. There are no complexity analyses, runtime reports, or implementation details such as neighborhood truncation, approximate nearest neighbors, or caching of metric evaluations. As presented, the method appears to scale poorly in N, D, K, and S.

4) Metric design: Restricting to diagonal local covariances makes the metric non-invariant to linear transforms and discards local correlations. The authors acknowledge this but do not quantify the impact. Experiments should include sensitivity to rotations/whitening and an ablation with full local covariances (even on small D) to demonstrate that diagonal is not unduly limiting.

Empirical validation
5) Evaluation protocol on synthetic data: The metric reported—“mean NLL of the true generative distribution using samples drawn from each fitted model”—is nonstandard and can be misleading; it estimates E_{x~p_model}[−log p_true(x)], not the held-out log-likelihood under the model. Standard practice is to evaluate test log-likelihood under the model (or a consistent proxy) and to report KLs symmetrically if intended. Please revise the evaluation.

6) Baselines: Comparisons are limited to GMM and a LS variant. Stronger baselines include: mixtures of factor analyzers/PPCA, DP-GMM, variable-bandwidth KDE, spectral clustering or manifold clustering methods, mixture models on graph/geodesic distances with out-of-sample mapping, and Riemannian mixture models on fixed manifolds (e.g., Simo-Serra et al.) with proper normalization. An embedding+GMM baseline (with out-of-sample extension) would be informative.

7) Hyperparameter selection: For EEG, selecting σ by maximizing F-measure uses labels and compromises the unsupervised setting. Report results with σ chosen in a label-free manner (e.g., via unsupervised heuristics, stability, or a validation set). Provide sensitivity plots for σ and ρ.

8) Robustness and reproducibility: No ablations on S, initializations, step sizes, or failure cases; no error bars or multiple runs. Code/runtime should be provided given the algorithmic complexity.

Clarity
Generally well written, but important derivations are deferred to supplements; several references are incomplete. The paper would benefit from:
- A precise derivation of Eqs. 12–13, including the role of the exponential map Jacobian and assumptions needed.
- A clear complexity section with asymptotics and practical speed-ups.
- Details on computing Log maps (solver choice, tolerances).

Originality
Moderate. The Riemannian normal and learned metrics are known; the specific combination with tangent-space MC normalization and an ML training loop is a reasonable, but incremental, contribution. Novelty would be stronger with rigorous analysis of the estimator’s properties (consistency, convergence) or a learned metric with invariance/identifiability guarantees.

Significance
Potentially useful, but current empirical evidence is not compelling enough: limited baselines, nonstandard metrics, small-scale experiments, and unclear scalability.

Pros
- Principled geometric framing; interesting idea to Monte Carlo the normalizer in the tangent space.
- Visually convincing synthetic results; promising application domain.

Cons
- Questionable gradient derivation and lack of theoretical guarantees.
- Heavy computation without complexity or runtime evidence.
- Weak/insufficient baselines and problematic evaluation protocols.
- Strong sensitivity to hand-chosen σ; diagonal metric limits applicability.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). The approach is intriguing, but the paper needs stronger theoretical justification of the optimization, rigorous and fair empirical evaluation with stronger baselines and standard metrics, and a clear story on computational scalability.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide full, verifiable derivations of gradients; if approximations are used, quantify their impact.
- Add runtime/scaling experiments and practical implementation details (neighbor truncation, caching).
- Use standard held-out log-likelihood; add stronger baselines as listed above.
- Report sensitivity/robustness to σ, S, initialization; avoid label-informed hyperparameter tuning.
- Explore full local covariances (on low D) or show invariance via preprocessing (whitening/rotation).
