- Paper introduces Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND) using geodesic distance on a learned Riemannian manifold for density estimation.
- Manifold constructed from data via a locally adaptive metric based on Gaussian kernels; normalization handled by a scalable Monte Carlo approach.
- Gradient-based maximum likelihood estimation algorithm developed and extended to mixtures of LANDs; demonstrated on synthetic data and EEG sleep-stage data.
- Builds on Riemannian geometry and manifold learning literature, emphasizing learning the manifold rather than assuming it known.
- Novel contribution: combining Riemannian statistics with data-driven manifold learning to produce a parametric, locally adaptive density estimator.
- Strong theoretical foundation grounded in Riemannian geometry and connections to maximum entropy distributions.
- Practical algorithm provided, including Monte Carlo normalization and gradient optimization.
- Empirical results show LAND can outperform GMMs on synthetic and EEG datasets; sleep-stage analysis is compelling.
- Paper is generally well-written and explains the mathematical concepts clearly.
- Major weakness: computational cost due to ODE-based geodesic computation and Monte Carlo integration, raising scalability concerns.
- Performance likely sensitive to the smoothing parameter σ; experiments lack systematic sensitivity analysis.
- Experiments are limited in scope; more comparisons with state-of-the-art methods and complex datasets are needed.
- Metric is not invariant to linear transformations; implications and limitations are not thoroughly discussed.
- Algorithm 1 is described high-level; key gradient details and stopping criteria are moved to supplementary material.
- Quality score 8/10: technically sound but concerns about complexity and sensitivity.
- Clarity score 7/10: generally clear but notation dense and algorithm description lacking detail.
- Originality score 8/10: novel integration of geometry and manifold learning.
- Significance score 7/10: potentially impactful if computational issues are addressed.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically sound, promising empirics, clear presentation.
- Arguments against acceptance: computational and scalability concerns, parameter sensitivity, limited experiments, insufficient algorithmic detail.
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions.
- Required revisions: detail computational complexity and scalability strategies; perform thorough sensitivity analysis of σ; expand experiments and comparisons; provide a more detailed description of Algorithm 1 (gradients and stopping criteria).
