Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), obtained by replacing the Euclidean metric of a multivariate Gaussian with a data-driven Riemannian metric, thereby defining a Riemannian normal distribution on a learned manifold. The metric is constructed from a diagonal, kernel-weighted local covariance estimator (with Gaussian kernel width σ and regularizer ρ). Geodesic distances and the associated Exp/Log maps are computed by numerically solving the geodesic ODE under this metric. Because the Riemannian normal’s normalization constant C(µ, Σ) is intractable on a learned manifold, the authors derive a scalable Monte Carlo estimator by pulling back integration to the tangent space at µ: C = ∫TµM √|M(Expµ(v))| exp(−1/2 vᵀΣ−1v) dv, which they estimate with samples v ∼ N(0, Σ). They then propose gradient-based maximum likelihood for µ and Σ (Σ parameterized via A with Σ−1 = AᵀA), and extend the model to mixtures with an EM-like algorithm that properly accounts for the component-wise normalization constants.

Empirically, LAND is shown to better fit nonlinear synthetic manifolds (including clustering of non-ellipsoidal structures) and to yield improved clustering alignment with sleep-stage labels on EEG-derived features compared to Gaussian mixtures and least-squares (intrinsic mean/covariance) Riemannian estimators. The work connects statistics on known manifolds (e.g., Pennec; Zhang & Fletcher) with manifold learning (e.g., Isomap, LLE), but departs from discrete graph geodesics by learning a smooth metric so that Exp/Log are defined throughout RD, enabling a proper generative model.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The construction of a smooth, diagonal, kernel-based metric is standard and ensures solvable geodesics. Pulling back the normalization integral to TµM is elegant and computationally advantageous (IVPs rather than BVPs). Gradients for µ and A are derived; using steepest descent in µ to mitigate conditioning of Σ is reasonable. The mixture formulation correctly includes normalization constants per component, which some prior works neglected.
- However, some derivational details are opaque in the main text. In particular, the µ-gradient of log C (Eq. 12) appears to reduce to an expectation of m(µ, v) v under N(0, Σ); the dependence of m on µ through Expµ(v) and M(·) suggests Jacobians that are not explicitly shown. You mention supplements; bringing a succinct derivation or pointing to autodiff through the ODE solver would improve confidence. For Σ, the A-gradient (Eq. 13) is plausible from reparameterization/score-function identities; again, a brief intuition would help.
- Monte Carlo variance: Using S = 3000 samples per evaluation of C and its gradients could be expensive; there is no analysis of estimator variance, reuse of common random numbers across iterations, or variance-reduction (antithetics, quasi-MC, control variates). The choice S = 3000 is said to be empirically adequate, but a sensitivity/runtime plot would strengthen the claim.
- Computation: Evaluating m(µ, v) requires Exp and determinant of M at Exp. With kernel weights over all N points, M(x) evaluation is O(ND) unless truncated to sparse neighborhoods. Practical complexity and memory are not quantified, especially for mixtures (E-step involves K evaluations per data point). A k-NN graph or ball tree to sparsify wn(x) would be natural; please clarify whether you do this.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-written and well-structured; figures convincingly illustrate geodesics and learned contours. Algorithm 1 is clear. A brief complexity section and a schematic of the computational pipeline would increase readability.
- Some references are incomplete or malformed; please clean the bibliography (e.g., Tenenbaum et al.; Purves et al.).
- The limitations of the diagonal metric and non-invariance to linear transforms are acknowledged; an empirical note on whitening or a simple pre-processing to mitigate this would help practitioners.

Originality
- The central idea—building a Riemannian normal on a learned smooth manifold and estimating its normalization constant via tangent-space Monte Carlo—is, to my knowledge, novel. Prior Riemannian models mostly assume known manifolds; graph-geodesic methods lack continuous Exp/Log and a proper generative density.
- The mixture model with proper normalization per component addresses a gap in earlier “geodesic mixture” work.

Significance
- The approach provides a principled, maximum-entropy density adapted to local data geometry, with clear wins on synthetic manifolds and nontrivial improvements on EEG clustering. It suggests a path to manifold-aware generative modeling beyond embeddings and could be reused in downstream models (e.g., Naive Bayes, LDA, PCA variants).
- Scalability and sensitivity to σ currently limit breadth; nonetheless, the contribution is likely to spur follow-up work.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean bridge between manifold learning and Riemannian statistics.
- Sound probabilistic treatment with proper normalization and ML estimation.
- Practical tangent-space MC estimator; avoids expensive Log-map BVPs.
- Empirically compelling on synthetic manifolds; improved clustering on EEG.
- Extension to mixtures with an EM-like algorithm.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Metric construction:
  - Only diagonal, kernel-smoothed covariance; not affine-invariant. Consider whitening, local PCA (low-rank anisotropy), or mixtures of metrics to improve invariance and expressivity.
  - Learn σ (and ρ) by ML, evidence maximization, or cross-validation; provide a principled selection protocol instead of a sweep. A sensitivity analysis is needed.
  - Use sparse neighborhoods (k-NN) for wn to scale with N; discuss complexity and accuracy trade-offs.
- Monte Carlo estimation:
  - Report runtime vs S and the variance of estimates; reuse fixed random seeds across iterations for variance reduction; consider antithetics/quasi-MC; control variates via a Taylor expansion around v = 0 (using m(µ, 0) = √|M(µ)|).
  - For mixtures, consider importance sampling from a proposal tied to the previous Σ to reduce per-iteration cost.
- Optimization:
  - Include a line-search or adaptive step sizes; mention convergence diagnostics and failure modes.
  - Clarify gradient derivations for µ; if using autodiff through the ODE solver for Exp, state this.
- Evaluation:
  - Add baselines closer in spirit: spectral clustering, kernel k-means, mixtures of factor analyzers, t-mixtures, graph-geodesic GMM; and a comparison to using Isomap/LLE embeddings with GMM in the embedded space.
  - Higher-D tests and scalability study (effect of D, N, K; cost per iteration).
  - Report initialization strategy and robustness.
  - Ablations: effect of diagonal vs full local covariance (at least in 2–3D), effect of σ, and effect of S.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and principled modeling idea; proper probabilistic treatment on a learned manifold.
- Methodologically solid and broadly relevant; likely to inspire extensions.
- Clear empirical benefits on nonlinear structures.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited analysis of computational complexity, estimator variance, and scalability.
- Sensitivity to σ with no principled selection; diagonal metric limits invariance.
- Missing stronger baselines and ablations.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The conceptual contribution and the careful probabilistic formulation are strong and relevant to NeurIPS. The paper would be strengthened by deeper computational/variance analysis, principled σ selection, and broader baselines, but the core ideas are solid and empirically promising.
