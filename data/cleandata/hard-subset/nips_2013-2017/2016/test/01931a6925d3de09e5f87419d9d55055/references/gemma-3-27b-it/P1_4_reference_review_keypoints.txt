- Proposes the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND) to model data near nonlinear manifolds using a data-dependent Riemannian metric.  
- Defines the metric locally via a Gaussian-kernel-weighted covariance and computes geodesics to measure distances on the manifold.  
- Tackles normalization of the manifold-induced density using Monte Carlo integration.  
- Develops a gradient-based maximum likelihood estimator and an EM algorithm for mixtures of LANDs.  
- Demonstrated effectiveness on synthetic data and EEG sleep-stage measurements.  
- Novel combination of Riemannian statistics and manifold learning in a parametric, likelihood-based framework.  
- Technically sound application of Riemannian geometry with a clever Monte Carlo normalization scheme.  
- Gradient-based optimization and algorithmic design appear well-motivated.  
- Paper is generally clear, well-organized, and uses standard, readable notation.  
- Empirical results show LAND outperforming standard GMMs; EEG results are promising.  
- Ablation on kernel width σ provides some sensitivity analysis.  
- Strong theoretical foundation grounded in Riemannian geometry.  
- Computational cost of geodesic solving and Monte Carlo integration raises scalability concerns, especially in high dimensions.  
- Performance is sensitive to the smoothing parameter σ; automatic selection methods are lacking.  
- The learned metric is not invariant to linear transformations, limiting applicability.  
- Synthetic experiments are simple; broader comparisons (e.g., UMAP, t-SNE) and more complex manifolds are missing.  
- Algorithm 1 lacks detail on why the gradient is unstable and how steepest descent is implemented.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically sound, empirically promising, and bridges manifold learning with Riemannian statistics.  
- Arguments against acceptance: scalability, σ sensitivity, lack of linear invariance, and limited empirical evaluation.  
- Overall assessment: valuable contribution; recommend accept with minor revisions.  
- Suggested revisions: explicitly address scalability and complexity, provide methods for σ selection, expand experiments and baseline comparisons, elaborate on steepest-descent implementation, and prominently discuss linear-transformation limitations.
