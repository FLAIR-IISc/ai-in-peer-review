- Proposes the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND) that replaces Euclidean metric with a learned Riemannian metric for data near nonlinear manifolds.  
- Uses Monte Carlo integration to approximate the normalization constant and develops a gradient-based MLE algorithm and EM extension for mixtures.  
- Evaluated on synthetic data and EEG sleep-stage data, showing improvements over standard GMMs and least-squares Riemannian normals.  
- Builds on prior work in Riemannian normals (Pennec) and manifold learning (Isomap), extending to learned manifolds and continuous metrics.  
- Connects to Hauberg et al.’s smooth metric learning and situates itself within statistical methods on manifolds and nonparametric density estimation.  
- Novel, theoretically grounded idea with clear mathematical formulation and rigorous derivations.  
- Practical algorithmic contributions, including a scalable Monte Carlo scheme and IVP-based geodesic solving for scalability.  
- Empirical validation is compelling, particularly on real-world sleep-stage EEG data.  
- Paper is well-written with a helpful primer on Riemannian geometry and highlights the maximum entropy property.  
- Computational cost of geodesic computations and ODE solving is a major concern, especially in high dimensions.  
- Selection of the kernel width σ is heuristic and somewhat ad-hoc; a more systematic selection method is needed.  
- Lack of invariance to linear transformations is a significant limitation that may affect applicability.  
- Limited theoretical analysis of statistical properties (consistency, asymptotics) of the LAND estimator.  
- Algorithm 1 lacks implementation details (step size selection, stopping criteria).  
- Presentation is dense and notation-heavy; more intuitive explanations and examples would improve clarity.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, sound model with practical algorithm and promising empirical results bridging multiple fields.  
- Arguments against acceptance: computational cost, non-invariance to linear transforms, and insufficient theoretical analysis.  
- Overall assessment: strong, promising contribution that would benefit from addressing computational, invariance, and presentation issues.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions, contingent on clarifying Algorithm 1 and σ selection and addressing noted weaknesses.
