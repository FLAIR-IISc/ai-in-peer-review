Summary and contributions
The paper proposes the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND): a generative model that replaces the Euclidean metric in a Gaussian with a data-adaptive Riemannian metric on RD, thereby inducing geodesic distances that follow the data geometry. Concretely:
- A Riemannian metric is learned from data via a locally smoothed, diagonal inverse covariance field M(x) (Gaussian kernel with width σ and regularization ρ). Geodesics are then computed by numerically solving the geodesic ODE.
- The density is the Riemannian normal pM(x|µ,Σ) ∝ exp(−1/2 ⟨Logµ(x), Σ−1 Logµ(x)⟩), normalized with respect to the Riemannian volume.
- Since the normalizing constant C(µ,Σ) is intractable, the paper proposes a Monte Carlo estimator in the tangent space at µ by sampling v ∼ N(0,Σ) and averaging m(µ,v) = √|M(Expµ(v))|.
- Maximum likelihood estimation is done by block coordinate descent: update µ along a steepest-descent direction in TµM based on Log maps of the data and the Monte Carlo term, and update Σ via a factorization Σ−1 = AᵀA. A mixture extension and an EM algorithm are sketched.
- Experiments on synthetic manifolds and a sleep EEG clustering task indicate improvements over GMMs and over “least-squares” Riemannian estimators.

Relation to prior work
The work connects Pennec’s Riemannian normal (for known manifolds) with manifold learning: rather than using a known curved manifold (sphere, SPD, etc.), it endows RD with a data-adaptive metric as in Hauberg et al. (metric learning via local covariances), then uses Riemannian statistics on that learned geometry. Related Riemannian mixture models on known manifolds and geodesic mixtures are discussed. The link to Isomap and geodesic distances is apt, though here the metric is continuous and supports generative modeling.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual contribution: a principled way to bring the Riemannian normal into the manifold learning setting, yielding a generative model that adapts to local geometry.
- The maximum likelihood treatment (vs. intrinsic least-squares mean/covariance) is sound in spirit and addresses the classic issue of intrinsic means falling in low-density regions.
- Practical algorithmic ideas: work in TµM, use IVPs for Exp instead of BVPs for Log in the normalization constant, and block coordinate updates for µ and Σ.
- The model is likely useful where clusters follow curved supports; the synthetic results compellingly illustrate the advantage over standard GMMs.
- The EEG example suggests potential practical impact in real-world, nonlinearly structured data.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Change-of-variables/normalization: Equation (9) appears to be missing the Jacobian determinant of the exponential map. In general, dM(x) = √|M(x)| dx, and with x = Expµ(v), dx = |det DExpµ(v)| dv, so the induced measure in normal coordinates is √|G(v)| dv with G(v) = (DExpµ(v))ᵀ M(Expµ(v)) DExpµ(v). Replacing √|G(v)| by √|M(Expµ(v))| omits |det DExpµ(v)| and is not generally correct beyond v ≈ 0. This affects both the normalizing constant and its gradients. If this is an approximation, it should be stated, justified (e.g., small-Σ regime), and its impact empirically assessed.
- Statistical foundations: the metric is learned nonparametrically from the same data used for likelihood maximization. This couples the model to the empirical sample in a way that complicates statistical interpretation (e.g., maximum entropy and MLE properties hold conditional on the learned metric). No discussion of consistency (as N→∞, σ→0 or σ→σ(N)) or identifiability is provided.
- Metric design: the metric is diagonal in the ambient coordinates and not affine-invariant. This can be problematic when local structure is strongly correlated. At minimum, whitened or learned linear transforms, or shrinkage toward diagonal, should be discussed.
- Computational scalability: each evaluation of m(µ,v) requires Expµ(v) via solving an ODE and computing M at that endpoint, with O(ND) cost for the kernel sum; Monte Carlo with S=3000 inside iterative optimization is heavy. Mixtures exacerbate this (per-iteration C(µk,Σk) for all k, plus many Log computations for the data). There is no complexity analysis, no runtime/memory report, and no comparison against alternative flexible densities with similar capacity.
- Variance reduction and optimization: Monte Carlo estimators for C and its gradients are potentially high-variance. There is no reparameterization trick usage, control variates, antithetics, or sample reuse analysis, and no convergence guarantees. The method may be sensitive to Σ conditioning and step sizes.
- Evaluation methodology: in the EEG clustering, σ is tuned to maximize F-measure on labels (“best σ”), which uses ground-truth to set a hyperparameter in an unsupervised task; this can inflate gains. A principled unsupervised selection (e.g., held-out likelihood, stability) would be preferable. Baselines are limited (no kernel density, mixtures of factor analyzers, flows, or other manifold-adaptive models). Quantitative uncertainty (std devs) and ablations (S, σ, ρ) are sparse, and runtimes are missing.

Quality, clarity, originality, significance
- Quality: The modeling idea is compelling, but the missing Jacobian in Eq. (9) is a serious concern; either a derivation or a clear approximation statement is needed. Empirical evaluation is suggestive but limited; stronger baselines and careful hyperparameter selection would solidify claims.
- Clarity: The paper is generally well written. However, details crucial for reproducibility are deferred: how Log is computed in practice (BVP solver, tolerances), complexity and caching strategies, gradient variance mitigation, and precise EM steps for mixtures.
- Originality: The combination—learn a metric and perform maximum-likelihood Riemannian normal inference on it—is novel and interesting, beyond prior Riemannian models on known manifolds.
- Significance: If made robust and scalable, LAND could be a useful drop-in replacement for Gaussians in curved-data settings. As is, the practical impact is tempered by computational overhead, missing theoretical pieces, and limited evaluation.

Suggestions for improvement
- Correct or justify Eq. (9): include |det DExpµ(v)| in the measure, or argue why it can be neglected (e.g., small Σ) and quantify approximation error empirically (compare both versions on known manifolds where the exact C is known).
- Provide complexity and runtime analyses; show scaling in N, D, S, K; add sparse neighbor truncation for M(x) to reduce O(N) costs.
- Consider richer local metrics (low-rank plus diagonal with shrinkage) and/or an affine pre-whitening to mitigate non-invariance.
- Use variance-reduction for Monte Carlo and report the effect of S on stability and accuracy.
- For EEG, choose σ via unsupervised criteria; add stronger baselines (MFA, KDE, flows) and report uncertainty.
- Discuss asymptotics and conditions for existence/uniqueness of MLE under the learned metric.

Pros
- Conceptually elegant bridge between manifold learning and Riemannian statistics.
- ML formulation addresses drawbacks of intrinsic least-squares means.
- Clear practical benefit on curved synthetic datasets; promising real-data results.

Cons
- Potentially incorrect normalization via change-of-variables; missing Jacobian term.
- Heavy computation and limited scalability; no runtime evidence.
- Diagonal metric and lack of affine invariance.
- Evaluation with hyperparameter tuning on labels; limited baselines.

Recommendation: Weak reject (scores would improve if Eq. (9) is corrected/justified and experiments strengthened)

Confidence: High (familiar with Riemannian statistics and manifold learning)
