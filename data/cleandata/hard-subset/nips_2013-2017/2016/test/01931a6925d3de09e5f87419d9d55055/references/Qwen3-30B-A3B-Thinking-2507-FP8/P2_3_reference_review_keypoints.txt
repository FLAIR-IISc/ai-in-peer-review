- Introduces LAND: a multivariate normal using a locally adaptive Riemannian distance learned from data.  
- Constructs a smoothly varying Riemannian metric from local covariances with a Gaussian kernel.  
- Defines and normalizes the density on the learned manifold using tangent-space Monte Carlo integration.  
- Develops maximum likelihood estimation and mixture models for the LAND.  
- Bridges Riemannian statistics and manifold learning, extending Pennec’s Riemannian normal to learned manifolds.  

- Technical quality: sound theoretical foundations and correct handling of normalization and MLE.  
- Efficient normalization: tangent-space Monte Carlo avoids expensive manifold boundary value problems.  
- Empirical results: consistent improvements over GMMs and least-squares on synthetic and EEG data.  
- Clarity: well-written, clear mathematical exposition, helpful figures, and implementable algorithmic details.  
- Originality: novel adaptation of Riemannian normal to the manifold learning setting, differentiating from prior work.  
- Significance: enables probabilistic modeling on nonlinear manifolds and potential extensions of many ML methods; validated on EEG sleep-stage data.  

- Computational efficiency concern: evaluation of logarithmic maps/geodesics is expensive and lacks empirical scaling analysis on larger/high-dimensional datasets.  
- Hyperparameter selection issue: σ for the Gaussian kernel is used without a principled selection method or systematic sensitivity analysis.  
- Theoretical limitation: unclear whether LAND is a maximum-entropy distribution on the learned manifold, left as an open question.  

- Overall assessment: significant, well-executed contribution that bridges two research areas and merits acceptance.  
- Recommendation: Accept.  

- Arguments for acceptance: novel framework, theoretical soundness with practical implementation, clear experimental validation, excellent exposition, and wide potential impact.  
- Arguments against acceptance (minor): limited analysis of computational scaling, lack of principled σ selection, and incomplete theoretical connection to maximum-entropy properties.
