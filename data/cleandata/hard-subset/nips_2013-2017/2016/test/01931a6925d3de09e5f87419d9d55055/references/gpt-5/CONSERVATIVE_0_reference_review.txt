Summary
This paper proposes the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND): a Riemannian normal distribution defined on a data-driven Riemannian manifold in RD. The manifold is specified by a smoothly varying metric M(x) obtained from local (diagonal) covariance estimates with Gaussian kernels; geodesics tend to stay near high-density regions. The density uses the squared norm of Logµ(x) under Σ−1 and a normalization constant that depends on the learned metric. The authors approximate the normalization constant by Monte Carlo integration over the tangent space using samples from N(0, Σ) and the volume element |M(Expµ(v))|1/2. They develop gradient-based maximum likelihood estimation for µ and Σ (with Σ−1 = AᵀA) and extend to mixtures (EM-like). Experiments include synthetic nonlinear manifolds and EEG-derived 5D features for sleep-stage clustering, where LAND outperforms GMMs and least-squares Riemannian estimators.

Strengths
- Clear and principled construction connecting Riemannian statistics with manifold learning; the normalization treatment is carefully accounted for (unlike some prior mixtures on fixed manifolds).
- The tangent-space Monte Carlo normalization is a sensible design that avoids repeated Log BVPs.
- The paper articulates why MLE differs from intrinsic least-squares and shows that normalization penalizes low-density means.
- Demonstrates that LAND captures non-ellipsoidal structure better than GMMs in 2D synthetic settings.
- Addresses both single-component and mixture settings, and provides gradients and an optimization scheme.

Weaknesses
- Metric learning is ad hoc (diagonal local covariances, isotropic kernel, non-invariant under linear transforms). The choice of σ is critical and not learned; on EEG the “best σ” is selected per subject post hoc, which risks label leakage if informed by F-measure.
- Experimental validation is limited: baselines are weak (only GMM and a non-MLE Riemannian LS variant). Missing comparisons to established nonlinear generative alternatives circa 2016 (e.g., mixture of factor analyzers, kernel density estimators, mixtures with local metrics, GP-LVM with back-constraints, manifold mixtures, t-mixtures; for clustering, spectral clustering or density-based methods).
- No ablations on metric design (diagonal vs full, effect of ρ), sensitivity to S (MC samples), σ, or to dimensionality. No runtime or scaling analysis; the approach appears computationally heavy due to repeated ODE solves (Exp/Log).
- Theoretical guarantees are minimal: no analysis of estimator bias/variance from Monte Carlo, no convergence guarantees for block coordinate descent, and claims about D(µ) = RD and global Log uniqueness are not substantiated for the learned metric (cut-locus issues).
- The EEG evaluation lacks a clear, reproducible protocol (train/test splits, cross-validated hyperparameters, statistical significance). Tuning σ to maximize F-measure is particularly concerning.
- Much of the algorithmic detail (initialization, EM specifics) is deferred to the supplement; reproducibility would benefit from more in-paper detail or code.

Quality
Technically competent in formulating the density and gradients. However, the empirical evidence is not strong enough to support general claims. Key methodological choices (metric, σ) are insufficiently justified and evaluated. The Monte Carlo scheme lacks error analysis, and there is no complexity/time study. The EEG evaluation raises potential protocol issues.

Clarity
Overall well written and organized, with helpful intuition. Some important details are missing or deferred. The lack of invariance of the metric is acknowledged but not mitigated; guidance on preprocessing (e.g., whitening) would help. Explicit descriptions of ODE solvers, tolerances, and initialization would improve reproducibility.

Originality
Riemannian normals and intrinsic statistics are established; prior work used known manifolds. The main novelty is coupling a learned metric with a properly normalized Riemannian normal and providing an MLE procedure. This combination is interesting, but the metric construction is quite standard in manifold learning, and the EM/gradient machinery closely follows known formulations.

Significance
Potentially useful idea, but current evidence is limited. Without stronger baselines, robust hyperparameter selection, and scaling analysis, it is hard to recommend adoption. Impact would increase with demonstrations on larger, higher-dimensional datasets and comparisons against stronger nonlinear generative models.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually clean link between manifold learning and generative modeling with proper normalization.
- Simple, general procedure once Exp/Log are available; could be a building block for manifold-aware models.
- Promising synthetic visualizations and some improvements on EEG (even with fixed σ).

Cons for acceptance
- Weak empirical validation and limited baselines; possible hyperparameter overfitting on EEG.
- Computational concerns unaddressed; lack of scaling/time comparisons.
- Metric choice is simplistic and not invariant; no ablation/sensitivity analyses.
- Limited theory beyond repurposing known Riemannian normal properties.

Questions and suggestions
- How is σ selected on EEG without using labels? Please provide a label-agnostic protocol (e.g., cross-validation or unsupervised model selection) and report results accordingly.
- Provide runtime and scaling comparisons vs GMM and other baselines; report sensitivity to S and ODE solver tolerances.
- Compare to stronger baselines (mixture of factor analyzers, t-mixtures, kernel density, spectral clustering for clustering, GP-LVM for generative modeling).
- Explore pre-whitening or learning a linear transform to mitigate the lack of invariance; assess full vs diagonal local covariances.
- Include ablations on σ, ρ, and initialization; report confidence intervals.
- Clarify conditions under which Log is unique and D(µ) = RD holds for the learned metric; discuss cut loci and numerical failure modes.

Recommendation
Lean reject. The idea is appealing and the formulation is careful, but the empirical validation and analysis are not at the level needed to justify a new, computationally heavy modeling primitive. Strengthening the evaluation and addressing the methodological concerns would significantly improve the case for acceptance.
