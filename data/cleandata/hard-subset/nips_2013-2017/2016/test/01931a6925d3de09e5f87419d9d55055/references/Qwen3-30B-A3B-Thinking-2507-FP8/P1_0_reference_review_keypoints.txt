- Introduces the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND): replaces Euclidean distance with a learned Riemannian geodesic distance.  
- Constructs a smoothly varying data-driven Riemannian metric using local covariance matrices and a Gaussian kernel (Eq. 7).  
- Defines LAND as a Riemannian normal where Mahalanobis distance is replaced by geodesic distance under the learned metric.  
- Develops a Monte Carlo integration scheme for normalizing the distribution by integrating in the tangent space (Eq. 9–10).  
- Presents a gradient-based maximum likelihood estimation algorithm (Algorithm 1) and extends to mixture models with an EM algorithm.  
- Bridges Riemannian statistics and manifold learning by learning the manifold structure from data rather than assuming a known manifold.  
- Technically sound with a solid theoretical foundation, building on Pennec’s Riemannian normal distribution.  
- Experiments show LAND outperforms Gaussian mixture models and least-squares estimators on synthetic and real EEG sleep-stage data (F-measure improvements).  
- Acknowledges computational costs, especially evaluating logarithm maps, and potential scalability issues in high dimensions.  
- Notes the smoothing parameter σ is non-trivial to choose and requires manual tuning.  
- Paper is generally well-written, mathematically precise, and accompanied by helpful illustrative figures.  
- Needs stronger justification for restricting to diagonal covariances and discussion of its theoretical implications.  
- Could provide a clearer comparison to other manifold learning methods (Isomap, LLE) and metric learning approaches.  
- The intuition for performing Monte Carlo integration in the tangent space could be made more accessible.  
- The statement that the metric is not invariant to linear transformations lacks discussion of practical implications.  
- Original contribution: first to learn a Riemannian metric for a normal distribution in a manifold-learning setting.  
- Novel contribution: scalable Monte Carlo normalization and the insight that tangent-space integration is more efficient.  
- Significance: extends the fundamental multivariate normal to capture nonlinear manifold structure with broad applicability (e.g., Naïve Bayes, LDA, PCA).  
- The LAND has theoretical appeal as a maximum-entropy distribution on a learned manifold.  
- Overall recommendation: accept—original, technically sound, significant, and well-validated experimentally.  
- Strengths: novelty, theoretical foundation, practical Monte Carlo implementation, strong experimental validation, and broad applicability.  
- Weaknesses: diagonal covariance restriction insufficiently justified, computational cost of log maps, σ tuning, and limited discussion of high-dimensional scaling.  
- Suggested improvements: justify diagonal covariance choice, add σ sensitivity analysis, discuss extensions for high-dimensional data, and compare to adaptive metric methods.
