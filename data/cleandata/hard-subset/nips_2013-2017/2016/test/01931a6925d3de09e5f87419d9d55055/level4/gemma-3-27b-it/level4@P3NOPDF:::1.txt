## Review of LAND: Learning a Riemannian Metric via Parametric Nonparametric Bridging

This paper presents LAND, a novel parametric metric defined on a nonparametric space, effectively bridging concepts from metric learning, manifold learning, and Riemannian statistics. The core idea—representing the learned metric as a Riemannian normal distribution, akin to a Mahalanobis distance on Riemannian manifolds—is both insightful and mathematically grounded in Pennec [15]. The authors propose a maximum likelihood estimation approach to determine the mean and covariance of this distribution, utilizing geodesic distances (as in Hauberg et al. [9]) and learning a local metric based on the inverses of local diagonal covariances. The extension to mixtures of LANDs is also explored.

The primary strength of this work lies in its theoretical contributions, illuminating a compelling connection between metric learning and Riemannian statistics. The potential benefits of parametric, generative models that generalize beyond the training data are significant across numerous applications. However, a critical concern revolves around the practical applicability of LAND to real-world, large-scale, and high-dimensional datasets. 

The method inherently requires computation of a (diagonal) covariance matrix and its inverse for each data point, a computationally challenging operation, particularly in high dimensions. While the authors acknowledge this potential issue (lines 265-266), a more thorough analysis of the computational complexity and associated limitations is needed. Specifically, defining the threshold for “high-dimensional” would be valuable—is the method practical for 3, 5, 10, 50, or 100 dimensions? A discussion of potential mitigation strategies for high-dimensional settings, such as dimensionality reduction techniques, would strengthen the paper.

The existing literature on metric learning is extensive, and the paper would benefit from a more nuanced positioning within this context. While Hauberg et al. [9] provide some background, further discussion of relevant works—such as Frome et al. [1, 2], Malisiewicz and Efros [6], and even local PCA methods like Kambhatla’s work—would be beneficial. These works also explore learning local diagonal metrics, and explicitly outlining the novel aspects of LAND in relation to these approaches is crucial.

Several specific points warrant further clarification. The use of a Gaussian kernel to define locality (lines 101-103) raises questions regarding the sensitivity of the results to the bandwidth parameter, σ. While the paper discusses the choice of σ for the sleep data (Sect. 4.2), its impact on the synthetic data (Sect. 4.1) is not addressed. Robustness tests demonstrating the performance of LAND with varying values of σ would significantly enhance the paper. Furthermore, the consistency of σ across regions of differing data density (high vs. low) should be considered and discussed.

The presentation of results also requires refinement. While Fig. 4 compares LAND to a Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) and intrinsic estimators, the subsequent visualizations in Figs. 5 and 6 exclusively focus on comparisons with GMM, a method already shown to perform poorly in Fig. 4. This limits the insight gained from these figures. The supplementary material (Figs. 2 and 4) contains visual comparisons with intrinsic estimators that demonstrate superior performance, and these results should be incorporated into the main paper, either replacing the GMM comparisons or presented alongside them. 

A comparative analysis of the computational complexity of LAND versus intrinsic estimators would be valuable. This analysis could help delineate scenarios where LAND is favored and identify data structures best suited for each method. 

Finally, the exploration of mixtures of LANDs is a promising direction, as real-world data often originates from multiple underlying components. Expanding upon this aspect of the work and providing more detailed results would further strengthen the contribution.

Overall, this is a well-written and conceptually sound paper that proposes a novel approach to metric learning. Addressing the concerns regarding scalability, contextualization within the existing literature, parameter sensitivity, and result presentation will significantly enhance its impact.
