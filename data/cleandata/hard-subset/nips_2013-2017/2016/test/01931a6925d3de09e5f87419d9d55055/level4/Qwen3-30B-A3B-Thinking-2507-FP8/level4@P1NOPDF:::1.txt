

The paper introduces LAND, a novel parametric metric defined over a nonparametric manifold by bridging metric learning, manifold learning, and Riemannian statistics. The proposed metric models the data as a Riemannian normal distribution (Pennec [15]), with the authors deriving a maximum likelihood algorithm to estimate its mean and covariance—effectively a Mahalanobis distance tailored for Riemannian manifolds. Distances between points are computed as geodesics (following Hauberg et al. [9]), with the local metric learned via inverses of diagonal covariances. The paper also explores mixtures of LANDs. This work offers valuable theoretical insights into the intersection of metric learning and Riemannian statistics, particularly highlighting the utility of parametric generative models that generalize beyond training data. However, a critical concern is the method’s scalability to large-scale, high-dimensional real-world data. LAND requires computing a (diagonal) covariance matrix and its inverse for each data point, a computationally challenging task in high dimensions. While the authors briefly acknowledge this limitation (lines 265–266), the paper lacks a concrete analysis of computational complexity and practical constraints. Specifically, it fails to define what constitutes "high-dimensional" here—3, 5, 10, or 100 dimensions? Without this, the applicability of LAND remains unclear for typical high-dimensional problems.  

The paper also needs stronger contextualization within the extensive metric learning literature. Hauberg et al. [9] already reference related work, such as Frome et al. [1,2] and Malisiewicz and Efros [6], which similarly learn pointwise diagonal metrics. Local covariances have also appeared in methods like local PCA (Kambhatla), though not for every point. The authors’ approach thus lacks sufficient differentiation from prior work. Additionally, the choice of the Gaussian kernel bandwidth σ (lines 101–103) is inadequately justified. The paper only discusses σ for the sleep dataset (Section 4.2) but omits its impact on the synthetic data (Section 4.1). Robustness tests across σ values and its behavior in varying density regions (e.g., high vs. low density) are essential.  

The visualization in Figure 4 compares LAND against intrinsic estimators, showing comparable results. However, Figures 5 and 6 contrast LAND only with GMM, which Figure 4 already demonstrates underperforms relative to intrinsic estimators. This omission renders the visual analysis less informative. Supplementary Figures 2 and 4 show intrinsic estimators outperforming GMM, suggesting these results should replace the GMM comparisons in the main text. A comparative computational complexity analysis between LAND and intrinsic estimators would further clarify which method is preferable for specific data structures.  

The mixture extension of LAND is particularly compelling, as real-world data rarely stems from a single generative component. This aspect warrants deeper exploration, including empirical validation and theoretical justification for mixture modeling in this context. The paper’s technical content is sound, and its writing is clear and well-structured. However, addressing the scalability, literature context, and experimental rigor outlined above would significantly strengthen its contribution.
