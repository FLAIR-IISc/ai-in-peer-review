- Title claims learning a metric but Equation 7 uses a hand-crafted diagonal metric with fixed hyperparameters ρ and σ chosen empirically, so the metric is not actually learned.  
- The paper fails to explain the role of σ (e.g., how it controls curvature), lacks principled selection methods, and does not explore jointly learning ρ and σ with LAND parameters.  
- The diagonal metric is not coordinate invariant; rotating coordinates changes the metric despite the underlying geometry remaining the same, undermining the geometric interpretation.  
- The authors do not justify the diagonal approximation or discuss whether a full metric would resolve coordinate dependence.  
- Experimental comparison to GMM is flawed and biased: GMM is evaluated at a fixed small K (e.g., K=2) while LAND’s flexibility is advantaged; model complexity is not matched.  
- The paper observes LAND overfits with large K but misinterprets this as harmless; overfitting risk remains and is not properly addressed.  
- Model selection is missing—comparisons should use criteria like BIC to choose K for each method and compare at optimal complexity.  
- Computational cost and scalability are inadequately analyzed; no quantitative runtime, memory, or dataset/dimensionality limits are reported.  
- Empirical validation is limited to synthetic data; no benchmarks (e.g., MNIST) or fair comparisons to Gaussian mixtures under model selection are provided.  
- Related work on Riemannian metric learning and manifold learning is inadequately cited and discussed (e.g., Weinberger et al., Pennec et al., Wang et al.).  
- The authors’ response misinterprets overfitting claims (e.g., “geodesics become almost straight lines”) and does not resolve experimental shortcomings.  
- Recommendations: replace hand-crafted metric with a learnable one, address coordinate invariance (or justify diagonal form), redesign experiments with BIC and fair baselines, add scalability analysis and benchmark tests, and expand related work.  
- Overall: the idea of using Riemannian geometry for flexible distributions is promising, but implementation and evaluation flaws currently undermine the paper’s credibility.
