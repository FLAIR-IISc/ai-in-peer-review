This paper proposes a Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND) defined on a Riemannian manifold endowed with a smoothly varying metric M(x). Distances are computed via geodesics rather than Euclidean norms: the authors solve the Euler–Lagrange equations (a system of ODEs) to obtain geodesic curves, and use the exponential and logarithmic maps to move between the manifold and tangent spaces. The logarithm map sends a point y on the manifold to the tangent space at x, with the vector length equal to the geodesic distance between x and y. In that tangent space, the authors define a Gaussian using a Mahalanobis distance induced by the inner product, replacing Euclidean distances with those derived from M(x). They derive maximum-likelihood estimators for the mean and covariance; because closed-form solutions are not available, they use a steepest-descent procedure. The normalizing constant is also intractable and is approximated via Monte Carlo. Experiments include synthetic manifold data and a clustering task, with comparisons to a standard Gaussian mixture model (GMM). Overall, this is an interesting and useful extension of the normal distribution that adapts to local manifold structure by combining metric learning, geodesic computation, and probabilistic modeling. I recommend acceptance.

Technical quality:
- The manifold components (Euler–Lagrange geodesics, exponential/logarithmic maps) are sound and clearly presented. The steepest-descent algorithm and the Monte Carlo approximation for the normalizing constant are well motivated and described.
- However, the empirical analysis leaves questions. The choice of the kernel bandwidth σ appears to be tuned on the training data (or via the evaluation metric itself), which can bias results; the selection procedure should be made explicit. The paper would also benefit from a clearer discussion of when and why the learned distribution is practically advantageous relative to Euclidean Gaussians or GMMs.

Novelty:
- The main contribution is an integration of established ideas—kernel-induced metrics M(x), geodesic computation via Euler–Lagrange, and Gaussian modeling in tangent spaces—into a coherent probabilistic model, together with algorithms to estimate the mean, covariance, and normalizing constant. While each component has precedents, the overall formulation and learning procedure are a meaningful synthesis.

Potential impact:
- The synthetic experiments show that LAND fits manifold-structured data well, but it is unclear how σ was chosen. Results would be more convincing if σ were selected by cross-validation or on held-out data, and if performance were reported across a range of σ values.
- A missing baseline is performance on data generated from a single Euclidean Gaussian or a GMM; this would test whether LAND degrades gracefully when the manifold structure is absent.
- In the clustering experiment, it seems σ was optimized to maximize the F-measure and the same F-measure was then reported. To avoid overfitting, σ should be chosen on a validation set, with final metrics reported on held-out test data; if this was done, please clarify.
- It would be informative to examine higher-dimensional settings, where local metrics and geodesic computations can be more challenging and where the benefits over Euclidean models might be more pronounced.
- As a generative model for manifold data, LAND is promising. However, the paper assumes the intrinsic manifold dimension equals the ambient dimension D (tangent spaces and M(x) are full rank in D). Many applications involve data lying on a d-dimensional manifold embedded in R^D with d << D. Discussing how LAND could accommodate low-dimensional intrinsic structure (e.g., rank-deficient M(x), submanifold modeling, or preprocessing with dimensionality reduction) would broaden its applicability.

Clarity:
- The notation around Σ and M(x) is confusing. At several points the paper states M = Σ^{-1}, while Σ also appears as a (local) diagonal kernel matrix used to define M(x). Is Σ the learned global covariance of the LAND, the kernel bandwidth matrix defining the position-dependent metric M(x), or both? If M(x) varies with x but the fitted covariance is a single parameter, how are these reconciled? In the steepest-descent derivation you substitute M = Σ^{-1}; please make explicit which Σ this refers to and how it relates to the kernel-defined M(x).
- In Section 4.2, the procedure for assigning cluster membership is not fully specified. Please clarify whether the number of clusters is fixed in advance, how distances to cluster means are computed (geodesic distance on the manifold or Mahalanobis distance in the relevant tangent space), and how ties or mapping ambiguities are handled.
- The introductory exposition on manifolds is clear for readers familiar with Riemannian geometry. A brief reminder of conditions under which the logarithm map is well defined (e.g., local injectivity and cut loci) would help readers assess practical limitations.

In summary, the paper is technically solid and presents a compelling approach to probabilistic modeling on manifolds. Strengthening the evaluation protocol, clarifying the σ selection and Σ/M(x) relationship, and discussing intrinsic low-dimensional structure would significantly improve the work.
