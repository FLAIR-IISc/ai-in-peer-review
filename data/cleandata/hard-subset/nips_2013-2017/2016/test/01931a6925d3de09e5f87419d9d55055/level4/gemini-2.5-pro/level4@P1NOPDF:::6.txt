This paper introduces a locally adaptive normal distribution. The core idea is to replace the standard Euclidean distance with a geodesic distance derived from a smoothly varying Riemannian metric, M(x). Geodesic distances along the resulting manifold are calculated by solving the Euler-Lagrange equations, which define a set of ordinary differential equations, and by using the corresponding exponential and logarithmic maps. The logarithm map projects a point `y` from the manifold into the tangent space at a point `x`, such that the length of the resulting vector equals the geodesic distance between `x` and `y`.

The authors then define their distribution by applying a normal distribution in this tangent space, using a Mahalanobis distance computed via the log-mapped points. This effectively replaces the standard squared Euclidean distance from a point to the mean with a squared geodesic distance. Maximum likelihood estimators for the mean and covariance are derived, and since closed-form solutions are unavailable, the authors propose a steepest descent algorithm for their estimation. Similarly, as the normalization constant is intractable, a Monte-Carlo approximation method is presented. The model is evaluated on synthetic data generated on a manifold and in a real-world clustering task, with performance compared to a standard Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM).

Overall, the paper presents an interesting and useful extension of the normal distribution that adapts to local manifold structure by combining techniques from metric estimation, geodesic distance computation, and statistical learning. I believe this work is worthy of publication.

My detailed comments are below.

**Technical Quality**

The underlying manifold-based methods, including the use of Euler-Lagrange equations and the estimation of logarithmic maps, are technically sound and clearly presented. The proposed estimation schemes, namely the steepest descent algorithm for the parameters and the Monte-Carlo method for the normalization constant, are clever and well-described.

However, the analysis of the method's application is less thorough. The selection of the key hyperparameter `sigma` is not well-justified; it appears to have been chosen by optimizing performance on the training data, which risks overfitting. Furthermore, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the practical advantages and specific use cases of the learned distribution.

**Novelty**

The primary contribution appears to be the synthesis of several existing lines of work—specifically, using kernels to define a metric M(x), estimating geodesic distances via Euler-Lagrange equations, and applying a normal distribution in tangent space. The main novelty lies in combining these concepts into a single probabilistic model and developing a coherent algorithm for estimating its parameters (mean, covariance, and normalization constant).

**Potential Impact**

The authors provide a fair evaluation of their model, which they call LAND (Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution), against a GMM on synthetic data, using log-likelihood as the metric. However, it is unclear how the `sigma` hyperparameter for LAND was selected in these experiments. A crucial missing comparison is how LAND performs against a GMM when the data is genuinely generated from a single normal distribution or a GMM. This would help establish the robustness of the model when its core manifold assumption is not met.

In the clustering experiments on real-world data, the evaluation methodology appears problematic. The authors seem to have selected the `sigma` parameter for LAND by optimizing the F-measure on the entire dataset and then reported that same F-measure as their result. To avoid potential overfitting and provide a fair assessment, performance should be reported on a held-out test set after tuning hyperparameters on a separate validation set. This makes it difficult to judge the model's true generalization performance. An evaluation on high-dimensional datasets would have also strengthened the paper's claims.

A potentially significant limitation is the paper's unusual manifold assumption. The authors assume that the manifold dimension is equal to the ambient data dimension (i.e., d=D), with the metric M(x) being full rank. This contrasts with the standard manifold learning paradigm, where the primary interest lies in discovering a low-dimensional manifold (d << D) embedded in a high-dimensional space. This assumption may limit the applicability of the proposed model in many common scenarios.

**Clarity**

The paper's clarity is hampered by the overloading of the "Sigma" notation. The authors define the metric M = Sigma⁻¹, which is estimated via a local kernel matrix, implying that M(x) varies with `x`. However, they later refer to "the" fitted covariance matrix, which is a single global parameter for the distribution. It is unclear if these two Sigmas are related or are entirely different entities. This ambiguity is compounded in the proof of the steepest descent algorithm, where the substitution "M = Sigma⁻¹" is used explicitly. Clarifying the relationship between the local metric and the global covariance matrix is essential for the reader's understanding.

Additionally, in Section 4.2, the procedure for assigning cluster membership is not specified. I assume that points were assigned to the cluster with the closest mean, as measured by geodesic distance, but this should be explicitly stated.

Despite these points, the introduction to Riemannian manifolds was well-written and accessible to a reader with some background in the area.
