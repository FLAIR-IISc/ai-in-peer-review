Summary
This paper generalizes the multivariate normal distribution to a manifold-learning setting by replacing the Euclidean (Mahalanobis) metric with a smoothly varying, data-adaptive Riemannian metric. The resulting locally adaptive normal distribution (LAND) is a parametric model defined by a mean and covariance on a learned manifold embedded in RD, while the underlying metric is learned nonparametrically from local, kernel-weighted variances (diagonal for efficiency). The authors show that LAND recovers the maximum-entropy distribution under the learned metric (as in Pennec), derive a scalable Monte Carlo estimator of the normalization constant by integrating on the tangent space, develop gradient-based maximum-likelihood (ML) estimation for the mean and covariance, and extend the model to mixtures with an EM algorithm. Experiments on synthetic data and EEG sleep-stage features show that LAND captures nonlinearity better than a GMM and improves clustering alignment with sleep stages.

Relation to prior work: The approach bridges Riemannian statistics (e.g., Pennec’s Riemannian normal and maximum-entropy characterization; Riemannian PCA by Zhang and Fletcher) with manifold learning (Isomap, Laplacian Eigenmaps, LLE), and with learned metrics that bias geodesics toward regions of high data density (Hauberg et al.). Unlike mixtures of Riemannian normals on known manifolds (e.g., Simo-Serra et al.; Straub et al.), this work learns the metric from data and explicitly handles normalization constants for different mixture components, making the probabilistic treatment consistent.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: replacing the Euclidean metric in a Gaussian with a learned Riemannian metric is a clean idea with strong geometric grounding and a clear link to maximum entropy.
- Sound algorithmic design: the tangent-space Monte Carlo estimator for the normalization constant avoids repeated boundary-value problems for the log map and appears practically scalable; the gradients for µ and Σ are explicit, and the factorization Σ−1 = AᵀA handles positive-definiteness cleanly.
- Practicality: the mixture extension and EM algorithm make LAND a plausible alternative to GMMs in many applications.
- Empirical evidence: on synthetic data, LAND captures curved structures with fewer components than a GMM; on EEG data, LAND yields higher clustering alignment (F-measure) than a GMM, and the qualitative manifold geometry is compelling.
- Good fit to NIPS: builds on core topics (manifold learning, probabilistic modeling, Riemannian geometry) with both methodological and application value.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Computational complexity and scalability: Each evaluation of the normalization involves S samples and an exponential map (IVP) that, in turn, requires evaluating the local metric (a kernel-weighted sum over N points) along the ODE integration. The paper would benefit from:
  - Complexity analysis per iteration in terms of N, D, S, and integration steps.
  - Wall-clock comparisons vs. GMMs and vs. intrinsic least-squares estimators.
  - Discussion of approximation speedups (e.g., KD-trees/ball trees for local kernels, truncation radii, inducing points, or mixtures of metrics).
- Sensitivity to hyperparameters: Performance depends on σ (kernel width) and the number of MC samples S.
  - Provide a principled selection strategy (e.g., cross-validation on held-out likelihood, unsupervised criteria such as AIS/BIC; or automatic adaptation of σ).
  - Report sensitivity plots for σ and S, including variance of the Monte Carlo estimator and its effect on optimization stability.
  - Consider variance reduction (quasi-Monte Carlo, control variates using the Euclidean Gaussian as baseline).
- Metric design limitations: The diagonal local covariance metric is not invariant to linear transformations and may be limiting in the presence of correlated local structure.
  - Discuss when diagonal metrics suffice and when full local covariances (with appropriate regularization) might be necessary.
  - At minimum, evaluate the impact of a simple whitening pre-processing or local PCA in a supplement.
- Optimization and convergence: Both subproblems are non-convex, and convergence depends on initialization and step sizes.
  - Provide details on initialization heuristics, step-size selection, and stopping criteria; report optimization stability across runs.
  - Include training curves and, if feasible, a comparison to alternatives such as natural gradient or Riemannian gradient methods.
- Evaluation breadth: While the synthetic and EEG results are promising, broader baselines would strengthen the case.
  - Compare to GMMs in an embedded space (e.g., Isomap/LLE) and to manifold-aware density estimators (e.g., kernel density with geodesic distances).
  - For mixtures, compare to mixtures of factor analyzers or warped mixtures, which often capture nonlinearity with fewer components.
  - Report held-out (approximate) log-likelihoods where possible in addition to clustering alignment.
- Theoretical guarantees: Beyond maximum entropy, there is little on statistical properties (e.g., identifiability, consistency of ML under a learned metric).
  - Even a brief discussion of assumptions under which the ML estimator behaves well would be helpful.

Quality
Overall technically sound, with a reasonable approximation to an otherwise intractable normalization. The derivations for gradients and the tangent-space estimator are appropriate. The main concerns are computational scaling and sensitivity to hyperparameters; both are addressable with more analysis and experiments.

Clarity
The paper is well organized and readable. Algorithm 1 is helpful. Please clarify: (i) the choice and role of ρ in the metric, (ii) initialization of µ and Σ, (iii) numerical ODE settings (tolerances, step control), and (iv) details of the EM updates for mixtures (currently in the supplement).

Originality
The key novelty is applying the Riemannian normal with a learned, data-adaptive metric and correctly handling the normalization constant in both single and mixture models. This combination appears new relative to prior work on fixed manifolds or heuristic mixtures.

Significance
High potential impact: LAND provides a drop-in manifold-aware alternative to Gaussian models, with extensions to standard pipelines (mixtures, classification, dimensionality reduction). The EEG application suggests practical value beyond toy data.

Arguments for acceptance
- Elegant, well-motivated integration of Riemannian geometry with probabilistic modeling.
- Practical algorithm with promising empirical gains over GMMs on nonlinear data.
- Mixture extension broadens applicability; clear path to integrating into many models.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited analysis of computational complexity and scalability.
- Sensitivity to σ and S not fully characterized; selection is ad hoc in the real-data experiment.
- Restricted metric class (diagonal) and lack of invariance may limit performance in some settings; no ablation.

Overall assessment
A strong and timely contribution that meaningfully advances generative modeling on learned manifolds. With additional analysis on scalability and hyperparameter sensitivity (and a few broader baselines), this work would be even more compelling. I lean toward acceptance.
