Summary:
This paper proposes a Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND) as an alternative to Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs). The model is formulated on Riemannian manifolds, and the authors derive a maximum likelihood estimator (MLE) for its parameters. The approach is evaluated on both synthetic and real-world datasets. The manuscript is generally well written, with solid theoretical development and convincing experiments.

Strengths:
- Clear motivation and a principled Riemannian formulation that is appropriate for data with manifold structure.
- Derivation of an MLE and an accompanying inference procedure that appear technically sound.
- Experiments on synthetic and real datasets demonstrate the method’s practical viability.
- Empirical results are well presented and suggest the approach can capture local structure effectively.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Novelty relative to Simo-Serra et al. [19] is not convincingly articulated. The key conceptual and technical differences are only discussed near the conclusion, which weakens the positioning of the contribution.
- The related work and introduction do not sufficiently situate the proposed approach with respect to [19]; in particular, [19] is not cited in the introduction.
- It is not clear under what conditions LAND offers advantages over GMMs (e.g., in terms of expressiveness, sample efficiency, parameter efficiency, or robustness), nor how it compares to [19] on exactly the same benchmarks.
- The paper would benefit from a clearer early-stage intuition for LAND: what inductive bias is introduced by the Riemannian structure, and how does that bias differ from mixtures of Euclidean Gaussians?
- Computational aspects (e.g., optimization complexity, convergence behavior, and scalability to higher dimensions or larger datasets) are not sufficiently discussed.

Questions for the authors:
- What is the precise technical distinction between LAND and the approach in [19]? For example, are the metrics fixed or learned, how are local covariances defined, and what assumptions differ?
- How does LAND compare to (i) a well-tuned GMM with a similar number of effective parameters and (ii) the method in [19] on identical datasets with matched preprocessing and evaluation metrics?
- What are the computational costs of training and inference relative to GMMs and [19]? Can you provide wall-clock times and asymptotic complexity?
- Is the MLE identifiable, and under what regularity conditions? Are there failure modes (e.g., in regions of low curvature or high noise)?
- How sensitive is performance to initialization, step sizes, and manifold-related hyperparameters?

Suggestions for improvement:
- Clearly articulate the contributions in the introduction, with an explicit comparison to [19]. A short table or bulleted list contrasting model assumptions, parameterization, optimization, and theoretical guarantees would help.
- Include a direct empirical comparison with [19] on the same tasks, using common metrics and reporting statistical significance where appropriate.
- Provide an ablation or diagnostic analysis to isolate the benefit of the Riemannian components (e.g., compare to a Euclidean variant, or disable local adaptivity).
- Discuss computational trade-offs: provide runtime, memory usage, and scaling behavior with dimension and dataset size. Include convergence criteria and typical iteration counts.
- Move essential explanations earlier: define LAND and its intuition in the introduction or early in the methods section so readers can understand the novelty without waiting until the conclusion.
- Integrate [19] into the introduction and related work, and ensure that the narrative explains how this paper advances beyond or complements it.
- If possible, include a brief sensitivity analysis and guidance for practitioners on choosing metrics, initialization, and hyperparameters.

Minor comments:
- Ensure consistent notation and define all symbols when first introduced.
- Check that all references cited in the conclusion or results are also introduced earlier, especially [19].
- A schematic figure early in the paper contrasting GMMs, [19], and LAND could improve accessibility.

Overall assessment:
This is a well-executed paper with solid theory and promising empirical results. However, the contribution is currently under-positioned relative to Simo-Serra et al. [19], and the novelty does not clearly emerge until the end. Strengthening the introduction and related work, providing head-to-head comparisons with [19] and GMM baselines, and clarifying the computational and conceptual advantages would significantly improve the paper’s impact. In its current form, I view it as borderline; with the above clarifications and comparisons, it could become a strong contribution.
