The paper proposes LAND, a novel parametric model defined over a nonparametric space, by connecting concepts from metric learning/manifold learning and Riemannian statistics. Specifically, the authors leverage the Riemannian normal distribution (Pennec [15]) and propose a maximum likelihood algorithm to estimate its parameters – mean and covariance – within a learned Riemannian manifold. Distances between points are defined as geodesics, building upon the work of Hauberg et al. [9] for learning a local metric. The authors propose learning this metric using the inverses of local (diagonal) covariances. They also extend the model to mixtures of LANDs. This work provides a valuable contribution by bridging Riemannian statistics and manifold learning, offering a parametric and generative model capable of extending beyond the training data. 

However, a key concern lies in the method’s applicability to large-scale, high-dimensional real-world data. LAND necessitates the computation of covariance matrices and their inverses for each data point, a computationally challenging task in high dimensions. While the authors acknowledge this limitation (lines 265-266), a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity and scalability would strengthen the paper. Specifically, clarifying the dimensionality threshold where the method becomes impractical – e.g., 3, 5, 10, or 100 dimensions – would be beneficial. The landscape of metric learning is extensive, and the paper could benefit from a more comprehensive contextualization within this field. Hauberg et al. [9] already reference relevant work (e.g., Frome et al. [1,2], Malisiewicz and Efros [6]) that learns a diagonal metric tensor for each point, similar to LAND. Local covariances have also been employed in methods like local PCA (Kambhatla), although not necessarily for every point. 

Regarding the local metric learning, the choice of the Gaussian kernel and its parameter σ requires further attention. While the authors discuss the influence of σ for the sleep data (Sect. 4.2), its effect on the synthetic data (Sect. 4.1) remains unexplored. Robustness tests examining the sensitivity to σ would enhance the paper. Is σ uniform across all points, regardless of data density? The comparisons in Fig. 4 include intrinsic estimators, which yield comparable results, yet visual results for these estimators are absent in Figs. 5 and 6, where comparisons are limited to GMM – a method already shown to perform poorly in Fig. 4. Including visualizations with intrinsic estimators would provide more insightful comparisons. An analysis of the computational complexity of LAND versus intrinsic estimators would clarify which method is preferable under different data conditions. The exploration of mixtures of LANDs is a particularly promising aspect of this work, given the limitations of single-component models in real-world scenarios, and could be expanded further.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and a pleasure to read.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel combination of Riemannian statistics and manifold learning.
*   Provides a parametric, generative model capable of extending beyond training data.
*   Clear and concise writing style.
*   Demonstrated effectiveness on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Potential scalability issues in high-dimensional data.
*   Limited discussion of computational complexity.
*   Insufficient contextualization within the broader metric learning literature.
*   Lack of visual comparison with intrinsic estimators.
