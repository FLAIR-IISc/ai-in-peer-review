

The paper introduces a locally adaptive normal distribution (LAND), a novel generalization of the multivariate normal distribution to a Riemannian manifold setting. The authors bridge concepts from manifold learning (Hauberg et al. [9]) and Riemannian statistics (Pennec [15]), proposing a parametric distribution defined over a non-parametric metric space. LAND replaces the Euclidean distance in the standard normal distribution with a locally adaptive Riemannian metric, where the metric tensor is constructed from local diagonal covariances (Eq. 7). This approach enables the model to capture nonlinear data structures while maintaining the maximum entropy property of the Riemannian normal distribution. The authors develop a maximum likelihood estimation algorithm using gradient descent and Monte Carlo integration, extend the model to mixtures, and provide corresponding EM updates. The approach is evaluated on synthetic data and EEG sleep stage classification, demonstrating improved performance over Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) and intrinsic least squares estimators.

**Strengths**

*Quality*: The paper presents technically sound methods with appropriate theoretical grounding. The connection between Riemannian geometry and manifold learning is well-established, and the maximum likelihood framework for LAND is rigorously derived. The authors carefully address the normalization constant issue through Monte Carlo integration (Eq. 9-10), which is a significant contribution to the field. The experimental design is appropriate, with both synthetic data showing the model's ability to capture nonlinear structures and real-world EEG data demonstrating practical utility.

*Clarity*: The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear explanations of complex geometric concepts. The notation is consistent, and the mathematical derivations are presented logically. The authors effectively use figures to illustrate key concepts (Fig. 2, 3, 5, 6), and the algorithmic details (Algorithm 1) are sufficiently clear for implementation. The supplementary materials provide additional implementation details that would be helpful for replication.

*Originality*: While Pennec [15] established the Riemannian normal distribution and Hauberg et al. [9] developed a locally adaptive metric, this paper makes the novel contribution of combining these approaches to create a parametric generative model over a learned manifold. The LAND model represents a significant step beyond existing methods that either assume a known manifold (Simo-Serra et al. [19]) or use embedding approaches (Roweis & Saul [22]) without the generative capability.

*Significance*: LAND addresses a meaningful gap in generative modeling for nonlinear data structures. The ability to define a continuous generative model over a learned manifold has clear implications for applications where data resides on nonlinear manifolds. The EEG sleep stage classification results (Table 1) demonstrate practical significance, showing that LAND better captures the nonlinear structure of sleep data compared to GMMs.

**Weaknesses**

*Quality*: The computational complexity analysis is insufficient. The authors acknowledge high-dimensional data as a potential limitation (lines 265-266) but fail to provide concrete analysis of the algorithm's scalability. Specifically, the paper does not address how the diagonal covariance computation (O(D^2) per point) scales with dimensionality. For high-dimensional data (e.g., D > 100), the computational burden could become prohibitive, which is a critical consideration for practical applications.

*Originality*: The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with existing metric learning approaches. While Hauberg et al. [9] is cited, the connection to other metric learning methods like those of Frome et al. [1,2] and Kambhatla [12] (local PCA) is superficial. A more comprehensive discussion of how LAND relates to and differs from these methods would strengthen the novelty claim.

*Significance*: The parameter sensitivity analysis for σ (the Gaussian kernel bandwidth) is limited to the EEG experiment (Section 4.2). The synthetic experiments (Section 4.1) do not explore how σ affects results, which is crucial for understanding the model's robustness. A systematic analysis of σ across different data distributions would better demonstrate LAND's practical utility.

*Clarity*: While the paper is well-written, the comparison with intrinsic least squares estimators is confusing. The authors claim these estimators "overestimate the covariance" (Section 3.3) and show this in Fig. 4, but the visual results in Figs. 5 and 6 only compare against GMMs, which are already shown in Fig. 4 to perform poorly. Including visual comparisons with intrinsic estimators in the main paper would provide more meaningful insights.

**Arguments for Acceptance**
- Novel combination of Riemannian statistics and manifold learning
- Strong theoretical foundation with maximum entropy property
- Demonstrated practical utility in EEG sleep stage classification
- Well-written with clear mathematical derivations
- Provides a scalable Monte Carlo integration scheme for the normalization constant

**Arguments against Acceptance**
- Insufficient analysis of computational complexity in high dimensions
- Limited exploration of parameter sensitivity (σ) in synthetic experiments
- Superficial comparison with related metric learning literature
- Visual results do not consistently compare against the most relevant baselines

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the intersection of manifold learning and generative modeling. While the technical approach is sound and the results are promising, the authors should address the computational complexity concerns and provide a more comprehensive parameter sensitivity analysis to strengthen the paper's contribution. With these clarifications, LAND could become an important tool for generative modeling of nonlinear data structures. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the scalability and parameter sensitivity issues.
