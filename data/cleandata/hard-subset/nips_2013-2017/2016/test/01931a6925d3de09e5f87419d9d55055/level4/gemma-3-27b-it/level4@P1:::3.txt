This paper proposes to model the observation space with a learned Riemannian metric, and define a curved normal distribution based on the associated Riemannian geometry, allowing for better capture of the underlying data manifold. The paper addresses a fundamental problem: how to define a Riemannian metric from a set of high-dimensional real-valued observations, and subsequently how to define and estimate a curved Gaussian distribution based on this geometry. This presents a useful tool and an interesting application of Riemannian geometry in statistical learning. The method constructs the underlying Riemannian geometry as described in section 3.1. While the title suggests metric *learning*, the metric is actually hand-crafted in equation 7, based on the hyperparameters ρ and σ. These hyperparameters are fixed empirically. The paper should discuss in more detail the intuitive effect of varying σ, and how to select its value. Without this discussion, the paper feels incomplete. Is it possible to integrate the metric learning stage with the LAND learning stage, allowing ρ and σ to be learned directly from the data? A key benefit of a geometric approach is its invariance to coordinate transformations. However, the metric in equation 7 is *not* invariant. For example, consider data forming a line in a 2D observation space. Different coordinate systems (e.g., aligned with the line vs. at a 45-degree angle) will yield completely different metrics. It would be beneficial to discuss this in more detail, including whether a full (rather than diagonal) metric would be invariant, and to provide further intuition about the metric in equation 7. There is a rich literature connecting Riemannian geometry, metric learning, and manifold learning that could be related to this work (e.g., searching for "Riemannian" "metric learning" "manifold learning" on Google Scholar).

A significant criticism concerns the first experiment. The comparison is not entirely meaningful because the more flexible LAND naturally describes the data better with fewer components. However, a GMM can achieve similar performance with a larger number of components, potentially leading to overfitting. The authors should redesign the experiment to allow each method to select its optimal number of components based on a model selection criterion (e.g., BIC), and then compare the resulting performance. LAND is still expected to perform better due to its ability to capture the underlying manifold structure, and this comparison would be more informative. It would also be insightful to see how LAND performs with a large number of components and whether it overfits in such scenarios.

LAND is computationally more expensive than estimating a standard Gaussian. Are there quantitative measurements or experiments demonstrating this computational cost? What is the practical scalability limit of LAND? Could it be applied to datasets like MNIST and compared with Gaussian mixture learning?

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of Riemannian geometry to define a curved normal distribution.
*   Potential for improved modeling of data lying on nonlinear manifolds.
*   Theoretically sound, with connections to maximum entropy principles.
*   Demonstrated effectiveness on both synthetic and real (EEG) data.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The metric is hand-crafted with fixed hyperparameters, limiting the "learning" aspect.
*   Lack of detailed discussion on hyperparameter selection and sensitivity.
*   Non-invariance of the proposed metric to coordinate transformations.
*   Experimental design in the first experiment is not fully convincing.
*   Limited discussion of computational cost and scalability.
