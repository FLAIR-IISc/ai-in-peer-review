The paper proposes a Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution as an alternative to Gaussian Mixture Models, grounding this new distribution in the framework of Riemannian Manifolds. The authors derive a maximum likelihood estimator for the distribution’s parameters and demonstrate its effectiveness through experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets. Overall, the paper is well-written, technically sound, and supported by good experimental results. However, the work lacks a strong element of novelty when compared to existing approaches, particularly that of Simo-Serra et al. [19]. The distinctions between the proposed method and the current state-of-the-art are not adequately highlighted until the conclusion, and should have been clarified earlier—ideally, beginning with the introduction (where reference [19] is notably absent).

The central idea of leveraging Riemannian Manifolds to define a locally adaptive distribution is interesting. The mathematical formulation appears rigorous and the derivation of the MLE estimator seems correct. The experiments are well-designed and convincingly demonstrate that the proposed method performs competitively with existing techniques. The use of both artificial and real datasets strengthens the evaluation. 

However, the paper’s contribution feels incremental. The connection to Riemannian geometry, while mathematically elegant, doesn’t immediately translate into a substantial practical advantage over existing methods like Simo-Serra et al. [19].  A clearer explanation of *why* this Riemannian approach is superior, or addresses limitations of prior work, is needed. The paper currently describes *what* is done, but not sufficiently *why* it matters beyond the theoretical appeal of the formulation. 

Specifically, the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how the locally adaptive nature of the proposed distribution differs from, and improves upon, existing adaptive mixture models or density estimation techniques.  What specific challenges in density estimation are addressed by this approach that were previously unsolved or poorly handled? Quantitatively comparing performance against Simo-Serra et al. [19] on the same datasets, with a focused analysis of the cases where the proposed method outperforms (or underperforms) would be extremely valuable. 

Furthermore, the introduction could be significantly improved by acknowledging and positioning the work with respect to related literature, particularly [19]. Briefly outlining the similarities and differences upfront would help readers understand the paper’s contribution within the broader context of the field. This would avoid the impression that the novelty is only revealed at the end.

Minor points: While the writing is generally good, some sentences could be more concise. For example, “The proposed distribution is based on Riemannian Manifolds” could be slightly improved to “The proposed distribution is grounded in Riemannian Manifolds.”  These are minor stylistic suggestions, and do not detract significantly from the overall clarity of the paper.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a technically sound and well-executed approach to density estimation. However, its lack of substantial novelty and the delayed articulation of its relationship to existing work weaken its overall impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, contingent on the authors:

*   Providing a clearer justification for the use of Riemannian Manifolds and a more detailed comparison to Simo-Serra et al. [19], highlighting the advantages of the proposed approach.
*   Including reference [19] in the introduction and discussing its relationship to the proposed work upfront.
*   Considering a more focused quantitative comparison against [19] in the experimental section.
