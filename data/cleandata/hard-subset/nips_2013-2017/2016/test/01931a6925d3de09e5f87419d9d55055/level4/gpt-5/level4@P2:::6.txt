Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), a parametric distribution on a learned Riemannian manifold that replaces Euclidean/Mahalanobis distance with geodesic distance under a smoothly varying metric M(x). The metric is constructed from local, kernel-weighted (diagonal) covariances, making geodesics prefer regions of high data density. Building on Riemannian statistics (e.g., Pennec’s Riemannian normal) and manifold learning/metric learning (e.g., Isomap; Hauberg et al.), the authors define pM(x | µ, Σ) ∝ exp(−1/2 ⟨Logµ(x), Σ−1 Logµ(x)⟩), where Logµ is the logarithm map induced by the learned metric. Because the normalization constant depends on µ, Σ, and the manifold curvature, they express the integral over the tangent space at µ with Jacobian √|M(Expµ(v))| and estimate it by Monte Carlo with v ∼ N(0, Σ), relying on Exp maps (IVPs) rather than Log maps (BVPs) for efficiency. Parameters are obtained by maximum likelihood via block coordinate descent: µ is updated by steepest descent on the manifold; Σ is updated by optimizing a factorization Σ−1 = AᵀA. They also provide an EM algorithm for mixtures of LANDs.

Empirically, LAND better captures nonlinear structure on synthetic manifolds than GMMs and least-squares Riemannian baselines, and in EEG sleep-staging features it achieves higher F-measure alignment with labels than GMMs, particularly when tuning the metric bandwidth σ.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The geometric setup, use of Euler–Lagrange equations for geodesics, and tangent-space Monte Carlo normalization are appropriate and well-motivated. The optimization scheme is reasonable given nonconvexity; using steepest descent for µ to stabilize conditioning is sensible. The mixture extension and gradients are consistent with the probabilistic model (including normalization).
- Empirical evaluation: The synthetic experiments convincingly show advantages in non-ellipsoidal settings. However, key aspects of experimental design need clarification or strengthening:
  - σ selection appears to be tuned on the same data used for evaluation (both synthetic selection via AIC/BIC and especially EEG F-measure). A held-out or nested cross-validation protocol is needed to rule out overfitting.
  - Missing “sanity check” when the true data-generating process is Euclidean Gaussian/GMM: does LAND match GMM performance when the manifold assumption is unnecessary?
  - Limited discussion of scalability: computational cost per iteration (number of Exp evaluations), sensitivity to the number of Monte Carlo samples S, and behavior in higher dimensions are not quantified.
- Assumptions: The metric is diagonal and not invariant to linear transforms, which may limit modeling power; some discussion or ablation on this choice would help.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clear and self-contained for readers familiar with Riemannian geometry. The exposition of geodesics, Exp/Log maps, and the tangent-space integral is helpful. However:
- Notation is occasionally confusing. Σ denotes the model covariance in the LAND density, while M(x) is the learned, x-dependent metric. In a few places (e.g., the steepest-descent discussion) it reads as if M = Σ−1, which conflicts with M’s spatial dependence. Please disambiguate by using distinct symbols and explicitly stating that the optimization uses the model covariance Σ in the objective and the learned metric M(x) to define Exp/Log and the measure.
- Clarify clustering: in Sec. 4.2, were labels assigned via posterior responsibilities in the mixture model, or nearest mean under geodesic distance?
- Provide more detail on σ selection protocols and any validation splits.

Originality:
The paper’s main contribution is not a new distributional family per se (the Riemannian normal is known) but an integration of: (i) learning a data-adaptive metric; (ii) defining a maximum-entropy normal on that learned manifold; (iii) tractable ML estimation via tangent-space Monte Carlo; and (iv) mixture modeling with consistent normalization. This is a thoughtful and practically useful synthesis that extends prior Riemannian models, which typically assume a known manifold.

Significance:
LAND is a compelling building block for generative modeling on data with nonlinear structure. The approach can potentially benefit clustering, density estimation, and as a drop-in replacement for Gaussians in classical models. The empirical results are promising, but stronger validation (proper hyperparameter tuning, additional baselines, and complexity analysis) would bolster confidence and adoption. Scalability to high-D settings and robustness to σ remain open concerns.

Pros:
- Conceptually clean generalization of Gaussians to learned manifolds with a maximum-entropy interpretation.
- Elegant tangent-space Monte Carlo normalization and practical ML/EM algorithms.
- Avoids low-density bias typical of intrinsic LS means; fits non-ellipsoidal clusters well.
- Clear connections to and extension of prior Riemannian and manifold-learning work.

Cons:
- σ selection appears data-driven without held-out validation; risk of overfitting in EEG experiments.
- No baseline when the true model is Euclidean Gaussian/GMM.
- Limited discussion of computational cost, sensitivity to S and σ, and scalability to high D.
- Diagonal, non-invariant metric may be restrictive; notation around M and Σ is confusing in places.

Questions and suggestions for the authors:
- Please clarify the roles and notation of M(x) versus Σ, and the statement “M = Σ−1” in the steepest-descent derivation.
- Report held-out or nested-CV F-measure for EEG, and specify the clustering/assignment rule used.
- Include results on data generated from a single Gaussian/GMM to demonstrate that LAND does not degrade performance when manifold structure is absent.
- Provide time/complexity per iteration (number of IVPs/BVPs), scaling with N, D, and S; add a sensitivity study for S and σ.
- Discuss extensions to non-diagonal local metrics (e.g., local PCA) or mixtures of metrics, and potential invariance remedies.
- Comment on using d < D learned intrinsic dimensions and whether charts/embeddings could reduce computational load.

Overall assessment:
This is a well-motivated and technically solid contribution that brings a principled, tractable Riemannian normal to the manifold-learning setting. With clearer notation and stronger, unbiased empirical validation (especially σ selection and additional baselines), I believe it merits acceptance.
