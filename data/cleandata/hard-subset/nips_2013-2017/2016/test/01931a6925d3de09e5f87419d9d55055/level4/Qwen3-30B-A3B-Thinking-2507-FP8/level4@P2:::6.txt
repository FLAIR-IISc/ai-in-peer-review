

This paper introduces a locally adaptive normal distribution (LAND), which generalizes the standard multivariate normal distribution to a Riemannian manifold setting. The authors replace the Euclidean distance metric with a smoothly varying Riemannian metric M(x) that adapts to local data density. This metric is constructed using a local diagonal covariance matrix with entries derived from a Gaussian kernel, creating a smoothly changing metric tensor that favors regions of high data density. The LAND is defined as the maximum entropy distribution under this metric, with the density expressed using geodesic distances computed via logarithmic and exponential maps. The authors develop a maximum likelihood estimation algorithm that combines gradient descent with Monte Carlo integration for normalizing the distribution, and extend the approach to mixture models with a corresponding EM algorithm. The method is evaluated on synthetic data generated along nonlinear manifolds and EEG sleep stage data, with comparisons to Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) and least-squares estimators.

**Quality**. The technical contributions are sound and well-executed. The construction of the Riemannian metric using local covariances is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear mathematical derivation of the LAND as the maximum entropy distribution under the learned metric. The Monte Carlo approximation for the normalization constant and the steepest descent optimization algorithm are clever and well-described. However, the evaluation lacks depth in several aspects: the selection of the sigma parameter appears to be optimized on the training data rather than through cross-validation, and the authors do not demonstrate how the LAND performs when data is generated from a single normal distribution or standard GMM. The comparison with GMM on real data uses the same F-measure for both parameter selection and evaluation, raising concerns about potential overfitting. The authors should provide evaluation on held-out test sets to properly assess generalization.

**Clarity**. The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, with a clear exposition of the Riemannian geometry concepts needed to understand the approach. The mathematical derivations are thorough and accessible to readers familiar with manifold learning. However, there is significant confusion in notation regarding Sigma. The paper uses Sigma to denote both the covariance matrix (in the standard normal distribution sense) and the metric tensor M = Sigma^-1. This creates ambiguity, particularly when the authors state "in our case M = Sigma^-1" and use this substitution in their steepest descent derivation. It would be helpful to clarify whether the metric tensor M(x) is related to the estimated covariance matrix, and if so, how this relationship is maintained across different points in the manifold. The explanation of cluster assignment in the EEG experiment (Sec 4.2) is also somewhat vague, leaving unclear whether cluster membership was determined by geodesic distance to cluster means.

**Originality**. The paper combines several existing techniques (kernel-based metric learning, geodesic distance computation, Riemannian normal distributions) into a novel framework for learning distributions over manifolds. While Riemannian normal distributions have been studied before (e.g., Pennec), the authors' key contribution is adapting this framework to the manifold learning setting by constructing a data-dependent metric. This represents a meaningful extension of prior work, bridging statistics on Riemannian manifolds with manifold learning. However, the novelty is somewhat limited by the fact that the metric construction relies on standard kernel smoothing techniques, and the maximum likelihood estimation procedure is largely an adaptation of existing methods to the Riemannian setting.

**Significance**. The LAND provides a theoretically grounded approach to modeling data on nonlinear manifolds, with demonstrated improvements over GMMs on both synthetic and real EEG data. The approach has potential applications in clustering and generative modeling for manifold-structured data, and the authors correctly note that fundamental machine learning algorithms (Naive Bayes, LDA, PCA) could be extended using LAND. However, the paper does not adequately address the typical manifold learning scenario where data lies on a low-dimensional manifold embedded in high-dimensional space (D >> d). The authors assume the manifold dimension equals the ambient dimension (D), which is unusual and limits the practical applicability of the approach. The evaluation of high-dimensional data is also absent, which would be valuable for assessing real-world utility.

**Overall Assessment**. The paper presents a technically sound and interesting extension of the normal distribution to the Riemannian manifold setting. The mathematical development is rigorous, and the proposed LAND shows promising results on the evaluated datasets. However, the evaluation methodology has several weaknesses, including parameter selection practices that risk overfitting and a failure to demonstrate performance on standard benchmark scenarios. The notation confusion regarding Sigma is a significant clarity issue that needs resolution. The authors' assumption of manifold dimensionality equaling ambient dimensionality also limits the practical significance of the work.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the intersection of manifold learning and statistical modeling, but the evaluation and presentation could be strengthened to better demonstrate its significance. With minor revisions addressing the parameter selection methodology, notation clarity, and broader evaluation (particularly on high-dimensional data and standard distributions), this work would be a strong candidate for publication at NIPS. As it stands, the paper has merit but requires clarification and stronger empirical validation to fully demonstrate its value.
