

Summary: This paper introduces a Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND) framework that replaces the standard Mahalanobis distance in Gaussian modeling with a geodesic manifold traversal distance, learned adaptively from data. The approach extends naturally to mixtures of LANDs (M-LANDs), with evaluation on a synthetic dataset and real-world EEG recordings.  

Major Comments: The core idea of employing a geodesic distance (analogous to Isomap) is theoretically intriguing and potentially impactful. However, several clarity issues undermine the paper's contribution. First, the definition of the metric tensor inverse in Equation (7) appears ad hoc. The authors should provide deeper intuition for why this specific form is used, particularly regarding its relationship to the underlying manifold structure. Second, the connection between the local covariance matrix defined in Equation (7) and its subsequent MAP estimation in Equation (11) is unclear. These equations appear to describe the same quantity, but the transition lacks justification—readers are left uncertain whether Equation (11) redefines or estimates the covariance from Equation (7).  

The experimental section suffers from insufficient evaluation. While results on synthetic and EEG data are presented, they fail to demonstrate the method's advantages over strong baselines (e.g., standard Gaussian Mixture Models, Isomap-based approaches). Crucially, the paper omits any discussion of computational scalability. The authors must address: (1) the theoretical computational complexity of LAND learning relative to standard EM (e.g., O(n²) vs. O(n) per iteration?), and (2) empirical runtime comparisons on datasets of varying size. Without this, the practical viability of the method remains questionable, despite its theoretical novelty. The current evaluation does not convincingly establish that LANDs offer meaningful gains over existing techniques in real-world settings.  

Minor Comments: Algorithm 1, lines 4–5: "d" should be replaced with the gradient symbol (∇).  

Overall, the paper presents a novel theoretical concept with potential for manifold-based density estimation. However, the lack of clarity in key derivations, insufficient experimental validation, and unaddressed computational concerns significantly limit its impact. The authors should substantially revise the paper to resolve these issues before publication.
