- Proposes LAND: a locally adaptive normal distribution bridging metric learning, manifold learning, and Riemannian statistics using a parametric metric and Riemannian normal (Pennec).
- Introduces an MLE algorithm to estimate Riemannian mean and covariance using a Mahalanobis distance on manifolds and geodesic distances from a learned local metric.
- Learns the local metric via inverses of local (diagonal) covariances and also considers mixtures of LANDs.
- Provides new insights linking metric learning and Riemannian statistics and offers a generative parametric model capable of extrapolating beyond training data.
- Major concern: scalability and applicability to large-scale, high-dimensional data because computing and inverting per-point covariances is expensive.
- Requires a more detailed analysis of computational complexity and limitations of the proposed metric, particularly in high dimensions.
- Needs stronger positioning within the metric learning literature (e.g., Hauberg et al., Frome et al., Malisiewicz & Efros) and discussion of related local-covariance/PCA methods.
- The Gaussian kernel bandwidth sigma is under-discussed; robustness tests and clarification whether sigma is global or adapts to local density are needed.
- Empirical comparisons are incomplete: Fig.4 compares intrinsic estimators, but Figs.5–6 show only GMM baselines; intrinsic-model visualizations in the supplement outperform GMM and should be included in the main paper.
- Suggests adding computational-complexity comparisons between LAND and intrinsic estimators to guide method choice for different data structures.
- Recommends expanding the mixtures-of-LANDs discussion, as multi-component data is common and mixtures are a key application.
- Strengths summarized: novel approach, well-explored connection to Riemannian statistics, clear and well-written exposition.
- Weaknesses summarized: questionable scalability to high dimensions, insufficient literature context, unclear sigma choice/robustness, and incomplete intrinsic-estimator comparisons.
- Overall: interesting and well-presented method but needs deeper analysis of limitations, literature placement, parameter sensitivity, and more comprehensive empirical comparisons.
