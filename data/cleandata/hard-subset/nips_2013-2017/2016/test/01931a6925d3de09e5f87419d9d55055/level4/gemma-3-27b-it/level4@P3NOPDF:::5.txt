## Review of NIPS 2016 Submission – [Paper ID]

**Summary:** This paper introduces a Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND) defined using a geodesic distance computed via manifold traversal, learned directly from the data. This geodesic distance serves as a replacement for the standard Mahalanobis distance within the Gaussian distribution. The authors further extend the method to a mixture of LANDs and evaluate performance on both a synthetic dataset and a real-world EEG measurement dataset.

**Overall Assessment:** The core idea of employing a geodesic distance, reminiscent of Isomap, to adapt the shape of a Gaussian distribution is conceptually interesting and represents a potential contribution to density estimation. However, the paper suffers from clarity issues and a lack of comprehensive experimental validation, particularly regarding scalability, which raises concerns about its practical applicability. While the theoretical foundation holds promise, the current presentation requires significant refinement.

**Major Comments:**

The use of geodesic distances to inform the local shape of the Gaussian is the most compelling aspect of this work. However, several points require clarification and further justification. The definition of the inverse of the metric tensor in Equation (7) feels somewhat *ad hoc*. The authors should provide a more intuitive explanation for their choice of using a Gaussian kernel for the weights in the weighted sum of local covariances. What motivates this specific weighting scheme, and how does it relate to the underlying manifold structure? 

Furthermore, I found the transition between the definition of the local covariance matrix in Equation (7) and its subsequent MAP estimation in Equation (11) confusing. It is unclear whether these represent the same quantity calculated in different ways, or if they are distinct estimations. The authors need to explicitly clarify this relationship and explain the rationale for using a MAP estimate. A more detailed discussion of the differences and potential trade-offs would be beneficial.

The experimental evaluation is a significant weakness. While the results presented are promising, they lack sufficient depth. The synthetic dataset feels insufficient to demonstrate the benefits of the proposed method over established techniques. The real-world EEG dataset provides some validation, but a more rigorous comparison to existing state-of-the-art methods for EEG analysis is necessary. 

My primary concern lies with the scalability and computational efficiency of LAND. The computation of geodesic distances can be expensive, particularly for high-dimensional datasets. The authors do not adequately address this issue. A crucial addition would be an analysis of the computational complexity of the algorithm, ideally with a comparison to standard Expectation-Maximization (EM) for Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs). Reporting running times on datasets of varying sizes would further bolster the assessment of practical feasibility. Without such analysis, the applicability of LAND to large-scale problems remains questionable.

**Minor Comments:**

*   Algorithm 1, lines 4 and 5: Replace “d” with “∇” (gradient symbol). This will improve readability and precision.
*   Throughout the paper, consider adding more visual aids (e.g., diagrams) to illustrate the manifold traversal process and the adaptation of the Gaussian distribution.
*   The notation could be made more consistent. For example, ensure consistent use of boldface for vectors and matrices.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the motivation behind using a Gaussian kernel for weighting local covariances in Equation (7)?
2.  Please clarify the relationship between the local covariance matrix defined in Equation (7) and the MAP estimation in Equation (11). Are these equivalent, and if not, what are the differences and implications?
3.  What is the computational complexity of the LAND algorithm? How does it compare to standard EM, particularly as the dimensionality of the data and the number of data points increase?
4.  Have you considered any approximations or optimizations to reduce the computational cost of geodesic distance calculation?

**Recommendation:**

I currently lean towards **reject** with encouragement to revise. The idea is promising, but the lack of clarity in the presentation, coupled with the missing scalability analysis and limited experimental validation, prevents me from recommending acceptance in its current form. Addressing the major comments and incorporating a more thorough evaluation would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its potential impact.
