The paper proposes LAND, a parametric metric defined over a nonparametric space, bridging ideas from metric/manifold learning and Riemannian statistics. The learned parametric form is the Riemannian normal distribution of Pennec [15]. The authors develop an MLE procedure to estimate its mean and covariance—effectively a Mahalanobis distance on Riemannian manifolds. Distances are defined as geodesics on the learned manifold, following Hauberg et al. [9], and rely on a local metric; here, the metric is learned from the inverses of local (diagonal) covariances. The paper also considers mixtures of LANDs. Overall, it offers a valuable connection between metric learning and Riemannian statistics, and parametric generative models that extrapolate beyond the training set are indeed useful in many scenarios.

My main concern is applicability to real-world settings that are both large-scale and high-dimensional. LAND requires computing a (diagonal) local covariance matrix and its inverse for each data point; in high dimensions, this is known to be problematic, both statistically and computationally. The authors briefly mention high dimensionality as a potential issue (lines 265–266), but the paper needs a more explicit analysis of the algorithmic complexity, memory requirements, and numerical limitations of the proposed metric. What do the authors consider “high-dimensional” in practice—3, 5, 10, 100 dimensions? Concrete guidance would help readers assess when LAND is feasible.

Given the breadth of the metric learning literature, the paper should be more clearly situated within that context. Hauberg et al. [9] already discuss related work such as Frome et al. [1,2] and Malisiewicz and Efros [6], who also learn a diagonal metric tensor per point, similar in spirit to LAND. Moreover, local covariances have been widely used in approaches like local PCA (Kambhatla, “Dimension Reduction by Local Principal Component Analysis”), even if not at every point. A sharper comparison—conceptually and empirically—would help clarify LAND’s unique contributions.

On lines 101–103, locality is defined via a Gaussian kernel. How does the bandwidth σ affect performance and stability? The choice of σ is discussed for the sleep data in Sect. 4.2 but not for the synthetic data in Sect. 4.1. Robustness experiments with respect to σ would be valuable. Is a single global σ used for all points, including both low- and high-density regions, or is σ adapted locally?

Figure 4 compares LAND against intrinsic estimators and shows broadly similar performance. However, the visualization results in Figs. 5 and 6 are shown only against GMMs, which already underperform in Fig. 4. This makes those visualizations less informative. The supplementary material includes visualizations with the intrinsic model (Figs. 2 and 4) that outperform GMMs; these should be brought into the main paper—ideally replacing the GMM visualizations or including both for completeness.

It would also be helpful to include a computational complexity comparison between LAND and intrinsic estimators—both in theory and empirically—to indicate which method is preferable under different data structures, sample sizes, and dimensions.

Finally, the extension to mixtures of LANDs is one of the most compelling aspects of the work, since real data are rarely generated by a single component. This section merits further development, including details on initialization, identifiability, convergence, and scalability.

In summary, this is a well-written and enjoyable paper that makes a meaningful conceptual link between metric learning and Riemannian statistics. Addressing scalability and dimensionality, clarifying the role of σ, strengthening comparisons to intrinsic estimators and related local-metric methods, and expanding the mixture model analysis would significantly improve its impact.
