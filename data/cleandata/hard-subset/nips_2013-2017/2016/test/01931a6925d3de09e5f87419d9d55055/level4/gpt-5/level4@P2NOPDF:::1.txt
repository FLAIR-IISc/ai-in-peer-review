This paper introduces LAND, a parametric metric defined over a nonparametric space by connecting ideas from metric learning/manifold learning and Riemannian statistics. The learned parametric model is a Riemannian normal distribution in the sense of Pennec [15]. The authors propose a maximum-likelihood procedure to estimate its mean and covariance, yielding a Mahalanobis-type distance on Riemannian manifolds. Distances between points are geodesic distances along the manifold as in Hauberg et al. [9], with a learned local metric. Here, the local metric is constructed from the inverses of local (diagonal) covariance matrices. The paper also extends LAND to mixtures.

The work offers valuable insights into the relation between metric learning and Riemannian statistics. Having a parametric, generative model that can extrapolate beyond observed data is compelling. The mixture formulation is particularly appealing, since real data are rarely single-component.

My main concern is applicability to large-scale, high-dimensional data. LAND requires computing a local (diagonal) covariance and its inverse at each data point, which can be challenging in high dimensions and expensive at scale. While high dimensionality is acknowledged as a potential issue (lines 265–266), the paper needs a concrete analysis of computational and memory complexity, both per-iteration and overall, as a function of data size, intrinsic dimension, neighborhood size, and number of components. Please clarify what “high-dimensional” means in practice (e.g., 3, 5, 10, 100+ dimensions), and provide guidance on feasible regimes. A discussion of numerical stability (e.g., regularization/shrinkage of local covariances, conditioning of inverses) would also help.

The positioning relative to the metric learning literature could be stronger. Hauberg et al. [9] already survey related work, including Frome et al. [1,2] and Malisiewicz and Efros [6], who learn diagonal metric tensors per point, similar in spirit to LAND. Local covariance structures are also classical in local PCA (e.g., Kambhatla, “Dimension reduction by local principal component analysis”), albeit not at every point. A clearer comparison—conceptually and empirically—with these per-point and local-metric approaches would situate the contribution for future readers.

Regarding the learning of the local metric, lines 101–103 specify a Gaussian kernel to define locality. How sensitive is LAND to the kernel bandwidth σ? The choice of σ is discussed for the sleep data (Sect. 4.2) but not for the synthetic data (Sect. 4.1). Robustness analyses (e.g., performance vs. σ, automatic bandwidth selection, or adaptive bandwidths) would be valuable. Is σ fixed globally across regions of varying density, or adapted locally?

Experimental presentation could be improved. Figure 4 compares LAND with intrinsic estimators and shows similar performance, yet Figures 5 and 6 provide visualizations only against GMMs, which already underperform in Figure 4. This limits the insight gained from the visual comparisons. The supplementary material includes visual results for intrinsic models (Figs. 2 and 4) that appear to outperform GMMs; these visualizations should be included in the main paper (replacing or augmenting the GMM comparisons). In addition, a computational comparison of LAND vs. intrinsic estimators—wall-clock times, scaling with data size/dimension, and convergence behavior—would guide practitioners on when to prefer one approach over the other and for which data geometries.

The mixture extension is one of the most promising aspects of LAND. It would be useful to expand on initialization, component selection (e.g., model selection criteria), potential identifiability issues, and how the local metric estimation interacts with mixture assignments. Any analysis of when mixtures of LANDs significantly outperform single-component models would strengthen the contribution.

Overall, the paper is very well written and a pleasure to read. Addressing scalability and dimensionality limits, deepening the connection to prior metric learning work, clarifying the kernel bandwidth choice and robustness, and enhancing the experimental comparisons—especially against intrinsic estimators—would substantially improve the paper’s clarity and practical relevance.
