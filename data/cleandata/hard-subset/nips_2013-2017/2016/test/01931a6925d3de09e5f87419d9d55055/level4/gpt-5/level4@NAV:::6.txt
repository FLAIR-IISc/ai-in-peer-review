In this paper the authors propose a locally adaptive normal distribution. Rather than measuring distances with a Euclidean metric, they introduce a smoothly varying Riemannian metric M(x) over the dataset. Given M(x), geodesic distances along the manifold are computed via the exponential and logarithmic maps. The authors use the Euler–Lagrange equations, yielding a system of ordinary differential equations, to estimate geodesic distances. The logarithm map sends points on the manifold (y) to the tangent space at x such that the resulting vector’s length equals the geodesic distance from x to y along the manifold. They then apply a normal distribution by computing a Mahalanobis distance on points mapped by the logarithm map. This inner product is used to measure distances from a point x to the mean instead of Euclidean distances. The authors derive maximum-likelihood estimators for the mean and covariance. Because closed-form solutions are unavailable, they present a steepest descent algorithm to estimate them. Finally, since the normalization constant also lacks a closed form, they provide a Monte Carlo approximation. They apply the method to synthetic manifold data and to a clustering task, comparing against a standard mixture of Gaussians. Overall I find this an interesting and useful extension of the normal distribution that adapts to local manifold structure. The authors combine work in metric estimation, geodesic distance computation, and learning distributions. I believe this work merits publication. I have a few comments on my scores for the above:

Technical quality Comments: The manifold techniques (use of Euler–Lagrange equations, estimating logarithm maps) are sound and clearly explained. The Monte Carlo estimation of the normalization constant and the steepest descent algorithms are clever and well described. The analysis of the applications is thin (the sigma parameter appears to be chosen by optimizing on the training data?). The explanation of the practical usefulness of the learned distribution is also somewhat limited.

Novelty Comments: The main contribution appears to be combining prior work (using kernels to define the metric M(x), estimating geodesic distances via Euler–Lagrange, and applying a normal distribution) and providing an algorithm for estimating the parameters (mean, covariance, normalization constant).

Potential Impact Comments: The authors offer a reasonable evaluation of the GMM and LAND on synthetic data (examining log-likelihood on data generated by the learned distributions), but the choice of the sigma parameter is unclear. They show that LAND fits data generated along a manifold well, but do not show how it compares to a GMM when data are drawn from a single normal distribution or a GMM. In the real (clustering) data, they seem to choose the LAND sigma by optimizing the F-measure and then report that same F-measure. To be fair, they should have reported the F-measure on a held-out test set (or, if they did, made this explicit). Consequently, it is hard to assess possible overfitting. It also would have been helpful to examine very high-dimensional data. One potentially useful application the authors mention is using LAND as a generative model for manifold data; however, they adopt the unusual assumption that the observed dimension D equals the manifold dimension (tangent vectors are D-dimensional and M(x) is full rank). In manifold learning we typically study data on a d-dimensional manifold embedded in a D-dimensional Euclidean space with D much larger than d.

Clarity Comments: One notational issue was confusing: the overloading of Σ. They state M = Σ^{-1} but then estimate it via a (local) diagonal kernel matrix. Is this Σ also the fitted covariance matrix? If so, is the kernel merely an initialization? This seems odd since M(x) should vary with x, whereas the fitted covariance matrix is a single parameter. In the proof of the steepest descent they explicitly state “in our case M = Σ^{-1}” and use that substitution; clarification would help. In Sec. 4.2 they do not state it, but I assume cluster membership was determined by selecting two clusters and assigning by the closest (geodesic?) distance to the cluster means. Overall I found the manifold introduction well presented (for readers familiar with Riemannian manifolds).
