Summary:
The paper introduces the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), a parametric alternative to Gaussian models that replaces the Euclidean metric with a data-driven Riemannian metric that adapts to local density. The distribution retains mean and covariance parameters and is the maximum-entropy distribution under the learned metric. The authors derive a maximum likelihood estimator using gradient-based optimization and Monte Carlo integration on the tangent space to approximate the normalization constant. They further extend LAND to mixtures and present an EM algorithm. Experiments on synthetic datasets and EEG sleep-stage data demonstrate improved fit to non-linear structures compared to standard Gaussian mixtures.

Strengths:
- Clear problem motivation: standard Gaussians are ill-suited for data near non-linear manifolds.
- Solid theoretical grounding in Riemannian geometry and maximum-entropy arguments.
- Practical and scalable methodology: Monte Carlo normalization via the tangent space, avoiding expensive boundary-value problems.
- Extension to mixture models with an EM procedure.
- Empirical results on both synthetic and real EEG data support the approach and show clear advantages over GMMs for non-ellipsoidal structures.
- Writing and presentation are generally clear, and the method is well-explained.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Limited novelty relative to Simo-Serra et al. [19]: The paper’s distinct contributions—particularly learning the manifold metric from data and handling normalization constants—are not clearly articulated until late in the paper. These differences should be highlighted in the introduction and related work, with a precise comparison to [19], including the probabilistic consistency issues noted in Section 5.
- The introduction omits [19], which is central prior work on mixtures of Riemannian normal distributions. This citation should appear early, along with a concise taxonomy of settings (known vs. learned manifolds; handling of normalization constants; computational trade-offs).
- While the theory is sound, the contribution would be strengthened by an explicit empirical comparison against an implementation of [19] (if feasible), or at least a more thorough, quantitative discussion of how LAND’s learned metric and normalization affect performance.
- The paper would benefit from a brief ablation or sensitivity analysis clarifying the impact of the metric’s kernel width and the diagonal-metric assumption on performance and stability, and a short discussion of scalability and potential failure modes.

Overall assessment:
A well-executed and clearly presented paper with solid theory and convincing experiments. However, the novelty over [19] is not argued strongly enough in the early sections. Clarifying the contributions relative to prior work (and citing [19] in the introduction) would significantly strengthen the paper.
