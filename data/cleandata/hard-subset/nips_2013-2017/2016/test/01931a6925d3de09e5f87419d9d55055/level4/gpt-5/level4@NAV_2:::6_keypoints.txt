- Proposes LAND: a locally adaptive normal using a data-estimated Riemannian metric M(x) instead of Euclidean distances.  
- Computes geodesics and log maps by solving Euler–Lagrange ODEs so tangent vectors’ norms equal geodesic distances.  
- Defines a normal density by applying a Mahalanobis distance to log-mapped vectors in the tangent space.  
- Derives MLEs for mean and covariance but uses steepest-descent because closed-form solutions are unavailable.  
- Handles the intractable normalization constant via a Monte Carlo approximation.  
- Evaluated on synthetic manifold-structured data and a clustering task, compared to standard GMMs.  
- Reviewer views the approach as a compelling, practical extension that combines metric learning, geodesic computation, and probabilistic modeling, and leans toward acceptance.  
- Technical machinery (Euler–Lagrange geodesics, log maps) and the optimization/Monte Carlo strategies are sound and clearly presented.  
- Empirical analysis is somewhat thin: kernel width σ appears selected on evaluation data, raising concerns about overfitting.  
- Practical benefits of the learned distribution could be articulated and demonstrated more thoroughly.  
- Novelty is mainly in the synthesis and end-to-end estimation (kernel M(x), geodesics, tangent-space Gaussian) rather than new theoretical ingredients.  
- Comparisons: fair log-likelihood comparisons on synthetic data, but need baselines where ground-truth is single Gaussian or GMM to clarify off-manifold behavior.  
- Real clustering evaluation likely overfit because σ was tuned to maximize reported F-measure; recommends held-out validation or cross-validation.  
- Suggests testing performance in higher-dimensional settings to assess scalability and robustness.  
- Important modeling assumption: manifold dimension equals ambient D (M(x) full-rank); paper should discuss implications when intrinsic dimension d << D.  
- Clarity issues: notation around Σ and M is confusing—unclear relationship between global Σ and locally varying M(x), especially where M = Σ^-1 is stated.  
- Clarity issue: cluster-assignment rule in Section 4.2 is not explicitly stated (seems to assume K=2 and nearest-component assignment).  
- Aside from those notation/assignment issues, the Riemannian introduction is clear for readers familiar with the area.
