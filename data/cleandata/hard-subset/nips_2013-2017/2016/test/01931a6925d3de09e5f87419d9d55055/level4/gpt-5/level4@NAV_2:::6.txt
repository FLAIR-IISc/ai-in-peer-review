This paper proposes a locally adaptive normal distribution (LAND). Instead of using Euclidean distances, the authors endow the ambient space with a smoothly varying Riemannian metric M(x) estimated from data. With this metric, geodesic distances are computed via exponential and logarithmic maps obtained by solving the Euler–Lagrange equations as a system of ODEs. The logarithm map sends a point y on the manifold to the tangent space at x such that the vector’s norm equals the geodesic distance from x to y. A normal density is then defined by applying a Mahalanobis distance to these log-mapped vectors. The authors derive maximum-likelihood estimators for the mean and covariance; because closed-form solutions are unavailable, they present a steepest-descent procedure. They also address the lack of a closed-form normalization constant with a Monte Carlo approximation. The approach is evaluated on synthetic manifold-structured data and on a clustering task, with comparisons to standard Gaussian mixtures. Overall, the paper offers a compelling and practical extension of the normal distribution that adapts to local manifold structure by combining metric learning, geodesic computation, and probabilistic modeling. I am inclined to recommend acceptance.

Technical Quality Comments:
- The geometric machinery (Euler–Lagrange formulation for geodesics, computation of logarithm maps) is sound and clearly presented. The Monte Carlo strategy for the normalization constant and the steepest-descent optimization are sensible and well explained.
- The empirical analysis is thinner than desired in places. In particular, the procedure for selecting the kernel width σ appears to rely on optimization on the same data used for evaluation, which raises concerns. The practical benefits of the learned distribution could also be articulated more thoroughly.

Novelty Comments:
- The primary contribution is an integration of established components—kernel-based construction of M(x), geodesic computation via Euler–Lagrange ODEs, and a Gaussian defined in the tangent space—together with a practical ML algorithm for estimating mean, covariance, and the normalization constant. The novelty lies mainly in the synthesis and the end-to-end estimation procedure rather than in any single new theoretical ingredient.

Potential Impact Comments:
- On synthetic data, the comparison between GMM and LAND via log-likelihood appears fair, but the choice of σ is not fully justified. Additional baselines where the ground-truth is a single Gaussian or a GMM would clarify behavior off-manifold.
- For the real clustering case, σ seems to be tuned to maximize the F-measure and then the same F-measure is reported, which risks optimistic estimates. A held-out validation/test split (or cross-validation) should be used to guard against overfitting.
- It would be informative to examine performance in higher-dimensional settings.
- A notable modeling assumption is that the data live in D dimensions and the manifold dimension is also D (so M(x) is full rank and Log maps are D-dimensional). In many manifold-learning contexts, one expects data on a d-dimensional manifold embedded in RD with d << D. The paper would benefit from discussion of this assumption and its implications.

Clarity Comments:
- There is confusing notation around Σ and M. At points the text states M = Σ^-1 while elsewhere M(x) is described as a locally varying, kernel-smoothed, diagonal metric. Is Σ the fitted global covariance of the LAND, and is M(x) the learned ambient metric used to define geodesics and measures? If so, equating M and Σ^-1 (especially in the steepest-descent derivation where “in our case M = Σ^-1” is used) is hard to reconcile, since M(x) varies with x whereas Σ is a single parameter. Clarifying this relationship would help.
- In Section 4.2, the cluster-assignment rule is not explicitly stated; it seems to rely on K = 2 and assignment by closest component (geodesic distance to means?), but this should be spelled out.
- Aside from these points, the introduction to Riemannian concepts is clear for readers familiar with the area.
