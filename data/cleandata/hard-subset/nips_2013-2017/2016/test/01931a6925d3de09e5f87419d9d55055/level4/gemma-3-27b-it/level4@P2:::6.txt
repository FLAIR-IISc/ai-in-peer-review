This paper introduces a locally adaptive normal distribution (LAND) designed to better model data lying near a nonlinear manifold. The core idea is to replace the standard Euclidean metric used in the multivariate normal distribution with a locally adaptive Riemannian metric, learned from the data itself. This metric, denoted M(x), smoothly varies across the dataset, allowing for the calculation of geodesic distances via the exponential and logarithmic maps. The authors leverage the Euler-Lagrange equations to estimate these geodesics and then apply a normal distribution, computing Mahalanobis distances based on points mapped by the logarithm. They derive maximum likelihood estimators for the mean and covariance, employing a steepest descent algorithm and a Monte Carlo approximation for the intractable normalization constant. The method is evaluated on synthetic data and applied to EEG measurements during sleep stage classification, comparing performance to a Gaussian mixture model (GMM). Overall, the paper presents a compelling and potentially valuable extension of the normal distribution that adapts to local manifold structure, effectively combining techniques from metric estimation, geodesic distance computation, and distribution learning. This work is worthy of publication.

**Technical Quality:** The manifold techniques, including the use of Euler-Lagrange equations and estimation of logarithm maps, are sound and clearly explained. The Monte Carlo approximation for the normalization constant and the steepest descent algorithm demonstrate ingenuity and are well-described. However, the analysis of the applications feels somewhat incomplete. The selection of the smoothing parameter σ appears to be driven by optimization on the training data, and a more rigorous analysis of its impact would be beneficial. Furthermore, a more detailed explanation of the practical benefits of the learned distribution beyond fitting the data would strengthen the technical contribution.

**Novelty:** The primary contribution of this work lies in the combination of existing techniques—using kernel methods to define the metric M(x), estimating geodesic distances via the Euler-Lagrange equations, and applying a normal distribution—and providing a practical algorithm for estimating the key parameters (mean, covariance, and normalization constant). While each component is not entirely new, the integrated approach is a valuable contribution.

**Potential Impact:** The authors provide a reasonable evaluation comparing the LAND and GMM on synthetic data, assessing log-likelihood on data generated by the learned distributions. However, the impact assessment could be strengthened. The choice of σ is not sufficiently justified, and it's unclear how the method performs when data is generated from simpler distributions (e.g., a single normal distribution or GMM). In the real-world clustering experiment, the parameter σ is optimized based on the F-measure, raising concerns about overfitting; reporting results on a held-out test set would address this. Exploring the method’s performance on higher-dimensional datasets would also be valuable. The potential of LAND as a generative model for manifold data is mentioned, but the assumption that the manifold dimension equals the observation dimension (D) – a less common scenario in manifold learning – limits its broader applicability.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and the manifold concepts are introduced accessibly, even for readers familiar with Riemannian geometry. However, the notation could be improved. The overloading of the symbol Σ is confusing; it’s used to represent both M<sup>-1</sup> and the estimated covariance matrix. Clarifying whether the kernel used to define M(x) serves as an initialization for the covariance estimation would be helpful. The proof of the steepest descent algorithm explicitly states M = Σ<sup>-1</sup>, and this substitution should be clearly explained earlier. In Section 4.2, clarifying how cluster membership was determined (e.g., using geodesic distances to cluster means) would improve readability.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel combination of techniques provides a principled way to adapt the normal distribution to manifold structure.
*   Clear mathematical derivation and algorithm development.
*   Demonstrated improvement over GMM on synthetic data and a promising application to EEG sleep stage classification.
*   Addresses a relevant problem in statistical modeling and machine learning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The evaluation lacks thoroughness, particularly regarding the choice of the smoothing parameter σ and generalization to simpler data distributions.
*   Concerns about overfitting in the real-world experiment due to parameter optimization on the training set.
*   The assumption of equal manifold and observation dimensionality limits the broad applicability of the approach.
*   Lack of analysis of performance on high-dimensional data.
