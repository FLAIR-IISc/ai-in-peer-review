This paper introduces a locally adaptive normal distribution that incorporates manifold structure by defining a smooth, data-dependent Riemannian metric M(x) in place of the Euclidean distance. Geodesic distances are calculated using the exponential and logarithmic maps, with the Euler-Lagrange equations employed to estimate these distances. The logarithmic map projects points from the manifold (y) to the tangent plane at x, where the resulting vector’s length corresponds to the geodesic distance. A normal distribution is then applied, utilizing the Mahalanobis distance computed on these mapped points – effectively measuring distance from a point x to the mean using this inner product instead of Euclidean distance. The authors derive maximum likelihood estimators for the mean and covariance, and since closed-form solutions are unavailable, they propose a steepest descent algorithm for parameter estimation alongside a Monte Carlo approximation for the intractable normalization constant. They demonstrate the method on synthetic data generated on a manifold and on a clustering task, comparing performance to a standard Gaussian mixture model. Overall, this paper presents an interesting and valuable extension to the normal distribution, adapting it to local manifold geometry by combining techniques from metric estimation, geodesic distance computation, and distribution learning, and I believe it is suitable for publication.

Regarding technical quality, the manifold techniques, including the application of Euler-Lagrange equations and the estimation of logarithm maps, are sound and well-explained. The Monte Carlo estimation of the normalization constant and the steepest descent algorithm are both clever and clearly described. However, the analysis of the applications is somewhat limited; the sigma parameter appears to be selected by optimizing on the training data. Furthermore, the explanation of the practical benefits of the learned distribution could be strengthened.

The paper’s primary contribution appears to be the novel combination of existing techniques – leveraging kernels to define the metric M(x), estimating geodesic distances via the Euler-Lagrange equations, and applying a normal distribution – along with an algorithm for parameter estimation (mean, covariance, and normalization constant). 

Concerning potential impact, the evaluation comparing the proposed Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND) to a Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) on synthetic data, examining log-likelihood on data generated by the learned distributions, is fair. However, the method used to choose the sigma parameter remains unclear. The authors demonstrate the LAND’s ability to fit data generated along a manifold, but they do not show how it performs compared to a GMM when data is generated from a single normal distribution or a GMM. In the clustering application on real data, the sigma value for the LAND is optimized using the F-measure, and the same F-measure is then reported. A more rigorous evaluation would involve reporting the F-measure on a held-out test set to address potential overfitting concerns. It would also be valuable to examine performance on very high-dimensional data. While the authors suggest LAND’s potential as a generative model for manifold data, their assumption that the manifold dimension equals the observation dimension (i.e., D-dimensional data lies on a D-dimensional manifold with a full-rank metric M(x)) is unusual, as manifold learning typically deals with d-dimensional manifolds embedded in a higher-dimensional (D > d) Euclidean space.

With respect to clarity, I found the overloaded Sigma notation confusing. Specifically, the paper states M = Sigma<sup>-1</sup> but estimates it using a local diagonal kernel matrix. Is this Sigma identical to the fitted covariance matrix? If so, is the kernel simply an initialization strategy? This would be unexpected, as M(x) should vary with x, while a fitted covariance matrix is a single global parameter. The proof of the steepest descent algorithm explicitly states “in our case M = Sigma<sup>-1</sup>” and uses this substitution, so clarification is needed. Additionally, in Sec 4.2, the method for determining cluster membership – presumably by assigning points to the nearest (geodesic?) distance to cluster means – is not explicitly stated. Overall, the introduction to manifold concepts was well-explained, at least for readers familiar with Riemannian manifolds.
