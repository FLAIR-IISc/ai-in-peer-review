This work introduces a method for modeling the observation space by defining a handcrafted Riemannian metric. From this metric, the authors derive a curved normal distribution intended to more accurately capture the underlying data manifold. The paper tackles two central challenges: first, defining a suitable Riemannian metric from a collection of high-dimensional, real-valued data points, and second, defining and estimating a curved Gaussian distribution within this geometric framework. This approach represents a valuable tool and an intriguing application of Riemannian geometry to statistical learning.

The first part of the proposed method, detailed in Section 3.1, defines the underlying Riemannian geometry. While the title implies metric learning, the metric presented in Equation 7 is, in fact, handcrafted. It is determined by two hyperparameters, ρ and σ, which are selected empirically and fixed beforehand. It is likely that σ is a particularly sensitive hyperparameter, yet the paper lacks a discussion on the intuitive effect of varying σ or a principled strategy for its selection. The absence of this analysis makes the contribution feel incomplete. A potential improvement would be to integrate this stage with the main LAND learning procedure, enabling ρ and σ to be learned directly from the data.

A key advantage of geometric methods is their invariance to coordinate transformations; unfortunately, the metric proposed in Equation 7 does not possess this property. To illustrate, consider data points forming a line in a 2D space. The proposed method would yield entirely different metrics if the coordinate system's x-axis is parallel to this line versus if it is oriented at a 45-degree angle. The paper would be strengthened by a detailed discussion of this limitation, including an analysis of whether the corresponding full (non-diagonal) metric would be invariant, and more intuition behind the formulation in Equation 7. Furthermore, the authors should connect their work to the extensive existing literature on metric learning and manifold learning that leverages Riemannian geometry.

The primary experimental comparison is not sufficiently meaningful. Given that LAND is a more flexible model, it is unsurprising that it outperforms a Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) when using a small number of components. A GMM with a sufficient number of components could likely model the data more accurately, whereas LAND might be prone to overfitting in such a scenario. I strongly suggest redesigning this experiment. A more rigorous comparison would involve allowing each method to select its optimal number of components based on a standard model selection criterion (e.g., BIC) and then comparing the resulting performance. It is anticipated that LAND would still demonstrate superior performance due to its ability to capture the manifold structure, which would make for a much more compelling result. Additionally, demonstrating the overfitting behavior of LAND with a large number of components would provide valuable insight.

The LAND procedure is clearly more computationally intensive than estimating a standard Gaussian. The paper should provide quantitative analysis or experiments to clarify this computational overhead. What are the practical scaling limits of the LAND methodology? For instance, could the method be applied to a dataset like MNIST for a comparison against Gaussian mixture learning at a larger scale?

Regarding the model's flexibility, as the number of components increases, each local LAND model becomes more susceptible to fitting local noise compared to a more rigid local Gaussian. While the authors may argue that the geodesics become nearly straight lines in these cases, they remain curves, and this residual curvature is precisely what can lead to overfitting. I must therefore reiterate my strong recommendation that this experiment be substantially revised for the final version of the paper.
