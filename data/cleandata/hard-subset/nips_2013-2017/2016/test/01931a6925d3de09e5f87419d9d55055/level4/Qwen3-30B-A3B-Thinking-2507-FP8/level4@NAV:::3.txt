

This paper proposes modeling the observation space using a hand-crafted Riemannian metric and defining a curved normal distribution based on the associated Riemannian geometry to better capture the data manifold. It addresses the fundamental problem of defining a Riemannian metric from high-dimensional real-valued observations and subsequently defining and estimating a curved Gaussian distribution based on this geometry, presenting a useful tool and interesting application of Riemannian geometry in statistical learning. The first methodological component (Section 3.1) defines the underlying Riemannian geometry. However, despite the title suggesting "metric learning," the metric is hand-crafted in Equation 7 using fixed hyper-parameters ρ and σ, which are empirically set in advance. The paper should elaborate on the intuitive effect of varying σ and its selection procedure, as σ appears sensitive and this omission renders the approach incomplete. It is also unclear whether metric learning could be integrated with LAND learning to jointly optimize ρ and σ. A key strength of geometric approaches is coordinate invariance, yet the metric in Equation 7 lacks this property. For example, if data lies along a line in 2D space, the metric differs significantly between coordinate systems aligned with the line versus those at an angle (e.g., 45°). The paper should discuss this invariance issue in detail, including whether a full (non-diagonal) metric would be invariant and provide deeper intuition for Equation 7. The work should also connect to the rich literature on Riemannian metric learning and manifold learning. As a main criticism, the initial experiment is uninformative: LAND’s inherent flexibility allows better data description with fewer components, while GMM can match this with sufficient components (avoiding overfitting), making the comparison invalid. The authors should redesign the experiment to allow each method to select its optimal component count (e.g., via BIC), then compare performance—this would meaningfully assess LAND’s advantage in modeling manifold structure. It is also essential to quantify LAND’s computational cost (vs. Gaussian estimation) and scalability limits, and to demonstrate its application on a larger dataset like MNIST. Following the authors’ response, LAND’s flexibility does lead to overfitting with excessive components (as local LANDs become vulnerable to noise), and the geodesics, while nearly straight, remain curved. I strongly recommend revising the experimental section to address this.
