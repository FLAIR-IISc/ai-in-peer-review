This paper introduces a locally adaptive normal distribution, extending the standard normal by incorporating a data-dependent Riemannian metric M(x) to define geodesic distances. The authors leverage the exponential and logarithmic maps, calculated via solutions to the Euler-Lagrange equations, to estimate these geodesic distances. Specifically, the logarithmic map projects data points onto the tangent plane, and the resulting vector lengths represent geodesic distances. A normal distribution is then applied using a Mahalanobis distance computed on these mapped points, effectively measuring distances from a point to the mean along the manifold rather than using Euclidean distance. Maximum likelihood estimation is employed to derive estimators for the mean and covariance, with a steepest descent algorithm proposed to address the lack of closed-form solutions. Furthermore, a Monte Carlo approximation is presented for the intractable normalization constant. The method is evaluated on synthetic manifold data and a clustering task, with comparisons made to a standard Gaussian Mixture Model. 

Overall, this work represents a compelling and potentially valuable adaptation of the normal distribution to better reflect underlying manifold structure, successfully integrating concepts from metric estimation, geodesic distance computation, and distributional learning. I recommend publication.

Regarding specific aspects:

**Technical Quality:** The manifold techniques, including the application of Euler-Lagrange equations and estimation of logarithm maps, are technically sound and well-articulated. The use of Monte Carlo estimation for the normalization constant and the steepest descent algorithm demonstrates ingenuity and is clearly explained. However, the analysis of the applications feels somewhat incomplete; the selection of the sigma parameter appears to be driven by optimization on the training data, and a clearer explanation of the benefits of the learned distribution would be beneficial.

**Novelty:** The primary contribution appears to be a novel combination of existing techniques – utilizing kernels to define the metric M(x), estimating geodesic distances via Euler-Lagrange, and applying a normal distribution within this framework – along with a dedicated algorithm for parameter estimation (mean, covariance, normalization constant).

**Potential Impact:** The evaluation on synthetic data provides a reasonable comparison between the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND) and Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs), focusing on log-likelihoods for data generated from the learned distributions. However, the choice of the sigma parameter remains unclear. A more comprehensive evaluation would include comparisons against GMMs when the data is generated from a standard normal distribution or a GMM, not just manifold data. In the clustering application, the optimization of the sigma parameter using the F-measure, followed by presentation of the same F-measure, raises concerns about potential overfitting. A fairer assessment would involve reporting the F-measure on a separate, held-out test set. Exploring the performance on very high-dimensional data would also be valuable. While the authors suggest LAND as a generative model for manifold data, the assumption of equal dimensionality between the data space and the manifold itself (D=d) is somewhat restrictive, as manifold learning typically involves reducing dimensionality (D >> d).

**Clarity:** The overloading of the Sigma notation is a point of confusion. Specifically, the statement that M = Sigma<sup>-1</sup>, coupled with the estimation of Sigma via a local diagonal kernel matrix, requires clarification. Is this Sigma equivalent to the fitted covariance matrix? If so, does the kernel serve as an initialization, and if so, is it unusual to have M(x) vary with x while the covariance matrix remains a single parameter? The proof of the steepest descent algorithm reinforces this ambiguity by explicitly stating “in our case M = Sigma<sup>-1</sup>.” Further, in Section 4.2, the method of cluster membership assignment (based on minimizing geodesic distance to cluster means with two clusters) should be explicitly stated. Overall, the introduction to manifold concepts is well-explained, particularly for readers with some familiarity with Riemannian geometry.
