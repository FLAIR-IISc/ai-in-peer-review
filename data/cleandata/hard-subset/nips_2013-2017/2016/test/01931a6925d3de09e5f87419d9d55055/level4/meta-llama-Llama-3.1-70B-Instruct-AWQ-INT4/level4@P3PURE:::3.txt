This paper proposes to model the observation space with a hand-crafted Riemannian metric and define a curved normal distribution based on the associated Riemannian geometry, enabling better capture of the data manifold. The paper addresses a fundamental problem of defining a Riemannian metric based on a set of high-dimensional real-valued observations and subsequently defining and estimating a curved Gaussian distribution based on this geometry. This presents a useful tool and an interesting application of Riemannian geometry in statistical learning.

The first part of the proposed method defines the underlying Riemannian geometry in section 3.1. However, despite the title suggesting that it "learns a metric", the metric is actually hand-crafted in equation 7 based on two hyper-parameters, rho and sigma, which are fixed empirically in advance. I assume that sigma is a sensitive parameter, and the paper should provide a more detailed discussion on the intuitive effect of varying sigma and how to select this parameter. Furthermore, it would be beneficial to explore the possibility of merging the metric learning stage with the LAND learning stage, allowing rho and sigma to be learned.

A notable aspect of the geometric approach is its invariance to coordinate transformations. Nevertheless, the metric in equation 7 is not invariant. For instance, if the data forms a line in a 2D observation space, the metric would be different in a coordinate system with its x-axis parallel to this line compared to a coordinate system that forms an angle, e.g., 45 degrees, to this line. A more in-depth discussion on this topic, including the invariance of the corresponding full (rather than diagonal) metric, would be valuable, providing additional intuition about the metric in equation 7.

There is a rich literature on metric learning and manifold learning with connections to Riemannian geometry that can be related to this work (see Google "Riemannian" "metric learning" "manifold learning").

As a primary criticism, the comparison in the first experiment is not particularly meaningful, as the more flexible LAND can describe the data better with a smaller number of components. However, GMM can describe the data better with a large number of components when LAND will overfit. The authors are suggested to redesign the experiment, allowing each compared method to choose its favorable number of components based on a certain criterion (e.g., BIC), and then compare the corresponding performance. LAND is still expected to perform better, as it describes the manifold structure more accurately, and this comparison would be more meaningful. It would also be interesting to see how LAND with a large number of components will overfit.

Additionally, LAND is obviously a more expensive procedure than estimating a Gaussian. Quantitative measurements or experiments to clarify the expense of LAND would be beneficial. What is the limit of the scale that LAND can handle? Is it possible to learn LAND on MNIST and compare it with Gaussian mixture learning?

After reading the authors' response, it is clear that LAND is a more flexible model than the Gaussian distribution. If the number of components is increased, each local LAND will be more vulnerable to local noises compared to a local Gaussian. While the authors state that "the geodesics will almost be straight lines", they are still curves, and these curves will lead to overfitting. I highly suggest that the authors revise this experiment in the revision.
