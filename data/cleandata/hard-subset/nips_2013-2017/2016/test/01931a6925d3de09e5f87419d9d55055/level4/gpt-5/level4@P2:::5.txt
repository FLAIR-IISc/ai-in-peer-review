Summary
The paper introduces the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), which generalizes the multivariate normal by replacing the Euclidean/Mahalanobis distance with a geodesic distance induced by a data-driven Riemannian metric. The metric is learned from data via a smoothly varying, diagonal local covariance (weighted by a Gaussian kernel), which encourages geodesics to pass through high-density regions. On this learned manifold, the authors define a Riemannian normal (Pennec, 2006) and estimate its parameters by maximum likelihood, using Monte Carlo to approximate the normalization constant and gradient-based updates for the mean and covariance. They further develop a mixture of LANDs with an EM-style algorithm. Experiments on synthetic data and EEG sleep-stage features show that LAND can model non-elliptical structures more faithfully than a standard GMM and performs better than least-squares (intrinsic) estimators on the manifold.

Relation to prior work
The approach bridges manifold learning (e.g., Isomap) with statistics on Riemannian manifolds. Unlike classical embedding methods, the learned Riemannian structure supports geodesic computations for generative modeling. Compared to prior uses of Riemannian normals where the manifold is known a priori (e.g., Pennec; Zhang & Fletcher), this work learns the metric from data, which is novel and practically valuable.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: a clean, parametric distribution (with mean and covariance) defined on a nonparametric, learned metric space. It directly leverages the maximum-entropy characterization of the Riemannian normal.
- Sound formulation: careful use of the exponential/log maps, and a well-motivated Monte Carlo estimator for the normalization constant by integrating in the tangent space (computationally preferable to integrating on the manifold).
- Practical extensions: mixture model and EM-like updates, with attention to normalization constants per component.
- Empirical illustrations: LAND captures non-ellipsoidal structure and yields better alignment to sleep-stage labels in several subjects.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
Quality and clarity
- Metric construction (Eq. 7) feels ad hoc: the diagonal local covariance with Gaussian-kernel weights is reasonable, but the paper should provide more intuition and alternatives. Why diagonal only? How sensitive are results to σ and ρ? What happens under linear transformations, given the noted lack of invariance?
- Distinguish clearly between the learned, spatially varying metric M(x) (from Eq. 7) and the model covariance Σ (estimated by ML in Eq. 11). At several points it is easy to conflate the two. A brief subsection contrasting “metric (nonparametric) vs. model covariance (parametric)” would help.
- Provide guidance for choosing σ (and ρ). In EEG experiments σ is tuned per subject and materially affects performance; more principled selection (e.g., cross-validation, heuristics based on neighbor statistics) or an attempt to learn σ would strengthen the story.
- Monte Carlo approximation: report variance/precision diagnostics for estimating C(µ,Σ). How does likelihood or parameter error change with S? Are there control variates or reuse of samples across iterations/components?

Originality and significance
- The idea of learning a manifold metric and placing a Riemannian normal on it is original in this context and potentially impactful. However, the practical significance hinges on scalability and robustness in higher dimensions.

Scalability and computational efficiency
- The paper should quantify computational complexity and provide wall-clock comparisons to a GMM with EM. Per iteration, complexity appears to scale with: O(ND) for metric evaluations, O(S) geodesic IVP solves for C(µ,Σ) per component/iteration, and additional solves for gradients. How does runtime scale with N, D, S, and number of components K? How many ODE steps per geodesic on average?
- In mixtures, do you reuse the same Monte Carlo samples across components or iterations? Any caching of m(µ,v) or geodesic paths? These details could substantially change runtime.
- Report actual run times for synthetic and EEG experiments (with S=3000) versus GMM EM under comparable hardware.

Empirical evaluation
- Synthetic experiments are illustrative but limited. Consider adding:
  - Sensitivity analyses to σ, ρ, and S.
  - Ablations: full vs diagonal metric; LAND-ML vs LAND with intrinsic (LS) estimators; using fewer geodesic samples.
  - Comparisons to methods using graph geodesics or kernel-density–informed metrics; and to Riemannian-mixture baselines on known manifolds, if applicable.
- EEG results: provide per-subject breakdowns, confusion patterns, and statistical significance where possible. Discuss failure cases (subjects where LAND and GMM perform similarly or LAND underperforms).

Pros
- Elegant unification of manifold learning and Riemannian statistics.
- Maximum-entropy, parametric distribution with local adaptivity.
- Clear computational insight: tangent-space Monte Carlo reduces cost relative to manifold integration.
- Empirical gains on nonlinear synthetic data and several EEG subjects.

Cons
- Metric design and hyperparameter selection insufficiently justified.
- Scalability and runtime not rigorously characterized; reliance on many geodesic solves may limit applicability.
- Some notation/clarity issues (metric vs covariance, sensitivity of gradients to Σ conditioning).
- Experiments are not extensive enough to fully establish practical utility.

Questions for the authors
1. Please clarify the rationale for Eq. (7): why diagonal only, and why Gaussian-kernel weights? Have you tried full local covariances or alternative kernels?
2. Can you explicitly separate and define the roles of M(x) (from data) versus Σ (model parameter), and explain how each influences the density and its normalization?
3. What is the per-iteration time and asymptotic complexity as functions of N, D, S, and K? How many ODE solver steps are typical per exponential/log map?
4. How sensitive are results to S? Are there variance-reduction techniques you can use?
5. Could σ (and possibly ρ) be learned or adapted during training?

Minor comments
- Typographical: “Isomap,” “geodesics,” “covariance,” “complexity.”
- Algorithm 1: if dµφ denotes the steepest-descent direction (as defined later), please state it clearly at first use. If you intended the gradient, replace dµφ with ∇µφ in lines 4–5.
- Please ensure consistent notation and define all symbols at first appearance.

Overall assessment
A theoretically appealing contribution with promising empirical evidence, but clarity and scalability concerns limit its immediate practicality. Strengthening the motivation for the metric, clarifying the distinction between M(x) and Σ, and providing solid computational and sensitivity analyses would significantly improve the paper.
