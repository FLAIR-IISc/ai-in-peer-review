In this paper the authors present a locally adaptive normal distribution (LAND). Instead of measuring distances via a Euclidean distance they define a Riemannian metric M(x) which varies smoothly across the data set. Given the Riemannian metric M(x), geodesic distances along the manifold can be calculated via the exponential and logarithmic map. The authors use the Euler-Lagrange equations, which define a set of ordinary differential equations for estimating the geodesic distances. The logarithm map takes points from the manifold (y) into the tangent plane (at x) such that the length of the resulting vector is the geodesic distance from x to y along the manifold. The authors then apply a normal distribution computing a Mahalanobis distance on points mapped by the logarithm map. This inner product is used to compute distances from a point x to the mean instead of Euclidean distances. The authors then derive estimators for the mean and covariance via maximum likelihood. Since the solutions are not available in closed form, they present a steepest descent algorithm for estimating them. Finally, since the normalization constant is also not available in closed form, the authors present a Monte-Carlo approximation method. They apply this method to synthetic data generated on a manifold as well as in a clustering task. They compare to the standard mixture of Gaussians.

Overall, I feel that the paper provides an interesting and useful extension of the normal distribution, which adapts to local manifold structure. The authors combine work in metric estimation, computing geodesic distances, and learning distributions. I believe this work is worthy of publication.

Quality: The paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the manifold techniques used in the paper, including the Euler-Lagrange equations, estimating logarithm maps, and the Monte-Carlo estimation of the normalization constant. The steepest descent algorithm is also well-described. However, the analysis of the applications is somewhat lacking, and the explanation of the usefulness of the learned distribution could be improved.

Clarity: The paper is generally well-written, but there are a few notation points that could be clarified. For example, the overloading of the Sigma notation could be confusing, and it would be helpful to explicitly state how the kernel matrix is used to estimate the metric M(x). Additionally, the authors could provide more detail on how the cluster membership was chosen in the clustering task.

Originality: The paper appears to combine previous work in a novel way, and the authors provide a clear explanation of how their work differs from previous contributions. However, it would be helpful to provide more context on how the LAND compares to other approaches, such as Gaussian mixture models, in terms of its ability to capture non-linear structure in the data.

Significance: The authors provide a fair evaluation of the LAND and the Gaussian mixture model in the synthetic data, but it's not clear how they chose the sigma parameter. Additionally, the authors should provide more detail on how the LAND compares to the Gaussian mixture model in terms of its ability to capture non-linear structure in the data. The authors also mention that the LAND could be used as a generative model for manifold data, but this idea could be explored in more depth.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper provides a novel extension of the normal distribution, which adapts to local manifold structure.
* The authors combine work in metric estimation, computing geodesic distances, and learning distributions in a clear and well-explained way.
* The paper provides a fair evaluation of the LAND and the Gaussian mixture model in the synthetic data.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The analysis of the applications is somewhat lacking, and the explanation of the usefulness of the learned distribution could be improved.
* The authors could provide more detail on how the cluster membership was chosen in the clustering task.
* The paper could benefit from more context on how the LAND compares to other approaches, such as Gaussian mixture models, in terms of its ability to capture non-linear structure in the data.
