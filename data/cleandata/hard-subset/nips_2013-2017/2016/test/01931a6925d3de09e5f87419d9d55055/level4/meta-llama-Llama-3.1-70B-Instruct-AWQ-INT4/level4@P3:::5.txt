This paper introduces a locally adaptive normal distribution (LAND) that replaces the Euclidean distance in the ordinary normal distribution with a locally adaptive nonlinear distance measure. The authors learn a non-parametric metric space by constructing a smoothly changing metric that induces a Riemannian manifold, where they build their model. The LAND is a parametric model over a non-parametric space, and it is a natural extension of the normal distribution to the "manifold" setting.

The authors provide a clear and concise summary of the main ideas in the paper, and they relate these ideas to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere. They discuss the strengths and weaknesses of the paper, addressing the criteria described in the qualitative evaluation section. The authors also provide a list of arguments pro and con acceptance, which is useful for the program committee.

The paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the LAND. They develop a maximum likelihood algorithm to infer the distribution parameters, which relies on a combination of gradient descent and Monte Carlo integration. They also extend the LAND to mixture models and provide the corresponding EM algorithm.

The experimental evaluations are somewhat limited, but the authors demonstrate the efficiency of the LAND on both synthetic data and EEG measurements of human sleep. They compare their model with the Gaussian mixture model (GMM) and a mixture of LANDs using least squares (LS) estimators.

One of the main strengths of the paper is its theoretical contribution. The authors provide a clear and concise introduction to Riemannian geometry and its application to the LAND. They also discuss the relationship between the LAND and other manifold learning methods, such as Isomap.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. The definition of the inverse of the metric tensor in Eq. (7) seems ad hoc, and the authors could provide more intuition on the idea of using a Gaussian kernel for the weights in a weighted sum of local covariances. Additionally, the experimental evaluations are somewhat limited, and the authors could provide more details on the computational complexity and running time of their method compared to standard EM.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-written, but there are some minor issues. For example, the notation could be improved, and some of the equations could be better explained. Algorithm 1, lines 4 and 5, should be corrected to use the gradient symbol instead of "d".

Overall, the paper has an interesting theoretical contribution, but its practical applicability is limited by its computational complexity. The authors could improve the paper by providing more details on the computational efficiency of their method and by addressing the minor issues mentioned above.

Rating: 7/10
