This paper introduces a Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), bridging concepts from Riemannian statistics and manifold learning to propose a novel parametric metric defined over a non-parametric space. The core idea is to replace the Euclidean metric of the standard normal distribution with a locally adaptive Riemannian metric, learned from the data, to better represent data lying near a potentially low-dimensional manifold. The authors develop a maximum likelihood estimation algorithm and extend the model to mixtures of LANDs, demonstrating its effectiveness on both synthetic and EEG sleep stage data.

This work builds upon the foundational Riemannian normal distribution (Pennec [15]) and adapts it to a manifold learning setting, a valuable contribution. The method offers a principled approach to constructing generative models capable of going beyond the limitations of training data, which is a significant advantage in many applications. The connection between metric learning and Riemannian geometry is well-articulated, and the proposed approach presents a coherent and mathematically grounded framework.

However, the paper’s practical applicability to large-scale, high-dimensional data remains a primary concern. The computation of local covariance matrices and their inverses, essential components of the LAND framework, can become computationally prohibitive in high dimensions – a point acknowledged by the authors (lines 265-266). A more thorough analysis of the method's computational complexity and limitations with respect to dimensionality is needed. Specifically, defining the boundaries of "high-dimensional" – whether 3, 5, 10, or 100 dimensions – would be beneficial. The extensive literature on metric learning warrants more detailed consideration within the context of this work. While Hauberg et al. [9] cite some relevant papers (e.g., Frome et al. [1,2], Malisiewicz & Efros [6]), many others employing local metric learning techniques (e.g., local PCA – Kambhatla) are not mentioned.

The influence of the Gaussian kernel parameter, σ, on the results requires further investigation. The paper discusses its selection for the sleep data (Section 4.2) but lacks a corresponding analysis for the synthetic data (Section 4.1). Robustness tests for σ, including examining its sensitivity in both low and high-density regions, would strengthen the paper. The impact of using a fixed σ versus an adaptive σ would be valuable to explore. 

The presentation of experimental results could be improved. Comparing LAND against intrinsic estimators (Section 4.1) yields comparable results, yet visualizations of these intrinsic estimators are absent in Figures 5 and 6. Instead, comparisons are drawn against GMMs, which are already shown to perform poorly in Figure 4, offering limited insight. Including visual results with the intrinsic estimators, either alongside or replacing the GMM results, would provide a more balanced and informative assessment. Analyzing the computational complexity of LAND versus intrinsic estimators could guide recommendations on when to favor each method based on data characteristics.

The extension to mixtures of LANDs represents a promising direction, and expanding upon this aspect would be worthwhile. Investigating the model’s performance with varying numbers of components and exploring its robustness to initialization could enhance this section.

Overall, the paper is exceptionally well-written and a pleasure to read. The authors present a clear and compelling argument for their approach.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel combination of Riemannian statistics and manifold learning.
*   Provides a theoretically grounded and principled approach to adaptive density estimation.
*   Demonstrates promising results on both synthetic and real-world data.
*   Clear and well-structured presentation.
*   Potential for broader impact in generative modeling.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Computational complexity concerns in high-dimensional settings are not fully addressed.
*   Limited discussion of existing metric learning literature.
*   Insufficient analysis of the parameter σ and its influence on results.
*   Experimental presentation could be improved by including results with intrinsic estimators.



**Recommendation:** Weak Accept. While the paper presents a solid contribution, addressing the concerns regarding scalability, related work, and experimental presentation would significantly strengthen it. A minor revision addressing these points is recommended.
