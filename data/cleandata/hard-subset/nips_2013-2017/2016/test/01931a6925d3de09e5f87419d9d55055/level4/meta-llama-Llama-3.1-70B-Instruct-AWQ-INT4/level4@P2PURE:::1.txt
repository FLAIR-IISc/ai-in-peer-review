The paper proposes LAND, a novel parametric metric defined over a nonparametric space, by integrating concepts from metric learning, manifold learning, and Riemannian statistics. The parametric metric learned is a Riemannian normal distribution, as defined in Pennec [15]. The authors propose a maximum likelihood algorithm to estimate the mean and covariance of the Riemannian normal distribution, effectively defining a Mahalanobis distance for Riemannian manifolds. Distances between points are defined as geodesics along the Riemannian manifold, following Hauberg et al. [9], and rely on learning a local metric. Notably, the authors propose learning the metric by using the inverses of the local (diagonal) covariances. The authors also consider mixtures of LANDs, which is a valuable extension. The paper brings new insights into the connection between metric learning and Riemannian statistics. Having parametric and generative models that can generalize beyond the training data is highly useful in many scenarios.

However, my primary concern is the applicability of LAND to real-world data, which is often large-scale and high-dimensional. LAND requires computing a (diagonal) covariance matrix for each data point and its inverse, which are known to be challenging problems in high dimensions. Although the authors acknowledge high-dimensionality as a potential issue (lines 265-266), the paper would benefit from a thorough analysis of the complexity and limitations of the proposed metric. Specifically, it would be helpful to clarify what is meant by "high-dimensional" in this context (e.g., 3, 5, 10, or 100 dimensions). Additionally, the paper would benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of its position within the vast literature on metric learning, as Hauberg et al. [9] have already discussed relevant works, such as Frome et al. [1,2] and Malisiewicz and Efros [6], which also learn a diagonal metric tensor for every point.

Regarding the learning of the local metric, the authors use a Gaussian kernel to define locality. However, the influence of the parameter sigma on the results is not thoroughly explored. While the choice of sigma is discussed for the sleep data in Sect. 4.2, it is not examined for the synthetic data in Sect. 4.1. Robustness tests for sigma would strengthen the paper, and it would be valuable to investigate whether sigma is the same for all points, regardless of whether they are in low- or high-density regions.

The comparison of LAND against intrinsic estimators in Fig. 4 shows similar results, but the authors do not provide visual results using these intrinsic estimators in Figs. 5 and 6. Instead, they show visualization results against GMM, which have already been demonstrated to perform poorly in Fig. 4. This comparison is less insightful, as some visual results using the intrinsic model are shown in the supplementary material (Fig. 2 and 4) and outperform GMM. It would be beneficial to replace the GMM results in the initial paper with those of the intrinsic model or make room for both. Furthermore, an analysis of the computational complexity of LAND versus intrinsic estimators would be valuable in determining which method is more suitable for different data structures.

The consideration of mixtures of LANDs is an exciting application, as real-world data is rarely generated by a single component. This aspect is worth expanding upon, as it has significant potential for practical applications. Overall, the paper is well-written and a pleasure to read.
