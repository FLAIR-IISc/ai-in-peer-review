Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper introduces the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), which replaces the Euclidean metric in a Gaussian with a smoothly varying Riemannian metric adapted to the data. The resulting density is a Riemannian normal distribution parameterized by a mean and covariance, with normalization computed via Monte Carlo integration on the tangent space. The authors provide gradient-based maximum likelihood estimation for single components and an EM algorithm for mixtures, and evaluate on synthetic data and EEG sleep-stage features.

The work connects Riemannian statistics (e.g., Pennec’s Riemannian normal on known manifolds) with manifold learning (e.g., Isomap, kernel methods), by learning a metric over RD rather than assuming a known manifold. The idea of using data-adaptive metrics for geodesic modeling relates to metric learning and probabilistic geometries; however, the paper uses a specific hand-crafted, kernel-smoothed diagonal metric (Eq. 7) rather than learning a metric end-to-end.

Strengths:
- Clear conceptual bridge between manifold learning and generative modeling via Riemannian normals, with a practical ML estimation procedure.
- Sensible use of tangent-space Monte Carlo to approximate normalization constants, avoiding costly BVPs for log maps.
- Extension to mixture models with an EM-style algorithm.
- Experiments indicate LAND can capture nonlinear structure and avoid the low-density mean issue of intrinsic least squares estimators.

Weaknesses and detailed comments:

1) Metric construction and hyperparameters:
- Despite the title’s “learned metric” implication, the metric in Eq. (7) is hand-crafted with two hyperparameters, sigma and rho, fixed empirically. The method’s behavior appears particularly sensitive to sigma. The paper should:
  - Provide intuition for how varying sigma affects the geometry, geodesics, and the fitted densities (e.g., sigma too small: unstable/geodesics trapped; sigma too large: nearly Euclidean, losing adaptivity).
  - Offer guidance or a principled selection procedure for sigma (e.g., cross-validation, likelihood-based model selection, plug-in bandwidth rules), and discuss the role and sensitivity of rho.
  - Discuss whether sigma and rho can be estimated jointly with LAND parameters, and whether the metric-learning stage can be integrated into maximum likelihood (e.g., by differentiating through geodesic IVPs, or via an EM-like outer loop).

Without a clear treatment of sigma, the methodology feels incomplete, as performance hinges on this choice.

2) Invariance and metric design:
- A central appeal of geometric approaches is invariance under coordinate transformations. The diagonal local covariance metric in Eq. (7) is not invariant: for data that lie along a line in 2D, rotating the coordinate system yields different metrics and thus different geodesics and densities. The paper should:
  - Analyze this lack of invariance, explain its practical impact, and clarify whether a full (non-diagonal) local covariance metric would alleviate or eliminate the issue.
  - Provide more intuition and justification for restricting to diagonal metrics (beyond computational efficiency and overfitting concerns), and discuss potential remedies (e.g., pre-whitening, low-rank local metrics, mixtures of metrics).
  - More thoroughly relate to the literature on metric learning and manifold learning with Riemannian connections, and position Eq. (7) relative to alternatives (e.g., learned probabilistic metrics, mixtures of local metrics).

3) Experimental design and overfitting:
- In the first synthetic experiment, the comparison is not entirely fair: the more flexible LAND can fit manifold structure with fewer components, while a GMM typically needs more components to approximate curved densities. Conversely, LAND with too many components risks overfitting local idiosyncrasies because geodesics, while close to straight for large sigma, are still curved and can fit noise at small sigma or when components are overly localized.
- A more meaningful comparison would allow each method to select its own number of components using a common criterion (e.g., BIC or AIC), then compare performance. LAND is still expected to perform well, but this design avoids bias and also reveals whether LAND overfits when K is large. It would be helpful to show how LAND behaves as K increases (e.g., validation likelihood vs. K), and to visualize failure modes.
- The authors mention AIC/BIC in the supplement for the synthetic case; such results should be brought into the main paper.

4) Computational cost and scalability:
- LAND is substantially more expensive than a standard Gaussian (geodesic IVPs for Exp, Monte Carlo for normalization, repeated during ML/EM). Please quantify:
  - Runtime per iteration and total training time vs. GMM, as a function of N, D, K, S (MC samples), and ODE solver tolerances.
  - Memory and wall-clock comparisons on the reported datasets.
  - Limits of scale: what N and D are feasible? Could LAND be trained on MNIST-scale data, and how would it compare to GMMs in likelihood and runtime?
- Discuss strategies for scalability (subsampling, control variates for MC, learned surrogates for maps, amortized geodesics, or probabilistic ODE solvers).

Quality:
The methodology is technically sound at a high level, with appropriate use of Riemannian tools and MC integration. However, the absence of a principled treatment of sigma/rho and the non-invariance of the chosen metric leave important gaps. Experimental comparisons should more rigorously control model capacity.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written. It would benefit from a deeper discussion of the metric, hyperparameter selection, and invariance, and from bringing key experimental selection-criterion results into the main text.

Originality:
Adapting Riemannian normals to a data-adapted metric over RD is a novel and useful combination, beyond prior work on known manifolds.

Significance:
Potentially impactful as a generic drop-in replacement for Gaussians in manifold-structured data. Significance would be strengthened by fair model selection, scalability analysis, and broader benchmarks.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide principled selection/learning of sigma and rho, with sensitivity analysis and guidance.
- Discuss invariance properties, potential use of full or low-rank local metrics, and relate more deeply to metric learning literature.
- Redesign comparisons to allow each method to choose K via BIC/AIC, and report overfitting behavior of LAND as K grows.
- Add quantitative runtime/scale studies; consider a larger dataset (e.g., MNIST) to illustrate limits and trade-offs.
- Include visualizations of geodesics and densities under varying sigma to build intuition.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Conceptually elegant bridge between manifold learning and generative modeling.
- Practical ML/EM algorithms with thoughtful use of tangent-space MC.
- Promising empirical performance on nonlinear data and EEG features.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Metric is hand-crafted, non-invariant, and hyperparameter-sensitive; no principled selection/learning provided.
- Experimental comparisons do not fully control for model capacity; risk of overfitting with larger K not examined.
- Computational cost and scalability not quantified.

Post-rebuttal remarks:
I appreciate the authors’ response noting that geodesics become nearly straight with larger sigma; however, even mild curvature can encourage overfitting when K is large and components become overly local. I strongly recommend revising the experimental design to select K per method (e.g., via BIC) and to document LAND’s behavior as K increases. This would make the empirical comparison more convincing.
