This paper introduces a novel approach to modeling the observation space by employing a hand-crafted Riemannian metric, which is then used to define a curved normal distribution based on the associated Riemannian geometry. This allows for a more accurate capture of the data manifold. The paper tackles a fundamental issue in defining a Riemannian metric based on high-dimensional real-valued observations and estimating a curved Gaussian distribution from this geometry. This presents a valuable tool and an interesting application of Riemannian geometry in statistical learning. 

The proposed method's first part, described in section 3.1, establishes the underlying Riemannian geometry. However, the title's claim of "learning a metric" is somewhat misleading, as the metric is actually predefined in equation 7 using two hyper-parameters, rho and sigma, which are fixed empirically in advance. It would be beneficial for the paper to provide a more detailed explanation of the intuitive effects of varying sigma and how to select this parameter. Furthermore, it is worth exploring whether the metric learning stage can be merged with the LAND learning stage, allowing rho and sigma to be learned.

The geometric approach's strength lies in its invariance to coordinate transformations. However, the metric in equation 7 does not possess this property. For instance, if the data forms a line in a 2D observation space, the metric would differ significantly depending on the coordinate system's orientation. A more in-depth discussion of this limitation, including the invariance of the full metric, would be beneficial.

The paper could also benefit from a more comprehensive review of the existing literature on metric learning and manifold learning in the context of Riemannian geometry. A simple search for "Riemannian" "metric learning" "manifold learning" yields a wealth of relevant information.

A major criticism of the paper is that the comparison in the first experiment is not particularly meaningful, as the more flexible LAND can describe the data better with a smaller number of components. However, GMM can describe the data better with a large number of components when LAND will overfit. To address this, the authors are suggested to redesign the experiment, allowing each compared method to choose its optimal number of components based on a specific criterion (e.g., BIC), and then compare the corresponding performance. It is expected that LAND will still perform better due to its ability to describe the manifold structure more accurately.

Additionally, it would be useful to provide quantitative measurements or experiments to demonstrate the computational expense of LAND compared to estimating a Gaussian. What is the limit of the scale that LAND can handle? Is it possible to learn LAND on MNIST and compare it with Gaussian mixture learning?

After considering the authors' response, it is clear that LAND is a more flexible model than the Gaussian distribution. However, increasing the number of components makes each local LAND more vulnerable to local noises compared to a local Gaussian. Although the geodesics may be nearly straight lines, they are still curves that can lead to overfitting. Therefore, it is highly recommended that the authors revise this experiment in the revision.
