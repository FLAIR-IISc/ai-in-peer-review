This paper presents a compelling extension of the standard multivariate normal density by substituting the Euclidean metric with a locally adaptive Riemannian metric. This results in a generalization of the normal distribution suitable for manifold learning, with broad applicability demonstrated through the reported experiments. Notably, the paper provides a concise yet complete derivation of a maximum likelihood parameter estimation procedure. Extending this to a mixture model – a Locally Adaptive Normal Density with Mixtures (LANDS) – makes it a particularly practical alternative to the now-standard Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM). The experiments are, in this reviewer’s opinion, quite promising and suggest a rich direction for future research. The language, structure, bibliography, and relevance to the NIPS conference are all excellent.

The core idea of adapting the metric locally is clever and well-motivated. The authors convincingly argue for the benefits of this approach in capturing the underlying geometry of the data, moving beyond the limitations of Euclidean space when dealing with non-linear manifolds. The mathematical development is clear and the presentation of the parameter estimation procedure is remarkably succinct. 

I particularly appreciated the experimental evaluation. The results demonstrate the effectiveness of LANDS across a range of datasets and tasks, consistently outperforming or matching the performance of GMMs. The visualizations presented further support the claim that LANDS better adapts to the data’s structure. The choice of datasets feels appropriate and the comparisons are fair.

However, while the paper is strong overall, a few minor points could be addressed. Firstly, the computational cost of estimating the Riemannian metric could be further discussed. Although the authors mention its calculation, a more detailed analysis of its scalability with dataset size and dimensionality would be beneficial. Secondly, exploring the sensitivity of the method to the choice of neighborhood size (used in the Riemannian metric estimation) would strengthen the analysis. Is there a principled way to select this parameter, or is it largely empirical?

Despite these minor suggestions, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. The approach is novel, theoretically sound, and empirically validated. I am confident that this work will stimulate further research in manifold learning and density estimation. I recommend acceptance.
