This paper proposes a locally adaptive normal distribution using a smoothly varying Riemannian metric M(x) and geodesic distances computed via exponential/logarithmic maps (from Euler–Lagrange).

Distances are computed by mapping points to the tangent plane with the log map and applying a Mahalanobis distance there instead of Euclidean distance.

Maximum likelihood estimators for mean and covariance are derived but require a steepest-descent algorithm since closed-form solutions are not available.

The normalization constant is estimated via a Monte‑Carlo approximation because it lacks a closed-form expression.

Method evaluated on synthetic manifold data and a clustering task, compared against a standard Gaussian mixture model.

Technical methods (Euler–Lagrange geodesics, log map estimation, Monte‑Carlo normalization, steepest descent) are sound and well-explained.

Unclear selection and role of the sigma hyperparameter — appears chosen by optimizing on training data without justification.

Insufficient demonstration of usefulness: no experiments showing performance when data are generated from a single Gaussian or GMM for fair comparison.

Potential overfitting concern: sigma chosen to optimize F‑measure on the same data; no held-out test results reported.

No experiments or discussion of scalability to very high-dimensional data.

Unusual and limiting manifold assumption: ambient dimension D equals manifold dimension (full‑rank M(x)), which is uncommon in manifold learning where d << D.

Novelty is incremental: mainly a combination of prior components (kernel metric, geodesic estimation, normal distribution) plus an estimation algorithm.

Notation and clarity issues: confusing use of Sigma and M = Sigma^-1 versus a local diagonal kernel matrix — unclear if kernel is an initialization or the fitted covariance.

Clustering procedure is not fully specified (likely assigned by closest geodesic distance to cluster means), needs explicit description.

Overall, an interesting and potentially useful extension but requires clearer hyperparameter treatment, stronger comparative experiments, and clarification of notation/assumptions.
