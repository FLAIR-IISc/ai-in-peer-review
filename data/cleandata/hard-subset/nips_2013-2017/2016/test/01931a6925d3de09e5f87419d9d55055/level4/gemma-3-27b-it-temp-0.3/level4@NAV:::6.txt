This paper introduces a locally adaptive normal distribution, moving beyond Euclidean distance by defining a smooth, data-dependent Riemannian metric M(x). Geodesic distances are then computed on this manifold using the exponential and logarithmic maps, leveraging Euler-Lagrange equations to estimate these distances. Crucially, the authors map data points to the tangent plane via the logarithm map and apply a normal distribution based on a Mahalanobis distance calculated using this mapping, effectively measuring distances from a point to the mean along the manifold. Maximum likelihood estimation is employed to derive estimators for the mean and covariance, with a steepest descent algorithm presented to address the lack of closed-form solutions. Furthermore, a Monte Carlo approximation is developed to handle the intractable normalization constant. The method is demonstrated on synthetic manifold data and applied to a clustering task, with comparisons made to a standard Gaussian mixture model.

Overall, this work represents a compelling and valuable extension of the normal distribution, adapting it to the underlying local manifold structure. The authors successfully integrate techniques from metric estimation, geodesic distance computation, and distribution learning. I recommend this paper for publication.

Regarding technical quality, the manifold techniques – particularly the application of Euler-Lagrange equations and estimation of logarithm maps – are rigorous and clearly explained. The clever use of Monte Carlo estimation for the normalization constant and the well-described steepest descent algorithm are also strengths. However, the analysis of the applications feels somewhat limited; the selection of the sigma parameter appears to be driven by optimization on the training data. Additionally, a more thorough explanation of the practical benefits of the learned distribution would be beneficial.

The novelty of this work lies primarily in the combination of existing concepts – utilizing kernels to define the metric M(x), estimating geodesic distances via Euler-Lagrange, and applying a normal distribution within this framework – and the development of an algorithm for parameter estimation (mean, covariance, normalization constant).

Concerning potential impact, the evaluation comparing the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND) to a Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) on synthetic data, based on log-likelihood, is reasonable. However, the justification for the chosen sigma parameter remains unclear. The paper demonstrates good performance on data generated along a manifold but lacks a comparative analysis against a GMM when data is generated from a standard normal distribution or GMM. In the clustering application, the reported F-measure is obtained through sigma optimization, raising concerns about potential overfitting, which would be better addressed with a held-out test set. Exploring performance on high-dimensional data would also be valuable. While the authors suggest LAND as a generative model for manifold data, the assumption of equal manifold and data dimensionality (D) is somewhat restrictive, as manifold learning typically involves a lower-dimensional manifold (d) embedded in a higher-dimensional space (D, where D >> d).

With respect to clarity, the overloaded Sigma notation is confusing. The statement that M = Sigma<sup>-1</sup>, coupled with the estimation of Sigma via a local diagonal kernel matrix, requires clarification. Is this Sigma equivalent to the fitted covariance matrix? If so, is the kernel merely an initialization strategy? This is unclear, as M(x) should vary with x, while a single covariance matrix would not. The proof of the steepest descent algorithm explicitly states “M = Sigma<sup>-1</sup>,” reinforcing the need for clarification. Furthermore, in Section 4.2, the method for determining cluster membership – presumably based on minimizing geodesic distance to cluster means – should be explicitly stated. The introduction of manifold concepts is generally well-explained, assuming some familiarity with Riemannian geometry.
