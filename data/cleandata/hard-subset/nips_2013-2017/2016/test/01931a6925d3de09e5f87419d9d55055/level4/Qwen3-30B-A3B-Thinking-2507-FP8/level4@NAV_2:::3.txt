

This paper proposes a locally adaptive normal distribution (LAND) that replaces the Euclidean metric in standard normal distributions with a smoothly varying Riemannian metric to better capture nonlinear data manifolds. The authors develop a maximum likelihood estimation procedure for LAND parameters using gradient descent combined with Monte Carlo integration, and extend the model to mixture distributions with an EM algorithm. The work bridges statistics on Riemannian manifolds with manifold learning techniques, offering a theoretically grounded approach to modeling data on nonlinear manifolds.

The paper makes a valuable contribution by providing a parametric model that adapts to the local structure of data manifolds. The theoretical foundation is sound, with the LAND being shown to be the maximum entropy distribution under the learned metric. The experimental validation on synthetic data and EEG sleep stage classification demonstrates the model's ability to capture nonlinear structures that standard Gaussian distributions cannot.

However, the paper has several significant weaknesses that need addressing. The Riemannian metric (equation 7) is hand-crafted rather than learned, with hyperparameters ρ and σ fixed empirically without proper justification. The paper should provide deeper analysis of how σ affects the metric (e.g., showing the intuitive effect of varying σ) and explain how to select this sensitive parameter. It would be more compelling if the metric learning could be integrated with LAND parameter estimation to learn these hyperparameters jointly.

The metric in equation 7 is not invariant to coordinate transformations, which is problematic for a geometric approach. For instance, if data lies along a line in 2D space, the metric would differ significantly between coordinate systems aligned with the line versus those at an angle to it. The authors should discuss this invariance issue in detail, including whether a full (non-diagonal) metric would be invariant and provide better intuition about the metric's behavior.

The experimental comparison with Gaussian mixture models (GMM) is flawed because it compares models with fixed component numbers (K=1 to 4) rather than allowing each method to select its optimal number of components based on criteria like BIC. This makes the comparison unfair since GMM can achieve better performance with more components, while LAND's advantage in capturing manifold structure would be clearer when both methods use their optimal component counts. The paper should redesign the experiments to use component selection based on model selection criteria.

The computational cost of LAND is significantly higher than standard Gaussian estimation, yet the paper lacks quantitative analysis of this overhead. It would be valuable to include measurements of training time, memory requirements, and scalability limits. The authors should demonstrate LAND's performance on larger datasets (e.g., MNIST) to establish its practical applicability.

While the authors' response acknowledges that LAND becomes more vulnerable to local noise with increased components, the experimental design still needs revision to properly compare models with their optimal component counts. The paper would be much stronger if it addressed these methodological and experimental concerns.
