## Review: Locally Adaptive Normal Distributions on Manifolds

This paper introduces a novel approach to modeling data distributions by adapting the standard Gaussian distribution to incorporate local geometry. Specifically, it proposes a Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND) based on geodesic distances computed via manifold traversal, learned directly from the data. This replaces the traditional Mahalanobis distance and is further extended to a mixture of LANDs. The method is evaluated on both a synthetic dataset and a real-world EEG dataset.

The core idea of utilizing geodesic distances, reminiscent of techniques like Isomap, is intriguing and presents a potentially valuable contribution. However, the paper suffers from clarity issues that hinder full understanding and assessment. 

My primary concern lies with the justification for the metric tensor inverse calculation in Equation (7). The use of a Gaussian kernel to weight the sum of local covariances feels somewhat arbitrary. The authors should provide more detailed intuition behind this choice. Furthermore, the presentation of the local covariance matrix definition (Eq. 7) and its subsequent MAP estimation (Eq. 11) is confusing. It is unclear whether these refer to the same quantity or represent distinct steps in the process; clarification is crucial.

The experimental evaluation also requires substantial improvement. While results are presented, they are not compelling and lack sufficient detail regarding the experimental setup. More importantly, the paper does not address the practical limitations of the proposed method. A critical issue is scalability and computational efficiency. What is the computational complexity of LAND compared to standard Expectation-Maximization (EM)? Providing running times for both methods on datasets of varying sizes would significantly strengthen the paper. 

Despite these weaknesses, the paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution. However, without addressing the scalability concerns and improving the clarity of the methodology, its practical applicability remains questionable. I currently view this work as potentially impactful from a theoretical standpoint, but unlikely to be widely adopted in practice in its current form.
