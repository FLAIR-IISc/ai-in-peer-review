This paper proposes endowing the observation space with a data-adaptive Riemannian metric and defining a curved normal distribution based on that geometry, so as to better capture manifold structure. It tackles a fundamental question: how to specify a Riemannian metric from high-dimensional real-valued observations, and then how to define and estimate a Gaussian-like distribution under this geometry. The resulting model is a useful tool and an interesting application of Riemannian geometry in statistical learning. The first step, described in Section 3.1, sets up the underlying geometry. Although the title suggests “learning a metric,” the metric in Equation (7) is in fact hand-designed from two hyperparameters, ρ and σ, which are fixed empirically beforehand. σ in particular appears likely to be sensitive. The paper should explain in greater depth the practical effect of varying σ and how to choose it; without this, the method feels incomplete. Is it feasible to integrate the metric specification with LAND parameter learning so that ρ and σ are learned jointly?

One appeal of geometric approaches is invariance to coordinate transformations, yet the metric in Equation (7) is not invariant. For instance, if the data lie along a line in 2D, the induced metrics differ markedly between a coordinate system aligned with the line and one rotated by, say, 45 degrees. It would be helpful to discuss this issue in detail, including whether a full (non-diagonal) metric would restore any invariance properties, and to provide more intuition for the design choices in Equation (7). There is also extensive related work in metric learning and manifold learning connected to Riemannian geometry that could be positioned relative to this contribution.

A main concern is the first experiment: the comparison is not very informative because the more flexible LAND can represent the data with fewer components, whereas a GMM can often match or surpass performance with a larger number of components, at which point LAND may overfit. I recommend redesigning the experiment so each method selects its preferred number of components using a model selection criterion (e.g., BIC), and then comparing performance under those choices. LAND is still expected to benefit from better modeling of manifold structure, and such a comparison would be more meaningful. It would also be valuable to illustrate how LAND behaves as the number of components increases, including when it overfits.

LAND is clearly more computationally demanding than fitting a standard Gaussian. Please include quantitative timing/complexity measurements or scaling experiments to clarify the computational overhead and practical limits. What dataset sizes and dimensions are tractable? For example, is it feasible to fit LAND on MNIST, and how would that compare against a Gaussian mixture?

After reading the authors’ response: LAND is indeed more flexible than a Gaussian. As the number of components increases, each local LAND is likely more susceptible to local noise than a local Gaussian. The statement “the geodesics will almost be straight lines” may be true, but they are still curves, and this residual curvature can drive overfitting. I strongly encourage revising the experiment accordingly.
