- Proposes LAND, a parametric metric over a nonparametric space, connecting metric learning, manifold learning, and Riemannian statistics.  
- Models the metric as a Riemannian normal distribution (Pennec [15]) and learns it via maximum likelihood to estimate mean and covariance.  
- Learns a Mahalanobis-like distance on manifolds where distances are geodesics defined by a learned local metric.  
- Defines the local metric using inverses of per-point diagonal covariances.  
- Considers mixtures of LANDs to model multi-component real-world data distributions.  
- Offers new insights into linking metric learning with Riemannian statistical models and provides a parametric, generative model that can generalize beyond training data.  
- Major concern: scalability and applicability to large-scale, high-dimensional data due to per-point covariance computation and inversion.  
- Requests a thorough complexity analysis and concrete limits on feasible data dimensionality (e.g., when LAND becomes impractical).  
- Suggests stronger positioning within metric learning literature, noting related works (Hauberg et al., Frome et al., Malisiewicz & Efros, local PCA) that also use per-point diagonal metrics.  
- Notes insufficient exploration of the Gaussian kernel bandwidth sigma: robustness tests, impact on synthetic experiments, and whether sigma is global or adaptive are missing.  
- Criticizes visualization choices: intrinsic estimator results are not shown in main figures (Figs. 5–6); GMM comparisons are less informative given GMM’s poor performance in Fig. 4.  
- Recommends including computational complexity comparisons between LAND and intrinsic estimators, and expanding on mixtures of LANDs as a promising extension.  
- Praises the paper as well-written and enjoyable to read.
