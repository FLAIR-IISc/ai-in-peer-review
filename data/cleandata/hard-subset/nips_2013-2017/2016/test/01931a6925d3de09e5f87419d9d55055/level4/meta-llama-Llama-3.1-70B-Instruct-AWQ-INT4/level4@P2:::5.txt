This paper proposes a locally adaptive normal distribution (LAND) based on a geodesic manifold traversal distance, learned adaptively from the data. The distance replaces the standard Mahalanobis distance used in the Gaussian distribution. The extension to mixture of LANDs is also provided. The performance is evaluated on a synthetic dataset as well as a real-world dataset of EEG measurements.

The main idea of the paper is to replace the Euclidean distance in the ordinary normal distribution with a locally adaptive nonlinear distance measure. This is achieved by learning a non-parametric metric space, constructed using a local metric that is the inverse of a local covariance matrix. The locality is defined via a Gaussian kernel, which can be seen as a form of kernel smoothing.

The paper is well-motivated, and the proposed idea of using a geodesic distance, similar to Isomaps, is very interesting. However, there are a few weak spots in the clarity of the paper. The definition of the inverse of the metric tensor in Eq. (7) seems ad hoc, and it would be beneficial to provide more intuition on the idea of using a Gaussian kernel for the weights in a weighted sum of local covariances.

Additionally, the relationship between the definition of the local covariance matrix in Eq. (7) and the MAP estimation of the same quantity in Eq. (11) is not entirely clear. It would be helpful to clarify whether these refer to the same quantity and how they are related.

The experimental evaluations are somewhat limited, and it would be beneficial to include more comprehensive evaluations, such as comparisons with other state-of-the-art methods and analyses of the robustness of the proposed method.

My main concern is the scalability and computational efficiency of the method. The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity and running time compared to standard EM. It is essential to demonstrate that the proposed method is computationally feasible and efficient, especially for large-scale datasets.

In conclusion, the paper has an interesting theoretical contribution, but its practical applicability is uncertain due to the lack of comprehensive evaluations and analysis of computational efficiency. With some revisions to address these concerns, the paper has the potential to make a more significant impact in the field.

Strengths:

* The proposed idea of using a geodesic distance is interesting and has potential applications in various fields.
* The paper provides a clear and concise introduction to the concept of Riemannian manifolds and their application to machine learning.

Weaknesses:

* The definition of the inverse of the metric tensor in Eq. (7) seems ad hoc and requires more intuition.
* The relationship between the definition of the local covariance matrix in Eq. (7) and the MAP estimation of the same quantity in Eq. (11) is not entirely clear.
* The experimental evaluations are limited, and more comprehensive evaluations are necessary.
* The scalability and computational efficiency of the method are not adequately addressed.

Suggestions for improvement:

* Provide more intuition on the idea of using a Gaussian kernel for the weights in a weighted sum of local covariances.
* Clarify the relationship between the definition of the local covariance matrix in Eq. (7) and the MAP estimation of the same quantity in Eq. (11).
* Include more comprehensive evaluations, such as comparisons with other state-of-the-art methods and analyses of the robustness of the proposed method.
* Provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity and running time compared to standard EM.
* Address the scalability and computational efficiency of the method, especially for large-scale datasets.
