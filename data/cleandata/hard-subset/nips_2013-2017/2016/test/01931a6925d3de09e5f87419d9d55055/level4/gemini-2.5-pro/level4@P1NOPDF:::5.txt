**Summary**

This paper introduces a locally adaptive normal distribution (LAND) based on a geodesic manifold traversal distance that is learned adaptively from the data. This learned geodesic distance replaces the standard Mahalanobis distance typically used in the Gaussian distribution. The authors also present an extension to a mixture of LANDs. The method's performance is evaluated on a synthetic dataset and a real-world dataset of EEG measurements.

**Major Comments**

The core idea of using a geodesic distance, reminiscent of Isomap, to define a probability distribution is novel and interesting. However, the paper has several weaknesses, particularly regarding clarity and the scope of the experimental evaluation.

First, the presentation lacks clarity in a few key areas. The definition of the inverse metric tensor in Equation (7) appears ad hoc. The authors should provide more intuition for using a Gaussian kernel to compute the weighted sum of local covariances, as the motivation for this choice is not well explained. Furthermore, the connection between the initial definition of the local covariance matrix in Equation (7) and its subsequent MAP estimation in Equation (11) is confusing. It is unclear whether these two equations refer to the same quantity, and the paper would benefit from a clearer explanation of their relationship.

Second, the experimental evaluations are not sufficiently convincing. My primary concern, however, is the scalability and computational efficiency of the proposed method. The paper provides no analysis of the algorithm's computational complexity, nor does it compare its runtime against standard baselines like the EM algorithm for Gaussian mixtures. Without this analysis, it is difficult to assess the practical applicability of the approach, especially for large datasets.

In conclusion, while the paper offers an interesting theoretical contribution, its practical utility is questionable due to unresolved concerns about scalability and efficiency.

**Minor Comments**

In Algorithm 1, lines 4 and 5, the notation 'd' should likely be the gradient operator, '∇'.
