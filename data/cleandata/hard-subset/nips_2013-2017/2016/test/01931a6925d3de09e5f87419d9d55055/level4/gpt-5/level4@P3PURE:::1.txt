This paper introduces LAND, a parametric metric defined over a nonparametric space by bridging ideas from metric learning/manifold learning and Riemannian statistics. The learned parametric metric is a Riemannian normal distribution as defined in Pennec [15]. The authors propose a maximum likelihood procedure to estimate the mean and covariance of this Riemannian normal—effectively a Mahalanobis distance on Riemannian manifolds. Distances between points are geodesic distances along the manifold, as in Hauberg et al. [9], and the approach relies on learning a local metric. Here, the metric is learned via the inverses of local (diagonal) covariances. The authors also consider mixtures of LANDs. Overall, the paper offers fresh insights into the connection between metric learning and Riemannian statistics. Having parametric, generative models that extrapolate beyond the training data is valuable in many settings. The paper is also very well written and a pleasure to read.

My primary concern is applicability to real-world data that are both large-scale and high-dimensional. LAND requires computing a (here, diagonal) covariance matrix for each data point and its inverse; in high dimensions, these are known to be challenging. The authors briefly mention high dimensionality as a potential issue (lines 265–266), but the paper needs an explicit analysis of the computational complexity and practical limitations of the proposed metric. How high is “high-dimensional” in this context—3, 5, 10, 100 dimensions? Clear guidance and complexity analysis would help readers understand the operating regime and trade-offs.

Positioning within the metric learning literature could also be strengthened. Hauberg et al. [9] already survey relevant work such as Frome et al. [1,2] and Malisiewicz and Efros [6], who learn a diagonal metric tensor per point, which is similar in spirit to LAND. Moreover, local covariances have been used for related purposes, for example in local PCA (Kambhatla, “Dimension reduction by local principal component analysis”), albeit not necessarily at every point. A more explicit discussion of these connections would help future readers situate LAND among prior methods.

Lines 101–103: for learning the local metric, the authors use a Gaussian kernel to define locality. How does the choice of the bandwidth σ influence results? The paper discusses σ only for the sleep data in Section 4.2, but not for the synthetic data in Section 4.1. Robustness tests for σ would be valuable. Is σ held constant across all points, including both low- and high-density regions?

Figures and comparisons: Figure 4 compares LAND against intrinsic estimators and finds broadly similar performance, yet Figures 5 and 6 show visualizations only against GMMs—which Figure 4 already indicates perform poorly—making those comparisons less informative. The supplementary material (Figures 2 and 4) includes visual results for the intrinsic model, and these outperform GMMs. Those results should likely replace the GMM visualizations in the main paper, or make room for both, to provide a more insightful comparison.

It would also be useful to analyze the computational complexity of LAND relative to intrinsic estimators, to clarify which method should be preferred under which data structures and conditions.

Finally, the treatment of mixtures of LANDs is one of the most interesting aspects of the paper, since data are rarely generated by a single component. This part would benefit from expansion, both conceptually and empirically.
