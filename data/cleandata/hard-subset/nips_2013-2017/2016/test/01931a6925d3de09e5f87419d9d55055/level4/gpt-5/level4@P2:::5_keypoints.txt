- Summary: Introduces LAND, replacing Euclidean/Mahalanobis distance with geodesic distance from a data-driven Riemannian metric and defines a Riemannian normal estimated by ML with Monte Carlo normalization.
- Summary: Proposes a mixture of LANDs with an EM-like algorithm and shows improved modeling of non-elliptical structure on synthetic data and EEG features.

- Relation to prior work: Bridges manifold learning and Riemannian statistics by learning the metric from data rather than assuming a known manifold.

- Strength: Conceptual novelty — a parametric Riemannian normal on a nonparametric learned metric with maximum-entropy justification.
- Strength: Sound mathematical formulation using exponential/log maps and tangent-space Monte Carlo for normalization.
- Strength: Practical extensions including mixture model and attention to per-component normalization.
- Strength: Empirical illustrations show better alignment with sleep-stage labels and capture of non-ellipsoidal structures.

- Weakness: Metric construction (Eq.7) feels ad hoc — diagonal local covariance and Gaussian kernel need more intuition and justification.
- Weakness: Need to clearly distinguish learned spatially varying metric M(x) from model covariance Σ to avoid confusion.
- Weakness: Lack of guidance for choosing hyperparameters σ and ρ; σ tuned per-subject in EEG and materially affects performance.
- Weakness: Monte Carlo approximation diagnostics missing — report variance, sensitivity to sample size S, and possible variance-reduction strategies.

- Originality/significance: Novel and potentially impactful, but practical value depends on scalability and robustness in higher dimensions.

- Scalability: Paper lacks quantified computational complexity and wall-clock comparisons to standard GMM EM.
- Scalability: Per-iteration cost appears to scale with O(ND) for metric evals and O(S) geodesic IVP solves; details on ODE steps per geodesic are missing.
- Scalability: Unclear whether Monte Carlo samples, geodesic paths, or m(µ,v) are reused/cached across components/iterations to reduce runtime.
- Scalability: Report actual run times (e.g., for S=3000) versus GMM EM under comparable hardware.

- Empirical evaluation: Synthetic experiments are limited; need sensitivity analyses for σ, ρ, and S, and ablation studies (full vs diagonal metric, estimator variants).
- Empirical evaluation: Compare to graph-geodesic or kernel-informed metrics and Riemannian-mixture baselines on known manifolds.
- Empirical evaluation: EEG results need per-subject breakdowns, confusion matrices, statistical significance, and analysis of failure cases.

- Pros: Elegant unification of manifold learning and Riemannian statistics with local adaptivity and tangent-space Monte Carlo efficiency.
- Cons: Metric design and hyperparameter selection are insufficiently justified and may limit applicability.
- Cons: Heavy reliance on many geodesic solves raises runtime and scalability concerns.
- Cons: Notation and clarity issues (metric vs covariance, gradient sensitivity to Σ conditioning).

- Questions for authors: Why use diagonal local covariances and Gaussian-kernel weights in Eq.(7); have full local covariances or alternative kernels been tried?
- Questions for authors: Please explicitly separate and define M(x) (data-driven metric) versus Σ (model parameter) and explain their distinct effects on density and normalization.
- Questions for authors: What is per-iteration time and asymptotic complexity in N, D, S, K; how many ODE solver steps per exponential/log map typically?
- Questions for authors: How sensitive are results to Monte Carlo sample size S and can variance-reduction techniques be used?
- Questions for authors: Can σ (and ρ) be learned or adapted during training?

- Minor comments: Fix typos (e.g., “Isomap,” “geodesics,” “covariance,” “complexity”) and ensure consistent notation.
- Minor comments: Clarify Algorithm 1 notation (define dµφ or replace with ∇µφ) and define symbols at first use.

- Overall assessment: The work is theoretically appealing with promising results but needs clearer motivation for the metric, distinction between M(x) and Σ, and stronger computational and sensitivity analyses to establish practical utility.
