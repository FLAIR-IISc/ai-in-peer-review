Summary: The paper proposes a Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND) based on a geodesic manifold traversal distance learned from data, which replaces the standard Mahalanobis distance in the Gaussian distribution. An extension to mixtures of LANDs is presented. Performance is evaluated on a synthetic dataset and on a real-world EEG dataset.

Major comments:
- The idea of using a geodesic distance, akin to Isomap, is very interesting. However, the paper has several clarity issues.
- The definition of the inverse metric tensor in Eq. (7) appears ad hoc. Could the authors provide more intuition for using Gaussian kernel weights in the weighted sum of local covariances? Why is this particular kernel and weighting scheme appropriate for defining the metric, and how should one interpret it geometrically?
- I found the relationship between the local covariance matrix defined in Eq. (7) and the MAP estimate in Eq. (11) confusing. Do these refer to the same quantity, or are they different objects (e.g., a construction of the metric versus a parameter estimate)? Please make this relationship explicit and align the notation so the reader can track the object consistently through the derivations.
- The experimental evaluation is rather limited. My main concern is scalability and computational efficiency. What is the computational complexity of the method, and what are the actual running times compared to standard EM for Gaussian mixtures? A direct comparison (both asymptotic and empirical) would help assess practical applicability.

In my view, the paper makes an interesting theoretical contribution, but its practical utility is unclear without stronger evidence on efficiency and more comprehensive experiments.
