This paper introduces a locally adaptive normal distribution (LAND) that replaces the Euclidean distance in the ordinary normal distribution with a locally adaptive nonlinear distance measure. The authors define a Riemannian metric M(x) that varies smoothly across the data set, allowing for the calculation of geodesic distances along the manifold via the exponential and logarithmic map. The Euler-Lagrange equations are used to estimate these geodesic distances, which are then applied to a normal distribution to compute Mahalanobis distances on points mapped by the logarithm map.

The authors derive estimators for the mean and covariance via maximum likelihood, presenting a steepest descent algorithm for estimating them since the solutions are not available in closed form. Additionally, they propose a Monte-Carlo approximation method for estimating the normalization constant, which is also not available in closed form.

The LAND is applied to synthetic data generated on a manifold and a clustering task, and its performance is compared to that of a standard mixture of Gaussians. The results show that the LAND adapts well to local manifold structure and provides a useful extension of the normal distribution.

The technical quality of the paper is sound, with well-explained manifold techniques and clever Monte-Carlo estimation and steepest descent algorithms. However, the analysis of the applications could be improved, particularly with regards to the choice of the sigma parameter. The explanation of the usefulness of the learned distribution could also be expanded upon.

The novelty of the paper lies in combining previous work on metric estimation, computing geodesic distances, and learning distributions to provide an algorithm for estimating the parameters of the LAND. The potential impact of the paper is significant, as it provides a new tool for modeling data that lies on a manifold. However, further evaluation is needed to fully assess the performance of the LAND, particularly in comparison to other methods when data is generated according to a single normal distribution or GMM.

The clarity of the paper is generally good, but there are some notation points that could be clarified. For example, the overloading of the Sigma notation could be avoided, and the relationship between the kernel matrix and the fitted covariance matrix could be explicitly stated.

Overall, the paper is well-written and provides a useful contribution to the field of machine learning. With some minor revisions to address the points mentioned above, it has the potential to be a valuable addition to the literature.
