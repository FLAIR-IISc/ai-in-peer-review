Summary:
This paper extends the multivariate normal distribution by replacing the Euclidean metric with a locally adaptive, smoothly varying Riemannian metric learned from data. The resulting locally adaptive normal distribution (LAND) generalizes the Gaussian to a manifold-learning setting while remaining parametric (mean and covariance). The authors provide a concise yet complete maximum-likelihood estimation procedure that combines gradient-based optimization with Monte Carlo estimation of the normalization constant. They further extend the model to mixtures of LANDs and derive the corresponding EM algorithm. Experiments on synthetic data and on EEG recordings of human sleep demonstrate that LAND can model complex, nonlinear structures more effectively than standard Gaussian mixture models (GMMs), suggesting broad applicability.

Quality:
- The technical approach is sound. The paper clearly formulates the distribution under a learned metric, explains how the normalization constant depends on the geometry, and proposes a scalable Monte Carlo scheme to approximate it via sampling in the tangent space. The maximum-likelihood algorithm, including the block coordinate updates for mean and covariance (via a factorization), is well motivated and described clearly.
- The extension to mixture models is natural and practical, and the EM formulation follows the standard GMM derivation while accounting for the LAND-specific normalization.
- The experiments, though concise, are compelling. On synthetic data, LAND captures nonlinear structure with fewer components than GMMs. On EEG sleep-stage data, LAND yields better alignment with known categories across subjects, indicating practical utility.

Clarity:
- The manuscript is clearly written and well organized. The exposition of the geometric preliminaries is brief but adequate for the intended audience. The bibliography is appropriate, and the paper is well within the NIPS scope.

Originality:
- The key contribution is adapting the Riemannian normal distribution to a learned manifold metric, enabling a generative model that captures nonlinear structure in RD without an explicit low-dimensional embedding. The maximum-likelihood estimator and the mixture extension make the approach directly comparable to and competitive with standard GMMs.

Significance:
- The approach has wide potential applicability wherever Gaussian assumptions are used in the presence of nonlinear structure. The mixture model in particular provides a practical alternative to GMMs for clustering and density estimation on data that lie near nonlinear manifolds. The results are promising and open a clear avenue for further research.

Pros:
- Conceptually clean generalization of the Gaussian via a learned Riemannian metric.
- Complete and practical maximum-likelihood estimation procedure, including a scalable normalization strategy.
- Mixture extension with EM makes the method usable as a drop-in competitor to GMMs.
- Empirical results are strong and illustrate clear benefits on both synthetic and real data.
- Writing, structure, references, and relevance to NIPS are excellent.

Cons:
- No major weaknesses noted by this reviewer.

Recommendation:
A strong submission. The proposed LAND framework is well motivated, technically solid, practically implementable, and empirically effective. The extension to mixtures makes it particularly useful as a modern alternative to GMMs, and the paper opens an attractive direction for generative modeling on learned manifolds.
