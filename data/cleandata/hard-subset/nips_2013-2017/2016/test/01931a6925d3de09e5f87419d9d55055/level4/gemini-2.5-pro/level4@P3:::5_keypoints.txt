- Paper proposes Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), replacing global Mahalanobis distance with geodesic distance on a learned Riemannian manifold for data on/near nonlinear manifolds.  
- The Riemannian metric is learned non-parametrically from data (Gaussian-kernel–weighted local covariance) to bias geodesics toward high-density regions.  
- LAND is derived as the maximum-entropy distribution on the learned manifold with parameters µ and Σ, and inference uses ML with Monte Carlo integration on tangent space.  
- The authors provide gradient-based block coordinate descent updates for µ and Σ and extend to a mixture model with an EM algorithm.  
- Experiments on synthetic nonlinear data and EEG sleep-stage clustering show improvements over standard GMMs.  
- Strength: novel, significant fusion of manifold learning and Riemannian statistics, enabling manifold-aware extensions of classical models.  
- Strength: theoretically grounded—framed as maximum-entropy on a learned manifold with sound ML derivations.  
- Strength: generally clear exposition with necessary Riemannian background and algorithmic detail.  
- Weakness: metric construction (Equation 7) feels heuristic; need stronger justification for Gaussian-kernel local covariance choice and clear distinction between metric M(x) and model covariance Σ.  
- Weakness: major scalability/computational concerns—numerical ODEs for exp/log maps, per-iteration Logµ(xn) evaluations, and Monte Carlo S exponential maps likely make inference costly; lacks complexity analysis and runtime comparisons to GMM-EM.  
- Weakness: experimental evaluation limited—low-dimensional synthetics and unclear hyperparameter (σ) selection in EEG; reporting “best” σ without principled model selection is unfair.  
- Detailed suggestions: clarify notation for gradients in Algorithm 1, explain choice/use of steepest descent vs. gradient, and summarize initialization and S selection in the main text.  
- Conclusion: conceptually strong and worth acceptance, but authors should address computational feasibility and principled hyperparameter selection in the final version.
