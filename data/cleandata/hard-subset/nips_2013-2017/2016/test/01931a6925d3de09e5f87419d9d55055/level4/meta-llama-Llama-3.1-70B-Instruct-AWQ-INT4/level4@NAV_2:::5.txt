This paper introduces a locally adaptive normal distribution (LAND) that replaces the Euclidean distance in the ordinary normal distribution with a locally adaptive nonlinear distance measure. The authors propose a parametric model over a non-parametric space, constructing a smoothly changing metric that induces a Riemannian manifold. The LAND is a generalization of the normal distribution to the "manifold" setting, where data is assumed to lie near a potentially low-dimensional manifold embedded in R.

The authors develop a maximum likelihood algorithm to infer the distribution parameters, combining gradient descent and Monte Carlo integration. They also extend the LAND to mixture models and provide the corresponding EM algorithm. The performance of the LAND is evaluated on both synthetic data and EEG measurements of human sleep, demonstrating its efficiency in fitting non-trivial probability distributions.

The proposed idea of using a geodesic distance, similar to Isomaps, is very interesting. However, there are a few weak spots in the clarity of the paper. First, the definition of the inverse of the metric tensor in Eq. (7) seems ad hoc. Can authors provide more intuition on the idea of using a Gaussian kernel for the weights in a weighted sum of local covariances? Also, I got lost between the definition of the local covariance matrix in Eq. (7) and later, the MAP estimation of the same quantity in Eq. (11). Do these refer to the same quantity?

The experimental evaluations are rather poor, and my main concern is the scalability and computational efficiency of the method. For example, what is the computational complexity and the running time compared to standard EM? In my opinion, the paper has an interesting theoretical contribution, but might not be very applicable in practice.

Strengths:

* The proposed LAND is a novel and interesting extension of the normal distribution to the "manifold" setting.
* The authors develop a maximum likelihood algorithm to infer the distribution parameters, combining gradient descent and Monte Carlo integration.
* The performance of the LAND is evaluated on both synthetic data and EEG measurements of human sleep, demonstrating its efficiency in fitting non-trivial probability distributions.

Weaknesses:

* The definition of the inverse of the metric tensor in Eq. (7) seems ad hoc and requires more intuition.
* The connection between the definition of the local covariance matrix in Eq. (7) and later, the MAP estimation of the same quantity in Eq. (11) is unclear.
* The experimental evaluations are rather poor and do not provide a comprehensive comparison with other methods.
* The scalability and computational efficiency of the method are not thoroughly discussed, which is a major concern for practical applications.
