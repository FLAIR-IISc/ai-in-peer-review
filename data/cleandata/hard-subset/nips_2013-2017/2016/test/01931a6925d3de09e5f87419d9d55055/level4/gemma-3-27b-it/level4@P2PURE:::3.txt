This paper introduces a method to model the observation space using a hand-crafted Riemannian metric and defines a curved normal distribution based on the resulting Riemannian geometry to better capture the underlying data manifold. The work addresses the important problem of defining a Riemannian metric from high-dimensional, real-valued observations and subsequently defining and estimating a curved Gaussian distribution within this geometry, offering a potentially useful tool and an interesting application of Riemannian geometry to statistical learning. The proposed method begins by defining the underlying Riemannian geometry in Section 3.1. While the title suggests a learned metric, the metric presented in equation 7 is, in fact, hand-crafted and relies on two fixed hyperparameters, rho and sigma. I suspect that sigma is a sensitive parameter and the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of its intuitive effect and guidance on its selection. The absence of this analysis represents a significant omission. Furthermore, it is worth exploring whether the metric learning stage could be integrated with the LAND learning stage, allowing rho and sigma to be learned from the data. A key benefit of a geometric approach is its invariance to coordinate transformations; however, the metric defined in equation 7 lacks this property. For instance, consider data lying on a line in a 2D observation space – the resulting metric would differ depending on whether the coordinate system’s x-axis is aligned with the line or oriented at an angle (e.g., 45 degrees). A more in-depth discussion of this issue, including an examination of the invariance of the full (non-diagonal) metric, and further intuition regarding equation 7, would be valuable. The paper would also benefit from relating its approach to the existing literature in metric learning and manifold learning, particularly work connecting these fields to Riemannian geometry (a search for "Riemannian", "metric learning", and "manifold learning" would be a good starting point). A major criticism concerns the first experiment. The comparison is not particularly informative, as LAND's greater flexibility allows it to represent the data more effectively with fewer components. However, a Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) could achieve comparable performance with a larger number of components, while LAND would likely overfit. I recommend redesigning this experiment to allow each method to select its optimal number of components based on a principled criterion such as BIC, and then comparing performance. LAND is still anticipated to perform better due to its ability to capture the underlying manifold structure, and such a comparison would be more meaningful. Observing LAND’s performance as the number of components increases and overfitting occurs would also be insightful. LAND’s computational cost is likely higher than estimating a Gaussian. Providing quantitative measurements or experiments to assess this computational expense and define the scale at which LAND remains tractable would be beneficial. Could LAND be applied to datasets like MNIST and compared with Gaussian mixture learning? 

====== After reading the authors’ response, it is clear that LAND represents a more flexible model than a Gaussian distribution. Increasing the number of components makes each local LAND model more susceptible to noise than a local Gaussian. While the authors state that "the geodesics will almost be straight lines," they remain curves and are therefore prone to overfitting. I strongly encourage the authors to revise this experiment during the rebuttal phase.
