This paper proposes a novel approach to modeling the observation space using a hand-crafted Riemannian metric and defining a curved normal distribution based on the associated Riemannian geometry, allowing for a more accurate capture of the data manifold. The authors address a fundamental problem of defining a Riemannian metric based on high-dimensional real-valued observations and estimating a curved Gaussian distribution using this geometry, providing a useful tool and an interesting application of Riemannian geometry in statistical learning.

The proposed method defines the underlying Riemannian geometry in Section 3.1. However, despite the title suggesting that the method "learns a metric," the metric is actually hand-crafted in Equation 7 using two hyper-parameters, rho and sigma, which are fixed empirically in advance. I suspect that sigma is a sensitive parameter, and the authors should provide a more detailed discussion on the intuitive effect of varying sigma and how to select this parameter. Furthermore, it would be beneficial to explore the possibility of merging the metric learning stage with the LAND learning stage, allowing rho and sigma to be learned.

One of the strengths of the geometric approach is its invariance to coordinate transformations. However, the metric in Equation 7 does not possess this property. For instance, if the data forms a line in a 2D observation space, the metric would be different in a coordinate system with its x-axis parallel to this line compared to a coordinate system that forms an angle, e.g., 45 degrees, to this line. A more detailed discussion on this topic, including the invariance of the corresponding full metric, would be valuable.

The paper could also benefit from a more comprehensive review of the related literature on metric learning and manifold learning with connections to Riemannian geometry. A simple search for "Riemannian" "metric learning" "manifold learning" on Google reveals a rich body of work that can be related to this research.

As a major criticism, the comparison in the first experiment is not particularly meaningful, as the more flexible LAND model can describe the data better with a smaller number of components. However, GMM can describe the data better with a large number of components, at which point LAND would overfit. To address this, the authors are suggested to redesign the experiment, allowing each compared method to choose its favorable number of components based on a certain criterion (e.g., BIC), and then compare the corresponding performance. It is expected that LAND would still perform better due to its ability to describe the manifold structure, making the comparison more meaningful. It would also be interesting to see how LAND with a large number of components would overfit.

Additionally, the authors should provide quantitative measurements or experiments to demonstrate the computational expense of LAND compared to estimating a Gaussian. It would be valuable to know the limit of the scale that LAND can handle and whether it is possible to learn LAND on MNIST and compare it with Gaussian mixture learning.

After reading the authors' response, it is clear that LAND is a more flexible model than the Gaussian distribution. However, if the number of components is increased, each local LAND would be more vulnerable to local noises compared to a local Gaussian. Although the authors claim that "the geodesics will almost be straight lines," these curves can still lead to overfitting. I strongly suggest that the authors revise this experiment in the revision.
