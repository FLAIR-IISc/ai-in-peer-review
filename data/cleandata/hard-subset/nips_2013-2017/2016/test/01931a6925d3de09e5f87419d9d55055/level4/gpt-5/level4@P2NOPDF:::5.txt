Summary: The paper introduces a Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND) that replaces the standard Mahalanobis distance with a geodesic, manifold-traversal distance learned from data. The approach is extended to mixtures of LANDs. Experiments are presented on a synthetic dataset and an EEG dataset.

Major comments:
- The core idea of using a geodesic distance, reminiscent of Isomap-style shortest-path metrics, is compelling. However, several aspects of the presentation are unclear.
- The definition of the inverse metric tensor in Eq. (7) appears ad hoc. Please provide more intuition for constructing it as a Gaussian-kernel-weighted sum of local covariance matrices. Why choose a Gaussian kernel for the weights, how is the bandwidth selected, and how sensitive are the results to this choice?
- The relationship between the local covariance matrix in Eq. (7) and the MAP estimate in Eq. (11) is confusing. Do these refer to the same parameter at different stages (e.g., an initial local estimate versus a globally regularized MAP estimate), or do they serve different purposes? A clarifying paragraph and consistent notation would help resolve this.
- The experimental evaluation is relatively limited. Stronger empirical evidence would improve the paper: comparisons to standard Gaussians and GMMs (with Mahalanobis metrics), evaluations on additional real datasets, and metrics beyond accuracy (e.g., likelihood, calibration) would better illustrate the benefits and trade-offs.
- My main concern is scalability and computational efficiency. Please detail the computational complexity per E- and M-step, memory requirements, and wall-clock times compared with standard EM for GMMs across dataset sizes and dimensions. How does computing geodesic distances (e.g., graph construction, shortest paths) scale, and can these costs be amortized, approximated, or sparsified? What is the end-to-end runtime in your experiments relative to baseline methods?

Overall assessment: The paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution by integrating manifold-aware geodesic distances into Gaussian modeling and mixtures. However, the current clarity issues and limited experimental analysis—especially regarding efficiency and scalability—make it difficult to assess practical applicability.

Minor comments:
- Algorithm 1, lines 4–5: replace d with ∇.
