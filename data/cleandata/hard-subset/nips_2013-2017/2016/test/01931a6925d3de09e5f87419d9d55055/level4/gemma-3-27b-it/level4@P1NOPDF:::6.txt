In this paper, the authors present a locally adaptive normal distribution. Instead of using Euclidean distance, they define a Riemannian metric M(x) that varies smoothly across the dataset. Given this metric, geodesic distances along the manifold can be calculated using the exponential and logarithmic maps. The authors employ the Euler-Lagrange equations to define a set of ordinary differential equations for estimating these geodesic distances. The logarithmic map projects points from the manifold (y) to the tangent plane (at x) such that the length of the resulting vector equals the geodesic distance from x to y along the manifold. They then apply a normal distribution, computing a Mahalanobis distance on points mapped by the logarithm map. This inner product is used to compute distances from a point x to the mean, rather than using Euclidean distances. The authors subsequently derive estimators for the mean and covariance via maximum likelihood. Because closed-form solutions are unavailable, they present a steepest descent algorithm for estimating these parameters. Finally, since the normalization constant is also intractable in closed form, the authors present a Monte Carlo approximation method. They evaluate this method on both synthetic data generated on a manifold and a clustering task, comparing its performance to a standard mixture of Gaussians. Overall, I believe this paper provides an interesting and useful extension of the normal distribution that adapts to local manifold structure. The authors effectively combine work in metric estimation, geodesic distance computation, and distribution learning. I recommend this work for publication.

Regarding my scores:

**Technical Quality:** The manifold techniques (using Euler-Lagrange equations and estimating logarithm maps) are sound and well explained. The Monte Carlo estimation of the normalization constant and the steepest descent algorithm are clever and well described. However, the analysis of the applications feels somewhat limited – the sigma parameter appears to be selected by optimizing performance on the training data. Furthermore, the explanation of the usefulness of the learned distribution could be strengthened.

**Novelty:** The main contribution of this paper appears to be the combination of existing work – utilizing kernels for the metric M(x), estimating geodesic distances via Euler-Lagrange, and applying a normal distribution – and providing an algorithm for estimating the associated parameters (mean, covariance, and normalization constant).

**Potential Impact:** The authors provide a fair evaluation of LAND and GMM on synthetic data, examining log-likelihood on data generated by the learned distributions. However, it's unclear how the sigma parameter was chosen. The authors demonstrate that LAND performs well on data generated along a manifold, but they do not show how it compares to a GMM when data is generated from a single normal distribution or GMM. In the real-world clustering task, the sigma value for LAND appears to be optimized using the F-measure, and that same F-measure is presented. Ideally, they would have reported the F-measure on a held-out test set to provide a fair assessment and avoid potential overfitting. It would also be beneficial to investigate the method’s performance on very high-dimensional data. While the authors suggest LAND could serve as a generative model for manifold data, they make the somewhat unusual assumption that the data X is observed in D dimensions and the manifold itself is also D-dimensional (i.e., points on the tangent plane are D-dimensional, and the metric M(x) is full rank). Typically, manifold learning focuses on data lying on a d-dimensional manifold embedded in a D-dimensional Euclidean space, where D is significantly larger than d.

**Clarity:** I found one notation point particularly confusing: the overloading of the sigma notation. Specifically, they state M = Sigma<sup>-1</sup> but then estimate it using a (local) diagonal kernel matrix. Is this Sigma also the same as the (fitted) covariance matrix? If so, is the kernel simply an initialization? This seems strange, as M(x) should vary with x, whereas the fitted covariance matrix is a single parameter. In the proof of the steepest descent, they explicitly state “in our case M = Sigma<sup>-1</sup>” and use this substitution. Clarification here would be helpful. In Sec 4.2, it isn’t stated, but I assume cluster membership was chosen by assigning points to the closest cluster mean based on geodesic distance. Overall, the introduction to the manifold concepts was well explained, at least for someone familiar with Riemannian manifolds.
