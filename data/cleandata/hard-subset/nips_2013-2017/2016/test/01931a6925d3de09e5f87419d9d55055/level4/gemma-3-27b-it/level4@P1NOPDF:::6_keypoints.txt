- Paper proposes a Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND) using a smoothly varying Riemannian metric M(x) to measure geodesic distances instead of Euclidean distance.
- Geodesic distances are computed via exponential/logarithm maps and ODEs derived from the Euler–Lagrange equations.
- Points are mapped to tangent planes with the logarithm map and a Mahalanobis distance is applied there to define the normal distribution.
- Mean and covariance estimators are derived by maximum likelihood; a steepest-descent algorithm is proposed because closed-form solutions are unavailable.
- The normalization constant is intractable and is approximated using a Monte Carlo method.
- Evaluation includes synthetic manifold data and a clustering task, compared against a standard Gaussian mixture model (GMM).
- Reviewer recommends publication, calling the extension interesting and useful and praising the combination of metric estimation, geodesic computation, and distribution learning.
- Technical quality is strong: manifold techniques, Euler–Lagrange-based geodesic estimation, Monte Carlo normalization, and steepest-descent are sound and well explained.
- Concerns: application analysis is limited and the sigma parameter appears tuned on training data, risking optimistic results.
- Novelty is mainly in combining existing components (kernel-based M(x), Euler–Lagrange geodesics, normal distribution) and providing parameter-estimation algorithms.
- Potential impact: fair synthetic evaluation, but unclear sigma selection, no comparison when data are drawn from a single normal or GMM, and lack of held-out test F-measure in clustering raises overfitting concerns.
- Scalability/assumptions: authors assume the observed data and manifold are both D-dimensional with full-rank M(x), which is atypical for manifold learning where intrinsic dimension d < D.
- Clarity issues: sigma notation is overloaded and the relationship between kernel Sigma, fitted covariance, and M = Sigma^{-1} is confusing and needs clarification.
- Minor clarity point: cluster membership assignment (likely nearest mean by geodesic distance) is not explicitly stated and should be clarified.
- Suggestion: strengthen explanation of when and why the learned distribution is useful, test on held-out data, compare to GMM on non-manifold data, and evaluate high-dimensional performance.
