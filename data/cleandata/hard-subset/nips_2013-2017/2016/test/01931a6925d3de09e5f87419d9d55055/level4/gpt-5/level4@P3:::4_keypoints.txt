- Introduces LAND: a parametric Riemannian Gaussian using a learned, smoothly varying metric M(x) based on inverse local diagonal covariances estimated with Gaussian kernels.  
- Uses the Riemannian normal distribution pM(x|µ,Σ) and derives ML estimation for µ and Σ with a tangent-space Monte Carlo estimator for the normalization constant C(µ,Σ).  
- Computes gradients for µ (steepest descent on the manifold) and Σ (via factorization Σ−1 = AᵀA) and extends to mixtures with an EM algorithm that accounts for per-component normalization.  
- Empirically, LAND fits nonlinear synthetic data with fewer components than GMMs and outperforms GMMs and Riemannian least-squares on EEG sleep-stage clustering (10 subjects, F-measure).  
- Key difference to prior Riemannian mixture work (e.g., Simo-Serra et al.): the manifold is learned from data and component normalization constants are explicitly estimated.  
- Technical soundness: change-of-variables to integrate over TµM, use of m(µ,v)=√|M(Expµ(v))|, and gradient derivations are coherent and make ML tractable.  
- Practical choice of steepest descent for µ helps mitigate conditioning issues in Σ estimation.  
- Experiments convincingly demonstrate improved modeling of nonlinear structure and practical utility on EEG, but sensitivity to smoothing σ is evident.  
- Limitation: metric uses only diagonal local covariances, limiting modeling of anisotropic local structure.  
- Limitation: σ selection is critical and largely manual per subject, affecting performance.  
- Limitation: computational cost is high due to repeated Exp/Log evaluations and Monte Carlo sampling.  
- Limitation: the learned metric is not invariant under linear transforms.  
- Limitation: no theoretical analysis of estimator consistency or variance of the Monte Carlo normalization estimator.  
- Clarity: paper is well written, but novelty relative to prior Riemannian mixtures should be stated in the introduction rather than later.  
- Clarity: request for a complexity subsection with per-iteration cost in terms of S, N, and Exp/Log costs, plus empirical runtimes versus GMM.  
- Clarity: need more details on hyperparameter selection (σ, ρ), robustness to σ, and ablation on S for C(µ,Σ) estimation variance.  
- Originality: moderate — combines established Riemannian normals with a learned data-driven manifold and an ML tangent-space Monte Carlo estimator.  
- Significance: offers a practical way to give Gaussians locally adaptive geometry, useful for generative modeling and clustering, but broader benchmarks and scalability analysis are needed.  
- Strengths: principled connection between manifold learning and Riemannian statistics, practical ML algorithm, consistent mixture formulation, convincing results, and clear exposition.  
- Weaknesses: underemphasized novelty in intro, sensitivity to σ and diagonal metric choice, limited scalability/runtime analysis, lack of comparisons to alternative flexible densities, and non-invariance not mitigated.  
- Suggestions: move comparison to related Riemannian mixtures into the intro, add computational complexity and runtime comparisons, provide σ selection guidance and ablations on σ and S.  
- Suggestions: discuss diagonal vs full local covariances and, if feasible, include a small experiment with full local covariances; evaluate additional datasets and cross-subject EEG generalization.  
- Arguments for acceptance: technically sound, meaningful integration of learned Riemannian geometry with Gaussian modeling, practical algorithmic contributions, and credible empirical gains.  
- Arguments against acceptance: perceived incremental novelty over prior Riemannian mixtures, sensitivity to σ, scalability concerns, and limited comparisons/ablations.  
- Recommendation: borderline accept pending clearer positioning against prior work and modest additional analysis on σ selection and computational cost.
