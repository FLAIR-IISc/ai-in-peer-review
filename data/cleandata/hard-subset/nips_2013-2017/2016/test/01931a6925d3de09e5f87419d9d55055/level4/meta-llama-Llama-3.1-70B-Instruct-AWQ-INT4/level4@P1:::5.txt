Summary: The paper proposes a locally adaptive normal distribution (LAND) based on a geodesic manifold traversal distance, learned adaptively from the data. The distance replaces the standard Mahalanobis distance used in the Gaussian distribution. The extension to a mixture of LANDs is also provided. The performance is evaluated on a synthetic dataset as well as a real-world dataset of EEG measurements.

Major comments: The proposed idea of using a geodesic distance, similar to Isomap, is very interesting. However, there are a few weak spots in the clarity of the paper. Firstly, the definition of the inverse of the metric tensor in Eq. (7) seems ad hoc. Can the authors provide more intuition on the idea of using a Gaussian kernel for the weights in a weighted sum of local covariances? Additionally, the connection between the definition of the local covariance matrix in Eq. (7) and its later use in the MAP estimation in Eq. (11) is unclear. Do these refer to the same quantity?

Furthermore, the experimental evaluations are rather limited. However, my main concern is the scalability and computational efficiency of the method. For example, what is the computational complexity and the running time compared to standard EM? In my opinion, the paper has an interesting theoretical contribution, but might not be very applicable in practice.

Minor comments: Algorithm 1, lines 4 and 5, should use ∇ instead of d to denote the gradient.

Strengths:

* The paper proposes a novel approach to modeling data using a locally adaptive normal distribution.
* The idea of using a geodesic distance is interesting and has potential applications in various fields.
* The extension to a mixture of LANDs is a useful contribution.

Weaknesses:

* The paper lacks clarity in some sections, particularly in the definition and use of the local covariance matrix.
* The experimental evaluations are limited and do not provide a comprehensive comparison with other methods.
* The scalability and computational efficiency of the method are not well addressed.

Acceptance: Based on the current state of the paper, I would lean towards rejecting it. However, with some revisions to address the clarity issues and provide more comprehensive experimental evaluations, the paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field.
