Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper introduces the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), which replaces the Euclidean distance in a Gaussian density with geodesic distances induced by a smoothly varying Riemannian metric M(x) learned from data. Using the exponential and logarithm maps, distances are computed along the learned manifold, and the density is defined as a Mahalanobis distance in the tangent space at the mean, i.e., pM(x | µ, Σ) ∝ exp(−1/2 ⟨Logµ(x), Σ−1 Logµ(x)⟩). The geodesics are obtained by solving Euler–Lagrange equations (second-order ODEs), making the exponential/log maps computationally accessible. The authors provide maximum-likelihood estimators for the mean and covariance via gradient-based optimization, and approximate the intractable normalization constant via Monte Carlo integration performed in the tangent space, which avoids expensive boundary-value problems. They also extend the model to mixtures and derive an EM algorithm. Experiments on synthetic manifold data and EEG-based sleep-stage clustering show that LAND can better capture non-linear structure than standard Gaussian mixture models (GMMs).

This work connects Riemannian statistics (e.g., Pennec’s Riemannian normal distributions and intrinsic means) with manifold learning via a learned metric (e.g., Hauberg et al.), and differs from prior mixture models on known manifolds (e.g., Simo-Serra et al.) by learning the metric and consistently handling normalization across components.

Strengths:
- Conceptually clean generalization of the Gaussian that adapts to local geometry via a learned Riemannian metric, retaining a simple parametric form (mean and covariance).
- Solid use of Riemannian tools (geodesics, exp/log maps) and a practical Monte Carlo scheme to approximate the normalization constant by integrating in the tangent space.
- Clear gradient/steepest-descent updates for µ and Σ (with a factorization Σ−1 = AᵀA), and a straightforward extension to mixtures with an EM algorithm.
- Empirical results on synthetic data convincingly show improved fit over GMMs for data concentrated near a non-linear manifold, and the method appears promising for EEG sleep-stage clustering.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Model selection and overfitting: The metric bandwidth σ appears to be tuned on the same data used for evaluation. In the EEG experiment, the “best σ” is chosen by optimizing F-measure and then the same F is reported. This risks overfitting. Please (i) select σ via cross-validation or a held-out set, (ii) report test-set performance, and (iii) show sensitivity to σ.
- Baselines: There is no evaluation when the true data-generating process is Euclidean Gaussian (single normal or GMM). This is important to show that LAND does not degrade performance when the manifold structure is absent. Please add these comparisons.
- High-dimensional settings: The method assumes a full-rank metric in the ambient D-dimensional space; experiments do not examine very high-dimensional data. Given the local kernel-based metric, scalability and robustness in high D are a concern. An empirical study on higher-dimensional datasets, or a discussion of complexity and potential mitigations (e.g., dimensionality reduction, probabilistic manifold models, mixtures of metrics), would strengthen the paper.
- Manifold dimensionality assumption: The paper effectively models data on a D-dimensional Riemannian manifold embedded in RD (i.e., the manifold dimension equals ambient dimension). This deviates from the usual manifold-learning setting where d ≪ D. Please clarify the implications: does LAND rely on full-rank local metrics, and how does it behave when data lie near a lower-dimensional submanifold?
- Notation clarity: The use of Σ in two roles is confusing. The learned metric M(x) is defined as the inverse of a local (diagonal) covariance, while Σ denotes the global covariance parameter of the LAND density. In several places (e.g., the steepest descent derivation) it is stated that “M = Σ−1,” which seems to conflate the local metric with the model covariance. Please disambiguate: M(x) varies with x (non-parametric), while Σ is a single parameter of the distribution. Confirm that any substitution of M = Σ−1 is only used in a local context or for a specific derivation and not as a global identification.
- Clustering procedure details: In Sec. 4.2, please specify how the number of clusters K is chosen, how cluster membership is inferred (EM responsibilities vs. nearest-mean under geodesic/Mahalanobis distance), and whether any labels or class priors influenced initialization. Also clarify whether the manifold/geodesic computations used both electrodes or only the frontal channel (the features are derived from the frontal EEG).
- Empirical analysis: The synthetic evaluation is informative (AIC/BIC suggest fewer LAND components), but it would help to provide (i) computational costs (time for exp/log maps, Monte Carlo S, scaling with N and D), (ii) ablations (e.g., effect of diagonal vs. full local covariances, different S), and (iii) a fairness check where σ is fixed across methods or selected by the same criterion.

Quality:
- The geometric foundations (geodesics via ODEs, exp/log maps) are sound and well presented. The Monte Carlo normalization via tangent-space integration and the optimization scheme (including steepest descent for stability) are technically solid. However, the experimental methodology needs stronger controls for hyperparameter selection and generalization.

Clarity:
- Generally well written and organized, with a good introduction to the Riemannian concepts. The main clarity issue is the overloading of Σ and the statement “M = Σ−1,” which should be corrected. The clustering protocol in Sec. 4.2 should also be described more precisely.

Originality:
- The core components (learned Riemannian metric, geodesic distances, Riemannian normal distribution) are known individually. The contribution is a thoughtful integration: learning a data-adaptive metric, fitting a maximum-entropy Riemannian normal with a practical normalization scheme, and extending to mixtures with a consistent EM. This is a non-trivial and useful synthesis.

Significance:
- LAND is a compelling drop-in replacement for Gaussians in generative models when data reside near non-linear structures. If the evaluation is strengthened (proper hyperparameter selection, Euclidean baselines, higher-D settings), this could be a widely useful tool.

Pros:
- Elegant formulation; principled use of Riemannian geometry.
- Practical algorithms for normalization and parameter estimation.
- Strong results on synthetic manifold data; promising EEG clustering.

Cons:
- Hyperparameter selection appears to use the evaluation metric; no held-out tests reported.
- Missing baselines on data from true Gaussian/GMM.
- Limited exploration of high-dimensional regimes.
- Notational ambiguity between M(x) and Σ.

Overall recommendation:
I find the idea compelling and the technical development sound. With clearer notation and stronger experimental methodology (held-out evaluation for σ, additional baselines, and higher-D tests), I believe this paper is worthy of publication.
