This paper extends the common multivariate normal density by replacing the Euclidean metric with a locally adaptive Riemannian metric. The result is a generalization of the normal distribution to the manifold learning setting, with potentially wide applicability as illustrated by the reported experiments. Importantly, the paper provides a concise yet complete account of a maximum likelihood parameter estimation procedure. The extension to a mixture of LANDs makes it particularly practical as a competitor to the now-standard Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM). The experiments are compelling and open a promising avenue for future research. The language, structure, bibliography, and relevance to the NIPS remit are all excellent.

The paper begins by motivating the need for a distribution that can adapt to nonlinear data manifolds, contrasting the limitations of the standard normal distribution with the strengths of approaches like Isomap. It then introduces the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), bridging work on Riemannian statistics with manifold learning. The authors develop a maximum likelihood algorithm relying on gradient descent and Monte Carlo integration, extending it further to mixture models with a corresponding Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. Finally, they demonstrate the efficacy of LANDs on both synthetic data and EEG measurements of human sleep stages.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The LAND represents a novel approach to density estimation by combining Riemannian geometry with manifold learning. The idea of adapting the metric locally based on data density is insightful.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The work is grounded in solid mathematical principles, particularly Riemannian geometry, providing a strong theoretical basis for the proposed distribution.
*   **Algorithmic Contribution:** The development of a maximum likelihood estimation procedure, combined with efficient Monte Carlo integration, is a significant contribution.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on both synthetic and real-world data (EEG measurements) demonstrate the potential of the LAND in practical applications. The comparison with GMM and least squares estimators is well-executed.
*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making the complex concepts accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Cost:** While the authors address scalability with Monte Carlo integration, the computational cost of evaluating geodesics (solving ODEs) and the logarithm map remains a potential bottleneck, especially in high dimensions.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The choice of the smoothing parameter σ for defining the local covariance matrix is crucial and might require careful tuning. The paper mentions investigating this in the supplemental material, but more discussion within the main text would be beneficial.
*   **Metric Invariance:** The authors acknowledge that the proposed metric is not invariant to linear transformations, which could be a limitation in certain applications.
*   **Limited Theoretical Exploration:** While the paper establishes the maximum entropy property, further exploration of theoretical properties and limitations of the LAND could strengthen the contribution.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to density estimation.
*   The proposed algorithm is well-defined and empirically validated.
*   The experimental results are promising and demonstrate the potential of the LAND.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The computational cost could limit the scalability of the approach.
*   The sensitivity to the smoothing parameter requires careful consideration.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field. The proposed LAND offers a compelling alternative to traditional density estimation methods, particularly in scenarios where data lies on nonlinear manifolds. While the computational cost and parameter sensitivity are concerns, the authors have addressed these limitations to a reasonable extent. The clear presentation, solid theoretical foundation, and promising experimental results make this paper a valuable addition to the NIPS conference. I recommend acceptance.
