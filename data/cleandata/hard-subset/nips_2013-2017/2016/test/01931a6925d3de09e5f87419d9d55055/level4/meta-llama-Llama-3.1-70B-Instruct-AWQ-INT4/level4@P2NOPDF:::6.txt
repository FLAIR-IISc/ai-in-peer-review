This paper presents a locally adaptive normal distribution, which extends the traditional normal distribution by incorporating a Riemannian metric that varies smoothly across the data set. Instead of using Euclidean distance, the authors define a metric M(x) to calculate geodesic distances along the manifold. They utilize the Euler-Lagrange equations to derive a set of ordinary differential equations for estimating these geodesic distances. The logarithmic map is employed to map points from the manifold to the tangent plane, allowing for the computation of Mahalanobis distances. The authors then apply a normal distribution to these mapped points, using the inner product to calculate distances from a point to the mean. Estimators for the mean and covariance are derived via maximum likelihood, and a steepest descent algorithm is presented for estimating these parameters. Additionally, a Monte-Carlo approximation method is proposed to estimate the normalization constant, which is not available in closed form.

The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method on synthetic data generated on a manifold and in a clustering task, comparing it to a standard mixture of Gaussians. Overall, this paper provides an interesting and useful extension of the normal distribution that adapts to local manifold structure. The authors successfully combine techniques from metric estimation, geodesic distance computation, and distribution learning.

Technical quality: 7

The manifold techniques, including the use of Euler-Lagrange equations and estimation of logarithmic maps, are sound and well-explained. The Monte-Carlo estimation of the normalization constant and the steepest descent algorithm are clever and well-described. However, the analysis of the applications is somewhat lacking, and the explanation of the usefulness of the learned distribution could be improved. Specifically, it appears that the sigma parameter is chosen by optimizing on the training data, which could be problematic.

Novelty: 6

The main contribution of this paper appears to be the combination of previous work on using kernels for a metric M(x), estimating geodesic distances via Euler-Lagrange, and applying a normal distribution. While the authors provide an algorithm for estimating the parameters, the individual components are not particularly novel. However, the combination of these techniques is a valuable contribution to the field.

Potential Impact: 7

The authors provide a fair evaluation of their method, comparing it to a standard mixture of Gaussians on synthetic data. However, it is not clear how the sigma parameter was chosen, and the evaluation could be improved by including a comparison to a single normal distribution or a Gaussian mixture model. Additionally, the real-life clustering task evaluation could be improved by providing the F-measure on a held-out test set to avoid overfitting. It would also be beneficial to examine the performance of the method on very high-dimensional data.

One potentially useful application of the proposed method is as a generative model for manifold data. However, the authors make the assumption that the data X is observed in D dimensions and the manifold dimension is also D, which is not typically the case in manifold learning. Usually, data lies on a d-dimensional manifold embedded in a D-dimensional Euclidean space, where D is much larger than d.

Clarity: 6

The paper is generally well-written, but there are some notation issues that could be clarified. Specifically, the overloading of the Sigma notation is confusing, and it is not clear if the estimated M(x) is the same as the fitted covariance matrix. Additionally, the assumption that cluster membership is chosen by picking 2 clusters and computing the closest geodesic distance to the cluster means could be explicitly stated. Overall, the introduction to manifold concepts is well-explained, but some notation and assumptions could be clarified for improved readability.
