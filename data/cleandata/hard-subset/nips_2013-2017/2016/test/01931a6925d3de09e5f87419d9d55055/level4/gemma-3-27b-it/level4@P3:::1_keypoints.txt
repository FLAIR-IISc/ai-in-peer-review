- Proposes Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND) that replaces Euclidean metric with a learned local Riemannian metric to model data near manifolds, with MLE and mixture extensions demonstrated on synthetic and EEG sleep data.

- Builds on Pennec’s Riemannian normal and adapts it to manifold learning, offering a principled, mathematically grounded generative framework linking metric learning and Riemannian geometry.

- Major scalability concern: computing local covariance matrices and their inverses may be computationally prohibitive in high dimensions; paper lacks detailed complexity analysis and clear definition of “high-dimensional.”

- Related-work gap: discussion of extensive metric-learning literature is limited; many local metric methods (e.g., local PCA / Kambhatla, other local metric learning papers) are omitted.

- Gaussian kernel parameter σ is under-analyzed: selection described for sleep data but not synthetic data; robustness/sensitivity tests (low vs high-density regions, fixed vs adaptive σ) are needed.

- Experimental presentation issues: intrinsic estimator visualizations are missing in Figures 5–6; current comparisons focus on GMMs, which perform poorly and offer limited insight.

- Recommend adding complexity comparisons between LAND and intrinsic estimators to guide method choice based on data characteristics.

- Mixture-of-LANDs extension is promising but needs experiments on varying component counts and robustness to initialization.

- Paper is very well written and presents a clear, compelling argument for the approach.

- Arguments for acceptance: novel combination of Riemannian statistics and manifold learning, principled adaptive density estimation, promising synthetic and real-world results, clear presentation, and potential broader impact.

- Arguments against acceptance: unresolved scalability concerns, incomplete related-work coverage, insufficient σ analysis, and experimental presentation shortcomings.

- Recommendation: Weak Accept pending minor revisions to address scalability, related work, parameter analysis, and experimental visualization.
