The paper proposes a novel locally adaptive normal distribution (LAND) that bridges concepts from metric learning, manifold learning, and Riemannian statistics. The authors define a parametric metric over a non-parametric space, using a Riemannian normal distribution as in Pennec [15]. A maximum likelihood algorithm is proposed to estimate the mean and covariance of the Riemannian normal distribution, utilizing a Mahalanobis distance for Riemannian manifolds. Distances between points are defined as geodesics along the Riemannian manifold, relying on learning a local metric. The authors propose learning the metric using the inverses of local (diagonal) covariances. Mixtures of LANDs are also considered.

The paper provides new insights into the connection between metric learning and Riemannian statistics, and having parametric and generative models that can go beyond the training data is useful in many scenarios. However, the applicability of LAND to real-world data, which is often large-scale and high-dimensional, is a concern. The computation of a (diagonal) covariance matrix for each data point and its inverse can be challenging in high dimensions. Although the authors mention high-dimensionality as a potential issue, a more in-depth analysis of the complexity and limitations of the metric introduced is necessary.

The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of its position within the vast literature on metric learning. Hauberg et al. [9] discuss some of the relevant literature, including Frome et al. [1,2] and Malisiewicz and Efros [6], who learn a diagonal metric tensor for every point similar to LAND. Local covariances have also been used in methods like local PCA.

The choice of the parameter sigma in the Gaussian kernel used for defining locality is not thoroughly discussed, except for the sleep data in Sect. 4.2. Robustness tests for sigma would be beneficial, and it is unclear whether sigma is the same for all points, regardless of whether they are in low or high-density regions.

The comparison of LAND to intrinsic estimators in Fig. 4 shows similar results, but the authors do not provide visual results with these intrinsic estimators in Figs. 5 and 6. Instead, they show visualization results against GMM, which are already shown to perform poorly in Fig. 4. Some visual results using the intrinsic model are provided in the supplementary material (Fig. 2 and 4), and they outperform GMM. Therefore, these results should be included in the initial paper or made room for both.

An analysis of the computational complexity of LAND vs. intrinsic estimators would be valuable to determine which method is more suitable for different data structures. The consideration of mixtures of LANDs is one of the most interesting applications, as data is rarely generated by one component. This aspect would be worth expanding.

Strengths:

* The paper proposes a novel and interesting approach to defining a locally adaptive normal distribution.
* The connection between metric learning and Riemannian statistics is well-explored.
* The authors provide a clear and well-written explanation of their method.

Weaknesses:

* The applicability of LAND to real-world data, particularly in high-dimensional settings, is a concern.
* The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of its position within the literature on metric learning.
* The choice of the parameter sigma is not thoroughly discussed, and robustness tests would be beneficial.
* The comparison to intrinsic estimators could be more comprehensive.

Originality:

* The paper proposes a novel approach to defining a locally adaptive normal distribution.
* The connection between metric learning and Riemannian statistics is well-explored.

Significance:

* The paper provides new insights into the connection between metric learning and Riemannian statistics.
* The proposed method could be useful in various applications, particularly in scenarios where data is non-linearly structured.

Quality:

* The paper is well-written and clear.
* The explanation of the method is thorough and easy to follow.

Clarity:

* The paper is well-organized and easy to follow.
* The figures and tables are clear and helpful.

Overall, the paper is well-written and proposes an interesting approach to defining a locally adaptive normal distribution. However, it would benefit from a more thorough discussion of its position within the literature on metric learning and a more comprehensive analysis of its limitations and potential applications.
