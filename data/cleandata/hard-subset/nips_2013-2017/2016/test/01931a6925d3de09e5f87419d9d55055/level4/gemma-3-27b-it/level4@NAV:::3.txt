This paper introduces a method for modeling the observation space using a hand-crafted Riemannian metric and defines a corresponding curved normal distribution to better capture the underlying data manifold. The work addresses the important problem of defining a Riemannian metric from high-dimensional observations and subsequently leveraging this geometry for curved Gaussian estimation, offering a potentially valuable tool and an interesting application of Riemannian geometry to statistical learning. The definition of the Riemannian geometry is presented in Section 3.1.

While the title suggests a learned metric, the metric is, in fact, hand-crafted (Equation 7) and depends on two fixed hyperparameters, ρ and σ. The sensitivity of the method to σ warrants further investigation; a more detailed discussion of the parameter’s intuitive effect and selection process is needed for completeness. Exploring the possibility of integrating metric learning into the LAND learning process to allow for optimization of ρ and σ would be beneficial. 

A key benefit of a geometric approach is its invariance to coordinate transformations. However, the proposed metric (Equation 7) lacks this property; differing coordinate systems representing the same data (e.g., a line in 2D) would yield distinct metrics. A more thorough discussion of this limitation, including an analysis of the invariance of the full metric and further intuition regarding Equation 7, is required.  The paper would benefit from relating its work to the existing literature in Riemannian metric learning and manifold learning.

The initial experimental comparison is not entirely convincing. LAND’s greater flexibility inherently leads to better performance with fewer components, but GMM could achieve comparable results with a larger number, potentially overfitting.  A more robust experimental design is needed, allowing each method to select its optimal number of components based on a principled criterion (e.g., BIC) before performance comparison.  Observing LAND's overfitting behavior with a larger component count would also be insightful. 

Finally, the computational cost of LAND compared to standard Gaussian estimation should be quantified.  Exploring the scalability limits of the method and potentially demonstrating its applicability to larger datasets, such as MNIST, in comparison with Gaussian mixture learning would be valuable.

==== 
Following response to review: LAND’s increased flexibility compared to a Gaussian distribution means that increasing the number of components makes it more susceptible to noise than a comparable Gaussian model. While the statement that geodesics approach straight lines is correct, they are still curves and can still lead to overfitting. I strongly recommend the authors revise this experiment accordingly.
