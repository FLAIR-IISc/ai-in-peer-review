### Review

**Summary**

This paper proposes a novel probability distribution, the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), which is defined using a geodesic distance on a data manifold. This learned, data-adaptive distance replaces the standard Mahalanobis distance typically used in the Gaussian distribution. The authors also present a natural extension to a mixture model, the mixture of LANDs. The proposed method's performance is evaluated on a synthetic dataset and a real-world EEG dataset.

**Major Comments**

The central contribution of this paper—defining a probability distribution based on a learned geodesic distance, analogous to the principles used in methods like Isomap—is novel and theoretically interesting. However, the manuscript in its current form has several significant weaknesses that need to be addressed.

1.  **Clarity of Model Formulation:** The paper's clarity is a primary concern, particularly regarding the model's mathematical foundations.
    *   The definition of the inverse metric tensor in Equation (7) appears ad hoc and lacks sufficient justification. The authors should provide a more thorough intuition for constructing this tensor as a weighted sum of local covariance matrices. Specifically, what is the theoretical motivation for using a Gaussian kernel to determine the weights?
    *   The relationship between the definition of the local covariance matrix in Equation (7) and its subsequent Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) estimation in Equation (11) is unclear. The authors should explicitly state whether these two equations refer to the same quantity. If they do, the transition from the initial definition to the MAP estimation framework needs to be explained more transparently.

2.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation is not comprehensive enough to convincingly demonstrate the practical advantages of the proposed method. The experiments are limited to one synthetic dataset and one real-world application, which is insufficient to establish the generalizability and robustness of the LAND model compared to well-established alternatives. A more extensive comparison on a wider range of benchmark datasets is needed.

3.  **Scalability and Computational Efficiency:** My most significant concern is the computational efficiency and scalability of the proposed method, which are critical for practical applications. The paper provides no analysis of the algorithm's computational complexity. To properly assess the method's viability, the authors should include:
    *   A formal analysis of the computational complexity with respect to the number of data points and dimensionality.
    *   An empirical comparison of the running time against standard baselines, such as the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for Gaussian Mixture Models.

In summary, while the paper introduces an elegant theoretical concept, its practical applicability remains questionable without a more thorough treatment of its computational costs and a more convincing empirical evaluation. The paper presents an interesting idea but may be of limited practical use in its current state.

**Minor Comments**

*   In Algorithm 1, lines 4 and 5, the notation `d` appears to be a typo and should likely be replaced with `\nabla` to denote the gradient.
