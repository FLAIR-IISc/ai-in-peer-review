- The paper proposes LAND: a Riemannian-based “curved” normal for density estimation, aiming to capture manifold structure in high-dimensional data.  
- The claimed “learned” metric is actually hand-crafted (Eq.7) and depends on global hyperparameters ρ and σ fixed empirically, contradicting the framing.  
- No sensitivity analysis or principled method is provided for selecting σ, which strongly controls local curvature and affects reproducibility.  
- The authors do not attempt to jointly learn metric hyperparameters (ρ, σ) with LAND parameters (e.g., within EM), which would be a more genuine metric-learning approach.  
- The proposed diagonal metric is not coordinate-invariant (depends on coordinate system rotation), undermining the geometric motivation; the paper lacks discussion or exploration of full (non-diagonal) metrics.  
- Experimental comparison to GMMs is unfair: showing fewer components suffice for LAND is expected given its greater local flexibility and complexity.  
- A fair evaluation should use model selection (BIC or cross-validation) to choose component counts for both LAND and GMM, compare held-out log-likelihoods, and study overfitting vs. number of components.  
- No quantitative analysis of computational cost or scalability is given; complexity relative to GMMs and practical limits by dimensionality / dataset size are unaddressed.  
- The paper should test on larger standard benchmarks (e.g., MNIST) to demonstrate applicability and compare runtime against well-tuned baselines.  
- Related work coverage is insufficient: connections to metric learning, manifold learning, and information geometry are inadequately discussed.  
- Overall, the metric claims are overstated, key properties and invariances are unexplored, experimental methodology is flawed, and computational concerns are missing—major revisions are needed.
