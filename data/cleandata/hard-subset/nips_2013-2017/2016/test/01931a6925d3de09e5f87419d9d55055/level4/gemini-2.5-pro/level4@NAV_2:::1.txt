This paper introduces the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), a parametric generative model built upon a non-parametric, data-driven space. The core idea is to generalize the multivariate normal distribution by replacing the standard Euclidean metric with a smoothly varying Riemannian metric that adapts to the local data density. This work effectively bridges the fields of manifold learning and Riemannian statistics. The proposed distribution is an instance of the Riemannian normal distribution [15], with distances defined by geodesics on a learned manifold, similar to Hauberg et al. [9]. The manifold's metric is constructed non-parametrically, where the metric tensor at any point is the inverse of a local, diagonal covariance matrix. The authors derive a maximum likelihood estimation procedure for the model's parameters (mean and covariance) via gradient descent and Monte Carlo integration, and extend the framework to mixture models using an EM algorithm.

The primary strengths of this work lie in its novel synthesis of manifold learning and Riemannian statistics, its principled formulation as a maximum entropy distribution on the learned manifold, and its ability to function as a generative model that can generalize beyond the training data. The extension to mixture models is a valuable and practical contribution for handling multi-modal data. Furthermore, the paper is exceptionally well-written and clear, making complex concepts from differential geometry accessible.

Despite these strengths, there are several areas for improvement. A major concern is the scalability and practical applicability of the proposed method, particularly for high-dimensional data. The metric construction requires computing a local covariance matrix and its inverse for each point, and the paper lacks an analysis of the computational complexity and the practical limitations on data dimensionality. The paper would be significantly strengthened by such an analysis.

The positioning of this work within the broader metric learning literature could also be improved. Several methods learn point-wise diagonal metric tensors, and techniques like Local PCA have long used local covariance information. A more thorough comparison would help readers understand the specific contributions of LAND over these related approaches.

Regarding the model itself, the influence of the kernel bandwidth parameter, σ, is not sufficiently explored. Its selection is only discussed for the EEG dataset, and a sensitivity analysis showing how σ affects the learned manifold on the synthetic datasets would be highly beneficial. It is also unclear if a single, global σ is optimal for datasets with varying density.

Finally, the experimental comparisons could be more insightful. The quantitative results show that the intrinsic least squares (LS) estimators are a strong baseline, yet the qualitative visualizations only compare LAND to GMM, a model already established as a weaker baseline. Including visualizations for the LS model (currently in the supplement) in the main paper would provide a more compelling and fair comparison. A direct analysis of the computational complexity of LAND versus the intrinsic LS estimators would also be valuable to help practitioners understand the trade-offs between the two approaches. The mixture model application is one of the most interesting aspects of the paper, and this contribution could be expanded upon.
