

This paper introduces a locally adaptive normal distribution (LAND) that leverages Riemannian geometry to model data on manifolds. The authors define a smooth, position-dependent metric tensor $M(x)$, replacing Euclidean distance with geodesic distance computed via the exponential and logarithmic maps. Geodesic distances are estimated by solving Euler-Lagrange equations derived for geodesics. The LAND then computes a Mahalanobis distance in the tangent space (via the logarithmic map), enabling a locally adaptive normal distribution. Maximum likelihood estimation of the mean and covariance is performed using a steepest descent algorithm, while a Monte Carlo approximation handles the intractable normalization constant. The method is evaluated on synthetic manifold data and a clustering task, comparing against Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs).

**Strengths**  
The technical contributions are sound and well-motivated. The integration of metric learning, geodesic computation, and distribution estimation is coherent. The Euler-Lagrange derivation for geodesics, the steepest descent algorithm, and the Monte Carlo normalization approximation are clearly described and technically rigorous. The synthetic data experiments demonstrate LAND’s ability to model manifold-structured data effectively, and the clustering results show competitive performance against GMMs. The exposition of Riemannian concepts (e.g., logarithmic maps, geodesics) is accessible for readers familiar with manifold learning.

**Key Weaknesses**  
1. **Clarity on Notation and Assumptions**: The paper ambiguously overloads $\Sigma$. It states $M = \Sigma^{-1}$ but later estimates $M(x)$ via a *local* diagonal kernel matrix. This implies $\Sigma$ (the covariance) and $M(x)$ are distinct, yet the substitution $M = \Sigma^{-1}$ in the steepest descent proof suggests otherwise. The reader must infer whether the kernel is an initialization for $M(x)$ (which varies with $x$) or a direct estimator for $\Sigma$ (a global parameter). Clarification is essential: *Is the kernel used to initialize $M(x)$, and if so, how does it relate to the fitted covariance?* Additionally, the manifold assumption (data lies on a $D$-dimensional manifold embedded in $\mathbb{R}^D$) is atypical; standard manifold learning assumes $d \ll D$, where $d$ is the intrinsic dimension. The paper should address this limitation and discuss applicability to lower-dimensional embeddings.

2. **Evaluation Rigor**: The selection of the $\sigma$ parameter (governing the metric’s local scale) lacks justification. In synthetic experiments, $\sigma$ appears tuned to maximize training log-likelihood, risking overfitting. For clustering, $\sigma$ is optimized via F-measure on the *training set*, and the same F-measure is reported without a held-out test set. This obscures whether LAND overfits to the training data. The authors should: (a) report F-measures on a separate test set, (b) compare LAND to GMM on data *not* generated from a manifold (e.g., standard Gaussians), and (c) explore high-dimensional settings where manifold structure is typically relevant.

3. **Impact Justification**: The paper claims LAND is useful as a generative model for manifold data but fails to demonstrate *why* it is necessary over GMMs when data is not manifold-structured. The synthetic evaluation only tests data *generated on a manifold*; it does not show LAND’s advantage over GMMs for *non-manifold data* (e.g., standard Gaussians), which would validate its core contribution. The clustering results are promising but insufficient without a fair comparison (e.g., test-set F-measures) and analysis of performance degradation when the manifold assumption is violated.

**Recommendation**  
The core idea—adapting normal distributions to local geometry—is novel and well-executed technically. However, the evaluation lacks rigor, and critical ambiguities in notation and assumptions weaken the impact. With minor revisions addressing the above points, this work would be a strong contribution. Specifically:  
- Clarify the relationship between $M(x)$, $\Sigma$, and the kernel-based initialization.  
- Report results on held-out test sets for clustering.  
- Compare LAND to GMM on non-manifold data (e.g., standard Gaussians) to justify the manifold assumption.  
- Discuss the method’s scalability to $d \ll D$ settings.  

The paper is technically sound and addresses an important gap in manifold-based modeling, but the current presentation risks overclaiming its generality. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen its contribution and ensure the method’s utility is clearly demonstrated. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**.
