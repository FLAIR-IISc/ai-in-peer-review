

Summary: This paper introduces a locally adaptive normal distribution (LAND) that replaces the Euclidean metric in the standard normal distribution with a locally adaptive Riemannian metric, resulting in a geodesic-based distance measure. The approach generalizes the normal distribution to a manifold setting where data is assumed to lie near a low-dimensional manifold embedded in R^D. The authors develop a maximum likelihood estimation algorithm using gradient descent and Monte Carlo integration, extend the model to mixture distributions with a corresponding EM algorithm, and demonstrate its utility on synthetic data and EEG sleep stage measurements.

Major comments: The concept of utilizing a geodesic distance, similar in spirit to Isomap, represents an interesting theoretical contribution. However, the paper suffers from several clarity issues that need addressing. First, the definition of the inverse metric tensor in Equation (7) appears somewhat ad hoc without sufficient intuition regarding why a Gaussian kernel is used for weighting local covariances in the metric construction. Second, there is significant confusion between the local covariance matrix defined in Equation (7) and the parameter estimation process in Equation (11); it remains unclear whether these represent the same quantity or if there's a disconnect in the presentation. Third, the experimental evaluation is notably limited, with insufficient comparisons to relevant baselines and inadequate demonstration of the method's practical advantages. The most significant concern is the lack of comprehensive analysis of computational scalability and efficiency; the paper does not provide a detailed computational complexity analysis or comparative running times with standard EM algorithms, which is essential for assessing the method's practical applicability despite its theoretical appeal.

Minor comments: Algorithm 1, lines 4 and 5: The notation "d" should be replaced with "\nabla" for consistency with standard gradient notation.
