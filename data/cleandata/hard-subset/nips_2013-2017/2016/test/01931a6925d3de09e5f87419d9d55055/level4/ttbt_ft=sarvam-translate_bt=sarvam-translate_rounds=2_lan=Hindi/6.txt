In this paper, the authors present a locally adapted normal distribution. Instead of measuring distance via Euclidean distances, they define a Riemannian metric M(x) that smoothly varies across the data set. According to the given R. metric M(x), geometric distances with manifolds can be computed using exponential and logarithmic maps. The authors use Euler-Lagrange equations, which define a set of simple difference equations for estimating geometric distances. The logarithmic map moves points from the manifold (on the tangent plane) to the tangent plane (on x) in such a way that the resulting vector has geometric distances from x to y along the manifold. The authors then apply a normal distribution to the points mapped by the logarithmic map, which calculates the Mahalanobis distance. This inner product is used to calculate the distance from a point x to the mean instead of Euclidean distances. The authors then obtain estimates for the mean and covariance through maximum likelihood. Since the solutions are not available in closed form, they present a steepest descent algorithm to estimate them. Finally, since the normalization constant is also not available in closed form, the authors present a Monte-Carlo approximation method. They apply this method to the synthetic data generated on the manifold, as well as in the clustering task. They compare with the standard Gaussian mixture. Overall, I think this paper provides an interesting and useful extension of the normal distribution that is adapted to the local manifold structure. The authors combine work in metric estimation, geometric distance computation, and distribution learning. I believe this work is worthy of publication. Here are some of my comments on my scores for the above: Technical Quality Comments: The manifold techniques (using Euler-Lagrange equations, estimating logarithmic maps) are good and well explained. The Monte-Carlo estimate of the normalization constant and the steepest descent algorithm is clever and well described. The analysis of applications is missing (is the sigma parameter chosen by optimizing over the training data?). The explanation of the utility of the learned distribution is also somewhat missing. Novelty Comments: It seems that the main contribution of this paper is to add previous work (using a kernel for a metric M(x), estimating geometric distances via Euler-Lagrange, applying a normal distribution) and provide an algorithm for estimating parameters (mean, covariance, normalization constant). Potential Impact Comments: The authors provide a reasonable assessment of GMM and LAND in synthetic data (given the log-probability generated by the learned distributions) but it is not clear how they chose the sigma parameter. The authors show that LAND fits well to the data generated on a manifold but do not show how well it compares to GMM when the data is generated according to the normal distribution or GMM. In real-life (clustering) data, they seem to choose the sigma value for LAND by optimizing the F measure and then present the same F measure. What they should have done (or if they had clarified it further) was to provide an F measure on a hold-out test set to be unbiased. Therefore, it is difficult to see if there is a problem with over-fitting. It would also be good to check very high-dimensional data. A potentially useful application that the authors present is that LAND can be used as a generative model for manifold data, however, they make an unusual manifold assumption that the data X is viewed in D dimensions and the manifold dimension is also D (i.e., the points on the tangent plane are D-dimensional and the metric M(x) is full rank). In manifold learning, we are usually interested in the data that is located on a d-dimensional manifold embedded in a D-dimensional Euclidean space where D is much larger than d. Clarity Comments: There is a particular notation point that I found confusing: the overloading of the sigma notation. Specifically, they say M = sigma^-1 but then estimate it through a (local) diagonal kernel matrix. Is this also the same as the sigma (fitted) covariance matrix? If so, is the kernel just an initialization? This would be strange because M(x) should be replaced by x but the fitted covariance matrix is a single parameter. In the proof of the steepest descent, they explicitly state that "in our case M = sigma^-1" and use that substitution. Clarification would be good here. In section 4.2, they do not specify but I assume that cluster membership was chosen by selecting 2 groups and calculating the nearest (geometric?) distance to the cluster mean. Overall, I thought the manifold introduction was well explained (at least for someone familiar with Riemannian manifolds).
