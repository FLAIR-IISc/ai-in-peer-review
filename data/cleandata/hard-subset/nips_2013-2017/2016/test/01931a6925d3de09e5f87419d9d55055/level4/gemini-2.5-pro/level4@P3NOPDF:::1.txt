This paper introduces LAND, a novel parametric metric defined over a non-parametric space. The core contribution is the elegant connection it establishes between metric learning, manifold learning, and Riemannian statistics. The proposed metric takes the form of a Riemannian normal distribution, as defined by Pennec [15], where the local metric at each point is learned from the inverse of a local covariance matrix. Distances are then computed as geodesics on the resulting manifold. The authors also extend this framework to mixtures of LANDs to model more complex, multi-modal data distributions.

**Strengths:**

*   The primary strength of this work is its novel synthesis of ideas from metric learning and Riemannian statistics, offering a new perspective on learning distances on manifolds.
*   The development of a parametric and generative model is a significant advantage, as it allows the metric to generalize beyond the training data—a valuable property for many applications.
*   The extension to mixtures of LANDs is particularly promising, as real-world data is often multi-modal.
*   The paper is exceptionally well-written, clear, and a pleasure to read, making complex concepts accessible.

**Major Concerns:**

1.  **Scalability and Complexity:** My primary concern revolves around the practical applicability and scalability of LAND to large-scale, high-dimensional datasets. The method requires computing and inverting a local covariance matrix for *each* data point. This is a computationally intensive operation, and its feasibility in high dimensions is questionable, especially as covariance estimation itself becomes notoriously difficult. While the authors briefly acknowledge high dimensionality as a potential issue (lines 265-266), the paper would be substantially strengthened by a formal analysis of the computational complexity and a discussion of the method's limitations. For instance, what is the practical upper bound on dimensionality where this approach remains viable? Is it 10, 50, or 100+ dimensions?

2.  **Context and Experimental Comparisons:** The paper could better position itself within the extensive metric learning literature. While some related work is cited (e.g., [9]), a more thorough comparison to other local metric learning approaches would be beneficial. For example, methods like those by Frome et al. [1,2] also learn a diagonal metric tensor per point, and techniques like local PCA have long used local covariance information. A clearer delineation of LAND's unique contributions against this backdrop would be valuable for readers.

    This concern extends to the experimental evaluation. In Figure 4, LAND's quantitative performance is shown to be comparable to that of intrinsic estimators. However, the qualitative visualizations in Figures 5 and 6 only compare LAND against GMMs, a baseline already shown to perform poorly in Figure 4. This comparison is not very insightful. The more compelling visual comparisons against the intrinsic model are relegated to the supplementary material (Figs. 2 and 4). These results, which demonstrate the intrinsic model's superior performance over GMMs, should be included in the main paper—perhaps replacing the GMM results—to provide a fairer and more informative assessment of LAND's capabilities. Furthermore, an empirical analysis of the computational complexity of LAND versus these intrinsic estimators would be extremely useful. This would help readers understand the trade-offs and determine which method is more suitable for different data structures and computational budgets.

**Minor Points and Suggestions:**

*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity (sigma):** The locality of the metric is determined by a Gaussian kernel with a bandwidth parameter, sigma (lines 101-103). The paper lacks a thorough analysis of the model's sensitivity to this crucial hyperparameter. Its selection is only discussed for the sleep dataset, and it is unclear how it was chosen for the synthetic experiments. The paper would benefit from a robustness analysis for sigma. Additionally, is a single global sigma optimal, or would an adaptive sigma that varies based on local data density be more appropriate?

*   **Mixture of LANDs:** The mixture of LANDs is one of the most compelling aspects of the paper. This section feels somewhat brief, and I believe the paper's impact could be increased by expanding on this contribution, perhaps with more detailed experiments or analysis on a complex, multi-modal dataset.

In summary, this paper presents a theoretically interesting and elegant approach to metric learning. The connection to Riemannian statistics is novel and insightful. However, its practical impact is limited by unaddressed concerns about scalability and a lack of rigorous experimental comparison against the most relevant baselines in the main text. Addressing these issues would significantly strengthen the paper.
