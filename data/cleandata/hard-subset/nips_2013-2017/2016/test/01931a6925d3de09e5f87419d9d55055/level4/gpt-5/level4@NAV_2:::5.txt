Summary: This paper introduces a locally adaptive normal distribution (LAND) that replaces the standard Mahalanobis distance in a Gaussian with a geodesic distance defined by a smoothly varying, data-driven Riemannian metric. The metric encourages paths to stay in regions of high density, enabling modeling of data concentrated near nonlinear manifolds. The authors also present a mixture version of LAND with an EM-style procedure and evaluate the approach on synthetic data and EEG sleep-stage data.

Major comments: The central idea of substituting a geodesic distance—akin in spirit to Isomap—is compelling. That said, several aspects of the presentation lack clarity. First, the construction of the inverse metric tensor in Eq. (7) appears somewhat ad hoc. Could the authors provide stronger intuition for using a Gaussian kernel to weight local covariance estimates, and discuss alternatives or sensitivity to σ and ρ? Second, the link between the locally defined covariance in Eq. (7) and the subsequent MAP estimation in Eq. (11) is unclear—are these referring to the same covariance conceptually, or is Eq. (7) exclusively for the manifold metric while Eq. (11) estimates the parametric model covariance? The experimental section feels limited. Most importantly, I am concerned about scalability and computational efficiency. Please report computational complexity, memory usage, and wall-clock time, and compare them explicitly to a standard Gaussian mixture EM implementation under comparable settings. Overall, the theoretical contribution is interesting, but the practical applicability remains uncertain without stronger evidence on efficiency and robustness.

Minor comments: In Algorithm 1, lines 4 and 5, replace d with ∇.
