- Proposes LAND: a locally adaptive Riemannian metric and a curved normal distribution replacing Euclidean distance.
- Introduces a parametric locally adaptive normal distribution that uses a nonlinear, location-dependent distance.
- Learns a smoothly varying non-parametric metric by inverting local covariance matrices with locality defined via a Gaussian kernel.
- Notes scalability concerns: the local-metric construction may not scale well to high-dimensional inputs.
- Develops a maximum likelihood learning algorithm using gradient descent combined with Monte Carlo integration.
- Extends LAND to mixture models and derives an EM algorithm for mixtures of LANDs.
- Validates the method on synthetic datasets and EEG sleep-stage data, showing it can fit non-trivial distributions.
- Strength: novel application of Riemannian geometry to model observation spaces adaptively.
- Strength: provides a useful new tool (curved normal) for statistical learning.
- Strength: demonstrates empirical effectiveness on real and synthetic data.
- Limitation: metric learning is separate from LAND learning; hyperparameters rho and sigma are fixed empirically rather than learned jointly.
- Limitation: paper lacks discussion on the effect and selection of sigma (kernel scale).
- Limitation: metric in Equation 7 is not invariant to coordinate transformations, which may be problematic.
- Experimental critique: GMM comparison is misleading—LAND can use fewer components but may overfit; GMM could outperform with more components.
- Suggestion: compare methods by allowing each to choose component counts via a criterion (e.g., BIC) before comparing performance.
- Request for more analysis: provide quantitative runtime/complexity comparisons versus Gaussian estimation and clarify the scale limits (e.g., applicability to MNIST).
