The paper proposes LAND, a new parametric metric defined over a nonparametric space, which bridges concepts from metric learning, manifold learning, and Riemannian statistics. The learned parametric metric takes the form of a Riemannian normal distribution, as defined in Pennec [15]. The authors propose a maximum likelihood algorithm to estimate the mean and covariance of this distribution, which in turn defines a Mahalanobis-like distance on the manifold. Following Hauberg et al. [9], distances between points are measured as geodesics, which requires learning a local metric. Here, the authors propose learning this metric from the inverse of local (diagonal) covariance matrices. The paper also introduces an extension for mixtures of LANDs.

The paper offers new insights into the connection between metric learning and Riemannian statistics. The development of a parametric, generative model that can generalize beyond the training data is a valuable contribution. However, my primary concern is the model's applicability to large-scale, high-dimensional real-world data. LAND requires computing a per-point (diagonal) covariance matrix and its inverse, operations that are known to be challenging in high-dimensional settings. While the authors briefly acknowledge this issue (lines 265-266), the paper would be significantly strengthened by a more thorough analysis of the proposed metric's computational complexity and its practical limitations. For instance, what is the effective upper limit for dimensionality? Is it 3, 5, 10, or 100 dimensions?

Given the vast literature on metric learning, the paper also needs to be more clearly situated within this context. For example, while the authors build on Hauberg et al. [9], that work already discusses related methods like Frome et al. [1,2] and Malisiewicz and Efros [6], which also learn a per-point diagonal metric tensor. A clearer discussion of LAND's novelty with respect to these methods is needed. Similarly, the use of local covariances is not new and has appeared in methods like local PCA (Kambhatla), even if not computed at every single point.

Several aspects of the experimental evaluation could also be improved:

*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** For learning the local metric (lines 101-103), the authors use a Gaussian kernel to define locality. The paper lacks an analysis of how the kernel bandwidth parameter, sigma, influences the results. The choice of sigma is only discussed for the sleep dataset (Sec. 4.2), with no mention of its selection for the synthetic data experiments (Sec. 4.1). The paper would benefit from a sensitivity analysis for this parameter. Furthermore, is sigma held constant for all points, or is it adapted to regions of varying data density?

*   **Baseline Comparisons:** In the quantitative comparison, Figure 4 shows that LAND performs similarly to "intrinsic estimators." However, the qualitative visualizations in Figures 5 and 6 omit these strong competitors and instead compare only against GMM, a model already shown to perform poorly in Figure 4. This makes the visualizations less insightful. I noted that visual comparisons with the intrinsic model are included in the supplementary material (Figs. 2 and 4), where it clearly outperforms GMM. These more informative comparisons should be included in the main paper, either replacing or augmenting the GMM results.

*   **Complexity Analysis:** An analysis of the computational complexity of LAND versus these intrinsic estimators would be valuable, helping readers understand the trade-offs and determine which method is preferable for different types of data.

The extension to mixtures of LANDs is one of the most promising aspects of this work, as real-world data is rarely generated from a single component. This section of the paper would be worth expanding upon.

Overall, the paper is very well-written and a pleasure to read. The core idea is interesting, but its practical impact is somewhat obscured by the concerns raised above regarding scalability, positioning within the literature, and the experimental evaluation.
