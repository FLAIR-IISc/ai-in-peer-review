- Novel idea: leverages Riemannian geometry to model observation space and defines a curved normal distribution, addressing a fundamental problem in statistical learning.  
- Interest: application of Riemannian geometry to a practical ML problem is compelling.  
- Clarity: method is clearly outlined, with Riemannian geometry defined in Section 3.1.  
- Major limitation: despite the title, the metric (Eq. 7) is hand-crafted with fixed hyperparameters ρ and σ rather than learned.  
- Missing analysis: no sensitivity study or guidance on selecting σ; intuitive effects of varying σ are not explored.  
- Recommendation: consider integrating metric learning into the LAND stage so ρ and σ can be learned from data.  
- Invariance issue: the proposed metric is not invariant to coordinate transformations (e.g., orientation of a line in 2D), which may affect applicability.  
- Suggestion: discuss whether a full (non-diagonal) metric would remedy non-invariance and provide more intuition about Eq. 7’s properties.  
- Related work gap: related work section should better cover literature linking Riemannian geometry with metric and manifold learning.  
- Experimental concern: initial comparisons are uninformative—LAND’s flexibility leads to better performance with fewer components, but GMM could match by increasing components.  
- Recommendation for experiments: use model selection (e.g., BIC) to allow each method to choose the number of components and compare LAND vs GMM with optimal component counts.  
- Overfitting worry: increasing LAND components risks overfitting and susceptibility to local noise; experiments should show this behavior.  
- Computational cost: quantify LAND’s computational burden versus standard Gaussian estimation, report scalability limits, and test on larger datasets (e.g., MNIST) to validate applicability.
