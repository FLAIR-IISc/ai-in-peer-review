This paper proposes to model the observation space with a hand-crafted Riemannian metric, and define a curved normal distribution based on the associated Riemannian geometry, aiming to better capture the underlying data manifold. The paper addresses a fundamental problem: how to define a Riemannian metric from a set of high-dimensional real-valued observations, and subsequently, how to define and estimate a curved Gaussian distribution based on this geometry. This presents a useful tool and an interesting application of Riemannian geometry in statistical learning. The proposed method defines the underlying Riemannian geometry in section 3.1. While the title suggests that the metric is *learned*, it is, in fact, hand-crafted in equation 7 based on two fixed hyperparameters, ρ and σ. I suspect σ is a sensitive parameter; the paper should discuss in more detail the intuitive effect of varying σ and how to appropriately select its value. The omission of this discussion leaves the paper incomplete. Furthermore, it would be valuable to consider whether the metric learning stage could be integrated with the LAND learning stage, allowing ρ and σ to be learned directly. A key benefit of a geometric approach is invariance to coordinate transformations. However, the metric defined in equation 7 lacks this invariance. For example, consider data forming a line in a 2D observation space; the metric will differ significantly between coordinate systems aligned with the line and those rotated, e.g., by 45 degrees. This point requires further discussion, including an examination of whether the corresponding full (non-diagonal) metric exhibits invariance, and a more detailed intuition behind the metric in equation 7. There is a substantial body of literature connecting Riemannian geometry, metric learning, and manifold learning, which should be related to this work (e.g., search for "Riemannian" "metric learning" "manifold learning" on Google).

A significant criticism concerns the first experiment. The comparison is not particularly meaningful as LAND, being more flexible, naturally describes the data better with a smaller number of components. However, a GMM with a sufficiently large number of components could achieve similar performance, while LAND would likely overfit. I suggest redesigning the experiment to allow each method to select its optimal number of components based on a principled criterion such as BIC, and then compare performance. LAND is still expected to outperform the GMM due to its better representation of the manifold structure, and this comparison would be more informative. It would also be insightful to observe how LAND performs with a large number of components and demonstrate the resulting overfitting. LAND is computationally more expensive than estimating a standard Gaussian. Are there any quantitative measurements or experiments to clarify the computational cost? What is the practical limit on the scale of data LAND can handle? Could LAND be applied to a dataset like MNIST and compared to Gaussian mixture modeling?

Following the authors’ response, it is clear LAND represents a more flexible model than a Gaussian distribution. Increasing the number of components increases the vulnerability of each local LAND model to local noise compared to a local Gaussian. While the authors state "the geodesics will almost be straight lines", they are still curves, and these curves contribute to overfitting. I strongly encourage the authors to revise this experiment accordingly.
