In this paper, the authors present a locally adaptive normal distribution. Instead of measuring distances via a Euclidean distance, they define a Riemannian metric M(x) that varies smoothly across the dataset. Given this Riemannian metric, geodesic distances along the manifold can be calculated via the exponential and logarithmic maps. The authors utilize the Euler-Lagrange equations to estimate these geodesic distances, defining a set of ordinary differential equations. The logarithm map takes points from the manifold (y) to the tangent plane (at x) such that the length of the resulting vector corresponds to the geodesic distance from x to y along the manifold. The authors then apply a normal distribution, computing a Mahalanobis distance on points mapped by the logarithm map. This inner product is used to compute distances from a point x to the mean, instead of Euclidean distances. They derive estimators for the mean and covariance via maximum likelihood. Since closed-form solutions are unavailable, they present a steepest descent algorithm for estimating these parameters. Finally, as the normalization constant is also not available in closed form, the authors propose a Monte Carlo approximation method. They apply this method to synthetic data generated on a manifold, as well as in a clustering task, comparing their results to a standard Gaussian mixture model. Overall, I believe this paper provides an interesting and useful extension of the normal distribution, which adapts to local manifold structure. The authors successfully combine work in metric estimation, geodesic distance computation, and distribution learning. I believe this work is worthy of publication.

Regarding the scores:

**Technical Quality:** The manifold techniques (using Euler-Lagrange equations, estimating logarithm maps) are sound and well explained. The Monte Carlo estimation of the normalization constant and steepest descent algorithms are clever and well described. However, the analysis of the applications is somewhat lacking – the sigma parameter appears to be selected by optimization on the training data. Furthermore, the explanation of the usefulness of the learned distribution could be strengthened.

**Novelty:** The main contribution of this paper appears to be the combination of previous work (using kernels for the metric M(x), estimating geodesic distances via Euler-Lagrange, and applying a normal distribution) alongside providing an algorithm for estimating the parameters (mean, covariance, normalization constant).

**Potential Impact:** The authors provide a fair evaluation of the GMM and LAND on synthetic data (looking at log-likelihood on data generated by the learned distributions), but the method for selecting the sigma parameter is not entirely clear. The authors demonstrate that the LAND fits well on data generated along a manifold, but do not demonstrate its performance compared to a GMM when data is generated according to a single normal distribution or GMM. In the real-world (clustering) data, the sigma value for the LAND appears to be chosen by optimizing the F-measure, and the reported F-measure is obtained on the training data. A fairer comparison would involve reporting the F-measure on a held-out test set to assess potential overfitting. It would also be beneficial to examine the performance on very high-dimensional data. One potentially useful application that the authors highlight is using the LAND as a generative model for manifold data; however, they make the somewhat unusual assumption that the data X is observed in D dimensions and the manifold dimension is also D (i.e., points on the tangent plane are D-dimensional and the metric M(x) is full rank). Typically, in manifold learning, we are interested in data that lies on a d-dimensional manifold embedded into a D-dimensional Euclidean space where D is much larger than d.

**Clarity:** There’s one particular notation point that I found confusing: the overloading of the Sigma notation. Specifically, they state M = Sigma<sup>-1</sup> but then estimate it via a (local) diagonal kernel matrix. Is this Sigma also the same as the (fitted) covariance matrix? If so, is the kernel merely an initialization? This is somewhat unclear since M(x) should vary with x, while the fitted covariance matrix is a single parameter. In the proof of the steepest descent, they explicitly state, “in our case M = Sigma<sup>-1</sup>” and use that substitution. Clarification here would be helpful. In Section 4.2, it is not explicitly stated, but I assume cluster membership was chosen by selecting 2 clusters and computing the closest (geodesic?) distance to the cluster means. Overall, I felt the manifold introduction was well explained, at least for someone familiar with Riemannian manifolds.
