

The paper introduces LAND, a novel parametric metric defined over nonparametric spaces by integrating metric learning, manifold learning, and Riemannian statistics. LAND models data using a Riemannian normal distribution (Pennec [15]), learning its mean and covariance via maximum likelihood to define a Mahalanobis distance on Riemannian manifolds. Distances are computed as geodesics (Hauberg et al. [9]), with the metric learned through inverses of local diagonal covariances. The authors also explore mixtures of LANDs, which could significantly enhance applicability to multi-component data. The work offers valuable insights into the intersection of metric learning and Riemannian statistics, particularly for generating models that generalize beyond training data.

However, the primary concern is LAND’s scalability to large-scale, high-dimensional real-world data. The method requires computing a diagonal covariance matrix and its inverse for each data point. In high dimensions, such computations are computationally prohibitive and statistically unstable. While the authors briefly acknowledge high-dimensionality as a challenge (lines 265–266), they lack a rigorous analysis of the metric’s complexity, limitations, and practical dimensionality thresholds. Specifically, the paper does not specify what constitutes "high-dimensional" (e.g., 3, 5, 10, or 100 dimensions) or how LAND’s performance degrades beyond a certain dimensionality. This omission is critical, as metric learning literature has long grappled with scalability (e.g., Frome et al. [1,2], Malisiewicz and Efros [6]), and LAND’s approach overlaps with these methods in using diagonal pointwise metrics. The paper would benefit from explicitly positioning itself within this established context to guide future readers.

A second concern involves the Gaussian kernel parameter σ, used to define locality (lines 101–103). The authors only discuss σ for the sleep dataset (Section 4.2), omitting its analysis for synthetic data (Section 4.1). Crucially, they do not address whether σ is uniform across regions of varying density (e.g., sparse vs. dense areas), nor do they provide robustness tests across σ values. Without this, the sensitivity of LAND to σ remains unverified, weakening the method’s practical reliability.

The experimental evaluation also presents inconsistencies. Figure 4 shows LAND performing comparably to intrinsic estimators, yet Figures 5 and 6 visualize results against GMM—already demonstrated in Figure 4 to underperform relative to intrinsic methods. This omission renders the visual comparisons less insightful. Notably, the supplementary material (Figs. 2, 4) includes intrinsic estimator visualizations that outperform GMM, suggesting these should replace the GMM results in the main paper or be included alongside them. Additionally, a computational complexity analysis comparing LAND to intrinsic estimators would clarify which method is preferable for specific data structures (e.g., manifold density, dimensionality).

The mixture-of-LANDs extension is particularly promising, as real-world data often arises from multiple generative processes. This aspect merits deeper exploration, including scalability of mixture learning and empirical validation on multi-modal datasets. The paper’s strong writing and clear exposition make it a pleasure to read, but this potential should be leveraged more thoroughly.

In summary, LAND presents a theoretically interesting framework for Riemannian metric learning with generative capabilities. Its core contribution is well-motivated, but the work requires substantial strengthening in three areas: (1) a detailed analysis of scalability limitations in high dimensions, (2) rigorous treatment of the locality parameter σ across datasets and densities, and (3) improved experimental design to fairly compare against relevant baselines (particularly intrinsic estimators) with appropriate visualizations. Addressing these gaps would significantly enhance the paper’s impact and relevance to the metric learning community.
