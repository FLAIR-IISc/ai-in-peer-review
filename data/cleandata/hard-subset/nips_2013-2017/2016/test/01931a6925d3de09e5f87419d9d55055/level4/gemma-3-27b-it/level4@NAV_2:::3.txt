This paper proposes a locally adaptive normal distribution (LAND) by replacing the Euclidean metric of the standard normal distribution with a learned Riemannian metric, aiming to better capture the underlying data manifold. The paper addresses a fundamental problem of defining a Riemannian metric from high-dimensional observations and subsequently defining and estimating a curved Gaussian distribution based on this geometry, presenting a useful tool and an interesting application of Riemannian geometry in statistical learning. The core idea, constructing a Riemannian metric to adapt to data structure, is well-motivated and builds upon prior work in manifold learning and Riemannian statistics.

The method constructs a Riemannian metric in section 3.1, however, the metric is largely hand-crafted, defined by equation (7) with hyperparameters ρ and σ, determined empirically. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the impact of varying σ, and a principled method for its selection. Exploring whether the metric learning stage can be integrated with LAND estimation, allowing ρ and σ to be learned, would strengthen the approach. A key strength of geometric approaches is coordinate invariance; however, the proposed metric in equation (7) lacks this property. The paper should discuss this limitation in more detail, potentially considering the invariance properties of a full (non-diagonal) metric, and provide further intuition regarding the metric’s behavior. There is a substantial body of literature connecting Riemannian geometry, metric learning, and manifold learning that this work could more fully engage with (e.g., searching "Riemannian" "metric learning" "manifold learning" reveals relevant work).

A primary critique concerns the experimental design. In the first experiment, the comparison to GMM is not entirely fair, as the more flexible LAND naturally performs better with fewer components. GMM can achieve comparable performance with a larger number of components, potentially leading to overfitting. Redesigning this experiment to allow each method to select its optimal number of components based on a criterion like BIC would provide a more meaningful comparison. It would be insightful to observe LAND’s performance with a larger number of components and investigate potential overfitting behavior. Furthermore, quantifying the computational cost of LAND compared to standard Gaussian estimation, and exploring its scalability limits (e.g., applying it to datasets like MNIST), would be valuable.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel approach to distribution estimation by leveraging Riemannian geometry, offering a potentially powerful tool for modeling data on manifolds.
*   The method is theoretically grounded, relating to maximum entropy principles and existing work in Riemannian statistics.
*   The experiments demonstrate promising results on both synthetic and real-world data (EEG measurements).
*   The implementation details (e.g., Monte Carlo integration scheme) appear well-considered and contribute to the practical applicability of the method.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The metric construction is largely hand-crafted, relying on empirically chosen hyperparameters without a clear optimization strategy.
*   The lack of coordinate invariance in the proposed metric is a potential limitation.
*   The experimental evaluation could be strengthened by a more rigorous comparison with GMM and an analysis of computational cost and scalability.
*   The connection to relevant literature in metric learning and manifold learning could be more thoroughly explored.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a technically sound and innovative approach. While the limitations regarding metric construction and experimental validation should be addressed, the potential benefits of the LAND model and its connection to fundamental principles of statistical learning justify consideration for acceptance. I recommend acceptance contingent on addressing the points raised above in a revision.
