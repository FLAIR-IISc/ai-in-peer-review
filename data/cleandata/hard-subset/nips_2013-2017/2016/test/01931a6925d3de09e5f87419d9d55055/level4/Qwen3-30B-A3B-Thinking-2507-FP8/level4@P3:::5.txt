

**Review of "A Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution"**

This paper introduces a locally adaptive normal distribution (LAND) that generalizes the standard multivariate normal distribution to a Riemannian manifold setting. The authors replace the Euclidean distance in the Mahalanobis metric with a locally adaptive Riemannian metric, which is learned from the data via a Gaussian kernel-weighted local covariance structure. The LAND is shown to be the maximum entropy distribution under the learned metric, and the authors provide a maximum likelihood estimation algorithm using Monte Carlo integration, as well as an EM algorithm for mixture models. The work builds on Riemannian statistics (Pennec, 2006; Zhang & Fletcher, 2013) and manifold learning (Tenenbaum et al., 2000; Hauberg et al., 2013), but differs by learning the manifold structure from data rather than assuming it is known.

**Strengths**

*Originality*: The paper makes a significant contribution by bridging Riemannian statistics with data-driven manifold learning. While Riemannian normal distributions have been studied previously (Pennec, 2006; Zhang & Fletcher, 2013), the authors' approach of learning the metric from data represents a novel and useful extension. The LAND concept is clearly differentiated from prior work, particularly in its focus on non-parametric metric learning.

*Quality*: The theoretical development is sound, with appropriate mathematical formalism and justification for the LAND as the maximum entropy distribution under the learned metric. The authors carefully address the normalization constant issue through Monte Carlo integration, which is a practical and well-motivated solution. The algorithmic approach (Algorithm 1) is well-justified, and the theoretical properties of the LAND are properly supported.

*Significance*: The LAND model has clear potential to improve modeling of data lying on nonlinear manifolds, which is a common challenge in machine learning. The EEG sleep stage classification results demonstrate the practical utility of the approach, showing better alignment with sleep stage labels than standard Gaussian mixtures. The paper also suggests promising extensions to other generative models (Naïve Bayes, LDA, PCA).

**Weaknesses**

*Clarity*: The paper would benefit from clearer exposition of key concepts. The definition of the metric tensor in Equation (7) appears somewhat ad hoc without sufficient intuition about why a Gaussian kernel-weighted local covariance is the right choice. The relationship between the local covariance defined in Equation (7) and the covariance used in the maximum likelihood estimation (Equation 11) is confusingly presented, and it's unclear whether these represent the same quantity or different aspects of the model.

*Quality*: The experimental evaluation is limited and somewhat superficial. While the synthetic data experiments show the LAND's ability to capture nonlinear structures, the comparison to GMM is not rigorous—there's no statistical significance testing, and the analysis of model complexity (via AIC/BIC) is relegated to the supplement. The EEG experiments lack comparison to other manifold-based approaches (e.g., Isomap, LLE) and don't thoroughly address the sensitivity to the parameter σ, which is critical for the metric learning.

*Significance*: The computational complexity and scalability of the LAND are not adequately addressed. The paper mentions the bottleneck is evaluating logarithm maps, but provides no concrete complexity analysis or runtime comparisons with standard EM. For real-world applications, this is a major concern, especially since the authors use S = 3000 Monte Carlo samples per iteration. Without addressing computational efficiency, the practical applicability of the method remains questionable.

**Specific Concerns**

1. The definition of the metric tensor in Equation (7) needs stronger justification. Why is the inverse of a local diagonal covariance matrix with Gaussian kernel weights the right choice? The authors should provide more intuition about how this metric relates to the underlying data structure.

2. The relationship between the local covariance in Equation (7) and the covariance used in the likelihood (Equation 11) is unclear. These appear to be different quantities, but the paper doesn't adequately explain this distinction.

3. The computational cost of the LAND estimation is not properly assessed. How does it scale with data size and dimensionality compared to standard GMM? What are the actual runtime comparisons?

4. The EEG experiments don't explore the sensitivity to σ thoroughly. The paper states that "σ is less straightforward to select because of significant intersubject variability," yet only reports results for σ = 1 and a limited range. A more comprehensive analysis would strengthen the empirical claims.

**Overall Assessment**

This is an interesting theoretical contribution that extends Riemannian statistics to a data-driven manifold learning setting. The LAND concept is novel and well-motivated, with promising empirical results on both synthetic and real data. However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the clarity issues and providing more thorough experimental validation, particularly regarding computational efficiency.

**Arguments for Acceptance**:
- Novel integration of Riemannian statistics with data-driven manifold learning
- Sound theoretical foundation with clear maximum entropy properties
- Promising empirical results on EEG data showing better performance than GMM
- Well-justified algorithmic approach for parameter estimation

**Arguments against Acceptance**:
- Key aspects of the model (metric definition) lack sufficient intuition
- Experimental evaluation is limited and lacks proper comparison to relevant baselines
- Computational complexity and scalability are not adequately addressed
- Some conceptual confusion between different covariance representations

The paper makes an interesting theoretical contribution that could advance the field of manifold learning and generative modeling. However, the current version has clarity issues and insufficient experimental validation to fully demonstrate its practical utility. With revisions addressing these concerns, this paper would be a strong candidate for acceptance. As it stands, I recommend a borderline decision with the expectation that the authors address the concerns raised in this review.
