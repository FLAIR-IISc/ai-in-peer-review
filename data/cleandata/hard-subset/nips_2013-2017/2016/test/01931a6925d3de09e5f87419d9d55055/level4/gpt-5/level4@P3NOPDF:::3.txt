Summary
This paper proposes to endow the observation space with a hand-crafted Riemannian metric and to define a curved normal distribution (LAND) induced by that geometry. The goal is to better capture data concentrated near low-dimensional manifolds. The paper tackles two linked problems: (i) defining a Riemannian metric from high-dimensional real-valued observations; and (ii) defining and estimating a curved Gaussian distribution on that geometry. This is an interesting and potentially useful application of Riemannian geometry to statistical learning.

Strengths
- The problem is important: many high-dimensional datasets lie near manifolds, and Euclidean Gaussians can be inadequate.
- The paper offers a clear geometric framework and a practical algorithmic instantiation.
- The idea of a curved analogue of a Gaussian is conceptually appealing and could be broadly useful.

Main concerns
1) “Learning” the metric vs. hand-crafted design
- Section 3.1 and Equation 7 define the metric through two hyperparameters, ρ and σ, fixed a priori. Despite the title’s claim to “learn a metric,” the current method specifies a metric form and tunes constants, rather than learning the metric from data.
- Sensitivity to σ seems likely. Please provide:
  - Intuition about how σ affects curvature, locality, and effective model capacity.
  - A principled selection procedure (e.g., marginal likelihood, cross-validated log-likelihood, BIC/AIC on LAND fits, or gradient-based tuning if differentiable).
  - An empirical sensitivity analysis across datasets.
- Can the metric stage be merged with LAND estimation so that ρ and σ are learned jointly (e.g., by maximizing data likelihood under LAND, with regularization to avoid degenerate metrics)?

2) Coordinate invariance and metric form
- A core appeal of geometric approaches is invariance under reparameterization. The metric in Eq. 7 appears coordinate-dependent. For instance, if data lie along a line in 2D, rotating coordinates (e.g., by 45 degrees) would change the diagonal metric and thus the geometry.
- Please discuss:
  - Whether a full (non-diagonal) metric constructed from the same ingredients would be invariant under orthogonal transforms, and the trade-offs in complexity/estimation.
  - The geometric intuition for Eq. 7, including when it is appropriate and when it may be misleading.
  - Any practical strategies to mitigate coordinate dependence (e.g., pre-whitening, PCA alignment, or learning off-diagonal terms).
- The paper would benefit from a more thorough positioning relative to prior work on metric learning and manifold learning with Riemannian connections (e.g., “Riemannian metric learning,” “information geometry of mixtures,” diffusion metrics, Laplace–Beltrami-based approaches). Clarify how your construction differs and what advantages it offers.

3) Experimental design and fairness of comparisons
- In the first experiment, comparing a flexible LAND with a small number of components to a GMM with the same number of components is not very informative: increasing GMM components often closes the gap, while increasing LAND components risks overfitting.
- A more meaningful comparison would allow each model to choose the number of components via a common criterion (e.g., BIC, held-out log-likelihood, or cross-validation), then compare the best-performing configurations. This would highlight whether LAND’s geometric bias improves generalization at matched model selection rigor.
- Please also show how LAND behaves as the number of components increases (training vs. test log-likelihood), to illustrate potential overfitting and the role of curvature when components become small and geodesics approach straight lines.

4) Computational cost and scalability
- LAND appears more expensive than Euclidean Gaussian estimation due to geodesic computations and metric-dependent operations. Please quantify:
  - Time and memory complexity in terms of sample size N, dimension D, and number of components K.
  - Wall-clock benchmarks versus GMM on standard datasets.
  - Practical limits on N, D, and K (e.g., where runtime or memory becomes prohibitive).
- A larger-scale demonstration (e.g., MNIST) would clarify scalability. If infeasible, provide a clear justification and an analysis of bottlenecks.

Clarity and presentation
- The title and claims should reflect that the metric is currently hand-crafted with fixed hyperparameters, not learned, unless you introduce a learning mechanism.
- Provide more intuition and visualization for the induced geometry: e.g., example geodesics, volume elements, and how σ alters local curvature and effective neighborhood size.
- Include an ablation and sensitivity study for ρ and σ.
- Clearly state the optimization objectives and regularization needed to avoid pathological metrics.

Post-rebuttal comments
- I appreciate the authors’ response noting that geodesics become nearly straight as components proliferate. However, even small residual curvature can still induce overfitting locally, and LAND remains more flexible than Euclidean Gaussians in each component. This reinforces the need for a fair model selection protocol (e.g., BIC or held-out validation) that lets both LAND and GMM choose their preferred number of components. I strongly encourage revising the experiments accordingly and explicitly demonstrating how overfitting manifests as the number of components increases.

Overall assessment
This work presents a promising geometric approach with the potential to improve density modeling on manifolds. However, key issues remain: the metric is hand-crafted rather than learned; sensitivity to σ is not analyzed; the proposed metric is not invariant to coordinate changes; and the experimental design does not fairly compare models at matched model selection rigor. Clarifying the geometric choices, strengthening the empirical evaluation (including component selection and overfitting analyses), and providing computational complexity benchmarks would substantially improve the paper. Pending these revisions, I view the contribution as promising but currently incomplete.
