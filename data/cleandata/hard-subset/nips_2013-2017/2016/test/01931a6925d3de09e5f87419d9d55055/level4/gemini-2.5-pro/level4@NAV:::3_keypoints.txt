- Proposes modeling observation space with a handcrafted Riemannian metric and a derived curved normal distribution to capture data manifolds.  
- Addresses two challenges: defining a Riemannian metric from high-dimensional data and defining/estimating a curved Gaussian in that geometry.  
- The metric (Eq.7) is handcrafted, controlled by hyperparameters ρ and σ which are fixed empirically rather than learned.  
- Paper lacks analysis of σ’s effect or a principled strategy for selecting ρ and σ; recommends integrating their learning into the LAND procedure.  
- The proposed metric is not invariant to coordinate transformations; this limitation needs discussion and intuition.  
- Should analyze whether a full (non-diagonal) metric would restore invariance and relate formulation choices to existing metric/manifold learning literature.  
- Experimental comparison is weak: LAND is compared to GMM with few components, a setting biased toward LAND’s flexibility.  
- Recommend model selection (e.g., BIC) for each method to choose component counts and show whether LAND still outperforms, plus demonstrate LAND’s overfitting behavior as components increase.  
- Computational cost concerns: LAND is more expensive than standard Gaussians and the paper lacks quantitative runtime/scaling analysis (e.g., applicability to datasets like MNIST).  
- Increasing component count may make local LANDs prone to fitting noise due to residual geodesic curvature, reinforcing the need for revised experiments.
