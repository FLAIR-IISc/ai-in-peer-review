Here is a proposed naturally adapted spatial distribution based on a geodesic manifold traversal distance, learned adaptively from data. This distance replaces the standard Mahalanobis distance used in Gaussian distributions. An extension to this is also provided in the LANDs mix. Its performance has been evaluated on a synthetic dataset and a real-world dataset of EEG measurements. Key comments: The idea of using a geodesic distance like Isomaps is very interesting. However, there are some weaknesses in the clarity of the article. Firstly, the definition of the inverse of the metric tensor in equation (7) seems immediate. Could the authors provide more insight into the idea of using a Gaussian kernel for weighting in a weighted sum of local coefficients? Also, I got lost in the definition of the matrix of local coefficients in equation (7) and later, the MAP assumption of the same quantity in equation (11). Do these refer to the same quantity? Furthermore, the experimental evaluations are quite weak. However, my main concern is the scalability and computational efficiency of the method. For example, what is the computational complexity and running time compared to standard EM? In my opinion, the article has an interesting theoretical contribution, but it may not be very applicable in practice. Minor comments: Algorithm 1, lines 4 and 5, d -> \nabla
