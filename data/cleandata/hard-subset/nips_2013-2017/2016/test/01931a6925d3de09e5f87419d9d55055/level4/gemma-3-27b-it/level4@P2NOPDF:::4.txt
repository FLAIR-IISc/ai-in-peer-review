This paper introduces a Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND) as an alternative to Gaussian Mixture Models, grounded in the framework of Riemannian Manifolds. The authors develop a maximum likelihood estimator (MLE) for the distribution’s parameters and demonstrate its performance through experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets. Overall, the paper is well-written, theoretically sound, and supported by compelling experimental results.

However, the work’s novelty relative to existing approaches, particularly that of Simo-Serra *et al.* [19], feels incremental. While the paper ultimately highlights distinctions in the conclusion, a clearer articulation of the proposed method's advantages over the state-of-the-art would significantly strengthen the contribution. Specifically, the key differences – and why LAND offers improvements – should be established earlier in the paper, ideally beginning in the introduction. The omission of Simo-Serra *et al.* [19] from the introduction is a noticeable gap, as it represents a closely related and important point of comparison. 

To elaborate, the reader is left to infer the nuances of LAND’s approach *after* reviewing the experimental results. A more proactive comparison, outlining how LAND addresses limitations of existing techniques (including [19]) from the outset, would greatly enhance the paper's impact and clarity. This isn’t a criticism of the technical work itself, which is clearly well-executed, but rather a suggestion for improving the framing of the contribution within the broader field. Establishing a clear position within the existing literature earlier would make the paper more compelling and accessible to readers.
