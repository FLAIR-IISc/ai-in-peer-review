In this paper, the authors present a locally adaptive normal distribution (LAND) that extends the traditional normal distribution by incorporating a locally adaptive, nonlinear distance measure. The LAND is defined on a Riemannian manifold, which is learned from the data using a local covariance matrix. The authors provide a comprehensive introduction to Riemannian geometry and explain how the LAND can be used for density estimation and clustering tasks.

The technical quality of the paper is high, with sound and well-explained manifold techniques, including the use of Euler-Lagrange equations and estimation of logarithm maps. The Monte-Carlo estimation of the normalization constant and steepest descent algorithms are clever and well described. However, the analysis of the applications is somewhat lacking, and the explanation of the usefulness of the learned distribution could be improved.

The novelty of the paper lies in combining previous work on metric estimation, computing geodesic distances, and learning distributions. While the individual components are not new, the authors provide a useful extension of the normal distribution that adapts to local manifold structure. The potential impact of the paper is significant, as it provides a new tool for density estimation and clustering tasks on manifold data.

The authors provide a fair evaluation of the LAND and a Gaussian mixture model (GMM) on synthetic data, but the choice of the sigma parameter could be better explained. Additionally, the authors should provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the LAND on real-life data, including a comparison with other clustering algorithms and an examination of its performance on high-dimensional data.

The clarity of the paper is generally good, but there are some notation issues that could be improved. For example, the overloading of the Sigma notation is confusing, and the relationship between the local diagonal kernel matrix and the fitted covariance matrix could be clarified.

In terms of the criteria described in the qualitative evaluation section, the paper scores well on technical quality and novelty. However, the analysis of the applications and the explanation of the usefulness of the learned distribution could be improved. The potential impact of the paper is significant, but the evaluation could be more comprehensive.
