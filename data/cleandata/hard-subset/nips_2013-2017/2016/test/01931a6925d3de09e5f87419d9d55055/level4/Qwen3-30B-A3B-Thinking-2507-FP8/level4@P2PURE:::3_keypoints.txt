- Proposes modeling observation space with a hand-crafted Riemannian metric and defines a curved normal distribution (LAND) to better capture data manifolds.  
- Addresses the important problem of defining a Riemannian metric from high-dimensional observations and estimating a curved Gaussian; useful and interesting application of Riemannian geometry.  
- The metric is hand-crafted (Eq. 7) with hyperparameters ρ and σ fixed empirically, contrary to the paper title’s implication of “learning a metric.”  
- Paper lacks detailed discussion of the intuitive effect of σ and a principled method for selecting it; σ appears sensitive and the approach is incomplete without this.  
- Suggestion to consider jointly learning ρ and σ with the LAND parameters instead of fixing them separately.  
- The proposed metric (Eq. 7) is not coordinate-invariant; the paper should discuss invariance, whether a full (non-diagonal) metric would be invariant, and provide deeper intuition for Eq. 7.  
- The work should connect to existing literature on Riemannian metric learning and manifold learning.  
- The first experiment is flawed/unfair: comparing LAND with a fixed number of components against GMM is not meaningful; each method should be allowed to select its optimal component count (e.g., via BIC).  
- LAND can overfit with many components because local LANDs are more vulnerable to local noise than local Gaussians; this behavior should be analyzed and reported.  
- LAND is computationally more expensive than Gaussian estimation; the paper should include quantitative runtime/scalability measurements and report maximum feasible dataset sizes.  
- Recommend additional experiments on larger benchmarks (e.g., MNIST) and a revised experimental design to validate claims and compare performance fairly.
