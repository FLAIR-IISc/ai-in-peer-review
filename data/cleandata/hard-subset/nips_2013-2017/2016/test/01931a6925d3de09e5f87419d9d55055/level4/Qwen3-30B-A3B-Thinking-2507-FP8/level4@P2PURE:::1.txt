

The paper introduces LAND, a novel parametric metric defined over a nonparametric space by integrating concepts from metric learning/manifold learning and Riemannian statistics. The parametric metric is formulated as a Riemannian normal distribution (Pennec [15]), with the authors proposing a maximum likelihood algorithm to estimate its mean and covariance—effectively defining a Mahalanobis distance for Riemannian manifolds. Distances between points are computed as geodesics along the manifold (Hauberg et al. [9]), relying on learned local metrics. Specifically, the authors learn the metric via the inverses of local (diagonal) covariances and also consider mixtures of LANDs. The work provides valuable insights into the connection between metric learning and Riemannian statistics, highlighting the utility of parametric, generative models that generalize beyond training data. However, the primary concern is the method's applicability to real-world data, which is typically large-scale and high-dimensional. LAND requires computing a diagonal covariance matrix and its inverse for each data point, a computationally challenging task in high dimensions. While the authors acknowledge high-dimensionality as a potential issue (lines 265–266), the paper lacks a thorough analysis of the metric's complexity and limitations. Specifically, it does not clarify the threshold for "high-dimensional" (e.g., 3, 5, 10, or 100 dimensions), nor does it adequately contextualize the work within the extensive literature on metric learning. Hauberg et al. [9] already reference relevant prior work (e.g., Frome et al. [1,2], Malisiewicz and Efros [6]), which similarly learn diagonal metric tensors per point, and local covariances have been used in methods like local PCA (Kambhatla) even if not point-wise. Regarding locality, lines 101–103 state that a Gaussian kernel defines locality, but the influence of the parameter σ on results is only analyzed for the sleep data (Section 4.2), not for the synthetic data (Section 4.1). Robustness tests for σ across low- and high-density regions are needed. Figure 4 compares LAND against intrinsic estimators, showing similar performance, yet Figures 5–6 visualize results against GMM—which Figure 4 already demonstrates performs poorly—rather than against intrinsic estimators. Supplementary Figures 2 and 4 show intrinsic estimators outperforming GMM, suggesting these results should replace the GMM visualizations in the main paper. A computational complexity analysis comparing LAND to intrinsic estimators would also clarify which method is preferable for specific data structures. The mixture-of-LANDs approach is particularly promising, as real-world data rarely stems from a single component; this aspect merits expansion. Overall, the paper is exceptionally well-written and a pleasure to read.
