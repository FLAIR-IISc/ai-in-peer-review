- Proposes a principled generalization of the multivariate normal by replacing Euclidean metric with a locally adaptive Riemannian metric (LAND), suited for manifold-structured data.  
- Provides a clear maximum-likelihood estimation procedure and a mixture extension (mixtures of LANDs) making the approach practically useful.  
- Experiments are convincing, showing improved fit and modeling flexibility over Euclidean baselines.  
- Writing, structure, and bibliography are strong and appropriate for the conference.  
- Concern about computational complexity and scalability: need quantification of training/inference time, memory, and scaling with dimension, sample size, and component count.  
- Questionable normalization and numerical stability: need details on approximations used for normalization constants and their reliability in high curvature or sparse-data regimes.  
- Unclear how the local metric is learned and regularized; risk of degenerate/overly curved solutions and potential identifiability issues with mixture components.  
- Empirical comparisons are incomplete: should compare to flexible GMM covariances, embedding-then-GMM pipelines, and prior Riemannian Gaussian/mixture work.  
- Lacks ablations toggling local adaptivity versus a global metric to isolate where performance gains come from.  
- Robustness concerns: sensitivity to initialization, metric bandwidth/smoothing, number of components, local minima, and overfitting in low-sample/high-dim regimes.  
- Theoretical properties are missing: no guarantees on MLE consistency, existence/uniqueness, or convergence rates under manifold assumptions.  
- Suggestion to provide complexity analysis and wall-clock benchmarks versus GMMs across datasets and component counts.  
- Suggest adding broader baselines and ablations, including embedding-then-GMM and existing Riemannian mixture methods.  
- Ask to clarify metric parameterization, learning procedure, regularization, and hyperparameter selection (bias–variance trade-off).  
- Request details on numerical strategies for normalization and gradients (e.g., Monte Carlo, quadrature) with diagnostics for accuracy and failure modes.  
- Recommend reporting uncertainty estimates or confidence intervals on likelihood and clustering metrics for statistical significance.  
- Propose adding qualitative visualizations of learned metrics (e.g., curvature or local covariance fields) to improve intuition.  
- Encourage releasing code and scripts to facilitate reproducibility.  
- Minor fixes: correct typos (“experients”, “sinthetically”, “estructura”) and ensure consistent terminology for the proposed distribution and mixture components.  
- Suggest adding a related-work note situating the paper with Riemannian normal distributions and mixtures on manifolds.  
- Overall assessment: timely, well-written, and impactful work bridging geometry and probabilistic modeling, but needs more thorough practical, comparative, and theoretical validation to strengthen the contribution.
