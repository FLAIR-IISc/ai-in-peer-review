This paper proposes modeling the observation space via a manually specified Riemannian metric and defining a curved normal distribution induced by that geometry to better capture the data manifold. It addresses the core problem of how to construct a Riemannian metric from high-dimensional real-valued observations, and then how to define and estimate a curved Gaussian distribution on this geometry. The contribution provides a useful tool and a compelling application of Riemannian geometry in statistical learning.

The first stage defines the underlying Riemannian structure in section 3.1. Although the title suggests it “learns a metric,” the metric in equation 7 is in fact hand-crafted, controlled by two hyperparameters, rho and sigma, which are set empirically ahead of time. I suspect sigma is particularly sensitive. The paper should more thoroughly explain the intuition for how sigma affects the metric and provide guidance on how to choose it; without this, the work feels incomplete. Can the metric specification be integrated with the LAND learning phase so that rho and sigma are learned jointly?

A key strength of geometric methods is invariance under coordinate transformations. However, the metric in equation 7 is not invariant. For instance, if the data lie along a line in a 2D observation space, the resulting metric differs between a coordinate system whose x-axis aligns with the line and one rotated by, say, 45 degrees. It would be valuable to discuss this more deeply, including whether a full (non-diagonal) metric would recover invariance, and to provide additional intuition for equation 7. There is also a substantial literature on metric learning and manifold learning connected to Riemannian geometry that should be related to this work (see Google “Riemannian” “metric learning” “manifold learning”).

My main concern is with the first experiment: the comparison is not very informative, since the more flexible LAND unsurprisingly fits the data better with fewer components, whereas a GMM with many components could fit better in regimes where LAND would overfit. I recommend redesigning the experiment so each method selects its preferred number of components via a model-selection criterion (e.g., BIC), and then comparing performance. LAND should still have an advantage by capturing manifold structure, and this would be a more meaningful comparison. It would also be useful to show how LAND behaves (and overfits) with a large number of components.

LAND is evidently more computationally demanding than fitting a Gaussian. Please provide quantitative measures or experiments to clarify the computational overhead. What are the practical scaling limits? Could LAND be trained on MNIST and compared with Gaussian mixtures?

====== After reading the authors’ response

LAND is indeed more flexible than a Gaussian. As the number of components increases, each local LAND will be more susceptible to local noise than a local Gaussian. “The geodesics will almost be straight lines” may be true, but they are still curves, and that curvature can induce overfitting. I strongly encourage the authors to revise the experiment accordingly in the revision.
