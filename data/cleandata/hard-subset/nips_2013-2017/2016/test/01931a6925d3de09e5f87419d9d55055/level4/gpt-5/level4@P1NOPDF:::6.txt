This paper presents a locally adaptive normal distribution (LAND). Instead of Euclidean distances, the authors define a smoothly varying Riemannian metric M(x) over the dataset. Given M(x), geodesic distances on the manifold are computed via the exponential and logarithmic maps. The authors use the Euler–Lagrange equations to define a system of ordinary differential equations for estimating geodesics. The logarithm map sends a point y on the manifold to the tangent space at x such that the norm of the resulting vector equals the geodesic distance from x to y. They then define a normal distribution by computing a Mahalanobis distance in the tangent space, using this inner product to measure distances from a point x to the mean instead of Euclidean distances.

They derive maximum-likelihood estimators for the mean and covariance but, since closed forms are unavailable, propose a steepest-descent algorithm to compute them. Because the normalization constant is also intractable, they approximate it via Monte Carlo. They evaluate the approach on synthetic manifold data and a clustering task, comparing against a standard Gaussian mixture model (GMM).

Overall, the paper offers an interesting and useful extension of the normal distribution that adapts to local manifold structure. It combines ideas in metric estimation, geodesic computation, and distribution learning. I believe the work is worthy of publication.

Technical quality comments:
- The manifold machinery (Euler–Lagrange geodesic estimation, logarithm map construction) is sound and clearly explained. The steepest-descent optimization and Monte Carlo estimation of the normalization constant are well motivated and described.
- The empirical analysis is weaker. The choice of the kernel bandwidth σ appears to be tuned on the training data, but this is not clearly specified. The paper also does not sufficiently explain practical benefits of the learned distribution beyond the synthetic demonstrations.

Novelty comments:
- The main contribution seems to be a principled integration of known components—kernel-based construction of M(x), geodesic estimation via Euler–Lagrange, and a Gaussian defined in tangent spaces—together with algorithms for estimating the mean, covariance, and normalization constant. While each component exists in prior work, the combination and end-to-end learning procedure are novel and useful.

Potential impact comments:
- On synthetic data, the comparison between GMMs and LAND based on log-likelihood under models learned from data generated by each method is fair, but the procedure for selecting σ is unclear. Clarifying this is important because σ controls the smoothness of M(x) and can significantly affect performance.
- The paper shows that LAND fits data generated along a manifold well, but does not report how LAND compares to a GMM when the data are actually generated from a single Gaussian or a GMM. Such a baseline would help isolate when LAND provides tangible gains over classical models.
- For the real-world clustering experiment, it appears that σ is chosen to maximize the F-measure, and then that same F-measure is reported. To avoid overfitting, the F-measure should be reported on a held-out test set after hyperparameters are selected on validation data (or this procedure should be made explicit if already done).
- It would be informative to evaluate on very high-dimensional data, where local metric learning and geodesic computation can become challenging and where manifold assumptions are often invoked.
- The authors suggest LAND as a generative model for manifold data. However, they make an unusual assumption: the observed dimension is D and the manifold dimension is also D (the tangent space is D-dimensional and M(x) is full rank). In manifold learning, one typically assumes data lie on a d-dimensional manifold embedded in R^D with D >> d. Discussing whether and how LAND could accommodate d < D (e.g., a rank-deficient M(x) or an explicit low-dimensional chart) would strengthen the practical relevance.

Clarity comments:
- Notational overloading of Σ is confusing. The paper states M = Σ^{-1}, but also estimates M(x) via a local diagonal kernel matrix. Is this Σ the same as the fitted covariance matrix of the LAND? If so, is the kernel-based M(x) merely an initialization? That would be odd because M(x) should vary with x, whereas the fitted covariance is a single global parameter. In the steepest-descent derivation, the substitution “in our case M = Σ^{-1}” is used explicitly. Please clarify the relationship among the kernel-defined M(x), the global covariance Σ of the distribution, and any local approximations, and specify which quantities vary with x and which are global.
- In Section 4.2, the procedure for assigning cluster membership is not stated. I assume you fixed the number of clusters to 2 and assigned points to the closest cluster mean by geodesic distance (via the logarithm map). If this is not the case, please specify the criterion used.
- The introductory exposition on Riemannian manifolds is clear and accessible for readers familiar with the basics.

In summary, the methodology is technically solid and the idea is compelling, but the empirical evaluation needs clearer hyperparameter selection procedures, stronger baselines, and more discussion of dimensionality assumptions. Clarifying the notation and the roles of M(x) and Σ would also improve readability.
