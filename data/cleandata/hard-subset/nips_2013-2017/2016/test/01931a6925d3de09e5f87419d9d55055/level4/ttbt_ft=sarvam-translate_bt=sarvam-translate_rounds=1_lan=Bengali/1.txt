This paper proposes a new parametric metric called LAND, which is defined based on a nonparametric space, drawing concepts from metric learning/manifold learning and Riemannian statistics. The parametric metric is a Riemannian normal distribution defined in Panek [15]. The authors propose a maximum likelihood algorithm to estimate the mean and covariance of the Riemannian normal distribution, i.e., a Mahalanobis distance for the Riemannian manifold. The distance between points is defined as the geodesic with the Riemannian manifold, as in Hauberg et al. [9], and these depend on learning a local metric. Here, the authors propose metric learning using the inverse of the local (কর্ণীয়) covariance. The authors also consider the combination of LAND. This paper brings new insights into the connection between metric learning and Riemannian statistics. Parametric and generative models that can go beyond the training data are very useful in many situations. However, I am mainly concerned about its applicability to real-world data, which is both large-scale and high-dimensional. For LAND, it is necessary to calculate a (here, diagonal) covariance matrix and its inverse matrix for each data point, which is known as a difficult problem in high dimensions. The authors mention high dimensionality as a potential problem (lines 265-266), but the complexity and limitations of the metric presented in the paper need to be analyzed. How high is the dimensionality? 3, 5, 10, 100 dimensions? The literature on metric learning is quite extensive, and I think the paper should be better suited to this context for future readers. Hauberg et al. [9] have already discussed some of the literature on metric learning, such as Fromm et al. [1,2], Malicivec and Efros [6], who all learn a diagonal metric tensor for each point similar to LAND. Local covariance has also been used in methods like local PCA (PCA - Principal Component Analysis through Eigen values and Eigen vectors), even if not for every point. Lines 101-103: For local metric learning, the authors use a Gaussian kernel to define locality. How does the parameter sigma affect the results? The choice of sigma is only discussed for the slip data in sector 4.2, but not for the synthetic data in sector 4.1. The paper should be further tested for sigma. Is sigma the same for all points in low or high-density regions? Figure 4 compares LAND against the underlying assumptions, and the results are quite similar, but the authors do not show the visual results with these underlying assumptions in figures 5 and 6. Instead, visualization results are shown against GMM, which is poorly displayed in figure 4. Therefore, it is less insightful. Some visual results using the underlying model are shown in the supplementary materials (figures 2 and 4), and they surpass GMM. Therefore, these results should probably replace the GMM results in the initial paper or make room for both. An analysis of the computational complexity of LAND versus underlying assumptions would be interesting to see which method should be preferred over the other and for which data structure. The authors also consider the combination of LAND. I think this is one of the most interesting applications of LAND, because data is rarely composed of a single element. This aspect is worth expanding on. The paper is very well written, it is enjoyable to read.
