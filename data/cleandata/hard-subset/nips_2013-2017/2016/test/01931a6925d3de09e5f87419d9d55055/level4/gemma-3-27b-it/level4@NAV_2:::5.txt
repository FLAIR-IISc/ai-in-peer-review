The paper proposes a locally adaptive normal distribution (LAND) grounded in Riemannian geometry, replacing the standard Mahalanobis distance with a geodesic distance learned adaptively from the data. This allows the model to capture nonlinear data structures. The authors also extend the LAND to mixture models and demonstrate its effectiveness on both synthetic and EEG datasets.

The core idea of utilizing a geodesic distance, akin to Isomap, is compelling and represents a valuable contribution. The work bridges Riemannian statistics and manifold learning, providing a theoretical foundation for adapting the normal distribution to nonlinear manifolds. However, several aspects of the paper could benefit from clarification. Specifically, the definition of the inverse covariance weighting in Eq. (7) feels somewhat arbitrary; further intuition regarding the choice of a Gaussian kernel for weighting local covariances would be beneficial. Furthermore, the connection between the local covariance matrix definition in Eq. (7) and its subsequent use in the maximum likelihood estimation (Eq. 11) is unclear and requires more explicit explanation. Are these intended to represent the same quantity, or are they distinct estimations?

My primary concern centers around the scalability and computational efficiency of the proposed method. While the authors mention Monte Carlo integration, a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity – and a comparison of running times to standard EM algorithms – is crucial for assessing its practical applicability. The experimental evaluations, while present, feel somewhat limited. The discussion of the trade-offs involved in choosing the smoothing parameter σ would be particularly helpful, and a more thorough ablation study would strengthen the empirical validation.

Despite these concerns, the paper presents a theoretically interesting contribution with potential for impact. However, its practical relevance remains questionable without further investigation into its computational feasibility.

Minor comments: In Algorithm 1, line 4, “d” should be replaced with “∇” (gradient symbol). Also, throughout the paper, consider using consistent notation; for example, explicitly state when tangent space vectors are column vectors.
