This paper models the observation space with a hand-crafted Riemannian metric and defines a curved normal distribution on the resulting manifold to better capture data geometry. It tackles two fundamental questions: how to specify a Riemannian metric from high-dimensional real-valued observations, and how to define and estimate a curved Gaussian distribution on that geometry. This is a potentially useful tool and an interesting application of Riemannian geometry to statistical learning.

However, there are important concerns about the “metric learning” component in Section 3.1. Despite the title’s suggestion that the metric is learned, Equation (7) specifies a hand-crafted form controlled by two hyperparameters, ρ and σ, which are chosen empirically and fixed. σ, in particular, appears likely to be sensitive. The paper should provide clear intuition about how varying σ affects the geometry and the resulting distribution, practical guidance for selecting it, and an assessment of robustness. Without this discussion, the method feels incomplete. Relatedly, can the metric specification be integrated with LAND estimation so that ρ and σ are learned jointly (e.g., via marginal likelihood or cross-validation), rather than set a priori?

A key advantage of geometric approaches is invariance to coordinate transformations. The metric in Equation (7) does not appear invariant. For example, if the data lie along a line in R2, the metric induced when the x-axis is aligned with the line differs from that obtained after rotating the axes by 45 degrees. The paper should analyze this issue in more depth: under what transformations, if any, is the metric invariant? Would moving from a diagonal to a full metric restore invariance, at least approximately? More intuition for the specific form in Equation (7) would help justify the design choices.

The paper would also benefit from a broader discussion of related work. There is a substantial literature on metric learning and manifold learning with connections to Riemannian geometry (e.g., Riemannian metric learning, information-geometric approaches, and graph/Laplacian-based manifold methods). Positioning the proposed metric and LAND within this context would clarify what is novel and what is inherited.

The experimental evaluation, especially the first experiment, is not fully convincing. As presented, LAND is more flexible than a single Gaussian and can fit the data well with relatively few components. Conversely, a Gaussian mixture can match or exceed performance by increasing the number of components, while LAND risks overfitting as components proliferate. A more meaningful comparison would allow each method to choose its own number of components using a principled criterion (e.g., BIC, AIC, held-out likelihood, or cross-validation), and then compare their selected models. It would also be informative to show how LAND behaves as the number of components grows (e.g., overfitting curves, sensitivity to noise).

Computational cost is another concern. LAND is clearly more expensive than fitting a Gaussian, but the paper does not quantify this. Please report complexity (as a function of n, d, and the number of components), wall-clock times, memory usage, and any acceleration strategies (e.g., approximations for geodesics, sparse neighborhoods). What is the practical scale limit? Can LAND be trained on datasets of the size of MNIST, and how would it compare to Gaussian mixtures under the same selection criterion?

After reading the authors’ response: I agree that LAND is more flexible than a Gaussian. However, increasing the number of components can make each local LAND more susceptible to noise than a local Gaussian. Even if geodesics “almost” become straight lines, they remain curved and can facilitate overfitting in noisy regions. I strongly recommend revising the first experiment to let each method choose its model complexity automatically and to document the overfitting behavior of LAND as components increase.

Overall, the idea is promising and the application of Riemannian geometry is appealing, but the paper would be significantly strengthened by: (1) principled treatment and/or learning of ρ and σ with intuition about their effects; (2) analysis of invariance and clearer justification of the metric in Equation (7); (3) a more rigorous experimental design that controls for model complexity; (4) quantitative assessment of computational cost and scalability; and (5) a more comprehensive connection to related literature.
