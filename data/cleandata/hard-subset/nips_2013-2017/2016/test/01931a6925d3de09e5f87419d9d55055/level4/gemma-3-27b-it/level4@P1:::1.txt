The paper proposes LAND, a novel parametric distribution defined over a non-parametric space, bridging concepts from metric learning/manifold learning and Riemannian statistics. Specifically, LAND leverages a Riemannian normal distribution (Pennec [15]) with a learned, locally adaptive metric. The authors develop a maximum likelihood algorithm to estimate the distribution parameters, effectively defining a Mahalanobis distance on Riemannian manifolds. Distances are computed as geodesics along the learned manifold, utilizing a local metric learned via inverse local (diagonal) covariances, inspired by Hauberg et al. [9]. The authors also extend this to mixtures of LANDs. This work offers valuable insights into the connection between metric learning and Riemannian statistics, offering a parametric and generative model capable of extending beyond the training data – a benefit in many applications. However, a primary concern is its scalability to large-scale, high-dimensional real-world datasets. LAND requires computing a (diagonal) covariance matrix and its inverse for each data point, a computationally challenging task in high dimensions. While the authors acknowledge this limitation (lines 265-266), a more detailed analysis of the complexity and limitations of the proposed metric is needed. Specifically, defining what constitutes “high-dimensional” – 3, 5, 10, 100 dimensions? – would be beneficial. The metric learning literature is extensive, and the paper would gain impact by better situating itself within this context. Hauberg et al. [9] already reference relevant work (e.g., Frome et al. [1,2], Malisiewicz and Efros [6]) that learns a diagonal metric tensor for each point, similar to LAND. Local covariances are also utilized in techniques like local PCA (Kambhatla), though not necessarily for every point. Lines 101-103 discuss the use of a Gaussian kernel to define locality. The influence of the bandwidth parameter σ on the results warrants further investigation. While discussed for the sleep data in Section 4.2, it is not addressed for the synthetic data in Section 4.1. Robustness tests regarding σ would strengthen the paper. Is σ consistent across all points, regardless of density? Fig. 4 compares LAND against intrinsic estimators, showing comparable performance, yet visual results with these intrinsic estimators are absent in Figs. 5 and 6. Instead, comparisons are made to GMMs, which are already shown to perform poorly in Fig. 4, making the visualization less insightful. Including visual results using the intrinsic model – as provided in the supplementary material (Figs. 2 and 4) – would be valuable, potentially replacing the GMM results in the main paper or providing a more comprehensive comparison. Analyzing the computational complexity of LAND versus intrinsic estimators to determine their respective strengths and optimal application scenarios would also be insightful. The authors’ exploration of mixtures of LANDs is particularly promising, as real-world data is rarely generated by a single component. This aspect deserves further expansion. Overall, the paper is exceptionally well-written and a pleasure to read.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel combination of Riemannian statistics and metric learning.
*   Provides a parametric, generative model capable of extrapolating beyond training data.
*   Solid theoretical foundation with clear connections to maximum entropy principles.
*   Demonstrated effectiveness on both synthetic and real datasets (EEG).
*   Well-written and clear presentation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Scalability concerns regarding high-dimensional data.
*   Limited analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed metric.
*   Insufficient positioning within the broader context of metric learning.
*   Incomplete discussion of the influence of the bandwidth parameter σ.
*   Visualization choices could be improved to better highlight the advantages of LAND over intrinsic estimators.
