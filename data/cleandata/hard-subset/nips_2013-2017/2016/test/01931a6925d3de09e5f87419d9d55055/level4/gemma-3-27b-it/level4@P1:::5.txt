The paper proposes a locally adaptive normal distribution based on a geodesic distance on a manifold learned adaptively from the data. This distance replaces the standard Mahalanobis distance used in the Gaussian distribution. The authors also extend this concept to mixtures of LANDs and demonstrate its performance on both synthetic and real-world EEG datasets.

The idea of utilizing a geodesic distance, reminiscent of Isomap, is quite interesting and offers a potentially powerful way to model data lying on nonlinear manifolds. However, several areas could benefit from increased clarity. First, the definition of the inverse of the metric tensor in Eq. (7) appears somewhat ad hoc. Providing more intuition behind using a Gaussian kernel for weighting the local covariance contributions would be valuable. Furthermore, the connection between the local covariance matrix defined in Eq. (7) and its estimation via maximum likelihood in Eq. (11) is unclear. Are these representing the same quantity, or are they distinct estimations? 

My primary concern lies with the scalability and computational efficiency of the proposed method. While the results are promising, a discussion of the computational complexity and runtime compared to standard EM algorithms is crucial. Specifically, details regarding the computational cost of solving the ODEs involved in calculating geodesics and evaluating the exponential and logarithmic maps are needed. Without this information, it's difficult to assess the practical applicability of the LAND. In my opinion, the paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution, but its practical viability remains uncertain.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to adapting the normal distribution to nonlinear manifolds using Riemannian geometry.
*   Theoretical foundation based on maximum entropy principles.
*   Demonstrated performance improvements over standard GMMs on both synthetic and real data.
*   Clear presentation of the mathematical framework.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of detailed computational analysis and scalability discussion.
*   Limited experimental evaluation – more comprehensive comparisons and ablations are needed.
*   Clarity issues regarding the derivation and interpretation of certain equations (e.g., Eq. 7 and its relation to Eq. 11).
*   The reliance on a specific kernel choice (Gaussian) for defining locality without substantial justification.

**Minor comments:**

*   Algorithm 1, line 4: ‘d’ should be replaced with ‘∇’ (gradient).
*   Consider expanding the discussion of related work, specifically mentioning recent advances in manifold learning and generative models.



I recommend accepting this paper contingent upon the authors addressing the concerns raised regarding scalability, computational efficiency, and clarity. A more detailed analysis of the runtime and complexity, along with a clearer explanation of the mathematical derivations and justification of key choices (like the Gaussian kernel), would significantly strengthen the contribution of this work.
