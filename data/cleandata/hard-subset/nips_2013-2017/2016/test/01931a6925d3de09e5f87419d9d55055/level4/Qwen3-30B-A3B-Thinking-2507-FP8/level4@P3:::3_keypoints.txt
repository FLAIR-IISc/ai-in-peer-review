- Introduces LAND: a locally adaptive normal distribution extending multivariate normal to a data-driven Riemannian manifold.  
- Bridges Riemannian statistics and manifold learning by learning a smoothly changing metric from data while keeping mean/covariance parametric.  
- Develops MLE via gradient descent with Monte Carlo integration, extends to mixtures with an EM algorithm, and evaluates on synthetic data and EEG sleep-stage classification.  
- Strength — Originality: meaningful extension of Riemannian statistics to the manifold learning setting; LAND is a natural max-entropy generalization.  
- Strength — Significance: provides a principled parametric model for nonlinear manifold-structured data with demonstrated practical utility (EEG).  
- Strength — Quality: sound theoretical development and a justified, efficient Monte Carlo approach for normalization.  
- Weakness — Metric construction relies on a hand-crafted formula with hyperparameters (ρ, σ); selection and sensitivity of σ are not adequately discussed.  
- Weakness — Coordinate invariance: the proposed metric depends on coordinates and is not invariant under transformations; using full covariance might help.  
- Weakness — Experimental comparison: GMM comparison is unfair since the number of components for GMM is not optimally selected (e.g., via BIC).  
- Weakness — Scalability and cost: lacks quantitative runtime/memory measurements and large-scale experiments to demonstrate scalability.  
- Arguments for acceptance: meaningful theoretical extension, addresses a real limitation of Gaussian models on manifolds, and shows promising EEG results.  
- Arguments against acceptance: unresolved hyperparameter selection, unaddressed coordinate-invariance concern, and unfair/insufficient experimental comparisons.  
- Overall recommendation: promising contribution but needs significant revisions on metric choice, invariance, experimental fairness, and scalability to be strong.  
- Author action items: discuss/consider learning σ, address coordinate invariance (or use full covariance), redesign experiments to select optimal components (e.g., BIC), and provide computational-cost/scalability results.
