Summary:
This paper proposes a Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND) defined on a Riemannian manifold with a smoothly varying metric M(x). Instead of Euclidean distances, the model uses geodesic distances computed via the exponential and logarithm maps. The authors estimate geodesic paths using the Euler–Lagrange equations, yielding ordinary differential equations. The logarithm map takes a point y on the manifold to the tangent space at x, with the vector’s length equal to the geodesic distance from x to y. A normal distribution is then applied in the tangent space by computing a Mahalanobis distance on the mapped points. The authors derive maximum-likelihood estimators for the mean and covariance, solve them via steepest descent, and approximate the intractable normalization constant with Monte Carlo. Experiments include synthetic manifold data and a clustering task, with comparisons to a standard Gaussian mixture model (GMM). Overall, this is an interesting and useful extension of the normal distribution that adapts to local manifold structure by combining metric estimation, geodesic computation, and density learning. I believe it is worthy of publication.

Technical quality:
- The manifold aspects (use of Euler–Lagrange equations and estimation of logarithm maps) are sound and clearly explained.
- The Monte Carlo approximation of the normalization constant and the steepest descent algorithms are clever and well described.
- The analysis of applications is limited. In particular, the choice of the σ parameter appears to be made by optimizing on training data.
- The paper could better articulate the practical usefulness of the learned distribution.

Novelty:
- The main contribution is a thoughtful integration of existing components—kernel-based estimation of a metric M(x), geodesic computation via Euler–Lagrange equations, and a normal distribution in the tangent space—together with an algorithm for estimating the mean, covariance, and normalization constant.

Potential impact:
- The synthetic-data evaluation comparing GMM and LAND by log-likelihood of data generated from the learned distributions is fair, but it is not clear how σ was chosen.
- The paper shows LAND fits data generated along a manifold, but does not evaluate how LAND compares to a GMM when the data are generated from a single Gaussian or a GMM.
- In the real-world clustering experiments, σ for LAND appears to be selected by optimizing the F-measure and then the same F-measure is reported. A fair assessment should report performance on a held-out test set to guard against overfitting; as presented, it is difficult to assess overfitting risk.
- It would be valuable to examine very high-dimensional data.
- One promising application is using LAND as a generative model for manifold data. However, the paper makes an unusual assumption that the observed data are D-dimensional and the manifold dimension is also D (i.e., the tangent vectors are D-dimensional and M(x) is full rank). In manifold learning, one typically considers data lying on a d-dimensional manifold embedded in D-dimensional Euclidean space with d << D.

Clarity:
- Notation for Σ is confusing. The paper states M = Σ^{-1}, but also estimates M via a local diagonal kernel matrix. Is this Σ the same as the fitted covariance matrix? If so, is the kernel just an initialization? This would be odd because M(x) varies with x, whereas the fitted covariance is a single global parameter. In the steepest descent derivations, the paper explicitly substitutes M = Σ^{-1}. Please clarify this relationship.
- In Section 4.2, the procedure for assigning cluster membership is not explicitly stated. I assume two clusters were used and assignments were made by the smallest (geodesic?) distance to cluster means; please make this explicit.
- The introduction to the Riemannian manifold setting is otherwise clear and accessible (for readers familiar with the basics).
