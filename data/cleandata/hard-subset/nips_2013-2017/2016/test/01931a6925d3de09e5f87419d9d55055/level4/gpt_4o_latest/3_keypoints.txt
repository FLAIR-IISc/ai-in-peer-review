- Introduces a hand-crafted Riemannian metric and a curved normal (LAND) to better model data manifolds — a valuable application of Riemannian geometry in statistical learning.  
- Fundamental challenge addressed: constructing a Riemannian metric from high-dimensional observations and defining/estimating a curved Gaussian on that geometry.  
- The metric is not learned despite the title; Eq.7 is hand-crafted with hyperparameters ρ and σ fixed a priori, and σ appears particularly sensitive.  
- The paper lacks discussion on the intuitive effects of varying σ and guidance for selecting ρ and σ; joint learning of these parameters with LAND is suggested.  
- The chosen metric (Eq.7) is not invariant to coordinate transformations; this limitation and whether a full (non-diagonal) metric would restore invariance need discussion.  
- The manuscript should better connect to existing literature on Riemannian metric learning and manifold learning.  
- Experiment 1 comparison is weak: LAND with fewer components naturally fits better, but GMMs could outperform with more components; experiments should let each method select optimal component counts (e.g., via BIC).  
- Need to investigate whether LAND overfits with many components and how local LAND components respond to local noise.  
- Computational cost concerns: LAND is more expensive than Gaussians — provide quantitative runtime/memory measurements and clarify the largest datasets (e.g., MNIST) LAND can handle.  
- After authors’ response, reviewer reiterates that LAND’s flexibility can increase susceptibility to overfitting as component count grows and reaffirms recommendation to redesign the experiments.
