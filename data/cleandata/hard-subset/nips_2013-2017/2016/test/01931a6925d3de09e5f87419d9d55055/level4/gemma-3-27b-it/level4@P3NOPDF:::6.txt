This paper introduces a locally adaptive normal distribution (LAND) that incorporates manifold structure into density estimation. Rather than relying on Euclidean distance, the authors define a Riemannian metric, M(x), which varies smoothly across the dataset, and utilize geodesic distances computed via the exponential and logarithmic maps. They leverage the Euler-Lagrange equations to estimate these geodesic distances and subsequently apply a normal distribution, computing a Mahalanobis distance on points transformed by the logarithm map. This allows for distance calculations from a point *x* to the mean that account for the local manifold geometry. The authors derive maximum likelihood estimators for the mean and covariance, and, because closed-form solutions are unavailable, propose a steepest descent algorithm for parameter estimation, alongside a Monte Carlo approximation for the intractable normalization constant. The method is demonstrated on synthetic data generated on a manifold and applied to a clustering task, comparing its performance to a standard Gaussian mixture model (GMM). Overall, the paper presents a compelling and potentially useful extension of the normal distribution, effectively integrating concepts from metric estimation, geodesic distance computation, and distribution learning. I believe this work is worthy of publication.

**Technical Quality:** The manifold techniques employed – specifically, the use of Euler-Lagrange equations and estimation of logarithm maps – are sound and well explained. The proposed Monte Carlo estimation of the normalization constant and the steepest descent algorithm are both clever and clearly described. However, the analysis of the applications feels somewhat incomplete. The selection of the σ parameter appears to be driven by optimization on the training data, and further justification is needed. Additionally, a more thorough explanation of the practical utility of the learned distribution itself would strengthen the technical presentation.

**Novelty:** The primary contribution of this work appears to be the combination of existing techniques – utilizing kernels to define a metric M(x), estimating geodesic distances via Euler-Lagrange equations, and applying a normal distribution in this learned metric space – and, critically, the development of an algorithm for estimating the key parameters: mean, covariance, and the normalization constant. While not a fundamentally new theoretical breakthrough, the integrated approach and algorithmic development represent a valuable contribution.

**Potential Impact:** The authors provide a reasonable evaluation of the LAND compared to GMM on the synthetic data, assessing log-likelihood performance on data generated from the learned distributions. However, the rationale for choosing the σ parameter remains unclear. Furthermore, the evaluation would benefit from including scenarios where data is generated from a single normal distribution or a standard GMM, allowing for a more comprehensive comparison. In the real-world clustering experiment, the authors optimize the σ value for LAND based on the F-measure, and then present the resulting F-measure. A more rigorous evaluation would involve reporting the F-measure on a held-out test set to address potential overfitting concerns. Exploring the method's performance on very high-dimensional data would also be valuable. The authors highlight the potential of LAND as a generative model for manifold data, but the assumption that the manifold dimension equals the observation dimension (D) is somewhat unusual, particularly given the typical context of manifold learning where data lies on a lower-dimensional manifold *embedded* in a higher-dimensional space.

**Clarity:** The manifold introduction is generally well-explained, assuming some familiarity with Riemannian manifold concepts. However, the notation is occasionally confusing, specifically the overloading of the symbol Σ. The authors state M = Σ<sup>-1</sup>, yet estimate it using a (local) diagonal kernel matrix. It is unclear whether this Σ is the same as the fitted covariance matrix. If so, is the kernel merely used for initialization? This is somewhat puzzling because M(x) should vary with *x*, while a single fitted covariance matrix would be constant. In the proof of the steepest descent algorithm, the authors explicitly state "in our case M = Σ<sup>-1</sup>" and use this substitution, reinforcing the need for clarification. Furthermore, Section 4.2 lacks explicit details regarding cluster membership assignment – the assumption is that clusters are assigned by selecting the closest (geodesic?) distance to the cluster means, but this should be stated directly.
