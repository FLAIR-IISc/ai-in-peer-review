- Paper introduces Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND): replaces Euclidean/Mahalanobis distance with a data-adaptive Riemannian geodesic distance learned nonparametrically.
- Derives tangent-space Monte Carlo scheme for the intractable normalization constant, gradient-based ML for mean/covariance, and an EM algorithm for mixtures.
- Empirical results show better contour fitting on synthetic data and improved clustering alignment on EEG compared to GMMs.
- Connects Riemannian statistics (Pennec) with manifold learning by learning a continuous metric rather than assuming a known manifold.
- Metric is constructed from inverse local diagonal covariances weighted by a Gaussian kernel, inducing geodesics that favor high-density regions.
- Strength: elegant theoretical integration of learned geometry with maximum-entropy modeling and feasible algorithmic workarounds (tangent-space MC, EM mixtures).
- Strength: clear intuition that ML means avoid low-density regions and produce locally adapted contours.
- Weakness: metric construction (Eq.7) seems ad hoc—need intuition/justification for the inverse diagonal form, effects of σ and ρ, and sensitivity to diagonal assumption.
- Weakness: potential confusion between local covariances used to define M(x) and the global model covariance Σ—notation and roles need clearer separation.
- Weakness: missing scalability and runtime analysis—complexity per iteration (Log/Exp map ODE solves), dependence on N, D, K, S, solver tolerance, and wall-clock comparisons to GMMs.
- Weakness: Monte Carlo partition estimation variance unclear (e.g., S=3000); need diagnostics, variance reduction (CRNs, importance sampling, control variates) and stability discussion.
- Weakness: limited empirical evaluation and baselines (no full-covariance GMM, KDE, spectral/manifold clustering or held-out likelihoods); risk of σ overfitting on EEG.
- Weakness: learned metric not invariant to linear transformations—practical implications and remedies (pre-whitening, adaptive metric families) should be discussed.
- Reproducibility: missing solver settings, convergence criteria, S sensitivity, and initialization details—summaries should appear in main text.
- Quality: derivations are sound and optimization choices reasonable, but practical efficiency and robustness remain unclear without runtime/variance analyses.
- Clarity: generally well written but key modeling choices and separation of metric estimator vs model covariance need clearer exposition.
- Originality: moderate-to-strong—learning the metric and ML estimation with tractable normalization ties together separate literatures meaningfully.
- Significance: promising for generative modeling on curved data but currently limited by computational cost and modest empirical scope; stronger scalability and experiments needed.
- Minor: fix notation (use ∇µφ in Alg.1), and correct typos (e.g., “Isomap,” “geodesic,” “covariance,” “complexity”).
- Acceptance arguments: elegant integration of learned Riemannian geometry, solid derivations, practical Monte Carlo approach, and promising qualitative/EEG results.
- Rejection arguments: ad hoc metric design with sensitivity concerns, no complexity/runtime comparison, limited experiments and baselines, potential overfitting in σ.
- Overall assessment: interesting and well-motivated contribution but borderline—requires clearer metric justification, scalability/runtime analysis, variance diagnostics, and broader experiments.
