This paper introduces LAND (Locally Aligned Non-parametric Distributions), a novel parametric metric defined over a non-parametric space. The work bridges concepts from metric learning and Riemannian statistics by modeling the data with a Riemannian normal distribution. This approach effectively learns a Mahalanobis distance on the manifold, where distances between points are measured as geodesics. The local metric at each data point is learned from the inverse of a local, diagonally-constrained covariance matrix. The framework is also extended to handle mixtures of LANDs, allowing for the modeling of more complex, multi-modal data distributions.

The paper's main strengths lie in its novel connection between metric learning and Riemannian statistics, offering fresh insights into the problem. The development of a parametric and generative model that can generalize beyond the training data is a valuable contribution with many potential applications. The paper is exceptionally well-written and clear, making it a pleasure to read.

Despite these strengths, there are several areas where the paper could be significantly improved:

**1. Scalability and Applicability to High-Dimensional Data:**
The primary concern is the method's practical applicability to the large-scale, high-dimensional datasets common in machine learning. LAND's core mechanism requires computing and inverting a local covariance matrix for every single data point. This operation is computationally intensive, and its feasibility in high-dimensional settings is questionable. While the authors briefly acknowledge high-dimensionality as a potential issue (lines 265-266), the paper lacks a formal analysis of the algorithm's computational complexity and its limitations. It is crucial to quantify these limitations and provide guidance on what constitutes "high-dimensional" in this context. For instance, is the method practical for 10, 100, or more dimensions? An analysis of complexity would be essential for a practitioner to assess the suitability of LAND for their problem.

**2. Positioning within the Metric Learning Literature:**
While the paper does a good job of framing its contributions in the context of Riemannian statistics, its positioning within the vast metric learning literature could be stronger. The idea of learning a local, diagonal metric tensor is not new; related approaches exist in works by Frome et al. [1,2] and Malisiewicz and Efros [6], among others. Similarly, the use of local covariance information has precedents in methods like Local Principal Component Analysis. A more thorough discussion of these related works would better contextualize the specific contributions of LAND and clarify its advantages over existing local metric learning techniques.

**3. Experimental Comparisons and Visualizations:**
The experimental evaluation could be made more compelling. In the quantitative results (Fig. 4), LAND is shown to perform on par with intrinsic estimators. However, in the qualitative visualizations (Figs. 5 and 6), LAND is compared only against Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs), which Figure 4 already established as a weak baseline for this data. This makes the visual evidence for LAND's superiority less insightful than it could be. The supplementary material includes visualizations comparing LAND to the intrinsic model, which are far more informative. These results should be moved into the main paper, either replacing or augmenting the GMM comparisons, to provide a stronger and more direct visual validation of the method's benefits. Furthermore, a computational time comparison between LAND and the intrinsic estimators would be valuable to understand the trade-offs between the two similarly performing methods.

**4. Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**
The method relies on a Gaussian kernel to define locality, which is governed by a bandwidth parameter `sigma`. The paper's analysis of this critical hyperparameter is insufficient. The choice of `sigma` is only discussed for the sleep dataset, with no mention of its selection or influence on the synthetic data results. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a robustness analysis demonstrating how the model's performance varies with `sigma`. Additionally, it is unclear if `sigma` is a global parameter. If so, its performance in regions of varying data density could be suboptimal. A discussion on the potential for an adaptive bandwidth would be welcome.

**5. Mixtures of LANDs:**
The extension of LAND to mixtures is one of its most interesting and practically relevant contributions, as real-world data is rarely generated from a single component. This aspect of the paper feels somewhat underdeveloped and would be worth expanding. A more detailed exploration of this model, perhaps with additional experiments on multi-modal datasets, could elevate its impact.

In summary, the paper presents a novel and elegant idea with theoretical merit. However, its practical utility is hampered by significant, unaddressed concerns about scalability and a lack of thorough experimental validation against strong baselines. Addressing the points above, particularly regarding computational complexity, hyperparameter sensitivity, and more rigorous experimental comparisons, would substantially strengthen the paper.
