

The paper introduces LAND, a novel parametric metric defined over a nonparametric space by integrating concepts from metric learning, manifold learning, and Riemannian statistics. The parametric metric is formulated as a Riemannian normal distribution (Pennec [15]), with the authors proposing a maximum likelihood algorithm to estimate its mean and covariance—effectively defining a Mahalanobis distance for Riemannian manifolds. Distances between points are computed as geodesics along the manifold (Hauberg et al. [9]), with the metric learned using inverses of local (diagonal) covariances. The authors also explore mixtures of LANDs. The work provides valuable insights into the intersection of metric learning and Riemannian statistics, highlighting the utility of parametric, generative models that generalize beyond training data. However, the primary concern is the method’s applicability to real-world data, which is typically large-scale and high-dimensional. LAND requires computing a (diagonal) covariance matrix and its inverse for each data point, a computationally challenging task in high dimensions. While the authors acknowledge high-dimensionality as a potential issue (lines 265–266), the paper lacks a thorough analysis of the metric’s complexity and limitations. Specifically, it does not clarify the practical threshold for "high-dimensional" (e.g., 3, 5, 10, or 100 dimensions), which is critical for readers assessing scalability.  

The paper also needs stronger contextualization within the extensive literature on metric learning. Hauberg et al. [9] already reference related work (e.g., Frome et al. [1,2], Malisiewicz and Efros [6]), which similarly learn a diagonal metric tensor per point—making LAND’s novelty less distinct. Local covariances have also appeared in methods like local PCA (Kambhatla), though not per point. Additionally, the Gaussian kernel parameter σ (lines 101–103) governing locality is only analyzed for the sleep dataset (Section 4.2), not the synthetic data (Section 4.1). Robustness tests across σ values are needed, including whether σ is uniform across low- and high-density regions.  

Figure 4 compares LAND against intrinsic estimators, showing similar performance, yet Figures 5–6 visualize results only against GMM (which Fig. 4 already demonstrates underperforms). This omission renders the visualizations less insightful. Supplementary material (Figs. 2, 4) shows intrinsic estimators outperforming GMM, suggesting these results should replace or supplement the main paper’s GMM comparisons. A computational complexity analysis of LAND versus intrinsic estimators would clarify which method is preferable for specific data structures.  

The mixture-of-LANDs extension is particularly compelling, as real-world data rarely arises from a single component. This aspect warrants deeper exploration. Despite these concerns, the paper is exceptionally well-written and a pleasure to read.
