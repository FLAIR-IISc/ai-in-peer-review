This paper proposes a locally adaptive normal distribution (LAND) that generalizes the normal distribution to the "manifold" setting, where data is assumed to lie near a potentially low-dimensional manifold embedded in R^D. The LAND is defined using a locally adaptive Riemannian metric that favors regions of high local density, and is the maximum entropy distribution under the given metric. The authors develop a maximum likelihood algorithm to infer the distribution parameters, which relies on a combination of gradient descent and Monte Carlo integration. They also extend the LAND to mixture models and provide the corresponding EM algorithm.

The paper addresses a fundamental problem in statistical learning, namely how to define a distribution that can capture the nonlinear structure of high-dimensional data. The proposed method is a useful tool for modeling complex data, and the application of Riemannian geometry in statistical learning is an interesting and novel approach.

The authors demonstrate the efficiency of the LAND on both synthetic and real data, including EEG measurements of human sleep stages. The results show that the LAND can capture the nonlinear structure of the data and outperform a standard Gaussian mixture model.

However, there are some limitations and potential improvements that can be made. For example, the choice of the parameter σ used for smoothing the Riemannian metric is fixed empirically in advance, and it would be useful to discuss the effect of varying σ and how to select this parameter. Additionally, the metric learning stage can be merged with the LAND learning stage, so that the parameters can be learned jointly.

The paper also lacks a quantitative comparison of the computational cost of the LAND algorithm with other methods, such as Gaussian mixture models. It would be useful to provide some experiments or measurements to demonstrate the scalability of the LAND algorithm and its limit.

Overall, the paper proposes a novel and useful approach to modeling complex data, and the results demonstrate its potential. However, there are some limitations and potential improvements that can be made to make the paper more complete and convincing.
