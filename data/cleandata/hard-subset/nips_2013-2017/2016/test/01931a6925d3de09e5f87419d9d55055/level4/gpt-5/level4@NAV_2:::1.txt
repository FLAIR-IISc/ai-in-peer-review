The paper presents LAND, a parametric generative model defined over a nonparametric space, connecting ideas from metric/manifold learning and Riemannian statistics. Concretely, it instantiates the Riemannian normal distribution of Pennec [15] and provides a maximum-likelihood procedure to estimate its mean and covariance (i.e., a Mahalanobis structure on a Riemannian manifold). Distances are geodesics on a learned manifold following Hauberg et al. [9], with the local metric specified as the inverse of pointwise (diagonal) local covariances; mixtures of LANDs are also developed. The work helps clarify the link between metric learning and Riemannian statistics, and a parametric, generative model that extrapolates beyond training data is valuable in many settings. My primary concern, however, is applicability to real-world data that are both large-scale and high-dimensional. LAND requires, for each point, computing a (diagonal) local covariance and its inverse, which becomes challenging in high dimensions. While the authors mention high dimensionality as a potential issue (lines 265–266), the paper would benefit from a concrete analysis of the computational complexity and limitations of the proposed metric. What dimensional ranges remain practical—3, 5, 10, 100? The metric learning literature is extensive, and stronger contextualization would aid future readers. Hauberg et al. [9] already discuss related work, e.g., Frome et al. [1,2] and Malisiewicz and Efros [6], who also learn a diagonal metric tensor per point similar to LAND. Local covariances are also common in methods such as local PCA (Kambhatla—Dimension reduction by local principal component analysis), even if not at every point. Lines 101–103: for learning the local metric, the authors use a Gaussian kernel to define locality. How sensitive are results to the kernel width σ? The choice of σ is only discussed for the sleep data in Sect. 4.2, but not for the synthetic data in Sect. 4.1; robustness analyses w.r.t. σ would be helpful. Is σ fixed across the space, regardless of local density? Fig. 4 compares LAND against intrinsic estimators and the performance is fairly close, yet Figs. 5 and 6 visualize only GMM baselines, which already underperform in Fig. 4; this is therefore less informative. Some visual comparisons with intrinsic estimators appear in the supplement (Figs. 2 and 4) and they outperform GMM; including these in the main paper—or alongside the GMM—would strengthen the case. It would also be useful to analyze the computational complexity of LAND versus intrinsic estimators to understand which approach is preferable for which data structures. The extension to mixtures of LANDs is, in my view, one of the most compelling aspects, since real data are seldom unimodal; this part could be expanded. The paper is very well written and a pleasure to read.
