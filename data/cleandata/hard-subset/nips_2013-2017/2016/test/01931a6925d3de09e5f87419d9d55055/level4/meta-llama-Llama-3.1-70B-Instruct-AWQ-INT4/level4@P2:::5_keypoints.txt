- Proposes a locally adaptive normal distribution (LAND) using a learned geodesic manifold traversal distance replacing the Mahalanobis/Eculidean distance.  
- Extends the idea to mixtures of LANDs and evaluates on a synthetic dataset and EEG data.  
- Core idea: replace Euclidean distance with a locally adaptive nonlinear distance by learning a non-parametric metric from local inverse covariance matrices.  
- Locality is defined via a Gaussian kernel weighting (kernel smoothing) over local covariances.  
- Approach is conceptually similar to Isomap and using geodesic distances on Riemannian manifolds is an interesting idea.  
- Clarity issues: the inverse metric tensor definition in Eq. (7) appears ad hoc and needs more intuition.  
- Unclear relationship between the local covariance in Eq. (7) and its MAP estimation in Eq. (11); need clarification if they are the same and how they relate.  
- Experimental evaluation is limited (only synthetic + EEG); lacks comparisons to state-of-the-art methods and robustness analyses.  
- Main concern: scalability and computational efficiency are not analyzed or compared to standard EM.  
- Paper lacks detailed computational complexity and running-time analysis for large datasets.  
- Conclusion: interesting theoretical contribution but practical applicability is uncertain without stronger experiments and efficiency analysis.  
- Strength: geodesic distance idea is promising and broadly applicable.  
- Strength: provides a clear, concise introduction to Riemannian manifolds for ML readers.  
- Weakness: Eq. (7) definition seems ad hoc; intuition for Gaussian-weighted covariance sums is missing.  
- Suggestions: add intuition for Gaussian kernel weighting, clarify Eq.(7) vs Eq.(11), include broader empirical comparisons and robustness checks, and provide detailed complexity/runtime and scalability analysis.
