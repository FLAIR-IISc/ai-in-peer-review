The paper introduces LAND, a novel parametric metric defined on a nonparametric space, effectively bridging metric learning, manifold learning, and Riemannian statistics. Specifically, the learned metric takes the form of a Riemannian normal distribution, as defined by Pennec [15], and the authors propose a maximum likelihood approach to estimate its mean and covariance – effectively a Mahalanobis distance adapted for Riemannian manifolds. Point-to-point distances are computed as geodesics along the manifold, leveraging a locally learned metric. This metric learning process utilizes the inverses of local (diagonal) covariances. The authors also extend LAND to mixtures of distributions. This work offers valuable new insights into the connections between metric learning and Riemannian statistics, and the development of parametric, generative models capable of generalizing beyond training data is a significant contribution. 

However, a primary concern is the practical applicability of LAND to large-scale, high-dimensional real-world datasets. The method requires computing and inverting a (diagonal) covariance matrix for each data point, operations known to be computationally challenging in high dimensions. While the authors acknowledge high-dimensionality as a potential limitation (lines 265-266), a more detailed analysis of the method’s complexity and limitations is necessary. Specifically, it would be helpful to define what constitutes “high-dimensional” in this context – is it 3, 5, 10, or 100 dimensions? The field of metric learning is extensive, and the paper would benefit from a more thorough positioning within this existing literature. Hauberg et al. [9] already survey relevant work, including Frome et al. [1,2] and Malisiewicz and Efros [6], who similarly learn point-wise diagonal metric tensors. The use of local covariances also appears in methods like local PCA (Kambhatla), although not necessarily for every point. 

Regarding implementation details, lines 101-103 describe the use of a Gaussian kernel to define locality for metric learning. The influence of the kernel parameter, sigma, on the results warrants further investigation. While discussed for the sleep data in Section 4.2, it is not addressed for the synthetic data in Section 4.1, and robustness tests concerning sigma’s value would strengthen the paper. Specifically, is a single sigma value appropriate for both low- and high-density regions? In Figure 4, LAND is compared to intrinsic estimators with comparable performance, yet Figures 5 and 6 omit visual results for these intrinsic estimators, instead focusing on comparisons against GMM, which Figure 4 already demonstrates performs poorly. This limits the insights gained from these figures. Visual results for the intrinsic model, present in the supplementary material (Figures 2 and 4) and showing superior performance to GMM, would be more informative in the main paper, either replacing the GMM results or being presented alongside them. A comparative analysis of the computational complexity of LAND versus intrinsic estimators would also be valuable, clarifying which method is preferable for different data types and structures. 

The consideration of mixtures of LANDs is a particularly interesting aspect of the work, as real-world data is rarely generated by a single component. This area merits further exploration and expansion. Overall, the paper is exceptionally well-written and a pleasure to read.
