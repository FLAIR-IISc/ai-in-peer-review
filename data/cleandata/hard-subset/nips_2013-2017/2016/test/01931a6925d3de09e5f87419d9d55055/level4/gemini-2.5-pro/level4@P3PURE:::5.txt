**Summary**

This paper proposes a locally adaptive normal (LAND) distribution based on a geodesic manifold traversal distance that is learned adaptively from the data. This geodesic distance replaces the standard Mahalanobis distance used in the Gaussian probability density function. The paper also presents an extension to a mixture of LANDs model. The performance of the proposed method is evaluated on a synthetic dataset and a real-world EEG dataset.

**Major Comments**

The core idea of defining a probability distribution using a geodesic distance, similar to the approach in Isomap, is novel and interesting. However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses, primarily related to clarity and the experimental evaluation.

1.  The definition of the inverse metric tensor in Equation (7) appears ad hoc. The authors should provide more intuition for constructing it as a weighted sum of local covariances using a Gaussian kernel. What is the justification for this specific choice?

2.  The connection between the definition of the local covariance matrix in Equation (7) and its subsequent MAP estimation in Equation (11) is unclear. It was difficult to follow whether these equations refer to the same quantity. The authors should clarify this relationship.

3.  The experimental evaluations are not sufficiently thorough. The results on a single synthetic dataset and one real-world dataset provide limited evidence of the method's effectiveness and advantages over existing approaches.

4.  My main concern is the scalability and computational efficiency of the proposed method. The paper does not include an analysis of the computational complexity or a comparison of running times against standard methods like the EM algorithm for Gaussian mixtures. This omission makes it difficult to assess the practical applicability of the method. While the paper offers an interesting theoretical contribution, its utility in practice is questionable without such an analysis.

**Minor Comments**

In Algorithm 1, lines 4 and 5, the notation 'd' should likely be the gradient symbol '∇'.
