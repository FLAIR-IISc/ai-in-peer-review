- Paper proposes modeling observation space with a hand-crafted Riemannian metric and defining a curved normal (LAND) to better capture data manifolds — useful and interesting application of Riemannian geometry.
- Section title “learns a metric” is misleading: metric (Eq.7) is hand-crafted and depends on fixed hyperparameters rho and sigma chosen empirically.
- Sigma likely sensitive; paper lacks intuition, guidance, or experiments showing effects of varying sigma and how to select it.
- Authors should consider or discuss jointly learning rho and sigma with LAND instead of fixing them a priori.
- Metric in Eq.7 appears not invariant to coordinate transformations; this invariance issue requires a clearer discussion and justification.
- Question whether a full (non-diagonal) metric would restore invariance and why the diagonal choice was made; more intuition for Eq.7 is needed.
- Related work in metric learning, manifold learning, and Riemannian methods is under-discussed and should be positioned relative to this paper.
- Current experimental comparison to GMM is uninformative; each method should select its number of components via a principled criterion (e.g., BIC) before comparison.
- Experiments should show how LAND behaves and overfits as number of components grows, since curvature can increase vulnerability to local noise.
- Computational cost concerns: provide quantitative runtime, memory, and scaling measurements; clarify practical dataset size limits (e.g., can LAND be trained on MNIST?).
- Reviewer’s response note: LAND’s added flexibility means local LANDs are more prone to overfitting/noise than local Gaussians even when geodesics are nearly straight; experimental design should be revised accordingly.
