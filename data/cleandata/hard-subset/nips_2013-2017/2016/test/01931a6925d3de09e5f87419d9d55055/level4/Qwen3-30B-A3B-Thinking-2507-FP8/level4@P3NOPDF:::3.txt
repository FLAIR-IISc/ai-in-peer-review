

This paper proposes a novel approach to modeling data manifolds by defining a Riemannian metric and a "curved normal distribution" (LAND) based on this geometry. The core idea—leveraging Riemannian geometry to better capture data structure—is theoretically interesting and potentially valuable for statistical learning. However, the paper contains significant technical gaps and experimental shortcomings that undermine its contribution. Below, I detail key concerns.

**Critical Technical Gaps:**  
The paper claims to "learn a metric" (Section 3.1), yet the metric in Equation 7 is explicitly hand-crafted using fixed hyper-parameters ρ and σ, determined empirically without justification. The sensitivity of σ to the metric's curvature is unexplored: how does σ affect the local geometry? What is the intuitive effect of varying σ? Crucially, the authors do not discuss *how* to select σ or ρ, rendering the method incomplete. A major opportunity is missed: could ρ and σ be jointly optimized with the LAND parameters? The paper should address this to align with the stated goal of metric learning.

Furthermore, the metric in Equation 7 violates a fundamental principle of Riemannian geometry: coordinate invariance. For instance, consider data lying on a line in ℝ². In a coordinate system where the x-axis aligns with this line, the metric tensor (Equation 7) becomes diagonal with small off-diagonal terms. In a rotated system (e.g., 45°), the metric’s off-diagonal terms become significant, yielding a qualitatively different geometry. This inconsistency contradicts the strength of geometric approaches. The authors must clarify whether the full (non-diagonal) metric would resolve this, and provide intuition for Equation 7’s design. Related work on Riemannian metric learning (e.g., [1,2]) should be cited to contextualize this omission.

**Experimental Weaknesses:**  
The first experiment compares LAND and GMM using *fixed* component counts (e.g., LAND with K=5 vs. GMM with K=5). This is misleading: LAND’s flexibility allows it to model complex manifolds with fewer components, while GMM requires more components to achieve similar expressiveness. The comparison unfairly favors LAND when using small K, and GMM outperforms LAND at large K (where GMM overfits). A fairer evaluation would let each model select its optimal K via BIC (or similar), then compare performance. The authors’ response notes LAND overfits with many components—a valid point—but this should be demonstrated *in the main experiments* (e.g., by plotting performance vs. K for both models). The current setup obscures LAND’s true advantage: better manifold capture *with fewer components*.

**Missing Scalability Analysis:**  
LAND is computationally more expensive than Gaussian estimation (e.g., due to geodesic computations). The paper lacks quantitative analysis of this cost: How does runtime scale with data size? What is the practical upper limit for LAND (e.g., on MNIST)? A concrete comparison on MNIST—where LAND’s geometric advantage could be tested against GMM—would strengthen the paper. The authors should include runtime benchmarks and scalability tests to address feasibility.

**Overall Assessment:**  
The theoretical motivation is compelling, but the work is incomplete without addressing the metric’s design, invariance, and experimental rigor. The current version risks misleading readers about LAND’s advantages. To be accepted, the authors must:  
1. Justify and optimize hyper-parameters (ρ, σ) via principled learning.  
2. Resolve the coordinate invariance issue and clarify Equation 7’s geometric interpretation.  
3. Redesign experiments to use model-selection criteria (e.g., BIC) for fair component-count comparison.  
4. Provide scalability analysis (runtime, MNIST test).  

The authors’ response acknowledges LAND’s overfitting risk with excessive components, but this insight should be integrated into the revised experiments. Without these revisions, the paper’s contribution remains speculative rather than demonstrated.
