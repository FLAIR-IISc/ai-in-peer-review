The paper proposes LAND, a new parametric metric defined over a nonparametric space, by bridging concepts from metric learning, manifold learning, and Riemannian statistics. The parametric metric learned is a Riemannian normal distribution as defined in Pennec [15]. The authors propose a maximum likelihood algorithm to estimate the mean and covariance of the Riemannian normal distribution, effectively learning a Mahalanobis distance for Riemannian manifolds. Distances between points are defined as geodesics along the Riemannian manifold, similar to Hauberg et al. [9], and rely on learning a local metric. Notably, the authors propose to learn the metric by using the inverses of the local (diagonal) covariances. Additionally, the authors consider mixtures of LANDs, which is a promising direction for modeling complex data. The paper brings new insights into the connection between metric learning and Riemannian statistics, and having parametric and generative models that can generalize beyond the training data is valuable in many scenarios.

However, my primary concern is the applicability of LAND to real-world data, which is often large-scale and high-dimensional. LAND requires computing a (diagonal) covariance matrix for each data point and its inverse, which can be challenging in high dimensions. Although the authors mention high-dimensionality as a potential issue (lines 265-266), a thorough analysis of the complexity and limitations of the metric introduced is necessary. Specifically, it would be helpful to know how high-dimensional the data can be before LAND becomes impractical (e.g., 3, 5, 10, or 100 dimensions).

Furthermore, the paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of its position within the vast literature on metric learning. Hauberg et al. [9] already discuss some relevant literature, such as Frome et al. [1,2] and Malisiewicz and Efros [6], who all learn a diagonal metric tensor for every point, similar to LAND. Local covariances have also been used in methods like local PCA (Kambhatla – Dimension reduction by local principal component analysis), although not for every point.

In learning the local metric, the authors use a Gaussian kernel to define locality, but the influence of the parameter sigma on the results is not thoroughly explored. While the choice of sigma is discussed for the sleep data in Sect. 4.2, it is not discussed for the synthetic data in Sect. 4.1. Robustness tests for sigma would be beneficial, and it is unclear whether sigma is the same for all points, regardless of whether they are in low- or high-density regions.

The comparison of LAND to intrinsic estimators in Fig. 4 shows similar results, but the authors do not provide visual results using these intrinsic estimators in Figs. 5 and 6. Instead, they show visualization results against GMM, which are already shown in Fig. 4 to perform poorly. This is less insightful, as some visual results using the intrinsic model are shown in the supplementary material (Fig. 2 and 4) and outperform GMM. Therefore, it would be beneficial to replace the GMM results in the initial paper with the intrinsic model results or make room for both.

A comparison of the computational complexity of LAND versus intrinsic estimators would be valuable in determining which method is favored over the other and for which data structures. The authors' consideration of mixtures of LANDs is also a promising direction, as real-world data is rarely generated by a single component. This aspect is worth expanding upon.

Overall, the paper is well-written and a pleasure to read.
