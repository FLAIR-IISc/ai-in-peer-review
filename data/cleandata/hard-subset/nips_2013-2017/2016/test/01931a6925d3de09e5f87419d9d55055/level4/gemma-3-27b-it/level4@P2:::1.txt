This paper introduces a Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), bridging manifold learning and Riemannian statistics to create a parametric model defined over a non-parametric space. The core idea is to replace the Euclidean metric in a standard normal distribution with a locally adaptive Riemannian metric, learned from the data. This results in a Riemannian normal distribution, estimated via a novel maximum likelihood approach. The authors develop algorithms for both single and mixtures of LANDs, demonstrating their efficacy on synthetic and EEG sleep stage data. 

The work offers a compelling connection between metric learning and Riemannian statistics, potentially offering a generative model capable of extrapolating beyond training data – a valuable attribute in many machine learning scenarios. It builds upon prior work in Riemannian statistics [15] and metric learning [9], presenting a unique approach to defining a parametric distribution over a non-parametrically learned space. 

However, a key concern lies in the scalability of LAND to high-dimensional, large-scale datasets. The method requires computing and inverting local covariance matrices for each data point, a computationally demanding task in high dimensions. While the authors acknowledge this limitation (lines 265-266), a more detailed analysis of the complexity and practical limitations with respect to dimensionality is needed. Specifically, clarifying the dimensionality threshold where the approach becomes computationally prohibitive (e.g., 3, 5, 10, 100 dimensions) would be beneficial. The extensive literature on metric learning warrants a more thorough discussion; the authors should position their work more clearly within this context, perhaps referencing works such as Frome et al. [1,2], Malisiewicz and Efros [6], and local PCA methods like Kambhatla’s work.

The choice of the Gaussian kernel bandwidth, σ, for defining locality deserves further attention. While discussed for the sleep data (Sect. 4.2), its impact on the synthetic data results is not explored. Investigating the robustness of the method to variations in σ, and whether a single global σ is appropriate for both high- and low-density regions, would strengthen the analysis. 

The comparative evaluation primarily focuses on a Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM), which, as shown in Fig. 4, consistently underperforms LAND. While comparisons to intrinsic estimators are reported, the lack of visual comparisons in Figs. 5 and 6 hinders a comprehensive understanding of the benefits of LAND. The supplementary material includes results with intrinsic estimators that outperform GMM, suggesting these should be incorporated into the main paper, either replacing or supplementing the GMM comparisons. A computational complexity analysis comparing LAND to intrinsic estimators would also be valuable for guiding practical application.

The extension to mixtures of LANDs is a promising direction, representing a more realistic modeling assumption. Expanding on this aspect, potentially exploring different mixture model selection criteria, would enhance the contribution.

Overall, the paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound approach bridging manifold learning and Riemannian statistics.
*   Demonstrated efficacy on both synthetic and real-world data.
*   Clear and well-structured presentation.
*   Potential for broader application in generative modeling.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Scalability concerns regarding high-dimensional data.
*   Limited discussion of the method's positioning within the broader context of metric learning.
*   Insufficient analysis of the impact of the Gaussian kernel bandwidth (σ).
*   Comparative evaluation primarily against a relatively weak baseline (GMM).
