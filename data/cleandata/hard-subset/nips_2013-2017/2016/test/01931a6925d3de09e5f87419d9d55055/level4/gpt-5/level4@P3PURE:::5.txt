Summary: The paper introduces a locally adaptive normal distribution (LAND) that replaces the standard Mahalanobis distance with a geodesic manifold traversal distance learned adaptively from data. The approach is extended to mixtures of LANDs. Performance is evaluated on a synthetic dataset and a real-world EEG dataset.

Major comments:
- The central idea of using a geodesic distance—akin to the intuition behind Isomap—is compelling and theoretically interesting. However, several aspects of the presentation lack clarity.
- The definition of the inverse metric tensor in Eq. (7) appears ad hoc. Please provide more intuition for using a Gaussian kernel to weight the local covariance matrices in the weighted sum. Why is a Gaussian kernel the right choice here, and how sensitive is the method to the kernel bandwidth?
- There is confusion between the local covariance matrix defined in Eq. (7) and the subsequent MAP estimation in Eq. (11). Are these referring to the same quantity, or are they conceptually distinct (e.g., an initial definition versus an estimated parameter)? Clarifying the relationship and the role each plays in the learning procedure would help significantly.
- The experimental evaluation is rather weak. The scope and depth of the experiments do not convincingly demonstrate the practical benefits of LANDs over standard methods.
- My primary concern is scalability and computational efficiency. Please provide a clear analysis of computational complexity and report running times, especially in comparison to standard EM for Gaussian mixtures. Without this, it is difficult to assess whether the method is practical for larger datasets.

Overall assessment: The paper offers an interesting theoretical contribution, but in its current form it is unlikely to be broadly applicable in practice due to concerns about clarity, scalability, and empirical validation.

Minor comments:
- Algorithm 1, lines 4 and 5: replace d with ∇.
