

This paper proposes modeling the observation space using a hand-designed Riemannian metric and defines a curved Gaussian distribution based on the resulting geometry to better capture the data manifold. It addresses two key challenges: learning a Riemannian metric from high-dimensional real-valued data and estimating the corresponding curved Gaussian distribution. This offers a valuable application of Riemannian geometry in statistical learning.  

The method defines the underlying Riemannian geometry in Section 3.1. However, the title claims the metric is "learned," while Equation 7 shows it is hand-designed using two hyperparameters, ρ and σ, which are fixed empirically. The paper should explicitly discuss the intuitive impact of varying σ and provide guidance for its selection, as σ appears sensitive. Crucially, it is unclear whether ρ and σ could be jointly learned with the LAND model—this would strengthen the approach.  

A key strength of geometric methods is coordinate invariance, but the metric in Equation 7 lacks this property. Consider data lying on a line in 2D space: the metric becomes diagonal in a coordinate system aligned with the line but gains off-diagonal terms when the coordinate axes are rotated (e.g., by 45°). This alters the geometry, contradicting the expected invariance. The paper should clarify whether the full metric (not just the diagonal form) achieves invariance and provide deeper intuition for Equation 7. The work would benefit from connecting to established literature on Riemannian metric learning and manifold learning (e.g., search "Riemannian metric learning manifold learning").  

The primary criticism concerns the first experiment. The comparison is unbalanced: LAND’s flexibility allows it to describe the data better with fewer components, while GMM requires more components to achieve similar fit (and may overfit with many components). Instead, the authors should let each model select its optimal component count via a criterion like BIC, then compare performance. This would fairly assess LAND’s advantage in capturing manifold structure, rather than conflating model flexibility with geometry. The observation that LAND overfits with many components is useful, but the experiment design obscures this.  

The paper also lacks quantitative analysis of computational cost. LAND is inherently more expensive than Gaussian estimation—how much more? What is its scalability limit? Experiments on larger datasets (e.g., MNIST) would clarify practical feasibility and provide a meaningful benchmark against Gaussian mixture models.  

Regarding the authors’ response: They note that increasing LAND components makes local models more vulnerable to noise and that "geodesics will almost be straight lines." While geodesics approximate straight lines in the limit, their curvature still induces overfitting, especially with many components. This point requires explicit revision in the paper. The suggested experiment redesign (using BIC for component selection) remains essential to demonstrate LAND’s geometric advantage fairly.  

In summary, the paper presents an interesting geometric framework but requires critical revisions: (1) clarify the metric’s dependence on fixed hyperparameters and explore joint learning; (2) address coordinate invariance and provide intuition for Equation 7; (3) redesign experiments to fairly compare models using optimized component counts; and (4) add computational scalability analysis. Without these, the method’s contribution and practical value remain unclear.
