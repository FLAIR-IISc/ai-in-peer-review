The paper introduces LAND, a novel parametric metric constructed within a nonparametric framework by integrating concepts from metric learning, manifold learning, and Riemannian statistics. Specifically, LAND learns a Riemannian normal distribution – a Mahalanobis distance on Riemannian manifolds, as defined by Pennec [15] – utilizing a maximum likelihood approach to estimate its mean and covariance. The method defines distances via geodesics on the manifold (Hauberg et al. [9]) and learns the metric through the inverse of local diagonal covariances. The authors extend the framework to mixtures of LANDs, offering potentially valuable generative capabilities beyond the training data. This work offers insightful connections between metric learning and Riemannian statistics. 

However, a key concern lies in the practical applicability of LAND to large-scale, high-dimensional datasets. Computing and inverting covariance matrices for each data point, as required by LAND, poses known challenges in high dimensions. While the authors acknowledge this limitation (lines 265-266), a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity and scaling behavior is needed. Defining the threshold for “high-dimensional” – for example, 3, 5, 10, or 100 dimensions – would be beneficial. Furthermore, the paper would benefit from a more comprehensive positioning within the existing literature on metric learning. Relevant works such as Frome et al. [1,2], Malisiewicz and Efros [6], and even local PCA (Kambhatla), which employ similar local diagonal metrics or covariance estimations, deserve more thorough discussion, building upon the related work mentioned in Hauberg et al. [9].

Several specific points warrant clarification. The influence of the Gaussian kernel parameter, sigma, on the learned metric is not consistently addressed; while discussed for sleep data (Sect. 4.2), it’s absent from the analysis of synthetic data (Sect. 4.1). Robustness tests concerning sigma’s selection, and whether a uniform sigma is optimal across varying data densities, would strengthen the paper. The comparison in Fig. 4 includes intrinsic estimators, yet Figs. 5 and 6 lack corresponding visualizations, instead focusing on GMM, which Fig. 4 already demonstrates performs poorly. Including results comparing LAND to intrinsic estimators – as shown in the supplementary material (Figs. 2 & 4) – would provide more informative insights. A computational complexity analysis contrasting LAND and intrinsic estimators would help determine appropriate use cases for each method based on data characteristics. 

The extension to mixtures of LANDs is a particularly promising direction, given the prevalence of multi-component data. Expanding on this aspect would further enhance the paper's contribution. Overall, the paper is exceptionally well-written and a pleasure to read.
