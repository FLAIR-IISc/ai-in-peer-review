Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper introduces the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), a parametric distribution on a learned Riemannian manifold meant to capture nonlinear structure in Euclidean data. The key idea is to replace the Euclidean/Mahalanobis distance in a multivariate normal with a geodesic distance induced by a smoothly varying metric that favors regions of high data density. The metric is constructed nonparametrically from data via a Gaussian kernel and local (diagonal) covariances (Eq. 7). Given this metric, the density is the Riemannian normal (Pennec, 2006 [15]) with maximum-entropy properties on the manifold. The authors propose: (i) a Monte Carlo estimate of the normalization constant by integrating over the tangent space using the exponential map, (ii) gradient-based maximum likelihood (ML) for the mean and covariance with a practical steepest-descent variant, and (iii) an EM algorithm for mixtures of LANDs. Experiments on synthetic data and EEG-based sleep-stage features suggest that LAND fits nonlinear structure with fewer components than a GMM and yields improved clustering alignment.

The paper connects two lines of work: statistics on known manifolds (e.g., Pennec [15], Zhang & Fletcher [23], Simo-Serra et al. [19]) and manifold learning/metric learning (e.g., Isomap [21], Hauberg et al. [9], Tosi et al. [22]). Its main novelty is to estimate a Riemannian normal and mixture model on a learned (data-adaptive) metric rather than a known manifold, and to address the resulting normalization/inference challenges.

Strengths:
- Conceptual contribution: a clean bridge from manifold learning to Riemannian statistics by using the Riemannian normal with a data-adaptive metric. The maximum-entropy interpretation is appealing.
- Technical contribution: computing the normalization constant via tangent-space Monte Carlo is elegant and practically important; it avoids repeated BVPs for Log maps.
- Algorithmic clarity: the ML and mixture formulations are well specified, with gradients and a workable optimization recipe.
- Empirical value: on synthetic nonlinear manifolds and EEG sleep features, LAND captures curved clusters more parsimoniously than GMMs and avoids intrinsic-mean pathologies (means drifting to low-density regions).

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- “Learning” the metric vs. hand-crafted form: The metric in Eq. 7 is determined by kernel smoothing with two hyperparameters (σ, ρ) and a diagonal structure. Calling this “learned” is somewhat misleading; it is a fixed functional form with user-chosen hyperparameters. The paper would be stronger with:
  - A principled procedure for selecting σ (and ρ), e.g., cross-validation on held-out likelihood, plug-in rules tied to local sampling density, or Bayesian evidence maximization. A sensitivity analysis beyond the brief discussion (and subject-wise tuning in the EEG experiment) is important, as σ critically shapes geodesics and normalization.
  - Discussion of the qualitative effect of σ: small σ yields highly curved geodesics that can overfit noisy local structure; large σ approaches Euclidean behavior. Please include ablations showing likelihood and fitted geometry vs. σ.
  - Consideration of learning σ jointly with model parameters (e.g., alternating updates, or a hyperprior/empirical Bayes approach).
- Coordinate dependence: The diagonal local covariance makes the metric axis-aligned and not invariant to linear reparameterizations. This can materially change geodesics and densities under rotations of the data. Please discuss:
  - Whether using full (or locally whitened) covariances would restore invariance and at what computational cost.
  - Practical mitigations (e.g., pre-whitening, local PCA frames) and an empirical test showing the effect of rotations.
- Model selection and fairness in comparisons: In the synthetic experiments, LAND achieves good fit with fewer components; however, GMMs can match curved distributions with more components. A fair comparison should allow each method to select K via AIC/BIC or held-out likelihood, then compare the resulting performance. Also show how LAND behaves as K grows (expected overfitting when each component adapts to local curvature/noise). The author response acknowledges this point; revising the experiment accordingly would strengthen the paper.
- Computational complexity and scalability: LAND requires repeated Exp/Log evaluations and Monte Carlo averaging. The paper should quantify:
  - Per-iteration and total runtime vs. GMM, as a function of N, D, S (MC samples), and ODE solver tolerances.
  - Practical limits (e.g., max N, D) and wall-clock times for reported experiments.
  - Variance/bias trade-offs for the Monte Carlo estimator and how S affects stability and convergence.
  - Feasibility on mid-scale datasets (e.g., MNIST features) or strategies such as subsampling, sparse neighborhoods, or approximate geodesics.
- Mixture inference details: Responsibilities depend on approximate normalization constants that vary across components. Please discuss numerical stability, convergence, and whether bias in C(µ,Σ) affects EM monotonicity.
- Broader related work: Expand discussion of metric learning and probabilistic geometries (e.g., Tosi et al. [22]) and alternatives for building Riemannian metrics from data; cite additional manifold learning approaches and density models on learned manifolds where appropriate.

Quality:
- The derivations are sound and the tangent-space MC trick is a highlight. However, the paper lacks a careful treatment of hyperparameter selection, complexity, and sensitivity analyses needed to support robust ML estimation and fair comparisons.
- Proof-of-concept experiments are convincing but limited in scale.

Clarity:
- Generally well organized and readable. Please clarify the distinction between “learned” vs. “constructed” metric and provide practical guidance on σ, ρ selection.
- Add runtime tables and ablations (σ, S, diagonal vs. full local covariances or pre-whitening).

Originality:
- Combining a data-adaptive Riemannian metric with full ML for Riemannian normals and mixtures, including normalization via tangent-space MC, is novel and useful, though the metric construction itself is standard kernel smoothing.

Significance:
- Potentially impactful as a drop-in replacement for Gaussians in generative models on curved data. The practical significance depends on demonstrating scalability and robust hyperparameter selection.

Pros for acceptance:
- Elegant and principled integration of manifold learning and Riemannian statistics.
- Practical Monte Carlo normalization with implementable ML and EM procedures.
- Clear empirical advantages on curved data and an interesting real application.

Cons for acceptance:
- Metric hyperparameters are hand-tuned; sensitivity and selection procedures are underdeveloped.
- Lack of model selection fairness (K) and missing scalability/runtime evaluations.
- Coordinate dependence due to diagonal local covariances is not addressed experimentally.

Recommendation:
Borderline. The conceptual and technical contributions are solid and relevant, but the paper would benefit from (i) principled and analyzed σ/ρ selection, (ii) fair model selection across methods (e.g., BIC-based K), (iii) runtime/scalability reports, and (iv) discussion/experiments on coordinate invariance. If these issues are addressed in a revision, I would lean toward acceptance.
