The paper proposes LAND, a novel parametric metric defined over a nonparametric space, by establishing connections between metric learning, manifold learning, and Riemannian statistics. Specifically, the learned parametric metric is a Riemannian normal distribution, as defined in Pennec [15]. The authors present a maximum likelihood algorithm for estimating the mean and covariance of this distribution, effectively learning a Mahalanobis distance on Riemannian manifolds. They define distances between points as geodesics along the manifold, relying on learning a local metric, which they achieve by utilizing the inverses of local (diagonal) covariances. The paper also explores mixtures of LANDs. This work offers valuable new insights into the interplay between metric learning and Riemannian statistics, and the development of parametric, generative models capable of generalizing beyond the training data is beneficial in numerous applications. 

My primary concern lies with the applicability of LAND to real-world data, particularly large-scale and high-dimensional datasets. The method requires computing a (diagonal) covariance matrix and its inverse for each data point, operations known to be computationally challenging and ill-conditioned in high dimensions. While the authors acknowledge high-dimensionality as a potential issue (lines 265-266), a more thorough analysis of the computational complexity and limitations of the proposed metric is necessary. Specifically, it would be helpful to understand what dimensionality constitutes “high-dimensional” in this context – is it 3, 5, 10, or 100 dimensions? The field of metric learning is extensive, and the paper would benefit from a more detailed positioning within this literature. Hauberg et al. [9] already discuss relevant work, such as Frome et al. [1,2] and Malisiewicz and Efros [6], who also learn a diagonal metric tensor for each point, similar to LAND. Furthermore, local covariances have been employed in methods like local PCA (Kambhatla – Dimension reduction by local principal component analysis), albeit not necessarily for every point.

Regarding the local metric learning process (lines 101-103), the authors employ a Gaussian kernel to define locality. The influence of the kernel parameter σ on the results warrants further investigation. While the choice of σ is discussed for the sleep data in Section 4.2, it is not addressed for the synthetic data in Section 4.1. Robustness tests exploring the sensitivity of the method to σ would be valuable. Is a uniform σ applied across all points, regardless of local data density? 

In Fig. 4, LAND is compared to intrinsic estimators, with results appearing comparable. However, the authors do not present visual comparisons with these intrinsic estimators in Figs. 5 and 6, instead opting to show results against a GMM, which Fig. 4 already demonstrates performs poorly. This limits the insight gained from these latter figures. Visual results using the intrinsic model *are* included in the supplementary material (Figs. 2 and 4) and demonstrate superior performance compared to the GMM. These results should ideally be incorporated into the main paper, either replacing the GMM comparisons or being presented alongside them. A comparative analysis of the computational complexity of LAND versus intrinsic estimators would also be insightful, clarifying which method is more suitable for different data characteristics.

The authors’ exploration of mixtures of LANDs is particularly compelling, as real-world data is rarely generated from a single component. This aspect of the work deserves further expansion. 

Overall, the paper is exceptionally well-written and a pleasure to read.
