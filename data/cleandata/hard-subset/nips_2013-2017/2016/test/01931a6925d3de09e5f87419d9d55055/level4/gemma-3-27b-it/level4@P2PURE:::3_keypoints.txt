- Proposes modeling observation space with a hand-crafted Riemannian metric and defining a curved normal (LAND) distribution on that geometry.  
- Addresses an important problem and provides an interesting application of Riemannian geometry to statistical learning.  
- Despite the title, the metric (Eq. 7) is hand-crafted rather than learned and depends on fixed hyperparameters rho and sigma.  
- Sigma is likely sensitive; the paper lacks discussion/intuitive guidance on choosing sigma.  
- Suggests exploring joint learning of the metric hyperparameters (rho, sigma) together with LAND.  
- The metric in Eq. 7 is not invariant to coordinate transformations; this invariance issue needs in-depth discussion and examination of the full (non-diagonal) metric.  
- Paper needs more intuition and explanation for Eq. 7 and its properties.  
- Recommend relating the approach to metric learning and manifold learning literature that connects to Riemannian geometry.  
- First experiment comparison is uninformative: LAND’s flexibility lets it fit with fewer components, but GMMs could match with more components and LAND may overfit.  
- Recommend redesigning the experiment to let each method choose component count via a principled criterion (e.g., BIC) and report performance and overfitting behavior as components increase.  
- LAND’s computational cost is likely higher than Gaussian estimation; provide quantitative cost measurements and tractability limits.  
- Suggest testing LAND on larger datasets (e.g., MNIST) and comparing with Gaussian mixture learning.  
- After the authors’ response: LAND is confirmed more flexible but more susceptible to noise as components increase; geodesic curvature can induce overfitting—revise experiment in rebuttal.
