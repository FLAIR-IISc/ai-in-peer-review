

This paper proposes modeling the observation space using a hand-crafted Riemannian metric and defines a curved normal distribution based on the associated Riemannian geometry to better capture the data manifold. It addresses the fundamental problem of defining a Riemannian metric from high-dimensional real-valued observations and subsequently defining and estimating a curved Gaussian distribution based on this geometry. This presents a useful tool and an interesting application of Riemannian geometry in statistical learning.  

The first part of the method (Section 3.1) defines the underlying Riemannian geometry. While the title suggests "learning a metric," the metric is actually hand-crafted in Equation (7) using two hyperparameters, ρ and σ, which are fixed empirically in advance. The paper should provide a more detailed discussion of the intuitive effect of varying σ and a principled method for selecting it, as σ appears sensitive. Without this, the approach is incomplete. Could the metric learning stage be merged with the LAND learning stage to jointly learn ρ and σ?  

A key strength of geometric approaches is coordinate invariance, yet the metric in Equation (7) lacks this property. For example, if data forms a line in 2D observation space, the metric becomes radically different under coordinate systems where the x-axis is parallel to the line versus one rotated by 45 degrees. The paper should discuss this in detail, including whether the full (non-diagonal) metric is invariant and provide deeper intuition for the metric in Equation (7). The work should also connect to relevant literature on metric learning and manifold learning with Riemannian geometry (e.g., search "Riemannian metric learning manifold learning").  

As a main criticism, the first experiment is not meaningful: LAND’s superior flexibility with fewer components does not fairly compare to GMM, which can better describe data with more components (while LAND overfits with large component counts). The authors should redesign the experiment to allow each method to select its optimal number of components (e.g., via BIC), then compare performance. LAND should still outperform GMM due to better manifold modeling, making the comparison valid. It is valuable to observe LAND’s overfitting with many components.  

LAND is computationally more expensive than Gaussian estimation. The paper should include quantitative measurements or experiments clarifying this cost and the scalability limit (e.g., maximum dataset size). Testing LAND on MNIST and comparing it to Gaussian mixture learning would strengthen the evaluation.  

After reading the authors' response: LAND is indeed more flexible than a Gaussian. Increasing components makes each local LAND more vulnerable to local noise compared to a local Gaussian. While "the geodesics will almost be straight lines" is true, they remain curves and cause overfitting. I highly recommend revising this experiment in the revision.
