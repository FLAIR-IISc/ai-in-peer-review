This paper proposes a novel method for modeling the observation space by defining a hand-crafted Riemannian metric and a corresponding curved normal distribution, termed Locally Adaptive Normal Distributions (LAND). The goal is to better capture the underlying data manifold. The paper addresses the fundamental problem of defining a suitable Riemannian geometry from a set of high-dimensional observations and then developing the necessary statistical tools for density estimation within this geometry. This represents a potentially useful tool and an interesting application of Riemannian geometry in statistical learning.

**Major Concerns and Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Metric Learning vs. Metric Definition:** The title and framing of the paper suggest that a metric is "learned." However, the metric presented in Section 3.1 (Equation 7) appears to be hand-crafted, defined by two hyperparameters, ρ and σ, which are fixed empirically. This raises several questions:
    *   **Sensitivity:** The parameter σ, in particular, seems likely to be highly sensitive. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more detailed discussion of the intuitive effect of varying σ and a principled method for its selection. Without this, the proposed method feels incomplete.
    *   **Joint Learning:** Have the authors considered integrating the estimation of the metric's hyperparameters (ρ and σ) into the main LAND learning stage? A joint optimization procedure could potentially yield a more data-driven and robust model.

2.  **Invariance of the Metric:** A significant advantage of many geometric approaches is their invariance to coordinate transformations. The metric defined in Equation 7, being diagonal, does not appear to possess this property. For instance, consider data points forming a straight line in a 2D space. The resulting metric would differ significantly depending on whether the coordinate system's x-axis is parallel to this line or oriented at an angle (e.g., 45 degrees). This lack of rotational invariance seems to be a notable limitation. A discussion of this issue is warranted. Is a corresponding full (non-diagonal) metric invariant, and what are the trade-offs? More intuition behind the specific choice of the metric in Equation 7 would be beneficial.

3.  **Experimental Comparison:** The primary experimental comparison with Gaussian Mixture Models (GMM) is not entirely convincing.
    *   **Unfair Comparison:** LAND is a more flexible model than a standard GMM. It is therefore unsurprising that it can describe the data more effectively with a smaller number of components. Conversely, a GMM might achieve a better fit with a larger number of components, a regime where LAND could be prone to overfitting.
    *   **Suggested Revision:** A more meaningful and rigorous comparison would involve allowing each method to select its optimal number of components based on a standard model selection criterion (e.g., BIC). The performance of the best model for each method should then be compared. We would still expect LAND to perform well due to its ability to capture manifold structure, but this revised experiment would provide a much stronger validation.
    *   **Overfitting Analysis:** It would also be highly instructive to include an analysis of how LAND behaves with a large number of components to demonstrate its overfitting characteristics. While the authors may argue that geodesics become nearly straight lines in such cases, they are still curves. This residual curvature, driven by local noise, is precisely what can lead to overfitting compared to a simpler local Gaussian model. A revised experiment demonstrating this behavior would make the paper more complete. I strongly suggest that the authors redesign this experiment for the final version.

4.  **Computational Cost and Scalability:** The LAND procedure is clearly more computationally expensive than estimating a standard Gaussian or GMM. The paper lacks a quantitative analysis of this cost.
    *   What is the computational complexity of the method?
    *   What are the practical limits on the dimensionality and number of data points that LAND can handle?
    *   An experiment on a larger, standard benchmark dataset like MNIST, with a direct comparison to GMM, would provide a valuable assessment of the method's scalability and practical applicability.

5.  **Relation to Existing Work:** There is a rich and extensive literature on metric learning and manifold learning, much of which has deep connections to Riemannian geometry. The paper could be improved by more thoroughly situating the proposed method within this existing body of work to better highlight its specific contributions and novelty.
