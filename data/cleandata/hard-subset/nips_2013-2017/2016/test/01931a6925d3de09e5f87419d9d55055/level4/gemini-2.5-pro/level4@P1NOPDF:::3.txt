This paper proposes a method to better model data manifolds by defining a hand-crafted Riemannian metric on the observation space and then constructing a curved normal distribution based on the resulting geometry. The work addresses the fundamental problems of defining a suitable metric from high-dimensional data and subsequently estimating a curved Gaussian distribution within that geometric framework. This represents an interesting application of Riemannian geometry to statistical learning and a potentially useful tool.

The method for defining the underlying Riemannian geometry is detailed in Section 3.1. Although the paper's title suggests that the metric is "learned," it is in fact constructed via Equation 7, which depends on two hyperparameters, ρ and σ, that are fixed empirically. It seems likely that σ is a particularly sensitive parameter, yet the paper does not discuss the intuitive effect of varying its value or provide a principled method for its selection. This omission is a significant weakness that makes the proposed method feel incomplete. Could the metric definition stage be merged with the main learning stage to allow ρ and σ to be learned directly from the data?

Furthermore, a key advantage of many geometric approaches is their invariance to coordinate transformations. However, the metric proposed in Equation 7 does not appear to possess this property. For instance, consider data forming a line in a 2D space. The resulting metric would be completely different depending on whether the coordinate system is aligned with the line or rotated (e.g., by 45 degrees). The authors should discuss this issue in more detail. Specifically, is the corresponding full (non-diagonal) metric invariant? More intuition behind the choice of metric in Equation 7 would also be beneficial. The paper would also be strengthened by connecting its contributions to the rich existing literature on metric learning and manifold learning, particularly work that also leverages Riemannian geometry.

My main criticism concerns the experimental comparison with Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs). The current setup, which shows the proposed model (LAND) fitting the data better with fewer components, is not particularly insightful. LAND is intrinsically a more flexible model, so this outcome is expected. A more meaningful comparison would acknowledge that GMMs can also achieve a good fit with a sufficiently large number of components, a regime where the more complex LAND model might be prone to overfitting.

I strongly suggest redesigning this experiment to allow for fair model selection. For example, each method could be allowed to select its optimal number of components using a criterion like the Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC), after which the performance of the best-selected models could be compared. While LAND is still expected to outperform GMMs due to its ability to capture the underlying manifold structure, this revised experimental design would provide a much more convincing and meaningful demonstration of its advantages.

While one might argue that for a large number of components, the geodesics within each local LAND model will approximate straight lines, this does not fully resolve the concern about overfitting. As a more flexible model than a standard Gaussian, LAND remains more susceptible to fitting local noise. Even slight curvatures in the geodesics can contribute to overfitting when the number of components is high and each component is supported by fewer data points. Therefore, a revised experiment demonstrating this behavior would be highly instructive.

Finally, the proposed LAND model is clearly more computationally expensive than a standard Gaussian model. The paper would benefit from a quantitative analysis of this cost. What are the practical limits on the scale (in terms of data dimensionality and number of samples) that LAND can handle? To demonstrate its scalability and practical utility, it would be valuable to see the method applied to a standard, larger-scale dataset like MNIST, with a comparison against a GMM.
