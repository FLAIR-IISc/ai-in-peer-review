This paper presents a novel locally adaptive normal distribution that leverages a Riemannian metric, M(x), to define distances between points on a manifold. The authors deviate from traditional Euclidean distance measurements and instead utilize the exponential and logarithmic maps to compute geodesic distances. This approach is grounded in the Euler-Lagrange equations, which provide a set of ordinary differential equations for estimating geodesic distances.

The logarithmic map plays a crucial role in this framework, as it takes points from the manifold (y) and maps them to the tangent plane (at x), resulting in a vector whose length corresponds to the geodesic distance from x to y along the manifold. The authors then apply a normal distribution to compute the Mahalanobis distance between points mapped by the logarithmic map, utilizing this inner product to calculate distances from a point x to the mean, rather than relying on Euclidean distances.

The authors derive estimators for the mean and covariance via maximum likelihood, although the solutions are not available in closed form. To address this, they propose a steepest descent algorithm for estimating these parameters. Furthermore, due to the unavailability of the normalization constant in closed form, the authors present a Monte-Carlo approximation method.

The proposed method is applied to synthetic data generated on a manifold, as well as in a clustering task, and compared to the standard mixture of Gaussians. The results demonstrate the effectiveness of the locally adaptive normal distribution in adapting to local manifold structure.

Overall, this paper provides an interesting and useful extension of the normal distribution, combining work in metric estimation, computing geodesic distances, and learning distributions. The authors' approach is technically sound, and the manuscript is well-organized and clear. However, there are some areas that require further clarification and improvement.

Technical quality is generally high, with the manifold techniques (using Euler-Lagrange equations, estimating logarithm maps) being sound and well-explained. The Monte-Carlo estimation of the normalization constant and steepest descent algorithms are clever and well-described. However, the analysis of the applications is somewhat lacking, particularly with regards to the choice of the sigma parameter, which appears to be picked by optimizing on training data. Additionally, the explanation of the usefulness of the learned distribution could be more comprehensive.

The novelty of this paper lies in combining previous work (using kernels for a metric M(x), estimating geodesic distances via Euler-Lagrange, applying a normal distribution) and providing an algorithm for estimating the parameters (mean, covariance, normalization constant). While the authors build upon existing techniques, their integration and extension of these methods are innovative and contribute to the field.

The potential impact of this work is significant, as it provides a fair evaluation of the proposed method (LAND) and the Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) on synthetic data. However, it is unclear how the authors chose the sigma parameter, and the comparison to GMM when data is generated according to a single normal distribution or GMM is not provided. In the real-life clustering data, the authors appear to choose the sigma value for LAND via optimizing the F measure and then present that same F measure. To ensure fairness, it would be beneficial to provide the F measure on a held-out test set. Moreover, examining very high-dimensional data would further demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

One potentially useful application of LAND is as a generative model for manifold data. However, the authors make the assumption that the data X is observed in D dimensions and the manifold dimension is also D, which is unusual in manifold learning. Typically, data lies on a d-dimensional manifold embedded in a D-dimensional Euclidean space, where D is much larger than d.

The clarity of the manuscript is generally good, with the manifold introduction being well-explained. However, there are some notation points that require clarification. For instance, the overloading of the Sigma notation is confusing, particularly when the authors state M = Sigma^-1 but estimate it via a (local) diagonal kernel matrix. It is unclear whether this Sigma is the same as the (fitted) covariance matrix, and if the kernel is just an initialization. Clarification on this point would be beneficial.

In conclusion, this paper presents a novel and useful extension of the normal distribution, adapting to local manifold structure. While there are some areas that require further clarification and improvement, the authors' approach is technically sound, and the manuscript is well-organized and clear. With some revisions to address the mentioned concerns, this work has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field.
