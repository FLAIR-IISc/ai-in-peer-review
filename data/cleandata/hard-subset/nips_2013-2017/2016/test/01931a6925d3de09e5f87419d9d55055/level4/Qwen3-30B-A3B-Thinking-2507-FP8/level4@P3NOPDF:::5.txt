

Summary: This paper introduces Locally Adaptive Normal Distributions (LANDs), a novel framework extending Gaussian distributions via a data-adaptive geodesic manifold traversal distance, replacing the standard Mahalanobis metric. The approach generalizes to mixtures (LAND mixtures), with evaluation on synthetic data and a real-world EEG dataset. The core idea—leveraging geodesic distance akin to Isomap—holds theoretical promise, but clarity and practical validation require significant improvement.

Major Comments:  
1. **Theoretical Clarity (Critical):** The definition of the inverse metric tensor in Eq. (7) appears ad hoc without justification. The authors employ a Gaussian kernel for weighting local covariances in the weighted sum (Eq. 7), yet provide no intuition for this choice. Why a Gaussian kernel? How does it align with the geodesic distance framework? This lack of motivation undermines confidence in the formulation. Furthermore, the relationship between the local covariance defined in Eq. (7) and its MAP estimate in Eq. (11) is unclear. Are these intended to represent the *same* quantity, or is Eq. (11) a distinct derivation? The paper must explicitly resolve this ambiguity to avoid confusion.  

2. **Experimental Evaluation (Insufficient):** The empirical validation is limited and fails to demonstrate practical utility. The synthetic dataset is small-scale and lacks complexity to test robustness. The EEG experiment uses a single, relatively modest real-world dataset without comparison to relevant baselines (e.g., standard GMMs, kernel density estimators, or manifold-based methods like Isomap). Crucially, the paper omits scalability analysis: *What is the computational complexity of LANDs compared to standard EM?* How does runtime scale with dataset size (e.g., N=10³ vs. 10⁵)? Without this, the claim of "practical applicability" remains unsubstantiated. The current experiments do not address whether LANDs are feasible for real-world problems beyond small-scale cases.  

3. **Practical Relevance (Major Concern):** The paper’s theoretical novelty is promising, but the absence of computational analysis and limited experiments cast doubt on real-world viability. For a venue like NIPS, which prioritizes both theory and scalability, this is a significant drawback. The authors should explicitly discuss trade-offs: *Is the gain in modeling flexibility worth the computational overhead?* If the method is prohibitively slow for large datasets, its utility is severely constrained. A single runtime comparison against standard EM on a medium-sized dataset would be essential.  

Minor Comments:  
- Algorithm 1, lines 4–5: Replace "d" with "\nabla" (gradient operator) for correctness.  
- Typos: "gedesic" → "geodesic" (multiple instances), "compleixty" → "complexity", "covarance" → "covariance".  

Overall, the paper presents an interesting theoretical extension to Gaussian distributions. However, the current version suffers from insufficient clarity in key derivations, a weak experimental validation lacking scalability analysis, and an overstatement of practical relevance. Addressing the theoretical ambiguities (particularly regarding Eq. 7 vs. Eq. 11) and providing concrete computational benchmarks are *essential* for this work to be considered for publication. Without these, the contribution risks being viewed as a theoretical curiosity with limited applicability.  

**Recommendation:** Weak Reject. The core idea has merit but requires substantial revision to clarify the methodology, validate scalability, and strengthen empirical claims. The current work does not sufficiently demonstrate that LANDs offer a meaningful advancement over existing methods in terms of both performance and practicality.
