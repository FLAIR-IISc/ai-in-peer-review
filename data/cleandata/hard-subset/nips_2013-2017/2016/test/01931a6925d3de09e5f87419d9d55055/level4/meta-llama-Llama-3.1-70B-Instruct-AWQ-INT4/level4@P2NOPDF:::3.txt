This paper proposes a novel approach to modeling the observation space using a hand-crafted Riemannian metric and defines a curved normal distribution based on the associated Riemannian geometry. This method aims to better capture the data manifold, addressing a fundamental problem in defining a Riemannian metric for high-dimensional real-valued observations and estimating a curved Gaussian distribution based on this geometry. The application of Riemannian geometry in statistical learning is an interesting and useful tool.

The proposed method defines the underlying Riemannian geometry in section 3.1. However, the title suggests that the method "learns a metric," whereas the metric is actually hand-crafted in equation 7 using two hyper-parameters, rho and sigma, which are fixed empirically in advance. I assume that sigma is a sensitive parameter, and the paper should provide a more detailed discussion on the intuitive effect of varying sigma and how to select this parameter. Furthermore, it would be beneficial to explore the possibility of merging the metric learning stage with the LAND learning stage, allowing rho and sigma to be learned.

The geometric approach is appealing due to its invariance to coordinate transformations. However, the metric in equation 7 is not invariant. For instance, if the data forms a line in a 2D observation space, the metric would be different in a coordinate system with its x-axis parallel to this line compared to a system that forms an angle, e.g., 45 degrees, to this line. A more detailed discussion on this topic, including the invariance of the corresponding full metric and the intuition behind the metric in equation 7, would be helpful.

There is a rich literature on metric learning and manifold learning connected to Riemannian geometry that can be related to this work. A main criticism of the paper is that the comparison in the first experiment is not meaningful, as LAND can describe the data better with a smaller number of components. However, GMM can describe the data better with a large number of components, whereas LAND would overfit. I suggest redesigning the experiment to allow each compared method to choose its favorable number of components based on a certain criterion (e.g., BIC) and then comparing the corresponding performance. LAND is expected to perform better due to its ability to describe the manifold structure, making this comparison more meaningful.

It would be interesting to see how LAND with a large number of components would overfit. Additionally, LAND is a more expensive procedure than estimating a Gaussian. Quantitative measurements or experiments that demonstrate the expense of LAND would be helpful. What is the limit of the scale that LAND can handle? Is it possible to learn LAND on MNIST and compare it with Gaussian mixture learning?

After reading the authors' response, it is clear that LAND is a more flexible model than the Gaussian distribution. However, if the number of components is increased, each local LAND will be more vulnerable to local noises compared to a local Gaussian. Although the geodesics will almost be straight lines, they are still curves, and these curves can lead to overfitting. I highly suggest that the authors revise this experiment in the revision.
