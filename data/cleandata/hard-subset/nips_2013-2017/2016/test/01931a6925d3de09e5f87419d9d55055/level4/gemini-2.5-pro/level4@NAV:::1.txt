This paper introduces LAND, a novel parametric metric on a nonparametric space that effectively merges ideas from metric learning, manifold learning, and Riemannian statistics. The learned metric is formulated as a Riemannian normal distribution, following the definition by Pennec [15]. To estimate the mean and covariance of this distribution—which defines a Mahalanobis distance on Riemannian manifolds—the authors present a maximum likelihood estimation algorithm. Following Hauberg et al. [9], point-to-point distances are computed as geodesics. The core proposal for learning the required local metric is to use the inverse of a local, diagonal covariance matrix at each point. The framework is also extended to support mixtures of LANDs.

The work offers valuable insights by connecting metric learning with Riemannian statistics, and the development of a parametric, generative model capable of generalizing beyond the training set is a significant contribution. My primary concern, however, lies with the method's practical applicability to large-scale and high-dimensional real-world datasets. Specifically, LAND necessitates computing and inverting a local covariance matrix for every data point, a procedure known to be computationally challenging and unstable in high-dimensional settings. While the authors briefly acknowledge this limitation (lines 265-266), the paper would be substantially strengthened by a formal analysis of the proposed metric's computational complexity and its practical limitations. For instance, a clarification is needed on what constitutes "high-dimensional" in this context—is it 3, 10, or 100+ dimensions?

Given the extensive body of work in metric learning, the paper could better position itself within the existing literature for future readers. Several prior methods, some of which are cited in Hauberg et al. [9] (e.g., Frome et al. [1,2], Malisiewicz and Efros [6]), also learn a point-wise diagonal metric tensor, similar to LAND. Furthermore, the concept of using local covariances has appeared in techniques like Local PCA (Kambhatla), albeit not on a per-point basis.

In lines 101-103, locality for learning the metric is established using a Gaussian kernel. The sensitivity of the results to the kernel width parameter, sigma, is a critical point that is not fully addressed. Its selection is only justified for the sleep dataset (Sect. 4.2), leaving its choice for the synthetic data (Sect. 4.1) unexplained. The paper would benefit from a robustness analysis of sigma. Additionally, it is unclear if sigma is a global parameter or if it adapts to regions of varying data density.

The experimental comparison could be more insightful. While Figure 4 shows that LAND performs comparably to intrinsic estimators, the qualitative visualizations in Figures 5 and 6 surprisingly omit these strong competitors. Instead, they feature comparisons against GMM, a model already demonstrated to be a weak baseline in Figure 4, making these visualizations less impactful. The supplementary material contains more compelling visualizations against the intrinsic model (Figs. 2 and 4), where it clearly outperforms GMM. These results should be included in the main paper, either replacing the GMM comparison or presented alongside it. To complement this, an analysis of the computational complexity of LAND versus these intrinsic estimators would be valuable. Such a comparison would help readers understand the trade-offs and determine which method is preferable for different data characteristics.

The extension to mixtures of LANDs is a particularly compelling aspect of this work, as real-world data is often multimodal. This application is highly promising and deserves a more detailed exploration in the paper.

Overall, the paper is exceptionally well-written and clear.
