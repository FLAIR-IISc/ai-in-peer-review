This paper introduces a locally adaptive normal distribution (LAND). Instead of using Euclidean distances, the authors define a smoothly varying Riemannian metric M(x) over the dataset. With this metric, geodesic distances on the manifold are computed via the exponential and logarithmic maps. The authors use the Euler–Lagrange equations to obtain ordinary differential equations for estimating geodesics. The logarithmic map sends a point y on the manifold to the tangent space at x so that the resulting vector has length equal to the geodesic distance from x to y. They then apply a normal distribution by computing a Mahalanobis distance in the tangent space using the log-mapped points. This inner product is used to measure distances from a point x to the mean in place of Euclidean distances. The authors derive maximum-likelihood estimators for the mean and covariance; because closed-form solutions are unavailable, they propose a steepest-descent algorithm. Since the normalization constant also lacks a closed form, they provide a Monte Carlo approximation. They evaluate on synthetic manifold data and a clustering task, comparing against a standard Gaussian mixture model (GMM). Overall, the paper offers an interesting and useful extension of the normal distribution that adapts to local manifold structure by integrating metric estimation, geodesic computation, and distribution learning. I believe the work merits publication.

Technical quality comments:
- The manifold-based techniques—using Euler–Lagrange equations and estimating logarithm maps—are sound and clearly described.
- The Monte Carlo estimation of the normalization constant and the steepest-descent algorithms are clever and well explained.
- The empirical analysis is lacking in some respects (the sigma parameter appears to be chosen by optimizing on the training data?).
- The paper could better articulate the practical usefulness of the learned distribution.

Novelty comments:
- The main contribution seems to be the integration of existing components—kernel-based construction of M(x), geodesic estimation via Euler–Lagrange, and a normal distribution in the tangent space—together with an algorithm for estimating the parameters (mean, covariance, normalization constant).

Potential impact comments:
- The synthetic-data evaluation comparing LAND and GMM via log-likelihood on data generated from the learned models is fair, but the method for choosing the sigma parameter is unclear.
- The authors show that LAND fits data generated along a manifold well, but they do not show how it compares to a GMM when data are generated from a single Gaussian or a GMM.
- For the real-world clustering task, sigma for LAND appears to be selected by optimizing the F-measure, and then that same F-measure is reported. A fairer procedure would report F-measure on a held-out test set; as is, it is difficult to assess potential overfitting.
- An exploration of very high-dimensional data would be valuable.
- A promising application is using LAND as a generative model for manifold data. However, the paper adopts an unusual manifold assumption: the observed data X live in D dimensions and the manifold dimension is also D (i.e., tangent vectors are D-dimensional and M(x) is full rank). In manifold learning, we typically consider data on a d-dimensional manifold embedded in a D-dimensional Euclidean space with D much larger than d.

Clarity comments:
- The notation around Sigma is confusing. The paper states M = Sigma^-1 but estimates M via a (local) diagonal kernel matrix. Is this Sigma the same as the fitted covariance matrix? If so, is the kernel merely an initialization? That would be odd, because M(x) varies with x, whereas the fitted covariance is a single global parameter. In the steepest-descent derivation, the authors explicitly write “in our case M = Sigma^-1” and use that substitution; clarification is needed.
- In Section 4.2, it is not stated explicitly, but I assume cluster assignments were made by selecting two clusters and assigning points based on the closest (geodesic?) distance to the cluster means; please clarify.
- The introduction to manifolds is well presented (at least for readers familiar with Riemannian geometry).
