The paper introduces LAND, a novel parametric metric that integrates concepts from metric learning, manifold learning, and Riemannian statistics. By leveraging a Riemannian normal distribution, as defined by Pennec [15], the authors develop a maximum likelihood algorithm to estimate the mean and covariance of this distribution, effectively yielding a Mahalanobis distance for Riemannian manifolds. The distance between points is determined by geodesics along the Riemannian manifold, similar to Hauberg et al. [9], with the added innovation of learning the metric through the inverses of local (diagonal) covariances. The authors also explore mixtures of LANDs, offering a valuable extension to the method. This work provides new insights into the intersection of metric learning and Riemannian statistics, and the potential for parametric and generative models to generalize beyond training data is significant. However, a primary concern is the method's applicability to large-scale, high-dimensional real-world data, as LAND necessitates the computation of a (diagonal) covariance matrix and its inverse for each data point, a challenging task in high dimensions. Although the authors acknowledge the potential issue of high-dimensionality (lines 265-266), a thorough analysis of the complexity and limitations of the proposed metric is necessary. It is unclear at what dimensionality (e.g., 3, 5, 10, 100 dimensions) the method becomes impractical. Furthermore, the paper would benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of its position within the broader context of metric learning literature. Notably, Hauberg et al. [9] and other works, such as Frome et al. [1,2] and Malisiewicz and Efros [6], have explored similar concepts, including the use of local diagonal metric tensors. The influence of the parameter sigma on the results, particularly in the context of learning the local metric using a Gaussian kernel, is not adequately addressed. The choice of sigma is only discussed for the sleep data in Sect. 4.2, and its impact on the synthetic data in Sect. 4.1 is unclear. Robustness tests for sigma would be beneficial, as well as an examination of whether sigma is consistent across all points, regardless of density. The comparison of LAND to intrinsic estimators in Fig. 4 yields similar results, but the visualization of these estimators is not included in Figs. 5 and 6, instead focusing on GMM, which is shown to perform poorly in Fig. 4. The inclusion of visual results using the intrinsic model, as presented in the supplementary material (Figs. 2 and 4), would provide more valuable insights. An analysis of the computational complexity of LAND versus intrinsic estimators would be valuable in determining the preferred method for different data structures. The consideration of mixtures of LANDs is a promising application, as real-world data is often generated by multiple components. This aspect warrants further exploration. Overall, the paper is well-written and enjoyable to read.
