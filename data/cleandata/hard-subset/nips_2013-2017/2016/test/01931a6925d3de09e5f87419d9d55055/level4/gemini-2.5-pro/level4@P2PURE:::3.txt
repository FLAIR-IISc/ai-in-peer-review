This paper proposes modeling the observation space with a hand-crafted Riemannian metric, which is then used to define a curved normal distribution capable of better capturing the data manifold. The work addresses the fundamental problems of defining a Riemannian metric from high-dimensional observations and subsequently defining and estimating a curved Gaussian distribution on the resulting geometry. This represents an interesting application of Riemannian geometry in statistical learning.

The proposed method begins by defining the underlying Riemannian geometry in Section 3.1. A key concern is that while the title suggests the metric is "learned," it is actually hand-crafted via Equation 7, based on two hyperparameters, ρ and σ, which are fixed empirically. The sensitivity of the model to the hyperparameter σ is a potential concern. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a discussion of the intuitive effect of varying σ and a principled method for its selection. Without this, a crucial part of the methodology is left unaddressed. Could the metric-defining stage be merged with the main learning stage to allow ρ and σ to be learned from data?

A significant issue is the metric's lack of invariance to coordinate transformations, a property typically desired in geometric approaches. For instance, for data forming a line in a 2D space, the metric would differ significantly between a coordinate system aligned with the line and one rotated by 45 degrees. The authors should discuss this limitation and clarify whether a full (non-diagonal) metric would restore invariance. More intuition behind the formulation in Equation 7 is also needed. Furthermore, the paper would benefit from positioning itself more clearly within the extensive literature on Riemannian metric learning and manifold learning.

A primary criticism concerns the experimental comparison with the Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM). The proposed LAND model has greater flexibility per component, so it is unsurprising that it outperforms a GMM with the same small number of components. To create a fair comparison, the experiment should be redesigned. Each method should be allowed to select its optimal number of components using a standard criterion, such as the Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC), and the performance of the best-selected models should then be compared. This would provide a more meaningful evaluation, as LAND is still expected to perform well due to its ability to model the manifold structure.

The authors might argue that with many components, the local regions become small and the geodesics become nearly straight. While true, these are still curves, not lines. This added flexibility makes each component more susceptible to fitting local noise compared to a standard Gaussian, exacerbating the risk of overfitting. Therefore, the need for a revised, fair experimental comparison remains a critical point. Demonstrating how LAND overfits as the number of components increases would also provide valuable insight into the model's behavior.

Finally, the computational cost of LAND appears to be significantly higher than that of a standard Gaussian model. The paper should include a quantitative analysis of its computational complexity and runtime. It is also important to discuss the scalability of the method—what are the practical limits on dataset size or dimensionality? An experiment on a larger-scale dataset like MNIST, with a comparison to GMMs, would be a valuable addition to demonstrate its applicability.
