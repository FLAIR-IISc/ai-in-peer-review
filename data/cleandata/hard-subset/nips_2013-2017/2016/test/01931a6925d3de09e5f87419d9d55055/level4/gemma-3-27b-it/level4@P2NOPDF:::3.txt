This paper introduces a method for modeling the observation space using a hand-crafted Riemannian metric, and subsequently defines a curved normal distribution based on this geometry to better capture the underlying data manifold. The work addresses the important problem of defining a Riemannian metric from high-dimensional, real-valued observations and then utilizing this geometry to define and estimate a curved Gaussian distribution. This provides a potentially valuable tool and an interesting application of Riemannian geometry to statistical learning. The paper details the definition of the Riemannian geometry in Section 3.1.

However, despite the title suggesting a learned metric, the metric presented in Equation 7 is, in fact, hand-crafted and relies on two fixed hyperparameters, ρ and σ. The values for these hyperparameters are determined empirically. The sensitivity of the method to the choice of σ is a key concern; the paper should provide a more detailed discussion of the intuitive effect of varying σ and guidance on its selection. The lack of this analysis represents a significant omission. Furthermore, the authors should explore the possibility of integrating metric learning into the LAND (Local Affine Normal Distribution) learning stage, allowing ρ and σ to be learned directly from the data.

A core advantage of a geometric approach is its potential invariance to coordinate transformations. Unfortunately, the metric defined in Equation 7 lacks this property. For example, consider data lying on a line within a 2D observation space. The resulting metric will differ significantly depending on the coordinate system – one aligned with the line versus one at an angle (e.g., 45 degrees). A more thorough discussion of this limitation is necessary, including an analysis of whether a full (non-diagonal) metric would exhibit invariance, and a deeper intuitive explanation of the metric’s behavior in Equation 7. The authors should also relate their work to the existing literature in metric learning and manifold learning, particularly concerning connections to Riemannian geometry (a search using terms like “Riemannian”, “metric learning”, and “manifold learning” would be beneficial).

A major criticism concerns the first experiment. The comparison is unconvincing as LAND, being more flexible, predictably outperforms GMM with a smaller number of components. However, GMM can achieve comparable or better performance with a sufficiently large number of components, potentially leading to overfitting in LAND. I recommend redesigning this experiment to allow each method to optimize its number of components based on a model selection criterion like the Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC), and then compare performance. A meaningful comparison would then demonstrate whether LAND consistently outperforms GMM *given optimal component selection for each*. Examining the performance of LAND with a large number of components and observing the onset of overfitting would also be valuable.

Finally, LAND is computationally more demanding than estimating a standard Gaussian distribution. The paper lacks quantitative measurements or experiments to illustrate this computational cost. It would be helpful to understand the scalability limits of LAND and whether it could be applied to larger datasets. Evaluating LAND on a dataset like MNIST and comparing its performance to Gaussian mixture modeling would provide a useful benchmark.

Following the author response, it is clear that LAND provides a more flexible model than a Gaussian distribution. However, increasing the number of components in LAND makes it more susceptible to local noise compared to a Gaussian. While the authors correctly note the geodesics approach straight lines with increasing components, they remain curves, and this curvature contributes to overfitting. I strongly encourage the authors to revise the experiment accordingly.
