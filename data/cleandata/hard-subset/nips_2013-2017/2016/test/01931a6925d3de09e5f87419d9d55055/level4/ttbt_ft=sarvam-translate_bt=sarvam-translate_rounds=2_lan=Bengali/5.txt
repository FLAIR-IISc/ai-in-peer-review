Here is a proposed naturally adapted spatial distribution based on a geodesic manifold traversal distance, which has been learned adaptively from data. This distance replaces the standard Mahalanobis distance used in the Gaussian distribution. An extension of this is also provided in LANDs Mix. Its performance has been evaluated on a synthetic dataset and a real-world dataset of EEG measurements. Key comments: The idea of using a geodesic distance like an isogram is very interesting. However, there are some weaknesses in the clarity of the article. Firstly, the definition of the inverse of the metric tensor in equation (7) seems immediate. Could the authors provide more insight into the concept of using a Gaussian kernel to weight a weighted sum of local coefficients? Also, I got lost in the definition of the matrix of local coefficients in equation (7) and later, the MAP estimate of the same quantity in equation (11). Do these refer to the same quantity? Furthermore, the experimental evaluations are quite weak. However, my main concern is the scalability of the method and the computational efficiency. For example, what is the computational complexity and run time compared to standard EM? In my opinion, the article has an interesting theoretical contribution, but it may not be very applicable in practice. Minor comments: Algorithm 1, lines 4 and 5, d -> \nabla
