

This paper introduces a Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), which extends the standard multivariate normal distribution by replacing the Euclidean metric with a locally adaptive Riemannian metric. The authors construct a smoothly varying metric tensor using local covariance matrices (via a Gaussian kernel), enabling the definition of geodesic distances that follow the data manifold. The LAND is then defined as the Riemannian normal distribution under this metric, with the authors deriving maximum likelihood estimation procedures using gradient descent and Monte Carlo integration for the normalization constant. The approach is extended to mixture models with a corresponding EM algorithm, and evaluated on synthetic data and EEG sleep stage clustering.

The paper builds on two key strands of previous work: (1) Riemannian normal distributions (Pennec, 2006; Zhang & Fletcher, 2013), which consider the normal distribution on known manifolds, and (2) manifold learning techniques (Tenenbaum et al., 2000; Belkin & Niyogi, 2003), which typically focus on dimensionality reduction rather than generative modeling. The authors' key contribution lies in bridging these areas by learning the Riemannian metric from data, thus creating a generative model that adapts to local manifold structure.

**Strengths:** The technical development is strong and well-executed. The authors provide a clear mathematical foundation for the LAND, carefully addressing the challenges of normalization and parameter estimation. The Monte Carlo integration scheme for the normalization constant (Equation 10) is particularly clever, offering a scalable alternative to direct integration on the manifold. The algorithmic details, including the steepest descent optimization (Algorithm 1), are well-motivated and clearly presented. The experimental evaluation demonstrates the LAND's advantage over standard Gaussian mixtures on both synthetic data (Figure 5) and EEG sleep stage clustering (Table 1), where the LAND achieves better alignment with sleep stages.

**Weaknesses:** The paper has several important limitations that need addressing. First, the choice of the bandwidth parameter σ for the Gaussian kernel is not properly justified. The authors appear to optimize σ on the training data (e.g., "we choose σ as small as possible while ensuring smoothness" in Section 4), which risks overfitting. A more rigorous approach would involve cross-validation on held-out data or a more principled selection method. Second, the comparison to Gaussian mixtures is incomplete: the authors demonstrate LAND's superiority on manifold-generated data but do not show how LAND performs when data is generated from a standard normal distribution or a GMM. This is crucial for establishing the model's practical utility. Third, the real-world experiment (Section 4.2) evaluates the LAND using the same data to select σ and then reports the F-measure on the same data, which is not a fair comparison. The authors should report results on held-out test data to avoid overfitting concerns.

The paper also makes an unusual assumption about the manifold dimensionality: it treats the manifold as D-dimensional (same as the ambient space), whereas most manifold learning applications assume a lower-dimensional manifold embedded in a higher-dimensional space. This limits the applicability of the approach to high-dimensional data, which is common in machine learning. While the authors acknowledge scalability concerns in Section 6, they do not address how their method would perform on truly high-dimensional data.

The notation could be clarified, particularly the use of Σ. The authors state "M = Σ⁻¹" (Equation 7), but Σ is later used for the covariance matrix, creating confusion. It would help to explicitly distinguish between the metric tensor M(x), the global covariance matrix Σ, and the local covariance used in metric construction.

**Overall Assessment:** The paper presents a technically sound and interesting extension of the normal distribution to locally adaptive Riemannian spaces. The combination of Riemannian statistics with manifold learning is novel and well-executed, with clear experimental validation. However, the limitations in parameter selection, comparison methodology, and dimensionality assumptions reduce the paper's impact. The authors should address these concerns to strengthen the contribution.

**Recommendation:** Accept (with minor revisions). The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the intersection of manifold learning and probabilistic modeling, and with the suggested clarifications and additional experiments, would be a strong addition to the NIPS program. The work is technically rigorous, well-motivated, and has clear potential for broader application in generative modeling on manifolds.
