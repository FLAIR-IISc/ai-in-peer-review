**Summary of the Main Ideas**

The paper introduces a locally adaptive normal distribution (LAND) that generalizes the normal distribution to the manifold setting. The authors replace the Euclidean distance with a locally adaptive Riemannian distance, which is learned from the data. The resulting distribution is a parametric model that depends only on a mean and a covariance, but is defined on a non-parametric space. The authors develop a maximum likelihood algorithm to infer the distribution parameters, which relies on a combination of gradient descent and Monte Carlo integration. They also extend the LAND to mixture models and provide the corresponding EM algorithm.

**Relation to Previous Work**

The paper builds upon previous work on Riemannian normal distributions, manifold learning, and metric estimation. The authors combine these techniques to create a new distribution that adapts to local manifold structure. The work is related to previous contributions by Pennec, Zhang, and Fletcher, but differs in that it adapts the distribution to the "manifold learning" setting by constructing a Riemannian metric that adapts to the data.

**Strengths**

* The paper provides an interesting and useful extension of the normal distribution that adapts to local manifold structure.
* The authors combine work in metric estimation, computing geodesic distances, and learning distributions in a novel way.
* The manifold techniques, Monte-Carlo estimation of the normalization constant, and steepest descent algorithms are sound and well-explained.
* The paper provides a fair evaluation of the LAND in the synthetic data and demonstrates its advantages over the Gaussian mixture model (GMM).

**Weaknesses**

* The analysis of the applications is lacking, and the explanation of the usefulness of the learned distribution is somewhat unclear.
* The choice of the sigma parameter appears to be picked by optimizing on training data, which may lead to overfitting.
* The paper does not provide a clear evaluation of the LAND on high-dimensional data or data generated according to a single normal distribution or GMM.
* The notation is sometimes confusing, particularly with regards to the overloading of the Sigma notation.

**Originality**

* The paper's main contribution is combining previous work on metric estimation, computing geodesic distances, and learning distributions to create a new distribution that adapts to local manifold structure.
* The authors provide a novel algorithm for estimating the parameters of the LAND, which relies on a combination of gradient descent and Monte Carlo integration.

**Significance**

* The paper provides a useful extension of the normal distribution that can be used as a generative model for manifold data.
* The authors demonstrate the advantages of the LAND over the GMM in the synthetic data and provide a fair evaluation of the two models.
* The paper has the potential to impact the field of machine learning by providing a new tool for modeling complex data.
