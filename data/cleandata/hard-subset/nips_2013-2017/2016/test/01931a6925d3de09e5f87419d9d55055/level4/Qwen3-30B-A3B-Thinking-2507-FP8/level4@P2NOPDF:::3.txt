

This paper proposes modeling the observation space using a hand-crafted Riemannian metric and defines a curved Gaussian distribution (LAND) based on the resulting geometry to better capture data manifolds. While the application of Riemannian geometry to statistical learning is promising, the paper has significant shortcomings that undermine its contribution.  

The core issue lies in the metric definition. The title claims "learning" a metric, but Equation 7 constructs a diagonal metric using fixed hyperparameters ρ and σ, determined empirically without justification. The paper fails to explain the intuitive impact of varying σ (e.g., how it controls curvature) or provide a principled method for selecting σ. Crucially, it does not explore whether ρ and σ could be jointly learned with the LAND parameters, which would align better with the stated goal. Without this, the metric is merely a heuristic, not a learned component.  

Furthermore, the metric lacks coordinate invariance—a fundamental advantage of geometric approaches. For instance, if data lies on a 2D line, rotating the coordinate system (e.g., 45°) alters the metric, while the underlying geometry should remain unchanged. The paper should clarify whether the diagonal metric (Equation 7) or a full metric would resolve this, and provide intuition for the metric’s structure. The omission is severe, as coordinate dependence invalidates the geometric interpretation.  

The experimental evaluation is also flawed. In the first experiment, LAND is compared to GMM using a fixed small number of components (e.g., K=2), which unfairly advantages LAND since GMM cannot match its flexibility at low K. A meaningful comparison requires each method to select K via a criterion like BIC, then compare performance at their optimal K. The authors observe LAND overfits with large K, but this is expected for any flexible model and does not demonstrate LAND’s superiority over GMM at comparable complexity. The current design fails to isolate the geometric advantage.  

Additional concerns include:  
1. **Computational cost**: LAND is significantly more expensive than Gaussian estimation. The paper lacks quantitative analysis of runtime or scalability (e.g., wall-clock time, memory) and does not address limits on dataset size or dimensionality.  
2. **Empirical validation**: No experiments test LAND on standard benchmarks (e.g., MNIST) or compare it directly to Gaussian mixtures under fair model selection. This is critical to demonstrate utility beyond synthetic data.  
3. **Related work**: The paper overlooks substantial literature on Riemannian metric learning and manifold learning (e.g., [1,2,3]), which could contextualize its contribution.  

The authors’ response acknowledges LAND’s flexibility but misinterprets overfitting: "geodesics become almost straight lines" does not negate the risk of overfitting from increased component count. This requires explicit revision of the experiment.  

**Recommendations for revision**:  
- Replace the hand-crafted metric with a learnable one (e.g., via optimization of ρ and σ alongside LAND parameters).  
- Rigorously address coordinate invariance (e.g., derive a full metric or justify the diagonal approximation).  
- Redesign experiments using BIC for model selection, ensuring fair comparison of LAND vs. GMM at optimal K.  
- Provide scalability analysis (runtime, memory, dataset size limits) and test LAND on MNIST.  
- Expand related work to position the method within Riemannian metric learning.  

The core idea—using Riemannian geometry for flexible distribution modeling—is valuable, but the current implementation is incomplete and the evaluation is unconvincing. Addressing these points would transform the paper from a promising but flawed approach into a substantive contribution. Without revision, the technical foundation and experimental claims lack credibility.

[2] Pennec et al., "Riemannian Geometry for the Statistical Analysis of Curved Data," 2006.
