This paper proposes modeling the observation space with a hand-crafted Riemannian metric and defining a curved normal distribution induced by the associated geometry, with the goal of better capturing the data manifold. It tackles the fundamental problems of how to define a Riemannian metric from high-dimensional real-valued observations and how to define and estimate a curved Gaussian distribution on this geometry. This provides a useful tool and an interesting application of Riemannian geometry to statistical learning.

The first part of the method defines the underlying Riemannian geometry in Section 3.1. The title suggests that it “learns a metric,” but in fact the metric in Equation 7 is hand-crafted using two hyperparameters, rho and sigma, which are fixed empirically in advance. I suspect sigma is a sensitive parameter. The paper should discuss in more detail the intuitive effect of varying sigma and provide guidance on how to select it; without this, the paper feels incomplete. Is it possible to integrate the metric construction stage with the LAND learning stage so that rho and sigma are learned jointly?

A strength of geometric approaches is invariance to coordinate transformations. However, the metric in Equation 7 is not invariant. For example, if the data lie along a line in a 2D observation space, the resulting metrics will differ markedly between a coordinate system whose x-axis is aligned with the line and one rotated by, say, 45 degrees. It would be useful to discuss this in more depth, including whether the corresponding full (rather than diagonal) metric would be invariant, and to provide more intuition for the design of Equation 7.

There is a rich literature on metric learning and manifold learning connected to Riemannian geometry that could be related to this work (e.g., see searches for “Riemannian” “metric learning” “manifold learning”).

My main criticism concerns the first experiment: the comparison is not very meaningful. It is unsurprising that the more flexible LAND can fit the data better with a small number of components. However, a GMM can fit the data better with a large number of components, in regimes where LAND would overfit. I recommend redesigning the experiment so that each method selects its preferred number of components using a model selection criterion (e.g., BIC), and then comparing performance. I still expect LAND to perform better because it captures manifold structure, but this would be a fairer and more informative comparison. It would also be instructive to show how LAND behaves—and overfits—with a large number of components.

LAND is clearly more expensive computationally than estimating a Gaussian. Please provide quantitative measurements or experiments to clarify this cost. What are the practical limits of scale that LAND can handle? Is it feasible to train LAND on MNIST and compare it against Gaussian mixture learning?

After reading the authors’ response: LAND is indeed more flexible than a Gaussian distribution. As the number of components increases, each local LAND becomes more vulnerable to local noise than a local Gaussian. Although “the geodesics will almost be straight lines,” they are still curves and can lead to overfitting. I strongly recommend revising the first experiment accordingly.
