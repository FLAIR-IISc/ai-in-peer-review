This paper proposes LAND, a novel parametric metric over a nonparametric space that bridges concepts from metric learning, manifold learning, and Riemannian statistics. The learned metric is a Riemannian normal distribution, as defined by Pennec [15], for which the authors propose a maximum likelihood algorithm to estimate the mean and covariance. This effectively defines a Mahalanobis distance on Riemannian manifolds. Following Hauberg et al. [9], distances are defined as geodesics along the manifold, which relies on learning a local metric. In this work, the local metric is learned from the inverses of local (diagonal) covariance matrices. The paper also introduces an extension for mixtures of LANDs.

The paper offers valuable new insights into the connection between metric learning and Riemannian statistics. The development of a parametric, generative model that can generalize beyond the training data is a useful contribution.

My primary concern, however, is the method's applicability to large-scale, high-dimensional real-world data. LAND requires computing a local covariance matrix and its inverse for each data point. These operations are known to be computationally challenging and potentially unstable in high dimensions. While the authors briefly acknowledge high-dimensionality as a potential issue (lines 265-266), the paper would be significantly strengthened by a formal analysis of the proposed metric's computational complexity and limitations. For instance, what is the practical limit for "high-dimensional" in this context: 10, 100, or more dimensions?

The paper could also be better contextualized within the vast metric learning literature. For example, Hauberg et al. [9] discuss several related methods that learn a diagonal metric tensor for each point, similar to LAND (e.g., Frome et al. [1,2], Malisiewicz and Efros [6]). Additionally, local covariances have been used in other contexts, such as Local PCA, albeit not for every single point. A more thorough comparison would help future readers understand LAND's position in the field.

Several specific points would also benefit from clarification:

*   **Kernel Bandwidth (sigma):** In lines 101-103, a Gaussian kernel is used to define locality for learning the local metric. The influence of the parameter sigma on the results is critical. Its selection is discussed for the sleep dataset but omitted for the synthetic data experiments. The paper would benefit from a robustness analysis for sigma. Furthermore, is a single global sigma used for all points, or does it adapt to regions of varying data density?

*   **Experimental Comparisons:** In Figure 4, the performance of LAND is shown to be fairly similar to that of intrinsic estimators. However, the visualizations in Figures 5 and 6 only compare LAND against GMMs, which Figure 4 already demonstrated to be a weak baseline. These comparisons are therefore less insightful. The supplementary material contains visualizations (Figs. 2 and 4) where the intrinsic model outperforms GMM. These results should be included in the main paper, perhaps replacing the GMM comparisons, to provide a more meaningful evaluation.

*   **Complexity Analysis:** Related to the point above, a computational complexity analysis comparing LAND to the intrinsic estimators would be very useful. This would help clarify which method is preferable for different types of data structures and scales.

*   **Mixtures of LANDs:** The extension to mixtures of LANDs is one of the most interesting applications of the proposed model, as real-world data is rarely unimodal. This aspect is compelling and would be worth expanding upon.

Despite these concerns, the paper is exceptionally well-written and a pleasure to read.
