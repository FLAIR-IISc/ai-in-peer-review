### Review

**Summary**

This paper introduces the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), a novel generalization of the multivariate normal distribution designed for data lying on or near a non-linear manifold. The core idea is to replace the standard Euclidean metric, which underlies the Mahalanobis distance, with a smoothly varying Riemannian metric that is learned directly from the data. This data-driven metric is constructed to favor regions of high data density, effectively "pulling" geodesics towards the data manifold.

The resulting LAND is a parametric distribution defined by a mean and a covariance, but it exists on a non-parametric space defined by the learned metric. The authors show that the LAND is the maximum entropy distribution for a given mean and covariance under this learned metric. To make the model practical, they propose a maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) procedure for the model parameters. This involves a clever Monte Carlo integration scheme performed in the tangent space to compute the otherwise intractable normalization constant. The paper further extends the single distribution to a mixture of LANDs, providing the corresponding Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm.

The proposed method is evaluated on both synthetic datasets and a real-world application of modeling EEG measurements of human sleep stages. The experiments demonstrate that the LAND can effectively capture complex, non-linear data structures where traditional Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) fail. This work effectively bridges the fields of manifold learning (by learning a metric from data) and statistics on Riemannian manifolds (by defining a distribution on that metric space).

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

The paper presents a compelling and theoretically elegant model with several key strengths, though its presentation and some practical aspects could be improved.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary contribution—defining a parametric generative model on a non-parametrically learned manifold—is both novel and significant. It offers a principled approach to density estimation for complex data structures, moving beyond the linear assumptions of standard models like the GMM. By learning the manifold's geometry from the data itself, this work makes techniques from Riemannian statistics applicable to a much wider range of problems where the underlying manifold is not known *a priori*. This is a valuable contribution that could inspire further work in generative modeling.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical development is sound. The formulation of the LAND as a maximum entropy distribution provides a strong theoretical foundation. The proposed MLE framework is well-reasoned, particularly the use of tangent space Monte Carlo integration to estimate the normalization constant. This approach is computationally advantageous over direct integration on the manifold, as it relies on solving Initial Value Problems (IVPs) instead of more expensive Boundary Value Problems (BVPs). The derivations for the gradient-based parameter updates appear correct.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured. The introductory sections provide a clear motivation for the problem and a concise background on Riemannian geometry, which is helpful for the reader. The experimental results are presented clearly and effectively demonstrate the advantages of the LAND over competing methods.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against Acceptance):**

1.  **Positioning Against Prior Work:** The most significant weakness is the paper's failure to adequately position its contributions with respect to existing literature until the final sections. Specifically, the work of Simo-Serra et al. [19], which deals with mixtures of Riemannian normal distributions, is a critical point of comparison. While the authors correctly note in Section 5 that [19] assumes a known manifold and does not correctly handle the normalization constant, this discussion is buried at the end of the paper. This omission from the introduction and main body of the work undersells the novelty of the current paper and may lead the reader to misunderstand the true nature of its contribution. The key difference—that this paper *learns* the manifold while prior work assumes it is given—is the central contribution and should be highlighted from the outset.

2.  **Practicality and Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The method for constructing the metric relies on a key hyperparameter, the kernel bandwidth σ (Eq. 7). The paper provides little guidance on how to set this parameter, stating it should be chosen to be "as small as possible" while ensuring numerical stability. The experiments on the sleep data highlight this sensitivity, as results are presented for both a fixed σ and an optimized σ, with the latter performing significantly better. The lack of a principled method for selecting or learning σ is a practical limitation that reduces the model's autonomy and reproducibility.

3.  **Scalability Concerns:** The construction of the metric tensor at a point `x` requires a sum over all `N` data points (Eq. 7). This suggests that computing geodesics, which is a core operation, will be computationally expensive for large datasets. The authors acknowledge this as a potential limitation in the discussion, but a more thorough analysis of the computational complexity in the main text would be beneficial.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Major Revision of Introduction/Related Work:** I strongly urge the authors to revise the introduction to clearly state the contributions in the context of prior work like Simo-Serra et al. [19]. Highlighting early on that the main novelty is learning the manifold itself would significantly improve the paper's impact and clarity. The related work section should be expanded and perhaps integrated earlier.
*   **Address Hyperparameter Selection:** The authors should provide a more detailed discussion on the selection of σ. Including a sensitivity analysis in the main paper (perhaps moving some content from the supplement) and suggesting a concrete cross-validation strategy would greatly strengthen the paper's practical value.
*   **Discuss Computational Complexity:** A brief analysis of the computational complexity with respect to the number of data points (N), dimensions (D), and Monte Carlo samples (S) would be a valuable addition for practitioners considering this model.

In summary, this paper presents a high-quality, novel, and significant contribution to generative modeling. The core idea is excellent, and the theoretical and experimental support is strong. The main drawback is in the presentation, which fails to properly contextualize the work and clarify its novelty. This is, however, an addressable issue. With a revision to improve the positioning of the work and address the practical issue of hyperparameter selection, this would be a very strong paper.
