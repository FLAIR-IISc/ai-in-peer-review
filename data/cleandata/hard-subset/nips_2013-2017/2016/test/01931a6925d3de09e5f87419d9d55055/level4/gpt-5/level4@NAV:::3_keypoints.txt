- Positive summary: Proposes using a manually specified Riemannian metric and a curved normal distribution (LAND) to model observation geometry, offering a useful tool and compelling application of Riemannian geometry in statistical learning.
- Metric is hand-crafted: Equation (7) defines a metric controlled by hyperparameters rho and sigma rather than being learned, despite the paper title implying metric learning.
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: Sigma (and rho) likely strongly affect the metric; the paper lacks intuition and guidance for choosing them.
- Suggestion to learn metric: Ask whether rho and sigma can be integrated into the LAND learning phase and learned jointly with the model.
- Lack of coordinate invariance: The proposed metric is not invariant under coordinate transformations (e.g., rotations), which undermines a core strength of geometric methods.
- Missing discussion on alternatives: Recommend discussing whether a full (non-diagonal) metric would restore invariance and providing more intuition for equation (7).
- Literature connections: Paper should relate to substantial prior work on Riemannian metric learning and manifold learning.
- Experimental comparison flaw: First experiment is uninformative—LAND fits better with fewer components by design; comparison should use model-selection (e.g., BIC) so each method picks its preferred number of components.
- Overfitting analysis needed: Recommend showing how LAND behaves and overfits as the number of components increases.
- Computational cost concerns: LAND is more demanding than Gaussians; authors should provide quantitative runtime/complexity measures, practical scaling limits, and testability on datasets like MNIST.
- After authors’ response: Concern persists that LAND’s curvature can induce overfitting even when geodesics are nearly straight; experiments should be revised accordingly.
