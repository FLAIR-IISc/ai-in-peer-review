

Summary: The paper introduces a locally adaptive normal distribution (LAND), which generalizes the standard multivariate normal distribution to a Riemannian manifold setting. Instead of using the Euclidean distance (or Mahalanobis distance) in the standard normal density, LAND employs a geodesic distance derived from a locally adaptive Riemannian metric learned from the data. The authors develop a maximum likelihood estimation procedure for LAND, including a Monte Carlo integration method for normalization, and extend it to mixture models with a corresponding EM algorithm. This work bridges statistics on Riemannian manifolds [15, 23] with manifold learning [21], addressing a significant gap where the manifold is learned from data rather than assumed known a priori.

Quality: The paper presents a technically sound approach with a solid theoretical foundation. The derivation of LAND as a maximum entropy distribution under the learned metric is well-motivated, and the authors provide a detailed algorithm for maximum likelihood estimation. The experimental evaluation on synthetic data demonstrates the model's ability to capture nonlinear structures better than standard Gaussian mixtures, and the EEG sleep staging application shows practical utility. However, the paper lacks thorough computational complexity analysis and comparison with standard EM in terms of running time, which is a significant gap for a method that relies on expensive geodesic calculations. The authors acknowledge this limitation in their discussion, noting that evaluating logarithm maps is computationally expensive, but do not provide concrete timing comparisons.

Clarity: The paper is generally well-written, though some sections could be clearer. The connection between the local covariance matrix in Equation (7) and the maximum likelihood estimation in Equation (11) is somewhat confusing. The authors could better explain whether these represent the same quantity or different concepts. The description of the normalization constant estimation via Monte Carlo integration (Equation 10) is technically sound but could be more intuitive for readers unfamiliar with Riemannian geometry. The paper would benefit from a more detailed explanation of why the tangent space integration (Equation 9) is preferred over direct integration on the manifold (Equation 8).

Originality: The paper makes a clear contribution by adapting the Riemannian normal distribution to the manifold learning setting, where the manifold is learned from data rather than assumed known. This is a significant extension of previous work by Pennec [15] and Zhang and Fletcher [23], who consider the scenario where the manifold is known a priori. While the use of a Gaussian kernel for local metric construction is similar to approaches in manifold learning [21, 9], the authors provide a novel statistical interpretation as a locally adaptive normal distribution. The extension to mixture models and the associated EM algorithm are also novel contributions.

Significance: The LAND provides a theoretically grounded approach for modeling data on nonlinear manifolds, which is important for many real-world applications where data naturally lies on low-dimensional manifolds. The EEG sleep staging application demonstrates practical relevance, showing that LAND better captures the underlying structure than standard Gaussian mixtures. However, the scalability concerns raised by the computational complexity of geodesic calculations may limit the method's applicability to high-dimensional data, which is a common scenario in modern machine learning. The authors acknowledge this limitation in their discussion, suggesting that for high-dimensional data, probabilistic manifold learning [22] or mixture of metrics [9] might be more practical.

Strengths:
- Solid theoretical foundation with connections to Riemannian statistics and manifold learning
- Clear derivation of LAND as a maximum entropy distribution
- Practical demonstration on both synthetic and real EEG data
- Well-developed algorithm for maximum likelihood estimation
- Extensive discussion of limitations and potential extensions

Weaknesses:
- Limited computational complexity analysis and comparison with standard EM
- Some confusing connections between local covariance in Equation (7) and maximum likelihood estimation in Equation (11)
- Insufficient explanation of why tangent space integration (Equation 9) is preferred over direct integration
- Limited discussion of how to select the smoothing parameter σ, which is critical for performance
- The EEG application, while interesting, lacks comparison with other state-of-the-art manifold-based methods

Pros for acceptance:
- Theoretical contribution is significant and fills an important gap in the literature
- Clear connection to established work in Riemannian statistics and manifold learning
- Demonstrates practical utility in a real-world application
- Well-motivated approach with solid mathematical foundation

Cons for acceptance:
- Computational efficiency concerns may limit practical applicability
- Lack of thorough comparison with standard methods in terms of running time
- Some technical aspects (local covariance definition and normalization) need clearer explanation

Overall, this is a technically sound paper with a novel theoretical contribution that bridges two important areas (Riemannian statistics and manifold learning). While the computational concerns are valid, the authors provide a clear path forward for addressing them. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field, and with minor improvements in clarity and additional computational analysis, it would be a strong candidate for NIPS.
