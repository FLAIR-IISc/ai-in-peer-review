This paper introduces a method for modeling the observation space using a hand-crafted Riemannian metric and defines a curved normal distribution based on the resulting Riemannian geometry to better capture the underlying data manifold. The work addresses the important problem of defining a Riemannian metric from high-dimensional observations and subsequently defining and estimating a curved Gaussian distribution within this geometry, offering a potentially valuable tool and an interesting application of Riemannian geometry to statistical learning. The definition of the Riemannian geometry is presented in Section 3.1. While the title suggests a learned metric, the metric in equation 7 is, in fact, hand-crafted and determined by the fixed hyperparameters ρ and σ. The sensitivity of the method to the choice of σ warrants further investigation; a more detailed discussion of the intuitive effects of varying σ and guidance on its selection would strengthen the paper. The potential for integrating metric learning into the LAND learning process, allowing for optimization of ρ and σ, should be explored. A key benefit of a geometric approach is invariance to coordinate transformations, however, the proposed metric in equation 7 lacks this property. For instance, a dataset forming a line in a 2D space would yield different metrics depending on the coordinate system’s alignment with that line. A more thorough discussion of this limitation, including analysis of the full metric’s invariance and further intuition regarding equation 7, is needed. The paper would benefit from relating its approach to the existing literature in metric learning and manifold learning, particularly work connecting these fields to Riemannian geometry. 

A significant concern arises in the first experiment. The comparison is currently skewed, as the greater flexibility of LAND naturally leads to better performance with fewer components. However, a GMM with a sufficiently large number of components could achieve comparable or superior performance, potentially overfitting the data. I recommend redesigning the experiment to allow each method to select its optimal number of components based on a principled criterion (e.g., BIC), enabling a more meaningful comparison. Observing LAND’s performance with a large number of components and its susceptibility to overfitting would also be valuable. Finally, LAND’s computational cost compared to Gaussian estimation is not quantified. Providing quantitative measurements of this cost, identifying the scalability limits of LAND, and potentially demonstrating its application to a larger dataset like MNIST alongside Gaussian mixture learning would be beneficial.

Following review of the authors’ response, it is clear that LAND represents a more flexible model than a standard Gaussian distribution. Increasing the number of components increases LAND’s vulnerability to local noise compared to a Gaussian. While the authors state geodesics will approximate straight lines, they remain curves and can contribute to overfitting. I strongly recommend revising the experiment to address this point.
