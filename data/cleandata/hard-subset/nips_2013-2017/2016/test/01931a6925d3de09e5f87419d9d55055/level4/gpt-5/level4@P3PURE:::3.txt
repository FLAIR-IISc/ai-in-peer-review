This paper proposes to model the observation space with a hand-crafted Riemannian metric and to define a curved normal (Gaussian) distribution based on the associated geometry, with the goal of better capturing the data manifold. It tackles a fundamental problem: how to define a Riemannian metric from high-dimensional real-valued observations and then how to define and estimate a curved Gaussian distribution on that geometry. This is a useful tool and an interesting application of Riemannian geometry in statistical learning.

Strengths
- Clear motivation for using geometry to capture manifold structure in high-dimensional data.
- A coherent framework that combines a metric on the observation space with a curved Gaussian family.
- Potentially broad applicability in statistical learning.

Main concerns and suggestions
1) “Learning” the metric vs. hand-crafted design
- Section 3.1 defines the underlying Riemannian geometry. While the title suggests that the method “learns a metric,” the metric in Equation (7) is hand-crafted, controlled by two hyperparameters, ρ and σ, which are fixed empirically.
- σ in particular appears likely to be sensitive. The paper should discuss in detail the intuitive effect of varying σ (e.g., how it changes local curvature, effective neighborhood size, and the resulting geodesics) and provide principled guidance for selecting σ. Without this discussion, the contribution feels incomplete.
- Is it possible to merge the metric specification stage with the LAND learning stage so that ρ and σ are learned jointly from data (e.g., by maximizing the likelihood with appropriate regularization or priors)? Even partial adaptation (e.g., per-dataset or per-component tuning) would strengthen the claim of “learning” a metric.

2) Coordinate invariance
- A key appeal of geometric approaches is invariance to coordinate transformations. However, the metric in Equation (7) is not invariant. For example, if the data lie approximately on a 1D line in 2D space, rotating the coordinate system (e.g., by 45°) will induce different metrics under Eq. (7).
- The paper should discuss this lack of invariance more explicitly and provide intuition for Eq. (7): why is a diagonal form appropriate, what information is being captured or discarded, and how does this choice affect geodesics and densities?
- Please clarify whether using a full (rather than diagonal) metric would address or mitigate the invariance issue, and, if so, what trade-offs it would introduce (estimation stability, sample complexity, computational cost).

3) Relation to prior work
- There is a rich literature on metric learning and manifold learning with connections to Riemannian geometry. Positioning the proposed metric construction and the curved Gaussian model relative to this body of work would improve the paper’s clarity and impact. At minimum, discuss similarities/differences in objectives (e.g., local vs. global structure), invariance properties, and learning procedures.

4) Experimental design and model comparison
- In the first experiment, the comparison is not very informative: a more flexible model like LAND can fit the data better with fewer components, but a GMM with a larger number of components will often match or exceed performance, while LAND is likely to overfit as the number of components grows.
- A more meaningful comparison would allow each method to select its own number of components via a standard criterion (e.g., BIC), and then compare performance under those selections. LAND is still expected to perform better if it truly captures manifold structure, but this protocol avoids biasing the comparison by fixing the same number of components across models with different flexibility.
- It would also be useful to explicitly demonstrate how LAND behaves as the number of components increases, including cases where it overfits.

5) Computational cost and scalability
- LAND is clearly more computationally expensive than estimating a Euclidean Gaussian. The paper should include quantitative measurements (e.g., runtime, memory, and their scaling with number of samples, dimensionality, and number of components), and clarify the dominant computational bottlenecks (e.g., geodesic computations, numerical integration, normalization).
- What is the practical scale limit that LAND can currently handle? An experiment on a larger dataset (e.g., MNIST) with a comparison to a GMM baseline would help establish empirical scalability and trade-offs.

After reading the authors’ response
- I agree that as the number of components increases, geodesics may become “almost straight lines.” However, they are still curved, and that residual curvature can amplify sensitivity to local noise. Consequently, each local LAND component can be more vulnerable to overfitting than a local Gaussian under comparable conditions.
- I strongly recommend revising the first experiment accordingly: allow data-driven component selection (e.g., via BIC), and include a study of overfitting behavior as the number of components increases for both LAND and GMM.

Overall assessment
- The paper presents an interesting and potentially impactful application of Riemannian geometry to density modeling. However, the current version would benefit from: (i) a clearer and more principled treatment of the metric (including the role and learning of ρ and σ), (ii) a discussion of invariance and the implications of the chosen metric form, (iii) stronger positioning within related work, (iv) a more balanced experimental design, and (v) quantitative analysis of computational cost and scalability. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the contribution.
