The paper proposes LAND, a novel parametric metric defined over a nonparametric space, bridging concepts from metric learning, manifold learning, and Riemannian statistics. The learned parametric metric is a Riemannian normal distribution, as defined in Pennec [15]. The authors propose a maximum likelihood algorithm to estimate the mean and covariance of this distribution, effectively learning a Mahalanobis distance for Riemannian manifolds. They define distances between points as geodesics along the manifold, relying on learning a local metric – which they achieve by utilizing the inverses of local (diagonal) covariances. The paper also considers mixtures of LANDs. This work offers new insights into the connection between metric learning and Riemannian statistics. The development of parametric, generative models capable of extrapolating beyond the training data is valuable in numerous scenarios. 

However, my primary concern lies with the applicability of LAND to real-world data, which is often large-scale and high-dimensional. LAND requires computing a (diagonal) covariance matrix, and its inverse, for each data point. These computations are known to be challenging in high dimensions. While the authors acknowledge high-dimensionality as a potential issue (lines 265-266), a more thorough analysis of the metric’s complexity and limitations is needed. Specifically, how does the dimensionality of the data impact performance? What constitutes “high-dimensional” in this context – 3, 5, 10, or 100 dimensions? 

The literature on metric learning is extensive, and the paper would benefit from a more detailed positioning within this context. Hauberg et al. [9] already discuss relevant work, such as Frome et al. [1,2] and Malisiewicz and Efros [6], all of which learn a diagonal metric tensor for each point, similar to LAND. Local covariances have also been used in methods like local PCA (Kambhatla – Dimension reduction by local principal component analysis), albeit not necessarily for every point. 

Lines 101-103 state that the authors employ a Gaussian kernel to define locality for learning the local metric. How does the parameter σ influence the results? The choice of σ is discussed only for the sleep data in Section 4.2, and not for the synthetic data in Section 4.1. Robustness tests for σ would strengthen the paper. Is σ consistent for all points, regardless of whether they reside in low- or high-density regions?

Figure 4 compares LAND to intrinsic estimators, with fairly similar results. However, the authors do not present visual results comparing LAND to these intrinsic estimators in Figures 5 and 6. Instead, visualization results are shown against a Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM), which Figure 4 already demonstrates performs poorly. This comparison is therefore less informative. Visual results using the intrinsic model *are* shown in the supplementary material (Figures 2 and 4), and these outperform GMM. Consequently, these results should likely replace the GMM results in the main paper, or space be made for both. 

An analysis of the computational complexity of LAND versus intrinsic estimators would be valuable, helping to determine which method is preferable and for what types of data. The authors also consider mixtures of LANDs, which I believe is one of the most promising applications of this work, as real-world data is rarely generated by a single component. This aspect warrants further exploration.

Overall, the paper is exceptionally well-written and a pleasure to read.
