This paper proposes modeling the observation space with a hand-crafted Riemannian metric and defining a curved normal distribution based on the associated geometry to better capture the data manifold. The work addresses the fundamental problems of defining a Riemannian metric from high-dimensional observations and then defining and estimating a curved Gaussian distribution based on this geometry. This represents an interesting application of Riemannian geometry in statistical learning and presents a potentially useful tool.

My primary concerns are as follows:

**1. Metric Definition and Hyperparameters:**
The method for defining the underlying Riemannian geometry is described in Section 3.1. While the section is titled "Learning a Metric," the metric in Equation 7 is actually hand-crafted, determined by two hyperparameters, ρ and σ, which are fixed empirically. I suspect that σ is a sensitive parameter, yet the paper lacks a discussion of its intuitive effect or a principled method for its selection. This omission makes the method feel incomplete. The paper should discuss the sensitivity to σ and provide guidance on how to set these hyperparameters. A further suggestion would be to investigate whether it is possible to merge the metric learning stage with the main LAND learning stage, allowing ρ and σ to be learned from data.

**2. Invariance of the Metric:**
A key advantage of geometric approaches is their invariance to coordinate transformations. However, the diagonal metric proposed in Equation 7 does not possess this property. For example, if the data form a line in a 2D observation space, the resulting metric will be completely different in a coordinate system with its x-axis parallel to this line versus one that is rotated by 45 degrees. The paper would be strengthened by a detailed discussion of this issue. For instance, the authors could analyze whether a corresponding full (non-diagonal) metric would be invariant and provide more intuition behind the specific form chosen in Equation 7.

**3. Relation to Existing Work:**
The paper could be better situated within the existing literature. There is a rich body of work in metric learning and manifold learning that connects to Riemannian geometry, and referencing these related methods would provide valuable context for the proposed contribution.

**4. Experimental Evaluation (Main Criticism):**
The comparison in the first experiment is not particularly meaningful. It is unsurprising that the more flexible LAND model can describe the data better with a smaller number of components than a Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM). A more insightful comparison would investigate the trade-off between model complexity and performance, as a GMM might perform better with a large number of components, a regime where LAND is likely to overfit.

I strongly suggest that the authors redesign this experiment. Each compared method should be allowed to select its optimal number of components based on a model selection criterion (e.g., BIC), after which their best-case performances can be fairly compared. This comparison would be more meaningful and would likely still highlight LAND's strengths in capturing manifold structure.

Furthermore, the paper should explicitly discuss and demonstrate the overfitting behavior of LAND. As a more flexible local model than a standard Gaussian, each LAND component is more vulnerable to fitting local noise when the number of components is large. Even if the geodesics become "almost straight lines," they are still curves and can lead to overfitting. I highly recommend that this experiment be revised to address these points.

**5. Computational Cost and Scalability:**
LAND is obviously a more computationally expensive procedure than estimating a standard Gaussian. The paper would benefit significantly from a quantitative analysis of this cost. What is the scalability of the algorithm, and what are the practical limits on the dataset size and dimensionality that LAND can handle? An experiment applying LAND to a larger dataset like MNIST, with a comparison to GMMs, would provide a much clearer picture of the method's practicality and performance.
