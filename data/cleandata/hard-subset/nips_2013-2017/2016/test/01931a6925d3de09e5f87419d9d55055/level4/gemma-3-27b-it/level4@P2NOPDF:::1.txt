This paper introduces LAND, a novel parametric metric learning approach that cleverly combines insights from metric learning, manifold learning, and Riemannian statistics. Specifically, LAND defines a metric as a Riemannian normal distribution – a Mahalanobis distance generalized to Riemannian manifolds, as formalized by Pennec [15]. The authors develop a maximum likelihood estimation algorithm to learn the mean and covariance parameters of this distribution, effectively learning a local metric by leveraging the inverses of local diagonal covariances. The consideration of mixtures of LANDs further enhances the model's potential.

The paper’s core strength lies in its insightful connection between metric learning and Riemannian statistics, offering a parametric and generative framework that can, in principle, generalize beyond the training data—a valuable property in many applications. However, a primary concern is the practical applicability of LAND to large-scale, high-dimensional datasets. The method relies on computing and inverting covariance matrices for each data point, operations that are computationally challenging and ill-conditioned in high dimensions. While the authors acknowledge this limitation (lines 265-266), a more thorough analysis of the computational complexity and scalability of LAND is crucial. Quantifying “high-dimensional” is important – for example, does the method become impractical beyond 10, 50, or 100 dimensions?

The existing metric learning literature is extensive, and the paper would benefit from a more comprehensive positioning within this context. Hauberg et al. [9] provide a starting point, but works like Frome et al. [1,2] and Malisiewicz and Efros [6] also learn per-point diagonal metric tensors similar to LAND. Furthermore, local covariance estimation appears in related approaches such as local PCA (Kambhatla), though not necessarily on a per-point basis. 

Several specific points warrant further attention. The authors utilize a Gaussian kernel to define locality for learning the local metric (lines 101-103). The sensitivity of the results to the kernel bandwidth parameter, sigma, requires investigation. While sigma’s influence is discussed for the sleep data (Sect. 4.2), its effect on the synthetic data (Sect. 4.1) remains unexplored. Robustness tests varying sigma would strengthen the paper. It is also unclear whether a single sigma value is used for all points, regardless of local data density.

The comparison in Fig. 4 includes intrinsic estimators, but the results are comparable to LAND. However, Figs. 5 and 6 only present visualizations against a Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM), which Fig. 4 already demonstrates performs poorly. This comparison is therefore less informative. Visual results with the intrinsic estimators, which are shown in the supplementary material (Figs. 2 and 4) and appear more promising than GMM, should be incorporated into the main paper—either replacing the GMM comparisons or presented alongside them. A comparative analysis of the computational complexity of LAND versus intrinsic estimators would be valuable, helping to delineate scenarios where each method is preferable based on data characteristics.

Finally, the exploration of mixtures of LANDs is a particularly interesting contribution, as real-world data is often generated by multiple components. This aspect of the work deserves further elaboration and investigation. 

Overall, this is a well-written and conceptually strong paper. Addressing the concerns regarding scalability, contextualizing the work within the broader metric learning landscape, and expanding the comparative analysis would significantly enhance its impact.
