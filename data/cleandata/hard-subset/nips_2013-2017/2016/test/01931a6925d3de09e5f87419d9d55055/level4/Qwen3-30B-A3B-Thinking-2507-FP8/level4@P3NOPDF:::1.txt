

The paper introduces LAND (Learning a Riemannian Normal Distribution), a novel parametric metric framework bridging metric learning, manifold learning, and Riemannian statistics. LAND defines a Riemannian normal distribution (Pennec [15]) over a data manifold, where distances are computed via geodesics (Hauberg et al. [9]). The core innovation lies in learning a local Mahalanobis metric per point using inverses of diagonal covariances, enabling a generative model that extends beyond training data. Mixtures of LANDs are also proposed, offering potential for multi-component data. The theoretical connection between metric learning and Riemannian statistics is well-articulated, and the paper is clearly written.

However, the work requires significant refinement to establish its practical relevance and novelty within the metric learning literature. The primary concern is scalability to large-scale, high-dimensional data—a critical requirement for real-world applications. LAND computes a diagonal covariance matrix and its inverse for each data point, a computationally demanding task in high dimensions. The authors briefly note this challenge (lines 265–266) but provide no concrete analysis of complexity or dimensionality limits. Specifically:  
- What constitutes "high-dimensional" in this context? (e.g., 3D, 10D, 100D?)  
- How does computational cost scale with dimensionality and dataset size?  
- Are there practical dimensionality thresholds where LAND becomes infeasible?  

Without this analysis, the paper’s utility for real-world data remains unclear. The literature on metric learning is extensive, and LAND’s connection to prior work needs sharper contextualization. Hauberg et al. [9] already discuss diagonal metric learning (e.g., Frome et al. [1,2], Malisiewicz and Efros [6]), where local metrics are similarly defined per point. Local PCA (Kambhatla) also uses local covariances, though not pointwise. The paper should explicitly differentiate LAND’s contribution from these existing approaches—particularly in terms of generative modeling, Riemannian formalism, and mixture extensions.

The hyperparameter $\sigma$ (Gaussian kernel bandwidth for locality) is inadequately addressed. Its influence on results is only analyzed for the sleep dataset (Section 4.2), with no discussion for synthetic data (Section 4.1). Key questions remain:  
- Is $\sigma$ uniform across regions of varying data density?  
- How sensitive are results to $\sigma$’s choice?  
- What is the optimal $\sigma$ strategy for different data structures?  
Robustness tests across $\sigma$ values and density regimes would strengthen the methodology.

Visualization choices further weaken the empirical evaluation. Figure 4 compares LAND against intrinsic estimators and shows similar performance, yet Figures 5–6 only contrast LAND with GMM—despite Figure 4 demonstrating GMM’s inferiority. Supplementary Figures 2 and 4 (intrinsic estimators) outperform GMM, yet the main paper does not incorporate these results. This omission reduces the paper’s insightfulness. The authors should either replace GMM visualizations with intrinsic estimator results or include both for direct comparison.

The mixture extension of LAND is promising but underdeveloped. The paper states that real-world data rarely follows a single component, yet it offers no analysis of mixture convergence, component selection, or scalability. This aspect warrants deeper exploration, as it could significantly enhance LAND’s practical utility.

In summary, LAND presents a theoretically interesting fusion of Riemannian statistics and metric learning, but its real-world viability hinges on addressing scalability and contextualizing its novelty. The following revisions are essential:  
1. **Complexity analysis**: Quantify computational costs and dimensionality limits.  
2. **Literature positioning**: Clarify how LAND advances beyond Hauberg [9], Frome [1,2], and local PCA.  
3. **Hyperparameter rigor**: Provide $\sigma$ sensitivity analysis and density-aware strategies.  
4. **Empirical presentation**: Replace GMM visualizations with intrinsic estimator results (from supplementary material).  
5. **Mixture extension**: Expand on mixture learning, convergence, and practical implementation.  

The paper’s theoretical contributions are valuable, but without these clarifications, its impact on the metric learning community will be limited. Addressing these points would transform LAND from a promising concept into a compelling, actionable framework. The writing is strong, but the empirical and analytical rigor must be elevated to match the theoretical novelty.
