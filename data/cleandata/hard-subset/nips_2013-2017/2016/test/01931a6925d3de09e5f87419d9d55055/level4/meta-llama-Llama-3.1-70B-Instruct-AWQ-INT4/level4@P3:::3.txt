This paper proposes a novel approach to modeling the observation space with a locally adaptive Riemannian metric, defining a curved normal distribution based on the associated Riemannian geometry. The authors address a fundamental problem in statistical learning by introducing a parametric locally adaptive normal distribution (LAND), which replaces the Euclidean distance in the ordinary normal distribution with a locally adaptive nonlinear distance measure. The paper presents a useful tool and an interesting application of Riemannian geometry in statistical learning.

The main idea of the paper is to learn a non-parametric metric space by constructing a smoothly changing metric that induces a Riemannian manifold, where the model is built. The non-parametric space is constructed using a local metric that is the inverse of a local covariance matrix, with locality defined via a Gaussian kernel. This indicates that the scheme for learning a manifold might not scale to high-dimensional input spaces.

The authors develop a maximum likelihood algorithm to infer the distribution parameters, relying on a combination of gradient descent and Monte Carlo integration. They further extend the LAND to mixture models and provide the corresponding EM algorithm. The usefulness of the model is verified on both synthetic data and EEG measurements of human sleep stages.

The paper has several strengths. Firstly, it introduces a novel approach to modeling the observation space with a locally adaptive Riemannian metric. Secondly, it provides a useful tool for statistical learning by defining a curved normal distribution based on the associated Riemannian geometry. Finally, the authors demonstrate the efficiency of the LAND to fit non-trivial probability distributions over both synthetic data and real-world data.

However, there are some limitations and potential improvements. One limitation is that the metric learning stage is not merged with the LAND learning stage, and the hyper-parameters rho and sigma are fixed empirically in advance. It would be beneficial to discuss the intuitive effect of varying sigma and how to select this sigma. Additionally, the metric in equation 7 is not invariant to coordinate transformations, which could be problematic in certain applications.

The comparison with the Gaussian mixture model (GMM) in the first experiment is not entirely meaningful, as the more flexible LAND can describe the data better with a smaller number of components. However, GMM can describe the data better with a large number of components when LAND will overfit. It would be more meaningful to redesign the experiment to let each compared method choose its favorable number of components based on a certain criterion (e.g., BIC) and then compare the corresponding performance.

The authors should also provide quantitative measurements or experiments to make it clear how expensive the LAND procedure is compared to estimating a Gaussian. Furthermore, it would be interesting to know the limit of the scale that LAND can handle and whether it is possible to learn LAND on MNIST and compare it with Gaussian mixture learning.

Overall, the paper presents a novel and interesting approach to statistical learning. With some revisions to address the limitations and potential improvements, it has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field.
