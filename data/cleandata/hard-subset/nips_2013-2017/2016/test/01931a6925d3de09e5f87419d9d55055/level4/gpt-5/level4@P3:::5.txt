Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), a parametric density defined on a Riemannian manifold whose metric is learned nonparametrically from data. The key idea is to replace the Euclidean/Mahalanobis distance in a Gaussian density with a geodesic distance induced by a smoothly varying metric that favors regions of high data density. Under this metric, the LAND coincides with the Riemannian normal distribution of Pennec (maximum entropy given mean and covariance), but crucially the manifold is not known a priori; it is learned from data via a kernel-smoothed local covariance (diagonal) metric. The paper derives maximum-likelihood estimators for the mean and covariance using gradient descent combined with Monte Carlo estimation of the normalization constant, and extends the model to a mixture of LANDs with an EM algorithm. Experiments on synthetic data and EEG sleep data suggest LAND better captures nonlinear structure than Gaussian mixtures, and its ML mean avoids low-density regions compared to intrinsic least-squares estimators. The work connects manifold learning (e.g., Isomap) with statistics on manifolds and complements prior Riemannian modeling on known manifolds (e.g., Pennec; Zhang & Fletcher) and geodesic mixture models (Simo-Serra et al., Straub et al.).

Strengths:
- Conceptually elegant generalization of Gaussians to data-adaptive Riemannian geometries, with clear grounding in maximum-entropy arguments.
- Practical ML estimation using tangent-space Monte Carlo for the partition function; avoids expensive boundary-value problems for Log maps during normalization.
- Mixture extension with a principled EM that accounts for component-specific normalization constants.
- Empirical demonstrations show improved fit to nonlinear structure and more meaningful cluster contours compared to GMMs.

Weaknesses and questions:

Quality (soundness and evaluation):
- Metric construction (Eq. 7) is intuitive but appears ad hoc: a diagonal inverse local covariance with Gaussian kernel weights. The choice of diagonal structure and isotropic kernel may limit fidelity and invariance; more justification and ablations (e.g., k-NN neighborhoods, full vs. diagonal, pre-whitening) would help.
- The paper asserts that for the manifolds considered D(μ̂)=R^D and that Log/Exp are well-behaved, but this is nontrivial for learned metrics. Please discuss existence/uniqueness of geodesics and practical handling of cut loci and numerical failures.
- Computational complexity and runtime are not reported. Each evaluation of m(μ,v)=|M(Expμ(v))|^(1/2) requires solving an ODE IVP to compute Expμ(v), and computing M(x) via Eq. 7 involves summing over all N data points per evaluation unless a sparse neighborhood is used. With S≈3000 samples per iteration and K mixture components, this can be expensive. Provide per-iteration complexity, wall-clock runtimes, and scaling with N, D, S, and K, and compare to standard GMM-EM on the same hardware.
- The Monte Carlo estimator of C(μ,Σ) can have high variance, especially when Σ adapts. Discuss variance reduction (e.g., reparameterizing via A with fixed base samples, control variates) and sensitivity to S. Confidence intervals on likelihoods would strengthen the claims.
- Empirical evaluation is narrow: mostly one synthetic setup and a single real domain. Additional baselines (e.g., KDE, spectral clustering for clustering tasks, geodesic mixture models without normalization) and held-out likelihoods or cross-validated metrics would better support significance. For the EEG application, report variability across subjects, calibration of σ, and comparisons beyond F-measure.

Clarity:
- Clarify the relationship between Eq. 7 and Eq. 11. Eq. 7 defines the Riemannian metric M(x) (nonparametric, learned once from data) used to compute geodesics and the induced measure; Eq. 11 then maximizes the likelihood of the parametric distribution pM(x|μ,Σ) over μ,Σ on that fixed manifold. These are distinct quantities; Eq. 11 is ML (not MAP).
- Provide more intuition for Eq. 7: why Gaussian kernels, why diagonal, the role of ρ, and how σ is chosen. A short discussion of invariance (e.g., effects of scaling/rotation, need for whitening) would be helpful.
- Algorithm 1: the notation dμφ vs. ∇μφ is potentially confusing. You ultimately use the steepest descent direction dμφ = −Σ∇μφ; please state this explicitly and adopt consistent notation in both text and pseudocode.
- A figure illustrating the tangent-space Monte Carlo normalization and the difference between intrinsic mean vs. ML mean would aid understanding.

Originality:
- The contribution lies in bringing the Riemannian normal to learned manifolds and making ML estimation practical by tangent-space Monte Carlo, together with a properly normalized mixture. This is a meaningful extension beyond prior work that assumed known manifolds or neglected normalization. Please more clearly delineate novelty relative to Hauberg et al. (metric learning), Tosi et al. (probabilistic geometries), and Simo-Serra et al. (geodesic mixtures).

Significance:
- The approach is promising for generative modeling near nonlinear structures and could influence manifold-aware variants of classic models. However, practical impact depends on scalability and robustness in higher dimensions. Discussion and experiments on higher-D datasets or strategies (e.g., local neighborhoods, inducing points, mixtures of metrics) would increase confidence.

Specific questions and requests for authors:
- Provide complexity analysis and wall-clock runtimes per iteration and per EM step, including typical ODE step counts and whether you restrict Eq. 7 to k nearest neighbors.
- Detail how σ and ρ are chosen; can σ be learned (e.g., via marginal likelihood, cross-validation, or an EM outer loop)?
- Do you reuse Monte Carlo samples across iterations by reparameterization (e.g., fixed standard normals transformed by A), and do you employ variance reduction?
- How often do IVP solvers fail or produce inaccurate geodesics, and how is this detected/handled?
- How sensitive are results to the diagonal-metric assumption? Any ablation?

Pros for acceptance:
- Elegant and principled model bridging manifold learning and Riemannian statistics.
- Solid theoretical framing (maximum entropy) and correct handling of normalization in mixtures.
- Clear empirical benefit on nonlinear synthetic data and a real EEG task.

Cons against acceptance:
- Limited experimental scope and missing scalability/runtimes.
- Metric design and hyperparameter choices are heuristic; lack of sensitivity analyses.
- Clarity issues around notation and the distinction between metric learning and parameter estimation.

Minor comments:
- Fix typos and grammar throughout (e.g., “geodesic,” “Isomap,” “complexity,” “covariance”).
- State that Eq. 11 is maximum likelihood, not MAP.
- Algorithm 1: clarify the use of the steepest descent direction dμφ and ensure consistent symbols between text and pseudocode.
- Consider pre-whitening to mitigate lack of linear invariance of Eq. 7, or discuss alternative metrics (e.g., local PCA frames or full local covariances).
- Consider reporting AIC/BIC and held-out likelihoods for all models, with error bars over multiple random initializations.

Overall:
An interesting and potentially impactful contribution with solid ideas, but the paper would benefit from clearer exposition of the metric vs. model parameters, stronger empirical evaluation, and a convincing discussion of computational complexity and scalability.
