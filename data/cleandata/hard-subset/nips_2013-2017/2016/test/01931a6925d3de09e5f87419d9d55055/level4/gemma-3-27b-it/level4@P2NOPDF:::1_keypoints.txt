- Proposes LAND, a parametric metric learning method defining a metric as a Riemannian normal (generalized Mahalanobis) distribution and learns mean/covariance via maximum likelihood.  
- Combines metric learning, manifold learning, and Riemannian statistics into a generative framework that can generalize beyond training data.  
- Uses local diagonal covariance inverses to learn per-point local metrics and considers mixtures of LANDs.  
- Main practical concern: scalability to large-scale, high-dimensional data due to per-point covariance computation and inversion—may be computationally challenging and ill-conditioned.  
- Authors note this limitation (lines 265–266) but need a more thorough computational-complexity and scalability analysis, and to quantify what “high-dimensional” means (e.g., >10, 50, 100 dims).  
- Paper insufficiently positions itself in the broader metric-learning literature; should relate to prior per-point/diagonal metric work (e.g., Frome et al., Malisiewicz & Efros) and local covariance/PCA methods.  
- The Gaussian kernel used to define locality raises questions about sensitivity to the bandwidth sigma; robustness tests and clarification on whether sigma is global or adaptive are needed.  
- Experimental comparisons: Fig.4 includes intrinsic estimators, but Figs.5–6 only compare to GMM (a poor baseline); visualizations with intrinsic estimators should be moved into main paper or shown alongside GMM.  
- A comparative analysis of computational cost between LAND and intrinsic estimators would clarify when each method is preferable.  
- The mixtures-of-LANDs idea is promising and warrants more detailed exploration and empirical evaluation.  
- Overall: conceptually strong and well-written, but needs work on scalability, literature context, robustness to kernel choices, and stronger comparative analyses to enhance impact.
