

This paper proposes modeling the observation space using a hand-crafted Riemannian metric and defines a curved Gaussian distribution based on the associated Riemannian geometry to better capture the data manifold. It addresses the fundamental problem of defining a Riemannian metric from high-dimensional real-valued observations and subsequently estimating a curved Gaussian distribution based on this geometry. This represents a useful tool and an interesting application of Riemannian geometry in statistical learning.  

The method’s first component, presented in Section 3.1, defines the underlying Riemannian geometry. While the title suggests "learning a metric," the metric is actually hand-crafted in Equation (7) using fixed hyper-parameters ρ and σ, determined empirically in advance. The paper should elaborate on the intuitive effect of varying σ and provide a principled method for selecting it. Without this, the approach remains incomplete. Crucially, it should be explored whether the metric learning stage can be merged with the LAND learning stage to jointly optimize ρ and σ.  

A core strength of geometric approaches is coordinate invariance, yet the metric in Equation (7) lacks this property. For example, consider data lying on a line in 2D space: the diagonal metric in Equation (7) yields completely different values under coordinate systems where the x-axis is parallel to the line versus one rotated by 45 degrees. The paper must discuss this in detail, including whether a full (non-diagonal) metric would be invariant and provide deeper intuition for Equation (7). The work should also engage more thoroughly with the rich literature on Riemannian metric learning and manifold learning (e.g., via "Riemannian metric learning manifold learning" on Google Scholar).  

A critical flaw lies in the first experiment. The comparison is uninformative because LAND’s flexibility inherently allows it to describe data with fewer components than GMM, while GMM can achieve better fit with sufficient components (where LAND overfits). The authors should redesign the experiment: allow each method to select its optimal number of components (e.g., via BIC), then compare performance. LAND should still outperform GMM due to its superior manifold modeling, making this comparison meaningful. The current experiment’s observation of LAND overfitting with high component counts is valuable but requires proper contextualization.  

Additionally, LAND is computationally more expensive than Gaussian estimation. The paper should provide quantitative measurements of this cost (e.g., runtime scaling) and clarify the practical scalability limits (e.g., maximum dataset size or dimensionality it can handle). It would be valuable to demonstrate LAND on MNIST and compare its performance with GMMs under equivalent computational constraints.  

After reading the authors’ response, the point about LAND’s increased vulnerability to local noise with more components (as geodesics approach straight lines) is noted. However, the curved nature of geodesics still leads to overfitting, and the experiment design must be revised to ensure a fair comparison. The authors should address these points in the revision.
