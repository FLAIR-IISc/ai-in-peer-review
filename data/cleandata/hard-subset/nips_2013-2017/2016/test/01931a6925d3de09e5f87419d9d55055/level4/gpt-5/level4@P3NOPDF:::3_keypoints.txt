- Proposes a hand-crafted Riemannian metric and a curved normal (LAND) to better model data near low-dimensional manifolds.  
- Strength: Addresses an important problem where Euclidean Gaussians can fail on manifold-like data.  
- Strength: Provides a clear geometric framework and a practical algorithmic instantiation.  
- Strength: The curved analogue of a Gaussian is conceptually appealing and broadly useful.  
- Major concern: Claim to “learn a metric” is misleading—metric form is fixed with hyperparameters ρ and σ tuned a priori, not learned from data.  
- Major concern: σ likely strongly affects curvature, locality, and model capacity; the paper lacks intuition, principled selection methods, and empirical sensitivity analysis for σ.  
- Major concern: Authors should consider jointly learning ρ and σ with LAND (e.g., via likelihood maximization with regularization) to avoid hand-tuning.  
- Major concern: The proposed diagonal metric (Eq. 7) appears coordinate-dependent and not invariant under reparameterizations like rotations.  
- Major concern: Need discussion of non-diagonal metrics, invariance under orthogonal transforms, trade-offs in complexity/estimation, and practical mitigations (pre-whitening, PCA, learning off-diagonals).  
- Major concern: Better position the work relative to prior metric- and manifold-learning literature (Riemannian metric learning, diffusion/Laplace–Beltrami approaches, information geometry).  
- Experimental concern: Comparisons to GMMs are unfair—fixing equal component counts misrepresents model selection; recommend letting models choose K via BIC/validation and comparing best configs.  
- Experimental concern: Show how LAND behaves as the number of components increases (training vs test likelihood) to reveal overfitting and curvature effects.  
- Computational concern: LAND is likely more expensive—paper needs time/memory complexity in N, D, K, wall-clock benchmarks vs GMM, and practical scalability limits.  
- Clarity/request: Title/claims should reflect the metric is hand-crafted unless a learning mechanism is added; include geodesic/volume visualizations and ablation/sensitivity studies for ρ and σ.  
- Post-rebuttal note: Acknowledging geodesics become straight with many components is good, but residual curvature can still cause local overfitting—reinforces need for fair model selection experiments.  
- Overall assessment: Promising geometric approach but incomplete—requires learned or better-justified metrics, invariance discussion, stronger empirical design, and computational benchmarks before being convincing.
