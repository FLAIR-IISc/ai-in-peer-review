In this paper, the authors present a locally adaptive normal distribution. Instead of relying on Euclidean distance, they define a Riemannian metric M(x) that varies smoothly across the dataset. Given this metric, geodesic distances along the manifold can be calculated via the exponential and logarithmic maps. The authors leverage the Euler-Lagrange equations to define a set of ordinary differential equations for estimating these geodesic distances. The logarithm map projects points from the manifold (y) into the tangent plane (at x) such that the length of the resulting vector corresponds to the geodesic distance from x to y along the manifold. They then apply a normal distribution, computing a Mahalanobis distance on points mapped by the logarithm map, effectively using an inner product computed via M(x) to measure distances from a point x to the mean, rather than Euclidean distances. The authors derive estimators for the mean and covariance via maximum likelihood. Because closed-form solutions are unavailable, they present a steepest descent algorithm for parameter estimation, along with a Monte-Carlo approximation method for the intractable normalization constant. They validate their approach on synthetic data generated on a manifold and apply it to a clustering task, comparing its performance to a standard mixture of Gaussians. Overall, the paper provides an interesting and useful extension of the normal distribution, adapting it to local manifold structure. The authors effectively combine techniques from metric estimation, geodesic distance computation, and distribution learning. I believe this work is worthy of publication.

**Technical Quality Comments:** The manifold techniques—specifically, the use of Euler-Lagrange equations and estimation of logarithm maps—are sound and well explained. The Monte-Carlo estimation of the normalization constant and the steepest descent algorithm are clever and clearly described. However, the analysis of the applications feels somewhat incomplete; the sigma parameter appears to be selected by optimizing on the training data. Furthermore, the explanation of the usefulness of the learned distribution is not fully developed.

**Novelty Comments:** The primary contribution of this paper appears to be the combination of existing work—employing kernels to define a metric M(x), estimating geodesic distances via Euler-Lagrange equations, and applying a normal distribution—and the provision of an algorithm for estimating the resulting parameters (mean, covariance, and normalization constant).

**Potential Impact Comments:** The authors provide a fair evaluation of their Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND) and Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) on synthetic data, assessing log-likelihood on data generated by the learned distributions. However, the rationale for choosing the sigma parameter remains unclear. The authors demonstrate that LAND fits well on data generated along a manifold but do not demonstrate its performance relative to a GMM when data is generated from a single normal distribution or a GMM. In the clustering application, the sigma value for LAND appears to be optimized using the F measure, and that same F measure is then presented without a clear indication of evaluation on a held-out test set. Consequently, it is difficult to determine whether overfitting is occurring. It would also be beneficial to examine the performance of this method on very high-dimensional data. While the authors suggest using LAND as a generative model for manifold data, they make the somewhat unusual assumption that the data X is observed in D dimensions and the manifold also has dimension D (i.e., points on the tangent plane are D-dimensional and M(x) is full rank). Typically, manifold learning focuses on data lying on a d-dimensional manifold embedded in a D-dimensional Euclidean space, where D is significantly larger than d.

**Clarity Comments:** I found one particular notational point confusing: the overloading of the Sigma notation. Specifically, the authors state M = Sigma<sup>-1</sup> but then estimate it using a (local) diagonal kernel matrix. Is this Sigma also identical to the fitted covariance matrix? If so, is the kernel simply used for initialization? This would be unusual, as M(x) should vary with x, while a single fitted covariance matrix is independent of x. In the proof of the steepest descent algorithm, the authors explicitly state “in our case M = Sigma<sup>-1</sup>” and use that substitution. Clarification on this point would be appreciated. In Section 4.2, the method for determining cluster membership is not explicitly stated, but I assume it involves assigning points to the nearest cluster mean based on geodesic distance. Overall, the introduction of manifold concepts was well explained, at least for readers familiar with Riemannian manifolds.
