- Novel contribution: defines a "curved normal" distribution using a Riemannian metric to better model data on manifolds.  
- Method summary: constructs a Riemannian metric for the observation space and defines a distribution based on the induced geometry.  
- Theory: presents a theoretical framework for metric construction (Section 3.1).  
- Metric implementation issue: current metric is hand-crafted (Eq.7) with hyperparameters ρ and σ; sensitivity and selection guidelines are missing.  
- Suggestion: ideally integrate metric learning with LAND estimation so ρ and σ can be learned from data.  
- Invariance concern: the proposed metric is not invariant to coordinate transforms; this limitation needs detailed discussion and comparison to full (non-diagonal) metrics.  
- Literature gap: broader Riemannian/metric/manifold learning literature should be cited and discussed.  
- Experimental weakness: LAND shows advantage over GMMs with few components, but comparisons are unfair—GMMs could match performance with more components.  
- Experimental suggestion: allow each method to choose component count via a criterion like BIC, test LAND with more components, and analyze overfitting behavior.  
- Scalability concern: computational cost and complexity of LAND vs. Gaussian estimation are not quantified; scalability to larger datasets (e.g., MNIST) should be demonstrated.  
- Pros for acceptance: novel geometry+statistical modeling, addresses manifold learning, promising synthetic and EEG results, clear mathematical presentation.  
- Cons for acceptance: hand-crafted metric hyperparameters, lack of coordinate invariance, weak experimental comparisons, limited discussion of computational complexity.  
- Overall verdict: interesting and promising but has significant methodological and experimental gaps → weak reject pending substantial revisions.
