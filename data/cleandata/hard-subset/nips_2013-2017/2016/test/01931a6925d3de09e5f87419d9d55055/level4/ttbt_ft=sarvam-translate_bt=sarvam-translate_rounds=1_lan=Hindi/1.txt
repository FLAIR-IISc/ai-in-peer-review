This paper proposes a new parametric metric called LAND, defined on a nonparametric space by combining concepts from metric learning/manifold learning and Riemannian statistics. The learned parametric metric is a Riemannian normal distribution, as defined in Penek [15]. The authors propose a maximum likelihood algorithm to estimate the mean and covariance of the Riemannian normal distribution, i.e., a Mahalanobis distance for Riemannian manifolds. The distances between points are defined as the geodesics with the Riemannian manifold, as in Huber et al. [9], and they rely on a local metric learning. Here, the authors propose metric learning using the inverses of the local (diagonal) covariance. The authors also consider LANDs' mixtures. This paper brings new insights into the relationship between metric learning and Riemannian statistics. Having parametric and generative models that can go beyond the training data is very useful in many scenarios. However, I am mainly concerned about its applicability to real-world data that is both large-scale and high-dimensional. LAND requires the computation of a (here, diagonal) covariance matrix and its inverse for each data point, which are considered difficult problems in high dimensions. The authors mention high-dimensionality as a potential issue (lines 265-266), but the paper will need to analyze the complexity and limitations of the presented metric. How high is the high-dimensionality? 3, 5, 10, 100 dimensions? The literature on metric learning is quite vast, and I think the paper needs to be better fitted in this context for future readers. Huber et al. [9] already discuss some literature on metric learning, for example, From et al. [1,2], Malysiewicz and Afros [6], which all learn a diagonal metric tensor for each point similar to LAND. Local covariance has also been used in methods such as local PCA (principal component analysis by Kambuṭṭala-local), even if not for each point. Lines 101-103: For local metric learning, the authors use a Gaussian kernel to define locality. How does the parameter sigma affect the results? The choice of sigma is only discussed for sleep data in Section 4.2, but not for synthetic data in Section 4.1. The paper would benefit from robustness tests for sigma. Is the sigma the same for all points, whether in low- or high-density regions? Figure 4 compares LAND to internal estimators, and the results are quite similar, but the authors never show visual results with these internal estimators in Figures 5 and 6. Instead, visualization results are shown against GMM, which already performs poorly in Figure 4. Therefore, it is less practical. Some visual results using the internal model are shown in the supplementary material (Figures 2 and 4), and they perform better than GMM. Therefore, these results should probably replace the GMM results in the initial paper or make room for both. Analyzing the computational complexity of LAND versus internal estimators would be interesting to see which method should be preferred over the other and for which data structures. The authors also consider LANDs' mixtures. I think this is one of the most interesting applications of LAND, as data is rarely generated by a single component. This aspect would be worth expanding on. The paper is very well written, it is pleasant to read.
