Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), which replaces the Euclidean metric in a Gaussian with a data-driven Riemannian metric so that density contours and geodesics adapt to local data structure. The resulting distribution is the Riemannian normal (maximum entropy for a given mean and covariance) defined on a learned manifold. The authors (i) construct a smoothly varying metric that favors high-density regions using a kernel-weighted local (diagonal) covariance; (ii) estimate the normalization constant via Monte Carlo in the tangent space; (iii) perform maximum-likelihood estimation of the mean and covariance using gradient-based optimization; and (iv) extend to mixtures with an EM-like procedure. Experiments on synthetic data and EEG sleep-stage features illustrate improved fit over standard Gaussians/GMMs. The work bridges Riemannian statistics (e.g., Pennec) and manifold learning (e.g., Isomap-style geodesics) by learning a metric directly from data rather than assuming a known manifold.

Strengths:
- Conceptually appealing unification of manifold learning with parametric generative modeling; the LAND retains a simple parameterization (mean, covariance) while adapting to nonlinearity through the metric.
- Clear algorithmic contributions: tangent-space Monte Carlo for normalization; practical gradients for ML estimation; and a mixture extension.
- Empirical results indicate that LAND captures nonlinear structure that GMMs struggle with, especially in clustering tasks and for compact manifold-supported distributions.
- The maximum-entropy perspective provides a principled justification for the distribution once a metric is fixed.

Weaknesses and points for clarification:
- “Learning” the metric vs. hand-crafting: The metric in Eq. 7 is specified by a kernel-weighted, diagonal local covariance with two hyperparameters (σ for locality and ρ for regularization), chosen a priori. This is closer to a hand-crafted metric than a learned one. The paper would benefit from:
  - A sensitivity analysis of σ (and ρ) and clear guidance on selection. Intuitively, larger σ smooths the metric (geodesics become straighter; LAND approaches a Gaussian), whereas smaller σ emphasizes local structure (more curvature and potentially overfitting). Please discuss this trade-off and provide a selection strategy (e.g., cross-validation, likelihood-based model selection, or joint optimization).
  - Exploring whether σ and ρ can be estimated jointly with the LAND parameters, or integrated out, possibly via a hierarchical prior or bilevel optimization.
- Invariance concerns: The diagonal metric in Eq. 7 is not invariant under linear coordinate transformations. For data lying along a line in 2D, rotating coordinates can produce different metrics. Please discuss:
  - Whether using a full local covariance (rather than diagonal) would restore affine invariance, and if so, what computational or overfitting issues arise.
  - Practical guidance on preprocessing (e.g., whitening) to mitigate coordinate-system dependence.
  - Additional intuition for the chosen diagonal form and its implications.
- Experimental design and fairness: The first synthetic experiment fixes the number of mixture components across methods. Since LAND is more flexible per component, the comparison is biased. A more meaningful design would allow each method to choose K via a standard criterion (e.g., BIC/AIC or held-out likelihood) and then compare performance at those K. LAND should still benefit from modeling the manifold, but the comparison would be more balanced. It would also be instructive to show how LAND behaves as K grows (e.g., whether curvature plus more components induces overfitting).
- Computational cost and scalability: LAND is clearly heavier than fitting a Gaussian or GMM. Please quantify:
  - Runtime and memory vs. GMM as a function of N, D, and S (Monte Carlo samples), including the cost of evaluating Exp/Log maps and geodesics.
  - Practical limits (e.g., can LAND be trained on MNIST-like datasets?) and any approximations or accelerations (e.g., subsampling for metric estimation, sparse kernels, low-rank structures, or fast ODE solvers).
- Related work: The paper could more deeply position itself within metric learning and manifold learning. There is substantial literature on data-driven Riemannian metrics and probabilistic geometries; more explicit comparisons and discussion of how LAND differs (e.g., use of diagonal local covariances, tangent-space normalization, mixture EM with normalization constants) would strengthen the contribution.

Evaluation by review criteria:
- Quality: The formulation is sound and the optimization is well motivated. However, the reliance on fixed σ and the lack of quantitative scalability analysis limit practical guidance.
- Clarity: The main ideas are conveyed clearly. The paper would be stronger with a deeper discussion of σ, invariance, and computational trade-offs, plus clearer experimental protocols for model selection.
- Originality: Moderately high. While Riemannian normals and geodesic mixtures have been studied on known manifolds, adapting them to a data-defined metric with a practical ML/EM procedure is novel and useful.
- Significance: Potentially impactful as a flexible drop-in replacement for Gaussians in manifold-structured data. Demonstrations are promising, but broader, more rigorous comparisons and scalability evidence are needed.

Pros for acceptance:
- Elegant, principled extension of Gaussians to learned manifolds.
- Practical algorithmic framework with mixture modeling.
- Empirical gains on tasks where manifold structure matters.

Cons for acceptance:
- Metric is hand-specified with sensitive hyperparameters; no joint learning or principled selection.
- Lack of invariance due to diagonal metric and limited discussion of consequences.
- Experimental comparisons not fully fair; scalability and cost not quantified.

Suggestions:
- Add a principled procedure for selecting or learning σ (and ρ), with sensitivity plots.
- Include model selection over K (e.g., via BIC) and report performance at the chosen K for each method.
- Provide runtime and complexity benchmarks against GMM, and discuss practical limits.
- Expand related-work discussion on metric learning and probabilistic geometries; consider a full local covariance variant and/or preprocessing recommendations.

Post-rebuttal note:
Even if geodesics are “almost straight lines” for large σ, they can still introduce curvature that, together with many mixture components, risks overfitting local noise more than GMMs. I strongly recommend revising the experimental design to let each method choose K via a standard criterion and to explicitly show how LAND behaves as K increases. This would make the empirical case substantially stronger.
