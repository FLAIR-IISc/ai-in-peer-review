This paper proposes a novel approach to modeling the observation space using a hand-crafted Riemannian metric and defines a curved normal distribution based on the associated Riemannian geometry. The goal is to better capture the data manifold. The paper tackles a fundamental problem: how to define a Riemannian metric based on a set of high-dimensional real-valued observations, and subsequently, how to define and estimate a curved Gaussian distribution based on this geometry. This presents a useful tool and an interesting application of Riemannian geometry in statistical learning.

The first part of the proposed method defines the underlying Riemannian geometry in section 3.1. However, the title suggests that it "learns a metric," whereas the metric is actually hand-crafted in equation 7 using two hyper-parameters, rho and sigma. These hyper-parameters are fixed empirically in advance. I assume that sigma is a sensitive parameter, and the paper should provide a more detailed discussion on the intuitive effect of varying sigma and how to select this sigma. Without this discussion, the paper feels incomplete. It would be beneficial to explore the possibility of merging the metric learning stage with the LAND learning stage, allowing rho and sigma to be learned.

One of the advantages of the geometric approach is its invariance to coordinate transformations. However, the metric in equation 7 does not possess this property. For instance, if the data forms a line in a 2D observation space, the metric would be different in a coordinate system with its x-axis parallel to this line versus a coordinate system that forms an angle, e.g., 45 degrees, to this line. A more in-depth discussion on this topic, including the invariance of the corresponding full (rather than diagonal) metric, would be helpful in providing more intuition about the metric in equation 7.

There is a rich literature on metric learning and manifold learning with connections to Riemannian geometry that can be related to this work (see Google search results for "Riemannian" "metric learning" "manifold learning"). 

A major criticism of the paper is that the comparison in the first experiment is not meaningful. It is obvious that the more flexible LAND can describe the data better with a smaller number of components. However, GMM can describe the data better with a large number of components, whereas LAND will overfit. The authors are suggested to redesign the experiment, allowing each compared method to choose its favorable number of components based on a certain criterion (e.g., BIC), and then compare the corresponding performance. LAND is still expected to perform better due to its ability to describe the manifold structure, making this comparison more meaningful. It would be interesting to see how LAND with a large number of components will overfit. 

LAND is clearly a more expensive procedure than estimating a Gaussian. Quantitative measurements or experiments that demonstrate the expense of LAND would be beneficial in understanding its limitations. What is the limit of the scale that LAND can handle? Is it possible to learn LAND on MNIST and compare it with Gaussian mixture learning?

After reading the authors' response, it is clear that LAND is a more flexible model than the Gaussian distribution. If the number of components is increased, each local LAND will be more vulnerable to local noises compared to a local Gaussian. Although "the geodesics will almost be straight lines," they are still curves, and these curves will lead to overfitting. I highly suggest that the authors revise this experiment in the revision.
