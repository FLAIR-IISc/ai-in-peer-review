The paper proposes a novel parametric metric, LAND, which bridges concepts from metric learning, manifold learning, and Riemannian statistics. By leveraging the Riemannian normal distribution, as defined in Pennec [15], the authors develop a maximum likelihood algorithm to estimate the mean and covariance of this distribution, effectively learning a Mahalanobis distance for Riemannian manifolds. The proposed approach defines distances between points as geodesics along the Riemannian manifold, similar to Hauberg et al. [9], but introduces a key innovation by learning the metric using the inverses of local (diagonal) covariances.

The paper offers valuable insights into the connection between metric learning and Riemannian statistics, providing a parametric and generative model that can generalize beyond the training data. This is particularly useful in various scenarios. However, a major concern is the applicability of LAND to real-world data, which is often large-scale and high-dimensional. The requirement to compute a (diagonal) covariance matrix for each data point and its inverse is a significant challenge, especially in high dimensions. Although the authors acknowledge the potential issue of high-dimensionality (lines 265-266), a thorough analysis of the complexity and limitations of the proposed metric is necessary.

To better situate the paper within the vast literature on metric learning, it would be beneficial to provide a more comprehensive discussion of related work. For instance, Hauberg et al. [9] discuss the literature on metric learning, including Frome et al. [1,2] and Malisiewicz and Efros [6], who learn a diagonal metric tensor for every point, similar to LAND. Local covariances have also been employed in methods like local PCA (Kambhatla – Dimension reduction by local principal component analysis), although not for every point.

The choice of the Gaussian kernel parameter, sigma, for defining locality in learning the local metric, is not thoroughly discussed. While the authors provide some insight into the influence of sigma on the results for the sleep data in Sect. 4.2, a more comprehensive analysis of the impact of sigma on the results, particularly for the synthetic data in Sect. 4.1, is necessary. Additionally, it is unclear whether sigma is the same for all points, regardless of their location in low or high-density regions.

The comparison of LAND to intrinsic estimators in Fig. 4 yields similar results, but the authors do not provide visual results for these estimators in Figs. 5 and 6. Instead, they show visualization results against GMM, which are already demonstrated to perform poorly in Fig. 4. This makes the comparison less insightful. However, some visual results using the intrinsic model are presented in the supplementary material (Fig. 2 and 4), which outperform GMM. It would be beneficial to include these results in the main paper or make room for both.

An analysis of the computational complexity of LAND versus intrinsic estimators would be valuable in determining which method is more suitable for different data structures. The authors' consideration of mixtures of LANDs is a promising application, as real-world data is rarely generated by a single component. This aspect deserves further exploration.

Overall, the paper is well-written and a pleasure to read.
