- Proposes modeling observation space with a learned Riemannian metric and defining a curved normal (LAND) to better capture data manifolds.  
- Addresses how to define a Riemannian metric from high-dimensional observations and estimate a curved Gaussian on that geometry.  
- Metric construction (Eq. 7) is hand-crafted and depends on fixed hyperparameters ρ and σ rather than being learned.  
- Paper lacks discussion of the intuitive effect of σ and guidance on how to select it; this omission makes the method feel incomplete.  
- Suggestion to investigate integrating metric parameter learning (ρ, σ) with the LAND estimation so they can be learned from data.  
- Proposed metric in Eq. 7 is not invariant to coordinate transformations (example: a line in 2D yields different metrics under rotation).  
- Need discussion on whether a full (non-diagonal) metric would restore invariance and more intuition about Eq. 7’s properties.  
- Should relate to existing literature linking Riemannian geometry, metric learning, and manifold learning.  
- First experiment comparison is unfair: LAND’s flexibility lets it fit better with fewer components, whereas a GMM could match performance with more components.  
- Recommend redesigning experiments to let each method pick its optimal number of components (e.g., via BIC) and compare resulting performance and overfitting behavior.  
- Explore how LAND behaves with many components to assess overfitting risk.  
- LAND is computationally more expensive than standard Gaussian estimation; paper lacks quantitative runtime/scalability analysis.  
- Need experiments or measurements showing practical scalability limits (e.g., applicability to datasets like MNIST and comparison to GMMs).  
- Pros: novel Riemannian application, potential to model nonlinear manifolds better, theoretically grounded (max-entropy links), and demonstrated on synthetic and EEG data.  
- Cons: metric is hand-crafted with fixed hyperparameters, insufficient hyperparameter sensitivity discussion, metric non-invariance, unconvincing experimental design, and limited computational-cost analysis.
