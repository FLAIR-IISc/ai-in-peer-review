- Proposes LAND: a locally adaptive Riemannian normal distribution bridging metric learning/manifold learning and Riemannian statistics (based on Pennec [15]).
- Introduces an MLE algorithm to estimate mean and covariance (a Mahalanobis-like distance on manifolds) using geodesic distances and local metrics.
- Learns local metric via inverses of per-point diagonal covariances and also considers mixtures of LANDs.
- Offers novel insight connecting metric learning and Riemannian statistics and provides a parametric generative model that can generalize beyond training data.
- Main concern: scalability to large-scale, high-dimensional data because computing per-point covariances and inverses is costly and potentially infeasible.
- Paper lacks a complexity analysis and a clear definition of what counts as "high-dimensional" (e.g., 3, 5, 10, 100 dims).
- Needs better positioning within the broader metric learning literature, since related works also learn per-point diagonal metrics and use local covariances.
- Use of a Gaussian kernel for locality raises questions: influence of sigma is unclear, robustness tests are missing, and it’s unclear if sigma is uniform across density regions.
- Experimental/visualization issue: Figs. 5–6 compare against GMM (which performs poorly) instead of intrinsic estimators that perform similarly or better; intrinsic visualizations should be included in main paper.
- Suggests adding computational-complexity comparisons between LAND and intrinsic estimators to guide method choice based on data structure.
- Mixtures of LANDs are highlighted as particularly interesting and merit further expansion in the paper.
- Quality: technically sound with a thorough analysis, but missing complexity/limitations discussion.
- Clarity: paper is well-written and explanations are clear.
- Originality: novel contribution in learning Riemannian normal distributions.
- Significance: has potential for notable impact in machine learning.
- Pros for acceptance: novel approach, thorough analysis, potential impact.
- Cons for acceptance: lacks complexity/limitations analysis, unclear sigma influence without robustness tests, and visualization comparisons should include intrinsic estimators.
