- Introduces Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), replacing Euclidean with a learned Riemannian metric to define a parametric distribution on a nonparametric manifold.  
- Estimates the Riemannian normal via a novel maximum likelihood approach and provides algorithms for single LANDs and mixtures.  
- Demonstrates efficacy on synthetic datasets and EEG sleep-stage data, showing potential to extrapolate beyond training data.  
- Strong conceptual bridge between metric learning and Riemannian statistics, offering a generative modeling perspective.  
- Builds on prior Riemannian statistics and metric learning work but needs clearer positioning within the extensive metric-learning literature.  
- Major scalability concern: computing and inverting local covariance matrices per point is computationally heavy in high dimensions; authors acknowledge but don’t quantify practical dimensionality limits.  
- Request for detailed complexity analysis and explicit dimensionality thresholds (e.g., 3, 5, 10, 100 dims) where method becomes impractical.  
- Choice of Gaussian kernel bandwidth σ requires deeper analysis; robustness to σ and suitability of a single global σ across varying density regions is not fully explored (missing for synthetic data).  
- Comparative evaluation relies primarily on a GMM baseline, which underperforms; stronger intrinsic estimator comparisons exist in the supplement and should be moved into the main paper.  
- Lack of visual comparisons (Figs. 5 and 6) for intrinsic estimators reduces clarity on LAND’s advantages.  
- Suggest adding a computational-complexity comparison between LAND and intrinsic estimators to guide practical use.  
- Mixtures of LANDs are a promising extension; recommend expanding this section and exploring mixture model selection criteria.  
- Paper is exceptionally well written and clearly presented.  
- Acceptance arguments: novel theoretically sound approach, demonstrated on real and synthetic data, clear presentation, potential for broad generative use.  
- Rejection concerns: scalability to high-dimensional data, limited literature positioning, insufficient σ analysis, and weak baseline comparisons.
