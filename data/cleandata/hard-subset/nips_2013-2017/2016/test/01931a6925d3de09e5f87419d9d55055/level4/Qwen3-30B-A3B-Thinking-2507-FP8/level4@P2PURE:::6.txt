

Technical quality: The manifold techniques (using Euler-Lagrange equations to estimate geodesic distances and logarithmic maps) are sound and well explained. The Monte-Carlo approximation for the normalization constant and the steepest descent algorithm for parameter estimation are cleverly designed and well described. However, the analysis of applications is lacking: the sigma parameter appears to be selected by optimizing on training data, and the explanation of the learned distribution's practical utility is insufficient.  

Novelty: The primary contribution appears to be combining prior work (using kernel-based metrics for M(x), Euler-Lagrange-based geodesic distance estimation, and applying a normal distribution) with a novel algorithm for estimating the mean, covariance, and normalization constant. The novelty is not sufficiently emphasized.  

Potential impact: The evaluation on synthetic data (comparing log-likelihoods of learned distributions) fairly assesses performance against GMM and LAND, but it remains unclear how the sigma parameter was selected. The authors show LAND fits manifold-generated data well but fail to compare it with GMM when data is generated from a single normal distribution or GMM. In the clustering task, the sigma value for LAND is chosen by optimizing the F-measure on training data, and the same F-measure is reported without a held-out test set. This obscures whether overfitting occurs. Additionally, testing on high-dimensional data would strengthen the impact. While the LAND’s potential as a generative model for manifold data is noted, the manuscript makes an unusual assumption: data X is observed in D dimensions with the manifold also having dimension D (i.e., tangent planes are D-dimensional and M(x) is full rank). This contradicts standard manifold learning, where data lies on a d-dimensional manifold embedded in a higher-dimensional space (D ≫ d).  

Clarity: A significant notational confusion arises from overloading the symbol Σ. The paper states M = Σ⁻¹ but later estimates M via a local diagonal kernel matrix. It is unclear whether this Σ refers to the fitted covariance matrix (a single parameter) or the metric inverse. The proof of the steepest descent algorithm explicitly assumes M = Σ⁻¹, but M(x) should vary with x while the covariance matrix is a global parameter. Clarification is needed. In Section 4.2, cluster assignment appears to be based on minimal geodesic distance to cluster means, though this is not explicitly stated. The introduction to manifolds is clear for readers familiar with Riemannian geometry.  

Overall, the paper presents an interesting extension of the normal distribution that adapts to local manifold structure by combining metric estimation, geodesic distance computation, and distribution learning. The method is technically sound, and the work is worthy of publication pending revisions addressing the above points.
