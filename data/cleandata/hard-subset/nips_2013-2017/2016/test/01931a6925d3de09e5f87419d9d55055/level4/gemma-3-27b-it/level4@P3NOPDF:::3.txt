This paper proposes a novel approach to modeling the observation space using a hand-crafted Riemannian metric and defining a curved normal distribution based on the resulting Riemannian geometry. The core idea – leveraging Riemannian geometry to better capture the underlying data manifold – addresses a fundamental problem in statistical learning and presents a potentially useful tool. The work is particularly interesting for its application of Riemannian geometry to a practical machine learning problem.

The paper clearly outlines the method, with the definition of the Riemannian geometry detailed in Section 3.1. However, despite the title’s suggestion of “learning a metric,” the metric in Equation 7 is ultimately hand-crafted, defined based on fixed hyperparameters, ρ and σ. This is a significant limitation. The paper lacks a thorough discussion of the sensitivity of the approach to σ, and guidance on its selection is absent. Exploring the intuitive effect of varying σ is crucial, and the possibility of integrating metric learning directly into the LAND (Locally Affine Normal Distribution) learning stage – allowing ρ and σ to be learned from the data – should be investigated. 

Furthermore, the proposed metric exhibits a lack of invariance to coordinate transformations. As an example, consider data lying on a line within a 2D observation space; the metric derived will differ significantly depending on the coordinate system’s orientation relative to that line. A more detailed discussion of this non-invariance is necessary, including whether a full (non-diagonal) metric would address this issue and providing further intuition about the properties of the metric in Equation 7. The authors should acknowledge this limitation and its potential impact on the applicability of the method.

The related work section would benefit from a more comprehensive review of existing literature connecting Riemannian geometry to metric and manifold learning. A search combining keywords like “Riemannian,” “metric learning,” and “manifold learning” reveals a substantial body of relevant research that could contextualize this work more effectively.

A major concern arises with the experimental evaluation. The initial comparison is not particularly insightful. As the proposed LAND method is inherently more flexible, it naturally achieves better performance with fewer components. However, a standard Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) could readily achieve comparable performance by increasing the number of components, at the risk of overfitting. To create a more meaningful comparison, I recommend redesigning the experiment to allow each method to optimize its number of components using a model selection criterion such as the Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC).  Observing LAND’s performance relative to a GMM with an optimally chosen number of components would provide a more convincing demonstration of its benefits – and would also be informative to see LAND overfit with a large number of components.

Finally, the computational cost of LAND compared to a standard Gaussian estimation needs to be quantified. Are there any performance measurements or experiments that demonstrate the computational burden? What is the scalability limit of LAND? Exploring its applicability to a more complex dataset like MNIST, and comparing its performance to GMM learning, would further validate its potential. 



**Post-Rebuttal Comments:** 

The authors’ response clarifies that LAND is a more flexible model than a Gaussian distribution. While true, simply increasing the number of components in LAND introduces a risk of overfitting due to increased vulnerability to local noise, even if the resulting geodesics approximate straight lines. I strongly urge the authors to revise the experiment accordingly in the revision phase.
