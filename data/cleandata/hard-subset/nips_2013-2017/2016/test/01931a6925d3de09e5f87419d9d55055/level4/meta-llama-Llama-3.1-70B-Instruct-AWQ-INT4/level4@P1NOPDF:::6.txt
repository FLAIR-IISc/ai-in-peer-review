In this paper, the authors present a locally adaptive normal distribution that generalizes the traditional normal distribution by incorporating a Riemannian metric. Instead of relying on Euclidean distances, they define a metric M(x) that varies smoothly across the data set, allowing for more accurate modeling of the underlying manifold structure. The authors utilize the exponential and logarithmic maps to compute geodesic distances along the manifold, leveraging the Euler-Lagrange equations to define a set of ordinary differential equations for estimating these distances.

The logarithmic map projects points from the manifold (y) onto the tangent plane at a given point x, such that the length of the resulting vector corresponds to the geodesic distance from x to y along the manifold. The authors then apply a normal distribution, computing the Mahalanobis distance on points mapped by the logarithmic map, which serves as an inner product for calculating distances from a point x to the mean. This approach replaces traditional Euclidean distances. The authors derive estimators for the mean and covariance via maximum likelihood, and since closed-form solutions are not available, they propose a steepest descent algorithm for estimating these parameters. Additionally, they present a Monte-Carlo approximation method to estimate the normalization constant, which is also not available in closed form.

The authors apply this method to synthetic data generated on a manifold and to a clustering task, comparing their approach to the standard mixture of Gaussians. Overall, I believe that the paper provides an interesting and useful extension of the normal distribution, adapting to local manifold structure by combining work in metric estimation, geodesic distance computation, and distribution learning. The authors' work is worthy of publication.

Technical quality: The manifold techniques, including the use of Euler-Lagrange equations and estimation of logarithmic maps, are sound and well-explained. The Monte-Carlo estimation of the normalization constant and the steepest descent algorithm are clever and well-described. However, the analysis of the applications is lacking, particularly with regards to the selection of the sigma parameter, which appears to be optimized on the training data. Furthermore, the explanation of the usefulness of the learned distribution could be improved.

Novelty: The main contribution of this paper appears to be the combination of previous work, including the use of kernels for a metric M(x), estimation of geodesic distances via Euler-Lagrange equations, and application of a normal distribution. The authors provide an algorithm for estimating the parameters, including the mean, covariance, and normalization constant.

Potential Impact: The authors provide a fair evaluation of the GMM and LAND on synthetic data, examining the log-likelihood of the data generated by the learned distributions. However, it is unclear how the sigma parameter was chosen. The authors demonstrate that the LAND fits well to data generated along a manifold but do not show how it compares to a GMM when data is generated according to a single normal distribution or GMM. In the clustering task, the authors appear to choose the sigma value for the LAND by optimizing the F-measure and then present the same F-measure. To ensure a fair comparison, it would be better to provide the F-measure on a held-out test set. This would help to assess potential overfitting issues. It would also be beneficial to examine the performance on very high-dimensional data. One potentially useful application of the LAND is as a generative model for manifold data. However, the authors make the assumption that the data X is observed in D dimensions and the manifold dimension is also D, which is unusual in manifold learning where we typically deal with data lying on a d-dimensional manifold embedded in a D-dimensional Euclidean space, with D being much larger than d.

Clarity: There is a notation point that I found confusing: the overloading of the Sigma notation. The authors state that M = Sigma^-1 but then estimate it via a local diagonal kernel matrix. It is unclear whether this Sigma is the same as the fitted covariance matrix. If so, is the kernel just an initialization? This would be strange, as M(x) should vary with x, but the fitted covariance matrix is a single parameter. In the proof of the steepest descent, the authors explicitly state that "in our case M = Sigma^-1" and use this substitution. Clarification on this point would be helpful. In Section 4.2, the authors do not specify how cluster membership was determined, but I assume it was done by picking two clusters and computing the closest geodesic distance to the cluster means. Overall, the introduction to the manifold concepts is well-explained, at least for readers familiar with Riemannian manifolds.
