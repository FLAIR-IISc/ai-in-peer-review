Summary: This paper proposes a locally adaptive normal distribution (LAND) based on a geodesic manifold traversal distance, learned adaptively from the data. This distance replaces the standard Mahalanobis distance typically used in Gaussian distributions. The authors also extend the method to a mixture of LANDs. Performance is evaluated on a synthetic dataset and a real-world dataset of EEG measurements.

Major comments: The core idea of using a geodesic distance, reminiscent of Isomap, is very interesting. However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses in clarity. First, the definition of the inverse of the metric tensor in Eq. (7) appears ad hoc. The authors should provide more intuition regarding the use of a Gaussian kernel for the weights in the weighted sum of local covariances. Furthermore, the notation is confusing; I found myself lost between the definition of the local covariance matrix in Eq. (7) and its subsequent MAP estimation in Eq. (11). Do these equations refer to the same quantity? Next, the experimental evaluations are unconvincing. My primary concern, however, is the scalability and computational efficiency of the method. Specifically, what is the computational complexity, and how does the running time compare to that of standard Expectation-Maximization (EM)? In my opinion, the paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution, but its practical applicability is questionable. 

Minor comments: Algorithm 1, lines 4 and 5: replace 'd' with '\nabla'.
