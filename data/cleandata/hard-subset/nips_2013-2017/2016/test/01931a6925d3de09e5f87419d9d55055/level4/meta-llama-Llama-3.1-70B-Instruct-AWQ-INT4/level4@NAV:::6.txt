This paper introduces a locally adaptive normal distribution that leverages a Riemannian metric M(x) to measure distances across the data set, deviating from traditional Euclidean distance calculations. By employing this metric, the authors can calculate geodesic distances along the manifold using exponential and logarithmic maps. They utilize Euler-Lagrange equations to define a set of ordinary differential equations for estimating these geodesic distances. The logarithmic map transforms points from the manifold to the tangent plane, allowing for the computation of Mahalanobis distances. This approach enables the calculation of distances from a point x to the mean, replacing traditional Euclidean distances. The authors then derive estimators for the mean and covariance through maximum likelihood, presenting a steepest descent algorithm for estimation due to the lack of closed-form solutions. Additionally, they propose a Monte-Carlo approximation method to address the non-closed-form normalization constant. The proposed method is applied to synthetic data generated on a manifold and a clustering task, with comparisons drawn to the standard mixture of Gaussians. Overall, this paper presents an interesting extension of the normal distribution, adapting to local manifold structure by combining metric estimation, geodesic distance computation, and distribution learning.

Technical quality: The application of manifold techniques, including the use of Euler-Lagrange equations and logarithmic maps, is sound and well-explained. The Monte-Carlo estimation of the normalization constant and steepest descent algorithms are clever and well-described. However, the analysis of applications could be improved, particularly in regards to the selection of the sigma parameter, which appears to be optimized on the training data. Furthermore, the explanation of the learned distribution's usefulness could be expanded upon.

Novelty: The primary contribution of this paper lies in combining existing work, such as using kernels for a metric M(x), estimating geodesic distances via Euler-Lagrange, and applying a normal distribution, to provide an algorithm for estimating parameters like the mean, covariance, and normalization constant.

Potential Impact: The authors provide a fair evaluation of the GMM and LAND on synthetic data, examining log-likelihood on data generated by the learned distributions. However, the choice of the sigma parameter is unclear. In the clustering task, the authors optimize the F measure for the LAND and present this value, but a more accurate assessment would involve providing the F measure on a held-out test set to prevent overfitting. Additionally, exploring high-dimensional data would be beneficial. One potential application of the LAND is as a generative model for manifold data, although the assumption that the manifold dimension equals the data dimension is unusual in manifold learning. Typically, data lies on a d-dimensional manifold embedded in a D-dimensional Euclidean space, where D is significantly larger than d.

Clarity: A point of confusion arises from the overloading of the Sigma notation, where M = Sigma^-1 is stated, but estimated via a local diagonal kernel matrix. Clarification is needed to determine if this Sigma is equivalent to the fitted covariance matrix and if the kernel serves as an initialization. The manifold introduction is well-explained, particularly for those familiar with Riemannian manifolds.
