This paper proposes LAND, a novel parametric metric defined over a nonparametric space, by bridging concepts from metric learning, manifold learning, and Riemannian statistics. The parametric metric learned is a Riemannian normal distribution, as defined in Pennec [15]. The authors propose a maximum likelihood algorithm to estimate the mean and covariance of the Riemannian normal distribution, effectively a Mahalanobis distance for Riemannian manifolds. Distances between points are defined as geodesics along the Riemannian manifold, similar to Hauberg et al. [9], and rely on learning a local metric. Notably, the authors propose to learn the metric by using the inverses of the local (diagonal) covariances. The authors also consider mixtures of LANDs, which is a promising direction for future research.

The paper brings new insights into the connection between metric learning and Riemannian statistics, providing a parametric and generative model that can generalize beyond the training data. This is particularly useful in many scenarios. However, the applicability of LAND to real-world data, which is often large-scale and high-dimensional, is a major concern. LAND requires the computation of a (diagonal) covariance matrix for each data point and its inverse, which are known to be challenging problems in high dimensions. Although the authors mention high-dimensionality as a potential issue (lines 265-266), a thorough analysis of the complexity and limitations of the proposed metric is necessary. It would be beneficial to provide a clear understanding of how high-dimensional the data can be (e.g., 3, 5, 10, or 100 dimensions) and how LAND performs in such cases.

The paper could also be improved by better situating it within the context of the existing literature on metric learning. Hauberg et al. [9] already discuss some of the relevant literature, including Frome et al. [1,2] and Malisiewicz and Efros [6], who all learn a diagonal metric tensor for every point, similar to LAND. Local covariances have also been used in methods like local PCA (Kambhatla – Dimension reduction by local principal component analysis), although not for every point. A more comprehensive discussion of the related work would enhance the paper's clarity and impact.

Furthermore, the choice of the Gaussian kernel parameter sigma for learning the local metric warrants further discussion. While the authors discuss the choice of sigma for the sleep data in Sect. 4.2, they do not provide a similar analysis for the synthetic data in Sect. 4.1. Robustness tests for sigma would be beneficial in understanding its influence on the results. Additionally, it is unclear whether sigma is the same for all points, regardless of whether they are in low- or high-density regions.

The comparison of LAND with intrinsic estimators in Fig. 4 is insightful, but the authors do not provide visual results for these estimators in Figs. 5 and 6. Instead, they show visualization results against GMM, which are already shown to perform poorly in Fig. 4. This is less informative, and it would be more useful to include visual results for the intrinsic model, which are shown in the supplementary material (Fig. 2 and 4) and outperform GMM. It would be valuable to include these results in the main paper or make room for both.

An analysis of the computational complexity of LAND versus intrinsic estimators would also be beneficial in understanding which method to favor over the other and for which data structures. This would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the proposed method's strengths and limitations.

The consideration of mixtures of LANDs is one of the most interesting applications of the proposed method, as real-world data is rarely generated by a single component. This aspect is worth expanding upon, as it has significant potential for future research.

Overall, the paper is well-written and a pleasure to read. With some revisions to address the concerns mentioned above, it has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field.
