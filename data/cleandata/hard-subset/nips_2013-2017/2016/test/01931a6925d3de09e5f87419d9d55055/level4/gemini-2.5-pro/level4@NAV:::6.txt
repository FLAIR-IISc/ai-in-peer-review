This paper introduces a locally adaptive normal distribution by defining a Riemannian metric, M(x), that varies smoothly across the data space, replacing the standard Euclidean distance. Geodesic distances on the manifold defined by this metric are then computed by solving a set of ordinary differential equations derived from the Euler-Lagrange equations. The authors employ the logarithmic map to project points from the manifold onto the tangent space at a given point x, ensuring the resulting vector's length equals the geodesic distance.

A normal distribution is then applied in this tangent space, where the Mahalanobis distance replaces the standard inner product for measuring distances from a point to the mean. The paper proceeds to derive maximum likelihood estimators for the model's mean and covariance. As these estimators lack a closed-form solution, a steepest descent algorithm is proposed for their numerical approximation. Furthermore, since the distribution's normalization constant is intractable, the authors present a Monte-Carlo estimation technique. The model's effectiveness is demonstrated on synthetic manifold data and a real-world clustering task, with performance compared against a standard Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM).

In summary, this paper presents a compelling and valuable extension of the normal distribution capable of adapting to local manifold structures. It successfully integrates concepts from metric estimation, geodesic distance computation, and distributional learning. This work represents a significant contribution and is worthy of publication.

My scores are based on the following observations:

**Technical Quality:**
The manuscript is technically sound. The use of manifold techniques, such as employing the Euler-Lagrange equations to estimate logarithmic maps, is robust and clearly presented. The numerical methods, including the steepest descent optimization and the Monte-Carlo approximation of the normalization constant, are elegant and well-described. However, the analysis of the applications could be more thorough. For example, the selection process for the sigma parameter is not well-justified and appears to be optimized on the training data. The paper would also benefit from a more detailed discussion of the practical utility of the learned distribution.

**Novelty:**
The primary contribution of this work appears to be the novel synthesis of several existing techniques into a single, cohesive framework. The paper effectively combines kernel-based metric estimation (for M(x)), geodesic distance computation via Euler-Lagrange equations, and the application of a normal distribution in the tangent space. The development of a complete algorithm to estimate all model parameters (mean, covariance, and the normalization constant) is a key novel aspect.

**Potential Impact:**
The authors provide a fair evaluation on synthetic data, comparing the log-likelihood of their Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND) to a GMM. However, the method for choosing the sigma parameter is not made clear. The experimental evaluation would be stronger if it included a scenario where data is generated from a standard GMM to assess how the LAND model performs when the underlying data structure is not on a complex manifold.

In the real-world clustering application, the sigma parameter for the LAND model seems to have been selected by optimizing the F-measure on the dataset, and this same F-measure is then reported as the result. This raises concerns about potential overfitting. A more rigorous evaluation would report the F-measure on a held-out test set. Consequently, it is difficult to assess the model's ability to generalize. Additionally, an investigation into the model's performance on very high-dimensional data would have been a welcome addition.

The authors propose that LAND could serve as a generative model for manifold data. However, they make the unconventional assumption that the manifold dimension is equal to the ambient space dimension (i.e., the metric M(x) is full rank). This contrasts with the typical manifold learning setting, where data lies on a low-dimensional manifold (d) embedded in a high-dimensional space (D), with d << D. This assumption warrants further discussion.

**Clarity:**
The paper is mostly well-written, but one notational choice is confusing: the overloading of the symbol "Sigma". The authors state that the metric M = Sigma^-1, yet they estimate M(x) using a local diagonal kernel matrix. It is unclear whether this "Sigma" is the same as the global covariance matrix parameter that is subsequently estimated. Since M(x) varies with x, it cannot be the inverse of a single, fixed covariance matrix. This ambiguity should be resolved, especially as the substitution M = Sigma^-1 is explicitly used in the derivation of the steepest descent algorithm.

Additionally, in Section 4.2, the criterion for cluster assignment is not specified. One assumes membership is determined by assigning points to the cluster with the closest geodesic distance to its mean, but this should be explicitly stated. On a positive note, the introduction to Riemannian manifolds is clear and provides an excellent foundation for readers familiar with the subject.
