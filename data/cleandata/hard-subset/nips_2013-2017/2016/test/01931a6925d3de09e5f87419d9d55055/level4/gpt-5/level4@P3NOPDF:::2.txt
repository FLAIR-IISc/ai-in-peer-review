This concise paper proposes a principled generalization of the multivariate normal distribution by replacing the Euclidean metric with a locally adaptive Riemannian metric. The resulting density family is well-motivated for data lying on or near manifolds and is paired with a clear maximum-likelihood estimation procedure. The extension to mixtures (a mixture of LANDs) makes the approach a practical alternative to standard Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs). The experimental results are compelling and suggest broad applicability.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and relevance: Framing a “Gaussian-like” model under a learned, locally adaptive Riemannian metric is natural for manifold-structured data and addresses a real gap between geometric manifold learning and probabilistic modeling.
- Methodological completeness: The paper gives a succinct, end-to-end account of maximum-likelihood parameter estimation, making the approach actionable. The mixture extension is particularly valuable for practice.
- Empirical promise: The experiments (as presented) are convincing and showcase both modeling flexibility and improved fit relative to Euclidean baselines. The work opens promising directions for future research.
- Presentation quality: The writing, structure, and bibliography are strong, and the paper fits the NIPS remit well.

Weaknesses and questions
- Computational complexity and scalability: Adopting a locally adaptive Riemannian metric raises concerns about the cost of computing geodesic distances, volume elements, and gradients, especially in mixtures. Please quantify training and inference complexity, memory usage, and how the method scales with dimensionality, sample size, and number of mixture components.
- Normalization and numerical stability: Computing or approximating the normalization constant under a general metric can be challenging. What approximations are used, and how accurate/stable are they in practice? Are there regimes (e.g., high curvature, sparse data regions) where the normalization or likelihood evaluation becomes unreliable?
- Metric learning and identifiability: How is the local metric learned or specified, and what regularization prevents degenerate or overly curved solutions? In the mixture setting, are there identifiability issues between component parameters and the metric’s degrees of freedom? A discussion of invariances and potential parameter couplings would be helpful.
- Comparisons and ablations: It would strengthen the empirical case to compare against (i) GMMs with flexible covariance structures (full, tied, regularized); (ii) models that first learn an embedding (e.g., via autoencoders or Laplacian eigenmaps) and then fit GMMs; and (iii) prior work on Riemannian Gaussians and mixtures on manifolds. An ablation that toggles local adaptivity versus a global metric would clarify where gains originate.
- Robustness and sensitivity: Please report sensitivity to initialization, metric bandwidth/smoothing parameters, and the number of components. How robust is training to local minima? Are there safeguards against overfitting in low-sample or high-dimensional regimes (e.g., priors, early stopping, metric regularization)?
- Theoretical properties: Any guarantees on consistency of the MLE, conditions for existence/uniqueness, or rates under manifold assumptions would significantly bolster the contribution. Even partial results or references to applicable theory would be valuable.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide complexity analysis and wall-clock benchmarks versus GMMs across datasets and component counts.
- Include broader baselines and ablations, especially embedding-then-GMM pipelines and Riemannian mixture models from prior literature.
- Clarify how the local metric is parameterized, learned, and regularized; describe the bias–variance trade-off in its adaptivity and how hyperparameters are chosen.
- Detail numerical strategies for normalizing constants and gradients (e.g., approximations, Monte Carlo, quadrature), with diagnostics for accuracy and failure modes.
- Report uncertainty estimates or confidence intervals on likelihood and clustering metrics to convey statistical significance.
- Add qualitative visualizations of learned metrics (e.g., curvature or local covariance fields) to build intuition about what the model captures.
- Release code and scripts to facilitate reproducibility.

Minor comments
- Fix typos: “experients” → “experiments,” “sinthetically” → “succinctly,” “estructura” → “structure.”
- Ensure consistent terminology for the proposed distribution (e.g., LAND, LANDs, or the full name) and for mixture components.
- If space permits, add a brief related-work note situating this paper with Riemannian normal distributions and mixtures on manifolds.

Overall assessment
This is a timely and well-written paper that offers a clean, useful bridge between geometric manifold modeling and probabilistic density estimation. The mixture extension is practically compelling, and the empirical results are promising. The main reservations concern computational scalability, clarity on metric learning and normalization, and breadth of comparisons. Addressing these points—ideally with additional experiments, complexity analysis, and discussion of theoretical underpinnings—would significantly strengthen the paper. As it stands, I view the contribution as strong and relevant to the NIPS community, with high potential impact provided the practical and comparative aspects are made more thorough.
