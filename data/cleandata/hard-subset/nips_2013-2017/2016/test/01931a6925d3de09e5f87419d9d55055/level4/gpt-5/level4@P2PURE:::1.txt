The paper introduces LAND, a new parametric metric defined over a nonparametric space by integrating ideas from metric/manifold learning and Riemannian statistics. The learned parametric metric is a Riemannian normal distribution as in Pennec [15]. The authors present a maximum likelihood procedure to estimate the mean and covariance of this Riemannian normal distribution, effectively yielding a Mahalanobis distance on Riemannian manifolds. Distances are measured as geodesics on the manifold following Hauberg et al. [9], and the approach relies on learning a local metric. Here, the metric is learned by using the inverses of local (diagonal) covariances. The paper also explores mixtures of LANDs. Overall, it offers fresh perspectives on the link between metric learning and Riemannian statistics. Having parametric, generative models that extrapolate beyond the training set is valuable in many settings.

My main concern is applicability to real-world data that are both large-scale and high-dimensional. LAND requires computing a (diagonal) covariance matrix for each data point and its inverse, operations that are known to be challenging in high dimensions. While the authors briefly acknowledge high dimensionality as a potential issue (lines 265–266), the paper would benefit from a thorough analysis of the computational complexity and practical limitations of the proposed metric. What qualifies as “high-dimensional” here—3, 5, 10, 100 dimensions?

The metric learning literature is extensive, and the paper could be better positioned within it for the benefit of future readers. Hauberg et al. [9] already covers portions of this literature, including Frome et al. [1,2] and Malisiewicz and Efros [6], which learn a diagonal metric tensor at each point in a way similar to LAND. Moreover, local covariances have been used in methods like local PCA (Kambhatla — Dimension Reduction by Local Principal Component Analysis), albeit not at every point.

Lines 101–103: The local metric is learned using a Gaussian kernel to define locality. How does the choice of sigma affect the results? Sigma is discussed only for the sleep data in Sect. 4.2, but not for the synthetic data in Sect. 4.1. The paper would benefit from robustness analyses for sigma. Is sigma kept constant across points in both low- and high-density regions?

Figure 4 compares LAND with intrinsic estimators and shows broadly similar performance. However, Figs. 5 and 6 do not include visualizations using these intrinsic estimators; instead, they compare against GMM, which Fig. 4 already indicates performs poorly. This comparison is therefore less informative. Some visual results with the intrinsic model appear in the supplementary material (Figs. 2 and 4) and they outperform GMM. These intrinsic visualizations should likely replace the GMM results in the main paper or be shown alongside them. It would also be valuable to analyze the computational complexity of LAND versus intrinsic estimators to clarify which approach is preferable under which data characteristics and structures.

The consideration of mixtures of LANDs is one of the most compelling aspects, as real data are rarely generated by a single component. This direction deserves further development.

The paper is very well written and a pleasure to read.
