This paper introduces LAND, a parametric, generative metric defined over a nonparametric data space by bridging metric/manifold learning with Riemannian statistics. The learned metric corresponds to a Riemannian normal distribution in the sense of Pennec [15], yielding a Mahalanobis-like distance on manifolds. The authors estimate the mean and (diagonal) covariance via maximum likelihood, define distances through manifold geodesics as in Hauberg et al. [9], and learn local metrics from the inverses of local covariances. Mixture models of LANDs are also considered. The work articulates a compelling connection between metric learning and Riemannian statistics and offers a generative framework that can extrapolate beyond the observed data.

Strengths
- Clear, well-written exposition with strong conceptual motivation and links to Riemannian statistics.
- A principled parametric formulation (Riemannian normal) that unifies metric learning and generative modeling.
- The mixture extension is natural and practically important since most real data are multi-modal.
- Empirical results are suggestive, and the approach is generally elegant.

Main concerns and questions
1) Scalability and high dimensionality
- The method estimates a (diagonal) local covariance for every data point and relies on geodesic computations. Even with diagonal structure, estimating and storing per-point covariances scales at least linearly in both N and d, and geodesic computations can be expensive. While inverting a diagonal matrix is trivial, obtaining reliable local covariances in high d is statistically and computationally challenging.
- Please provide a complexity analysis with explicit big-O scaling in terms of: N (samples), d (dimension), k (effective neighborhood size under the kernel), C (mixture components), and I (number of EM/optimization iterations), including the cost of geodesic distance and its gradients. A memory footprint estimate would also help.
- Clarify and quantify the practical regime of “high-dimensional”: up to what d does LAND remain stable and efficient in your implementation (e.g., 10s, 100s, 1000s)? How do runtime and accuracy scale empirically with d and N?
- Discuss numerical stability and regularization for local covariances (e.g., shrinkage, minimum variance floors) in sparse/high-d neighborhoods.

2) Relation to prior metric learning
- The paper should be positioned more clearly with respect to the substantial literature on local metric learning (e.g., Frome et al. [1,2], Malisiewicz and Efros [6]) and approaches like local PCA (Kambhatla). Several of these learn per-point or region-specific diagonal metrics, similar in spirit to LAND’s local covariances.
- Please articulate the novelty beyond Hauberg et al. [9] and prior local metric tensor learning: What is uniquely gained by casting the problem as maximum likelihood for a Riemannian normal? How does this affect estimation, regularization, and generalization compared with prior local metric learners?

3) Kernel bandwidth and locality
- Lines 101–103: The local metric depends on a Gaussian kernel with bandwidth σ. How is σ chosen in general (beyond the sleep data in Sec. 4.2)? Is σ global or adaptive across the dataset? If global, how does it handle varying density? If adaptive, how is it set (e.g., k-NN distances, local Silverman/median heuristics)?
- Please include robustness/sensitivity analyses for σ (on synthetic and real data). Report performance across a reasonable σ grid and/or provide a procedure for data-driven selection.

4) Baselines, visualizations, and fairness of comparisons
- Figure 4 shows that intrinsic estimators are competitive, yet visualizations in Figs. 5–6 only compare to GMMs, which underperform in Fig. 4. To make the qualitative comparison more informative, please include the intrinsic estimators in the main paper visualizations (or show both intrinsic and GMM), not only in the supplement.
- Provide a runtime and complexity comparison between LAND (and mixture LANDs) and intrinsic estimators, ideally with identical geodesic infrastructure and comparable regularization. This would clarify which approach should be preferred for specific data structures and scales.
- If possible, include additional local metric learning baselines (or at least a more thorough discussion) to contextualize LAND’s performance relative to established local diagonal metric learners.

5) Mixture of LANDs
- The mixture extension is one of the most compelling aspects. Please expand the experimental evaluation: performance vs number of components; initialization strategy; regularization to prevent component collapse; sensitivity to σ in the mixture context; and comparisons with mixtures of intrinsic models.
- Clarify the optimization/EM details for mixtures, including convergence criteria and per-iteration complexity.

Additional suggestions
- Discuss smoothness of the learned field of local metrics across the manifold. Are neighboring per-point covariances encouraged to vary smoothly? If not, would spatial smoothing or shared low-rank structure help stability and scalability?
- Consider practical accelerations: approximate k-NN neighborhoods; shared “anchor” points or region-wise metrics instead of fully per-point metrics; shrinkage or low-rank-plus-diagonal models; multi-scale σ selection; and GPU/parallel implementations.
- Provide guidance on regularization hyperparameters, default settings, and reproducibility (e.g., code availability).

Minor comments
- Clarify the notation early for geodesic computation and the precise manifold structure assumed or induced.
- Where relevant, indicate confidence intervals or variance across runs for quantitative results.

Overall assessment
This is a well-written and conceptually strong paper that draws an insightful connection between metric learning and Riemannian statistics, with a promising mixture extension. My main reservation concerns scalability and the limited analysis of bandwidth sensitivity and baseline comparisons. I lean toward acceptance if the authors (i) provide a clear complexity and scalability analysis with supporting experiments, (ii) include intrinsic estimator visualizations and runtime comparisons in the main paper, and (iii) expand the discussion and evaluation of the mixture model and σ selection.
