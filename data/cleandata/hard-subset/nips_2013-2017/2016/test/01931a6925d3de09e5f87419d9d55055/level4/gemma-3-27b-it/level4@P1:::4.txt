The paper proposes a locally adaptive normal distribution (LAND) as an alternative to Gaussian Mixture Models, grounded in the theory of Riemannian manifolds. The authors develop a maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) procedure for the distribution’s parameters and demonstrate its effectiveness through experiments on both synthetic and real-world data. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and presents a clear theoretical foundation alongside compelling experimental results. However, the novelty relative to existing work, particularly Simo-Serra et al. [19], could be more explicitly addressed. The distinctions between the proposed method and current state-of-the-art approaches are not fully highlighted until the conclusion, and should be clarified earlier, ideally beginning in the introduction (note that ref [19] is currently absent from the introduction).

The authors begin by motivating the limitations of the standard normal distribution when modeling data lying near nonlinear manifolds. They propose constructing a Riemannian metric that adapts to the local data density, effectively capturing nonlinear structure. This allows defining geodesics and, consequently, a Riemannian normal distribution that generalizes the conventional normal distribution to the manifold setting. The LAND is parametric, depending on a mean and covariance, while the underlying metric is non-parametric. A crucial contribution is the development of a scalable Monte Carlo integration scheme for normalizing the density and a gradient-based algorithm for maximum likelihood estimation. The authors extend the LAND to mixture models and derive the corresponding Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The method is evaluated on both synthetic datasets and EEG measurements of human sleep stages, demonstrating its efficacy.

**Strengths:**

*   **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper builds upon solid mathematical principles from Riemannian geometry, providing a rigorous framework for the proposed distribution.
*   **Novel Approach:**  The idea of learning a Riemannian metric to adapt the normal distribution to the data's underlying structure is a novel and promising direction.
*   **Scalable Implementation:** The authors address the computational challenges of working with Riemannian manifolds by developing a scalable Monte Carlo integration scheme.
*   **Empirical Validation:**  The experiments on both synthetic and real-world data demonstrate the effectiveness of the LAND, showing improvements over standard Gaussian mixture models.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the mathematical concepts and algorithmic details.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Discussion of Related Work:** The paper acknowledges Riemannian statistics and manifold learning but lacks a comprehensive discussion of related work, particularly Simo-Serra et al. [19], which also considers Riemannian normal mixture models. The specific contributions of the LAND compared to existing approaches aren't sufficiently highlighted throughout the paper.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance of the LAND relies on the choice of the smoothing parameter σ, and the paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of its sensitivity and potential adaptive selection strategies.
*   **Computational Complexity:** While the authors address scalability, the computational cost of solving the ODEs for geodesics and evaluating the logarithm map remains a potential bottleneck, especially for high-dimensional data.
*   **Lack of Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper focuses on empirical performance, and lacks theoretical guarantees on convergence or statistical consistency of the proposed algorithms.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The LAND provides a novel and theoretically sound approach to modeling data on nonlinear manifolds.
*   The proposed algorithms are practical and demonstrate promising results on both synthetic and real-world datasets.
*   The paper addresses a challenging problem in statistical modeling and offers a potentially valuable tool for various machine learning applications.
*   The work bridges connections between Riemannian statistics and manifold learning, opening avenues for future research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The novelty of the approach could be stronger, given related work like Simo-Serra et al. [19].
*   The sensitivity to the parameter σ and the computational cost of geodesic calculations remain potential limitations.
*   The absence of theoretical guarantees weakens the claims of robustness and reliability.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid contribution with a well-defined problem, a theoretically motivated approach, and promising empirical results. The main concern is the limited discussion of related work and the need for a clearer articulation of the LAND’s unique contributions compared to existing methods. If the authors can address these points by expanding the related work section and highlighting the specific advantages of their approach, the paper would be a strong candidate for acceptance. 

**Recommendation:**  Weak Accept. I recommend acceptance pending minor revisions to address the points raised above, specifically regarding related work and novelty.
