This paper presents a locally adaptive normal distribution that generalizes the traditional normal distribution by incorporating a Riemannian metric M(x) that varies smoothly across the data set. Instead of using Euclidean distances, the authors define geodesic distances along the manifold using the exponential and logarithmic maps, which are calculated via the Euler-Lagrange equations. The logarithmic map takes points from the manifold (y) into the tangent plane (at x) such that the length of the resulting vector is the geodesic distance from x to y along the manifold. The authors then apply a normal distribution by computing a Mahalanobis distance on points mapped by the logarithmic map, using this inner product to compute distances from a point x to the mean instead of Euclidean distances.

The authors derive estimators for the mean and covariance via maximum likelihood, but since the solutions are not available in closed form, they present a steepest descent algorithm for estimating them. Additionally, they propose a Monte-Carlo approximation method to estimate the normalization constant, which is also not available in closed form. The proposed method is applied to synthetic data generated on a manifold and in a clustering task, where it is compared to the standard mixture of Gaussians. Overall, I believe that this paper provides an interesting and useful extension of the normal distribution that adapts to local manifold structure, combining work in metric estimation, computing geodesic distances, and learning distributions.

Technical quality: The manifold techniques, such as using Euler-Lagrange equations and estimating logarithmic maps, are sound and well-explained. The Monte-Carlo estimation of the normalization constant and steepest descent algorithms are clever and well-described. However, the analysis of the applications is lacking, particularly in the explanation of how the sigma parameter was chosen (it appears to be picked by optimizing on training data). Furthermore, the explanation of the usefulness of the learned distribution is somewhat lacking.

Novelty: The main contribution of this paper appears to be combining previous work, including using kernels for a metric M(x), estimating geodesic distances via Euler-Lagrange, and applying a normal distribution, and providing an algorithm for estimating the parameters (mean, covariance, and normalization constant).

Potential Impact: The authors provide a fair evaluation of the GMM and LAND in the synthetic data by looking at the log-likelihood on data generated by the learned distributions. However, it is not clear how they chose the sigma parameter. The authors show that the LAND fits well on data generated along a manifold, but they do not show how well it compares to a GMM when data is generated according to a single normal distribution or GMM. In the real-life clustering data, they appear to choose the sigma value for the LAND via optimizing the F measure and then present that same F measure. To be fair, they should have provided the F measure on a held-out test set to examine potential over-fitting issues. It would also have been beneficial to examine very high-dimensional data. One potentially useful application that the authors present is that the LAND could be used as a generative model for manifold data. However, they make the unusual manifold assumption that the data X is observed in D dimensions and the manifold dimension is also D (i.e., points on the tangent plane are D-dimensional and the metric M(x) is full rank). In manifold learning, we are usually interested in data that lie on a d-dimensional manifold embedded into a D-dimensional Euclidean space where D is much larger than d.

Clarity: There is one particular notation point that I found confusing: the overloading of the Sigma notation. Specifically, the authors state that M = Sigma^-1 but then estimate it via a (local) diagonal kernel matrix. It is unclear whether this Sigma is also the same as the (fitted) covariance matrix. If so, is the kernel just an initialization? This would be strange since M(x) should vary with x, but the fitted covariance matrix is a single parameter. In the proof of the steepest descent, they explicitly state that "in our case M = Sigma^-1" and use that substitution. Clarification on this point would be appreciated. In Sec 4.2, they do not state but I assume cluster membership was chosen by picking 2 clusters and computing the closest (geodesic?) distance to the cluster means. Overall, I felt that the manifold introduction was well-explained, at least for someone familiar with Riemannian manifolds.
