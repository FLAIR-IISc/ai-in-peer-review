The paper introduces LAND, a new parametric metric defined over a nonparametric space, unifying ideas from metric/manifold learning and Riemannian statistics. The parametric model learned is a Riemannian normal distribution as in Pennec [15]. The authors present a maximum likelihood procedure to estimate the mean and covariance of the Riemannian normal distribution, effectively yielding a Mahalanobis distance on Riemannian manifolds. Distances between points are taken as geodesics along the manifold as in Hauberg et al. [9], relying on a learned local metric. Here, the metric is learned via the inverses of local (diagonal) covariance matrices. The authors also explore mixtures of LANDs. The paper offers fresh perspectives on the links between metric learning and Riemannian statistics. Having parametric, generative models that extend beyond the training data is valuable in many applications. However, my primary concern is applicability to real-world datasets that are simultaneously large-scale and high-dimensional. LAND requires computing a (here, diagonal) covariance matrix and its inverse for each data point—tasks that are known to be difficult in high dimensions. The authors do note high dimensionality as a potential issue (lines 265–266), but the paper would benefit from a careful analysis of the computational complexity and limitations of the proposed metric. What is considered high-dimensional here? 3, 5, 10, 100 dimensions? The metric learning literature is extensive, and the paper could be more firmly situated within it for future readers. Hauberg et al. [9] already cover some relevant work, e.g., Frome et al. [1,2] and Malisiewicz and Efros [6], who also learn a per-point diagonal metric tensor, similar to LAND. Local covariances have additionally been used in approaches like local PCA (Kambhatla – Dimension reduction by local principal component analysis), even if not at every point. Lines 101–103: For learning the local metric, the authors use a Gaussian kernel to define locality. How does the parameter sigma affect the results? The choice of sigma is discussed only for the sleep data in Sect. 4.2., but not for the synthetic data in Sect. 4.1. The paper would benefit from robustness checks with respect to sigma. Is sigma kept the same for all points, including those in low- versus high-density regions? Fig. 4 also compares LAND to intrinsic estimators with fairly similar results, but the authors do not include visual results for these intrinsic estimators in Figs. 5 and 6. Instead, visualizations are shown against GMMs, which Fig. 4 already indicates perform poorly; this is thus less informative. Some visual results using the intrinsic model appear in the supplementary material (Fig. 2 and 4) and they outperform GMM. These should likely replace the GMM visuals in the main paper or be shown alongside them. It would be valuable to analyze the computational complexity of LAND versus intrinsic estimators to understand which method is preferable in practice and for which data structures. The authors also consider mixtures of LANDs; I view this as one of the most promising applications, since data are rarely generated by a single component. This aspect merits further development. The paper is very well written and a pleasure to read.
