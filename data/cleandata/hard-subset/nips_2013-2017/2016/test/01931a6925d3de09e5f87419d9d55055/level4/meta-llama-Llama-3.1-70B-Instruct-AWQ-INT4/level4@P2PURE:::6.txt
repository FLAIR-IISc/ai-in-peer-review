This paper presents a locally adaptive normal distribution, which generalizes the traditional normal distribution by incorporating a Riemannian metric M(x) that varies smoothly across the data set. Instead of relying on Euclidean distances, the authors utilize this metric to compute geodesic distances along the manifold using the exponential and logarithmic maps. The logarithmic map projects points from the manifold onto the tangent plane at a given point x, yielding a vector whose length corresponds to the geodesic distance from x to the projected point. The authors then apply a normal distribution, computing the Mahalanobis distance on points mapped by the logarithmic map, which serves as an inner product for calculating distances from a point x to the mean. This approach replaces traditional Euclidean distances. The authors derive estimators for the mean and covariance via maximum likelihood, but as the solutions are not available in closed form, they propose a steepest descent algorithm for estimating these parameters. Additionally, they introduce a Monte-Carlo approximation method to estimate the normalization constant, which is also not available in closed form. The proposed method is applied to synthetic data generated on a manifold and to a clustering task, where it is compared to a standard mixture of Gaussians. Overall, the paper provides an interesting and useful extension of the normal distribution that adapts to local manifold structure, combining techniques from metric estimation, geodesic distance computation, and distribution learning. The work is worthy of publication.

Technical quality: The manifold techniques, including the use of Euler-Lagrange equations and estimation of logarithmic maps, are sound and well-explained. The Monte-Carlo estimation of the normalization constant and the steepest descent algorithm are clever and well-described. However, the analysis of the applications is lacking, as the choice of the sigma parameter appears to be based on optimizing the training data. Furthermore, the explanation of the usefulness of the learned distribution could be improved.

Novelty: The main contribution of this paper appears to be the combination of previous work on using kernels for metric estimation, Euler-Lagrange equations for geodesic distance estimation, and normal distribution learning, along with the development of an algorithm for estimating the parameters (mean, covariance, and normalization constant).

Potential Impact: The authors provide a fair evaluation of the proposed method (Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution, LAND) and the Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) on synthetic data, assessing the log-likelihood of the data generated by the learned distributions. However, the choice of the sigma parameter is not clearly explained. Moreover, the authors do not demonstrate how the LAND compares to a GMM when the data is generated according to a single normal distribution or a GMM. In the real-world clustering task, the authors appear to choose the sigma value for the LAND by optimizing the F-measure and then report the same F-measure. To ensure a fair comparison, it would be more appropriate to provide the F-measure on a held-out test set. This would also help to assess potential overfitting issues. It would be beneficial to examine the performance of the method on high-dimensional data. One potential application of the LAND is as a generative model for manifold data, but the authors make the unusual assumption that the data X is observed in D dimensions and the manifold dimension is also D. In manifold learning, it is more common to encounter data that lies on a d-dimensional manifold embedded in a D-dimensional Euclidean space, where D is significantly larger than d.

Clarity: One notation point that caused confusion is the overloading of the Sigma notation. The authors state that M = Sigma^-1 but estimate it using a local diagonal kernel matrix. It is unclear whether this Sigma is the same as the fitted covariance matrix. If so, it is unclear whether the kernel is used solely for initialization. This would be unusual, as M(x) should vary with x, but the fitted covariance matrix is a single parameter. In the proof of the steepest descent algorithm, the authors explicitly state that "in our case M = Sigma^-1" and use this substitution. Clarification on this point would be helpful. In Section 4.2, the authors do not explicitly state how cluster membership was determined, but it is assumed that it was chosen by picking two clusters and computing the closest (geodesic?) distance to the cluster means. Overall, the introduction to manifolds is well-explained, at least for readers familiar with Riemannian manifolds.
