Summary:
This paper introduces the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), which replaces the Euclidean/Mahalanobis distance in the Gaussian density with a geodesic distance defined by a smoothly varying, data-adaptive Riemannian metric. The metric is learned nonparametrically from data via local (diagonal) covariances weighted by a Gaussian kernel; the resulting manifold induces geodesics that favor regions of high data density. The model is parametric in mean and covariance, and, following Pennec’s framework, is the maximum-entropy distribution on the manifold for a given mean and covariance. The paper derives: (i) a Monte Carlo scheme for the intractable normalization constant by integrating in the tangent space using the metric volume element, (ii) a gradient-based maximum likelihood algorithm for the mean and covariance, and (iii) an EM algorithm for mixtures of LANDs. Empirically, the method is evaluated on synthetic data and on a sleep-stage EEG dataset, where LAND mixtures provide more faithful contours and improved clustering alignment relative to GMMs.

Relation to prior work:
The approach bridges Riemannian statistics (e.g., Pennec’s Riemannian normal distributions; Zhang and Fletcher’s probabilistic PCA on manifolds) with manifold learning (e.g., Isomap) by learning the metric rather than assuming a known manifold. It also relates to geodesic mixture models on known manifolds (Simo-Serra et al.) and spherical DP mixtures (Straub et al.). Unlike graph-based geodesic distances, the learned metric yields a continuously defined geometry over RD suitable for generative modeling.

Strengths:
- Conceptually elegant link between manifold learning and Riemannian exponential-family modeling; the maximum-entropy interpretation is compelling.
- Practical algorithmic ideas: tangent-space Monte Carlo for the partition function avoids expensive BVPs for Log maps; mixture extension via EM is natural.
- Clear intuition for why ML means avoid low-density regions, improving upon intrinsic LS estimators.
- Demonstrations show locally adapted contours that better fit non-elliptical structure; EEG results indicate improved clustering alignment over GMMs.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification:
- Metric construction (Eq. 7): The choice of an inverse local diagonal covariance with Gaussian-kernel weights appears ad hoc. Please expand the intuition for this particular form, discuss the effect of σ and ρ, and justify the diagonal restriction beyond “efficiency and preventing overfitting.” How sensitive are results to σ and to the diagonal assumption? Any reason not to use a regularized full local covariance?
- Role of covariances: There is potential confusion between (a) the local covariance used to define the metric M(x) in Eq. (7) and (b) the global model covariance Σ optimized in Eq. (11). Please clarify their distinct roles and notation, and explicitly state that Eq. (11) is ML for Σ (not MAP), whereas Eq. (7) is a nonparametric metric estimator.
- Scalability/complexity: The paper lacks a complexity analysis and runtime comparisons. Each iteration involves O(N) Log-map evaluations and O(S) Exp-map evaluations (ODE solves). How do costs scale with N, D, K (for mixtures), S, and the geodesic solver tolerance? How does wall-clock time compare to a standard GMM EM on the same data? A runtime table and sensitivity to S would greatly strengthen the case for practical use.
- Normalization and gradient estimation: The partition function is estimated with Monte Carlo under N(0, Σ). What is the variance of this estimator for typical S (e.g., S=3000), and how does it affect optimization stability? Do you reuse common random numbers across iterations to reduce variance? Any importance sampling or control variates considered?
- Experimental evaluation: The empirical section is relatively limited. The synthetic study is small, and real-data evaluation focuses on clustering alignment on EEG. Additional baselines (e.g., full-covariance GMM, kernel density models, spectral or manifold clustering) and held-out likelihood comparisons would be informative. On EEG, σ is selected per subject for best alignment; please discuss the risk of overfitting and whether σ can be selected via cross-validation or learned.
- Invariance: The learned metric is not invariant to linear transformations. Please discuss practical implications and whether pre-whitening or an adaptive metric family could mitigate this.
- Reproducibility details: Provide ODE solver settings, convergence criteria, S sensitivity, and initialization strategy (the supplement is referenced; a brief summary in the main text would help).

Quality:
The formulation is technically sound and the derivations are consistent with Riemannian statistics. The optimization is nonconvex, but the use of steepest descent with Σ preconditioning is reasonable. However, the practical efficiency and robustness remain unclear without complexity and runtime analyses, variance diagnostics for the Monte Carlo estimators, and broader empirical validation.

Clarity:
Overall well written, but key modeling choices need more intuition and clearer separation of the metric estimator vs. the model covariance. The mixture EM details being in the supplement is acceptable, though a brief outline in the main text would help.

Originality:
Moderate to strong. Riemannian normals are known on fixed manifolds, but learning the metric and performing full ML estimation with a tractable normalization scheme is a meaningful contribution that connects two largely separate literatures.

Significance:
Promising for generative modeling on data with curved structure, but current evidence of practical impact is limited by computational cost and modest empirical scope. With stronger scalability analysis and broader experiments, the impact could be substantial.

Minor comments:
- Algorithm 1, lines 4–5: use ∇µφ for the gradient; be consistent with the steepest descent direction definition.
- Typos: “Isomap,” “geodesic,” “covariance,” “complexity,” etc.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Elegant integration of learned Riemannian geometry with maximum-entropy modeling.
- Solid derivations; practical tangent-space Monte Carlo; mixture extension.
- Compelling qualitative fits and improved clustering alignment on EEG.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Ad hoc and weakly justified metric design; sensitivity to σ not thoroughly addressed.
- No complexity/runtime comparison; unclear scalability and practicality.
- Limited experiments and baselines; potential overfitting in σ selection.

Overall assessment:
An interesting and well-motivated theoretical contribution with promising ideas, but currently limited by clarity on the metric design and by missing scalability and empirical depth. I lean borderline; a stronger empirical and computational evaluation would significantly improve the case for acceptance.
