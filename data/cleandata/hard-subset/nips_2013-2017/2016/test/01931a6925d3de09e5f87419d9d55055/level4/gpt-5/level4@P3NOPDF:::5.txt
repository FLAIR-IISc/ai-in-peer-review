Summary:
The paper introduces the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), which replaces the global Mahalanobis distance in a Gaussian with a geodesic distance induced by a data-driven Riemannian metric. The metric’s inverse is constructed as a weighted combination of local covariance matrices, with weights determined by a kernel centered at the evaluation point. The approach is extended to mixtures of LANDs, and evaluated on synthetic data and an EEG dataset.

Strengths:
- Conceptually appealing idea that connects Gaussian modeling with manifold geometry by substituting the Euclidean/Mahalanobis distance with a geodesic distance.
- The use of a spatially varying metric captures local anisotropy and curvature, potentially improving modeling on non-linear manifolds.
- The extension to mixture models is natural and could broaden applicability.
- The paper is timely given ongoing interest in geometry-aware probabilistic models.

Major concerns and requests for clarification:
1) Definition and intuition for the inverse metric (Eq. 7):
- The construction of the inverse metric tensor as a kernel-weighted sum of local covariance matrices feels ad hoc. Please provide stronger intuition and, if possible, theoretical justification. Why should a Nadaraya–Watson-type smoothing of local covariances produce a suitable Riemannian metric for density modeling?
- Clarify the role of the Gaussian kernel: how is the bandwidth chosen, does it vary with dimension or local density, and how sensitive are results to this choice? Are the weights normalized, and does normalization affect positive-definiteness?
- Discuss regularity: under what conditions on the kernel and data does this construction yield a smooth, well-conditioned metric field?

2) Consistency between Eq. (7) and Eq. (11):
- It is unclear whether the “local covariance” in Eq. (7) is the same quantity that is estimated via MAP in Eq. (11). Are these the same parameters, or is Eq. (7) defining a nonparametric, data-dependent object that is then approximated/regularized via MAP? Please unify notation and clearly distinguish:
  - Empirical/local covariances computed from neighborhoods,
  - The inverse metric tensor at a location,
  - Any global parameters shared across the dataset.
- A concise paragraph walking through the generative model and the role of each parameter before presenting the MAP objective would greatly help.

3) Computation of geodesic distances:
- How are geodesics computed in practice? If you discretize via a k-NN graph and shortest paths (Isomap-style), the distance is only piecewise consistent with the continuous metric. If you integrate along curves with respect to the spatially varying metric, how do you solve the associated ODEs efficiently?
- Are these distances differentiable with respect to parameters in the optimization? If not, what surrogates are used? Please discuss the implications for convergence and bias.

4) Complexity, scalability, and efficiency:
- Provide a clear complexity analysis per iteration for single LAND and mixture training, in terms of N (samples), D (dimension), K (mixture components), and k (neighbors), including:
  - Cost to build and maintain neighborhood structures,
  - Cost to evaluate geodesic distances needed in the E- and M-steps,
  - Any precomputations (e.g., local covariances) and their memory footprint,
  - Complexity of the normalization term for the LAND density (if approximated).
- Compare wall-clock times against a standard GMM with full covariances on the same datasets and hardware. Report iteration counts and final likelihoods so readers can judge efficiency/quality trade-offs.
- Discuss scalability strategies (e.g., landmarks, Nyström approximations, mini-batching, sparse updates) if applicable.

5) Experimental evaluation:
- The current experiments (synthetic and EEG) are too limited to support strong claims. Please add:
  - Stronger baselines: GMM (diag/tied/full), mixtures of t-distributions, kernel density estimation, Isomap followed by GMM, and any recent manifold-aware density/clustering models.
  - Quantitative metrics: held-out log-likelihood, clustering/classification accuracy (if labels exist), and calibration.
  - Sensitivity analyses: effect of kernel bandwidth, number of neighbors, and number of mixture components. Include ablations isolating the contribution of the geodesic metric versus Euclidean/Mahalanobis alternatives.
  - Runtime and memory comparisons, with convergence plots.
- For the EEG dataset, clarify preprocessing, dimensionality reduction (if any), and task (density estimation vs. classification via mixture responsibilities). Provide confidence intervals or multiple runs.

6) Statistical and numerical issues:
- How do you prevent degeneracy/overfitting in local covariance estimates (e.g., small neighborhoods in high-D)? Describe regularization (e.g., shrinkage, floor on eigenvalues) and its effect.
- Clarify whether the LAND density is properly normalized under the spatially varying metric and how the normalization constant is computed or approximated.
- Initialization strategy and convergence: provide details and practical guidance.

Minor comments:
- Algorithm 1, lines 4–5: replace d with ∇ to denote gradient.
- Fix typographical errors throughout (e.g., “gedesic” → “geodesic,” “covarance” → “covariance,” “scalibility” → “scalability,” “compleixity” → “complexity”).
- Define all symbols on first use and avoid overloading notation between local covariances, inverse metric tensors, and learned parameters.
- Improve figure readability and ensure axes/units are labeled; include captions that describe what to conclude.

Overall assessment:
The paper presents an intriguing and principled idea—adapting Gaussian models to data manifolds via a learned Riemannian metric and geodesic distances. However, key aspects of the construction (intuition and justification for the inverse metric), algorithmic clarity (relationship between Eqs. 7 and 11), computational tractability, and empirical validation need substantial strengthening. With clearer exposition, a thorough complexity/running-time analysis, and more comprehensive experiments against strong baselines, this work could make a meaningful contribution. In its current form, I am concerned about practical applicability and would lean against acceptance unless these issues are addressed.
