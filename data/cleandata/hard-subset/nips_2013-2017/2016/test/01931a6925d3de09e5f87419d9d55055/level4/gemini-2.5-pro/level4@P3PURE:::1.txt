This paper introduces LAND, a novel parametric metric defined over a nonparametric space, by bridging concepts from metric learning, manifold learning, and Riemannian statistics. The learned metric is a Riemannian normal distribution, as defined by Pennec [15], which effectively creates a Mahalanobis distance for Riemannian manifolds. In this framework, distances between points are geodesics along the manifold, and the authors propose to learn the local metric from the inverse of local (diagonal) covariance matrices. The paper also extends this model to mixtures of LANDs.

The paper offers valuable new insights into the connection between metric learning and Riemannian statistics. The development of a parametric and generative model that can generalize beyond the training data is a significant contribution with potential for broad applicability.

However, my main concern is the method's applicability to large-scale, high-dimensional real-world data. LAND requires the computation and inversion of a covariance matrix for each data point. Even with a diagonal assumption, this is a known challenge in high-dimensional settings. While the authors briefly acknowledge this issue (lines 265-266), the paper would be substantially strengthened by a formal analysis of the computational complexity and practical limitations of the proposed metric. For instance, what is the feasible dimensionality for this method—is it on the order of 10, 100, or more?

The paper could also be better situated within the vast metric learning literature. While Hauberg et al. [9] is cited, a more direct discussion is needed to contrast LAND with related methods that also learn a local, diagonal metric tensor for each point (e.g., Frome et al. [1,2], Malisiewicz and Efros [6]). Additionally, the use of local covariances has precedents in methods like Local PCA, and discussing this relationship would provide useful context for readers.

Further points for improvement are:

1.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The local metric is learned using a Gaussian kernel to define locality (lines 101-103). The choice of the kernel width, sigma, is critical, yet its influence on the results is only discussed for the sleep dataset. The paper would benefit from a robustness analysis for sigma, including for the synthetic data. It is also unclear if sigma is held constant across the dataset, which may be suboptimal for regions with varying data density.

2.  **Experimental Comparisons:** The experimental evaluation could be more insightful. In Figure 4, LAND's performance is shown to be comparable to that of intrinsic estimators. However, the qualitative visualizations in Figures 5 and 6 only compare LAND against GMMs, a baseline already shown to perform poorly. These comparisons are therefore not very informative. The supplementary material contains visual results against the intrinsic model (Figs. 2 and 4), which appears to outperform GMMs. These stronger comparisons should be moved into the main paper, replacing or supplementing the GMM results.

3.  **Complexity Analysis:** To complement the improved experimental comparisons, an analysis of the computational complexity of LAND versus the intrinsic estimators would be valuable. This would help readers understand the trade-offs and determine which method is preferable for different data structures.

4.  **Mixtures of LANDs:** The extension to mixtures of LANDs is one of the most interesting applications of this work, as real-world data is rarely generated by a single component. This aspect is very promising and would be worth expanding upon.

Despite these concerns, the paper is very well-written and a pleasure to read. Addressing the issues raised, particularly regarding scalability, experimental comparisons, and hyperparameter sensitivity, would significantly enhance the impact of this promising work.
