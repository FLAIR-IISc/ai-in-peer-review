Summary
The paper introduces the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), which replaces the Euclidean metric in a Gaussian with a learned, smoothly varying Riemannian metric that emphasizes regions of high data density. The resulting model is a parametric Riemannian normal distribution (in the sense of Pennec), defined by a mean and covariance, but evaluated on a nonparametrically learned manifold. The metric is built from the inverse of local (diagonal) covariance estimates computed via a Gaussian kernel. The authors develop: (i) a Monte Carlo scheme to estimate the normalization constant by integrating in the tangent space at the mean; (ii) a gradient-based maximum likelihood procedure that alternates between updating the mean via exponential/logarithm maps and updating the covariance via a factorization; and (iii) a mixture of LANDs with an EM algorithm. Experiments on synthetic data and EEG sleep-stage features suggest LAND captures nonlinear structure better than GMMs and avoids the pathologies of intrinsic least-squares estimators of mean/covariance on manifolds.

Relation to prior work
This work connects manifold learning (e.g., Isomap; Riemannian metrics learned from data as in Hauberg et al.) with statistics on manifolds (Pennec’s Riemannian normal, Fletcher and others). Unlike classical manifold learning, which yields embeddings but not a generative density, LAND provides a generative model defined everywhere in RD. Relative to prior Riemannian models that assume a known manifold, the novelty is to learn the metric from data and incorporate it into a normalized probabilistic model (including mixtures). The paper could be positioned more explicitly within local metric learning (e.g., work surveyed by Hauberg et al., including Frome et al., Malisiewicz and Efros) and local covariance methods such as local PCA (e.g., Kambhatla).

Strengths
- Conceptually elegant bridge between manifold learning and Riemannian statistics; the use of a learned metric to define a maximum-entropy Riemannian normal is appealing.
- Practical inference: integrating in the tangent space avoids repeated BVP solves for the log map; the algorithmic choices (S samples, IVPs for Exp maps) make the approach implementable.
- Empirical evidence that ML estimates of LAND means avoid low-density regions better than intrinsic least-squares means.
- Mixture extension is natural and increases applicability to realistic multimodal data.
- The paper is clearly written and generally easy to follow.

Weaknesses and questions
- Scalability/high dimensionality: The metric requires computing a local (diagonal) covariance for every point and its inverse, with weights from an isotropic Gaussian kernel. This raises concerns for large N and moderate-to-high D. Please provide a complexity analysis in terms of N, D, and neighborhood size (or kernel bandwidth support), as well as memory costs. What dimensional ranges are practically tractable (e.g., D≈3–5 vs. 10 vs. 100)? How does runtime scale with S (Monte Carlo samples) and the cost of Exp/Log solves?
- Choice and robustness of σ: σ is pivotal in defining locality and thus the metric. How sensitive are results to σ, and is σ the same across low- and high-density regions? In Sec. 4.2 you explore σ, but a similar robustness study is missing in the synthetic experiments (Sec. 4.1). Please add sensitivity analyses, or propose a data-driven selection scheme (e.g., likelihood-based selection or local/adaptive σ).
- Baselines and visualizations: Fig. 4 shows that intrinsic least-squares estimators are competitive with LAND and clearly outperform GMMs in some settings, yet Figs. 5 and 6 visualize only GMMs as a baseline. The supplementary figures show intrinsic estimators producing better contours than GMM. For a fairer and more informative comparison, please include intrinsic estimator visualizations in the main paper figures (or in addition to GMM) and discuss computational trade-offs.
- Complexity comparisons: A side-by-side empirical comparison of time/memory between LAND (including Monte Carlo normalization and geodesic computations) and intrinsic least-squares estimators would help clarify when to prefer which approach and for which data geometries.
- Mixture model: This is one of the most compelling use cases. The paper would benefit from an expanded evaluation of Mixture-of-LANDs (e.g., model selection across K, sensitivity to σ in the mixture, comparisons with mixtures based on intrinsic estimators).
- Metric properties: The learned metric is not invariant under linear transformations. A brief discussion of the practical implications (e.g., need for whitening or preprocessing) would be useful.
- Implementation details: Provide sensitivity of results to the Monte Carlo sample size S (some is in the supplement), and include wall-clock runtimes for synthetic and EEG experiments.

Quality
The methodology is technically sound and the derivations for gradients and normalization are appropriate. However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by a thorough analysis of computational complexity, scalability, and parameter sensitivity, and by more comprehensive baselines in the main text.

Clarity
The paper is well written and organized. Suggestions:
- Add a subsection discussing σ selection and robustness across datasets.
- Bring key baseline visualizations (intrinsic estimators) from the supplement into the main paper.
- Include runtime and scaling plots.

Originality
Adapting the Riemannian normal to a learned manifold with a proper normalization constant, combined with ML estimation and a mixture model, is a novel and valuable contribution at the intersection of manifold learning and Riemannian statistics.

Significance
A generative, maximum-entropy model that adapts to nonlinear structure is potentially impactful. Its practical significance will depend on scalability and robustness; addressing the points above would broaden applicability, especially for higher-dimensional settings.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, elegant formulation with solid theoretical grounding.
- Practical inference procedure with reasonable approximations.
- Demonstrated improvements over GMMs; promising application to EEG.
- Natural extension to mixtures.

Cons for acceptance
- Unclear scalability to higher dimensions and large datasets; no complexity analysis.
- Sensitivity to σ not systematically evaluated outside one dataset.
- Main-text baselines/visualizations underemphasize intrinsic estimators.
- Limited empirical exploration of the mixture model.

Overall
A strong and well-written paper with a compelling idea and solid methodology. I recommend acceptance if the authors can provide complexity/scalability analysis, expand σ robustness studies, include intrinsic baseline visualizations in the main text, and strengthen the mixture-model evaluation.
