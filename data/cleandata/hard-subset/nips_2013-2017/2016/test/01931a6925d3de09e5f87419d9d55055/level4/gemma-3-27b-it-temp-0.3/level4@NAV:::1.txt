The paper introduces LAND, a novel parametric metric constructed within a nonparametric framework, effectively integrating concepts from metric learning, manifold learning, and Riemannian statistics. Specifically, the learned metric takes the form of a Riemannian normal distribution, as defined by Pennec [15], and is estimated via a maximum likelihood approach determining the mean and covariance – essentially a Mahalanobis distance adapted for Riemannian manifolds. Leveraging geodesic distances on the manifold, as in Hauberg et al. [9], the method learns a local metric using the inverses of local (diagonal) covariances, and extends to mixtures of LANDs. This work offers valuable new perspectives on the relationship between metric learning and Riemannian statistics, and the resulting parametric, generative model holds promise for generalization beyond the training data in various applications.

However, a key concern is the practical applicability of LAND to large-scale, high-dimensional datasets. The method necessitates the computation and inversion of covariance matrices for each data point, operations known to be computationally challenging in high dimensions. While the authors acknowledge high-dimensionality as a potential limitation (lines 265-266), a more thorough analysis of the metric’s complexity and scalability is needed. Clarification on what constitutes “high-dimensional” in this context – for example, 3, 5, 10, or 100 dimensions – would be beneficial. Furthermore, the paper would benefit from a more comprehensive positioning within the broader landscape of metric learning literature. Hauberg et al. [9] cite relevant work, such as Frome et al. [1,2] and Malisiewicz and Efros [6], which also employ point-wise diagonal metric tensors, and local covariances are utilized in techniques like local PCA (Kambhatla), though not universally per point. 

Regarding implementation details, the influence of the Gaussian kernel parameter, sigma, on the learned metric warrants further investigation. While discussed for the sleep data (Sect. 4.2), its impact on the synthetic data (Sect. 4.1) is not addressed, and robustness tests for sigma would strengthen the work. Specifically, it’s unclear if a uniform sigma is optimal across regions of varying data density. The comparison in Fig. 4 includes intrinsic estimators, yielding comparable results, yet Figs. 5 and 6 lack corresponding visualizations. Instead, comparisons are made against GMM, which Fig. 4 already demonstrates to be a weaker performer, thus reducing the insightfulness of these latter figures. The supplementary material (Figs. 2 and 4) shows superior performance of the intrinsic model compared to GMM, suggesting these results should be incorporated into the main paper, either replacing or complementing the GMM comparisons. A computational complexity analysis contrasting LAND with intrinsic estimators would be valuable in guiding practitioners toward the most appropriate method based on data characteristics. Finally, the exploration of mixtures of LANDs is a particularly compelling aspect of this work, given the commonality of multi-component data, and expanding on this application would be worthwhile. 

Overall, the paper is exceptionally well-written and a pleasure to read.
