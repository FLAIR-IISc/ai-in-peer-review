- Paper introduces LAND: a generative model replacing the Euclidean metric with a data-driven Riemannian metric to model manifold-structured data.  
- Uses maximum likelihood with gradient-based optimization and Monte Carlo integration to handle the intractable normalization constant; extended to mixtures via EM.  
- Strength: novel and significant approach that parametricizes a distribution over a learned manifold, generalizing the multivariate normal.  
- Strength: solid theoretical grounding — presented as the maximum-entropy distribution for a specified mean and covariance on the learned manifold.  
- Strength: complete inference pipeline including a scalable Monte Carlo estimator for the normalization constant and gradient-based parameter updates.  

- Weakness: the Riemannian metric depends on hyperparameters (ρ, σ) chosen empirically rather than learned from data.  
- Suggestion: discuss the practical effects of kernel bandwidth σ and provide guidance or methods for its selection.  
- Suggestion: the claim that the method “learns a metric” is misleading unless σ (and similar hyperparameters) are integrated into the learning objective.  
- Weakness: the proposed metric (Eq. 7) is not coordinate-invariant; the paper should discuss this limitation and whether using full local covariance matrices would help and at what cost.  
- Experimental weakness: the synthetic comparison to GMMs is not fully convincing; comparisons should let each method select its optimal number of components (e.g., via AIC/BIC).  
- Suggestion: analyze whether LAND with many components can overfit local noise due to flexible curved geodesics, compared to linear GMM components.  
- Weakness: computational cost and scalability are unclear; the paper needs quantitative runtime/scalability analysis w.r.t. N, D, and Monte Carlo samples.  
- Suggestion: demonstrate applicability on larger, more complex datasets (e.g., MNIST) to show practical limits.  
- Weakness: literature context is insufficient; the paper should better situate itself within metric learning, Riemannian geometry, and manifold-learning prior work to clarify novelty.
