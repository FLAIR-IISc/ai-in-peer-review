

The paper introduces LAND, a novel parametric metric operating within a nonparametric framework, by integrating metric learning/manifold learning principles with Riemannian statistics. The proposed parametric metric corresponds to a Riemannian normal distribution (Pennec [15]), with the authors developing a maximum likelihood approach to estimate its mean and covariance—effectively defining a Mahalanobis distance for Riemannian manifolds. Pointwise distances are computed via geodesics (following Hauberg et al. [9]), leveraging a learned local metric. Specifically, the metric is derived from inverses of local diagonal covariance matrices. The work also explores mixtures of LANDs. While the paper offers valuable insights into the intersection of metric learning and Riemannian statistics, and generative models extending beyond training data are indeed advantageous, the primary concern lies in applicability to large-scale, high-dimensional real-world data. LAND necessitates computing a (diagonal) covariance matrix and its inverse per data point, which becomes computationally prohibitive in high dimensions. Although the authors acknowledge high-dimensionality as a challenge (lines 265–266), the paper lacks a rigorous analysis of computational complexity and practical limitations. Clarification is needed: what constitutes "high-dimensional" here (e.g., 3, 5, 10, or 100 dimensions)? The literature context also requires strengthening; Hauberg et al. [9] already cite relevant work (e.g., Frome et al. [1,2], Malisiewicz and Efros [6]) that similarly learns diagonal pointwise metrics, while local covariances appear in methods like local PCA (Kambhatla). Regarding the Gaussian kernel for locality (lines 101–103), the influence of parameter σ on results is inadequately addressed—σ is only tuned for sleep data (Sect. 4.2), not synthetic data (Sect. 4.1). Robustness testing across σ values and density regions is essential. Figure 4 compares LAND against intrinsic estimators with similar results, yet Figures 5–6 omit these intrinsic comparisons, instead favoring GMM (already shown inferior in Fig. 4). Supplementary material (Figs. 2, 4) demonstrates intrinsic estimators outperforming GMM, suggesting these should replace GMM visuals in the main text. A comparative computational complexity analysis of LAND versus intrinsic estimators would clarify method selection for different data structures. The mixture model extension is particularly compelling, as real data rarely stems from a single component; this aspect warrants deeper exploration. Overall, the paper is exceptionally well-written and a pleasure to read.
