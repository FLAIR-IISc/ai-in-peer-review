Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), a parametric distribution defined on a learned Riemannian manifold. The key idea is to replace the Euclidean metric in a Gaussian with a smoothly varying, data-adaptive Riemannian metric that favors high-density regions. The resulting model is a Riemannian normal distribution in the sense of Pennec (maximum entropy under fixed mean and covariance), with parameters (mean, covariance) inferred by maximum likelihood. The paper proposes: (i) a nonparametric metric defined as the inverse of local diagonal covariances estimated via a Gaussian kernel; (ii) a Monte Carlo estimator of the normalization constant computed in the tangent space at the mean via exponential maps; (iii) gradient-based updates for mean and covariance; and (iv) an EM algorithm for mixtures of LANDs. Experiments on synthetic manifolds and EEG sleep data suggest the LAND fits nonlinear structure better than GMMs and intrinsic least-squares estimators.

The work connects Riemannian statistics (e.g., Pennec; Zhang & Fletcher on probabilistic PCA on known manifolds) with manifold learning (e.g., Isomap), but departs from classical embedding methods by learning a continuous Riemannian metric over RD and building a generative model on that metric. It is also related to local metric learning and local covariance modeling (e.g., diagonal per-point metrics in prior metric learning work; local PCA), and to mixtures of Riemannian normals on known manifolds (e.g., Simo-Serra et al.; Straub et al.).

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and clarity: An elegant and principled way to “Gaussianize” data on a learned manifold, retaining maximum-entropy properties while adapting locally to nonlinear structure.
- Sound modeling choices: Using tangent-space Monte Carlo to avoid expensive BVPs for the log map is a practical and effective design; gradients for both mean and covariance are derived clearly.
- Empirical evidence: On synthetic examples and EEG features, LAND captures non-ellipsoidal structure better than GMMs, and mixture LANDs improve clustering alignment with sleep stages.
- Useful extension: The mixture model and its EM formulation are natural and important for real data, where single components rarely suffice.
- Writing: Generally well written and accessible.

Weaknesses and points to address

Quality (technical soundness and evaluation)
- Scalability and complexity: Constructing M(x) requires evaluating kernel weights to all N samples and computing a (diagonal) local covariance per query x. Each evaluation of m(μ, v) during Monte Carlo requires an Exp map and a metric determinant, which implies O(ND) per sample unless sparsified. The paper should provide a concrete complexity analysis in N, D, S (Monte Carlo samples), and number of gradient iterations, and report runtimes. Are approximate k-NN kernels, compact-support kernels, or subsampling used? How does complexity scale when D=10, 50, 100 and N≥10^5?
- High dimensionality: The method is demonstrated in 2D and on 5D EEG features. Please clarify empirically and/or theoretically where the approach remains practical (e.g., up to D≈10 or D≈100?), and whether prewhitening or learning the metric in a low-dimensional latent space (e.g., via PPCA) could mitigate the known curse of dimensionality for local covariance estimates.
- Sensitivity to kernel bandwidth σ and regularization ρ: Results hinge on σ, yet sensitivity analyses are limited to one real-data task. Please (i) report σ and ρ for all experiments; (ii) include robustness curves on the synthetic data; and (iii) discuss whether σ is global or adaptive (e.g., per-point based on local density), and how to select it in practice.
- Monte Carlo normalization: Since C(μ, Σ) depends on μ via Exp maps, the Monte Carlo estimator adds variance to both the objective and gradients. Please quantify variance vs. S, discuss use of common random numbers and variance-reduction, and report convergence behavior. Are gradients unbiased? Any convergence guarantees?
- Invariance and diagonal metric: The learned metric is not invariant to linear transforms, and diagonal restriction may underfit anisotropic local structure. Consider recommending prewhitening, or discuss the trade-off vs. full local covariances and the conditions under which diagonal is sufficient.

Clarity
- Provide more details of the EM algorithm for mixtures in the main text (currently deferred to the supplement), including how component-specific normalization constants are handled.
- Explain practical settings for step sizes, stopping criteria, and initialization (the paper notes sensitivity of the mean gradient to the condition number of Σ; advise on safeguards).
- Report the choice and impact of ρ, and any numerical stabilization when computing determinants of M(x).

Originality
- The paper thoughtfully bridges Riemannian normals with learned metrics, which appears novel. To better position the work, please expand the discussion of related local metric learning and local covariance methods (e.g., diagonal per-point metrics and local PCA), clarifying similarities and differences (e.g., continuous generative density, maximum-entropy interpretation, mixture modeling with proper normalization).

Significance
- The approach is promising for generative modeling on structured data. To strengthen practical impact, consider: (i) visual and quantitative comparisons against intrinsic least-squares estimators in the main text (not only in the supplement); (ii) a computational comparison (runtime and memory) vs. intrinsic estimators and GMMs; and (iii) expanded mixture experiments (e.g., cluster purity/ARI on synthetic non-ellipsoidal clusters, more real datasets), as mixtures are likely the most useful setting.

Specific questions and suggestions
- How is neighbor search implemented for kernel weights? Any sparsification (k-NN), KD-trees, or approximate neighbors?
- What are typical values of S and their effect on runtime and accuracy? Could quasi-Monte Carlo or control variates reduce variance?
- Are there failure modes where the LAND mean drifts to low-density regions despite the normalization penalty?
- Could σ be learned (e.g., via cross-validation, marginal likelihood, or gradient-based tuning), or made spatially adaptive?
- Consider reporting Fisher consistency or asymptotic properties under the learned metric, if available.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, principled formulation; bridges two areas in a meaningful way.
- Practical algorithm with thoughtful numerical design (tangent-space Monte Carlo).
- Strong qualitative improvements on nonlinear data; mixture extension is compelling.
- Well written and likely to inspire follow-up work.

Cons for acceptance
- Scalability to high-D and large-N not convincingly demonstrated or analyzed.
- Limited sensitivity analysis for σ and ρ; lack of guidance for practitioners.
- Missing runtime/complexity comparisons; intrinsic estimator baselines underemphasized in main text.
- EM and several key implementation details relegated to the supplement.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The conceptual contribution and empirical promise are strong, particularly for mixture modeling on nonlinear structure. I would lean to accept if the authors add a clear complexity analysis, sensitivity studies for σ (and ρ), runtime and convergence reports, and move intrinsic estimator visualizations and mixture details into the main paper.
