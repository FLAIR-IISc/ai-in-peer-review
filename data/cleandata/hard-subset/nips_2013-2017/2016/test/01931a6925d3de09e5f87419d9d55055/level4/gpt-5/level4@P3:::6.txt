Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), a parametric probability model that replaces the Euclidean metric in the multivariate normal with a smoothly varying Riemannian metric M(x). The metric is learned nonparametrically from data via local diagonal covariances weighted by a Gaussian kernel, yielding geodesics that are attracted to regions of high data density. With this metric, the authors define a Riemannian normal distribution pM(x | µ, Σ) that uses the logarithm map Logµ(x) in the quadratic form, and they estimate its normalization constant by Monte Carlo integration over the tangent space at µ. Parameters µ and Σ are learned by maximum likelihood using block coordinate descent: steepest descent on µ (via exponential maps) and gradient descent on a Cholesky-like factor A with Σ−1 = AᵀA. They further extend the model to mixtures and derive an EM algorithm. Experiments on synthetic manifolds and EEG sleep-stage data show that LAND (and LAND mixtures) can better capture non-ellipsoidal structure than Euclidean GMMs and Riemannian least-squares estimators.

The paper bridges Riemannian statistics (e.g., Pennec’s Riemannian normal and intrinsic estimators) with manifold learning. Unlike prior Riemannian generative models that assume a known manifold (e.g., probabilistic PCA on manifolds, spherical mixtures), the manifold here is learned via a data-adaptive metric, conceptually akin to Isomap’s replacement of Euclidean distances by geodesics but with a continuous metric on RD. The proposed normalization scheme leverages integration in the tangent space to avoid repeated logarithm map solves.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean and well-motivated: a parametric maximum-entropy distribution built on a learned Riemannian geometry that favors high-density regions.
- Algorithmic contributions: (i) a scalable Monte Carlo estimator of the normalization constant via tangent-space sampling, (ii) practical gradient/steepest-descent updates for µ and Σ, and (iii) an EM formulation for LAND mixtures.
- Empirical evidence that LAND captures nonlinear structure that defeats Euclidean Gaussians, with clear visualizations of geodesics and density contours.
- Good exposition of necessary Riemannian tools (geodesics, exponential/log maps), making the approach accessible to readers familiar with differential geometry.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality
- Model selection and potential overfitting: In the sleep-stage clustering, σ is first fixed (σ=1) and then tuned over a grid, with performance reported on the same labels used for tuning. This risks optimistic estimates. A held-out validation/test split or cross-validation should be used for σ selection, and test-set F-measure reported.
- Missing control experiments: It would be informative to assess LAND on data generated from a single Gaussian or a GMM in RD to check whether LAND degrades in well-modeled Euclidean settings. This would clarify when LAND is warranted.
- Computational aspects: The cost and variance of the normalization Monte Carlo estimator (S=3000) are not analyzed. Reporting variance across runs and a cost/accuracy trade-off would strengthen the technical evaluation. Similarly, a complexity discussion in N, D, and S (and sensitivity to initialization) would help.
- High-dimensional regimes: The metric estimation uses kernel smoothing with diagonal local covariances and is not invariant to linear transforms; scalability and robustness in high D are unclear. Some exploration or guidance would be useful.

Clarity
- Notation and conceptual separation of M(x) and Σ: At places the manuscript suggests “M = Σ−1,” which is misleading. M(x) is the learned Riemannian metric that defines geodesics, Log, and the measure, while Σ is the dispersion of the LAND in TµM. Please clarify this throughout (especially in the steepest-descent derivation) and avoid symbol overloading. If “M = Σ−1” appears in a specific local inner product, state the scope and intent precisely.
- Mixture/cluster assignment details: In Sec. 4.2, clarify whether clustering uses a LAND mixture with K=3 components and whether responsibilities are computed with geodesic Logµk(·). If a single-component LAND is used with k-means on geodesic distances, please state this explicitly.
- σ selection: Clearly distinguish fixed-σ results from tuned-σ results, and indicate whether any label information was used in tuning.

Originality
- The novelty lies in combining (i) a learned, continuous Riemannian metric, (ii) the Riemannian normal with maximum entropy properties, and (iii) practical ML estimation including normalization via tangent-space Monte Carlo, and extending to mixtures. While the ingredients build on known techniques (e.g., geodesic ODEs, Pennec’s distribution, kernel-based metrics), the integration into a workable, probabilistic generative model on a learned manifold is interesting and, to my knowledge, not previously demonstrated at NIPS.

Significance
- If validated more rigorously, LAND could be a useful drop-in replacement for Gaussians in generative models when data live near nonlinear structures (e.g., clustering, density estimation, LDA-like models in curved spaces). The maximum-entropy interpretation and mixture extension are compelling. However, broader impact depends on addressing model selection (σ), computational scaling, and robustness in higher dimensions.

Questions and requests for clarification
- Please clarify the relationship and distinct roles of M(x) and Σ, and revise any derivations that suggest equality. Is the preconditioning by Σ in the µ-update merely a steepest-descent step under the Σ−1-induced norm?
- How are responsibilities computed in the LAND mixture (14) in practice? Do you recompute C(µk, Σk) for each component at every iteration, and how do you control Monte Carlo noise across EM steps?
- What is the train/validation/test protocol for σ in the EEG experiment? Can you report test F-measure with σ chosen on validation only?
- How sensitive are results to S, the number of Monte Carlo samples, and to the kernel bandwidth σ in synthetic and real data?
- What is the computational complexity per iteration (for mean and covariance updates) as a function of N, D, and S?

Pros for acceptance
- Well-motivated, principled generalization of the Gaussian to learned Riemannian geometries.
- Practical algorithms for normalization and ML estimation; mixture extension.
- Clear empirical gains on nonlinear manifolds and intuitive visualizations.
- Potential to influence generative modeling where local geometry matters.

Cons for acceptance
- Evaluation lacks train/test separation for σ; potential overfitting in the real-data study.
- Missing baselines in Euclidean settings and limited exploration of high-dimensional performance.
- Some notational ambiguity (M vs. Σ) and incomplete clarity on clustering setup and EM details.
- Nonconvex optimization and computational costs are not fully characterized.

Overall evaluation
This is an interesting and timely contribution that brings probabilistic modeling to learned Riemannian geometries with a coherent maximum-entropy foundation. With clearer notation, stronger evaluation protocols (particularly for σ selection and Euclidean baselines), and a brief computational analysis, the paper would be a strong addition to NIPS. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on addressing the clarity and evaluation issues above.
