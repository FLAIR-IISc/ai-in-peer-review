

The paper introduces a Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), which generalizes the multivariate normal distribution to a Riemannian manifold setting by replacing the Euclidean metric with a locally adaptive metric that favors regions of high data density. This work extends Pennec's [15] Riemannian normal distribution, which assumes a known manifold, to the manifold learning setting where the metric is learned from data. The authors develop a maximum likelihood estimation procedure using gradient descent and Monte Carlo integration, and extend the model to mixture models with a corresponding EM algorithm. The method is evaluated on synthetic data and EEG measurements of human sleep stages.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with a well-defined mathematical framework. The derivation of the LAND, normalization constant, and optimization procedure is thorough, and the experimental results show that LAND outperforms Gaussian Mixture Models (GMM) and least squares estimators on both synthetic and real-world datasets, particularly in capturing nonlinear structures. The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with consistent notation and sufficient detail for reproducibility.

Regarding originality, the core contribution of learning the manifold from data is interesting, but the paper fails to adequately distinguish this contribution from Simo-Serra et al. [19], who also consider mixtures of Riemannian normal distributions on known manifolds. The paper should have explicitly compared with Simo-Serra et al. [19] in the introduction and related work section, as their work is structurally similar but assumes the manifold is known a priori. The absence of reference [19] in the introduction is particularly problematic, as the key difference (learning the manifold vs. assuming it is known) is not clearly articulated until later sections, weakening the novelty claim.

The significance of the work is demonstrated through its application to modeling nonlinear structures in EEG data, which is a meaningful real-world problem. However, the significance could be strengthened by more clearly articulating how the LAND differs from existing approaches and why this difference matters for practical applications.

In summary, the paper presents a technically sound approach with good experimental validation. The main weakness is the lack of clear distinction from Simo-Serra et al. [19], which undermines the novelty claim. To improve the paper, the authors should explicitly compare with Simo-Serra et al. [19] in the introduction and related work, clearly articulating how their approach differs (learning the manifold from data vs. assuming it is known).

Pro: Strong technical foundation, clear writing, good experimental validation
