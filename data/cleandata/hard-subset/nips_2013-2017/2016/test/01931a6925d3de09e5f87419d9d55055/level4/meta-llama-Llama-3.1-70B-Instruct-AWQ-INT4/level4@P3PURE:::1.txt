The paper proposes LAND, a novel parametric metric defined over a nonparametric space, by bridging concepts from metric learning, manifold learning, and Riemannian statistics. The parametric metric learned is a Riemannian normal distribution, as defined in Pennec [15]. The authors propose a maximum likelihood algorithm to estimate the mean and covariance of the Riemannian normal distribution, effectively yielding a Mahalanobis distance for Riemannian manifolds. Distances between points are defined as geodesics along the Riemannian manifold, similar to Hauberg et al. [9], and rely on learning a local metric. Notably, the authors propose to learn the metric by using the inverses of the local (diagonal) covariances. The authors also consider mixtures of LANDs, which is an interesting application of the proposed method.

The paper brings new insights into the connection between metric learning and Riemannian statistics. Having parametric and generative models that can generalize beyond the training data is valuable in many scenarios. However, my primary concern is the applicability of LAND to real-world data, which is often both large-scale and high-dimensional. LAND requires the computation of a (diagonal) covariance matrix for each data point and its inverse, which are known to be challenging problems in high dimensions. Although the authors mention high-dimensionality as a potential issue (lines 265-266), the paper would benefit from a thorough analysis of the complexity and limitations of the proposed metric. Specifically, it would be helpful to know the dimensions at which the method starts to degrade (e.g., 3, 5, 10, 100 dimensions).

Furthermore, the paper could be better situated within the context of the existing literature on metric learning. Hauberg et al. [9] already discuss some of the relevant literature, such as Frome et al. [1,2] and Malisiewicz and Efros [6], who all learn a diagonal metric tensor for every point, similar to LAND. Local covariances have also been used in methods like local PCA (Kambhatla – Dimension reduction by local principal component analysis), although not for every point.

In the proposed method, the authors use a Gaussian kernel to define locality for learning the local metric. However, the influence of the parameter sigma on the results is not thoroughly discussed. While the choice of sigma is mentioned for the sleep data in Sect. 4.2, it is not discussed for the synthetic data in Sect. 4.1. The paper would benefit from robustness tests for sigma, and it would be interesting to know whether sigma is the same for all points, regardless of whether they are in low- or high-density regions.

The comparison of LAND to intrinsic estimators in Fig. 4 shows similar results, but the authors do not provide visual results for the intrinsic estimators in Figs. 5 and 6. Instead, they show visualization results against GMM, which are already shown to perform poorly in Fig. 4. This makes the comparison less insightful. Some visual results using the intrinsic model are shown in the supplementary material (Figs. 2 and 4), and they outperform GMM. Therefore, it would be beneficial to replace the GMM results in the initial paper with the intrinsic model results or make room for both.

An analysis of the computational complexity of LAND versus intrinsic estimators would be valuable to determine which method should be favored over the other and for which data structures. The authors' consideration of mixtures of LANDs is an interesting application of the proposed method, as real-world data is rarely generated by a single component. This aspect would be worth expanding upon.

Overall, the paper is well-written and a pleasure to read.
