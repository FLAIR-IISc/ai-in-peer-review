This paper extends the common multivariate normal density by replacing the Euclidean metric with a locally adaptive Riemannian metric. The result is a generalization of the normal distribution to the manifold learning setting, which has wide applicability as illustrated by the reported experiments. Importantly, it provides a complete account of a parameter estimation procedure of the maximum likelihood type. The extension to a mixture of LANDs makes it particularly practical as a competitor to the now standard Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM). The experiments are exciting and open a wide avenue for future research. 

The paper is well-structured, with a clear and concise introduction to the problem and the proposed solution. The authors provide a thorough explanation of the mathematical background and the derivation of the locally adaptive normal distribution (LAND). The experimental results are also well-presented, with a comparison to the GMM and a mixture of LANDs using least squares estimators.

The paper's main strength is its ability to generalize the normal distribution to the manifold learning setting, providing a powerful tool for modeling complex data distributions. The use of a locally adaptive Riemannian metric allows the LAND to capture non-linear relationships in the data, making it a valuable contribution to the field.

The paper's weakness is the potential computational cost of evaluating the logarithm maps, which may be a bottleneck for large datasets. However, the authors suggest that this can be improved with specialized solvers or manifold-specific heuristics.

In terms of relevance to NIPS, the paper is highly relevant as it presents a novel approach to modeling complex data distributions, which is a key area of research in machine learning. The paper's focus on manifold learning and Riemannian geometry also aligns with the NIPS conference's emphasis on theoretical and methodological advances in machine learning.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel and powerful approach to modeling complex data distributions.

* The extension to a mixture of LANDs makes it particularly practical as a competitor to the now standard GMM.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The potential computational cost of evaluating the logarithm maps may be a bottleneck for large datasets.
