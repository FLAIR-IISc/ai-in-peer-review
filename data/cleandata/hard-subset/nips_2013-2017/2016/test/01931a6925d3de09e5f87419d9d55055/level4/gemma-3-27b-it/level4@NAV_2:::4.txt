The paper proposes a locally adaptive normal distribution (LAND) as a generalization of the standard normal distribution to Riemannian manifolds, aiming to better model data lying near potentially low-dimensional nonlinear manifolds. The core idea is to replace the Euclidean metric with a locally adaptive, smoothly changing metric learned from the data, effectively capturing nonlinear structures. The authors develop a maximum likelihood estimation algorithm combining gradient descent and Monte Carlo integration, extend it to mixture models via an EM algorithm, and demonstrate its effectiveness on both synthetic and EEG sleep stage data.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, presenting a sound theoretical foundation. The connection to Riemannian geometry is well-motivated, and the proposed method builds logically upon prior work in manifold learning (e.g., Isomap [21]) and Riemannian statistics (e.g., Pennec [15]). The experimental evaluation provides compelling evidence for the LAND’s ability to fit non-trivial distributions and outperform Gaussian mixture models on the considered datasets. The inclusion of both synthetic and real-world data strengthens the evaluation.

A key strength of this work lies in bridging Riemannian statistics with manifold learning, offering a parametric distribution grounded in a non-parametric metric. The proposed Monte Carlo integration scheme appears to be a practical solution for estimating the normalization constant, and the extension to mixture models adds to the method’s versatility. The authors also acknowledge and discuss limitations, such as the computational cost of evaluating logarithm maps.

However, the novelty relative to existing work could be more clearly articulated. While the authors mention Hauberg et al. [9] in the introduction, the connection to and differentiation from Simo-Serra et al. [19], which also considers Riemannian normal mixture models, is not thoroughly discussed until the related work section. A more upfront comparison would benefit the reader and better position the contribution. The discussion of how the proposed metric learning approach differs from others (e.g., [22, 9]) could also be expanded.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Technically sound and well-motivated approach to distribution estimation on manifolds.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation with a solid theoretical foundation.
*   Demonstrated effectiveness on both synthetic and real-world data.
*   Practical algorithm for parameter estimation and extension to mixture models.
*   Bridges Riemannian statistics and manifold learning, offering a valuable contribution to the field.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The novelty compared to Simo-Serra et al. [19] is not sufficiently highlighted throughout the paper.
*   The differences between the proposed metric learning method and existing approaches could be more thoroughly discussed.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper with significant potential. While the novelty could be more clearly articulated, the technical soundness, clarity, and experimental validation are compelling. Addressing the concerns regarding the comparison with related work would further strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to enhance the discussion of novelty and related work.
