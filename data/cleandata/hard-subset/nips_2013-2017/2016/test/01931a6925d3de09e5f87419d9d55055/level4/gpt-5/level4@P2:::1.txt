This paper introduces the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), which replaces the Euclidean metric in the multivariate normal with a smoothly varying, data-driven Riemannian metric. The resulting distribution is the Riemannian normal of Pennec, but instantiated on a learned manifold rather than a known one. The metric is constructed from the inverse of local (diagonal) covariance estimates weighted by a Gaussian kernel, which encourages geodesics to remain in regions of high data density (cf. Hauberg et al.). The authors develop maximum-likelihood estimation for the mean and covariance by combining gradient-based optimization with a Monte Carlo estimate of the normalization constant evaluated in the tangent space. They further extend the model to mixtures with an EM algorithm. Experiments on synthetic data and EEG sleep-stage features illustrate improved fit and clustering compared to Gaussian mixtures and intrinsic least-squares estimators.

Relation to prior work: The paper sits at the interface of manifold learning and Riemannian statistics. It connects Isomap-style geodesic modeling and learned metrics with the Riemannian normal (Pennec), and differs from prior Riemannian generative models that assume a known manifold. It also relates to metric learning approaches that estimate local, often diagonal, metrics and to local PCA methods that use local covariances to describe structure.

Strengths:
- Clear, well-organized presentation; the mathematical setup and algorithmic choices are well motivated.
- Conceptually appealing bridge: a parametric, maximum-entropy distribution defined on a nonparametric, data-adaptive geometry.
- Practical algorithm: computing C(µ, Σ) via tangent-space Monte Carlo avoids expensive boundary value problems and makes the method implementable.
- Empirical results indicate improved modeling of non-ellipsoidal structure and better clustering of EEG sleep stages compared to GMMs.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Scalability to high dimensions and large datasets is insufficiently analyzed. Even with diagonal local covariances, computing and storing per-point metrics and repeatedly evaluating exponential/log maps may be costly. A complexity analysis in terms of N (samples), D (dimension), S (MC samples), and K (mixture components) is needed.
- Bandwidth selection for the Gaussian kernel (σ) is only explored on the EEG data. The method’s dependence on σ, and whether a single global σ suffices across regions of varying density, is not systematically studied.
- Positioning within the metric learning literature could be strengthened. Prior work has learned pointwise diagonal metrics and exploited local covariances (e.g., local PCA; and metric learning methods such as those by Frome et al. and by Malisiewicz & Efros). Clarifying how LAND’s metric differs in objective, regularization, and guarantees would help delineate novelty.
- Comparative evaluation: Figures in the main paper emphasize GMM baselines, while intrinsic least-squares estimators appear competitive in Fig. 4 and only get visual comparisons in the supplement. Including those comparisons in the main text would be more informative, together with runtime comparisons.
- The mixture extension is promising but underexplored. More thorough experiments (model selection, initialization strategies, sensitivity to K, and comparisons with GMMs under AIC/BIC) would bolster significance.

Quality: The methodology is technically sound, and the Monte Carlo normalization via tangent space is elegant. Claims are supported by experiments, but the work would benefit from a detailed complexity analysis, sensitivity studies for σ, and more comprehensive baselines in the main text.

Clarity: Excellent. The paper is easy to follow, with precise notation and clear intuition. Suggestions: briefly summarize the computational burden of Exp/Log evaluations and provide guidance on choosing σ.

Originality: Adapting the Riemannian normal to a learned, data-adaptive manifold is original and timely. The parametric-on-nonparametric formulation and the practical MLE procedure are novel contributions relative to prior work assuming known manifolds.

Significance: The approach is potentially impactful for generative modeling on data with curved structure. Demonstrations on synthetic and EEG data are encouraging; however, understanding limits in higher dimensions and at larger scale is essential for broader adoption.

Specific questions and suggestions:
- Provide a time and space complexity breakdown for: (i) building the metric (including kernel neighbor search), (ii) computing Exp and Log, and (iii) estimating C(µ, Σ) with S samples, per iteration and per component. Include empirical runtimes and scaling plots in N, D, and K.
- Report sensitivity/robustness to σ on the synthetic datasets, and consider adaptive bandwidths (e.g., k-NN distances) to handle heterogeneous densities.
- Discuss memory/computation trade-offs (e.g., caching local statistics, using sparse neighborhoods, or sub-sampling for metric construction).
- In the mixture setting, detail initialization, convergence behavior, and how normalization constants are handled across components. Compare LAND mixtures and GMMs under identical K using AIC/BIC and held-out likelihood.
- Include main-text visualizations and runtime comparisons against intrinsic least-squares estimators; clarify when ML LAND should be preferred.
- Consider ablations: Euclidean vs learned metric; effect of diagonal vs full (or low-rank) local covariances; and the impact of the normalization term.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear, well-executed idea that unifies Riemannian statistics and manifold learning with a practical ML estimator.
- Strong qualitative and quantitative evidence on nontrivial data; improved modeling of curved structures over GMMs.
- Potential to serve as a drop-in replacement for Gaussians in manifold-aware generative models.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Insufficient analysis of computational complexity, scalability, and high-dimensional behavior.
- Limited sensitivity study for σ and limited main-text comparisons against the intrinsic estimator baseline.
- Mixture model evaluation is promising but not yet comprehensive.

Overall, this is a carefully written and conceptually compelling paper. Addressing the scalability analysis, σ selection robustness, and strengthening baselines—especially for the mixture case—would substantially increase its impact.
