This paper introduces a locally adaptive normal distribution (LAND) that moves beyond Euclidean distance by defining a smooth, data-dependent Riemannian metric M(x). Geodesic distances are then computed using the exponential and logarithmic maps, leveraging Euler-Lagrange equations for efficient estimation. Crucially, the authors map data points to the tangent plane via the logarithm map before applying a normal distribution and calculating a Mahalanobis distance—effectively measuring distance from a point to the mean *along* the manifold. Maximum likelihood estimation is used to derive estimators for the mean and covariance, but due to the lack of closed-form solutions, a steepest descent algorithm is proposed for parameter estimation, alongside a Monte Carlo approximation for the intractable normalization constant. The method is evaluated on both synthetic data generated on a manifold and a clustering task, with comparisons to standard Gaussian mixture models (GMMs). Overall, the paper presents a compelling and potentially valuable extension to the standard normal distribution, adeptly integrating concepts from metric estimation, geodesic distance computation, and distribution learning. I recommend this work for publication.

**Technical Quality:** The manifold techniques, particularly the application of Euler-Lagrange equations and estimation of logarithm maps, are sound and clearly explained. The proposed Monte Carlo normalization constant estimation and steepest descent algorithm demonstrate ingenuity and are well-described. However, the analysis of the applications feels somewhat incomplete. The selection of the sigma parameter appears to be driven by optimization on the training data, which raises concerns about potential overfitting. Furthermore, the paper could benefit from a clearer explanation of the practical utility of the learned distribution.

**Novelty:** The primary contribution of this work appears to be a novel combination of existing techniques – utilizing kernels to define the metric M(x), estimating geodesic distances via Euler-Lagrange, and applying a normal distribution within this framework. The algorithm developed for estimating the mean, covariance, and normalization constant adds to this novelty.

**Potential Impact:** The evaluation on synthetic data, including comparisons of log-likelihoods for data generated from the learned distributions, is fair. However, the choice of the sigma parameter remains unclear, impacting the interpretability of the results. While the LAND demonstrates good performance on manifold-generated data, a comparison to GMMs on data originating from a standard normal distribution or GMM would strengthen the analysis. The clustering evaluation, using an optimized F-measure for sigma selection, is similarly problematic. Reporting the F-measure on a held-out test set is essential to address potential overfitting concerns and provide a more robust assessment of performance. Exploring the method’s performance on very high-dimensional data would also be beneficial. The authors suggest using LAND as a generative model for manifold data, but the assumption that the manifold dimension equals the observation dimension (D) is unusual in manifold learning, where the typical scenario involves a lower-dimensional manifold (d) embedded in a higher-dimensional space (D, where D >> d).

**Clarity:** One point of notational confusion arises from the overloading of the sigma (Σ) symbol. The paper states M = Σ<sup>-1</sup> but then estimates Σ using a local, diagonal kernel matrix. It is unclear if this Σ is the same as the fitted covariance matrix. If so, is the kernel merely an initialization step? This is potentially problematic, as M(x) is intended to vary with x, while a single covariance matrix is static. The proof of the steepest descent algorithm explicitly states “in our case M = Σ<sup>-1</sup>,” reinforcing the need for clarification. Furthermore, in Section 4.2, the method for determining cluster membership—presumably by selecting the nearest cluster mean based on geodesic distance—is not explicitly stated and could be clarified. Despite this, the introduction to manifold concepts is generally well-explained, assuming some familiarity with Riemannian manifolds.
