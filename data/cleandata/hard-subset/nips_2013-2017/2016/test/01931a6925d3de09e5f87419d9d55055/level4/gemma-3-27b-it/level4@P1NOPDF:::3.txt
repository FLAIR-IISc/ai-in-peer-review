This paper proposes modeling the observation space with a hand-crafted Riemannian metric and defining a curved normal distribution based on the associated Riemannian geometry, aiming to better capture the underlying data manifold. The paper addresses a fundamental problem: how to define a Riemannian metric from a set of high-dimensional, real-valued observations, and subsequently, how to define and estimate a curved Gaussian distribution based on this geometry. This presents a useful tool and an interesting application of Riemannian geometry to statistical learning. The first part of the proposed method defines the underlying Riemannian geometry in Section 3.1. While the title suggests that the metric is “learned,” it is actually hand-crafted in Equation 7, based on two hyperparameters, ρ and σ, which are fixed empirically. I suspect that σ is a sensitive parameter. The paper should discuss in more detail the intuitive effect of varying σ and how to select an appropriate value. Without this discussion, the paper feels incomplete. Could the metric learning stage be integrated with the LAND learning stage, allowing ρ and σ to be learned directly? 

A key advantage of a geometric approach is its invariance to coordinate transformations. However, the metric in Equation 7 is *not* invariant. For example, consider data forming a line in a 2D observation space. The resulting metric would differ significantly depending on whether the coordinate system’s x-axis is parallel to the line or oriented at an angle, such as 45 degrees. A more detailed discussion of this point is warranted, including whether the full (non-diagonal) metric would exhibit invariance, and further intuition regarding the metric in Equation 7. There is a substantial body of literature connecting Riemannian geometry with metric and manifold learning (a search for "Riemannian" "metric learning" "manifold learning" will reveal relevant work), and this paper would benefit from relating to this existing research.

A major criticism concerns the first experiment. The comparison is not particularly meaningful because, unsurprisingly, the more flexible LAND model can describe the data with fewer components. However, a Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) could achieve comparable performance with a larger number of components, while LAND would likely overfit. I suggest redesigning the experiment to allow each method to select its optimal number of components based on a principled criterion (e.g., BIC), and then compare their performance. LAND is still expected to perform better due to its ability to capture the underlying manifold structure, but this comparison would be more informative. It would also be insightful to see how LAND performs with a high number of components and observes the onset of overfitting.

LAND is demonstrably more computationally expensive than estimating a Gaussian. Are there any quantitative measurements or experiments to clarify the computational cost? What is the scalability limit of LAND? Could it be applied to datasets like MNIST and compared with Gaussian mixture learning?

======

Following review of the authors’ response, it’s clear that LAND is a more flexible model than a Gaussian distribution. Increasing the number of components makes each local LAND model more susceptible to local noise compared to a local Gaussian. While the authors state that "the geodesics will almost be straight lines," they are still curves, and these curves can lead to overfitting. I strongly suggest the authors revise this experiment in their rebuttal and subsequent revision.
