In this paper, the authors present a naturally distributed metric adapted to a local context. Instead of measuring distances using Euclidean distances, they define a Riemannian metric M(x) that smoothly varies within the data set. Given R, the geometric distances of the metric M(x) can be computed using exponential and logarithmic maps. The authors use the Euler-Lagrange equation, which defines a set of ordinary differential equations for measuring geometric distances. The logarithmic map takes points from the manifold (y) to the tangent plane (at x), so that the length of the resulting vector is the geometric distance from x to y along the manifold. Then, the authors apply a normal distribution to the points mapped by the logarithmic map and calculate the Mahalanobis distance. This inner product is used for the average distance from a point x instead of Euclidean distance. Next, the authors create estimates for the mean and covariance using maximum likelihood. Since the solutions are not found in closed form, they present a steepest descent algorithm to measure them. Finally, since the normalization constant is also not found in closed form, the authors present a Monte-Carlo approximation method. They apply this method for a clustering task as well as for synthetic data generated in a manifold. They compare it with Gaussian standard deviation. Overall, I believe this paper provides an interesting and useful extension of the normal distribution that fits into the local manifold framework. The authors have combined metric measurements, geometric distance calculations, and learning distribution tasks. I believe this work is worthy of publication. I have a few comments about the above score:

Technical Quality: Comments: The manifold technique (using the Euler-Lagrange equation, measuring the logarithmic map) is correct and well-explained. The Monte-Carlo measure of the normalization constant and the steepest descent algorithm is clever and well-described. The analysis of applications is absent (does the sigma parameter seem to be optimized in the training data?). The utility explanation of the learning distribution is also somewhat absent.

Novelty: Comments: It seems that the main contribution of this paper is to integrate previous work (using a kernel for a metric M(x), measuring geometric distances via Euler-Lagrange, applying a normal distribution) and provide an algorithm for parameter (mean, covariance, normalization constant) measurements.

Potential Impact: Comments: The authors provide a fair assessment of GMM and LAND in synthetic data (looking at log-probability in data generated by the learning distribution), but it is not clear how they selected the sigma parameter. The authors show that LAND fits well with data generated along a manifold, but they do not show how well it compares with GMM if the data is generated according to a single normal distribution or GMM. In real-life (clustering) data, they optimize the F measure to select the sigma value for LAND and then present the same F measure. What they should have done (or made clearer) is to provide an F measure in a held-out test set so that it is fair. Therefore, it is difficult to see if there is any over-fitting problem. It would also be good to test very high-dimensional data. The authors present a potentially useful application that can be used as a generative model for LAND manifold data, but they rather assume an unusual manifold that the data X is observed in D dimensions and the manifold dimension is also D (i.e., the points on the tangent plane are D-dimensional and the metric M(x) is full rank). In manifold learning, we are usually interested in data that is located in a d-dimensional manifold embedded in a D-dimensional Euclidean space, where D is much larger than d.

Transparency: Comments: A particular notation point seemed confusing to me: overloading of sigma notation. Specifically, they say M = Sigma^-1 but then assume it through a (local) kernel matrix. Is this sigma (fitted) like a covariance matrix? If so, is the kernel just a start? This would be strange because M(x) should change with x but the fitted covariance matrix is a single parameter. In the proof of the steepest descent, they explicitly say "in our case M = Sigma^-1" and use that substitution. Clarification would be good here. They do not mention this in Sec 4.2, but I assume that cluster membership is chosen by selecting 2 clusters and calculating the nearest (geometric?) distance from the cluster mean. Overall, I believe the role of the manifold is well explained (at least for someone familiar with Riemannian manifolds).
