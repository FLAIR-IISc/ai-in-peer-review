This paper proposes a novel approach to modeling the observation space using a hand-crafted Riemannian metric and defining a curved normal distribution based on the associated Riemannian geometry. This approach aims to better capture the data manifold, addressing a fundamental problem in defining a Riemannian metric for high-dimensional real-valued observations and estimating a curved Gaussian distribution based on this geometry. The proposed method presents a useful tool and an interesting application of Riemannian geometry in statistical learning.

The first part of the proposed method defines the underlying Riemannian geometry in section 3.1. However, despite the title suggesting that the method "learns a metric," the metric is actually hand-crafted in equation 7 using two hyper-parameters, rho and sigma, which are fixed empirically in advance. I suspect that sigma is a sensitive parameter, and the paper should provide a more detailed discussion on the intuitive effect of varying sigma and how to select this parameter. Furthermore, it would be beneficial to explore the possibility of merging the metric learning stage with the LAND learning stage, allowing rho and sigma to be learned.

One of the advantages of the geometric approach is its invariance to coordinate transformations. However, the metric in equation 7 does not possess this property. For instance, if the data forms a line in a 2D observation space, the metric would be different in a coordinate system with its x-axis parallel to this line versus a coordinate system that forms an angle, e.g., 45 degrees, to this line. A more detailed discussion on this aspect, including the invariance of the corresponding full (rather than diagonal) metric, would be useful. Additionally, providing more intuition on the metric in equation 7 would be beneficial.

There is a rich literature on metric learning and manifold learning with connections to Riemannian geometry that can be related to this work. A more thorough discussion of these connections would strengthen the paper.

As a main criticism, the comparison in the first experiment is not meaningful because the more flexible LAND can describe the data better with a smaller number of components. However, GMM can describe the data better with a large number of components, at which point LAND will overfit. To address this, the authors are suggested to redesign the experiment, allowing each compared method to choose its favorable number of components based on a certain criterion (e.g., BIC), and then compare the corresponding performance. LAND is still expected to perform better due to its ability to describe the manifold structure, making this comparison more meaningful. It would also be interesting to see how LAND with a large number of components will overfit.

LAND is obviously a more expensive procedure than estimating a Gaussian. Quantitative measurements or experiments that demonstrate the computational cost of LAND would be beneficial. Additionally, discussing the limit of the scale that LAND can handle and exploring the possibility of learning LAND on MNIST and comparing it with Gaussian mixture learning would provide valuable insights.

After reading the authors' response, it is clear that LAND is a more flexible model than the Gaussian distribution. However, increasing the number of components makes each local LAND more vulnerable to local noises compared to a local Gaussian. While the geodesics may be close to straight lines, they are still curves, which can lead to overfitting. I highly suggest that the authors revise this experiment in the revision.
