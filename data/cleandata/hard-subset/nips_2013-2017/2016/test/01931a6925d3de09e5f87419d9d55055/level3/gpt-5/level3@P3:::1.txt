Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes LAND, a parametric probability distribution defined over a learned nonparametric Riemannian metric space. The key idea is to retain the maximum-entropy Riemannian normal form of Pennec while replacing the Euclidean metric with a data-adaptive Riemannian metric learned from local covariances. Concretely, the metric tensor is diagonal with entries given by the inverse of a Gaussian-kernel–weighted local variance; geodesic distances under this tensor define the “nonlinear” distance in the normal’s exponent. The authors develop (i) a tangent-space Monte Carlo estimator for the normalization constant that avoids expensive BVPs, (ii) a gradient-based ML procedure for the mean and covariance, and (iii) an EM algorithm for mixtures of LANDs. Experiments on synthetic data and EEG sleep-stage features show that LAND fits non-elliptical structure and often outperforms GMMs; mixtures can capture multiple modes. The work builds a bridge from manifold learning (e.g., Isomap; learned metrics of Hauberg et al.) to Riemannian statistics (Pennec; intrinsic estimators), offering generative modeling that extrapolates off-sample via geodesics.

Strengths
- Conceptual elegance: a principled link between local metric learning/manifold learning and Riemannian maximum-entropy modeling. Clear articulation of why intrinsic least-squares estimators can place means in low-density regions, and how ML under LAND counteracts this via the normalization term.
- Sound algorithmic design: integrating over the tangent space greatly reduces the cost relative to manifold-space integration; using Exp maps within Monte Carlo is a nice practical compromise.
- Mixture extension and EM derivation are natural and broadly useful.
- Generative extrapolation beyond the training set is compelling in this setting.
- Writing is excellent: clear, well organized, and pleasant to read.

Weaknesses and requested clarifications
- Scalability and “high-dimensional”: The paper acknowledges potential issues but does not quantify them. A precise complexity analysis is needed:
  • Metric evaluation M(x): naive O(ND) to compute diagonal variances via all N kernels; with kNN sparsification, O(kD). Derivatives ∂M/∂x used in geodesic ODEs have similar cost.
  • Geodesic IVP (for Exp): with L integration steps, Tgeo ≈ L·(cost of M + cost of ∂M). For diagonal metrics with Gaussian kernels this is typically O(L·kD) if neighbors are precomputed.
  • Log map (for data terms): BVPs are substantially more expensive than IVPs; per point cost Tlog >> Tgeo. In mixtures, E-steps require NK evaluations of Logµk(xn) unless approximations are used; this is likely the main bottleneck.
  • Normalizer C(µ,Σ): O(S·Tgeo) for S Monte Carlo samples; gradients add O(SD2) for moment terms and repeated metric evaluations.
  • Overall per-iteration costs: single-component LAND roughly O(N·Tlog + S·Tgeo + D3) (the last for covariance updates), mixture adds a factor K in the E-step and M-step normalizer evaluations.
  Please include wall-clock/runtime-vs-(N,D,K,S,L,k) plots and memory scaling. Also define “high-dimensional” concretely: e.g., what happens at D ∈ {3, 5, 10, 50, 100}? At what D do BVPs become impractical on typical hardware?
- Positioning within metric learning: The per-point diagonal metric is closely related to local metric learning and local PCA. Please discuss connections to: local Mahalanobis/diagonal metrics (e.g., Frome et al.; Malisiewicz & Efros/per-exemplar local models), LMNN/NCA variants with local metrics, and local PCA/tangent-space alignment approaches. This would clarify novelty relative to prior local metric constructions and how LAND’s Riemannian-statistical treatment differs.
- Kernel bandwidth σ: The metric depends critically on σ, which sets locality. Questions:
  • Is σ uniform across the space, or adaptive to local density? A variable bandwidth (kNN bandwidth, balloon estimators, or perplexity-like rules) could improve robustness across heterogeneous densities.
  • Please add sensitivity/robustness analyses: likelihood and clustering performance vs σ, including per-subject curves, and report whether σ selected on one region generalizes to others.
  • How is ρ chosen, and how sensitive are results to ρ?
- Baselines and figures: Fig. 4 shows LAND vs intrinsic estimators; however, the visual comparisons in Figs. 5–6 are only against GMMs, which are known to underfit curved manifolds. This weakens the qualitative story.
  • Replace or supplement with visualizations of intrinsic estimator fits (some are in the supplement and appear stronger than GMMs).
  • Consider adding local-PCA-based generative baselines and local metric-learning baselines using per-point diagonal metrics.
  • Report computational complexity comparisons (runtime, #ODE/BVP solves) between LAND and intrinsic estimators to guide method choice by data structure.
- Invariance: The learned diagonal metric is not invariant to linear scalings/rotations. Please recommend or evaluate preprocessing (e.g., whitening) or include a global linear transform jointly learned with LAND.
- Mixtures of LAND: This is a particularly promising direction (different manifolds/regions, class-conditional modeling). The paper would benefit from deeper experiments: model selection over K, qualitative geodesics within/between components, and comparison to mixtures of intrinsic Riemannian normals on known manifolds.

Quality
Technically sound overall; the tangent-space Monte Carlo and gradients are reasonable and explained. The biggest gap is a missing complexity/bottleneck analysis and limited baselines on the more informative figures. Claims about scalability and applicability would be strengthened by quantitative runtime/memory results and Log/Exp solver details.

Clarity
Very well written and organized; derivations are readable. Adding a dedicated complexity section, a schematic of computational flow (where Log vs Exp are used), and a practical guide to choosing σ would further improve clarity.

Originality
The combination of a learned, data-adaptive Riemannian metric with maximum-entropy Riemannian normals and an ML/EM estimation framework is novel and timely. The diagonal local metric is related to prior local metric learning and local PCA; clearer positioning will help.

Significance
If computational issues are addressed and baselines broadened, LAND could be a useful generative building block for manifold-structured data, with mixtures opening up many applications. The general idea may inspire Riemannian variants of standard probabilistic models.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide formal complexity analysis and empirical scaling across N, D, K, S, L, and k (neighbors).
- Define “high-dimensional” operationally and show performance/runtimes at D ∈ {3, 5, 10, 50, 100}.
- Add robustness analyses and adaptive strategies for σ; clarify whether σ is uniform or density-adaptive.
- Include intrinsic estimator visualizations in main figures; add local-PCA and local metric-learning baselines.
- Report computational comparisons to intrinsic estimators.
- Expand on mixture-of-LAND experiments and model selection.
- Discuss/mitigate lack of invariance (e.g., whitening).

Reasons to accept
- Elegant, well-motivated bridge between manifold learning and Riemannian statistics with a practical ML estimator.
- Demonstrates generative extrapolation and improved fits to curved structure.
- High-quality writing and clear technical development.

Reasons to reject
- Missing complexity analysis and unclear scalability to higher D and larger N.
- Limited and somewhat uninformative qualitative baselines in the main text.
- Sensitivity to σ not systematically addressed; locality choice may dominate performance.
