This paper introduces the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), a generalization of the multivariate normal distribution designed for data that lie on or near a non-linear manifold. The core idea is to replace the standard Mahalanobis distance, which is based on a linear Euclidean metric, with a geodesic distance defined on a learned Riemannian manifold. The manifold's metric is constructed non-parametrically to be data-dependent, favoring regions of high data density. The resulting LAND is a parametric distribution (defined by a mean and covariance) on this non-parametric space. The authors derive a maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) procedure for the model parameters, which involves a clever Monte Carlo integration scheme over the tangent space to compute the normalization constant. This is extended to a mixture of LANDs using an EM algorithm. The approach bridges concepts from manifold learning, specifically the learning of a Riemannian metric from data (e.g., Hauberg et al. [9]), with statistical methods on known Riemannian manifolds (e.g., Pennec [15]). The model's effectiveness is demonstrated on synthetic data with non-linear structure and on a real-world task of clustering EEG measurements of human sleep stages.

### Strengths

1.  **Principled and Elegant Formulation:** The paper's main contribution is a well-founded generative model for manifold-structured data. Grounding the distribution in Riemannian geometry and showing it to be the maximum entropy distribution for a given mean and covariance under the learned metric provides a strong theoretical justification. This is a more principled approach than many ad-hoc methods for modeling non-linear data.

2.  **Complete Technical Development:** The authors provide a full pipeline for using the model, from constructing the data-dependent metric to a practical MLE algorithm for parameter inference. The derivation of the gradient-based optimization for the mean and covariance is thorough. The use of Monte Carlo integration on the tangent space (Eq. 10) to estimate the normalization constant is a key practical contribution, as it avoids the much more computationally expensive boundary value problem (BVP) required for repeated logarithm map computations.

3.  **Strong Experimental Results:** The experiments clearly demonstrate the advantages of the LAND over Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) for data with inherent non-linear structure. The visualizations on synthetic data (Fig. 5 and 6) are particularly convincing, showing how the LAND contours adapt to the data manifold where GMM components cannot. The application to EEG sleep stage data shows the model's utility on a real, complex dataset, outperforming the GMM baseline.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. It provides a concise but sufficient background on Riemannian geometry, clearly states its contributions, and walks the reader through the derivation of the model and its inference algorithm. The figures are illustrative and support the paper's claims effectively.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Positioning and Novelty:** The primary weakness of the paper lies in its positioning with respect to prior work, specifically Simo-Serra et al. [19]. This work also proposes a mixture of Riemannian normal distributions with an EM algorithm. While the authors cite this paper in the Related Work section and note key differences (this work learns the manifold and correctly handles the normalization constant), this distinction is crucial and should be made much earlier and more forcefully. The introduction frames the problem as replacing the Euclidean distance with a geodesic one, but it does not acknowledge that this has been done before on *fixed*, known manifolds. This omission weakens the perceived novelty of the contribution until the very end of the paper. A reader familiar with [19] might initially see this work as incremental. The paper's main novelty is the combination of *learning* the manifold and performing MLE on it, which is a significant step, but this is not highlighted clearly enough.

2.  **Scalability and Parameter Selection:** The construction of the Riemannian metric depends on a kernel smoothing parameter, σ. The authors state that this is chosen to be "as small as possible, while ensuring that the manifold is smooth enough," which is a heuristic rather than a principled method. The sleep stage experiment demonstrates that performance is sensitive to this parameter, requiring a grid search for optimal results. A more systematic or data-driven method for selecting σ would strengthen the method's practical applicability. Furthermore, the metric construction in Eq. 7 involves a sum over all N data points, suggesting that computing the metric and geodesics could be computationally expensive for large datasets. The paper could benefit from a discussion of the computational complexity and potential scalability issues.

### Detailed Comments

-   In the discussion of Simo-Serra et al. [19], the authors mention that their approach is "inconsistent with the probabilistic formulation" because they do not account for normalization constants. This is a strong claim and an important technical distinction. It would be beneficial to briefly elaborate on the implications of this, as it further solidifies the current paper's contribution as being more probabilistically rigorous.
-   The choice of a diagonal covariance for the local metric in Eq. 7 is justified for computational efficiency and to prevent overfitting. It would be interesting to know if the authors experimented with a full covariance and what the trade-offs in performance versus complexity were.
-   The initialization of the optimization algorithm is mentioned to be in the supplements. Given the non-convex nature of the optimization problem, the quality of the solution could be sensitive to initialization. A brief note on the strategy used (e.g., using least-squares estimates) in the main text would be helpful.

### Summary Evaluation

-   **Quality:** High. The paper is technically sound, with rigorous derivations and a complete methodology. The claims are well-supported by the experiments.
-   **Clarity:** Excellent. The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized.
-   **Originality:** Good, but could be presented better. The core idea of combining a learned, data-adaptive Riemannian metric with a proper MLE framework for a normal distribution is novel and significant. However, the failure to clearly distinguish this from prior work on fixed manifolds early in the paper unnecessarily obscures this originality.
-   **Significance:** High. The proposed LAND provides a powerful and flexible alternative to GMMs that could be widely applicable for generative modeling of data with non-linear structures. The idea of replacing the standard normal distribution with LAND in other classical models (as suggested in the discussion) is a promising avenue for future work.

Overall, this is a strong paper presenting an elegant, well-developed, and useful model. The main flaw is in the presentation of its novelty relative to existing literature. If the authors can revise the introduction and related work to more clearly and upfront articulate their contribution over prior methods like Simo-Serra et al. [19], it would significantly strengthen the paper. I lean towards acceptance.
