Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), a parametric probability distribution defined on a data-adaptive Riemannian manifold. The key idea is to generalize the Euclidean Gaussian by replacing the global Mahalanobis distance with a geodesic distance induced by a smoothly varying metric learned from data. Concretely, the metric tensor at each point is the inverse of a diagonal, kernel-weighted local covariance; this creates a Riemannian geometry in which geodesics are “attracted” to regions of high sample density. On this learned manifold, the authors adopt the Riemannian normal distribution of Pennec (maximum entropy given mean and covariance), and develop a maximum likelihood estimator for the mean and covariance using block coordinate descent and Monte Carlo estimation of the normalization constant via integration in the tangent space. They further extend to mixtures of LAND components with an EM algorithm. Experiments on synthetic datasets and EEG sleep staging demonstrate that LAND better captures non-ellipsoidal structure than GMMs and avoids the well-known failure modes of intrinsic least-squares estimators (e.g., means drifting into low-density regions).

The work bridges two previously separate lines: (i) Riemannian statistics on known manifolds (e.g., Pennec 2006; Zhang & Fletcher 2013; mixture models on spheres/known manifolds), and (ii) manifold/metric learning (e.g., Isomap; related learned metric ideas in Hauberg et al. 2012, Tosi et al. 2014). It would also benefit from positioning within the broader metric learning literature that learns local metrics or per-point models (e.g., Frome et al., NIPS’06; Malisiewicz & Efros, per-exemplar models; NCA, LMNN), and local-PCA-based metrics.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The construction is principled: a data-adaptive Riemannian metric, maximum-entropy density on that manifold, and ML estimation with gradients that incorporate the normalization term. The tangent-space Monte Carlo for C(µ,Σ) is a sensible choice and avoids expensive log maps. The derivations for gradients and the A-factorization for Σ are appropriate.
- Empirical support: Synthetic and EEG results are encouraging and qualitatively illustrate the benefit of locally adaptive geometry. However, the main-text comparisons are mostly against GMMs; intrinsic least-squares Riemannian estimators appear stronger than GMMs and should be featured prominently in the main paper (not only in the supplement), both qualitatively and quantitatively.
- Computational aspects: The paper acknowledges scalability concerns but does not provide a concrete complexity analysis. Because evaluating m(µ,v) requires Expµ(v), and each geodesic ODE step needs metric and metric derivatives that depend on kernel weights over the dataset, a naïve implementation can be O(S · T · N · D) per iteration (S MC samples, T solver steps). Even with a diagonal metric, this can be costly as N and D grow. Please quantify per-iteration complexity (in N, D, S, K for mixtures), wall-clock times, and practical limits. Also discuss/implement standard accelerations (kNN sparsification of the kernel, kd-tree/ball-tree queries, neighbor caches along geodesics, fast Gauss transforms) and the impact on accuracy. The variance of the Monte Carlo estimator for C and its gradients merits discussion; adaptive S, control variates, or common-random-numbers across iterations could stabilize optimization.
- Hyperparameters: The Gaussian kernel bandwidth σ is crucial. The paper currently fixes σ or selects it by sweep; robustness analyses and guidance are needed. In particular: is σ uniform across the space, or adaptive (e.g., kNN bandwidths) to handle variable density? How sensitive are results and runtimes to σ? Can σ be learned (evidence maximization, cross-validation, gradient-based tuning)?
- Mixtures: The mixture extension is promising; including the proper normalization constants in responsibilities is a notable improvement over prior work that ignored them. It would strengthen the paper to expand mixture experiments: compare against intrinsic LS-based mixtures and an ablation that omits C to show the importance of proper normalization, and report clustering metrics and likelihoods.

Clarity
The paper is very well written, well organized, and a pleasure to read. Explanations of Riemannian preliminaries and the algorithm are clear. A few clarity requests:
- Provide an explicit complexity/runtimes section and define what you consider “high-dimensional” in practice (e.g., D≈5 vs 50 vs 100).
- Specify ODE solver details, step sizes, convergence criteria, and whether Monte Carlo samples are reused between iterations.
- Quantify “smooth enough that geodesics can be computed” and how this interacts with σ and ρ.

Originality
The combination of a learned Riemannian metric with maximum-entropy Riemannian normals, ML estimation with a tractable normalization scheme, and mixture models on the learned manifold is, to my knowledge, novel. While local metric learning and Riemannian normals each exist, the paper’s integration into a generative model that extrapolates beyond the training set is original and interesting.

Significance
If scalable and reasonably robust, LAND could serve as a drop-in replacement for Gaussians in many models (GMMs, LDA, PPCA, Bayes classifiers) when data exhibit curved structures. The empirical results are promising, especially for clustering and modeling curved densities. Broader impact will depend on addressing scalability and hyperparameter selection.

Pros
- Principled, maximum-entropy generalization of Gaussians to learned manifolds; elegant use of tangent-space Monte Carlo.
- Addresses known pitfalls of intrinsic LS estimators; means/covariances behave sensibly in low-density regions.
- Mixture extension is well-motivated and probabilistically consistent.
- Clear writing and solid geometric grounding.

Cons
- Limited complexity analysis and scalability guidance; unclear practical limits in N, D.
- Heavy reliance on σ with limited robustness study; no adaptive bandwidth mechanism.
- Baselines in the main text underrepresent stronger competitors (intrinsic LS estimators).
- Mixture experiments could be expanded; current comparisons against GMMs are somewhat uninformative.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a thorough complexity and runtime study vs N, D, S (and K), plus ablations (neighbor truncation, sample reuse, adaptive S).
- Include intrinsic LS estimators as main-text baselines in all figures/tables; add BIC/AIC and test log-likelihood comparisons.
- Study robustness to σ; consider adaptive/kNN bandwidths and/or learning σ.
- Expand mixture results; show the effect of including normalization constants.
- Position the work within local metric learning (Frome et al. 2006; Malisiewicz & Efros; NCA; LMNN) and local-PCA metrics, clarifying similarities/differences.
- Discuss invariance issues (diagonal, non-rotation-invariant metric) and possible remedies (pre-whitening, local PCA).

Overall assessment
A strong, well-written paper with a principled and promising idea, good theory-to-algorithm path, and encouraging results. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on adding a concrete complexity analysis, stronger baselines in the main text, and a more systematic treatment of σ.
