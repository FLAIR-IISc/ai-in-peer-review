This paper proposes a locally adaptive normal distribution (LAND) that replaces the Euclidean distance in the ordinary normal distribution with a locally adaptive nonlinear distance measure. The LAND is a generalization of the normal distribution to the "manifold" setting, where data is assumed to lie near a potentially low-dimensional manifold embedded in R^D. The authors develop a maximum likelihood algorithm to infer the distribution parameters that relies on a combination of gradient descent and Monte Carlo integration.

The paper is well-motivated, and the idea of using a geodesic distance (akin to Isomap) is interesting and promising. The authors provide a clear and concise introduction to Riemannian geometry and its application to manifold learning. The proposed LAND model is a natural extension of the normal distribution to the manifold setting, and the authors provide a thorough derivation of the model and its properties.

However, there are some concerns regarding the clarity and justification of certain aspects of the paper. For example, the definition of the inverse metric tensor in Eq. (7) appears ad hoc and needs more justification. The authors should provide more intuition on why this particular definition is chosen and how it relates to the underlying manifold structure. Additionally, the use of a Gaussian kernel to weight local covariances in the weighted sum is not well-motivated, and the authors should provide more explanation on this choice.

There is also some confusion between the local covariance defined in Eq. (7) and the MAP estimation of that quantity in Eq. (11). It is unclear if they refer to the same thing, and the authors should clarify this point. Furthermore, the experimental evaluation is weak and insufficiently thorough. The authors only provide a limited number of experiments on synthetic and EEG data, and the results are not comprehensive enough to fully evaluate the performance of the LAND model.

A major concern is the scalability and computational efficiency of the proposed algorithm. The authors do not provide any complexity or runtime comparison to standard EM, which makes it difficult to assess the practical applicability of the LAND model. The authors should provide more details on the computational complexity of the algorithm and its scalability to high-dimensional data.

Overall, the paper provides an interesting theoretical contribution, but its practical applicability is questionable. The authors should address the concerns mentioned above and provide more comprehensive experimental results to fully evaluate the performance of the LAND model.
