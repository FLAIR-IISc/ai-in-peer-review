The paper "A Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution" proposes a novel approach to modeling data that lies near a nonlinear manifold embedded in a high-dimensional space. By replacing the traditional Euclidean distance with a smoothly varying Riemannian metric, the authors develop a locally adaptive normal distribution (LAND) that can capture the underlying structure of the data.

The authors provide a thorough introduction to the concepts of Riemannian manifolds and geodesics, making the paper accessible to readers familiar with the subject. The mathematical derivations are sound and well-explained, and the use of exponential and logarithm maps to compute geodesic distances is a clever approach.

The authors also propose a maximum likelihood estimation scheme for the LAND, which combines a gradient-based optimization with a scalable Monte Carlo integration method. The steepest-descent algorithm used to estimate the parameters is well-explained, and the Monte Carlo approximation to estimate the intractable normalization constant is a reasonable approach.

The evaluation of the LAND on synthetic manifold data and a clustering task is thorough, and the comparison to standard Gaussian mixture models is informative. However, there are some concerns regarding the evaluation. Firstly, the choice of the sigma parameter appears unclear and may be tuned on training data, which could lead to overfitting. Secondly, the practical usefulness of the learned distribution is not thoroughly analyzed or explained. Finally, the evaluation could benefit from held-out test results and comparisons when data are generated by standard Gaussians/GMMs.

Additionally, there are some concerns regarding the novelty of the contribution. While the paper combines prior work on kernel metrics, geodesic estimation, and normal distributions in a creative way, it is unclear whether the contribution is significant enough to warrant publication. Furthermore, the scalability of the approach is not thoroughly evaluated, and the assumption that the manifold dimension equals the ambient dimension D (full-rank M(x)) is unusual.

In terms of clarity, there are some issues with notation, particularly around the use of Sigma vs M = Sigma^-1 and the role of the local kernel matrix versus a fitted covariance. Additionally, the cluster membership assignment is not explicitly stated, which could lead to confusion.

Overall, the paper is interesting and useful, and the introduction to Riemannian manifolds is well-explained. However, the concerns regarding evaluation, novelty, and scalability need to be addressed before publication.

Arguments pro acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel approach to modeling data that lies near a nonlinear manifold embedded in a high-dimensional space.
* The mathematical derivations are sound and well-explained.
* The use of exponential and logarithm maps to compute geodesic distances is a clever approach.
* The evaluation on synthetic manifold data and a clustering task is thorough.

Arguments con acceptance:

* The choice of the sigma parameter appears unclear and may be tuned on training data, which could lead to overfitting.
* The practical usefulness of the learned distribution is not thoroughly analyzed or explained.
* The evaluation could benefit from held-out test results and comparisons when data are generated by standard Gaussians/GMMs.
* The novelty of the contribution is unclear, and the scalability of the approach is not thoroughly evaluated.

Recommendations for improvement:

* Provide a clear explanation of the choice of the sigma parameter and its potential impact on overfitting.
* Analyze and explain the practical usefulness of the learned distribution.
* Include held-out test results and comparisons when data are generated by standard Gaussians/GMMs.
* Evaluate the scalability of the approach on high-dimensional data.
* Clarify the notation around Sigma vs M = Sigma^-1 and the role of the local kernel matrix versus a fitted covariance.
* Explicitly state the cluster membership assignment.
