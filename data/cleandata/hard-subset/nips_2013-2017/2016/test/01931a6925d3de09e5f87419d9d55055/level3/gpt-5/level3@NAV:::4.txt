This paper proposes the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), obtained by replacing the Euclidean metric in a Gaussian with a smoothly varying Riemannian metric learned from data. The authors define the metric via local diagonal covariances (Gaussian-kernel smoothed), use the induced geodesic distance to instantiate the Riemannian normal pM(x|µ,Σ), and develop an MLE procedure that estimates parameters by block coordinate descent. A key technical device is estimating the normalization constant by Monte Carlo in the tangent space, avoiding expensive boundary-value logarithm maps. The approach is extended to mixtures with an EM-like algorithm. Experiments on synthetic data and EEG sleep staging show clear gains over GMMs and over least-squares (intrinsic) estimators. The work connects statistics on manifolds (Pennec) with manifold learning (e.g., Isomap) and relates to prior NIPS work on probabilistic geodesic analysis (Zhang & Fletcher) and learned probabilistic geometries (Tosi et al.).

Quality: The theoretical development is sound, with clear gradients for µ and Σ−1 factorization and a sensible Monte Carlo estimator for the partition function. The nonconvexity and dependence on geodesic numerics are acknowledged. Empirically, the model captures curved clusters and improves likelihoods and clustering F-measures. However, analysis of estimator variance/bias vs. number of Monte Carlo samples S, computational complexity and wall-clock scaling with N, D, and S, and convergence behavior of the EM extension are limited.

Clarity: The paper is well written and organized. Figures effectively illustrate geodesics and contour behavior. A key clarity issue is positioning: differences to state of the art, especially Simo‑Serra et al. [19], are not explained early, and [19] is missing from the introduction despite being central to mixtures of Riemannian normals.

Originality: The main novelty is combining learned Riemannian metrics with MLE for the Riemannian normal (including mixture modeling with proper normalization). Relative to [19] and prior Riemannian statistics, the contribution feels incremental; the paper would benefit from a sharper articulation of what is genuinely new beyond “learned vs. known manifold” and the normalization treatment.

Significance: If robust and scalable, LAND could be a useful drop-in for manifold-aware generative modeling. The EEG application is interesting, though additional baselines (e.g., graph-geodesic GMMs, alternative manifold densities) and sensitivity to σ would strengthen claims.

Pros:
- Solid theory and careful algorithmic design
- Clear empirical gains on curved data and EEG
- Useful link between manifold learning and generative modeling

Cons:
- Novelty vs. [19] not clearly established; missing citation in intro
- Limited analysis of computational cost, scalability, and Monte Carlo accuracy
- Diagonal, non–affine-invariant metric choice not justified or ablated
