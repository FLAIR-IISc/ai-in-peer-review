Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), a parametric distribution that replaces the Euclidean/Mahalanobis distance in a Gaussian with a geodesic distance induced by a data-adaptive Riemannian metric. The metric is learned nonparametrically from data via a smoothly varying local covariance (Eq. 7), and the resulting Riemannian normal distribution (Pennec [15]) is used as the base model; mixtures of LANDs are also considered with an EM-like algorithm. Because the normalization constant depends on the mean, covariance, and curvature, it is estimated by Monte Carlo integration in the tangent space, leveraging exponential maps and avoiding expensive boundary-value problems for logarithm maps when possible. The paper targets generative modeling of data that lies near nonlinear manifolds, showcasing synthetic examples and clustering of sleep-stage EEG features.

Relative to prior work, Riemannian normal distributions and PCA on known manifolds have been studied (e.g., Pennec [15]; Zhang & Fletcher [23]); mixtures on known manifolds have also appeared (Simo-Serra et al. [19]; Straub et al. [20]). The novelty here is to learn a smoothly varying metric from data (cf. manifold learning and metric learning: Isomap [21], Tosi et al. [22], Hauberg et al. [9]) and then perform likelihood-based inference for a Riemannian normal with that learned metric, including normalization and gradients. This bridges manifold learning and Riemannian statistics in a generative setting.

Quality:
- Modeling and algorithms: The overall construction is sound in spirit and aligns with Riemannian statistics. The tangent-space Monte Carlo estimator for the normalization constant is reasonable, though its variance and sample complexity are not analyzed; practical guidance beyond S=3000 would be helpful (e.g., variance estimates, control variates, importance sampling; reuse of samples across iterations). Gradients for µ and Σ (via Σ−1=AᵀA) are derived and plausible; the use of steepest descent for µ to improve conditioning is sensible.
- Metric construction: The inverse metric in Eq. (7) (diagonal local covariance with Gaussian kernel weights) feels ad hoc and is neither affine-invariant nor rotationally invariant. The paper acknowledges lack of invariance but does not justify why this particular choice is preferable or how sensitive results are to σ and ρ. Please provide intuition for the Gaussian kernel weighting (e.g., as kernel smoothing of local second moments) and discuss alternatives (full covariances with shrinkage; k-NN kernels; adaptive bandwidths; mixtures of local metrics [9]; probabilistic metrics [22]).
- Notational/semantic clarity: There is a potential confusion between (i) the local covariance defining the metric M(x) (Eq. 7) and (ii) the global Σ parameter of the LAND that appears in the likelihood (Eq. 11). They are distinct quantities with different roles, but the manuscript occasionally blurs this distinction. It would help to change notation (e.g., denote the metric by G(x) with entries gdd(x)) and add a paragraph explicitly contrasting “geometric metric vs. model covariance.”
- Computational aspects: The method likely requires many expensive operations per iteration, including computing Logµ(xn) for all data points (BVPs) and S evaluations of Expµ(v) per normalization estimate (IVPs), each with O(N) kernel evaluations for M(x) unless sparsified. There is no complexity analysis or runtime/memory comparison to standard GMM EM. This is a major omission. Please quantify costs as functions of N, D, K, S, ODE steps, and neighborhood size; report wall-clock times and speedups from practical tricks (k-NN truncation, KD-trees, precomputation/caching of neighborhoods, sparse kernels, batching, GPU).
- Experiments: The synthetic demonstrations are illustrative, and the EEG example is interesting, but overall evaluation is limited. There is no held-out likelihood comparison, no runtime comparison, no ablations (σ, ρ, S), no sensitivity to initialization, and few baselines. Stronger baselines (e.g., mixtures of factor analyzers, flows [contemporary works], GMMs in learned embeddings like Isomap/LLE, or Riemannian models on known manifolds when applicable) would better establish empirical value.

Clarity:
The paper is generally readable and well organized, with clear geometric background. However:
- Clarify the roles and notations of the metric vs. the model covariance (see above).
- Provide more intuition for Eq. (7): why Gaussian kernels, why diagonal only, and how to choose σ and ρ. The current guidance (“as small as possible while geodesics are computable”) risks hidden tuning and instability.
- Algorithm 1: minor typo — use ∇ instead of d in lines 4–5 for consistency with the text.
- Please bring essential EM details from the supplement into the main paper or at least summarize the E/M-step forms and normalization handling across components.

Originality:
Moderate to high. Using a learned Riemannian metric to define and train a Riemannian normal (with proper normalization) and extending to mixtures appears novel relative to prior work that assumes known manifolds. The algorithmic choice of tangent-space Monte Carlo for normalization in this setting is also a useful idea.

Significance:
Conceptually significant: this is a clean, plausible way to port Gaussian-based models to data with nonlinear geometry. Practical significance is less clear without stronger empirical evidence and scalability analyses. As presented, applicability to higher dimensions and larger datasets is questionable.

Pros:
- Conceptually elegant bridge between manifold learning and Riemannian statistics.
- Proper probabilistic treatment (normalization, gradients) rather than heuristic use of geodesic distances.
- Insightful observation that ML estimates avoid low-density regions better than intrinsic LS means.
- Mixture extension and EM-like algorithm.

Cons:
- Ad hoc and non-invariant metric construction with limited justification; only diagonal local covariances.
- Insufficient experiments: few datasets, limited baselines, no runtime/complexity, minimal ablations.
- Scalability concerns: heavy use of Log/Exp maps and kernel evaluations; no quantitative profiling.
- Hyperparameter σ selection is unclear; per-subject tuning on EEG risks overfitting.
- Minor clarity issues and notation confusion; typo in Algorithm 1.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide a complexity analysis and wall-clock runtime comparisons against GMM EM and other baselines; include D and N scaling experiments. Report the number of ODE solves per iteration and practical strategies (sparse neighborhoods, caching, batching).
- Add ablations: sensitivity to σ, ρ, S; study estimator variance for C(µ,Σ) and explore variance reduction (e.g., control variates using Taylor expansion of m(µ,v) near v=0, importance sampling).
- Evaluate on more and larger datasets, with held-out likelihoods and clustering metrics; compare to stronger baselines (MFA, GMMs in manifold embeddings, Riemannian mixtures on known manifolds where possible).
- Justify Eq. (7) more thoroughly; consider k-NN kernels or adaptive bandwidths; at least show that k-NN truncation preserves performance and improves speed. Discuss (or try) full local covariances with shrinkage for invariance, or pre-whitening to mitigate axis-alignment issues.
- Clarify the relationship between the learned metric and the model covariance; adjust notation to avoid confusion. Briefly discuss the “maximum entropy under a data-dependent metric” claim and its implications.
- Consider learning σ by maximizing the (approximate) likelihood, potentially with adjoint sensitivity for ODEs or a simple bilevel search with validation.

Overall:
An interesting and potentially impactful idea with a solid geometric foundation, but the current empirical evaluation and scalability analysis are not sufficient to judge practical utility. Strengthening experiments, clarifying/justifying the metric construction, and adding computational analysis would significantly improve the paper.
