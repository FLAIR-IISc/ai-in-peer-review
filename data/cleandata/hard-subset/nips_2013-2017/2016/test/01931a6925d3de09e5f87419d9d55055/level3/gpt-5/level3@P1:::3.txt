This paper proposes the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), a parametric density obtained by replacing the Euclidean metric in a Gaussian with a smoothly varying, data-adaptive Riemannian metric. The model defines pM(x | µ, Σ) ∝ exp(−1/2 ⟨Logµ(x), Σ−1 Logµ(x)⟩), where distances and volume are measured with respect to a learned metric. The metric is constructed nonparametrically via a local (diagonal) covariance estimated with Gaussian kernel weights (Eq. 7), so geodesics are attracted to regions of high sample density. The normalization constant C(µ, Σ), which depends on both µ and Σ and on the metric/curvature, is estimated by Monte Carlo integration over the tangent space using exponential maps, which is computationally cheaper than integrating directly on the manifold. Maximum likelihood estimation alternates between updating µ (via steepest descent using exponential maps) and Σ (via a Cholesky-like factor A with Σ−1 = AᵀA). A mixture of LANDs and an EM algorithm are also presented. Experiments on synthetic manifold-like data and EEG-based sleep-stage features show improved likelihood and clustering alignment compared to GMMs and to least-squares intrinsic estimators.

Relation to prior work: The Riemannian normal is known (Pennec, 2006), as are mixtures on known manifolds (Simo-Serra et al., 2012; Straub et al., 2015) and Riemannian PCA (Zhang & Fletcher, NIPS’13). The novelty here is to operate in a “manifold learning” setting: construct a data-adaptive Riemannian metric (akin to Hauberg et al., NIPS’12; Tosi et al., UAI’14) and build a properly normalized, maximum-entropy (given mean and covariance on the learned manifold) density for generative modeling. The paper also connects to classical manifold learning (Isomap, LLE, Laplacian Eigenmaps) by adopting geodesic distances but retains a generative, continuous model.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The construction of C(µ, Σ) as an expectation with respect to N(0, Σ) over the tangent space is correct and well-motivated; using Exp for sampling avoids expensive Log solves. The gradients for µ and A are sensible (steepest descent for µ mitigates ill conditioning). The EM extension accounts for different normalization constants across components, an important point often overlooked.
- Key concerns: (i) The metric is not learned in a statistical sense; it is a hand-crafted, kernel-smoothed, diagonal inverse covariance with two hyperparameters (σ, ρ) set empirically. Claims and the title should reflect this. (ii) The metric (Eq. 7) is not coordinate-invariant; rotating or rescaling features changes the model, and only diagonal structure is retained. This design choice needs more justification, discussion of invariance, and ideally ablations comparing diagonal versus full or structured metrics with shrinkage. (iii) Sensitivity to σ is likely substantial; the paper provides limited intuition/analysis on how σ affects geodesics, C(µ, Σ), and fitted µ/Σ, and gives no principled selection strategy. (iv) Experimental comparisons stack the deck by using few LAND components versus many GMM components; model selection (e.g., BIC/AIC or held-out likelihood) should be used per method to choose K fairly. (v) Computational aspects: the approach involves thousands of Exp solves per iteration and per component. There is no quantitative runtime or scaling study (e.g., variance of the estimator for C, effect of S, solver tolerances, memory, dimension limits), making it hard to assess practicality beyond small D.

Clarity:
- The exposition of Riemannian preliminaries and the LAND objective is clear. The role of C(µ, Σ) in pulling µ toward high-density regions is well explained.
- Missing details: selection of σ and ρ; numerical ODE solver settings and step-size strategies; MC variance reduction; convergence diagnostics; sensitivity plots (σ, S, diagonal vs full metric). The title overstates “learning a metric.” Please either rename or explicitly caveat early on.

Originality:
- Moderate. Using a learned (data-adaptive) Riemannian metric to define a properly normalized, maximum-entropy Gaussian family and to derive a workable MLE/EM procedure is a neat and nontrivial combination of ideas. Prior work considered known manifolds; moving to learned metrics expands applicability.

Significance:
- Potentially useful as a plug-in replacement for Gaussians in generative models and clustering when data lie near nonlinear manifolds. However, current empirical evidence is limited (small synthetic setups and a single EEG feature pipeline), and practicality in higher dimensions is unclear. The lack of principled hyperparameter learning and the coordinate dependence limit robustness.

Specific suggestions to strengthen the paper:
- Be precise about the metric: it is constructed, not learned by likelihood. Add discussion and experiments on invariance: show what happens under feature rotations/rescalings; consider pre-whitening or learning a low-rank/full metric with shrinkage (e.g., diagonal + low-rank).
- Learn σ (and ρ) rather than fixing them: (i) cross-validated held-out log-likelihood; (ii) bilevel hyperparameter optimization with hypergradients through the MLE; (iii) a hierarchical prior on σ with EM or variational updates. Provide sensitivity curves and best practices.
- Fairer comparisons: choose K per method with BIC/AIC or a validation set; report held-out log-likelihood. Include baselines such as GMMs in embeddings (Isomap/LLE/LE), kernel density estimation, and mixtures on known manifolds where appropriate. Demonstrate that accounting for C(µ, Σ) matters by comparing to a version that omits it.
- Overfitting analysis: vary K and document train vs test likelihood; show LAND’s flexibility can overfit and how regularization (priors on Σ, constraints on σ) mitigates this. After rebuttal, I remain concerned that LAND’s flexibility (metric plus mixture) can overfit; revising experiments with model selection is important.
- Compute/scaling: report wall-clock times, number of ODE solves per iteration, variance of the MC estimate of C, effect of S, and performance in higher D. Explore variance reduction (common random numbers, control variates) and caching of Exp evaluations.
- Theoretical clarifications: precisely state the scope of the maximum-entropy claim (it is conditional on the chosen metric); discuss identifiability of (µ, Σ) under different σ; relate to information geometry and probabilistic geometry more extensively.

Pros:
- Principled, properly normalized density on a learned manifold with intuitive behavior (mean avoids low-density regions).
- Clear algorithmic contributions (tangent-space MC, gradients, EM), potentially reusable.
- Demonstrated gains on synthetic manifolds and an EEG clustering task.

Cons:
- Metric is hand-crafted and coordinate-dependent; hyperparameters not learned and likely sensitive.
- Some claims/title overstate “learning the metric.”
- Comparisons do not use model selection; potential overfitting and scalability are not characterized.
- Limited empirical breadth and missing stronger baselines.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The idea is appealing and potentially impactful, but the current version needs a more rigorous and fair experimental protocol, a candid treatment of the metric’s invariance and hyperparameter sensitivity, and basic compute/scaling evidence. With these revisions, I would be inclined to support acceptance.
