- Introduces the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), replacing Mahalanobis/Euclidean distance with geodesic distance from a learned, data-dependent Riemannian metric constructed from weighted local covariances.  
- Proposes a full statistical framework: tackles intractable normalization constant via Monte Carlo integration on the tangent space and derives MLE with block coordinate descent.  
- Extends the model to a mixture of LANDs as an alternative to GMMs for clustering and density estimation on non-linear data.  
- Theoretical development is sound; gradient derivations and MLE appear correct and the tangent-space Monte Carlo is a practical solution.  
- Experimental evaluation is thorough, with synthetic illustrations and a challenging EEG dataset supporting the claims.  
- Paper is clearly written and well-organized; background and figures effectively convey the method and its advantages over GMMs.  
- Major clarity weakness: fails to position and discuss closely related work by Simo-Serra et al. [19] early, creating a misleading impression of novelty.  
- Original contribution: learning the Riemannian metric from data and applying a Riemannian normal in the manifold-learning setting (vs prior work on known manifolds).  
- Key distinction from [19]: this work learns the manifold geometry and properly handles the normalization constant, making a principled probabilistic model.  
- Significance: addresses fundamental limits of Gaussians/GMMs on non-linear data and shows practical promise (EEG); could inspire manifold-aware variants of classical models.  
- Main practical drawback: computational cost, particularly solving boundary value problems for the log map, may limit large-scale applicability.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, technically sound, principled MLE, strong experiments, and clear writing linking Riemannian statistics and manifold learning.  
- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient early comparison to [19] weakening novelty claims and high computational cost.  
- Recommendation: Strong accept contingent on revising the introduction to properly contextualize and highlight novel contributions relative to Simo-Serra et al. [19].
