- Proposes Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND): a curved normal using a Riemannian metric to model observation spaces.  
- Replaces Euclidean distance with a locally adaptive nonlinear distance learned from data via Riemannian geometry.  
- LAND is presented as a natural generalization of the Gaussian on manifolds and the maximum-entropy distribution under the metric.  
- Paper is well-written with clear, concise mathematical exposition and balanced theory–practice coverage.  
- Experiments (synthetic and real) are well-designed and show LAND effectively captures data manifolds.  
- LAND offers a powerful parametric model for complex data distributions with promising empirical results.  
- Major limitation: metric is hand-crafted and relies on empirical hyperparameters rather than being learned.  
- Lacks discussion on the intuitive effect of the hyperparameter sigma and guidance for selecting it.  
- Recommendation to merge metric learning with LAND learning so hyperparameters can be learned adaptively.  
- Flawed experimental comparison to GMM: number of components should be chosen via model selection (e.g., BIC) for fairness.  
- Authors should demonstrate overfitting risk with many components and report quantitative computational cost and scalability.  
- Related-work review should more comprehensively cover metric learning and manifold learning tied to Riemannian geometry.  
- Overall: technically sound, original approach with potential significance despite noted limitations.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novelty, clear exposition, solid experiments, and potential impact.  
- Arguments against acceptance: hand-crafted metric/hyperparameters, missing sigma intuition, flawed GMM comparison, and lack of overfitting/scalability analysis.
