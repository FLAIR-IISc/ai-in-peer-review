This paper introduces the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), an elegant generalization of the multivariate normal distribution to non-linear data manifolds. The core idea is to define a parametric distribution over a non-parametric space by first learning a data-driven Riemannian manifold. On this manifold, defined by local inverse covariance metrics, LAND is formulated as a Riemannian normal distribution (Pennec [15]) where distances are geodesics. The authors derive a maximum likelihood estimation procedure and extend the framework to mixtures of LANDs. This work creatively bridges manifold learning and Riemannian statistics, offering a generative model that respects the data's intrinsic geometry.

The paper's strengths are significant. **(Originality)** The primary contribution—a parametric model on a non-parametrically learned space—is novel and conceptually appealing. **(Significance)** The extension to mixtures of LANDs is particularly promising for clustering non-linear data structures, demonstrating a clear practical application. **(Clarity)** The paper is exceptionally well-written, making complex geometric concepts clear and accessible.

However, several weaknesses need addressing. **(Quality/Scalability)** The main concern is computational complexity. The per-point metric construction and geodesic computation appear computationally intensive, potentially limiting applicability to large-scale, high-dimensional data. The paper needs a formal complexity analysis and clarification on the practical limits of data dimensionality (e.g., is D=10 or D=100 considered high-dimensional?).

**(Positioning)** The related work section could be strengthened. The authors should better position LAND relative to prior local metric learning methods that also use per-point diagonal metrics (e.g., Frome et al., Malisiewicz & Efros) or local PCA to clarify its unique contributions.

**(Experimental Rigor)** The experimental validation requires improvement. First, the choice of the Gaussian kernel bandwidth `sigma` is critical but lacks justification; a sensitivity analysis is needed to assess robustness. Second, the visual comparisons in the main paper are primarily against GMMs, a weak baseline for this problem. These visualizations would be far more compelling if compared against the more relevant intrinsic manifold estimators mentioned in the text and supplement. Including a runtime comparison against these methods would also provide valuable guidance for practitioners on when to choose LAND.

In summary, LAND is an interesting and well-presented idea. However, its practical viability is questionable without a clearer analysis of its computational cost and more rigorous experimental comparisons. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the submission.
