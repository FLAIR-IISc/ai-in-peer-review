## Review of "A Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution"

This paper proposes a Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND) as a generalization of the standard Gaussian, motivated by the limitations of the Euclidean metric when modeling data residing on nonlinear manifolds. The core idea is to replace the standard Mahalanobis distance with a locally adaptive Riemannian metric learned from the data itself, resulting in a distribution that better conforms to the underlying manifold structure. The authors develop a Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE) algorithm, including an EM algorithm for mixtures of LANDs, and demonstrate its effectiveness on both synthetic and real-world EEG datasets. 

The paper is exceptionally well-written and theoretically sound. The derivation of the LAND, grounded in Riemannian manifold theory, is clear and rigorous. The proposed MLE estimator, leveraging Monte Carlo integration for normalization, appears practical and scalable. The experimental validation, using both synthetic data to demonstrate the method's ability to capture complex distributions and real EEG data to showcase its applicability, is convincing. The results consistently outperform standard Gaussian Mixture Models, highlighting the benefit of adapting to the data's intrinsic geometry.

However, the paper’s novelty, while present, feels incremental. The authors acknowledge the related work of Simo-Serra et al. [19] but downplay its significance, failing to adequately address the differences early in the paper. Simo-Serra et al. also work with Riemannian normal distributions in mixture models, and a clearer explanation of how the LAND approach differs – particularly concerning the learning of the metric versus assuming a known manifold – is crucial. The introduction would benefit from explicitly referencing and contrasting with this work. Furthermore, while the authors mention the connection to manifold learning, the discussion lacks a deeper engagement with existing techniques in that field, particularly regarding the choice of the Gaussian kernel for defining locality.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical foundation and rigorous derivation.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Convincing experimental results demonstrating improved performance over standard GMMs.
*   Practical and scalable MLE estimation procedure.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited novelty compared to existing work, specifically Simo-Serra et al. [19].
*   Insufficiently clear explanation of the differences from state-of-the-art early in the paper.
*   Missing reference to Simo-Serra et al. [19] in the introduction.



**Overall:** This is a solid contribution that presents a well-developed and practical extension to the standard Gaussian distribution. However, the lack of a more thorough discussion of related work, particularly Simo-Serra et al. [19], weakens the paper’s impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing these points. The authors should explicitly acknowledge and contrast their work with Simo-Serra et al. in the introduction and throughout the paper, clarifying the specific advantages of their approach.
