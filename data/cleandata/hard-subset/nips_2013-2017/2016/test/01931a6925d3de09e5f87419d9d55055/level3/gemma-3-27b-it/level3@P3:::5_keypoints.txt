- Proposes LAND: replace Euclidean metric with a learned Riemannian metric, use geodesic distance, extend to mixtures, evaluate on synthetic and EEG data.  
- Well-motivated and clearly articulated with appropriate links to Riemannian geometry and manifold learning literature.  
- Authors show good theoretical understanding and attempt to bridge statistical modeling on manifolds with practical manifold learning.  
- Technical soundness concern: the inverse metric tensor definition in Eq. (7) appears ad hoc.  
- Regularization term ρ is introduced without strong justification; a principled method to ensure positive definiteness is needed.  
- Insufficient rationale for using a Gaussian kernel to weight local covariances; alternatives and impacts not discussed.  
- Confusion between local covariance in Eq. (7) and MAP estimation in Eq. (11); unclear if they represent the same quantity or different stages.  
- Synthetic experiments show some improvement over GMM but lack comparisons to more advanced manifold-learning methods.  
- EEG experiments are application-driven but lack justification for feature extraction and parameter tuning (notably σ).  
- Selection of σ is ad hoc, undermining confidence in experimental results.  
- No discussion of computational complexity or runtime; scalability and efficiency are not analyzed.  
- Costs of geodesic distance computation and Monte Carlo integration are not evaluated and may be prohibitive in high dimensions.  
- Overall: interesting theoretical contribution but practical applicability is questionable without stronger justification and validation.  
- Pros for acceptance: novel idea, clear motivation, demonstrated potential improvement over GMM, real-world EEG application.  
- Cons for acceptance: ad hoc metric definition, unjustified kernel choice, covariance-definition confusion, weak experiments, no complexity analysis, limited novelty.  
- Minor issue: Algorithm 1 has a typo—‘d’ should be ‘∇’.  
- Recommendation: reject but encourage revisions—provide stronger theoretical justification, more comprehensive experiments, and thorough complexity analysis.
