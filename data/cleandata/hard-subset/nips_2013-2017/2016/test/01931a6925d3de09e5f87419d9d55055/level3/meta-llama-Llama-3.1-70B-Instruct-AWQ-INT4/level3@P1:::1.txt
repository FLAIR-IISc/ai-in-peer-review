This paper proposes a Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), a parametric model that leverages a non-parametric space to link metric learning and manifold learning with Riemannian statistics. The authors model the metric as a Riemannian normal distribution and learn the mean and covariance via maximum likelihood. The distances are defined as geodesics on the learned Riemannian manifold, using local metrics from inverse diagonal covariances.

The paper is well-written and pleasant to read, making it easy to understand the concepts and ideas presented. The authors provide a clear and concise introduction to Riemannian geometry and its application to manifold learning. The development of the LAND model is well-motivated, and the use of local metrics and geodesics is a natural extension of the Riemannian normal distribution.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to connect different areas of research, including metric learning, manifold learning, and Riemannian statistics. The authors provide a clear overview of the related work and position their contribution within the broader context of the field.

The experimental results are promising, and the authors demonstrate the effectiveness of the LAND model on both synthetic and real data. The comparison with Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) is informative, but it would be beneficial to include comparisons with other intrinsic estimators, as suggested by the reviewer. Additionally, the authors could provide more details on the computational complexity of the LAND model and compare it to other methods.

The use of a Gaussian kernel bandwidth (sigma) to define locality is an important aspect of the LAND model. However, the reviewer raises concerns about the choice and influence of sigma, and the authors should provide more robustness tests and analysis of the impact of sigma on the results.

Overall, the paper presents a novel and interesting approach to modeling data distributions on manifolds. The LAND model has the potential to be a useful tool in various applications, and the authors' contribution is a significant step forward in the field of Riemannian statistics.
