- Introduces LAND: a Riemannian normal distribution replacing Euclidean metric with a smoothly varying, data-adaptive Riemannian metric and density p_M(x|μ,Σ) ∝ exp(−1/2 ⟨Log_μ(x), Σ⁻¹ Log_μ(x)⟩) normalized by the manifold measure.

- Learns the metric nonparametrically via local (diagonal) covariances smoothed by a Gaussian kernel so geodesics follow high-density regions.

- Provides a practical MLE: gradient-descent estimation of μ and Σ, tangent-space Monte Carlo to compute normalization constant C(μ,Σ), and an EM extension for mixtures with per-component normalization.

- Experiments on synthetic data and EEG sleep features show LAND captures non-ellipsoidal structure better than GMMs and avoids low-density mean issues of intrinsic least-squares estimators.

- Technical soundness: principled maximum-entropy formulation, correct accounting for manifold measure, derived gradients, and block coordinate descent implementation.

- Empirical support: superior likelihood with fewer components on synthetic tasks and improved F-measure on EEG with moderate σ tuning.

- Evaluation limitations: missing runtime/complexity analysis and ablations (sensitivity to kernel bandwidth σ and number of Monte Carlo samples S).

- Clarity: well-written and organized; geometric preliminaries and tangent-space Monte Carlo explained clearly; suggests moving concise EM pseudocode to main text and expanding practical guidance on σ and regularization ρ.

- Originality: novel use of a learned metric to define a parametric Riemannian normal and tractable MLE/EM, distinct from prior work that assumes known manifolds.

- Significance: a promising drop-in replacement for Gaussian components on nonlinear data with potential clustering and density-estimation utility; impact hinges on scalability and hyperparameter robustness.

- Scalability concern: metric evaluation sums over all points and normalization requires many geodesic solves — needs complexity/wall-clock reporting and possible approximations (sparse neighborhoods, kd-trees, Nyström, minibatching).

- Hyperparameter sensitivity: performance depends on σ and currently requires per-subject tuning; recommend data-driven selection (cross-validation, marginal likelihood, joint learning) or multi-scale/mixture-of-metrics.

- Metric expressiveness: diagonal local covariances sacrifice invariance and ignore local correlations; suggest whitening, local PCA alignment, or learning full/low-rank local covariances with regularization.

- Optimization issues: nonconvex objective with sensitivity to initialization and Monte Carlo variance; recommend discussing convergence, initialization heuristics, and variance-reduction techniques (control variates, antithetic sampling).

- Baseline comparisons: limited to GMM and LS-based Riemannian estimators; should include stronger nonlinear baselines (mixtures of factor analyzers, variable-bandwidth KDE, warped mixtures, normalizing flows) and more datasets with error bars and sensitivity analyses.

- Arguments for acceptance: clear, principled geometric formulation with correct normalization, practical MLE/EM and tangent-space Monte Carlo, demonstrated empirical gains and a meaningful EEG application, and a conceptual bridge between manifold learning and Riemannian statistics.

- Arguments against acceptance: limited empirical breadth, lack of scalability/runtime analysis, sensitivity to σ, restrictive diagonal metric, and missing comparisons to stronger nonlinear density models.

- Overall assessment: novel and useful contribution with solid writing; would be much stronger with broader empirical evaluation and automated metric/σ selection — reviewer leans positive.
