This paper introduces the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), which replaces the Euclidean Mahalanobis distance in a Gaussian with a geodesic distance induced by a smoothly varying, data-dependent Riemannian metric M(x). The metric is learned nonparametrically via kernel-smoothed, diagonal local covariances; geodesic operations (Exp, Log) are computed by integrating an ODE, enabling distances and tangent-space calculations. The resulting density is the Riemannian normal pM(x | µ, Σ) ∝ exp(−1/2 ⟨Logµ(x), Σ−1 Logµ(x)⟩) with respect to the manifold measure. Because the normalization constant C(µ, Σ) is intractable on a learned manifold, the authors derive a Monte Carlo estimator by integrating in the tangent space at µ: C = Z · EN(0,Σ)[m(µ, v)], where m(µ, v) = |M(Expµ(v))|1/2 and Z is the Euclidean Gaussian normalizer. They propose maximum likelihood estimation for µ, Σ using gradient-based updates: steepest descent in µ (to mitigate conditioning) and optimizing the precision factor A with Σ−1 = AᵀA, with gradients that include both data-fit and normalization terms. They extend to mixtures, giving an EM scheme with responsibilities that properly include C(µk, Σk). Experiments on synthetic manifold data and EEG-based sleep-stage clustering show that LAND better captures nonlinear structure than GMMs and least-squares Riemannian estimators, and that the ML estimator avoids low-density means.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on Riemannian statistics (e.g., Pennec’s Riemannian normal and intrinsic mean/covariance), manifold learning (Isomap and related), and mixtures on known manifolds. Its main novelty is to deploy a learned, nonparametric Riemannian metric inside a parametric maximum-entropy normal model; to derive a scalable tangent-space Monte Carlo normalization; and to provide ML/EM algorithms in this learned-metric setting. This bridges known manifold statistics with data-driven metrics in a probabilistic, generative way.

Quality: The formulation is technically sound. Using tangent-space MC avoids expensive BVPs for Log, and the gradients for µ and A (Σ) are coherent. The observation that log-normalization penalizes low-density means is well explained and is borne out in experiments. However:
- The choice of kernel bandwidth σ is critical and appears ad hoc. In EEG, selecting the best σ over a grid per subject risks overfitting; the effect on generalization is not quantified.
- There is no held-out test likelihood; model selection and performance should be reported on separate validation/test splits.
- Scalability is not analyzed. Each C(µ, Σ) estimate requires S IVPs for Expµ(v); per-iteration cost is O(N) for data terms plus O(S) ODE solves per component. Empirical runtime vs N, D, S and solver tolerances should be reported.
- The assumption M(x) is full-rank in RD implies the “manifold” is the ambient space endowed with a metric, not a low-dimensional submanifold. This is reasonable but should be emphasized, along with consequences for high-D settings.

Clarity: The paper is generally clear for readers familiar with Riemannian geometry. Two points could be improved:
- Notation: keep M(x) (metric) and Σ (global covariance) clearly separated; avoid overloading “covariance” for both the local kernel-smoothed diagonal (defining M) and the model parameter Σ. Consider denoting the precision Λ = Σ−1 to reduce confusion in gradients.
- Mixture details: explicitly state the E-step responsibilities (they do include C(µk, Σk)) and the M-step updates, and how C is estimated per component per iteration (S, reuse of samples, variance control).

Originality: Moderate. The individual elements (learned metric, Riemannian normal, mixture EM) exist, but the integration into a practical, maximum-likelihood generative model with a principled normalization strategy is nontrivial and, to my knowledge, new in the learned-metric regime.

Significance: Potentially useful as a drop-in replacement for Gaussians in generative models when data concentrate near nonlinear structures. The maximum-entropy property under the learned metric is appealing. The empirical section demonstrates modeling/clustering gains, though stronger validation would bolster impact.

Pros:
- Elegant probabilistic treatment that respects the manifold measure; correct handling of normalization constant per component.
- Clever use of tangent-space Monte Carlo to avoid expensive Log maps.
- Clear algorithmic contributions (ML for single LAND and EM for mixtures) with practical considerations (steepest descent for stability).
- Compelling synthetic and EEG examples showing advantages over GMMs and LS Riemannian baselines.

Cons:
- σ selection is under-specified; evidence of potential tuning on training data; no test-set evaluation.
- Limited ablations: no study of S vs accuracy/run time; no comparison on data generated by Gaussians/GMMs to assess when LAND reduces to standard Gaussian.
- Scalability and high-dimensional behavior are untested; the diagonal M(x) implies lack of linear invariance.
- Some notation/algorithmic details (mixture E/M steps, assignment in clustering) could be clearer.

Requests for clarification and suggestions:
- Provide a principled strategy for σ: e.g., cross-validated likelihood on a validation set, median k-NN distance heuristic, or learning σ by gradient (differentiating through m and Expµ), with regularization. Report sensitivity curves.
- Add held-out negative log-likelihood for synthetic and EEG. For synthetic, also include a setting where data truly come from a GMM to show LAND does not overfit when the manifold is linear.
- Report computational complexity and wall-clock times vs N, D, K, and S; include solver details (integrator, tolerances, step caps) and variance reduction for estimating C (common random numbers, control variates).
- Explore sparsifying m via k-NN neighborhoods to scale N, and consider mini-batch estimates for the data-fit terms.
- Discuss and, if possible, test invariance: evaluate models under random affine transforms to quantify sensitivity due to diagonal M(x).
- Clarify mixture E/M steps in the main text and the exact cluster assignment used in the clustering experiments.
- Consider an ablation without the normalization term (i.e., improper constant) to quantify its effect on pushing µ out of low-density regions.

Minor:
- Explicitly state that the “manifold” is RD with a learned Riemannian metric, not necessarily a lower-dimensional submanifold.
- Typos and notation consistency could be polished; consider using Λ for precision and reserving Σ for covariance.

Overall assessment: Interesting and useful contribution with solid methodology and promising results. With stronger validation (principled σ selection, held-out evaluation, complexity analysis), the paper would be a clear accept. As is, I lean toward acceptance given the novelty of a practical, normalized, learned-metric Riemannian Gaussian and its demonstrated benefits.
