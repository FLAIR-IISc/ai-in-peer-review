This paper proposes a locally adaptive normal distribution (LAND) that replaces the Euclidean distance in the ordinary normal distribution with a locally adaptive nonlinear distance measure. The authors construct a Riemannian manifold using a learned metric that adapts to the data, and build a generative model on this manifold. The LAND is a parametric model that depends only on a mean and a covariance, and is the maximum entropy distribution under the given metric.

The authors provide a clear and concise summary of the main ideas in the paper, and relate them to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere. They discuss the strengths and weaknesses of the paper, addressing the criteria described in the Qualitative Evaluation section.

One of the main strengths of the paper is its interesting theoretical contribution. The idea of using a geodesic distance to replace the Mahalanobis distance is promising, and the authors provide a clear and well-motivated explanation of their approach. The paper also provides a good overview of the related work in the field, and the authors do a good job of positioning their contribution within the broader context of manifold learning and Riemannian statistics.

However, there are also some weaknesses in the paper. One of the main concerns is the definition of the inverse metric tensor in Eq. (7), which appears ad hoc and needs more justification. The authors could provide more intuition on why they chose this particular definition, and how it relates to the rest of the paper.

Another concern is the experimental evaluation, which is weak and insufficiently thorough. The authors only provide a few examples of synthetic and EEG data, and do not provide a comprehensive evaluation of the performance of the LAND on a variety of datasets. They also do not provide a comparison to other state-of-the-art methods, which makes it difficult to assess the significance of their contribution.

Additionally, there are some minor issues with the paper. For example, there is a typo/symbol correction needed in Algorithm 1, lines 4-5, where "d" should be replaced with "∇". There is also some confusion between the local covariance defined in Eq. (7) and the MAP estimation of that quantity in Eq. (11), and it is unclear if they refer to the same thing.

In terms of scalability and computational efficiency, the authors do not provide a complexity or runtime comparison to standard EM. This is a major concern, as the LAND algorithm appears to be computationally expensive, and it is unclear whether it can be applied to large-scale datasets.

Overall, the paper provides an interesting theoretical contribution, but its practical applicability is questionable. The authors need to address the concerns mentioned above, and provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the performance of the LAND on a variety of datasets.

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper provides an interesting theoretical contribution to the field of manifold learning and Riemannian statistics.
* The authors provide a clear and well-motivated explanation of their approach, and relate it to previous work in the field.
* The paper has the potential to inspire new research directions in the field of machine learning.

Arguments Against Acceptance:

* The experimental evaluation is weak and insufficiently thorough.
* The definition of the inverse metric tensor in Eq. (7) appears ad hoc and needs more justification.
* The paper does not provide a complexity or runtime comparison to standard EM, which is a major concern for scalability and computational efficiency.
* The practical applicability of the LAND algorithm is questionable.
