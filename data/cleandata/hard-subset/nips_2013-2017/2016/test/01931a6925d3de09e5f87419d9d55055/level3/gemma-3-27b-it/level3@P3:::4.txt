This paper proposes a Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND) as a generalization of the Gaussian distribution, grounded in Riemannian manifold theory. The authors develop a maximum likelihood estimator for the distribution parameters and demonstrate its effectiveness on both synthetic and real-world EEG datasets. The work is well-written, theoretically sound, and supported by good experimental results. However, the novelty appears incremental compared to existing work, particularly Simo-Serra et al. [19], and the key differences from state-of-the-art methods aren’t immediately clear, with [19] notably absent from the introduction.

The paper effectively builds upon the established framework of Riemannian geometry to address limitations of the standard Gaussian distribution when modeling data lying on nonlinear manifolds. The core idea – adapting the metric to the data density – is intuitively appealing and mathematically well-motivated. The use of a locally adaptive metric, learned via a Gaussian kernel, provides a smooth and differentiable distance measure suitable for generative modeling. The Monte Carlo integration scheme for normalizing the density is a clever solution to a challenging computational problem, and the presented gradient-based MLE algorithm seems practical. The extension to mixture models further increases the flexibility and applicability of the LAND.

A major strength of the paper lies in its theoretical rigor. The authors carefully connect their work to existing literature on Riemannian statistics and manifold learning, providing a solid foundation for their proposed method. The derivations are clear and concise, and the mathematical notation is consistent throughout. The experimental evaluation is also well-executed. The synthetic data experiments effectively demonstrate the ability of the LAND to capture nonlinear distributions, while the real-world EEG data analysis provides evidence of its utility in a practical application. The comparison with GMMs and least squares LAND estimators is fair and informative.

However, the originality of the work is somewhat limited. The paper frames its contribution as bridging Riemannian statistics and manifold learning, but this connection has been explored previously. More critically, the work of Simo-Serra et al. [19] is highly relevant and should have been discussed more prominently, particularly in the introduction. While the authors acknowledge it briefly in the related work section, the core idea of fitting Riemannian normal mixtures is present in [19], though with a different approach to normalization. The authors claim the difference lies in not accounting for normalization constants in [19], which is an inconsistency as their work does not inherently solve for the constants either, relying instead on Monte Carlo approximation. The significance of this difference, therefore, feels overstated. 

Furthermore, the paper could benefit from a more explicit discussion of the limitations of the proposed approach. The computational cost of evaluating geodesics and performing the Monte Carlo integration could be a bottleneck for high-dimensional datasets. The sensitivity of the method to the choice of the smoothing parameter σ, and how this affects the learned manifold, is also an area for further investigation. While explored in the EEG experiment, expanding on this sensitivity would strengthen the analysis.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a technically sound and mathematically well-motivated approach to distribution estimation on manifolds.
*   The proposed method is empirically validated on both synthetic and real-world data, demonstrating its effectiveness.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making it easy to follow the authors’ reasoning.
*   The introduction of a scalable Monte Carlo integration scheme is a valuable contribution.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The novelty of the work is incremental, particularly in light of Simo-Serra et al. [19].
*   The differences from state-of-the-art methods are not clearly explained early in the paper, and [19] is missing from the introduction.
*   The computational complexity of the method and its sensitivity to parameter choices are not fully addressed.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper is a solid contribution with sound theoretical foundations and encouraging experimental results, the limited novelty and lack of thorough comparison with relevant prior work (specifically [19]) raise concerns. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should:

1.  Expand the introduction to explicitly acknowledge and discuss the work of Simo-Serra et al. [19], clearly articulating the key differences and similarities between the two approaches.
2.  Refrain from overstating the significance of the normalization constant difference with [19] and acknowledge the reliance on Monte Carlo approximation in both.
3.  Include a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity and scalability of the method, and its sensitivity to parameter choices.
