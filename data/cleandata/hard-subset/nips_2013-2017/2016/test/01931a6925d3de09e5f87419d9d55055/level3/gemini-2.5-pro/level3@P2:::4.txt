This paper introduces the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), a generative model designed for data that lies on or near a non-linear manifold. The core idea is to generalize the multivariate normal distribution by replacing the standard Mahalanobis distance, which is based on a Euclidean metric, with a geodesic distance derived from a learned, data-dependent Riemannian metric. The authors propose a method to construct this metric from data by using a weighted sum of local covariances, effectively creating a space where distances are shorter in high-density regions.

The paper then develops a full statistical framework for this new distribution. A key challenge is the intractable normalization constant, which the authors address with a scalable Monte Carlo integration scheme performed over the tangent space of the distribution's mean. They derive a maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) procedure for the distribution's parameters (mean and covariance) using a block coordinate descent algorithm. This framework is further extended to a mixture of LANDs, providing a powerful alternative to Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) for clustering and density estimation on non-linear data. The work builds upon foundational concepts in Riemannian statistics [15, 23] and connects them to the field of manifold learning [9, 21].

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical development is sound, grounding the proposed distribution in the well-established mathematics of Riemannian geometry. The derivation of the MLE procedure, including the gradients for the mean and covariance, appears correct and is a non-trivial contribution. The use of Monte Carlo integration over the tangent space is a clever and practical solution to the problem of estimating the normalization constant, which is essential for a principled probabilistic model. The experimental evaluation is thorough, using both synthetic datasets that clearly illustrate the model's advantages and a challenging real-world EEG dataset. The claims are well-supported by these experiments.

**Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The introduction provides excellent motivation for the problem. The background section on Riemannian geometry is concise and gives readers the necessary tools to understand the main contributions. The figures, particularly Figures 5 and 6, are highly effective in visualizing how the LAND contours adapt to the non-linear structure of the data compared to the ellipsoidal contours of a GMM.

One significant point of weakness in clarity, however, is the positioning of the work with respect to prior art. The paper does not mention the highly related work of Simo-Serra et al. [19] until the "Related Work" section. This gives the impression in the introduction that the idea of a mixture of Riemannian normal distributions is entirely novel, which is not the case. This should be addressed by discussing [19] and clarifying the key differences earlier in the paper.

**Originality:** The primary contribution of this work is the fusion of a *learned* Riemannian metric with the formal definition of a Riemannian normal distribution to create a practical, generative model. While the Riemannian normal distribution has been studied before on manifolds with a *known* geometry [15, 23], this paper's novelty lies in adapting it to the manifold learning setting where the geometry is unknown and must be inferred from the data itself.

However, the originality is somewhat lessened by the work of Simo-Serra et al. [19], who also proposed a mixture model of Riemannian normal distributions. The key distinctions, which should be made more prominent, are that (1) this paper learns the underlying manifold structure from the data, whereas [19] assumes known manifolds (e.g., spheres, pose manifolds), and (2) this work provides a rigorous MLE framework by properly handling the normalization constant, which [19] appears to omit, making their approach not a fully consistent probabilistic model. These are crucial and valuable distinctions, but their placement late in the paper currently weakens the perceived originality of the contribution.

**Significance:** The results presented are significant. The paper addresses a fundamental limitation of the Gaussian distribution and models based on it (like GMMs), namely their inability to gracefully handle data with intrinsic non-linear structure. The LAND provides a principled and effective solution. The demonstrated success on the EEG sleep stage dataset shows its potential for practical applications where data may not conform to simple Euclidean assumptions. The idea of replacing a fundamental building block like the normal distribution with a manifold-aware counterpart could inspire further work in extending other classical machine learning models (e.g., LDA, PCA, Naive Bayes) to the manifold setting. The primary barrier to broad adoption may be the computational complexity of solving boundary value problems for the logarithm map, which is a necessary step in the optimization.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper proposes a novel and elegant generalization of the normal distribution for manifold-structured data.
*   The work is technically sound, with a complete and principled MLE framework for parameter estimation.
*   The experimental results are strong, demonstrating clear advantages over standard GMMs on both synthetic and real-world data.
*   The paper is well-written and connects two important fields: Riemannian statistics and manifold learning.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The paper fails to adequately position its contributions against closely related prior work [19] in the introduction, which tempers the claims of novelty. This is the main weakness.
*   The computational cost of the method is high, which may limit its practical applicability in large-scale settings.

**Recommendation:**
This is a strong paper with a valuable contribution. The main flaw is the lack of a clear comparison to prior work in the introductory sections. I would strongly recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors revising the introduction to properly contextualize their work with respect to Simo-Serra et al. [19], highlighting their key novel contributions: the learning of the metric itself and the principled handling of the normalization constant. This would strengthen the paper and more accurately reflect its contribution to the field.
