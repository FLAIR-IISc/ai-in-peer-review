The paper "A Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution" proposes a novel approach to modeling data distributions by replacing the traditional Euclidean distance with a locally adaptive, smoothly changing Riemannian metric. This allows the authors to define a locally adaptive normal distribution (LAND) that is a generalization of the normal distribution to the "manifold" setting. The LAND is parametric, depending only on a mean and a covariance, and is the maximum entropy distribution under the given metric.

The authors provide a thorough introduction to Riemannian geometry and its application to manifold learning. They define a Riemannian manifold as a smooth manifold equipped with a Riemannian metric, which encodes a smoothly changing inner product on the tangent space of each point. The authors then introduce the concept of geodesics, which are length-minimizing curves connecting two points on the manifold.

The authors propose a maximum likelihood algorithm to infer the distribution parameters, which relies on a combination of gradient descent and Monte Carlo integration. They also extend the LAND to mixture models and provide the corresponding EM algorithm. The authors demonstrate the efficiency of the LAND to fit non-trivial probability distributions over both synthetic data and EEG measurements of human sleep.

The paper is well-written and provides a clear explanation of the proposed approach. The authors provide a thorough review of related work and position their contribution within the broader context of manifold learning and Riemannian statistics. The experiments are well-designed and demonstrate the advantages of the LAND over traditional Gaussian mixture models.
