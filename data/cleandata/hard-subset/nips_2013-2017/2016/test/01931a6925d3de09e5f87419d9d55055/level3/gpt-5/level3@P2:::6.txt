Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), a parametric generative model that replaces the Euclidean metric in a Gaussian with a smoothly varying, data-adaptive Riemannian metric M(x). The core idea is to model density as a monotone function of geodesic distance to the mean on a learned manifold, keeping global parameters (mean µ and covariance Σ) while letting the metric be nonparametric. Specifically, M(x) is the inverse of a diagonal, kernel-smoothed local covariance (with Gaussian kernel bandwidth σ and regularizer ρ), so geodesics are pulled toward high-density regions. The density is the Riemannian normal (Pennec), p(x|µ,Σ) ∝ exp(−1/2⟨Logµ(x), Σ−1 Logµ(x)⟩), with normalization under the metric-induced measure.

Because the normalization constant C(µ,Σ) is intractable on a learned manifold, the authors derive an efficient Monte Carlo estimator by pulling the integral back to the tangent space at µ: C = Z E[v∼N(0,Σ)] m(µ,v), where m(µ,v)=√|M(Expµ(v))| and Z is the Euclidean Gaussian normalizer. This avoids repeated solutions of boundary value problems by using exponential maps (IVPs). Maximum likelihood estimation is performed via block coordinate descent: a steepest-descent update on µ on the manifold using Expµ, and a covariance update via Σ−1 = AᵀA and gradient steps on A. The method extends to mixtures with an EM algorithm that correctly accounts for component-specific normalization constants. Experiments on synthetic curved manifolds and EEG sleep-stage features show improved fit and clustering over Euclidean GMMs and over least-squares (intrinsic moment) estimators on the manifold.

The work bridges Riemannian statistics (e.g., Pennec’s Riemannian normal; Zhang & Fletcher’s probabilistic geodesic PCA) and manifold learning (e.g., Isomap) and builds on learned Riemannian metrics that emphasize data regions (Hauberg et al.). It differs from prior mixture models on known manifolds (e.g., Simo-Serra et al.) by learning the metric and by handling normalization consistently.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean and well-motivated: a principled generalization of the Gaussian to data-adaptive geometries.
- Sound derivations: the tangent-space Monte Carlo estimator of C(µ,Σ) is clever, computationally attractive, and consistent with the geometry. The optimization (steepest descent on µ; A-factorization for Σ) is sensible and well explained.
- Mixture extension with proper handling of component normalizers is valuable and often overlooked.
- Experiments convincingly illustrate how LAND adapts to non-ellipsoidal structures where GMMs fail.
- The geometric preliminaries and algorithmic description are clear overall and accessible to readers familiar with Riemannian geometry.

Weaknesses and points for improvement
- Hyperparameter selection: The choice of σ (kernel bandwidth) is critical, yet selection is unclear and appears tuned (e.g., selecting the best σ across a grid for each subject). This risks overfitting and confounds conclusions.
- Evaluation rigor: No held-out test log-likelihoods are reported; the synthetic study evaluates likelihood against the true generator using samples rather than generalization to unseen data. There are no comparisons when the true data are Euclidean Gaussians/GMMs to test for regressions in the “easy” regime. On the EEG task, stronger baselines (e.g., Euclidean GMM with cross-validated K, spectral clustering with RBF affinity, KDE, or simple HMMs) would contextualize gains.
- Scalability and complexity: Each evaluation of m(µ,v) requires an Exp map and computing M at that point, which, as written, aggregates over all N data via kernel weights. With S=3000 samples per iteration and many iterations/components, the cost can be substantial. The paper lacks runtime/complexity analyses, approximate neighbor strategies, or ablations on S.
- Assumptions on manifold dimension: The model keeps ambient dimension D (full-rank M(x)), which may be mismatched when data lie near a low-dimensional manifold. How sensitive are results when there is strong intrinsic low dimensionality? Could pre-whitening or a learned latent chart help?
- Clarity issues:
  - Potential confusion between the global Σ and the local metric M(x). Consider a clearer separation of roles, unify notation early (e.g., introduce Σ−1=AᵀA upfront), and emphasize that M(x) determines geodesics and volume element, while Σ shapes the component in TµM.
  - The lack of affine invariance (due to diagonal local covariances) is a practical concern; recommendations such as pre-whitening or metric choices with invariance would help.
  - Clustering/mixture: explicitly state the assignment rule (e.g., argmax responsibilities rnk after EM). Provide details on initialization, ρ, and stopping criteria (the algorithm box appears truncated).

Quality
- The approach is technically sound overall. The Monte Carlo estimator is natural; gradients are derived correctly; the choice of steepest descent in µ mitigates conditioning issues. That said, the paper should report variance/accuracy of C(µ,Σ) estimates (e.g., S sensitivity), discuss convergence behavior, and include runtime to support claims of scalability.

Clarity
- Generally well written with a helpful geometry primer. Address the noted notation/assignment issues, add a paragraph on computational complexity (per iteration as a function of N, D, K, S), and provide guidance on σ selection (e.g., cross-validation on held-out log-likelihood).

Originality
- Moderate. The core pieces (Riemannian normal, learned metrics, geodesic computation) are established, but integrating them into a practical, likelihood-based estimator with a tractable normalizer and consistent mixture EM is a useful and nontrivial contribution.

Significance
- Potentially impactful as a drop-in replacement for Gaussians in generative models where local geometry matters. The empirical section is promising but would benefit from stronger validation and scalability evidence to bolster practical adoption.

Questions for the authors
1. How do you select σ without supervision? Can you report held-out log-likelihood as a function of σ and provide a recommended procedure?
2. What are the computational costs per EM/MLE iteration (wall-clock, as a function of N, D, K, S)? Any use of approximate neighbors or inducing points to evaluate M(x) faster?
3. How sensitive are results to S? Can variance reduction (quasi-MC, control variates, antithetic sampling) reduce S substantially?
4. Do you observe regressions on data generated by Euclidean Gaussians? Please report such a sanity check.
5. Can you characterize limiting cases (σ→∞ gives Euclidean Gaussian; σ→0 behavior) and discuss regularization via ρ?
6. How are mixture components initialized, and how robust is EM to poor starts?

Suggestions
- Adopt cross-validated σ (unsupervised: held-out likelihood) and report sensitivity curves.
- Add runtime/complexity tables and high-dimensional experiments; use approximate nearest neighbors or a sparse kernel to compute M(x).
- Include comparisons on Euclidean Gaussian data and to simple nonparametric baselines (KDE) and manifold clustering methods (e.g., spectral clustering).
- Improve clarity of notation and mixture assignment; discuss affine invariance and recommend pre-whitening.
- Explore variance reduction for the normalizer estimator and reuse of Expµ(v) samples across iterations when feasible.

Pros
- Principled formulation; elegant use of tangent-space MC for normalization.
- Practical MLE/EM algorithms; consistent treatment of mixture normalizers.
- Strong qualitative performance on nonlinear structures; good exposition.

Cons
- Hyperparameter tuning and evaluation methodology risk overfitting; lack of held-out metrics.
- Missing scalability analysis and high-dimensional tests.
- Some clarity and notation issues; limited comparisons to strong baselines.

Overall assessment
An interesting and useful contribution that makes Riemannian generative modeling practical on learned geometries. With tightened evaluation (held-out likelihoods, fair σ selection), added complexity analysis, and minor clarity fixes, this would be a strong paper. I lean toward acceptance.
