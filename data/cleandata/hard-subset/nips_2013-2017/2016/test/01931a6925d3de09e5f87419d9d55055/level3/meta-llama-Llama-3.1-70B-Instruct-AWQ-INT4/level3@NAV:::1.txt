This paper proposes a Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND) that generalizes the normal distribution to the "manifold" setting, where data is assumed to lie near a potentially low-dimensional manifold embedded in R. The LAND is a parametric distribution that depends only on a mean and a covariance, but is defined over a non-parametric space. The authors develop a maximum likelihood algorithm to infer the distribution parameters, which relies on a combination of gradient descent and Monte Carlo integration. They also extend the LAND to mixture models and provide the corresponding EM algorithm.

The paper is well-written and pleasant to read. The authors provide a clear and concise introduction to Riemannian geometry and its application to manifold learning. The proposed LAND model is well-motivated and has the potential to be a useful tool for modeling complex data.

The strengths of the paper include:

* The authors provide a clear and concise introduction to Riemannian geometry and its application to manifold learning.
* The proposed LAND model is well-motivated and has the potential to be a useful tool for modeling complex data.
* The authors develop a maximum likelihood algorithm to infer the distribution parameters, which relies on a combination of gradient descent and Monte Carlo integration.
* The paper includes experimental results on both synthetic and real data, which demonstrate the effectiveness of the LAND model.

However, there are also some weaknesses and concerns:

* The paper raises concerns about the applicability of the LAND model to large-scale, high-dimensional data, due to the computational challenges of per-point covariance estimation and inversion.
* The authors do not provide a clear analysis of the complexity of the LAND model, and it is unclear how the model will perform on high-dimensional data.
* The paper does not provide a thorough comparison with other metric learning methods, and it is unclear how the LAND model compares to other approaches.
* The authors do not provide a clear explanation of how the Gaussian kernel bandwidth sigma is chosen, and how it affects the results.
* The paper includes some visualizations that are not informative, such as the comparison with GMMs in Figs. 5-6.

To improve the paper, the authors could:

* Provide a clear analysis of the complexity of the LAND model and its applicability to high-dimensional data.
* Include a thorough comparison with other metric learning methods, such as per-point diagonal metrics and local PCA approaches.
* Provide a clear explanation of how the Gaussian kernel bandwidth sigma is chosen and how it affects the results.
* Replace or supplement the visualizations with more informative comparisons, such as intrinsic estimator visualizations.
* Add computational-complexity comparisons between the LAND model and intrinsic estimators to guide method choice by data structure.
* Expand on the mixtures of LAND as a particularly interesting direction.

Overall, the paper is well-written and proposes a potentially useful tool for modeling complex data. However, there are some weaknesses and concerns that need to be addressed to improve the paper.
