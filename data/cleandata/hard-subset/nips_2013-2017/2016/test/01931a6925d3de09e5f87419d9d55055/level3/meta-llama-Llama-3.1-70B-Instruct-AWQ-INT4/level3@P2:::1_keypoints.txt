- Proposes Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), a parametric model using a data-adaptive Riemannian manifold to capture nonlinear data structure.
- Constructs a Riemannian metric that adapts locally to data and uses geodesics to compute manifold distances.
- Generalizes the normal distribution to manifold settings assuming data near a low-dimensional manifold in R^D.
- Develops a maximum-likelihood inference algorithm using gradient descent combined with Monte Carlo integration.
- Extends LAND to mixture models and provides an EM algorithm for mixtures.
- Paper is well-written with a clear, concise introduction to Riemannian geometry and its statistical application.
- Experiments on synthetic data and EEG sleep measurements demonstrate LAND’s ability to fit non-trivial distributions.
- LAND effectively captures local data properties and non-ellipsoidal clusters, shown clearly in clustering visualizations.
- Major concern: scalability to large-scale, high-dimensional data due to per-point covariance estimation and matrix inversions.
- Authors should provide a detailed computational complexity analysis of their approach.
- Choice of Gaussian kernel bandwidth sigma for locality is not well justified; robustness and sensitivity analyses are needed.
- Unclear whether sigma is uniform across density regions; authors should clarify.
- Experimental comparisons rely mostly on visual results against GMMs, which are weak baselines and less informative.
- Recommend replacing/supplementing visuals with intrinsic estimator visualizations (some appear in supplement and outperform GMM).
- Suggest providing computational-complexity comparisons between LAND and intrinsic estimators to guide method selection.
- Mixtures of LAND are a promising direction and should be expanded with more details and applications.
- Overall assessment: technically sound and original but with applicability and robustness concerns to be addressed (Quality 8/10, Clarity 9/10, Originality 9/10, Significance 9/10).
- Arguments for acceptance: novel bridge between Riemannian statistics and manifold learning, clear exposition, and strong empirical fitting of complex distributions.
- Arguments against acceptance: unresolved scalability and robustness issues, unclear sigma choice, and insufficient computational comparisons.
