Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), a parametric density model that replaces the Euclidean metric in a multivariate normal with a learned, smoothly varying Riemannian metric that adapts to the local data density. Concretely, the metric tensor M(x) is defined as the inverse of a local (diagonal) covariance estimated via Gaussian kernel smoothing; geodesics under M(x) thus tend to follow high-density regions. Building on the Riemannian normal distribution (Pennec), the authors define a density pM(x|µ,Σ) ∝ exp(−1/2 〈Logµ(x),Σ−1Logµ(x)〉) with respect to the volume measure induced by M. They derive a maximum likelihood estimation procedure for µ and Σ, where the intractable normalization constant C(µ,Σ) is approximated efficiently by Monte Carlo integration in the tangent space at µ using samples v ∼ N(0,Σ) and the exponential map Expµ(v). The paper also develops a mixture of LANDs and an EM algorithm that accounts for component-specific normalization constants. Empirically, LAND is evaluated on synthetic non-linear data and on EEG features for sleep-stage clustering, showing improved likelihood and F-measure over Gaussian mixtures and over Riemannian least-squares estimators.

The work connects manifold learning (e.g., Isomap; Hauberg et al. on learned Riemannian metrics), statistics on Riemannian manifolds (Pennec; Zhang & Fletcher), and mixture modeling. Related works on Riemannian mixtures on known manifolds include Simo-Serra et al. (Geodesic finite mixture models) and Straub et al. (DP mixtures on spheres). The main difference here is that the manifold is learned nonparametrically from data, and the normalization constant is handled explicitly within ML/EM.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The construction is principled: a data-adaptive metric induces geodesic distances; the resulting Riemannian normal is maximum-entropy given mean and covariance on the learned manifold. The Monte Carlo estimate of C(µ,Σ) in the tangent space is a sensible variance-reduction strategy versus integrating on the manifold; using Exp rather than Log avoids BVPs. Gradients for µ and Σ (via Σ−1 = AᵀA) are derived and used in a block coordinate scheme. The mixture EM correctly includes component normalization constants (a point omitted in some prior work).
- Completeness and care: Experiments cover synthetic likelihood recovery and real EEG clustering; the role of σ is acknowledged and partially explored. However, the work would benefit from: quantitative runtime/complexity reporting; sensitivity analyses for σ and the regularizer ρ; convergence diagnostics for MC estimates (S=3000) and the optimizer; and more extensive comparisons.
- Potential weaknesses: The learned metric is diagonal and not invariant to linear transformations, which can bias geodesics and densities; the implications are acknowledged but not fully explored. The metric and model are learned from the same data; while standard in manifold learning, some discussion of potential overfitting and cross-validation of σ would help. The approach may face scalability issues in high D due to ODE solves for many Exp evaluations; practical performance and approximations are not reported in detail.

Clarity
- The paper is clearly written, with a helpful geometric prelude, well-motivated algorithmic choices, and an explicit algorithm. The exposition of the normalization constant via tangent-space MC is particularly clear.
- Suggestions: Move the discussion contrasting with Simo-Serra et al. [19] to the Introduction and spell out the novelty earlier (learned manifold vs known, treatment of normalization constants, ML vs LS estimation). Summarize computational costs (per iteration, per sample), provide pseudo-code for the mixture EM in the main text, and describe initialization strategies briefly (currently in the supplement).

Originality
- The core ingredients (Riemannian normal, learned Riemannian metrics, mixtures on manifolds) have precedents. The paper’s contribution lies in combining them into a practical, fully probabilistic, maximum-likelihood framework on a learned manifold, with a workable estimator for the normalization constant and an EM algorithm that properly handles component normalizers.
- That said, the novelty relative to Simo-Serra et al. [19] and other manifold density models is not emphasized early or crisply enough. In particular, [19] considers mixtures on known manifolds and does not handle differing normalization constants; this paper’s extension to learned metrics and proper normalization is meaningful but should be articulated more clearly upfront.

Significance
- LAND is a useful modeling primitive: a drop-in replacement for Gaussians that respects non-linear data geometry and can be used in mixtures. The EEG clustering experiment suggests practical benefits; synthetic experiments show improved likelihood and shape capture.
- Impact could be higher with stronger baselines (e.g., adaptive KDE, mixture of factor analyzers, probabilistic manifold models, or normalizing flows), more complex real datasets, and ablations testing diagonal vs full local covariances or linear pre-whitening to mitigate invariance issues.
- The maximum-entropy perspective is appealing, though it hinges on the learned metric; discussion of identifiability and consistency (given the data-dependent geometry) would increase theoretical significance.

Pros
- Clear, principled formulation grounded in Riemannian geometry.
- Practical ML estimator via tangent-space MC; avoids expensive BVPs.
- Mixture EM that correctly includes component normalizers.
- Empirical gains over GMMs and LS baselines on synthetic and EEG data.
- Well-written and easy to follow.

Cons
- Novelty over prior Riemannian mixtures (esp. Simo-Serra et al.) is not highlighted early and may be perceived as incremental.
- Metric restricted to diagonal, not invariant to linear transforms; impact not thoroughly studied.
- Limited baselines and scalability analysis; no runtime/convergence reporting.
- Sensitivity to σ and ρ and robustness of MC normalization (S) not fully characterized.

Questions and suggestions for authors
1) Please move and expand the contrast with Simo-Serra et al. [19] to the Introduction, highlighting learned vs known manifolds and handling of normalization constants in EM.
2) Provide complexity and runtime per iteration (numbers of Exp/Log solves, effect of S), and wall-clock comparisons vs GMMs.
3) Add sensitivity analyses for σ and ρ, and guidance for selecting them. Consider learning σ or using local/adaptive bandwidths.
4) Discuss and, if possible, mitigate lack of linear invariance (e.g., via pre-whitening or PCA); comment on using non-diagonal local covariances.
5) Include stronger baselines (adaptive KDE, manifold density estimators, or contemporary flow models) and report performance on additional real datasets.
6) Report convergence diagnostics and variance of the MC estimate of C(µ,Σ); justify S=3000 and show how performance changes with S.
7) Clarify initialization for µ, Σ, and mixture components in the main text.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper is well-executed and useful, but the contribution feels incremental without a sharper differentiation from prior Riemannian mixture models and a stronger empirical/complexity evaluation. With clearer positioning and expanded experiments/analysis, this work would make a solid contribution.

Reviewer confidence: Medium.
