Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), which replaces the Euclidean metric in a multivariate normal with a smoothly varying Riemannian metric that adapts to local data density. Given a nonparametric, data-driven metric M(x) constructed via kernel-smoothed local (diagonal) variances (Eq. 7), the authors define a Riemannian normal pM(x|µ,Σ) that is maximum entropy under the fixed metric (Pennec, 2006). They derive a maximum-likelihood estimator for µ and Σ that requires evaluating normalization constants C(µ,Σ) that depend on geometry; this is approximated by Monte Carlo in the tangent space at µ using Expµ(·) to account for volume distortion. They also derive an EM algorithm for mixtures of LANDs. Experiments on synthetic manifolds and sleep EEG features show that LAND can capture curved, non-elliptical structure better than Euclidean GMMs and Riemannian least-squares estimators.

This work bridges Riemannian statistics on known manifolds (e.g., Pennec 2006; Zhang & Fletcher 2013; Simo-Serra et al. 2015; Straub et al. 2015) with manifold learning, leveraging the “geodesics follow the data” idea of Hauberg et al. (NIPS 2012) and related probabilistic geometries (Tosi et al., UAI 2014). It differs from embedding-based approaches (Isomap; LLE; Laplacian Eigenmaps; kernel PCA) by keeping a continuous geometry in ambient space that supports generative modeling.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and usefulness: A principled way to endow Euclidean space with a data-adaptive geometry and build a maximum-entropy normal on it. Provides an appealing alternative to Euclidean Gaussians and to discrete-graph geodesics.
- Solid geometric formulation: Consistent use of Exp/Log maps, correct recognition that C(µ,Σ) depends on µ and curvature; tangent-space Monte Carlo is a sensible and scalable choice versus manifold-space BVPs.
- Practical algorithms: Gradient expressions for µ and Σ (with Σ−1 = AᵀA), and an EM for mixtures that correctly accounts for per-component C(µ,Σ), fixing an inconsistency in some prior work.
- Empirical indications: LAND captures nonlinear cluster shapes on synthetic data and improves F-measure in unsupervised sleep-stage clustering for several subjects.

Weaknesses
Quality (soundness, evaluation)
- “Learned” metric vs hand-crafted: Eq. 7 is a kernel smoother with fixed form and diagonal structure. It is not learned by optimizing an objective, and its behavior hinges on σ (and ρ). The title/text repeatedly imply learning the metric; this needs to be clarified and/or σ,ρ should be learned jointly with (µ,Σ).
- Sensitivity to σ: Results (especially sleep) depend on σ, but the paper offers limited guidance. Please analyze σ’s effect (bias-variance tradeoff, geodesic curvature, effective neighborhood size), provide a principled selection strategy (e.g., marginal likelihood, cross-validation, plug-in rules based on local density), and show robustness curves.
- Coordinate dependence: The diagonal metric in Eq. 7 is not invariant under linear reparameterizations; even axis rescaling changes geodesics and likelihoods. This should be discussed, with guidance (e.g., prewhitening, local PCA/full metric, or an invariant construction). The lack of invariance can confound comparisons to GMMs if features are not standardized.
- Overfitting and model selection in mixtures: Comparisons that fix K across methods can favor LAND when curved structure is present, while GMMs can close the gap with larger K. Although the supplement reports per-method AIC/BIC optima, the main experiments should consistently select K per method via model selection on held-out data and report both fit and complexity. Please also show how LAND behaves as K grows (does it overfit?), and include a diagnostic (e.g., held-out likelihood, BIC) to demonstrate generalization.
- Computational analysis: The main cost is repeated IVPs for Expµ(v) inside Monte Carlo per gradient step (S=3000). Provide wall-clock/runtime breakdowns, scaling with N, D, K, and S; show the effect of S on variance of C(µ,Σ) and gradient noise (with convergence diagnostics). Discuss/measure limits (e.g., higher-D datasets). Without this, scalability claims are hard to assess.
- Baselines: Consider alternatives that also account for nonlinearity, e.g., GMMs in an embedded space (Isomap/LLE/LE) with out-of-sample extension, or density models based on local PCA mixtures. This helps decouple “curved structure” from “mixture expressivity.”

Clarity
- Clarify the status of “learning” the metric: it is constructed, not learned by an optimization principle. Be precise in the title/abstract and main text.
- Add intuition for Eq. 7: discuss why diagonal, how m(µ,v)=|M(Expµ(v))|^1/2 penalizes low-density regions, and what happens under rescaling of coordinates.
- Expand related work on metric learning and invariance: e.g., Xing et al. (2003), ITML (Davis et al., 2007), LMNN (Weinberger & Saul, 2009), NCA (Goldberger et al., 2005), and their relation to Riemannian approaches (including Hauberg et al., 2012; Tosi et al., 2014).
- Provide more implementation details: ODE solver settings, backprop through IVPs, step-size selection, initialization, and stopping criteria.

Originality
- The combination of a data-adaptive ambient-space Riemannian metric with a maximum-entropy normal and tractable MLE is a nice contribution that extends prior Riemannian statistics (largely on known manifolds) into the manifold-learning setting. Using tangent-space Monte Carlo to handle C(µ,Σ) is practical.

Significance
- Potentially useful for generative modeling, clustering, and as a drop-in replacement in classical models on curved data. However, current empirical scope is modest (low-D synthetic, 5D EEG features), and scalability is unclear. A more thorough experimental suite (including higher-D benchmarks) and robust model selection would strengthen significance.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Elegant, principled formulation; correct handling of normalization across components.
- Clear empirical advantage in capturing curved structure; promising real-data application.
- Bridges two active areas (Riemannian statistics and manifold learning).

Cons:
- Metric is fixed-form and coordinate-dependent; limited analysis of σ and invariance.
- Experimental methodology needs stronger, per-method model selection and overfitting analysis.
- Missing computational/scalability characterization; limited baselines.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Can σ and ρ be learned (e.g., via EM-like inner loop, evidence maximization, or gradient-based bilevel optimization) rather than set heuristically?
- Provide robustness curves over σ and feature rescaling; recommend preprocessing (e.g., whitening) or an invariant metric (local full covariance or local PCA).
- Report per-method K chosen by BIC/AIC/cross-validated likelihood in the main figures; add results for larger K to illustrate overfitting tendencies of LAND vs GMM.
- Add runtime and scaling plots; analyze how S trades off speed/accuracy; consider variance reduction (control variates, quasi-MC, importance resampling across iterations).
- Consider additional baselines (embedded-space GMMs with out-of-sample extension; mixtures of local PCAs).
- Clarify the scope of the maximum-entropy claim: it holds for fixed metric; when the metric is data-induced, discuss the implications and potential double-counting of information.

Overall assessment
Interesting and potentially impactful idea with a sound geometric foundation, but the paper overstates “metric learning,” is sensitive to hyperparameters with limited analysis, and needs more rigorous, fair experimental comparisons and computational characterization. My inclination is a weak reject in its current form; with the above revisions, this could become a strong submission.
