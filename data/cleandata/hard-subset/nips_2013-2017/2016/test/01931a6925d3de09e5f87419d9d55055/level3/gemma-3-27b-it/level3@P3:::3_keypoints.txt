- Proposes locally adaptive normal distribution (LAND) by replacing Euclidean metric with a Riemannian metric learned from data; conceptually interesting application of Riemannian geometry.
- Title is misleading: metric is hand-crafted (Eq.7) with fixed hyperparameters ρ and σ, not learned via optimization.
- Metric sensitivity to σ is not explored; paper lacks intuition, systematic study, or guidance on choosing σ.
- Authors recommend small σ heuristically, but provide no rigorous justification or learning scheme for ρ and σ.
- The metric form (Eq.7) is not coordinate-invariant and is tied to a specific coordinate system; implications are not discussed.
- No explanation for choosing a diagonal metric versus a full metric or discussion of resulting limitations.
- Paper lacks connection to broader metric-learning and manifold-learning literature (missing refs like Schölkopf et al., Roweis & Saul, Pennec, Zhang & Fletcher).
- Experimental comparison to GMMs is flawed: LAND uses fewer components advantageously and GMM could match performance with more components.
- Model selection (e.g., BIC/AIC) is not used to fairly choose component counts across methods.
- Claims of faster learning are unsupported: likelihoods shown without statistical significance testing.
- Risk of overfitting with many LAND components is not demonstrated or addressed.
- No quantitative evaluation of computational cost or scalability; larger-scale experiments (e.g., MNIST) and resource usage are missing.
- Positive points: adapting metric to manifold is conceptually sound, theoretical connection to maximum entropy is solid, and Monte Carlo normalization seems scalable.
- Overall assessment: interesting idea with theoretical merit but major implementation, evaluation, and presentation flaws; recommend rejection with encouragement to revise and resubmit after addressing concerns.
