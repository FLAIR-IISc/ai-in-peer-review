This paper proposes a Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), a parametric model that leverages a non-parametric Riemannian manifold to capture the nonlinear structure of data. The authors bridge the gap between Riemannian statistics and manifold learning by constructing a Riemannian metric that adapts to the data. This approach enables the definition of geodesics, which are used to compute distances between points on the manifold.

The LAND model is a generalization of the normal distribution to the manifold setting, where data is assumed to lie near a potentially low-dimensional manifold embedded in R^D. The authors develop a maximum likelihood algorithm to infer the distribution parameters, which relies on a combination of gradient descent and Monte Carlo integration. They also extend the LAND to mixture models and provide the corresponding EM algorithm.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise introduction to Riemannian geometry and its application to statistics. The experimental results demonstrate the efficiency of the LAND in fitting non-trivial probability distributions over both synthetic data and EEG measurements of human sleep.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to capture the local properties of the data, which is particularly evident in the clustering task experiment. The LAND is substantially better at capturing non-ellipsoidal clusters, and the authors provide a clear visualization of the results.

However, there are some concerns regarding the applicability of the LAND to large-scale, high-dimensional data. The per-point covariance estimation and inversion are computationally challenging, and the authors should provide a more detailed analysis of the complexity of their approach.

Additionally, the choice of the Gaussian kernel bandwidth sigma for defining locality is not thoroughly justified, and the authors should provide more robustness tests to demonstrate the sensitivity of the results to this parameter. It is also unclear whether sigma is uniform across density regions, and the authors should clarify this point.

The experimental results are mostly visual comparisons against Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs), which perform poorly and are less informative. The authors should consider replacing or supplementing these visualizations with intrinsic estimator visualizations, some of which appear in the supplementary material and outperform GMM.

Furthermore, the authors should provide computational-complexity comparisons between the LAND and intrinsic estimators to guide method choice by data structure. This would help readers understand the trade-offs between the two approaches.

On the other hand, the mixtures of LAND are a particularly interesting direction that should be expanded. The authors should consider providing more details on this aspect and exploring its potential applications.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise introduction to their approach. However, there are some concerns regarding the applicability and robustness of the LAND, which should be addressed in future work.
