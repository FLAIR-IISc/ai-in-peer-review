This paper proposes a locally adaptive normal distribution (LAND) by replacing the Euclidean metric with a learned Riemannian metric, effectively adapting the Mahalanobis distance with a geodesic distance. The extension to mixtures of LANDs and evaluation on both synthetic and EEG data are presented. While the core idea of utilizing a geodesic distance, reminiscent of Isomap, is intriguing and promising for modeling data residing on nonlinear manifolds, several aspects of the work require significant refinement.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulating the limitations of the standard normal distribution when applied to data with underlying manifold structure. It appropriately links to prior work in both Riemannian geometry and manifold learning, positioning the contribution within a relevant context. The authors demonstrate a good understanding of the theoretical foundations and attempt to bridge the gap between statistical modeling on manifolds and the practical challenges of manifold learning.

However, the technical soundness of certain aspects is questionable. Specifically, the definition of the inverse metric tensor in Eq. (7) feels *ad hoc*. The regularization term 'ρ' is introduced to avoid singularities but lacks strong justification; a more principled approach to ensure positive definiteness, potentially tied to the Gaussian kernel’s parameters, would strengthen the formulation.  Furthermore, the intuition behind using a Gaussian kernel to weight local covariances in the weighted sum (Eq. 7) is not sufficiently explained. Why a Gaussian kernel specifically? A discussion of alternative kernels and their potential impact would be valuable. 

There’s also confusion regarding the local covariance. The definition in Eq. (7) appears distinct from the MAP estimation presented in Eq. (11). It's unclear whether these refer to the same quantity or represent different stages of the estimation process. Clarifying this distinction is crucial for understanding the methodology.

The experimental evaluation is a significant weakness. While experiments on both synthetic and real EEG data are conducted, they are insufficiently thorough. The synthetic data experiments, while showing some improvement over GMM, lack a comprehensive comparison with more sophisticated manifold learning techniques. The EEG data analysis, although application-driven, lacks detailed justification for feature extraction and parameter tuning (particularly σ).  The impact of σ is acknowledged, but the ad-hoc selection process diminishes confidence in the results.

A major concern revolves around scalability and computational efficiency. The paper doesn’t address the computational complexity of the proposed method, nor does it offer a runtime comparison to standard EM algorithms. Evaluating the geodesic distance computation and Monte Carlo integration costs are vital for assessing practical applicability. Without this information, the method appears potentially intractable for high-dimensional datasets.

Overall, the paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution, but its practical applicability remains questionable. The idea is sound, but the lack of rigorous justification for certain choices, weak experimental validation, and absence of scalability analysis severely limit its impact.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel idea of adapting the normal distribution to a learned Riemannian manifold.
*   Clear articulation of the problem and motivation.
*   Demonstration of potential improvement over GMM on synthetic data.
*   Application to a real-world dataset (EEG).

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   *Ad hoc* definition of the inverse metric tensor.
*   Lack of justification for the Gaussian kernel choice.
*   Confusion regarding local covariance definitions.
*   Weak and insufficiently thorough experimental evaluation.
*   No discussion of computational complexity or scalability.
*   Limited novelty beyond adapting existing concepts.

**Minor Issue:**

*   Algorithm 1, lines 4–5: Typo/symbol correction required. 'd' should be '∇'.

**Recommendation:** I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to address the outlined weaknesses. Specifically, the authors should provide stronger theoretical justification for key choices, conduct more comprehensive experiments, and thoroughly analyze the computational complexity of their approach. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the contribution and enhance its potential impact on the field.
