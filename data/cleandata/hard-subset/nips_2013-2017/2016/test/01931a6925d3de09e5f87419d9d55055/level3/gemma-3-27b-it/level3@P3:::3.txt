This paper proposes a locally adaptive normal distribution (LAND) by replacing the Euclidean metric with a Riemannian metric learned from the data. The core idea – adapting the metric to better capture the underlying manifold – is a useful and interesting application of Riemannian geometry to statistical modeling. However, the implementation and evaluation contain significant weaknesses.

The title is misleading. While the paper frames the contribution as "learning a metric," the metric in Eq. 7 is explicitly hand-crafted with hyperparameters ρ and σ fixed empirically, rather than learned through an optimization process. This fundamentally shifts the contribution from metric *learning* to metric *design*. The authors acknowledge this in the response, but the title remains problematic.

A major concern is the sensitivity of the metric to the choice of σ. The paper lacks discussion on the intuitive effect of varying σ – how does a smaller or larger σ influence the resulting geodesics and the learned distribution? A systematic investigation of σ's impact, and guidance on selecting an appropriate value, are crucial. The authors suggest that a smaller σ is used where possible, but this lacks rigor.  Merging metric learning with LAND learning, to allow for ρ and σ to be learned rather than fixed, would significantly strengthen the work and align it with the title’s implication.

The chosen metric (Eq. 7) is not coordinate-invariant; it’s inherently tied to a specific coordinate system. The paper needs a thorough discussion of the implications of this choice. Why a diagonal metric? What are the limitations compared to a full metric? A deeper intuition regarding the rationale behind this specific metric form is needed. Relating this work to the broader literature on metric learning – both Euclidean and Riemannian – and manifold learning would provide valuable context. Key references such as Schölkopf et al. (1998) on kernel PCA, Roweis & Saul (2000) on LLE, and more recent developments in learned metrics are missing. Furthermore, citing Pennec (2006) and Zhang & Fletcher (2013) would position the work better within the existing Riemannian statistics literature.

The experimental comparison is flawed. The LAND is advantaged by requiring fewer components in the mixture model compared to the GMM. However, the authors don't adequately address that a GMM *can* match LAND's performance with more components. A fair comparison requires selecting the number of components per method via model selection criteria, such as BIC or AIC, as suggested in the original review. The claim of LAND “learning faster” is also not sufficiently supported – likelihood values are presented, but statistical significance isn’t addressed. 

Demonstrating LAND's potential for overfitting with a large number of components is essential. The reviewer’s observation that LAND’s flexibility makes it prone to overfitting, even with nearly straight geodesics, is valid and needs to be addressed. Furthermore, providing quantitative measures of computational cost and scalability is necessary. Experiments on a larger dataset like MNIST, with reported training times and memory usage, would provide valuable insights into the practical limitations of the approach. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The idea of adapting the metric to the data manifold is conceptually sound and offers a novel approach to statistical modeling.
*   The theoretical connection to maximum entropy distributions provides a strong foundation.
*   The Monte Carlo integration scheme appears to be a scalable method for normalizing the density.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The title is misleading, as the metric is hand-crafted, not learned.
*   The sensitivity to σ is not adequately addressed, and guidance on its selection is missing.
*   The experimental evaluation is flawed, and the comparison to GMM is unfair.
*   The lack of discussion regarding coordinate invariance and the rationale behind the chosen metric limits understanding.
*   The paper lacks a thorough connection to relevant literature in metric learning and Riemannian geometry.



**Overall assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting idea with some theoretical merit, but significant weaknesses in implementation, evaluation, and presentation prevent strong support for acceptance in its current form. While the concept is promising, the authors need to address the concerns outlined above—particularly the misleading title, the lack of discussion around σ, the flawed experimental evaluation, and the limited connection to relevant literature—before this work can be considered a significant contribution. I recommend rejection with encouragement to revise and resubmit after addressing these concerns.
