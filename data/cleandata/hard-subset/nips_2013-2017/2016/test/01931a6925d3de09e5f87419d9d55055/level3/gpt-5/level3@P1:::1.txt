This paper proposes the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), which replaces the Euclidean/Mahalanobis metric in a Gaussian with a smoothly varying Riemannian metric learned from data. The density takes the Riemannian normal form [15], pM(x|µ,Σ) ∝ exp(−1/2 ⟨Logµ(x), Σ−1 Logµ(x)⟩), but the metric M(x) is nonparametric and learned: it is the inverse of a diagonal local covariance estimated via Gaussian kernel weights. Distances are geodesic lengths under M(x), computed by solving ODEs for Exp/Log maps. The model is parametric in µ, Σ, and maximum entropy given the metric; normalization requires integrating over the tangent space using Monte Carlo with samples v ∼ N(0, Σ) and a volume correction √|M(Expµ(v))|. The authors derive a maximum-likelihood algorithm via block coordinate descent: update µ along the steepest descent direction on the manifold, and update Σ using a factorization Σ−1 = AᵀA. They extend to mixtures with a consistent EM that includes normalization constants per component. Experiments on synthetic manifolds and an EEG sleep-stage feature space suggest LAND better captures nonlinear structure than GMMs and avoids the low-density bias of intrinsic least-squares estimators.

Relation to prior work: The paper bridges manifold learning (Isomap [21], LLE [17], Laplacian Eigenmaps [1]) and Riemannian statistics [15, 23], but departs from embedding methods by learning a continuous metric over RD (as in Hauberg et al. [9]) and then building a generative model on that manifold. It contrasts with prior Riemannian-normal-based models on known manifolds (e.g., spheres, SPD) by learning the metric. The mixture formulation addresses a key shortcoming in [19] by incorporating the per-component normalization C(µk, Σk). The local diagonal metric is related to local metric learning and per-point metrics in computer vision (e.g., Frome et al.; Malisiewicz & Efros) and local PCA schemes; positioning with respect to that literature could be strengthened.

Quality: The formulation is sound and well-motivated. The normalization via tangent-space Monte Carlo is principled and leverages IVPs (faster than BVPs). The optimization derivations are clear; using steepest descent in µ stabilizes updates when Σ is ill-conditioned. Empirically, LAND captures curved clusters and improves sleep-stage clustering alignment. However, the experimental comparisons underemphasize intrinsic least-squares estimators in visuals, and computational complexity/runtime analysis is missing. Sensitivity to the bandwidth σ and the sample size S in Monte Carlo deserves more systematic study.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written and well organized. The geometric preliminaries are concise, and the algorithm is easy to follow. Some aspects need clarification: exact computational costs per iteration; the neighborhood selection for M(x) (full N vs. k-NN); how σ is chosen in practice; and what “smooth enough for geodesics” operationally means.

Originality: The core novelty is combining a learned Riemannian metric with the maximum-entropy Riemannian normal as a parametric generative model, and deriving ML (and EM) estimators that account for the metric-induced measure. This is a meaningful and nontrivial extension beyond fixed-manifold Riemannian statistics and beyond discrete-graph geodesics in manifold learning.

Significance: LAND provides a generic drop-in replacement for Gaussians in settings where data lie near curved structures, enabling generative modeling and clustering that respect geometry. The mixture extension is particularly promising. If computational issues are addressed, this could influence modeling in a range of applications (e.g., neuroscience, vision, shape analysis).

Strengths
- Elegant unification of learned Riemannian geometry with maximum-entropy Riemannian normals.
- Principled normalization via tangent-space Monte Carlo; avoids expensive Log map evaluations in the integrand.
- Consistent mixture model with EM that includes per-component normalizers.
- Clear empirical evidence of modeling non-ellipsoidal structure; improved clustering alignment on EEG.
- Very well written.

Weaknesses and questions
- Scalability: Each evaluation of m(µ, v) requires Expµ(v) via ODE integration and computing M at the endpoint, where M(x) itself is a weighted sum over data. What is the per-iteration complexity for µ and Σ updates? Roughly O(S · T · (cost of evaluating M and its derivatives)), where S is Monte Carlo samples and T ODE steps; and computing M(x) naively is O(ND) but could be O(kD) with k-NN. Please quantify, provide runtime/memory, and define the practical regime of D and N (e.g., D ≈ 5–10 vs. 100).
- High-dimensional behavior: With diagonal M(x), how well does the metric capture structure when D is 50–100? Do ODE solvers remain stable? Empirically characterize performance and runtime vs. D.
- Bandwidth σ: The metric depends critically on σ. Is σ global or adaptive across regions with varying density? Provide robustness curves (NLL/cluster F-measure vs. σ) on synthetic and EEG; consider local bandwidths or learning σ.
- Baselines and visuals: Figs. 5–6 compare to GMMs, which are weak baselines on curved manifolds. Include visual and quantitative comparisons to intrinsic least-squares estimators (some appear in the supplement) and report AIC/BIC consistently across methods in main text.
- Sensitivity to Monte Carlo S and variance reduction: Show accuracy/speed trade-offs; consider control variates or quasi-MC.
- Invariance: The learned metric is not invariant to linear transforms; discuss preprocessing (e.g., whitening) and its impact.
- Positioning within metric learning: Cite and compare to local metric learning and per-point diagonal metrics (e.g., Frome et al., Malisiewicz & Efros), and local PCA approaches; clarify conceptual differences (continuous Riemannian geometry, geodesics, and measure vs. purely distance-based methods).

Suggestions
- Add a computational complexity section with empirical runtimes across N, D, K, S, T; compare to intrinsic estimators and GMMs to guide method selection.
- Provide systematic σ selection (e.g., cross-validation, plug-in rules, or learn σ) and/or spatially adaptive bandwidths.
- Expand the mixture experiments: demonstrate K > 2, cluster separation on challenging nonconvex mixtures, and compare to mixture-of-intrinsic-normals and kernel mixtures.
- Explore approximations to accelerate M(x) and its derivatives (k-NN truncation, KD-trees, Nystrom, precomputed grids) and ODE solves (learned surrogates, probabilistic solvers [10]).
- Discuss failure modes (sparse sampling, noisy high-D data) and diagnostics (e.g., geodesic distortion metrics).

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptually novel and well-grounded bridge between learned geometry and probabilistic modeling.
- Solid algorithmic contributions with careful derivations.
- Empirically compelling on synthetic and real data; mixture formulation is timely and useful.
- High clarity and potential for impact as a Gaussian replacement in manifold-aware models.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficient analysis of computational complexity and scalability; unclear practical limits.
- Limited comparisons to strong geometric baselines in the main text.
- Sensitivity to σ not systematically addressed; selection currently ad hoc.

Overall, this is a strong and polished submission. Addressing scalability, σ selection, and stronger baselines would substantially strengthen the paper.
