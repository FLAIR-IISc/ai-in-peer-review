- Novelty: Introduces LAND, a parametric normal distribution using a learned Riemannian metric that bridges Riemannian statistics and manifold learning.
- Theoretical foundation: Method is grounded in Riemannian geometry and connects to existing statistics on manifolds (e.g., Pennec).
- Generative capability: Supports generative modeling and mixture extensions, enabling potential extrapolation beyond training data.
- Clarity: Paper is exceptionally well-written, clear, and mathematically consistent.
- Practical algorithm: Proposes a plausible MLE scheme using gradient descent and Monte Carlo integration, with an EM algorithm for mixtures.

- Scalability: Primary limitation is computational cost—per-point covariance estimation and inversion do not scale well to higher dimensions; “high-dimensional” is not clearly defined.
- Positioning in metric learning: Insufficient comparison to related metric learning and local PCA methods (e.g., Frome, Malisiewicz & Efros).
- Kernel bandwidth (σ) sensitivity: Lack of robustness analysis for σ and no adaptive selection strategy; unclear if σ should vary with density.
- Limited baselines: Visual comparisons mainly against poor-performing GMMs; intrinsic estimator results are relegated to supplementary and should be in the main paper.
- Missing computational comparison: No comparative analysis of computational complexity between LAND and intrinsic estimators.

- Arguments for acceptance: Novel, theoretically sound approach; excellent writing; promising initial experiments; valuable mixture model extension.
- Arguments against acceptance: Significant unresolved scalability concerns; inadequate comparisons to related methods and baselines; sensitivity to kernel bandwidth without robust optimization strategy.

- Overall assessment: Significant contribution but practical limitations and insufficient comparisons warrant acceptance only with major revisions.

- Required revisions: provide detailed complexity analysis (dimensionality scaling), expand related-work comparisons with metric learning methods, perform σ robustness/adaptive analyses, include intrinsic-estimator visual baselines in the main paper, add computational comparisons with intrinsic methods, and further develop the mixtures-of-LAND discussion.
