This paper proposes the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), obtained by replacing the Euclidean metric in a Gaussian with a smoothly varying, data-adaptive Riemannian metric, then fitting a Riemannian normal (and mixture) via MLE with Monte Carlo normalization. The idea connects Riemannian statistics (Pennec; Riemannian PPCA) with manifold learning (e.g., Isomap) by defining a continuous geometry that favors high-density regions and using geodesic structure in density evaluation and parameter learning. The application to EEG sleep staging and synthetic manifolds is compelling, and the maximum-entropy characterization under a given metric is attractive.

Quality: The technical development is mostly sound and the algorithms are clearly specified. However, the claimed “learned metric” is actually hand-crafted (Eq. 7) with fixed hyperparameters ρ and σ chosen empirically. The paper does not analyze sensitivity to σ, nor provide principled selection procedures. Eq. 7 is diagonal in ambient coordinates and not coordinate-invariant, which warrants discussion (e.g., diagonal vs full metric; invariance under linear transforms). Experimental comparisons are not fully fair: LAND is shown superior with few components, yet GMMs can match performance when allowed more components; model order should be chosen per method (e.g., BIC/AIC or validation) to avoid structural advantage. There is also no demonstration of LAND’s overfitting behavior as K grows, nor quantitative reporting of computational cost and scalability (complexity in N, D, K; timing; limits; a larger benchmark such as MNIST).

Clarity: The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The title is misleading; it suggests learning the metric, but the metric is constructed, not inferred. The manuscript should add intuition about how varying σ deforms the geometry and density, and how to select it.

Originality: The combination of a hand-crafted Riemannian metric with a properly normalized Riemannian normal/mixture and an MLE procedure is a useful synthesis. Related work on metric learning and manifold-based density modeling should be expanded (e.g., LMNN, ITML, neighborhood component analysis, surveys by Bellet et al.; probabilistic/diffusion-based metrics).

Significance: The approach is promising and likely of interest for generative modeling on manifolds, but its practical impact hinges on robust metric specification/learning and fair evaluation.

Pros:
- Conceptually appealing bridge between manifold learning and Riemannian statistics
- Maximum-entropy rationale; mixture EM with proper normalization
- Interesting applications and visuals; practical algorithmic recipe

Cons:
- Metric is not learned; title and framing overstate
- Sensitivity and selection of σ/ρ unaddressed; lack of invariance discussion
- Fairness of comparisons and overfitting analyses incomplete
- No scalability/cost quantification

Post-rebuttal: I remain concerned that LAND’s flexibility increases overfitting risk. Please revise experiments to select K per method via model selection, include curves showing overfitting for large K, and add σ-sensitivity analyses and runtime/scaling results.
