Summary
This paper proposes the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), a parametric family obtained by replacing the Euclidean/Mahalanobis distance in a Gaussian with a geodesic distance induced by a data-adaptive Riemannian metric. The metric is defined pointwise by the inverse of a local diagonal covariance estimated via Gaussian kernel smoothing (Eq. 7), so geodesics are “pulled” toward high-density regions. The authors derive an MLE procedure for the mean and covariance using (i) Monte Carlo estimation of the normalization constant by integrating over the tangent space at the mean via exponential maps, and (ii) gradient-based updates (and an EM algorithm for mixtures). Experiments on synthetic 2D manifolds and EEG sleep-stage features suggest LAND can model non-ellipsoidal structure better than GMMs and that ML-based LAND outperforms intrinsic LS estimators.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on Riemannian statistics (e.g., Pennec’s Riemannian normal and intrinsic means) and on manifold learning via learned metrics (e.g., Hauberg et al.). Compared to Riemannian mixture models on known manifolds (e.g., Simo-Serra et al.; Straub et al.), this work adapts the metric from data and deals with normalization constants rigorously. The idea is also related to density-sensitive metrics and anisotropic diffusion, diffusion maps, and metric learning.

Strengths
- Interesting and useful idea: using a smoothly varying, density-sensitive Riemannian metric to define a “curved Gaussian” that better matches manifold-structured data. The maximum-entropy characterization (given the metric) is appealing.
- Technical contributions: a practical ML scheme for mean/covariance with tangent-space Monte Carlo for the partition function; an EM for mixtures.
- Clear empirical visualizations showing how LAND components bend to the data; improved clustering alignment on EEG compared to GMM in the presented setups.
- Bridges manifold learning and Riemannian statistics in a way that could be reused in other generative models.

Weaknesses and concerns
1) Title/claims about “learning a metric”: The metric in Eq. 7 is hand-crafted via kernel smoothing with fixed hyperparameters σ (kernel width) and ρ (regularizer). There is no learning objective for M(x), nor are σ, ρ estimated by likelihood. The title and narrative should be revised to avoid implying that the metric is learned by optimization; alternatively, extend the method to learn σ, ρ (and possibly structure of M) jointly with the LAND parameters.

2) Sensitivity and selection of σ: The metric’s behavior (and thus geodesics, normalization constant, and likelihood) will be highly sensitive to σ. The paper acknowledges picking σ “as small as possible while ensuring smooth geodesics” and shows a limited sweep on EEG, but lacks:
- An intuitive discussion: small σ → highly curved, tightly hugging data; large σ → nearly Euclidean, LAND approaches Gaussian. What regimes are desirable?
- Principled selection: e.g., maximize validation likelihood/BIC over σ, plug-in rules from KDE (Scott/Silverman), local adaptive σ(x) based on kNN distances, or direct gradient-based optimization of σ/ρ by differentiating through m(µ, v) and the ODE solver (practical albeit heavier). At minimum, provide a sensitivity analysis and guidance.

3) Coordinate invariance and metric design: Eq. 7 uses diagonal local covariances; this is not invariant to rotations/rescalings, and results depend on preprocessing. Please discuss:
- Invariance properties (what transformations leave the model unchanged?).
- The trade-off behind diagonal vs full local covariance; whether a full metric or a locally whitened basis (e.g., from local PCA) could be supported, and the effect on geodesics and C(µ, Σ).
- Empirical robustness under random orthonormal transforms of the data.

4) Relation to broader metric/manifold learning literature: Cite and discuss connections to:
- Metric learning: LMNN (Weinberger & Saul), ITML (Davis et al.), Xing et al., and surveys (e.g., Kulis). Clarify differences: LAND uses an unsupervised, density-driven metric, but supervised/unsupervised metric learning could be integrated.
- Diffusion geometry/anistropic diffusion: diffusion maps (Coifman & Lafon), constructions of Riemannian metrics from data densities and Laplace–Beltrami operators.
This context would strengthen the positioning of Eq. 7 and possible alternatives.

5) Experimental comparison and model selection: The comparison appears to favor LAND with few components; GMMs can match performance with more components (even noted by the authors). For a fair comparison:
- Choose K per method via the same model selection criterion (e.g., BIC/AIC or held-out likelihood), allowing GMMs to use full/diagonal covariances comparable to LAND’s parameterization.
- Report test NLL (or BIC) vs K for both methods. If LAND truly “learns faster,” this should appear as better performance at small K, while GMM catches up as K grows.

6) Overfitting risk: LAND components can flex via both covariance and geometry. Please:
- Demonstrate overfitting with many LAND components via train vs test curves, and show that LAND is more prone to overfitting than GMM under comparable capacity.
- Clarify whether restricting M(x) to diagonal meaningfully regularizes the model.

7) Computational cost and scalability: The method repeatedly evaluates exponential maps and m(µ, v) for S Monte Carlo samples.
- Provide quantitative runtime/memory vs N, D, K, and S; include wall-clock times per iteration and per dataset, and accuracy vs S (error bars).
- Discuss limits: high-D kernel smoothing and ODE solves may be prohibitive. Suggest/assess practical accelerations (kNN sparsification for M(x), caching/local grids, minibatch Monte Carlo with control variates, quasi-MC, common random numbers, GPU ODEs).
- A medium-scale benchmark (e.g., MNIST features) would help demonstrate scalability boundaries.

Quality
- Sound construction and derivations; the Monte Carlo normalization strategy is reasonable. However, claims about metric “learning” are overstated. The experimental methodology needs a more careful, model-selection-based comparison and explicit overfitting/scalability analyses.

Clarity
- Generally well written and well organized. Please clarify invariance, justify diagonal M, and add an intuitive section on σ. Report implementation details (e.g., GMM covariance type; preprocessing/standardization; ODE solver tolerances; S sensitivity).

Originality
- The specific combination—data-adaptive Riemannian metric with a Riemannian normal and practical ML estimation—is novel and interesting, though built from known ingredients.

Significance
- Potentially useful primitive for generative modeling on manifolds and clustering. Impact depends on demonstrating robustness, fair baselines, and scalability.

Pros
- Elegant idea; principled statistical object (max-entropy on a given metric); clear algorithms; compelling qualitative fits.

Cons
- Hand-crafted, non-invariant metric with sensitive hyperparameters and limited guidance.
- Experimental comparisons not fully fair; overfitting and scalability not rigorously assessed.

Suggestions for revision
- Reframe title/claims or learn σ, ρ (and possibly richer M) by maximizing marginal likelihood; include sensitivity plots and guidance.
- Discuss and, if possible, experiment with invariance (rotations) and full vs diagonal M.
- Use BIC/held-out likelihood to select K per method; show train/test curves to illustrate overfitting.
- Provide quantitative cost/accuracy trade-offs in S and dataset size; add a larger dataset experiment or a clear scaling study.
- Expand related work to metric learning and diffusion geometry.

Questions for authors
- How sensitive are results to σ across datasets? Can you differentiate through the ODE and m(µ, v) to learn σ?
- What covariance type is used for the GMM baselines?
- How do results change under random rotations of the input?
- What is the complexity per EM/MLE iteration (as a function of N, D, K, S), and typical runtimes?

After-rebuttal note
Even if geodesics are nearly straight, LAND mixtures retain additional flexibility via the geometry, making them more prone to overfitting than GMMs at comparable K. Please revise experiments to use principled model selection per method, include train/test curves, and explicitly characterize overfitting.
