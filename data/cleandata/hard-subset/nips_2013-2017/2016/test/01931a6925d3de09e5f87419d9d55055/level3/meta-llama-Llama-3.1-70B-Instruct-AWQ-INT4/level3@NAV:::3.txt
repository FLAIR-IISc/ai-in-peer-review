This paper proposes a Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND) as a generalization of the normal distribution to the "manifold" setting, where data is assumed to lie near a potentially low-dimensional manifold embedded in R. The LAND is parametric, depending only on a mean and a covariance, and is the maximum entropy distribution under the given metric. The authors develop a maximum likelihood algorithm to infer the distribution parameters that relies on a combination of gradient descent and Monte Carlo integration.

The paper's main contribution is the construction of a Riemannian metric that adapts to the data, which is a novel approach to manifold learning. The authors demonstrate the efficiency of the LAND to fit non-trivial probability distributions over both synthetic data and EEG measurements of human sleep. The results show that the LAND learns faster the underlying true distribution than the Gaussian mixture model (GMM) and achieves better alignment with sleep stages.

Strengths:

* The paper proposes a novel approach to manifold learning by constructing a Riemannian metric that adapts to the data.
* The LAND is a parametric model that can be used for generative modeling, which is a significant advantage over traditional manifold learning methods.
* The authors provide a maximum likelihood algorithm to infer the distribution parameters, which is a well-established method in statistics.

Weaknesses:

* The title of the paper is misleading, as it claims to "learn a metric" but the metric is actually hand-crafted in Eq.7 with hyperparameters rho and sigma fixed empirically.
* The paper lacks discussion on the intuitive effect of varying sigma and how to select it.
* The experimental comparison is flawed, as LAND is advantaged with few components, but GMM can match with more.
* The paper does not provide quantitative measures of computational cost and scalability.

Suggestions for improvement:

* The authors should revise the title to reflect the actual contribution of the paper.
* The authors should provide more discussion on the effect of varying sigma and how to select it.
* The authors should revise the experimental comparison to make it more fair and provide quantitative measures of computational cost and scalability.
* The authors should consider merging metric learning with LAND learning so that rho and sigma can be learned rather than fixed.

Overall, this paper proposes a novel approach to manifold learning and provides a parametric model for generative modeling. However, it has some weaknesses that need to be addressed to make it a more solid contribution to the field.
