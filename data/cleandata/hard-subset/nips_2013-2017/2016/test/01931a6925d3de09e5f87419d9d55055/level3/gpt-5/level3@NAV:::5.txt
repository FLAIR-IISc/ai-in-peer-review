This paper proposes a Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND) by replacing the Euclidean/Mahalanobis distance in a Gaussian with a geodesic distance induced by a learned Riemannian metric. The metric is built from kernel-weighted local covariances, yielding a smooth geometry on RD that encourages geodesics to traverse high-density regions. The authors provide MLE for mean and covariance via gradient-based optimization and Monte Carlo estimation of the normalization constant in the tangent space, and extend to mixtures with an EM-like algorithm. The work connects Riemannian statistics (e.g., Pennec’s normal on known manifolds) with manifold learning (e.g., Isomap’s use of geodesic distance), and follows Hauberg et al. on data-adaptive metrics.

Strengths
- Conceptually appealing bridge between manifold learning and generative modeling; the idea of substituting geodesic distance (Isomap-like) within a maximum-entropy Riemannian normal is novel and promising.
- Clever computational device: tangent-space Monte Carlo avoids costly BVPs for log maps.
- Mixture extension and algorithmic details are clearly laid out at a high level.

Weaknesses and questions
- The inverse metric tensor in Eq. (7) appears ad hoc. Why this particular diagonal kernel-smoothed variance with additive ρ, and why diagonality? Please justify the choice, discuss invariance issues (sensitivity to scaling/rotations), and relate to KDE/information-geometric metrics or alternatives (e.g., full local covariances, mixtures of metrics).
- Please provide intuition for using a Gaussian kernel to weight local covariances in the sum; how are σ and ρ set, and how sensitive are results to them?
- Notational/semantic confusion: the “local covariance” defining the metric in Eq. (7) versus the model covariance Σ estimated in Eq. (11). Are these distinct quantities (geometry vs model), or is any form of MAP estimation of the metric implied? Clarify roles and training regimes.
- Experimental evaluation is limited. Beyond GMM and LS, comparisons to Isomap+GMM, Riemannian GMMs on known manifolds, spectral or kernel mixtures, and modern density models would help. Provide ablations on σ and S, sensitivity to D and N, and out-of-sample evaluation.
- Major scalability concern: complexity and runtime are not analyzed. Per-iteration cost scales with S geodesic IVPs per component; the mixture E-step seems expensive. Please report asymptotic complexity in N, D, K, S and wall-clock vs standard EM on all datasets.

Minor
- Algorithm 1, lines 4–5: use ∇ instead of d (or explicitly define the steepest-descent direction); d → ∇.

Decision arguments
Pros: novel and elegant formulation; principled maximum-entropy perspective; interesting geodesic-based distance; solid mathematical framing.
Cons: ad hoc metric choice insufficiently justified; weak empirical validation; unclear scalability and missing complexity/runtime comparisons; notation clarity issues.
