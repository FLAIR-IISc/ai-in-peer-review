- Introduces LAND: a probabilistic model replacing the Euclidean metric with a learned, smoothly varying Riemannian metric to model data on nonlinear manifolds.
- Novelty: combines Riemannian statistics and manifold learning to make a parametric distribution on a non-parametric space.
- Theoretical foundation: solid use of Riemannian geometry and connection to maximum entropy distributions.
- Algorithmic development: maximum likelihood estimation with scalable Monte Carlo normalization and an EM algorithm for mixtures.
- Empirical validation: synthetic and EEG sleep-stage experiments show LAND outperforms standard GMMs, especially for non-ellipsoidal data.
- Clarity: paper is very well written, well organized, and notation is consistent.
- Weakness — computational complexity: per-point covariance estimation/inversion may be costly, especially in higher dimensions; detailed scaling analysis is missing.
- Weakness — related work: discussion should better situate LAND among metric learning and local PCA approaches (e.g., per-point diagonal metrics).
- Weakness — σ sensitivity: Gaussian kernel bandwidth σ strongly affects the learned metric; robustness tests and adaptive σ methods are needed.
- Weakness — limited comparisons: main paper visualizations compare mainly to GMMs; intrinsic-estimator comparisons are relegated to supplementary material.
- Weakness — computational trade-offs: lacked comparison of computational cost and scalability between LAND and intrinsic estimators.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically sound, empirically promising, practical estimation algorithm, and excellent writing.
- Arguments against acceptance: concerns about computational complexity in high dimensions, σ sensitivity, and incomplete comparative analysis.
- Overall assessment: strong contribution with weaknesses that do not outweigh novelty and rigor.
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions; request detailed complexity analysis, expanded related work, σ robustness/adaptive methods, main-paper visualizations with intrinsic estimators, and discussion of computational trade-offs.
