This paper proposes a Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND) as an alternative to Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs). The LAND is grounded in Riemannian manifold theory, which provides a mathematical framework for modeling nonlinear data structures. The authors introduce a Maximum Likelihood Estimator (MLE) for the distribution parameters and evaluate the method with experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

The main idea of the paper is to replace the Euclidean distance in the ordinary normal distribution with a locally adaptive nonlinear distance measure. This is achieved by learning a non-parametric metric space, which is constructed using a local metric that is the inverse of a local covariance matrix. The locality is defined via a Gaussian kernel, allowing the manifold learning to be seen as a form of kernel smoothing.

The authors provide a thorough theoretical development of the LAND, including a derivation of the MLE estimator and a discussion of the relationship between the LAND and other Riemannian normal distributions. The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the LAND in capturing nonlinear data structures and outperforming GMMs in several cases.

However, the paper lacks clear, strong novelty compared to Simo-Serra et al. [19], which is not clearly explained early in the paper. The differences from the state-of-the-art are not clearly explained, and reference [19] is missing from the introduction. This makes it difficult to understand the contribution of the paper and how it advances the state-of-the-art.

In terms of quality, the paper is well-written, and the theoretical development is sound. The experiments are well-designed and demonstrate the effectiveness of the LAND. However, the paper could benefit from a clearer explanation of the novelty and contributions of the work.
