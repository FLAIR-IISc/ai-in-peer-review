This paper proposes LAND, a locally adaptive normal distribution, bridging Riemannian statistics and manifold learning. The core idea is to replace the Euclidean metric of the standard normal distribution with a learned Riemannian metric, favoring regions of high data density. The metric is constructed using local diagonal covariances estimated with a Gaussian kernel, and parameters are inferred via maximum likelihood estimation, including an EM algorithm for mixtures of LANDs. The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of Riemannian geometry with parametric density estimation is a novel approach.  The concept of a parametric distribution *over* a non-parametric metric is compelling.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** Grounding the method in Riemannian geometry provides a strong mathematical basis and links it to existing work in statistics on manifolds (Pennec [15]).
* **Generative Capability:**  The method’s generative aspect, particularly with mixtures, is highlighted as a strength, potentially allowing extrapolation beyond training data.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is remarkably clear, well-organized, and explains complex concepts accessibly.  The notation is consistent, and the mathematical derivations seem sound.
* **Practical Algorithm:**  The proposed maximum likelihood estimation scheme, combining gradient descent and Monte Carlo integration, seems practical and well-motivated.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Scalability:** The authors themselves acknowledge the primary limitation: computational cost.  Per-point covariance estimation and inversion become problematic in high dimensions.  The paper doesn't offer concrete solutions for scaling beyond moderate dimensionality, and the definition of “high-dimensional” remains vague. A more detailed complexity analysis is crucial. Is 3, 5, 10, or 100 dimensions considered “high”?
* **Positioning within Metric Learning:** The paper lacks sufficient context within the broader metric learning literature.  Similar per-point diagonal metrics (Frome et al., Malisiewicz & Efros) and local PCA approaches are mentioned but not thoroughly compared. A more comprehensive discussion of how LAND differs from and improves upon these existing methods is needed.
* **Kernel Bandwidth (σ) Sensitivity:** The choice of the Gaussian kernel bandwidth (σ) is a critical parameter, affecting locality. The paper doesn’t thoroughly investigate its robustness.  Is σ uniform across different density regions, or should it be adaptive? Sensitivity analyses are required.
* **Limited Baselines in Visualization:**  The visualizations primarily compare LAND against Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs), which consistently perform poorly in the presented examples. This makes it difficult to assess the true benefits of LAND. The authors acknowledge the superior performance of intrinsic estimators shown in the supplementary material and should include these in the main paper for a more informative comparison.
* **Missing Computational Comparison:** A crucial omission is a comparative analysis of computational complexity between LAND and established intrinsic estimators.  Providing such a comparison would help guide practitioners in choosing the appropriate method based on data characteristics and computational resources.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to density estimation, bridging two important fields.
* The writing quality is exceptional, making the complex concepts readily accessible.
* The initial experimental results are promising, demonstrating the potential of LAND to capture nonlinear data structures.
* The mixture model extension adds significant value and potential for broader applications.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The scalability concerns are significant and not adequately addressed. 
* The lack of a thorough comparison with related metric learning methods and more informative baselines weakens the paper’s claims.
* The sensitivity of the method to the kernel bandwidth and the absence of a robust optimization strategy for this parameter raise concerns.




**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a significant contribution to the field, offering a novel and theoretically grounded approach to density estimation. However, the practical limitations regarding scalability and the lack of thorough comparisons with existing methods raise concerns. I recommend acceptance with **major revisions**. The authors should address the following points:

1. **Expand the Complexity Analysis:** Provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of LAND, especially concerning the dimensionality of the input data.
2. **Strengthen Related Work Discussion:**  Thoroughly compare LAND with relevant metric learning and manifold learning techniques.
3. **Robustness Analysis of σ:**  Investigate the sensitivity of LAND to the choice of the kernel bandwidth (σ) and propose adaptive strategies for its selection.
4. **Improved Visualizations:** Replace or supplement the GMM comparisons with visualizations against intrinsic estimators (as shown in the supplementary material).
5. **Computational Comparison:** Include a computational comparison between LAND and intrinsic estimators.
6. **Expand Mixture Model Discussion:** Further explore and discuss the potential of mixtures of LANDs.



Addressing these revisions will significantly strengthen the paper and solidify its contribution to the field.
