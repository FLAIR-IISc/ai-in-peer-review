- Proposes the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND): a Gaussian-like density on a learned Riemannian metric with pM(x|µ,Σ) ∝ exp(−1/2 ⟨Logµ(x), Σ−1 Logµ(x)⟩).  
- Metric is constructed nonparametrically as a diagonal, kernel-smoothed local covariance (Eq. 7), attracting geodesics to high-density regions.  
- Normalization constant C(µ,Σ) is estimated by Monte Carlo integration on the tangent space using exponential maps to avoid expensive manifold integration.  
- MLE alternates updating µ via steepest descent with exponential maps and Σ via a Cholesky-like factor A with Σ−1 = AᵀA; a LAND mixture and EM algorithm are provided.  
- Experiments on synthetic manifold data and EEG sleep-stage features show improved likelihood and clustering alignment versus GMMs and intrinsic least-squares estimators.  
- Novelty: extends Riemannian normal work to a manifold-learning setting by using a data-adaptive metric to build a normalized, maximum-entropy Gaussian family.  
- Technical soundness: construction of C(µ,Σ) via tangent-space expectation and the gradient updates are correct and well-motivated.  
- Important point: EM accounts for component-specific normalization constants, which many works overlook.  
- Major concern: the metric is hand-crafted (not statistically learned) with two hyperparameters (σ,ρ) set empirically; title/claims should reflect this.  
- The metric is not coordinate-invariant (diagonal form): rotations/rescalings change the model, requiring more justification and ablation (diagonal vs full/structured metrics).  
- Sensitivity to σ is likely substantial; the paper lacks intuition, analysis, and principled selection strategies for σ (and ρ).  
- Experimental comparisons are unfairly biased by using few LAND components versus many GMM components; model selection (BIC/AIC/held-out likelihood) is needed for fairness.  
- Computational cost is high (thousands of Exp solves per iteration/component); no runtime, scaling, or variance-of-estimator analysis is reported.  
- Clarity is generally good for preliminaries and the role of C(µ,Σ), but missing details on σ/ρ selection, ODE solver settings, MC variance reduction, and convergence diagnostics.  
- Originality is moderate: a nontrivial combination of existing ideas to enable normalized generative modeling on learned metrics.  
- Significance is potential but limited by small-scale experiments, unclear high-dimensional practicality, and lack of principled hyperparameter learning.  
- Suggested improvements: clarify that the metric is constructed not learned; test invariance under rotations/scales; consider pre-whitening or learning low-rank/full metrics with shrinkage.  
- Suggested improvements: learn σ/ρ via cross-validation, bilevel hypergradient methods, or hierarchical priors; provide sensitivity curves and best practices.  
- Suggested improvements: use fair model selection for K, report held-out log-likelihoods, add baselines (GMMs in embeddings, KDE, mixtures on known manifolds), and ablate the effect of including C(µ,Σ).  
- Suggested improvements: analyze overfitting vs K, add regularization/prior on Σ and σ, and document train/test likelihood gaps.  
- Suggested improvements: report wall-clock times, ODE solve counts, MC estimator variance vs S, effect of solver tolerances, and performance at higher D; explore variance reduction and caching.  
- Theoretical clarifications requested: scope of the maximum-entropy claim (conditional on chosen metric), identifiability under σ changes, and links to information/probabilistic geometry.  
- Pros: principled, normalized density on a learned manifold, intuitive mean behavior, and reusable algorithmic contributions (tangent-space MC, gradients, EM).  
- Cons: metric is hand-crafted and coordinate-dependent, hyperparameters not learned, comparisons lack model selection, potential overfitting, and limited empirical breadth.  
- Recommendation: Weak reject — promising idea but needs fairer experiments, candid treatment of metric invariance and hyperparameter sensitivity, and compute/scaling evidence before acceptance.
