

**Review for "A Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution"**

This paper introduces the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), a generalization of the multivariate normal distribution that replaces the Euclidean metric with a locally adaptive Riemannian metric. The metric is constructed using a local covariance matrix defined via a Gaussian kernel (Eq. 7), creating a smoothly changing metric that favors regions of high data density. The LAND is defined as the maximum entropy distribution under this metric, and the authors develop maximum likelihood estimation and EM algorithms for mixture models.

The work builds on Pennec's Riemannian normal distribution [15] and Hauberg et al.'s metric learning [9], bridging statistics on Riemannian manifolds with manifold learning. It extends the Riemannian normal distribution to the case where the manifold is learned from data rather than known a priori.

**Strengths:**
- The mathematical foundations are sound and well-explained, with clear derivations of the normalization constant and optimization algorithms.
- The LAND model demonstrates clear advantages over Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) in capturing nonlinear data structures on both synthetic data and EEG sleep stage classification.
- The algorithms for maximum likelihood estimation and mixture modeling are practical and well-developed.

**Weaknesses:**
- The title and main claim are misleading: the paper claims to "learn a metric" but the metric (Eq. 7) is hand-crafted with fixed hyperparameters (rho and sigma), not learned. This significantly limits the approach's flexibility.
- The paper lacks discussion about the sensitivity of results to sigma, a critical hyperparameter. There's no intuition on how varying sigma affects the model or guidance on selecting it.
- The experimental comparison with GMM is flawed: LAND is compared with fewer components than GMM can use to achieve similar performance. A fair comparison would involve model selection (e.g., BIC) for each method.
- The metric uses a diagonal approximation, which is not coordinate-invariant. The paper needs to discuss implications of this choice and why a full covariance metric isn't used.
- The paper could better relate to the rich literature on metric learning and manifold learning connected to Riemannian geometry.

**Arguments for acceptance:**  
The core idea of adapting the normal distribution to a locally learned Riemannian metric is novel and valuable. The LAND model shows clear advantages on the presented experiments for capturing nonlinear data structures, particularly in revealing underlying manifold structure.

**Arguments against acceptance:**  
The main claim of "learning a metric" is undermined by the hand-crafted metric with fixed hyperparameters. The experimental comparison is not fair, as LAND is disadvantaged by using fewer components. The paper lacks a thorough discussion of critical hyperparameters and their impact. The approach's scalability and limitations are not adequately explored (e.g., no high-dimensional experiments).

**Overall assessment:**  
The paper presents an interesting extension of the Riemannian normal distribution to the case where the manifold is learned from data. However, the misleading title and flawed experimental comparison significantly undermine the paper's contribution. The metric is hand-crafted rather than learned, and the paper fails to discuss critical hyperparameters and their impact.

The paper's significance is limited by these issues, though the core idea has merit. I recommend major revisions to address these concerns: clarify that the metric is hand-crafted (not learned), provide a thorough discussion of the sigma hyperparameter, and redo experiments with fair model selection.

**Final Recommendation: Reject**
