This paper proposes the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), a generalization of the multivariate normal distribution designed for data lying on or near a non-linear manifold. The core idea is to replace the standard Euclidean metric with a locally adaptive Riemannian metric learned directly from the data. The work is well-grounded in Riemannian manifold theory, drawing on prior work in both manifold learning [9] and statistics on known manifolds [15]. The authors present a complete framework, including a maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) procedure for the distribution's parameters that cleverly uses Monte Carlo integration over the tangent space, and an extension to mixture models via an EM algorithm. The method is validated through compelling experiments on both synthetic and real-world EEG datasets, showing advantages over standard Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs).

**Strengths:**
*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound, presenting a complete and well-developed piece of work. The theoretical derivations are clear, and the claims are well-supported by a solid experimental evaluation.
*   **Clarity:** The manuscript is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introductory background on Riemannian geometry is helpful, and the proposed algorithms are detailed with sufficient clarity for reproducibility.
*   **Significance:** The proposed LAND model is a powerful and elegant tool for density estimation on non-linear data structures. By providing a parametric model over a non-parametric space, it addresses a fundamental problem and is likely to be useful for practitioners and a foundation for future research in manifold-aware generative modeling.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Originality:** The primary weakness is the unclear positioning with respect to prior art, specifically Simo-Serra et al. [19]. While this paper learns the manifold and [19] assumes it is known, this crucial distinction is not highlighted early on. The absence of ref. [19] in the introduction makes the novelty appear less significant than it is, as a reader familiar with the field might initially see this as an incremental step. The core contribution—bridging data-driven manifold learning with Riemannian density estimation—needs to be stated more forcefully and in contrast to existing work from the outset.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper with a significant contribution. However, its acceptance would be more certain if the authors revised the introduction and related work sections to clearly and explicitly differentiate their work from Simo-Serra et al. [19], thereby strengthening the claims of originality.
