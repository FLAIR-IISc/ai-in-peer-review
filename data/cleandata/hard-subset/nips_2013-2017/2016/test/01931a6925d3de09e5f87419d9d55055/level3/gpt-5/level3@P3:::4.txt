Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), a parametric density that replaces the Euclidean/Mahalanobis metric in the multivariate normal with a smoothly varying Riemannian metric learned from data. The metric is defined as the inverse of a locally smoothed (diagonal) covariance computed with a Gaussian kernel, following the manifold-learning viewpoint of Hauberg et al. [9]. The resulting density is the Riemannian normal (Pennec [15]) on the learned manifold. The authors derive a maximum likelihood (ML) estimator for the mean and covariance by (i) estimating the normalization constant via Monte Carlo integration in the tangent space at the mean (which avoids expensive boundary-value problems), and (ii) using gradient-based updates for the mean (steepest descent with exponential map) and covariance (reparameterized as A with Σ−1 = AᵀA). They also develop an EM algorithm for mixtures of LANDs.

Empirically, LAND is evaluated on synthetic data lying along a curved manifold and on real EEG sleep-stage data. LAND (and its mixture) model the non-ellipsoidal structure better than GMMs and outperform least-squares (intrinsic) estimators. On EEG, LAND yields higher alignment with sleep stages than GMMs for fixed and tuned kernel bandwidths.

Relative to prior work, this connects Riemannian statistics (e.g., [15, 23]) with manifold learning, moving from distributions on known manifolds to a data-adaptive learned metric. The most closely related paper is Simo‑Serra et al. [19], who propose mixtures of Riemannian normals on known manifolds with a geodesic EM; the present work differs by learning the manifold and by carefully handling normalization constants across components. However, the paper does not make this contrast sufficiently early, and [19] is not cited in the introduction.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation is coherent and grounded in Riemannian geometry. The tangent-space Monte Carlo estimator for the partition function is sensible and computationally advantageous. Gradients for mean and covariance are derived and implemented with appropriate manifold-aware steps. The mixture extension naturally mirrors GMM EM.
- Empirical support: Synthetic experiments convincingly demonstrate that LAND captures non-linear structure with fewer components. Real-data evaluation on EEG (unsupervised clustering) shows consistent improvements over GMMs; sensitivity to σ is explored.
- Caveats:
  - The learned metric is diagonal and defined by a kernel density–like smoother; this restricts expressivity and invariance (the authors note lack of linear invariance) and may over-simplify anisotropic local structure.
  - Computational cost and stability: Monte Carlo with S=3000 samples and repeated ODE solves for Exp/Log maps could be expensive in higher dimensions; more detailed time/memory complexity and scaling experiments would help. The sensitivity of results to S is deferred to the supplement; a brief main-text summary would improve reproducibility.
  - Baselines: Comparisons are primarily to GMMs and LS estimators. Given the generative aim, comparisons to graph geodesic-based kernels/densities or manifold-aware clustering baselines would strengthen the case. For mixtures, ensure GMMs are tuned with full/diag covariances and regularization parity.
  - Hyperparameters: σ selection is acknowledged as important; automated selection (e.g., likelihood, CV, or plug-in rules) would be valuable.

Clarity
- The paper is clearly written, with helpful geometric intuition and figures. Algorithm 1 is precise; the supplement fills in derivations.
- Suggestions:
  - Move a concise statement of differences to Simo‑Serra et al. [19] into the introduction and cite [19] there. Clarify early that the novelty is learned (non-parametric) metrics + ML estimation with proper normalization.
  - Provide a short complexity discussion (per-iteration cost in D, N, S, and K for mixtures) and practical runtimes.
  - Summarize in the main text the sensitivity to S and σ (now in the supplement).
  - Clarify solver choices and tolerances for ODEs; note any caching/approximation of Exp/Log to reduce cost.

Originality
- Moderate. The ingredients—Riemannian normal distributions [15], learned metrics for manifold learning [9], and mixture modeling—are known. The contribution lies in combining them into a practical, properly normalized ML estimator on a learned manifold, plus a mixture EM. The tangent-space Monte Carlo for the partition function and the practical optimization scheme are valuable. That said, the gap to [19] (mixtures on known manifolds with geodesic EM) is not emphasized strongly enough, which weakens the perceived novelty.

Significance
- The approach addresses an important limitation of Euclidean Gaussians for data near curved structures, and the results indicate improved modeling and clustering. The framework is likely to be of interest to researchers in generative modeling on manifolds and could influence adaptations of standard Gaussian-based methods (e.g., LDA, PCA) to learned Riemannian settings. Practical impact may be tempered by computational cost and the diagonal metric assumption; scaling to higher D or larger N will require careful engineering or alternative manifold learners.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, well-motivated formulation grounded in Riemannian geometry and maximum entropy.
- Sound ML estimator with careful handling of normalization; mixture extension with EM.
- Convincing synthetic and real-data results showing gains over GMMs.
- Bridges two communities (manifold learning and Riemannian statistics) in a useful way.

Cons for acceptance
- Novelty vs. Simo‑Serra et al. [19] is not clearly articulated early; [19] missing from introduction.
- Restrictive diagonal, kernel-smoothed metric; lack of linear invariance.
- Limited baseline coverage and limited discussion of computational scaling and sensitivity.
- σ selection is manual; no automated procedure demonstrated.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Please add [19] to the introduction and explicitly contrast goals, assumptions (known vs learned manifold), and handling of normalization.
- Can you report runtimes and scaling with D, N, S, K, and compare cost to GMM EM?
- How sensitive are results to σ and S across datasets? Could you learn σ by maximizing the ML objective or via cross-validation?
- Would using full local covariances (with shrinkage) materially improve performance, or is diagonal crucial for stability?
- Could you cache or approximate Exp/Log (e.g., via local quadratic metrics or learned surrogate models) to reduce cost?

Overall recommendation
A solid, well-executed paper with clear theory and good empirical evidence. The primary weakness is the positioning and perceived novelty relative to [19] and related Riemannian mixture models. With improved framing in the introduction and a brief complexity/sensitivity discussion, I lean toward acceptance.
