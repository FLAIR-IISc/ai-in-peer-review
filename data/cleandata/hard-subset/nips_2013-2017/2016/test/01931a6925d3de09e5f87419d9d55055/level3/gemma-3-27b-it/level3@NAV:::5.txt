## Review of "A Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution"

This paper proposes a novel approach to modeling data distributions by replacing the standard Euclidean metric in the multivariate normal distribution with a locally adaptive Riemannian metric learned from the data itself. The resulting Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND) aims to better capture the underlying manifold structure when data resides near a non-linear manifold. The authors extend this to mixtures of LANDs and demonstrate the method on both synthetic and EEG datasets. 

The core idea – leveraging geodesic distances to adapt the normal distribution – is interesting and builds on established work in manifold learning (Isomap [21]) and Riemannian statistics [15, 23]. The motivation for adapting the metric to local data density is sound, and the proposed LAND offers a potentially powerful generalization of the standard normal distribution. The extension to mixtures and the accompanying EM algorithm are also valuable contributions.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The definition of the inverse metric tensor in Eq. (7) feels *ad hoc* and lacks sufficient justification. While the authors mention a regularization parameter, the rationale for this specific form, particularly the direct inverse relationship, isn’t clearly articulated.  Furthermore, the intuition behind using a Gaussian kernel to weight local covariances (Eq. 7) could be strengthened; why this particular choice, and what are its implications? There’s also some ambiguity surrounding Eq. (7) and Eq. (11): it's unclear whether they both define the same local covariance, or if one is a MAP estimate of the other – this needs clarification. 

The experimental evaluation is the most significant concern. While the synthetic experiments show some benefit, they aren't comprehensive enough. The real-world EEG analysis, while relevant, lacks a strong comparison to more standard methods beyond a GMM. Critically, there is no discussion of computational complexity or runtime, making it difficult to assess the scalability of the method.  The authors claim scalability through tangent space integration but provide no empirical evidence. A comparison to the standard EM algorithm for GMMs on comparable datasets would be crucial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:** Novel theoretical contribution, interesting connection between manifold learning and Riemannian statistics.
**Arguments against Acceptance:** Weak experimental evaluation, questionable scalability, lack of justification for certain design choices (Eq. 7), ambiguity in notation.

**Overall:** This paper presents a theoretically interesting contribution, but its practical applicability remains unproven. The lack of thorough experimental validation and discussion of computational efficiency raises serious concerns. While the idea is promising, significant revisions are needed before it would be suitable for NIPS. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to address the outlined weaknesses.  A minor correction: Algorithm 1, lines 4–5, should be ∇ instead of d.
