The paper "A Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution" proposes a novel approach to modeling observation spaces with a hand-crafted Riemannian metric, defining a curved normal (LAND) to better capture data manifolds. This application is both useful and interesting, as it bridges work on statistics on Riemannian manifolds with manifold learning.

The authors' idea to replace the linear Euclidean distance in the ordinary normal distribution with a locally adaptive nonlinear distance measure is well-motivated and has the potential to improve upon traditional Gaussian mixture models. The paper is well-structured and easy to follow, with a clear introduction to the problem, a thorough explanation of the proposed method, and a discussion of related work.

However, there are several concerns that need to be addressed. Firstly, the title of the paper is misleading, as it claims to "learn a metric" but the metric is actually hand-crafted in Eq. 7 with hyperparameters rho and sigma fixed empirically. This lack of clarity may lead to confusion among readers.

Furthermore, the paper lacks a discussion on the intuitive effect of varying sigma and how to select it. Sigma is likely to be sensitive, and its value can significantly impact the performance of the LAND model. The authors should provide more insight into the choice of sigma and its implications on the model's behavior.

Another issue is that the Eq. 7 metric is not coordinate-invariant, which may lead to inconsistent results. The authors should discuss the implications of this choice and consider alternative metrics that are more robust.

Additionally, the experimental comparison with Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) is flawed, as the LAND model is advantaged with few components, while GMMs can match with more. The authors should consider choosing the number of components per method via model selection (e.g., BIC) for a fair comparison.

The paper would also benefit from a demonstration of LAND overfitting with many components and a quantitative measure of computational cost and scalability (e.g., limits, experiments on MNIST). This would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the model's strengths and limitations.

In terms of related work, the authors should relate and cite richer literature on metric learning and manifold learning connected to Riemannian geometry. This would provide a more complete picture of the research landscape and help to contextualize the contributions of the paper.

Overall, the paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of machine learning, but it requires revisions to address the concerns mentioned above.
