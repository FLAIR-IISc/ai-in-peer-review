

This paper proposes LAND (Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution), a novel approach that bridges Riemannian statistics with manifold learning by defining a parametric distribution over a nonparametric metric space. The authors model the metric as a Riemannian normal distribution, learning mean and covariance via maximum likelihood while using geodesic distances defined by local inverse diagonal covariances. The work extends to mixture models and demonstrates generative extrapolation capabilities.

The paper makes a valuable contribution by formalizing a maximum entropy distribution on learned Riemannian manifolds, addressing a gap between Riemannian statistics (Pennec) and manifold learning (Isomap). The writing is exceptionally clear and well-organized, making complex geometric concepts accessible.

Strengths include the elegant theoretical foundation, the practical Monte Carlo integration approach for normalization, and the promising empirical results on synthetic and EEG data. The mixture extension is particularly compelling and merits further exploration.

However, several concerns warrant attention. The paper lacks clarity on scalability: it doesn't specify what constitutes "high-dimensional" data (e.g., 10 vs. 100 dimensions) or provide computational complexity analysis against intrinsic estimators. The experimental comparisons against GMMs (which perform poorly) are misleading; the authors should replace these with comparisons against intrinsic estimators (shown in supplementary material to outperform GMMs). The positioning within metric learning literature is insufficient—similar per-point diagonal metrics (Frome et al., Malisiewicz & Efros) and local PCA approaches aren't adequately contrasted. The Gaussian kernel bandwidth sigma is treated as a fixed hyperparameter without robustness analysis or discussion of its uniformity across density regions.

While the theoretical foundation is sound and the writing excellent, these omissions limit the paper's impact. The computational challenges for large-scale applications remain unaddressed, and the significance is somewhat diminished by the lack of comprehensive comparisons. With proper scaling analysis, clearer positioning, and more informative visualizations, this work could make a stronger contribution to the field.
