This paper proposes the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), a generalization of the multivariate normal distribution designed to model data lying on or near a nonlinear manifold. The core idea is to replace the standard Euclidean metric (or Mahalanobis distance) with a locally adaptive Riemannian metric. This metric is constructed non-parametrically from the data itself, using a kernel-weighted local covariance, causing geodesics to be "pulled" towards high-density regions. The LAND is then defined as the maximum entropy distribution on this learned Riemannian manifold, analogous to Pennec's [15] Riemannian normal distribution. The authors present a complete framework, including a maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) procedure for the distribution's mean and covariance, which cleverly combines gradient-based optimization with Monte Carlo integration over the tangent space to handle the complex normalization constant. The model is further extended to a mixture of LANDs with a corresponding EM algorithm. The approach is evaluated on synthetic and real-world EEG data, demonstrating its ability to capture complex, non-linear data structures more effectively than standard Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs).

This work bridges the fields of manifold learning and statistics on Riemannian manifolds, a valuable and interesting direction. Prior work has extensively studied statistics on known, pre-defined manifolds (e.g., spheres, spaces of SPD matrices), while this paper applies these statistical tools to a manifold that is itself learned from the data.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete and well-thought-out contribution. The theoretical development, grounded in Riemannian geometry, is solid. The derivation of the MLE procedure, including the gradients for the mean and covariance (Eq. 12, 13) and the Monte Carlo method for the normalization constant (Eq. 10), is thorough and provides a practical algorithm for a complex problem.
2.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. It begins with a concise and helpful primer on Riemannian geometry, which makes the subsequent material accessible. The motivation is clear, the methodology is presented logically, and the algorithms are laid out explicitly. The figures, particularly the visualizations of the geodesics and density contours (Fig. 5, 6), are effective at illustrating the model's behavior and advantages.
3.  **Experimental Validation:** The experiments on both synthetic and real-world data are compelling. The synthetic examples clearly show how LAND can model non-linear structures with a single component where a GMM would require several, and how it can capture non-ellipsoidal clusters. The application to EEG sleep stage data demonstrates the model's utility on a real, challenging problem, showing improved clustering performance over GMMs.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Originality and Positioning:** The primary weakness of the paper is its positioning with respect to prior work, specifically Simo-Serra et al. [19]. This reference, which also explores mixtures of Riemannian normal distributions, is critically important for context but is mentioned only in the "Related Work" section at the very end of the paper. The introduction should clearly and upfront differentiate the proposed work from [19]. The key distinction appears to be that this paper *learns* the manifold from the data, whereas [19] assumes the manifold is known a priori. This is a significant and novel contribution, but its impact is diminished by the failure to frame it properly. The claim in Section 5 that Simo-Serra et al. [19] do not account for normalization constants also seems to be a key difference that should be highlighted much earlier and more prominently.
2.  **Clarity of Contribution:** As a result of the above point, the paper's core novelty is not as clear as it should be from the outset. A reader familiar with the literature might initially see this as an incremental step. By placing the "Related Work" section at the end, the authors miss the crucial opportunity to frame their contribution in the introduction and motivate why learning the manifold is a challenging and important extension.
3.  **Practical Limitations:** The metric construction depends on a kernel bandwidth parameter `σ`, and the paper provides little guidance on how to set it. The EEG experiment shows that performance is sensitive to this parameter (Table 1, comparing LAND(1) with LAND(best)), but an ad-hoc search or oracle selection was used. A more principled method for selecting `σ` (e.g., via cross-validation or marginal likelihood) would significantly improve the method's practicality. The authors also rightly acknowledge that the computation of logarithm maps is a bottleneck, which may limit scalability.

**Detailed Comments:**

-   The choice of a diagonal covariance for the local metric in Eq. 7 is justified by computational efficiency and preventing overfitting. It would be interesting to briefly discuss the potential trade-offs, i.e., what modeling capacity is lost by not using a full covariance.
-   In the mixture model (Section 3.4), the normalization constant C(µk, Σk) for each component depends on the shared, data-driven manifold. This is an interesting contrast to a standard GMM where components are independent. It might be worth a short comment on the implications of this shared structure.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper describing an elegant and powerful extension of the normal distribution to learned non-linear manifolds. The theoretical work is sound, the presentation is clear, and the experiments are convincing. The main drawback is the suboptimal positioning of the work relative to existing literature, which obscures the novelty of the contribution. This is a correctable flaw, and I would strongly encourage the authors to revise the introduction to better contextualize their work against key references like [19]. Despite this issue, the paper represents a strong scientific contribution to the field.
