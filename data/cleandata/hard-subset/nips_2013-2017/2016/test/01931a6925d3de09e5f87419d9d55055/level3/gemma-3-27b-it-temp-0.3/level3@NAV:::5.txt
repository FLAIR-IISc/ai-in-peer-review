## Review of “A Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution”

This paper proposes a novel extension to the multivariate normal distribution, the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), which replaces the standard Mahalanobis distance with a learned geodesic distance based on a locally adaptive Riemannian metric. The authors extend this to mixtures (LANDs) and demonstrate its performance on synthetic and EEG data. The core idea of adapting the metric to the data’s manifold structure, akin to Isomap, is interesting and represents a potentially valuable contribution to probabilistic modeling of non-linear data. The work builds upon existing research in Riemannian statistics and manifold learning, bridging these areas in a compelling way.

The paper’s strength lies in its theoretical foundation. The authors correctly position the LAND as a maximum entropy distribution under the learned metric, grounding it in established statistical principles. The proposed Monte Carlo integration scheme for normalizing the density appears to be a pragmatic approach to a challenging problem. However, the justification for the definition of the inverse metric tensor in Eq. (7) feels somewhat *ad hoc*. While the Gaussian kernel weighting local covariances is a common technique, the intuition behind its specific application here – why a Gaussian weighting is optimal or appropriate – is not clearly articulated. Furthermore, there’s a lack of clarity regarding the relationship between the local covariance defined in Eq. (7) and its MAP estimation in Eq. (11); it’s unclear if these represent the same quantity or different stages of estimation.

The experimental evaluation is a significant weakness. While the synthetic data experiments show some improvement over GMMs, the analysis lacks depth. The EEG data experiments are promising, but the parameter selection (σ) feels somewhat arbitrary and lacks a robust justification. Critically, there’s no discussion of computational complexity or runtime comparison to standard EM, raising major concerns about scalability and practical applicability. The authors acknowledge this implicitly, but a more thorough analysis is needed. The minor typo in Algorithm 1 (d -> ∇) is easily corrected.

**Arguments for acceptance:** Novel theoretical contribution, bridging Riemannian geometry and manifold learning; potentially useful extension to standard probabilistic models.

**Arguments against acceptance:** Weak experimental validation; lack of scalability analysis; unclear justification for certain design choices (metric tensor definition, Gaussian kernel weighting); confusion regarding covariance definitions.

**Overall:** This paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution, but its practical applicability remains questionable due to the limited experimental evaluation and concerns about computational efficiency. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to strengthen the experimental validation, provide a more rigorous analysis of computational complexity, and clarify the theoretical justifications for key design choices.
