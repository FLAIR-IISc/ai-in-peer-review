The paper "A Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution" proposes a novel extension of the multivariate normal distribution to the manifold learning setting, where data lies near a potentially low-dimensional manifold embedded in R^D. The authors introduce the locally adaptive normal distribution (LAND), which replaces the Euclidean metric in the multivariate normal with a locally adaptive Riemannian metric. This allows the LAND to capture nonlinear relationships in the data and adapt to regions of high local density.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the background and motivation for their work. The technical sections are well-organized, and the notation is consistent throughout the paper. The authors also provide a thorough review of related work, including manifold learning and Riemannian statistics.

The key contributions of the paper are the introduction of the LAND and the development of a maximum likelihood estimation procedure for its parameters. The authors also extend the LAND to mixture models and provide the corresponding EM algorithm. The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the LAND in modeling nonlinear data manifolds and its advantages over traditional Gaussian mixture models.

The strengths of the paper include:

* The introduction of a novel and well-motivated extension of the multivariate normal distribution to the manifold learning setting.
* The development of a maximum likelihood estimation procedure for the LAND parameters, which is both scalable and efficient.
* The extension of the LAND to mixture models and the provision of the corresponding EM algorithm.
* The thorough evaluation of the LAND on both synthetic and real data, including EEG measurements of human sleep stages.

The weaknesses of the paper include:

* The assumption of a diagonal covariance matrix for computational efficiency and to prevent overfitting, which may not be suitable for all applications.
* The use of a Gaussian kernel to define the locality of the covariance matrix, which may not be the most effective choice for all datasets.
* The lack of a clear discussion on the choice of the parameter σ used for smoothing the Riemannian metric, which may require further investigation.
