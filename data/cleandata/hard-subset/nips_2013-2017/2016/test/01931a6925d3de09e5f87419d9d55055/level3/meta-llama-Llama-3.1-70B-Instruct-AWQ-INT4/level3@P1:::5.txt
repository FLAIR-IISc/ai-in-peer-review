This paper proposes a novel approach to modeling data distributions by introducing a locally adaptive normal distribution (LAND) that leverages a learned geodesic manifold traversal distance to replace the traditional Mahalanobis distance. The authors extend this concept to mixtures of LANDs and evaluate its performance on both synthetic and EEG data. The key idea of using a geodesic distance, akin to Isomap, is interesting and promising.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a thorough introduction to the concepts of Riemannian manifolds and geodesic distances. The mathematical derivations are sound, and the proposed algorithm for maximum likelihood estimation is well-explained.

However, there are some concerns regarding the practical applicability of the proposed method. The definition of the inverse metric tensor in Eq. (7) appears ad hoc and needs more justification. The use of a Gaussian kernel to weight local covariances in the weighted sum is not thoroughly explained, and it would be beneficial to provide more intuition behind this choice.

Additionally, there seems to be some confusion between the local covariance defined in Eq. (7) and the MAP estimation of that quantity in Eq. (11). It is unclear if they refer to the same thing, and further clarification would be helpful.

The experimental evaluation is weak and insufficiently thorough. While the results on synthetic data are encouraging, the evaluation on EEG data is limited, and more extensive experiments would be necessary to fully assess the performance of the proposed method.

A major concern is the scalability and computational efficiency of the proposed method. The authors do not provide any complexity or runtime comparison to standard EM algorithms, which makes it difficult to assess the practical applicability of the method.

Overall, the paper makes an interesting theoretical contribution, but its practical applicability is questionable. With some further refinement and more thorough evaluation, the proposed method could potentially become a valuable tool for modeling complex data distributions.

Strengths:

* Novel approach to modeling data distributions using a learned geodesic manifold traversal distance
* Well-written and thorough introduction to Riemannian manifolds and geodesic distances
* Sound mathematical derivations and clear explanation of the proposed algorithm

Weaknesses:

* Ad hoc definition of the inverse metric tensor
* Lack of intuition behind the use of a Gaussian kernel to weight local covariances
* Confusion between local covariance and MAP estimation
* Weak and insufficiently thorough experimental evaluation
* Concerns about scalability and computational efficiency

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper makes a novel and interesting theoretical contribution to the field of machine learning
* The proposed method has the potential to be a valuable tool for modeling complex data distributions

Arguments against acceptance:

* The practical applicability of the proposed method is questionable due to concerns about scalability and computational efficiency
* The experimental evaluation is weak and insufficiently thorough
* Further refinement and more thorough evaluation are necessary to fully assess the performance of the proposed method.

Recommendation:

* Accept the paper, but require the authors to address the concerns about scalability and computational efficiency
* Request additional experimental evaluation to fully assess the performance of the proposed method
* Suggest that the authors provide more intuition behind the use of a Gaussian kernel to weight local covariances and clarify the confusion between local covariance and MAP estimation.
