This paper proposes a locally adaptive normal distribution (LAND) that generalizes the normal distribution to the manifold setting. The LAND is a parametric distribution that depends on a mean and a covariance, but is defined on a non-parametric Riemannian manifold. The authors develop a maximum likelihood algorithm to infer the distribution parameters, which relies on a combination of gradient descent and Monte Carlo integration.

The paper starts by reviewing the multivariate normal distribution and its limitations when modeling nonlinear data. The authors then introduce the concept of Riemannian manifolds and the Riemannian normal distribution, which is a natural extension of the normal distribution to the manifold setting. They provide a brief summary of Riemannian geometry and the intrinsic least squares estimators for the mean and covariance.

The authors then propose a locally adaptive normal distribution (LAND) that replaces the linear Euclidean distance with a locally adaptive Riemannian distance. They construct a metric that captures the nonlinear structure of the data and enables the computation of geodesics. The LAND is defined as the Riemannian normal distribution with the learned metric.

The paper presents a maximum likelihood algorithm to infer the distribution parameters, which involves optimizing the mean and covariance using gradient descent and Monte Carlo integration. The authors also extend the LAND to mixture models and provide the corresponding EM algorithm.

The experiments demonstrate the efficiency of the LAND in fitting non-trivial probability distributions over both synthetic data and EEG measurements of human sleep. The results show that the LAND learns faster the underlying true distribution than the Gaussian mixture model (GMM) and achieves better alignment with the sleep stages.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the proposed method. The use of Riemannian geometry and the locally adaptive normal distribution is novel and interesting. The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in modeling nonlinear data.

However, there are some concerns and suggestions for improvement. The selection of the sigma parameter appears unclear and may be tuned on training data. The paper lacks sufficient analysis and explanation of the practical usefulness of the learned distribution. The contribution of the paper mainly combines prior work on kernel metric, geodesic estimation, and normal distribution, and provides estimation algorithms.

The evaluation of the method is limited, and the paper lacks comparisons with other methods when the data are generated by standard Gaussians or GMMs. The scalability of the method is also a concern, as the experiments are limited to low-dimensional data. The notation around Sigma vs M = Sigma^-1 and the role of the local kernel matrix versus a fitted covariance is confusing.

Overall, the paper is interesting and useful, and the introduction of Riemannian geometry is well-explained for readers familiar with the topic. The proposed method is novel and has the potential to be applied to various machine learning problems. However, the paper requires further clarification and improvement in terms of the selection of the sigma parameter, the practical usefulness of the learned distribution, and the evaluation of the method.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel and interesting method for modeling nonlinear data using Riemannian geometry.
* The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in fitting non-trivial probability distributions.
* The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the proposed method.

* The selection of the sigma parameter appears unclear and may be tuned on training data.
