This paper proposes a Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), a parametric model built upon a non-parametric Riemannian metric learned from the data. The authors bridge the gap between Riemannian statistics and manifold learning, offering a novel approach to density estimation that adapts to the underlying data structure. The key idea is to replace the Euclidean metric in the standard normal distribution with a locally adaptive, smoothly changing metric, effectively defining a Riemannian normal distribution. The paper provides a maximum likelihood estimation procedure for the LAND, extending it to mixture models with a corresponding EM algorithm, and demonstrates its efficacy on both synthetic and real-world EEG data.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and a pleasure to read. The motivation is clearly articulated, and the technical details are presented in a digestible manner, building upon the necessary foundations of Riemannian geometry. The connection to existing work in both manifold learning and Riemannian statistics is thoughtfully discussed, positioning the LAND within the broader research landscape. The extension to mixture models is a natural and valuable addition, and the empirical results demonstrate the potential of the approach.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The LAND represents a novel combination of Riemannian statistics and manifold learning, offering a principled approach to non-parametric density estimation. The idea of adapting the metric to the local data density is compelling and potentially powerful.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The work is grounded in a strong mathematical framework, leveraging the properties of Riemannian manifolds and maximum entropy distributions.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed maximum likelihood estimation procedure, combining gradient descent and Monte Carlo integration, appears technically sound and well-motivated.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, making complex concepts accessible to a broad audience. The figures are informative and well-designed.
*   **Generative Capability:** The approach allows for extrapolation beyond the training data, a key advantage over non-parametric methods that are limited to the observed data points.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Complexity:** The computational cost of per-point covariance estimation and inversion is a significant concern, particularly for high-dimensional data. The paper acknowledges this but lacks a detailed analysis of the complexity and a clear definition of what constitutes “high-dimensional” in this context (e.g., 3, 5, 10, 100 dimensions).
*   **Related Work Positioning:** While the paper acknowledges related work, it could benefit from a more thorough discussion of existing metric learning techniques, specifically those employing per-point diagonal metrics (e.g., Frome et al., Malisiewicz & Efros) and local PCA approaches. A more detailed comparison would help clarify the unique contributions of the LAND.
*   **Bandwidth Sensitivity:** The choice of the Gaussian kernel bandwidth (σ) for defining locality is crucial. The paper lacks a systematic investigation of the robustness of the approach to different σ values and whether a uniform σ is appropriate across regions of varying density. Adaptive bandwidth selection strategies should be considered.
*   **Limited Comparison Baseline:** The visual comparisons in Figs. 5 and 6 are primarily against Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs), which consistently perform poorly. While this highlights the advantage of the LAND, it doesn’t provide a comprehensive assessment. Including comparisons to intrinsic least squares estimators, which are briefly mentioned and appear to perform well (as seen in the supplementary material), would provide a more informative evaluation.
*   **Complexity Trade-offs:** A discussion of the computational complexity trade-offs between LAND and intrinsic estimators (like those in Pennec [15]) would be valuable for guiding method choice based on data structure and computational resources.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The LAND presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to non-parametric density estimation.
*   The paper is exceptionally well-written and provides a clear and compelling presentation of the method.
*   The empirical results demonstrate the potential of the LAND to capture complex data distributions.
*   The extension to mixture models is a significant contribution.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The computational complexity of the LAND is a concern, particularly for high-dimensional data.
*   The lack of a thorough comparison to intrinsic estimators limits the assessment of the LAND's performance relative to existing methods.
*   The sensitivity to the bandwidth parameter (σ) requires further investigation.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential. The proposed LAND is a novel and well-motivated approach to density estimation, and the paper is exceptionally well-written. However, the concerns regarding computational complexity, comparison to alternative methods, and bandwidth sensitivity should be addressed. I recommend acceptance contingent upon the authors addressing these points. Specifically, I suggest: 1) providing a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity; 2) including comparisons to intrinsic estimators in the main paper; and 3) investigating the robustness of the approach to different bandwidth settings. Expanding on these points will further strengthen the paper and increase its impact on the field.
