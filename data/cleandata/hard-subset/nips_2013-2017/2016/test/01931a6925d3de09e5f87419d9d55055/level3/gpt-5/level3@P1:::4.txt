This paper introduces the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), which replaces the Euclidean/Mahalanobis metric in the multivariate normal with a smoothly varying, data-adaptive Riemannian metric. The resulting density has the familiar exponential-quadratic form but uses geodesic distances and is properly normalized with respect to the manifold-induced measure. The metric is learned nonparametrically via local (diagonal) covariances under a Gaussian kernel; geodesics therefore tend to follow high-density regions. The authors derive a maximum-likelihood (ML) estimator for the mean and covariance by combining Monte Carlo estimation of the normalization constant—performed efficiently in the tangent space—with gradient-based optimization; they further extend to a mixture of LANDs and provide an EM algorithm. Experiments on synthetic manifolds and EEG sleep-stage data show that LAND can model non-elliptical structure and outperform Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) and least-squares (intrinsic) estimators.

Relation to prior work: Riemannian normal distributions and intrinsic statistics on known manifolds are well established [15, 23]. Mixtures on manifolds are also known [19, 20]. The main contribution here is to bring these ideas to the manifold-learning setting by learning a Riemannian metric from data (cf. Hauberg et al. [9, 22]) and performing full ML inference, including correct normalization constants, on that learned manifold. This bridges manifold learning (e.g., Isomap [21]) and Riemannian statistics and provides a generative model with geodesics defined continuously in RD.

Quality: The technical development is sound and careful. The normalization constant is treated correctly via tangent-space Monte Carlo, avoiding the expensive logarithm map; gradients for both mean and covariance (via Σ−1 = AᵀA) are derived; the preconditioned steepest-descent update is sensible given conditioning issues. The mixture EM is consistent (unlike [19], which ignores component-dependent normalizers). Empirically, LAND captures nonlinear structure, avoids low-density means typical of intrinsic least squares, and performs better than GMMs on synthetic manifolds and achieves higher F-measure on sleep-stage clustering. Limitations include computational cost (thousands of exponential maps per likelihood evaluation), sensitivity to the kernel width σ, and the diagonal, non-invariant local covariance choice.

Clarity: The paper is generally very well written and organized. The geometric preliminaries and algorithmic choices are explained clearly; pseudocode aids reproducibility. However, differences from the most closely related work [19] should be highlighted earlier (currently deferred to Related Work), and the introduction should cite [19] explicitly. A brief complexity/runtime discussion and practical guidance on σ and the regularizer ρ would improve clarity.

Originality: The constituent pieces (Riemannian normals, learned Riemannian metrics, mixtures) exist in the literature, but their combination into a parametric, ML-trained generative model on a learned manifold—with careful treatment of normalization—is novel. That said, the novelty over [19] and prior Riemannian mixture models may feel incremental to some readers; the key distinction (learned metric + proper normalization on a data-adaptive manifold) should be emphasized and empirically ablated.

Significance: A locally adaptive, maximum-entropy normal with a tractable ML estimator is conceptually appealing and could impact density modeling and clustering where data lie near nonlinear structures. The experimental evidence is positive but modest in scope (low to moderate D, small to mid-size datasets). Scalability concerns (ODE solves per MC sample, σ selection) may limit immediate uptake; broader comparisons (e.g., kernel density with geodesic distances, graph-based generative models) would strengthen the case.

Strengths
- Clear, principled construction of a distribution on a learned manifold; correct handling of manifold measure and normalization.
- Practical ML algorithm combining tangent-space Monte Carlo and preconditioned gradients; mixture extension with EM.
- Demonstrates advantages over GMMs and intrinsic LS estimators on synthetic manifolds; solid real-data case study on EEG sleep.
- Bridges two active areas (manifold learning and Riemannian statistics) and provides a portable template for other models.

Weaknesses
- Novelty vs. prior manifold mixture work [19, 20] not made explicit early; [19] absent from the introduction.
- Computational scalability is a concern (thousands of Exp map evaluations per iteration; dependence on σ; diagonal metric).
- Limited ablations: sensitivity to σ and ρ, to number of MC samples S, and to using diagonal vs. full local covariances.
- Metric is not linearly invariant; preprocessing (e.g., whitening) and its effect are not discussed.
- Theoretical guarantees (consistency, convergence rates, bias from learned metric) are not provided.

Suggestions for improvement
- Move the discussion differentiating LAND from [19, 20] into the introduction; clearly state that you learn the metric and correctly include component-dependent normalizers.
- Add an ablation on σ, S, and ρ, and a short runtime/memory analysis; report wall-clock times and solver details.
- Discuss preprocessing (PCA/whitening) to mitigate non-invariance; consider evaluating a full or low-rank local covariance metric.
- Provide guidance or a data-driven procedure for selecting σ (e.g., cross-validation, likelihood on held-out data).
- Include more diverse baselines (e.g., graph-geodesic-based GMMs, kernel density along manifolds).

Arguments for acceptance
- Principled, well-executed method that unifies learned Riemannian geometry with generative modeling.
- Correct probabilistic treatment (normalization constants) and practical optimization strategy.
- Clear empirical gains on problems where Gaussian assumptions fail.

Arguments against acceptance
- Incremental feel relative to existing Riemannian mixture literature; differentiation could be stronger.
- Scalability and hyperparameter sensitivity not fully addressed; experiments limited in breadth.
- Some key implementation details and ablations deferred to the supplement.

Questions for the authors
- How does performance and runtime scale with dimensionality and dataset size? Any success above D≈20?
- How sensitive are results to σ and S across datasets? Can σ be learned jointly?
- Did you try non-diagonal local covariances (e.g., low-rank + diagonal) for the metric?
- How stable is the ML estimator across random initializations for mixtures?

Overall: A solid and thoughtfully executed paper with clear contributions, though the novelty relative to prior manifold mixtures should be clarified and experimental breadth expanded. I lean toward borderline accept if the positioning and empirical analysis are strengthened.
