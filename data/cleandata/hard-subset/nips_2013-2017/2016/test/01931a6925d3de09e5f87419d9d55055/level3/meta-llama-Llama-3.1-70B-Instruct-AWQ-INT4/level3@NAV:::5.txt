This paper proposes a locally adaptive normal distribution (LAND) that replaces the linear Euclidean distance in the ordinary normal distribution with a locally adaptive nonlinear distance measure. The LAND is a generalization of the normal distribution to the “manifold” setting, where data is assumed to lie near a potentially low-dimensional manifold embedded in R. The authors develop a maximum likelihood algorithm to infer the distribution parameters that relies on a combination of gradient descent and Monte Carlo integration. They further extend the LAND to mixture models and provide the corresponding EM algorithm.

The idea of using a geodesic distance (akin to Isomap) is interesting and promising. The paper demonstrates the efficiency of the LAND to fit non-trivial probability distributions over both synthetic data and EEG measurements of human sleep. The experimental evaluation shows that the LAND learns faster the underlying true distribution than the Gaussian mixture model (GMM) and achieves better alignment with sleep stages.

However, there are several concerns and areas for improvement. The definition of the inverse metric tensor in Eq. (7) appears ad hoc and needs more justification. The use of a Gaussian kernel to weight local covariances in the weighted sum is not well-motivated, and more intuition is needed. There is also confusion between the local covariance defined in Eq. (7) and the MAP estimation of that quantity in Eq. (11), and it is unclear if they refer to the same thing.

The experimental evaluation is weak and insufficiently thorough. There is no comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, and the results are not replicated across multiple datasets. The evaluation metrics used are limited, and more comprehensive metrics should be employed to assess the performance of the LAND.

A major concern is the scalability and computational efficiency of the LAND. The algorithm relies on Monte Carlo integration and gradient-based optimization, which can be computationally expensive. There is no analysis of the time complexity or runtime comparison with standard EM, which makes it difficult to assess the practical applicability of the LAND.

Overall, the paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution, but the practical applicability is questionable. The authors should address the concerns mentioned above and provide more comprehensive evaluations to demonstrate the effectiveness and efficiency of the LAND.
