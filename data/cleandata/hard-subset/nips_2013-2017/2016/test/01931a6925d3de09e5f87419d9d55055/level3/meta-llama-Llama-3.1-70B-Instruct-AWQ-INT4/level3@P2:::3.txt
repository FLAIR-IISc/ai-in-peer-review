This paper proposes a novel approach to modeling observation spaces using a Riemannian metric and defines a curved normal distribution, referred to as the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND). The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of LAND in capturing data manifolds and show promising results in both synthetic and real-world experiments.

The main idea of the paper is to replace the traditional Euclidean distance with a locally adaptive nonlinear distance measure, which is learned from the data. This approach is based on Riemannian geometry and provides a more flexible and accurate way of modeling complex data distributions. The authors show that the LAND is a natural generalization of the normal distribution to the "manifold" setting and is the maximum entropy distribution under a given metric.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the mathematical concepts and techniques used in the paper. The experiments are well-designed, and the results are presented in a clear and easy-to-understand manner.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to capture complex data distributions using a parametric model. The LAND is a powerful tool for modeling data manifolds, and the authors demonstrate its effectiveness in a variety of experiments. The paper also provides a good balance between theoretical and practical aspects, making it accessible to a wide range of readers.

However, there are some limitations and potential improvements that can be made to the paper. One of the main limitations is that the metric used in the LAND is hand-crafted and relies on empirical hyperparameters. The authors suggest merging metric learning with LAND learning, which would allow the hyperparameters to be learned rather than fixed. This would make the approach more flexible and adaptive to different data distributions.

Another limitation is that the paper lacks a discussion on the intuitive effect of varying the hyperparameter sigma and how to select it. The authors should provide more insight into the effect of sigma on the LAND and how to choose an optimal value.

This would provide a fairer comparison between the two methods.
