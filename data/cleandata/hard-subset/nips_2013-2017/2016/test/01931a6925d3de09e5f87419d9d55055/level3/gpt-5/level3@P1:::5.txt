This paper proposes the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), a Riemannian generalization of the multivariate normal that replaces the global Mahalanobis metric with a learned, smoothly varying metric that favors regions of high empirical density. Given a metric M(x) constructed from kernel-weighted local (diagonal) covariance estimates, the model defines a Riemannian normal pM(x|µ,Σ) with geodesic distance and measure induced by M. The authors provide a practical normalization strategy by Monte Carlo integration over the tangent space at µ (sampling v ~ N(0,Σ) and weighting by √|M(Expµ(v))|), and a maximum-likelihood procedure combining gradient descent on µ (via Exp/log maps) and a factorized parameterization of Σ−1 = AᵀA. They extend the construction to mixtures with an EM algorithm. Experiments on synthetic data and sleep EEG features suggest the model fits non-elliptical, manifold-like distributions more faithfully than GMMs and least-squares (intrinsic) estimators.

Relation to prior work: The distribution is the Riemannian normal studied by Pennec and others when the manifold geometry is known; here the metric is learned from data in the spirit of manifold learning and metric learning (Isomap, Hauberg et al., Tosi et al.). The paper situates itself as bridging Riemannian statistics with data-driven manifold learning, and proposes a computational scheme that avoids repeated BVP solves by integrating in tangent space.

Strengths
- Conceptually elegant unification: a parametric normal defined on a nonparametric, data-adaptive Riemannian geometry, preserving the maximum-entropy characterization (given the chosen metric).
- Sound geometric implementation details: integrating in tangent space to avoid expensive BVPs; clear gradients for µ and Σ; reasonable use of Σ−1 factorization.
- The mixture extension is natural and potentially useful, with responsibilities based on properly normalized components (addressing issues in some prior spherical/unnormalized Riemannian mixtures).
- Qualitative results convincingly show LAND contours align with nonlinear manifolds in synthetic data; EEG example suggests potential for modeling structured biomedical data.

Weaknesses and requested clarifications

Quality
- Metric construction (Eq. 7) feels ad hoc. M(x) is the inverse of a diagonal, kernel-smoothed local variance with isotropic Gaussian weights. Please justify: (a) Why diagonal only? What is lost by ignoring local cross-covariances, and what regularization or low-rank/shrinkage alternatives were considered? (b) The metric is not invariant under linear reparameterizations; do you recommend whitening the input or adapting the metric to be affine-invariant? (c) Why isotropic kernels rather than anisotropic kernels aligned to local structure?
- Distinguish more clearly between the learned manifold metric M(x) and the model covariance Σ. In Eq. (7), M(x) is a property of the data geometry (nonparametric, fixed during ML), whereas Eq. (11) optimizes µ,Σ of the LAND. The text occasionally blurs this distinction; please disentangle and, if helpful, rename the metric (e.g., G(x)) to avoid overloading M for both “manifold” and “metric.”
- Complexity and scalability are not analyzed. Each evaluation of C(µ,Σ) requires S geodesic IVP solves for Expµ(v); Algorithm 1 invokes this twice per outer iteration and per mixture component in EM. Provide Big-O complexity in terms of N (data), D (dimensionality), K (mixture components), S (MC samples), and the average cost of an IVP solve. Include wall-clock runtime and iteration counts versus GMM EM on the same hardware.
- Variance and accuracy of the Monte Carlo normalization. With S=3000 samples, what is the estimator variance for C(µ,Σ) and its gradients? Are control variates, quasi-MC, or importance sampling (e.g., tempering by curvature) helpful? Convergence plots of φ and responsibilities across iterations would be valuable.
- Overfitting/double use of data. The metric is learned from the full dataset and then used to fit a density to that same data. Consider leave-one-out kernel weights when evaluating m(µ,v) for points associated to a sample, or a train/validation split for σ and ρ to avoid biasing both geometry and likelihood to the same samples.

Clarity
- Provide more intuition for Eq. (7): the Gaussian kernel induces a local neighborhood; the diagonal entries are inverse local variances, thus expanding geodesic length in low-density regions and shrinking it along data-supported directions. A schematic showing how varying σ affects geodesic flow and density normalization would help.
- Algorithm 1: minor typo in lines 4–5; the symbol d should be ∇ (or, if you deliberately use steepest descent direction, please state so explicitly and keep notation consistent).
- Notation is occasionally overloaded (M for manifold and metric); tightening this would improve readability.

Originality
- The main novelty lies in coupling a learned, data-adaptive metric with a Riemannian normal and giving a workable ML/EM algorithm with tangent-space Monte Carlo normalization. This is a meaningful step beyond prior Riemannian normals on known manifolds and beyond discrete geodesic graph approaches (Isomap).

Significance
- Potentially high, but current empirical evidence is limited. Beyond toy data and a single application, the paper lacks comparisons to strong alternatives that also address nonlinearity:
  - GMMs on embeddings (Isomap/Diffusion Maps/LLE) with out-of-sample extensions.
  - Probabilistic manifold models (e.g., GP-LVM) and manifold-regularized density estimators.
  - Riemannian mixture models on known manifolds when applicable.
Include held-out log-likelihoods, clustering metrics, and calibration of responsibilities, with ablations on σ, S, diagonal vs. full local covariance, and sensitivity to initialization. Report performance and runtime across D and N (e.g., D∈{5,20,50}, N∈{1e3,1e5}).

Suggestions
- Learn σ (and possibly ρ) by maximizing marginal likelihood on a validation set or via empirical Bayes; consider multi-scale metrics (mixtures over σ).
- Explore low-rank local covariances with shrinkage toward diagonal to balance expressivity and stability; or learn local principal directions.
- Use variance-reduced or quasi-MC estimators for C(µ,Σ); precompute metric fields or adopt neural ODE solvers with adjoints for faster Exp/Log and gradients.
- Consider a natural gradient or trust-region method for µ in the manifold parameterization to mitigate conditioning issues, and report its effect.

Pros for acceptance
- Elegant and principled formulation connecting Riemannian statistics and manifold learning.
- Practical algorithmic contributions (tangent-space MC normalization, ML/EM) with clear derivations.
- Compelling qualitative fits on nonlinear data; promising application to EEG.

Cons for acceptance
- Metric design appears heuristic and not sufficiently justified; invariance concerns.
- Insufficient experimental depth, baselines, ablations, and no runtime/complexity analysis.
- Open questions about estimator variance, convergence, and scalability to higher D/N.
- Some clarity and notation issues.

Minor comments
- Fix Algorithm 1 notation (d → ∇ or explicitly define the steepest descent direction and keep symbols consistent).
- Clarify whether σ is fixed per dataset, per component, or learned; report selection procedure for EEG across subjects.
- Consider renaming the metric tensor to avoid ambiguity with M as the manifold.
