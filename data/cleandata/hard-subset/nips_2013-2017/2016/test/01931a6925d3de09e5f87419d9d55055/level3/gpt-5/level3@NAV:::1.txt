This paper proposes LAND, a parametric distribution defined over a learned nonparametric Riemannian metric, thereby linking metric/manifold learning with Riemannian statistics. The metric at each point is taken as the inverse of a local diagonal covariance estimated with a Gaussian kernel, yielding geodesic distances that prefer regions of high sample density. A Riemannian normal (à la Pennec) is then fit by maximum likelihood via Monte Carlo integration in the tangent space and gradient-based updates; mixtures of LANDs are trained with an EM-style scheme. Empirically, LAND models nonlinear structure and extrapolates beyond the support of the training data, outperforming GMMs on synthetic and EEG clustering tasks. The work relates to Riemannian statistics on known manifolds (Pennec; Zhang & Fletcher; Simo-Serra et al.) and to manifold learning (Isomap) but uses a learned continuous metric. It also bears connections to local/diagonal per-point metrics (e.g., Frome et al.; Malisiewicz & Efros) and local PCA-based metric learning.

Quality: The formulation is technically sound and well-motivated; the tangent-space Monte Carlo for the normalization constant is elegant. However, scalability is a concern: computing M(x) involves O(ND) kernel-weighted accumulators per evaluation (and per ODE step), geodesic IVPs/BVPs and S exponential-map calls per likelihood evaluation, and mixture E-steps compound this. Please analyze asymptotic and wall-clock complexity as functions of N, D, S, path solver accuracy, and K, and clarify what “high-dimensional” means (e.g., d = 3, 5, 10, 100). The paper acknowledges non-invariance to linear transforms; some discussion or a simple whitening pre-processing baseline would help.

Clarity: The paper is very well written and pleasant to read. Provide more detail on σ selection, whether σ is uniform across heterogeneous density regions, and robustness to σ (e.g., variable bandwidth/kNN kernels and sensitivity plots).

Originality: The “parametric over nonparametric space” formulation and maximum-entropy interpretation are novel in this learned-metric setting. Better positioning relative to local-metric and local-PCA literatures would strengthen the contribution.

Significance: LAND is promising for generative modeling on manifolds and mixtures are particularly compelling. To guide method choice, please (i) compare visually and quantitatively against intrinsic estimators beyond Fig. 4 (some appear in the supplement) and (ii) report computational complexity/timings versus intrinsic estimators across dimensions.

Pros for acceptance:
- Elegant link between learned Riemannian metrics and maximum-entropy modeling
- Sound ML/EM derivations; generative extrapolation
- Strong writing; clear exposition

Cons against acceptance:
- Insufficient complexity analysis and scalability evidence
- Limited positioning vs related local-metric work
- Sensitivity to σ not thoroughly evaluated
- Visual/complexity comparisons focus on weak GMM baselines rather than intrinsic estimators
