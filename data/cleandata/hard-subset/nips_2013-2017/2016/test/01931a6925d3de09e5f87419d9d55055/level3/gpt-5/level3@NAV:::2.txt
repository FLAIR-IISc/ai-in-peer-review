This paper proposes the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND), which replaces the Euclidean/Mahalanobis metric in the multivariate normal with a smoothly varying, data-adaptive Riemannian metric. Building on Riemannian statistics (e.g., Pennec’s Riemannian normal) and manifold learning (e.g., Isomap/LLE), the authors learn a nonparametric metric from data and then define a parametric maximum-entropy density on the induced manifold, with maximum-likelihood estimation via gradient descent and Monte Carlo estimation of the normalization constant. They further derive an EM algorithm for mixtures of LANDs, positioning the method as a practical competitor to GMMs. Experiments on synthetic manifolds and EEG sleep-stage features illustrate clear advantages over Euclidean models and over least-squares manifold baselines.

Quality: The technical development is careful and sound. Using tangent-space Monte Carlo with exponential maps to estimate the partition function is sensible and scalable relative to alternatives requiring logarithm maps. The gradients for mean and covariance (via Σ−1 = AᵀA) are derived cleanly, and the mixture EM is consistent with the full probabilistic model (unlike some prior manifold-mixture work). Weaknesses are mostly practical: sensitivity to the smoothing parameter σ, diagonal local covariances (lack of linear invariance), and computational overhead from repeated geodesic solves; more thorough timing and convergence diagnostics would strengthen the case. Some discussion of statistical properties (consistency/identifiability; variance of the Monte Carlo estimator, possible control variates) would help.

Clarity: The paper is very well written and organized; notation and algorithmic steps are clear. The bibliography is appropriate and situates the work relative to Riemannian statistics, manifold learning, and mixtures.

Originality: The key novelty is to bring a properly normalized Riemannian normal to the manifold-learning setting with a learned metric, along with an ML estimator and a mixture model. This goes beyond prior work assuming known manifolds (e.g., Pennec; Zhang & Fletcher) or mixtures that ignore normalization (e.g., Simo-Serra et al.).

Significance: The approach is broadly applicable as a drop-in replacement for Gaussians in generative models where data lie near nonlinear structures. The mixture extension is compelling, and the experiments suggest meaningful practical gains and interesting future directions.

Pros:
- Elegant generalization of the normal to learned manifolds; maximum-entropy characterization.
- Complete ML/EM procedures with practical Monte Carlo normalization.
- Strong empirical benefits over GMMs on nonlinear data and EEG.
- Clear writing and high relevance to NeurIPS.

Cons:
- Computational cost and scalability not fully quantified.
- Dependence on σ and diagonal local covariances; not invariant to affine transforms.
- Limited theoretical guarantees and ablations (e.g., sensitivity to S, σ).

Overall: Strong paper; I recommend acceptance.
