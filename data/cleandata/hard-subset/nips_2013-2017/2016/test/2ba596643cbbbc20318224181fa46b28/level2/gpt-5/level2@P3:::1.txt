Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper introduces a stochastic K-armed bandit formulation where each arm can be probed at M fidelities: lower fidelities are cheaper but produce biased proxies of the highest-fidelity reward. A cost-weighted, budgeted pseudo-regret is defined over a total capital Λ, where each play at fidelity m incurs cost λ(m), and the instantaneous pseudo-reward is taken to be the highest-fidelity mean µ(M) of the chosen arm. The proposed algorithm, MF-UCB, builds upper confidence bounds on µ(M) using observations from all fidelities: for each arm, it takes the minimum over m of a UCB on µ(m) enlarged by the known bias bound ζ(m). The policy selects the arm with the largest bound and then chooses the lowest fidelity whose uncertainty is above a threshold γ(m) that balances bias and cost (γ(m) = ψ−1(λ(m)/λ(m+1) ψ(ζ(m))). The analysis yields a regret bound showing that MF-UCB uses cheap fidelities to eliminate clearly suboptimal arms and reserves expensive evaluations for near-optimal arms. A lower bound (under Bernoulli rewards) shows near-optimality except for a specific subset of arms where MF-UCB may exceed the information-theoretic cost due to conservative switching. Simulations on synthetic problems corroborate the theory, showing improved regret over standard UCB run only at the highest fidelity.

This work connects to (i) classical UCB-based bandits (Auer ’02; Bubeck & Cesa-Bianchi ’12), (ii) budgeted bandits (e.g., Tran-Thanh et al.; Xia et al.), which optimize reward/cost per pull rather than use structured fidelities, (iii) multi-fidelity optimization (Huang et al.; Rajnarayan et al.) without regret analysis, (iv) weak/strong labeler active learning (Zhang & Chaudhuri ’15) and multi-fidelity RL (Cutler et al.), which differ in objective, and (v) subsequent GP-based multi-fidelity bandits (Kandasamy et al. NIPS ’16), which build on the present finite-armed setting.

Strengths:
- Clear formalization of multi-fidelity finite-armed bandits with explicit bias bounds ζ(m) and costs λ(m), and a principled, cost-weighted regret for an anytime policy with budget Λ.
- Simple, interpretable algorithm: a UCB on µ(M) formed from multi-fidelity estimates, plus a cost-aware switching rule with thresholds derived from concentration ψ and bias-cost trade-offs.
- Rigorous analysis: a partition of arms by fidelity-dependent gaps yields a regret bound that is uniformly competitive with high-fidelity UCB and strictly better when lower fidelities can eliminate many arms. The lower bound demonstrates near-optimality on a broad subset of instances and explains the residual gap.
- Insightful discussion of when multi-fidelity helps (large K, small near-optimal set K(M), favorable λ and ζ profiles), and of the role of the regularity assumption on ζ decay.
- Empirical results, though synthetic, match the theoretical picture (fewer expensive pulls on clearly suboptimal arms and improved slope).

Weaknesses and areas for improvement:
- Practicality hinges on known, monotone bias bounds ζ(m) and the decay regularity (Assumption 1). In many applications ζ(m) may be unknown or mis-specified; robustness and estimation of ζ(m) are not addressed.
- The lower bound is stated for Bernoulli rewards with µ* ∈ (1/2,1) and ζ(1) < 1/2. A more general sub-Gaussian lower bound (matching the ψ-based analysis) would strengthen the theoretical completeness.
- The identified suboptimality on K(m)7 arms suggests room for improved switching; no heuristic or adaptive refinement is proposed beyond flagging it as open.
- Experimental evaluation is limited to synthetic setups and a UCB-only baseline. Additional baselines (e.g., cost-aware successive elimination variants, Thompson sampling adaptations, heuristic hierarchical screening) and sensitivity analyses (to ζ mis-specification, λ ratios, M, ψ choices) would improve the empirical case.
- The regret definition is well motivated for settings where only high-fidelity reward matters, but the paper briefly notes alternative application regimes with different utility models; a short appendix formalizing at least one alternative would broaden applicability.

Evaluation by criteria:
- Quality: The technical development is sound and careful. The use of ψ-based concentration, the fidelity-partitioned analysis, and the comparison to high-fidelity UCB are convincing. The lower bound is informative but not fully general.
- Clarity: Generally clear and well organized. The choice of γ(m) is well motivated. Some proofs and constants are deferred; providing a more compact main-text statement of Theorem 2 with explicit constants would help. A brief algorithmic complexity discussion (storage, per-step time) would be useful.
- Originality: The multi-fidelity, biased-observation finite-armed bandit formalization and the MF-UCB design are novel relative to prior budgeted bandits and multi-fidelity optimization without regret guarantees.
- Significance: Multi-fidelity evaluations are common in practice (ads, simulations, AutoML). The results provide a principled method with quantifiable gains and near-optimality. The framework is likely to spur follow-up work (as evidenced by GP extensions).

Suggestions for improvement (prioritized and feasible):
- Essential: Discuss robustness to ζ(m) mis-specification and propose a conservative variant (e.g., online estimation of upper bounds on ζ(m) via cross-fidelity discrepancies, with confidence inflation) together with a qualitative or preliminary theoretical guarantee.
- Essential: Extend the lower bound beyond Bernoulli to general sub-Gaussian rewards (ψ(·) ≍ ·²), relaxing µ* and ζ constraints.
- Important: Add experiments with (a) additional baselines (successive elimination with cost-aware sampling, Thompson sampling with fidelity-dependent likelihoods), (b) sensitivity to λ and ζ, and (c) a semi-synthetic or real task (e.g., proxy-based algorithm selection), even if small scale.
- Useful: Provide guidance for choosing ρ and default γ(m) when ψ is sub-Gaussian, including a table of constants; include an ablation showing the impact of γ(m) scaling.
- Useful: Clarify the oracle in the regret definition and, in an appendix, formalize an alternative regret notion for settings where low-fidelity rewards have intrinsic utility, to delineate when the proposed objective is appropriate.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses a practically motivated setting with a clean, novel formulation and an elegant algorithm.
- Provides strong theoretical guarantees that explain and quantify the benefit of multi-fidelity information.
- Offers clear insights about when and why gains occur, with supportive simulations.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Reliance on known bias bounds and a regularity assumption may limit immediate applicability; no robustness analysis.
- Lower bound not fully general; empirical evaluation limited in breadth and baselines.

Recommendation: Leaning toward acceptance. The conceptual contribution and analysis are solid and timely; addressing robustness and adding broader empirical comparisons would make the work even stronger.
