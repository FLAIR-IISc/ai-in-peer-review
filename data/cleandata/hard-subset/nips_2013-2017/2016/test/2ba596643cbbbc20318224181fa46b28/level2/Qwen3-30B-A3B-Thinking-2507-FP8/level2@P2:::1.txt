

This paper introduces the multi-fidelity bandit problem, where each arm can be evaluated at multiple fidelities with different costs and accuracy levels. The authors propose MF-UCB, an algorithm that adaptively selects fidelities to balance exploration and exploitation, and provide theoretical guarantees on its regret performance. The work is well-motivated by practical applications such as online advertising and algorithm selection, where high-fidelity evaluations are expensive but cheaper approximations are available.

The paper's main strengths include a clear problem formalization, a novel algorithm design, and strong theoretical results. The regret analysis is technically sound, with a well-constructed upper bound that demonstrates MF-UCB's superiority over standard UCB when lower fidelities are significantly cheaper than the highest fidelity. The lower bound nicely complements the upper bound, showing that MF-UCB is nearly optimal for many arms. The paper also makes a thoughtful effort to situate its contribution within the broader bandit literature, citing relevant work on budgeted bandits and multi-fidelity optimization while clearly distinguishing their novel approach.

The clarity of the paper is generally good, with a well-structured presentation of the problem, algorithm, and analysis. The key technical innovations are well-explained, and the regret definition appropriately accounts for the cost of different fidelities. The empirical results on synthetic data support the theoretical claims, showing MF-UCB outperforming UCB across multiple settings.

However, I have a few concerns about the paper's practical relevance and theoretical completeness. First, the assumption that ζ(m) (the bias bounds between fidelities) are known is quite strong for many applications. In practice, these bounds would likely need to be estimated, which could significantly impact performance. The paper doesn't discuss how to handle this estimation challenge or how it might affect the theoretical guarantees. This is a notable limitation that should be addressed.

Second, the regret bound comparison between MF-UCB and UCB is somewhat confusing. While the paper claims MF-UCB is "nearly optimal," there's a gap between the upper and lower bounds for arms in K(m)7. The authors acknowledge this but don't fully explain how significant this gap is in practice or whether it can be closed. A more detailed discussion of this gap's implications would strengthen the theoretical contribution.

Third, the empirical section is limited to synthetic data. While the synthetic results are consistent with the theory, the paper would benefit from at least one real-world application example to demonstrate practical utility. For instance, showing how MF-UCB could be applied to optimize ad campaigns with different audience segments would make the contribution more concrete.

The paper also makes a minor but important point about the choice of qt in the regret definition, noting that using qt = µ(m)t would not capture the motivating applications. This careful consideration of the problem's practical context is a strength, but the paper could further elaborate on how this regret definition differs from other cost-sensitive bandit formulations.

Overall, this is a solid contribution to the bandit literature that extends the classical framework to accommodate multi-fidelity evaluations. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the problem is well-motivated, and the algorithmic approach is novel. While the assumption of known ζ(m) and the limited empirical validation are drawbacks, they don't undermine the paper's core theoretical contribution. The work provides a valuable foundation for future research on multi-fidelity bandits, particularly for applications where high-fidelity evaluations are costly.

I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions to address the concerns about ζ(m) estimation and to clarify the regret bound comparison. Specifically, the authors should:
1) Discuss how to handle unknown ζ(m) values and potential impacts on performance
2) Provide a clearer explanation of the gap between the upper and lower bounds for arms in K(m)7
3) Add at least one more empirical example to demonstrate practical relevance

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to bandit algorithms that is well-suited for NIPS, and with these minor clarifications, it would be a strong addition to the literature.
