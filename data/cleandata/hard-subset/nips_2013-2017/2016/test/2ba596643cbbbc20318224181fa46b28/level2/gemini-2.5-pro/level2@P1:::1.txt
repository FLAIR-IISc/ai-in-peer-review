### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a novel and practical variant of the stochastic K-armed bandit problem, termed the "multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit". In this setting, playing an arm is associated with a cost, but cheaper, biased approximations of the arm's true reward are available at different "fidelities". The core idea is to leverage these cheap approximations to quickly discard suboptimal arms, thereby reserving expensive, high-fidelity evaluations for a small set of promising candidates.

The main contributions of the paper are threefold:
1.  A formal problem definition for the multi-fidelity bandit, including a new cost-aware notion of regret that is suitable for a fixed-budget (capital) setting.
2.  A new algorithm, Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB), which extends the classic UCB algorithm. MF-UCB intelligently selects not only which arm to play (based on an optimistic bound) but also at which fidelity to play it, based on a criterion that trades off the information gain at a given fidelity against its cost and inherent bias.
3.  A thorough theoretical analysis of MF-UCB, providing an upper bound on its regret. This bound demonstrates that the algorithm effectively adapts to the problem structure, outperforming naive strategies that only use the highest fidelity. The authors also provide a lower bound for the problem, showing that MF-UCB is near-optimal under certain conditions.

This work connects to the extensive literature on multi-armed bandits but carves out a new and significant niche. It is distinguished from budgeted bandits, where costs are typically per-arm, and from multi-fidelity methods in optimization or active learning, which do not address the exploration-exploitation trade-off via regret minimization.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The problem formulation is sound, and the proposed MF-UCB algorithm is well-motivated and intuitive. The theoretical analysis is the main strength of the paper. The regret bound in Theorem 2 is insightful, as it explicitly shows how the regret associated with a suboptimal arm depends on the cost of the fidelity level at which it can be identified as poor, rather than uniformly on the most expensive fidelity. The inclusion of a problem-specific lower bound (Theorem 4) provides a strong benchmark for the algorithm's performance and demonstrates its near-optimality. The authors are also commendably transparent about the limitations of their analysis, pointing out the gap between the upper and lower bounds for a specific subset of arms (`K(m)7`) and explaining the algorithmic trade-off that causes it. The synthetic experiments, while simple, effectively corroborate the theoretical claims.

A potential point of discussion is Assumption 1, which requires the fidelity biases `ζ(m)` to decay sufficiently fast. The authors state this assumption is not strictly necessary but makes the performance gains over standard UCB more apparent. It would be beneficial for the authors to briefly comment on how the regret bounds might change if this assumption were relaxed, as some practical problems might not exhibit such regular structure.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The introduction provides excellent motivation using relatable examples like online advertising and algorithm selection. The problem setting, the new regret notion, and the algorithm are all explained with precision. The partitioning of the arm set into `K(m)` is a key conceptual tool for the analysis, and its intuition is conveyed effectively, aided by the helpful illustration in Figure 1. The proof sketches provide a clear roadmap of the main analytical arguments. The organization is logical, flowing from motivation and formulation to the algorithm, analysis, and experiments.

**Originality:** The paper's contributions are highly original. The multi-fidelity bandit framework itself is a novel and meaningful extension of the classical bandit problem. While the idea of using cheap approximations is not new in a broader machine learning context (e.g., active learning, Bayesian optimization), this paper is the first to formalize it within the stochastic bandit setting and provide a rigorous regret analysis. The MF-UCB algorithm is a non-trivial adaptation of UCB, with the fidelity selection rule being a key innovation. This work clearly carves out a new direction for bandit research.

**Significance:** The problem addressed is of considerable practical significance. Many sequential decision-making tasks, from hyperparameter tuning and scientific experimentation to clinical trials, naturally fit the multi-fidelity model where evaluation cost can be traded for accuracy. This paper provides a principled and provably efficient approach for these scenarios. The framework is general and the ideas presented are likely to inspire follow-up work, such as extending the multi-fidelity concept to more complex settings like linear or contextual bandits. Practitioners in various fields could directly benefit from implementing the proposed algorithm. Demonstrating the algorithm's performance on a more realistic simulation or a real-world dataset would further enhance its impact, but the current theoretical contribution is strong enough on its own.

### Arguments for and against acceptance:

**Pro:**
*   Introduces a novel, well-motivated, and practically important problem.
*   Proposes an elegant and intuitive algorithm (MF-UCB).
*   Provides a strong and insightful theoretical analysis, including both upper and lower regret bounds, which establish near-optimality.
*   The paper is very clearly written and structured.

**Con:**
*   The experimental validation is limited to synthetic data.
*   Assumption 1 on the decay of fidelity bias might be strong in some practical settings.

In conclusion, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the multi-armed bandit literature. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses. It introduces a compelling new problem, provides a well-founded algorithm, and supports it with rigorous theory. I strongly recommend acceptance.
