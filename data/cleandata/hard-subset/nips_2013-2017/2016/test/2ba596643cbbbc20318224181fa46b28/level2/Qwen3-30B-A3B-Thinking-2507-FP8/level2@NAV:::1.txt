

This paper introduces a novel multi-fidelity bandit framework where each arm can be evaluated at multiple fidelities with different costs and biases. The authors propose MF-UCB, a principled extension of the classical UCB algorithm that strategically uses cheaper lower-fidelity evaluations to eliminate suboptimal arms before committing to expensive high-fidelity assessments. The theoretical contribution is strong, with a regret bound demonstrating MF-UCB's advantage over standard UCB when the number of promising arms is small, and a matching lower bound showing near-optimality under certain conditions. The paper effectively situates its work within the bandit literature, clearly distinguishing from related budgeted bandit approaches and multi-fidelity optimization methods.

The paper excels in quality and clarity. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with well-motivated assumptions and careful treatment of bias from lower-fidelity approximations. The algorithm is clearly presented with a precise pseudocode, and the synthetic experiments robustly support the theoretical claims, showing consistent performance advantages over UCB across multiple problem configurations. The writing is professional and accessible to bandit researchers.

The novelty is significant, as the multi-fidelity bandit problem represents a meaningful extension of the classical bandit framework to a more realistic setting with multiple cost-benefit trade-offs. The paper correctly references related work (notably Kandasamy et al.'s Gaussian process bandit extension) while clearly establishing its unique contribution.

The significance is strong, with clear practical relevance to applications like online advertising where cheaper approximations (shorter ad displays) can filter out poor options before expensive full-scale experiments. The approach could substantially reduce resource consumption in real-world bandit applications.

The main limitation noted in the paper is that MF-UCB may not be optimal for arms with misleading lower-fidelity estimates (those in K(m)7), though the authors properly acknowledge this gap between their upper and lower bounds. A minor suggestion for improvement would be to briefly discuss computational complexity compared to standard UCB, which would strengthen the practical relevance.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a clear, significant contribution to bandit theory with strong theoretical foundations and empirical validation. It advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way and should be accepted.
