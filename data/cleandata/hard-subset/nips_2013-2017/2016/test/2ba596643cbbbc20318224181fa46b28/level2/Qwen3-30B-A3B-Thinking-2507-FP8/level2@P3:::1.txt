

The paper introduces a multi-fidelity bandit framework where each arm can be observed at multiple fidelities with different costs and biases. This extends the classical stochastic K-armed bandit problem by incorporating cheaper approximations to the desired high-fidelity outcome, motivated by practical applications like online advertising where lower-fidelity observations (e.g., shorter ad displays or narrower audiences) can provide biased but informative estimates. The paper formalizes the problem, develops the MF-UCB algorithm, and provides theoretical guarantees showing that MF-UCB achieves better regret than standard UCB by strategically using lower-fidelity observations to eliminate suboptimal arms before investing in expensive high-fidelity evaluations.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it establishes a novel formalism for multi-fidelity bandit problems with cost-weighted regret. Second, it develops MF-UCB, a principled algorithm that dynamically selects fidelities based on confidence intervals and known bias parameters. Third, it provides a regret bound showing MF-UCB achieves O((λ(1)K + λ(2)|Kg|)log(Λ/λ(2))) regret compared to UCB's O(λ(2)K log(Λ/λ(2))) regret, where Kg is the set of promising arms identified through low-fidelity observations. The paper complements this with a matching lower bound demonstrating near-optimality under certain conditions.

The quality of the paper is strong. The theoretical analysis is technically sound, with careful definitions of the multi-fidelity setting, clear assumptions (particularly Assumption 1 regarding the decay of bias parameters), and rigorous proofs of the regret bound and lower bound. The paper properly distinguishes its approach from related work, noting that previous multi-fidelity methods [7, 10, 16] do not formalize regret in the bandit setting, while budgeted bandits [13, 15] address a different problem where each arm has a random cost rather than multiple fidelities. The synthetic experiments provide empirical validation of the theoretical insights, showing MF-UCB outperforms UCB across various scenarios.

The clarity of the paper is excellent. The problem is well-motivated with concrete examples, the notation is consistent and well-explained, and the algorithm is described with sufficient detail for reproducibility. The figures (particularly Figure 1) effectively illustrate the partitioning of arms and the fidelity selection strategy. The paper is well-organized, with a logical flow from problem formulation to algorithm design to theoretical analysis and empirical validation.

The originality of the work is significant. While multi-fidelity methods exist in optimization [7, 10], this paper is the first to formally study regret in the multi-fidelity bandit setting with the specific cost-bias trade-offs relevant to exploration-exploitation problems. The paper clearly articulates how its approach differs from previous work, particularly in addressing the fundamental challenge of bias in low-fidelity observations rather than treating them as simply noisier high-fidelity observations.

The significance of the contribution is substantial. The multi-fidelity bandit problem addresses a practical limitation in many real-world applications where high-fidelity evaluations are expensive. The paper demonstrates that the proposed approach can substantially reduce regret compared to standard bandit algorithms, which is valuable for practitioners in fields like online advertising, algorithm selection, and clinical trials. The theoretical results provide a clear framework for understanding when multi-fidelity strategies will be beneficial, and the empirical results support the theoretical claims.

The paper has a few minor weaknesses. The assumption that the bias parameters ζ(m) are known may be restrictive in some applications, though this is common in bandit literature. The experimental section is somewhat brief (with details relegated to an appendix), and more comprehensive empirical validation would strengthen the claims. The paper acknowledges the gap between the lower bound and MF-UCB's performance for certain arms (those in K(m)7), which is appropriately framed as an open problem rather than a flaw.

Overall, this is a strong contribution that advances the multi-armed bandit literature by addressing a practically relevant extension of the problem. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the algorithm is well-motivated, and the empirical results support the theoretical claims. The paper makes a clear case for the novelty and significance of the work while properly situating it within the existing literature. The results are likely to be valuable for both researchers and practitioners working on bandit problems with costly evaluations. The paper meets all criteria for high-quality NIPS work and should be accepted.
