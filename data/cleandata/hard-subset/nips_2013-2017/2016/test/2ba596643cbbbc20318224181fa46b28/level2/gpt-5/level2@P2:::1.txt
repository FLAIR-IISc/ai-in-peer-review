Summary
The paper introduces a stochastic K-armed bandit setting where each arm admits M fidelities: lower fidelities are cheaper but biased approximations of the highest fidelity, with known bias bounds |μ(M)k − μ(m)k| ≤ ζ(m), and costs λ(1) < … < λ(M). The authors define a capital-limited regret that credits the highest-fidelity mean μ(M)It per unit cost spent at the chosen fidelity. They propose MF-UCB, which maintains per-fidelity UCBs that upper bound μ(M)k via empirical means plus confidence radii and the known bias ζ(m), and selects both the arm (by the tightest upper bound across fidelities) and the fidelity (by continuing to sample at fidelity m until its confidence radius drops below a threshold γ(m) depending on λ(m), λ(m+1), and ζ(m), then escalating). They prove a regret upper bound showing that MF-UCB concentrates most low-cost effort on obviously suboptimal arms at low fidelities and reserves high-fidelity plays for a small set of near-optimal arms; they provide a lower bound (for Bernoulli rewards under a mild sub-polynomial play assumption) demonstrating near-optimality, up to a gap on a specific subset of instances. Synthetic experiments corroborate the theoretical savings.

Relation to prior work
The formulation is distinct from budgeted bandits (reward/cost ratios) and from weak/strong label active learning, and complements multi-fidelity optimization literature that lacks bandit regret guarantees. It connects to UCB-style algorithms and to more recent GP-based multi-fidelity bandits; the authors correctly position their work within this landscape.

Quality
- Theory: The construction of per-fidelity UCBs for μ(M)k via μ(m)k UCBs plus ζ(m) is sound. The fidelity-selection rule is principled: it harvests information at fidelity m up to the point where bias ζ(m) caps further utility. The main regret bound (Theorem 2) is plausible and informative: it decomposes regret by an analysis partition K(m) capturing which fidelity is sufficient to eliminate each arm, and shows gains proportional to λ(JkK)/λ(M) when lower fidelities suffice. The lower bound (Theorem 4) uses change-of-measure and matches the upper bound on a large subset of arms; the identified gap on K(m)7 is clearly explained and seems inherent to the simple switching rule.
- Assumptions: The paper assumes known bias bounds ζ(m) and uniform concentration ψ across fidelities. This is a limitation in practice; nevertheless, it is clearly stated and allows tight finite-time analysis. Assumption 1 (on ζ(m) decay) is a technical regularity that simplifies comparisons; it is not essential for algorithm correctness. The lower bound’s Assumption 3 (sub-polynomial pulls of suboptimal arms) is standard for asymptotic lower bounds.
- Proof sketch: While details are deferred, the lemmas controlling per-fidelity play counts and the argument that total plays N ≲ nΛ with high probability are convincing. The role of ρ > 4 is explained.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and well organized. The algorithmic description is clear, and the intuition behind γ(m) is helpful. The analysis uses heavy notation (K(m), JkK, Δ(m)k), but the narrative and figures alleviate this. The regret definition is nonstandard but well motivated (competing on μ(M) with capital-weighted cost). It would help to:
- Explicitly restate that ζ(M) = 0 and ensure the min-over-m UCB yields a valid high-probability upper bound on μ(M)k (the appendix reportedly does this).
- Provide more guidance on selecting ρ and sensitivity to mis-specified ζ(m).
- Summarize experimental setups in the main text (M, K, λ ratios, ζ schedule), not only in the appendix.

Originality
The formalization of multi-fidelity stochastic bandits with biased approximations and capital-weighted regret appears novel. The MF-UCB fidelity selection rule and the resulting regret characterization, including the partition-based interpretation, provide new insights beyond classical UCB and budgeted bandits.

Significance
The work addresses a realistic and important setting (e.g., online ads, algorithm selection, simulation vs real-world evaluation). The algorithm is simple and practical given ζ(m), and the analysis shows real gains when many arms can be eliminated cheaply. The lower bound strengthens the contribution. This is likely to spur follow-on work (e.g., learning ζ(m), richer feedback models, contextual extensions).

Strengths
- Clear, principled problem formulation with realistic cost/bias trade-offs.
- Simple algorithm with strong intuition and finite-time guarantees.
- Regret bound that adapts to fidelity structure and shows concrete savings over naive UCB.
- Lower bound and careful discussion of where the algorithm is or is not optimal.
- Empirical results consistent with theory.

Weaknesses
- Requires known bias bounds ζ(m); robustness to misspecification is not studied.
- Assumption 1 and the choice of γ(m) optimized under those conditions may be restrictive; practical guidance is limited.
- Lower bound restricted to Bernoulli rewards; extension to general ψ would strengthen claims.
- Experiments are only synthetic; no real-world case study.
- Gap on K(m)7 remains open; could a more adaptive switching strategy close it without incurring high-fidelity cost?

Questions and suggestions
- Can ζ(m) be learned online (e.g., via a global calibration phase or conservative upper bounds) with performance guarantees? Even a robust variant that treats ζ(m) as unknown within known envelopes would broaden applicability.
- How sensitive is MF-UCB to moderate over/underestimation of ζ(m)? A small simulation exploring this would be valuable.
- Could one incorporate a cheap hypothesis test at low fidelities to detect small but positive Δ(`)k and avoid unnecessary escalation in the problematic K(m)7 cases, while keeping overall high-fidelity usage sublinear?
- The regret metric is appropriate for the motivating applications; still, showing how the algorithm adapts to alternative metrics (e.g., fidelity-dependent rewards) would make Section 7’s discussion more concrete.
- Please report exact experimental settings in the main text (K, M, λ, ζ, Δ distributions, ψ), and include standard errors over multiple runs.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and timely problem formulation with clear practical relevance.
- Solid theoretical contributions (upper/lower bounds) with insightful interpretation.
- Algorithm is simple, implementable, and empirically effective.
- Well-situated within related work.

Cons for acceptance
- Dependence on known bias bounds; limited robustness analysis.
- Lower bound scope (Bernoulli) and remaining optimality gap on specific instances.
- Lack of real-world experiments.

Overall, this is a high-quality, original, and significant contribution. With minor clarifications and an expanded experimental description, it would be a strong addition to NIPS.
