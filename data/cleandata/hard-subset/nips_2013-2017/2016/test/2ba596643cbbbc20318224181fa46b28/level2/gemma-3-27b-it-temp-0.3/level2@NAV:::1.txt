## Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

This paper introduces a novel multi-fidelity bandit framework where obtaining outcomes for each arm is expensive, but cheaper approximations are available. The authors formalize this setting and propose MF-UCB, a new Upper Confidence Bound algorithm designed to leverage these approximations. The core idea – using lower fidelities for initial exploration and focusing expensive evaluations on promising candidates – is intuitively appealing and addresses a practical challenge in many real-world applications like online advertising and algorithm selection.

The paper’s strength lies in its theoretical analysis. The authors provide a regret bound for MF-UCB and demonstrate its near-optimality under certain conditions, establishing a clear advantage over naive strategies that ignore fidelity levels. The analysis carefully considers the trade-offs between cost and information gain at different fidelities, and the assumptions made (e.g., Assumption 1 regarding fidelity decay) are clearly stated. The inclusion of a lower bound further strengthens the theoretical contribution. 

However, the paper could benefit from more extensive empirical evaluation. While simulations are presented, they appear limited in scope. Exploring more complex scenarios, potentially with varying cost ratios and fidelity biases, would bolster confidence in the algorithm’s practical performance.  The connection to related work, while present, could be more thoroughly explored. Specifically, the discussion of budgeted bandits and active learning with weak/strong labelers feels somewhat cursory.  A more detailed comparison of MF-UCB to existing approaches in these areas would be valuable.

Clarity is generally good, although the notation can be dense at times. Providing more intuitive explanations for some of the key equations (e.g., the choice of γ(m)) would improve readability.  The paper is well-organized, and the proofs are sketched clearly, indicating a solid understanding of the underlying theory. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and well-motivated problem formulation.
*   Strong theoretical guarantees demonstrating the algorithm’s performance.
*   Clear and well-structured presentation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited empirical evaluation.
*   Could benefit from a more in-depth comparison to related work.
*   Some notation is dense and could be clarified.

**Overall:**

Despite the minor weaknesses, this paper presents a significant contribution to the field of bandit algorithms. The multi-fidelity setting is a relevant and practical extension of the classical bandit problem, and the proposed MF-UCB algorithm offers a promising solution. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically requesting a more comprehensive empirical evaluation and a more detailed discussion of related work.
