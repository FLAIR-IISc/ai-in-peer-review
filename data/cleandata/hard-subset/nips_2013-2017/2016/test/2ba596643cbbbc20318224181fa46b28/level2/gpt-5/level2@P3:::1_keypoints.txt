- Paper introduces a stochastic K-armed bandit with M fidelities per arm, biased cheaper proxies, and a cost-weighted budgeted pseudo-regret over total capital Λ.  
- Proposes MF-UCB: UCB on highest-fidelity means built from all fidelities using bias bounds ζ(m), with cost-aware switching thresholds γ(m) balancing bias and cost.  
- Analysis shows MF-UCB uses cheap fidelities to eliminate clearly suboptimal arms and saves expensive pulls for near-optimal arms, with regret bounds and a near-optimality lower bound (Bernoulli case).  
- Connects to classical UCB, budgeted bandits, multi-fidelity optimization, weak/strong labeler active learning, and subsequent GP-based multi-fidelity bandits.  

Strengths:
- Clear formalization of multi-fidelity finite-armed bandits with explicit ζ(m), λ(m), and a principled cost-weighted regret.  
- Simple, interpretable algorithm combining multi-fidelity UCBs with a principled cost-aware switching rule.  
- Rigorous, fidelity-partitioned analysis yielding competitive regret bounds and an informative lower bound.  
- Insightful discussion on when multi-fidelity helps (large K, small near-optimal set, favorable ζ and λ profiles).  
- Empirical simulations match theory, showing fewer expensive pulls and improved regret slope.

Weaknesses:
- Method requires known, monotone bias bounds ζ(m) and a regularity assumption; robustness to mis-specification is not addressed.  
- Lower bound limited to Bernoulli rewards under restrictive conditions; no general sub-Gaussian lower bound.  
- Conservative switching creates a residual suboptimality for certain arms, with no proposed adaptive remedy.  
- Experiments are synthetic and compare only to high-fidelity UCB; additional baselines and sensitivity analyses are missing.  
- Regret objective focuses solely on high-fidelity reward; alternative utility models are only briefly noted, not formalized.

Evaluation (criteria):
- Quality: Technical development is sound, with convincing ψ-based concentration and partitioned analysis; lower bound informative but not fully general.  
- Clarity: Generally well organized; some proofs/constants deferred and algorithmic complexity not discussed.  
- Originality: Formalization and MF-UCB design are novel relative to prior budgeted and multi-fidelity work.  
- Significance: Practical relevance is high (ads, simulations, AutoML), and framework likely to spur follow-up work.

Prioritized suggestions:
- Essential: Address robustness to ζ(m) mis-specification, e.g., propose online estimation or conservative variants with preliminary guarantees.  
- Essential: Extend the lower bound to general sub-Gaussian rewards to match the ψ-based analysis.  
- Important: Add experiments with more baselines (cost-aware successive elimination, Thompson sampling variants), sensitivity to λ/ζ/M, and at least one semi-synthetic or real task.  
- Useful: Provide guidance for choosing ρ and γ(m) under sub-Gaussian ψ, with constants and an ablation on γ scaling.  
- Useful: Clarify the oracle in regret and formalize at least one alternative regret notion for settings where low-fidelity rewards have intrinsic utility.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses a practical and timely setting with a clean, novel formulation and an elegant algorithm.  
- Strong theoretical guarantees explain when multi-fidelity information yields quantifiable gains.  
- Provides insight into when and why gains occur, supported by simulations.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Heavy reliance on known bias bounds and a regularity assumption limits immediate applicability without robustness analysis.  
- Lower bound not fully general and empirical evaluation lacks breadth and diverse baselines.

Recommendation:
- Leaning toward acceptance, contingent on addressing robustness and broadening empirical comparisons.
