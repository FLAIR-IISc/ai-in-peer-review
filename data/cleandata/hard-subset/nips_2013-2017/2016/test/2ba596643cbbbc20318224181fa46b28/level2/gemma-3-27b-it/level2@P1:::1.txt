This paper introduces a multi-fidelity bandit problem, where obtaining precise rewards is costly, but cheaper, noisy approximations are available. The authors propose MF-UCB, an upper confidence bound algorithm tailored to this setting, and provide theoretical guarantees demonstrating near-optimality. The problem is well-motivated with examples from online advertising and algorithm selection, and the proposed solution seems intuitively sound.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The introduction effectively sets the stage, motivating the problem and outlining the contributions. The formalization of the multi-fidelity bandit is clear, and the notation is consistent throughout. The algorithm, MF-UCB, is presented in a straightforward manner, and the rationale behind the fidelity selection criterion (based on γ(m)) is well-explained. The theoretical analysis, while dense, appears rigorous and the connection between the regret bound and the algorithm's design is convincing.  The inclusion of simulations provides empirical support for the theoretical results, and the comparison with the standard UCB algorithm reinforces the benefits of the proposed approach.  The related work section is comprehensive and appropriately cites relevant literature. 

A strength of this work is the careful consideration of the biases introduced by lower-fidelity approximations. The use of ζ(m) to quantify the approximation error and the inclusion of this error in the confidence bounds is a key contribution.  Another strength is the thorough theoretical analysis, including both an upper bound on the regret of MF-UCB and a complementary lower bound. This demonstrates a deep understanding of the problem and the algorithm's performance. The connection made between the theoretical results and the practical implications is also quite strong. The simulation results further support the theoretical claims and make the benefits of MF-UCB clear.

However, there are some areas where the paper could be improved. The assumption of known ζ(m) values might be unrealistic in some applications. While the authors acknowledge this, a discussion of how to handle unknown ζ(m) values (e.g., through estimation) would be valuable. The notation can be a bit overwhelming at times, particularly in the analysis section. More liberal use of inline explanations or intuitive examples could improve readability.  While the proof sketches are provided, a full proof would provide additional confidence in the results.  The discussion of the lower bound is somewhat disconnected from the main analysis; a tighter connection and discussion of the gap between the upper and lower bounds would be beneficial. The simulations presented are synthetic. Including results on a more realistic dataset or a real-world application would further strengthen the paper. The argument regarding the separation between identifying good arms and fine tuning them is a bit hand-wavy. Some additional justification would be appreciated.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The problem is well-motivated and practically relevant.
*   The proposed algorithm, MF-UCB, is intuitive and well-designed.
*   The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides strong performance guarantees.
*   The empirical results support the theoretical findings.
*   The paper is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The assumption of known ζ(m) values may limit the applicability of the algorithm.
*   The notation can be dense and challenging to follow.
*   The lower bound analysis could be strengthened and better integrated with the upper bound.
*   The lack of real-world experimental results.



**Overall assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of bandit algorithms. The problem is important, the proposed solution is well-designed and theoretically sound, and the empirical results are encouraging. While there are some minor areas for improvement, I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS 2016. The strengths of the paper significantly outweigh its weaknesses.
