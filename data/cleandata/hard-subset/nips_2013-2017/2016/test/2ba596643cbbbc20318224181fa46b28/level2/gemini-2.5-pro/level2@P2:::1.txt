This paper introduces the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit (MF-MAB) problem, a novel and practical extension of the classic stochastic K-armed bandit setting. The key idea is that for each arm, an agent can choose to pull it at one of M "fidelities". Lower fidelities are cheaper to sample but provide a biased estimate of the true (highest fidelity) reward mean. The goal is to minimize a cost-weighted notion of regret given a total budget Λ. The authors propose an elegant algorithm, the Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB) algorithm, which adapts the UCB principle to this new setting. Intuitively, MF-UCB uses the cheap, low-fidelity samples to quickly identify and discard highly suboptimal arms, reserving the expensive high-fidelity pulls for a small set of promising candidates.

The paper's main contributions are the formalization of the MF-MAB problem, the MF-UCB algorithm itself, and a rigorous theoretical analysis. This analysis includes an upper bound on the regret of MF-UCB, which demonstrates its superiority over naive strategies that ignore the fidelity structure. Furthermore, the authors provide a problem-dependent lower bound, showing that MF-UCB is near-optimal for a significant class of arms. This work is well-positioned relative to the extensive literature on bandits, clearly distinguishing itself from related concepts like budgeted bandits by focusing on the use of biased approximations for exploration.

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical analysis is sound and provides strong performance guarantees for the proposed algorithm.
*   **Strengths:** The regret analysis is comprehensive. The decomposition of the arm set into partitions `K(m)` (as illustrated in Figure 1) is insightful and provides a clear intuition for why the algorithm works: arms in `K(m)` are primarily explored at fidelity `m` and are unlikely to be explored at higher, more expensive fidelities. The inclusion of a lower bound (Theorem 4) is a major strength, as it rigorously establishes the near-optimality of MF-UCB and highlights the fundamental difficulty of the problem. The authors are also commendably transparent about the gap between their upper and lower bounds for certain arm configurations (`K(m)7`), correctly identifying this as an interesting direction for future work.
*   **Weaknesses:** The analysis relies on the assumption that the bias bounds `ζ(m)` for each fidelity are known. This may be a strong requirement in practice. It would strengthen the paper to include a discussion on the robustness of MF-UCB to misspecified `ζ(m)` values, or to suggest methods for estimating them. Similarly, Assumption 1, which governs the decay rate of `ζ(m)`, feels somewhat technical and is mainly used to simplify the final regret bound. While the authors note it is not strictly necessary, a clearer discussion of the performance without this assumption would be beneficial.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow.
*   **Strengths:** The motivation for the multi-fidelity problem is clearly articulated with compelling examples. The problem is formally defined with clear notation. The MF-UCB algorithm is presented simply and intuitively, and the logic behind the fidelity-switching criterion (Equation 4) is well-motivated. The proof sketches in Section 5 provide an excellent high-level overview of the main analytical arguments, which is very helpful for the reader.

**Originality:** The work is highly original.
*   **Strengths:** To my knowledge, this is the first work to formally define and analyze the multi-fidelity setting within the bandit regret-minimization framework. This opens up a new and promising direction for bandit research. While multi-fidelity methods exist in other domains like Bayesian optimization, their application and analysis in the context of UCB-style regret bounds is novel. The algorithm and the associated analysis are a non-trivial extension of existing bandit theory.

**Significance:** The paper's contributions are significant and likely to be impactful.
*   **Strengths:** The MF-MAB formulation captures a structural property of many real-world sequential decision-making problems where cheap approximations are available (e.g., hyperparameter tuning using smaller datasets, A/B testing with shorter run times, simulations preceding physical experiments). The proposed algorithm is practical and comes with strong theoretical guarantees, making it a valuable tool for practitioners. This foundational work will likely inspire a great deal of follow-up research, such as extending the multi-fidelity concept to contextual bandits, adversarial settings, or reinforcement learning.
*   **Weaknesses:** The empirical evaluation, while supportive of the theory, is limited to synthetic problems. The paper's impact could be further amplified by demonstrating the algorithm's effectiveness on a more realistic semi-synthetic or real-world dataset. For example, a simulation of an algorithm selection task where fidelities correspond to running algorithms on increasing subsets of a large dataset would be a very compelling demonstration.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Introduces a novel, well-motivated, and practical bandit formulation with broad applicability.
2.  Proposes an elegant and intuitive algorithm (MF-UCB) that naturally extends the UCB principle.
3.  Provides a strong and rigorous theoretical analysis, including a near-optimality result established via a lower bound.
4.  The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
1.  The practical applicability is somewhat limited by the assumption that bias parameters `ζ(m)` are known a priori.
2.  The experimental validation is confined to synthetic data.

In summary, this is an excellent paper that introduces a novel and important problem, proposes a natural and effective algorithm, and supports it with strong theoretical results. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses, which mostly represent opportunities for future work. This paper is a significant contribution to the multi-armed bandit literature and is a clear accept for NIPS.
