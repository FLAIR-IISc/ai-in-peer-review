This paper introduces a novel and practical variant of the stochastic multi-armed bandit problem, termed the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit. The key idea is that for each arm, an agent can choose to observe its outcome at one of several "fidelities," where lower fidelities are cheaper but provide biased approximations of the true, high-fidelity reward. This setting is well-motivated by real-world applications like online advertising or algorithm configuration. The authors propose MF-UCB, an elegant extension of the classic UCB algorithm, which intelligently uses the low-cost fidelities to quickly prune suboptimal arms and reserves expensive, high-fidelity pulls for a smaller set of promising candidates. The paper's main contributions are the problem formalism, the MF-UCB algorithm, and a thorough theoretical analysis.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The multi-fidelity formulation is a significant and timely contribution. It captures a common practical challenge not addressed by standard bandit models. The potential impact is high, as the framework could be applied to numerous resource-constrained exploration-exploitation problems.
*   **Quality:** The paper is technically strong. The proposed MF-UCB algorithm is intuitive and well-designed. The claims are rigorously supported by a regret analysis (Theorem 2) that clearly demonstrates the advantage over naive UCB. Crucially, the authors also provide a lower bound (Theorem 4), showing that their algorithm is near-optimal under certain conditions. The inclusion of synthetic experiments provides clear empirical validation of the theoretical findings.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem is motivated clearly, the notation is defined, and the algorithm is presented accessibly. The authors are honest about the limitations of their analysis, noting the gap between the upper and lower bounds for a specific class of arms and leaving it as an open problem.

**Weaknesses:**
*   Assumption 1, requiring a fast decay of the bias terms `ζ(m)`, is critical for the most appealing theoretical results. While reasonable, a brief discussion on the algorithm's performance under milder conditions would be welcome.
*   The experiments are conducted on synthetic data. While sufficient to validate the theory, exploring the algorithm's performance on a semi-synthetic or real-world problem where fidelities naturally arise would further strengthen the paper's claims of practical utility.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Introduces a novel and significant problem formulation.
2.  Provides a strong, near-optimal algorithm with rigorous theoretical guarantees.
3.  The work is clear, complete, and likely to inspire follow-up research.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  The experimental validation is limited to synthetic problems.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper presenting a novel framework with strong theoretical and empirical support. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses. I strongly recommend acceptance.
