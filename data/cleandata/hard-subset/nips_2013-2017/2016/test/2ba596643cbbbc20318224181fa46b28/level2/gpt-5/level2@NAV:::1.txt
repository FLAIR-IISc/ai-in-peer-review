Summary and relation to prior work: The paper introduces a multi-fidelity variant of the stochastic K-armed bandit in which each arm can be probed at one of M fidelities with increasing cost and decreasing bias relative to the highest-fidelity reward. The main claims are: (1) a formalization of a cost-weighted regret under a capital budget Λ where reward is always measured against the highest-fidelity mean; (2) MF-UCB, an upper-confidence-bound algorithm that integrates fidelity-specific confidence intervals with bias offsets and a fidelity-switching rule; (3) a regret upper bound showing improved scaling vs. naive single-fidelity UCB when lower fidelities can eliminate many suboptimal arms cheaply; and (4) a problem-dependent lower bound showing MF-UCB is near-optimal except for a delineated class of instances. The work connects to UCB (Auer et al.), regret analyses (Bubeck & Cesa-Bianchi), budgeted bandits, and multi-fidelity optimization/learning, but differs by directly modeling biased low-fidelity probes and providing bandit-style regret guarantees.

Strengths:
- Technical quality: Clear algorithmic design with principled upper bounds B(m) augmented by bias ζ(m), and a fidelity selection rule via γ(m) tied to costs λ(m). Theoretical support includes nonasymptotic upper bounds, a lower bound via change-of-measure, and a precise characterization of when multi-fidelity helps.
- Novelty: The explicit multi-fidelity bandit formalism with biased approximations and cost-aware confidence composition is original relative to budgeted bandits and multi-fidelity optimization without bandit regret.
- Significance: Useful in settings like ads, AutoML, and sim-to-real RL where cheap proxies are common. The analysis reveals when and how lower fidelities reduce regret.
- Clarity: Generally well-written with careful notation and intuition; limitations and an open gap are candidly discussed.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Practicality hinges on knowing ζ(m) and ψ; mis-specification could harm performance. Suggest adding methods to estimate or adapt to unknown bias bounds (e.g., conservative bias learning, robust UCB with bias inflation).
- Assumption 1 (fast-decaying ζ) may be restrictive; sensitivity analyses or variants that adapt to poorly separated fidelities would strengthen impact.
- Empirics are limited to synthetic data; include real or semi-synthetic case studies, ablations on ζ/λ misspecification, and comparisons to alternative baselines (e.g., algorithms that treat low fidelity as high-variance surrogates, Thompson-sampling variants, successive elimination).
- The regret notion fixes reward to µ(M); a short experimental section exploring alternative objectives (as hinted in the conclusion) would broaden applicability.

Evaluation:
- Quality: Strong theoretical backing; experiments modest.
- Clarity: Good, though notation-heavy; a schematic for γ(m) choice and an algorithm box helps.
- Originality: High.
- Significance: High when ζ, λ are available/estimable.

Arguments:
- Pros: Novel problem/formalism; solid theory with near-optimality; practical insight; clear exposition.
- Cons: Dependence on known bias bounds; limited empirical validation; gap to lower bound on K(m)7 instances; restrictive regularity assumption.
