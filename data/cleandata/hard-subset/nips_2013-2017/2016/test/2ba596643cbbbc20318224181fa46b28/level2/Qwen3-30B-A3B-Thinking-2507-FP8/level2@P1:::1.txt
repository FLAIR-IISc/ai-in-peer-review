

This paper introduces a novel multi-fidelity variant of the multi-armed bandit problem, where each arm can be observed at multiple fidelities with different costs and biases. The authors formalize this setting and propose MF-UCB, an adaptive algorithm that leverages cheaper approximations to reduce regret compared to standard UCB approaches. The problem is well-motivated by practical applications like online advertising, where displaying ads for shorter periods or to narrower audiences provides cheaper but biased performance estimates.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it provides a clear formalization of the multi-fidelity bandit problem with a cost-weighted regret definition that accounts for the trade-off between fidelity costs and the quality of estimates. Second, the authors design MF-UCB, which intelligently allocates plays across fidelities by using lower fidelities for exploration and higher fidelities for exploitation. Third, they establish theoretical guarantees showing MF-UCB achieves regret bounds that are nearly optimal compared to a lower bound, with significant improvements over standard UCB when the number of promising arms is small relative to the total number of arms.

The theoretical analysis is particularly strong, with careful attention to the trade-offs between fidelities. The authors establish both an upper bound for MF-UCB and a matching lower bound, demonstrating that the algorithm is nearly optimal under certain conditions. The proof sketches provide sufficient intuition for the main results, and the paper carefully addresses the challenges of handling biases across fidelities rather than treating them as simply higher-variance observations.

The empirical results on synthetic data support the theoretical claims, showing that MF-UCB consistently outperforms standard UCB across different problem settings. The paper includes a useful visualization showing how MF-UCB strategically allocates plays across fidelities, with most suboptimal arms being explored at lower fidelities.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, though the notation is somewhat dense for readers unfamiliar with bandit literature. The related work section appropriately contextualizes the contribution within the bandit literature and distinguishes it from multi-fidelity optimization work in other domains. The authors acknowledge limitations of their approach, particularly the gap between their regret bound and the lower bound for certain arms (those in K(m)7), and discuss this as an important direction for future work.

Some minor suggestions for improvement: The paper could provide more intuition for the choice of γ(m) in equation (4) beyond the brief discussion. While the synthetic experiments are convincing, the paper might benefit from a brief discussion of how the algorithm would behave in a real-world application beyond the online advertising example. The paper also might clarify how the assumption about ζ(m) decay (Assumption 1) affects practical implementation.

The main contribution of this paper is significant: it formalizes a practically important problem that had not been systematically studied in the bandit literature, and provides a theoretically grounded solution. The multi-fidelity framework extends the applicability of bandit algorithms to domains where observations have varying costs and quality. This is particularly relevant for applications where high-fidelity evaluations are expensive but lower-fidelity approximations are available.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and the empirical results complement the theoretical findings. The paper is complete as a scientific contribution, with appropriate attention to both strengths and limitations.

The clarity of the paper is generally good, though the notation might be challenging for readers new to bandit problems. The authors clearly explain the intuition behind their approach, which helps readers understand the algorithm's design.

The originality is strong: while multi-fidelity methods exist in other domains, this paper is the first to formalize and analyze regret in the multi-fidelity bandit setting. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work on budgeted bandits and multi-fidelity optimization.

The significance of this work is high: it provides a practical framework for efficiently solving exploration-exploitation problems when high-cost observations are available alongside cheaper approximations. This has immediate applicability to online advertising, algorithm selection, and other domains where experiments have varying costs and quality.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a clear contribution to the bandit literature. The theoretical analysis is thorough, the empirical results support the claims, and the problem formulation is well-motivated by practical applications. The minor suggestions above are not critical to the paper's contribution but could further strengthen it.

The paper meets all the criteria for acceptance at NIPS: it is of high quality, clear, original, and significant. The authors have made a valuable contribution to the field by formalizing and solving an important problem that had not been systematically addressed before. I recommend acceptance.
