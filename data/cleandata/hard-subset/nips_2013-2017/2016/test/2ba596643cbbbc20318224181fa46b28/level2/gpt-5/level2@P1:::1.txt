Summary and relation to prior work
The paper introduces a stochastic K-armed bandit setting where, for each arm, multiple “fidelities” of feedback exist: lower fidelities are cheaper but biased approximations of the highest-fidelity reward. The authors formalize a cost-weighted pseudo-regret under a fixed capital budget Λ, choose to measure reward with respect to the highest-fidelity mean regardless of the fidelity played, and propose MF-UCB, an optimism-based policy that (i) uses upper confidence envelopes corrected by known bias bounds ζ(m) and (ii) adaptively chooses the fidelity via thresholds γ(m) that trade off cost and informativeness. Theoretically, they prove an upper bound showing MF-UCB concentrates most high-cost plays on a small “hard” subset of arms near-optimal at lower fidelities, yielding regret improvements over naive UCB that ignores approximations. They also prove a lower bound (for Bernoulli rewards) showing near-optimality up to a gap on a particular subset of arms. Experiments on synthetic data corroborate the improved regret scaling and illustrate fidelity usage patterns.

This problem differs from classical “budgeted bandits” (which optimize reward/cost ratios per arm) and from multi-fidelity optimization in GP settings, and is closest in spirit to bandits with biased proxies or side observations; the specific combination of bias-bounded fidelities, cost-aware regret, and UCB-style control seems novel in the stochastic MAB literature.

Strengths
- Novel and well-motivated problem formulation capturing practical scenarios (advertising, algorithm selection, simulation-to-real transfer).
- Clear algorithmic idea: a single UCB per arm formed by the min of fidelity-specific high-probability upper bounds corrected for known bias, plus a principled switching rule γ(m) that depends on cost ratios and the concentration function ψ.
- Solid theoretical analysis:
  - Upper bound decomposes regret by partitions K(m) determined by fidelity-level near-optimality; it cleanly explains how most cheap exploration is spent at lower fidelities and expensive plays are reserved for few promising arms.
  - Lower bound (Bernoulli) matches the upper bound up to a gap on K(m)7, clearly identifying why the switch criterion cannot be optimal on those instances.
  - Conditions and assumptions are explicit (concentration via ψ, known bias ζ(m), Assumption 1 to avoid nearly redundant fidelities).
- Intuitive insights borne out by simulations: MF-UCB focuses high-fidelity plays on a small set and attains better slopes than UCB.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Practicality of known bias bounds ζ(m): The algorithm and guarantees rely critically on tight, known bounds |µ(M)k − µ(m)k| ≤ ζ(m). In many applications these bounds may be unknown, heterogeneous across arms, or only learnable. Robustness to misspecification (ζ too small/large) is not analyzed.
- Dependence on ψ and γ(m): The switching rule requires ψ and cost ratios to set γ(m). While ψ is known for sub-Gaussian rewards, guidance for misspecified ψ or heavy-tailed rewards would help. Sensitivity to ρ and γ choices is not empirically studied.
- Assumption 1 (fast decay of ζ) is stronger than necessary for correctness; it is used for the most appealing comparison. The paper notes this, but it remains unclear how performance degrades when fidelities are “dense” (λ(m+1) ≈ λ(m), ζ(m+1) ≈ ζ(m)). A heuristic to skip redundant fidelities could be valuable.
- Lower bound restricted to Bernoulli and uses Assumption 3 to exclude pathological policies. Extending the lower bound to general sub-Gaussian rewards and clarifying tightness without Assumption 3 would strengthen the contribution.
- Experiments are limited to synthetic setups and compare only to UCB. Including simple multifidelity heuristics (e.g., sequential elimination using only low fidelities, Thompson sampling variants, or cost-aware successive elimination) and a semi-synthetic or real task would improve empirical support. An ablation varying K, M, cost ratios, and ζ schedules to verify predicted scaling would be helpful.
- Regret definition: Competing against Λµ* with q_t = µ(M)It is reasonable for the motivating applications, but a brief empirical discussion of alternative objectives (e.g., fidelity-specific utilities or asymmetrical penalties) would broaden impact.

Quality
- The results are technically sound under stated assumptions. Proof ideas are sensible (UCB-style confidence control, partition-based accounting, change-of-measure lower bounds). The gap on K(m)7 is acknowledged and well explained. Some steps rely on constants hidden in . notation; providing cleaner constants or a supplementary with explicit constants would aid reproducibility.

Clarity
- Overall clearly written and well organized, with useful intuition (e.g., Figure 1 partitioning). Suggestions:
  - Include the algorithm box in the main text with all parameters; explicitly restate assumptions and the ψ, ζ, λ dependencies near Theorem 2.
  - Provide more intuition for γ(m) beyond the brief motivation, possibly a back-of-the-envelope derivation for sub-Gaussian rewards.
  - Clarify the oracle interpretation under the regret definition and emphasize that the reward is always evaluated at the highest fidelity.

Originality
- The multifidelity MAB formalism and MF-UCB design appear novel. The blend of bias-corrected UCB aggregation and cost-aware switching is a distinctive contribution relative to prior budgeted bandits and GP-based multifidelity optimization.

Significance
- Addresses a practically important setting and provides a principled, near-optimal solution with clear performance improvements when many arms can be eliminated cheaply. Likely to spur follow-up work on unknown bias learning, robust variants, and alternative regret notions.

Detailed questions and suggestions
- Robustness to ζ(m) misspecification: Can the analysis be adapted if ζ(m) are upper bounds that are loose, or if only order-of-magnitude is known? Could one learn per-arm bias envelopes online?
- Skipping fidelities: Can MF-UCB incorporate a data-driven rule to bypass near-redundant fidelities (violating Assumption 1) while preserving guarantees?
- Heavy tails: Do the results extend with robust UCB (e.g., Catoni/trimmed means) replacing X̄ and a corresponding ψ?
- Lower bound: Is an extension to sub-Gaussian families possible? Can Assumption 3 be relaxed or replaced by a standard uniform efficiency requirement?
- Experiments: Add baselines (e.g., phased elimination using increasing fidelities, Thompson sampling with bias bounds), and sensitivity plots over λ and ζ.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, novel formulation of a relevant problem.
- Well-motivated, simple algorithm with strong theoretical guarantees and insightful analysis.
- Demonstrated improvement over standard UCB consistent with theory.

Arguments against acceptance
- Heavy reliance on known, fidelity-wide bias bounds and ψ; unclear robustness in practice.
- Limited empirical evaluation (only synthetic; narrow set of baselines).
- Lower bound scope limited to Bernoulli and a gap remains for K(m)7.

Overall
A strong and timely contribution with solid theory and clear ideas. I lean toward acceptance, with the caveat that enriching the empirical section and discussing robustness to unknown ζ/ψ would substantially strengthen the paper.
