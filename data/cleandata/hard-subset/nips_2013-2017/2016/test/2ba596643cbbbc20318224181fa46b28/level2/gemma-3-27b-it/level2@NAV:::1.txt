## Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

This paper introduces a novel multi-fidelity bandit framework where obtaining rewards is costly, but cheaper, approximate rewards are available. The authors formalize this problem, motivated by examples like online advertising and algorithm selection, and propose a Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB) algorithm. The core idea is to leverage lower-fidelity approximations to quickly eliminate suboptimal arms, reserving expensive high-fidelity evaluations for promising candidates.

The paper's strength lies in its clear problem formulation and the intuitive appeal of MF-UCB. The algorithm is well-defined, and the theoretical analysis provides a regret bound showing that MF-UCB adapts to available approximations and can outperform naive strategies that ignore them. The inclusion of a lower bound demonstrates near-optimality under certain conditions, strengthening the contribution.  The connection to existing UCB methods is well-articulated, and the analysis highlights the conditions under which MF-UCB provides significant improvements.  The simulations further support the theoretical results, demonstrating practical gains over standard UCB. 

However, the paper has some weaknesses. The assumptions, particularly Assumption 1 regarding the decay of ζ(m), feel somewhat restrictive. While justified, the implications of violating this assumption are not fully explored. The discussion of the regret definition and alternative choices for *qt* feels somewhat cursory and could be expanded. While the lower bound is presented, the gap between it and the MF-UCB bound, particularly for certain arms, warrants further investigation, as acknowledged by the authors. The notation, while generally clear, could be more consistent (e.g., the use of “T(m)k,t” and “T(>m)k,t”). 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and relevant problem formulation with clear practical applications.
*   Well-designed algorithm (MF-UCB) with a strong theoretical justification.
*   Demonstrated near-optimality with a matching lower bound.
*   Empirical results support the theoretical findings.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Restrictive assumptions that limit the applicability of the results.
*   Some aspects of the analysis (regret definition, lower bound gap) could be more thoroughly explored.
*   Minor notational inconsistencies.



**Overall:**

Despite some minor weaknesses, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the multi-armed bandit literature. The introduction of the multi-fidelity framework is a significant step forward, and MF-UCB offers a promising approach to handling scenarios where expensive evaluations can be supplemented by cheaper approximations. I recommend acceptance, with a request that the authors address the points raised regarding the assumptions and the lower bound in a revised version.  The paper is well-written and clearly explains its contributions, making it a worthwhile addition to NIPS.
