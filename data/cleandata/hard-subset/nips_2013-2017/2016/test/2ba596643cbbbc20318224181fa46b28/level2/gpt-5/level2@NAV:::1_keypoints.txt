- Paper proposes a multi-fidelity stochastic K-armed bandit where each arm can be probed at M fidelities with increasing cost and decreasing bias relative to the highest fidelity.  
- Main contributions: (1) cost-weighted regret under a capital budget Λ measured against highest-fidelity mean; (2) MF-UCB algorithm combining fidelity-specific confidence intervals with bias offsets and a fidelity-switching rule; (3) regret upper bound showing gains over naive single-fidelity UCB when low fidelities can cheaply eliminate arms; (4) problem-dependent lower bound showing near-optimality except for a delineated class of instances.  
- Positions work relative to UCB, regret literature, budgeted bandits, and multi-fidelity optimization, emphasizing modeling of biased low-fidelity probes with bandit-style regret guarantees.  

Strengths:
- Strong technical quality: principled algorithm design, nonasymptotic upper bounds, change-of-measure lower bound, and clear conditions for when multi-fidelity helps.  
- Novelty: explicit formalism for biased multi-fidelity bandits and cost-aware confidence composition is original.  
- Significance: relevant to practical domains (ads, AutoML, sim-to-real RL) and provides insight into when low fidelities reduce regret.  
- Clarity: generally well-written with careful notation and transparent discussion of limitations and open gaps.  

Weaknesses / suggestions:
- Practicality depends on knowing bias bounds ζ(m) and parameter ψ; mis-specification could hurt performance — recommend methods to estimate/adapt to unknown biases (conservative bias learning, robust UCB).  
- Assumption 1 (fast-decaying ζ) may be restrictive; include sensitivity analyses or adaptive variants for poorly separated fidelities.  
- Experiments limited to synthetic data; add real/semi-synthetic case studies, ablations on ζ/λ misspecification, and comparisons to alternative baselines (e.g., high-variance surrogates, TS variants, successive elimination).  
- Regret objective fixes reward to µ(M); consider experimental exploration of alternative objectives as suggested in the conclusion.  

Evaluation summary:
- Quality: strong theoretical backing, experiments modest.  
- Clarity: good overall but notation-heavy; add schematic for γ(m) choice and an algorithm box.  
- Originality: high.  
- Significance: high when ζ and λ are available or estimable.  

Pros:
- Novel problem formalism, solid near-optimal theory, practical insights, and clear exposition.  

Cons:
- Reliance on known bias bounds, limited empirical validation, gap to lower bound on certain instances, and potentially restrictive regularity assumption.
