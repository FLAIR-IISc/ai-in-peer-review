## Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

This paper introduces a multi-fidelity extension to the classical K-armed bandit problem, motivated by scenarios where evaluating arms is costly but cheaper, approximate evaluations are available (e.g., online advertising, algorithm selection). The authors formalize this as a multi-fidelity bandit and propose MF-UCB, a novel UCB-based algorithm that adaptively chooses fidelity levels. They provide theoretical guarantees demonstrating near-optimality, complemented by empirical validation.

The paper builds directly on the foundational work of Robbins [11] and the UCB framework [3, 9, 12]. It distinguishes itself from related work in multi-fidelity optimization [7, 10] and reinforcement learning [6] by explicitly focusing on the exploration-exploitation dilemma inherent in bandit settings, and providing regret bounds.  It also differentiates itself from budgeted bandits [13, 15] by exploring cheaper *approximations* rather than costs associated with rewards.  The recent work of Kandasamy et al. [8] on Gaussian process bandits with multi-fidelity evaluations is acknowledged and this paper provides a more general analysis beyond the GP assumption.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clear Problem Formulation:** The multi-fidelity bandit problem is well-defined and convincingly motivated with practical examples.
*   **Strong Theoretical Results:** The regret analysis is rigorous, establishing near-optimality and demonstrating how MF-UCB adapts to varying fidelity costs and approximation qualities.
*   **Algorithm Design:** The MF-UCB algorithm is intuitively appealing, intelligently balancing exploration at low fidelities with exploitation at high fidelities.
*   **Empirical Validation:** Simulations support the theoretical findings, showing the algorithm's performance improvements over a naive UCB strategy.
*   **Well-Written & Organized:** The paper is generally clearly written and logically structured.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Assumption 1:** While the authors acknowledge it’s not *necessary* for the analysis, Assumption 1 (on the decay of ζ(m)) seems somewhat restrictive.  The impact of violating this assumption on performance isn't thoroughly discussed.
*   **Lower Bound Gap:** The authors identify a gap between their achieved regret bound and the lower bound in certain scenarios, particularly when the fidelity switching is suboptimal. This warrants further investigation.
*   **Limited Scope of Simulations:** The simulation section feels somewhat brief. More comprehensive experiments, perhaps with varying problem parameters and comparisons to other bandit algorithms, would strengthen the empirical claims.
* **Discussion on qt:** The discussion of alternative choices for qt and their implications, alluded to at the end of Section 7, could be expanded upon to offer further insights.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and well-motivated problem formulation.
*   Strong theoretical guarantees demonstrating near-optimality.
*   Intuitively designed and empirically validated algorithm.
*   Significant contribution to the understanding of exploration-exploitation in multi-fidelity settings.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Relatively restrictive Assumption 1.
*   Identified gap between achieved bound and lower bound.
*   Somewhat limited empirical evaluation.

**Overall:**

This is a solid and valuable contribution to the multi-armed bandit literature. While the identified weaknesses are present, they do not overshadow the paper’s strengths. The novel problem formulation, strong theoretical results, and practical algorithm design make this paper a worthy addition to NIPS. I recommend **acceptance**.
