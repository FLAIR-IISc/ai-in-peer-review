

This paper introduces the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit problem, extending the classical K-armed bandit framework to situations where observing outcomes at high fidelity (expensive) is costly, but cheaper approximations (lower fidelities) are available. The authors formalize this as a setting where each arm can be observed at M fidelities with increasing cost λ(1) < λ(2) < ... < λ(M), and each fidelity provides a biased estimate of the true reward. They propose MF-UCB, a novel algorithm that adapts the UCB principle to this setting, using lower fidelities to eliminate suboptimal arms and reserving high-fidelity observations for promising candidates. Theoretical analysis shows MF-UCB achieves regret O(∑_k∈K(m) Δ^(M)_k λ^(m)/ψ(Δ^(m)_k)) under certain conditions, which outperforms standard UCB when |Kg| < K and λ(1) < λ(2). The authors complement this with a lower bound demonstrating near-optimality.

The paper builds on classic bandit work (e.g., [3, 9]) and related multi-fidelity optimization methods [7, 10, 16], but makes a significant contribution by formalizing the regret in this specific context and providing a principled algorithm. The theoretical analysis is thorough, with careful consideration of bias and cost trade-offs, and the empirical results confirm the theoretical advantages.

Strengths: The problem formulation is clear and relevant to practical applications like online advertising and algorithm selection. The algorithm design is elegant, the theoretical results are well-supported, and the lower bound analysis provides strong theoretical grounding. The paper is well-written and organized, making complex ideas accessible.

Weaknesses: The lower bound shows a gap for arms in K^(m)_7 (where some lower fidelity suggests an arm might be optimal when it's not), which the authors acknowledge. While this is a minor limitation, it's worth noting that the gap arises from the algorithm's design to avoid excessive sampling at lower fidelities, which is actually a strength in practice.

Quality: High - technically sound with comprehensive theoretical analysis. Clarity: High - well-organized with clear explanations. Originality: High - introduces a meaningful extension to bandit theory with clear distinctions from related work. Significance: High - addresses a practical problem with demonstrated advantages over standard approaches in both theory and simulation.

This paper makes a solid contribution to bandit theory with clear practical relevance. It meets all criteria for acceptance at NIPS, demonstrating high quality, clarity, originality, and significance. The minor gap in the lower bound analysis doesn't detract from the strong overall contribution.
