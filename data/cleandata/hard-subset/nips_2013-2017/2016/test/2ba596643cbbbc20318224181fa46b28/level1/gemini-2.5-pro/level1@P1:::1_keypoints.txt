- Introduces the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit problem where each arm can be played at M fidelities trading cost for bias/noise, with goal to minimize cost-weighted pseudo-regret under budget Λ.

- Proposes the MF-UCB algorithm that maintains per-arm-per-fidelity confidence bounds, selects the arm with highest overall UCB, and chooses the cheapest fidelity that still provides useful information.

- Provides a rigorous regret upper bound showing MF-UCB adaptively uses low fidelities to prune arms and saves cost versus naive high-fidelity UCB.

- Derives a problem-specific lower bound and shows MF-UCB is near-optimal for a broad class of instances.

- Supports theoretical claims with synthetic simulations.

- Novel and significant problem formulation with clear practical motivation (e.g., hyperparameter tuning, experiments), likely to spark new research.

- Technical quality is high: elegant algorithm, insightful analysis, interpretable arm partitioning (K(m)), and inclusion of lower bound.

- Paper is very clear, well-written, and presents motivation, notation, algorithm, and proof sketches effectively.

- Main practicality concern: analysis assumes known bias bounds ζ(m) for each fidelity, which is unrealistic in many applications and limits direct applicability.

- Assumption 1 (decay rate of ζ(m)) and its role are ambiguously presented; clearer explanation of consequences if dropped is needed.

- There is a acknowledged gap between upper and lower bounds for a subset of arms (K(m)); the discussion is brief and could explore whether the gap is unavoidable or closable.

- Experimental evaluation is minimal and purely synthetic; a real-like case study (e.g., hyperparameter tuning with varying data/epochs) would strengthen practical claims.

- Arguments for acceptance: novel important setting, elegant effective algorithm, strong theoretical analysis, and excellent exposition.

- Arguments against acceptance: reliance on known ζ(m) limits practicality and experiments lack real-world validation.

- Overall recommendation: strongly recommend acceptance—strengths substantially outweigh weaknesses and the work opens a promising research direction.
