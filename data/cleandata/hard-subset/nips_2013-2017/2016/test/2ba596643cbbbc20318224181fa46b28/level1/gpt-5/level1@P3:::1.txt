Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies a stochastic K-armed bandit where observing the “true” (highest-fidelity) reward is expensive but cheaper, biased approximations are available. Each arm k has M fidelities with costs λ(1) < … < λ(M) and means μk(m), with known bias bounds |μk(M) − μk(m)| ≤ ζ(m), where ζ(m) decreases to 0. The paper proposes MF-UCB, which maintains UCB-style upper bounds for μk(M) constructed from data at all fidelities by adding ζ(m) to the confidence radii at fidelity m and taking the minimum across fidelities. It also specifies a fidelity-selection rule: after choosing the arm with largest bound, the algorithm escalates from low to high fidelities until the confidence radius at fidelity m exceeds a threshold γ(m) = ψ−1((λ(m)/λ(m+1)) ψ(ζ(m))). The key idea is to use cheap fidelities to eliminate clearly suboptimal arms and reserve expensive high-fidelity pulls for arms near-optimal at the highest fidelity.

The authors formalize a cost-weighted pseudo-regret suitable for this setting, derive an upper bound for MF-UCB that decomposes arms into partitions K(m) according to where they can be eliminated, and show MF-UCB is never much worse than single-fidelity UCB and can be substantially better when many arms are eliminated at low fidelities. They also provide a lower bound (for Bernoulli rewards) under a mild regularity assumption that excludes strategies that sample suboptimal arms polynomially often, and show MF-UCB is near-optimal except for a specific subset of arms where eliminating them at a lower fidelity would be theoretically cheaper, but the algorithm elects to eliminate them higher up.

The paper connects to classical UCB [Auer et al.], minimax regret for stochastic bandits [Lai & Robbins], budgeted bandits [Tran-Thanh et al.; Xia et al.], and multi-fidelity optimization (gradient-free, kriging). It is distinct from budgeted bandits in that here fidelities are biased proxies for the same arm’s reward, and the goal is cumulative regret measured in high-fidelity units. It would also be useful to discuss relations to Bandits with Knapsacks (resource-constrained bandits) and to pure-exploration with costs.

Quality
- Soundness: The algorithmic design is well motivated. The high-probability bounds on confidence sets that combine fidelity-specific uncertainty with bias envelopes are standard. The fidelity-selection threshold γ(m) is carefully chosen to equilibrate the cost of eliminating an arm at fidelity m versus escalating. The regret analysis is technically nontrivial: the partition K(m) clarifies where effort is spent; the proof controls both expected counts and tail probabilities to bound the total number of plays under a capital Λ and derives E[R]/log(nΛ) rates. The lower bound uses a change-of-measure argument tailored to biased fidelities and highlights a gap for a subset K(m)7.
- Assumptions: (i) Known bias bounds ζ(m) and known concentration ψ for all fidelities; (ii) Assumption 1 (fast decay of ζ(m)) for the most appealing gains; (iii) independence and stationarity of observations at each fidelity; and (iv) the lower-bound Assumption 3. These are reasonable for theory but somewhat strong for practice. In particular, knowing tight ζ(m) is nontrivial; mis-specification could hurt both correctness and performance. The independence across fidelities for the same arm (not explicitly discussed) may simplify analysis but ignores potentially useful correlation structures.
- Empirics: Simulations support the theory and illustrate that MF-UCB invests high-fidelity pulls only on promising arms. However, the empirical section is quite limited: only synthetic problems, few baselines (no heuristic multi-fidelity filters), and no robustness studies to mis-specified ζ(m) or ψ. Parameter sensitivity (ρ, γ) is not explored.

Clarity
The paper is mostly clear, with a careful setup and consistent notation. The regret definition with cost-weighted pseudo-reward is well motivated by applications where only the highest-fidelity reward “counts.” The K(m) partitions and the role of γ(m) are explained, although the notation K(m)3 and K(m)7 in the lower bound is abrupt and could be renamed more descriptively. A few equations could be streamlined (the two-term presentation of the MF-UCB bound in Theorem 2 may confuse readers; consider presenting the dominant term and conditions under which the first term is dominated). A brief discussion of dependence assumptions across fidelities would help.

Originality
The formalization of a multi-fidelity bandit with biased proxies and costs, and the associated regret notion, is novel relative to prior work on budgeted bandits, side observations, or multi-fidelity optimization without regret analysis. The MF-UCB design and its analysis (with the γ(m) threshold tied to costs and bias envelopes) are original and technically interesting. The lower bound tailored to biased fidelities is a valuable addition.

Significance
The setting is practically important (ads, algorithm selection, simulation-based evaluation), and the results provide principled guidance: use cheap proxies to prune aggressively and spend budget on near-optimal candidates. The regret improvements can be substantial when K is large and only a small set of arms are near-optimal. The work should stimulate further research (adaptive/unknown ζ, correlated fidelities, contextual extensions, best-arm identification with multi-fidelity costs).

Strengths
- Clear and compelling problem formulation with appropriate regret metric.
- Simple, implementable algorithm with a principled fidelity-selection rule.
- Nontrivial regret analysis with interpretable partitions and near-optimality guarantees; lower bound highlights limits.
- Good intuition for when gains over UCB are large (small K(M), large K overall).
- Connections to several application domains.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Strong knowledge assumptions (ζ(m), ψ). Suggest: discuss procedures to estimate or upper bound ζ(m) online with confidence (leading to conservative but valid bounds), and analyze robustness to mis-specification.
- Limited empirical evaluation. Suggest: add baselines that (i) run UCB at low fidelity to filter arms then run high-fidelity UCB on survivors; (ii) simple cost-aware escalation heuristics; evaluate sensitivity to incorrect ζ. Include at least one semi-realistic scenario (e.g., synthetic proxies derived from a real dataset).
- Assumption 1 (fast bias decay) is not essential but underpins the most appealing improvements; more discussion of performance when it is violated would be useful.
- The lower-bound gap for K(m)7 is explained; however, could an adaptive variant that occasionally “probes” at low fidelities to detect small positive gaps reduce this gap without blowing the budget? Even a negative result or discussion would help.
- Clarify statistical assumptions: are samples across fidelities independent? If they are correlated (as in many simulators), exploiting correlation might further tighten bounds.

Pros
- Novel and relevant problem and regret notion.
- Elegant algorithm with clear intuition.
- Solid, detailed analysis and useful lower bound.
- Potentially significant practical impact.

Cons
- Practical assumptions (known ζ, ψ); no robustness study.
- Thin experiments and lack of stronger baselines.
- Some notation and presentation can be streamlined; dependence assumptions unclear.

Recommendation: Weak Accept. The theoretical contribution and formulation are strong and of clear interest to the NIPS community; with improved empirical evaluation and discussion of practicality, this could be an influential paper.
