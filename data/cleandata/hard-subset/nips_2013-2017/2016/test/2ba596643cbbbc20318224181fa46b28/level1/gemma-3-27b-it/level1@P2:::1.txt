This paper introduces a multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit (MF-MAB) problem where obtaining rewards is costly, but cheaper approximations are available. The authors present MF-UCB, a novel UCB-based algorithm, analyze its regret, and provide a lower bound demonstrating near-optimality. This work builds upon the extensive literature on MABs (Agrawal, 1995; Auer, 2002; Bubeck & Cesa-Bianchi, 2012) and extends it to a practically relevant setting.  It also connects to work on multi-fidelity optimization (Huang et al., 2006) and active learning (Zhang & Chaudhuri, 2016), though it explicitly focuses on the exploration-exploitation trade-off inherent in the bandit setting, which distinguishes it from those areas. The introduction clearly motivates the problem with examples like online advertising and algorithm selection.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The regret analysis is rigorous, building on standard UCB analysis techniques. The derivation of the regret bound (Theorem 2) appears correct, and the lower bound (Theorem 4) strengthens the claim of near-optimality. The assumptions, such as the regularity condition on ζ(m), are clearly stated, although the impact of violating Assumption 1 isn't fully explored. The inclusion of both an upper and lower bound significantly increases the confidence in the result. The discussion of the relationship between the algorithm's performance and the parameter ρ is also valuable.  The analysis logically connects the algorithm's design choices (like the γ(m) threshold) to its theoretical performance.  However, the proofs are relegated to the appendix, which makes it difficult to quickly assess the details without significant effort. While understandable given space constraints, a more concise explanation of the core proof ideas within the main text would be beneficial.  The simulations are helpful and support the theoretical results. 

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is mostly clear, though the multiple indices (m, k, t, s) can become a bit cumbersome. The motivation and intuition behind MF-UCB are well-explained. The algorithm is presented in a clear, step-by-step manner. However, the detailed analysis in the appendices makes it harder to fully grasp the technical intricacies without a deeper dive. A more intuitive explanation of the intuition behind the lower bound would be helpful. Also, the jump between equations (1) and (5) feels abrupt; briefly explaining the connection between regret and the cost would smooth the flow.

**Originality:** The MF-MAB formulation is novel, and the proposed MF-UCB algorithm is a substantial contribution. While the algorithm builds on the UCB framework, adapting it to the multi-fidelity setting is not trivial, as evidenced by the complexity of the analysis.  The paper clearly differentiates itself from existing work on multi-fidelity optimization and active learning by explicitly addressing the bandit setting's exploration-exploitation dilemma. The treatment of biased estimates from lower fidelities using the ζ(m) parameter is a clever approach.  The paper performs a thorough review of related work, demonstrating a good understanding of the field.

**Significance:** This work addresses a significant problem with practical relevance in areas such as online advertising, algorithm selection, and robotics.  The theoretical results—particularly the near-optimality guarantee—establish a strong foundation for future research. The ability to leverage cheaper approximations to accelerate learning and reduce the cost of experimentation is a valuable contribution.  The findings are likely to be of interest to researchers and practitioners working on bandit problems, reinforcement learning, and optimization. The insights gained from this work could be applied to various real-world scenarios where costly evaluations can be approximated with lower-fidelity alternatives.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** Introduces a new problem formulation (MF-MAB) and a corresponding algorithm (MF-UCB).
*   **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** Rigorous regret analysis and a near-optimality lower bound.
*   **Practical Relevance:** Addresses a real-world problem with applications in several domains.
*   **Clear Presentation:** Well-written and organized (despite some complexity in the analysis).
*   **Empirical Validation:** Simulations support the theoretical findings.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Proof Complexity:** The proofs are detailed and reside in the appendix, hindering quick understanding.
*   **Limited Discussion of Assumption Violations:** The consequences of violating Assumption 1 are not fully explored.
*   **Intuition for Lower Bound:** The intuition behind the lower bound could be more clearly explained.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. The MF-MAB formulation is novel and relevant, the theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the experimental results are promising. While the complexity of the analysis and the lack of detailed discussion on assumption violations are minor drawbacks, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to improve the clarity of the lower bound explanation and to provide a higher-level overview of the core ideas within the proofs (without necessarily including the full proofs in the main text).
