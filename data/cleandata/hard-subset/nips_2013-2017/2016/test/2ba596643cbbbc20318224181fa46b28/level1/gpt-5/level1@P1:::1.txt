Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper introduces a multi-fidelity variant of the stochastic K-armed bandit in which each arm can be evaluated at one of M fidelities with increasing costs λ(1) < … < λ(M) and decreasing bias bounds ζ(1) > … > ζ(M)=0. The central modeling assumption is a known uniform bias bound: |μ(M)
k − μ(m)
k| ≤ ζ(m). The objective is to maximize cumulative high-fidelity reward under a total capital budget Λ; regret is defined as capital-weighted pseudo-regret relative to an oracle that always plays an optimal arm (at any fidelity) but receives reward at the highest fidelity. The proposed algorithm, MF-UCB, maintains UCB-style indices per fidelity incorporating both statistical uncertainty and bias (adding ζ(m) to each index) and selects the minimum across fidelities as the arm index. After choosing the arm It, MF-UCB selects a fidelity by comparing the confidence radius ψ−1(ρ log t / T(m)
It,t−1) to a fidelity-dependent threshold γ(m) = ψ−1( (λ(m)/λ(m+1)) ψ(ζ(m)) ), thereby using cheaper fidelities to eliminate clearly suboptimal arms and reserving expensive fidelities for near-optimal arms.

Theoretical results include:
- An upper bound (Theorem 2) on expected regret showing MF-UCB’s cost-weighted regret scales like log(Λ/λ(M)) times a sum over arms that is dominated by the fidelity at which the arm can be eliminated, yielding a savings factor roughly λ(JkK)/λ(M) relative to naïve UCB that only uses fidelity M. A partition K(1),…,K(M) formalizes which arms are eliminated at which fidelities.
- A lower bound (Theorem 4) for Bernoulli rewards (ψ(·) ∈ Θ(·2)) for strategies with sub-polynomial pulls on suboptimal arms, showing MF-UCB is near-optimal for a large subset of arms (K(m)

This work is distinct from budgeted/bwK bandits (optimize reward per cost), active learning with weak/strong labelers, and multi-fidelity RL/BO (e.g., GP-based methods), and builds on classical UCB (Auer et al., Agrawal; Bubeck and Cesa-Bianchi). The setup also relates to resource-constrained bandits (Bandits with Knapsacks) but differs in its fidelity-structured bias and in using cumulative regret at the high-fidelity objective.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The algorithm design thoughtfully integrates bias bounds and costs into indices and fidelity selection. The analysis uses nontrivial partitioning of arms, high-probability control of the total number of plays N under capital Λ, and general concentration via a function ψ. The comparison to single-fidelity UCB is careful, showing MF-UCB is never much worse and can be substantially better. The lower bound is appropriate for Bernoulli rewards and highlights where MF-UCB is optimal vs. suboptimal.
- Assumptions: (i) Known bias bounds ζ(m) and costs λ(m); (ii) concentration (2) with ψ at least linear; (iii) a regularity condition on ζ decay (Assumption 1) to avoid redundant fidelities; (iv) a mild technical condition (Assumption 3) for the lower bound. While (ii) is standard, (i) and (iii) may be strong in practice. The authors acknowledge (iii) is not essential for analysis but for the most appealing comparisons.
- Experiments: Synthetic simulations show consistent gains and fidelity-wise play patterns aligning with theory. However, the empirical section is limited (no real data, no sensitivity to ζ mis-specification, and few baselines beyond naïve UCB).

Clarity:
- The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The regret definition is motivated and justified with an advertising example. MF-UCB is precisely specified; the role of ζ and γ(m) is explained. The K(m) partition and its intuition are helpful. Proof sketches are informative. Suggestions: move some constants from the appendix into the main text to improve readability; explicitly discuss tuning/choice of ρ; provide a brief complexity analysis; expand the experimental setup in the main paper to improve self-containment.

Originality:
- The modeling of multi-fidelity bandits with known bias bounds and fidelity-dependent costs, the capital-weighted regret definition tied to the highest fidelity, and the MF-UCB construction with fidelity-specific thresholds are novel in the cumulative-regret MAB literature. The lower bound and near-optimality characterization under this structure are also original. The work is clearly positioned relative to related areas (budgeted bandits, BwK, multi-fidelity BO/RL).

Significance:
- The problem is timely and practically motivated (ads, algorithm selection, simulation-vs-real evaluation). The algorithm is simple and likely to be adopted. The theory provides concrete conditions under which substantial improvements over standard UCB are guaranteed. This can influence follow-up work on cost-aware, structured information bandits and on multi-fidelity best-arm identification.

Pros:
- Clear and principled formulation of multi-fidelity bandits with costs and bias bounds.
- Simple, computationally light algorithm with strong theoretical guarantees and clear intuition.
- Regret improvements over single-fidelity UCB are quantified and interpretable.
- Matching lower bound for a broad subset of instances; careful discussion of the gap cases.

Cons:
- Requires known and correct bias bounds ζ(m); robustness to misspecification is neither analyzed nor tested.
- Regularity Assumption 1 may be restrictive; performance guarantees under arbitrary ζ sequences would strengthen the work.
- Experimental evaluation is limited to synthetic settings and a single baseline; lacks ablation/sensitivity studies and comparisons to other plausible heuristics (e.g., cost-aware successive elimination, Thompson sampling variants, BwK-inspired baselines).
- Lower bound mismatch for K(m)
7 suggests potential room for algorithmic improvement in switching policies.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add experiments on robustness to ζ(m) misspecification and to heavy-tailed rewards (ψ linear) and report sensitivity to ρ and γ(m).
- Compare against additional baselines: (i) a two-stage screen-then-exploit policy using low fidelities; (ii) Thompson sampling adapted with fidelity-aware priors; (iii) BwK-style algorithms treating fidelity choice as an action.
- Discuss practical ways to estimate or upper bound ζ(m) online, possibly with conservative confidence sets on fidelity biases.
- Elaborate on connections/differences with Bandits with Knapsacks and with best-arm identification under multi-fidelity.

Recommendation: Accept. The paper presents a well-motivated and novel problem formulation, a clean and effective algorithm with solid analysis (including a near-matching lower bound), and compelling intuition. Strengthening the empirical section and discussing robustness to ζ would further increase impact, but the theoretical contribution is strong and of clear interest to the NIPS community.
