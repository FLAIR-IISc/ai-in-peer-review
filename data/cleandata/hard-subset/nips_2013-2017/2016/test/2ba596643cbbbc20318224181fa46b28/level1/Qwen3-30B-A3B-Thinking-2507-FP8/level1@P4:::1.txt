

# Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

This paper introduces a novel multi-fidelity bandit framework that extends the classical K-armed bandit problem by incorporating multiple fidelity levels for each arm, where each fidelity provides a cheaper but potentially biased estimate of the highest-fidelity outcome. The authors formalize this setting with a cost-weighted regret definition, propose the MF-UCB algorithm that adaptively selects fidelities for exploration and exploitation, and provide theoretical guarantees showing MF-UCB achieves better regret bounds than standard UCB strategies. The work builds on the UCB framework but introduces significant algorithmic and theoretical challenges related to the multi-fidelity structure.

The paper is well-positioned within the bandit literature, with the authors correctly contextualizing their work against classical bandit results [9, 11, 12], UCB methods [2, 3], and related multi-fidelity work in optimization [7, 10] and active learning [16]. The authors acknowledge their prior work on multi-fidelity bandits with Gaussian process payoffs [8] and carefully distinguish their current contribution from budgeted bandit problems [13, 15] and other related approaches.

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical analysis is rigorous and comprehensive. The authors provide a clear regret bound for MF-UCB that demonstrates how the algorithm strategically uses lower fidelities to eliminate suboptimal arms and reserves higher fidelities for promising arms. The lower bound analysis shows MF-UCB is nearly optimal under certain conditions, with a clear explanation of the gap for specific arm types. The proofs are well-structured with appropriate lemmas and theorems.

2. **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem setting is clearly defined with precise notation. The intuition behind MF-UCB's design is effectively explained, and the figures (particularly Fig. 1) help illustrate the partitioning of arms by fidelity. The experimental results on synthetic data complement the theoretical findings, demonstrating that MF-UCB outperforms naive UCB strategies as predicted.

3. **Novelty:** The multi-fidelity bandit framework represents a meaningful extension of classical bandit problems. The authors' formalization of the problem with known biases (ζ(m)) and costs (λ(m)) is novel and addresses an important practical consideration in real-world applications like online advertising, algorithm selection, and robotics. The MF-UCB algorithm is a natural yet non-trivial adaptation of UCB to this setting.

4. **Significance:** The problem addressed has clear practical relevance across multiple domains where cheaper approximations to expensive experiments exist. The theoretical results provide a principled way to trade off between exploration cost and accuracy, with the potential for significant practical savings. The paper demonstrates that multi-fidelity bandits can substantially outperform standard approaches when the number of promising arms is small relative to the total number of arms.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The theoretical analysis assumes knowledge of the bias parameters ζ(m) and cost parameters λ(m) in advance. While this is reasonable for the formalization, the paper could more thoroughly discuss how these parameters might be estimated in practice when not known a priori.

2. The lower bound analysis reveals that MF-UCB is not optimal for all arms (specifically for arms in K(m)7), and while the authors provide a reasonable explanation for this gap, the paper could more explicitly address how to handle these cases in practice.

3. The experimental validation is limited to synthetic problems. While the paper mentions that real-world applications like online advertising are motivating examples, additional empirical validation on more realistic or real-world data would strengthen the practical significance.

**Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- The paper introduces a novel and well-motivated problem extension with clear theoretical contributions.
- The MF-UCB algorithm is a natural yet significant adaptation of UCB to the multi-fidelity setting.
- The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides clear insights into the algorithm's behavior.
- The results demonstrate meaningful improvements over standard approaches in both theory and experiments.
- The work is clearly positioned within the bandit literature and properly contextualizes related work.

*Con:*
- The assumption of known bias and cost parameters may limit immediate practical applicability.
- The experimental validation is limited to synthetic data.
- The paper doesn't fully address how to handle the cases where MF-UCB is suboptimal according to the lower bound.

**Conclusion:**

This paper presents a technically sound, clearly written, and novel contribution to the bandit literature that extends the classical framework to accommodate multi-fidelity evaluations. The theoretical results are rigorous and well-motivated, with clear practical implications for domains where cheaper approximations to expensive experiments exist. The paper is well-positioned within the NIPS community and makes a meaningful contribution to the field of bandit problems. The minor limitations regarding parameter knowledge and experimental validation do not detract from the paper's strong theoretical contributions. This work advances the state of the art in bandit theory and has potential for significant practical impact. I recommend acceptance.
