- Introduces the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit: cheaper, biased low-fidelity observations vs. expensive, accurate high-fidelity ones with a cost-aware regret objective under a budget.  
- Formal model: each arm k and fidelity m has cost λ^(m) and mean μ_k^(m), with known bias bounds |μ_k^(M) − μ_k^(m)| ≤ ζ^(m).  
- Proposes MF-UCB: combines information across fidelities to form tight UCBs on highest-fidelity means, selects arm with highest bound, then picks the cheapest fidelity whose uncertainty exceeds a threshold γ^(m).  
- Main theory: provides a regret upper bound for MF-UCB and a problem-specific lower bound, showing near-optimality on many instances.  
- Key analytic tool: partitions arms into sets K(m) to explain how cheap fidelities eliminate suboptimal arms and reduce regret.  
- Technical significance: extends classical UCB methods to a new fidelity-structured setting and fills a gap in regret analysis for multi-fidelity optimization.  
- Clarity and presentation: well-written, clearly motivated, with understandable algorithmic intuition; experiments (though brief) support theory.  
- Weakness — strong assumptions: requires known bias bounds ζ^(m), which may be unrealistic and could hurt practical applicability without robustness or online estimation.  
- Weakness — optimality gap: gap remains between upper and lower bounds for arms in K(m), suggesting the fidelity-selection rule may be suboptimal in some cases.  
- Weakness — minor clarity issues: Figure 1 axes unlabeled and caption typo; need explanation of necessity/effect of Assumption 1 on regret bounds.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel and important problem, elegant algorithm, strong and comprehensive theoretical analysis, and high potential impact.  
- Arguments against acceptance: reliance on known ζ^(m) limits practicality and demonstrated suboptimality for a specific class of arms.  
- Conclusion: overall an excellent, high-quality paper whose strengths outweigh weaknesses; reviewer strongly recommends acceptance.
