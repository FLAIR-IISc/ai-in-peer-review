This paper introduces a multi-fidelity variant of the stochastic K-armed bandit where each arm can be queried at multiple fidelities with differing costs and known bias bounds. The authors define a cost-weighted regret over a budget Λ that evaluates reward w.r.t. the highest-fidelity mean regardless of which fidelity was played, and propose MF-UCB, which maintains UCBs at all fidelities and adaptively selects a fidelity via thresholds γ(m) that trade off cost and approximation bias. They prove an upper bound showing MF-UCB eliminates clearly suboptimal arms using cheap fidelities and reserves expensive queries for near-optimal arms, yielding regret improvements over naïve single-fidelity UCB when lower fidelities are informative and cheaper. A lower bound (for Bernoulli rewards) shows near-optimality except in a specific regime where MF-UCB switches “too early,” which the authors discuss. Simulations on synthetic data corroborate the theory. The work relates to classic UCB (Auer et al., JMLR 2002; Bubeck and Cesa-Bianchi, FnT 2012), budgeted/bwK settings (e.g., Badanidiyuru et al., NIPS 2013, not cited) and multi-fidelity optimization (Huang et al., 2006), while being distinct in modeling biased approximations with known error bounds.

Quality: The technical development is careful, with clearly articulated assumptions (concentration, known ζ(m), regularity on ζ decay). The proofs (as summarized) are convincing, and the gap between the upper and lower bounds is transparent and well-motivated. However, the lower bound is limited to Bernoulli and relies on Assumption 3; robustness to model misspecification (unknown or violated ζ) is not analyzed.

Clarity: Generally well-written and organized, with helpful intuition for γ(m) and the K(m) partition. Some notation is dense; a brief intuitive walk-through of Algorithm 1 and a small running example would help. Minor typos and formatting issues exist.

Originality: The formalization of multi-fidelity bandits with biased proxies and cost-aware regret is novel, and the fidelity-selection rule is a principled adaptation of UCB.

Significance: The setting is practically relevant (ads, ML algorithm selection) and the results are likely to influence follow-up work; nevertheless, empirical evaluation is limited to synthetic data and there is no direct comparison to BwK-style baselines.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, novel problem formalization with practical motivation.
- Sound algorithm with near-optimal regret guarantees and informative analysis.
- Transparent discussion of limitations and where the bound is loose.

Cons for acceptance:
- Strong assumptions: known bias bounds ζ(m), ψ, and regularity; no robustness analysis.
- Lower bound restricted to Bernoulli and Assumption 3.
- Limited experiments; no real-world or BwK-type baselines; sensitivity to mis-specified ζ/costs not explored.
- Missing discussion/citation of Bandits with Knapsacks and closer empirical comparisons.

Suggestions: Add experiments with misspecified ζ, include BwK baselines and real-world proxy, and discuss/extend to unknown ζ estimation or robust variants.
