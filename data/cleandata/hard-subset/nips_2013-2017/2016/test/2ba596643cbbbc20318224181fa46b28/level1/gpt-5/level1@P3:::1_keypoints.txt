- Problem: studies K-armed stochastic bandits where high-fidelity rewards are costly and cheaper biased fidelities with known bias bounds ζ(m) are available.

- Algorithm: proposes MF-UCB that builds UCBs for true means by adding bias envelopes ζ(m) to fidelity-specific confidence radii and taking minima across fidelities.

- Fidelity selection: after choosing the arm with highest bound, MF-UCB escalates fidelities from low to high until confidence radius exceeds a threshold γ(m) tied to costs and ζ(m).

- Key idea: use cheap fidelities to eliminate clearly suboptimal arms and reserve expensive high-fidelity pulls for arms near-optimal at the highest fidelity.

- Regret metric: defines a cost-weighted pseudo-regret measured in high-fidelity units appropriate for this setting.

- Upper bound: derives MF-UCB regret bounds that partition arms into K(m) according to where they can be eliminated and show MF-UCB is never much worse than single-fidelity UCB.

- Gains: MF-UCB can be substantially better when many arms are eliminated at low fidelities; analysis yields E[R]/log(nΛ) rates.

- Lower bound: provides a Bernoulli lower bound (under a mild regularity assumption) showing near-optimality except for a specific subset of arms (K(m)7) where MF-UCB may be suboptimal.

- Relation to prior work: connects to classical UCB, Lai & Robbins minimax regret, budgeted bandits, and multi-fidelity optimization but is distinct because fidelities are biased proxies for the same arm.

- Soundness: algorithmic design and high-probability confidence bounds are well motivated; fidelity threshold γ(m) is carefully chosen and proofs control counts and tails nontrivially.

- Assumptions: requires known bias bounds ζ(m), known concentration ψ, fast decay Assumption 1 for main gains, independence/stationarity across fidelities, and lower-bound Assumption 3 — reasonable for theory but strong for practice.

- Practical concern: knowing tight ζ(m) is nontrivial and mis-specification could harm correctness and performance; independence across fidelities may ignore exploitable correlations.

- Empirics: simulations support theory and show targeted high-fidelity sampling, but experiments are limited to synthetic tasks, few baselines, no robustness or sensitivity studies (ρ, γ), and no semi-realistic scenarios.

- Clarity: generally clear setup and notation, well-motivated regret; some notation (K(m)3/K(m)7) abrupt and presentation of Theorem 2 could be streamlined; dependence assumptions should be clarified.

- Originality: formalization of biased multi-fidelity bandits with a cost-aware regret notion, MF-UCB design, and tailored lower bound are novel and technically interesting.

- Significance: setting is practically important (ads, algorithm selection, simulation), and results give principled guidance for budgeted evaluation and when gains over UCB are large.

- Strengths: clear problem formulation, simple implementable algorithm, nontrivial interpretable analysis with near-optimality guarantees, and good application intuition.

- Weaknesses/suggestions: strong knowledge assumptions (ζ, ψ) — suggest discussing online estimation/robustness; expand experiments with stronger baselines and mis-specification sensitivity; discuss performance when Assumption 1 fails; consider adaptive probing to close lower-bound gap.

- Pros: novel, relevant problem and regret notion; elegant algorithm and solid analysis; potential practical impact.

- Cons: strong practical assumptions, thin experiments, unclear dependence assumptions, and some presentation issues.

- Recommendation: Weak Accept — strong theoretical contribution of interest, but needs improved empirical evaluation and discussion of practicality to be more influential.
