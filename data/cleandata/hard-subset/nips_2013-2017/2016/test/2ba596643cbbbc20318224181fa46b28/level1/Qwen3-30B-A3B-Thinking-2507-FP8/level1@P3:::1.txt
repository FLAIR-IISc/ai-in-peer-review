

# Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

This paper introduces a novel extension to the classical multi-armed bandit problem where each arm has multiple fidelity levels with different costs and biased approximations of the true reward. The authors formalize this as a multi-fidelity bandit problem and propose MF-UCB, an upper confidence bound algorithm that adaptively selects fidelities to minimize regret. The paper builds upon the seminal work of Robbins [11], Lai and Robbins [9], and Auer et al. [3] on classical bandits, while extending to the multi-fidelity setting where cheaper approximations can be used to eliminate suboptimal arms before expensive high-fidelity evaluations. This work distinguishes itself from related multi-fidelity optimization approaches (e.g., Huang et al. [7], Cutler et al. [6]) by providing a formal regret analysis for the bandit setting, which previous works lacked.

**Quality:** The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with a carefully constructed regret bound for MF-UCB that shows it achieves better regret than naive UCB strategies when lower fidelities are sufficiently cheaper and informative. The lower bound in Theorem 4 provides a strong complement to the upper bound, demonstrating near-optimality under certain conditions. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, particularly noting that MF-UCB may not meet the lower bound for arms in K(m)7 due to the design of the switching criterion. The synthetic experiments in Section 6 provide empirical validation that aligns with the theoretical insights. A minor weakness is the reliance on Assumption 1 (fast decay of ζ(m)), which may not hold in all practical scenarios. The paper would benefit from discussing how to estimate ζ(m) from data, as it assumes these biases are known.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The formalism for multi-fidelity bandits is clearly motivated and defined, with careful attention to the biases introduced by lower fidelities. The intuition behind MF-UCB is effectively communicated through the algorithm description and the illustrative Fig. 1. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-explained. The appendix provides sufficient detail for reproduction of the results. The paper successfully bridges the gap between theoretical analysis and practical intuition.

**Originality:** This work makes a clear contribution to the bandit literature by formalizing and analyzing the multi-fidelity bandit problem. While multi-fidelity methods have been used in optimization (e.g., Huang et al. [7]), the authors are the first to provide a regret analysis for this setting in the bandit context. The MF-UCB algorithm is a novel combination of UCB principles with multi-fidelity considerations, and the partitioning of arms into K(m) sets is a key theoretical insight. The paper properly situates itself against related work, particularly distinguishing from budgeted bandits [13, 15] and active learning with labelers [16].

**Significance:** The problem addressed has significant practical relevance to domains where high-fidelity evaluations are expensive (e.g., online advertising, algorithm selection, clinical trials). The theoretical guarantees and empirical results demonstrate that MF-UCB can achieve substantially better regret than naive strategies when the multi-fidelity structure is exploited. The paper identifies a clear regime where multi-fidelity bandits provide substantial benefits (when |Kg| < K and λ(1) < λ(2)). The discussion of limitations and future directions (Section 7) shows good awareness of the broader context.

**Strengths:** 
- Novel problem formulation with strong practical motivation
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with matching upper and lower bounds
- Clear algorithm design that naturally adapts to fidelity structure
- Effective empirical validation through synthetic experiments
- Careful treatment of biases in lower-fidelity approximations

**Weaknesses:**
- Reliance on known biases ζ(m) (the paper doesn't address how to estimate these from data)
- Assumption 1 (fast decay of ζ(m)) may not hold in all practical settings
- Limited to synthetic experiments; real-world validation would strengthen the contribution
- No discussion of computational complexity for large-scale problems

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1. The paper addresses a meaningful extension to a fundamental problem in bandit theory with clear practical applications.
2. The theoretical analysis is strong, providing both an upper bound and a matching lower bound.
3. The proposed MF-UCB algorithm is a natural and effective extension of UCB to the multi-fidelity setting.
4. The paper is well-written and clearly demonstrates the benefits of the multi-fidelity approach.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
1. The assumption that ζ(m) are known is strong and may limit practical applicability.
2. The reliance on Assumption 1 could be problematic for some real-world applications.
3. The lack of real-world experiments limits the demonstration of practical impact.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to bandit literature. The work is clearly differentiated from previous NIPS papers on bandits and multi-fidelity optimization. The theoretical results are compelling and the empirical validation supports the claims. While the assumption of known biases is a limitation, this is a reasonable starting point for the theoretical analysis, and the paper's main contribution is the formal framework and regret analysis. The paper is well-suited for NIPS 2016 and would be of interest to the broader machine learning community. I recommend acceptance.
