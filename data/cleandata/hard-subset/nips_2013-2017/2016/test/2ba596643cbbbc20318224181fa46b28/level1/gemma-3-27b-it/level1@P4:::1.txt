This paper introduces a multi-fidelity bandit problem where obtaining rewards for arms is costly, but cheaper approximations of those rewards are available. The authors propose MF-UCB, an upper confidence bound algorithm adapted to this setting, and provide theoretical guarantees on its performance, demonstrating near-optimality under certain conditions.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a practical scenario common in many online learning applications like online advertising and algorithm selection. The problem formulation is clear and well-defined, extending the standard multi-armed bandit framework in a natural way. The introduction does a good job of contextualizing the problem and highlighting its relevance. The proposed algorithm, MF-UCB, builds logically on the well-established UCB approach, adapting it to leverage the cheaper fidelity information. The theoretical analysis, including the regret bound and the lower bound, demonstrates a solid understanding of the problem and the algorithm’s performance characteristics. The connection between the regret bound and the standard UCB regret is clearly articulated, highlighting the benefits of utilizing lower fidelities. The simulations support the theoretical findings, showing that MF-UCB outperforms a naive UCB implementation.

A strength of the paper is the rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors carefully define the notion of regret in the multi-fidelity setting, considering both the cost of playing arms and the bias of the lower-fidelity approximations. The analysis is detailed, and the proofs appear sound (though a detailed verification would require significant effort). The inclusion of a lower bound provides a strong argument for the near-optimality of MF-UCB. The clear explanation of the assumptions (e.g., Assumption 1) and their implications enhances the paper's clarity. Furthermore, discussing the limitations and potential improvements (e.g. the gap between the upper and lower bounds) strengthens the paper.

However, the paper could benefit from some improvements. While the simulations demonstrate that MF-UCB performs well, they are somewhat limited in scope.  Expanding the experimental evaluation to include more diverse problem settings and comparing against other relevant baseline algorithms (beyond naive UCB) would increase confidence in the practical effectiveness of the algorithm. The parameter choice for γ(m) feels somewhat ad-hoc. A more detailed justification or exploration of alternative choices could be beneficial.  The notation is occasionally dense; while generally clear, some equations could be more readable with additional spacing or explanations. The discussion of related work could be more comprehensive. Although the authors cite relevant papers, there might be other works in areas like Bayesian optimization with noisy evaluations or active learning that could be considered. The section discussing potential future work could be expanded with a discussion of potential applications beyond advertising and algorithm selection.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The multi-fidelity bandit problem is a novel extension of the classical bandit framework, addressing a practical challenge in many real-world applications.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of MF-UCB, including a regret bound and a lower bound demonstrating near-optimality.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the problem, the proposed algorithm, and the theoretical results.
*   **Significance:** The results have the potential to be impactful in areas such as online advertising, algorithm selection, and robotics, where obtaining accurate evaluations is costly.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The simulations are relatively limited in scope and could benefit from more diverse problem settings and baseline comparisons.
*   **Parameter Choice Justification:** The justification for the choice of γ(m) could be more detailed.
*   **Dense Notation:** While largely clear, the notation could be improved for readability in certain sections.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to the multi-fidelity bandit problem. While the empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive, the theoretical contributions are significant, and the paper is well-written and clear. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the suggestions outlined above.
