

# Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

This paper introduces a novel variant of the multi-armed bandit problem where each arm has multiple fidelities (approximations) with different costs. The authors formalize this as a multi-fidelity bandit problem, where playing an arm at fidelity m costs λ(m) and provides a biased estimate of the true reward (at highest fidelity M). They propose MF-UCB, a new algorithm that adaptively uses lower fidelities to eliminate suboptimal arms and reserves higher fidelities for promising candidates. The paper provides theoretical analysis showing MF-UCB achieves better regret than naive strategies that ignore fidelity information, along with a matching lower bound demonstrating near-optimality under certain conditions. The work extends classical bandit theory to practical scenarios where cheaper approximations are available (e.g., online advertising, algorithm selection), with clear connections to previous UCB work [3, 5] while addressing a distinct problem from budgeted bandits [13, 15] and multi-fidelity optimization [7, 10].

**Strengths:**

1. **Quality:** The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis. The regret bound for MF-UCB (Theorem 2) is carefully derived, and the lower bound (Theorem 4) demonstrates near-optimality. The authors appropriately account for the biases introduced by lower fidelities and provide a clear justification for their choice of parameters (e.g., γ(m) in Equation 4). The synthetic experiments in Section 6 convincingly validate the theoretical claims, showing MF-UCB outperforms UCB across multiple scenarios. The paper acknowledges limitations (e.g., the gap between MF-UCB and the lower bound for certain arms in K(m)7), demonstrating intellectual honesty.

2. **Originality:** This is a genuinely novel contribution to bandit theory. While related work has studied multi-fidelity methods in optimization [7, 10] and active learning [16], none have formalized the regret in the bandit setting or provided theoretical guarantees for the exploration-exploitation trade-off. The paper clearly differentiates itself from budgeted bandits [13, 15] and other related work. The multi-fidelity framework is a significant extension of the classical bandit problem with clear practical relevance.

3. **Significance:** The problem has high practical significance in domains like online advertising (where lower-fidelity ad testing can be cheaper), algorithm selection (using cheaper data subsets), and robotics (using cheaper simulations). The regret bound shows MF-UCB can achieve substantially better performance than standard UCB when lower fidelities are significantly cheaper (λ(1) ≪ λ(2)). The paper advances the state of the art in bandit theory by addressing a practically relevant generalization of the problem.

4. **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem statement is motivated with concrete examples, the algorithm is clearly described (Algorithm 1), and the theoretical results are presented with sufficient detail. The partitioning of arms into K(m) (Figure 1) effectively illustrates the algorithm's intuition. The appendix provides the necessary technical details for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The lower bound (Theorem 4) shows MF-UCB is not optimal for arms in K(m)7 (where some lower fidelity suggests the arm might be good, but higher fidelity reveals it's suboptimal). While the authors explain this gap, they could more thoroughly discuss the practical implications—how often this situation occurs in real applications.

2. The paper assumes the bias ζ(m) is known, which might be restrictive in some applications. While the authors acknowledge this in the conclusion, they don't explore how the algorithm would perform if ζ(m) were unknown or had to be estimated.

3. The empirical evaluation is limited to synthetic problems. While the synthetic experiments are well-designed, adding a real-world case study (even a small one) would strengthen the practical significance claim.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- Novel extension of bandit theory with clear theoretical and empirical advantages
- Addresses a practically important problem with significant cost savings potential
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with a matching lower bound
- Well-written, clear, and well-organized paper
- Strong connection to practical applications in online advertising and algorithm selection

*Con:*
- The gap between MF-UCB and the lower bound for certain arms (K(m)7) is somewhat unsatisfying
- Limited to synthetic experiments; no real-world validation
- Requires known bias parameters ζ(m), which may be restrictive in practice

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to bandit theory. The multi-fidelity framework addresses an important practical problem that has been overlooked in previous bandit research. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and the empirical results confirm the theoretical advantages. The paper meets all the NIPS criteria: it is technically sound (Quality), well-written (Clarity), represents a novel extension of bandit theory (Originality), and has clear practical significance (Significance).

The minor weaknesses (known bias assumption, synthetic experiments only) do not detract from the paper's substantial contribution. The work provides a clear path for future research on multi-fidelity bandits and has potential for immediate application in fields like online advertising and algorithm selection. I recommend acceptance.
