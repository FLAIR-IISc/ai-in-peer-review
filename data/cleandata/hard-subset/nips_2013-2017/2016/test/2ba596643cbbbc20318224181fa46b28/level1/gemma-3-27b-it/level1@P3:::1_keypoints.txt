- Introduces the multi-fidelity bandit problem where rewards have differing costs and accuracies.
- Proposes MF-UCB, an extension of UCB tailored to multi-fidelity settings.
- Provides theoretical guarantees including near-optimality and a meaningful lower bound.
- Carefully models bias from lower-fidelity estimates via the ζ(m) parameterization.
- Formalization and notation are precise and consistent throughout the paper.
- The MF-UCB algorithm is intuitive and builds logically on classic UCB.
- The theoretical analysis is dense but appears rigorous, with plausible proof sketches.
- Simulation results support the theoretical findings but are somewhat limited in scope.
- Related work coverage is comprehensive and situates this work relative to budgeted bandits and active learning.
- Discussion of future directions and alternative regret definitions is valuable.
- Assumption 1 (decay of ζ(m)) may be restrictive and its practical implications are underexplored.
- The paper should analyze how MF-UCB’s benefit degrades when Assumption 1 is violated (is degradation linear?).
- Empirical evaluation needs more diverse settings and comparisons to additional baselines beyond standard UCB.
- Sensitivity analysis to parameters (ρ and γ(m)) is missing; consequences of constant γ(m) should be tested.
- Practical guidance for parameter tuning (e.g., choosing ρ) and translating bounds to real-world performance is lacking.
- Computational complexity and overhead of maintaining multiple fidelities are not discussed.
- Arguments for acceptance: novelty, theoretical rigor, clear presentation, practical relevance, and supportive simulations.
- Arguments against acceptance: potentially restrictive assumption, limited simulations, insufficient practical guidance, and missing complexity analysis.
- Overall assessment: strong, well-motivated, and technically sound paper; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to expand simulations and practical implementation guidance.
