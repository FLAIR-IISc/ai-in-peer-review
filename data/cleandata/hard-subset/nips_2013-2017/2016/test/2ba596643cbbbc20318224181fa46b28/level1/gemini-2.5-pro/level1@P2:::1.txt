**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a novel and practical variant of the stochastic K-armed bandit problem, which the authors term the "multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit." In this setting, each of the K arms can be evaluated at one of M different "fidelities." Playing an arm at a lower fidelity is cheaper but yields a reward that is a biased approximation of the "true" reward, which is only observed at the most expensive, highest fidelity level. The costs (λ(m)) increase with fidelity, while the maximum possible bias (ζ(m)), which is assumed to be known, decreases. The goal is to design a strategy that minimizes a cost-aware notion of cumulative regret given a total budget Λ.

The authors propose a new algorithm, the Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB), which extends the classic UCB framework to this setting. The core intuition of MF-UCB is to leverage the cheap, low-fidelity evaluations to quickly identify and discard clearly suboptimal arms. This allows the algorithm to reserve its expensive, high-fidelity pulls for a small, promising subset of arms that are difficult to distinguish. The algorithm maintains confidence bounds for each arm at each fidelity level and includes a principled rule for deciding which fidelity to use for a chosen arm, switching to higher fidelities only when the uncertainty at lower levels has been sufficiently reduced.

The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of MF-UCB, deriving an upper bound on its regret that demonstrates how it effectively leverages the fidelity structure to outperform a naive UCB strategy that only uses the highest fidelity. This analysis is complemented by a problem-specific lower bound, which shows that MF-UCB is nearly optimal, particularly for arms that are not misleading at lower fidelities. The theoretical claims are supported by simulations on synthetic problems.

This work relates to the extensive literature on multi-armed bandits, particularly UCB methods. However, it is distinct from prior work on budgeted bandits, which typically associate a single cost with each arm, and from general multi-fidelity optimization, which usually does not address the exploration-exploitation tradeoff through regret minimization. It also shares conceptual similarities with active learning from weak and strong labelers but is formalized within the bandit framework.

**Critique and Justification**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The problem formulation is sound, well-motivated, and captures a scenario of significant practical interest. The proposed MF-UCB algorithm is an elegant and intuitive adaptation of the UCB principle. The theoretical analysis is the paper's main strength; providing both an upper bound for the proposed algorithm and a lower bound for the problem class is a sign of a complete and mature piece of work. The authors are careful and honest in their analysis, for instance, by explicitly discussing the gap between their upper and lower bounds for the subset of arms K(m)7 and explaining the algorithmic trade-off that causes it. The assumptions, such as the known bias bounds ζ(m) and the regularity condition on their decay (Assumption 1), are clearly stated and justified. A minor weakness is that the practicality of knowing the exact bias bounds ζ(m) in real-world applications is not deeply discussed. In many scenarios, these might need to be estimated, which would add another layer of complexity.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction provides clear motivating examples (online advertising, algorithm selection) that make the abstract problem formulation easy to grasp. The notation is dense but necessary for the topic and is defined and used consistently. The authors do an excellent job of providing intuition for their key technical ideas, such as the arm partitioning scheme (K(m)) and the fidelity switching criterion (γ(m)), which is further aided by the helpful illustration in Figure 1. The main theorems are stated clearly, and their implications are discussed in a way that makes the contribution accessible. The proof sketches in the main body effectively convey the high-level argument, while correctly deferring the full details to the appendix.

**Originality:** The primary contribution of this paper is the novel formulation of the multi-fidelity bandit problem. While the algorithmic solution builds upon the well-known UCB framework, its application to this new, structured setting is non-trivial and original. The specific mechanism for selecting not just an arm but also a fidelity level based on confidence intervals and pre-defined thresholds is a novel algorithmic component. The accompanying theoretical analysis is tailored to this new problem and represents a significant new contribution. This work is clearly distinct from prior bandit research and successfully carves out a new and interesting subfield.

**Significance:** The results presented in this paper are highly significant. The multi-fidelity setting is a natural model for a wide range of real-world sequential decision-making problems where preliminary, cheaper evaluations are possible (e.g., simulations vs. physical experiments, small-scale user studies vs. full launches, partial computations vs. full model training). By providing the first formal regret analysis for this setting and a provably near-optimal algorithm, the authors lay a strong foundation for future research. Practitioners in fields like automated machine learning (hyperparameter optimization), experimental design, and robotics are likely to find these ideas useful and build upon them. The work demonstrably advances the state of the art by showing that intelligently using cheaper, approximate information can lead to substantial gains over strategies that ignore it.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Pro:** Introduces a novel, important, and practical problem formulation (the multi-fidelity bandit).
*   **Pro:** Proposes an elegant and intuitive algorithm (MF-UCB) with strong theoretical guarantees.
*   **Pro:** Provides a thorough theoretical analysis, including both upper and lower regret bounds, demonstrating near-optimality.
*   **Pro:** The paper is very clearly written, well-motivated, and well-structured.
*   **Pro:** The contribution is significant and likely to inspire a new line of research in the bandit community.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   **Con:** The assumption of known bias bounds ζ(m) may be strong for some practical applications, somewhat limiting the immediate off-the-shelf applicability of the method. This is a minor point, however, as it is a common and necessary assumption for obtaining initial theoretical guarantees in a new problem setting.

In summary, this is an excellent paper that introduces a novel and important problem, provides a well-designed algorithm, and backs it up with strong theoretical results. It is a complete, high-quality contribution that will be of great interest to the NIPS community. I strongly recommend acceptance.
