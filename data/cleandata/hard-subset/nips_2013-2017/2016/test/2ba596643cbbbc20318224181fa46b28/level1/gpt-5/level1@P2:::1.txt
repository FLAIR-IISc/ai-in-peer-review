Summary and relation to prior work
The paper introduces a stochastic K-armed bandit model with M fidelity levels per arm, where lower fidelities are cheaper but provide biased approximations to the highest-fidelity reward. The authors assume known bias bounds |µ(M)
k − µ(m)
k| ≤ ζ(m) with ζ(m) decreasing in m, and per-pull costs λ(m) increasing in m. They define a cost-weighted pseudo-regret for a capital budget Λ that credits any pull (at any fidelity) according to the highest-fidelity mean of the chosen arm. They propose MF-UCB: for each arm k, maintain UCBs at each fidelity m, B(m)
k,t = empirical mean + bonus + ζ(m), then choose the arm maximizing the minimum across fidelities; given the chosen arm, select the lowest fidelity whose statistical uncertainty is still above a threshold γ(m) that trades off cost and bias via γ(m) = ψ^{-1}(λ(m)/λ(m+1) ψ(ζ(m))). They prove an upper bound showing that MF-UCB spends most of its effort on low-cost fidelities to eliminate clearly suboptimal arms, reserving high-cost queries for a small set near-optimal arms. They complement this with a lower bound (for Bernoulli rewards) under a natural efficiency assumption, showing near-optimality except in a particular gap case. Simulations on synthetic problems corroborate the gains over naïve UCB on the highest fidelity.

This work sits between classical stochastic bandits (Auer et al., Agrawal; Lai & Robbins), budgeted bandits (Tran-Thanh et al.; Xia et al.), and multi-fidelity optimization (Huang et al.; Rajnarayan et al.) and relates to multi-fidelity RL (Cutler et al.) and active learning with weak/strong labelers (Zhang & Chaudhuri). Unlike budgeted bandits (which optimize reward/cost per arm) or side-observation bandits, this model explicitly handles biased, cost-graded approximations and defines a regret against the highest-fidelity objective. The authors also mention their related GP extension (Kandasamy et al., NIPS), which tackles continuous-armed settings.

- Empirical support: Simulations show consistent improvements and the expected “flatter” regret slope, with per-fidelity pull counts illustrating behavior. However, experiments are limited to synthetic cases and lack comparisons to alternative baselines (e.g., TS variants, elimination heuristics that use cheap fidelities more aggressively).
- Completeness: Proof sketches are convincing; full proofs are said to be in the appendix. The regret definition is carefully motivated, and limitations (other regret notions) are discussed.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with good intuition for the fidelity-switching rule. The partitioning K(1),...,K(M) is for analysis only; making this explicit earlier would help. The regret decomposition R̃ vs r̃ is useful, but r̃’s role could be clarified succinctly at first mention. It would help to provide more guidance on choosing ρ and the practical effect of Assumption 1 (with concrete ζ, λ examples). Notation is heavy but standard for bandit theory.

Originality
- The formulation of a multi-fidelity stochastic bandit with known bias envelopes and explicit cost-aware regret is, to my knowledge, new in the K-armed setting. The MF-UCB design—min over fidelity-specific UCBs plus a principled switching threshold derived from cost/bias—is a neat, nontrivial extension of UCB. The lower bound tailored to this model is also original.

Significance
- The model captures realistic scenarios (ads with short-run proxies; algorithm selection with subsampling) and provides a principled way to leverage biased proxies. The theoretical gains are meaningful when K is large and many arms are obviously suboptimal at low fidelities. The results should stimulate follow-up work (e.g., best-arm identification with costs and biases; robustness to unknown ζ). Practical impact will hinge on how well ζ(m) (bias envelopes) can be specified or learned.

Pros
- Clear, principled problem formulation with a well-justified regret notion.
- Elegant algorithm with interpretable fidelity selection; rigorous analysis with near-optimal guarantees.
- Insightful decomposition showing where multi-fidelity saves cost.
- Honest discussion of limitations and alternative regret notions.

Cons
- Requires known bias bounds ζ(m); robustness to misspecification is not analyzed.
- Assumption 1 on ζ-decay is somewhat technical and its practical scope is unclear.
- Lower bound restricted to Bernoulli and excludes the gap class K(m)
7.
- Experimental evaluation is limited; missing comparisons to stronger baselines and real data.

Suggestions for improvement
- Discuss practical ways to obtain/estimate ζ(m), and sensitivity analyses for misspecified ζ or ψ. Could adaptive calibration of ζ(m) be integrated with MF-UCB?
- Compare against additional baselines: TS-style multi-fidelity adaptation, hierarchical elimination that treats low-fidelity as screening, and cost-aware best-arm identification algorithms.
- Provide ablations varying λ, ζ, and M to empirically validate Assumption 1’s effect and the γ(m) design.
- Clarify guidance on ρ and constants; include computational complexity per step (O(KM)) and any tricks to reduce overhead.
- Consider an extension that partially exploits cross-fidelity correlations rather than worst-case bias bounds.

Recommendation
Overall, the paper meets the NIPS criteria well: solid theory, clear writing, novel and relevant problem, and likely to influence subsequent research. I lean toward acceptance, with the main caveat being the reliance on known ζ(m) and the modest empirical section.
