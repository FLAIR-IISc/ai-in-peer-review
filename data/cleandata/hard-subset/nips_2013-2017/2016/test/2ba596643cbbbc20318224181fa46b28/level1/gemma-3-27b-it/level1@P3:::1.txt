This paper introduces a multi-fidelity bandit problem, where obtaining rewards from arms has varying costs and accuracies. The authors propose MF-UCB, an extension of the UCB algorithm, and provide theoretical guarantees demonstrating near-optimality under certain conditions. This is a well-motivated and technically sound contribution to the bandit literature, addressing a practical challenge in various applications like online advertising and algorithm selection.

The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The introduction does a good job of motivating the problem and highlighting its relevance. The formalization of the multi-fidelity bandit setting is precise, and the notation is consistent throughout. The proposed MF-UCB algorithm is intuitive and builds logically upon the classic UCB approach. The theoretical analysis, while dense, appears rigorous and the proofs (sketched) seem plausible.  The inclusion of simulation results further strengthens the paper and provides empirical validation of the theoretical findings. The related work section is comprehensive and appropriately cites relevant literature, including previous NIPS papers (e.g., [8, 15, 16]).

A significant strength of this work is the careful consideration of the bias introduced by lower-fidelity estimates. The ζ(m) parameterization provides a principled way to model the accuracy of these approximations, and the analysis explicitly accounts for these biases.  The lower bound result, demonstrating near-optimality, is a strong result and provides further confidence in the efficacy of MF-UCB. The connection to existing work on budgeted bandits and active learning is also appropriately discussed, and the authors clearly articulate how their work differs from those areas. The discussion of potential future directions regarding alternative regret definitions and settings is also valuable.

However, there are some areas where the paper could be improved. 

Firstly, the assumption 1 regarding the decay of ζ(m) seems a bit restrictive.  While the authors acknowledge it isn’t strictly necessary, its importance in achieving the performance gains over standard UCB isn’t fully explored. A more detailed discussion of scenarios where this assumption doesn't hold and the implications for MF-UCB’s performance would be beneficial. Is the benefit diminished linearly with the violation of this assumption?

Secondly, the simulation results, while positive, are somewhat limited.  More diverse problem settings and a more thorough comparison with other potential baseline algorithms (beyond standard UCB) would strengthen the empirical evaluation. Specifically, exploring the sensitivity of MF-UCB to different values of ρ and γ(m) would provide valuable insights.  What happens when γ(m) is set to a constant value?

Thirdly, the connection between the theoretical analysis and the practical implications isn't always immediately clear.  While the theoretical bounds are tight, translating them into concrete guidelines for parameter tuning or expected performance in real-world applications could be enhanced.  For instance, how does one choose an appropriate value for ρ in practice?

Fourthly, while the paper does a good job of establishing the theoretical guarantees of MF-UCB, it would be worthwhile to consider computational complexity. How does the cost of implementing MF-UCB (e.g., the overhead of maintaining and updating the confidence bounds for each fidelity) compare to standard UCB, particularly as the number of arms and fidelities increases?

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:**  The multi-fidelity bandit problem is a novel and relevant extension of the classic bandit framework.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of MF-UCB, including a near-optimality result.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The problem and proposed solution have applications in various domains, including online advertising and algorithm selection.
*   **Empirical Validation:** Simulation results support the theoretical findings.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Assumption 1:** The assumption regarding the decay of ζ(m) might be restrictive in some applications.
*   **Limited Simulations:** The simulation results could be more comprehensive and explore a wider range of scenarios.
*   **Practical Guidance:** The paper could benefit from more practical guidelines for parameter tuning and performance prediction.
*   **Complexity Analysis:** A discussion of the computational complexity of MF-UCB would be valuable.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite these minor weaknesses, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the bandit literature. The problem is well-motivated, the proposed solution is elegant and theoretically sound, and the empirical results are encouraging.  I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance to NIPS 2016.  I would recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above, particularly expanding the simulation results and providing more guidance on practical implementation.
