Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper introduces a stochastic K-armed bandit setting where each arm can be evaluated at multiple fidelities (M levels), with higher fidelities being more accurate but more costly. Lower fidelities provide biased approximations of the highest-fidelity reward, with known bias bounds ζ(m) decreasing in m, and known costs λ(m) increasing in m. The paper defines a capital-based pseudo-regret appropriate to this setting: given total capital Λ, the regret compares to an oracle that always plays an optimal arm at any fidelity, measuring the true (highest-fidelity) reward per unit capital expended.

The proposed algorithm, MF-UCB, extends UCB by maintaining, for each arm and fidelity, an upper confidence bound on the highest-fidelity mean via X̄(m) + confidence width + ζ(m), and selecting the arm with the minimum across fidelities; fidelity selection uses a threshold schedule γ(m) to decide when to move up to a higher fidelity, aiming to eliminate clearly suboptimal arms cheaply. The thresholds are chosen as γ(m) = ψ^{-1}(λ(m)/λ(m+1) ψ(ζ(m))) to balance cost and informativeness.

The analysis provides: (i) an upper bound on regret showing MF-UCB pays logarithmic-in-budget exploration cost predominantly at the lowest fidelity that can eliminate an arm, yielding a speedup vs. single-fidelity UCB by a factor roughly λ(JkK)/λ(M) per suboptimal arm; (ii) a lower bound (under Bernoulli rewards and a mild visitation assumption) showing near-optimality except for a specific subset of arms where MF-UCB may pay more than the lower bound due to its conservative switching rule. Simulations on synthetic problems corroborate the theoretical improvements.

Relative to classic UCB (Auer et al.), budgeted bandits (e.g., Tran-Thanh et al.; Xia et al.) and multi-fidelity optimization in engineering (Huang et al.; Rajnarayan et al.), the paper formalizes a distinct setting: biased multi-fidelity observations with explicit costs and a regret defined against the highest-fidelity reward. It also differs from weak/strong labeler active learning (Zhang & Chaudhuri) and multi-fidelity RL simulators (Cutler et al.) by focusing on exploration-exploitation and regret minimization. The multi-fidelity GP bandit work by the same authors is cited as a related extension.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The UCB construction and min-over-fidelities bound are sound under the concentration model (Eq. 2). The regret analysis carefully partitions arms by the finest fidelity at which they can be eliminated, controls play counts per fidelity, and bounds the total number of plays under budget. The need for ρ > 4 and Assumption 1 is clearly stated. The lower bound uses a standard change-of-measure approach and identifies where MF-UCB is suboptimal. Proof sketches are coherent; full details are said to be in the appendix.
- Assumptions: Requires known ζ(m) bias bounds and concentration function ψ, and a regularity condition (Assumption 1) to avoid near-duplicate fidelities. Lower bound is for Bernoulli rewards and strategies satisfying a subpolynomial pull condition. These are reasonable but somewhat restrictive for practice.
- Empirics: Synthetic experiments are consistent with theory; however, no real-world or extensive ablations are provided in the main text.

Clarity:
The paper is mostly clear and well organized. The novel regret is well motivated, the algorithm is concise, and the intuition behind γ(m) and the arm partition K(m) is explained. Some notation is heavy (e.g., multiple ∆(m), JkK), and the dependence on known ζ(m) and ψ could be highlighted earlier. The residual regret term r̃ is briefly discussed; a short bound in the main text would help. The figures illustrating partitions and fidelity usage help.

Originality:
The formalization of multi-fidelity bandits with biased approximations, a capital-based regret against the highest-fidelity reward, and a UCB scheme that takes the min of fidelity-specific UCBs with fidelity-adaptive switching thresholds appears novel. The lower bound tailored to this setting and the identification of a gap set K(m)7 are original contributions.

Significance:
The setting is practically relevant (advertising, algorithm selection, simulation-to-real transfer), and the results show concrete improvements over naive high-fidelity-only strategies with near-optimality under conditions. The framework could influence subsequent work on hierarchical/budgeted evaluations and structured bandits.

Pros:
- Clear and well-motivated problem formulation for multi-fidelity, biased observations with costs.
- Simple, implementable MF-UCB algorithm with principled fidelity selection.
- Theoretical guarantees: regret upper bound showing substantial savings, plus a matching lower bound up to a clearly explained gap.
- Good intuition and partition-based analysis that clarifies where gains arise.
- Synthetic experiments consistent with theory.

Cons:
- Requires known bias bounds ζ(m) and concentration function ψ; robustness to misspecification is not studied.
- Assumption 1 (fast decay of ζ(m)) limits generality; no adaptive handling of “redundant” fidelities.
- Lower bound limited to Bernoulli rewards and a strategy class; MF-UCB can be suboptimal on K(m)7 arms.
- Empirical evaluation limited; no real-world datasets or comparisons to additional baselines (e.g., Thompson sampling variants, Successive Rejects/Elimination tailored to multi-fidelity).
- Practical guidance on choosing ρ and handling unknown ψ/ζ(m) could be expanded.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Discuss or propose methods to estimate or adapt ζ(m) and ψ online, and analyze robustness to misspecification.
- Provide ablations on γ(m)’s form and sensitivity to λ(m), ζ(m), and M; include cases violating Assumption 1.
- Compare against more baselines, including heuristic multi-fidelity strategies (e.g., staged elimination at low fidelity) and Bayesian methods (e.g., Thompson sampling with hierarchical priors).
- Extend lower bound beyond Bernoulli and/or tighten the gap for K(m)7, or propose a variant that better handles small positive ∆(`) at low fidelities without excessive high-fidelity pulls.
- Add at least one real-world experiment or more detailed synthetic setups in the main text.

Overall assessment:
The paper offers a clear, technically solid, and novel contribution to bandit theory and practice by formalizing and addressing multi-fidelity evaluations. Despite some assumptions and limited empirical breadth, the core ideas and guarantees are strong and of interest to the NIPS community.
