

**Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"**

This paper introduces a novel variant of the multi-armed bandit problem where each arm can be pulled at multiple fidelities, with lower fidelities providing cheaper but biased estimates of the true reward. The authors formalize this "multi-fidelity bandit" setting, develop the MF-UCB algorithm, and provide theoretical guarantees demonstrating that it achieves better regret than standard UCB algorithms when lower fidelities are sufficiently cheaper. The work is motivated by practical applications like online advertising (where shorter ad displays provide cheaper approximations of long-term click-through rates) and algorithm selection (where cheaper performance estimates can be used to narrow down promising algorithms before expensive high-fidelity evaluations).

The paper builds on established UCB bandit literature [3, 9, 12] but makes a significant conceptual extension to handle multiple fidelities with different costs and biases. It carefully distinguishes between the bias (captured by ζ(m)) and cost structure (λ(m)), providing a precise formalism that correctly accounts for the biased nature of lower fidelity observations rather than treating them as just higher-variance observations. The authors appropriately cite relevant related work, including budgeted bandits [13, 15], multi-fidelity optimization [7, 10], and active learning with weak/strong labelers [16], making clear how their work differs from these approaches.

**Strengths:**
1. **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis with both an upper bound on MF-UCB's regret and a matching lower bound. The regret analysis is particularly impressive for its careful partitioning of arms into sets K(m) based on their performance at different fidelities. The proof sketches provide valuable intuition about the algorithm's behavior, showing how MF-UCB uses lower fidelities to eliminate suboptimal arms and reserves high-fidelity plays for promising candidates.

2. **Practical Motivation:** The paper convincingly demonstrates the relevance of the multi-fidelity setting with clear, concrete examples from online advertising and algorithm selection. The explanation of how MF-UCB would use lower fidelities to quickly eliminate poor-performing ads before investing in expensive high-fidelity measurements is particularly compelling.

3. **Empirical Validation:** Experiments on synthetic problems clearly demonstrate MF-UCB outperforming standard UCB across multiple settings, with regret curves showing better scaling as predicted by the theory. The visualization of how MF-UCB uses different fidelities for different arms provides strong empirical support for the theoretical claims.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Implementation Considerations:** While theoretically sound, the paper doesn't address practical implementation challenges, such as how to estimate the ζ(m) parameters or handle cases where the bias assumptions don't hold perfectly. This is a notable gap given the practical applications discussed.

2. **Assumption 1 Restrictiveness:** The regularity condition (Assumption 1) requires ζ(m) to decay sufficiently fast, which may not hold in all practical applications. While the authors note this isn't necessary for the algorithm to work, it's needed for the clean comparison to UCB, limiting the scope of the theoretical results.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Novel formalism addressing an important practical problem with clear motivation
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with well-justified regret bounds
- Empirical results that validate the theoretical claims
- Clear presentation with well-motivated examples
- Demonstrates significant improvement over standard bandit algorithms in the multi-fidelity setting

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
- The problem setting, while novel, may be too specialized for a general NIPS audience
- Some theoretical results require assumptions that might not hold in all applications

**Overall Assessment:**
This is a strong contribution to bandit literature that makes a meaningful advance in understanding bandit problems with varying fidelity experiments. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the practical motivation is compelling, and the empirical results confirm the theoretical advantages. The minor weaknesses are outweighed by the paper's clear contributions to the field.

The paper addresses a problem increasingly relevant in practice (where expensive experiments are common and cheaper approximations exist), and the results have clear implications for applications like online advertising, algorithm selection, and experimental design. The theoretical advances represent a meaningful step forward in bandit theory, extending the classic UCB framework to handle a more realistic setting with multiple fidelity levels.

Given the high quality of the theoretical analysis, the clear practical motivation, and the empirical validation, I recommend this paper for acceptance at NIPS 2016. The work provides a valuable framework for handling bandit problems with varying fidelity experiments, with clear potential for impact in both theory and practice.
