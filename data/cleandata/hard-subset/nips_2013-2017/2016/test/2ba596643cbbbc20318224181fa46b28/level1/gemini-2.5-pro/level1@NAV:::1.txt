This paper introduces the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit, a novel and well-motivated extension of the classic stochastic K-armed bandit problem. In this setting, each arm can be queried at multiple "fidelities," where lower fidelities are cheaper but provide biased approximations of the true (highest fidelity) reward. The authors formalize this problem with a cost-weighted regret metric and propose the Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB) algorithm. This approach cleverly uses cheap, low-fidelity pulls to rapidly eliminate poor arms, reserving expensive, high-fidelity evaluations for a small set of promising candidates. This work is well-situated within bandit theory, distinguishing itself from related areas like budgeted bandits, which have a different cost structure, and multi-fidelity optimization, which typically does not involve regret minimization.

The paper’s main strengths are its high significance and technical quality. The multi-fidelity formulation is an original and important contribution, capturing a practical trade-off present in many domains like A/B testing and hyperparameter tuning where cheap, approximate evaluations are available. The proposed MF-UCB algorithm is an elegant and intuitive adaptation of the UCB principle to this new setting. The theoretical analysis is comprehensive and technically sound, providing both an upper regret bound for the algorithm and a problem-specific lower bound. This demonstrates a deep understanding of the problem and establishes that MF-UCB is near-optimal under certain conditions. The synthetic experiments, while simple, effectively corroborate the theoretical insights, clearly showing MF-UCB's advantage over a standard UCB baseline.

The paper has only minor weaknesses. The clarity of the theoretical analysis, specifically the partitioning of the arm set into K(m), could be improved with more intuition in the main text to aid reader comprehension. The authors commendably acknowledge a gap between their upper and lower bounds for a subset of arms (K(m)7). A slightly deeper discussion on whether this gap is an artifact of the analysis or a fundamental limitation of the proposed switching strategy would further strengthen the paper.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Novel and significant problem formulation with clear practical relevance.
*   Technically sound algorithm with a rigorous theoretical analysis, including near-optimal regret bounds.
*   Clearly advances the state of the art in stochastic bandits.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The clarity of the theoretical constructs could be slightly improved for a broader audience.

Overall, this is a high-quality, original, and significant contribution to the field that is well-suited for NIPS. I strongly recommend acceptance.
