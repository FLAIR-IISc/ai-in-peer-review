## Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

This paper introduces a multi-fidelity variant of the classical K-armed bandit problem, motivated by scenarios where evaluating arms is costly but cheaper approximations are available. The authors formalize this setting and propose a novel algorithm, MF-UCB, an adaptation of the UCB algorithm. They provide a theoretical analysis, including regret bounds, and demonstrate empirically that MF-UCB outperforms naive UCB strategies.

**Summary and Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon the well-established foundation of multi-armed bandit theory, particularly UCB algorithms [Auer, 2002, Audibert & Bubeck, 2004]. It distinguishes itself by explicitly incorporating the notion of varying fidelity in arm evaluations, a scenario frequently encountered in practical applications such as online advertising and algorithm selection (as highlighted in the introduction). While related work acknowledges multi-fidelity optimization [Huang et al., 2006], and active learning with varying label costs [Zhang & Chaudhuri, 2016], these works lack the specific focus on the exploration-exploitation trade-off inherent to the bandit setting. The authors correctly position their work as bridging this gap.  Recent work by Kandasamy et al. [2016] extends ideas related to this work to Gaussian process payoffs, highlighting the increasing relevance of the multi-fidelity setting. The contribution of this paper lies in providing a formal bandit framework and a provably near-optimal algorithm tailored to this setting.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty and Relevance:** The multi-fidelity bandit problem is a relevant and increasingly important extension of the classical bandit problem. The motivating examples (online advertising, algorithm selection) are well-chosen and clearly illustrate the potential benefits of the proposed approach.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of MF-UCB, establishing regret bounds and demonstrating near-optimality under certain conditions. The derivation of the regret bound, while complex, appears sound.
* **Clear Algorithm Description:**  The MF-UCB algorithm is presented clearly and concisely, with a well-defined switching criterion between fidelities based on confidence interval widths.
* **Empirical Validation:** The simulations demonstrate the effectiveness of MF-UCB compared to the standard UCB algorithm, providing empirical support for the theoretical results.
* **Well-written and Organized:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, making it relatively easy to follow the authors' line of reasoning.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions:**  The assumption that the lower fidelity means are within a known quantity ζ(m) of the highest fidelity means, while reasonable, could be limiting in practice.  The sensitivity of the algorithm to inaccuracies in estimating ζ(m) is not discussed.  Further exploration of robustness to misspecified ζ(m) would be valuable.
* **Regularity Condition (Assumption 1):** The necessity of Assumption 1 (decay rate of ζ(m)) is not fully justified. While the authors state it improves the comparison to UCB, a more in-depth analysis of its impact would be beneficial.  Is there a scenario where MF-UCB *underperforms* UCB when this assumption is violated?
* **Lower Bound Gap:**  The authors acknowledge a gap between their upper bound (MF-UCB's performance) and the derived lower bound. While they offer some intuition for this gap, a more thorough explanation and potential avenues for closing it would strengthen the paper.
* **Limited Scope of Simulations:**  The simulations are somewhat limited in scope. While they demonstrate improvement over UCB, exploring a wider range of problem settings (different values of λ, K, ζ, and reward distributions) would provide more comprehensive validation.
* **Regret Definition:** The choice of qt = µ(M)It as the reward, while justified, is somewhat restrictive. The authors briefly acknowledge this, but a discussion of alternative regret definitions and their implications would be insightful.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and relevant extension of the multi-armed bandit problem.
* The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides strong guarantees on the performance of the proposed algorithm.
* The empirical results support the theoretical findings.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the concepts.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The assumptions (particularly ζ(m) estimation and the regularity condition) may limit the practical applicability of the algorithm.
* The gap between the upper and lower bounds weakens the optimality claims.
* The limited scope of the simulations leaves some questions unanswered.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of multi-armed bandit research. The authors have identified a practical and important problem, developed a theoretically sound algorithm, and provided compelling evidence of its effectiveness. While some limitations exist (assumptions, lower bound gap, limited simulations), the strengths of the paper outweigh these weaknesses.

**Rating:**  Strong Accept.  I recommend accepting this paper to NIPS. Addressing the weaknesses, particularly with further analysis of the assumptions and expanded simulations, would elevate the paper even further.
