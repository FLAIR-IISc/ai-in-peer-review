## Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

This paper introduces a novel multi-fidelity bandit framework where obtaining outcomes for each arm is costly, but cheaper approximations are available. The authors formalize this setting and propose MF-UCB, a UCB-based algorithm that intelligently leverages these approximations to minimize regret. The work is well-motivated, addressing a common scenario in applications like online advertising and algorithm selection.

The paper clearly builds upon the established literature of multi-armed bandits (e.g., Auer, 2002; Lai & Robbins, 1985) and extends it to incorporate the notion of fidelity. It correctly identifies the limitations of naive UCB strategies in this setting and proposes a solution that adapts to the cost and accuracy of different fidelities. The connection to related work in multi-fidelity optimization (e.g., Huang et al., 2017) and active learning (e.g., Zhang & Chaudhuri, 2016) is also appropriately acknowledged. 

A key strength of the paper is the theoretical analysis. The authors provide a regret bound for MF-UCB and demonstrate near-optimality under certain conditions, establishing a strong theoretical foundation for their approach. The partitioning of arms into K(m) sets provides valuable insight into the algorithm's behavior and the analysis is well-structured. The inclusion of a lower bound further strengthens the theoretical contribution. The simulations, while limited in detail, support the theoretical findings, showing that MF-UCB outperforms standard UCB.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. The assumption of known ζ(m) values might be restrictive in practice. While acknowledged, the impact of this assumption and potential alternatives are not thoroughly discussed. The discussion of the regret definition and alternative choices for qt could be expanded.  The simulations are somewhat limited and could benefit from more extensive experimentation with varying parameters and problem settings. The notation is dense and could be improved for readability. The connection between the theoretical results and the practical implications of the algorithm could be further emphasized.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and well-defined problem formulation.
*   Strong theoretical guarantees with a near-optimal regret bound.
*   Clear connection to existing bandit literature.
*   Empirical results supporting the theoretical findings.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Restrictive assumption of known ζ(m) values.
*   Limited scope of simulations.
*   Dense notation and potential for improved clarity.



**Overall:**

Despite some minor weaknesses, this is a solid contribution to the field of bandit algorithms. The problem is well-motivated, the proposed solution is theoretically sound, and the empirical results are promising. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the clarity and simulation aspects.
