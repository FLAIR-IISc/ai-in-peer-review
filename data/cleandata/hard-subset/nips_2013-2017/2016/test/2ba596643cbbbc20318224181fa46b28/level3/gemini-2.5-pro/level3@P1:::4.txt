### Review

**Summary**

This paper introduces the Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit (MF-MAB) problem, a novel extension of the classical stochastic K-armed bandit setting. The key idea is that for each of the K arms, an agent can choose to pull from one of M "fidelities." Lower fidelities are cheaper to sample but provide a more biased or noisy approximation of the true reward distribution, which is only available at the highest (and most expensive) fidelity. The authors formalize this problem with a cost-aware notion of regret, where the goal is to minimize the opportunity cost against an oracle that always plays the best arm, considering a total budget Λ.

To tackle this problem, the paper proposes the Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB) algorithm. This algorithm adaptively chooses which arm to play based on a UCB principle and then selects a fidelity for that arm based on a switching criterion designed to balance the information gain against the sampling cost. The authors provide a theoretical analysis of MF-UCB, deriving a regret bound that shows the algorithm effectively uses cheaper fidelities to eliminate clearly suboptimal arms, reserving expensive high-fidelity pulls for a smaller set of promising candidates. This is contrasted with a standard UCB approach that would only use the highest fidelity. The paper complements this with a lower bound for the MF-MAB problem, demonstrating that MF-UCB is nearly optimal under certain conditions. The theoretical claims are supported by experiments on synthetic data, which show MF-UCB outperforming a standard UCB baseline.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance)**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper formulates a novel and practically important problem. The multi-fidelity setup is highly relevant to many real-world scenarios where preliminary, cheaper experiments can be run before committing to expensive ones (e.g., simulations vs. physical experiments, small-scale A/B tests vs. full rollouts, cheap computational models vs. expensive ones). Formalizing this within the bandit framework is a valuable contribution.

2.  **Technical Soundness:** The paper presents a complete piece of work, including a novel algorithm (MF-UCB), a corresponding upper-bound analysis, and a lower bound for the problem class. The theoretical analysis appears to be sound and captures the core intuition that lower fidelities should be used to prune the set of arms under consideration.

3.  **Potential Impact:** The ideas presented could be influential for researchers and practitioners in areas like automated machine learning (hyperparameter tuning), experimental design, and online advertising, where managing the cost of exploration is critical.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against acceptance)**

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is difficult to read in its current form, which significantly hinders its quality. The notation is dense, often introduced without sufficient immediate context, and dispersed throughout the text. For instance, the variable `s` in the definition of `B(m)k,t(s)` is used before its role as the number of samples is made clear. This lack of clear organization and unwieldy prose makes it challenging to follow the technical arguments and intuitions behind the algorithm and its analysis. A significant revision is needed to streamline the presentation.

2.  **Misleading Motivation:** The primary motivating example of online advertising, where fidelities correspond to shorter display times, is problematic. Shorter or longer ad displays often introduce non-stationarity (e.g., user fatigue, novelty effects wearing off), which is a fundamentally different challenge from the stationary, biased-mean assumption made in the paper's model. This mismatch between the motivation and the formal model weakens the paper's framing.

3.  **Questionable Regret Formulation:** A more fundamental issue lies in the definition of regret. The paper defines regret relative to an oracle that achieves `µ*` on every pull, effectively aiming to match the performance of the best arm at the highest fidelity. This framing seems to ignore the core trade-off. In many practical settings, the goal is to find the arm with the best *reward-to-cost ratio*. An arm with a slightly lower mean `µ(M)` but a drastically lower cost `λ(M)` might be preferable. The current regret definition `R(Λ,A) = Λµ* − Σ λ(mt)µ(M)It` effectively measures the total cost required to identify and exploit the arm with the highest `µ(M)`, rather than maximizing the total reward obtained within the budget Λ. This formulation may obscure the true cost of low-fidelity exploration, as these costs are only implicitly accounted for in how they enable (or fail to enable) plays that yield `µ(M)`.

4.  **Alternative Framing May Be More Appropriate:** Following the previous point, the problem might be more naturally framed as one of sample/cost complexity for best-arm identification. For many real-world testing scenarios, the goal is not to minimize cumulative regret over time but to use a fixed budget (or time) to identify the best option with high probability. A cost-complexity analysis focusing on this objective could be more insightful and better motivated than the current regret analysis. The current setup seems to force a cumulative regret framework onto a problem that feels more aligned with fixed-budget exploration.

**Overall Recommendation**

This paper introduces an interesting and potentially impactful problem, and the authors' approach of using low-fidelity samples to prune the search space is intuitive and powerful. However, the paper is not ready for publication in its current form. The significant issues with clarity, a questionable motivating example, and a potentially mismatched problem formulation (regret minimization vs. cost-effective best-arm identification) are major concerns. While the technical core seems solid, these framing and presentation issues prevent me from recommending acceptance. A major revision that clarifies the writing and notation, re-evaluates the regret definition, and better aligns the motivation with the model could turn this into a strong paper for a future conference.
