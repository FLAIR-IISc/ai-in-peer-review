

This paper introduces a multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit framework where arms can be sampled at multiple fidelities with varying costs, proposing an adaptive algorithm called MF-UCB. The authors formalize a cost-weighted regret measure, prove theoretical guarantees including a lower bound, and demonstrate empirical superiority over UCB on synthetic problems. The work builds on classical bandit literature (e.g., UCB) but extends it to incorporate multiple fidelity levels, a novel direction that could be impactful for resource-constrained applications.

The paper's strengths include a well-motivated problem formulation, a theoretically grounded algorithm with provable regret bounds, and promising experimental results showing MF-UCB outperforms UCB in synthetic settings. The theoretical analysis is technically sound, and the multi-fidelity perspective represents a meaningful extension to the bandit literature.

However, several critical issues prevent publication in its current form. The notation is confusing and poorly organized (e.g., variable 's' is defined after first use and rarely used afterward), significantly harming readability. The advertising example is misleading, as the paper incorrectly references "display time correlations" and "non-stationarity" in a context where these concepts are irrelevant to the proposed framework. Most critically, the regret analysis treats the optimal arm as the one with highest mean reward regardless of cost, which contradicts the cost-aware nature of the problem—optimal policies should maximize reward/cost ratio, not just reward. The authors would be better served by providing a sample-complexity (cost-complexity) analysis focused on finding the best arm with high probability under budget constraints, which would better align with real-world testing scenarios.

The writing is also unwieldy in parts and requires significant streamlining. The paper would benefit from clearer organization, more precise terminology, and a more appropriate motivating example. While the core idea is promising, these fundamental issues must be addressed before the paper can be considered for publication at NIPS. The authors should revise the regret formulation, clarify the advertising example, and reorganize the presentation to highlight the novel contributions more effectively.
