## Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

This paper introduces a valuable extension to the classical K-armed bandit problem by incorporating the notion of multi-fidelity evaluations, where cheaper, approximate observations are available alongside expensive, precise measurements. The authors formalize this setting and propose MF-UCB, a novel algorithm building on the UCB framework, specifically designed to exploit the varying costs and fidelities. This is a relevant and timely contribution, as scenarios with inexpensive approximations are common in areas like online advertising, algorithm selection, and robotics – all popular topics within the NIPS community. Existing work on bandits (e.g., Auer, 2002; Bubeck & Cesa-Bianchi, 2012) largely assumes a single cost per arm pull, and while related work exists on budgeted bandits (Tran-Thanh et al., 2013) and active learning (Zhang & Chaudhuri, 2016), it doesn’t directly address the exploration-exploitation dilemma inherent in the multi-fidelity setting as formalized here.

The strengths of this paper are substantial. The problem formulation is clear and well-motivated. The theoretical analysis, including regret bounds and a near-optimality lower bound, provides a strong foundation for the proposed algorithm. Demonstrating that MF-UCB adapts to the available approximations and performs better than naive strategies is a key achievement. The simulations convincingly show the effectiveness of the approach, and the provided illustration effectively visualizes the algorithm’s behavior. Writing and presentation are excellent; the paper is well-organized and easy to follow. 

However, a potential weakness lies in the assumptions required for the theoretical guarantees. The regularity condition on the decay of ζ(m) might be restrictive in practice, and its impact on performance when violated would be valuable to explore further. While the authors acknowledge this and discuss potential avenues for future work, a more detailed discussion of its practical implications would be beneficial. Further, the analysis of the lower bound reveals potential gaps, particularly concerning scenarios where the algorithm's switching criterion might not perfectly align with the theoretical optimum. Addressing these points could further strengthen the paper.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and well-defined problem formulation with significant practical relevance.
*   Strong theoretical guarantees with regret bounds and a near-optimal lower bound.
*   Clear and convincing empirical validation.
*   Excellent writing and presentation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Restrictive assumption on the decay of fidelity approximations.
*   Potential gap between theoretical lower bound and algorithm's behavior in certain scenarios.



**Overall:** This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of bandit algorithms. Despite some minor limitations, the novelty, theoretical depth, and practical relevance of the work strongly support its acceptance to NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above.
