

This paper introduces a multi-fidelity bandit framework where each arm can be sampled at multiple fidelities with different costs, with lower-fidelity samples providing biased approximations to the highest-fidelity reward distribution. The authors propose MF-UCB, an adaptive algorithm that dynamically selects fidelities to balance exploration and exploitation, and provide theoretical regret bounds and a matching lower bound. This work extends the classical K-armed bandit literature (Robbins, Lai & Robbins, Auer et al.) by incorporating multiple fidelity levels, which is particularly relevant to applications like online advertising where cheaper approximations to user behavior exist.

The paper's main strengths include a well-motivated problem statement with clear potential applications, a theoretically grounded algorithm with provable regret guarantees, and empirical results demonstrating MF-UCB's superiority over standard UCB on synthetic problems. The theoretical analysis is technically sound, with the regret bound showing how MF-UCB leverages cheaper fidelities to eliminate suboptimal arms before investing in expensive high-fidelity evaluations. The lower bound demonstrates that MF-UCB is nearly optimal under certain conditions.

However, several significant weaknesses prevent this paper from being suitable for publication in its current form. First, the notation is confusing and inconsistent throughout the paper. The variable 's' (representing the number of samples) is defined after its first use and then rarely used, creating unnecessary confusion. The paper also lacks a clear definition of the cost structure, with the regret formulation failing to properly account for the cost-reward tradeoff that is central to the multi-fidelity setting.

The advertising example is particularly problematic as it misrepresents the problem. The paper discusses display time correlations and non-stationarity in user behavior, which are irrelevant to the bandit framework being proposed. The regret analysis contains a fundamental flaw: it defines the optimal arm as the one with the highest mean reward regardless of cost, rather than the arm with the highest reward-to-cost ratio. This misalignment with the multi-fidelity framework's core premise undermines the theoretical contribution.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by shifting from a regret minimization framework to a sample-complexity (or cost-complexity) analysis focused on identifying the best arm with high probability under budget constraints. This approach would better align with real-world testing scenarios where the primary goal is to identify the optimal arm within a fixed budget, rather than minimizing regret over time. The current regret formulation obscures the key tradeoff between fidelity cost and estimation accuracy.

The writing is also unwieldy in parts, with sections that could be streamlined for better clarity. The partitioning of arms into sets K(m) is theoretically sound but presented in a way that obscures the main intuition. The experimental section lacks sufficient detail to understand the synthetic problem settings, and the comparison to UCB would be more convincing with a more detailed analysis of when the advantage of MF-UCB is most pronounced.

Regarding the four key criteria:
- Quality: The theoretical analysis is sound but the regret formulation contains a fundamental flaw that weakens the contribution. The lower bound is correctly derived but the regret bound's interpretation is misleading.
- Clarity: The paper suffers from poor notation and confusing presentation, particularly regarding the cost structure and regret formulation. The advertising example is misleading and detracts from the technical contribution.
- Originality: The multi-fidelity bandit framework is novel, and the MF-UCB algorithm represents a meaningful extension of UCB methods. However, the related work section could better distinguish this contribution from recent work on multi-fidelity optimization (e.g., Kandasamy et al., 2016).
- Significance: The problem is important for applications where high-fidelity evaluations are expensive, but the current analysis does not sufficiently demonstrate how the framework improves practical decision-making. A sample-complexity analysis would better showcase the significance.

The paper's core idea is promising but requires substantial revision to address the theoretical flaw in the regret formulation, improve the clarity of the presentation, and better align the analysis with practical applications. The authors should consider shifting to a sample-complexity framework that directly addresses the cost constraints in real-world settings. With these revisions, this work could make a meaningful contribution to the bandit literature. As currently written, the paper's theoretical contribution is weakened by the flawed regret formulation, and the practical significance is not clearly demonstrated.
