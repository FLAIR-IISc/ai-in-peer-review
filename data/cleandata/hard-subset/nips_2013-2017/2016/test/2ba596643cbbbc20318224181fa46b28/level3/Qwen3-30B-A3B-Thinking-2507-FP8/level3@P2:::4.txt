

# Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

This paper introduces a novel multi-fidelity bandit framework where each arm can be sampled at multiple fidelities, each with different costs and providing biased estimates of the highest fidelity outcome. The authors propose MF-UCB, an algorithm that adaptively leverages lower fidelity approximations to reduce regret compared to standard UCB, and provide theoretical guarantees including a regret bound and lower bound. This work extends the classical bandit literature [3, 9, 11] and builds on recent multi-fidelity optimization approaches [7, 10, 16] by providing a formal regret analysis for bandit settings.

**Strengths:**
- The multi-fidelity bandit framework addresses a natural extension of classical bandit problems with clear practical relevance to domains like online advertising, algorithm selection, and experimental design.
- The MF-UCB algorithm is well-motivated and provides a principled way to leverage lower fidelity approximations, with theoretical analysis showing it can achieve better regret than naive strategies.
- The regret bound and lower bound together demonstrate near-optimality under certain conditions, which is a significant theoretical contribution.
- Synthetic experiments support the theoretical claims, showing MF-UCB outperforms UCB across multiple settings.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's regret formulation is questionable. The authors define regret as R(Λ) = Λμ* - ∑λ(mt)qt, but the optimal policy should maximize reward/cost ratio rather than simply highest mean reward. The paper treats the best arm as having highest mean regardless of cost, potentially obscuring the cost of low-fidelity sampling. This misalignment with standard optimization objectives weakens the theoretical contribution.
- The advertising example is misleading. The paper discusses display time correlations and non-stationarity, which are irrelevant to the bandit setting and add confusion to the core problem.
- Notation is inconsistent and dispersed (e.g., variable s is defined after first use and then barely used), significantly harming readability and clarity.
- The writing is unwieldy in parts, with unnecessary complexity in the regret formulation and theoretical arguments that could be streamlined.

**Quality:** The paper makes a technically sound contribution with a clear regret bound and lower bound, but the regret formulation's misalignment with standard optimization objectives weakens the theoretical contribution. The experiments are reasonable but limited to synthetic problems.

**Clarity:** The paper suffers from confusing notation and an unwieldy writing style. Key concepts like the regret formulation and the role of ζ(m) are not clearly explained. The advertising example adds unnecessary confusion rather than clarity.

**Originality:** The multi-fidelity bandit framework extends classical bandit literature in a meaningful way. While related to recent work on multi-fidelity optimization [7, 8, 16], the paper provides the first formal regret analysis for bandit settings with multiple fidelities, making it a significant contribution to the literature.

**Significance:** The problem is highly relevant to real-world applications where cheaper approximations to expensive experiments exist. However, the questionable regret formulation limits the paper's practical significance. A more appropriate analysis would focus on sample complexity (cost-complexity) for finding the best arm with high probability under budget constraints, which would better align with real-world testing scenarios.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Novel multi-fidelity bandit framework addressing an important extension of classical bandit problems.
- Theoretical contribution with regret bound and lower bound demonstrating near-optimality.
- Strong empirical results on synthetic problems supporting the theoretical claims.
- Clear potential impact across multiple application domains.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- Fundamental issue with the regret formulation that misaligns with standard optimization objectives.
- Confusing notation and unwieldy writing that significantly hinder readability.
- Misleading advertising example that adds confusion rather than clarity.
- Theoretical contribution weakened by the questionable regret formulation.

**Recommendation:** The paper addresses an important problem with a theoretically sound approach, but the fundamental issues with the regret formulation and clarity of presentation prevent it from being ready for publication. The authors should revise the regret formulation to properly account for cost in the optimization objective, streamline the writing, and provide a clearer example that aligns with the bandit setting. With these revisions, the paper would be a strong candidate for NIPS. As it stands, it does not meet the quality standards for publication.
