This paper introduces and studies a novel multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit (MF-MAB) problem. In this setting, each of the K arms can be played at one of M "fidelities". Higher fidelities provide more accurate reward estimates but incur a higher cost, while lower fidelities are cheaper but provide biased approximations. The goal is to minimize a cost-weighted regret over a total budget. This formulation is well-motivated by practical applications like online advertising, where short ad displays can serve as cheap approximations for long-term click-through rates, or hyperparameter optimization, where models can be trained on smaller data subsets.

The authors make several contributions. They formalize the MF-MAB problem with a new, appropriate definition of regret. They propose the MF-UCB algorithm, an elegant extension of the classic UCB algorithm, which uses the different fidelities to efficiently eliminate suboptimal arms. The algorithm uses cheap, low-fidelity pulls to explore broadly and reserves expensive, high-fidelity pulls for a smaller set of promising arms. The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis of MF-UCB, including an upper bound on its regret and a corresponding problem-specific lower bound. The analysis shows that MF-UCB is nearly optimal under certain conditions. Finally, the authors present simulation results on synthetic data that validate their theoretical findings and demonstrate the practical benefits of their approach over a standard U-CB baseline that only uses the highest fidelity. This work is related to the classic stochastic bandit literature but introduces a novel and challenging structural element with the fidelity hierarchy, distinguishing it from budgeted bandits or other variants.

**Strengths**

*   **Novelty and Significance:** The paper introduces a new, interesting, and practically relevant problem setting. The idea of leveraging cheap, biased approximations in a sequential decision-making context is powerful and has clear applications in many domains. This work opens up a promising new direction for bandit research.
*   **Completeness and Technical Quality:** The paper represents a complete and technically sound piece of research. It provides a clear problem formulation, a novel algorithm, a rigorous theoretical analysis with both upper and lower regret bounds, and empirical validation. The analysis is non-trivial, particularly in how it partitions the arm set to characterize the behavior of the algorithm at different fidelities.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clear, the algorithm is intuitive, and the main theoretical results are stated plainly. The key idea—using low fidelities to filter out clearly suboptimal arms—is communicated effectively.

**Weaknesses**

The primary weakness of this work lies in a crucial assumption that may limit its direct practical applicability.

*   **Major Concern: Known Fidelity Biases (`ζ^(m)`)**: The algorithm and its analysis rely on the assumption that the maximum deviation `ζ^(m)` between the mean reward of the `m`-th fidelity and the highest fidelity (`|µ^(M)_k - µ^(m)_k| ≤ ζ^(m)`) is known for all `m` and `k`. This is a very strong assumption. It is unclear how a practitioner would obtain these `ζ^(m)` values in a real-world scenario. The paper does not discuss potential methods for estimating these parameters or analyze the algorithm's robustness to their misspecification. If these values are chosen too conservatively (too large), the benefit of low-fidelity pulls might be lost. If chosen too optimistically (too small), the algorithm may incorrectly eliminate good arms. Addressing this assumption, for instance, by proposing a method to learn these bounds online, would significantly strengthen the paper and broaden its impact. The authors should, at a minimum, discuss the sensitivity of their method to this assumption.

**Detailed Comments and Corrections**

The manuscript would benefit from a careful proofreading, as there appear to be several typos and notational errors, primarily in the analysis section (likely in the appendix, based on the line numbers). Correcting these would improve the clarity and readability for experts trying to verify the proofs.

*   Minor 1: In lines 326, 357, and 386, it seems "arm k ∈ H^(m)" should be "k ∈ K^(m)".
*   Minor 2: In line 343, the last indicator term seems incorrect. "β_{k*,t} ≤ s" should likely be related to the true mean, perhaps "s = µ_*".
*   Minor 3: In line 345, γ should probably be fidelity-dependent, i.e., γ^(m).
*   Minor 4: In lines 346–347, the inequalities in the condition "ψ(Δ_k^(m) − γ^(m)) ≤ ψ(γ^(m)) when ψ(Δ_k^(m)) ≤ 2 γ^(m)" appear to be reversed. It should likely be "≥" in both cases for the argument to hold.
*   Minor 5: In line 384, the summation seems to be over arms that are eliminated at fidelities "below" `m`, not "above".
*   Minor 6: In line 407, on the right side of the inequality, `δ^(m)` should be `δ^(M)`, and the final term should probably be `κ λ^(m)` instead of `μ_* κ λ^(m)`.
*   Minor 7: In line 431, "for l ≥ m" should be "for l ≥ p".
*   Minor 8: In line 440 / Eq.(17), "l ≥ m" should also be "l ≥ p".
*   Minor 9: In line 450, P(A_{n,3}) should be P(A_{n,2}).

**Summary of Evaluation**

*   **Quality:** High. The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by rigorous proofs.
*   **Clarity:** Good. The paper is well-organized and clearly written, though it would benefit from correction of the minor errors listed above.
*   **Originality:** High. The multi-fidelity bandit formulation is novel and the proposed algorithm and its analysis are original contributions.
*   **Significance:** Potentially high. The problem is important, but the practical significance is currently limited by the strong assumption of known fidelity biases.

Overall, this is a strong paper that introduces a novel and interesting problem with a solid theoretical contribution. The main drawback is the strong assumption about knowing the fidelity biases, which raises questions about its immediate practicality. However, for a theoretical conference like NIPS, this is an acceptable limitation given the novelty and quality of the contribution. This work lays excellent groundwork for future research into this important area. I recommend acceptance.
