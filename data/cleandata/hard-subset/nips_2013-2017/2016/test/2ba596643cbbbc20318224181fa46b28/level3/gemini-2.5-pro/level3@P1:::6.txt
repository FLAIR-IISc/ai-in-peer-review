This paper introduces and studies the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit problem, a novel and practical extension of the classical stochastic K-armed bandit setting. In this formulation, each of K arms can be evaluated at one of M "fidelities". Lower fidelities are cheaper to sample but provide biased approximations of the true reward, which is only observed at the most expensive, highest fidelity. The authors provide compelling motivation for this setting from domains like online advertising and algorithm selection.

The main contributions are threefold. First, the paper formalizes the problem, introducing a cost-aware notion of regret for a fixed capital budget. Second, it proposes the Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB) algorithm, an elegant extension of the UCB principle. The core idea of MF-UCB is to use cheap, low-fidelity pulls to quickly identify and eliminate grossly suboptimal arms, thereby reserving the expensive, high-fidelity budget for a smaller, more promising set of candidates. Third, the authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis of MF-UCB, deriving a regret upper bound that demonstrates its advantage over a standard UCB approach that ignores the lower fidelities. This is complemented by a problem-specific lower bound, showing that MF-UCB is near-optimal under certain conditions. The theoretical claims are supported by simulations on synthetic problems.

The work builds upon the extensive literature on UCB methods for multi-armed bandits but addresses a new set of algorithmic and theoretical challenges posed by the multi-fidelity structure. It is well-distinguished from related work on budgeted bandits, where costs are associated with arms rather than fidelities, and from multi-fidelity optimization, which typically does not consider regret or the exploration-exploitation trade-off.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical analysis is thorough and appears sound. Providing both an upper bound for the proposed algorithm and a matching lower bound is a significant strength, demonstrating a deep understanding of the problem's complexity. The claims are well-supported by the proofs, and the algorithm's design, particularly the switching criterion between fidelities, is well-justified by the subsequent analysis. The experiments, while synthetic, effectively illustrate the theoretical results and the practical advantage of the multi-fidelity approach.

The main weakness lies in a crucial assumption: that the bias bounds, ζ(m) (the maximum deviation of the m-th fidelity mean from the highest fidelity mean), are known to the agent a priori. This assumption is strong and may not hold in many real-world applications where this paper is motivated. The paper does not discuss how these parameters might be obtained in practice, nor does it analyze the algorithm's robustness to their mis-specification. This limits the immediate applicability of the proposed method and frames it more as a foundational theoretical contribution than a ready-to-use practical tool.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The problem formulation is clear, and the motivation is compelling. The MF-UCB algorithm is presented intuitively, and the high-level description of the analysis, including the partitioning of the arm set into K(m), is helpful for building an understanding of the main theoretical results. The figures and tables effectively communicate the experimental outcomes. However, the manuscript would benefit from careful proofreading, as there appear to be several minor errors and typos in the analysis and proofs (presumably in the appendix, based on the line numbers).

**Originality:** The work is highly original. The multi-fidelity bandit problem, as formulated here, is novel and poses a new set of interesting challenges. While the algorithmic solution is inspired by UCB, its adaptation to handle multiple fidelities with varying costs and biases is non-trivial and creative. This paper opens up a new and promising direction for research within the bandit literature. The authors have done a good job of situating their contribution with respect to prior work.

**Significance:** The results are important and likely to be influential. The problem setting captures a common feature of many real-world sequential decision-making tasks where cheap approximations are available. By providing a principled and provably efficient algorithm for this setting, the paper makes a significant contribution that could lead to substantial efficiency gains in applications. The theoretical framework is likely to inspire follow-up work on relaxing the core assumptions or extending the setting to more complex scenarios (e.g., contextual or non-stationary environments).

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Introduces a novel, well-motivated, and important problem (the multi-fidelity MAB).
*   Proposes an elegant and intuitive algorithm (MF-UCB) to solve it.
*   Provides a strong and comprehensive theoretical analysis, including upper and lower bounds that establish the algorithm's near-optimality.
*   The work opens up a promising new area for bandit research with high potential for practical impact.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The practical utility is somewhat limited by the strong assumption that the fidelity bias bounds ζ(m) are known in advance.

**Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors:**
The primary concern is the assumption of known ζ(m) values. Could you please comment on the practicality of this assumption? How sensitive is MF-UCB's performance to an incorrect specification of these parameters? Is it possible to learn or estimate these bounds online, and how might that affect the regret guarantees? A discussion of these points would greatly strengthen the paper's practical relevance.

Additionally, please consider correcting the following minor issues in the manuscript:
*   Lines 326, 357, 386: "arm k ∈ H^(m)" should probably be "k ∈ K^(m)".
*   Line 343: It seems the indicator condition "β_{k*,t} ≤ s" should be "s = μ_*".
*   Line 345: γ should be γ^(m).
*   Lines 346–347: The inequalities in "ψ(Δ_k^(m) − γ^(m)) ≤ ψ(γ^(m)) when ψ(Δ_k^(m)) ≤ 2 γ^(m)" seem to be reversed; they should likely be "≥".
*   Line 384: The sum should probably be over arms in partitions "below" K^(m), not "above".
*   Line 407: On the RHS, δ^(m) should be δ^(M), and the final term seems to be missing μ_*, i.e., it should be μ_* κ λ^(m).
*   Line 431: "for l ≥ m" should be "for l ≥ p".
*   Line 440 / Eq.(17): "l ≥ m" should be "l ≥ p".
*   Line 450: "P(A_{n,3})" should likely be "P(A_{n,2})".

Overall, this is an excellent paper that introduces an important new problem and provides a high-quality, near-complete theoretical treatment. Despite the strong assumption regarding ζ(m), the novelty and significance of the contribution are high, making it a strong candidate for acceptance.
