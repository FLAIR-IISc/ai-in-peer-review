Summary and relation to prior work
The paper introduces a new stochastic K-armed bandit formulation where each arm can be evaluated at one of M fidelities. Higher fidelities produce the “true” reward but at higher cost; lower fidelities are cheaper but provide biased approximations with known fidelity-specific bias bounds ζ(m) that shrink with m. The objective is to minimize a cost-weighted pseudo-regret under a capital budget Λ, where regret is measured against the highest-fidelity mean µ(M) even when a lower fidelity is queried.

The authors propose MF-UCB, a UCB-style index that, for each arm, forms upper confidence bounds on µ(M) using empirical means at each fidelity plus the corresponding bias bound, and chooses the minimum across fidelities. A fidelity-selection rule uses thresholds γ(m) to decide whether to continue sampling at a cheap fidelity or escalate to a more expensive one, balancing the information that can be gained against the intrinsic bias at that fidelity. They provide a regret bound showing that MF-UCB concentrates expensive samples on a small set of near-optimal arms while eliminating clearly suboptimal arms using cheap fidelities, yielding regret improvements vs standard UCB that ignores multi-fidelity structure. A lower bound demonstrates near-optimality, with a precisely identified gap in a subset of cases where MF-UCB’s escalation policy is conservative.

This work is closely related to classical stochastic bandits and UCB algorithms [Agrawal; Auer et al.; Bubeck & Cesa-Bianchi], but differs by incorporating biased, cheaper proxies with explicit costs. It is distinct from budgeted bandits (reward/cost tradeoffs), weak/strong labeler active learning, and multi-fidelity optimization in engineering (which typically lacks regret analysis). The paper also connects to the authors’ later work on GP-based multi-fidelity bandits.

Quality
Strengths:
- Clear problem formalization with costs and known bias envelopes ζ(m); well-motivated through practical examples (ads, algorithm selection).
- MF-UCB is natural and well-justified: arm indices aggregate multi-fidelity information to upper bound µ(M), and the γ(m) rule encodes an optimal tradeoff (under assumptions) between cheap elimination and expensive confirmation.
- Theorem 2 provides a regret bound that decomposes cost by fidelity and arm partitions K(m), elucidating when multi-fidelity helps (many clearly suboptimal arms, significant cost gaps λ(m+1)/λ(m)).
- Lower bound (Theorem 4) via change-of-measure identifies near-optimality for a broad subset of arms K(m)3 and pinpoints where the algorithm may be suboptimal (K(m)7), with a thoughtful discussion.
- Simulation results are consistent with the theory (reduced slope and cheap-fidelity filtering).

Weaknesses / questions:
- Practicality of known bias bounds ζ(m): How are these obtained? Are they arm-agnostic and conservative upper bounds? What if ζ(m) are mis-specified or only approximately known? Can MF-UCB be made adaptive to unknown ζ(m) (e.g., estimate or validate bias online) and what would be the impact on guarantees?
- Assumption 1 (fast decay of ζ(m)) seems tailored to ensure benefits do not evaporate when many closely spaced fidelities are present. Discussion of performance without this assumption is brief; can the authors bound the additive penalty when it fails?
- The lower bound is for Bernoulli rewards; the upper bound handles general ψ via concentration. Can a matching lower bound be extended beyond Bernoulli?
- Comparisons are limited to vanilla UCB. Stronger baselines (e.g., KL-UCB, UCB-V, Thompson sampling variants) and ablations (e.g., naive escalation heuristics) would strengthen the empirical case.
- The regret notion fixes the comparator to µ(M). While well-argued, it would help to more explicitly discuss alternative objectives (e.g., fidelity-aware utilities) and how MF-UCB might generalize.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The K(m) partition and the role of γ(m) are well motivated, and the proof sketches convey the main ideas. A few editorial issues remain (see minor comments below). Suggestions:
- Briefly illustrate how one might obtain ζ(m) in practice (domain knowledge, pilot experiments, worst-case modeling) and discuss robustness to misspecification.
- Add a small example showing the switch logic and K(m) partitions numerically.

Originality
The formulation of multi-fidelity bandits with biased proxies and explicit costs, together with the MF-UCB algorithm and associated regret analysis, is novel and distinct from prior bandit models and from multi-fidelity optimization in engineering. The explicit integration of bias envelopes into UCB indices is a neat idea.

Significance
The results are theoretically meaningful and practically relevant for settings with cheap-but-biased proxies (ads, simulators, cheap dataset subsampling). The analysis provides insight into when multi-fidelity is beneficial and should stimulate follow-up work (e.g., adaptive estimation of bias, Bayesian variants, nonuniform/arm-dependent fidelities). Even if some assumptions may be strong in practice, the work advances the state of the art in a clear way.

Pros for acceptance
- Well-motivated new problem with a principled algorithm.
- Solid, nontrivial theoretical analysis with interpretable bounds and a complementary lower bound.
- Clear explanation of when multi-fidelity helps; partitions K(m) provide insight.
- Empirical evidence consistent with theory.

Cons against acceptance
- Reliance on known ζ(m) and Assumption 1; limited discussion of robustness and estimation.
- Empirical evaluation uses synthetic data only and limited baselines.
- Lower bound mismatch on K(m)7 and Bernoulli-only lower bound.

Questions for the authors
- How would you obtain ζ(m) in practice, and how sensitive is MF-UCB to over/under-estimation of these bounds? Can you provide experiments with mis-specified ζ(m)?
- Can the bias bounds be arm-dependent (ζk(m))? Would your analysis or algorithmic design change materially?
- Can MF-UCB be adapted when ψ is unknown (e.g., empirical Bernstein bounds) or when costs vary slightly over time?
- Could you compare against stronger baselines (KL-UCB, UCB-V, Thompson sampling) and simple multi-fidelity heuristics?
