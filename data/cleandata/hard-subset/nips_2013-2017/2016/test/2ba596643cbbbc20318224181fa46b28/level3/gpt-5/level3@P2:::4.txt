Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces a multi-fidelity stochastic K-armed bandit model in which each arm can be sampled at M fidelities with costs λ(1) < … < λ(M), and lower-fidelity observations are biased but within known deviations ζ(m) from the highest-fidelity mean. The authors define a cost-weighted pseudo-regret for a strategy that expends a capital Λ and propose MF-UCB, an optimism-based policy that maintains upper confidence bounds for each arm at each fidelity, inflating by ζ(m), and selects both the arm and the fidelity. A key design choice is a sequence of thresholds γ(m) that determine when to escalate to higher fidelities, balancing bias and cost via ψ−1(λ(m)/λ(m+1) ψ(ζ(m))). The main theorem shows that MF-UCB concentrates its capital on lower fidelities for clearly suboptimal arms and reserves high-fidelity plays for near-optimal arms, yielding regret roughly of order ∑k Δ(M)
k λ(Jk)/ψ(Δ(Jk)
k), which improves over naive UCB that ignores fidelities when |K(M)| ≪ K and λ(1) ≪ λ(M). A lower bound (for Bernoulli rewards) shows near-optimality except for a subset of arms where MF-UCB’s switching criterion can be suboptimal. Experiments on synthetic problems indicate improved performance over UCB.

The paper builds on classical UCB algorithms [Auer; Bubeck & Cesa-Bianchi], and is related to (but distinct from) budgeted bandits [Tran-Thanh; Xia], weak/strong labeler active learning [Zhang & Chaudhuri], and multi-fidelity optimization/BO [Huang; Kandasamy et al.] and RL [Cutler et al.]. To my knowledge, the combination of biased multi-fidelity observations with explicit cost-aware regret for stochastic bandits is new.

Strengths
- A clearly motivated algorithmic idea: use low-cost, biased proxies to eliminate arms early and invest expensive samples only on promising arms; the γ(m) choice operationalizes a cost–bias tradeoff.
- Nontrivial theoretical development: regret upper bounds that reflect fidelity allocation, with accompanying lower bounds and a precise characterization of when the algorithm is nearly optimal versus potentially loose.
- The framework may be useful in AutoML, simulation-to-real evaluation, or ad testing when cheap proxies exist.
- Synthetic experiments corroborate the predicted monotone benefit of increasing fidelity costs.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Quality
- Objective mismatch: The regret definition treats the oracle as receiving μ* per unit cost regardless of which fidelity is chosen, and credits the learner according to μ(M) of the played arm, weighted by cost. This implies that lower-fidelity plays contribute no immediate utility beyond consuming budget, which is appropriate in some simulation/proxy settings but not in others (e.g., ads shown to real users at low fidelity do generate revenue). Many applications seem better captured by maximizing reward per unit cost (or constrained average reward) or by fixed-budget best-arm identification with cost heterogeneity. As written, the analysis may hide the true cost–benefit tradeoff of low-fidelity sampling.
- The advertising example is misleading: it invokes non-stationarity (short vs. long display affecting user behavior), whereas the analysis assumes fixed (stationary) θ(m)
k with bounded bias. I suggest replacing it with an example where proxies are truly biased but stationary (e.g., training with fewer epochs or subsampled data).
- Assumptions and robustness: The approach relies on known, uniform bias bounds ζ(m) and Assumption 1 (a decay condition related to ψ) for clean comparisons and constants. The paper should discuss how performance degrades if ζ(m) are misspecified or unknown, and whether γ(m) can be learned or chosen adaptively. Also clarify the independence structure across fidelities for the same arm.
- Lower bound scope: The lower bound is for Bernoulli distributions and strategies satisfying Assumption 3 (subpolynomial pulls of suboptimal arms). Please clarify whether the assumption is standard and how it relates to typical “uniformly efficient” policies. The gap for K(m)7 is well explained; it would be useful to outline a principled modification that might close it, or argue impossibility.
- Empirics: Only synthetic experiments are provided, and MF-UCB is compared only to vanilla UCB at the highest fidelity. Stronger baselines (e.g., budgeted bandits, cost-aware Thompson sampling, or tuned escalation heuristics) and sensitivity to λ and ζ would strengthen the claims. Report how γ(m), ρ, and ψ−1 are instantiated, and whether results are robust to misspecified ζ.

Clarity
- The notation is heavy and sometimes introduced out of order. Examples: variable s appears before definition; the sets J(m)
η and K(m) are introduced with multiple nested conditions. Reorganize Section 2–3 to present assumptions, ψ and ν, ζ and λ, then the regret, then the algorithm and γ rationale. Streamline symbols (avoid redefining s and t).
- The regret decomposition r̃(Λ,A) vs. R̃(Λ,A) should be motivated earlier, with an explicit bound and a short intuitive proof that the residual is negligible.
- Algorithm 1 would benefit from clearer pseudocode and a caption summarizing the escalation logic. Define ψ and ψ−1 concretely for sub-Gaussian rewards in the main text.
- The advertising example should be revised as above to avoid implying non-stationarity.

Originality
- The formulation with biased approximations and explicit cost-aware regret, and the specific γ(m) design, are novel relative to existing budgeted bandits and multi-fidelity optimization work. The connection to best-arm identification with costs could be made more explicit to position the contribution.

Significance
- Potentially impactful for problems with abundant cheap proxies and scarce expensive measurements. However, the current regret notion limits applicability for many practical scenarios. A complementary treatment of (a) reward-per-cost regret, or (b) fixed-budget cost-aware best-arm identification with sample complexity guarantees, would substantially increase practical relevance.

Suggestions for improvement
- Justify the chosen regret formally and discuss alternatives: reward-per-cost maximization, constrained average reward, or cost-aware best-arm identification; provide at least one additional theorem in one of these variants, or state limitations.
- Add robustness analysis when ζ(m) are unknown: propose conservative bounds or online estimation, and quantify effects on regret.
- Expand empirical section: include baselines that also use multi-fidelity information; ablations on γ(m) and ρ; sensitivity to λ and ζ; and, if possible, a small real-world proxy task (e.g., subsampled training epochs as fidelity).
- Improve exposition and notation as noted; move some definitions to a concise notation table; clarify Assumptions 1 and 3 and their necessity.

Pros for acceptance
- New and timely formulation of multi-fidelity bandits with biased proxies and costs.
- Sound and mostly careful theory; insightful upper/lower bounds and allocation interpretation.
- Simple, implementable algorithm with principled parameterization.

Cons for acceptance
- Objective choice is debatable for many motivating applications; examples are misleading w.r.t. stationarity.
- Clarity and notation issues hinder accessibility.
- Limited empirical validation and weak baselines.
- Lower bound scope and robustness questions remain.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea and theory are interesting and potentially impactful, but the current formulation/justification of the objective, clarity, and empirical evaluation need significant strengthening to meet NIPS standards. I encourage the authors to revise along the lines above.

Reviewer confidence: High on bandits and cost-aware formulations; moderate on the specific lower-bound technicalities.
