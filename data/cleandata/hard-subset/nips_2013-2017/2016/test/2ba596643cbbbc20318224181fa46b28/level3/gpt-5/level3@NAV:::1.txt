This paper studies a stochastic K-armed bandit with M discrete fidelity levels per arm, where lower fidelities are cheaper but biased by known ζ(m) (with ζ(M)=0), and costs λ(m) increase with fidelity. The authors propose MF-UCB, an upper-confidence-based strategy that chooses both arm and fidelity to maximize a cost-weighted net reward. They provide a finite-time regret upper bound and a lower bound, and argue improvement over a highest-fidelity-only UCB baseline. The work connects to UCB methods (Auer), budgeted bandits (e.g., Tran-Thanh; Xia et al.), multi-fidelity optimization (Huang et al.), weak/strong labelers (Zhang & Chaudhuri), and more recent multi-fidelity bandits (e.g., Kandasamy et al., GP setting), but focuses on a stochastic, known-bias, multi-fidelity MAB with fidelity-dependent costs.

Quality: The technical development appears solid overall: the arm–fidelity partition K(m), the choice of thresholds γ(m), and the proof strategy (confidence bounds, play-count control, and change-of-measure for the lower bound) are thoughtful. However, the regret definition multiplies per-round regret by cost, which feels unnatural; an additive formulation (reward regret plus additive cumulative cost penalty or explicit budgeted horizon) should be analyzed and contrasted. The lower-bound gap for K(m)7 is nontrivial and may call for a redesigned switching rule or a robustified policy.

Clarity: The paper is difficult to parse in key places. The statement and interpretation of Theorem 2, the dependence on Δ and the role of the sets K(m), and the comparison to UCB need a clearer, more explicit walk-through (e.g., a small worked example, simplified sub-Gaussian constants, and a figure mapping terms to plays). Assumption 1 is opaque; its necessity, generality, and empirical plausibility should be better motivated. If ζ(m) do not decay as required, the authors should show how to select an effective subsequence of fidelities and quantify performance. Overall exposition would benefit from more intuition before technicalities.

Originality: Modeling known, fidelity-dependent biases with cost-aware selection and giving matching-style upper/lower bounds is novel relative to prior budgeted or weak-labeler settings.

Significance: The tradeoff between information, cost, and reward is important and timely, and the results could influence practical bandit designs. However, motivation beyond the ad example is limited; broader use-cases and a discussion of when the reward/cost model fits would increase impact. Empirical evaluation is thin.

Pros for acceptance:
- Interesting, relevant problem; technically competent analysis.
- Clear improvement over high-fidelity-only UCB under stated conditions.
- Lower bound highlights where the design is nearly optimal.

Cons against acceptance:
- Regret formulation may not match many applications; additive alternatives are not treated.
- Heavy reliance on Assumption 1; unclear robustness when ζ(m) violate it.
- Hard-to-parse results and limited empirical/real-data support.
- Gap in the lower bound suggests potential suboptimality in important regimes.
