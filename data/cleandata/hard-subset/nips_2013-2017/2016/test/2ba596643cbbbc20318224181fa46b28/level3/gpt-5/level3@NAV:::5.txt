This paper introduces a multi-fidelity (MF) variant of the stochastic K-armed bandit in which each arm admits multiple, progressively costlier fidelities whose lower-fidelity observations are biased approximations of the highest-fidelity reward. The authors formalize a capital-based pseudo-regret competing against an oracle that always accrues highest-fidelity reward per unit cost, propose MF-UCB—a UCB-style algorithm that chooses both an arm and a fidelity using calibrated confidence radii and cost/approximation thresholds—and provide upper bounds on regret that improve over naively ignoring approximations. A lower-bound analysis shows the approach is nearly optimal except for a clearly identified subset of arms. Simulations on synthetic tasks illustrate the savings: cheap fidelities rapidly eliminate poor arms and concentrate expensive evaluations on near-optimal ones. The work is well situated relative to classical UCB, budgeted bandits, weak/strong labelers, and multi-fidelity optimization and RL simulators (e.g., Auer et al.; Tran-Thanh et al.; Zhang & Chaudhuri; Huang et al.; Cutler et al.).

Quality: The theory is careful and technically sound. The regret upper bound decomposes by fidelity and matches a lower bound on a broad class of instances, with an explicit gap for K(m)7 arms that is well explained. Assumptions are clearly stated (concentration (2), known bias bounds ζ(m), regularity Assumption 1), and parameter choices (γ(m), ρ > 4) are motivated. Experiments convincingly validate the qualitative predictions, though they are limited to synthetics and a small set of baselines.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written and well organized. The regret notion and its decomposition are well motivated. Minor suggestions: discuss computational overhead of maintaining per-fidelity indices and provide a brief recipe for ψ−1 for common distributions.

Originality: The formulation of MF bandits with biased approximations and a cost-aware regret is novel relative to budgeted MAB and prior MF optimization work that lacks regret analysis. The MF-UCB design and its fidelity-switching criterion are original and insightful.

Significance: The problem is important and timely for domains with simulation vs. real-world evaluations (advertising, robotics, A/B testing, AutoML). Theoretical guarantees and practical intuition are likely to influence subsequent work.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, novel MF bandit formulation and algorithm
- Strong regret upper bounds and informative lower bounds
- Intuitive mechanism that aligns with practice
- Clear writing and good positioning

Cons for acceptance:
- Requires known ζ(m) and ψ; robustness to misspecification not evaluated
- Partial optimality gap (K(m)7) remains open
- Empirics limited to synthetic data; comparisons to broader baselines (e.g., Thompson-style MF variants, budgeted bandits) are sparse

Suggestions: add robustness experiments to misspecified ζ(m), compare against stronger baselines, and discuss learning ζ(m) online.
