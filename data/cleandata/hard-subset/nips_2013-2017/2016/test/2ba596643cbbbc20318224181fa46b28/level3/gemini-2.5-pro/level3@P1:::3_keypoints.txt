- Introduces the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit problem where each arm can be sampled at M fidelities with known bias bounds ζ(m).
- Proposes the MF-UCB algorithm that integrates bias terms into UCB-style confidence bounds and selects fidelity by checking uncertainty thresholds γ(m).
- Algorithm uses cheap low-fidelity samples to eliminate clearly suboptimal arms and reserves high-fidelity samples for promising arms.
- Provides theoretical analysis: an upper bound on cost-weighted regret showing dependence on fidelity at which arms are identified as suboptimal.
- Supplies a regret lower bound and shows MF-UCB is near-optimal under certain conditions.
- Empirical simulations on synthetic problems support theoretical claims, showing significant improvements over standard UCB.
- High technical quality: well-motivated algorithm, thorough theoretical analysis, and sound proofs.
- Modeling assumption critique: assumes lower fidelities produce bounded bias (not increased variance), which is not convincing for all motivating examples (e.g., online advertising).
- Regret definition concern: regret measured against highest-fidelity mean µ(M) may not reflect application goals where low-fidelity rewards still matter.
- Experimental weakness: synthetic parameter choices (e.g., large ζ(m)) appear to favor MF-UCB; lacks sensitivity analysis or realistic grounding.
- Clarity strength: paper is well-written, organized, and illustrated; main algorithm and intuition are clearly explained.
- Clarity weakness: Theorem 2's regret bound is expressed via a complex arm partitioning K(m) and is hard to parse; a simplified corollary would help.
- Originality: problem formulation is novel and significant; MF-UCB is an elegant but incremental adaptation of UCB principles.
- Significance: addresses a practical and important setting (cheap approximate evaluations) with potential for follow-up work and practical use.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel problem, intuitive/effective algorithm, solid theoretical upper/lower bounds, and practical insight about fidelity trade-offs.
- Arguments against acceptance: modeling assumption not universally justified, limited algorithmic novelty, empirical setup may overstate gains, and complex regret statement.
- Overall: strong and valuable contribution with sound theory, but would benefit from stronger justification of modeling choices and more robust experiments.
