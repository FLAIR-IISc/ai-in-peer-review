This paper introduces a novel framework for the multi-armed bandit problem, where observing the outcome of each arm is expensive, but cheap approximations to this outcome are available. The authors formalize this task as a multi-fidelity bandit, where at each time step, the forecaster may choose to play an arm at any one of M fidelities. The highest fidelity (desired outcome) expends cost λ, while the m fidelity (an approximation) expends λ < λ and returns a biased estimate of the highest fidelity.

The authors propose a new algorithm, MF-UCB, which adapts the classical Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) strategies to the multi-fidelity setting. They prove that MF-UCB naturally adapts to the sequence of available approximations and costs, thus attaining better regret than naive strategies that ignore the approximations. The authors also provide a lower bound and show that MF-UCB is nearly optimal under certain conditions.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the problem, including practical motivations and a thorough literature review. The proposed algorithm and its analysis are novel and contribute significantly to the field of multi-armed bandits.

The strengths of the paper include:

* A new and interesting take on the classical multi-armed bandit problem
* A well-designed algorithm that adapts to the multi-fidelity setting
* A thorough analysis of the algorithm's performance, including a lower bound
* A clear and concise writing style

However, there are some minor issues with the paper:

* There are several typos and notation inconsistencies throughout the paper (e.g., lines 326, 357, 386, 343, 345, 346-347, 384, 407, 431, and 440).
* The assumption of known ε^(m) (deviation of mth-fidelity mean from highest-fidelity mean) is unclear and may not be realistic in practice. It is unclear how these values are obtained and whether the assumption can be relaxed.

Overall, the paper is well-written and contributes significantly to the field of multi-armed bandits. The proposed algorithm and its analysis are novel and provide a new perspective on the classical problem. However, the minor issues mentioned above should be addressed in future revisions.
