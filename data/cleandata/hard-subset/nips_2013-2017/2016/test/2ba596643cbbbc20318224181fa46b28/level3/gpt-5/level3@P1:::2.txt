This paper introduces a multi-fidelity stochastic K-armed bandit formulation in which each arm can be queried at one of M fidelities. Lower fidelities are cheaper but provide biased approximations to the highest-fidelity reward. The authors define a cost-aware pseudo-regret that credits every pull by its cost times the best arm’s highest-fidelity mean, thus comparing against an oracle that always invests capital in the optimal arm at any fidelity. They propose MF-UCB, which maintains per-fidelity upper bounds on the highest-fidelity mean by adding the known fidelity bias ζ(m) to standard confidence radii, and selects the least expensive fidelity whose uncertainty is still above a threshold γ(m) that trades off bias and cost. The analysis partitions arms into sets K(m) based on how close their m-th fidelity means are to optimal, and shows MF-UCB spends most of its budget on each arm at the lowest fidelity that can certify suboptimality. The main regret bound improves over naive UCB that ignores approximations by replacing λ(M) with λ(JkK) for most suboptimal arms, and a lower bound shows near-optimality on a large subset of arms. Simulations corroborate the theory.

Relation to prior work: The setting extends classical UCB (Auer et al.) and draws connections to budgeted bandits (e.g., Tran-Thanh et al.; Xia et al.), but differs because multiple biased fidelities approximate the same target payoff rather than arms having stochastic costs. It also relates to multi-fidelity optimization in engineering (Huang et al.; Rajnarayan et al.) and robotics RL with simulators (Cutler et al.), and to weak/strong labelers in active learning (Zhang & Chaudhuri), while focusing on exploration–exploitation with regret guarantees. The paper also complements the authors’ later GP-based multi-fidelity bandits by providing a clean stochastic, per-arm foundation.

Quality: The technical contribution is solid. The regret definition is appropriate for settings where only the highest-fidelity outcome counts, and the analysis carefully handles biased lower fidelities rather than treating them as high-variance proxies. The partitioning argument and the choice γ(m) ∝ ψ−1((λ(m)/λ(m+1))ψ(ζ(m))) are well motivated by cost–bias tradeoffs. Assumption 1 (on the decay of ζ(m) relative to ψ) is reasonable for sub-Gaussian rewards and clarifies when inserted near-duplicate fidelities do not help. The upper bound degrades gracefully to UCB in the worst case and can be substantially better when few arms are near-optimal. The lower bound identifies a gap on arms in K(m)7 where a cheaper lower fidelity could, in principle, certify suboptimality more efficiently than MF-UCB’s switch criterion; acknowledging this and articulating it as an open problem is commendable. Empirical results match the predicted fidelity usage and regret slopes.

Points to improve:
- Practical sensitivity to ζ(m): The algorithm and guarantees require known fidelity biases. In practice, ζ(m) are rarely known and may be heterogeneous across arms. The paper should discuss robustness to mis-specification (e.g., overestimating ζ(m) leads to premature switching to expensive fidelities; underestimating risks over-pruning) and potential mitigations:
  - Estimating per-fidelity bias upper bounds online via cross-fidelity disagreement tests with union bounds.
  - Using conservative, data-driven γ(m) that inflate ζ(m) by confidence terms.
  - Reporting regret sensitivity plots vs. ζ(m) misspecification.
- Constants and “up-to-constant” notation: Hiding constants that depend on algorithm parameters (notably ρ) makes guidance for practitioners difficult. Present explicit inequalities, make ρ’s role in tail bounds and sample complexities transparent, and provide a tuning recommendation.
- Assumption 1: Clarify behavior when exponential decay of ζ(m) does not hold or M is large; give a bound that scales with M to indicate degradation.
- Experiments: Realistic case studies (e.g., ad allocation with time-window fidelities or ML model selection with subsampled training) would better demonstrate impact. Also report capital-normalized regret and fidelity counts to connect to Λ.

Clarity: The main paper is clear and well organized. The regret definition and algorithm are explained with good intuition. However, the supplementary material contains many typos and minor errors that impede verification. Fixing these will materially improve readability. In addition, consider standardizing the partition notation (consistently use K(m)), explicitly stating all events used in high-probability arguments, and avoiding switching between equalities/inequalities “up to constants” in the middle of proofs.

Originality: The formalization of biased, multi-cost fidelities within bandit regret, the MF-UCB design with fidelity-adaptive switching thresholds, and the matching upper/lower bounds are novel and a meaningful step beyond budgeted bandits or prior multi-fidelity heuristics.

Significance: The work provides a principled way to exploit cheap biased proxies to reduce exploration cost, which is relevant in large-K problems where only a few arms are near-optimal. The theory is clean and should spur follow-up on learning fidelity biases and extending to contextual and structured settings.

Pros:
- Clear, well-motivated formalization of multi-fidelity bandits with biased proxies.
- MF-UCB is simple, principled, and enjoys tight regret guarantees with informative intuition.
- Near-optimal lower bound; identification of a precise gap clarifies limits.
- Empirical results align with theory.

Cons:
- Heavy reliance on known ζ(m) and Assumption 1; limited discussion of robustness and estimation.
- Hidden constants and “up to constants” can obscure practical guidance.
- Supplement has numerous typos; some proof steps could be made more explicit.
- Experiments are synthetic; lack of real-world validation.

Overall: A strong theoretical contribution that advances bandit methodology for realistic multi-fidelity settings. With corrections to the supplement and added discussion on estimating/robustifying ζ(m), I recommend acceptance.
