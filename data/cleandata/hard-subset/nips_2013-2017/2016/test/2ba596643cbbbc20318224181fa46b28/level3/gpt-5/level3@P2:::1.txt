Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper introduces a stochastic K-armed bandit setting in which each arm can be evaluated at multiple fidelity levels with fidelity-dependent costs. Lower fidelities provide biased but cheaper information about the highest-fidelity reward. The authors assume known fidelity-bias envelopes ζ(m) that shrink with fidelity and define a cost-weighted regret under a fixed capital Λ that measures how well a policy accrues high-fidelity reward while paying fidelity costs. They propose MF-UCB, which maintains upper confidence bounds for the highest-fidelity mean per arm by combining empirical means at each fidelity with bias offsets ζ(m), and selects both an arm and a fidelity using thresholds γ(m) that balance residual uncertainty at fidelity m against its cost relative to the next fidelity. They analyze the regret and show improvements over high-fidelity-only UCB when many arms can be eliminated at low cost. A lower bound shows near-optimality under certain conditions, with a gap identified for a subset of arms where early fidelities are only weakly informative.

This work differs from budgeted bandits (reward/cost ratios) and from weak/strong labeler active learning: the key novelty is a principled way to use biased low-cost approximations within stochastic bandits. It complements multi-fidelity optimization in engineering and robotics and relates to UCB-style bandits (Auer 2002; Bubeck & Cesa-Bianchi 2012). The GP-based multi-fidelity bandits of Kandasamy et al. (NIPS 2016) are a Bayesian counterpart; this paper addresses the stochastic, arm-wise setting with explicit bias envelopes.

Evaluation

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The MF-UCB design is well motivated: the confidence inflation by ζ(m) yields valid high-fidelity upper bounds; the switching thresholds γ(m) are derived by balancing cost and information. The main theorem partitions arms by how soon they can be ruled out and shows that MF-UCB concentrates expensive plays on a small subset near-optimal arms. The lower bound uses a change-of-measure argument and clarifies where MF-UCB is tight and where it may be loose.
- Caveats: (i) The regret notion multiplies instantaneous regret by the fidelity cost and includes a residual for unused capital. This is nonstandard and its behavioral implications are not fully discussed. Additive cost penalization or utility-based objectives (reward minus price) are natural alternatives in many applications. (ii) The analysis leans on Assumption 1 (fast decay of ζ) in a way that is not fully transparent: while the authors note it is not strictly necessary, the clearest gains vs. UCB appear to rely on it. (iii) The lower bound gap for arms in K(m)7 highlights a potential suboptimality of the switching rule; more discussion of whether the gap is fundamental or an artifact of the policy would strengthen the paper.

Clarity:
- The paper is densely written; several key definitions (K(m) partitions, Δ(m)k, γ(m), and the regret decomposition) are hard to parse on first read. The statement and interpretation of Theorem 2 would benefit from a simplified two-fidelity walk-through and a table or figure mapping arms to partitions and dominant costs. The comparison to vanilla UCB could be made explicit with a concrete corollary for M=2, sub-Gaussian rewards, and simple λ, ζ schedules.
- The role and necessity of Assumption 1 should be explained with examples; a constructive recipe to prune nearly redundant fidelities (so the assumption holds on a subsequence) would improve robustness.

Originality:
- The multi-fidelity stochastic bandit formalization, analysis with known bias envelopes, and the MF-UCB algorithm are, to the best of my knowledge, new in the bandit literature circa NIPS 2016. The framing is distinct from budgeted bandits and weak/strong labelers. The lower bound tailored to multi-fidelity structure is also novel.

Significance:
- The setting is practically relevant (ads, algorithm selection, simulation vs. real runs). The results are likely to influence follow-on work on resource-aware bandits and hierarchical evaluation. However, the chosen regret metric may not align with several applications; at minimum, discussing and analyzing an additive cost model would broaden impact. The empirical section is promising but limited to synthetic data; sensitivity to ζ and λ and additional baselines would strengthen significance.

Pros (arguments for acceptance):
- Clear, well-motivated algorithm leveraging biased, cheap proxies to reduce regret.
- Nontrivial analysis that cleanly separates cheap elimination from expensive discrimination near the optimum.
- Lower bound and near-optimality under identifiable conditions.
- Relevant connection to emerging multi-fidelity optimization literature; timely contribution.

Cons (arguments against acceptance):
- Regret formulation may not be the most natural for many applications; alternatives not analyzed.
- Assumption 1 is opaque and its practical validity unclear; algorithmic robustness to slower-decaying ζ not demonstrated.
- The lower bound gap suggests potential policy suboptimality in a meaningful regime.
- Clarity issues (heavy notation; difficult theorem interpretations); harder than necessary for readers and PC members to assess contributions.

Detailed comments and suggestions:
- Regret model: Please justify the multiplicative cost weighting more thoroughly and discuss alternatives: (a) additive cost penalty (maximize Σ μ(M)I_t − c λ(m_t)), (b) constrained budget but uniform per-step reward (as in your current abstraction) vs. per-time reward rates, (c) per-unit-cost regret (average regret per cost). A short theoretical note on how MF-UCB or a variant behaves under (a) would be valuable.
- Assumption 1 and fidelity pruning: Provide a constructive procedure to select a subsequence of fidelities so that the assumption holds (e.g., greedily drop fidelities where λ increases but ψ(ζ) barely improves), and argue MF-UCB’s performance on the pruned hierarchy. Include a proposition quantifying the loss from pruning.
- Thresholds γ(m): Offer an intuitive derivation in the main text (balance of λ(m) sampling cost with the bias ceiling ζ(m) vs. switching to m+1), with a worked M=2 example. State the constants and conditions under sub-Gaussian ψ explicitly.
- Lower bound gap: Expand the discussion of K(m)7. Could a cost-aware early stopping at low fidelity (with arm-dependent thresholds proportional to λ ratios) close the gap without incurring high-fidelity samples? Even if you leave it as open, provide guidance on when practitioners might expect the gap to matter (e.g., when λ(2)/λ(1) is large and ζ(1) modest).
- Robustness to ζ misspecification: In practice ζ(m) may be unknown. Discuss sensitivity and whether conservative upper bounds suffice. Could one adaptively calibrate ζ using cross-fidelity samples?
- Experiments: Add (i) a 2-fidelity ablation showing the arm partitioning and cost breakdown, (ii) sensitivity to λ and ζ schedules, (iii) comparison to simple baselines that pre-screen arms at low fidelity (successive rejects/elimination variants) and a Thompson-sampling adaptation.
- Notation and presentation: Consider a boxed summary of the model and regret notion; move some quantifier-heavy definitions to an appendix with a concise main-text paraphrase. Provide a concrete corollary of Theorem 2 in the sub-Gaussian case with explicit constants.
- Minor issues/typos: “near-optimal” claim should be qualified precisely (where it holds). Remove the stray period before “for all m < M.” Use curly braces in the set notation for the partition index JkK.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The problem is relevant and the technical core is solid and interesting, but the paper’s modeling choice for regret, reliance on an opaque assumption, and clarity issues hinder accessibility and impact. With clearer exposition, a more thorough discussion (or analysis) of alternative cost models, and robustness to fidelity schedules, this would be a strong submission. Confidence: high.
