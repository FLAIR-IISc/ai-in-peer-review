## Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

This paper introduces a novel multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit (MAB) setting, motivated by practical applications like online advertising where cheaper approximations of reward signals are readily available. The authors formulate a new regret definition accounting for varying costs associated with different fidelities and propose MF-UCB, a UCB-based algorithm tailored to this setting. The paper provides a theoretical analysis, including a regret lower bound, and validates the algorithm through simulations. Overall, the work presents a significant contribution to the MAB literature by addressing a practically relevant problem with a sound theoretical framework.

The core idea of leveraging cheaper, albeit noisy, approximations to accelerate learning and reduce overall cost is well-motivated and clearly articulated. The proposed regret definition is a natural extension of the standard bandit regret, appropriately incorporating fidelity costs. The theoretical analysis is a major strength, demonstrating the algorithm’s near-optimality under reasonable assumptions. The simulation results provide further evidence of MF-UCB’s effectiveness compared to a naive UCB approach. The connection to related work, particularly in UCB methods and multi-fidelity optimization, is adequately discussed.

However, a significant concern revolves around the assumption of known ζ^(m), representing the deviation of the m-th fidelity mean from the highest-fidelity mean. While acknowledged by the authors, the practical implications of this assumption are not fully addressed. Obtaining these values in real-world scenarios could be challenging, and relaxing this assumption would significantly broaden the applicability of the proposed framework. 

Several minor technical issues were identified: lines 326, 357, 386 should read "K" instead of "H"; line 343, the indicator term should be "s = μ_*"; line 345, γ should be γ^(m); lines 346–347, the inequalities should be reversed to "≥"; line 384, the summation index should be over arms "below" K^(m); line 407, δ^(m) should be δ^(M), and the last term should be κ λ^(m); line 431, "for l ≥ m" should be "for l ≥ p"; line 440 / Eq.(17), "l ≥ m" should be "l ≥ p"; and line 450, P(A_{n,3}) should be P(A_{n,2}). Addressing these would improve the clarity and correctness of the presentation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel problem formulation with strong practical motivation.
*   Sound theoretical analysis with a near-optimal regret bound.
*   Promising simulation results demonstrating algorithm performance.
*   Clear and well-structured presentation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Strong assumption of known ζ^(m) limiting practical applicability.
*   Minor technical errors requiring correction.

**Overall:** Despite the limitations regarding the ζ^(m) assumption, the paper presents a valuable contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the technical errors and a more thorough discussion of the practical challenges associated with estimating ζ^(m) and potential avenues for relaxing this assumption.
