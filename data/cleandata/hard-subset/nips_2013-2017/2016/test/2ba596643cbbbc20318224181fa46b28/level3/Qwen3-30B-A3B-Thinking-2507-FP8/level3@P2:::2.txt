

# Review for "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

This paper introduces a novel variant of the classical stochastic K-armed bandit problem where each arm can be observed at multiple fidelities, with lower fidelity observations being cheaper but providing biased estimates of the true reward. The authors formalize this as a multi-fidelity bandit setting and propose MF-UCB, an upper confidence bound algorithm that adaptively selects fidelities to minimize regret. This builds on the classical bandit literature (e.g., [3, 9, 11]) but extends it to incorporate the cost-accuracy trade-off inherent in many practical applications like online advertising (where ads can be tested for shorter durations or narrower audiences) and algorithm selection (where cheaper approximations of algorithm performance are available).

**Quality:** The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution with both an upper bound on the regret of MF-UCB and a matching lower bound under certain conditions. The analysis is technically sound, with careful attention to the bias introduced by lower fidelities and the cost structure. The regret bound demonstrates that MF-UCB outperforms standard UCB algorithms when the number of promising arms (Kg) is small relative to K and lower fidelities are sufficiently cheaper (λ(1) < λ(2)). The theoretical contribution is significant, as it provides a rigorous framework for the multi-fidelity bandit problem, which has been previously studied in optimization contexts without formal regret analysis. However, the supplementary materials contain numerous typos and small errors (e.g., page 10 eq.(8): typo in k/K, page 10 line 328: ν/(rho-2) should be ν/2, page 11 eq.(10): γ should be γ^(m)), which could confuse readers and undermine confidence in the proofs.

**Clarity:** The main paper is clearly written, well-organized, and provides excellent intuition for the algorithm and its theoretical guarantees. The problem formulation is motivated with compelling examples, and the figures help illustrate the partitioning of arms across fidelities. The use of "up-to-a-multiplicative-constant" notation is problematic, as hidden constants (e.g., ρ) are algorithm parameters that directly affect performance. Presenting exact inequalities would improve clarity and allow for better comparison of the algorithm's theoretical guarantees. The paper also lacks discussion of practical limitations: MF-UCB's performance depends on accurate estimation of fidelity parameters ζ(m), and the paper does not address how to handle estimation errors or the sensitivity of the algorithm to these parameters.

**Originality:** The paper makes a genuinely novel contribution by formalizing the multi-fidelity bandit problem and providing the first regret analysis for this setting. While there is related work on multi-fidelity methods in optimization [7, 10], these do not address the exploration-exploitation trade-off inherent in bandit problems. The paper distinguishes itself from previous bandit work by explicitly accounting for the bias introduced by lower fidelity observations, rather than treating them as simply noisier high-fidelity observations. The authors also properly reference related work from NIPS proceedings, including their own prior work [8] on multi-fidelity bandits with Gaussian process payoffs.

**Significance:** The results are important for applications where high-fidelity evaluations are expensive (e.g., online advertising, algorithm selection, clinical trials). The paper demonstrates that MF-UCB can significantly outperform standard UCB algorithms when the cost of high-fidelity evaluations is substantially higher than lower fidelities. The theoretical guarantees are compelling, and the empirical results on synthetic problems corroborate the theoretical insights. However, the paper does not sufficiently address how this framework applies to real-world scenarios where fidelity parameters ζ(m) might be unknown or difficult to estimate accurately, which is a critical limitation for practical adoption.

**Arguments for acceptance:** The paper presents a novel problem formulation with a well-motivated algorithm, strong theoretical guarantees, and clear empirical validation. The contribution advances the state of the art in bandit theory by providing the first formal regret analysis for the multi-fidelity bandit problem. The technical quality of the main paper is high, and the results have clear potential impact in applications where high-fidelity evaluations are expensive.

**Arguments against acceptance:** The supplementary materials contain numerous typos and errors that need correction before publication. The paper does not sufficiently address practical limitations, particularly the sensitivity to estimation errors in fidelity parameters ζ(m). The use of asymptotic "up-to-constant" notation obscures the practical impact of algorithm parameters like ρ.

**Overall recommendation:** This is a strong paper with a significant theoretical contribution that advances the state of the art in bandit theory. The main paper is well-written and the theoretical analysis is sound. The main issues are with the supplementary materials (typos/errors) and the lack of discussion regarding practical limitations. With minor revisions to correct the typos in the appendix and add a brief discussion of practical limitations and sensitivity to ζ(m) estimation, this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
