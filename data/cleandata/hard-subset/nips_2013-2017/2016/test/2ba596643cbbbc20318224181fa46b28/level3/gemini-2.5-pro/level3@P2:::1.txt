This paper introduces the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit problem, a variant of the classical stochastic K-armed bandit setting. In this formulation, each of the K arms can be evaluated at one of M "fidelity" levels. Lower fidelities are cheaper to sample but provide more biased estimates of the true (highest fidelity) reward, while higher fidelities are more expensive but more accurate. The bias of each fidelity level `m` is assumed to be bounded by a known value `ζ(m)`. The paper proposes a novel cost-based regret metric for a fixed capital budget `Λ` and introduces the Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB) algorithm. The core idea of MF-UCB is to leverage cheap, low-fidelity samples to quickly identify and eliminate clearly suboptimal arms, thereby saving the expensive, high-fidelity evaluations for a smaller set of promising candidates. The authors provide a theoretical analysis of MF-UCB, deriving an upper bound on its regret and comparing it to a new lower bound for this problem setting. This work extends the rich literature on UCB-style algorithms to a novel setting that captures the trade-off between evaluation cost and accuracy, a scenario relevant to hyperparameter optimization, simulation-based design, and online advertising.

### Quality and Technical Soundness

The paper appears to be technically solid. It presents a complete theoretical analysis, including the proposal of a new algorithm, an upper regret bound, and a problem-specific lower bound. The proofs seem plausible, following the structure of standard bandit analysis but adapted for the multi-fidelity setting. However, there are some concerns regarding the assumptions and the gap in the theoretical results:

1.  **Assumption 1:** This assumption, which requires `ζ(m)` to decay rapidly (e.g., exponentially for sub-Gaussian rewards), feels quite strong and is not well-motivated. It is unclear how often this condition would hold in practice. The paper's strength would be significantly improved by discussing the necessity of this assumption. Is it a technical convenience for the proof, or does the algorithm's performance fundamentally degrade without it? If the provided fidelities do not satisfy this assumption, could a practitioner simply select a subsequence of fidelities that do? Guidance on this would be valuable.

2.  **Lower Bound Gap:** The authors are upfront about the gap between their upper and lower bounds for arms in the `K^(m)7` sets. This is commendable. However, this gap indicates that the proposed strategy may not be optimal for arms that are "deceptively" good at lower fidelities. A more detailed discussion of why the current strategy fails here and potential ideas for closing this gap would strengthen the paper.

### Clarity

The paper's primary weakness is its lack of clarity in explaining the main results and assumptions. While the high-level intuition is clear, the details are difficult to parse, which hinders a full appreciation of the contribution.

1.  **Interpretation of Theorem 2:** The regret bound in Theorem 2 is complex and its implications are not immediately obvious. The comparison with the standard UCB regret is qualitative ("MF-UCB outperforms UCB by a factor of `λ(JkK)/λ(M)` on arm k"). To make the benefit concrete, it would be extremely helpful to include a simplified corollary or a worked example. For instance, under what conditions on the problem parameters (`Δ`, `λ`, `ζ`) does MF-UCB provide, say, a constant factor or logarithmic factor improvement over UCB? The reliance on the recursively defined sets `K(m)` makes the result particularly hard to interpret without more guidance.

2.  **Motivation for Regret Formulation:** The regret definition in Equation (1) is non-standard. Penalizing cost multiplicatively within the reward term (`λ(mt)qt`) and using an oracle of `Λµ*` (which is likely unachievable) is an unusual choice. A more common approach in budgeted settings is to define regret relative to the best performance achievable within the same budget, or to treat cost as an additive penalty. The paper should provide a stronger justification for this specific formulation and perhaps discuss how the analysis might change under alternative, more standard definitions of regret.

### Originality and Significance

The paper's main strength lies in its originality and the potential significance of the problem it addresses.

1.  **Novel Problem Formulation:** The multi-fidelity bandit problem is a novel and compelling extension of the classical MAB framework. It formalizes a practical trade-off that researchers and practitioners face in numerous domains where cheap approximations or simulations are available (e.g., tuning machine learning models on data subsets, robotics simulations vs. real-world experiments, early-stage clinical trials).

2.  **Intuitive and Practical Algorithm:** The core idea of MF-UCB is intuitive and sensible. Using cheap evaluations for broad exploration and expensive ones for fine-grained exploitation is a powerful heuristic, and this paper provides a principled algorithmic instantiation of it.

However, the significance is somewhat limited by the aforementioned concerns about the generality of the assumptions and the problem formulation. The reliance on the online advertising example is helpful, but broadening the motivation to other domains could better illustrate the general applicability of the chosen reward and cost model.

### Summary of Strengths and Weaknesses

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Introduces and formalizes a novel, interesting, and practically relevant bandit problem.
*   Presents a complete piece of work, including a new algorithm (MF-UCB), a regret upper bound, a lower bound, and simulations.
*   The core algorithmic idea of progressively filtering arms using increasing fidelities is elegant and intuitive.

**Arguments for Rejection/Revision:**
*   The paper suffers from significant clarity issues, particularly in the statement and interpretation of the main regret bound (Theorem 2).
*   Key components of the problem setup, including the regret definition and the strong Assumption 1 on fidelity quality, are not sufficiently motivated, raising questions about the generality of the results.
*   A theoretical gap remains between the upper and lower bounds for a specific class of arms.

In conclusion, this paper tackles an important and novel problem with a technically sound approach. However, its current presentation makes the key contributions difficult to fully assess and appreciate. The paper would be much stronger if the authors significantly improve the clarity of their main results and provide better motivation for their modeling choices and assumptions.

**Minor Issues:**
*   Line 56: The term "near-optimal" should be used carefully, especially given the acknowledged gap in the bounds.
*   Line 126: There is an unnecessary period before "for all m < M".
*   Line 179: Standard set notation would use curly braces `{k}` instead of `[[k]]`.
