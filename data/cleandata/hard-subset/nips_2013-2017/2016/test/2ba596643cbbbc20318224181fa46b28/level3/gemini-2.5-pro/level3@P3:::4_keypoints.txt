- Proposes a novel Multi-Fidelity Multi-Armed Bandit (MF-MAB) framework where arms can be evaluated at multiple fidelities that are cheaper but biased approximations of the highest-fidelity reward.
- Introduces MF-UCB, an adaptive algorithm that leverages lower fidelities to reduce cumulative regret.
- Provides theoretical contributions: a regret upper bound showing improvements when low fidelities are informative and cheap, and a corresponding lower bound.
- Empirical validation on synthetic problems demonstrates MF-UCB outperforms a naive high-fidelity-only UCB baseline.
- Central weakness: the regret definition targets the best arm at highest fidelity and ignores evaluation costs, yielding a cost-insensitive objective.
- The formulation decouples reward from cost, potentially penalizing practically preferable cheap, accurate low-fidelity arms.
- Clarity and notation are poor: dense, forward-referenced variables and scattered definitions hinder readability and accessibility.
- Motivating examples (e.g., online advertising) conflict with the paper’s i.i.d. reward assumptions, weakening practical motivation.
- Suggests reframing as a best-arm identification / cost-complexity problem (minimize budget to find best arm with probability 1−δ) as a more natural objective.
- Overall: idea is valuable and impactful but requires major revisions to formulation, clarity, and motivation before publication.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, practically relevant framework; new algorithm with full theoretical analysis; promising empirical results.
- Arguments for rejection: questionable cost-insensitive regret formulation, poor clarity/notation, mismatched motivating examples, and possibly a better-suited problem formulation.
