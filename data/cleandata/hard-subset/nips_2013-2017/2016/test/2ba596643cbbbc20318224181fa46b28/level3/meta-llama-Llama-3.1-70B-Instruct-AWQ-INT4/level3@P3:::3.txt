This paper introduces a novel multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit model, where each arm has multiple fidelities with varying costs and biases. The authors propose a UCB-based algorithm, MF-UCB, that adapts to the sequence of available approximations and costs, achieving better regret than naive strategies that ignore the approximations. The paper also provides a lower bound and shows that MF-UCB is nearly optimal under certain conditions.

The main contribution of this paper is the problem formulation, which is a significant departure from the classical K-armed bandit. The authors' use of multiple fidelities to model cheaper approximations to a desired experiment is a valuable contribution to the field. The MF-UCB algorithm is a natural extension of the classical UCB algorithm, and its performance is well-analyzed in the paper.

The paper's strengths include its clear and concise writing style, making it easy to follow the authors' arguments. The use of simulations to demonstrate the empirical improvement of MF-UCB over UCB is also a strong point. The authors' discussion of the limitations of their model and the potential for future work is also appreciated.

However, there are some weaknesses in the paper. The theoretical regret bounds are indeed messy and hard to interpret, which may limit the paper's impact. Additionally, the reviewer questions the modeling of low fidelity as increased bias rather than increased variance, which is a valid concern. Some of the motivating examples, such as short ad displays, are not entirely convincing, and the regret objective is unclear for certain applications.

The link between the model and the motivating applications is weak, but not fatal. The main novelty of the paper is the problem formulation, and the algorithm itself uses standard optimism-in-the-face-of-uncertainty ideas. The simulation parameter choices in Appendix C lack justification, which may affect the reported 3-4x improvement.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of their algorithm. However, the claims could be better supported by more extensive experimental results. The paper is a complete piece of work, but the authors could have explored more variations of their algorithm and provided more insights into its behavior.

The paper's clarity is excellent, making it easy for readers to understand the authors' ideas. The organization is also well-structured, and the authors provide enough information for expert readers to reproduce their results.

The originality of the paper is high, as it introduces a new problem formulation and a novel algorithm. The authors adequately reference related work and clearly explain how their work differs from previous contributions.

The significance of the paper is moderate, as it addresses a specific problem in the multi-armed bandit setting. While the results are important, they may not have a broad impact on the field. However, the paper does advance the state of the art in a demonstrable way and provides a unique theoretical and pragmatic approach to the problem.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper introduces a novel problem formulation that is relevant to the field.
* The authors propose a well-analyzed algorithm that achieves better regret than naive strategies.
* The paper provides a lower bound and shows that the algorithm is nearly optimal under certain conditions.
* The authors' use of simulations to demonstrate the empirical improvement of their algorithm is a strong point.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The theoretical regret bounds are messy and hard to interpret.
* The modeling of low fidelity as increased bias rather than increased variance is questionable.
* The link between the model and the motivating applications is weak.
* The simulation parameter choices lack justification, which may affect the reported improvement.

Overall, I recommend accepting this paper, but with some revisions to address the concerns mentioned above. The authors should provide more extensive experimental results, explore more variations of their algorithm, and provide more insights into its behavior. Additionally, they should clarify the modeling of low fidelity and provide more justification for the simulation parameter choices.
