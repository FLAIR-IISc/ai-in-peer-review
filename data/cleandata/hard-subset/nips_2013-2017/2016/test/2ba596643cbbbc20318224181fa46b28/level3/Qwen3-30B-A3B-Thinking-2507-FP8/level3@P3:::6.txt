

# Review: The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit

This paper introduces a novel multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit setting where each arm can be played at M different fidelities, with higher fidelities providing better reward approximations at higher cost. The authors formalize a new regret definition accounting for the cost of playing at different fidelities and propose MF-UCB, a new algorithm that adaptively uses lower fidelities to eliminate suboptimal arms before investing in higher fidelities. The work builds upon classic bandit literature (e.g., UCB algorithms) but extends it to a more practical setting where cheaper approximations to expensive experiments are available, with strong motivation from online advertising (where short displays approximate long-run clicks).

The paper's main strengths include its clear problem formalization, novel regret definition that properly accounts for cost, elegant algorithm design, and thorough theoretical analysis. The authors prove an upper bound on MF-UCB's regret that demonstrates it outperforms naive UCB strategies when lower fidelities are sufficiently cheaper and when the number of promising arms is small. They complement this with a lower bound showing MF-UCB is nearly optimal under certain conditions. The empirical validation on synthetic problems supports their theoretical claims.

The paper's most significant contribution is its formalization of the multi-fidelity bandit problem and its theoretical analysis of the trade-offs between fidelity levels. This addresses a gap in the literature where previous work on multi-fidelity methods (e.g., in optimization or robotics) did not formalize regret or analyze exploration-exploitation trade-offs.

However, the paper makes a critical assumption that the deviations ε^(m) (the maximum difference between mth-fidelity mean and highest-fidelity mean) are known. This is a major practical limitation, as in real applications these values would need to be estimated, and the paper does not address how this estimation would be done or how the algorithm would behave without this knowledge. The authors acknowledge this limitation but do not provide a compelling argument for why this assumption is reasonable or how it could be relaxed. This is a significant concern that needs to be addressed for the work to have practical impact.

The paper also contains several minor technical errors that need correction (as noted in the key points):
- Lines 326, 357, 386: "arm k ∈ H^(m)" should be "K" instead of "H"
- Line 343: "β_{k*,t} ≤ s" should be "s = μ_*"
- Line 345: γ should be γ^(m)
- Lines 346-347: The "≤" should be "≥"
- Line 384: Summation index should be over arms "below" K^(m), not "above"
- Line 407: δ^(m) should be δ^(M), and "μ_* κ λ^(m)" should be "κ λ^(m)"
- Lines 431 and 440/Eq. (17): "for l ≥ m" should be "for l ≥ p"
- Line 450: P(A_{n,3}) should be P(A_{n,2})

These are straightforward to fix and do not undermine the core contributions, but they do affect the paper's technical accuracy.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound with well-supported claims, though the known ε^(m) assumption weakens its practical applicability. The clarity is excellent overall, with clear explanations of the problem, algorithm, and analysis. The originality is strong, as the paper introduces a novel setting that extends the bandit framework in a meaningful way. The significance is high, with clear applications in online advertising, algorithm selection, and other domains where cheaper approximations to expensive experiments are available.

The major concern regarding the assumption of known ε^(m) is significant but not fatal to the paper's theoretical contribution. The authors have made a valuable contribution to the bandit literature by formalizing this setting and providing a rigorous analysis. However, this assumption needs to be addressed in future work or the paper should acknowledge its limitation more clearly.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to the multi-armed bandit literature. While the assumption of known ε^(m) is a notable limitation, the paper's theoretical insights and empirical validation are compelling. With the minor technical corrections addressed and a more thorough discussion of the ε^(m) assumption, this would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.

Recommendation: Accept (with minor revisions to address the technical errors and strengthen the discussion of the ε^(m) assumption)
