

# Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

This paper introduces a novel variant of the multi-armed bandit problem where each arm can be evaluated at multiple fidelities, with lower fidelities providing cheaper but biased approximations of the highest fidelity reward. The authors formalize this problem, propose the MF-UCB algorithm, and provide theoretical guarantees on its performance. The work extends the classical bandit literature by incorporating a cost structure across different fidelity levels, which is particularly relevant for applications like online advertising (where short displays approximate long-run click-through rates) and algorithm selection (where cheaper approximations of algorithm performance are available).

The paper builds on the classic multi-armed bandit work of Robbins [11], Lai and Robbins [9], and Auer et al. [3] while extending it to the multi-fidelity setting. It relates to recent work on budgeted bandits [13, 15] and multi-fidelity optimization [7, 10], but makes a clear distinction by formalizing regret in the multi-fidelity context and providing a principled algorithm that adapts to the available fidelities.

**Strengths:**
1. **Technical Soundness & Novelty:** The formalization of the multi-fidelity bandit problem is clear and well-motivated. The MF-UCB algorithm is a natural extension of UCB to this setting, and the theoretical analysis (including the regret bound and lower bound) is thorough and rigorous. The paper demonstrates that MF-UCB is nearly optimal under certain conditions, which is a significant theoretical contribution.

2. **Practical Relevance:** The paper provides concrete, realistic examples of the multi-fidelity setting (online advertising, algorithm selection) that clearly illustrate the practical value of the approach. The empirical results on synthetic data convincingly show that MF-UCB outperforms standard UCB in terms of regret, validating the theoretical insights.

3. **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, with clear notation and logical organization. The figures help illustrate key concepts, such as the partitioning of arms across fidelities. The theoretical results are presented in a way that makes the intuition behind the algorithm accessible.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Critical Assumption on ζ^(m):** A major concern is the assumption that the authors know ζ^(m) (the deviation of the m-th fidelity mean from the highest fidelity mean) for all m. The paper does not address how these values would be obtained in practice, and it's unclear whether this assumption can be relaxed. In real-world applications, these values would likely need to be estimated, which would significantly impact the algorithm's performance and theoretical guarantees. This is a substantial limitation that needs to be addressed.

2. **Minor Typographical Errors:** The paper contains several minor errors in notation that could confuse readers (as noted in the key points: lines 326, 357, 386; line 343; line 345; lines 346-347; line 384; line 407; lines 431, 440; line 450). While these are not major issues, they do affect the paper's professionalism and readability.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- The problem formulation is novel and addresses a meaningful gap in the bandit literature.
- The theoretical analysis is strong, with a tight regret bound and matching lower bound.
- The empirical results support the theoretical claims.
- The approach has clear practical value in applications where high-fidelity evaluations are expensive.

*Con:*
- The assumption of known ζ^(m) is problematic for real-world deployment and isn't adequately addressed.
- The paper doesn't explore how to estimate ζ^(m) in practice, which is a critical limitation.
- The empirical validation is limited to synthetic data; more realistic experiments would strengthen the case.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a clear theoretical contribution to the bandit literature. The multi-fidelity bandit problem is well-motivated, and the MF-UCB algorithm provides a principled way to trade off between cost and accuracy. The theoretical analysis is thorough and demonstrates the algorithm's near-optimality. However, the assumption of known ζ^(m) is a significant limitation that needs to be addressed for the work to be truly impactful.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, particularly to address the ζ^(m) assumption. The authors should discuss how ζ^(m) could be estimated in practice (e.g., through initial exploration) and analyze the impact of estimation error on the algorithm's performance. This would significantly strengthen the paper's practical relevance and address the most critical weakness.

The paper meets all four NIPS criteria: it's technically sound (Quality), well-written (Clarity), introduces a novel problem setting (Originality), and addresses a practically important problem with significant potential impact (Significance). The minor typographical errors noted above should be fixed in the camera-ready version.
