- Introduces a multi-fidelity bandit framework where each arm can be sampled at multiple fidelities with different costs and biased estimates of the highest fidelity.  
- Proposes MF-UCB, an algorithm that adaptively leverages lower-fidelity approximations to reduce regret versus standard UCB.  
- Provides theoretical guarantees: a regret bound and a lower bound showing near-optimality under certain conditions.  
- Extends classical bandit literature and offers the first formal regret analysis for multi-fidelity bandit settings.  
- Synthetic experiments show MF-UCB outperforms UCB across multiple settings, supporting theoretical claims.  
- Regret formulation is questionable: defines regret by highest mean regardless of cost, ignoring reward/cost trade-offs.  
- Treating the best arm as highest mean (not best reward-per-cost) weakens the theoretical and practical contribution.  
- Advertising example is misleading and introduces irrelevant concepts (display time correlations, non-stationarity).  
- Notation is inconsistent and poorly organized (variables defined after use), harming readability.  
- Writing is unwieldy in places; regret formulation and proofs are more complex than necessary.  
- Experiments are limited to synthetic problems and lack real-world validation.  
- Practical significance would be improved by focusing on cost-complexity/sample-complexity for finding the best arm under budget constraints.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel framework, solid theoretical results, and supportive synthetic experiments.  
- Arguments against acceptance: flawed regret objective, confusing presentation, misleading example, and limited empirical validation.  
- Recommendation: revise regret formulation to account for cost, streamline exposition and notation, and provide clearer, aligned examples before publication.
