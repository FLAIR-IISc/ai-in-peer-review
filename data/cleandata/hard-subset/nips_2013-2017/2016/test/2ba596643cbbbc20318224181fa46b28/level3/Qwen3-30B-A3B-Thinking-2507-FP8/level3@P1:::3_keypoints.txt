- Introduces a multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit framework with cheaper, biased lower-fidelity observations and a cost-weighted regret metric.  
- Proposes MF-UCB, an algorithm extending UCB by widening confidence intervals to account for fidelity-induced bias.  
- Proves theoretical regret bounds showing MF-UCB can outperform standard UCB by leveraging cheaper approximations.  
- Main strength: novel problem formulation addressing observation cost and availability of cheaper, approximate measurements.  
- Theoretical analysis meaningfully characterizes how MF-UCB allocates observations, eliminating suboptimal arms at low fidelities and reserving high-fidelity evaluations for promising arms.  
- Empirical results: synthetic experiments show consistent improvements over UCB with lower absolute regret and better scaling.  
- Theorem 2 bounds are complex and hard to interpret, obscuring conditions where MF-UCB has clear advantages.  
- Claim of near-optimality (Theorem 4) is dubious because the gap between upper and lower bounds is non-trivial and conditions for near-optimality are unclear.  
- Modeling assumption of bounded bias for lower fidelities (|μ^(M)_k - μ^(m)_k| ≤ ζ^(m)) is questionable for many applications where variance, not bias, may dominate.  
- Motivating examples mismatch the model: online advertising may introduce variance rather than bias, and clinical trials may produce no reward at low fidelity.  
- Simulation results appear highly sensitive to chosen ζ^(m) values and lack justification in the main text, raising concerns about robustness.  
- Connection between the formal model and motivating applications is weak; some applications fit better (algorithm selection) than others (ad displays).  
- Algorithmic novelty is limited: MF-UCB is largely a straightforward UCB extension with fidelity bookkeeping; primary novelty lies in the problem formulation.  
- Overall judgment: reasonable contribution in formalizing a practical problem but insufficient theoretical clarity and empirical support; recommendation to reject.
