- Introduces a stochastic K-armed bandit with M fidelities per arm where lower fidelities are cheaper but provide biased approximations of the highest-fidelity reward.  
- Proposes MF-UCB, an optimism-based policy that widens confidence intervals to account for fidelity-induced bias and selects arm and fidelity via a thresholding rule.  
- Provides upper bounds on regret and a lower bound showing near-optimality under certain conditions.  
- Empirical simulations suggest MF-UCB can substantially outperform using only the highest fidelity (claimed 3–4× gains).  
- Novelty lies in the specific multi-fidelity-with-bias bandit formulation and its regret analysis, connecting to UCB, budgeted bandits, multi-fidelity optimization, and active learning.  
- Algorithmic design is principled and proofs appear careful, but the main regret bounds are difficult to parse and relate to instance properties.  
- Reviewers request cleaner special-case corollaries (e.g., M=2, sub-Gaussian) with explicit constants and thresholds to improve interpretability.  
- Modeling choice to encode low fidelities as bounded bias (not increased variance or mixtures) is only partially justified and may not fit all applications (advertising example unconvincing).  
- The regret objective (crediting only high-fidelity rewards) is application-dependent and needs stronger justification.  
- Empirical evaluation lacks sensitivity analysis and ablations for ζ(m), λ(m), and γ(m); improvements may depend on favorable parameter settings.  
- Paper is generally well written and organized, but exposition should sharpen the regret objective, role of Assumption 1, and practical meaning of ζ(m).  
- A running two-fidelity example and interpretable plots of instance-dependent terms would aid clarity.  
- Related work coverage is mostly adequate, but should better situate the contribution vis-à-vis biased/corrupted feedback bandits and side-observation models.  
- Potentially impactful if strengthened, but currently theoretical message is obscured by complexity and empirical evidence is not yet robust.  
- Pros: clear and timely formulation, principled MF-UCB, nontrivial analysis with lower bound, and practical potential.  
- Cons: bounds hard to interpret, modeling and regret choices weakly justified for some applications, and empirical evaluation lacks parameter sensitivity and strong baselines.  
- Overall recommendation: lean toward weak reject, conditional on revisions including clarifications, sensitivity studies, and simplified theoretical corollaries.
