

# Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

This paper introduces a novel variant of the stochastic K-armed bandit problem where each arm can be observed at multiple fidelities, each with different costs and accuracy levels. The authors formalize this as a multi-fidelity bandit problem, where lower fidelities provide cheaper but biased estimates of the highest fidelity reward. The paper's main contribution is the MF-UCB algorithm, which adapts the classical UCB approach to this setting, along with theoretical analysis showing its regret bounds are superior to standard UCB when lower fidelities are cheaper.

The work builds on the extensive literature on multi-armed bandits (e.g., [3, 9, 11]) and UCB methods [2, 3], but makes a significant extension to handle the multi-fidelity setting. The authors clearly distinguish their work from related approaches: while [7, 10] study multi-fidelity optimization, they don't formalize regret in bandit settings; [16] considers active learning with weak/strong labelers but not exploration-exploitation trade-offs; and [8] (by the same authors) analyzes multi-fidelity bandits with Gaussian process payoffs, which is a different setting.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors establish both an upper bound for MF-UCB and a matching lower bound, demonstrating that MF-UCB is nearly optimal under certain conditions. The regret analysis is thorough and well-motivated, with careful handling of the bias introduced by lower fidelities. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations (e.g., the gap between MF-UCB and the lower bound for certain arms), which shows intellectual honesty. The empirical results complement the theory, showing MF-UCB outperforms UCB in simulations across multiple synthetic problems.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem formulation is clear from the outset, with well-chosen notation and helpful illustrations (Figure 1). The algorithm description is precise and easy to follow. The theoretical results are presented with sufficient detail for experts to understand the analysis, and the connection between the theory and practical implications is well-explained. The writing is concise yet comprehensive.

**Originality:** The multi-fidelity bandit framework is genuinely novel within the NIPS context. While some related work exists on multi-fidelity methods in optimization, the paper's focus on the exploration-exploitation trade-off inherent to bandit problems represents a significant conceptual advance. The MF-UCB algorithm is a clever adaptation of UCB to this setting, and the theoretical analysis demonstrates meaningful improvements over standard approaches. The paper properly cites related work, including [8] (by the same authors), which is a different setting (Gaussian process payoffs).

**Significance:** The problem is well-motivated by practical applications like online advertising (where ads can be tested for shorter durations or narrower audiences at lower cost) and algorithm selection (where cheaper approximations of algorithm performance are available). The theoretical and empirical results demonstrate clear improvements over standard bandit methods when lower fidelities are available. The approach could be broadly applicable to any domain where cheap approximations of expensive experiments exist, making it a potentially impactful contribution to the field.

**Strengths:**
- Novel problem formulation with clear practical motivation
- Well-designed algorithm (MF-UCB) with strong theoretical guarantees
- Comprehensive analysis including upper and lower bounds
- Clear empirical validation across multiple synthetic problems
- Excellent writing and organization

**Weaknesses:**
- The gap between MF-UCB's performance and the lower bound for arms in K(m)7 could be addressed in future work (as the authors acknowledge)
- While the paper mentions applications like clinical trials and gold mining robots, it doesn't provide concrete case studies to demonstrate the method's utility in real-world settings
- The theoretical analysis assumes known ζ(m) and ψ(·), which might be restrictive in practice

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- The paper introduces a genuinely novel framework with strong theoretical backing
- The multi-fidelity bandit problem is well-motivated and addresses a practical gap
- MF-UCB achieves provable improvements over standard bandit methods
- The writing is exceptionally clear and the paper is well-structured
- The empirical results robustly support the theoretical claims

*Con:*
- The gap between MF-UCB and the lower bound for certain arms is slightly concerning
- The practical applicability, while well-motivated, lacks concrete case studies
- The assumption of known ζ(m) and ψ(·) may limit real-world applicability

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the bandit literature. The problem is well-motivated, the theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the empirical results support the claims. The paper is clearly written and well-organized, making it accessible to the NIPS audience. While there are minor limitations, they don't detract from the paper's strong technical contribution. I believe this paper is a good fit for NIPS 2016 and would be of interest to many researchers in machine learning and optimization. I recommend acceptance.
