

This paper introduces a multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit framework where each arm can be observed at multiple fidelities, with lower fidelities being cheaper but providing biased estimates of the highest fidelity outcome. The authors formalize the problem by introducing a cost-weighted regret metric that accounts for both the regret from suboptimal arm choices and the cost of using lower fidelities. They propose MF-UCB, a novel algorithm that adapts UCB principles to this setting by widening confidence intervals to account for fidelity-induced bias, and prove theoretical regret bounds demonstrating that MF-UCB can outperform standard UCB by leveraging cheaper approximations.

The paper's main strength lies in its novel problem formulation, which addresses a practical consideration often overlooked in bandit literature: the cost of observations and the availability of cheaper, approximate measurements. This is particularly relevant for applications like online advertising (where shorter ad displays provide cheaper but biased estimates of long-term performance) and algorithm selection (where cheaper, less accurate performance estimates can be obtained). The theoretical analysis provides a meaningful characterization of how MF-UCB should allocate observations across fidelities, showing it effectively eliminates suboptimal arms at lower fidelities while reserving expensive high-fidelity evaluations for promising candidates. The empirical results demonstrate consistent improvements over standard UCB in synthetic experiments, with the regret curves showing both lower absolute regret and better scaling.

However, several weaknesses undermine the paper's contribution. The theoretical regret bounds (Theorem 2) are complex and difficult to interpret, making it challenging to understand the precise conditions under which MF-UCB provides significant advantages. The paper claims MF-UCB "nearly" matches a lower bound (Theorem 4), but the gap between the upper and lower bounds is non-trivial and the conditions for near-optimality are not clearly articulated. More fundamentally, the modeling choice of bounded bias for lower fidelities (|μ^(M)_k - μ^(m)_k| ≤ ζ^(m)) is questionable for many motivating examples. For instance, in the online advertising example, displaying an ad for a shorter duration might introduce increased variance rather than systematic bias, and the paper fails to justify why bias is the appropriate model. The clinical trials example in Section 7 further highlights this inconsistency, as the paper acknowledges that low-fidelity "treatments" might not even yield rewards.

The simulation results are also problematic. The reported 3-4× improvement over UCB (Figure 2) appears highly sensitive to parameter choices, particularly the ζ^(m) values that define the bias. The paper provides no justification for these choices in the main text (only in Appendix C), making it unclear whether these results reflect typical performance or are artifacts of specific parameter settings. The connection between the formal model and the motivating applications is weak, with some examples (like algorithm selection) fitting the bias model better than others (like short ad displays).

While the problem formulation represents a meaningful contribution to the bandit literature, the algorithm itself (MF-UCB) is largely a straightforward extension of standard UCB principles with additional bookkeeping for fidelity levels. The core insight—using cheaper observations to eliminate arms before committing to expensive evaluations—is not novel in the broader optimization literature (e.g., multi-fidelity optimization in Huang et al. [10]). The main novelty is thus primarily the problem formulation rather than the algorithmic contribution.

Pro: Novel problem formulation addressing practical cost-quality trade-offs in bandit settings; meaningful theoretical analysis showing how MF-UCB should allocate observations across fidelities; empirical results demonstrating consistent improvements over UCB in simulations.

Con: Theoretical bounds are complex and difficult to interpret; questionable modeling assumption of bounded bias rather than increased variance; weak connection between formal model and motivating applications; simulation results sensitive to parameter choices without justification.

Overall, the paper makes a reasonable contribution to the bandit literature by formalizing a practically relevant problem, but the theoretical analysis is not sufficiently compelling, and the empirical results are not convincingly supported. The main novelty is the problem formulation rather than the algorithm, and the paper would be significantly stronger with a clearer justification for the bias model and more robust experimental validation. I recommend rejection due to insufficient theoretical and empirical support for the claims.
