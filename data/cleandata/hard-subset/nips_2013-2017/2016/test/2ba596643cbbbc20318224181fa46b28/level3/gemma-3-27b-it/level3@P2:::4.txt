This paper introduces the Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit problem, a compelling extension of the classical bandit setting where obtaining rewards from each arm incurs a cost that can be reduced by using cheaper, albeit noisier, approximations (fidelities). The authors propose MF-UCB, an algorithm adapting the Upper Confidence Bound approach to this new setting, and provide theoretical guarantees on its performance, including a near-optimality result. The work is motivated by applications like online advertising and algorithm selection, where cheaper evaluations can guide exploration before committing to expensive, high-fidelity experiments.

The core idea of leveraging lower-fidelity samples to efficiently prune suboptimal arms and focus high-fidelity evaluations on promising candidates is sound and intuitively appealing. The formalization of the multi-fidelity bandit problem, with associated costs and approximation quality (ζ(m)), is a valuable contribution. The theoretical analysis, presenting regret bounds and a matching lower bound, demonstrates a strong technical foundation. Empirical evaluation on synthetic problems supports the algorithm's effectiveness compared to the standard UCB approach.

However, despite these strengths, I believe the paper requires significant revisions before it would be suitable for publication at NIPS. My primary concerns relate to clarity, motivation, and the nuanced interpretation of regret in this multi-fidelity setting.

Firstly, the writing is often unwieldy and lacks conciseness. The notation is particularly problematic. For example, the variable ‘s’ is defined after its first use and then appears infrequently, creating confusion. More generally, the presentation of mathematical notation could be streamlined for better readability.  A more rigorous editing pass is crucial.

Secondly, the motivating example of online advertising feels somewhat misleading. The authors discuss correlations in display time and potential non-stationarity, which are not directly addressed within the mathematical framework of the paper. The core problem is presented as one of biased estimates, but the discussion of user behavior changes introduces complexities not captured in the formal model. This disconnect detracts from the clarity of the central contribution.

A more significant issue lies in the definition and interpretation of regret. The paper adopts a rather unconventional notion of regret that prioritizes maximizing the absolute reward (µ?) without explicit consideration of the cost of obtaining that reward. In a scenario with varying fidelity costs, a truly optimal policy should focus on maximizing *reward per unit cost*. The current framework appears to treat the highest-mean arm as the optimal choice regardless of its associated cost, which feels counterintuitive and potentially obscures the benefits of lower-fidelity sampling.  The choice of qt = µ(M)It, while motivated, feels arbitrary and doesn’t inherently reflect a cost-aware objective.

Furthermore, the analysis feels somewhat divorced from practical considerations. While the theoretical bounds are valuable, a more compelling evaluation would focus on *sample complexity* – specifically, how many samples (and at which fidelities) are required to identify the best arm with high probability under a given budget constraint.  This type of analysis would be directly relevant to real-world testing scenarios where resources (e.g., advertising spend) are limited. Analyzing the cost-complexity trade-off would arguably provide a stronger justification for the proposed approach.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and well-defined problem formulation.
*   Sound theoretical analysis with regret bounds and a matching lower bound.
*   Empirical results demonstrating performance improvement over UCB.
*   Potential impact on applications involving costly evaluations.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Clarity and readability issues due to cumbersome writing and notation.
*   Misleading motivation and disconnect between the advertising example and the mathematical framework.
*   Questionable definition of regret that doesn't explicitly incorporate cost.
*   Lack of a sample-complexity analysis relevant to practical budget constraints.
*   The writing style needs significant improvement for clarity and conciseness.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as a **weak reject**. The ideas presented are interesting and potentially valuable, but the current presentation suffers from clarity issues, a somewhat questionable definition of regret, and a lack of focus on practical considerations like sample complexity. Addressing these concerns through substantial revisions would significantly strengthen the paper and make it a more compelling contribution to the NIPS community. I encourage the authors to revise and resubmit, focusing on clarifying the motivation, refining the regret definition, and incorporating a cost-complexity analysis.
