

This paper introduces a multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit model where each arm can be observed at multiple fidelities (M total), with lower fidelities being cheaper but introducing bounded bias rather than increased variance in estimates. The authors propose MF-UCB, a UCB-based algorithm that widens confidence intervals to account for fidelity-induced bias, and provide theoretical regret bounds and empirical validation.

The main contribution is a novel problem formulation extending classical bandit theory to incorporate multiple fidelity levels, which is well-motivated for applications like algorithm selection where cheaper approximations to performance exist. The theoretical analysis demonstrates that MF-UCB can achieve better regret than naive UCB strategies that only use the highest fidelity, particularly when the number of "good" arms (Kg) is much smaller than K and lower fidelity costs are significantly cheaper. The empirical results consistently show MF-UCB outperforming baseline UCB in simulations.

However, several weaknesses undermine the paper's impact. The theoretical regret bounds are complex and difficult to interpret, weakening the theoretical contribution. The modeling choice of bias rather than variance for lower fidelities is questionable - while algorithm selection partially justifies bias, examples like short ad displays seem better modeled as increased variance. The connection between the model and motivating applications is inconsistent, with some examples (e.g., short ad displays) poorly justified. Additionally, simulation parameter choices (Appendix C) lack justification, making the reported 3-4× improvement suspect as it may reflect specific parameter selections rather than general performance.

While the problem formulation is original and the framework is potentially valuable, the algorithm itself builds on standard UCB principles rather than introducing novel techniques. The paper would be stronger with clearer application examples, better justification for the bias model, and more transparent parameter choices. The main novelty lies in the problem formulation rather than the algorithmic approach.

Overall, the paper presents a technically sound extension to bandit theory with practical potential, but the theoretical contribution is limited by messy bounds and the weak application justification. The empirical results are promising but require more careful parameter justification to be fully convincing. The work makes a modest contribution to the field but falls short of significant advancement.
