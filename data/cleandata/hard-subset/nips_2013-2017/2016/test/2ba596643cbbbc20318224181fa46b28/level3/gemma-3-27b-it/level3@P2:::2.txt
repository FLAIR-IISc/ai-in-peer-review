This paper introduces a novel multi-armed bandit (MAB) setting where observing the outcome of each arm is expensive, but cheaper, albeit biased, approximations are available – a “multi-fidelity” bandit. The authors formalize this scenario and propose a new algorithm, Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB), along with theoretical guarantees demonstrating near-optimality. This work builds upon the established UCB framework for bandits, adapting it to exploit the trade-off between cost and accuracy offered by the multiple fidelities.

The core idea is intuitive: use lower fidelities to quickly eliminate suboptimal arms and reserve expensive, high-fidelity experiments for promising candidates. The paper clearly articulates this intuition and presents a well-defined cost-weighted notion of regret suitable for this setting. The theoretical analysis, including an upper bound on the regret of MF-UCB and a matching lower bound, is a significant contribution and demonstrates a strong understanding of the problem. The connection to existing work on bandits and multi-fidelity optimization is well-established.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The multi-fidelity bandit setting is a valuable generalization of the classical MAB problem, with clear relevance to practical applications like online advertising, algorithm selection, and robotics.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The analysis provides strong theoretical guarantees on the performance of MF-UCB, demonstrating its near-optimality under certain conditions. The derivation of both upper and lower bounds strengthens the validity of the results.
* **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the problem formulation, the proposed algorithm, and the theoretical analysis. The motivation is easy to follow, and the key ideas are presented concisely.
* **Significance:** The problem addressed is important, and the proposed solution has the potential to be useful in a variety of applications where acquiring accurate measurements is costly.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Practical Limitations:** The paper lacks a detailed discussion of the practical challenges in estimating the bias (ζ(m)) associated with each fidelity. In real-world scenarios, accurately quantifying this bias could be difficult. Further discussion of mitigation strategies, or robustness to inaccurate ζ(m) estimates, would be beneficial.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance of MF-UCB likely depends on the choice of parameters, such as ρ. The paper does not provide practical guidance on how to tune these parameters for different problem instances.
* **Appendix Errors:** The supplementary material (proofs) contains numerous typos and minor errors, some of which could confuse readers. While not impacting the overall correctness of the results, addressing these errors would significantly improve the readability and trustworthiness of the paper. For example, on page 10, equation (8) contains a typo ("k/K"), and Lemma 5 appears mislabeled as "Theorem" on page 17. (A full list of observed errors is included in the provided key points).
* **Constant Factors:** The use of "up-to-a-multiplicative-constant" notation obscures the dependence of the regret bounds on algorithm parameters (e.g., ρ) and can hinder a clear understanding of the practical performance implications. Presenting more precise inequalities would improve clarity.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and well-motivated problem with a theoretically sound solution.
* The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides strong guarantees on the performance of the proposed algorithm.
* The clarity of the presentation makes the paper accessible to a broad audience.
* The contribution is significant and has the potential to advance the state-of-the-art in bandit optimization.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of discussion regarding practical limitations, particularly the estimation of fidelity biases, weakens the paper's overall impact.
* The numerous errors in the appendix detract from the paper's credibility.
* The use of imprecise notation (e.g., "up-to-a-constant") hinders a complete understanding of the algorithm’s performance characteristics.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution to the field of bandit optimization. The introduction of the multi-fidelity bandit setting is a valuable generalization of the classical MAB problem, and the theoretical analysis provides compelling evidence of the algorithm's effectiveness. While the practical limitations and errors in the appendix are concerns, they do not overshadow the significant theoretical contributions of the work.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend accepting this paper, contingent on the authors addressing the errors in the appendix and providing a more detailed discussion of the practical challenges and limitations of the proposed approach. Specifically, addressing the estimation of ζ(m) and guidance on parameter tuning (ρ) would be helpful.
