This paper introduces a novel multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit setting where obtaining precise reward estimates is costly, but cheaper, less accurate approximations are available. The motivation, drawing parallels to online advertising and algorithm selection, is well-articulated and provides a compelling justification for the work. The authors propose MF-UCB, a UCB-based algorithm adapted for this setting, and provide a theoretical analysis including a regret lower bound. Empirical validation through simulation further supports their claims. Overall, this is a strong contribution that addresses a relevant and under-explored problem.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The multi-fidelity bandit setting is a genuinely new contribution. While bandit problems and multi-fidelity optimization have been studied independently, the combination and formalization presented here are novel.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for MF-UCB, including a regret bound and a matching lower bound, demonstrating near-optimality under certain conditions. This is a significant strength, especially in the bandit literature.
* **Practical Relevance:** The problem is motivated by practical applications, like online advertising, making the work relevant to a broad audience. The described scenarios are realistic and clearly illustrate the potential benefits of the proposed approach.
* **Clear Algorithm:** The MF-UCB algorithm is relatively straightforward to understand and implement, building upon the well-established UCB framework.
* **Empirical Validation:** Simulations show that MF-UCB outperforms standard UCB, providing further evidence of its effectiveness.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Strong Assumption:** The primary weakness lies in the assumption that the deviation (ζ^(m)) between the mth-fidelity mean and the highest-fidelity mean is *known*. This is a strong assumption that limits the practical applicability of the algorithm. Obtaining these values in real-world scenarios could be challenging or even impossible. Relaxing this assumption would significantly strengthen the paper.
* **Assumption 1 Regularity:** While not critical, the necessity of Assumption 1 on the ζ(m) decay rate feels somewhat restrictive. A discussion of the sensitivity of the results to violations of this assumption would be helpful.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with a rigorous analysis and clear presentation of results. The theoretical guarantees are significant. While the assumption about known ζ^(m) detracts from its practical applicability, the completeness of the work, including the lower bound, is commendable.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, and the algorithm is presented in a straightforward manner. The notation is mostly consistent, although some minor inconsistencies (addressed above) exist.

**Originality:** The multi-fidelity bandit setting itself is highly original. While the algorithm builds upon existing UCB techniques, adapting them to this new setting requires non-trivial modifications and analysis.

**Significance:** This work has the potential to be significant, particularly in applications where obtaining accurate feedback is expensive or time-consuming. The framework provides a principled approach to balancing exploration and exploitation in such scenarios, and the theoretical results offer valuable insights into the performance of multi-fidelity bandit algorithms.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Novel and theoretically sound approach to a relevant problem.
* Strong theoretical guarantees (regret bound and lower bound).
* Clear and well-motivated algorithm.
* Positive empirical results.
* Addresses an important gap in the bandit literature.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The strong assumption of known ζ^(m) limits practical applicability. This is the primary concern.
* Assumption 1 on zeta decay might be restrictive.




**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the limitation regarding the assumption of known ζ^(m), I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field. The novelty of the problem, the rigor of the analysis, and the potential practical impact outweigh the drawbacks. I recommend **acceptance** with a request that the authors address the limitations of the assumption in the discussion and potentially explore avenues for relaxing it in future work.
