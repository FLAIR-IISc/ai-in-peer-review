

# Review: The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit

This paper introduces a novel multi-armed bandit framework where each arm can be pulled at multiple fidelities (levels of accuracy/cost), with higher fidelity providing more accurate but more expensive reward estimates. The authors formalize a new regret measure that accounts for both the regret and the cost of fidelity used (cost × pseudo-regret), and propose the MF-UCB algorithm that adaptively switches between fidelities to minimize this cost-weighted regret. The paper demonstrates that MF-UCB can significantly outperform standard UCB algorithms by using lower-fidelity, cheaper pulls to eliminate suboptimal arms early, reserving higher-fidelity pulls for promising candidates.

The paper builds on classical multi-armed bandit work [9, 12] and upper confidence bound techniques [3], but introduces a fundamentally new setting where the cost of exploration varies with fidelity. It relates well to recent work on budgeted bandits [13, 15] and multi-fidelity optimization [7, 10], but distinguishes itself by formally analyzing regret in the multi-fidelity setting with both theoretical and empirical results.

**Strengths:**

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution with a novel problem formulation and analysis. The main paper is clear and well-written, making the problem and solution accessible. The theoretical analysis is solid, with tight upper and lower bounds on regret that demonstrate MF-UCB's near-optimality under certain conditions. The empirical results complement the theory by showing MF-UCB outperforms UCB in synthetic experiments.

**Weaknesses:**

The most significant issue is the poor quality of the supplementary materials. The appendix contains numerous typos and mathematical errors that would confuse readers and undermine confidence in the proofs. Specifically:

- Page 10, eq. (8): typo in k/K
- Page 10, Section A1: H^(m) should be K^(m)
- Page 10, line 328: ν/(rho-2) should be ν/2
- Page 11, after line 343: s should be μ
- Page 11, eq. (10): γ should be γ^(m)
- Page 11, eq. (11): extra comma
- Page 12, line 375: "Lemma" should be "Theorem"
- Page 12, last line & page 13 first equation: missing parentheses
- Page 13, definition of x_n: K^2 should be K
- Page 13, definition of y_n: exponent ^{1/2} should be ^{1/(ρ-2)}
- Page 13, lines 388-389: logical implication sequence appears incorrect
- Page 13, eq. (15): x → x_{n,δ}
- Page 14, line 406: N should be log(N)
- Page 14, equation after line 406: inequality direction wrong (N < 2n → N > 2n)
- Page 14, equation before line 408: missing μ*, +1, and κ_k
- Page 15: variables p and m interchanged
- Page 15, line 432: opposite inequality should be verified
- Page 15, equation after line 445: missing tildes (˜)
- Page 15, line 448: union (∪) should be intersection (∩)
- Page 15, equation after line 448: superscript ^(l) dropped
- Page 16, equation after line 451: P() missing rightmost term
- Page 17, line 454: "theorem" should be "lemma"
- Section C: "distribtuions" should be "distributions"

Additionally, the paper uses "up-to-a-multiplicative-constant" notation in a misleading way. The hidden constants (e.g., ρ) are algorithm parameters, so presenting exact inequalities would improve clarity and avoid confusion about the constants' dependence on the problem.

The practical applicability of MF-UCB is unclear. The algorithm assumes perfect knowledge of the fidelity parameters ζ(m) and λ(m), which may not be realistic in practice. The paper should discuss practical limitations and potential mitigations for inaccurate estimation of fidelity parameters.

**Quality:** The theoretical contribution is strong with solid proofs, but the errors in the supplementary materials significantly undermine the quality assessment. The main paper is technically sound, but the appendix errors suggest insufficient proofreading.

**Clarity:** The main paper is well-written and clear, but the appendix errors severely reduce clarity. The misleading "up-to-a-multiplicative-constant" notation also reduces clarity.

**Originality:** The multi-fidelity bandit framework is novel and significantly extends the classical bandit problem. The problem formulation and MF-UCB algorithm represent a clear advance beyond previous work on bandits with varying costs or approximations.

**Significance:** The results address a practical problem in many domains (e.g., online advertising, algorithm selection) where cheaper approximations to expensive experiments are available. The theoretical results show potential for significant regret reduction in practice, though the lack of discussion about practical limitations reduces the perceived significance.

**Recommendation:**

The paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable contribution to bandit theory. However, the numerous errors in the supplementary materials significantly undermine the paper's credibility, and the lack of discussion about practical limitations reduces the paper's impact. The authors should fix the appendix errors and provide a more thorough discussion of practical considerations before the paper can be accepted. With these revisions, this would be a strong contribution to the field. As it stands, the paper's quality is compromised by the errors, though the core idea remains valuable.

Overall, this paper has significant theoretical value but requires substantial revisions to the supplementary materials and additional discussion of practical limitations before it can be accepted.
