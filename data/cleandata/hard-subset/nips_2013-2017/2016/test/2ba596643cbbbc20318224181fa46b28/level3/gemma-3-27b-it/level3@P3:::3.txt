This paper introduces a multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit (MF-MAB) problem, where evaluating arms at lower fidelities is cheaper but introduces bounded bias. The authors propose MF-UCB, a UCB-based algorithm that adjusts confidence intervals to account for this bias, and provide theoretical regret bounds alongside empirical validation.

The work builds logically upon the extensive literature of multi-armed bandits (e.g., Auer, 2002; Lai & Robbins, 1985; Bubeck & Cesa-Bianchi, 2012). It distinguishes itself from prior work by explicitly modeling low-fidelity evaluations as biased estimates rather than simply noisier ones, and tackling the resulting exploration-exploitation trade-off. While multi-fidelity optimization has been explored in other domains (e.g., Huang et al., 2006; Cutler et al., 2017), this work is among the first to formalize and analyze regret in this multi-fidelity bandit setting. Related approaches like budgeted bandits (Tran-Thanh et al., 2013) and active learning with weak labelers (Zhang & Chaudhuri, 2016) address related resource constraints but differ in problem formulation.  Kandasamy et al. (2016) explored multi-fidelity bandits with Gaussian process payoffs, but this work's focus on biased lower-fidelity estimates and resulting theoretical analysis is novel.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Problem Formulation:** The MF-MAB formulation is a valuable contribution, offering a more nuanced way to model situations where cheaper, approximate evaluations are available. This is applicable to several real-world scenarios, as highlighted by the authors.
* **Algorithm Design:** MF-UCB is a conceptually sound algorithm, building on the well-established UCB framework while intelligently adjusting for fidelity-induced bias. The idea of widening confidence intervals based on fidelity levels is intuitive and promising.
* **Theoretical Analysis:** Providing regret bounds, despite their complexity, demonstrates a rigorous approach to analyzing the algorithm's performance. The lower bound establishes a degree of optimality.
* **Empirical Validation:** The simulation results provide initial evidence that MF-UCB outperforms standard UCB in the proposed setting.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Regret Bound Complexity:** The regret bounds are exceedingly messy and difficult to interpret, hindering practical utility and comparisons. While mathematically sound, they lack clear intuition regarding scaling with problem parameters.
* **Bias vs. Variance Modeling:** The choice to model low-fidelity evaluations as introducing *bias* rather than *variance* is debatable. The reviewer’s question regarding this modeling choice is well-taken. While motivating examples like algorithm selection lend some justification, others (e.g., short ad displays) seem less convincing, and the benefit of this modeling choice is not fully elucidated.
* **Application Linkage:** The connection between the theoretical model and motivating applications feels somewhat weak. The regret objective (minimizing regret with respect to the *highest* fidelity reward) doesn’t align well with all stated examples.  For instance, in the ad display scenario, minimizing long-term customer value may be a more relevant objective than simply maximizing clicks estimated from short displays.
* **Simulation Parameter Justification:** The lack of justification for the simulation parameters, particularly ζ^(m), is concerning. The reported 3–4x improvement could be highly sensitive to these choices, potentially overstating the algorithm’s practical benefits.  Without a sensitivity analysis, it's difficult to assess the robustness of the empirical findings.
* **Incremental Novelty:** While the problem formulation is novel, the algorithm itself largely relies on standard optimism-in-the-face-of-uncertainty principles (UCB). The core algorithmic innovation is the fidelity-aware confidence interval adjustment, but this builds directly on existing UCB techniques.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and well-defined problem with potential relevance to various applications.
* The proposed algorithm is a logical extension of existing bandit techniques and appears promising.
* The theoretical analysis, despite its complexity, demonstrates a commitment to rigorous evaluation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The regret bounds are too complex to be readily useful.
* The modeling assumption of bias over variance needs stronger justification.
* The link between the model and applications is tenuous.
* The simulation results lack sufficient justification and robustness analysis.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of multi-armed bandits by introducing a novel and relevant problem setting. However, several weaknesses prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance in its current form. The complexity of the theoretical results, the questionable modeling assumptions, and the limited justification for the simulation setup detract from the paper’s overall impact. I recommend **rejection** but encourage the authors to address the raised concerns, particularly regarding the regret bound simplification, bias modeling justification, and more thorough simulation analysis, before resubmission. Focusing on a clearer articulation of the benefits of the bias-modeling approach, and providing a more compelling connection between the theoretical framework and practical applications, would significantly strengthen the paper.
