Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces a multi-fidelity stochastic K-armed bandit model in which each arm can be sampled at M fidelities with increasing cost and decreasing bias relative to the highest-fidelity distribution. The authors propose MF-UCB, an extension of UCB that (i) builds per-fidelity upper confidence bounds on the highest-fidelity mean via known bias bounds ζ(m), (ii) adaptively chooses the lowest fidelity whose confidence radius exceeds a threshold γ(m) tied to costs, and (iii) escalates to higher fidelities only when lower fidelities cannot safely eliminate an arm. They analyze cost-weighted regret under a capital budget Λ, provide upper and lower bounds, and show MF-UCB is near-optimal under certain conditions. Synthetic experiments indicate MF-UCB outperforms a naïve single-fidelity UCB baseline.

Relative to classical UCB (Auer et al., Bubeck & Cesa-Bianchi), budgeted bandits (Tran-Thanh et al.; Xia et al.), and multi-fidelity optimization (Huang et al.; Rajnarayan et al.), this work formalizes a distinct setting: biased low-cost proxies combined with a capital budget. It also connects to multi-fidelity RL/simulation (Cutler et al.) and multi-fidelity GP bandits (Kandasamy et al., NIPS 2016) but focuses on stochastic MABs with known approximation error bounds.

Strengths
- Clear motivation for using cheaper, biased proxies to screen arms and reserving expensive evaluations for near-optimal arms.
- A principled algorithmic design that uses fidelity-specific UCBs on the highest-fidelity mean via ζ(m), with an escalation rule γ(m) that trades off cost and informativeness.
- Nontrivial theory: an adaptive regret bound that separates arms into fidelity-dependent partitions K(m), showing MF-UCB spends most capital at the right fidelity level; and a lower bound demonstrating near-optimality for a large subset of arms.
- Empirical results on synthetic data align with theory and are easy to interpret (fewer expensive pulls on bad arms).

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Quality
- Core objective and regret notion are questionable for the stated applications. The regret compares against an oracle that always plays the highest-mean arm, regardless of cost, with instantaneous “reward” q_t defined as the highest-fidelity mean µ(M)It even when the algorithm actually plays a cheaper, biased fidelity. This mixes two interpretations: (a) low-fidelity plays yield real utility (e.g., short ad displays produce clicks), in which case rewards should reflect the realized low-fidelity reward, not µ(M); or (b) low-fidelity plays are pure proxies (e.g., simulation) that yield no utility, in which case cumulative regret should treat proxy pulls as opportunity cost without assigning µ(M) as if the oracle could collect high-fidelity utility at any fidelity. Under the current definition, the “best arm” is the largest µ(M) arm irrespective of the per-unit cost of acquiring that reward, so the optimal baseline ignores reward/cost trade-offs. This can hide the true cost of spending budget on low-fidelity sampling and confound comparisons to more natural objectives where the oracle maximizes reward per unit cost (or per time).
- The lower bound and upper bound are carefully done, but the gap on K(m)7 arms is acknowledged and left open. The practicality and frequency of these cases should be discussed; the present algorithm may spend too much budget at higher fidelities in precisely the regimes where cost savings matter most.
- Experimental evaluation is limited to synthetic setups and a single baseline (UCB at the top fidelity). Given the stated motivation, comparisons to budgeted bandits (e.g., Tran-Thanh et al., Xia et al.) and pure-exploration best-arm identification (BAI) with budgets would strengthen the empirical case and help position the proposed regret notion.

Clarity
- Notation is heavy and sometimes introduced after use (e.g., s appears before it is defined and then is barely used). Key symbols (µ(m)k, ζ(m), γ(m), ∆(m)k, T(m)k,t, B(m)k,t, K(m), J sets) are dispersed. This significantly harms readability.
- The advertising example is misleading. The text mentions that longer display intervals change user behavior and that low-fidelity samples are “biased,” implicitly invoking nonstationarity or time-correlations, which are outside the stationary i.i.d. assumptions used in the analysis. Either revise the example to fit the model assumptions, or broaden the model to handle nonstationarity (which is nontrivial).
- Organization: the regret definition, oracle baseline, and modeling choices should be motivated and contrasted against alternatives early and crisply. The current flow mixes motivation, definitions, and technical lemmas in ways that make it hard to follow.

Originality
- The formal model of multi-fidelity, biased proxies within stochastic MABs is novel and interesting. The algorithmic idea of combining fidelity-specific UCBs with cost-aware escalation is a natural but nontrivial extension of optimism. The lower bound is a valuable contribution.

Significance
- Potentially impactful for domains with costly evaluations and cheap proxies (simulation, A/B testing pipelines, hyperparameter screening). However, the current regret notion and empirical scope limit practical relevance. A cost-aware best-arm identification perspective (finding the best high-fidelity arm with high probability under a capital budget) would be more aligned with many real-world testing workflows.

Suggestions for improvement
- Revisit the objective:
  - If low-fidelity pulls yield real reward, define reward at fidelity m as µ(m) and compare against an oracle that maximizes expected cumulative realized reward under capital Λ; analyze regret in reward-per-cost terms.
  - If low-fidelity pulls are proxies with no direct utility, adopt a fixed-budget BAI formulation (simple regret or probability of error) with explicit cost/budget and provide a cost-complexity analysis for identifying the best high-fidelity arm with high probability.
  - Alternatively, define the oracle as one that maximizes µ(M)/λ(M) (reward per unit cost) and adjust the regret accordingly; discuss how this changes MF-UCB and the bounds.
- Clarify modeling assumptions: stationarity, independence across fidelities, known ζ(m), and whether ζ(m) must be globally valid or arm-dependent. Explain how these are obtained in practice.
- Strengthen experiments:
  - Include baselines from budgeted MABs and pure-exploration BAI (fixed budget/horizon) adapted to costs.
  - Add stress tests for the K(m)7 regime where MF-UCB may be suboptimal, and ablations on the γ(m) choice.
  - Provide sensitivity to ζ(m) misspecification.
- Streamline writing:
  - Consolidate notation in a single table early; define all symbols before use.
  - Move long technical definitions and partitions (J sets, K(m)) to an appendix with a short main-text intuition and a small running example.
  - Replace the advertising example with one consistent with stationarity, or clearly declare it only as a metaphor.

Pros
- Interesting and timely problem; clear potential impact.
- Novel algorithm with intuition consistent with practice.
- Solid theoretical development with upper and lower bounds.
- Empirical trends match theory.

Cons
- Objective and regret notion do not match key applications and obscure reward/cost trade-offs.
- Clarity and notation issues impede comprehension.
- Misleading example relative to assumptions.
- Limited empirical baselines; no real-data or practical case study.
- Gap in lower bound coverage for certain arms remains unresolved.

Overall assessment
Interesting and potentially impactful, but not ready for publication in its current form. I recommend a substantial revision focusing on a better-aligned objective (cost-aware cumulative reward or costed BAI), clearer exposition, and stronger empirical validation against appropriate baselines.
