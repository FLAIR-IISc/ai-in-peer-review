- Introduces a multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit problem where pulling arms at different fidelities incurs different costs and noise levels.  
- Main contribution is the MF-UCB algorithm that balances exploration/exploitation across fidelities, with a regret bound and matching lower bound.  
- Motivated by settings with cheap noisy approximations (e.g., online advertising, algorithm selection).  
- Builds on standard UCB literature and relates to multi-fidelity optimization and active learning, but focuses on bandit-specific exploration-exploitation and a tailored regret measure.  
- Strength: tackles a practical, natural extension of classical bandits with potential cross-domain impact.  
- Strength: MF-UCB is intuitively reasonable—use low fidelities for exploration, high fidelities for promising arms.  
- Strength: Theoretical analysis appears rigorous, providing regret and lower bounds.  
- Strength: Experiments, though limited, show MF-UCB outperforms naive UCB baselines.  
- Weakness: Presentation suffers from clarity and weak motivation in places.  
- Weakness: Regret penalizes cost multiplicatively, which feels unnatural; additive/budget-based penalties should be discussed.  
- Weakness: Motivating examples (e.g., online advertising) are narrow; broader applicability discussion needed.  
- Major concern: Assumption 1 imposes an artificial constraint on fidelity error decay (zeta_m); justification and robustness when violated are unclear.  
- Weakness: Algorithm seems tailored to the specific zeta_m configuration; unclear if Assumption 1 is essential for correctness or performance.  
- Weakness: Theorem 2 and definitions (dependence on Δ and sets K^(m)) are dense and need more intuitive explanation.  
- Weakness: Comparison to standard UCB is hard to follow without explicit decomposition of regret terms.  
- Weakness: Gap in the lower bound suggests room for improving MF-UCB or tightening analysis.  
- Specific comments: quantify "near-optimal" (Line 56); remove stray period (Line 126); consider consistent set notation (curly braces).  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, relevant problem; intuitive algorithm; supporting experiments; rigorous theoretical contributions.  
- Arguments against acceptance: unclear presentation/motivation; artificial regret formulation and Assumption 1; hard-to-interpret theoretical results; lower-bound gap.  
- Overall assessment: weak accept contingent on improving clarity, better justification of assumptions (esp. Assumption 1), clearer theoretical exposition, and broader discussion of limitations/extensions.
