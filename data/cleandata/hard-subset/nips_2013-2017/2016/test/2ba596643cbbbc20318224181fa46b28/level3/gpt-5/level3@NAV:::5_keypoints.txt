- Introduces a multi-fidelity stochastic K-armed bandit where lower-fidelity observations are biased approximations and performance is measured by capital-based pseudo-regret against an oracle maximizing high-fidelity reward per cost.  
- Proposes MF-UCB, a UCB-style algorithm that jointly selects arms and fidelities using calibrated confidence radii and cost/approximation thresholds.  
- Provides upper bounds on regret that decompose by fidelity and improve over naively ignoring approximations.  
- Gives a lower-bound analysis showing near-optimality except for a clearly identified subset of arms.  
- Assumptions and parameter choices are clearly stated and motivated (concentration, known bias bounds ζ(m), regularity Assumption 1, γ(m), ρ>4).  
- Experiments on synthetic tasks demonstrate savings: cheap fidelities quickly eliminate poor arms and concentrate expensive evaluations on near-optimal ones.  
- Writing is clear and well organized; regret notion and decomposition are well motivated.  
- Minor clarity suggestions: discuss computational overhead of maintaining per-fidelity indices and provide recipes for ψ^{-1} for common distributions.  
- Originality: novel MF bandit formulation with cost-aware regret and an original fidelity-switching criterion.  
- Significance: relevant for simulation vs real-world domains (advertising, robotics, A/B testing, AutoML) and likely to influence follow-up work.  
- Pros: clear novel formulation and algorithm, strong upper bounds and informative lower bounds, intuitive/practice-aligned mechanism, and good writing/positioning.  
- Cons: requires known ζ(m) and ψ with no robustness analysis; partial optimality gap for a subset of arms remains open; experiments limited to synthetics and comparisons to stronger baselines are sparse.  
- Suggestions: add robustness experiments for misspecified ζ(m), compare to stronger baselines (e.g., Thompson-style MF, budgeted bandits), and discuss or propose learning ζ(m) online.
