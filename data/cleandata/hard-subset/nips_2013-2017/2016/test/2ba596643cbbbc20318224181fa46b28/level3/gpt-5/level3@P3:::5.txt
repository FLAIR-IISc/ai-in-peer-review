Summary and relation to prior work
The paper introduces a variant of the stochastic K-armed bandit where, for each arm, the learner can query multiple “fidelities” with different costs and biases relative to the true (highest-fidelity) reward. This is formalized as a multi-fidelity (MF) bandit: fidelity m has cost λ(m) with λ(1) < … < λ(M), and its mean µ(m)k is within a known bias ζ(m) of the highest-fidelity mean µ(M)k, with ζ(m) decreasing to 0. The objective is to minimize a capital-weighted regret that measures missed highest-fidelity reward per unit cost spent, even when playing at lower fidelities. The authors propose MF-UCB, an upper-confidence-bound policy that (i) computes per-fidelity upper bounds on µ(M)k by adding the bias ζ(m) to the µ(m) estimate, (ii) plays the arm with the tightest optimistic bound, and (iii) adaptively chooses the cheapest fidelity that can still shrink uncertainty meaningfully, via thresholds γ(m) tuned to costs and bias via ψ (the concentration function).

Theoretical results show that MF-UCB invests most of its budget at low fidelities for clearly suboptimal arms, and reserves high-fidelity plays for a small set of near-optimal arms. The regret bound (Theorem 2) compares favorably to classic UCB (Auer et al. 2002) that ignores approximations, with improvements when cheap fidelities suffice to eliminate many arms. A lower bound (Theorem 4) for Bernoulli rewards shows MF-UCB is nearly optimal except for a specific class of arms where very small gaps at low fidelities make a more aggressive low-fidelity discrimination potentially better. Simulations on synthetic tasks corroborate the theory: MF-UCB achieves lower regret and allocates few expensive plays to clearly suboptimal arms.

This paper connects to several lines: UCB-style stochastic bandits (Agrawal 1995; Auer et al. 2002; Bubeck & Cesa-Bianchi 2012), budgeted bandits (Tran-Thanh et al.; Xia et al.), multi-fidelity optimization in engineering (Huang et al.; Rajnarayan et al.) and RL with multi-fidelity simulators (Cutler et al.), and weak/strong labeler active learning (Zhang & Chaudhuri). Unlike budgeted bandits that maximize reward/cost per arm, here lower fidelities are biased proxies of the same underlying arm, and the regret targets highest-fidelity performance—a novel combination.

Strengths
- Clear and compelling formulation of MF bandits with known bias envelopes ζ(m) and cost λ(m).
- Algorithmic design that is simple, principled, and computationally light: per-fidelity UCBs plus a cost/bias-driven switching rule γ(m) = ψ^{-1}(λ(m)/λ(m+1) ψ(ζ(m))).
- Strong theory: a nuanced regret bound that decomposes per-arm contributions by the fidelity where an arm is effectively eliminated, and a lower bound showing near-optimality under standard concentration assumptions. The analysis also controls the total number of plays under a capital budget (N ≈ Λ/λ(M)).
- Lower-bound analysis identifies precisely when MF-UCB may be loose and explains the difficulty of improving it without incurring high-fidelity cost.
- Simulations align with the theory and illustrate reduced slope and fidelity allocation behavior.
- Writing is clear, definitions and assumptions are explicit, and proofs are well sketched.

Weaknesses
- Requires known ζ(m) and ψ (or at least a correct sub-Gaussian tail), and assumes a regularity condition (Assumption 1) on decay of ζ(m). Practical guidance for estimating/upper bounding ζ(m) and robustness to misspecification is limited.
- Lower bound optimality gap for the K(m)7 class indicates that thresholding may be conservative when small positive gaps exist at lower fidelities; no practical heuristic is explored to mitigate this.
- Experiments are synthetic only. No comparisons to simple MF heuristics (e.g., fixed low-fidelity screening followed by high-fidelity UCB) or to Thompson-sampling baselines; no sensitivity analyses over ζ, λ, M.
- The regret notion, while motivated, may not cover settings where low-fidelity plays have their own utility (e.g., simulation-only tasks) or when the oracle is constrained to use high fidelity. Broader empirical discussion of alternative regret choices is brief.

Quality
The paper is technically solid. Assumptions are explicit; the choice of γ(m) is well motivated; the regret and lower bound proofs (sketched in the main text, detailed in the appendix) are coherent and match intuition. The decomposition into K(m) partitions is insightful and underpins tight accounting of fidelity-wise pulls. The acknowledgment of the gap on K(m)7 arms is honest and the difficulty of fixing it is convincingly argued.

Clarity
Very well organized and easy to follow. The problem setup, regret definition, algorithm steps, and proof sketches are clear. Definitions, notation, and assumptions are carefully introduced. Minor suggestions: provide a short practical recipe for choosing ψ in practice (e.g., default Hoeffding for sub-Gaussian) and a paragraph on how to upper bound ζ(m) in typical applications.

Originality
The MF bandit setting with biased, cheaper fidelities and a cost-weighted regret against highest-fidelity performance is novel and clearly distinguished from budgeted bandits, multi-fidelity optimization without regret, and weak/strong labeler settings. The MF-UCB design and its analysis are new.

Significance
The work addresses an important, realistic problem and provides both conceptual and technical advances. The potential impact is high: many applications (ads, algorithm selection, simulation-based evaluation) can benefit from principled use of approximations. The lower bound underscores theoretical value; the clean algorithm encourages adoption and follow-up work.

Suggestions and questions for the authors
- Robustness: How sensitive is MF-UCB to over- or under-estimating ζ(m)? Can one use data-driven upper bounds on ζ(m) with confidence guarantees?
- ψ choice: In practice, will you recommend a fixed sub-Gaussian ψ, or can ψ be adapted online?
- Thresholds: Could a variant that occasionally validates “borderline” arms at a cheaper lower fidelity reduce the K(m)7 gap without raising overall high-fidelity cost?
- Experiments: Add baselines such as (i) screen all arms at low fidelity then run UCB at high fidelity on survivors, (ii) a TS-based MF variant; and provide sensitivity to λ ratios, ζ schedules, and M.
- Scope of regret: A brief empirical illustration of an alternative regret when low fidelity has intrinsic utility (e.g., simulation-only reward) would broaden applicability.

Pros
- Novel and well-motivated problem formulation.
- Simple, elegant algorithm with strong theoretical guarantees.
- Lower bound and near-optimality under standard conditions.
- Clear writing and convincing synthetic experiments.

Cons
- Requires known bias envelopes and concentration; robustness not fully explored.
- Suboptimality on a specific class of arms (K(m)7) with no practical mitigation.
- Only synthetic experiments; limited baseline comparisons.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper is original, technically sound, clearly presented, and addresses a meaningful problem. The theoretical framework and MF-UCB algorithm are likely to influence subsequent research and practice.

Confidence
High.
