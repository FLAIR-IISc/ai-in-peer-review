

This paper introduces a multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit framework where each arm can be observed at multiple fidelity levels with decreasing error (ζ_m) and increasing cost (λ_m). The authors propose MF-UCB, an algorithm that adaptively selects fidelities to maximize net reward (accounting for both reward and cost), and provides regret and lower bounds. The work builds on UCB methods [3] and extends budgeted bandit literature [13,15] to incorporate fidelity-dependent costs and biases.

The paper's main strength lies in its interesting problem formulation addressing a relevant tradeoff between information acquisition, cost, and reward in practical applications like online advertising. The theoretical analysis appears technically sound, and the algorithm demonstrates empirical improvements over standard UCB in simulations.

However, several critical weaknesses undermine the contribution. The regret formulation penalizing cost multiplicatively is unnatural; additive cost penalization should be considered and discussed, as it would better align with standard bandit formulations. The motivation is limited to an ad-display example that may not generalize to broader applications. Assumption 1 (on ζ_m decay) is artificial and opaque, with unclear general validity. The paper fails to address what happens when ζ_m don't decay as required, and doesn't show how to select a subsequence of fidelities to satisfy the assumption. The lower bound gap (Theorem 4) requires further investigation, as MF-UCB may not meet the lower bound for certain arms. The interpretation of Theorem 2 and comparison with UCB is difficult due to complex dependencies on Δ and sets K^(m), which need clearer explanation.

Minor issues include "near-optimal" (l.56), unnecessary period before "for all m < M" (l.126), and inconsistent set notation (l.179). While the core idea is promising, these issues significantly reduce the paper's clarity and scientific contribution. The authors should revise to address the regret formulation, strengthen motivation, clarify assumptions, and improve exposition of key results. The work has potential significance for applications with fidelity tradeoffs, but requires substantial revisions to meet the quality standards expected at NIPS.
