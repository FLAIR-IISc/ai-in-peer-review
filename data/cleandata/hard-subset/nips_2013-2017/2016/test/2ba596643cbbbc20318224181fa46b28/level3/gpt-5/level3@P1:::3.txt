This paper introduces a multi-fidelity variant of the stochastic K-armed bandit in which each arm can be queried at one of M fidelities. Lower fidelities are cheaper but provide only biased approximations of the highest-fidelity reward; the bias is bounded by known fidelity-specific constants ζ(m) that decrease with m. The authors define a cost-weighted notion of pseudo-regret under a capital budget Λ, where instantaneous reward is measured with respect to the highest-fidelity mean (irrespective of which fidelity was actually played). They propose MF-UCB, an optimism-based algorithm that maintains per-fidelity confidence bounds inflated by ζ(m), selects the arm with the tightest upper bound on the high-fidelity mean, and then selects the lowest-cost fidelity whose statistical uncertainty has not yet been driven below a fidelity-specific threshold γ(m) chosen to trade off bias and cost. The analysis shows MF-UCB uses low fidelities to cheaply eliminate clearly suboptimal arms and restricts expensive high-fidelity plays to a small set near-optimal arms. The paper provides a (nonasymptotic) upper bound on regret relative to Λ and a complementary lower bound, and shows that MF-UCB is near-optimal for a subset of arms. Synthetic experiments demonstrate improvements over naive highest-fidelity UCB.

Relation to prior work: The algorithmic approach follows the optimism-in-the-face-of-uncertainty principle (UCB) [2,3], and the analysis leverages standard concentration assumptions [5]. The formulation differs from budgeted bandits [13,15], which optimize reward per unit cost for each arm without multi-fidelity structure. It is related to multi-fidelity optimization in engineering [7,10], multi-fidelity RL [6], and weak/strong labeler active learning [16], but unlike those, the objective here is bandit regret under a new cost-weighted metric. The GP-based extension in [8] is complementary and outside the scope here.

Strengths
- Clear and relevant formulation: multi-fidelity decisions arise in many domains; formalizing bias-bounded approximations and costs is well-motivated.
- Conceptual algorithmic contribution: MF-UCB elegantly augments UCB with fidelity-aware confidence sets and a principled fidelity-selection rule via γ(m) = ψ−1(λ(m)/λ(m+1) · ψ(ζ(m))).
- Theoretical guarantees: upper bound shows that most cost for suboptimal arms is spent at low fidelities; lower bound (with arm partition K(m) and the K(m)3/K(m)7 split) clarifies when near-optimality is achieved versus when a gap remains.
- Empirical results broadly align with the theory, exhibiting lower regret slopes than UCB.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Quality: While technically solid, some assumptions limit applicability. The model requires known bias bounds ζ(m); in practice these are rarely known and may be data-dependent. Assumption 1 (fast decay of ζ(m) relative to ψ) is invoked to make the comparison to UCB “most appealing,” but its practical prevalence is unclear. The regret bounds, although correct, are hard to parse; constants and the layered partitioning K(m) obscure the main takeaways. The lower bound reveals a non-negligible gap for arms in K(m)7; the paper leaves closing this gap as open, which weakens the optimality claim in realistic regimes where fidelities can be misleading but not too misleading.
- Clarity: The regret definition R(Λ) and the choice qt = µ(M)It deserve more discussion. This is appropriate for settings where the only “true” payoff is the highest fidelity, but for examples like short ad displays, the mapping from short-horizon performance to long-horizon payoff is not clearly justified; alternative objectives could be reasonable. The main theorems would benefit from digestible corollaries (e.g., M=2, sub-Gaussian case) that translate the bound into an explicit improvement factor over UCB. The arm partition K(m), while insightful, is dense; a self-contained illustrative example in the main text (not just the figure) would help.
- Originality: The main novelty lies in the problem formulation and the regret metric; the algorithm itself is a carefully adapted UCB. That is fine, but should be made explicit. A discussion contrasting bias- vs variance-type approximations would better position the work relative to more standard noisy-observation models.
- Significance: The potential impact is high if the framework matches practice. However, the necessity of known ζ(m) and fidelity costs λ(m), and the lack of sensitivity analyses, make it hard to judge robustness. The empirical section lacks justification for parameter choices (ζ(m), λ(m), ψ), and the claimed 3–4× gains may hinge on favorable settings; additional baselines (e.g., “filter-then-UCB” heuristics; Thompson sampling at top fidelity; simple cost-aware allocations) would strengthen the case.

Specific suggestions for improvement
- Provide simple, interpretable corollaries of the main bound (e.g., sub-Gaussian, M=2) that state explicit improvement factors in terms of λ(1)/λ(M) and the fraction of near-optimal arms.
- Add sensitivity experiments sweeping ζ(m), λ(m), and M to show robustness and delineate regimes where MF-UCB helps or hurts; include ablations of the γ(m) choice.
- Compare against additional baselines: (i) highest-fidelity UCB (already present), (ii) a two-stage heuristic that screens at low fidelity then runs UCB at high fidelity, (iii) Thompson sampling at high fidelity, and (iv) a variant that treats low fidelity as higher-variance unbiased noise to empirically test the bias model.
- Discuss how to estimate or upper bound ζ(m) from data and how MF-UCB degrades if ζ(m) are misspecified.
- Clarify the regret objective and its applicability across domains (ads, algorithm selection, robotics), possibly mentioning alternative definitions when low-fidelity “rewards” are not meaningful (as partially noted in the conclusion).

Arguments for acceptance
- Well-motivated new bandit setting with costs and fidelity-specific bias; timely and likely to spur follow-up work.
- Clean algorithmic idea with nontrivial analysis; lower bound provides meaningful insight.
- Empirical results consistent with theory and show tangible improvements.

Arguments against acceptance
- Practical realism of known fidelity bias bounds and Assumption 1 is not established; sensitivity analyses are missing.
- Theoretical bounds are difficult to interpret; optimality gap exists for a natural subset of arms.
- Empirical section is limited, with parameter choices insufficiently justified and few baselines; improvements may reflect favorable tuning.

Quality: Moderate. Technically sound under stated assumptions, but with an unresolved gap and limited empirical validation.
Clarity: Moderate. Generally well written but dense; key messages could be made more accessible.
Originality: Good in formulation and analysis; algorithmic mechanics are standard UCB adapted to the setting.
Significance: Promising, contingent on further empirical support and practical handling of unknown ζ(m) and costs.
