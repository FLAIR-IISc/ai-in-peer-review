This paper introduces a novel and practical variant of the multi-armed bandit problem, termed the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit (MF-MAB). In this setting, each arm can be pulled at one of M different fidelities, where higher fidelities provide more accurate reward estimates but incur greater cost. This formulation is well-motivated by applications like online advertising and hyperparameter tuning. The authors propose a new cost-aware regret metric, develop the Multi-Fidelity UCB (MF-UCB) algorithm, and provide a thorough theoretical analysis, including both an upper regret bound and a corresponding lower bound for the problem. This work extends the classical stochastic bandit framework by explicitly modeling a trade-off between cost and approximation quality, which is a significant and original contribution.

The paper’s strengths are numerous. The MF-MAB formulation is novel, significant, and addresses an important gap between standard bandit theory and real-world scenarios. The MF-UCB algorithm is an elegant extension of the UCB principle, and its mechanism for escalating fidelities is intuitive. The technical quality of the paper is high; the theoretical analysis is comprehensive, rigorously characterizing the algorithm's performance and establishing its near-optimality under certain conditions. The simulations effectively support the theoretical claims, showing a clear advantage over a naive UCB baseline.

The primary weakness is the strong assumption that the fidelity deviation bounds, ζ^(m), are known in advance. In many practical applications, these bias parameters would be unknown, potentially limiting the method's direct applicability. The paper does not address how these could be estimated or how robust the algorithm is to their misspecification. A discussion on relaxing this assumption would significantly enhance the work's practical relevance.

Additionally, the analysis section contains several minor errors and typos that require correction. These include: incorrect set notation (e.g., "H^(m)" instead of "K^(m)" in lines 326, 357, 386), an incorrect indicator term (line 343), incorrect inequality directions (lines 346–347), and various incorrect superscripts, indices, and terms throughout the proofs (lines 345, 384, 407, 431, 440, 450).

Overall, the paper's strengths—particularly its novel formulation and rigorous analysis—outweigh its weaknesses. The assumption of known biases highlights a clear avenue for important future work rather than invalidating the core contribution. I recommend acceptance.
