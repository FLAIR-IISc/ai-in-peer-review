This paper introduces a novel variant of the stochastic K-armed bandit problem, where cheap approximations for each arm are available. The authors formalize this setting as a multi-fidelity (MF) bandit problem and develop a new algorithm, MF-UCB, which adapts the classical Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) strategy to this multi-fidelity setting. The paper provides theoretical results, including regret bounds for the proposed method, and offers a lower-bound analysis showing the theoretical improvement of the approach. The authors also present simulations that demonstrate the effect and benefits of the MF bandit method.

The main ideas of the paper are well-connected to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere. The K-armed bandit problem has been extensively studied in the past, and the authors build upon this existing work by introducing a new twist with the availability of cheap approximations. The paper also relates to other work on multi-fidelity methods for optimization in various domains, but the authors argue that their work is distinct in its focus on the exploration-exploitation trade-off in the bandit setting.

The strengths of the paper include its clear and well-motivated presentation of the problem, the development of a novel algorithm that adapts to the multi-fidelity setting, and the provision of theoretical results that demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach. The simulations presented in the paper also provide useful insights into the behavior of the MF-UCB algorithm.

However, there are some potential weaknesses and areas for improvement. One potential concern is that the assumptions made about the problem, such as the availability of cheap approximations and the structure of the reward distributions, may not hold in all practical scenarios. Additionally, the paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with other existing algorithms for the K-armed bandit problem, to better understand the relative advantages and disadvantages of the MF-UCB approach.

In terms of the four criteria for evaluation, the paper scores well on quality, clarity, and originality. The technical soundness of the paper is strong, and the authors provide a clear and well-organized presentation of their ideas. The paper is also well-written and easy to follow. The originality of the paper is high, as it introduces a new variant of the K-armed bandit problem and develops a novel algorithm to address it.

However, the significance of the paper could be improved by providing more insights into the practical applications and implications of the MF-UCB algorithm. While the simulations presented in the paper are useful, they are limited to synthetic problems, and it would be beneficial to see the algorithm applied to real-world problems or datasets. Additionally, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the potential limitations and challenges of the MF-UCB approach, and how these might be addressed in future work.
