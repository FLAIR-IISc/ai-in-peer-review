

# Review: The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit

This paper introduces a novel multi-fidelity bandit framework where each arm can be observed at multiple fidelities with different costs and biases, extending the classical multi-armed bandit problem. The authors formalize the setting where observing an arm at fidelity m incurs cost λ(m) and returns a biased estimate of the true reward with bias bounded by ζ(m), where ζ(1) > ζ(2) > ... > ζ(M) = 0. They propose MF-UCB, an algorithm that adaptively selects both arms and fidelities to maximize net reward, and provide regret and lower bounds demonstrating that MF-UCB can outperform standard UCB when lower fidelities are sufficiently cheaper. This builds on classical bandit work (e.g., Auer et al. 2002) and extends recent multi-fidelity optimization approaches (e.g., Cutler et al. 2014, Kandasamy et al. 2016).

**Quality**: The paper presents technically sound analysis with a well-defined regret bound for MF-UCB and a matching lower bound. The regret bound (Theorem 2) is derived carefully, and the lower bound (Theorem 4) provides a meaningful comparison. However, the regret formulation in Equation (1) is problematic: penalizing cost multiplicatively (Λµ? - ∑λ(mt)qt) is unnatural and differs from standard budgeted bandit formulations that typically use additive cost penalties. The authors should consider and discuss an additive cost formulation, as this would better align with standard bandit literature. The assumption that ζ(m) decays sufficiently fast (Assumption 1) appears artificial and lacks justification for its general validity. The paper fails to address what happens when this assumption is violated, which is critical for practical applications.

**Clarity**: The paper suffers from significant clarity issues. The motivation (ad-display example) is limited and doesn't generalize well to the chosen reward/cost model. The interpretation of Theorem 2 and the comparison with UCB is particularly difficult due to the complex sets K^(m) and unclear dependence on Δ. The explanation of the algorithm's fidelity selection (Equation 4) lacks sufficient intuition, and the connection between the chosen γ(m) and the regret bound is not transparent. Several minor issues (e.g., "near-optimal" on line 56, period before "for all m < M" on line 126) further detract from clarity.

**Originality**: The problem formulation is novel, extending bandit theory to incorporate multiple fidelities with associated costs and biases. This is a meaningful extension of the classical bandit problem with clear practical applications. The paper distinguishes itself from related work on budgeted bandits (Tran-Thanh et al. 2012, Xia et al. 2014) and multi-fidelity optimization (Huang et al. 2006, Kandasamy et al. 2016) by providing a formal regret analysis in the multi-fidelity setting.

**Significance**: The problem addressed is relevant to many practical applications (e.g., online advertising, algorithm selection) where cheaper approximations to expensive experiments are available. The potential for significant cost savings by using lower fidelities to eliminate suboptimal arms before committing to expensive high-fidelity evaluations is compelling. However, the significance is somewhat diminished by the lack of broader motivation for the specific reward/cost model and the unclear practical applicability when Assumption 1 is violated.

**Major Weaknesses**: 
1. The regret formulation's multiplicative cost penalty is unnatural and should be reconsidered.
2. Assumption 1 is poorly justified and appears artificial, with no discussion of how to handle violations.
3. The paper fails to address the robustness of MF-UCB when ζ(m) doesn't decay as required.
4. The lower bound gap (Theorem 4) is not adequately explained or addressed, with the authors acknowledging it as an open problem without suggesting how to resolve it.
5. The interpretation of key results (Theorem 2 and comparison with UCB) is unnecessarily complex and difficult to parse.

**Strengths**: 
1. The multi-fidelity bandit framework is well-motivated and relevant to practical applications.
2. The MF-UCB algorithm is well-designed and intuitively makes sense for the problem.
3. The theoretical analysis is technically sound and provides meaningful regret bounds.
4. The simulation results support the theoretical claims.

**Arguments for Acceptance**: 
- Novel problem formulation extending classical bandit theory
- Well-designed algorithm with solid theoretical analysis
- Relevant practical applications
- Clear experimental validation

**Arguments against Acceptance**: 
- Fundamental issues with the regret formulation
- Artificial and poorly justified assumption (Assumption 1)
- Lack of discussion on robustness when assumptions are violated
- Significant clarity issues that make the paper difficult to interpret

Overall, this is a technically sound paper with a meaningful extension of bandit theory, but the significant clarity issues, unnatural regret formulation, and questionable assumption severely limit its impact. The authors should reconsider the regret formulation, provide a stronger justification for Assumption 1, and address the robustness of their approach when ζ(m) doesn't decay as required. Without these changes, the paper's contribution is substantially weakened, and the results may not generalize to practical scenarios where Assumption 1 is violated. I recommend rejection pending major revisions to address these critical concerns.
