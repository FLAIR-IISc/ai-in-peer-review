This paper introduces a novel multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit (MF-MAB) setting, motivated by scenarios where obtaining precise reward estimates is costly but cheaper approximations are available. The authors formulate a regret definition accounting for both reward and fidelity cost, propose a new algorithm, MF-UCB, with a theoretical analysis including a matching lower bound, and validate their approach through simulations. This work builds upon the extensive literature on bandit algorithms (Agrawal, 1995; Auer, 2002; Bubeck & Cesa-Bianchi, 2012) and extends it to a practical and potentially important new domain. The problem formulation and analysis are well-motivated and significantly different from existing work on budgeted bandits (Tran-Thanh et al., 2013; Xia et al., 2015) or multi-fidelity optimization (Huang et al., 2006; Kandasamy et al., 2016), which do not directly address the exploration-exploitation trade-off inherent in the bandit setting.

The strengths of this paper lie in its clear problem formulation, the novelty of the proposed algorithm and its theoretical guarantees, and the demonstration of improved performance compared to a naive UCB approach in simulations. The regret analysis, proving near-optimality, is a particularly strong contribution. The introduction provides a compelling rationale for the MF-MAB framework with relatable examples like online advertising and algorithm selection. The authors also carefully consider the impact of biased approximations (fidelities) and provide a regularity assumption to ensure meaningful results.

However, a key weakness of the paper is the reliance on knowing the deviation ζ(m) between the m-th fidelity mean and the highest fidelity mean. This assumption seems strong, and the paper does not adequately address how these values might be estimated in practice or the sensitivity of the algorithm to inaccuracies in these estimates. Relaxing this assumption would significantly broaden the applicability of the proposed framework. Furthermore, while the theoretical analysis is rigorous, the numerous assumptions and the complexity of the derived bounds might hinder practical adoption. 

Here's a breakdown of specific concerns, including minor issues:

* **ζ(m) Assumption:** The practical applicability hinges on knowing ζ(m). A discussion of estimation strategies or robustness to estimation error is missing.
* **Lower Bound Gap:** The authors acknowledge a gap between their algorithm’s performance and the lower bound for certain arms. Understanding and potentially closing this gap could further strengthen the theoretical contributions.
* **Notation and Typos:** Several minor errors were identified:

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* **Novelty:** The MF-MAB setting is a significant extension to the standard bandit framework.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The algorithm is accompanied by a strong theoretical analysis, including a lower bound.
* **Potential Impact:** The framework has practical applications in various domains, such as online advertising and machine learning.
* **Sound Experimental Validation:** Simulations demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm.

**Arguments for rejection:**

* **Strong Assumption:** The requirement of knowing ζ(m) limits the practical applicability of the algorithm.
* **Complexity:** The theoretical analysis and resulting bounds are complex, potentially hindering adoption.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of bandit algorithms. The proposed framework is novel and well-motivated, and the theoretical analysis is rigorous. However, the strong assumption regarding knowledge of ζ(m) is a significant limitation. 

**Rating:** Weak Accept. I recommend acceptance contingent on the authors addressing the limitations of the ζ(m) assumption in the camera-ready version, perhaps by discussing potential estimation strategies or analyzing the algorithm’s robustness to estimation error. Correcting the minor typos is also essential. If the ζ(m) issue cannot be sufficiently addressed, a rejection would be warranted.
