This paper introduces and analyzes a multi-fidelity variant of the stochastic K-armed bandit in which each arm can be probed at one of M fidelities: lower fidelities are cheaper but provide biased, progressively tighter approximations to the reward at the highest fidelity. The authors formalize this setting by assuming known bias envelopes ζ(m) with ζ(1) > … > ζ(M) = 0 and fidelity costs λ(1) < … < λ(M). They define a cost-weighted pseudo-regret that credits only the highest-fidelity mean reward irrespective of which fidelity is played, thereby targeting long-horizon utility in applications like ads and algorithm selection. The main algorithm, MF-UCB, maintains UCB-style upper bounds on the highest-fidelity mean via the minimum over fidelity-specific confidence bounds X̄(m) + confidence width + ζ(m). It selects the arm with the largest bound, then chooses the cheapest fidelity whose confidence width exceeds a threshold γ(m) that trades off the marginal information at fidelity m against its cost, with γ(m) chosen via ψ and the cost ratio λ(m)/λ(m+1). The analysis shows that MF-UCB uses low-cost fidelities to quickly rule out clearly suboptimal arms and reserves high-cost plays for a small set near-optimal arms.

The theoretical results include an upper bound on cost-weighted regret E[R(Λ)] that scales like log(Λ/λ(M)) times a sum over arms of λ(Jk)/ψ(Δ(Jk)) where Jk is the “active” fidelity for arm k and Δ(m) is the margin at fidelity m. Compared to standard UCB that pays λ(M) for every arm, MF-UCB pays λ(Jk) for most arms, yielding strictly better regret whenever the lower fidelities separate many suboptimal arms. A lower bound shows MF-UCB is near-optimal under a regularity assumption on the decay of ζ(m) (so inserted fidelities cannot game the analysis), and matches the lower bound on a large subset of arms. Synthetic experiments corroborate the theory: MF-UCB eliminates many arms at low fidelities and achieves lower regret than a naive UCB that ignores approximations.

Relation to prior work: The paper is solidly grounded in UCB methods [Auer 2002; Agrawal 1995; Bubeck & Cesa-Bianchi], budgeted bandits [e.g., Tran-Thanh et al.; Xia et al.], weak/strong labeler active learning [Zhang & Chaudhuri], and multi-fidelity optimization in engineering and RL [Huang et al.; Cutler et al.]. Unlike budgeted bandits (which optimize reward/cost per pull) or weak/strong labeler settings (which do not handle repeated exploration-exploitation), this work formalizes and analyzes multi-fidelity approximations per arm with explicit bias bounds and cost-aware regret. It also complements later GP-based multi-fidelity bandits.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The construction of MF-UCB is principled. The regret analysis is careful, with high-probability control of per-fidelity pulls, partitioning arms into K(m) sets, and a lower bound via change-of-measure. The near-optimality statements are well justified where assumptions hold.
- Assumptions/limitations: (i) The algorithm needs known bias envelopes ζ(m) and their sufficiently fast decay (Assumption 1). While motivated, this may be strong in practice; robustness to misspecification is not analyzed. (ii) Concentration is handled via a generic ψ, but results and constants are clearer for sub-Gaussian cases. (iii) The regret definition credits only the highest-fidelity means; while appropriate for long-run outcomes (ads), some applications may prefer alternative notions (the authors briefly discuss this).
- Empirics: Simulations support the claims and illustrate fidelity allocation, but real-world experiments or broader baselines (e.g., variants that treat low fidelities as noisy proxies without explicit bias bounds, cost-normalized UCB, racing/Successive Rejects with fidelities) would strengthen the case.

Clarity:
- The paper is clearly written and well organized. The choice of γ(m) and the fidelity selection rule are well motivated. Proof sketches are intuitive; full details are deferred appropriately. Minor suggestions: clarify how to set ρ in practice; provide guidance on estimating or bounding ζ(m) from pilot data; and explicitly discuss sensitivity to mis-specified ζ(m).

Originality:
- The formalization of multi-fidelity bandits with known bias envelopes and costs, the cost-weighted regret notion that credits only highest-fidelity means, and the MF-UCB design and analysis are, to my knowledge, novel within the stochastic MAB literature at the time. The clear separation from budgeted bandits and weak/strong labeler settings is well articulated.

Significance:
- The work addresses a practically important regime where cheap biased proxies exist (ads, algorithm selection with subsampling, simulation-based evaluation). The theoretical results show genuine improvements over single-fidelity baselines, and the algorithm is simple and potentially widely usable. The need for known ζ(m) may limit immediate plug-and-play adoption; nonetheless, the conceptual and analytical advances are valuable and likely to spur follow-on work (as seen in GP-based extensions).

Pros:
- Clear and compelling problem formalization with realistic cost/reward trade-offs.
- Simple, principled algorithm that adapts exploration across fidelities.
- Strong theory: upper bounds, lower bounds, and a near-optimality characterization.
- Good intuition linking fidelity selection to information-cost trade-offs.
- Clean writing and helpful partition-based analysis.

Cons:
- Requires known, decaying bias bounds ζ(m); robustness and estimation are not treated.
- Lower bound mismatch on a subset of arms (K(m)7) due to conservative switching; optimal switching remains open.
- Experimental validation is limited to synthetic data and a narrow set of baselines.
- The regret definition, while motivated, may not capture all application regimes.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add experiments with real or semi-synthetic tasks (e.g., hyperparameter tuning with subsampled datasets; ad-click logs with controlled exposure windows), and compare against additional baselines such as cost-normalized UCB, proxy-as-noisy-sensor reduction, and racing strategies.
- Provide a procedure to estimate ζ(m) (confidence envelopes on bias) from pilot data or via conservative concentration bounds, and analyze robustness to misspecification.
- Discuss adaptive tuning of γ(m) without explicit ζ(m), or data-driven switching heuristics, and their theoretical risks.
- Explore alternative regret notions relevant to settings where low-fidelity pulls yield utility (or different penalties), and clarify how MF-UCB would adapt.
- Consider a Thompson sampling analogue and whether the partition-based analysis extends.

Overall recommendation: Strong paper with a novel and well-motivated formulation, clear algorithmic contribution, and solid theory. With expanded empirical validation and some discussion on estimating/relaxing ζ(m), this work would be a valuable addition to NIPS.
