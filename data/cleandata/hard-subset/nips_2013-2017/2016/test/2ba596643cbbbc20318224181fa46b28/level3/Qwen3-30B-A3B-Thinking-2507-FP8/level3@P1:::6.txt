

This paper introduces a novel multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit framework where each arm has M fidelities with varying costs and quality approximations of the true reward. The authors formalize a cost-weighted regret definition that accounts for the different costs of using various fidelities, which is a significant departure from standard bandit regret definitions. They propose MF-UCB, an algorithm that adaptively chooses which fidelity to use for each arm, theoretically analyzing its performance with a regret bound that demonstrates better scaling than standard UCB when lower fidelities are sufficiently cheap and informative. The paper provides a matching lower bound showing MF-UCB is nearly optimal under certain conditions and validates the approach with synthetic experiments.

The paper builds on the rich literature of multi-armed bandits (e.g., UCB algorithms [3]) but extends it to a practical setting where cheaper approximations to expensive experiments are available. This is a meaningful extension with clear motivation in domains like online advertising (where short displays approximate long-run click-through rates) and algorithm selection (where cheaper computations approximate full algorithm performance). The authors properly contextualize their work against related multi-fidelity optimization methods [7, 10, 16] which don't address the exploration-exploitation trade-off inherent to bandit problems.

The main strengths of this paper are its clear problem formalization, the novel regret definition, the elegant theoretical analysis showing MF-UCB's near-optimality, and the empirical validation demonstrating significant improvements over naive UCB strategies. The partitioning of arms into K(m) sets (based on fidelity quality) is particularly insightful and well-motivated, providing a clear explanation for why MF-UCB outperforms standard approaches. The lower bound analysis is thorough and carefully constructed to match the algorithm's performance.

However, the paper's main weakness is the assumption of known ζ^(m) values (the deviation of m-th fidelity mean from the highest fidelity mean). The paper states "the ζ(m)'s are known," but doesn't address how these values would be obtained in practice. In real-world applications, these deviations would typically need to be estimated, which introduces a new challenge not addressed in the paper. This is a significant practical limitation that weakens the applicability of the proposed approach. The authors should discuss how to estimate ζ^(m) and analyze the impact of estimation errors on regret performance.

These minor issues are easily fixable and don't significantly impact the main contributions, but they do affect the paper's clarity and precision.

The paper makes a clear contribution to bandit theory with practical relevance. The theoretical analysis is sound and the experimental results support the claims. The main concern about the known ζ^(m) assumption is important but could potentially be addressed in future work. Given the novelty of the problem setting, the clean theoretical analysis, and the practical motivation, the paper makes a strong contribution to the bandit literature.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and the technical content is accessible to the NIPS audience. The authors have done an excellent job of connecting their theoretical analysis to the practical implications of their approach. The minor issues noted above are common in technical papers and don't detract significantly from the main contribution.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The experimental results complement the theoretical analysis effectively. The clarity is excellent, with clear definitions and well-structured arguments. The originality is significant - while some multi-fidelity work exists in optimization, this is the first to properly formalize and analyze the regret in the multi-fidelity bandit setting. The significance is high, as the problem has clear applications in online advertising, algorithm selection, and other domains where expensive experiments can be approximated with cheaper alternatives.

I recommend acceptance. The paper addresses an important gap in bandit theory with a well-motivated problem, sound theoretical analysis, and practical implications. The main weakness (known ζ^(m)) is a significant limitation but not a fatal flaw, and the authors could potentially address this in future work or by acknowledging it as an open problem. The minor issues noted are easily fixable and don't undermine the main contribution.
