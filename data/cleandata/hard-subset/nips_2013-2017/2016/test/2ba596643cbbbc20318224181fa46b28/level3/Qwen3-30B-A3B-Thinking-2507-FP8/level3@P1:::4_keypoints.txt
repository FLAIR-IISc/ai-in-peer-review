- Introduces a multi-fidelity bandit framework and MF-UCB algorithm with theoretical regret bounds and a matching lower bound, extending K-armed bandits for settings with cheaper biased approximations.
- Strength: well-motivated problem with clear applications, a theoretically grounded algorithm, and empirical results showing MF-UCB outperforms standard UCB on synthetic tasks.
- Strength: technical analysis is sound; regret bound explains how cheaper fidelities help eliminate suboptimal arms and the lower bound shows near-optimality under certain conditions.
- Weakness: confusing and inconsistent notation (e.g., variable 's' defined after first use and seldom used), which harms readability.
- Weakness: unclear and incomplete cost structure — the regret formulation fails to properly account for the cost–reward tradeoff central to multi-fidelity settings.
- Major flaw: defines the optimal arm by highest mean reward regardless of cost rather than by reward-to-cost ratio, undermining the multi-fidelity premise and theoretical contribution.
- Criticism of example: the advertising use-case misrepresents the problem by invoking display-time correlations and non-stationarity that are irrelevant to the proposed framework.
- Recommendation: shift to a sample-complexity or cost-complexity formulation (best-arm identification under budget) to better capture practical testing and cost constraints.
- Presentation issues: writing is unwieldy, partitioning into K(m) obscures intuition, and experiments lack sufficient detail and analysis of when MF-UCB’s advantage holds.
- Evaluation by criteria: Quality — theory mostly sound but flawed regret formulation; Clarity — poor notation and misleading examples; Originality — novel framework but related work comparisons are weak; Significance — important problem but current analysis doesn’t demonstrate practical impact.
- Overall: promising core idea but requires substantial revision to fix the regret formulation, improve clarity, and better align analysis with real-world cost-constrained objectives before publication.
