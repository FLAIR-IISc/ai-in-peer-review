### Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

**Summary**

This paper introduces and formalizes the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit (MF-MAB) problem, a novel and practical variant of the stochastic K-armed bandit setting. In this framework, each of the K arms can be evaluated at M different "fidelities." Pulling an arm at a lower fidelity is cheaper but provides a reward that is a biased approximation of the true, high-fidelity reward. The goal is to minimize a cost-weighted pseudo-regret given a total budget. The authors propose the Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB) algorithm, which extends the classic UCB principle to this setting. The core idea of MF-UCB is to leverage cheap, low-fidelity evaluations to quickly explore all arms and eliminate those that are clearly suboptimal, thereby reserving the expensive, high-fidelity evaluations for a smaller set of promising candidates.

The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of MF-UCB, including an upper bound on its regret. This bound demonstrates that the algorithm adapts to the problem structure, incurring costs proportional to the cheapest fidelity at which an arm can be identified as suboptimal. This is a significant improvement over a naive UCB strategy that would only use the most expensive fidelity, whose regret scales with the highest cost for all suboptimal arms. The authors complement this result with a problem-specific lower bound, showing that MF-UCB is near-optimal for a large class of arms. The theoretical findings are supported by simulations on synthetic problems that clearly illustrate the benefits of the multi-fidelity approach over standard UCB.

**Strengths (Pros)**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The formalization of the multi-fidelity bandit problem is a significant contribution. This setting is well-motivated by numerous real-world applications (e.g., online advertising, hyperparameter tuning, scientific experimentation) where cheap approximations are available. This work opens up a new and promising research direction by elegantly bridging the gap between bandit optimization and multi-fidelity methods, extending the applicability of bandit algorithms to more practical, cost-sensitive scenarios.

*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The proposed MF-UCB algorithm is intuitive and cleverly designed, especially the fidelity selection criterion (Eq. 4), which strikes a balance between gathering cheap information and committing to more expensive, accurate evaluations. The theoretical analysis is rigorous. The main regret bound (Theorem 2) provides a clear and interpretable result that quantifies the benefit of the multi-fidelity approach. The inclusion of a lower-bound analysis (Theorem 4) is a major strength, as it establishes the near-optimality of the proposed method and provides a deeper understanding of the problem's fundamental difficulty.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and situates it within the existing literature. The problem formulation is precise, and the algorithm is described clearly. The analytical sections are supported by good intuition, and the use of the arm partitions `K(m)` is an effective device for explaining the algorithm's behavior and structuring the proofs.

**Weaknesses (Cons and Suggestions for Improvement)**

*   **Gap between Upper and Lower Bounds:** As the authors commendably point out, a gap exists between the derived upper and lower bounds for a subset of arms (the `K(m)7` set). These are arms that are suboptimal at a higher fidelity `m`, but whose lower-fidelity means are misleadingly close to the optimal value. While the authors provide a clear explanation for why MF-UCB is not optimal for these specific cases, this constitutes a minor theoretical limitation. It would be interesting to see a discussion on whether alternative fidelity-switching criteria could potentially close this gap in future work.

*   **Strength of Assumptions:** The analysis relies on Assumption 1, which posits that the bias bounds `ζ(m)` decay sufficiently fast relative to the cost increase. This ensures that introducing new fidelities is always beneficial. While this condition is reasonable, it may not hold in all practical scenarios. The paper would be strengthened by a brief discussion of the algorithm's behavior or performance guarantees if this assumption is relaxed or violated.

*   **Synthetic Nature of Experiments:** The simulations effectively validate the theoretical claims and demonstrate the advantage of MF-UCB over a standard UCB baseline. However, the experiments are limited to synthetic data. The paper's impact could be significantly amplified by demonstrating its performance on a more realistic problem, even a semi-synthetic one. For instance, an experiment on a hyperparameter optimization task for a standard machine learning model, where fidelities correspond to training on different-sized data subsets or for a varying number of epochs, would be a compelling addition.

---
**Summary of Pros and Cons for Acceptance:**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Introduces and formalizes a novel, important, and practical problem (MF-MAB).
2.  Proposes an elegant and intuitive algorithm (MF-UCB) that effectively addresses the problem.
3.  Provides strong and comprehensive theoretical results, including both upper and lower regret bounds that establish near-optimality.
4.  The paper is very clearly written and presented, making complex ideas accessible.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
1.  The experiments are purely synthetic, and the paper would benefit from evaluation in a more realistic setting.
2.  There is a small gap between the upper and lower bounds for certain problem instances.

**Conclusion**

This is an excellent paper that makes a strong contribution to the multi-armed bandit literature. The introduction of the multi-fidelity setting is both novel and highly relevant to practical applications. The proposed algorithm is simple and effective, and it is backed by solid theoretical guarantees. Despite the minor weaknesses regarding the experimental evaluation and the small theoretical gap, the strengths of this work—its originality, technical quality, and clarity—are overwhelming. This paper is a clear accept and is likely to inspire significant follow-up research.
