This paper introduces a multi-fidelity stochastic K-armed bandit model in which each arm can be queried at M fidelities: higher fidelities are more accurate but more expensive, and lower fidelities yield biased approximations. The authors propose a cost-weighted regret that credits the highest-fidelity mean regardless of the fidelity played, and present MF-UCB, a UCB-style algorithm that adaptively selects both arm and fidelity via thresholded confidence radii. They derive a regret upper bound that allocates most effort to cheap fidelities for clearly suboptimal arms and reserves expensive plays for near-optimal arms, and prove a complementary lower bound highlighting a gap on a specific subset of arms. Simulations on synthetic problems corroborate the theoretical behavior. The work relates to UCB-style bandits, budgeted bandits, and multi-fidelity optimization (e.g., NIPS work on GP bandits with multi-fidelity evaluations) and differs by explicitly modeling fidelity-dependent bias and introducing a tailored regret.

Quality: The technical development is solid and largely self-consistent. The upper bound, fidelity-switching criterion, and lower bound are interesting and, to my knowledge, novel in this form. The analysis clearly explains when MF-UCB beats single-fidelity UCB. The main weakness is an assumption critical to correctness and constants: the bias envelopes ζ(m) (called ε(m) in places) are known. It is unclear how these are obtained in practice and how sensitive MF-UCB is to misspecification. Please discuss practical estimation (e.g., pilot calibration, domain-provided upper bounds), robustness (worst-case safe choice), or an algorithm that learns/upper-bounds ζ(m) online. Also comment on Assumption 1 and its necessity/scope. Experiments are limited to synthetic cases; even a small-scale real or semi-synthetic study and baselines (e.g., budgeted bandits, variants using only top two fidelities) would strengthen the case.

Clarity: Generally well written, but notation is heavy; a running example mapping ζ, λ, γ to the ad scenario would help. Please fix the minor issues below.

Originality: The setting, regret, and analysis appear original and relevant relative to prior NIPS work on weak/strong labelers and multi-fidelity GP bandits.

Significance: Addresses a practical and timely problem; the algorithm and analysis should spur follow-up on learning bias envelopes and broader regret notions.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel formulation and regret; principled algorithm with near-optimal guarantees
- Clear insight into how to use cheap fidelities to prune arms
- Complementary lower bound; good positioning vs prior work

Cons for acceptance:
- Strong known-ζ(m) assumption without guidance/robustness
- Limited empirical validation; no real data
- Some technical conditions (Assumption 1) under-discussed
