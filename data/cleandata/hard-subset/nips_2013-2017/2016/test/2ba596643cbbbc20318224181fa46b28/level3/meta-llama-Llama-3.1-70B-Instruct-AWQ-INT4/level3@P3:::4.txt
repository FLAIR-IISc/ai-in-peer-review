This paper proposes a novel framework for studying exploration-exploitation trade-offs in the presence of cheaper approximations to a desired experiment, which is formalized as a multi-fidelity bandit. The authors develop a new algorithm, MF-UCB, based on upper confidence bound techniques, which adapts to the sequence of available approximations and costs, achieving better regret than naive strategies that ignore the approximations.

The paper is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the problem and the proposed solution. The technical contributions of the paper are significant, including a regret analysis and a lower bound. The experiments on synthetic problems demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm, MF-UCB, which outperforms UCB.

However, there are some issues with the paper that need to be addressed. The notation is confusing and dispersed, which harms readability. For example, the variable s is defined after its first use and then barely used. The advertising example is misleading, and references to display time correlations and non-stationarity are inappropriate for the paper's scope.

The regret analysis is questionable, as the optimal policy should maximize reward/cost, but the paper treats the best arm as the highest mean regardless of cost, potentially hiding the costs of low-fidelity sampling. A sample-complexity (cost-complexity) analysis focused on finding the best arm with high probability under budget/constraints would be more appropriate and motivated by real-world testing scenarios.

The writing is unwieldy in parts and needs streamlining and clearer organization. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the related work, particularly in the context of multi-fidelity methods for optimization.

Overall, while the paper has some significant contributions, it is not ready for publication in its current form. The authors need to address the issues mentioned above and provide a more polished and well-organized manuscript.
