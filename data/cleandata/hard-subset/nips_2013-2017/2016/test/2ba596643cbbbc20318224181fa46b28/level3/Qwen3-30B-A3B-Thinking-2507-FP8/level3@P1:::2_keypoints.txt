- Introduces a novel multi-fidelity bandit framework where each arm has M fidelities with tradeoffs between cost and biased low-fidelity estimates.  
- Formalizes the setting: lower fidelities incur lower cost but provide biased estimates of the highest-fidelity reward.  
- Proposes MF-UCB, a UCB adaptation that allocates pulls across fidelities to minimize cost-weighted regret.  
- Provides a significant theoretical regret bound showing MF-UCB improves over standard UCB by exploiting low-fidelity screening (involving set Kg).  
- Complements upper bounds with a lower bound showing MF-UCB is nearly optimal under certain assumptions.  
- Builds on classical bandit work (Lai & Robbins, Auer et al.) and extends multi-fidelity ideas previously studied in optimization to bandit regret analysis.  
- Distinguishes this work from budgeted bandits and multi-fidelity optimization by focusing on exploration-exploitation with multiple fidelities.  
- Theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-motivated, with clear intuition about fidelity allocation.  
- Synthetic experiments convincingly show MF-UCB outperforms vanilla UCB in practice.  
- Supplementary materials contain numerous typos and mathematical errors that undermine credibility and make proofs hard to follow.  
- Use of “up-to-a-multiplicative-constant” notation is misleading because hidden constants (e.g., ρ) affect practical performance; exact inequalities and parameter guidance are needed.  
- Practical applicability is under-discussed: the algorithm assumes known ζ(m) (biases) without methods for estimation or robustness analysis.  
- Main paper is clear, but supplement errors and lack of practical guidance significantly reduce overall quality.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel formalism, strong theoretical contributions, solid algorithmic design, and convincing experiments.  
- Arguments against acceptance: supplementary typos/math errors, obscuring notation and implementation details, and insufficient treatment of practical limitations (especially ζ(m) estimation); recommend acceptance with major revisions.
