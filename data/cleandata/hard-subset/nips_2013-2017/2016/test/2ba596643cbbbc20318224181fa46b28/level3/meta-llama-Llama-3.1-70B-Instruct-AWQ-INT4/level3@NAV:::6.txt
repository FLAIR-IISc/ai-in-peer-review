The paper "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit" studies a variant of the classical stochastic K-armed bandit problem where observing the outcome of each arm is expensive, but cheap approximations to this outcome are available. The authors formalize this task as a multi-fidelity bandit, where at each time step, the forecaster may choose to play an arm at any one of M fidelities. The highest fidelity (desired outcome) expends cost λ, while the m fidelity (an approximation) expends λ < λ and returns a biased estimate of the highest fidelity.

The authors propose a novel upper confidence bound procedure for this setting, called MF-UCB, and prove that it naturally adapts to the sequence of available approximations and costs, thus attaining better regret than naive strategies that ignore the approximations. They also derive a lower bound and show that MF-UCB is nearly optimal under certain conditions.

Strengths:

* The paper provides a novel formalism for studying bandit tasks when one has access to multiple fidelities for each arm.
* The authors propose a new algorithm, MF-UCB, that adapts the classical Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) strategies to the multi-fidelity setting.
* The paper provides a theoretical characterization of the performance of MF-UCB, including a regret bound and a lower bound.
* The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of MF-UCB through simulations on synthetic problems.

Weaknesses:

* The paper assumes known ε^(m) (deviation of mth-fidelity mean from highest-fidelity mean); it is unclear how these values are obtained in practice and whether the assumption can be relaxed.
* The paper does not provide a clear explanation of how the choice of γ(m) affects the performance of MF-UCB.
* The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of MF-UCB.
