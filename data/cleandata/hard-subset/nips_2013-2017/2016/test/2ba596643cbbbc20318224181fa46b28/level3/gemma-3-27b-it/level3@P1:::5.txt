## Review of The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit

This paper introduces a novel and well-motivated extension to the classic multi-armed bandit (MAB) problem, termed the multi-fidelity bandit. The core idea—leveraging cheaper, albeit noisier, approximations of arm rewards to accelerate learning and reduce overall cost—is particularly relevant to numerous practical applications, including online advertising and algorithm selection. The paper provides a solid theoretical analysis, demonstrating near-optimality of the proposed MF-UCB algorithm, complemented by empirical validation through simulations. 

The MAB problem has been extensively studied, with foundational work by Robbins [11] and subsequent advancements like the UCB algorithm [3], which achieves optimal regret bounds. This work builds upon this foundation, acknowledging the limitations of traditional MAB formulations when reward acquisition is costly. The authors astutely observe that in many real-world scenarios, cheaper approximations of arm rewards are readily available (e.g., shorter ad display times, smaller training datasets). This observation forms the basis of the MF bandit framework. Related work in multi-fidelity optimization [7, 10] and reinforcement learning [6] is discussed, but the authors convincingly argue that these approaches do not address the exploration-exploitation dilemma inherent to the bandit setting. Recent work on budgeted bandits [13, 15] and active learning [16] are also appropriately distinguished, highlighting the unique contributions of this paper.

The strength of this work lies in its rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors introduce a cost-weighted notion of regret and develop MF-UCB, an adaptation of the UCB algorithm tailored to the multi-fidelity setting. The regret bound for MF-UCB, presented in Theorem 2, demonstrates a clear advantage over naive strategies that ignore the available approximations, particularly when the number of optimal arms is small. The accompanying lower bound (Theorem 4) further strengthens the claim of near-optimality. The analysis is detailed and insightful, carefully considering the impact of approximation biases and the trade-off between exploration and exploitation at different fidelities. The introduction of the K(m) partitions to categorize arms based on their behavior across fidelities is a particularly clever technique that aids in the analysis and provides intuition about the algorithm's behavior. 

The clarity of the paper is commendable. The problem is well-defined, the algorithm is clearly explained, and the theoretical results are presented with sufficient detail. The notation is consistent, and the writing style is accessible without sacrificing rigor. The inclusion of a simulation section is valuable, providing empirical evidence to support the theoretical findings. The simulation results demonstrate that MF-UCB consistently outperforms the standard UCB algorithm in the tested scenarios. The discussion of limitations and potential future work (e.g., handling cases where lower fidelities provide misleading signals, exploring alternative regret definitions) adds to the paper’s overall credibility.

However, the paper is not without minor weaknesses. While the theoretical analysis is thorough, it relies on several assumptions (e.g., concentration inequalities, Assumption 1) that might not hold in all practical settings. The sensitivity of the algorithm’s performance to violations of these assumptions could be further investigated. Furthermore, the simulations, while helpful, are somewhat limited in scope. Exploring a wider range of problem instances and comparing MF-UCB to other relevant baselines could provide a more comprehensive evaluation.  The gap between the upper and lower bounds, specifically regarding arms within K(m)7, warrants further investigation as acknowledged by the authors. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* **Novelty:** Introduces a relevant and previously unexplored extension to the classic MAB problem.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** Provides a rigorous theoretical analysis with clear regret bounds and a near-optimality result.
* **Clarity:**  Well-written and clearly presented, making the paper accessible to a broad audience.
* **Potential Impact:** The proposed MF bandit framework has the potential to be widely applicable in various domains.
* **Empirical Validation:**  Simulation results support the theoretical claims.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* **Assumptions:**  The theoretical analysis relies on assumptions that may not always hold in practice.
* **Limited Simulations:** The simulation section could be more extensive and include a wider range of benchmarks.
* **Gap between bounds:** The difference between upper and lower bounds in certain cases needs further work.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of bandit optimization. The problem is well-motivated, the proposed solution is theoretically sound and empirically validated, and the presentation is clear and concise. Despite minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper far outweigh its limitations. I recommend acceptance to NIPS 2016.
