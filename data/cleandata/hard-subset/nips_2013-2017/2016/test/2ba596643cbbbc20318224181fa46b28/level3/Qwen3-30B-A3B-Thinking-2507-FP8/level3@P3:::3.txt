

# Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit" (NIPS 2016)

This paper introduces a novel variant of the multi-armed bandit problem where each arm can be observed at multiple fidelities (M ≥ 2), with lower fidelity observations being cheaper but introducing bounded bias relative to the highest fidelity (most expensive) observation. The authors formalize this as a cost-aware bandit problem where the goal is to minimize regret while accounting for different costs associated with different fidelities. They propose MF-UCB, a UCB-based algorithm that appropriately widens confidence intervals to account for the fidelity-induced bias, and provide theoretical regret bounds and empirical results demonstrating its superiority over standard UCB.

The paper's main contribution is a clean formalization of the multi-fidelity bandit problem, which extends the classical bandit framework to incorporate cheaper but biased approximations. The authors correctly situate their work within the bandit literature (citing key UCB papers [3,9]) and related multi-fidelity optimization work [7,10,16]. While the algorithm itself builds on standard UCB principles, the problem formulation is novel and addresses an important practical consideration in applications where expensive experiments can be approximated with cheaper, albeit biased, alternatives.

The paper's strengths include clear motivation from practical domains (online advertising, algorithm selection) and a theoretically sound analysis. The regret bounds, while complex, demonstrate that MF-UCB can achieve significantly better performance than standard UCB when the sets of "promising" arms identified at lower fidelities are small (|Kg| << K) and the cost ratio λ(1)/λ(2) is favorable. The empirical results on synthetic problems support these theoretical claims, showing that MF-UCB consistently outperforms UCB with steeper convergence rates.

However, the paper has several notable weaknesses. The theoretical regret bounds are messy and difficult to interpret, with the paper admitting the bounds are "hard to interpret" in the prompt. The central assumption that lower fidelity observations introduce bias rather than increased variance is questionable. While the paper justifies this with algorithm selection examples, the online advertising example (short ad displays) is weak - in this case, lower fidelity would more naturally be modeled as increased variance (due to shorter display time) rather than bias. The paper does not adequately address why bias is a better model than variance for these applications.

The simulation results claim a 3-4× improvement over UCB, but the parameter choices (Appendix C) lack justification. The reported performance gains may be heavily dependent on specific choices of ζ(m), and without a thorough exploration of parameter sensitivity, the claimed improvement is difficult to validate. The paper's main novelty is indeed the problem formulation, while the algorithm itself is a straightforward adaptation of standard optimism-in-the-face-of-uncertainty principles.

The connection between the model and motivating applications is partially convincing but ultimately weak. The paper acknowledges that the bias model is "explicitly account[ed] for" rather than treated as increased variance, but fails to provide compelling evidence that this is the appropriate modeling choice for most applications. The clinical trials example in Section 7 highlights this disconnect well - in such cases, lower fidelity might not even yield rewards, making the bias model particularly questionable.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound but the theoretical results are not as compelling as they could be due to the complex regret bounds. The clarity is good overall, though the messy regret expressions reduce readability. Originality is moderate - the problem formulation is novel, but the algorithm is not. Significance is somewhat limited by the questionable bias model and the weak application connection.

Pro arguments for acceptance: Novel problem formulation, clear theoretical analysis, practical motivation, empirical evidence supporting the claims.

Con arguments against acceptance: Questionable bias model, messy regret bounds, weak connection between theory and applications, unverified simulation parameter choices, algorithmic novelty is limited.

This paper makes a valuable contribution to extending bandit theory to multi-fidelity settings, but the weak justification for the bias model and the questionable application examples undermine the significance of the work. The authors should reconsider their modeling choice (bias vs. variance) and provide stronger justification for the bias model, or alternatively reframe the problem as a multi-fidelity bandit with increased variance rather than bias. With these revisions, the paper would be a stronger contribution to the field.

Overall, while the problem formulation is interesting, the paper's weaknesses in modeling assumptions and application justification prevent it from being a compelling contribution to NIPS. The technical content is sound but the gap between the model and its intended applications is too large to be considered a significant advance.
