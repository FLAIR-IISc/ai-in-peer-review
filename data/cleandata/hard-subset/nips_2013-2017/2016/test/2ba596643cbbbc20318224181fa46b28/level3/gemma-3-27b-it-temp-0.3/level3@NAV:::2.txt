## Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

This paper introduces a novel multi-armed bandit (MAB) setting where evaluating arms at higher fidelity is more expensive but provides more accurate reward estimates. The authors formalize this as a multi-fidelity bandit problem and propose MF-UCB, an upper confidence bound algorithm tailored to this setting. The core contribution lies in demonstrating that MF-UCB adapts to the available fidelity levels and achieves improved regret compared to naive strategies that ignore fidelity differences. The paper provides both upper and lower bounds on the cumulative pseudo-regret, establishing near-optimality under certain conditions.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presents the problem, proposed solution, and theoretical analysis. The motivation is compelling, with relevant examples in online advertising and algorithm selection. The introduction effectively builds intuition for the benefits of exploiting lower-fidelity approximations to efficiently eliminate suboptimal arms. The mathematical formulation is rigorous, and the proof sketches provide a good overview of the analysis. The inclusion of simulation results further strengthens the claims, demonstrating the practical advantage of MF-UCB.

However, the paper suffers from a significant issue: the supplementary material (proofs) contains numerous typos and minor errors. While the core ideas are understandable, these errors create unnecessary confusion and hinder a thorough understanding of the technical details. Addressing these errors is crucial for improving the paper’s readability and impact. Furthermore, the authors’ use of “up-to-a-multiplicative-constant” notation is misleading. The constants hidden within (e.g., ρ) are algorithm parameters, and presenting exact inequalities would enhance clarity and precision. 

A key limitation is the unclear practical applicability of MF-UCB. The algorithm’s performance relies on accurately estimating the fidelity ζ, which may be challenging in real-world scenarios. A discussion of these practical limitations and potential mitigation strategies (e.g., adaptive estimation of ζ) would significantly strengthen the paper.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** Introduces a new and relevant MAB setting with practical applications.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** Provides solid theoretical guarantees with both upper and lower bounds on regret.
*   **Clarity:** The main paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow.
*   **Significance:** Advances the state-of-the-art in MAB research and offers a potentially valuable tool for resource allocation in various domains.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   **Supplementary Material:** The numerous errors in the proofs significantly detract from the paper’s quality.
*   **Practical Concerns:** The reliance on accurate fidelity estimation raises concerns about real-world applicability.
*   **Notational Clarity:** The use of “up-to-a-multiplicative-constant” obscures important details.



**Overall:** Despite the issues with the supplementary material and practical considerations, the theoretical contribution of this paper is significant. I recommend **acceptance with major revisions**, contingent upon a thorough correction of the errors in the supplementary material and a more detailed discussion of the algorithm’s practical limitations and potential solutions. Addressing the notational clarity would also be beneficial.
