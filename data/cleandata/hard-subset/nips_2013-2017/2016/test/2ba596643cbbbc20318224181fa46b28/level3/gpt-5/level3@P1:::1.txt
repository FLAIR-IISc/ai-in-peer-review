Summary and relation to prior work
The paper formulates a stochastic K-armed bandit with multiple fidelity levels per arm. Each fidelity m yields samples from a biased distribution whose mean differs from the highest-fidelity mean by at most a known bound ζ(m), with ζ(1) > … > ζ(M) = 0. Pulling fidelity m incurs cost λ(m), increasing with m. The objective is to minimize a capital-weighted pseudo-regret under a total capital budget Λ by leveraging cheap low-fidelity pulls to eliminate suboptimal arms and reserving expensive high-fidelity pulls for promising arms.

The authors propose MF-UCB, which maintains per-(arm,fidelity) upper bounds Bk,t(m) = X̄k,s(m) + ψ−1(ρ log t / s) + ζ(m) and uses Bk,t = minm Bk,t(m) to pick the arm. The fidelity is chosen via thresholds γ(m) = ψ−1( (λ(m)/λ(m+1)) ψ(ζ(m)) ), playing at the smallest m where uncertainty at that fidelity exceeds γ(m). The analysis yields an upper bound on regret scaling like log(nΛ) times, for each arm k, λ(Jk)/ψ(Δ(Jk)), where Jk is the highest fidelity at which k is eliminated and Δ(m) is an appropriate gap at fidelity m. Compared to high-fidelity-only UCB (regret ∼ λ(M)/ψ(Δ(M))), MF-UCB saves a factor roughly λ(Jk)/λ(M) whenever elimination can occur at lower fidelities. A lower bound (for Bernoulli rewards) matches MF-UCB’s scaling on a subset of arms and reveals a gap on arms whose lower-fidelity gaps are very small but positive, for which a more aggressive low-fidelity elimination could in principle be cheaper.

This contribution is distinct from: (i) classical UCB (Auer et al. 2002; Lai & Robbins 1985; Bubeck & Cesa-Bianchi 2012), (ii) budgeted bandits that optimize reward-per-cost (e.g., Tran-Thanh et al. 2010; Xia et al. 2015), and (iii) multi-fidelity optimization in BO/kriging that lacks regret analyses. It also differs from weak/strong labeler active learning (Zhang & Chaudhuri 2015), where the objective is not cumulative regret.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The algorithmic design is principled—upper bounds at each fidelity are safely shifted by ζ(m), and the switching rule γ(m) optimally (for the given assumptions) trades off eliminating arms cheaply versus avoiding over-investment at low fidelities. The regret proof carefully partitions arms by the fidelity where they can be excluded and bounds plays at each fidelity. The lower bound uses a change-of-measure construction and clarifies where MF-UCB is tight versus loose.
- Assumptions: Assumption 1 on the decay of ζ(m) relative to ψ is pivotal for appealing comparisons and to dominate lower-fidelity costs; it is reasonable for sub-Gaussian tails with exponentially decaying ζ(m), but its general validity is unclear and it feels opaque in the main text.
- Experiments: Synthetic results corroborate the qualitative story (most budget spent at low fidelities on bad arms; better slopes than UCB). However, the empirical section is thin and lacks sensitivity analyses and real-world cases.

Clarity
- The main ideas are good, but several presentation choices make the results hard to parse:
  - The regret definition and the choice qt = μ(M)It deserve a fuller justification up front, with contrasting alternatives and implications.
  - The sets K(m), the Δ(m) gaps, and the partition Jk require a simple two-fidelity corollary that spells out the bound in standard sub-Gaussian form (ψ(x) ∼ x^2), with explicit constants suppressed, to aid intuition.
  - A brief high-probability statement that N ≈ Λ/λ(M) for MF-UCB would help interpret the capital-weighted regime.
  - Assumption 1 should be motivated with examples and a short remark on when it does not hold.
- Minor issues/typos: “near-optimal” claim should be qualified; superfluous period before “for all m < M”; use standard braces rather than [[k]] for set notation.

Originality
- The multi-fidelity formalism with known bias bounds and costs, the MF-UCB construction with ζ-shifted UCBs and fidelity-dependent switching thresholds, and the paired upper/lower bounds are, to my knowledge, novel in the stochastic bandit literature. The work clearly differs from budgeted bandits and multi-fidelity optimization without cumulative regret.

Significance
- The setting is practically relevant (engineering simulators, A/B testing at different granularities) and the method advances the state of the art by turning approximations into provable savings in regret. If ζ(m) decays fast and λ(m) grows, the potential gains are substantial when K is large and only a small subset of arms is near-optimal.
- Limitations: sensitivity to ζ(m) (known, correct bounds), the restrictive Assumption 1, and the lower-bound gap on certain arms.

Main concerns and suggestions
- Regret formulation: The capital-weighted pseudo-regret R(Λ) = Λμ* − ∑t λ(mt)μ(M)It makes the cost effectively multiply the value of each decision. This may be unnatural in applications where low-fidelity actions yield no real reward (simulations) or where cost should be penalized additively. Please (i) discuss alternative regret notions (e.g., maximize ∑t μ(M)It subject to ∑t λ(mt) ≤ Λ; or utility ∑t μ(M)It − α∑t λ(mt); or per-unit-cost regret), (ii) explain which applications match your choice, and (iii) clarify how the analysis would change for the additive-cost objective.
- Assumption 1: Provide intuition, examples, and a practical check. If ζ(m) do not satisfy it, can one select a subsequence of fidelities that does (i.e., compress M by skipping near-duplicate fidelities) without hurting guarantees? An explicit “fidelity pruning” lemma would strengthen robustness.
- Robustness to ζ mis-specification: Discuss how performance degrades if ζ(m) are unknown or misspecified, and whether one can estimate conservative bounds online.
- Lower-bound gap: The identified gap for K(m)7 suggests that a more cost-aware switching rule could, in some regimes, stop at a cheaper fidelity even when Δ(`) is small but positive. Consider an adaptive rule that compares estimated λ(`)/ψ(Δ̂(`)) across fidelities with confidence safeguards; or a two-phase scheme that occasionally verifies suboptimality at a cheaper fidelity when the projected cost is lower.
- Clarity of Theorem 2: Add a corollary for M=2, sub-Gaussian ψ(x)=cx^2, making the comparison to UCB explicit. Include a small table/figure mapping K(m) to where capital is spent, and a summarized statement like “MF-UCB spends O(λ(Jk)/Δ(Jk)^2 log nΛ) on arm k.”

Pros for acceptance
- Introduces a timely and useful formalism and an algorithm that leverages multi-fidelity information.
- Provides nontrivial theory: upper and lower bounds with insightful partition-based analysis.
- Demonstrates clear regimes where MF-UCB strictly improves over UCB.

Cons for acceptance
- Regret objective and assumptions are not sufficiently motivated; unclear generality across applications.
- Presentation could be significantly clearer; current notation burdens readers and program committee.
- Experiments are limited; no robustness studies or real data.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The contribution is technically solid and novel, and the idea is likely to influence subsequent work on cost-sensitive bandits. I would support acceptance if the authors (i) clarify and motivate the regret choice, (ii) discuss/mitigate Assumption 1 (e.g., fidelity pruning), (iii) expand the empirical section with sensitivity to ζ and λ and a real or semi-real case, and (iv) add simplified statements that make Theorem 2’s implications transparent.
