The paper "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit" proposes a novel framework for studying exploration-exploitation trade-offs in stochastic bandits with multiple fidelity levels. The authors introduce the MF-UCB algorithm, which adapts the classical Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) strategy to the multi-fidelity setting, and provide a regret bound and a lower bound for this problem. The paper claims that MF-UCB improves over high-fidelity-only UCB algorithms by leveraging cheaper approximations to the desired experiment.

The main idea of the paper is to model the multi-armed bandit problem with discrete fidelity levels, where each arm has multiple fidelities with known fidelity errors and costs. The authors formalize this problem as a multi-fidelity bandit, where the forecaster can choose to play an arm at any one of M fidelities. The highest fidelity has the desired outcome, while the lower fidelities provide biased estimates of the highest fidelity.

The authors propose the MF-UCB algorithm, which maintains an upper confidence bound for each arm and fidelity based on previous plays. The algorithm selects the arm with the highest upper bound and determines the fidelity to play based on the uncertainty interval and the cost of each fidelity. The authors prove that MF-UCB achieves a regret bound of O((λ(1)K + λ(2)|Kg|) log(Λ/λ(2))), where Kg is a small subset of arms with high expected reward that can be identified using plays at the cheaper low fidelity.

The paper also provides a lower bound for the multi-fidelity bandit problem, which shows that MF-UCB is near-optimal under certain conditions. The lower bound is proved using a change of measure argument and a partitioning of the set of arms into two sets, K(m)3 and K(m)7.

The authors compare MF-UCB with UCB and show that MF-UCB outperforms UCB on synthetic problems. They also provide simulations to illustrate the performance of MF-UCB and UCB on these problems.

Strengths:

* The paper studies an interesting and relevant trade-off between information, cost, and reward in stochastic bandits.
* The technical analysis appears solid, and the authors provide a regret bound and a lower bound for the multi-fidelity bandit problem.

Weaknesses:

* The paper lacks adequate explanation of the results and assumptions, making interpretation difficult.
* The regret formulation penalizes cost multiplicatively, which may be unnatural. The authors should consider additive cost penalization and discuss its implications.
* The motivation for the chosen reward/cost model is limited to an ad-display example, and the authors should provide broader motivation for this model.
* Assumption 1, which constrains the decay of the fidelity errors, is artificial and opaque. The authors should clarify the validity of this assumption and its impact on the results.
* The algorithm design seems tailored to specific fidelity configurations, and it is unclear whether Assumption 1 critically affects performance.
* The gap in the lower bound warrants further investigation and may require redesigning the strategy.
* The results, particularly Theorem 2 and the comparison with UCB, are hard to parse, and the authors should provide clearer and more explicit explanations.

Minor issues:

* The paper uses the term "near-optimal" without clear definition or explanation.
* There is an unnecessary period before "for all m < M" on line 126.
* The authors should use curly braces for set notation, as in [[k]], on line 179.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel framework for studying exploration-exploitation trade-offs in stochastic bandits with multiple fidelity levels.
* The authors provide a regret bound and a lower bound for the multi-fidelity bandit problem, which are technically solid and interesting.
* The paper compares MF-UCB with UCB and shows that MF-UCB outperforms UCB on synthetic problems.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper lacks adequate explanation of the results and assumptions, making interpretation difficult.
* The regret formulation penalizes cost multiplicatively, which may be unnatural.
* The motivation for the chosen reward/cost model is limited, and the authors should provide broader motivation for this model.
* Assumption 1 is artificial and opaque, and the authors should clarify its validity and impact on the results.

Overall, the paper proposes an interesting and relevant framework for studying exploration-exploitation trade-offs in stochastic bandits with multiple fidelity levels. While the technical analysis appears solid, the paper lacks adequate explanation of the results and assumptions, and the motivation for the chosen reward/cost model is limited. The authors should address these weaknesses and provide clearer and more explicit explanations of the results.
