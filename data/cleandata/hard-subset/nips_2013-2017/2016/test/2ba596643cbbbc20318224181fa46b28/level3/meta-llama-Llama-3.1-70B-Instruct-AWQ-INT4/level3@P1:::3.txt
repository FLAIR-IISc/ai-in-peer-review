The paper "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit" presents a novel framework for studying exploration-exploitation trade-offs in the presence of cheaper approximations to a desired experiment. The authors propose a multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit model, where each arm has multiple fidelities, and the lower fidelity is cheaper but introduces bounded bias in estimates. They develop a UCB-based algorithm, MF-UCB, that widens confidence intervals to account for fidelity-induced bias.

The authors provide theoretical regret bounds for MF-UCB, which are somewhat complex and hard to interpret. However, they argue that MF-UCB's regret is often much better than always-using highest fidelity UCB. They also present simulation results that claim empirical improvement of MF-UCB over UCB.

Strengths:

* The paper introduces a novel problem formulation that captures the idea of cheaper approximations to a desired experiment.
* The authors develop a UCB-based algorithm that adapts to the multi-fidelity setting.
* The paper provides theoretical regret bounds for MF-UCB, which demonstrates its performance guarantees.
* The simulation results show empirical improvement of MF-UCB over UCB.

Weaknesses:

* The modeling of low fidelity as increased bias rather than increased variance is questionable. This assumption may not be justified in all applications.
* The link between the model and motivating applications is weak. Some examples, such as algorithm selection, partially justify the bias model, but others, such as short ad displays, are unconvincing.
* The regret objective is unclear for certain applications, such as clinical trials.
* The simulation parameter choices lack justification, which makes it difficult to interpret the reported 3-4x improvement.

Originality:

* The paper's main novelty is the problem formulation, which captures the idea of cheaper approximations to a desired experiment.
* The algorithm itself uses standard optimism-in-the-face-of-uncertainty ideas, but the adaptation to the multi-fidelity setting is new.

Significance:

* The paper addresses a relevant problem in the field of bandit optimization, where cheaper approximations to a desired experiment are available.
* The results have implications for applications such as online advertising, finance, and robotics.
* The paper's contribution is significant, as it provides a new framework for studying exploration-exploitation trade-offs in the presence of cheaper approximations.

Clarity:

* The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the problem formulation, algorithm, and theoretical results.
* The simulation results are presented clearly, but the parameter choices could be better justified.

Quality:

* The paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the algorithm's performance.
* The results are well-supported by theoretical and empirical evidence.

Overall, the paper presents a novel framework for studying exploration-exploitation trade-offs in the presence of cheaper approximations to a desired experiment. While the modeling assumptions and simulation parameter choices could be improved, the paper's contribution is significant, and the results have implications for various applications.
