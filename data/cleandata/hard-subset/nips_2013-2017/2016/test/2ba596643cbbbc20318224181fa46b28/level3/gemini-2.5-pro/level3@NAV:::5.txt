This paper introduces a novel and practical variant of the stochastic K-armed bandit problem, where cheap, biased approximations (fidelities) of each arm's outcome are available alongside the expensive, true outcome. The authors formalize this setting as a multi-fidelity (MF) bandit problem with a cost-weighted regret notion. The core contribution is the MF-UCB algorithm, which intelligently leverages low-fidelity information to quickly eliminate suboptimal arms, thereby reserving expensive high-fidelity evaluations for a smaller set of promising candidates. This work builds upon the classical UCB framework but carves out a new direction, distinct from related areas like budgeted bandits or active learning with weak labelers, by focusing on a hierarchy of approximations within the regret minimization setting.

The paper has numerous strengths across all evaluation criteria.

**Quality & Originality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete and novel contribution. The formalization of the MF bandit problem is original and well-motivated. The theoretical analysis is a major strength, providing upper regret bounds for MF-UCB and a corresponding lower-bound analysis that demonstrates the near-optimality of the proposed approach. These results rigorously support the claim that leveraging multi-fidelity information leads to significant improvements in regret.

**Clarity:** The manuscript is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly articulated through practical examples, the problem is formalized precisely, and the algorithm is described intuitively. The theoretical results are stated clearly, and the accompanying simulations effectively demonstrate the practical benefits of MF-UCB over a standard UCB baseline.

**Significance:** The problem setting is highly significant and addresses a common scenario in real-world applications such as hyperparameter tuning, scientific simulation, and online advertising, where evaluation costs can vary. This work opens a promising new avenue for bandit research. The proposed algorithm and its analysis provide a strong foundation that other researchers and practitioners are likely to build upon, representing a demonstrable advance for the field.

A minor weakness, which the authors openly acknowledge, is the small gap between the upper and lower bounds for a specific subset of arms, leaving a compelling question for future work. Additionally, a discussion on the practical estimation of the fidelity bias parameters (ζ(m)) would further strengthen the paper's applicability.

In summary, this is an excellent paper presenting a novel problem, a theoretically-grounded algorithm, and strong supporting results. It is a significant and high-quality contribution that I strongly recommend for acceptance.
