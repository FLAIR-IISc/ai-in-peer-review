Summary
The paper introduces a new stochastic K-armed bandit formulation in which each arm can be queried at multiple fidelities with increasing accuracy and cost. The highest fidelity corresponds to the true reward distribution, while lower fidelities yield biased but cheaper observations with known bias envelopes ζ(m) that shrink with fidelity. The authors propose MF-UCB, an optimism-based algorithm that maintains fidelity-specific UCBs adjusted by ζ(m), selects the arm with the tightest upper bound on the highest-fidelity mean, and then adaptively chooses the lowest fidelity that still provides informative constraints using thresholds γ(m) that balance information gain and cost. They provide an upper bound on cost-weighted pseudo-regret under a capital budget Λ and a complementary lower bound, showing that MF-UCB is near-optimal except for a well-characterized subset of “borderline” arms. Experiments on synthetic problems show consistent gains over standard UCB that ignores lower fidelities.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on classical UCB methods [Auer; Bubeck & Cesa-Bianchi] and regret lower bounds [Lai & Robbins], connects to budgeted bandits [Tran-Thanh; Xia et al.] but addresses a distinct setting with fidelity-specific approximations, and is complementary to multi-fidelity optimization in engineering [Huang et al.; Rajnarayan et al.] and RL simulators [Cutler et al.]. The Gaussian-process multi-fidelity bandit work [Kandasamy et al.] is closely related but considers structured reward functions; here the focus is the stochastic K-armed setting with explicit bias envelopes and costs. To my knowledge, the formal regret analysis in this exact setting is novel.

Strengths
- Clear and principled modeling of multi-fidelity bandits with explicit bias envelopes and costs.
- MF-UCB is simple, intuitive, and grounded in UCB principles; the fidelity-switching rule γ(m) is well-motivated by a cost–information trade-off.
- Solid theory: upper bound that highlights savings from eliminating clearly suboptimal arms at low cost; lower bound showing near-optimality except for a transparent gap on K(m)7.
- The analysis cleanly explains where gains come from (most cost is spent at the fidelity JkK appropriate for arm k), and when multi-fidelity does not help (arms close to optimal).
- Synthetic experiments align with theory.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Practical applicability: The method assumes known ζ(m) and that Assumption 1 holds. In real systems, ζ(m) may be unknown or misspecified. Please discuss:
  - Robustness when ζ(m) are upper bounds rather than tight values.
  - How to estimate ζ(m) online (e.g., periodic cross-fidelity calibration, adaptive bias estimation using occasional higher-fidelity checks, conservative envelopes with monotone shrinkage).
  - Sensitivity of MF-UCB’s decisions to ζ(m) and λ(m) misspecification (ablation/robustness plots).
- Regret definition: Using the highest-fidelity reward for every pull is reasonable for applications like advertising, but a brief, more prominent discussion of alternative regret notions (e.g., fidelity-dependent utility, simulations with no intrinsic reward) would help readers map the framework to other domains (robotics simulators, clinical trials).
- Empirics: Only synthetic data are shown. Consider adding:
  - A semi-synthetic or real evaluation (e.g., ad-click logs with downsampled audiences/time or ML model selection where fidelities correspond to subsamples/epochs).
  - Baselines beyond UCB: a cost-aware UCB that treats lower fidelities as noisy observations of the top fidelity; Thompson sampling variants with cost; heuristic multi-fidelity elimination.
  - Sensitivity to M, ζ decay rates, and λ ratios.
- Presentation: The heavy use of “up-to-constant” notation (.,.,&) is occasionally misleading because the hidden constants depend on algorithmic parameters (notably ρ). Where feasible, present explicit inequalities with constants in the main statements or a lemma that isolates the dependence on ρ.

Quality
The technical development is sound and careful. The upper bound clearly decomposes regret by arm and fidelity, and the partition K(1), …, K(M) is insightful. The lower bound is standard via change-of-measure but adapted well to the multi-fidelity structure, and the identification of the K(m)7 gap is honest and informative. Assumption 1 is reasonable (e.g., for sub-Gaussian ψ and exponentially decaying ζ), but its practical implications deserve a short discussion.

Clarity
The main paper is well written and easy to follow. The algorithm and intuition for γ(m) are clear. The write-up would benefit from fewer asymptotic shorthands and more explicit constants where they affect interpretation. The supplementary has many small glitches that impede readability; see list below.

Originality
Formulating bandits with biased, multi-fidelity observations and cost-weighted regret, and designing MF-UCB with fidelity-aware switching, is novel to the best of my knowledge. The theoretical characterization and lower bound are new and valuable.

Significance
The setting is natural and increasingly relevant (ads, AutoML, simulators). The results advance understanding of when and how lower-fidelity information can reduce cost without sacrificing regret. I expect others to build on this (e.g., unknown ζ, non-stationary biases, contextual bandits, Thompson sampling, structured priors).

Arguments for acceptance
- Well-motivated new problem with clear practical relevance.
- Simple, principled algorithm; strong theory with near-optimality.
- Clear explanation of where gains arise; experimental corroboration.

Arguments against acceptance
- Relies on known ζ(m) and a regularity assumption whose practical verification is nontrivial; limited robustness discussion.
- Empirical evaluation limited to synthetic settings; lacks stronger baselines and sensitivity analyses.
- Supplement contains numerous typos that hinder verification.

Questions for authors
1. How sensitive is MF-UCB to over/under-estimates of ζ(m)? Can you provide robustness plots?
2. Could γ(m) be adapted online (e.g., based on empirical cross-fidelity discrepancies) to reduce reliance on ζ(m)?
3. How would you extend the approach to contextual bandits or Thompson sampling?
4. Can the K(m)7 gap be narrowed with a hybrid switching rule that occasionally validates low-fidelity elimination using cheap cross-checks?
