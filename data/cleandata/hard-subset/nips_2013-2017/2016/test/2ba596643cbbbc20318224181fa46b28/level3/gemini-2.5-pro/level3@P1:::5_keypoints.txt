- Introduces the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit problem where arms have costly high-fidelity rewards and cheaper biased low-fidelity approximations with known bias bounds ζ(m).
- Formalizes a cost-aware regret that accounts for suboptimal arm pulls and budget expenditure across fidelities.
- Proposes the MF-UCB algorithm that uses low-fidelity pulls to eliminate clearly suboptimal arms and reserves high-fidelity pulls for promising arms.
- Provides rigorous theoretical analysis: an upper regret bound for MF-UCB and a problem-specific lower bound, showing near-optimality for many arm classes.
- Analysis partitions arms into sets K(m) by the fidelity needed to identify suboptimality, explaining the algorithm’s efficiency.
- Empirical simulations on synthetic problems validate MF-UCB’s lower regret and focused use of high-fidelity pulls compared to standard UCB.
- Originality/significance: novel, well-motivated problem with practical relevance (A/B testing, hyperparameter tuning, simulations).
- Technical quality: elegant algorithm and comprehensive proofs, including Theorems 2 and 4 and near-optimality results.
- Clarity: well-written exposition with clear motivation, formulation, algorithm description, and intuitions (e.g., γ(m) threshold).
- Empirical support: synthetic experiments effectively demonstrate theoretical claims and pull-allocation behavior.
- Weakness: gap remains between upper and lower bounds for “deceptive” arms in set K(m), leaving potential sub-optimality in analysis.
- Weakness: reliance on Assumption 1 (fast decay of ζ(m)) for comparative performance to UCB; robustness to violations is not explored.
- Weakness: experiments are exclusively synthetic; lack of real-world benchmarks limits empirical support for practical applicability.
- Question: choice and sensitivity of γ(m) threshold need clearer intuition and empirical robustness analysis.
- Question: condition ρ > 4 in Theorem 2 may be tightened (possibly to ρ > 2) with improved K(m) design — worth investigating.
- Question: brief discussion requested on how MF-UCB and its analysis would adapt under fidelity-dependent reward/regret definitions.
