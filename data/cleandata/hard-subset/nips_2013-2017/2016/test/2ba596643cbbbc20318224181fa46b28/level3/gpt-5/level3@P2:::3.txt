Summary and relation to prior work
The paper introduces a multi-fidelity stochastic K-armed bandit model in which each arm can be queried at one of M fidelities. Higher fidelities yield the true reward distribution but are more costly; lower fidelities are cheaper but provide biased approximations whose bias is upper-bounded by known fidelity-specific constants ζ(m), decreasing with m. The objective is to minimize a cost-weighted pseudo-regret under a capital budget Λ, where the benchmark is the highest-fidelity optimal arm played continuously.

Building on optimism-in-the-face-of-uncertainty, the authors propose MF-UCB, which maintains per-fidelity UCBs that are widened by the fidelity bias bounds; the active upper bound for an arm is the minimum across fidelities. A fidelity-selection rule escalates to higher fidelities only when uncertainty at a lower fidelity has been reduced to below a threshold γ(m) that trades off fidelity cost λ(m) against the informativeness of the bias bound ζ(m). The analysis shows MF-UCB uses lower fidelities to eliminate clearly suboptimal arms and reserves high-fidelity plays for near-optimal arms.

On the theory side, the paper provides an upper bound on the expected regret showing improvements over naive UCB that always uses the highest fidelity, when costs are stratified and lower fidelities can eliminate many arms. A lower bound demonstrates near-optimality except for a class of “borderline” arms where the algorithm may escalate too early. Simulations on synthetic problems indicate several-fold gains over high-fidelity UCB.

This work connects to a large literature on stochastic bandits and UCB methods (Auer et al.; Bubeck & Cesa-Bianchi), and is distinct from budgeted bandits where costs are arm-dependent but there is no fidelity structure (Tran-Thanh et al.; Xia et al.). It is related to multi-fidelity optimization in design and reinforcement learning (Huang et al.; Cutler et al.) and to weak/strong labelers in active learning (Zhang & Chaudhuri), but here the focus is the bandit exploration–exploitation trade-off with fidelity-specific costs and bias. The authors also mention a GP-based extension in recent work.

Strengths
- Clear and timely formalization of a multi-fidelity bandit setting with per-fidelity costs and bias bounds, bridging bandits and multi-fidelity evaluation.
- Conceptually simple algorithm adapting UCB with fidelity-aware confidence sets and an intuitive escalation criterion.
- Nontrivial analysis: upper bound that decomposes regret by fidelity-dependent arm partitions and a lower bound that clarifies when gains are achievable and when they are not.
- Sound intuition: use cheap, biased signals to prune, saving expensive plays for promising arms.
- Empirical results qualitatively match the theory (most cost spent at lower fidelities for clearly suboptimal arms).

Weaknesses
- The modeling choice of “bounded bias with known ζ(m)” is strong; in many applications these bias bounds are unknown, difficult to validate, or may not hold uniformly. The paper does not study robustness to misspecified ζ(m).
- The chosen pseudo-regret (always scoring highest-fidelity reward even when playing low fidelities) is reasonable for some domains but not for others. Several motivating examples (e.g., short ad displays) make the objective ambiguous, and the discussion of alternative objectives is brief.
- The main algorithmic component is a fairly direct UCB adaptation; the novelty lies more in the problem setup and analysis than in algorithmic technique.
- The upper bound expressions are intricate and hard to interpret; more guidance or corollaries for salient regimes (e.g., two-fidelity cases, exponential ζ decay, cost ratios) would help.
- Theoretical gains depend critically on Assumption 1 (rapid decay of ζ relative to ψ) and known costs; the scope of improvement when this assumption is relaxed is unclear.
- Simulations: Appendix C parameter choices (ζ(m), λ(m), noise) are not justified, and sensitivity analyses are limited. The reported 3–4× improvements may depend on favorable ζ/λ settings.

Quality
- Technical development appears careful, with reasonable concentration assumptions and a clear fidelity-selection rationale. The gap identified by the lower bound for certain arms is honestly discussed.
- However, the practicality of requiring known, valid ζ(m) and the consequences of misspecification are not evaluated. The regret bounds, while correct, could be distilled into more interpretable forms.

Clarity
- Overall well written and organized, with helpful intuition (e.g., why to stop at lower fidelity once uncertainty drops below γ). Yet the regret definition and its application-specific implications deserve a longer discussion, and the main theorems could be accompanied by simplified corollaries. Simulation details and parameter justification should be brought into the main text or summarized clearly.

Originality
- The problem formulation is novel for the bandit community. The algorithm is a natural UCB variant; the analytical decomposition by fidelity-based arm partitions and the lower bound are valuable.

Significance
- The setting is important for many practical domains where cheap proxies exist. When ζ decays sufficiently and costs are stratified, the potential regret improvements are meaningful, especially for large K. Impact hinges on how often one can obtain credible ζ estimates or design algorithms robust to unknown bias.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Provide sensitivity analyses to ζ(m) misspecification and, ideally, an algorithmic variant that does not require known ζ but learns or upper-bounds it online.
- Expand the discussion and experiments on alternative regret notions (e.g., settings where low-fidelity plays carry little/no reward or different penalties).
- Add interpretable special cases (e.g., M=2, sub-Gaussian) with simplified bounds that expose dependence on K, |K(M)|, λ ratios, and ζ.
- Justify simulation parameters and include sweeps over ζ decay rates, cost ratios, and noise levels; add baselines such as highest-fidelity Thompson Sampling and naive mixtures that use low-fidelity samples as noisy surrogates.
- Clarify when Assumption 1 holds, and show empirical behavior when it does not.
- Discuss potential extensions to unknown cost regimes or adaptive budget settings.

Pros
- Novel and relevant problem formulation.
- Intuitive, simple algorithm with solid analysis.
- Lower bound clarifies near-optimality regime.
- Empirical results support the main claims.

Cons
- Strong modeling assumptions (known bounded bias) with limited robustness analysis.
- Bounds are difficult to interpret; reliance on a technical assumption.
- Algorithmic novelty is modest; experiments lack parameter justification and breadth.
- Motivation/examples for the chosen regret could be clearer.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The formulation and analysis are interesting and likely to stimulate follow-on work; strengthening the empirical section and clarifying robustness and applicability would further improve the paper.

Reviewer confidence
Medium.
