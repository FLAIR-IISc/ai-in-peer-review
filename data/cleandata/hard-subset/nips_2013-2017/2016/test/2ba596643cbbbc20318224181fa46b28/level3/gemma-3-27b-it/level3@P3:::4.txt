This paper introduces a multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit (MF-MAB) framework, motivated by scenarios where evaluating arms is costly but cheaper approximations are available. The authors propose MF-UCB, an adaptive algorithm leveraging these approximations, and provide a regret analysis along with a lower bound, demonstrating performance improvements over standard UCB on synthetic problems. While the core idea is interesting and potentially impactful, the paper requires significant revisions before being suitable for publication.

The MF-MAB formulation is a natural extension of the classical bandit problem, addressing a practical challenge encountered in diverse applications like online advertising and algorithm selection. Building upon UCB, MF-UCB’s adaptive approach to fidelity selection is intuitively appealing. The theoretical results, specifically the regret bound and lower bound, demonstrate a solid understanding of the problem's complexity. The empirical validation, showing improvements over UCB, provides preliminary support for the proposed algorithm. Relevant work in bandit algorithms (Agrawal, Auer, Bubeck & Cesa-Bianchi) and multi-fidelity optimization (Huang et al., Cutler et al.) are acknowledged, though the connection to budgeted bandits (Tran-Thanh et al., Xia et al.) and active learning (Zhang & Chaudhuri) could be more explicitly discussed.

However, several weaknesses hinder the paper’s clarity and impact. The notation is consistently confusing. Variables are frequently defined *after* their first use (e.g., ‘s’ is defined in the regret analysis section) and some, like ‘s’, are sparsely used after definition, obscuring their purpose. The overall presentation lacks a logical flow, making it difficult to follow the argument.

The motivating example of online advertising is misleading. The references to display time correlations and non-stationarity are not central to the paper's theoretical framework, which assumes a stationary bandit setting. These aspects introduce unnecessary complexity and detract from the core contribution. The paper frames the problem as using lower fidelities to *approximate* the highest fidelity outcome, but doesn't sufficiently address the potential for biased estimates and the implications of acting on those biases, particularly when lower fidelities give misleading signals (as acknowledged in the text).

More critically, the regret analysis is questionable. The paper treats the optimal arm as having the highest *mean* reward regardless of cost. This implicitly prioritizes reward maximization over cost-effectiveness. A more appropriate metric would explicitly consider the reward-to-cost ratio, and the regret should be defined relative to the optimal *cost-normalized* reward. By ignoring cost in the determination of the optimal arm, the analysis may underestimate the benefits of using lower-fidelity sampling and overestimate the actual regret.

A more compelling analysis would focus on sample (or cost) complexity: how many samples (at what fidelities) are needed to find the best arm with high probability *under a budget constraint*? This approach aligns directly with real-world testing scenarios where resources (time, money, etc.) are limited. Instead of minimizing cumulative regret, the goal would be to maximize the probability of identifying the optimal arm given a fixed cost, which is a more practical objective for many applications. This would also directly address the question of whether the cheaper fidelities are sufficiently informative to justify their use. 

Finally, the writing is often unwieldy and could benefit from significant streamlining. Several sentences are overly complex and would be more effective if broken down into shorter, more focused statements. The organization could also be improved by clearly separating the theoretical contributions from the empirical results.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel formulation of the multi-fidelity bandit problem.
*   Intuitive and adaptive algorithm (MF-UCB).
*   Theoretical regret bound and lower bound.
*   Empirical results showing performance improvements over UCB.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Confusing notation and poor clarity.
*   Misleading motivating example.
*   Questionable regret analysis that doesn’t account for cost.
*   More relevant analysis would focus on cost-complexity.
*   Unwieldy writing and organization.



**Recommendation:** Reject with request for major revisions. The core idea has merit, but substantial changes are required to address the issues outlined above. Specifically, the authors should revise the notation, clarify the motivations, refine the regret analysis (or propose a cost-complexity analysis), and improve the overall clarity and organization of the paper.
