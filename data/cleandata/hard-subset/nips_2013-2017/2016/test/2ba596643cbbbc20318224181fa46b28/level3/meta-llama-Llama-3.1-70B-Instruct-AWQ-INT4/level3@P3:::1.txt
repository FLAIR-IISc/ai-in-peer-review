This paper introduces the concept of multi-fidelity multi-armed bandits, where observing the outcome of each arm is expensive, but cheap approximations to this outcome are available. The authors propose a novel upper confidence bound procedure, MF-UCB, for this setting and prove that it naturally adapts to the sequence of available approximations and costs, thus attaining better regret than naive strategies which ignore the approximations.

The paper is well-motivated, and the problem setup is clearly explained. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the MF-UCB algorithm, including a regret bound and a lower bound. The paper also includes simulations on synthetic problems, which demonstrate the effectiveness of MF-UCB compared to UCB.

The strengths of the paper include its technical soundness and the novelty of the problem setup. The authors have made a significant contribution to the field of multi-armed bandits by introducing the concept of multi-fidelity bandits and proposing an algorithm that adapts to the sequence of available approximations and costs.

However, there are some weaknesses in the paper. One major issue is the lack of adequate explanation of the results and assumptions, which makes interpretation difficult. For example, the choice of γ(m) in the MF-UCB algorithm is not well-motivated, and the authors could provide more insight into why this choice is optimal. Additionally, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the implications of the lower bound and how it compares to the regret bound of MF-UCB.

Another concern is the penalization of cost in the regret formulation. The authors use a multiplicative penalization, which may not be the most natural choice. An additive penalization could be more appropriate, and the authors should discuss the implications of this choice.

The paper also lacks a broader motivation for the chosen reward/cost model. The ad-display example is not well-generalized, and the authors should provide more context for why this model is relevant in practice.

Furthermore, Assumption 1, which constrains the ζ(m) values, is artificial and opaque. The authors should provide more insight into why this assumption is necessary and how it affects the performance of MF-UCB.

Finally, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the robustness of MF-UCB to deviations from the assumptions. For example, what happens if the ζ(m) values do not decay as required? How does the algorithm perform in this case?

In terms of originality, the paper is novel in its introduction of the concept of multi-fidelity bandits and the proposal of the MF-UCB algorithm. However, the paper builds on existing work on upper confidence bound methods, and the authors could provide more context for how their work differs from previous contributions.

In terms of significance, the paper addresses an important problem in the field of multi-armed bandits and provides a novel solution. The results have implications for a wide range of applications, including online advertising, finance, and healthcare.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the authors have made a significant contribution to the field of multi-armed bandits. However, there are some weaknesses in the paper that could be addressed with further clarification and discussion.
