

# Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

This paper proposes a novel framework for multi-armed bandit problems where each arm can be sampled at multiple fidelities with varying costs, with lower-fidelity samples providing approximate information about the highest-fidelity outcome. The authors formalize this as a multi-fidelity bandit problem, where playing an arm at fidelity m costs λ(m) and returns a biased estimate of the highest-fidelity reward. They develop MF-UCB, an adaptive algorithm that uses lower-fidelity samples to eliminate suboptimal arms and reserves higher-fidelity plays for promising candidates, and provide theoretical regret bounds and a matching lower bound.

The paper builds on the classical bandit literature (e.g., Auer et al. [3], Bubeck and Cesa-Bianchi [5]) by introducing a cost-aware multi-fidelity setting. While budgeted bandits [13, 15] consider arm-specific costs, and multi-fidelity optimization [7, 10] exists in other domains, this paper is the first to formalize regret in the multi-fidelity bandit context with a theoretical analysis. The related work section appropriately situates the contribution.

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound framework with a clear algorithm (MF-UCB) and rigorous regret analysis. The regret bound (Theorem 2) and lower bound (Theorem 4) demonstrate that MF-UCB achieves better regret than standard UCB when lower fidelities are sufficiently cheap and informative. The experimental results on synthetic problems support the theoretical claims. However, the regret analysis contains a critical flaw: the authors treat the "best arm" as the one with highest mean reward regardless of cost, whereas the optimal policy should maximize reward/cost ratio. This undermines the core motivation of the paper, as the value of lower fidelities depends on their cost-effectiveness. The regret formulation in Equation (1) incorrectly sets qt = µ(M)It rather than using a cost-adjusted reward metric. A sample-complexity (cost-complexity) analysis focused on finding the best arm with high probability under budget constraints would be more appropriate and better motivated by real-world testing scenarios.

**Clarity:** The paper suffers from significant clarity issues. The notation is confusing and dispersed (e.g., variable s is defined after its first use in Equation 3 and then barely used). The advertising example is misleading: references to "display time correlations" and "non-stationarity" are inappropriate for the paper's scope, as the model assumes stationary distributions. The writing is unwieldy in parts, particularly in the regret analysis section, and would benefit from clearer organization and more precise language.

**Originality:** The multi-fidelity bandit formalism is novel for the bandit setting, though related concepts exist in multi-fidelity optimization and active learning. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work, particularly in its cost-aware regret formulation. The MF-UCB algorithm is a natural extension of UCB to the multi-fidelity setting, but the theoretical analysis provides new insights about how to optimally allocate sampling resources across fidelities.

**Significance:** The problem addressed is important for applications where high-fidelity experiments are expensive (e.g., online advertising, algorithm selection). The potential impact of the framework is significant, as it provides a principled way to leverage cheaper approximations. However, the current regret analysis weakens the significance claim, as it does not properly account for the cost-efficiency of lower fidelities. A properly formulated cost-complexity analysis would substantially strengthen the significance.

**Summary of Strengths and Weaknesses:**

*Strengths:*
- Novel multi-fidelity bandit formalism
- Clear algorithm design (MF-UCB)
- Good theoretical analysis (regret bounds)
- Strong experimental validation on synthetic problems

*Weaknesses:*
- Flawed regret analysis (ignores cost-efficiency of lower fidelities)
- Misleading advertising example
- Confusing/disorganized notation
- Poorly motivated regret formulation
- Unwieldy writing that obscures key contributions

The paper is promising but requires significant revision to address the theoretical and clarity issues. Specifically, the authors should:
1. Reformulate the regret to properly account for cost-efficiency (reward/cost ratio)
2. Replace the misleading advertising example with a more appropriate application
3. Streamline the notation and clarify the definition of key variables
4. Provide a cost-complexity analysis focused on finding the best arm under budget constraints

Without these revisions, the paper's contribution is undermined by the fundamental flaw in the regret analysis. The core idea is strong, but the current theoretical formulation does not adequately support the claimed advantages of the multi-fidelity approach over standard bandit methods. With the suggested revisions, this paper could make a significant contribution to the bandit literature. As it stands, it is not ready for publication in its current form.
