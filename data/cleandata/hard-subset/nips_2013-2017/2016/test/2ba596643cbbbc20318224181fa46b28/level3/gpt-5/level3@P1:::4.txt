This paper formalizes a multi-fidelity variant of the stochastic K-armed bandit in which each arm can be queried at M fidelities, with higher fidelities yielding unbiased rewards at higher cost and lower fidelities providing cheaper but biased approximations. The authors define a cost-weighted pseudo-regret for a strategy operating under a capital budget Λ, propose MF-UCB, an upper-confidence-bound algorithm that adaptively selects both arm and fidelity, and provide regret upper bounds that reflect how low-fidelity queries can efficiently eliminate poor arms. They also derive a lower bound showing near-optimality under certain conditions, and present synthetic experiments demonstrating improved performance over a naïve single-fidelity UCB baseline. The work relates to classical UCB methods [2,3,5], budgeted bandits [13,15], multi-fidelity optimization [7,10], multi-fidelity RL [6], and weak/strong labeler active learning [16], but differs by explicitly modeling biased approximations and the cost of fidelity.

Strengths
- Novel and timely problem formulation: Explicitly modeling multiple biased fidelities per arm with fidelity-dependent costs is well-motivated and under-explored in the stochastic K-armed bandit literature.
- Algorithmic contribution: MF-UCB is a clean extension of UCB, combining per-fidelity UCBs with a principled switching criterion γ(m) that trades off cost and bias.
- Theoretical analysis: The regret decomposition clearly shows how MF-UCB concentrates expensive queries on a small near-optimal set K(M), with most exploration at lower fidelities for arms far from optimality. The comparison to single-fidelity UCB and the partition-based argument are insightful.
- Lower bound: The lower bound captures the structure of the problem and identifies a gap for a subset of arms (K(m)7), clarifying where MF-UCB may be suboptimal due to its conservative switching rule.
- Empirical evidence: Synthetic examples corroborate the theory; MF-UCB reduces slope and total regret relative to UCB.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Objective and regret definition: The paper optimizes cumulative expected “true” reward subject to a cost budget by defining the instantaneous pseudo-reward as μ(M)It regardless of fidelity and charging the incurred cost λ(mt). This makes the optimal arm independent of cost (it is the arm with maximum μ(M)), which is at odds with many cost-sensitive decision problems where performance per unit cost or per unit time is the relevant metric. In addition, in settings where low-fidelity plays do not produce true reward (e.g., simulation vs. real-world deployment), the current notion arguably understates the opportunity cost of low-fidelity exploration. While Section 7 briefly mentions alternatives, the main analysis does not justify this modeling choice against more standard cost-normalized objectives.
- Examples risk conflating modeling assumptions: The advertising example invokes shorter displays and narrower audiences, which can induce non-stationarity or covariate shift inconsistent with i.i.d. reward assumptions and a fixed μ(M) per arm. If stationarity is intended, the example should be reframed to avoid implying time- or context-dependence.
- Clarity and notation: The exposition is hard to follow in places. Notation is heavy (e.g., J(m)η, K(m), Δ(m)k, T(>m)k,t), s is defined after first use and then seldom used, and several definitions are scattered. The proof sketch requires repeated back-and-forth to parse assumptions and constants. This hampers reproducibility.
- Assumptions and robustness: Assumption 1 on the decay of ζ(m) seems mainly to facilitate clean comparisons and may be restrictive. The analysis assumes known ζ(m); the algorithm’s robustness to mis-specified ζ or to redundant fidelities (where λ(m+1) ≈ λ(m) and ζ(m+1) ≈ ζ(m)) is not empirically examined. The choice of ρ and sensitivity to γ(m) also deserve empirical tuning guidance.
- Lower-bound gap: For arms in K(m)7, the algorithm can miss the lower bound because it avoids distinguishing very small Δ(`)k at low fidelities, even when doing so would be more cost-efficient. The paper acknowledges this and leaves it open; some heuristic remedy (e.g., occasional low-fidelity refinement when λ(`)/Δ(`)k^2 is favorable) or analysis of adaptive switching that depends on λ and estimated gaps would strengthen the contribution.
- Limited baselines: Experiments compare only to a single-fidelity UCB. Given the UCB lineage, comparisons to KL-UCB, UCB-V, or Thompson sampling variants adapted to multi-fidelity would be informative, as would sensitivity analyses across ψ, ζ, λ ratios, heavy-tailed noise, and mis-specified ζ.

Quality
- The theoretical development is careful and technically sound under the stated assumptions, with clear partition-based counting arguments. The main concern is alignment between the regret definition and motivating applications, and the practical implications of known ζ and Assumption 1. Proof details appear solid; some important lemmas are only sketched in the main text.

Clarity
- The paper would benefit from a streamlined notation block and earlier, cleaner definitions (variables s, t; all T(·); all sets K(m), J(m)η). Replace forward references; reduce symbol overload; provide an intuition-first overview of γ(m); and rewrite the motivating examples to align with the stochastic i.i.d. model.

Originality
- The formal multi-fidelity K-armed bandit setting with biased lower fidelities and explicit costs, and the MF-UCB design, are original relative to prior NIPS work on budgeted bandits and multi-fidelity optimization.

Significance
- The idea is significant and likely to influence follow-on work, including best-arm identification with costs and GP-based multi-fidelity bandits (as the authors note). However, the current mismatch between objective, examples, and common cost-sensitive goals limits immediate impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify and justify the regret/objective. Either (a) add an analysis for reward-per-cost optimality, or (b) clearly scope to settings where only high-fidelity yields true reward and low-fidelity is purely informational, and adjust examples accordingly. Consider adding a best-arm identification (cost-complexity) result under a budget.
- Consolidate notation and improve organization; include a notation table; define all quantities before use; simplify set constructions where possible.
- Expand experiments: ablate ζ mis-specification; vary λ and ζ schedules (including “redundant fidelity” cases and violations of Assumption 1); compare to stronger baselines; report sensitivity to ρ and γ(m).
- Discuss robustness to model misspecification and provide guidance on choosing ζ(m) in practice.
- Provide more intuition for the lower-bound gap and explore a practical switching refinement.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel, well-motivated problem formulation and algorithm.
- Solid theoretical guarantees with insightful decomposition and near-optimality.
- Empirical gains consistent with theory.

Arguments against acceptance
- Objective and examples are misaligned with many cost-sensitive applications; regret definition is not fully convincing for the motivating scenarios.
- Clarity and notation issues impede accessibility and reproducibility.
- Limited empirical validation and baselines; untested robustness to key assumptions.

Overall, this is an interesting and potentially impactful contribution, but I recommend revision to address objective alignment, clarity, and empirical robustness before publication.
