

This paper introduces a novel multi-fidelity bandit framework where each arm can be pulled at multiple fidelities with varying costs and accuracy, addressing an important practical concern in applications like online advertising and algorithm selection. The authors formalize the problem by considering M fidelities per arm, where lower fidelities have lower cost but provide biased estimates of the true reward (highest fidelity). They propose MF-UCB, an adaptation of UCB that intelligently allocates pulls across fidelities to minimize cost-weighted regret. The theoretical contribution is significant, with a regret bound showing MF-UCB achieves O((λ(1)K + λ(2)|Kg|)log(Λ/λ(2))) regret compared to UCB's O(λ(2)K log(Λ/λ(2))) regret, where Kg is the set of promising arms identified via low-fidelity pulls. The authors complement this with a lower bound demonstrating MF-UCB is nearly optimal under certain conditions.

The paper builds upon the foundational work of Lai and Robbins (1985) and UCB algorithms (Auer et al., 2002), extending them to handle multi-fidelity settings that have been explored in optimization contexts (e.g., Huang et al., 2006) but not formally analyzed for bandit regret. Unlike budgeted bandits (Tran-Thanh et al., 2012) or multi-fidelity optimization, this work specifically addresses the exploration-exploitation trade-off in bandit settings with multiple fidelity options, making it a meaningful contribution to the NIPS community.

The paper excels in theoretical rigor. The MF-UCB algorithm is well-motivated and the regret analysis is thorough, with clear intuition about how the algorithm allocates pulls across fidelities. The lower bound analysis demonstrates the near-optimality of MF-UCB under the given assumptions, and the synthetic experiments convincingly show MF-UCB outperforms UCB in practice. The theoretical contribution is strong, representing a significant advancement in bandit theory with practical implications.

However, the paper has notable weaknesses that impact its quality and clarity. The supplementary materials contain numerous typos and mathematical errors (e.g., page 10 eq.(8): typo in k/K; page 11 eq.(10): γ should be γ^(m); page 14 equation before line 408: inequality direction wrong). These errors undermine the paper's credibility and make it difficult for readers to follow the proofs. The authors should carefully proofread the supplementary materials before publication.

The use of "up-to-a-multiplicative-constant" notation is misleading, as the hidden constants (e.g., ρ) are algorithm parameters that affect performance. Presenting exact inequalities would improve clarity and allow for more precise comparisons with other methods. The paper would benefit from explicitly stating how these parameters should be chosen in practice.

The practical applicability requires more discussion. The algorithm assumes knowledge of ζ(m), the bias between fidelities, which is often unknown in practice. The paper lacks discussion of how to estimate ζ(m) accurately and how errors in this estimation would affect performance. It would be valuable to include a discussion of practical limitations and potential mitigations, such as adaptive estimation of ζ(m) or robustness analysis.

The main paper is well-written and clear, but the supplementary materials' errors significantly reduce the paper's quality. The authors should fix these before publication. The significance of the work is high, as multi-fidelity bandits represent an important practical extension to the classical bandit problem with clear applications in online advertising, algorithm selection, and experimental design.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel formalism for multi-fidelity bandits addressing an important practical problem
- Strong theoretical contributions with near-optimal regret bounds
- Clear algorithmic design with solid experimental validation
- Significant contribution to bandit literature with practical relevance

Arguments against acceptance:
- Supplementary materials contain numerous typos and mathematical errors that undermine credibility
- Over-reliance on "up-to-a-multiplicative-constant" notation obscures practical implementation details
- Insufficient discussion of practical limitations, particularly regarding ζ(m) estimation
- Theoretical analysis assumes knowledge of ζ(m) which is often unavailable in real applications

This paper presents a theoretically strong contribution to bandit theory with clear practical motivation. The core ideas are sound, and the regret bounds provide valuable insights into how to leverage multi-fidelity information. However, the presence of numerous errors in the supplementary materials and the lack of discussion about practical implementation challenges are significant drawbacks. With careful revision to address the typos and mathematical errors and to expand on practical considerations, this would be a strong paper for NIPS. As it stands, the errors in the proofs are too substantial to ignore, and the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of practical limitations. I recommend acceptance with major revisions to address the supplementary material errors and strengthen the practical discussion.
