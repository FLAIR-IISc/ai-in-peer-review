This paper introduces a novel and interesting variant of the stochastic K-armed bandit problem, which the authors term the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit. In this setting, each arm can be played at one of M different "fidelities". Lower fidelities are cheaper but provide biased and noisy estimates of the true reward, while higher fidelities are more expensive but more accurate. The goal is to minimize a cost-weighted pseudo-regret given a total budget. This formulation is well-motivated by real-world problems such as online advertising or hyperparameter optimization, where cheap approximations of an expensive evaluation are often available.

The authors propose the Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB) algorithm, an elegant extension of the classic UCB strategy. The core idea is to use cheap, low-fidelity pulls to quickly explore and eliminate clearly suboptimal arms, reserving the expensive, high-fidelity pulls for a smaller set of promising candidates. The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis of MF-UCB, deriving an upper bound on its regret that demonstrates its advantage over naive strategies that only use the highest fidelity. This is complemented by a regret lower bound, which shows that MF-UCB is nearly optimal under certain conditions.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The formulation of the multi-fidelity bandit problem is a novel and valuable contribution. It formally captures an important practical aspect of sequential decision-making that has been largely overlooked in the classical bandit literature. The problem is well-motivated and the proposed framework is likely to inspire follow-up work.
*   **Quality of Contribution:** The proposed MF-UCB algorithm is intuitive and technically sound. The accompanying theoretical analysis is rigorous, providing both upper and lower regret bounds. The results clearly demonstrate how the algorithm leverages the fidelity structure to achieve lower regret than standard UCB, offering a significant advance for problems that fit this new setting.
*   **Clarity (Main Paper):** The main body of the paper is very well-written and organized. The problem setup, algorithm, and main theoretical results are explained with excellent clarity, making the paper easy to follow and appreciate.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity of Supplementary Material:** The primary weakness of this work lies in the supplementary material. While the high-level proof sketches in the main paper are insightful, the detailed proofs in the appendix are unfortunately marred by a large number of typos, notational inconsistencies, and minor errors. This makes it very difficult for a reviewer to verify the technical correctness of the results and would hinder future readers from fully understanding the analysis. A thorough proofreading and revision of the appendix is essential. Some non-exhaustive examples include:
    *   Page 10, eq.(8): Typo in the set notation for k.
    *   Section A1: H^(m) appears to be a typo for K^(m).
    *   Page 10, line 328: ν/(rho-2) should likely be ν/2.
    *   Page 11, line 343: A variable 's' seems to be used where 'μ' was intended.
    *   Page 11, eq.(10): γ should be γ^(m).
    *   Page 13, definition of x_n: K^2 should be K.
    *   Page 13, definition of y_n: The exponent should be 1/(ρ-2).
    *   Page 14, line 406: N should likely be log(N).
    *   Page 15: Variables 'p' and 'm' are used inconsistently in several places.

2.  **Clarity of Theoretical Bounds:** The use of "up-to-a-multiplicative-constant" notation (e.g., `.`, `&`) in the statement of the main theorems is somewhat misleading. These notations hide constants that may depend on algorithm parameters, such as ρ. This makes the bounds less precise than they could be and obscures the exact dependence on key parameters. Presenting the main theorems as explicit inequalities would significantly improve the clarity and formal rigor of the paper's theoretical claims.

3.  **Discussion of Practical Applicability:** The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of its practical limitations. A key assumption is that the bias bounds ζ(m) for each fidelity are known a priori. In many real-world applications, these values would be unknown and difficult to estimate accurately. The paper should discuss the sensitivity of MF-UCB to misspecified ζ(m) values. Is the algorithm robust to such errors? Could these parameters be learned online? Addressing these practical concerns would strengthen the paper's impact and provide valuable guidance for practitioners wishing to apply the method.

**Summary:**

This paper makes a significant and novel contribution by formalizing the multi-fidelity bandit problem and providing a near-optimal algorithm, MF-UCB, with strong theoretical guarantees. The core ideas are excellent and the main paper is clearly written. However, the work is let down by the poor state of the supplementary materials, which requires substantial revision to be verifiable.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Introduces a novel, well-motivated, and important bandit setting.
*   Proposes an elegant and intuitive algorithm (MF-UCB).
*   Provides a solid theoretical analysis with upper and lower regret bounds, demonstrating the algorithm's near-optimality.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The appendix containing the proofs has a very high density of typos and errors, making the results difficult to verify.
*   The practical assumption of knowing the fidelity bias parameters ζ(m) is strong and its implications are not discussed.

**Recommendation:**
This is a strong paper with a significant contribution. I recommend acceptance, but I strongly urge the authors to perform a thorough revision of the appendix to correct the numerous errors and improve its clarity. A discussion on the practical challenges of setting the ζ(m) parameters should also be added to the main text.
