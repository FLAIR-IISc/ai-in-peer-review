The paper introduces the Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit (MF-MAB) problem, a novel extension to the classical bandit setting where observing the outcome of an arm is costly, but cheaper, albeit biased, approximations are available. The motivation stems from scenarios like online advertising, algorithm selection, and experimental design where higher-fidelity evaluations are expensive. The authors propose MF-UCB, an upper confidence bound algorithm adapted to this setting, and provide a theoretical analysis with regret bounds and a corresponding lower bound. Empirical results on synthetic data demonstrate performance improvements over a naive UCB strategy.

This work builds on a rich history of bandit research, originating with Robbins’ seminal work [11] and subsequent developments like UCB [3, 12]. It differentiates itself from prior work by explicitly addressing the trade-off between cost and fidelity, which is missing in standard bandit formulations. While related work acknowledges multi-fidelity methods in optimization [7, 10] and robotics [6], these do not focus on the exploration-exploitation dilemma inherent in the bandit framework.  Connections to budgeted bandits [13, 15] and active learning with weak labelers [16] are noted, but the MF-MAB problem is distinct due to its focus on sequential decision-making with biased approximations rather than cost per reward or label quality.

The primary strength of this paper lies in its formalization of a practically relevant problem and the development of a tailored algorithm. The theoretical analysis, providing regret bounds and a matching lower bound, is commendable. The empirical evaluation, though limited to synthetic data, supports the claim that MF-UCB outperforms a standard UCB approach.  The core idea of using lower fidelities to efficiently eliminate suboptimal arms and focusing expensive evaluations on promising candidates is intuitively appealing and potentially impactful.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses that hinder its clarity and impact.  The notation is inconsistent and often confusing; variables are introduced before definition (e.g., ‘s’ appears before its meaning is clarified) and sometimes defined but rarely used thereafter. This impacts readability and makes following the derivations challenging. The example used to motivate the problem – online advertising – is somewhat misleading. Discussions about the correlation of display time and non-stationarity introduce complexities not central to the core theoretical contribution of the paper, which focuses on biased estimation.  

More crucially, the presented regret analysis feels somewhat artificial. The paper frames the problem as minimizing the difference between the cumulative reward and an oracle that *always* plays the best arm, irrespective of cost. This is a questionable approach; in a realistic scenario, the optimal policy would explicitly consider the reward/cost ratio.  By effectively ignoring the cost of lower-fidelity sampling in the reward calculation, the analysis may obscure the true benefits – and potential drawbacks – of utilizing these approximations. This also renders the comparison with standard UCB less meaningful, as UCB implicitly maximizes reward without considering cost.

A more compelling analysis would focus on sample-complexity (or cost-complexity) – how many samples (and thus, cost) are required to identify the best arm with high probability under a given budget constraint. Such an analysis would be more aligned with real-world applications and testing scenarios where resources are limited.

Finally, the writing could benefit from streamlining and improved organization. The paper feels unwieldy in parts, with lengthy sentences and complex phrasing.  A more concise and structured presentation would significantly enhance clarity.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and relevant problem formulation.
*   Development of a tailored algorithm (MF-UCB).
*   Theoretical analysis with regret bounds and a lower bound.
*   Empirical results demonstrating performance improvement.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Confusing notation and poor readability.
*   Misleading motivational example.
*   Questionable regret analysis that doesn't reflect real-world cost considerations.
*   Lack of a cost-complexity analysis.
*   Unwieldy writing and organization.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents an interesting idea with promising theoretical results. However, the significant weaknesses in clarity, motivation, and the analysis prevent me from recommending acceptance in its current form. I believe the paper has the potential to be a valuable contribution, but requires substantial revisions to address the concerns outlined above. I recommend a **reject** decision, with encouragement to resubmit after addressing these issues. Specifically, the authors should focus on simplifying the notation, refining the problem motivation, performing a cost-complexity analysis, and improving the overall clarity and organization of the manuscript.
