

This paper introduces a novel multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit framework where each arm can be observed at M different fidelities, with higher fidelities providing better reward approximations but at higher cost. The authors formalize a new regret definition that properly accounts for the cost of different fidelities and propose MF-UCB, a novel upper confidence bound algorithm that strategically uses lower-fidelity observations to eliminate suboptimal arms before committing to expensive high-fidelity experiments. The theoretical analysis includes a regret upper bound and a matching lower bound, with simulation results demonstrating MF-UCB's superiority over standard UCB.

The paper makes several strong contributions. The problem setting is well-motivated by practical applications like online advertising, where lower-fidelity approximations (e.g., shorter ad displays) can provide valuable preliminary information. The regret definition appropriately captures the cost structure of the multi-fidelity setting, and the theoretical analysis is technically sound with novel contributions to the bandit literature. The simulation results effectively support the theoretical claims, showing MF-UCB's ability to strategically allocate resources across fidelities.

However, a major concern arises from the assumption of known ε^(m) (the deviation of mth-fidelity mean from highest-fidelity mean). The paper does not address how these values would be obtained in practice, which significantly impacts the applicability of the approach. While the authors note this is a known quantity, they provide no discussion of how to estimate these values or whether the assumption can be relaxed without degrading performance. This is a critical limitation for real-world deployment.

The paper also contains several minor notational errors that should be corrected (as detailed in the key points). These include incorrect indices in equations, inconsistent use of notation, and typographical errors that could confuse readers.

Overall, this is a technically strong paper with a well-motivated problem and novel contributions to the bandit literature. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the practical motivation is compelling. However, the assumption of known ε^(m) significantly weakens the practical applicability, and the paper would benefit from addressing this limitation or at least discussing its implications more thoroughly. The minor errors should be fixed before publication. Given the theoretical novelty and strong analysis, I recommend acceptance with major revisions to address the key concern about ε^(m).
