This paper introduces a novel multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit (MF-MAB) framework, a relevant extension of the classical stochastic bandit problem. The core idea is that each of the K arms can be evaluated at one of M fidelities, where lower fidelities are cheaper but return reward estimates with a known, bounded bias. The authors propose MF-UCB, an algorithm that extends the UCB principle by widening confidence bounds to account for this fidelity-induced bias, aiming to use cheap evaluations to quickly eliminate suboptimal arms. This work is well-situated within the bandit literature, proposing a new cost-versus-accuracy trade-off that is distinct from prior work on budgeted or variable-cost bandits.

**Strengths:**
The paper's main strength is its originality in defining a new and interesting problem. The MF-MAB formulation is elegant and captures a practical challenge in domains where cheap, approximate evaluations exist (e.g., hyperparameter tuning, simulation-based optimization). This formulation is a significant contribution that could inspire future research. The paper presents a complete piece of work, providing a new algorithm, corresponding theoretical analysis, and empirical simulations.

**Weaknesses:**
Despite the novelty of the setting, there are several weaknesses. The primary concern relates to the quality and justification of the core modeling assumption: that lower fidelity introduces bounded bias rather than increased variance. For many applications, a cheaper evaluation would more naturally be modeled as a noisier (higher variance) estimate, not a systematically biased one. The justification for this choice feels tenuous for some motivating examples (e.g., online advertising), weakening the overall significance and link between the model and practice.

Furthermore, while the algorithm is technically sound, its novelty is limited, as it is a relatively direct application of the standard "optimism-in-the-face-of-uncertainty" principle to the new problem. The provided theoretical regret bounds are messy and hard to interpret, limiting the practical insights gained from the analysis. Finally, the empirical results are not entirely convincing. The simulation parameter choices (Appendix C) lack justification, meaning the reported 3–4× improvement might reflect a hand-picked scenario favorable to MF-UCB rather than typical performance.

In summary, the paper introduces a compelling problem but the execution has some flaws in its core assumptions and empirical validation that temper its overall impact.
