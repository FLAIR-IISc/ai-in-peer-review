This paper introduces a novel and well-motivated extension of the stochastic multi-armed bandit problem, termed the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit. In this setting, each arm can be pulled at various fidelities, where higher fidelities yield more accurate reward estimates but incur greater costs. The authors propose a new cost-weighted regret notion that balances pseudo-regret with the capital expended. They introduce the MF-UCB algorithm, an adaptation of the classic UCB principle, designed to leverage cheap, low-fidelity pulls to quickly eliminate suboptimal arms and reserve expensive, high-fidelity pulls for promising candidates. The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis, including upper bounds for MF-UCB and corresponding lower bounds for the problem, demonstrating near-optimality under certain conditions.

The paper's primary strength lies in its significant and original contribution. The multi-fidelity formulation is a natural and important problem that extends the bandit framework to better model real-world scenarios. The theoretical analysis appears solid, and the core ideas behind the MF-UCB algorithm are intuitive and well-defended. The main body of the paper is exceptionally clear and well-written, making the core concepts easy to understand. This work represents a valuable advancement in the field of multi-armed bandits.

However, the paper has notable weaknesses concerning clarity, primarily in the supplementary materials. The appendix containing the proofs is unfortunately riddled with numerous typos and minor errors that significantly impede readability and verification. For example, variables are swapped (e.g., H^(m) for K^(m)), constants are misstated (ν/(rho-2) for ν/2), and many equations contain small but confusing mistakes. A thorough proofreading of the appendix is essential. Furthermore, the clarity of the theoretical bounds could be improved. The use of "up-to-a-multiplicative-constant" notation can be misleading, as it hides algorithm parameters like ρ. Presenting exact inequalities would provide a more transparent result.

Finally, the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion on its practical applicability. The MF-UCB algorithm's performance relies on knowing the fidelity parameters ζ, and it is unclear how robust the method is to inaccurate estimations of these values. Discussing these limitations and potential mitigation strategies would strengthen the work's practical significance. Despite these required revisions, the theoretical contribution is strong. With careful correction of the appendix and additional discussion on practical considerations, this will be an excellent contribution.
