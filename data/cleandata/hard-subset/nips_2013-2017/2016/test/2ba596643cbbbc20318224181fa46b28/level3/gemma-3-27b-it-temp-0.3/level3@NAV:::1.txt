## Review of “The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit”

This paper introduces a multi-fidelity bandit problem where obtaining rewards from arms has varying costs and accuracies. The authors propose MF-UCB, a novel UCB-based algorithm, and provide a regret bound, a lower bound, and empirical evidence suggesting improvement over a high-fidelity-only UCB approach. The problem setup is relevant and addresses a practical challenge in many applications, such as online advertising and algorithm selection, where cheaper, approximate evaluations are readily available. The technical analysis appears solid, with a clear attempt to provide theoretical guarantees for the algorithm’s performance.

A key strength lies in the study of the trade-off between information, cost, and reward in a stochastic bandit setting. The proposed algorithm intuitively leverages lower-fidelity evaluations to efficiently eliminate suboptimal arms, focusing expensive high-fidelity experiments on promising candidates. The theoretical results, including the regret bound and lower bound, contribute to a deeper understanding of the problem’s complexity and the algorithm’s optimality. 

However, the paper suffers from clarity issues that hinder full comprehension. The explanation of the results and underlying assumptions is inadequate. For example, Assumption 1, concerning the decay of ζm, feels somewhat arbitrary and its practical validity is unclear. The authors should provide more justification for this assumption and discuss potential scenarios where it might not hold, along with strategies to address those cases (e.g., selecting a subsequence of fidelities). 

Furthermore, the multiplicative cost penalization in the regret formulation feels unnatural. An additive cost penalization would align more closely with intuitive notions of regret and should be considered and discussed. The motivation based on the ad-display example feels limited; broadening the discussion to encompass a wider range of applications would strengthen the paper’s impact.

The algorithm’s design seems heavily tailored to the specific ζm configurations, raising concerns about its robustness. It’s unclear whether Assumption 1 critically affects performance, and the authors should investigate this. The gap between the regret bound and the lower bound warrants further investigation, potentially requiring a redesign of the strategy. Finally, the interpretation of Theorem 2 and the comparison with UCB are difficult to parse due to the dependence on Delta and the complicated sets K^(m); a clearer, more explicit explanation is needed. Minor issues include “near-optimal” (l.56), an unnecessary period before “for all m < M” (l.126), and inconsistent set notation (use curly braces for [[k]]).

**Arguments for acceptance:** The problem is relevant, the technical analysis appears sound, and the algorithm shows promise.
**Arguments against acceptance:** Clarity issues, questionable assumptions, and a gap in the lower bound limit the paper’s impact.

**Overall Assessment:** While the paper presents a valuable contribution to the field, the clarity issues and unanswered questions prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance in its current form. I recommend rejection with encouragement to revise and address the concerns raised.
