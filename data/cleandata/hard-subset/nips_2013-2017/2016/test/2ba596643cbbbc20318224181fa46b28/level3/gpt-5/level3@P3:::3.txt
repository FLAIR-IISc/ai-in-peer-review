Summary and relation to prior work
This paper formulates a multi-fidelity stochastic K-armed bandit in which each arm admits M fidelities with increasing cost λ(1) < … < λ(M), and lower fidelities provide biased approximations to the highest fidelity with known, decreasing bias bounds ζ(1) > … > ζ(M) = 0. The objective is a cost-weighted pseudo-regret for a total budget Λ, where the instantaneous “reward” qt is always the highest-fidelity mean µ(M) of the chosen arm (irrespective of the fidelity actually queried). The proposed algorithm, MF-UCB, builds UCBs that are widened by ζ(m) at each fidelity and takes the minimum across fidelities; it then adaptively chooses the fidelity using a threshold γ(m) ≍ ψ−1((λ(m)/λ(m+1))ψ(ζ(m))) so that cheaper fidelities are used to eliminate clearly suboptimal arms.

The authors provide: (i) an upper bound (Theorem 2) showing MF-UCB’s cost-regret is often smaller than that of UCB run only at the highest fidelity, as it invests most capital in lower fidelities for obviously suboptimal arms; (ii) a lower bound (Theorem 4) establishing near-optimality except on a subset of arms where MF-UCB’s switching rule can be conservative; and (iii) simulations indicating 3–4× improvement over always-using-highest-fidelity UCB. The paper situates the work relative to classical UCB (Auer et al.; Audibert et al.; Bubeck & Cesa-Bianchi), budgeted bandits (Tran-Thanh et al.; Xia et al.), multi-fidelity optimization (Huang et al.; Rajnarayan et al.), multi-fidelity RL (Cutler et al.), and weak/strong labelers in active learning (Zhang & Chaudhuri). The main novelty lies in the problem formulation and regret notion; the algorithm follows standard optimism principles with bias-aware confidence sets.

Strengths
- Clear, interesting formalization of multi-fidelity bandits with biased approximations and costs; the ζ-bias model partially matches scenarios like early-stopped training or subsampled data for algorithm selection.
- Simple, principled algorithm that integrates bias bounds into UCB and adaptively chooses fidelity via a clean threshold rule.
- Nontrivial analysis: upper and lower bounds, with interpretable qualitative takeaway that lower fidelities screen out many arms, saving capital for expensive queries near the optimum. The near-optimality discussion (and where the gap remains) is thoughtful.
- Empirical curves align with the theoretical picture: MF-UCB plays many bad arms only at low fidelity and attains lower slopes than UCB.

Weaknesses
- Theoretical bounds are algebraically heavy and hard to interpret; the main theorem requires multiple nested partitions K(m), Assumption 1 on ζ-decay, and large-Λ regimes, making it difficult to glean simple, problem-facing guidance (e.g., in terms of λ ratios and ζ scales).
- The central modeling choice—low fidelity as bounded bias rather than increased variance—is only partially justified. It fits early stopping/less data in algorithm selection, but the online advertising example (shorter displays/narrow audiences) is less convincing: the direction and magnitude of bias are context-dependent and ζ(m) may not be known or tight.
- Practicality hinges on knowing ζ(m), ψ, and costs precisely; there is no analysis of mis-specified or unknown bias bounds, nor adaptive estimation of ζ(m).
- The regret definition fixes qt = µ(M)It even when playing low fidelity, which is reasonable for some settings (screening alternatives before committing to expensive evaluations) but is misaligned for others (short ad displays do produce immediate rewards; the paper’s objective for that application is unclear).
- Lower bound gap for K(m)7 arms reflects a real inefficiency in the switching rule; no partial remedy or heuristic is explored.
- Experiments are purely synthetic; Appendix C parameter choices (notably ζ(m), variance proxies, cost ratios) are insufficiently justified and no sensitivity analysis is shown. The 3–4× gains could be driven by favorable ζ/λ settings rather than robustness across regimes. Baselines beyond “UCB@highest fidelity” are absent.

Quality
- Technical development is largely sound, with appropriate concentration assumptions and careful proofs. However, interpretability suffers. The need for Assumption 1, known ζ, and Λ > Λ0 should be better contextualized, and robustness to misspecification is not addressed.
- Empirical evidence supports the qualitative claims but lacks breadth, sensitivity studies, and real-data validation.

Clarity
- Writing is mostly clear, but the regret definition and the mapping to applications deserve a more explicit, consistent narrative.
- The main bound would benefit from simplified corollaries in canonical sub-Gaussian, two-fidelity cases with concrete constants. A small worked example would help.

Originality
- The problem formulation and cost-weighted regret for biased multi-fidelity observations are original and timely. The algorithmic core is standard UCB with bias-aware envelopes. The lower bound is a meaningful contribution.

Significance
- Moderate. The model is appealing and could spur follow-up work on more realistic/robust assumptions (unknown bias, data-driven calibration) and on other objectives. Adoption will depend on demonstrating practical gains beyond favorable synthetic settings.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide simplified bounds for sub-Gaussian, M=2 and M=3, with explicit constants to aid interpretability; include a table comparing to high-fidelity UCB as a function of λ and ζ.
- Strengthen empirical section: (i) sensitivity to ζ(m), λ(m), and noise; (ii) misspecified ζ(m) (under/overestimates); (iii) ablations on the γ(m) design; (iv) additional baselines (e.g., always-low-fidelity screening + high-fidelity elimination; successive elimination with costs; heuristics that treat low fidelity as high-variance unbiased).
- Clarify and better motivate the regret for each application. For ads, explain why qt should be µ(M) rather than µ(mt), or provide an alternative objective and discuss how MF-UCB would change.
- Discuss how ζ(m) could be estimated or upper-bounded online; analyze robustness to misspecification.
- Give more convincing application case studies (e.g., algorithm selection/AutoML with early stopping) and, if possible, small real-data experiments.
- Explain Assumption 1’s practical plausibility and what happens when it fails.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel, well-posed problem formulation with clear potential.
- Solid theoretical treatment (upper and lower bounds) and intuitive algorithmic design.
- Empirical results consistent with theory.

Cons for acceptance
- Weak linkage and justification for some motivating applications; regret objective unclear in places.
- Heavy, hard-to-interpret bounds; reliance on known ζ and other assumptions.
- Experimental evaluation narrow; parameter choices insufficiently justified; possible overstatement of gains.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The formulation is interesting and the analysis is careful; despite modeling concerns and limited experiments, the paper advances a meaningful direction and should stimulate further work.
