This paper introduces a stochastic K-armed bandit model with M fidelities per arm, where lower fidelities are cheaper but yield biased approximations of the highest-fidelity reward. The authors propose MF-UCB, an optimism-based policy that inflates confidence intervals to account for fidelity-induced bias and selects both arm and fidelity via a thresholding rule. They provide upper bounds on regret and a lower bound showing near-optimality under certain conditions, and present simulations suggesting MF-UCB can substantially outperform using only the highest fidelity. The idea connects to UCB methods in bandits, budgeted bandits, and multi-fidelity optimization (e.g., in BO and RL simulators), and to weak/strong labeler active learning; the main novelty here is the specific multi-fidelity-with-bias bandit formulation and its regret analysis.

Quality: The algorithmic design is principled and the proofs appear careful, but the central regret bounds are difficult to parse and to relate to instance properties; readers would benefit from cleaner special-case corollaries (e.g., M=2, sub-Gaussian) with explicit constants and thresholds. The modeling choice to encode low fidelities as bounded bias (rather than increased variance or a mixture) is only partially justified by the examples; for advertising, the “short display” story is unconvincing and the chosen regret (crediting only high-fidelity rewards) is application-dependent. Empirically, the claimed 3–4× gains are promising, yet Appendix C lacks justification/sensitivity for ζ(m), λ(m), and γ(m); the improvement may hinge on favorable parameterizations.

Clarity: The paper is generally well written and organized. However, the regret objective, the role/necessity of Assumption 1, and the practical meaning of ζ(m) would benefit from sharper exposition and a running two-fidelity example. Providing interpretable plots of instance-dependent terms would help.

Originality: The formulation is new and interesting; the algorithm is a natural UCB instantiation with biased-confidence widening. The lower bound adds value. Related work is mostly adequate, but discussion of biased/corrupted feedback bandits and side-observation models would situate the contribution better.

Significance: If strengthened, this could influence work where proxy evaluations are ubiquitous. As is, the theoretical message is obscured by complexity, and the empirical evidence is not yet robust.

Pros:
- Clear, timely problem formulation; principled MF-UCB design
- Nontrivial analysis with lower bound and insightful partitions
- Potentially impactful in practice

Cons:
- Bounds hard to interpret; limited special-case insight
- Modeling choice (bias vs variance) and regret objective weakly justified for some applications
- Empirical evaluation lacks parameter sensitivity/ablation; baselines limited

Overall: I lean toward a weak reject, but with targeted clarifications, sensitivity studies, and simplified theoretical corollaries, this could become a solid contribution.
