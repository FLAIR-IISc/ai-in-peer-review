- Introduces the novel multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit problem where each arm has M fidelities with known approximation errors and a budgeted cumulative net reward objective.  
- Proposes the MF-UCB algorithm that selects arms via UCB-style bounds and chooses fidelities sequentially to trade cost for information.  
- Main contributions: problem formulation, MF-UCB algorithm, regret analysis showing improvement over standard UCB by using cheap fidelities, and a near-optimal lower bound.  
- Strength: clean, relevant model capturing cost–information trade-offs important in domains like HPO, clinical trials, and simulations.  
- Strength: MF-UCB is intuitive and technically sound; uses cheap approximations for broad search then expensive evaluations on promising arms.  
- Strength: non-trivial theoretical analysis that formalizes the intuition and provides performance insights.  
- Clarity issue: Theorem 2 and regret bound are hard to parse; authors should add a simplified corollary or table (e.g., M=2) and concrete examples.  
- Explanation issue: The arm partitioning K^(m) is defined formally but lacks intuition; include step-by-step examples showing why arms fall into each partition.  
- Modeling concern: Regret uses an unconventional multiplicative cost penalty λ(mt)*rt; authors must justify this choice, compare to additive cost models, and discuss implications.  
- Assumption critique: Assumption 1 (fast-decaying ζ_m) seems artificial and tailored to proofs; paper should explain practical meaning, what fails if violated, and robustness strategies (e.g., selecting a valid subsequence of fidelities).  
- Theoretical gap: There is a gap between the upper and lower bounds for arms in K^(m)_7; authors should analyze whether this is a proof artifact or a limitation of the switching rule and suggest how to close it.  
- Minor editorial issues: several typos/formatting errors (e.g., "near-optimal" split, unnecessary punctuation, set notation) need correction.
