This paper introduces a novel framework for studying exploration-exploitation trade-offs in a multi-armed bandit setting with variable-fidelity pulls, where the cost of a pull increases with accuracy. The authors propose the MF-UCB algorithm and provide upper and lower bounds on cumulative pseudo-regret. The main paper is clear and well-written, but the supplementary materials (proofs) contain many typos and small errors that can confuse readers.

The authors formalize the task as a multi-fidelity bandit, where at each time step, the forecaster may choose to play an arm at any one of M fidelities. The highest fidelity (desired outcome) expends cost λ, while the m fidelity (an approximation) expends λ < λ and returns a biased estimate of the highest fidelity. They develop MF-UCB, a novel upper confidence bound procedure for this setting, and prove that it naturally adapts to the sequence of available approximations and costs, thus attaining better regret than naive strategies that ignore the approximations.

The authors provide a thorough analysis of the algorithm, including a regret bound for MF-UCB and a lower bound for any strategy satisfying a certain condition. They also present simulation results comparing MF-UCB to UCB on synthetic problems, which demonstrate that MF-UCB outperforms UCB in terms of regret.

The paper's main contributions are: (1) a novel formalism for studying bandit tasks with multiple fidelities for each arm, (2) a new algorithm (MF-UCB) that adapts the classical Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) strategies to the multi-fidelity setting, and (3) a theoretical characterization of the performance of MF-UCB that shows that the algorithm uses the lower fidelities to explore all arms and eliminates arms with low expected reward, and reserves the higher fidelity plays for arms with rewards close to the optimal value.

The authors also discuss the practical applicability of MF-UCB, noting that it may suffer from inaccurate estimation of fidelity ζ and requires discussion of practical limitations and mitigations.
