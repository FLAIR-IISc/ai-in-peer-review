Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces and studies the multi-fidelity stochastic K-armed bandit, where each arm admits M fidelities with strictly increasing costs and decreasing biases relative to the highest-fidelity reward. The central modeling assumption is that each lower-fidelity mean is within a known bias bound ζ(m) of the highest-fidelity mean, with ζ(1) > … > ζ(M) = 0, and that observations at any fidelity satisfy a concentration inequality defined by ψ. The authors define a cost-weighted pseudo-regret in which the baseline is an oracle playing the best arm at any fidelity, while the reward qt used in the regret is always the highest-fidelity mean of the chosen arm. They propose MF-UCB, an upper-confidence-bound algorithm that: (i) maintains per-fidelity UCBs on the highest-fidelity mean via bias-corrected confidence intervals, (ii) selects the arm with the tightest optimistic bound, and (iii) adaptively chooses the fidelity by escalating only when the uncertainty at the current fidelity drops below a threshold γ(m) that trades off bias and cost: γ(m) = ψ^{-1}( (λ(m)/λ(m+1)) ψ(ζ(m)) ). The analysis shows that lower fidelities are used to cheaply eliminate obviously suboptimal arms, while higher-fidelity pulls are concentrated on near-optimal arms.

On the theory side, the paper proves an upper bound on cost-weighted regret that, up to log factors and constants, is ∑_k Δ_k^(M) · λ(J_k)/ψ(Δ_k^(J_k)), where J_k is the “elimination fidelity” for arm k defined by a partition of arms into sets K(m) according to how close their m-th fidelity means are to optimal. This improves over naïve single-fidelity UCB that pays λ(M) for all arms. A lower bound (for Bernoulli rewards) shows that MF-UCB is near-optimal for arms whose lower fidelities are not misleading, and isolates a gap for a subset of arms K(m)_7 where some lower fidelities are slightly optimistic; closing this gap is left as an open problem. Synthetic experiments corroborate that MF-UCB uses cheaper fidelities to prune heavily suboptimal arms and achieves lower regret than UCB.

The work relates to classical UCB bandits [Agrawal; Auer; Bubeck & Cesa-Bianchi], budgeted bandits [Tran-Thanh; Xia et al.], and multi-fidelity methods in optimization and RL [Huang et al.; Cutler et al.]. It is complementary to Kandasamy et al. (NIPS 2016) on Gaussian-process-based multi-fidelity bandits; here the focus is on finite arms with per-fidelity costs/biases and regret minimization.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The algorithmic design (bias-corrected UCBs and an escalation rule based on γ(m)) is well motivated by the cost–bias trade-off. The main regret bound carefully accounts for where cost is spent via the K(m) partition. The lower bound uses a change-of-measure argument tailored to fidelity structure and clarifies when lower fidelities must be used. Assumptions include known bias bounds ζ(m), concentration function ψ, and a regularity condition on ζ decay (Assumption 1). These are nontrivial but clearly stated; the authors discuss when Assumption 1 is not needed (small M).
- Empirics: Simulations are consistent with theory and illustrate fidelity allocation. However, evaluation is limited to synthetic data and a single baseline (standard UCB at highest fidelity). Additional baselines (e.g., cost-aware or hierarchical elimination variants) and sensitivity analyses would strengthen the empirical case.
- Limitations: The regret definition is one of several plausible choices; the authors acknowledge alternatives. The lower bound is stated for Bernoulli rewards with µ* ∈ (1/2,1) and ζ(1) < 1/2, and near-optimality has a known gap on K(m)_7. Practical use may require estimating ζ(m) and ψ; robustness to misspecification is not explored.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The problem formulation, notation, and algorithm are precise. The K(m) partition and the intuition for fidelity selection (how ζ(m) caps informativeness) are well explained. Minor suggestions:
- Provide an earlier, intuitive example of γ(m) and its effect (e.g., for sub-Gaussian ψ(x)=Θ(x^2)).
- Summarize the main regret bound in a simplified form for sub-Gaussian rewards to aid intuition.
- Expand the discussion on the residual term r̃(Λ) and the “anytime” nature of the algorithm.

Originality
This is, to my knowledge, the first regret-minimization formulation of stochastic multi-armed bandits with multiple biased, costed fidelities per arm, together with an algorithm and matching (near-optimal) bounds. Prior multi-fidelity work in optimization/RL did not formalize regret in this setting, and budgeted bandits optimize reward/cost ratios rather than leveraging biased approximations. The combination of bias-aware UCBs with fidelity selection via γ(m) is novel.

Significance
The contributions are significant for settings where expensive evaluations are available alongside cheaper, biased proxies (e.g., ads with short-run proxies, algorithm selection with subsampling or reduced compute). The theoretical results make a compelling case that substantial savings are possible when many arms are clearly suboptimal at low fidelities. The framework is likely to inspire follow-up work on (i) learning ζ(m) adaptively, (ii) richer fidelity models, and (iii) broader empirical validation.

Pros
- Clear, well-motivated formulation of multi-fidelity MAB with costs and biases.
- Simple, principled algorithm (MF-UCB) with interpretable fidelity-selection rule.
- Strong theoretical analysis: upper bound that improves over UCB when lower fidelities help; lower bound showing near-optimality and clarifying limits.
- Simulations that align with theory and show practical gains.
- Good discussion of design choices and open problems.

Cons
- Requires known bias bounds ζ(m) and concentration ψ; no adaptive/robust variant or empirical sensitivity study.
- Empirical evaluation is limited (synthetic only; few baselines).
- Near-optimality gap remains for K(m)_7; conditions for when this set is substantial are not quantified.
- Assumption 1 (ζ decay) is helpful for the nicest comparisons; its practical prevalence is not extensively discussed.
- The regret notion, while reasonable, may not match all applications; alternative formulations are only briefly discussed.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Can γ(m) be adapted online when ζ(m) and/or ψ are unknown? For example, can one use empirical cross-fidelity discrepancies to calibrate conservative γ(m)?
- How sensitive is MF-UCB to misspecified ζ(m) (under- vs over-estimation)? A small empirical study would be valuable.
- Could you compare against simple hierarchical racing baselines that eliminate arms at low fidelities by empirical means and only escalate survivors (even without theoretical tuning)?
- Provide sub-Gaussian specializations of the main bounds (explicit constants where feasible), and a brief discussion of how large K(M) needs to be for MF-UCB to match UCB.
- Any insights on characterizing when K(m)_7 is non-negligible, or heuristics to mitigate the gap (e.g., occasional low-fidelity re-checks)?

Overall recommendation
This is a solid and timely contribution with clear novelty and strong theory, addressing an important practical scenario. Despite limited empirical breadth and some assumptions on known bias bounds, I recommend acceptance.
