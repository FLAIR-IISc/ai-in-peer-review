This paper studies a multi-fidelity variant of stochastic K-armed bandits in which each arm can be sampled at multiple costs, with lower-fidelity draws providing biased approximations to the highest-fidelity reward. The authors propose MF-UCB, a cost-aware UCB-style algorithm that adaptively selects fidelity via thresholds derived from the bias bounds ζ(m) and costs λ(m). They provide a regret upper bound and a lower bound, and show on synthetic data that MF-UCB outperforms a naive UCB baseline that ignores lower fidelities. The setting relates to prior work on UCB (Auer; Bubeck & Cesa-Bianchi), multi-fidelity optimization/BO (Huang et al.; Kandasamy et al.), weak/strong labelers in active learning (Zhang & Chaudhuri), and budgeted/bandits-with-costs (Tran-Thanh et al.; Xia et al.).

Strengths: The problem is timely and practically motivated; the algorithmic idea of using cheaper fidelities to prune arms is appealing; the analysis exhibits a clear mechanism—most capital is spent at the fidelity where an arm can be eliminated—and the lower bound helps position the results. The synthetic experiments, though limited, are aligned with the theory.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Quality: The regret formulation and “optimal arm” notion are questionable. The analysis compares to the arm with highest mean irrespective of cost, while practical budgeted settings often optimize reward-per-cost or maximize reward under capital constraints. This choice risks obscuring the true trade-offs and the effective cost of low-fidelity sampling. A complementary cost-complexity (PAC) analysis—identifying the best arm under budget/constraints with high probability—would better match real testing scenarios.
- Clarity: Notation is confusing and dispersed (e.g., s is used before definition and then scarcely used). The manuscript would benefit from a compact notation block, fewer overloaded symbols, and a clearer organization of assumptions (ζ(m), ψ, costs) before presenting MF-UCB.
- Motivation: The advertising example invokes display-time correlations and non-stationarity that are outside the model’s i.i.d., stationary assumptions; this is misleading.
- Empirics: Comparisons are only to vanilla UCB. Please include cost-aware baselines (e.g., budgeted UCB/TS) and sensitivity to ζ(m), λ(m), and misspecification. Discuss the practicality of assuming known ζ(m).

Originality: The formal multi-fidelity bandit model and analysis are novel relative to existing cost/budgeted bandits and multi-fidelity optimization.

Significance: Potentially impactful if the objective and evaluation are aligned with realistic cost-aware decision-making.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel, well-motivated setting with clear algorithmic insight
- Nontrivial upper/lower bounds; near-optimal in regimes

Cons for acceptance:
- Objective misalignment with budgeted practice; regret notion may be ill-suited
- Clarity/notation issues; misleading example
- Limited baselines and sensitivity analysis

Overall: Interesting and promising, but not ready for publication without addressing objective, clarity, and empirical scope.
