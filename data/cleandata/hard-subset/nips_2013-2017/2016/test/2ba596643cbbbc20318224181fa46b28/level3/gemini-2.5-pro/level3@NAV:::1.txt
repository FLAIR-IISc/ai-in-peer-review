This paper studies the multi-armed bandit problem in a multi-fidelity setting, where each arm can be pulled at one of several fidelity levels. Lower fidelities are cheaper but provide biased estimates of the true reward, which is only observed at the highest, most expensive fidelity. This work extends the classical stochastic bandit framework by incorporating a trade-off between information quality, cost, and reward. The authors propose the MF-UCB algorithm, an extension of UCB, designed to leverage cheap fidelities to quickly eliminate suboptimal arms. This problem setting is novel and distinct from related work in budgeted bandits, where costs are arm-dependent but fidelities are not considered.

The primary strength of the paper is its formulation of an interesting and practically relevant problem. The theoretical analysis, which includes a regret bound for the proposed MF-UCB algorithm and a corresponding lower bound, appears technically solid. The core idea of using low-cost approximations for initial exploration is intuitive and significant for applications where experimentation is expensive.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses that limit its impact. The most significant issue is a lack of clarity in its presentation. The main results, particularly Theorem 2 and its comparison to standard UCB, are difficult to parse due to a complex dependence on the sets K^(m). The paper would benefit greatly from a more intuitive and explicit explanation of these results.

Several modeling choices and assumptions also raise concerns. The multiplicative penalization of cost in the regret formulation is unnatural; a discussion comparing it to a more standard additive cost model is needed. Furthermore, the motivation relies heavily on an ad-display example, and it is unclear if the proposed reward/cost model generalizes to other domains. Assumption 1, which constrains the decay of fidelity errors, feels artificial. The authors should justify its generality or, failing that, discuss how to select a subset of fidelities to satisfy it, thereby showing robustness. The algorithm's design appears tightly coupled to this assumption, and its performance under different conditions is unclear. Finally, the acknowledged gap between the regret bound and the lower bound warrants further investigation, as it may suggest a fundamental limitation of the proposed strategy.

Minor issues include the use of "near-optimal" (l.56), an unnecessary period (l.126), and inconsistent set notation (l.179). In summary, while the paper addresses an important problem, it requires substantial revision to improve clarity and justify its modeling assumptions before it can be considered for acceptance.
