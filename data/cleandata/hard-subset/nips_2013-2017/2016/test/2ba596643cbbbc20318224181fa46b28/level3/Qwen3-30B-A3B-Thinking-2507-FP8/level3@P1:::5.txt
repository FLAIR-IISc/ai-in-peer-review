

The paper introduces a novel multi-fidelity bandit framework where each arm has multiple fidelities (approximations) with varying costs, extending the classical K-armed bandit problem. This setting is motivated by practical applications like online advertising, where displaying ads for shorter periods or narrower audiences provides cheaper but biased estimates of long-term performance. The authors formalize this as a multi-fidelity bandit problem, develop the MF-UCB algorithm, and provide theoretical guarantees including regret bounds and a matching lower bound. The work builds on UCB methods [3] but introduces significant new algorithmic and theoretical challenges, distinguishing it from previous work on multi-fidelity optimization [7, 10] and budgeted bandits [13, 15].

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The theoretical analysis is comprehensive, with a detailed regret bound showing MF-UCB achieves O((λ(1)K + λ(2)|Kg|) log(Λ/λ(2))) regret, outperforming standard UCB strategies that ignore multi-fidelity information. The authors carefully handle the biases introduced by lower fidelities, explicitly accounting for the fact that lower fidelity estimates may be misleading. The lower bound analysis demonstrates that MF-UCB is nearly optimal under certain conditions, with a gap only for specific arms (K(m)7) where the algorithm's switching criterion prevents optimal exploration. The simulations on synthetic problems effectively corroborate the theoretical findings, showing MF-UCB outperforms UCB in all cases with steeper improvement curves, and illustrating how the algorithm allocates plays across fidelities according to theoretical predictions.

Clarity is excellent. The problem formulation is well-motivated with concrete examples, and the notation is consistent and well-explained. The algorithm description is precise, and the regret bound is presented with insightful discussion of its implications. The partitioning of arms across fidelities (illustrated in Figure 1) makes the theoretical results accessible. The paper clearly distinguishes its contribution from related work, particularly noting that previous multi-fidelity methods [6, 7, 10] don't formalize regret in this bandit setting.

Originality is strong. The multi-fidelity bandit framework represents a significant conceptual advance beyond the classical bandit problem. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work by formalizing regret in this setting and providing a theoretical analysis of how to leverage the multi-fidelity structure. The authors properly acknowledge related work on active learning [16] but note its different objective (not handling exploration-exploitation trade-offs).

Significance is high. The problem addresses a practical challenge in many domains where expensive evaluations are common, and the paper demonstrates that MF-UCB can significantly reduce exploration costs. The theoretical guarantees show meaningful improvements over standard approaches, particularly when |Kg| << K and λ(1) << λ(2). The results are likely to be useful for practitioners in online advertising, algorithm selection, and experimental design.

The main weakness is the gap between MF-UCB's performance and the lower bound for arms in K(m)7, which the authors acknowledge as an open problem. While this is a minor limitation, it suggests a direction for future work. Another minor point is the focus on the stochastic setting, though the authors clearly state their assumptions.

The paper makes a clear, well-executed contribution to the bandit literature. The multi-fidelity bandit framework is novel, the theoretical analysis is rigorous and insightful, and the empirical results support the claims. The writing is exceptionally clear, making complex theoretical concepts accessible. This work advances the state of the art in bandit problems by providing a principled approach to leverage cheaper approximations, with demonstrated improvements over standard methods. The paper is technically sound, well-motivated, and makes a significant contribution to the field.
