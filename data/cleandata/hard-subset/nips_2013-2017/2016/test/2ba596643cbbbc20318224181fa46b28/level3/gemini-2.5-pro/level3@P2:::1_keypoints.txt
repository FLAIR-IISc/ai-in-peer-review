- Introduces the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit problem where arms can be sampled at M fidelities with cost-accuracy trade-offs and bounded fidelity bias ζ(m).  
- Proposes a cost-based regret for fixed budget Λ and the MF-UCB algorithm that uses cheap low-fidelity samples to screen arms before expensive high-fidelity evaluations.  
- Provides a theoretical analysis: an upper regret bound for MF-UCB and a problem-specific lower bound, with proofs adapting standard bandit techniques.  
- Concern: Assumption 1 requiring rapid decay of ζ(m) (e.g., exponential) is strong, poorly motivated, and may not hold in practice.  
- Suggestion: Authors should discuss whether Assumption 1 is a technical convenience, its necessity for performance, and whether practitioners can select subsequences of fidelities.  
- Concern: A gap remains between upper and lower bounds for arms in certain K(m) sets, implying MF-UCB may be suboptimal for arms that appear deceptively good at low fidelities.  
- Suggestion: Provide more analysis or ideas on why MF-UCB fails for these arms and potential ways to close the gap.  
- Clarity issue: Theorem 2’s regret bound is complex and hard to interpret; include a simplified corollary or worked example showing when MF-UCB improves over standard UCB.  
- Clarity issue: The regret definition (multiplicative cost within rewards and oracle Λµ*) is non-standard and needs stronger justification or discussion of alternatives (e.g., budget-relative or additive cost penalties).  
- Originality: The multi-fidelity bandit formulation is novel and practically relevant across domains like hyperparameter tuning, simulations vs. real tests, and early-stage trials.  
- Practicality: MF-UCB’s core idea—use cheap evaluations for broad exploration and expensive ones for exploitation—is intuitive and useful.  
- Limitation: Significance is constrained by strong assumptions and the unusual regret formulation; broader motivation across domains would help.  
- Acceptance arguments: novel problem, complete technical contribution (algorithm, upper/lower bounds, simulations), and elegant filtering idea.  
- Revision/rejection arguments: poor clarity on main results, weak motivation for key modeling choices (Assumption 1 and regret), and unresolved theoretical gap.  
- Minor issues: careful use of "near-optimal" (line 56), remove stray punctuation (line 126), and prefer standard set notation {k} over [[k]] (line 179).
