Summary and relation to prior work: The paper introduces a multi-fidelity stochastic K-armed bandit in which each arm can be pulled at one of M fidelities with increasing cost and decreasing bias, and defines a cost-weighted pseudo-regret. It proposes MF-UCB, an optimism-based algorithm that adaptively chooses both arm and fidelity, proves cumulative pseudo-regret upper bounds, and establishes lower bounds showing near-optimality up to constants under mild conditions. This setting is distinct from budgeted bandits (which optimize reward/cost ratios) and relates to weak/strong labeler problems and multi-fidelity optimization, but provides a new bandit-theoretic formalization and analysis.

Quality: The theory is interesting and largely sound, with a clear decomposition explaining when low fidelities help. The upper and lower bounds are well motivated; the identified gap for certain K(m)7 cases is acknowledged. However, the supplemental proofs contain many typos that impede verification, and the pervasive “up to a multiplicative constant” notation is sometimes misleading because hidden constants include algorithm parameters (e.g., ρ). Please provide exact inequalities with explicit constants in the main theorems/lemmas and carry ρ, ζ(m), and λ(m) through.

Clarity: The main text is clear and well organized. The supplementary material requires substantial proofreading; fixing it will significantly improve readability (non-exhaustive list below).

Originality: High. The formulation of variable-fidelity pulls with cost-weighted regret and the MF-UCB design are novel relative to NIPS work on budgeted bandits, multi-fidelity optimization, and weak/strong labelers.

Significance: The results are theoretically significant and likely to influence follow-on work on resource-aware exploration. Practical impact is less clear: MF-UCB assumes known fidelity biases ζ(m). Mis-specification may harm performance. Please discuss: (i) robustness if ζ(m) are misspecified; (ii) procedures to estimate or adapt ζ(m) online (e.g., cross-fidelity calibration, conservative inflation of γ(m), joint estimation-and-control); (iii) sensitivity analyses in experiments.

Pros for acceptance:
- New, well-motivated problem and near-optimal analysis.
- Clear main paper; insightful partitioning argument.
- Potentially broad relevance.

Cons against acceptance:
- Practical applicability unclear; no robustness study to ζ.
- Heavy reliance on “up-to-constant” hides algorithmic parameters.
- Numerous typos in the appendix.
