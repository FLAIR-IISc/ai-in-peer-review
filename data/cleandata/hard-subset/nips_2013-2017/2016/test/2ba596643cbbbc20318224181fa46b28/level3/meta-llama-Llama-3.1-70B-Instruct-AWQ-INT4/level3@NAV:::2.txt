This paper introduces a new multi-armed bandit setting with variable-fidelity pulls, where the cost of pulling an arm increases with accuracy. The authors propose the MF-UCB algorithm, which adapts the classical Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) strategy to this multi-fidelity setting. The algorithm uses the lower fidelities to eliminate suboptimal arms and reserves the higher fidelity plays for a small set of arms with high expected reward.

The paper provides a thorough analysis of the MF-UCB algorithm, including upper and lower bounds on the cumulative pseudo-regret. The authors show that the algorithm is nearly optimal under certain conditions and outperforms naive strategies that ignore the approximations.

The main strengths of the paper are:

* The introduction of a new and interesting problem setting, which is relevant to many real-world applications.
* The proposal of a novel algorithm, MF-UCB, which is specifically designed for this setting.
* The thorough analysis of the algorithm, including upper and lower bounds on the cumulative pseudo-regret.

However, there are also some weaknesses and areas for improvement:

* The supplementary materials (proofs) contain many typos and small errors, which can confuse readers. Fixing these errors would greatly improve readability.
* The use of "up-to-a-multiplicative-constant" notation is misleading, as the hidden constants are algorithm parameters. Presenting exact inequalities would improve clarity.
* The practical applicability of the algorithm is unclear, and the authors should discuss the limitations and mitigations of using MF-UCB in practice.
* The paper could benefit from more comparisons with other algorithms and techniques, to better understand the advantages and disadvantages of MF-UCB.

In terms of originality, the paper introduces a new problem setting and a novel algorithm, which is a significant contribution to the field. The analysis of the algorithm is also thorough and well-done.

In terms of significance, the paper addresses an important problem in the field of multi-armed bandits, and the results have the potential to impact many real-world applications. The algorithm and analysis presented in the paper could be useful for practitioners and researchers working in this area.

Overall, I would recommend accepting this paper, but with some revisions to address the weaknesses mentioned above.
