Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces a multi-fidelity variant of the stochastic K-armed bandit. Each arm can be pulled at one of M fidelities with increasing cost λ(m) and decreasing bias ζ(m); the highest fidelity (m = M) corresponds to the true reward. The authors define a cost-weighted pseudo-regret appropriate for budgeted evaluation, R(Λ) = (unused-capital term) + sum over plays of cost × instantaneous pseudo-regret with respect to the highest-fidelity mean. They propose MF-UCB, which maintains upper confidence bounds per fidelity and chooses both the arm and the fidelity adaptively: lower fidelities are used to cheaply eliminate clearly suboptimal arms; expensive high-fidelity pulls are reserved for near-optimal candidates. They prove an upper bound on cumulative (cost-weighted) pseudo-regret and a matching lower bound up to constants for a broad subset of arms, showing near-optimality under conditions.

Relative to prior work, this differs from classical UCB (Auer et al.), budgeted bandits (e.g., Tran-Thanh et al.; Xia et al.), and weak/strong labeler active learning (Zhang & Chaudhuri). It also complements multi-fidelity optimization (Huang et al.; Rajnarayan et al.) and multi-fidelity GP bandits (Kandasamy et al. 2016), by formalizing regret with biased low-fidelity observations and cost-aware exploration. The fidelity-switching rule, the partition K(m) based on ζ(m) and γ(m), and the Λ-budget analysis are the key technical novelties.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The main results (upper bound under ψ-concentration and Assumption 1; lower bound for Bernoulli under Assumption 3) are well motivated and largely correct. The proof ideas (arm partitioning, fidelity-specific UCBs, high-probability control of cross-fidelity pulls) are standard yet adapted thoughtfully to biased observations and cost. The gap identified for arms in K(m)7 is transparent and clearly discussed as an open problem.
- Rigor vs. presentation: The main paper is clear and careful; however, the supplementary proofs contain many typos and small errors that make verification unnecessarily hard. These are mostly cosmetic but can confuse readers following the argument.
- Experiments are synthetic but appropriate for illustrating the mechanism; stronger empirical validation would help.

Clarity
- The main text is well organized and clearly explains the new setting, regret, MF-UCB, and the high-level proof structure. Figures and the partition intuition are helpful.
- However, pervasive “up-to-a-multiplicative-constant” notation (e.g., using symbols like ., &, and phrases like “ignoring constants”) is misleading because the hidden constants include algorithm parameters such as ρ and problem-dependent quantities that directly affect choices like γ(m) and the fidelity-switching behavior. Stating exact inequalities (with explicit constants and parameter dependence) at least for the theorem statements and main lemmas would greatly improve clarity and reproducibility.

Originality
- The problem formulation (multi-fidelity pulls with biased approximations and cost-aware regret) and the MF-UCB design are novel for stochastic bandits. While inspired by UCB, the fidelity-aware confidence aggregation and switching thresholds are new.
- The lower bound tailored to the multi-fidelity structure and the partition-based near-optimality are original contributions.

Significance
- The theory is meaningful and likely to influence subsequent work on cost-aware exploration with biased proxies. The results clarify when and why low-fidelity data help, and where they cannot.
- Practical impact is promising but under-discussed: the algorithm assumes known ζ(m), which can be difficult to estimate in applications, and its sensitivity to mis-specification is not evaluated.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, well-motivated new bandit setting with a principled cost-weighted regret.
- Elegant algorithm that adapts UCB to multi-fidelity bias/cost trade-offs.
- Strong theoretical results: near-optimal upper bounds and informative lower bounds.
- Useful intuition connecting fidelity costs, bias envelopes, and exploration allocation.
- Potentially broad relevance (A/B testing, advertising, simulation-based evaluation).

Cons and suggestions
- Practical applicability: Discuss how ζ(m) can be estimated or bounded in practice; propose robust variants that tolerate mis-specified ζ(m). Suggestions:
  - Use conservative upper bounds ζ̂(m) with confidence intervals, updated online.
  - Calibrate γ(m) adaptively via pilot samples or cross-validation on proxy-vs-target discrepancies.
  - Provide sensitivity analyses to ζ(m) mis-specification and to cost ratios λ(m).
- Notation: Replace “up-to-constant” shorthand in theorem/lemma statements with exact inequalities; make the dependence on ρ, ν, ψ explicit. Clarify where ρ > 4 is truly required vs. ρ > 2.
- Assumptions: Comment on the practical reasonableness of Assumption 1 (decay of ζ) and Assumption 3 in the lower bound. For non-sub-Gaussian tails, specify ψ examples.
- Experiments: Add ablations varying ζ(m), λ(m), and ρ; include a robustness experiment with mis-specified ζ(m); if possible, a small real-world or simulators-to-real proxy case.

Overall assessment
A strong and timely theoretical contribution that cleanly formalizes a practically relevant setting and provides near-optimal guarantees. I recommend acceptance, contingent on fixing the appendix issues, clarifying constants in key bounds, and adding a discussion (and ideally experiments) about estimating ζ(m) and robustness to its mis-specification.
