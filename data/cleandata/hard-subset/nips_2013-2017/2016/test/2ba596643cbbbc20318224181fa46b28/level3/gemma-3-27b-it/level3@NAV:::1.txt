## Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

This paper introduces a multi-fidelity bandit problem where observing arm rewards is costly, but cheaper approximations are available. The authors propose the MF-UCB algorithm, a UCB variant adapted to this setting, and provide a regret bound, a lower bound, and empirical evidence suggesting improvement over a high-fidelity-only UCB approach. The problem is relevant, particularly in scenarios like online advertising and algorithm selection, where obtaining precise reward estimates can be expensive. The technical aspects, including the regret analysis and lower bound derivation, appear generally solid, building on existing bandit literature.

However, the paper suffers from significant clarity issues hindering full comprehension and assessment. While the core idea is intuitive – leveraging cheaper approximations to refine exploration – the justification for certain modeling choices and assumptions is weak. Specifically, the multiplicative cost penalization in the regret formulation feels unnatural; an additive cost would align better with practical considerations and deserves discussion. The motivation based on the ad-display example feels limited, failing to convey the broader applicability of their reward/cost model. The assumption that fidelity errors (zeta_m) are constrained in a specific way (Assumption 1) is particularly problematic. It’s unclear why this constraint is necessary or generally valid, and the paper doesn’t explore strategies for handling violations of this assumption – like selecting a suitable subsequence of fidelities.

Furthermore, the algorithm design seems heavily tailored to the specific zeta configuration, raising concerns about its robustness. It’s unclear how performance is affected if the fidelities don’t decay as assumed. The gap between the derived lower bound and the MF-UCB’s performance also warrants further investigation, potentially indicating room for improvement. Finally, the presentation of Theorem 2 and the comparison with UCB are difficult to parse, with the dependence on Delta and the sets K^(m) requiring more explicit explanation.

**Arguments for acceptance:** The problem is well-motivated and potentially impactful, and the technical analysis appears largely correct. The paper offers a novel approach to a relevant problem in stochastic bandits.

**Arguments against acceptance:** The lack of clarity regarding assumptions, modeling choices, and result interpretation severely limits the paper’s accessibility and impact. The potentially restrictive nature of Assumption 1 and the lower bound gap raise concerns about the algorithm’s practical applicability and optimality.

**Overall Assessment:** While the paper presents a technically sound contribution, the significant clarity issues and questionable assumptions prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance in its current form. Major revisions addressing these points are needed.

**Minor issues:** "near-optimal" (l.56), unnecessary period before "for all m < M" (l.126), use curly braces for [[k]] set notation (l.179).
