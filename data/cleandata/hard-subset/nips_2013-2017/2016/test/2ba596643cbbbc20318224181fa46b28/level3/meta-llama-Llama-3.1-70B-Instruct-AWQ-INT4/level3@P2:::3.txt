The paper "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit" introduces a novel framework for studying exploration-exploitation trade-offs in situations where cheaper approximations to a desired experiment are available. The authors propose a multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit model, where each arm has multiple fidelities, with lower fidelities being cheaper but introducing bounded bias in estimates. They then develop a UCB-based algorithm, MF-UCB, that widens confidence intervals to account for fidelity-induced bias.

The paper's main contribution is the problem formulation, which is a significant departure from traditional multi-armed bandit models. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the MF-UCB algorithm, including theoretical regret bounds and simulation results. However, the reviewer notes that the theoretical regret bounds are messy and hard to interpret, making it challenging to understand the algorithm's performance.

The paper argues that MF-UCB's regret is often much better than always-using highest fidelity UCB, particularly when the cost of playing an arm at different fidelities varies significantly. The simulation results provided in the paper support this claim, showing a 3-4x improvement of MF-UCB over UCB in certain scenarios. However, the reviewer questions the modeling of low fidelity as increased bias rather than increased variance, which may not be justified in all applications.

The link between the model and motivating applications is also weak. While some examples, such as algorithm selection, partially justify the bias model, others, such as short ad displays, are unconvincing, and the regret objective is unclear for certain applications. Nevertheless, the reviewer acknowledges that the main novelty of the paper is the problem formulation, which is a significant contribution to the field.

The algorithm itself uses standard optimism-in-the-face-of-uncertainty ideas, which is not particularly novel. However, the application of these ideas to the multi-fidelity setting is new and interesting. The simulation parameter choices, however, lack justification, and the reported improvement might reflect chosen parameters rather than typical performance.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the MF-UCB algorithm. However, the paper's clarity could be improved, particularly in the presentation of the theoretical regret bounds. The notation is sometimes confusing, and the text could benefit from additional explanations and examples to illustrate the key concepts.

The originality of the paper is high, as it introduces a new problem formulation and applies existing ideas to a novel setting. The significance of the paper is also high, as it has the potential to impact various applications, such as online advertising, finance, and robotics.

In conclusion, while the paper has some weaknesses, its contributions to the field of multi-armed bandits and exploration-exploitation trade-offs are significant. The reviewer recommends acceptance, but suggests that the authors address the concerns raised about the modeling of low fidelity and the link between the model and motivating applications.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper introduces a novel problem formulation that is a significant departure from traditional multi-armed bandit models.
* The authors provide a thorough analysis of the MF-UCB algorithm, including theoretical regret bounds and simulation results.
* The paper has the potential to impact various applications, such as online advertising, finance, and robotics.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The theoretical regret bounds are messy and hard to interpret.
* The link between the model and motivating applications is weak.
* The simulation parameter choices lack justification, and the reported improvement might reflect chosen parameters rather than typical performance.
