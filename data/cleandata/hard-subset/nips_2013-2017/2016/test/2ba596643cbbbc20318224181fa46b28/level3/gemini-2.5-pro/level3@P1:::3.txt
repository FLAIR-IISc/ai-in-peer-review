This paper introduces a novel variant of the stochastic multi-armed bandit problem, termed the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit. In this setting, for each of the K arms, a player can choose to pull from one of M "fidelities". Lower fidelities are cheaper to sample but provide a reward estimate that is biased with respect to the highest fidelity's true mean reward. The paper formalizes this by assuming a known upper bound, ζ(m), on the bias for each fidelity m < M.

The main contribution is the proposal and analysis of the Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB) algorithm. This algorithm adapts the classic UCB approach by incorporating the bias term ζ(m) into the confidence bounds. At each step, MF-UCB selects the arm with the highest optimistic estimate of the true (highest fidelity) reward. It then uses a clever rule to decide which fidelity to sample from: it sequentially checks from the cheapest fidelity upwards, sampling at the first level `m` where the statistical uncertainty is larger than a predefined threshold `γ(m)`. This mechanism is designed to use cheap, low-fidelity samples to quickly eliminate clearly suboptimal arms, while reserving expensive, high-fidelity samples for a smaller set of promising candidates.

The paper provides a theoretical analysis of MF-UCB, deriving an upper bound on a cost-weighted notion of regret. This bound shows that the algorithm's performance depends on the fidelity level at which an arm can be identified as suboptimal. For many arms, this can be a much lower, cheaper fidelity, leading to a significant improvement over a naive UCB strategy that only ever uses the most expensive, highest fidelity. The authors complement this with a regret lower bound, showing MF-UCB is near-optimal under certain conditions. The theoretical claims are supported by simulations on synthetic problems.

This work relates to the extensive literature on UCB methods for bandits, but the multi-fidelity formulation appears to be a novel and interesting direction. While multi-fidelity methods exist in optimization and reinforcement learning, their application to the exploration-exploitation trade-off in bandits is new.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:**
The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed algorithm is well-motivated and the theoretical analysis, including both upper and lower bounds, is thorough. The proofs appear sound. However, there are some weaknesses in the problem setup and evaluation.

*   **Modeling Assumptions:** A key assumption is that lower fidelities introduce bounded *bias* in the mean reward, rather than, for example, increased variance. While the authors explicitly state this is their goal, the justification for this modeling choice is not entirely convincing for all the motivating examples. The algorithm selection example is a good fit (e.g., training on less data may lead to a biased performance estimate). However, for the online advertising example, displaying an ad for a shorter time seems more likely to produce a higher-variance, unbiased estimate of the click-through rate, rather than a systematically biased one. A discussion of why the bias model is preferred over an increased-variance model would strengthen the paper.
*   **Regret Definition:** The regret is defined with respect to the highest fidelity mean `µ(M)` regardless of the fidelity played. While this makes sense in the context of finding the single best arm (at its highest fidelity), it may not match the goals of all applications. For instance, in an advertising scenario with a fixed budget, the goal might be to maximize total clicks, where clicks from cheap, low-fidelity displays are still valuable.
*   **Experimental Evaluation:** The simulations demonstrate that MF-UCB can outperform standard UCB. However, the choice of parameters for the synthetic problems (provided in the appendix) seems to be in a regime that strongly favors the proposed method. For example, the biases `ζ(m)` are chosen to be quite large. The impressive 3-4x improvement might not be representative of typical performance. The paper would be more convincing with a sensitivity analysis over these parameters or by grounding the simulation setup in a more realistic application scenario.

**Clarity:**
The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The problem formulation, algorithm, and the intuition behind it are explained effectively. The illustrations, particularly Figure 1 explaining the arm partitioning, are helpful. The primary weakness in clarity lies in the main theoretical result, Theorem 2. The regret bound is expressed in terms of a complex partitioning of the arms (`K(m)`) which makes it quite difficult to parse and interpret directly. While the surrounding text provides good high-level intuition, the formal statement itself is not very illuminating. A simplified corollary for an easier case (e.g., M=2) might have improved readability.

**Originality:**
The primary originality of this paper lies in its problem formulation. The multi-fidelity bandit is a novel, interesting, and practical extension of the classic MAB problem. It opens up a new direction for research. The MF-UCB algorithm itself, while sound, is a more incremental contribution. It is a natural and elegant adaptation of the standard "optimism in the face of uncertainty" principle to the new setting, but does not introduce fundamentally new algorithmic ideas.

**Significance:**
The problem addressed is significant. In many real-world sequential decision-making problems, from A/B testing to scientific experiment design and hyperparameter tuning, practitioners have access to cheap, approximate evaluations. This work provides the first formal framework and principled algorithm for leveraging such information to manage the exploration-exploitation trade-off. The ideas presented are likely to be built upon by other researchers and could potentially be used by practitioners.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Introduces a novel and well-motivated problem (multi-fidelity bandits) that is of practical and theoretical interest.
2.  Proposes an intuitive and effective algorithm (MF-UCB) that elegantly handles the trade-offs in this new setting.
3.  Provides a solid theoretical contribution with both upper and lower regret bounds, establishing the near-optimality of the proposed approach.
4.  The core idea of using cheap fidelities for broad exploration and expensive ones for fine-grained exploitation is powerful and clearly demonstrated.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  The core modeling assumption (bias, not variance) is not fully justified for all motivating applications, potentially limiting the scope of the contribution.
2.  The algorithmic novelty is modest, as MF-UCB is a direct extension of existing UCB principles.
3.  The empirical results, while positive, lack a strong justification for the chosen parameters, which may overstate the algorithm's typical performance benefits.
4.  The main regret bound is quite complex and difficult to interpret.

In summary, this is a strong paper that introduces an important new problem to the bandit literature. While the modeling assumptions and experimental setup could be strengthened, the novelty of the formulation and the quality of the accompanying theoretical analysis make this a valuable contribution to the field.
