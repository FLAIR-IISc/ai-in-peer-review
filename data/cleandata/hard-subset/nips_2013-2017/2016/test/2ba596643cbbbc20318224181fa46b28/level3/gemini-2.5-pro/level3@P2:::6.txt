This paper introduces a novel and interesting variant of the stochastic K-armed bandit problem, termed the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit. In this setting, each arm can be played at one of M different "fidelities". Playing at a lower fidelity is cheaper but provides a biased and potentially noisy estimate of the arm's true reward, which is only observable at the most expensive, highest fidelity. The paper provides strong practical motivation for this setting, citing examples like online advertising (approximating long-term click-through rates with short-term displays) and algorithm selection (approximating full-dataset performance using smaller subsets).

The main contributions of the paper are threefold. First, it provides a formal definition for the multi-fidelity bandit problem, including a cost-aware notion of regret suited for a fixed-budget setting. Second, it proposes a new algorithm, the Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB), which elegantly extends the classic UCB principle to this new setting. The core idea of MF-UCB is to use the cheaper, lower-fidelity information to rapidly identify and discard clearly suboptimal arms, thus saving the expensive high-fidelity evaluations for a smaller set of promising candidates. Third, the paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of MF-UCB, including an upper bound on its regret and a corresponding lower bound for the problem, demonstrating that MF-UCB is near-optimal under certain conditions. The theoretical claims are further supported by empirical simulations that show MF-UCB outperforming a standard UCB strategy. This work builds upon the extensive literature on UCB methods but addresses a new set of algorithmic and theoretical challenges posed by the multi-fidelity structure.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed MF-UCB algorithm is intuitive and the theoretical analysis is comprehensive and sound. The regret analysis, which characterizes how the algorithm allocates plays across different fidelities based on the properties of the arms, is particularly insightful. The inclusion of a nearly-matching lower bound significantly strengthens the theoretical contribution by showing the proposed algorithm is close to optimal.

The primary weakness, however, is a strong assumption that limits the paper's completeness and practical applicability. The analysis relies on the assumption that the bias parameters `ζ(m)`—the maximum deviation of the m-th fidelity's mean reward from the highest fidelity's mean—are known in advance for all fidelities `m`. In many real-world applications, it is unclear how one would obtain these values accurately. The paper does not discuss the sensitivity of the algorithm to misspecification of these parameters, nor does it suggest methods for learning them online. This is a significant practical limitation.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The problem formulation is clear, the algorithm is described precisely, and the main theoretical results are stated and interpreted effectively. The authors do an excellent job of providing intuition for their algorithmic design choices, such as the switching threshold `γ(m)`, and for the structure of their theoretical bounds. The paper is easy to follow for a reader familiar with the multi-armed bandit literature. However, the supplementary material appears to contain a number of minor errors and typos that should be corrected for the final version.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. The formulation of the multi-fidelity bandit problem is novel and addresses a practical gap in the existing literature. While related ideas exist in optimization and reinforcement learning, this paper is the first to formalize and provide a rigorous regret analysis for this problem in the stochastic bandit setting. The proposed MF-UCB algorithm and its analysis are also novel contributions.

**Significance:** The results are important and likely to have a significant impact. The multi-fidelity setting captures a common real-world trade-off between cost and accuracy of evaluation, and this work provides a principled framework for navigating it. Researchers and practitioners in areas such as hyperparameter optimization, A/B testing, and simulation-based science are likely to find these ideas useful and build upon them. The significance is somewhat tempered by the strong assumption of known `ζ(m)` values, but the foundational nature of the work remains.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Introduces a novel, well-motivated, and practical extension of the multi-armed bandit problem.
*   Proposes an elegant and intuitive algorithm (MF-UCB) for this new setting.
*   Provides a strong and thorough theoretical analysis, including both upper and lower regret bounds, demonstrating near-optimality.
*   The work opens up a promising new direction for research in bandits and sequential decision-making.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The assumption that the fidelity bias bounds `ζ(m)` are known a priori is a major limitation that may hinder direct practical application. The paper would be substantially stronger if this assumption were discussed, relaxed, or empirically tested for sensitivity.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions**

The main suggestion for improvement is to address the assumption of known `ζ(m)`. The authors should, at a minimum, add a discussion of this limitation. It would be even better to include an empirical study of the algorithm's robustness to misspecified `ζ(m)` values. An outline of how these parameters could be learned from data, perhaps as a direction for future work, would also strengthen the paper.

Additionally, the manuscript would benefit from careful proofreading of the supplementary material. We noted several small errors that should be corrected:
*   In several places (e.g., lines 326, 357, 386), "K(m)" appears to be mistakenly written as "H(m)".
*   Line 343: In the indicator function, "β_{k*,t} ≤ s" should likely be "s = µ*".
*   Line 345: The threshold should be specified as `γ(m)`.
*   Lines 346-347: In the expression "ψ(Δ_k^(m) − γ^(m)) ≤ ψ(γ^(m)) when ψ(Δ_k^(m)) ≤ 2 γ^(m)", both instances of "≤" should be "≥".
*   Line 384: The summation should be over arms in partitions "below" K(m), not "above".
*   Line 407: On the RHS, `δ(m)` should be `δ(M)`, and the final term should be `κ λ(m)`.
*   Lines 431 and 440: "for l ≥ m" should be "for l ≥ p".
*   Line 450: "P(A_{n,3})" should be "P(A_{n,2})".

In conclusion, this is a strong paper that introduces an important new problem and provides a high-quality, near-optimal solution. The main weakness is a practical one regarding a key assumption. Despite this, the novelty and technical depth of the contribution are significant, and the paper is a clear candidate for acceptance. The authors are strongly encouraged to address the comments above in the final version.
