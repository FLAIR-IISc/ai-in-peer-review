- New problem: stochastic K-armed bandits with M fidelities, fidelity-specific known bias bounds ζ(m), and costs; objective is minimize cost-weighted pseudo-regret against highest-fidelity mean µ(M) under budget Λ.
- Algorithm: MF-UCB forms UCBs on µ(M) by combining empirical means plus bias envelopes and selects the minimum across fidelities.
- Fidelity selection: uses thresholds γ(m) to decide whether to continue at cheaper fidelities or escalate, balancing bias vs. information gain.
- Upper bound: Theorem 2 gives regret bounds decomposed by fidelity and arm partitions K(m), showing cheap fidelities eliminate many suboptimal arms and concentrate expensive samples on near-optimal arms.
- Lower bound: Theorem 4 (change-of-measure) shows near-optimality for most arm partitions and precisely identifies cases (K(m)7) where MF-UCB may be conservative.
- Simulations: Empirical results match theory, showing reduced regret slope and effective cheap-fidelity filtering.
- Strength: Clear formalization, well-motivated examples (ads, algorithm selection), and natural integration of bias envelopes into UCB indices.
- Strength: The γ(m) rule is well-justified and encodes the tradeoff between cheap elimination and expensive confirmation.
- Strength: Theoretical analysis is solid and interpretable, providing insight when multi-fidelity helps (many suboptimal arms, large cost gaps).
- Weakness: Practicality of known ζ(m) is unclear — how to obtain them, arm-agnostic vs arm-dependent, and sensitivity to misspecification.
- Weakness: Assumption 1 (fast decay of ζ(m)) may be strong; limited discussion on performance and additive penalties when it fails.
- Weakness: Lower bound only for Bernoulli rewards; unclear if matching lower bounds extend to general ψ distributions.
- Weakness: Empirical comparisons limited to vanilla UCB; missing stronger baselines (KL-UCB, UCB-V, Thompson sampling) and ablation studies.
- Weakness: Regret comparator fixed to µ(M); alternative objectives (fidelity-aware utilities) and generalization are not fully discussed.
- Clarity suggestions: Explain practical ways to obtain ζ(m) (domain knowledge, pilots, worst-case modeling) and add a small numerical example illustrating switch logic and K(m) partitions.
- Pros for acceptance: principled new problem, natural algorithm, strong theoretical analysis with lower bound, and insightful characterization of when multi-fidelity helps.
- Cons against acceptance: reliance on known ζ(m) and Assumption 1, synthetic-only experiments with limited baselines, and lower-bound mismatch on some partitions plus Bernoulli-only lower bound.
- Questions for authors: How to obtain and how sensitive is MF-UCB to mis-specified ζ(m)? Can ζ be arm-dependent? Can algorithm handle unknown ψ or time-varying costs? Please compare to stronger baselines and heuristics.
- Minor editorial corrections: several line/notation fixes (e.g., K(m) vs H(m), indicator term s = µ*, γ→γ(m), inequality directions, summation indices, δ(m)→δ(M), index/lowercase typos listed).
- Recommendation: Weak accept — strong theory and novel formulation, but practical issues around ζ(m) and limited empirical baselines should be addressed.
- Reviewer confidence: High in bandits/UCB theory; moderate in application breadth and empirical assessment.
