- Paper formulates multi-fidelity stochastic K-armed bandits with fidelities having increasing costs and known decreasing bias bounds ζ(m), optimizing cost-weighted pseudo-regret under budget Λ.  
- MF-UCB algorithm widens UCBs by ζ(m), takes minima across fidelities, and adaptively chooses fidelity via a threshold γ(m) to use cheap fidelities to eliminate suboptimal arms.  
- Theorem 2: upper bound showing MF-UCB often outperforms single highest-fidelity UCB by investing low fidelity on clearly suboptimal arms.  
- Theorem 4: lower bound establishing near-optimality except on a subset of arms where the switching rule can be conservative.  
- Simulations show 3–4× improvement over always-using-highest-fidelity UCB and behavior consistent with theory (many bad arms played only at low fidelity).  
- Novelty lies in problem formulation and cost-weighted regret for biased multi-fidelity observations; algorithmic idea follows standard optimism with bias-aware confidence sets.  
- Strength: clear, relevant formalization matching scenarios like early-stopped training or subsampled data for algorithm selection.  
- Strength: simple, principled algorithm integrating bias bounds and a clean fidelity-threshold rule.  
- Strength: nontrivial, careful analysis with interpretable takeaway that lower fidelities screen many arms.  
- Strength: empirical curves align qualitatively with theoretical predictions.  
- Weakness: theoretical bounds are algebraically heavy, rely on nested partitions K(m), Assumption 1, and large-Λ regimes, limiting interpretability.  
- Weakness: modeling low fidelity as bounded bias (not increased variance) is only partially justified and may not fit many applications (e.g., ads).  
- Weakness: practicality depends on knowing ζ(m), ψ, and costs; no analysis or methods for misspecified or unknown ζ(m).  
- Weakness: regret definition fixes instantaneous reward to highest fidelity even when playing low fidelity, which misaligns with some applications.  
- Weakness: lower bound gap for certain arms indicates inefficiency in the switching rule with no explored remedy or heuristic.  
- Weakness: experiments are synthetic only, parameter choices unjustified, no sensitivity analysis, and limited baselines beyond UCB@highest-fidelity.  
- Quality: technical development and proofs are largely sound but interpretability and robustness to assumptions are lacking.  
- Clarity: generally clear writing but regret definition, application mapping, and main bound could use simpler corollaries and worked examples.  
- Originality: problem and regret notion are original and timely; algorithmic core is standard but lower bound is a meaningful contribution.  
- Significance: moderate—appealing model likely to spur follow-ups, but adoption depends on showing robustness and practical gains.  
- Suggestions: provide simplified sub-Gaussian M=2/3 corollaries with constants and comparison tables vs. high-fidelity UCB.  
- Suggestions: broaden experiments—sensitivity to ζ, λ, noise; misspecified ζ; γ(m) ablations; more baselines and real-data cases.  
- Suggestions: clarify regret objective per application (e.g., ads) and discuss estimating or upper-bounding ζ(m) online and robustness to misspecification.  
- Pros for acceptance: novel formulation, solid theoretical treatment, and intuitive algorithm design with supportive empirical results.  
- Cons for acceptance: weak application linkage, heavy assumptions (known ζ, Assumption 1), hard-to-interpret bounds, and narrow experimental evaluation.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept—interesting and careful work that needs stronger empirical validation and robustness analysis.
