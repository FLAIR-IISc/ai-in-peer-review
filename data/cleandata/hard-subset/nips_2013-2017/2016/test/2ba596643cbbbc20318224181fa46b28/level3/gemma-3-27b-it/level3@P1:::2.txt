This paper introduces a novel multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit (MF-MAB) problem where obtaining precise reward estimates is costly, but cheaper, albeit biased, approximations are available. The authors formalize this setting, motivated by applications like online advertising and algorithm selection, and propose a Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB) algorithm. They provide both upper and lower bounds on the cumulative pseudo-regret, demonstrating the algorithm's near-optimality under certain conditions.

The work builds upon the extensive literature of multi-armed bandits, notably the UCB algorithm [Auer, 2002], extending it to accommodate the multi-fidelity aspect.  While multi-fidelity optimization has been explored in other areas [Huang et al., 2006], this paper uniquely frames it within the exploration-exploitation trade-off of the bandit setting and rigorously analyzes the resulting regret.  Related work on budgeted bandits [Tran-Thanh et al., 2013] and active learning [Zhang & Chaudhuri, 2016] are also discussed, but are distinguished by differing problem formulations.

The strengths of this paper are significant. The MF-MAB problem is a relevant and practically motivated extension of the classical bandit problem. The MF-UCB algorithm is intuitively appealing, and the theoretical analysis providing both upper and lower bounds demonstrates a strong understanding of the problem's inherent complexities. The proofs, while lengthy, appear sound (modulo the issues discussed below) and contribute a substantial theoretical foundation for this new setting. The inclusion of simulation results further supports the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm.  Overall, this work represents a significant contribution to the field of bandit algorithms.

However, the paper is not without weaknesses. The most pressing concern is the sheer number of typographical errors and minor inconsistencies within the supplementary material (Appendix). While these errors do not invalidate the core arguments, they significantly hinder readability and potentially introduce confusion.  A thorough proofreading pass is strongly recommended.  Examples include typos in equations, incorrect lemma references, and inconsistent variable notation (as detailed in the provided list). 

Furthermore, the notation involving "up-to-a-multiplicative-constant" can be misleading.  Presenting explicit inequalities with the constants clearly stated would enhance clarity and allow for easier comparison with existing results.  While acknowledging the algorithmic parameters (e.g., ρ), stating inequalities precisely removes ambiguity.

The practical applicability of MF-UCB also warrants further discussion. The assumption that the fidelity bias (ζ(m)) is known is a strong one. In real-world scenarios, accurately estimating these biases can be challenging. The paper could benefit from a discussion of strategies for estimating ζ(m) or mitigating the impact of inaccurate estimates. The robustness of the algorithm to noisy or inaccurate fidelity information is an open question that deserves attention.  The paper also lacks a thorough discussion of computational costs associated with the algorithm.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and well-motivated problem formulation.
*   Strong theoretical results with both upper and lower bounds.
*   Clear and intuitive algorithm design.
*   Demonstrated empirical performance.
*   Significant contribution to the field of bandit algorithms.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Numerous typographical errors and inconsistencies in the appendix.
*   Potential ambiguity due to “up-to-a-multiplicative-constant” notation.
*   Strong assumption of known fidelity bias.
*   Limited discussion of practical limitations and robustness.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the concerns regarding the appendix and practical considerations, the paper presents a solid theoretical and algorithmic contribution to the field.  The novelty of the problem, the rigor of the analysis, and the potential impact of the results outweigh the weaknesses.  I recommend **acceptance** with the strong encouragement that the authors address the errors in the appendix before publication. A more thorough discussion of practical limitations and potential mitigation strategies would also strengthen the paper.
