This paper introduces the Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit, a novel and interesting extension of the classical stochastic bandit problem. In this framework, each arm can be sampled at multiple fidelities, where lower fidelities are cheaper but provide biased approximations of the true (highest-fidelity) reward distribution. This setup is well-motivated by real-world scenarios like A/B testing or hyperparameter tuning, where cheap, noisy evaluations can accelerate exploration. The authors propose an adaptive algorithm, MF-UCB, which extends the UCB principle to this setting. They provide a regret analysis and a lower bound, and demonstrate experimentally that their method outperforms a standard UCB baseline on synthetic problems. This work builds upon the extensive literature on UCB methods while differing from budgeted bandits by focusing on approximations rather than reward/cost ratios.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The problem formulation is novel and addresses a practical challenge in sequential decision-making under budget constraints. The idea of using cheap approximations to efficiently prune the action space has significant potential impact for practitioners. The paper presents a complete contribution, including a formal problem definition, an algorithm, theoretical analysis, and empirical validation.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Clarity:** The paper suffers from several clarity issues that hinder readability. The writing is unwieldy in sections, and the notation is confusing and dispersed throughout the text (e.g., the variable `s` is defined after its first use and then sparsely referenced), making the technical sections difficult to follow. Furthermore, the motivating advertising example is potentially misleading, as it introduces concepts like display time correlations and non-stationarity that are not addressed by the stationary model presented.

*   **Quality:** The technical soundness of the regret analysis is questionable. The paper defines the "best arm" as the one with the highest mean reward, independent of cost. However, an optimal policy in a cost-sensitive setting should arguably maximize a reward/cost ratio. The current regret definition compares against an oracle playing the highest-mean arm, which may obscure the true cost of exploration by effectively hiding the expense of low-fidelity sampling.

**Actionable Feedback:**
The paper would be significantly strengthened by reframing the analysis. A cost-complexity (or sample-complexity) analysis focused on identifying the best arm with high probability under a fixed budget would be a more natural fit for the motivating applications. This is a common objective in real-world testing scenarios. Additionally, the manuscript requires a thorough revision to improve writing clarity, streamline the presentation of notation, and better align the motivating examples with the paper's formal scope.

While the core idea is promising, the paper is not ready for publication due to significant concerns regarding the clarity of the presentation and the appropriateness of the theoretical formulation.
