## Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

This paper introduces a novel multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit (MF-MAB) framework, where evaluating each arm has varying costs and fidelities, with lower fidelities providing cheaper but biased estimates. The authors propose MF-UCB, a UCB-based algorithm that adjusts confidence intervals to account for fidelity-induced bias, and provide theoretical regret bounds. 

The work builds upon the well-established literature of multi-armed bandits (e.g., Auer, 2002; Lai & Robbins, 1985) and extends it to a practically relevant scenario where obtaining precise measurements is expensive. The connection to related work in multi-fidelity optimization (e.g., Huang et al., 2017) and active learning (e.g., Zhang & Chaudhuri, 2016) is acknowledged, but the paper clearly distinguishes itself by focusing on the exploration-exploitation trade-off inherent in the bandit setting. The introduction of the bias model, rather than solely focusing on variance, is a key contribution, though its justification is somewhat uneven across motivating examples. While algorithm selection lends itself well to this framing, the example of short ad displays feels less natural, and the regret objective in such cases isn’t fully clarified.

The strength of the paper lies in its problem formulation and the initial development of a principled algorithm. The theoretical analysis, while resulting in somewhat messy regret bounds, demonstrates a potential improvement over simply using the highest fidelity UCB. The simulation results provide empirical support for this claim, showing consistent gains with MF-UCB. However, the lack of justification for the simulation parameter choices (detailed in Appendix C) is a significant weakness. The reported 3-4x improvement could be sensitive to these choices, particularly ζ^(m), and may not generalize to other problem instances. 

The core novelty is the MF-MAB problem itself, with MF-UCB being a relatively straightforward application of UCB principles. While sound, this limits the algorithmic contribution. Furthermore, the lower bound analysis, while present, doesn't fully close the gap with the proposed algorithm, leaving room for future work. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Introduces a relevant and well-motivated problem formulation.
*   Proposes a reasonable algorithm (MF-UCB) with theoretical guarantees.
*   Empirical results support the potential benefits of the approach.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Theoretical regret bounds are complex and difficult to interpret.
*   Simulation parameter choices lack justification, raising concerns about generalizability.
*   Algorithmic novelty is incremental.
*   Link between model and motivating applications is somewhat weak.



**Overall:** I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field, but with some caveats. The problem formulation is valuable, and the algorithm is a reasonable first step. However, the lack of robustness analysis and the complex theoretical results temper my enthusiasm. I would recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically addressing the justification of simulation parameters and clarifying the connection between the bias model and various applications.
