- Paper introduces a multi-fidelity stochastic K-armed bandit with biased lower-fidelity observations, a cost-weighted pseudo-regret, and proposes MF-UCB using fidelity-escalation thresholds γ(m).
- Main theorem: MF-UCB concentrates low-cost plays on clearly suboptimal arms, reserves high-fidelity plays for near-optimal arms, achieving improved regret over naive UCB in many regimes.
- A Bernoulli lower bound shows near-optimality except for a subset of arms where MF-UCB’s switching rule can be suboptimal.
- Strength: Clear, practical algorithmic idea to use biased cheap proxies to eliminate arms early and invest expensive samples selectively.
- Strength: Nontrivial theoretical development with matching upper and lower bounds and characterization of when bounds are tight or loose.
- Strength: Potentially useful framework for AutoML, sim-to-real, or ad testing when cheap proxies exist.
- Strength: Synthetic experiments corroborate monotone benefit as fidelity costs rise.
- Weakness: Objective/regret definition is debatable—treats low-fidelity plays as consuming budget without immediate reward, which mismatches many applications where low-fidelity actions do yield reward.
- Weakness: Advertising example is misleading because it implies non-stationarity, while the model assumes stationary biased proxies.
- Weakness: Heavy reliance on known uniform bias bounds ζ(m) and Assumption 1; unclear robustness to misspecified or unknown ζ(m) and whether γ(m) can be learned adaptively.
- Weakness: Independence structure across fidelities for the same arm is not clearly stated.
- Weakness: Lower bound limited to Bernoulli rewards and strategies satisfying Assumption 3; need clarification on standardness and relation to uniformly efficient policies.
- Weakness: MF-UCB may be suboptimal for a subset of arms; paper should suggest principled modifications or argue impossibility.
- Weakness: Empirics limited to synthetic tasks and compared only to vanilla high-fidelity UCB; lacks stronger baselines (budgeted bandits, cost-aware TS) and sensitivity analyses.
- Weakness: Important implementation details (how γ(m), ρ, and ψ−1 are instantiated) and robustness to misspecified ζ are not reported.
- Clarity: Notation is heavy and sometimes introduced out of order; sections 2–3 should be reorganized and symbols streamlined.
- Clarity: Regret decomposition and negligible residual terms should be motivated earlier with an intuitive bound/proof.
- Clarity: Algorithm pseudocode needs clearer presentation and a caption explaining escalation logic; ψ and ψ−1 should be concretely defined for sub-Gaussian rewards.
- Originality: Formulation combining biased approximations with cost-aware regret and the γ(m) design is novel relative to budgeted bandits and multi-fidelity BO.
- Significance: Potentially impactful for problems with cheap proxies, but current regret notion limits applicability; adding reward-per-cost or cost-aware best-arm-ID variants would increase relevance.
- Suggestions: Justify regret choice or provide alternative formulations/theorems, add robustness analysis for unknown ζ(m), expand experiments with stronger baselines and ablations, and improve exposition/notation.
- Pros for acceptance: New formulation, sound theory, and a simple implementable algorithm with principled parameterization.
- Cons for acceptance: Debatable objective choice, misleading examples, clarity issues, limited empirical validation, and remaining lower-bound/robustness questions.
- Recommendation: Borderline reject; requires stronger justification of objective, improved clarity, expanded empirical validation, and robustness work before acceptance.
- Reviewer confidence: High on bandits and cost-aware formulations; moderate on lower-bound technicalities.
