## Review of “The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit”

This paper introduces a novel multi-armed bandit (MAB) setting incorporating variable-fidelity pulls, where acquiring outcomes at higher fidelity incurs greater cost. The authors formalize this problem, propose the MF-UCB algorithm, and provide theoretical guarantees on its performance, establishing both upper and lower regret bounds. 

The contribution is significant. The multi-fidelity setting is a natural extension of the classical MAB, relevant to numerous applications like online advertising and algorithm selection where approximate evaluations are readily available at lower cost. The proposed MF-UCB algorithm intuitively addresses the exploration-exploitation dilemma in this context by intelligently allocating resources to higher fidelities for promising arms.  The theoretical analysis, proving near-optimality, is a strong point, and builds naturally on existing UCB literature. The paper is also well-written and clearly presents the problem, algorithm, and analysis. The introduction effectively motivates the setting and highlights its practical relevance.

However, several areas could be improved. The notation using "up-to-a-multiplicative-constant" obscures the dependence on algorithm parameters like ρ. Presenting explicit inequalities would enhance clarity.  Furthermore, the practical applicability of MF-UCB is not fully convincing. The algorithm relies on accurately estimating the fidelity ζ, which may be challenging in real-world scenarios. A discussion of potential practical limitations and mitigation strategies (e.g., adaptive estimation of ζ) is needed. 

Crucially, the supplementary material (proofs) is riddled with typos and minor errors. While not fundamentally impacting the correctness of the results, these errors significantly hinder readability and could lead to confusion. A thorough proofreading pass is essential. Examples include: page 10 eq.(8) (k/K typo), Section A1 (H^(m) should be K^(m)), and numerous other inconsistencies detailed in the provided list.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and well-motivated problem setting.
*   Solid theoretical foundation with interesting regret bounds.
*   Clear and well-structured presentation.
*   Potential for significant impact in areas utilizing costly evaluations.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Practical limitations regarding fidelity estimation not adequately addressed.
*   Supplementary material riddled with errors, impacting readability.
*   "Up-to-a-constant" notation hinders clarity.



**Overall Assessment:** Despite the shortcomings, particularly the errors in the appendix and the limited discussion of practical challenges, the paper presents a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of bandit algorithms.  I recommend acceptance with the strong encouragement that the authors address the issues in the supplementary material and clarify the discussion of practical considerations.
