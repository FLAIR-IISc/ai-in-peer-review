This paper introduces a novel framework for studying exploration-exploitation trade-offs in stochastic bandits where cheaper approximations to a desired experiment are available. The authors propose a multi-fidelity bandit problem, where each arm has multiple fidelities that serve as approximations to the desired outcome. They develop a novel upper confidence bound procedure, MF-UCB, which adapts to the sequence of available approximations and costs, achieving better regret than naive strategies that ignore the approximations.

The paper's main contributions are:

1. A novel formalism for studying bandit tasks with multiple fidelities for each arm.
2. A new algorithm, MF-UCB, that adapts the classical Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) strategies to the multi-fidelity setting.
3. A theoretical characterization of the performance of MF-UCB, showing that it uses the lower fidelities to explore all arms and eliminates arms with low expected reward, reserving the higher fidelity plays for arms with rewards close to the optimal value.

The paper provides a regret bound for MF-UCB and a lower bound, demonstrating that MF-UCB is near-optimal under certain conditions. The authors also provide simulation results, showing that MF-UCB outperforms UCB on synthetic problems.

Strengths:

* The paper studies an interesting and relevant trade-off between information, cost, and reward in stochastic bandits.
* The technical analysis appears solid, and the authors provide a thorough theoretical characterization of the performance of MF-UCB.
* The paper provides a clear and well-organized presentation of the ideas.

Weaknesses:

* The paper lacks adequate explanation of results and assumptions, making interpretation difficult.
* The regret formulation penalizes cost multiplicatively, which may be unnatural; additive cost penalization should be considered and discussed.
* The ad-display example may not generalize; a broader motivation for the chosen reward/cost model is needed.
* Assumption 1, which constrains the decay of the fidelity errors, is artificial and opaque, and its general validity is unclear.
* The algorithm design seems tailored to specific fidelity configurations, and it is unclear whether Assumption 1 critically affects performance.
* The gap in the lower bound warrants further investigation and may require redesigning the strategy.

Minor issues:

* The paper contains minor typos, such as "near-optimal" (line 56) and an unnecessary period before "for all m < M" (line 126).
* The use of curly braces for set notation (line 179) is inconsistent.

Recommendation:

* The paper is well-written and provides a thorough analysis of the multi-fidelity bandit problem.
* The authors should address the weaknesses mentioned above, particularly the clarity of explanations and the generality of Assumption 1.
* The paper is a good fit for the conference, as it provides a novel and interesting contribution to the field of stochastic bandits.

Rating: 8/10
