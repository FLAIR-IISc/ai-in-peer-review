## Review of “The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit”

This paper introduces a multi-fidelity Multi-Armed Bandit (MAB) framework, where evaluating arms at different fidelities incurs varying costs and lower-fidelity evaluations provide approximate information about the highest-fidelity outcome. The authors propose MF-UCB, a novel UCB-based algorithm adapted to this setting, accompanied by a regret analysis and a lower bound. While the problem formulation is interesting and potentially impactful, the paper requires substantial revision before it is ready for publication.

The core idea of leveraging cheaper, approximate evaluations to accelerate learning in MABs is a valuable contribution. The proposed MF-UCB algorithm builds naturally on the well-established UCB approach and the empirical results demonstrate performance gains over a naive UCB baseline on synthetic problems. The inclusion of a lower bound, attempting to demonstrate near-optimality, is also a strong point. This work connects to the broader field of efficient exploration in bandits, drawing on classic results and extending them to a more practical setting.

However, several weaknesses detract from the paper’s overall quality. The notation is frequently confusing and inconsistent; variables are defined after their first use and then often remain sparsely utilized. This significantly hampers readability. More critically, the application example of online advertising is misleading. References to display time correlations and non-stationarity introduce complexities irrelevant to the paper’s scope and the core bandit problem being addressed. 

Furthermore, the regret analysis feels flawed. Defining the “best” arm solely by its highest mean, without considering the cost of achieving that mean (even at lower fidelities), obscures the true cost-benefit trade-off inherent to the multi-fidelity setting. A more appropriate analysis would focus on sample or cost-complexity, quantifying the resources needed to identify the best arm within a given budget. This would better align with real-world testing scenarios. The writing is also occasionally unwieldy, and a more streamlined, organized presentation would greatly improve clarity.

**Arguments for acceptance:** Interesting problem formulation; solid connection to existing bandit literature; empirical results show promise; inclusion of a lower bound.

**Arguments against acceptance:** Confusing notation; misleading example application; questionable regret analysis; lacks a cost-complexity analysis; writing requires substantial improvement.

**Overall Recommendation:** Reject with encouragement to revise and resubmit. The core idea is promising, but significant revisions are necessary to address the identified weaknesses and present a more rigorous and compelling contribution. Specifically, the authors should focus on clarifying the notation, removing the irrelevant advertising complexities, revising the regret analysis, and adding a cost-complexity analysis.
