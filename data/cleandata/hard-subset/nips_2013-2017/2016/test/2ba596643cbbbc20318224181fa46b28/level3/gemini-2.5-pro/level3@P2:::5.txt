This paper introduces and formalizes the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit problem, a novel and practical variant of the classical stochastic K-armed bandit setting. The key idea is that for each arm, instead of a single expensive way to draw a sample, there are multiple "fidelities" available. Lower fidelities are cheaper to sample from but provide a biased or noisy approximation of the true, highest-fidelity outcome. The quality of these approximations is assumed to be known. This setting is motivated by real-world applications such as online advertising, where ad performance can be cheaply approximated with shorter displays, or algorithm selection, where performance can be estimated on smaller datasets.

The authors propose the Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB) algorithm, an elegant extension of the classic UCB principle. The algorithm selects an arm based on the tightest confidence bound across all fidelities. Crucially, it then employs a sequential decision rule to select the fidelity for the chosen arm, starting with the cheapest one. It only proceeds to a more expensive fidelity if the uncertainty at the current level has been reduced below a certain threshold. This strategy allows the algorithm to use cheap samples to quickly identify and discard clearly suboptimal arms, reserving expensive, high-fidelity samples for a smaller set of promising candidates.

The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of MF-UCB. It presents a regret bound that, for many arms, scales with the cost of the cheapest fidelity required to distinguish that arm from the optimal one, rather than the cost of the most expensive fidelity. This formally demonstrates a significant improvement over a naive UCB strategy that would only use the highest fidelity. This upper bound is complemented by a problem-specific lower bound, showing that MF-UCB is near-optimal under certain conditions. The theoretical claims are supported by simulations on synthetic problems that clearly illustrate the benefits of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The formulation of the multi-fidelity bandit problem is a significant and novel contribution. It captures a common feature of many real-world sequential decision-making problems that has been largely overlooked in the standard bandit literature. This work opens up a new and promising research direction. The problem is well-motivated, and the ideas presented are likely to be used and built upon by other researchers.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically very strong. The proposed MF-UCB algorithm is intuitive and well-designed. The theoretical analysis is thorough and insightful, containing both an upper bound for the proposed algorithm and a lower bound for the problem itself. Establishing near-optimality is a solid theoretical result. The analysis successfully formalizes the intuition that cheap fidelities should be used for coarse-grained exploration.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The problem setup, the algorithm, and the theoretical results are explained with great clarity. The notation is consistent, and the inclusion of Figure 1 to illustrate the partitioning of the arm set is particularly helpful for building intuition about the analysis.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Knowledge of Approximation Quality:** The analysis assumes that the approximation bounds, `ζ(m)`, are known a priori. In practice, quantifying the precise bias of a cheaper approximation can be difficult. It would strengthen the paper to include a discussion on the robustness of MF-UCB to misspecified `ζ(m)` values or to suggest potential ways to estimate these parameters online.

2.  **Restrictive Assumptions:** Assumption 1, which requires the `ζ(m)` values to decay sufficiently fast, seems somewhat tailored to make the theoretical comparison with UCB clean. While the authors state it is not strictly necessary, a more detailed discussion of the algorithm's performance when this assumption is violated would be beneficial.

3.  **Synthetic Experiments:** While the simulations are clear and effectively support the theoretical claims, they are limited to synthetic data. Demonstrating the algorithm's performance on a more realistic semi-synthetic problem (e.g., using a real-world dataset to simulate a hyperparameter tuning task) would further bolster the paper's claims of practical impact.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Introduces a novel, well-motivated, and important problem formulation (multi-fidelity bandits).
*   Proposes an elegant and intuitive algorithm (MF-UCB) to solve it.
*   Provides a strong and comprehensive theoretical analysis, including both upper and lower regret bounds that establish near-optimality.
*   The paper is extremely clear and well-written.
*   The work is likely to inspire follow-up research in bandits and related fields.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The assumption of known approximation bounds (`ζ(m)`) may limit direct applicability in some practical scenarios without further adaptation.
*   The experimental validation is purely on synthetic data.

In summary, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the multi-armed bandit literature. The strengths, particularly the novelty of the problem and the high quality of the theoretical analysis, far outweigh the minor weaknesses. The paper is well-executed and presents a complete piece of research. I strongly recommend acceptance.
