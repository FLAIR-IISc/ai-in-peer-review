## Review of “The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit”

This paper introduces a novel multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit (MAB) setting where obtaining reward estimates at different fidelities involves varying costs. The motivation, exemplified by online advertising with varying ad display durations, is well-presented and practically relevant. The authors propose a new regret definition suitable for this setting, an algorithm – MF-UCB – based on upper confidence bounds, and provide a theoretical analysis including a regret lower bound, supported by simulation results. The core contribution lies in demonstrating how to effectively leverage cheaper, noisy approximations to quickly eliminate suboptimal arms and focus resources on promising candidates.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and highlights the advantages of a multi-fidelity approach over standard UCB strategies. The derivation of the regret bound and the lower bound analysis appear rigorous, demonstrating a solid understanding of bandit theory. The simulation results provide initial evidence supporting the effectiveness of MF-UCB, though a more comprehensive evaluation with diverse scenarios would strengthen these findings. The related work section is comprehensive, positioning the contribution within the broader context of bandit algorithms and multi-fidelity optimization techniques.

However, a significant concern is the assumption of known ζ^(m), representing the deviation of the mth-fidelity mean from the highest-fidelity mean.  While the authors acknowledge this limitation, the practicality of obtaining these values in real-world applications is unclear. Relaxing this assumption, perhaps through adaptive estimation techniques, would significantly enhance the paper’s impact.  Furthermore, the theoretical analysis, while sound, might benefit from a more intuitive explanation of how the algorithm adapts to the varying costs and fidelities.  

**Minor issues:** The paper contains several minor typographical errors as highlighted by the submission system. Specifically: lines 326, 357, 386 ("arm k ∈ H^(m)" should be "K"), line 343 ("β_{k*,t} ≤ s" should be "s = μ_*"), line 345 ("γ should be γ^(m)"), lines 346–347 ("ψ(Δ_k^(m) − γ^(m)) ≤ ψ(γ^(m)) when ψ(Δ_k^(m)) ≤ 2 γ^(m)" should be "≥"), line 384 (summation index over arms "below" K^(m), not "above"), line 407 (“δ^(m) should be δ^(M)”), line 431 (“for l ≥ m” should be “for l ≥ p”), line 440 / Eq.(17) (“l ≥ m” should be “l ≥ p”), and line 450 (“P(A_{n,3}) should be P(A_{n,2})”).

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novelty of the problem setting and proposed solution.
*   Strong theoretical foundation with regret bounds and a lower bound.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Potential for practical impact in various domains.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Strong assumption of known ζ^(m) limiting real-world applicability.
*   Minor typographical errors.



**Overall assessment:** I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Addressing the concern regarding the assumption of known ζ^(m) and correcting the typographical errors would significantly strengthen the paper. The work represents a valuable contribution to the field of multi-armed bandits and has the potential to stimulate further research in multi-fidelity optimization.
