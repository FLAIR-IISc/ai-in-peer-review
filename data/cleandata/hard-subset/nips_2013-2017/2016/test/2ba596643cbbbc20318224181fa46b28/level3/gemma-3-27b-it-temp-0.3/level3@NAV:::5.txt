## Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

This paper introduces a compelling variant of the classic K-armed bandit problem, motivated by scenarios where obtaining precise rewards is costly but cheaper, approximate evaluations are available. The authors formalize this as a multi-fidelity (MF) bandit problem and propose MF-UCB, a novel algorithm building upon the UCB framework. This work is a natural extension of the extensive bandit literature (e.g., Auer, 2002; Bubeck & Cesa-Bianchi, 2012) and addresses a practical challenge often encountered in applications like online advertising and algorithm selection. Recent work has explored related concepts like budgeted bandits (Tran-Thanh et al., 2017) and active learning with weak labelers (Zhang & Chaudhuri, 2016), but this paper uniquely focuses on the exploration-exploitation trade-off *within* the fidelity spectrum itself.

The paper’s strengths lie in its rigorous theoretical analysis and clear presentation. The authors provide regret bounds for MF-UCB, demonstrating its advantage over naive strategies that ignore fidelity information. Importantly, they complement this with a lower bound analysis, establishing near-optimality under certain conditions. The simulations convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the MF approach, showing improved performance compared to standard UCB. The writing is exceptionally clear, making the complex theoretical concepts accessible. The motivation is well-explained, and the connection to real-world applications is strong. The inclusion of a detailed discussion of related work is also appreciated.

However, the paper isn't without minor weaknesses. While the theoretical analysis is strong, the assumptions (particularly Assumption 1 regarding the decay of ζ(m)) could be further explored in terms of their practical implications. The simulations, while effective, could benefit from a wider range of problem settings and a more detailed analysis of the parameter sensitivity of MF-UCB. The discussion of alternative regret definitions in the conclusion is intriguing but could be expanded upon. Finally, the connection to work on multi-fidelity optimization (e.g., Huang et al., 2006) could be made more explicit, even if the focus and analysis differ.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** Introduces a well-defined and practically relevant extension to the classic bandit problem.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** Provides strong theoretical guarantees for the proposed algorithm.
*   **Empirical Validation:** Demonstrates the effectiveness of MF-UCB through simulations.
*   **Clarity:** Exceptionally well-written and easy to understand.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Assumptions could be more thoroughly discussed regarding their practical limitations.
*   Simulations could be expanded to cover a wider range of scenarios.



**Overall:** This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of bandit optimization. The strengths clearly outweigh the weaknesses, and I recommend acceptance. The work is likely to be of interest to both researchers and practitioners working on online decision-making problems.
