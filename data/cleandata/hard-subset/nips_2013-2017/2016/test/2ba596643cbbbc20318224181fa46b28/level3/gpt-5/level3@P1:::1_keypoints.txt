- Problem & setup: Stochastic K-armed bandits with M fidelities per arm, known bias bounds ζ(m) and increasing costs λ(m); aim to minimize a capital-weighted pseudo-regret under budget Λ using cheap low-fidelity pulls to eliminate arms.  
- Algorithm (MF-UCB): Uses ζ-shifted per-(arm,fidelity) UCBs Bk,t(m) and selects arm by Bk,t = minm Bk,t(m); fidelity chosen by thresholds γ(m) balancing cheap elimination vs over-investment.  
- Theoretical guarantee: Upper bound on regret scales like log(nΛ) · λ(Jk)/ψ(Δ(Jk)) per arm, yielding roughly λ(Jk)/λ(M) savings vs high-fidelity-only UCB when arms can be eliminated at low fidelities.  
- Lower bound: For Bernoulli rewards, a change-of-measure lower bound matches MF-UCB’s scaling on a subset of arms but shows a gap for arms with very small positive low-fidelity gaps.  
- Novelty: Formalism with known fidelity bias bounds, the ζ-shifted MF-UCB and fidelity-dependent switching, and paired upper/lower bounds are novel in stochastic bandits.  
- Relation to prior work: Distinct from classical UCB, budgeted bandits optimizing reward-per-cost, and BO multi-fidelity work lacking cumulative regret analyses; also differs from labeler active learning.  
- Technical soundness: Algorithm design and analysis are principled; proofs partition arms by elimination fidelity and the lower bound is well-constructed.  
- Key assumption concern: Assumption 1 (decay of ζ(m) relative to ψ) is pivotal for comparisons and costs but is opaque and its general validity is unclear.  
- Experiments: Synthetic results support the qualitative story (cheap pulls on bad arms, better slopes than UCB) but empirical section is thin and lacks sensitivity analyses and real-world cases.  
- Regret formulation critique: The capital-weighted pseudo-regret objective may be unnatural for some applications; authors should discuss alternative objectives and applicability.  
- Assumption 1 suggestions: Need intuition, examples, practical checks, and a fidelity-pruning lemma showing one can skip near-duplicate fidelities if Assumption 1 fails.  
- Robustness to ζ mis-specification: Reviewer asks for discussion on performance when ζ(m) are unknown/misspecified and whether conservative online estimation is possible.  
- Lower-bound gap suggestion: Consider more cost-aware/adaptive switching rules or two-phase schemes to exploit cases where cheaper fidelities could suffice despite small positive gaps.  
- Clarity issues: Regret definition, sets K(m)/Δ(m)/Jk, and partitioning are hard to parse; add a two-fidelity corollary (sub-Gaussian ψ), a high-probability N ≈ Λ/λ(M) statement, and examples motivating Assumption 1.  
- Presentation fixes: Minor typos and notation choices (e.g., “near-optimal” qualification, extraneous punctuation, use standard set braces) should be corrected.  
- Recommended additions: M=2 corollary with ψ(x) ∼ x^2, a small table/figure mapping K(m) to capital spent, and a summarized per-arm cost/regret statement.  
- Significance: Practically relevant for simulators and A/B testing; substantial gains when ζ decays fast and λ grows, especially with many arms and few near-optimal ones.  
- Limitations noted: Sensitivity to correct ζ, restrictive Assumption 1, and lower-bound gap on certain arms limit generality.  
- Pros for acceptance: Timely, novel formalism; nontrivial upper/lower theory; clear regimes of improvement over UCB.  
- Cons for acceptance: Regret objective and assumptions need stronger motivation; presentation and notation are burdensome; experiments lack robustness studies and real data.  
- Recommendation: Borderline accept conditional on clarifying/motivating the regret choice, addressing Assumption 1 (or providing fidelity pruning), expanding empirical sensitivity/real-case results, and adding simplified/special-case statements to make Theorem 2 transparent.
