

# Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit" (NIPS 2016)

This paper introduces a novel variant of the stochastic K-armed bandit problem where each arm can be evaluated at multiple fidelities (approximations), with lower fidelities being cheaper but less accurate. The authors formalize this as a multi-fidelity (MF) bandit problem, where the goal is to minimize regret while accounting for the cost of each fidelity level. The paper's main contribution is MF-UCB, a novel algorithm that adapts the classical UCB approach to this setting, along with theoretical guarantees showing its superiority over standard bandit methods that ignore the multi-fidelity structure.

The paper builds on classical bandit work (Robbins [11], Lai and Robbins [9], Auer et al. [3]) while addressing a practical gap in the literature. Unlike previous work on budgeted bandits [13, 15] or multi-fidelity optimization [7, 10], this paper formally defines regret in the multi-fidelity setting and provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis. The authors clearly distinguish their contribution from related work, particularly in the related work section where they address Zhang and Chaudhuri [16] and Kandasamy et al. [8], noting that their work provides regret guarantees for the bandit setting.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with a clear theoretical analysis. The authors provide a regret bound for MF-UCB that shows significant improvement over standard UCB when lower fidelities are sufficiently cheap, and they complement this with a lower bound analysis demonstrating that MF-UCB is nearly optimal under certain conditions. The proof techniques are sound and well-structured, with the theoretical results carefully derived and supported by appropriate assumptions (like Assumption 1). The simulations in Section 6 effectively demonstrate the practical benefits of the MF-UCB approach across multiple synthetic problems, showing both lower regret and more efficient use of the budget compared to standard UCB.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clear. The problem formulation is precise, the algorithm is well-motivated, and the theoretical results are presented in a logical, accessible manner. The use of Figure 1 to illustrate the partitioning of arms into different fidelity groups is particularly effective. The paper also thoughtfully addresses potential limitations, such as the gap between the lower bound and MF-UCB's performance for certain arms (Section 4.2), and discusses alternative regret formulations in the conclusion.

Strengths:
1. The multi-fidelity bandit framework addresses a significant practical problem in applications like online advertising and algorithm selection where cheaper approximations to expensive experiments are available.
2. The theoretical results are rigorous, with clear regret bounds that demonstrate the potential for significant improvement over standard bandit methods.
3. The paper provides both theoretical and empirical evidence of the approach's benefits.
4. The writing is clear, well-organized, and accessible to the NIPS audience.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper assumes known ζ(m) values (the bias between fidelities), which might not always be realistic in practice. The authors acknowledge this limitation but could explore how to learn these values online.
2. The lower bound analysis shows a small gap for certain arms (those in K(m)7), though the authors provide a reasonable explanation for this gap and note it as an open problem.
3. While the paper discusses applications like online advertising, more detailed real-world case studies would strengthen the practical significance.

In terms of the NIPS review criteria:

Quality: The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical results. The authors carefully address both strengths and weaknesses, and the simulations complement the theoretical analysis.

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized, making complex theoretical results accessible. The writing style is professional and engaging.

Originality: The multi-fidelity bandit framework is novel. The authors clearly distinguish their work from previous contributions and provide a formal analysis not present in related work.

Significance: The problem addressed has clear practical importance in many domains where expensive experiments can be approximated with cheaper alternatives. The results demonstrate both theoretical and practical advantages over existing methods, and the paper opens up new directions for research in bandit problems with multiple evaluation costs.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel problem formulation that addresses a practical gap in bandit literature
- Strong theoretical results with clear regret bounds
- Comprehensive analysis including lower bounds
- Effective empirical validation
- Clear and well-written presentation

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited discussion of how to handle unknown ζ(m) values in practice
- Small gap in the lower bound analysis for certain arms

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the bandit literature by formalizing and analyzing a practical multi-fidelity setting. The theoretical results are rigorous and meaningful, the empirical validation is convincing, and the writing is excellent. The minor limitations do not detract from the paper's substantial contribution to the field. This work is a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS 2016.
