Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies stochastic K-armed bandits when each arm can be queried at one of M discrete fidelity levels. Each lower fidelity m<M returns a biased but cheaper observation whose mean differs from the true (highest-fidelity) mean by at most a known zeta_m, with zeta_M = 0, and each fidelity has a known cost λ(m) that increases with m. The objective is to maximize net reward under a capital budget Λ. The authors propose MF-UCB, which maintains upper confidence bounds per fidelity B_k,t^(m) = empirical_mean + uncertainty + zeta_m and selects the arm with the minimum bound across fidelities (optimism), then chooses the fidelity via thresholds γ(m) that balance bias (zeta_m), statistical uncertainty, and cost. They analyze a cost-weighted regret notion R(Λ) = Λ μ* − Σ_t λ(m_t) μ^(M)_{I_t}, give an O(log Λ) regret bound that explicitly decomposes the spend across fidelities via partitions K^(m), and a lower bound for Bernoulli rewards showing MF-UCB is nearly optimal except on a certain subset of arms. Synthetic experiments compare MF-UCB to high-fidelity-only UCB and show reduced regret.

This work connects to classical UCB for stochastic bandits (Auer et al.; Bubeck & Cesa-Bianchi), budgeted bandits (Tran-Thanh et al.; Xia et al.), and multi-fidelity methods in optimization and RL (Huang et al.; Cutler et al.). It also relates to active learning with weak/strong labelers (Zhang & Chaudhuri). The primary novelty is formalizing multi-fidelity, biased observations with known bias bounds and fidelity-dependent costs in the stochastic bandit setting, and deriving regret bounds that reflect elimination at low cost.

Strengths
- Interesting and relevant problem: explicitly trades off information quality (bias), cost, and reward in bandits, with clear practical appeal.
- Solid technical core: the MF-UCB design is principled; the regret analysis captures how low fidelities eliminate suboptimal arms while reserving expensive plays for near-optimal ones; the lower bound highlights when MF-UCB is tight.
- Improvement over high-fidelity-only UCB is well motivated and supported in both theory (factors λ(m)/λ(M) for many arms) and experiments.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality
- Regret formulation: The cost enters multiplicatively (Σ λ(m_t) μ^(M)_{I_t}) against a Λ μ* benchmark. While consistent with the advertising example (μ as clicks per unit time and λ as time), this is modeling dependent. Please justify more thoroughly and discuss alternatives:
  - Additive penalization or scalarized utility: maximize Σ μ^(M)_{I_t} − α Σ λ(m_t).
  - Constrained budget with unweighted reward Σ μ^(M)_{I_t} subject to Σ λ(m_t) ≤ Λ (standard knapsack-style goal), comparing to an oracle allocation under the same budget.
  - How would MF-UCB and the bounds change under these alternatives?
- Lower bound gap: For arms in K^(m)_7, the lower bound suggests distinguishing suboptimality at a cheaper fidelity could be asymptotically better, while MF-UCB switches “too early.” This warrants deeper investigation. Could a cost-aware information allocation rule (e.g., choose fidelity minimizing λ(m)/ψ(Δ̂^(m)) subject to bias caps) or a sequential test across fidelities close the gap? Even a negative result or impossibility would add value.

Clarity
- Several results are hard to parse:
  - Theorem 2: The dependence on Δ’s and the role of the partitions K^(m) are difficult to internalize. Provide a simple 2- or 3-fidelity corollary with explicit constants and an informal explanation of each term. A worked toy example showing how arms flow through K^(m) would help.
  - Comparison to UCB: Make the “when and by how much” improvement explicit, e.g., state a corollary bounding MF-UCB’s regret by Σ_k λ(J_k)/ψ(Δ^(J_k)_k) up to constants, and contrast with Σ_k λ(M)/ψ(Δ^(M)_k).
  - Assumption 1: The condition ∑_{i≤m} 1/ψ(ζ(i)) ≤ 1/ψ(ζ(m+1)) is opaque. Provide intuition, concrete sufficient conditions (e.g., geometric decay of ζ for sub-Gaussians), and a short discussion of its necessity in each proof step.
- Broader motivation: The ad-display example is specific. Please articulate additional domains where μ^(m) represents reward per unit cost and the multiplicative regret is the right metric (e.g., materials screening, A/B testing with time-based exposure, hyperparameter evaluation with subsampling), and also discuss settings where that metric would not be appropriate.

Originality
- The problem setup with known fidelity-dependent bias bounds and costs appears original in the stochastic bandit literature and is distinct from budgeted bandits and weak/strong labeling. Please position more explicitly against closely related cost-aware/bias-aware bandit models, if any.

Significance
- If the assumptions hold and K^(M) is small, the method can yield substantial savings. The work is likely to spur follow-up on fidelity selection and cost-aware information allocation. However, empirical evaluation is limited:
  - Consider adding comparisons to stronger baselines beyond UCB: (i) “cheapest-first elimination” heuristics; (ii) Thompson sampling variants; (iii) policies that learn to skip near-redundant fidelities.
  - Ablate mis-specified ζ(m) and λ(m) to assess robustness.

Specific technical questions/suggestions
- Robustness to ζ mis-specification: MF-UCB relies on known ζ(m). How sensitive is performance if ζ(m) are upper bounds but loose, or underestimates? Could one adaptively learn effective bias envelopes or use high-probability bias bounds?
- If ζ(m) do not decay as in Assumption 1, can you propose and analyze a procedure to select a subsequence of fidelities (skip near-redundant levels) that satisfies the assumption? State the resulting regret bound in terms of the selected subsequence and clarify constants.
- Algorithm dependence on ζ configuration: γ(m) depends on ψ(ζ(m)) and cost ratios. How critical is Assumption 1 for MF-UCB’s practical performance? Provide a theorem or empirical study showing degradation when Assumption 1 fails.
- Statement of r̃(Λ) term: Give an explicit bound and a short lemma showing it’s negligible relative to R̃(Λ).

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a timely and practically important extension of bandits.
- Clear algorithmic idea with nontrivial analysis and a partially matching lower bound.
- Conceptual contribution: principled use of biased low-cost proxies.

Cons for acceptance
- Clarity issues in assumptions and main results impede accessibility.
- Regret objective’s modeling choices are insufficiently justified; alternative formulations not discussed.
- Lower bound gap suggests suboptimality in a meaningful regime; no attempt to mitigate.
- Empirical evaluation limited in scope and baselines.

Minor issues/typos
- “near-optimal” (l.56): hyphen usage inconsistent; define precisely (up to constants/log factors).
- Unnecessary period before “for all m < M” (l.126).
- Use curly braces for the set [[k]] notation (l.179).
- Notation: consider simplifying J^(m)_η and K^(m) definitions; ensure Δ^(m)_k is reintroduced where used.

Overall
Technically promising and relevant, but current presentation and modeling choices reduce clarity and generality. With improved exposition (esp. Theorem 2), a discussion of alternative regret formulations, and a deeper treatment of Assumption 1 and the lower bound gap, this would be a strong paper. As is, I lean toward a weak accept if the authors can address the clarity and motivation concerns; otherwise borderline.
