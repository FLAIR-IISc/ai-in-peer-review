## Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

This paper introduces a novel multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit (MF-MAB) framework, motivated by scenarios where evaluating arms is costly but cheaper approximations are available. The authors propose MF-UCB, an adaptive UCB algorithm tailored for this setting, and provide a regret analysis alongside a lower bound demonstrating near-optimality under certain conditions. While the problem formulation is interesting and potentially impactful, the paper requires significant refinement before publication.

The core idea of leveraging varying fidelity evaluations to accelerate learning and reduce overall cost is compelling. The proposed MF-UCB algorithm logically extends the classic UCB approach to incorporate fidelity levels, and the empirical results on synthetic problems demonstrate performance gains over standard UCB. The theoretical analysis, including the regret bound and lower bound, adds to the rigor of the work. However, several issues hinder the clarity and impact of the paper.

The notation is unnecessarily convoluted and dispersed. Variables are often defined after their first use, and some (like ‘s’) are introduced and then largely abandoned, hindering readability. The advertising example used to motivate the problem is misleading; the discussion of display time correlations and non-stationarity feels tacked on and doesn’t align with the paper’s core focus on approximating reward distributions. 

More critically, the regret analysis is questionable. Defining the “best” arm solely by its mean reward, irrespective of cost, obscures the fundamental trade-off between fidelity and expense. A more appropriate analysis would focus on sample/cost-complexity – finding the best arm with high probability *under a budget constraint*. This would better reflect real-world testing scenarios and align with the paper’s stated motivation. 

The writing is also unwieldy in places, and a more streamlined and organized presentation is needed. For example, the detailed discussion of Assumptions 1 and 3, while technically sound, could be more concisely presented.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel problem formulation with practical relevance.
*   Logical algorithm design (MF-UCB) building on established UCB principles.
*   Theoretical analysis (regret bound, lower bound) provides some guarantees.
*   Empirical results show performance improvement over baseline UCB.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Confusing notation and poor readability.
*   Misleading motivational example.
*   Questionable regret analysis that doesn’t adequately capture cost considerations.
*   Writing quality needs improvement.
*   Lack of a cost-complexity analysis more relevant to the problem.



**Overall:** This paper presents an interesting idea but is not ready for publication in its current form. Addressing the issues with notation, clarity, and the regret analysis would significantly strengthen the contribution. A shift in focus towards a cost-complexity analysis would also be highly beneficial. I recommend rejection with encouragement to revise and resubmit.
