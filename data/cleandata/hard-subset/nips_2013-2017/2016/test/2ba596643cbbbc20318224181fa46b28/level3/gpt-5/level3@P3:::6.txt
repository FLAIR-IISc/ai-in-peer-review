Summary
This paper introduces a stochastic multi-armed bandit model with M fidelity levels per arm, where higher fidelities yield less biased approximations of the true reward but at higher cost. The authors formulate a cost-weighted regret that accounts for capital expended across fidelities, propose MF-UCB, an optimism-based algorithm that adaptively selects both an arm and a fidelity, and provide theoretical guarantees: an upper bound on regret that improves over standard single-fidelity UCB whenever low-fidelity signals can eliminate many arms cheaply, and a problem-dependent lower bound showing near-optimality up to an identified gap on a subset of arms. Simulations on synthetic problems corroborate the theory, showing steeper gains when low fidelities are cheap and informative.

The work connects to UCB methods (Auer 2002; Audibert et al. 2009; Bubeck and Cesa-Bianchi 2012), budgeted bandits (Tran-Thanh et al.; Xia et al.), multi-fidelity optimization (Huang et al., Rajnarayan et al.), multi-fidelity RL (Cutler et al.), and weak/strong labeler active learning (Zhang and Chaudhuri). Unlike budgeted bandits, costs here are fidelity-dependent per arm, and unlike prior multi-fidelity optimization, the paper develops a bandit-specific regret formalism and matching analysis. It also relates to later work on GP-based multi-fidelity bandits.

Main contributions
- A principled multi-fidelity bandit formalism with a cost-weighted regret that credits actions by their true (highest-fidelity) value while penalizing capital spent.
- MF-UCB: a UCB-style policy with per-fidelity confidence bounds and a switching rule governed by thresholds γ(m) that trade off cost and informativeness across fidelities.
- Regret analysis: an upper bound showing that MF-UCB concentrates high-cost evaluations on a small candidate set and explores the rest using cheap fidelities; and a lower bound demonstrating near-optimality except on a specific subset where the switching heuristic is deliberately conservative.
- Empirical validation on synthetic data aligning with theory.

Quality
The technical development is careful and largely sound. The cost-sensitive regret definition is well-motivated for applications where only the highest-fidelity outcome matters, and the decomposition into spent-capital and instantaneous regret terms is clean. The high-level proof ideas are transparent: arm partitioning by fidelity-dependent gaps, control of plays per fidelity (lemmas bounding expectations and tails), and a change-of-measure lower bound. Assumption 1 clarifies when multiple fidelities are truly useful. The main caveat is a key modeling assumption: known fidelity biases ζ(m) (user’s ε(m)). The analysis and algorithm hinge on these values to set γ(m), but the paper does not explain how ζ(m) is obtained in practice nor how robust MF-UCB is to misspecification. Experiments are synthetic and do not probe this sensitivity.

Clarity
The paper is well-written overall, with clear motivation (online advertising), an intuitive description of MF-UCB, and helpful figures. The notation is heavy (multiple partitions K(m), J sets, ∆(m), ζ(m), γ(m)), but consistent. The regret notion is discussed explicitly and contrasted with alternatives. A number of minor typos and symbol glitches (listed below) should be fixed. It would help to add a short subsection discussing how practitioners might estimate or upper bound ζ(m), and to include a brief sensitivity plot to mis-specified ζ(m).

Originality
The setting and regret definition are novel and well-argued. The algorithmic idea of multi-fidelity UCB with fidelity selection via cost-informed thresholds and the accompanying analysis appear new. The lower bound and identification of the gap set K(m)7 are valuable and set a clear direction for future work.

Significance
This is a timely and practically relevant contribution. Many applications have cheap proxies for expensive evaluations (ads, A/B testing horizons, simulators, subsampled ML training). The results offer a principled way to exploit proxies with theoretical guarantees and show gains over single-fidelity UCB when low fidelities are informative and cheap. The lower-bound gap highlights an open problem likely to stimulate follow-up work.

Major concern and suggestions
- Assumption of known ζ(m): The algorithm and proofs assume known bounds on fidelity bias. In practice, these may be unknown or time-varying. It would strengthen the paper to:
  - Discuss how to obtain ζ(m): domain knowledge, pre-calibration via a small pilot budget comparing fidelities on a subset of arms, or conservative uniform bounds.
  - Provide robustness results: if ζ(m) is overestimated, MF-UCB becomes conservative (more high-fidelity plays); if underestimated, the method risks premature elimination. A theorem or empirical sensitivity study would help.
  - Consider an adaptive variant that maintains empirical cross-fidelity bias upper bounds with confidence (e.g., shared across arms, using union bounds), and plugs these into γ(m). Even a partial analysis would be valuable.
  - Alternatively, analyze a “safe” version that only assumes ζ(m) monotone and uses a tunable schedule for γ(m) that does not require precise ζ(m).

Additional comments and suggestions
- Experiments: Add ablations on λ(m), ζ(m), M, and mis-specified ζ(m); compare to simple baselines (e.g., “UCB at low fidelity until t, then high fidelity”, or successive elimination with fixed switching).
- Regret notion: Briefly discuss scenarios where realized lower-fidelity reward matters (e.g., when proxies have intrinsic value), and how the analysis would change.
- Presentation: Move Assumption 1 earlier with intuition; provide a small running toy example tracking an arm through K(m) partitions.

Pros
- Clear and well-motivated problem formulation with real applications.
- Elegant algorithm that unifies arm and fidelity selection with principled thresholds.
- Strong, mostly tight theoretical guarantees; insightful lower bound and gap characterization.
- Simulations match the theory and show meaningful gains.

Cons
- Practicality hinges on knowing ζ(m); no guidance or robustness analysis provided.
- Experiments are limited to synthetic data; lack of real-world case study or sensitivity analyses.
- A few technical typos and minor notational issues.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper introduces a compelling and well-analyzed multi-fidelity bandit framework with a simple, effective algorithm and near-optimal guarantees. Addressing the ζ(m) assumption—at least empirically—would materially strengthen the contribution.
