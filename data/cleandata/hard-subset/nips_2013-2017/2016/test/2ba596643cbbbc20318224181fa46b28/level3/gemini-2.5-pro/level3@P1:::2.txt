This paper introduces a novel and interesting variant of the stochastic K-armed bandit problem, which the authors term the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit. The core idea is that for each arm, in addition to the expensive, high-fidelity observation of its reward, one can access cheaper, lower-fidelity approximations. These approximations are biased, but their bias is bounded by a known, fidelity-dependent quantity ζ(m). The paper formalizes a cost-weighted notion of pseudo-regret for this setting, where the goal is to minimize the opportunity cost within a total budget Λ. The primary contribution is the Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB) algorithm, which intelligently leverages the different fidelities. The intuition is to use cheap, low-fidelity pulls to quickly explore and eliminate clearly suboptimal arms, reserving the expensive, high-fidelity pulls for a smaller set of promising candidates. The authors provide a theoretical analysis of MF-UCB, including an upper bound on its regret, and a corresponding lower bound for the problem, showing that MF-UCB is nearly optimal under certain conditions.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The multi-fidelity formulation is a novel and well-motivated extension of the classical multi-armed bandit problem. It captures a practical challenge present in many domains like online advertising, hyperparameter optimization, and scientific simulation, where preliminary, cheaper experiments can inform more expensive ones. The potential for other researchers to build upon this framework is high, making the contribution significant. The paper clearly distinguishes its work from related areas like budgeted bandits, which have a different cost structure.

2.  **Quality of Theoretical Contribution:** The paper presents a solid theoretical contribution. The proposed MF-UCB algorithm is intuitive and elegant. The analysis, which culminates in an upper regret bound (Theorem 2) and a problem-specific lower bound (Theorem 4), is rigorous. The result demonstrates that MF-UCB effectively adapts to the cost-accuracy trade-off, outperforming a naive UCB strategy that only uses the highest fidelity, particularly when many arms are far from optimal. The proofs appear fundamentally sound and provide valuable insight into the algorithm's behavior.

3.  **Clarity of the Main Paper:** The main body of the paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The problem is motivated with good examples, the notation is consistent, and the algorithm's logic is explained well. The illustrative figure of the arm partitions (Fig. 1) is helpful for understanding the analysis.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Clarity and Quality of the Appendix:** While the main paper is clear, the supplementary material containing the proofs is in poor condition. It is riddled with numerous typos, notational inconsistencies, and small errors that significantly hinder readability and the ability to verify the proofs. A non-exhaustive list of issues includes:
    *   Inconsistent variable names (e.g., H^(m) instead of K^(m) in Section A1).
    *   Incorrect references (e.g., referencing "Lemma" instead of "Theorem" on page 12).
    *   Typographical errors in equations (e.g., page 10, eq. (8) has a typo in k/K; page 13, definition of x_n uses K^2 instead of K).
    *   Missing parentheses, incorrect inequality signs, and dropped superscripts in multiple places.
    Fixing these errors is critical for the paper's completeness and for other researchers who wish to understand the technical details.

2.  **Clarity of Theoretical Bounds:** The presentation of the regret bounds in the analysis section (e.g., Theorem 2) uses "up-to-a-multiplicative-constant" notation (., &). While this simplifies the expressions, it can be misleading because some hidden constants, such as the algorithm parameter ρ, directly influence performance. Presenting the exact inequalities would provide a more complete and transparent picture of the dependencies, improving the clarity and utility of the theoretical results.

3.  **Practical Applicability and Assumptions:** The practical utility of MF-UCB hinges on knowing the bias bounds ζ(m) for each fidelity. In many real-world applications, these values would not be known a priori and would need to be estimated, potentially inaccurately. The paper does not discuss the sensitivity of the algorithm to errors in the estimation of ζ(m) or suggest potential methods for learning these parameters online. A discussion of these practical limitations and potential mitigation strategies would significantly strengthen the paper and broaden its applicability.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a novel, important, and practical extension to the multi-armed bandit framework.
*   The theoretical contribution is strong, with both upper and lower regret bounds that demonstrate the near-optimality of the proposed MF-UCB algorithm.
*   The main paper is well-written and communicates the core ideas effectively.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The appendix is in a very rough state, with many errors that obstruct verification of the proofs. This undermines the overall quality and polish of the work.
*   The practical assumption of knowing the fidelity bias parameters ζ(m) is strong and its implications are not discussed, limiting the direct applicability of the method.

**Recommendation:**

Overall, this is a strong paper with a significant and novel contribution to the bandit literature. The strengths in originality and theoretical quality outweigh the weaknesses. However, the weaknesses, particularly the state of the appendix, are substantial and must be addressed. I recommend acceptance, but I strongly urge the authors to perform a thorough revision of the supplementary material to correct the numerous errors and improve its clarity. Furthermore, adding a brief discussion on the practical challenges of estimating ζ(m) would make the work more complete.
