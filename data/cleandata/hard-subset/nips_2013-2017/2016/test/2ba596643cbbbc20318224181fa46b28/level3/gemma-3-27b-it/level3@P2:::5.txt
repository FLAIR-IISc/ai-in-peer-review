This paper introduces a compelling new variant of the multi-armed bandit (MAB) problem, the multi-fidelity bandit, where observing arm outcomes is expensive, but cheaper, albeit biased, approximations are available. This is a highly relevant and practical extension of the classic MAB framework, with clear applications in online advertising, algorithm selection, and robotics, among others. The authors formalize the problem, propose a novel algorithm (MF-UCB) based on upper confidence bounds, and provide strong theoretical guarantees – including regret bounds and a near-optimality result – supported by empirical validation.

The core idea of adapting UCB to exploit these varying fidelities is both intuitive and well-executed. The algorithm intelligently balances exploration using cheaper approximations to quickly eliminate suboptimal arms, with exploitation utilizing more expensive, accurate measurements on a smaller subset of promising candidates. The introduction of the γ(m) threshold for fidelity selection is a key component, and the justification for its choice is well-reasoned. The analysis is rigorous, building upon existing UCB theory and extending it to this more complex setting. The inclusion of a lower bound is a significant strength, solidifying the theoretical contribution.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The motivation is clearly articulated, and the problem is introduced in a manner accessible to readers familiar with the standard MAB literature. The notation is generally clear and consistent. The authors do a good job of relating their work to prior research, citing relevant papers on bandits, UCB methods, and multi-fidelity optimization. However, while the connections to multi-fidelity *optimization* are noted, the discussion of related work could be expanded to include more recent advances in active learning with weak and strong labelers (e.g., Zhang & Chaudhuri, NIPS 2016), which shares some conceptual similarities, though differing in its core objective. The simulations, while presented concisely, effectively demonstrate the performance benefits of MF-UCB over a standard UCB approach. 

Several minor points could be addressed to further strengthen the paper. The assumption regarding the ζ(m) decay (Assumption 1) could be elaborated on with more practical guidance; what types of decay patterns are likely to occur in real-world applications? Additionally, the discussion of alternative regret definitions in the conclusion is interesting, but could be fleshed out further. How sensitive are the results to different choices for *qt*? Exploring this trade-off would be valuable. Finally, while the proofs appear rigorous, their length and placement in the appendix may deter some readers.  Perhaps a more detailed sketch of the key proof ideas within the main text would improve accessibility.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Introduces a practically relevant and theoretically interesting extension to the classical MAB problem.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** Provides strong regret bounds and a near-optimality result for the proposed MF-UCB algorithm.
* **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and accessible to readers familiar with MABs.
* **Empirical Validation:** Simulations demonstrate the performance benefits of MF-UCB.
* **Well-motivated Design:** The algorithm's design and the choice of parameters are well-justified.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Related Work:** The discussion of related work could be expanded to include more recent advances in active learning with weak/strong labelers.
* **Assumption 1:** The practical implications of the ζ(m) decay assumption could be discussed in more detail.
* **Proof Accessibility:**  The proofs are relegated to the appendix, potentially limiting accessibility.
* **Regret Definition Sensitivity**: Further discussion on the choices for defining *qt* would be helpful.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant problem with clear practical relevance.
* The proposed algorithm is novel and well-motivated.
* The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides strong guarantees.
* The empirical results support the theoretical findings.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The weaknesses identified above are relatively minor and do not fundamentally detract from the paper's quality.
* While the connection to active learning could be strengthened, this does not invalidate the contribution.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the literature on multi-armed bandits. The proposed multi-fidelity bandit framework is both theoretically sound and practically relevant, and the MF-UCB algorithm provides a compelling solution. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points mentioned above. The paper is of high quality and appropriate for NIPS.
