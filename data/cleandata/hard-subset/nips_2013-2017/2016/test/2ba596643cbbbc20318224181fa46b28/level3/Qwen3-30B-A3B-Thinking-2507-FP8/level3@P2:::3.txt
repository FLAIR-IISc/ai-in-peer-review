

This paper introduces a multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit framework where each arm can be observed at multiple fidelities, with lower fidelities being cheaper but introducing bounded bias in reward estimates. The authors propose MF-UCB, an extension of the standard Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) algorithm that accounts for fidelity-induced bias by widening confidence intervals. They provide theoretical regret bounds showing MF-UCB can outperform standard UCB by using cheaper lower-fidelity observations to eliminate suboptimal arms before expensive high-fidelity evaluations.

The paper builds on the classic bandit literature [9, 11] and extends it to the multi-fidelity setting, which has been explored in optimization contexts [7, 10] but not with formal regret analysis. The related work section adequately covers relevant prior work, including recent multi-fidelity bandit approaches [8].

The main contributions are: (1) a formalization of the multi-fidelity bandit problem with bounded bias, (2) the MF-UCB algorithm, and (3) theoretical regret analysis demonstrating MF-UCB's advantage over standard UCB under certain conditions.

Strengths:
- The problem formulation is novel and addresses a practical need in applications where cheap approximations to expensive experiments exist (e.g., online advertising, algorithm selection).
- The theoretical analysis provides a rigorous regret bound showing MF-UCB's potential advantage over standard UCB.
- The empirical results consistently demonstrate MF-UCB's superiority over UCB on synthetic problems.
- The paper is technically sound with careful theoretical development.

Weaknesses:
- The regret bounds are complex and difficult to interpret, making it challenging to understand exactly when and by how much MF-UCB improves over UCB. This is a significant drawback for a theoretical paper.
- The modeling choice of lower fidelities introducing bias rather than variance is questionable for many applications (e.g., short ad displays). While the paper justifies this with the "clickbait" example, this doesn't convincingly explain why bias is the right modeling choice rather than variance.
- The connection between the model and motivating applications is weak in places. For instance, the online advertising example doesn't clearly explain why the regret objective is meaningful when using lower fidelity observations.
- The simulation parameters (especially ζ^(m)) lack justification, raising concerns that the reported 3-4x improvement might be sensitive to parameter choices rather than a general advantage.

Quality: The paper is technically sound with proper theoretical development, though the complexity of the bounds reduces their accessibility. The authors are careful to discuss both strengths and weaknesses.

Clarity: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, but the complex regret bounds and questionable modeling choices reduce clarity.

Originality: The core novelty is the problem formulation rather than the algorithm itself, which is a standard UCB extension. However, this problem formulation is novel to the bandit literature, as evidenced by the related work section.

Significance: The problem is relevant to several application domains, and the approach could be useful when cheap approximations to expensive experiments exist. However, the paper doesn't clearly articulate the magnitude of the improvement or the specific scenarios where this approach would be particularly valuable.

Pro and Con Arguments:
Pros: Introduces a meaningful extension to the bandit literature with clear practical motivation; provides rigorous theoretical analysis showing potential benefits; demonstrates consistent empirical improvements; well-written with careful attention to theoretical details.

Cons: Theoretical bounds are complex and hard to interpret; modeling choice (bias vs. variance) is questionable for some applications; connection to motivating examples is weak in places; simulation parameters lack justification.

Overall, this paper presents a novel problem formulation that extends the bandit literature to the multi-fidelity setting. While the algorithm itself is a natural extension of standard UCB techniques, the problem formulation and theoretical analysis provide valuable insights. The main weakness is the complexity of the theoretical results and the questionable modeling choice for some applications. However, the paper makes a solid contribution to the literature that would be valuable for practitioners working with bandit problems where cheap approximations to expensive experiments exist.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to improve the clarity of the theoretical results and strengthen the connection between the model and motivating applications.
