

This paper introduces a compelling extension of the classical multi-armed bandit problem by incorporating multiple fidelities (approximate evaluations) for each arm, where lower fidelities are cheaper but less accurate. The authors formalize this as a multi-fidelity bandit problem and propose MF-UCB, a novel algorithm that adaptively balances exploration across fidelities to minimize regret. The paper builds upon UCB methods [3,9] while addressing a practical gap in the literature where cheaper approximations exist but have not been formally integrated into bandit frameworks.

The paper's strengths are significant. The theoretical contribution is strong: the authors establish a regret bound for MF-UCB that demonstrates clear improvement over naive UCB strategies when lower fidelities are sufficiently cheap and informative. The lower bound analysis shows MF-UCB is nearly optimal under certain conditions, which is a substantial theoretical contribution. The paper is exceptionally clear in its exposition, with well-structured arguments and helpful figures. The experimental results on synthetic problems convincingly demonstrate MF-UCB's practical advantages over UCB, particularly in scenarios where the optimal set is small.

Weaknesses are minimal but noteworthy. The lower bound analysis reveals a small gap for certain arms (k ∈ K(m)7), which the authors acknowledge as an open problem. While this doesn't undermine the main contribution, it suggests potential for future work. The experimental section, while sufficient, could be strengthened with more diverse benchmarks from real-world applications.

The paper excels across all four criteria: it's technically sound (quality), well-written (clarity), introduces a novel formalism (originality), and addresses a practically important problem with demonstrable advantages (significance). The multi-fidelity framework has clear applicability in fields like online advertising, algorithm selection, and robotics where evaluation costs vary.

Pro: Strong theoretical foundation with matching lower bounds, clear writing, practical relevance. Con: Minor gap in lower bound analysis for specific arm types, limited empirical scope. Overall, this is an excellent contribution that advances the bandit literature with a well-motivated and rigorously analyzed approach, making it a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.
