Summary and relation to prior work
The paper introduces a multi-fidelity variant of the stochastic K-armed bandit in which each arm can be “pulled” at one of M fidelities with increasing cost and accuracy. The highest-fidelity reward is the quantity of interest; lower fidelities provide biased but informative observations whose means are assumed to be within known bounds ζ(m) of the true (highest-fidelity) mean. The authors define a cost-weighted pseudo-regret suitable for this setting, propose MF-UCB, and prove upper bounds showing that the algorithm uses cheaper fidelities to eliminate clearly suboptimal arms and concentrates expensive plays on a small subset of promising arms. They also prove a lower bound and show MF-UCB is near-optimal except in a specific regime, and they provide synthetic experiments demonstrating improvements over naive UCB that ignores lower fidelities.

The work connects to and extends classical UCB methods [Auer ’02; Bubeck & Cesa-Bianchi ’12], budgeted bandits [Tran-Thanh et al. ’10; Xia et al. ’15], and multi-fidelity ideas in optimization and RL [Huang et al. ’06; Cutler et al. ’14; Kandasamy et al. ’16, NIPS]. Unlike budgeted bandits, where each arm has a reward-cost ratio, here each arm has multiple approximation levels with known bias bounds, leading to distinct algorithmic and analytical challenges. The regret analysis adapts optimism under uncertainty to aggregate confidence bounds across fidelities and explicitly accounts for bias via ζ(m).

Strengths
- Well-motivated setting with clear practical relevance (e.g., online advertising, algorithm selection). The proposed regret notion matches the “true objective at high fidelity while paying for low-fidelity probes” viewpoint.
- A principled algorithm (MF-UCB) with a clear decision rule: select the arm by the tightest upper bound across fidelities, and select the fidelity by comparing current uncertainty to fidelity-dependent thresholds γ(m).
- Theoretical contributions: nontrivial regret upper bound that decomposes cost across fidelities and shows substantial savings relative to single-fidelity UCB when many arms are clearly suboptimal at lower fidelities; a complementary lower bound clarifying when the savings are unavoidable and where a gap remains.
- Good intuition and partitioning argument (K(m) sets) that explains where capital is spent; the proofs are technically careful and align with the algorithm’s design.
- Empirical simulations consistent with theory, showing both lower regret and the desired allocation behavior (many plays at low fidelities for bad arms).

Weaknesses and questions
- Major assumption: the bias bounds ζ(m) are known and monotone with m (and Assumption 1 requires sufficiently fast decay). In practice, how should ζ(m) be specified or learned? The guarantees and the fidelity-switch thresholds γ(m) depend on these values. The paper would benefit from:
  - A sensitivity analysis to ζ(m) mis-specification (e.g., conservative vs aggressive ζ), and to the cost ratios λ(m).
  - A discussion or extension that estimates ζ(m) on the fly (e.g., via occasional paired observations at fidelities m and m+1) with confidence bounds, and uses robustified thresholds that maintain guarantees under over/underestimation.
- Scope of experiments: results are only on synthetic problems and compare primarily to naive UCB. Stronger baselines would clarify empirical significance, e.g., UCB-V, Thompson Sampling, or a two-stage elimination strategy that uses low-fidelity samples to filter arms before running a high-fidelity UCB. Sensitivity plots for ρ and for violating Assumption 1 would also help.
- Lower bound gap: the paper identifies the gap for arms in K(m)7 (small positive gaps at some lower fidelity). While the discussion is candid, it would be useful to characterize how often this regime arises in realistic settings and whether simple heuristic modifications (e.g., occasional low-fidelity “re-checks” based on estimated cost-to-distinguish) close part of the gap in practice.

Quality
- The technical development is sound and well-structured. The MF-UCB index aggregating upper bounds across fidelities is natural, and the analysis carefully controls plays at each fidelity via the K(m) partition and the ψ-based concentration.
- Assumptions are clearly stated (sub-Gaussian-like tails via ψ, known ζ(m), Assumption 1). The anytime capital-based regret is sensible; the decomposition into a residual term and the main regret term is transparent.
- The lower bound uses a standard change-of-measure argument tailored to the multi-fidelity setting; the matching rates for K(m)3 reinforce the near-optimality claim.

Clarity
- Overall clear and readable, with strong intuition around the γ(m) choice and the fidelity selection rule. A few notational slips (listed below) should be fixed. It would help to instantiate ψ and γ(m) explicitly for sub-Gaussian rewards (e.g., ψ(x)=Θ(x^2)) and show the thresholds numerically for a simple setup.

Originality
- The multi-fidelity bandit formulation with biased, cost-graded fidelities and a cost-aware regret aligned to the highest fidelity is novel relative to prior bandit, budgeted bandit, and multi-fidelity optimization work.

Significance
- Conceptually significant for domains where cheap proxies abound; methodologically useful as a template to integrate biased proxies into bandit exploration. The results advance our understanding of how to allocate cost across fidelities and when large savings are theoretically achievable.

Suggestions for improvement
- Discuss how to choose/estimate ζ(m) in practice; provide robustness results under misspecification.
- Include stronger baselines and sensitivity analyses (ρ, γ(m), λ(m), ζ(m), and violations of Assumption 1). A small real or semi-synthetic case study (e.g., ads with short-horizon vs long-horizon CTR) would strengthen the empirical section.
- Briefly discuss extensions to arm-dependent costs or fidelity hierarchies that are not strictly nested, and to settings where lower fidelities yield no direct reward (as mentioned in the conclusion).

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Clear problem formulation and strong motivation
- Solid algorithm with insightful analysis; near-optimal in large regimes
- Empirical evidence consistent with theory

Cons:
- Practical reliance on known ζ(m); limited empirical breadth
- Lower bound gap for K(m)7 not addressed algorithmically

Overall recommendation
A strong and timely contribution to bandits with biased proxies. I lean toward acceptance, with a request to address the ζ(m) practicality (discussion and/or robustness) and to broaden the empirical comparisons.
