This paper introduces a novel multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit setting and proposes the MF-UCB algorithm, accompanied by theoretical regret bounds. The problem is well-motivated, addressing a common scenario where obtaining precise measurements is costly, but cheaper approximations are available. The core idea of leveraging lower-fidelity information to efficiently eliminate suboptimal arms is intuitive and promising. The paper clearly presents the problem formulation, the algorithm, and the theoretical analysis. Overall, this is a significant contribution to the bandit literature.

The strength of the paper lies in its theoretical rigor. The authors provide upper and lower bounds on the cumulative pseudo-regret, demonstrating near-optimality under certain conditions. The analysis appears sound, and the connection to classical UCB algorithms is clearly articulated. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and highlights its relevance to practical applications like online advertising and algorithm selection. The writing is generally clear and concise, making the paper accessible to researchers familiar with bandit algorithms.

However, several weaknesses detract from the paper’s impact. The most significant issue concerns the supplementary materials (proofs). The authors acknowledge these materials contain numerous typos and errors, which undoubtedly hinder reproducibility and understanding.  A thorough revision of the proofs is *critical*. The numerous specific errors listed (e.g., page 10 eq.(8): typo in k/K; Section A1: H^(m) should be K^(m); page 10 line 328: ν/(rho-2) should be ν/2; etc.) suggest a lack of careful proofreading.

Furthermore, the use of "up-to-a-multiplicative-constant" notation is misleading. The constant isn’t universal; it depends on algorithm parameters like ρ.  Presenting exact inequalities, even if more verbose, would significantly enhance clarity and allow for a more precise understanding of the algorithm’s performance. 

The practical applicability of MF-UCB is unclear. The algorithm relies on accurately estimating the fidelity ζ, which may be difficult in real-world scenarios. The paper lacks a thorough discussion of the challenges associated with ζ estimation and potential mitigation strategies. How sensitive is the performance of MF-UCB to inaccuracies in ζ?  Are there adaptive methods for learning ζ during the learning process? Addressing these questions would greatly strengthen the paper’s practical relevance. The simulations, while demonstrating performance gains, are somewhat limited and don’t fully explore the algorithm’s behavior in more complex scenarios.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** Introduces a new and relevant bandit setting with multi-fidelity pulls.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** Provides rigorous theoretical analysis with upper and lower regret bounds.
*   **Significance:**  Addresses a practical problem with potential applications in various domains.
*   **Clarity (main text):** The main paper is well-written and easy to follow.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Supplementary Material Quality:**  The numerous errors in the supplementary materials severely hamper reproducibility and understanding.
*   **Clarity (notation):** The "up-to-a-multiplicative-constant" notation obscures important details.
*   **Practical Applicability:** The paper lacks a discussion of practical challenges and limitations, particularly regarding ζ estimation.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the weaknesses, the theoretical contribution of this paper is substantial. The multi-fidelity bandit setting is a valuable addition to the literature, and the MF-UCB algorithm offers a promising approach to solving it. *However*, acceptance is contingent on a complete and meticulous revision of the supplementary materials to correct all identified errors. Addressing the concerns regarding the notation and providing a more in-depth discussion of practical considerations would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact.

**Recommendation:**  Accept with major revisions. The authors must address the issues with the supplementary materials as a priority.
