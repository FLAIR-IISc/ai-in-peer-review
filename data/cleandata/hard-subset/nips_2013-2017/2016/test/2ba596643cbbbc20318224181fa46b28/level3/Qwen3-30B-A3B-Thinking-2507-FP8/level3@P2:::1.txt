

# Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

This paper introduces the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit problem, where each arm can be observed at multiple fidelity levels, with higher fidelities providing more accurate but more expensive observations. The authors formalize a regret metric that accounts for both the cost of observations and the quality of the reward estimates. They propose MF-UCB, an algorithm that adaptively chooses both arms and fidelities, and prove a regret bound showing it outperforms standard UCB (which only uses the highest fidelity) when the cost of lower fidelities is significantly less than the highest fidelity. The authors also provide a matching lower bound demonstrating near-optimality under certain conditions.

The problem is well-motivated by practical scenarios like online advertising (where ad performance can be approximated through shorter display times or narrower audience targeting) and algorithm selection (where cheaper approximations of algorithm performance can be obtained through reduced data or computation). The paper builds on the rich literature of multi-armed bandits, extending the UCB framework to incorporate multiple fidelity levels. Related work on multi-fidelity optimization in other domains is briefly discussed, though the authors correctly note that previous work does not formalize or analyze regret in the bandit setting.

**Strengths:**
1. The problem formulation addresses an important practical tradeoff between cost, information quality, and reward in bandit settings.
2. The theoretical analysis is technically sound, with both upper and lower bounds provided, and the regret bound demonstrates clear advantages over standard UCB when lower fidelities are sufficiently cheaper.
3. The algorithm design is intuitive and well-motivated by the problem structure, with the partitioning of arms into sets K^(m) providing a clear explanation for the algorithm's behavior.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Clarity and Explanation:** The paper struggles to clearly explain the significance of the results and the intuition behind the regret bound. The dependence on complex sets K^(m) and the relationship between the regret terms and the parameters is difficult to parse without additional explanation. The motivation for the regret formulation (multiplying cost by regret) is not sufficiently justified - an additive cost penalty would be more natural and should be discussed.

2. **Assumption 1:** The requirement that ζ(m) decays fast enough (Assumption 1) is artificial and not well-justified. The authors should clarify why this assumption is necessary for the regret bound and whether the algorithm can be adapted to work without it (e.g., by selecting a subsequence of fidelities that satisfies the condition).

3. **Motivation and Generality:** The online advertising example is somewhat narrow. The paper would benefit from broader motivation for the chosen reward/cost model, particularly in cases where lower fidelity observations might not yield any reward (e.g., computer simulations in robotics).

4. **Lower Bound Gap:** The gap between the lower bound and MF-UCB's regret for arms in K^(m)_7 is not adequately addressed. The authors acknowledge this gap but don't provide a clear path forward for resolving it, which weakens the claim of "near-optimality."

5. **Algorithm Dependence:** The algorithm design appears highly tailored to the specific ζ(m) configuration satisfying Assumption 1. It's unclear how robust the approach is when this assumption doesn't hold.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- The problem is novel and relevant to practical bandit applications.
- The theoretical contribution is significant, with both upper and lower bounds establishing near-optimality under certain conditions.
- The algorithm is well-designed and provides a clear practical advantage over standard UCB when lower fidelities are sufficiently cheap.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- The regret formulation is unnatural (multiplying cost by regret) without adequate justification.
- Key assumptions (particularly Assumption 1) are not well-motivated and appear restrictive.
- The paper lacks sufficient clarity in explaining the results and their implications, making it difficult for readers to assess the contribution.
- The lower bound gap represents a notable weakness in the theoretical analysis.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
1. Clearly justify the regret formulation and discuss alternative formulations (e.g., additive cost penalty).
2. Provide more intuitive explanations for the regret bound and the role of the sets K^(m).
3. Address the robustness of the algorithm when Assumption 1 is violated.
4. Expand the motivation to cover more diverse applications where the multi-fidelity setting is relevant.
5. Discuss the lower bound gap more thoroughly and suggest potential paths to close it.

Overall, this is a technically sound paper that addresses an interesting problem with practical relevance. However, the clarity issues, questionable assumptions, and regret formulation concern significantly weaken the contribution. With substantial revisions addressing these concerns, the paper would be a strong candidate for NIPS. As it stands, it would be acceptable with major revisions.
