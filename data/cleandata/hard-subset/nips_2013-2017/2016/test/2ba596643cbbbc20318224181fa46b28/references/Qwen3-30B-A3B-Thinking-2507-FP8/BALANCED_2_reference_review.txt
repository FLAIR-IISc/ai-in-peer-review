

# Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

This paper introduces a novel multi-fidelity bandit framework where each arm has multiple fidelities (approximations) with different costs. The authors propose the MF-UCB algorithm that uses cheaper lower-fidelity approximations to eliminate "bad" arms and reserves expensive high-fidelity plays for promising arms. The paper builds on standard UCB bandit methods but extends them to the multi-fidelity setting, which is increasingly relevant in applications where high-fidelity evaluations are expensive (e.g., online advertising, algorithm selection).

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Framework:** The paper presents a well-motivated extension of the classical bandit problem to the multi-fidelity setting. The problem definition is clear, and the practical motivation (e.g., online advertising where shorter ad displays provide cheaper approximations) is compelling.

2. **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis with both upper and lower bounds for the regret. The regret bound (Theorem 2) demonstrates that MF-UCB can outperform standard UCB by a factor of λ(JkK)/λ(M) for arms in K(m) with m < M, which is a significant theoretical contribution.

3. **Empirical Validation:** The synthetic experiments (Section 6) support the theoretical claims, showing that MF-UCB outperforms UCB in terms of regret and using more high-fidelity plays on promising arms.

4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-organized, with clear definitions and a logical flow from problem statement to algorithm to analysis. The partitioning of arms into K(m) sets (Figure 1) is particularly helpful for understanding the algorithm's behavior.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Regret Definition Justification:** The paper introduces a new regret definition (Equation 1) that accounts for both the instantaneous regret and the cost of fidelity, but the justification for this specific form is somewhat weak. While the authors mention that choosing qt = μ^(m)_It would not reflect their motivating applications, they don't thoroughly explain why this particular formulation is superior to alternatives. A more detailed discussion of regret definitions in similar settings would strengthen the paper.

2. **Ad Hoc Parameter Choice:** The choice of γ(m) (Equation 4) appears somewhat arbitrary. While the authors provide a motivation for the specific form, a more thorough justification (e.g., showing it's optimal under certain conditions) would strengthen the theoretical contribution.

3. **Gap in Lower Bound Analysis:** The lower bound (Theorem 4) shows a gap for arms in K(m)_7, which the authors explain but don't quantify. It would be valuable to provide an example where this gap is significant in practice or discuss how large this gap might be in typical scenarios.

4. **Unknown ζ(m):** The paper assumes ζ(m) is known, but in practice, these bias parameters would likely be unknown. The authors should discuss how their algorithm would need to be modified to handle this common scenario.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a well-written paper that makes a significant contribution to the bandit literature by introducing a meaningful extension to the multi-fidelity setting. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and the empirical results support the claims. The paper meets the high standards of NIPS in terms of technical quality and significance.

The main weaknesses relate to the justification of the regret definition and the choice of γ(m), which slightly weaken the theoretical contribution. However, these are not fundamental flaws in the approach, and the paper's core contribution remains strong.

**Recommendation:**

Accept. The paper presents a novel and well-motivated extension of bandit theory with strong theoretical and empirical results. The minor weaknesses noted above are not sufficient to outweigh the paper's significant contribution to the field. With minor clarifications addressing the regret definition and γ(m) choice, the paper would be even stronger.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

1. Provide a more thorough justification for the regret definition (Equation 1), comparing it to alternative formulations and explaining why it's appropriate for the motivating applications.

2. Elaborate on the choice of γ(m) (Equation 4), potentially showing how it follows from an optimization of the regret bound.

3. Discuss how the algorithm would need to be modified if ζ(m) were unknown (which is likely the case in practice).

4. Quantify the gap between the upper and lower bounds for arms in K(m)_7 to better understand its practical significance.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS, addressing an important problem with a technically sound solution that advances the state of the art in bandit theory.
