

# Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

This paper introduces a novel framework for multi-armed bandit problems where each arm has access to multiple fidelities (approximations) with different costs. The authors propose a new algorithm, MF-UCB, which strategically uses lower-fidelity approximations to eliminate suboptimal arms before committing to expensive high-fidelity evaluations. The paper provides a rigorous regret analysis showing MF-UCB achieves significantly better performance than standard UCB when lower fidelities are cheaper, and establishes a matching lower bound demonstrating near-optimality.

The paper makes a clear conceptual contribution by extending the classical bandit framework to incorporate multiple fidelities - a problem that has been largely unaddressed in the literature despite its practical relevance in domains like online advertising, algorithm selection, and robotics. The authors effectively distinguish their work from related approaches (e.g., budgeted bandits, multi-fidelity optimization) by clearly articulating the unique challenges and opportunities presented by the multi-fidelity setting.

**Originality (5/5):** This is a genuinely novel framework that extends the classical bandit problem in a meaningful way. The paper identifies a gap in the literature that has practical importance but hasn't been properly formalized or analyzed before. The core idea of using cheap approximations to eliminate arms before high-fidelity evaluation is conceptually bold and represents a significant conceptual advance over existing work.

**Significance (4.5/5):** The problem addressed is highly significant for real-world applications where high-fidelity evaluations are expensive. The paper demonstrates both theoretically and empirically that MF-UCB can substantially outperform standard bandit algorithms in these settings. The authors provide concrete examples (online advertising, algorithm selection) that make the problem's relevance clear. The paper advances the state of the art by showing how to properly leverage multi-fidelity information in bandit settings.

**Quality (4.5/5):** The theoretical analysis is thorough and well-executed, with a carefully constructed regret bound and matching lower bound. The algorithm is well-motivated and the analysis clearly explains how the algorithm adapts to the multi-fidelity setting. The empirical results support the theoretical claims. The only minor weakness is the gap between the lower bound and MF-UCB for certain arms (K(m)7), which the authors acknowledge as an open problem.

**Clarity (5/5):** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem is clearly motivated with concrete examples, the notation is consistent, and the algorithm is presented in a way that makes its intuition transparent. The figures effectively illustrate the key concepts, particularly the partitioning of arms across fidelities.

The paper's main strength is its conceptual novelty - it introduces a fundamentally new way to view and solve bandit problems that has significant practical implications. While the algorithm builds on UCB principles, the multi-fidelity framework represents a substantial extension that opens up new research directions. The authors have carefully considered related work and clearly articulated how their approach differs from existing methods.

The paper addresses a problem that has been overlooked in the bandit literature despite its practical importance. The authors have made a compelling case for why this framework is valuable, and their analysis demonstrates that it can lead to significant improvements in regret performance. The paper's theoretical contributions are rigorous, and the empirical results confirm the theoretical insights.

I believe this paper makes a strong contribution to the field. It's not just a minor extension of existing work but represents a new direction in bandit research that will likely inspire future work. The authors have done an excellent job of establishing the theoretical foundations for this new framework and demonstrating its practical advantages.

While the paper makes the assumption that the bias ζ(m) is known, this is a common assumption in bandit literature (similar to assuming variance is bounded in UCB). The authors acknowledge limitations where appropriate, such as the gap in the lower bound for certain arms, which shows intellectual honesty.

This paper would be a valuable addition to NIPS, as it presents a genuinely novel framework with clear theoretical and practical significance. It advances the state of the art in bandit problems by introducing a new dimension (fidelity) that has been previously unexplored in this context.

**Recommendation: Accept**

The paper's novelty, significance, and strong theoretical foundation outweigh any minor limitations. It represents a bold conceptual advance in bandit problems that is likely to influence future research in the field.
