- Paper introduces multi-fidelity stochastic K-armed bandits with M biased, costed fidelities and proposes MF-UCB to minimize cost-weighted pseudo-regret under a budget.
- MF-UCB builds fidelity-specific UCBs incorporating bias bounds ζ(m) and chooses the cheapest fidelity whose uncertainty exceeds a designed threshold γ(m).
- Analysis partitions arms by fidelity-based closeness and gives an upper bound that charges lower costs for arms eliminable at low fidelities; standard UCB pays full high-fidelity cost.
- A Bernoulli lower bound shows MF-UCB is near-optimal for most arms, with a specific subset where early switching is suboptimal.
- Strength: problem formulation is realistic and well-motivated for applications like advertising and algorithm selection.
- Strength: algorithm is simple, principled, computationally light, and captures cost–accuracy trade-offs via γ(m).
- Strength: theoretical results cleanly decompose costs across fidelities and show improvements when few arms require high fidelity.
- Strength: conceptual clarity using optimism with bias corrections and the K(m) partition aids intuition.
- Strength: synthetic experiments corroborate theory, showing reduced regret slope and predicted fidelity usage.
- Weakness: regret definition (qt = µ(M) regardless of fidelity) is strong and may not reflect applications where low-fidelity actions produce real utility.
- Suggestion: formalize and analyze alternative regret metrics (e.g., realized reward-per-cost or bi-objective formulations).
- Weakness: assumes known, uniform per-fidelity bias bounds ζ(m), which is unrealistic in practice.
- Suggestion: add robustness analysis, sensitivity experiments for mis-specified ζ, and methods to estimate ζ(m) (e.g., paired evaluations or learned/arm-dependent bounds).
- Weakness: Assumption 1 (fast decay of ζ) is strong; need bounds or empirical results when fidelities are dense or improvements marginal.
- Weakness: myopic fidelity-switch rule can be suboptimal for arms with small low-fidelity gaps; consider heuristic “cheap certification” tests and analyze when they pay off.
- Weakness: literature positioning could be broadened to include side-observation/graph-structured bandits, bandits with knapsacks, and cost-heterogeneous best-arm identification.
- Weakness: experiments are limited to synthetic data and UCB baseline; recommend hierarchical elimination baselines, Thompson Sampling variants, sensitivity studies, and a real-world case study.
- Quality: technical development and proofs are sound, but some theorem statements and presentations could be clearer.
- Clarity: paper is generally well-organized; suggest explicitly noting K(m) is analytical only and providing practical guidance for choosing ψ, ρ, and computing ψ−1.
- Originality: formulation and algorithm are novel in bandit regret settings with biased approximations and per-fidelity costs.
- Significance: setting is practically relevant and likely to influence follow-up work on leveraging cheap biased proxies.
- Pros for acceptance: novel problem, simple principled algorithm, strong theory, clear intuition, supporting experiments.
- Cons for acceptance: strong assumptions (known ζ, Assumption 1), limited robustness and empirical breadth, possible misalignment of regret metric, unresolved lower-bound gap for some arms.
- Overall recommendation: reviewer leans toward acceptance—technically solid and novel, with limitations mainly in assumptions and empirical evaluation.
