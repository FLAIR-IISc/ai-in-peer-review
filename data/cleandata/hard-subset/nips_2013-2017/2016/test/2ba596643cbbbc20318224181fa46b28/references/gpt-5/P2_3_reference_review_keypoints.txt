- Introduces a multi-armed bandit model with multiple fidelities per arm where lower fidelities give biased, cheaper estimates with known uniform bias bounds ζ(m) and increasing costs λ(m).
- Defines a cost-weighted regret for fixed capital Λ that always rewards the highest-fidelity mean µ(M) of the chosen arm regardless of played fidelity.
- Proposes MF-UCB: per-fidelity UCBs augmented by ζ(m) and a fidelity-selection rule using thresholds γ(m) that trade off bias and cost.
- Proves an upper-bound on regret showing cheap fidelities can eliminate many suboptimal arms and concentrate expensive plays on promising arms.
- Provides a Bernoulli lower bound (ψ(·)≈·2) showing MF-UCB is near-optimal on a broad subset of arms, with a candid discussion of remaining gaps.
- Synthetic experiments show improved regret slopes and expected fidelity allocation compared to single-fidelity UCB.
- Positions the work relative to UCB/Lai–Robbins literature and distinguishes it from budgeted bandits and prior multi-fidelity optimization lacking regret analyses.
- Modeling assumptions are clearly stated: known ζ(m), monotone costs, sub-exponential concentration (ψ), and ζ decay regularity; regret accounts for unused capital.
- The fidelity threshold γ(m)=ψ−1((λ(m)/λ(m+1))ψ(ζ(m))) is well-motivated and appears optimal in their analysis.
- Theoretical analysis controls play counts under capital Λ and shows high-probability sublinearity of unnecessary low-fidelity plays.
- Empirical section is adequate but limited: details in appendix, few baselines, and no real-data evaluation.
- Paper is generally clear with helpful notation and partitioning K(m), but heavy notation and hidden constants hurt accessibility; sensitivity to ζ mis-specification is not discussed.
- The formalization, cost-aware regret, MF-UCB algorithm, fidelity-switching rule, and tailored lower bound are original contributions.
- The setting is practically relevant (ads, algorithm selection, simulation) and can yield substantial cost savings when many arms are clearly suboptimal at low fidelities.
- Strengths: principled problem formalization, simple implementable algorithm, strong theoretical results, and intuitive partitioning matching experiments.
- Weaknesses: strong assumptions (known arm-independent ζ and ψ, ζ regularity), lower bound limited to Bernoulli and gaps on some arms, limited experimental baselines, and narrow regret definition.
- Suggest evaluating robustness to ζ mis-specification and adaptive tuning of γ(m).
- Suggest extending to arm-dependent or learned bias bounds and discussing whether analysis can handle confidence-based ζ estimates.
- Recommend broader empirical evaluation: KL-UCB/Thompson variants, elimination heuristics, γ(m) ablations, and at least one real or semi-synthetic dataset.
- Ask authors to clarify ψ’s role beyond sub-Gaussian cases and to expand on alternative regret notions sketched in the conclusion.
- Overall recommendation: lean accept — technically solid and original, with empirical breadth and strong assumptions as main caveats.
