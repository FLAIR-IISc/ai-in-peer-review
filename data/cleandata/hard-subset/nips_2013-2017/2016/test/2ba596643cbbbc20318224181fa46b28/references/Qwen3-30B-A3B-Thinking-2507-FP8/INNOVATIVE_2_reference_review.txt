

# Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

This paper introduces a novel framework for multi-armed bandit problems when multiple fidelities (approximations) of each arm are available at different costs. The authors formalize a cost-weighted regret notion that accounts for the varying costs of different fidelity evaluations, and propose the Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB) algorithm. MF-UCB strategically uses cheaper lower-fidelity evaluations to eliminate suboptimal arms and reserves expensive high-fidelity evaluations for a small subset of promising arms. The paper provides a theoretical analysis showing MF-UCB achieves near-optimal regret bounds, with empirical validation on synthetic problems demonstrating clear advantages over standard UCB.

The paper makes a clear distinction from previous work. While there is related research on multi-fidelity optimization in other domains (e.g., [7, 10]) and budgeted bandits ([13, 15]), none formalize regret in the multi-fidelity bandit setting with cost considerations. The authors correctly identify that lower fidelities introduce biases that cannot be treated as simple high-variance observations, which is a key conceptual contribution. The problem formulation is well-motivated by practical applications in online advertising, algorithm selection, and other domains where expensive high-fidelity evaluations are common.

**Strengths:**
1. **Novel problem formulation:** The paper introduces a genuinely new framework that extends the classic bandit problem to multi-fidelity evaluations. This is not a minor extension but a significant conceptual advance that opens up a new direction for bandit research.
2. **Strong theoretical contribution:** The regret analysis is rigorous, with a near-matching lower bound that demonstrates the near-optimality of MF-UCB. The paper clearly shows how the algorithm's regret depends on the cost structure and fidelity levels.
3. **Clear intuition and motivation:** The paper effectively explains the intuition behind the algorithm (using cheap fidelities to eliminate arms, reserving expensive evaluations for promising arms) and connects it to practical applications.
4. **Well-executed empirical validation:** The synthetic experiments convincingly demonstrate MF-UCB's advantages over standard UCB, with results aligning with the theoretical insights.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The paper focuses only on synthetic experiments, though this is acceptable for a theoretical contribution. More real-world applications would strengthen the significance claim, but this is not a major flaw given the paper's theoretical focus.
2. The assumption that ζ(m) values (the bias bounds) are known may be restrictive in practice, though the authors acknowledge this limitation in the conclusion.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Introduces a genuinely novel problem setting with clear practical relevance
- Provides a near-optimal algorithm with rigorous theoretical guarantees
- Makes a significant conceptual advance beyond standard bandit research
- Well-written with clear exposition and strong theoretical analysis
- The framework could inspire future work in multi-fidelity optimization across multiple domains

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Limited empirical validation (only synthetic problems)
- Assumes knowledge of ζ(m) values, which may not always be available

This paper represents a bold and significant extension of bandit theory to a new setting with multiple fidelities. The novelty of the problem formulation is substantial, and the theoretical results are strong. The authors have clearly identified a gap in the literature and provided a compelling solution that advances the state of the art. While the empirical validation could be expanded to real-world applications, this is not a critical flaw for a theoretical contribution of this caliber.

The paper's approach aligns perfectly with the reviewer's preference for novel ideas over methodological perfection. The theoretical analysis is thorough, and the paper makes a clear contribution that would be valuable to the broader machine learning community. The authors correctly position their work as a new direction rather than an incremental improvement, and they have done an excellent job of distinguishing their approach from related work.

I believe this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of bandit problems and would be of interest to NIPS readers. The novelty of the framework, combined with strong theoretical results, makes this a compelling submission. The minor limitations (synthetic experiments, known ζ(m) assumption) are outweighed by the conceptual novelty and theoretical strength.

**Recommendation: Accept**
