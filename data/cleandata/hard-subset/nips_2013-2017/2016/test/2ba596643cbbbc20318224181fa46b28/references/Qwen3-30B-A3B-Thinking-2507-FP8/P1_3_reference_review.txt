

# Peer Review: The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper introduces a novel framework for multi-armed bandit problems where each arm has multiple fidelity levels, with lower fidelities being cheaper but less accurate approximations of the true reward. The authors formalize this as a cost-aware bandit problem where each fidelity level m has cost λ(m) and provides an estimate with error bounded by ζ(m). They propose the Multi-Fidelity UCB (MF-UCB) algorithm that strategically uses lower fidelity evaluations to eliminate suboptimal arms before committing to expensive high-fidelity evaluations. The paper provides a theoretical regret analysis showing MF-UCB achieves O((λ(1)K + λ(2)|Kg|) log(Λ/λ(2))) regret, which can be significantly better than standard UCB's O(λ(2)K log(Λ/λ(2))) when λ(1) ≪ λ(2) and |Kg| ≪ K. They also prove a matching lower bound showing MF-UCB is near-optimal.

This work extends the classic bandit literature (e.g., [3, 9]) by incorporating the cost of obtaining information, which is relevant to applications like online advertising (where shorter ad displays provide cheaper but biased estimates) and algorithm selection (where cheaper approximations of algorithm performance exist). It differs from prior work on budgeted bandits [13, 15] by explicitly modeling multiple fidelities for each arm rather than random costs per arm.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper provides a clean formalism for multi-fidelity bandits and a tight regret analysis. The regret bound is properly derived and compared to the standard UCB bound, showing clear theoretical advantages when lower fidelities are sufficiently cheaper and the set of promising arms is small.

2. **Algorithm Design**: MF-UCB is a natural extension of UCB that incorporates fidelity switching based on confidence intervals. The choice of γ(m) is well-motivated and the algorithm's behavior aligns with the intuition that low-fidelity evaluations should be used to eliminate arms, while high-fidelity evaluations should be reserved for promising arms.

3. **Empirical Validation**: The paper includes synthetic experiments demonstrating MF-UCB outperforms UCB in terms of regret, with results consistent with the theoretical analysis. The visualization of play allocation across fidelities (Fig. 2) effectively illustrates the algorithm's strategy.

4. **Clarity**: The paper is well-organized with clear definitions, intuitive explanations, and careful presentation of the regret decomposition. The technical content is accessible to readers familiar with standard bandit literature.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Scope of Applications**: While the paper mentions applications like online advertising and algorithm selection, it doesn't thoroughly explore how the multi-fidelity framework might apply to more complex settings (e.g., contextual bandits or reinforcement learning). The theoretical analysis is limited to the stochastic bandit setting.

2. **Assumption Strength**: The paper relies on Assumption 1 (ζ(m) decaying fast enough) to ensure the regret bound is meaningful. While the authors note this assumption is not necessary for algorithm analysis, it's somewhat restrictive for real-world applications where fidelities might not decay exponentially.

3. **Lower Bound Gap**: The lower bound (Theorem 4) reveals a gap between MF-UCB's performance and the theoretical minimum for some arms (K(m)7). The authors acknowledge this but don't provide a clear path to resolving it. It's unclear whether this gap is fundamental to the problem or could be closed with a different algorithm.

4. **Comparison to Recent Work**: The paper cites Kandasamy et al. [8] as related work but doesn't sufficiently contrast their Gaussian process approach with the current linear bandit framework. The relationship between these approaches could be clarified.

## Quality, Clarity, Originality, and Significance

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis and well-designed experiments. The regret bounds are properly derived and supported by empirical results. The authors are careful to discuss both strengths (e.g., MF-UCB's advantage when λ(1) ≪ λ(2)) and limitations (e.g., the gap in the lower bound).

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The key concepts (fidelities, regret definition, MF-UCB algorithm) are clearly explained with helpful figures (Fig. 1, 2). The notation is consistent and well-motivated.

**Originality**: The multi-fidelity bandit framework is novel in the bandit literature. While multi-fidelity optimization has been studied in other contexts [7, 10], this paper provides the first formal regret analysis for the bandit setting. The combination of fidelity levels with exploration-exploitation trade-offs is a meaningful contribution.

**Significance**: This work addresses a practical problem where obtaining high-quality data is expensive (e.g., in advertising, robotics, or algorithm selection). The theoretical and empirical results demonstrate a clear advantage of the multi-fidelity approach when lower fidelities are sufficiently cheaper, making it relevant to many real-world applications.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong theoretical contribution to the bandit literature that extends the classical framework to incorporate multiple fidelity levels. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and provides clear theoretical and empirical evidence for the advantages of the multi-fidelity approach. While the assumptions could be relaxed and the lower bound gap addressed in future work, the current results are compelling and demonstrate a meaningful improvement over standard bandit algorithms in practical scenarios.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by formalizing a practically relevant extension to bandit problems and providing a theoretically grounded algorithm with strong empirical performance. It is clearly within the scope of NIPS, addressing a fundamental problem in decision-making under uncertainty with clear applications.

**Recommendation**: Accept

**Arguments for Acceptance**: Novel framework, strong theoretical analysis, clear empirical validation, well-written, relevant to practical applications.

**Arguments against Acceptance**: Limited scope of applications, restrictive assumption for regret bound, unresolved gap in lower bound. However, these are relatively minor given the paper's overall strength.
