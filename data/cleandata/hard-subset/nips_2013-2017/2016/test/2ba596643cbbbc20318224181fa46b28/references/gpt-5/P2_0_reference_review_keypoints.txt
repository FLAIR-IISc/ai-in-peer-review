- Introduces multi-fidelity stochastic K-armed bandits with biased lower-fidelity evaluations and a cost-capital pseudo-regret.
- Proposes MF-UCB that uses cheap biased evaluations to eliminate clearly suboptimal arms and reserves high-fidelity plays for promising arms.
- Theoretical upper bound: MF-UCB can substantially outperform standard UCB when many arms are resolvable at low fidelities and costs are well-separated.
- Provides a Bernoulli lower bound showing MF-UCB is near-optimal for a large subset of arms and precisely characterizes the remaining gap (K(m)3 vs K(m)7).
- Problem formulation is distinct from budgeted bandits and multi-fidelity GP work and requires nontrivial UCB adaptations for bias and switching.
- Algorithm and analysis are technically sound; confidence indices combine statistical uncertainty and known bias bounds ζ(m).
- Regret decomposition shows most cost for obviously suboptimal arms occurs at low fidelities; high-fidelity cost is concentrated on near-optimal arms.
- Analysis assumes known uniform bias bounds ζ(m) with sufficiently fast decay (Assumption 1) and sub-exponential concentration — a strong but explicit assumption.
- Lower bound additionally assumes Bernoulli rewards and excludes pathological polynomial over-exploration.
- Claims are well-supported; MF-UCB is never asymptotically much worse than UCB and can be significantly better in favorable regimes.
- Synthetic experiments corroborate theory (shallower regret slope, fidelity-wise play allocation) but are limited in scope.
- Empirical limitations: only UCB baseline, no real-world or semi-realistic simulations, and no sensitivity study to ζ(m) misspecification.
- Exposition is clear and precise but dense; recommend moving intuition for K(m) and γ(m) earlier and adding a running example.
- Novelty: new multi-fidelity, biased-observation bandit formulation and a principled MF-UCB algorithm with new theoretical insights.
- Significance: conceptually and theoretically important for settings with cheap biased feedback; practical impact constrained by need to know ζ(m).
- Pros: clear formulation, simple intuitive algorithm, strong per-arm analysis, and honest discussion of limitations.
- Cons: requires known ζ(m) and regularity; limited empirical validation; lower bound limited to Bernoulli; some regret-definition applicability concerns.
- Suggested improvements: study robustness or online estimation of ζ(m); add baselines (heuristics, Thompson-sampling variants) and realistic simulations; analyze Assumption 1 practicality and alternative regret definitions.
- Overall recommendation: Accept — solid, well-motivated theoretical contribution relevant to NeurIPS.
- Reviewer confidence: 4/5.
