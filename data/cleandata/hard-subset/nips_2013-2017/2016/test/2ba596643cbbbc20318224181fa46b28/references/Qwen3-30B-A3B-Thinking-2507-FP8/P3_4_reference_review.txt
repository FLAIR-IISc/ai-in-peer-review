

# Peer Review: "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

## Summary
This paper introduces a multi-fidelity extension of the classic multi-armed bandit problem, where each arm can be sampled at multiple fidelities (approximations) with different costs and biases. The authors formalize a new regret notion that accounts for the cost of sampling at different fidelities, propose the MF-UCB algorithm that leverages cheaper lower-fidelity samples to eliminate "bad" arms and reserves expensive higher-fidelity samples for promising arms, and provide theoretical guarantees showing MF-UCB achieves regret O((λ(1)K + λ(2)|Kg|)log(Λ/λ(2))) where Kg is a small subset of promising arms. The paper makes a clear contribution to bandit literature by extending the problem to handle multiple fidelities, with well-motivated applications in online advertising and algorithm selection.

## Strengths
1. **Problem Formulation:** The multi-fidelity bandit framework is well-motivated with concrete examples from online advertising (where ad performance can be estimated at lower fidelities with shorter display times) and algorithm selection (where cheaper approximations can be obtained with less data). The formalization of the regret metric that accounts for both the cost of sampling and the quality of the estimate is particularly elegant.

2. **Algorithm Design:** MF-UCB is a natural and intuitive extension of UCB that effectively leverages the multi-fidelity structure. The algorithm's strategy of using lower fidelities to eliminate arms and reserving higher fidelities for promising arms aligns perfectly with the problem's intuition.

3. **Theoretical Analysis:** The regret bound and matching lower bound are technically sound. The partitioning of arms into K(m) sets is insightful and clearly explains how MF-UCB achieves better regret by playing different arms at different fidelities. The comparison to UCB is well-executed, showing how MF-UCB achieves better performance when lower fidelities are significantly cheaper and the set of promising arms is small.

4. **Experiments:** The synthetic experiments support the theoretical claims, showing MF-UCB outperforming UCB with a smaller gradient, as predicted by the theory.

## Weaknesses
1. **Known Bias Assumption:** The paper assumes ζ(m) (the bias between fidelities) is known, which is a significant limitation for practical applications. In most real-world settings, this bias would need to be estimated, and the paper doesn't address how this estimation affects performance or what happens when the bias is misestimated.

2. **Restrictive Regularity Condition:** Assumption 1 (that ζ(m) decays fast enough) is quite strong and may not hold in many practical scenarios. The authors acknowledge it's "not necessary for analysis" but state it's "most appealing" when satisfied. This condition essentially requires exponentially decaying ζ(m) for sub-Gaussian distributions, which may be difficult to justify in practice.

3. **Unresolved Gap in Lower Bound:** The lower bound only matches MF-UCB for arms in K(m)3 but not for those in K(m)7. The authors correctly identify that MF-UCB doesn't invest much effort distinguishing arms with small ∆(m)k, but they don't propose a solution to bridge this gap. This is a significant theoretical limitation.

4. **Parameter Sensitivity:** The algorithm depends on a parameter ρ (with ρ > 4 required for the analysis), but the paper doesn't discuss how to choose ρ in practice or how sensitive performance is to this choice.

5. **Limited Experimental Validation:** The experiments are limited to synthetic data. More realistic experiments would better demonstrate the practical utility of the approach.

## Overall Assessment
This is a technically sound paper with a clear contribution to the multi-armed bandit literature. The problem is well-motivated, the algorithm is well-designed, and the theoretical analysis is rigorous. The paper is original in its specific problem formulation and algorithm design, though it builds on the well-established UCB framework. The significance is moderate - it provides a nice extension to a classic problem that could be useful in specific application domains where multi-fidelity evaluations are available.

The paper is well-written and clear, making it easy to follow the technical content. The related work is appropriately cited and distinguishes the paper from previous approaches. The main limitations are the restrictive assumptions and the unresolved gap in the lower bound, which reduce the practical applicability of the results.

## Recommendation
I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the bandit literature, but the authors should address the following issues in revision:

1. Discuss how to handle the case where ζ(m) is unknown (e.g., through estimation).
2. Provide more justification for Assumption 1 or relax it.
3. Address the gap between the upper and lower bounds for arms in K(m)7.
4. Add a discussion of how to choose the parameter ρ.
5. Include more realistic experiments.

With these revisions, this paper would make a strong contribution to NIPS.
