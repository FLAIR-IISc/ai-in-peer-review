Summary
This paper formalizes a multi-fidelity variant of the stochastic K-armed bandit where each arm can be queried at multiple fidelities with different costs and biases. Lower fidelities are cheaper but provide biased approximations to the highest-fidelity reward; the bias at fidelity m is upper bounded by a known ζ(m) with ζ(1) > … > ζ(M)=0. The authors define a capital-constrained pseudo-regret that credits the high-fidelity mean µ(M) regardless of which fidelity was queried and debits the cost of the chosen fidelity. They propose MF-UCB, an extension of UCB that (i) maintains an upper confidence bound on µ(M) for each fidelity by adding ζ(m) to the usual confidence term, and (ii) selects the fidelity adaptively via thresholds γ(m) chosen to trade off cost and informativeness: play at the lowest fidelity whose uncertainty band remains above γ(m), otherwise escalate.

They prove an upper bound on regret that scales like O((λ(1)K + Σm λ(m)|K(m)|) log(Λ/λ(M))) with problem-dependent constants, where arms are partitioned by how close their lower-fidelity means are to the optimum. Intuitively, MF-UCB eliminates many bad arms using cheap fidelities and reserves expensive evaluations for a small set of promising arms. They also provide a lower bound (for Bernoulli rewards) showing near-optimality except for a specific subset of arms where lower fidelities are only mildly informative; the analysis clarifies a gap and leaves closing it as an open problem. Synthetic experiments demonstrate improved regret over vanilla UCB.

Relation to prior work
- Classical stochastic bandits and UCB: builds on [Auer 2002; Bubeck & Cesa-Bianchi 2012], but introduces fidelity-dependent costs and bias-aware confidence bounds.
- Budgeted bandits: differs from reward/cost ratio objectives [Tran-Thanh 2010; Xia 2015]; here, costs are fidelity-dependent and fidelities are biased approximations to the same target reward.
- Multi-fidelity optimization: prior work in engineering/BO [Huang 2006; Rajnarayan 2008] and in GP bandits with multi-fidelity [Kandasamy et al. 2016] did not study the finite-armed, stochastic bandit regret setting with explicit bias guarantees and switching policies.
- Weak/strong labelers in active learning [Zhang & Chaudhuri 2015] and RL with simulators [Cutler et al. 2014] are related, but objectives and analyses differ.

Quality
- Theory: The algorithm and analysis are technically sound under the stated assumptions. The construction of fidelity-aware UCB bounds B(m)k,t = X̄(m) + ψ−1(ρ log t / s) + ζ(m), and the fidelity selection rule via γ(m) = ψ−1( (λ(m)/λ(m+1)) ψ(ζ(m)) ), are well motivated and used carefully to derive arm-wise play counts and capital usage. The partition K(m) is a helpful device to track where arms are filtered.
- Upper bound: Clear intuition—MF-UCB is never much worse than using the top fidelity only, and can be substantially better when many arms can be eliminated cheaply. The bound recovers the standard UCB scaling on near-optimal arms and improves cost factors elsewhere.
- Lower bound: For Bernoulli cases, it matches the upper bound for a large subset K(m)3 and explains the gap for K(m)7 due to conservative switching to avoid over-spending at low fidelities with tiny positive gaps. Assumption 3 is reasonable for competitive strategies.
- Assumptions: Requires known ζ(m), concentration function ψ (e.g., sub-Gaussian), monotone costs λ(1)<…<λ(M), and a regularity condition (Assumption 1) to avoid pathologically dense fidelities. These are plausible in some applications but somewhat strong; the paper is honest about when Assumption 1 matters.
- Experiments: Synthetic only; they corroborate the theory qualitatively but are limited in scope. More baselines and sensitivity analyses would strengthen the empirical case.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized with clear intuition for the algorithm and proof sketches. Notation is heavy but mostly consistent. Some parts could be clarified:
- Regret definition: further discussion of why crediting µ(M) regardless of fidelity is appropriate, and comparisons to alternatives (e.g., credit actual observed fidelity) with concrete use cases.
- Practicality: emphasize what is needed in practice (estimates of ζ(m), ψ, costs) and discuss robustness to misspecification.
- Minor editorial issues (typos/formatting, figure placement/captioning) should be cleaned up.

Originality
The formulation of multi-fidelity stochastic K-armed bandits with explicit bias bounds and cost-aware regret, and the MF-UCB algorithm with fidelity-aware switching thresholds, appear novel. The lower bound and near-optimality characterization are also new for this setting.

Significance
The work addresses a realistic and important extension of bandits where cheaper proxies abound (ads, ML algorithm selection, simulations). The theoretical contributions are meaningful and could influence subsequent designs (including Thompson sampling variants, racing-style methods, or extensions to structured arms). The empirical section, while indicative, is not yet sufficient to demonstrate practical impact across domains.

Pros
- Clear, principled formulation of multi-fidelity bandits and regret.
- Simple, implementable algorithm with solid theory.
- Insightful arm partition and capital accounting; near-optimal lower bound with a transparent gap.
- Results recover classical behavior on hard arms and improve cost scaling on easy arms.

Cons
- Assumes known fidelity biases ζ(m) and concentration ψ; no adaptivity if misspecified.
- Assumption 1 (fast-decaying ζ) is important for the strongest gains; its realism varies by domain.
- Lower bound restricted to Bernoulli rewards; extension to general sub-Gaussian would be stronger.
- Experiments are limited (synthetic only, few baselines). No sensitivity to ζ/λ, no real data.
- Regret notion, while justified, may not fit all applications; alternatives are only briefly discussed.

Questions for authors
1. How sensitive is MF-UCB to misspecification of ζ(m) and ψ? Can you provide robustness bounds or empirical sensitivity?
2. Can γ(m) be adapted online (e.g., via cross-validation across fidelities) to mitigate unknown ζ(m)?
3. How does MF-UCB compare to natural baselines such as UCB-V, Thompson sampling variants with multi-fidelity likelihoods, or racing/successive halving adapted to fidelities?
4. Can the lower bound be extended to sub-Gaussian families via KL arguments?
5. Any real-world case studies (even small-scale) where costs and fidelity bias bounds can be approximated?

Suggestions
- Add experiments with: varying ζ decay rates (including violations of Assumption 1), different cost ratios, and comparisons to stronger baselines.
- Provide guidance on estimating ζ(m) (e.g., calibration phases) and discuss conservative choices that guarantee correctness at the expense of speed.
- Clarify the practical per-round complexity and memory; include pseudo-code in the appendix with all constants.
- Expand the discussion on alternative regret notions and how MF-UCB could be adapted.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The problem is timely and well-motivated, the algorithm is elegant, and the theoretical analysis is solid and insightful. The main weaknesses are empirical breadth and reliance on known ζ(m), but the contribution is significant enough for NIPS.

Confidence
High (expert familiar with bandit algorithms and multi-fidelity optimization).
