Summary
The paper introduces a multi-fidelity variant of the stochastic K-armed bandit. Each arm k has M fidelities with increasing cost λ(1) < ... < λ(M) and means µ(m)k that are biased approximations of the true (highest-fidelity) mean µ(M)k, with known bias bounds |µ(M)k − µ(m)k| ≤ ζ(m) and ζ(1) > ... > ζ(M) = 0. The authors define a capital-based pseudo-regret R(Λ) that values rewards proportional to cost spent and measures regret against an oracle that always plays the optimal arm (at any fidelity) but rewards are measured with respect to µ(M). They propose MF-UCB: per arm k, per fidelity m, maintain an upper bound B(m)k,t = X̄(m)k + β(m)(t) + ζ(m) where β(m) is a confidence radius from ψ−1(ρ log t / T(m)), and choose the arm with the minimum across fidelities Bk,t = minm B(m)k,t. A fidelity-selection rule escalates from low to high fidelities using thresholds γ(m) = ψ−1( (λ(m)/λ(m+1)) ψ(ζ(m)) ). They derive a partition of arms K(1),...,K(M) based on how close lower-fidelity means are to optimal, prove an upper bound on regret scaling like log(Λ/λ(M)) times, roughly, ∑k λ(JkK)/ψ(∆(JkK)k) with most effort spent at the fidelity JkK where k can be eliminated, and show MF-UCB is never much worse than UCB and can be better when many arms are eliminated cheaply. A lower bound for Bernoulli rewards shows near-optimality except for a specific subset K(m)7. Simulations on synthetic problems (appendix) suggest improvements over UCB.

Strengths
- Addresses a practically relevant extension of bandits with multiple biased, cheaper proxies; model captures costs and systematic bias, not just variance.
- Clear algorithmic idea: add bias offsets to UCBs and a principled fidelity-switching rule based on cost–bias tradeoff (γ(m)).
- Regret decomposition with arm partition K(m) is insightful and explains when multi-fidelity helps.
- Theoretical analysis includes both upper and lower bounds (even if limited to Bernoulli for the latter), and identifies a gap for a particular subset K(m)7.
- Conceptually connects to multi-fidelity optimization and budgeted bandits while being distinct in objective.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical correctness/clarity
- The regret definition is unconventional and only briefly justified. qt = µ(M)It regardless of fidelity implies one “earns” high-fidelity reward per unit cost even when only a cheap proxy is observed. While acceptable as pseudo-regret, the competing oracle “at any fidelity” is moot because the reward term is fidelity-independent; only costs matter. This needs a more careful discussion, and comparison to alternatives (e.g., fidelity-specific rewards, or realized rewards at played fidelity).
- Assumption 1 (∑i≤m 1/ψ(ζ(i)) ≤ 1/ψ(ζ(m+1))) is strong; it enforces extremely fast decay of ζ(m). Authors claim it’s not necessary for analysis but is used to dominate lower-fidelity regret. Presenting bounds without (or explicitly quantifying dependence on) this assumption would greatly strengthen the results.
- The lower bound only for Bernoulli rewards and with µ* ∈ (1/2,1), ζ(1) < 1/2, and an unspecified absolute constant c limits generality. The upper bound is stated for general ψ; the mismatch weakens the “near-optimality” claim.
- The gap on K(m)7 is explained but unresolved; there is no algorithmic modification or adaptive rule proposed to approach the lower bound in those cases.
- Several proof statements are imprecise in the main text. For instance, Theorem 2’s displayed bound appears to mix two different summations on one line; it’s unclear whether the bound is a sum or has two separate terms. Lemma statements and big-O/“.”/“&” notations are used idiosyncratically and hinder readability.

Algorithmic details
- The choice of ρ, handling of the ν constant in (2), and the exact form of ψ for common sub-Gaussian cases are not specified in the algorithmic description.
- Fidelity-selection Step 2 notation is awkward (∨ m = M inside a minimization). The logic is: escalate if the confidence radius at m is below γ(m); otherwise play at m. Write this explicitly.
- Practicality: MF-UCB requires known ζ(m). In most applications, bias envelopes are unknown and may be arm-dependent. The paper does not discuss robustness or estimation of ζ(m).

Experimental evaluation
- Experiments are underspecified (details moved to Appendix C; figures not included here). No information on K, M, λ(m), ζ(m), noise distributions, hyperparameters (ρ), or variance across runs; no baselines beyond vanilla UCB (e.g., Successive Elimination, lil’UCB variants adapted with costs, Thompson sampling variants, budgeted bandits). No ablations on γ(m) or sensitivity to mis-specified ζ(m).
- Claims such as “gradient of the curve is smaller” are qualitative; quantitative comparisons (slopes/coefficients) and confidence intervals are not shown.

Presentation and formatting issues
- Numerous formatting/notation glitches impede readability: dangling “th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016) …” in the intro; stray figure-caption text (“ζ(m)+2γ(m) are indicated…”) inside the main text; nonstandard use of “.,& to denote equality and inequalities ignoring constants”; inconsistent equation numbering; missing authors/years/venues in the references; inconsistent citation styles; missing Assumption 2 (Assumption numbering jumps from 1 to 3).
- The introductory claim “UCB achieves O(K log n) regret” is inaccurate as stated; standard UCB regret is O(∑k≠k* (log n)/∆k).
- Kg mentioned in the intro is not defined formally; the formal K(m) partition comes later with different notation.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide a version of the upper bound that does not rely on Assumption 1, even if looser, to clarify robustness?
- How sensitive is MF-UCB to mis-specified ζ(m)? Can one adaptively learn bias envelopes or use arm-specific bounds?
- Can the fidelity-selection rule be modified to address K(m)7 cases (e.g., cost-aware early stopping at low fidelity) without requiring O(log n/ψ(∆(M))) high-fidelity samples?
- Please clarify Theorem 2’s displayed formula and provide constants leading to a clean comparison with UCB.

Pros
- Novel and timely problem formulation with clear motivation.
- Clean algorithmic idea with interpretable fidelity-switching.
- Nontrivial theory with both upper and lower bounds, and insightful arm partitioning.

Cons
- Many presentation and notation issues; several statements are hard to parse or potentially misleading.
- Experimental section insufficient to support claims.
- Strong assumptions (known ζ, Assumption 1) and limited lower bound generality.
- Unresolved optimality gap on K(m)7 and lack of practical guidance.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). The core idea is interesting and potentially impactful, but the paper, as written, has too many clarity and completeness issues (both theoretical presentation and experiments). With substantial revision—cleaning up the exposition and proofs, strengthening/clarifying assumptions and bounds, and providing rigorous empirical validation—this work could be a strong submission.

Reviewer confidence: High on bandits/UCB; medium on multi-fidelity literature breadth.
