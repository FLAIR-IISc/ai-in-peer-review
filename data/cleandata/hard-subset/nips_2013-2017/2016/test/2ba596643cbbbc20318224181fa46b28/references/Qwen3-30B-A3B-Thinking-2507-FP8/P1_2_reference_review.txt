

# Peer Review: "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

## Summary
This paper introduces a novel framework for multi-armed bandit problems where each arm has multiple fidelities (approximations) with different costs and varying levels of accuracy. The authors formalize the problem by introducing a cost-weighted regret notion that accounts for the different costs of playing at different fidelities. They propose a Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB) algorithm that adaptively uses lower-fidelity approximations to eliminate suboptimal arms and reserves high-fidelity evaluations for promising arms. The theoretical analysis shows that MF-UCB achieves regret bounds of O((λ(1)K + λ(2)|Kg|)log(Λ/λ(2))), which can significantly improve upon standard UCB's O(λ(2)K log(Λ/λ(2))) when |Kg| << K and λ(1) < λ(2). The paper also provides a lower bound showing MF-UCB is near-optimal.

This work builds on the classical multi-armed bandit literature (e.g., Auer et al., 2002; Bubeck and Cesa-Bianchi, 2012) and extends it to the multi-fidelity setting, which has been explored in optimization contexts (e.g., Huang et al., 2006; Kandasamy et al., 2016) but not formally analyzed for bandit regret.

## Strengths

1. **Strong Motivation and Problem Formulation**: The paper provides excellent real-world examples (online advertising, algorithm selection) that clearly illustrate the practical need for multi-fidelity bandits. The formalization of the problem with the ζ(m) assumptions and cost structure is well-motivated and carefully considered.

2. **Theoretical Contribution**: The regret bound for MF-UCB is a significant theoretical contribution that clearly demonstrates the advantage of using lower-fidelity approximations. The analysis carefully handles the interplay between fidelities and shows that MF-UCB is near-optimal (matching the lower bound in many cases).

3. **Algorithmic Insight**: The MF-UCB algorithm is a natural and elegant extension of UCB to the multi-fidelity setting, with a clear rationale for the fidelity-switching mechanism. The design of γ(m) is particularly well-justified.

4. **Empirical Validation**: The synthetic experiments convincingly demonstrate MF-UCB's practical advantages over standard UCB, with smaller regret gradients and more efficient use of fidelity resources.

## Weaknesses

1. **Assumption Restrictiveness**: Assumption 1 (ζ(m) decay fast enough) is necessary for the most appealing regret comparison with UCB, but it may not hold in all practical applications. The paper acknowledges this but doesn't explore how violations might impact performance.

2. **Lower Bound Gap**: The lower bound analysis reveals a gap for arms in K(m)7 (where some lower fidelity suggests an arm is optimal but higher fidelity does not). While the paper explains why MF-UCB doesn't meet the lower bound for these arms, it doesn't suggest a way to close this gap.

3. **Limited Related Work Comparison**: The paper could better distinguish itself from budgeted bandits (e.g., Tran-Thanh et al., 2012) and other multi-fidelity optimization approaches. The distinction between "fidelity" (multiple approximations per arm) versus "cost" (per-arm cost structure) needs clearer articulation.

4. **Practical Implementation Considerations**: The paper doesn't address how to handle cases where lower fidelity approximations might be misleading in ways not captured by the ζ(m) assumptions, which could be critical in real applications.

## Evaluation

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis and well-designed experiments. The claims are well-supported by both theory and experiments. The authors are careful to discuss the limitations of their approach, including the gap between their upper and lower bounds. (Rating: Excellent)

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and well-organized. The problem is clearly defined, the algorithm is explained in detail, and the theoretical results are presented logically. The figures (e.g., Fig. 1) effectively illustrate the key concepts. The notation is consistent and well-defined. (Rating: Excellent)

**Originality**: The multi-fidelity bandit problem is a natural extension of the classical bandit problem, but the paper makes a significant contribution by formalizing the problem, developing a principled algorithm, and providing a tight theoretical analysis. The combination of UCB with multi-fidelity considerations is novel and well-motivated. (Rating: High)

**Significance**: The problem has clear practical importance in resource-constrained optimization settings across multiple domains (advertising, algorithm selection, robotics). The theoretical and empirical results demonstrate that multi-fidelity approaches can substantially reduce regret in these settings, making this work significant for both theory and practice. (Rating: High)

## Recommendation

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the multi-armed bandit literature. The multi-fidelity framework is well-motivated, the algorithm is elegant and well-analyzed, and the empirical results support the theoretical claims. While there are some limitations (particularly regarding Assumption 1 and the lower bound gap), these don't undermine the paper's overall contribution.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should consider:
1. Expanding the related work section to more clearly distinguish from budgeted bandits and other multi-fidelity optimization approaches
2. Discussing potential impacts of violating Assumption 1
3. Providing more detail on the practical implications of the K(m)7 gap

The paper advances the state of the art in bandit theory and provides a principled approach to incorporating cheaper approximations in resource-constrained settings, making it highly relevant to the NIPS community.

## Final Assessment

This is an excellent paper that meets all the criteria for NIPS acceptance. It's technically sound, clearly written, original, and significant. The work represents a meaningful advance in understanding bandit problems with multiple fidelity evaluations. I believe it would be a valuable contribution to the NIPS proceedings.
