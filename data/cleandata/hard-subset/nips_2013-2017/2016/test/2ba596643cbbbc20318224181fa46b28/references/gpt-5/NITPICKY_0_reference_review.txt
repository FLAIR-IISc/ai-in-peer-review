Summary
The paper introduces a stochastic K-armed bandit setting where each arm can be queried at multiple fidelities with increasing cost and decreasing bias relative to the highest-fidelity reward. The authors define a cost-weighted pseudo-regret for a fixed capital Λ, propose MF-UCB, an adaptation of UCB that maintains upper bounds across fidelities by inflating with the known fidelity bias ζ(m), and selects the cheapest fidelity for which the statistical error is above a threshold γ(m) tuned by the cost ratio λ(m)/λ(m+1). They prove an upper bound on the expected pseudo-regret that reflects that most capital is spent on promising arms at high fidelity while eliminating clearly suboptimal arms at low fidelities, and a lower bound (for Bernoulli rewards) showing near-optimality except for a specific subset of arms. Simulations on synthetic problems indicate improved performance over naive UCB.

Relation to prior work
The setting differs from classical budgeted bandits by modeling each arm with multiple biased approximations rather than random per-play costs. The approach is related to UCB variants for bandits, to multi-fidelity optimization in engineering, and to weak/strong labeler active learning, but those do not address exploration–exploitation with biased proxies. The paper also connects to the authors’ GP-based multi-fidelity work.

Strengths
- Problem formulation is interesting and practically motivated (expensive experiments with cheaper biased proxies).
- MF-UCB is simple and natural: min over fidelity-specific upper bounds by adding ζ(m) to the confidence radius, and a principled fidelity-switching rule based on γ(m).
- Upper bound captures a favorable tradeoff: cheap eliminations at low fidelity, reserve expensive queries for near-optimal arms.
- Lower bound provides perspective on when MF-UCB is tight and explains a gap case.
- The regret decomposition for capital-constrained settings is clear, and the decomposition into residual r̃ and main term R̃ is appropriate.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical correctness and clarity
- Inconsistent and likely incorrect definition of the partition sets K(m). You define J(m)η = {k: µ? − µ(m)k ≤ η}. Then K(1) = J(1)ζ(1)+2γ(1) is stated to equal {k: ∆(1)k > 2γ(1)}. This is contradictory: J(1)ζ(1)+2γ(1) corresponds to µ? − µ(1)k ≤ ζ(1)+2γ(1) i.e., ∆(1)k ≤ 2γ(1), not > 2γ(1). The subsequent property “for all k ∈ K(m), ∆(m)k > 2γ(m)” is not guaranteed by the recursive definition as written. This affects key lemmas and the interpretation of which fidelities are used for which arms.
- Theorem 2 is ambiguously stated. The displayed bound appears to include two separate sums; it is unclear whether they are additive or whether one is an alternative form. Please restate cleanly with all constants and quantifiers.
- Lemma 5 statement has indexing inconsistencies: T(`)k,n . log n / ψ(γ(m)) is written with ` < JkK but uses m; likely a typo but it obscures the logic.
- The high-probability control of N relative to nΛ depends on δ chosen as a function of Λ; the dependence and the conditioning steps are hand-wavy. A rigorous self-contained bound on E[log N] without Λ-dependent δ would strengthen the result.
- Assumption 3 for the lower bound (expected number of pulls of any suboptimal arm is o(n^a) for all a>0) is very strong and excludes many natural algorithms; it should be discussed and ideally relaxed or justified via a standard information-theoretic argument.
- Lower bound is restricted to Bernoulli rewards. The upper bound covers general ψ; the mismatch should be acknowledged more explicitly.
- The regret definition uses qt = µ(M)It, an unobservable counterfactual. While pseudo-regret is standard, a brief discussion of whether high-probability bounds for realized regret are obtainable would be helpful.

Modeling assumptions
- The algorithm assumes known bias bounds ζ(m) for all fidelities, and the analysis critically uses Assumption 1 (fast decay of ζ(m)). In many applications these bounds are unknown and can be arm-dependent; the paper should discuss robustness or estimation of ζ(m).
- The regularity Assumption 1 is quite restrictive; its necessity and practical plausibility deserve a more thorough discussion, and theorems should delineate how results degrade without it.

Algorithmic details
- The choice γ(m) = ψ^{-1}(λ(m)/λ(m+1) ψ(ζ(m))) is motivated qualitatively but not derived from an explicit optimization. A more formal derivation or a sensitivity analysis would help.
- ρ > 4 requirement could potentially be improved; authors claim ρ > 2 is attainable with changes—this should be precisely stated or omitted.

Empirical evaluation
- The experimental section is too thin for NIPS standards: only synthetic data, no details in the main text (appendix-only), no ablations (e.g., misspecified ζ, varying λ ratios, effect of M), no baselines beyond UCB (e.g., Thompson Sampling, Successive Rejects/Elimination variants adapted to multi-fidelity), and no real or semi-realistic tasks. Plots are referenced but not reproducible from the main text.

Presentation, notation, and references
- Numerous formatting/typo issues that hinder readability:
  - Spurious “th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016), Barcelona, Spain.” inserted mid-paragraph.
  - Figure caption text appears in the main body (“ζ(m)+2γ(m) are indicated...”).
  - Unconventional use of symbols “.,.,&” to denote inequalities “ignoring constants.”
  - Step 2 of Algorithm 1 uses “∨ m = M” and informal set notation; please standardize.
  - Several undefined or overloaded symbols and inconsistent indexing.
- References are incomplete and inconsistent (missing years, venues, pages; misspellings such as “Schenider”). Please fix to NIPS style and ensure comprehensive coverage.

Pros for acceptance
- Interesting and timely problem formulation with clear practical motivation.
- Conceptually simple algorithm with an intuitive fidelity selection rule.
- Nontrivial analysis that, modulo issues above, captures the main effect of multi-fidelity elimination.
- Lower bound insightfully identifies a gap case.

Cons for acceptance
- Technical inconsistencies in key definitions and theorem statements.
- Heavy reliance on strong and partially unjustified assumptions (known ζ, Assumption 1, Assumption 3).
- Empirical section is insufficient and lacks detail.
- Numerous clarity/formatting issues that make the paper hard to follow and reproduce.

Questions for authors
1. Please correct and restate the definitions of J(m)η and K(m), and show precisely that your recursive partition ensures the intended gap properties.
2. Can you provide a version of Theorem 2 with all constants and a clear single expression?
3. How sensitive is MF-UCB to misspecified ζ(m)? Can ζ(m) be learned online?
4. Can the lower bound be extended beyond Bernoulli? Can Assumption 3 be weakened?
5. Could you compare to more baselines and include ablations, and move key experimental details to the main text?
