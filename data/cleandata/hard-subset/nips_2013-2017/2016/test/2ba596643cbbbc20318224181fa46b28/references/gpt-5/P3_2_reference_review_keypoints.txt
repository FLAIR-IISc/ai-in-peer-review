- Problem: K-armed stochastic bandits with M fidelities where lower fidelities are cheaper but biased, with known fidelity-dependent bias bounds ζ(m) and costs λ(m) under budget Λ.  
- Objective: minimize cost-weighted pseudo-regret evaluated at highest fidelity µ(M), benchmarked against an oracle playing optimal arm at any fidelity.  
- Algorithm (MF-UCB): maintain per-fidelity empirical means + confidence bonuses + bias offsets ζ(m); pick arm with smallest cross-fidelity UCB and the lowest fidelity whose uncertainty exceeds a cost-aware threshold γ(m).  
- Theoretical upper bound: MF-UCB spends most capital on promising arms at high fidelity and uses low-fidelity plays to eliminate clearly suboptimal arms, improving over single-fidelity UCB by factors tied to λ(m)/λ(M).  
- Lower bound: matching for a broad class of arms, with a residual gap for arms whose lower-fidelity gaps are small but positive.  
- Analysis technique: clean partitioning of arms into K(m) layers to capture where exploration should occur; proofs are reasonable given cross-fidelity complexity.  
- Experiments: synthetic benchmarks show qualitative behavior and improved regret over naïve high-fidelity UCB.  
- Relation to prior work: distinct from classical UCB and budgeted bandits; related to multi-fidelity BO/RL and weak/strong labelers; missing discussion of bandits with knapsacks and side-observation models.  
- Strength: novel, well-motivated formalization with a principled cost-aware fidelity escalation rule and strong theoretical guarantees.  
- Strength: clear algorithmic idea and insightful K(m) partitioning that informs exploration strategy.  
- Weakness: requires known global ζ(m) and ψ; no empirical or theoretical robustness to misspecification or arm-dependent biases.  
- Weakness: Assumption 1 (fast decay of ζ relative to ψ) may be restrictive and its necessity/prevalence is not fully explored.  
- Weakness: regret evaluated at µ(M) (even for low-fidelity plays) is somewhat nonstandard and alternatives are not analyzed.  
- Weakness: lower bound relies on Assumption 3 (subpolynomial pulls of suboptimal arms), whose necessity for near-optimal algorithms is not discussed.  
- Weakness: limited empirical evaluation—only synthetic tasks and a single baseline; missing comparisons to two-phase elimination, BwK heuristics, and Thompson sampling variants; no sensitivity analyses.  
- Clarity issues: fidelity escalation condition could be more intuitive, some inequalities drop constants, and a constants table would aid reproducibility.  
- Quality: technically sound development, well-motivated γ(m), and appropriately adapted change-of-measure lower bound; acknowledged unresolved gap is honestly discussed.  
- Originality: novel problem formulation and regret notion for biased multi-fidelity feedback; original near-optimality characterization.  
- Significance: promising direction for practical settings with cheap biased proxies; theoretical insights likely influential but practical adoption needs stronger empirical/robustness validation.  
- Suggestions: analyze/experiment with misspecified or arm-dependent ζ, compare to additional baselines, probe sensitivity to λ ratios and M, clarify practical choices for ρ, and consider adaptive ψ estimation.  
- Pros for acceptance: novel setting and algorithm, strong theoretical results, clear improvement over standard UCB, and reproducible conceptual framework.  
- Cons for acceptance: reliance on known ζ and ψ, limited empirical validation, and restrictive Assumption 1.  
- Recommendation: Accept.
