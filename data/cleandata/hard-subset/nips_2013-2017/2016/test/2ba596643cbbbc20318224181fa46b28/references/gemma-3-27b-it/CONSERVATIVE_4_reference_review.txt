This paper introduces a multi-fidelity bandit framework, motivated by scenarios where evaluating arms at higher fidelity is costly, and cheaper approximations are available. The authors propose MF-UCB, an algorithm adapting the UCB approach, and provide a theoretical analysis demonstrating improved regret bounds compared to standard UCB in specific settings. This builds upon the extensive literature on bandit algorithms (e.g., Auer et al., 2002; Bubeck & Cesa-Bianchi, 2012) and extends it to a more practical, though complex, setup. Related work in multi-fidelity optimization (e.g., Huang et al., 2006) and active learning (e.g., Zhang & Chaudhuri, 2016) is acknowledged, but the authors appropriately highlight the novelty of their regret-based analysis within the bandit framework.

The core idea – leveraging cheaper, albeit potentially biased, evaluations to narrow down the set of promising arms – is intuitively sound. The theoretical analysis appears rigorous, providing a formal justification for the expected benefits of MF-UCB. The regret bound, O((λ(1)K + λ(2)|Kg|) log(Λ/λ(2))), is clearly stated and compared to the baseline UCB regret. The assumptions regarding the fidelity relationships (ζ(m)) and the cost function (λ(m)) are reasonable, although their impact on the performance gains could be further explored. The simulation results offer preliminary evidence supporting the algorithm's effectiveness.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. Firstly, the simulations are somewhat limited in scope. While they demonstrate an improvement over naive UCB, they do not explore a wide range of parameter settings or problem instances. More extensive experimentation, particularly with varying cost ratios (λ(1)/λ(2)), fidelity accuracies (ζ(m)), and the number of arms (K), is necessary to establish the robustness of MF-UCB. Secondly, the assumption that the lower fidelities provide *some* information about the highest fidelity, formalized by |µ(M)k − µ (m) k | ≤ ζ(m), feels relatively weak. The potential for misleading lower fidelities (discussed briefly) is acknowledged, but its practical implications are not thoroughly investigated. A scenario where a low-fidelity evaluation consistently overestimates the reward of a poor arm could significantly degrade performance, and the algorithm’s resilience to such biases requires further examination.  Thirdly, the constant factors hidden within the O() notation are not discussed. While the asymptotic behavior is important, practical performance can be heavily influenced by these constants. Fourthly, the paper relies heavily on theoretical results. A more thorough empirical validation, perhaps on a real-world application, would significantly strengthen the claims. Finally, the introduction of γ(m) feels somewhat *ad hoc*, and while a rationale is provided, its derivation could benefit from more detailed explanation and justification.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novelty: The paper presents a new framework and algorithm for multi-fidelity bandit problems.
*   Theoretical Soundness: The analysis appears rigorous and provides a formal justification for the algorithm's performance.
*   Potential Significance: The framework has the potential to be applicable to a range of real-world problems where high-fidelity evaluations are costly.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited Empirical Validation: The simulations are not comprehensive enough to convincingly demonstrate the algorithm's robustness.
*   Weakness of Assumptions: The assumption regarding the relationship between fidelities may be too lenient, potentially leading to performance degradation in certain scenarios.
*   Lack of Practical Demonstration: The absence of a real-world application limits the assessment of practical significance.
*   Ad-hoc parameter selection: The choice of γ(m) feels insufficiently motivated.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution to the field of bandit algorithms. However, the limited empirical validation and the reliance on potentially weak assumptions raise concerns about its practical applicability. While the paper demonstrates a solid understanding of the theoretical foundations, it falls short of providing compelling evidence that MF-UCB will consistently outperform existing methods in real-world scenarios.

**Recommendation:**

Reject. The paper has merit, and the ideas are promising. However, the authors need to significantly strengthen the empirical validation and address the concerns regarding the assumptions before it would be suitable for publication at NIPS. I recommend the authors undertake more extensive simulations, explore the algorithm’s robustness to noisy or misleading low-fidelity evaluations, and consider applying it to a real-world problem. A more detailed explanation of the parameter selection (γ(m)) would also be beneficial.
