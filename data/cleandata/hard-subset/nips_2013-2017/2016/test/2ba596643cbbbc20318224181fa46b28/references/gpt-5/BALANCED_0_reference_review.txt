Summary and relation to prior work
The paper introduces a multi-fidelity variant of the stochastic K-armed bandit where, for each arm, one can query multiple fidelities m=1,…,M that are increasingly expensive (costs λ(m)) and increasingly accurate approximations to the true reward distribution θ(M). Lower fidelities are biased but come with known bias bounds ζ(m) decreasing in m. The authors propose a cost-weighted notion of pseudo-regret for a fixed capital Λ: the oracle plays an optimal arm at any fidelity all the time, and the learner’s reward is measured w.r.t. the highest-fidelity mean regardless of the fidelity used. They present MF-UCB, which builds upper confidence bounds for each fidelity augmented with ζ(m), takes the tightest across fidelities to select an arm, and then chooses the cheapest fidelity whose current uncertainty is above a fidelity-specific threshold γ(m) calibrated by λ(m), λ(m+1), and ζ(m). Intuitively, lower fidelities are used to screen out clearly suboptimal arms; expensive queries are reserved for near-optimal arms.

Theoretical results include a high-level regret decomposition, a regret bound showing MF-UCB can replace λ(M) by λ(JkK) for many arms k (where JkK is the fidelity at which k is eliminated), and a lower bound (for Bernoulli rewards under a mild subpolynomial-pulls assumption) showing near-optimality on a large subset of arms. The paper positions itself relative to classical UCB [Auer 2002; Agrawal 1995], budgeted bandits [Tran-Thanh et al. 2010; Xia et al. 2015], multi-fidelity optimization and simulators [Huang et al. 2006; Cutler et al. 2014], and weak/strong labelers in active learning [Zhang & Chaudhuri 2015]. Unlike budgeted bandits, the objective is not reward/cost but the true reward measured at the highest fidelity. Compared to multifidelity optimization, this work introduces and analyzes a regret notion and exploration–exploitation algorithm for the bandit setting. The paper is complementary to Gaussian process multi-fidelity bandits [Kandasamy et al. 2016], focusing here on the finite-armed stochastic setting.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated formalism for multi-fidelity bandits with biased lower fidelities and costs.
- Simple, principled algorithm (MF-UCB) that adapts UCB by combining across fidelities and selecting fidelity via a cost/uncertainty threshold.
- Theoretical contributions: regret upper bound showing improvement over single-fidelity UCB when many arms can be discarded cheaply; lower bound establishing near-optimality on a substantial subset of cases and identifying a precise gap.
- Good intuition: partition of arms into sets K(m) that are eliminated at fidelity m; transparent comparison to UCB.
- Empirical simulations align with the theory (lower slope, fewer expensive pulls).

Weaknesses
- Strong knowledge assumptions: the bias bounds ζ(m) must be known and tight; performance and safety under mis-specification are not analyzed.
- Lower bound restricted to Bernoulli rewards and requires Assumption 3 (subpolynomial pulls), which is reasonable but not fully discussed.
- Assumption 1 (regularity on ζ decay via ψ) is introduced to make the comparison attractive; the extent to which results degrade when it fails is not quantified.
- Fidelity-switching criterion leaves a provable suboptimality gap for arms in K(m)7; no adaptive remedy is proposed.
- Experiments are limited to small synthetic cases; no real data or stronger baselines (e.g., Thompson sampling variants, naive staged-screening heuristics, elimination algorithms) are included; limited sensitivity analysis to λ, ζ, and ρ.
- Clarity issues: some notational density; occasional figure/text glitches; simulation details deferred.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: The analysis appears technically sound, with careful construction of UCBs, elimination thresholds, and high-probability control of pulls at each fidelity. The regret improvements are well-justified; the lower bound is meaningful though limited to Bernoulli and an assumption class. The authors are candid about the gap for K(m)7 and why naive fixes are undesirable.
- Clarity: Generally well-written with a clear algorithmic description and intuition. However, notation is heavy, and some statements (e.g., threshold selection and dependence on ψ, constants, and Λ0) could be clarified. Simulation setup details are too sparse in the main text.
- Originality: High. The problem formulation and regret analysis for multi-fidelity K-armed bandits with biased approximations seem novel at the time, distinct from budgeted bandits and weak/strong labeler work.
- Significance: Potentially impactful in domains with abundant low-cost approximations (ads, simulation-based evaluation, AutoML). The improvement factor—replacing λ(M) by λ(JkK) for many arms—can be substantial for large K. Practical impact depends on how one obtains or learns ζ(m).

Pros
- Novel, well-motivated problem and regret definition.
- Elegant algorithm with clear intuition.
- Solid theoretical bounds; near-optimal on a broad subset.
- Empirical evidence consistent with theory.

Cons
- Requires known fidelity bias bounds; robustness to mis-specification unaddressed.
- Lower bound scope limited; gap for K(m)7 unresolved.
- Experimental evaluation limited; missing stronger baselines and real-world tests.
- Some reliance on technical assumptions (Assumption 1, Λ > Λ0, ρ > 4) with limited discussion of their practical implications.

Suggestions for improvement
- Analyze robustness to mis-specified ζ(m) and propose ways to learn or adaptively calibrate ζ online; consider confidence sequences for cross-fidelity bias.
- Provide sensitivity analyses to λ, ζ, ρ; include ablations on γ(m) and comparisons to simpler screening heuristics and Thompson sampling adaptations with biased proxies.
- Extend lower bounds beyond Bernoulli to general sub-Gaussian classes; characterize constants in the upper bounds more explicitly.
- Investigate adaptive switching that narrows the K(m)7 gap without incurring many high-fidelity pulls (e.g., occasional low-cost tests to certify ∆(`)k > 0).
- Include at least one real or semi-synthetic application (e.g., ad scheduling proxy horizons, ML model evaluation with subsampled datasets).
- Clarify assumptions about independence across fidelities and discuss correlated/noisy proxies; explain when Assumption 1 holds in practice.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers a clear and original formulation of multi-fidelity bandits, a simple and effective algorithm, and meaningful theoretical guarantees with insightful comparisons to UCB. While the evaluation is limited and some assumptions are strong, the contribution is substantial and likely to stimulate follow-up work on more robust and practical variants.
