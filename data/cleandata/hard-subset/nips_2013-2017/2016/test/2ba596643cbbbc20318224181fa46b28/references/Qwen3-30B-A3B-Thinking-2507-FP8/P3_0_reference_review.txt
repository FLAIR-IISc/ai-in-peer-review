

# Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

This paper introduces a novel multi-fidelity extension to the classical multi-armed bandit problem, where each arm has multiple approximations (fidelities) with different costs and accuracies. The authors propose MF-UCB, a natural extension of the UCB algorithm that uses cheaper lower-fidelity evaluations to eliminate suboptimal arms and reserves expensive high-fidelity evaluations for promising arms. The key theoretical contribution is a regret bound of O((λ(1)K + λ(2)|Kg|) log(Λ/λ(2))), where Kg is a small subset of arms with high expected reward, which can be significantly better than standard UCB's O(λ(2)K log(Λ/λ(2))) when λ(1) << λ(2) and |Kg| << K.

**Strengths:**
The paper makes a clear and important contribution to bandit literature by formalizing a practically relevant setting that has not been systematically studied before. The problem is well-motivated by compelling real-world applications like online advertising (where shorter ad displays provide cheaper approximations of click-through rates) and algorithm selection (where cheaper model versions provide approximate performance estimates). The MF-UCB algorithm is a natural, well-justified extension of UCB, with a clear intuition behind its design. The theoretical analysis is thorough, providing both an upper bound and a matching lower bound that demonstrates near-optimality of the approach. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and effectively bridges theoretical analysis with practical intuition.

**Weaknesses:**
The most significant limitation is Assumption 1 (fast decay of ζ(m)), which is required for the strong regret comparison with UCB but is restrictive and not well-motivated. The authors acknowledge it's not necessary for the algorithm's analysis but is needed for the comparison. It's unclear how often this assumption holds in practice, and the paper doesn't provide empirical evidence on this point. The choice of γ(m) = ψ^{-1}(λ(m)/λ(m+1) ψ(ζ(m))) is theoretically justified but may be difficult to implement in practice without knowing the true ζ(m) values. The empirical evaluation is limited to synthetic problems with no discussion of practical implementation challenges. The paper also doesn't consider adaptive fidelity selection (where fidelity could change over time based on new information), which could be a significant limitation in practice.

**Comparisons to Related Work:**
The paper adequately discusses related work, including multi-fidelity optimization methods, budgeted bandits, and previous bandit algorithms. However, it could better distinguish itself from recent work on multi-fidelity Gaussian process bandits by Kandasamy et al. (2016), which is referenced but not contrasted in detail. The paper's focus on a general multi-fidelity framework rather than specific Gaussian process assumptions is a strength, but the relationship to this recent work could be clarified.

**Overall Assessment:**
This is a strong theoretical contribution to bandit literature with clear practical relevance. The problem formulation, algorithm design, and theoretical analysis are all sound and well-executed. While the assumptions are somewhat restrictive, the paper acknowledges these limitations and provides clear conditions under which the approach is beneficial. The empirical results support the theoretical claims, though more diverse experiments would strengthen the practical relevance.

**Recommendation:**
Accept. The paper makes a valuable contribution that advances the state of the art in bandit problems. While not perfect, the strengths (novel problem formulation, rigorous analysis, clear practical motivation) outweigh the weaknesses (restrictive assumption, limited empirical evaluation).

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**

**Pro:**
- Novel problem formulation with clear practical relevance to real-world applications
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with matching upper and lower bounds
- Natural algorithmic extension of UCB with strong theoretical guarantees
- Well-written and clearly organized, with effective use of figures to illustrate concepts

**Con:**
- Restrictive Assumption 1 that may limit practical applicability
- Limited empirical evaluation (only synthetic problems)
- Lack of discussion on practical implementation details
- No consideration of adaptive fidelity selection

**Specific Suggestions for Improvement:**
1. Discuss practical conditions under which Assumption 1 holds and provide examples from real applications.
2. Add a section on practical implementation details, including how to estimate ζ(m) and set γ(m) in practice.
3. Include more empirical results on more complex synthetic problems or real-world datasets.
4. Consider extending the algorithm to allow adaptive fidelity selection for a more comprehensive approach.

This paper represents a meaningful advancement in bandit literature with clear theoretical and practical implications. The proposed MF-UCB algorithm provides a principled approach to leveraging multiple fidelities in bandit problems, and the theoretical analysis thoroughly establishes its benefits when the conditions are met. While the assumptions could be relaxed, the paper provides a solid foundation for future work in this important area.
