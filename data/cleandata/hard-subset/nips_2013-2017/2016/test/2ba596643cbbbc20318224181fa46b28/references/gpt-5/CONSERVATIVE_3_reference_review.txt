Summary
The paper introduces a stochastic multi-armed bandit setting with multiple fidelities per arm, where lower fidelities are cheaper but biased approximations of the highest-fidelity reward. The authors define a cost-weighted pseudo-regret over a total capital budget Λ, propose MF-UCB—an extension of UCB that maintains upper bounds across fidelities with bias corrections—and select the fidelity adaptively via thresholds γ(m) depending on costs and known bias bounds ζ(m). They provide regret upper bounds showing that MF-UCB concentrates expensive plays on a small set of promising arms and uses cheap fidelities to eliminate clearly suboptimal arms. They also derive a lower bound (under Bernoulli rewards and an assumption on subpolynomial pulls of suboptimal arms) and argue near-optimality, with a gap on a specific subset of instances. Experiments on synthetic problems show MF-UCB outperforming naïve UCB.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on classical UCB algorithms and cost/budgeted bandits, and is related to multi-fidelity ideas in optimization and reinforcement learning. The setting differs from budgeted bandits that optimize reward/cost ratios and from GP-based multi-fidelity BO by using stochastic MAB with known bias bounds. The related work section is mostly appropriate, though comparisons to best-arm identification with budgets and to elimination schemes that exploit cheaper proxies would strengthen context.

Strengths
- Problem formulation is natural and practically motivated; the idea of using cheaper biased evaluations to prune arms is compelling.
- MF-UCB is simple, computationally light, and anytime.
- Theoretical analysis is substantial: clear decomposition of regret, partitioning of arms by fidelity, finite-time upper bounds, and a nontrivial lower bound showing near-optimality on a broad subset of arms.
- The analysis clarifies when multi-fidelity helps (large K, small high-fidelity candidate set, and nontrivial cost gaps).
- Empirical results, though limited, match the theoretical intuition: lower fidelities screen out many arms, and overall regret decreases vs. UCB.

Weaknesses
- Strong modeling assumptions: the bias bounds ζ(m) are assumed known and monotonically decreasing, with an additional regularity condition (Assumption 1). In most applications (ads, algorithm selection), ζ(m) is unknown and potentially arm-dependent. No robustness or adaptive estimation of ζ(m) is provided.
- The regret notion is a “pseudo-regret” that rewards plays according to the highest-fidelity mean regardless of which fidelity is played. This departs from observed rewards and can misalign with many applications (the paper acknowledges alternatives but does not analyze them). The choice of oracle (always accruing µ? per unit cost at any fidelity) reduces the role of fidelity in the benchmark, which may limit practical relevance.
- Lower bound is restricted to Bernoulli rewards and relies on Assumption 3; the gap for K(m)7 instances is left open. This weakens the near-optimality claim.
- Empirics are very limited: synthetic only, single baseline (naïve UCB), no sensitivity to mis-specified ζ(m), no ablations on γ(m), and no evaluation on more realistic settings (arm-dependent costs, heavy tails, non-sub-Gaussian noise).
- Practical deployability is unclear: MF-UCB requires ψ and ζ(m) to set γ(m), and assumes fidelity costs are uniform across arms. There is no guidance for tuning under model misspecification.
- Some proofs and constants are deferred; the main text repeatedly “ignores constants,” which obscures practical regimes and constant factors that matter for real use.

Quality
The core theoretical contributions appear sound and technically interesting. However, the assumptions (known ζ, Assumption 1, Bernoulli in the lower bound) and the limited experiments weaken the empirical support and practical credibility. The mismatch between pseudo-reward and observed fidelity reward is concerning for applications.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The partitioning K(m), the role of γ(m), and the high-level proof ideas are conveyed clearly, though the reliance on appendices and “ignoring constants” makes it harder to assess practical impact. Some notation is heavy; a clearer discussion of the regret choice and its implications would help.

Originality
Formulating a multi-fidelity stochastic bandit with bias-bounded fidelities and cost-weighted regret, and adapting UCB with fidelity-aware thresholds, is a novel combination. The lower-bound analysis tailored to this setting is also original.

Significance
The work advances theoretical understanding of multi-fidelity exploration-exploitation and could inform algorithms in domains with proxy evaluations. That said, practical significance is dampened by strong assumptions and limited validation. Without robustness to unknown ζ(m) and broader empirical comparisons, it is difficult to recommend for a venue with high standards.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide algorithms that learn or adapt to unknown ζ(m) (e.g., data-driven calibration/validation or robust choices of γ(m)) and analyze performance under mis-specification.
- Consider arm-dependent ζk(m) and λk(m), or at least discuss how to extend the analysis.
- Evaluate on more realistic benchmarks (e.g., synthetic settings with mis-specified ζ, heavy-tailed noise; semi-synthetic ad-click data; algorithm selection/hyperparameter tuning proxies), and compare against additional baselines (elimination schemes using low-fidelity filters, Thompson sampling variants, budgeted bandits).
- Make constants explicit in the main theorems or provide interpretable corollaries for sub-Gaussian cases.
- Discuss alternative regret notions more concretely and, if possible, extend MF-UCB or provide partial analysis in one alternative setting.

Arguments for acceptance
- Well-motivated problem and clean algorithmic idea.
- Nontrivial theoretical contribution: regret upper bound and lower bound framework indicating near-optimality in many cases.
- Clear conceptual insight into how to leverage cheap proxies to reduce exploration cost.

Arguments against acceptance
- Strong and potentially unrealistic assumptions (known ζ, Assumption 1) with no robustness analysis.
- Regret definition may not match many applications; empirical validation is sparse and synthetic only.
- Lower bound limited in scope and leaves a meaningful gap open.
- Insufficient experimental baselines and practical guidance.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The theoretical development is interesting, but the paper lacks convincing empirical evidence and robustness to make a strong case for adoption, and key modeling assumptions limit applicability. With stronger experiments, robustness analysis, and clearer practical guidance, this line of work could be impactful.
