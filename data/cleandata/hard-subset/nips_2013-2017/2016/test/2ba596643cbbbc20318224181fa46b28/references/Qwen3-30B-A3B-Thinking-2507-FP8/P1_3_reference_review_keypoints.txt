- Introduces a multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit framework modeling per-arm fidelity levels with costs λ(m) and bounded errors ζ(m), and proposes MF-UCB to exploit cheap low-fidelity evaluations.  
- Theoretical regret analysis shows MF-UCB can outperform standard UCB when low fidelities are much cheaper and few arms remain promising, with a near-optimal matching lower bound.  
- Extends classic bandit work by incorporating information costs; relevant to applications like online advertising and algorithm selection, differing from budgeted bandits by modeling fidelity structure.  

- Strength: Clean formalism and tight regret analysis demonstrating clear theoretical advantages under reasonable cost/fidelity regimes.  
- Strength: Natural algorithm design (MF-UCB) that uses fidelity switching guided by confidence intervals and well-motivated γ(m) choices.  
- Strength: Empirical validation on synthetic experiments aligns with theory and effectively visualizes fidelity allocation.  
- Strength: Paper is well-organized and accessible with clear definitions and regret decomposition.  

- Weakness: Limited scope—analysis restricted to stochastic (non-contextual) bandits and not explored for contextual bandits or RL.  
- Weakness: Relies on a strong assumption about ζ(m) decay, which may be restrictive in real-world settings.  
- Weakness: Lower bound reveals a gap for some arms (K(m)7), with no clear resolution or indication if the gap is fundamental.  
- Weakness: Insufficient contrast with related Gaussian-process multi-fidelity work (Kandasamy et al.) and unclear relationship between approaches.  

- Quality: Technically sound with rigorous analysis and supportive experiments.  
- Clarity: Exceptionally well-written with consistent notation and helpful figures.  
- Originality: Novel formalization of multi-fidelity bandits with the first formal regret analysis in this setting.  
- Significance: Addresses practical problems where high-quality data is costly and shows tangible benefits when low-fidelity data is cheap.  

- Overall assessment: Strong theoretical contribution, well-executed and relevant; minor limitations do not outweigh contributions.  
- Recommendation: Accept.  
- For acceptance: Novel framework, solid theory, clear empirical support, and strong presentation.  
- Against acceptance: Limited application scope, restrictive fidelity assumption, and unresolved lower-bound gap.
