## Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a multi-fidelity extension to the classic multi-armed bandit (MAB) problem. The authors motivate this extension with practical applications where cheaper, approximate evaluations of arm rewards are available (e.g., online advertising, algorithm selection). They propose a Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB) algorithm, analyze its regret performance, and demonstrate improvements over naive UCB strategies in simulations. The key insight is to leverage lower-fidelity evaluations to efficiently eliminate suboptimal arms, reserving expensive high-fidelity evaluations for a promising subset.

**Relationship to Prior Work:**

The paper builds upon the well-established UCB framework for MABs (Auer, 2002).  It distinguishes itself from existing work on multi-fidelity optimization (Huang et al., 2017) by explicitly focusing on the exploration-exploitation trade-off inherent in the bandit setting and providing a regret analysis.  It also differentiates from budgeted bandit problems (Tran-Thanh et al., 2013) by focusing on *approximations* to rewards, rather than random costs. The authors appropriately cite relevant work in these areas, including recent work by Kandasamy et al. (2016) extending their ideas to multi-fidelity bandits with Gaussian processes.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The formalization of a multi-fidelity bandit problem is a valuable contribution. It opens up a new avenue for research within the MAB framework.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of the MF-UCB algorithm, deriving a regret bound and demonstrating its near-optimality. The theorem 2 regret bound is well presented and clearly contrasts to standard UCB performance.
* **Clear Motivation & Intuition:**  The introduction effectively motivates the problem with compelling examples and explains the intuition behind the MF-UCB algorithm.
* **Empirical Validation:** The simulations demonstrate that MF-UCB outperforms naive UCB, supporting the theoretical findings.
* **Well-structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written, making it easy to follow the authors' line of reasoning.
* **Lower Bound:** The included lower bound provides theoretical grounding for the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions:** The assumption that lower fidelities provide biased but informative estimates ( |µ(M)k − µ (m) k | ≤ ζ(m) ) is crucial but potentially restrictive. The impact of significant bias or noise in lower fidelities isn’t thoroughly explored. While the authors mention this, a deeper investigation of robustness to biased estimates would be beneficial.
* **Regularity Condition (Assumption 1):** The regularity condition on the ζ(m) values feels somewhat arbitrary. While the authors state it's not *necessary* for analysis, its impact on the performance difference between MF-UCB and UCB isn't fully articulated.
* **Lower Bound Gap:**  The authors acknowledge a gap between the theoretical lower bound and the performance of MF-UCB on certain arms (K(m)7 in the analysis).  While they provide plausible explanations, resolving this gap would strengthen the theoretical contribution.
* **Simulation Scope:** The simulations, while illustrative, are relatively simple. Exploring more complex scenarios with varying levels of fidelity cost, bias, and noise would provide further confidence in the algorithm's practical performance.
* **Discussion of qt:** The authors provide good motivation for setting qt = µ(M)It, however, a more comprehensive discussion about the impact of other choices for qt and their corresponding regret definitions would be useful.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a relevant and practically motivated problem.
* The theoretical analysis is sound and provides valuable insights into the performance of MF-UCB.
* The empirical results support the theoretical findings.
* The paper is clearly written and well-organized.
* The contribution is novel and advances the state of the art in multi-armed bandit research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The assumptions and regularity condition may limit the applicability of the framework.
* The gap in the lower bound needs further investigation.
* The simulations could be more comprehensive.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of multi-armed bandit research. The authors have successfully formalized a novel and practically relevant problem, developed a theoretically sound algorithm, and demonstrated its effectiveness through simulations. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh them.

**Rating:** **Strong Accept**

**Recommendations:**

*  The authors should consider discussing the sensitivity of their results to violations of the assumption on ζ(m) and potential methods for handling more significant biases in lower-fidelity estimates.
*  Further investigation into closing the gap between the lower bound and the theoretical performance of MF-UCB, particularly for arms in K(m)7, would be valuable.
*  Expanding the simulations to include more complex scenarios and a wider range of parameter settings would strengthen the empirical validation.
