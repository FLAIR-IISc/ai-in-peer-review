

# Peer Review: The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit

## Summary and Context

This paper introduces a novel framework for multi-armed bandit problems where each arm has multiple "fidelities" (approximate evaluations at different costs and accuracies). The authors formalize a cost-weighted regret notion and propose the MF-UCB algorithm that strategically uses cheaper low-fidelity approximations to eliminate suboptimal arms, reserving expensive high-fidelity evaluations for promising arms. The paper builds on classic UCB work [3] but extends it to the multi-fidelity setting, which has practical relevance in applications like online advertising (where shorter ad displays provide cheaper but biased estimates) and algorithm selection (where cheaper approximations of algorithm performance are available).

The paper's main theoretical contribution is a regret bound showing MF-UCB achieves O((λ(1)K + λ(2)|Kg|)log(Λ/λ(2))) regret, which improves upon standard UCB's O(λ(2)K log(Λ/λ(2))) regret when |Kg| < K and λ(1) < λ(2). The paper also provides a lower bound demonstrating near-optimality.

## Strengths

1. **Problem Relevance**: The multi-fidelity setting is highly relevant to practical bandit applications where expensive evaluations are constrained. The motivation examples (online advertising, algorithm selection) are compelling and well-explained.

2. **Theoretical Contribution**: The regret bound (Theorem 2) is strong, clearly showing the theoretical advantage of MF-UCB over standard UCB. The analysis is technically sound, with careful handling of the multi-fidelity structure.

3. **Algorithm Design**: MF-UCB elegantly extends the UCB framework by incorporating fidelity-specific exploration. The choice of γ(m) (equation 4) is well-justified as the optimal trade-off between exploration and exploitation.

4. **Empirical Validation**: The synthetic experiments in Section 6 convincingly demonstrate MF-UCB's superiority over UCB across multiple problem settings, with the regret curves showing both lower magnitude and slower growth.

## Weaknesses

1. **Overly Strong Assumption 1**: The paper relies heavily on Assumption 1 (ζ(m) decays fast enough) to make the regret comparison meaningful. This assumption is necessary for the main theoretical claim but may not hold in many practical scenarios where fidelity biases decay slowly. The paper doesn't discuss how to handle cases where ζ(m) is unknown or varies over time.

2. **Lower Bound Gap**: The lower bound (Theorem 4) reveals a gap between MF-UCB's performance and the theoretical lower bound for arms in K(m)7. While the authors acknowledge this, they don't propose a solution or discuss how significant this gap is in practice. This is a notable limitation.

3. **Limited Related Work Comparison**: The related work section doesn't adequately engage with recent multi-fidelity optimization literature (e.g., Huang et al. [7], Cutler et al. [6]) that addresses similar problems in different contexts. The connection to Kandasamy et al. [8] (which appears to be a follow-up) is mentioned but not thoroughly analyzed.

4. **Practical Implementation Gaps**: The paper doesn't address how to handle cases where ζ(m) is unknown (which is likely the common case in practice) or how to adapt to changing fidelity biases over time. This limits the paper's immediate applicability.

5. **Computational Complexity**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational complexity of MF-UCB, which could become significant for large K or many fidelities.

## Overall Assessment

This is a technically strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the bandit literature by extending it to the multi-fidelity setting. The problem is well-motivated, the algorithm is elegant, and the theoretical analysis is sound. The empirical results support the theoretical claims.

The main limitation is the reliance on Assumption 1, which may restrict the method's practical applicability. The gap between the lower bound and MF-UCB's performance for certain arms (K(m)7) is also a notable weakness. However, these are common challenges in bandit theory, and the paper provides a solid foundation that could be extended in future work.

The paper makes a clear contribution to the literature, demonstrating a meaningful improvement over standard bandit algorithms when multiple fidelities are available. While the theoretical results depend on certain assumptions, the framework itself is valuable and could inspire further work on multi-fidelity bandits.

## Recommendation

**Accept**. This is a strong theoretical contribution with clear practical relevance. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and provides both theoretical and empirical evidence for its claims. While the assumptions have limitations, they are reasonable for the theoretical analysis, and the paper clearly identifies where the theory might not translate directly to practice. The work advances the state of the art in bandit algorithms and would be of significant interest to the NIPS community.

The paper would be strengthened by addressing the ζ(m) unknown case in future work, but this doesn't detract from the current contribution. The theoretical results are sufficiently novel and significant to warrant publication at NIPS.
