Summary
The paper introduces a multi-fidelity K-armed bandit formulation where each arm has M fidelities: lower fidelities are cheaper but biased approximations to the highest-fidelity (true) reward. Costs λ(m) increase with fidelity, and biases are bounded by known ζ(m) with ζ decreasing to zero. The authors define a capital-budgeted, cost-weighted pseudo-regret R(Λ) that rewards choices according to the highest-fidelity mean µ(M) regardless of the fidelity sampled. They propose MF-UCB: per-arm, per-fidelity UCBs on µ(M) via X(m) + uncertainty + ζ(m) and select the minimum across fidelities; a fidelity-selection rule samples the lowest fidelity whose uncertainty exceeds a threshold γ(m) = ψ−1(λ(m)/λ(m+1)·ψ(ζ(m))). They prove an upper bound showing that arms “eliminable” at low fidelity incur exploration cost primarily at that fidelity; only near-optimal arms are probed at the highest fidelity. A lower bound (for Bernoulli rewards) shows MF-UCB is near-optimal on a subset of arms, with a gap identified and discussed. Synthetic experiments indicate empirical gains over standard UCB.

Strengths
- Formulation: Clear articulation of a multi-fidelity bandit model with biased, cheaper proxies and explicit costs; the ζ(m) bias bounds make the role of lower fidelities precise.
- Algorithmic idea: Neat blend of UCB with fidelity-aware confidence bounds (adding ζ(m)) and a principled fidelity-selection threshold balancing bias vs cost.
- Theory: Upper bound in general ψ-concentration setting; lower bound (for Bernoulli) that isolates where MF-UCB is tight and where it is potentially suboptimal; useful partitioning of arms K(m) to structure the analysis.
- Insight: Convincing qualitative message—use cheap fidelities to prune heavily suboptimal arms, reserve expensive budget for promising ones.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Quality/technical correctness
- Several equations/statements appear incorrect or internally inconsistent:
  - Definition of K(1) as J(1)ζ(1)+2γ(1) conflicts with the alternative characterization {k: Δ(1)k > 2γ(1)}; J(m)η was defined as µ* − µ(m)k ≤ η, so these are complements. This needs correction and consistent complement notation.
  - Theorem 4 and related expressions omit squares where ψ(·) ~ ·^2 (Bernoulli). The bounds should scale like λ/Δ^2, but the statement uses λ/Δ; later text acknowledges λ log(n)/Δ^2. Please fix the exponents throughout.
  - Theorem 2’s displayed bound mixes two sums that look redundant/double-counting; it is not clear whether the first term is meant to dominate the lower-fidelity exploration sum or vice versa. As written, it is hard to parse what exactly is being bounded and how terms interact.
  - Lemma statements are “informal” in main text, with big-Oh/big-Theta hand-waving and unexplained use of “., &” to denote inequalities “ignoring constants.” Given the centrality of these lemmas, at least precise versions and main proof ideas should be in the main text.
  - Use of δ depending on Λ in the control of E[log N] is acknowledged as delicate; the main text should give a clear statement/lemma establishing E[log N] ≤ c log(Λ/λ(M)) under the algorithm’s sampling distribution.
- Assumptions:
  - Assumption 1 (fast decay of ζ) is strong and somewhat ad hoc; the paper claims it is unnecessary to analyze MF-UCB but needed to make comparisons appealing. This undercuts generality. Provide results without this assumption or clearly delimit the regimes where the gains are provable.
  - The algorithm and guarantees assume known ζ(m), λ(m), and ψ. Robustness to mis-specified ζ(m) (or unknown ψ) is not analyzed; this is a key practical limitation.
  - Independence/identical sampling across fidelities is implicit; correlations between fidelities (common random numbers) are not discussed.
- Regret definition: Setting qt = µ(M)It means the instantaneous reward does not depend on the sampled fidelity; this is a legitimate pseudo-regret but mismatched to many applications (the paper admits this). The experimental section should at least justify that the chosen notion aligns with considered scenarios, or provide a variant analysis for settings where lower-fidelity plays yield different or no rewards.
- Lower bound is only proved for Bernoulli arms; the upper bound is ψ-general. State clearly what generalization is expected/possible for other ψ.

Clarity and organization
- Numerous typographical/formatting errors severely hinder readability:
  - Extraneous header line “th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016)...”
  - Figure caption text appears mid-section (“ζ(m)+2γ(m) are indicated...”) without the figure.
  - Notation overload: A is used both as a strategy and as “complement” of a set; “A denotes the complement of a set A” is nonsensical. Define a clear complement operator (·)c.
  - Unconventional use of ., & to mean approximate equalities/inequalities; avoid in main statements.
  - Algorithm 1 is incomplete (no pseudocode beyond bullets and cross-references).
  - Several missing parentheses and undefined variables in equations (e.g., B(m)k,t(s) defined with s, then called with T(m)k,t−1).
- Bibliography is incomplete (missing years/venues/pages), and some key related directions are lightly treated (e.g., cost-sensitive bandits beyond budgeted MAB, hierarchical or biased-observation bandits, successive rejects/elimination with costs, Thompson sampling variants with costs).
- The discussion of the partition sets K(m) would benefit from a simple, fully worked example (with concrete λ, ζ, ψ) illustrating when an arm falls into K(m)7 vs K(m)3.

Originality
- The formalization of biased, multi-fidelity observations with explicit costs and the MF-UCB design are novel and interesting. The lower-bound analysis that pinpoints the gap for arms with small positive Δ at lower fidelities is a useful contribution.

Significance
- The setting is practically relevant and the conceptual message is valuable. However, the experimental support is too weak to judge practical impact:
  - Only synthetic experiments; details relegated to appendix; figures not visible; no error bars.
  - Baselines are limited to naïve UCB. Missing: two-phase elimination baselines, UCB variants adapted to costs, Thompson sampling with fidelity-aware priors, and ablations (e.g., alternative γ choices).
  - No sensitivity analysis to mis-specified ζ, varying fidelity spacing (dense vs coarse), or K and M scaling.

Pros for acceptance
- Timely problem and clean conceptual idea.
- Nontrivial theory with near-optimality on a large regime.
- Potential to impact practice where multi-fidelity evaluations are commonplace.

Cons for acceptance
- Numerous clarity/notation/formatting issues; some equations likely incorrect (missing squares, set definitions).
- Heavy reliance on strong/known parameters (ζ, ψ); no robustness analysis.
- Experiments are minimal and underspecified; missing key baselines and ablations.
- Lower bound restricted to Bernoulli; upper/lower gap not resolved.

Recommendation: Weak reject
This paper has a promising core idea and interesting theory, but the current presentation has too many technical and expository issues for NeurIPS. I encourage the authors to:
- Fix all notation, set definitions (J, K partitions), and missing exponents; provide precise theorem statements in the main text.
- Clarify and justify the regret notion; add experiments for alternative notions when lower fidelities yield no/partial reward.
- Add robust experimental evaluation with real or realistic workloads, stronger baselines, and sensitivity analyses.
- Discuss robustness to misspecified ζ and relax Assumption 1 or clearly bound its role.
With these improvements, the work could make a solid contribution.
