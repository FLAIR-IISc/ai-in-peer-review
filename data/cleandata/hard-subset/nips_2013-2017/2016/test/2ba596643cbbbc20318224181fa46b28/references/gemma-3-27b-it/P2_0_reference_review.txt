## Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel framework for multi-armed bandits (MABs) where each arm can be evaluated at multiple fidelities, representing varying costs and approximation accuracies. The authors propose a Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB) algorithm, analyze its theoretical regret bounds, and demonstrate its superior performance compared to naive UCB strategies in simulated environments. The core idea is to leverage cheaper, lower-fidelity evaluations to eliminate suboptimal arms quickly, reserving expensive, high-fidelity evaluations for a smaller subset of promising candidates.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon the established foundation of UCB algorithms in MABs (Auer, 2002). It distinguishes itself by addressing a scenario not explicitly considered in the classical bandit setting – the availability of multi-fidelity evaluations. While related work exists in multi-fidelity optimization (Huang et al., 2006) and reinforcement learning (Cutler et al., 2016), these primarily focus on optimization problems without the explicit exploration-exploitation trade-off characteristic of bandits. The paper acknowledges connections to budgeted bandits (Tran-Thanh et al., 2013) and active learning (Zhang & Chaudhuri, 2016), but highlights the distinct challenges of the multi-fidelity bandit setup, specifically the need to account for biases introduced by lower fidelities.  Recent work by Kandasamy et al. (2016) extends the ideas to Gaussian process payoffs, further solidifying the growing interest in this area.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Framework:** The paper presents a well-defined and practically relevant extension to the standard MAB problem. The multi-fidelity setting arises naturally in numerous applications, like online advertising, algorithm selection, and robotics.
* **Theoretical Analysis:** The theoretical regret bound, O((λ1K + λ2|Kg|) log(Λ/λ2)), is a significant contribution. It clearly demonstrates the benefit of leveraging lower fidelities when they are significantly cheaper (λ1 < λ2) and when a relatively small set of optimal arms exists (|Kg| < K).
* **Algorithm Design:**  The MF-UCB algorithm is intuitive and elegantly adapts the UCB principle to the multi-fidelity setting. The fidelity switching criterion, guided by γ(m), seems well-motivated and theoretically justified.
* **Empirical Validation:** The simulations provide compelling evidence that MF-UCB outperforms the naive UCB approach, supporting the theoretical findings. The visualizations of arm exploration at different fidelities are insightful.
* **Clear Writing and Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured. The notation is clear and the motivation for each step is explained.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumption 1:** The regularity assumption on the ζ(m) decay rate, while not strictly necessary for the analysis, feels somewhat artificial and limiting. Its impact on performance when violated isn’t fully discussed.
* **Lower Bound Gap:** The authors acknowledge a gap between their regret bound and the proposed lower bound (Theorem 4) for certain scenarios. While a complete gap-closing analysis is often challenging, further discussion on this discrepancy and potential directions for improvement would be beneficial.
* **Limited Scope of Simulations:** While the simulations demonstrate the effectiveness of MF-UCB, they are limited in scope. Exploring different parameter settings (e.g., varying the cost ratio λ1/λ2, the size of Kg, and the noise level) would strengthen the empirical evidence.
* **Discussion of Regret Definition:** The choice of *qt* as µ(M)It, effectively competing against the optimal *high-fidelity* reward, is justified, but the authors briefly mention alternative choices (e.g., µ(mt)It) without delving into their potential impact on the regret and algorithm behavior. A more in-depth discussion would be valuable.



**Arguments for/against acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* The paper introduces a novel and important problem with potential for significant impact.
* The theoretical analysis is sound and provides valuable insights into the algorithm's performance.
* The empirical results support the theoretical findings.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presents its contributions.

**Against Acceptance:**

* The regularity assumption limits the generality of the results.
* The gap between the regret bound and lower bound is a concern.
* The simulation scope could be broader.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of multi-armed bandits.  The introduction of the multi-fidelity setting is timely and relevant, and the proposed MF-UCB algorithm offers a practical and theoretically sound solution. While the identified weaknesses are not critical, addressing them would further strengthen the paper.

**Recommendation:**

**Strong Accept.** I believe this paper is suitable for NIPS and will be of interest to the broader machine learning community.
