Summary
The paper introduces a multi-fidelity variant of the stochastic K-armed bandit in which each arm can be played at multiple fidelities with increasing cost and decreasing bias relative to the true (highest-fidelity) reward. The authors formalize a cost-capital notion of pseudo-regret suited to this setting, propose a Multi-Fidelity UCB (MF-UCB) algorithm, and analyze its regret. The key idea is to use cheaper, biased lower-fidelity evaluations to eliminate clearly suboptimal arms and reserve expensive high-fidelity plays for a small set of promising arms. The regret analysis shows that MF-UCB can achieve substantial savings over standard UCB that only uses the highest fidelity, especially when many arms are clearly suboptimal at low fidelities and costs are well-separated. A lower bound for Bernoulli rewards shows MF-UCB is near-optimal on a broad subset of arms, with a precise characterization of a remaining gap. Synthetic experiments corroborate the theoretical advantages.

Relation to prior work: The formulation is distinct from budgeted bandits (which optimize reward/cost ratios per arm) and from prior multi-fidelity work in optimization/GP bandits that lacked regret analysis in this discrete-arm, biased-observation setting. The approach builds on classic UCB but requires nontrivial adaptations due to bias bounds and switching criteria. The paper appropriately situates itself with respect to UCB, budgeted bandits, and multi-fidelity RL/simulation.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The algorithm and analysis are carefully constructed. The confidence indices incorporate both statistical uncertainty and known bias bounds ζ(m). The partition K(m) and the switching thresholds γ(m) are well-motivated by cost–benefit trade-offs. The main upper bound (Theorem 2) decomposes regret per arm and shows that (i) most cost for obviously suboptimal arms accrues at low fidelities, and (ii) only near-optimal arms require high-fidelity cost, matching intuition. The lower bound (Theorem 4) under Bernoulli rewards and an assumption excluding pathological over-exploration is standard in spirit and highlights a precise set where MF-UCB is tight (K(m)3) and where a gap remains (K(m)7).
- Assumptions: The analysis assumes known uniform bias bounds |µ(M)k − µ(m)k| ≤ ζ(m), with ζ(m) decreasing and satisfying a regularity condition (Assumption 1) roughly requiring sufficiently fast decay (e.g., exponential for sub-Gaussian rewards). This is a strong but transparent assumption; the paper acknowledges it and explains why performance is most compelling under it. The concentration assumption (sub-exponential tails via ψ) is standard. The lower bound requires Bernoulli rewards and an additional mild assumption (no polynomial over-exploration of suboptimal arms).
- Claims vs. results: Claims are appropriately supported. The comparison to standard UCB is careful: MF-UCB is never asymptotically much worse and can be substantially better when many arms can be eliminated at low fidelities. The open gap for K(m)7 arms is candidly discussed.
- Experiments: The synthetic results match theory (shallower regret slope; fidelity-wise play allocation consistent with analysis). However, empirical evaluation is limited: only UCB baseline is considered, and there are no real-world or semi-realistic simulations. More baselines (e.g., naive heuristics that mix fidelities) and sensitivity to ζ(m) misspecification would strengthen the empirical case.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with precise definitions and notational discipline. The regret definition is carefully motivated and decomposed. The algorithm is succinctly described, and the role of γ(m) is well explained.
- That said, the exposition is dense. Suggestions:
  - Move intuition for K(m) partitions and γ(m) earlier and include a small running example.
  - Clarify implications and practicality of knowing ζ(m) and Assumption 1; discuss robustness to misspecification.
  - Present constants (or at least their scaling) in the main theorem statement to aid interpretability.

Originality
- The problem formulation—multi-fidelity, biased lower-cost observations per arm with an explicit cost-capital regret—is novel in the bandit literature. The MF-UCB algorithm’s design (min over fidelity-wise UCBs with bias offsets and a principled switching criterion) is a nontrivial adaptation of UCB to a biased, multi-cost setting.
- The theoretical contributions (upper bound, lower bound with a delineated gap) go beyond straightforward UCB analyses and contribute new insights on how fidelity costs and bias trade off.
- Related work is adequately covered, and differences are clear.

Significance
- Conceptual significance: The framework captures important practical scenarios (ads, algorithm selection, simulations) where approximate, biased, cheap feedback exists. The regret notion tied to capital is reasonable for such applications.
- Theoretical significance: Provides near-optimal rates in a new setting and sharp characterization of when multi-fidelity helps (many clearly suboptimal arms; strong cost separation; informative bias bounds).
- Practical impact: Implementation requires knowledge of ζ(m), which may be hard to obtain; a discussion of learning/estimating ζ(m) online would increase applicability. Likewise, broader empirical validation and comparisons would help adoption.

Pros
- Clear, novel formulation of multi-fidelity bandits with a principled regret.
- MF-UCB is simple, intuitive, and theoretically grounded.
- Strong analysis: per-arm decomposition, near-optimality, and honest discussion of a remaining gap.
- Synthetic experiments align with theory.

Cons
- Requires known, uniform bias bounds ζ(m) and a regularity condition; robustness to misspecification not studied.
- Empirical evaluation is limited (no real data; few baselines).
- Lower bound restricted to Bernoulli and assumes no polynomial over-exploration; tightness gap remains for K(m)7 arms.
- The chosen regret (qt = µ(M)It even for low-fidelity plays) may not match all applications; alternatives are acknowledged but not explored.

Suggestions for improvement
- Discuss or experiment with robustness to misspecified ζ(m), or propose a method to adaptively estimate conservative ζ(m) online.
- Add baselines (e.g., heuristics that switch by fixed budgets; Thompson-sampling variants) and at least one realistic simulation.
- Elaborate on when Assumption 1 holds in practice and how performance degrades when it doesn’t.
- Briefly analyze alternative regret definitions for settings where low-fidelity plays yield no/low reward.

Overall recommendation: Accept. This is a solid, well-motivated theoretical contribution that opens a useful new bandit setting, provides a clean algorithm with strong analysis, and is relevant to NeurIPS audiences.

Confidence: 4/5.
