This paper introduces a multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit (MF-MAB) problem, motivated by scenarios where cheaper approximations to reward signals are available. The authors propose MF-UCB, an algorithm extending the UCB strategy to this setting, and provide theoretical regret bounds demonstrating improved performance over naive UCB when lower fidelities offer useful information. The work builds upon existing bandit literature (e.g., [3, 9, 11]) and attempts to bridge the gap between multi-fidelity optimization (e.g., [7, 10]) and the exploration-exploitation dilemma inherent in bandit problems.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Problem Formulation:** The MF-MAB formulation is a valuable contribution. It captures a common scenario in many real-world applications where obtaining precise evaluations is costly, and approximations are readily available.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides a theoretical analysis of MF-UCB, demonstrating its performance advantages over standard UCB under certain conditions. The regret bound, while complex, offers valuable insights into the algorithm's behavior.
*   **Clear Algorithm Description:** MF-UCB is presented in a straightforward and easily understandable manner.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The simulations, though not extensively detailed in the paper (details relegated to Appendix C), support the theoretical findings.
* **Well-motivated**: The paper gives good motivation for why lower fidelity estimates are useful, and why it is important to account for bias introduced by them.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Assumptions:** The analysis relies heavily on the assumption that the fidelity means are within known bounds (ζ(m)). This assumption may not hold in practical scenarios, and the sensitivity of the results to violations of this assumption is not thoroughly investigated. Further, Assumption 1 regarding the decay of ζ(m) seems somewhat arbitrary, and its practical implications are not fully explored.
*   **Regret Bound Complexity:** The presented regret bound is quite intricate and difficult to interpret. Simplifying or providing more intuitive explanations of its components would be beneficial.
*   **Lower Bound Gap:** The lower bound (Theorem 4) doesn’t quite match the upper bound (Theorem 2), leaving a potential gap in understanding the optimality of MF-UCB. The authors acknowledge this, but further investigation is warranted. The conditions under which the gap appears (specifically, relating to the ∆(`)k) are not made fully clear.
*   **Experimental Detail:** The description of the simulations is sparse. More detail regarding the specific problems, parameter settings, and statistical significance of the results would strengthen the empirical validation. Specifically, the claim of outperformance should be supported with error bars or statistical tests.
* **Notational Inconsistency**: The notation for set complements (A) is not standard.
* **Minor Formatting Issues:** There is a slight formatting inconsistency on line 32 where “th Conference” appears.

**Specific Comments & Concerns:**

*   **Line 32:** “th Conference” should simply be “Conference”.
*   **Line 36:** The phrase “ignoring mean rewards” is unclear. It is better to clarify what is being ignored (e.g., constant rewards)
*   **Line 51:** The explanation for why a cheaper estimate can be useful is rather vague. Elaborating on the practical examples would enhance clarity.
*   **Line 67:** The definition of regret, R(Λ,A), introduces `qt` but the explanation of its choice is delayed until later. Bringing this explanation forward would improve readability.
*   **Equation (2):** The meaning of ν is not fully explained. Is it a constant or does it depend on other parameters?
*   **Section 4.1 & 4.2:** The proof sketches are very high-level and lack sufficient detail to assess the validity of the arguments. More detailed explanations, or references to complete proofs in the appendix, are needed.
*   **Fig. 1:** The illustration is helpful but could be improved with clearer labeling and a more descriptive caption.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a relevant and under-explored problem in the bandit literature.
*   The proposed MF-UCB algorithm is a reasonable and well-motivated extension of UCB.
*   The theoretical analysis provides valuable insights into the algorithm's performance.
*   The empirical results, while limited, support the theoretical findings.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The strong assumptions underlying the analysis limit the paper's generalizability.
*   The complexity of the regret bound and the gap between upper and lower bounds raise concerns about the completeness of the theoretical understanding.
*   The lack of detailed experimental results weakens the empirical validation.
*   The formatting and notational inconsistencies, while minor, detract from the paper’s overall quality.
* **Overall**: The paper is very strong, but its reliance on relatively strong assumptions, and a few minor flaws in presentation, detract from its overall quality.

**Overall Assessment:**

I am leaning towards **rejection**. While the paper presents a novel and potentially important contribution, the numerous minor flaws and the strong assumptions underlying the analysis raise significant concerns. The complexity of the theoretical results, coupled with the limited experimental detail, makes it difficult to fully assess the practical value of MF-UCB. A significant revision addressing these weaknesses would be necessary for reconsideration. Specifically, the authors need to provide a more robust analysis of the algorithm's performance under more realistic conditions and provide more detailed experimental results. The lower bound should be tightened or the discrepancy explained in more detail. Finally, the paper needs to be carefully proofread to address the formatting and notational inconsistencies.
