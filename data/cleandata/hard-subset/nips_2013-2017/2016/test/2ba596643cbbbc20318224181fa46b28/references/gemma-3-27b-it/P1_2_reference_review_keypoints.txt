- Introduces a novel multi-fidelity extension to K-armed bandits using cheaper, biased approximations.  
- Defines a cost-weighted regret metric and presents the MF-UCB algorithm to allocate fidelity resources.  
- Core idea: use low-fidelity evaluations to eliminate poor arms and focus high-fidelity evaluations on promising arms.  
- Theoretical analysis provides regret bounds showing MF-UCB can outperform naive UCB when high-fidelity costs are large.  
- Positions work within bandit, multi-fidelity optimization, and budgeted bandit literature, noting relation to Kandasamy et al.  
- Novelty: explicitly addresses biased lower-fidelity approximations rather than treating them as noise.  
- Theoretical rigor: careful derivation of regret bounds and meaningful comparisons to UCB baseline.  
- Clear formalization: cost-weighted regret, fidelity accuracy ζ(m), and cost λ(m) are well-defined.  
- Intuitive algorithm: MF-UCB’s fidelity-switching criterion (confidence width vs cost) is well-motivated.  
- Empirical validation: simulations show MF-UCB outperforming naive UCB, supporting theory (simulations are simple).  
- Detailed analysis: partitioning arms into K(m) sets yields deep insight into algorithm behavior.  
- Key weakness: assuming known ζ(m) values may be unrealistic and should be addressed or estimated.  
- Gap between upper and lower bounds remains; authors acknowledge it but should better characterize when it’s significant.  
- Limited simulation scope: experiments should include more complex scenarios, distributions, and parameter sweeps.  
- Notation density: some symbols (e.g., K(m)) are dense; a gradual introduction or symbol table would improve readability.  
- Practicality: paper needs a deeper discussion of real-world challenges (choosing fidelities, estimating ζ(m)).  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions, including sensitivity analysis for ζ(m), investigating the bound gap, expanded experiments, clearer notation, and practical implementation discussion.
