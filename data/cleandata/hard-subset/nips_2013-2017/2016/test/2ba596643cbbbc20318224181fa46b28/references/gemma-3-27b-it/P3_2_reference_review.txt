## Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a multi-fidelity extension to the classical K-armed bandit problem. The key idea is that obtaining rewards for each arm can be done at varying “fidelities” – levels of cost and accuracy. The authors formalize a cost-weighted regret notion, present a Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB) algorithm, and provide a theoretical analysis demonstrating its performance. The core result shows that MF-UCB can outperform standard UCB when lower fidelities provide useful information and are significantly cheaper than higher fidelities.  The paper builds upon the rich literature of bandit algorithms (UCB in particular) and addresses a practically relevant scenario, especially in contexts like online advertising, algorithm selection, and robotics.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty & Problem Formulation:** The multi-fidelity bandit setup is a natural and valuable generalization of the standard bandit problem, motivated by real-world applications where approximations are available. The formalization of regret in this setting, considering both reward and fidelity cost, is well-defined and insightful.
* **Algorithm Design:**  MF-UCB is a sensible adaptation of the UCB algorithm, intuitively leveraging lower fidelities for exploration and refining the search space. The switching criterion based on confidence bounds and fidelity costs (γ(m)) appears well-motivated.
* **Theoretical Analysis:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing a regret bound for MF-UCB.  The comparison with the regret bound of standard UCB clarifies the benefits of the multi-fidelity approach. The analysis demonstrates the algorithm’s ability to eliminate suboptimal arms early on using lower fidelities.
* **Lower Bound:**  The inclusion of a lower bound provides a strong indication of the optimality of the MF-UCB algorithm and complements the upper bound analysis. 
* **Empirical Validation:**  The simulated experiments support the theoretical findings and provide a practical demonstration of the algorithm’s performance.
* **Clarity of Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly presents the problem, the proposed solution, and the theoretical analysis.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions:** The paper relies on the assumption that the lower fidelity means are within a known bias (ζ(m)) of the high-fidelity means. While reasonable, this assumption may not always hold in practice. The impact of significant bias in the lower fidelities isn’t fully explored, and the paper doesn't offer robustness to such situations.
* **Regularity Assumption (Assumption 1):**  The assumption regarding the decay rate of ζ(m) feels somewhat arbitrary and could potentially limit the applicability of the theoretical results. The justification for this assumption feels weak—it primarily serves to make the analysis cleaner.  A discussion of the algorithm’s performance without this assumption would be valuable.
* **Gap Between Lower Bound & Upper Bound:** The discussion of the gap between the lower bound and the upper bound, particularly concerning the k ∈ K(m)7 arms, is insightful but leaves the reader wanting a more concrete path towards closing that gap.
* **Limited Experimental Evaluation:**  While the simulations demonstrate performance gains, the experimental setup is somewhat limited.  Exploring different parameter settings, more complex reward distributions, and potentially real-world datasets would strengthen the empirical validation.  A more thorough ablation study showing the impact of different γ(m) settings would also be beneficial.
* **Discussion of Qt:** The choice of qt = µ(M)It, while motivated, could be further justified. A brief exploration of other choices (e.g., using µ(mt)It) and their implications would be insightful.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of bandit algorithms. The multi-fidelity extension is well-motivated, the proposed algorithm is intuitive and theoretically sound, and the analysis is rigorous. While there are some assumptions that could be relaxed or further investigated, the overall quality of the work is high.  

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The problem is well-defined, important, and relevant to a wide range of applications.
* The proposed algorithm is a sensible and effective solution to the problem.
* The theoretical analysis provides strong guarantees on the algorithm’s performance.
* The inclusion of a lower bound further strengthens the theoretical results.
* The empirical validation supports the theoretical findings.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The reliance on potentially restrictive assumptions (e.g., regarding the decay of ζ(m)).
* The gap between the lower and upper bounds, suggesting potential for further improvement.
* The limited scope of the experimental evaluation.




**Rating:**  **Strong Accept (8/10)**

I believe this paper is of high quality and warrants acceptance to NIPS. The strengths significantly outweigh the weaknesses, and the contribution is both novel and theoretically sound.  Addressing the weaknesses outlined above (particularly regarding the assumptions and the gap between bounds) could potentially elevate this paper to an even higher level.
