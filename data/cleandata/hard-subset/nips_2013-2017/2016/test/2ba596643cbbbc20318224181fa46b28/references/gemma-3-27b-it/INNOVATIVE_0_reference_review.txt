This paper introduces a multi-fidelity bandit framework, motivated by scenarios where evaluating arms at higher fidelity is costly but approximations are available. The core idea – leveraging cheaper, noisier evaluations to quickly eliminate suboptimal arms and focus expensive evaluations on promising candidates – is compelling and addresses a practically relevant problem. The theoretical analysis provides a regret bound that, under reasonable assumptions, demonstrates a performance improvement over standard UCB when lower fidelity evaluations are significantly cheaper. The connection to existing bandit literature is well-established, and the paper clearly positions itself within that context.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The multi-fidelity bandit formulation is a genuinely new perspective within the classical bandit framework. While multi-fidelity optimization exists, applying it to the exploration-exploitation trade-off in bandits is a significant contribution.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The regret analysis is well-developed and provides a concrete justification for the proposed MF-UCB algorithm. The lower bound result further strengthens the theoretical foundation.
*   **Intuitive Algorithm:** MF-UCB builds directly on UCB, making it easy to understand and implement. The adaptation to handle multiple fidelities appears sensible and well-motivated.
*   **Potential Impact:** The proposed framework has direct applications in areas like online advertising, algorithm selection, and robotics, where obtaining accurate evaluations is expensive or time-consuming.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The notation is relatively clear, and the key ideas are effectively communicated.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Assumptions:** The analysis relies on several assumptions, including the bound on the difference between fidelities (zeta(m)) and the regularity condition on zeta decay. While these assumptions are reasonable, their impact on the algorithm's performance in real-world scenarios is not fully explored.
*   **Empirical Evaluation:** The simulations are limited. While they demonstrate that MF-UCB outperforms naive UCB, the synthetic problems are relatively simple. More extensive experiments on diverse and realistic datasets are needed to further validate the algorithm's effectiveness.
*   **γ(m) Choice:** While the choice of γ(m) is theoretically motivated, the sensitivity of the algorithm’s performance to the ρ parameter (used in calculating γ(m)) is not adequately investigated.
*   **Lower Bound Gap:** The gap between the theoretical upper bound and lower bound, particularly for arms where initial fidelity plays are misleading, suggests potential for further refinement.
* **Limited Discussion of Qt:** The choice of qt = µ(M)It is briefly justified but could benefit from more in-depth discussion regarding alternative choices and their implications.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles a genuinely novel problem with a clear theoretical justification.
*   The proposed algorithm is intuitive and builds upon well-established bandit techniques.
*   The theoretical analysis provides a solid foundation for understanding the algorithm’s performance.
*   The potential impact of this work is significant across multiple domains.
*   Even with the noted weaknesses, the contribution is substantial enough to warrant acceptance.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The assumptions might be restrictive in certain real-world scenarios.
*   The empirical validation is limited and could be strengthened with more diverse experiments.
*   The lower bound result isn't as tight as it could be, potentially indicating room for improvement in the algorithm.



**Overall Assessment:**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. While the empirical validation could be more comprehensive and the assumptions should be further investigated, the novelty and theoretical contribution are substantial. The multi-fidelity bandit framework is a promising new direction in bandit research, and this paper provides a solid foundation for future work. The potential impact of this research is considerable, and the paper’s clarity and rigor make it a valuable contribution to the NIPS community. I believe the innovative nature of the work outweighs its current limitations. I would encourage the authors to expand the empirical evaluation and explore the sensitivity of the algorithm to its key parameters in future work.
