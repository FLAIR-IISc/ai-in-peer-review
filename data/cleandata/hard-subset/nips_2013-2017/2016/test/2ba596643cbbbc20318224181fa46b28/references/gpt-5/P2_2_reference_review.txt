Summary
The paper introduces a multi-fidelity variant of the stochastic K-armed bandit where each arm admits M fidelities: low fidelities are cheaper but biased approximations of the highest-fidelity reward distribution. The bias is controlled by known fidelity-specific bounds ζ(m), with costs λ(1) < ... < λ(M). The authors define a cost-weighted pseudo-regret for a strategy that spends a total capital Λ: the reward at any pull is the highest-fidelity mean of the chosen arm, regardless of the fidelity used, multiplied by the cost spent; unspent capital incurs maximal regret. They propose MF-UCB, which maintains per-arm per-fidelity UCBs that inflate by ζ(m) and selects per-arm UCB as the minimum over fidelities; it then chooses the fidelity via a threshold rule that compares the current statistical uncertainty to γ(m) = ψ^{-1}((λ(m)/λ(m+1)) ψ(ζ(m))). Intuitively, the algorithm uses cheap fidelities to eliminate clearly suboptimal arms and reserves expensive high-fidelity pulls for near-optimal arms.

Theoretically, under standard concentration (ψ) and a mild decay condition on ζ(m), they prove a regret bound E[R(Λ)] = O(log(Λ/λ(M)) × Σ_k Δ(M)_k × λ(J_k)/ψ(Δ(J_k)_k)), where J_k is the fidelity at which arm k gets eliminated. This improves over single-fidelity UCB that pays λ(M) for all arms, and is near-optimal: a lower bound for Bernoulli rewards shows any reasonable strategy must pay (up to constants) at least Σ_k Δ(M)_k × min_ℓ λ(ℓ)/ψ(Δ(ℓ)_k), with a remaining gap only for a specific subset of “borderline” arms. Simulations on synthetic problems corroborate the gains.

Quality
- Soundness: The algorithm design is principled: the per-fidelity UCBs adjusted by ζ(m) furnish valid upper bounds on the true high-fidelity mean, and the fidelity-selection threshold γ(m) is derived from balancing marginal information gain against cost. The main regret theorem and proof sketch appear correct and align with intuition: cost is concentrated on fidelities just sufficient to eliminate an arm; only near-optimal arms are played at the top fidelity. The lower bound uses standard change-of-measure arguments and identifies exactly when lower fidelities should be used. The identified suboptimality class (K(m)_7) is clearly explained.
- Assumptions: The analysis assumes known uniform bias bounds ζ(m), known costs, standard concentration (sub-Gaussian is a special case), and a decay condition on ζ(m) (Assumption 1) to ensure that the advantage of using multiple fidelities is not nullified by nearly redundant adjacent fidelities. These are reasonable for a first theory, but the requirement that ζ(m) be known and uniform across arms may be strong in practice; sensitivity is not explored experimentally. The lower bound requires Bernoulli rewards and an assumption (subpolynomial plays of suboptimal arms), standard in lower-bound arguments.
- Experiments: Synthetic experiments show consistent improvements and align with the theory (most plays of bad arms at low fidelities; smaller slope). However, the empirical section is thin: details are deferred to the appendix; there is no robustness study (e.g., mis-specified ζ), no ablations on γ(m) or ρ, and no comparisons against stronger baselines (KL-UCB, Thompson sampling variants, or algorithms tailored to budget/resource constraints). This limits empirical persuasiveness but does not undermine the theory.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well organized for an expert reader. The regret definition is unusual; the authors motivate it with applications and contrast with alternatives, which is helpful. Still, the oracle interpretation and the decomposition into r̃ and R̃ could be clarified further (e.g., explicitly noting that zero regret corresponds to an oracle that knows k* and expends the entire budget on any fidelity). The partition K(1),...,K(M) is central; the intuition is conveyed, but the dense notation could be streamlined with an illustrative example early on. Some proofs and constants are deferred, which is acceptable for space but leaves a few steps opaque in the main text.
- Minor presentation issues: confirm the direction of the threshold inequality in Step 2; summarize in the main text the constants/conditions (ρ > 4, Λ > Λ0) and practical guidance on setting ρ.

Originality
- The formulation—multi-fidelity bandits with biased lower fidelities and a cost-weighted regret tied to the high-fidelity objective—is novel relative to prior work on budgeted/bwK bandits and multi-fidelity optimization. The algorithmic idea of combining per-fidelity UCBs with a cost-adaptive switching rule is a nontrivial extension of UCB to this setting. To my knowledge, regret analyses that quantify when and how much cheaper fidelities help in bandits are new. The relation to budgeted bandits and multi-fidelity RL/optimization is discussed; consider also citing Bandits with Knapsacks (e.g., Badanidiyuru et al.) to further position the cost model.

Significance
- The work addresses a meaningful and broadly applicable setting (ads, ML algorithm selection, simulation-to-real). The theoretical results are insightful: they cleanly separate arms by “elimination fidelity” and quantify improvements over single-fidelity strategies by factors λ(J_k)/λ(M). The lower bound clarifies optimal structure and highlights the remaining open case. The algorithm is simple enough to be used in practice when ζ(m) and costs are known. This is a solid theoretical contribution with credible potential impact; an immediate empirical impact would be stronger with more extensive experiments.

Pros
- Clear, well-motivated formalization of multi-fidelity bandits and a principled regret notion.
- Simple MF-UCB algorithm with intuitive fidelity-switching rule.
- Strong theory: near-optimal regret bounds; informative partition-based analysis; matching lower bound except for a precisely defined subset.
- Empirical results consistent with theory.

Cons and suggestions
- Requires known, arm-agnostic bias bounds ζ(m); no robustness analysis to mis-specification or arm-specific biases. Can ζ be learned online or upper-bounded adaptively?
- Assumption 1 (ζ decay) is important for appealing comparisons; discuss performance when it fails and whether adaptive skipping of near-redundant fidelities is possible.
- Lower bound gap for K(m)_7 remains open; even a heuristic modification (e.g., occasional lower-fidelity verification when costs are very uneven) and a discussion of trade-offs would strengthen the paper.
- Experiments are limited; add stronger baselines (KL-UCB, Thompson sampling), sensitivity to λ and ζ, and a real or semi-realistic task (e.g., subsampling or truncation as low-fidelity proxies).
- Clarify parameter choices (ρ, ψ) and provide guidance for non-sub-Gaussian noise.

Overall assessment
This is a solid and timely contribution that advances the theory of bandits in multi-fidelity settings with a simple algorithm and near-optimal guarantees. Despite some practical assumptions and modest empirical scope, the paper meets the NeurIPS criteria on quality, originality, and significance, and is reasonably clear. I lean toward acceptance, with the above clarifications and experimental extensions encouraged.
