

# Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

This paper proposes a multi-fidelity extension to the multi-armed bandit problem, where each arm has multiple "fidelities" (approximations) with different costs and varying levels of accuracy. The authors introduce MF-UCB, an algorithm that leverages cheaper lower-fidelity approximations to eliminate "bad" arms and reserves expensive high-fidelity evaluations for promising arms. The paper claims this approach achieves better regret bounds than standard UCB algorithms in certain regimes.

## Strengths

The paper makes a clear contribution to formalizing a multi-fidelity bandit problem, which has practical relevance in applications like online advertising and algorithm selection. The theoretical analysis follows standard bandit methodology and establishes a regret bound that, under specific assumptions, shows potential advantages over UCB. The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with reasonable assumptions about the relationship between fidelities (ζ(m) decay).

## Weaknesses

Despite these strengths, the paper suffers from significant methodological and validation shortcomings that undermine its claims of novelty and practical value.

First, the theoretical analysis relies on restrictive assumptions, particularly Assumption 1 (ζ(m) decay), which the paper acknowledges is not necessary for algorithm analysis but makes the performance comparison most appealing. Without this assumption, the regret bound's practical advantage over UCB becomes questionable. The paper doesn't demonstrate how the algorithm performs when this assumption doesn't hold, which is a significant limitation for real-world applications where such bounds may be difficult to verify.

Second, the empirical validation is severely limited. The paper only shows results on synthetic problems and doesn't compare against relevant baselines from the literature. The authors cite Kandasamy et al. [8] (Gaussian Process Bandit Optimisation with Multi-fidelity Evaluations), which is directly related work, but fail to compare against this approach. The comparison is only against UCB, which isn't designed for the multi-fidelity setting, making the "outperformance" claim misleading. Without comparison against other multi-fidelity bandit methods, it's impossible to determine if the approach is truly novel or just a minor extension of existing work.

Third, the paper doesn't address practical implementation challenges. The algorithm requires knowledge of ζ(m) (the bias bounds between fidelities), which would be difficult to estimate in practice. The paper doesn't discuss how these bounds might be learned or approximated, which is a significant practical limitation. Additionally, the paper doesn't provide any analysis of computational complexity, which could be a major drawback for large-scale applications.

Fourth, the theoretical claims are overstated. The paper claims MF-UCB is "near-optimal," but Theorem 4 reveals a gap between the upper and lower bounds for arms in K(m)7. This gap suggests the algorithm isn't as optimal as claimed, and the paper doesn't adequately explain or address this limitation.

## Comparison to Related Work

The paper cites relevant work but fails to properly contextualize its contribution. The authors reference Kandasamy et al. [8], which is nearly identical in problem setting and approach, yet the paper doesn't compare against this work or explain how it differs. This omission makes it difficult to assess the novelty of the proposed approach.

## Overall Assessment

While the multi-fidelity bandit problem is interesting and the paper makes a reasonable theoretical contribution, the lack of comprehensive empirical validation against appropriate baselines, the restrictive assumptions, and the failure to address practical implementation challenges significantly undermine the paper's claims. The paper presents an incremental extension to existing bandit methods rather than a substantial advancement that would justify acceptance at NIPS.

The paper's theoretical analysis is sound within its narrow assumptions, but these assumptions are too restrictive for the claims of practical advantage to hold. The empirical results are insufficient to support the paper's main claims, and the comparison to related work is inadequate.

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection. The paper presents an incremental extension to a well-studied problem without providing sufficient evidence of its practical advantages over existing methods. The theoretical claims are overstated, and the empirical validation is limited to synthetic problems with inadequate comparison to relevant baselines. For this work to be considered for NIPS, the authors would need to:
1) Compare against relevant multi-fidelity bandit approaches (like [8])
2) Demonstrate performance on real-world data
3) Address the practical challenges of implementing the approach
4) Show how the algorithm performs without the restrictive Assumption 1

Without these improvements, the paper doesn't provide a sufficiently novel or compelling contribution to justify publication at NIPS.
