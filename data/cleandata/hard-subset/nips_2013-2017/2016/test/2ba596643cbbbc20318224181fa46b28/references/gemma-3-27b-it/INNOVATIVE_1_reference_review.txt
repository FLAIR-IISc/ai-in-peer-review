This paper introduces a multi-fidelity bandit setting, motivated by scenarios where evaluating arms at higher fidelity is costly but provides more accurate rewards. The authors propose MF-UCB, an algorithm that intelligently allocates plays across different fidelities, leveraging cheaper, faster approximations to eliminate suboptimal arms and focusing higher-fidelity evaluations on promising candidates. The core idea – using lower fidelities for exploration and higher fidelities for exploitation – is intuitively appealing and well-motivated. The theoretical analysis provides regret bounds showing MF-UCB can outperform standard UCB in favorable scenarios (when lower fidelities are truly informative and cheaper). The connection to existing bandit literature and multi-fidelity optimization is clearly drawn.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The multi-fidelity bandit formulation is genuinely novel and addresses a practically relevant problem that hasn't received dedicated attention in the bandit literature. The formalization is clean and well-defined, providing a solid foundation for future work.
*   **Intuition & Algorithm Design:**  The MF-UCB algorithm is a natural extension of UCB, leveraging the multi-fidelity structure effectively. The switching criterion (γ(m)) appears well-motivated, balancing the need for exploration at lower fidelities with the desire to exploit promising arms at higher fidelity.
*   **Theoretical Analysis:** The regret bound is a significant contribution, rigorously demonstrating the potential benefits of the multi-fidelity approach. The connection between the regret bound and the parameters of the fidelity levels (λ(m), ζ(m)) is clear and insightful.
*   **Experimental Validation:** While the simulations appear limited in scope, they provide initial evidence supporting the effectiveness of MF-UCB compared to a naive UCB baseline.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The motivation, algorithm description, and theoretical analysis are presented in a logical and accessible manner.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Assumptions:** The analysis relies on several assumptions (e.g., Assumption 1 regarding the decay of ζ(m)) that may not hold in all practical scenarios.  The impact of violating these assumptions is not fully discussed. While these assumptions are standard for theoretical analysis, exploring their sensitivity would strengthen the work.
*   **Lower Bound Gap:** The authors acknowledge a gap between their regret bound and the proposed lower bound, particularly for specific arms. The explanation is plausible, but a tighter analysis to close this gap would be desirable.
*   **Simulation Scope:** The simulations are somewhat limited. Expanding the experiments to more diverse problem settings (e.g., different fidelity cost structures, varying degrees of fidelity bias) would strengthen the empirical validation. Demonstrating the algorithm's performance on a real-world dataset would be compelling.
*   **Regret Definition:** The chosen regret definition (Equation 1) may not be universally applicable. The authors briefly acknowledge this and suggest alternatives, but a more thorough discussion of different regret metrics and their implications would be beneficial.  The motivation for using the highest fidelity reward (µ(M)k) regardless of the fidelity played is a point that could be further justified, especially given potential biases at lower fidelities.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The impact of the parameter ρ on performance isn't discussed in detail. Some sensitivity analysis on ρ would be helpful.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a genuinely novel and potentially impactful contribution to the multi-armed bandit literature.
*   The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides valuable insights into the algorithm's performance.
*   The MF-UCB algorithm is well-motivated and appears to be a promising approach for handling multi-fidelity bandit problems.
*   The writing is clear and the paper is well-organized.
*   The identified weaknesses are not fatal and do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. The novelty and potential impact outweigh the minor flaws.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The assumptions underlying the analysis may be restrictive.
*   The simulations are limited in scope.
*   The gap between the regret bound and the lower bound remains open.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel contribution. The authors have identified an important problem, proposed a well-motivated algorithm, and provided a rigorous theoretical analysis. While some limitations exist (particularly regarding assumptions, simulations and the lower bound), the novelty and potential impact of the work are significant. I believe this paper is a valuable addition to the NIPS program. I lean towards acceptance and believe that addressing the minor concerns outlined above would further strengthen the paper.



**Recommendation:** Accept.
