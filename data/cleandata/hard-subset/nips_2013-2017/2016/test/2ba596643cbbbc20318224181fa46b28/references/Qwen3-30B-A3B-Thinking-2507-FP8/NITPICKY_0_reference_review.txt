

# Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

This paper proposes a multi-fidelity extension to the classical multi-armed bandit problem, where each arm has multiple approximations (fidelities) with varying costs and accuracy. The authors introduce MF-UCB, an algorithm that leverages cheaper low-fidelity approximations to eliminate suboptimal arms before committing expensive high-fidelity evaluations. The paper provides theoretical regret bounds showing that MF-UCB can outperform standard UCB when the number of high-reward arms is small and the cost of low fidelity is significantly less than high fidelity.

**Strengths:**
- The multi-fidelity bandit problem is well-motivated by practical applications in advertising and machine learning.
- The MF-UCB algorithm is a natural extension of UCB principles to the multi-fidelity setting.
- The theoretical analysis provides valuable insights about when multi-fidelity bandits can provide significant improvements.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Critical Notational Inconsistencies:** The paper contains multiple notational errors that undermine clarity. For example, the set of arms is denoted as both "K" and "K = {1, . . . ,K}" on page 1, creating immediate confusion. The notation "µ(M)k" appears inconsistently as both "µ(M)k" and "µ (M) k" throughout the paper. The definition of regret (equation 1) contains unclear notation with "r̃(Λ,A)" that is never properly defined.

2. **Incorrect Reference:** The paper cites "Robbins [11]" for the seminal work on bandits, but the reference list shows "Some aspects of the sequential design of experiments, Herbert Robbins, Bulletin of the American Mathematical Society, , , ," which is not Robbins' seminal bandit paper (that would be "Some aspects of the sequential design of experiments" from 1952). This undermines the paper's credibility.

3. **Unjustified Assumption:** Assumption 1 states "The ζ(m)'s decay fast enough such that ∑m i=1 1 ψ(ζ(i)) ≤ 1 ψ(ζ(m+1)) for all m < M" without proper motivation. The authors claim it's "not necessary to analyse our algorithm" but "makes the performance of MF-UCB when compared to UCB most appealing." This is problematic as the main claim (that MF-UCB outperforms UCB) relies on this assumption, yet it's presented as optional.

4. **Ambiguous Theoretical Claims:** The regret bound in Theorem 2 uses unclear notation ("E[R(Λ)] log(nΛ) . ∑..."), where the dot notation is never defined. The paper claims MF-UCB is "near-optimal" but Theorem 4 shows a gap for arms in K(m)7, contradicting this claim without proper explanation.

5. **Missing Critical Details:** The paper states "For sub-Gaussian distributions, the condition is satisfied for an exponentially decaying (ζ(1), ζ(2), . . . )" but provides no justification for the specific sequence given. The paper mentions "empirical results" but references "Figure 2" and "Appendix C," which are absent from the submission.

6. **Technical Errors:** The paper defines regret as "the comparison, in expectation, of the realised reward against an oracle," which is not the standard definition of regret in bandit literature. Regret is typically defined as the difference between the oracle's reward and the algorithm's reward, not a comparison.

**Conclusion:**
While the multi-fidelity bandit problem is interesting and the proposed algorithm is conceptually reasonable, the paper suffers from numerous technical and notational inconsistencies that undermine its scientific contribution. The critical errors in notation, reference, and theoretical presentation prevent readers from properly understanding and evaluating the claims. The paper appears to be a draft that has not been sufficiently polished for publication at NIPS.

**Recommendation: Reject** (The paper has novel ideas but the numerous technical errors and inconsistent presentation significantly diminish its scientific value.)
