## Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a multi-fidelity extension to the classic multi-armed bandit (MAB) problem, motivated by scenarios where cheaper, approximate evaluations of arm rewards are available alongside more expensive, accurate evaluations. The authors formalize this setting, define a cost-weighted regret metric, and propose a Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB) algorithm. They prove a regret bound for MF-UCB that, under certain conditions, outperforms naive UCB strategies that only utilize the highest fidelity. The key intuition is to leverage lower fidelities to quickly eliminate suboptimal arms and focus expensive high-fidelity evaluations on a promising subset.

**Related Work:**

The paper accurately positions itself within the existing literature. It acknowledges the extensive history of MAB research, particularly UCB methods.  It correctly differentiates its work from existing multi-fidelity optimization methods that lack a formal regret analysis, as well as related work in reinforcement learning and budgeted bandits, highlighting the unique exploration-exploitation tradeoff present in the bandit setting. The citation of Kandasamy et al. [8] on multi-fidelity bandits with Gaussian processes demonstrates awareness of recent, highly relevant work, though the authors rightly point out that their setting is broader.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Formulation:** The authors introduce a well-defined framework for multi-fidelity bandit problems, which is a natural extension of the classical MAB setting and has clear practical relevance in domains like online advertising, algorithm selection, and robotics.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:** The theoretical analysis provides a valuable bound on the regret of MF-UCB, demonstrating its performance relative to standard UCB. The conditions under which MF-UCB outperforms UCB are clearly stated (λ(1) < λ(2) and |Kg| < K), making the result actionable.
* **Intuitive Algorithm:** MF-UCB is a conceptually simple and elegant adaptation of UCB, making it easy to understand and implement. The adaptive fidelity selection strategy based on confidence bounds is well-motivated.
* **Empirical Validation:** The simulations provide evidence supporting the theoretical results, showing that MF-UCB outperforms naive UCB in various scenarios.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is consistent, and the explanations are relatively clear.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions:** The analysis relies on several assumptions, including the knowledge of ζ(m) (the bias at each fidelity) and Assumption 1 (on the decay of ζ(m)). While these are reasonable starting points, the sensitivity of the results to violations of these assumptions isn't fully explored.  In practical scenarios, ζ(m) is often unknown and may need to be estimated, which could significantly impact performance.
* **Lower Bound Gap:** The authors acknowledge a gap between the theoretical lower bound and their regret bound for certain arms (K(m)7).  While they provide a plausible explanation related to the switching criterion, resolving this gap would strengthen the theoretical contribution.
* **Simulation Scope:** While the simulations are encouraging, the range of tested scenarios is somewhat limited. Exploring more complex environments and varying the cost and fidelity parameters more broadly would bolster the empirical validation.
* **Practical Considerations:** The paper focuses primarily on the theoretical aspects. A discussion of practical challenges related to implementing MF-UCB, such as choosing appropriate values for ρ and γ(m) in real-world applications, would be beneficial.
* **Regret Definition:** The chosen regret definition (Equation 1) is somewhat convoluted and might not be the most intuitive. While the authors provide justification, a more straightforward definition might improve clarity.



**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with a solid theoretical foundation and supporting empirical results.  The claims are well-supported, though the reliance on assumptions should be acknowledged. This is a complete piece of work, though further exploration of the assumptions and practical considerations would strengthen it.

**Clarity:**  The paper is generally clear and well-organized. However, the regret definition and some aspects of the theoretical analysis could benefit from further simplification.

**Originality:** The multi-fidelity bandit formulation is a novel and valuable contribution. The adaptation of UCB to this setting is also original, and the theoretical analysis provides new insights into the performance of multi-fidelity algorithms.

**Significance:** The problem addressed in this paper is significant, as it has broad applicability to various real-world scenarios. The MF-UCB algorithm offers a promising approach to exploiting cheaper approximations to improve the efficiency of exploration-exploitation.  The theoretical results provide a strong foundation for future research in this area.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Presents a novel and well-motivated problem formulation.
* Provides a theoretically sound algorithm with a regret bound that demonstrates its potential advantages.
* Includes empirical validation supporting the theoretical results.
* Addresses an important and practical problem with potential for real-world impact.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Relies on assumptions that may not hold in all practical scenarios.
* There's a gap between the theoretical lower bound and the algorithm's regret bound.
* The simulation scope could be broader.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a **strong accept**.  The authors present a valuable contribution to the field of bandit optimization with a novel formulation, strong theoretical results, and supporting empirical evidence. While the assumptions and the gap in the lower bound are weaknesses, they do not overshadow the overall significance and quality of the work. The strengths of the paper, particularly the originality and potential impact, make it a worthwhile addition to the NIPS program.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses mentioned above – specifically, a more detailed discussion of the assumptions and their impact, and further exploration of the lower bound gap.
