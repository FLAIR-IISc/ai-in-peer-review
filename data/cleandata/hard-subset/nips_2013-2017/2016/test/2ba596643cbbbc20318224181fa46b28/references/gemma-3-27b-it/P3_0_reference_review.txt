## Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel multi-fidelity extension to the classical multi-armed bandit (MAB) problem. The authors address scenarios where evaluating arms at higher fidelities is costly, but cheaper, approximate evaluations are available. They propose a Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB) algorithm, analyze its theoretical performance, and demonstrate its superiority over standard UCB in simulations. The core idea is to leverage lower fidelity evaluations to quickly eliminate suboptimal arms, reserving expensive high-fidelity plays for a promising subset. The paper establishes a regret bound for MF-UCB that, under certain conditions, outperforms the standard UCB regret bound.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds naturally on the extensive literature of MABs, particularly UCB algorithms. It correctly identifies the gap in existing work regarding regret analysis in multi-fidelity settings.  While the paper acknowledges related work in multi-fidelity optimization (e.g., robotics, machine learning algorithm selection), it rightly points out their lack of focus on the exploration-exploitation trade-off inherent in the bandit setting. The connection to budgeted bandits is noted but appropriately distinguished by the different problem formulation. The authors also accurately reference their own prior work extending these ideas to Gaussian Process payoffs.  The comparison to Thompson Sampling for budgeted bandits [Xia et al., IJCAI 2016] would have been useful as a related work to contextualize their approach.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Formulation:** The paper presents a well-defined and practically relevant generalization of the classic MAB problem. The multi-fidelity setup is compelling and finds applications in various domains like online advertising, algorithm selection, and experimental design.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The analysis is thorough and provides a well-justified regret bound for MF-UCB.  The derivation appears sound and addresses the complexities introduced by the different fidelities. The lower bound further strengthens the contribution by demonstrating near-optimality.
* **Clear Algorithm:** MF-UCB is presented in a clear and concise manner, making it easy to understand and implement. The design of the fidelity switching criterion (γ(m)) is well-motivated.
* **Empirical Validation:** The simulations demonstrate the effectiveness of MF-UCB compared to standard UCB, validating the theoretical findings.
* **Well-Written:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is consistent, and the explanations are clear.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumption 1:** While the authors acknowledge Assumption 1 isn't *necessary*, the performance gains hinge on it. The conditions for satisfying this assumption (e.g., exponentially decaying ζ(m)) feel somewhat restrictive and could limit the applicability of the algorithm in practice. A discussion of scenarios where this assumption is likely to hold, or strategies to mitigate its impact, would be beneficial.
* **Lower Bound Gap:** The authors honestly acknowledge the gap between their upper bound (MF-UCB's performance) and the lower bound, particularly concerning arms with very similar expected rewards.  This gap is a valid point of discussion and further investigation.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The simulations are presented somewhat briefly. Expanding the empirical evaluation with more diverse test scenarios (e.g., varying cost ratios, different reward distributions) would strengthen the claims. A sensitivity analysis of the ρ parameter would also be informative.
* **Regret Definition:** The choice of `qt = µ(M)It` as the instantaneous pseudo-reward, while motivated by the long-term performance goal, implicitly prioritizes higher fidelities even when lower fidelity results are sufficient for decision making. The paper touches on alternative choices but doesn’t explore their implications.
* **Connection to Active Learning:** The problem bears a close relationship to active learning with cheap and expensive labelers [Zhang & Chaudhuri, NeurIPS 2016].  A more direct comparison and discussion of differences/similarities would strengthen the positioning of the work.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the MAB literature. The multi-fidelity formulation is both novel and practically relevant, and the theoretical analysis provides a solid foundation for understanding the performance of the proposed algorithm. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.  The authors have convincingly demonstrated the benefits of leveraging multi-fidelity information in the exploration-exploitation trade-off.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a novel and important problem.
* The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides valuable insights.
* The algorithm is well-designed and empirically validated.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The assumption concerning the decay of ζ(m) may limit the applicability of the algorithm.
* The gap between the upper and lower bounds warrants further investigation.
* The empirical evaluation could be more extensive.


**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS 2016, contingent on the authors addressing the points raised above. Specifically, expanding the discussion of Assumption 1, elaborating on the lower bound gap, and providing a more comprehensive empirical evaluation would significantly strengthen the paper.  A more detailed discussion of the regret definition and its impact would also be beneficial.
