- Paper introduces multi-fidelity multi-armed bandits, a cost-weighted regret metric, and MF-UCB to use cheap low-fidelity evaluations to eliminate arms before expensive high-fidelity pulls.
- Theoretical results: provides regret upper bound (Theorem 2) and lower bound (Theorem 4), showing when multi-fidelity helps over standard UCB.
- Novelty: formalizes a practically relevant extension to bandits with cost-aware multi-fidelity evaluations.
- Strong theoretical contribution: clear regret analysis that demonstrates conditions under which MF-UCB outperforms UCB.
- Algorithm design: MF-UCB is well-motivated, balances low-fidelity exploration and high-fidelity exploitation; γ(m) choice is theoretically grounded.
- Clarity: paper is well-written with clear notation, examples, and illustrations.
- Weak comparison to prior work: insufficient differentiation from Kandasamy et al. [8], who also study multi-fidelity bandits with GPs.
- Restrictive assumption: Assumption 1 (fast decay of ζ(m)) is crucial but not justified or quantified for practical M (e.g., 5–10 fidelities).
- Weak empirical evaluation: claims of outperforming UCB lack detailed experiments, comparisons to other multi-fidelity methods, sensitivity analyses, and real-world case studies.
- Theoretical gap: Theorem 4 indicates MF-UCB may be suboptimal for arms in K(m)7, but paper gives no remedy or guidance.
- Practical concerns: no discussion on handling unknown ζ(m) or violations of bias assumptions; algorithm may be sensitive in practice.
- Critical assessment: conceptually strong and theoretically solid but novelty is weakened without clear distinction from [8] and practicality is limited by Assumption 1.
- Required improvements: clarify difference from [8], add detailed experiments (baselines and sensitivity), discuss Assumption 1’s practical validity, and analyze parameter sensitivity.
- Recommendation: weak accept — valuable theoretical contribution but needs clearer novelty and substantially stronger empirical validation.
