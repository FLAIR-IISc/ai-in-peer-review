

The paper introduces a novel multi-fidelity extension of the classic multi-armed bandit problem, where each arm has access to multiple approximations (fidelities) of its reward distribution, with each higher fidelity being more expensive but more accurate. The authors formalize this setting, propose a new algorithm called MF-UCB that leverages cheaper lower fidelities to eliminate suboptimal arms and reserves expensive higher fidelity evaluations for promising arms, and provide theoretical guarantees showing that MF-UCB achieves regret bounds that can be significantly better than standard UCB algorithms. The paper also establishes a lower bound demonstrating that MF-UCB is near-optimal under certain conditions.

The paper builds upon the UCB framework [3, 5] but introduces significant new challenges in formalizing the multi-fidelity setting and analyzing regret. It distinguishes itself from related work on multi-fidelity optimization [7, 10], budgeted bandits [13, 15], and active learning [16] by explicitly addressing the exploration-exploitation trade-off in the bandit setting with multiple fidelities. The authors carefully address how their regret formulation differs from previous approaches, emphasizing the importance of accounting for both the cost of fidelity and the bias in lower fidelity approximations.

Strengths:
1. The formalism for multi-fidelity bandits is well-motivated and clearly defined, with appropriate assumptions (e.g., the ζ(m) bias bounds and Assumption 1).
2. The MF-UCB algorithm is intuitively designed and effectively leverages the multi-fidelity structure to reduce regret.
3. The theoretical analysis is comprehensive, with a regret bound (Theorem 2) showing MF-UCB's superiority over UCB when lower fidelities are significantly cheaper and the number of promising arms is small.
4. The lower bound (Theorem 4) demonstrates near-optimality of MF-UCB, with a clear explanation for the small gap in certain cases.
5. The simulations on synthetic data support the theoretical claims, showing MF-UCB outperforming UCB with smaller regret gradients.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper assumes knowledge of ζ(m) (the bias bounds), which may be difficult to obtain in practice. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, they don't explore how to estimate ζ(m) or handle cases where it's unknown.
2. The theoretical analysis relies on the regularity condition (Assumption 1), which may not hold in all practical scenarios. The authors note this is not necessary for the algorithm's analysis but is important for comparing MF-UCB to UCB.
3. The paper focuses on the stochastic setting, but it's unclear how the approach would extend to adversarial bandits or more complex settings.
4. While the regret bound shows improvement in cases where |Kg| < K and λ(1) < λ(2), the paper doesn't provide concrete examples of real-world problems where this gap is substantial enough to matter practically.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a logical flow from problem formulation to algorithm design to theoretical analysis. The notation is consistent and well-defined, and the figures (e.g., Fig. 1) effectively illustrate the key concepts. The related work section is thorough and appropriately contextualizes the contributions.

Regarding novelty, the paper presents a distinct formalism for multi-fidelity bandits that differs significantly from previous work. The authors correctly note that while multi-fidelity methods exist in optimization and active learning, none address the exploration-exploitation trade-off in the bandit setting as this paper does. The problem is sufficiently different from recent NIPS papers to qualify as novel.

The significance of this work is substantial, as it addresses a practical limitation in bandit applications where high-fidelity evaluations are expensive. The multi-fidelity framework could be broadly applicable in domains like online advertising, algorithm selection, and hyperparameter optimization, where cheaper approximations of expensive experiments are available. The theoretical improvements in regret bounds, particularly when the number of promising arms is small, provide a clear path for practical implementation.

The paper meets NIPS' quality criteria with sound technical analysis, well-supported claims, and a complete piece of work. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, such as the gap between the upper and lower bounds for certain arms and the requirement for known bias bounds.

In conclusion, the paper makes a significant contribution to the multi-armed bandit literature by introducing a well-motivated multi-fidelity framework and providing a theoretically sound algorithm with strong practical potential. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and the experimental results support the claims. The main limitations are the assumption of known bias bounds and the regularity condition, but these are acknowledged by the authors. The paper represents a clear advancement over previous work and would be valuable to the NIPS community.

Pro: Novel formalism for multi-fidelity bandits; well-designed algorithm; strong theoretical guarantees; practical significance; clear writing.

Con: Requires known bias bounds; relies on regularity condition; limited exploration of real-world applications.
