### Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper introduces a novel and practical extension to the classic K-armed stochastic bandit problem, which the authors term the "multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit." The key idea is that for each arm, one can obtain observations at different "fidelities." Lower fidelities are cheaper to sample but provide biased or noisy approximations of the true, highest-fidelity reward. The goal is to minimize a cost-weighted regret within a given total capital budget. The authors formalize this problem, propose a new algorithm called Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB), and provide a thorough theoretical analysis including both an upper regret bound for their algorithm and a problem-specific lower bound, demonstrating the near-optimality of their approach.

This work builds upon the extensive literature on UCB-style algorithms for the multi-armed bandit problem (e.g., Auer et al., 2002; Lai & Robbins, 1985). However, it distinguishes itself by introducing the multi-fidelity structure, which is a significant and non-trivial extension. While multi-fidelity methods are known in other fields like optimization (Huang et al., 2006) and reinforcement learning (Cutler et al., 2014), this paper appears to be the first to formally define and rigorously analyze this setting for regret minimization in the canonical bandit framework. It differs from budgeted bandits (Tran-Thanh et al., 2013), where costs are typically associated with arms rather than different evaluation modes of the same arm. The problem is also related to active learning with weak and strong labelers (Zhang & Chaudhuri, 2016), but focuses on the exploration-exploitation tradeoff inherent to bandits rather than pure exploration for sample efficiency.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality.

*   **Strengths:** The theoretical analysis is the main strength of the paper. It is comprehensive, providing both an upper bound for the proposed MF-UCB algorithm and a corresponding lower bound for the problem class. The analysis elegantly shows how MF-UCB leverages cheap fidelities to quickly discard clearly suboptimal arms (those in sets K(m) for small m) while saving the expensive, high-fidelity budget for the more promising arms. The authors are intellectually honest about the limitations of their analysis, clearly pointing out the small gap between their upper and lower bounds for a specific subset of arms (K(m)7) and explaining the technical reason for it. The proof sketches provide clear intuition for the main theoretical results. The experimental results, though synthetic, effectively corroborate the theoretical claims, demonstrating that MF-UCB outperforms a standard UCB baseline and that its arm-pulling strategy aligns with the theory.
*   **Weaknesses:** The analysis relies on some strong assumptions. The requirement that the bias bounds `ζ(m)` are known a priori may be restrictive for some real-world applications. The paper would be strengthened by a brief discussion on the sensitivity to these parameters or potential avenues for learning them online. Assumption 1, which governs the rate of decay for `ζ(m)`, feels somewhat technical and tailored to the analysis, although the authors fairly note it is not strictly necessary but simplifies the comparison with UCB.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized.

*   **Strengths:** The introduction provides excellent motivation using relatable examples like online advertising and algorithm selection, making the problem formulation easy to grasp. The new cost-weighted regret notion is well-motivated and clearly defined. The MF-UCB algorithm is presented intuitively; the logic for selecting an arm (tightest overall confidence bound) and then selecting a fidelity (based on whether statistical uncertainty exceeds a fidelity-specific threshold) is natural and well-explained. The partitioning of the arm set into K(m) is a key analytical device, and the authors, aided by Figure 1, do a good job of explaining its meaning.
*   **Weaknesses:** No major weaknesses in clarity. The notation is dense but necessary for the topic. The definition of regret in Equation (1) could perhaps benefit from a slightly more expanded textual explanation to ensure the reader fully grasps the role of the two components (unused capital penalty and cumulative cost-weighted regret).

**Originality:** The work is highly original.

*   **Strengths:** The formulation of the multi-fidelity bandit problem for regret minimization is novel and addresses a significant gap in the literature. While the components (bandits, UCB, multi-fidelity optimization) are known, their synthesis into a coherent framework with a new regret notion, a tailored algorithm, and a matching theoretical analysis is a substantial and original contribution. The MF-UCB algorithm itself, with its two-stage decision process for choosing an arm and then a fidelity, is a new algorithmic idea.
*   **Weaknesses:** The algorithm is an adaptation of the well-known UCB principle. However, this is not a significant weakness, as the adaptation is non-trivial and the core novelty lies in the problem formulation and its solution, not in inventing a learning principle from scratch.

**Significance:** The paper has the potential for high impact.

*   **Strengths:** The problem setting is very practical and relevant to a wide range of applications where evaluation costs vary (e.g., scientific experiments, complex simulations, hyperparameter tuning, clinical trials). This work provides the first principled, theoretically-backed approach for navigating the exploration-exploitation tradeoff in such settings. It opens up a new and promising direction for research within the bandit community. Other researchers are very likely to build on these ideas, for instance by extending them to different bandit variants (e.g., linear, contextual) or by working to relax the assumptions made in this paper. The paper demonstrably advances the state of the art by providing a strategy that can significantly outperform standard methods in a realistic and important setting.

### Summary of Pros and Cons for Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Introduces a novel, well-motivated, and practical bandit formulation (multi-fidelity MAB).
*   Proposes an elegant and intuitive algorithm (MF-UCB) that effectively leverages the problem structure.
*   Provides a strong and comprehensive theoretical analysis, establishing near-optimality with matching upper and lower regret bounds.
*   The paper is exceptionally clear, well-written, and easy to follow.
*   High potential for impact, likely to spur significant follow-up research.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The assumption of known bias bounds `ζ(m)` might limit immediate practical deployment in some scenarios, a point that could be discussed more.
*   The work is an extension of the UCB framework, though this is a minor point given the novelty of the problem setting and the non-trivial nature of the extension.

**Recommendation:** This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the multi-armed bandit literature. It identifies and formalizes an important practical problem, provides an effective algorithm, and supports it with rigorous theory. The clarity of presentation is a major plus. I strongly recommend acceptance.
