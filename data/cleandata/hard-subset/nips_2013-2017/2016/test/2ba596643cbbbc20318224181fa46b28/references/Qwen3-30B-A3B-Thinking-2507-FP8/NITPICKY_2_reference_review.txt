

# Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

This paper introduces a multi-fidelity extension to the classical multi-armed bandit problem, where each arm has multiple approximations (fidelities) with different costs and accuracy. The authors propose the Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB) algorithm that uses lower fidelity approximations to eliminate suboptimal arms before investing in higher fidelity evaluations, theoretically achieving better regret than standard UCB when the number of arms with near-optimal rewards is small.

## Strengths
The paper presents a novel formalism for multi-fidelity bandits, which is a natural extension to the classical bandit problem with clear applications in online advertising and algorithm selection. The theoretical analysis provides a regret bound for MF-UCB that demonstrates improved performance over UCB when lower fidelities are significantly cheaper. The lower bound analysis (Theorem 4) provides a useful benchmark, and the experimental results (though limited in detail) support the theoretical claims.

## Weaknesses

**1. Notational inconsistencies and poor clarity:**
- The paper uses "K" both as the number of arms and as the set of arms, causing confusion (e.g., "K = {1, . . . ,K}").
- The notation "µ(M)k" is inconsistent throughout the paper (sometimes "µ^M_k", sometimes "µ(M)k").
- The regret definition contains a clear typo: "R(Λ,A) = Λµ? − N∑ t=1 λ(mt)qt" should be "R(Λ,A) = Λµ? − ∑_{t=1}^N λ(mt)qt".
- The paper refers to "Fig. 1" but provides no figure, making it impossible to verify the claimed partitioning of arms.

**2. Theoretical concerns:**
- The paper claims MF-UCB is "near-optimal" but only matches the lower bound for arms in K(m)3, not for all arms (K(m)7). This is a significant gap that should be acknowledged.
- The paper states "Assumption 1 is not necessary to analyse our algorithm, however, the performance of MF-UCB when compared to UCB is most appealing when the above holds." This is contradictory - if the assumption isn't needed for analysis, why is it important for performance comparison?
- The choice of γ(m) = ψ^{-1} ( λ(m)/λ(m+1) ψ(ζ(m)) ) lacks sufficient justification. The paper claims it's an "optimal tradeoff" but doesn't prove it.

**3. Experimental section deficiencies:**
- The experimental section is severely underdeveloped. The paper states "MF-UCB outperforms UCB on all these problems" but provides no details about the synthetic problems, parameter settings, or how the results were generated (only referring to "Appendix C").
- The paper claims "the gradient of the curve is also smaller than that for UCB" but doesn't provide the actual curves or metrics to support this claim.

**4. Related work and novelty concerns:**
- The paper cites Kandasamy et al. [8] as having "extended ideas in this work to analyse multi-fidelity bandits with Gaussian process payoffs," suggesting significant overlap with prior work by the same authors. This should be more clearly addressed.
- The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its approach from existing multi-fidelity methods in optimization (e.g., Huang et al. [7]) that don't formally analyze regret.

**5. Technical inconsistencies:**
- In the regret definition (Equation 1), the term "r̃(Λ,A)" is described as "a residual term" but the definition doesn't match the description.
- The paper states "ζ(1) > ζ(2) > · · · > ζ(M) = 0" but then refers to "ζ(M) = 0" in the regret bound, which is inconsistent with the definition of ζ(m) as a known quantity characterizing approximations.

## Recommendation

While the paper addresses a meaningful extension to the multi-armed bandit problem with potential practical applications, the significant technical inconsistencies, theoretical gaps, and lack of detail in the experimental section prevent it from being a strong contribution to NIPS. The paper's claim that MF-UCB is "near-optimal" is not fully supported by the analysis, and the theoretical results are not as strong as they could be.

The paper would benefit from:
1. Correcting all notational inconsistencies
2. Providing a clear figure illustrating the arm partitioning
3. Clarifying the relationship with Kandasamy et al. [8]
4. Providing more detailed experimental results in the main text
5. More precisely stating the conditions under which MF-UCB is superior to UCB

Given the above issues, I recommend rejection. While the problem is interesting and the general approach has merit, the paper's technical flaws and incomplete analysis make it unsuitable for publication in NIPS at this stage. The authors would need to substantially revise the paper to address these concerns before it could be considered for publication.
