Summary
This paper introduces a multi-fidelity formulation of the stochastic K-armed bandit where each arm can be queried at multiple fidelities with differing costs and biases. The key modeling assumption is a known fidelity hierarchy with bias bounds |µ(M)k − µ(m)k| ≤ ζ(m), decreasing in m, and known costs λ(1) < … < λ(M). The authors define a cost-weighted pseudo-regret for a fixed capital Λ, where the instantaneous reward is always measured against the highest-fidelity mean. They propose MF-UCB, which maintains UCB-style upper bounds per fidelity with an additive ζ(m) slack and chooses both the arm and the fidelity adaptively via a threshold γ(m) that trades off information gain versus cost. The main results show an upper bound on regret scaling like O((λ(1)K + λ(2)|Kg|) log(Λ/λ(M))) under suitable partitioning of arms by fidelity, improving over standard UCB that only uses the highest fidelity. A lower bound establishes near-optimality except for a specific subset of arms where MF-UCB’s switching rule is conservative. Experiments on synthetic data corroborate the theory.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on classical UCB methods and regret analysis for stochastic bandits, but departs by explicitly modeling biased, cheaper approximations rather than just noisy observations. It distinguishes itself from budgeted bandits (which optimize reward/cost ratios per arm) and from multi-fidelity optimization and RL work that lack regret analyses in this bandit setting. The authors note a GP-based extension in their own follow-up work, but this submission focuses on the finite-armed setting with generic concentration via ψ.

Quality
- Theory: The algorithmic design is principled; the additive ζ(m) in confidence bounds and the fidelity-switching criterion γ(m) = ψ^{-1}(λ(m)/λ(m+1) ψ(ζ(m))) are well-motivated. The upper bound decomposes regret by fidelity-specific partitions K(m), matching the intuition that low fidelities cheaply eliminate clearly suboptimal arms and reserve expensive queries for near-optimal ones. The lower bound (for Bernoulli rewards) validates the rates on a large subset of arms and clarifies the remaining gap. Assumptions (concentration (2), regularity on ζ decay, and Assumption 3 to rule out pathological strategies) are standard-to-reasonable for this line of work, though Assumption 1 may be somewhat strong.
- Correctness: Proof sketches are coherent; the regret decomposition and control of plays across fidelities look sound. The identified suboptimality for K(m)7 arms is transparent.
- Empirics: Simulations show consistent gains and match qualitative predictions (heavy use of low fidelities for clearly suboptimal arms). However, the experimental section is limited to synthetic settings; sensitivity to mis-specified ζ(m) and λ(m) and comparisons to stronger multi-fidelity baselines are missing.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The formalism, notation, and partitioning K(m) are heavy but necessary, and helpful figures/pseudocode are provided. The regret definition is carefully motivated. Minor suggestions:
- Collect all assumptions (on ψ, ζ, λ) in a single subsection for quick reference.
- Provide more intuition/examples for the γ(m) choice (e.g., a simple sub-Gaussian instantiation).
- Clarify robustness when ζ(m) are only upper bounds and potentially loose.

Originality
High. Formalizing bandits with biased multi-fidelity observations and a cost-aware regret that still evaluates performance at the highest fidelity is a substantial conceptual contribution. The MF-UCB design and its fidelity-selection rule are novel, as is the associated analysis and matching lower bound over most arms.

Significance
High. Many practical domains (ads, algorithm selection, simulators) fit this multi-fidelity pattern. The results show clear improvements over single-fidelity UCB in a way that scales with cost ratios and the number of competitive arms. The framework seems fertile for extensions (contextual, continuous fidelities, Thompson Sampling variants, correlated fidelities), and the authors’ lower bound highlights where further algorithmic ideas might close remaining gaps.

Pros
- Fresh problem formalization capturing biased, cheaper proxies with explicit costs.
- Clean, intuitive algorithm with principled fidelity selection.
- Nontrivial theoretical results: regret upper bounds and near-matching lower bounds.
- Clear practical relevance and compelling intuition (cheap elimination of bad arms).
- Transparent discussion of when the algorithm is suboptimal and why.

Cons
- Requires known ζ(m), λ(m), and ψ; robustness to misspecification is not studied.
- Assumption 1 (ζ decay) may be restrictive in practice; utility without it is less clear.
- Lower bound proven for Bernoulli and specific parameter ranges; generality could be expanded.
- Experiments are synthetic and limited in scope; no comparisons to alternative structured bandit baselines (e.g., naive multi-phase filters, cost-aware heuristics).
- The chosen regret notion may not fit settings where low-fidelity interactions have different utilities (though the authors acknowledge this).

Questions and suggestions
- Robustness: How sensitive is MF-UCB to over/under-estimated ζ(m)? Can one adaptively estimate or upper-bound ζ(m) online?
- Continuous fidelities: Can the approach generalize to a continuum of fidelities/costs with a bandit over fidelity choice?
- Correlation: Many multi-fidelity settings have strong cross-fidelity correlations; can leveraging joint models tighten bounds or reduce reliance on ζ(m)?
- Alternative regret: For domains where low-fidelity rewards differ (e.g., simulators), can you formalize and analyze an alternative regret metric?
- Empirics: Please add sensitivity studies for ζ(m), λ(m) mis-specification; comparisons to a simple two-phase screen-then-UCB baseline; and a semi-realistic simulator (e.g., algorithm selection with subsampling).

Recommendation
Accept. The paper proposes a novel and timely formulation and provides a bold yet principled algorithm with strong theoretical backing. Despite limitations in empirical evaluation and some assumptions, the conceptual contribution and analysis are significant and likely to stimulate follow-on work.
