Summary
The paper introduces a multi-fidelity variant of the stochastic K-armed bandit in which each arm admits M fidelities: lower fidelities are cheaper but biased approximations to the highest-fidelity reward distribution. The bias is bounded by known ζ(m), with ζ(1) > ... > ζ(M)=0, and each fidelity has a known cost λ(m) with λ(1) < ... < λ(M). The authors define a cost-weighted pseudo-regret over a capital budget Λ and propose MF-UCB, which maintains per-fidelity UCBs for μ(M) by adding ζ(m) to the confidence radius and selects the arm with the minimum upper bound across fidelities. A fidelity-selection rule uses thresholds γ(m)=ψ^{-1}(λ(m)/λ(m+1)·ψ(ζ(m))) to determine when to move up in fidelity. They give an expected-regret upper bound scaling like log(Λ/λ(M)) with cost-weighted terms that depend on which fidelity suffices to eliminate an arm, and a lower bound (for Bernoulli rewards) showing near-optimality except on a specific subset of arms. Synthetic experiments indicate improvements over standard UCB that ignores low fidelities.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on UCB methods and is related to budgeted bandits and multi-fidelity optimization. The paper differentiates itself by explicitly modeling biased lower-fidelity feedback with known bias envelopes and costs, and by optimizing a cost-weighted regret against the highest fidelity. The connection to budgeted bandits (reward/cost trade-off) and weak/strong labelers is discussed. I would also expect discussion of elimination-style bandits (successive elimination/rejects) and hierarchical testing as natural baselines using low-fidelity screening, as well as bandits with knapsacks and partial monitoring, since these are close in spirit.

Strengths
- Clear problem formalization that explicitly models biased proxies with costs, which is practically motivated.
- Simple algorithmic adaptation of UCB with a principled (if idealized) fidelity-switching criterion.
- Theoretical analysis shows that lower fidelities can provably eliminate many suboptimal arms so that expensive queries focus on near-optimal arms, yielding regret improvements proportional to λ(JkK)/λ(M).
- Lower bound and discussion of the gap pinpoint where suboptimality arises, which is useful and honest.
- Insightful partition-based analysis and high-probability controls on plays per fidelity.

Weaknesses
- Strong knowledge assumptions. The algorithm and guarantees require known bias bounds ζ(m) and the concentration function ψ (and its inverse) to set both indices and γ(m). In practice, ζ(m) are rarely known. There is no robustness analysis to misspecification of ζ or ψ. The guarantees are fragile to these inputs.
- Regret definition is somewhat idiosyncratic. The pseudo-reward qt=μ(M)It regardless of fidelity means low-fidelity plays incur full regret proportional to μ*−μ(M)It while still consuming capital λ(m). This oracle/metric is reasonable for some applications but is not justified empirically, and alternative regret notions (briefly mentioned) could change conclusions materially. The paper does not explore sensitivity to this choice.
- Additional technical assumptions. Assumption 1 imposes a decay condition on ζ(m), and the main bound further assumes ρ>4 and Λ>Λ0. The lower bound is restricted to Bernoulli rewards with μ*∈(1/2,1) and ζ(1)<1/2, and assumes strategies that do not play suboptimal arms polynomially often. Overall, the scope of the lower bound is limited.
- Empirical evaluation is weak. Only synthetic problems are shown; experimental details are in an appendix, and baselines are limited to naive UCB that ignores fidelities. Missing are (i) comparisons to natural multi-stage elimination baselines that use low fidelity to prune arms, (ii) ablations on the number of fidelities, ζ(m) schedules, and cost ratios, and (iii) robustness to ζ mis-specification and to misleading low fidelities (which the model allows). No real-world or semi-realistic simulations are presented.
- Practicality concerns. MF-UCB presumes exact λ(m), ζ(m), and ψ; the fidelity-switch threshold depends on them in a precise way. The paper does not address how to estimate or adapt to these in practice. The analysis shows a gap for K(m)7 arms, and the algorithm design choice that creates the gap is motivated theoretically, but no practical mitigation is proposed.
- Clarity/organization. The notation and partitioning (K(m), J(m)η) are heavy; key intuitions could be streamlined. Some definitions (e.g., qt choice) deserve more justification in the main text. The experiments section is too terse for reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is MF-UCB to mis-specified ζ(m) and ψ? Can you prove robustness guarantees (e.g., regret degrades gracefully when ζ are upper bounds up to constant factors) or propose an adaptive scheme to learn ζ online?
- Can you compare against a two-stage or multi-stage elimination baseline that uses low fidelity(s) to prune arms and then runs standard UCB at the highest fidelity on survivors? This seems the most relevant baseline and would help isolate the benefit of your specific γ(m) schedule.
- Can the lower bound be extended beyond Bernoulli and beyond μ*>1/2? Are there matching upper bounds (up to constants) under milder conditions?
- How does performance change when Assumption 1 is violated? Provide empirical and, if possible, theoretical statements for small M without Assumption 1.
- Any real or semi-real data where fidelity bias bounds are approximately known (e.g., ad A/B tests of varying duration, subsampling in ML model selection)?

Minor comments and suggestions
- Include a brief, self-contained pseudocode for MF-UCB (with all parameters specified) and discuss choice of ρ in practice.
- Clarify that the sets K(m) are analysis constructs (not used by the algorithm).
- Expand the rationale for the regret definition and, if possible, report results under an alternative notion (e.g., fidelity-dependent rewards).
- Fix minor presentation issues (e.g., stray conference header, missing reference details).

Pros
- Interesting and timely problem formalization.
- Clean, simple algorithm with nontrivial analysis.
- Theoretical insights into when and why low fidelities help.

Cons
- Heavy reliance on unrealistic, known bias bounds and distributional knowledge.
- Limited empirical validation and weak baselines; no robustness or real-world evidence.
- Lower bound scope is narrow; gap remains unresolved for an important subset of arms.

Recommendation
Reject. While the theoretical development is careful and the problem is interesting, the method’s assumptions (known ζ and ψ) and the limited empirical validation against strong baselines make the practical significance uncertain. Strengthening the empirical section, adding robustness/adaptivity to unknown ζ, and broadening the lower bound would substantially improve the paper.

Confidence: 4/5
