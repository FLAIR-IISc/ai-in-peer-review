

# Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

## Summary
This paper introduces a multi-fidelity extension to the classical multi-armed bandit problem, where each arm has multiple approximations (fidelities) with varying costs and accuracy. The authors propose a Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB) algorithm that strategically uses cheaper lower-fidelity approximations to eliminate suboptimal arms before committing to expensive high-fidelity evaluations. The paper provides a regret analysis showing MF-UCB achieves better regret bounds than standard UCB when the number of high-reward arms is small and lower fidelities are significantly cheaper.

## Strengths
- The problem formulation addresses an important practical scenario where cheaper approximations to expensive experiments are available.
- The theoretical analysis provides meaningful regret bounds that capture the multi-fidelity trade-off.
- The paper is well-structured with clear definitions and a logical flow from problem formulation to algorithm to analysis.
- The experimental results (briefly described) support the theoretical claims.

## Weaknesses

### Formatting and Notational Inconsistencies
The paper contains several notational inconsistencies that undermine clarity:
- The abstract uses "Kg" as a set but later uses "Kg" as a cardinality (e.g., "When |Kg| < K").
- The partition notation K(m) is used without clear explanation of the meaning of the subscript (e.g., K(1), K(2), K(3), K(4) in Figure 1).
- The notation "JkK" to denote the partition to which arm k belongs is introduced without clear definition or explanation.
- The regret bound (Theorem 2) uses confusing notation "E[R(Λ)] log(nΛ) . ∑..." which should be "E[R(Λ)] = O(log(nΛ) · ∑...)".

### Theoretical Nuances
- The paper claims MF-UCB "outperforms the more standard UCB algorithms" when |Kg| < K and λ(1) < λ(2), but this is not rigorously established. The regret bound shows a potential improvement but doesn't quantify when this improvement is significant or meaningful in practice.
- The choice of γ(m) = ψ⁻¹(λ(m)/λ(m+1) ψ(ζ(m))) is presented without sufficient justification for why this specific form is optimal. The paper states it "represents an optimal tradeoff" but doesn't provide a clear derivation.
- The lower bound analysis (Theorem 4) reveals a gap between MF-UCB and the lower bound for arms in K(m)₇, which the paper acknowledges but doesn't adequately address. The explanation provided ("it might seem tempting to switch based on estimates for ∆(1)k, ∆(2)k, this idea is not desirable") is insufficient to justify why this gap cannot be closed.
- Assumption 1 (ζ(m) decay condition) is crucial for the regret bound comparison to UCB, but the paper doesn't sufficiently explain how often this assumption is satisfied in practice.

### Clarity and Presentation
- The paper's notation is inconsistent throughout (e.g., using "K" for both the set of arms and the number of arms).
- The proof sketch for Theorem 2 is too brief to be convincing. The paper states "The proof is given in Appendix A" but the appendix isn't included in the submission.
- The experimental section lacks detail. The claim "MF-UCB outperforms UCB on all these problems" is unsubstantiated without specific metrics or statistical significance.
- The paper cites Kandasamy et al. [8] as "extending ideas in this work" but this appears to be a self-citation that doesn't properly contextualize the relationship to previous work.

## Overall Assessment
This paper presents a novel extension to the multi-armed bandit problem with practical relevance. However, the numerous notational inconsistencies, unaddressed theoretical gaps, and lack of sufficient experimental detail prevent it from meeting the high standards expected at NIPS. The paper's claim of "near-optimality" is weakened by the gap between the regret bound and the lower bound for certain arms, and the theoretical justification for key design choices (like the choice of γ(m)) is insufficient.

While the problem is interesting and the algorithm has intuitive appeal, the paper fails to provide the level of technical rigor and clarity required for acceptance at NIPS. The authors should address the notational inconsistencies, provide a more thorough justification for their choice of γ(m), and either close the gap in the lower bound analysis or acknowledge it as a limitation rather than a claim of near-optimality.

## Recommendation
Reject

The paper presents an interesting idea but suffers from numerous technical inconsistencies and unaddressed theoretical gaps that undermine its contribution. The notational confusion, incomplete justification of key design choices, and unaddressed gap between the regret bound and lower bound for certain arms are significant flaws that prevent this from being a strong NIPS submission.
