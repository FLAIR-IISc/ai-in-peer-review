Summary
This paper formulates and studies a stochastic K-armed multi-armed bandit setting where each arm can be evaluated at multiple fidelities: lower fidelities are cheaper but provide biased approximations to the high-fidelity (true) reward distribution. The authors introduce a cost-weighted notion of pseudo-regret for a strategy that consumes a capital budget Λ, where the instantaneous pseudo-reward is defined with respect to the high-fidelity mean µ(M) regardless of which fidelity is played, and regret accounts for both the value of the chosen arm and the cost of the fidelity. The core assumptions are (i) known fidelity-dependent bias envelopes |µ(M)k − µ(m)k| ≤ ζ(m), with ζ(1) > ... > ζ(M)=0, and (ii) sub-Gaussian-like concentration (via a general ψ function).

They propose MF-UCB, an Upper Confidence Bound strategy that, for each arm, maintains fidelity-specific upper bounds on µ(M)k by extending the UCB radius at fidelity m with the bias term ζ(m), and uses the minimum across fidelities. MF-UCB selects the arm with the largest bound, then chooses the cheapest fidelity whose statistical uncertainty is still above a fidelity-specific threshold γ(m) tuned to costs and bias (γ(m) ∝ ψ−1((λ(m)/λ(m+1)) ψ(ζ(m))). Intuitively, cheap fidelities are used to eliminate clearly suboptimal arms, while expensive fidelities are reserved for a small set of promising arms.

The main theoretical results are:
- An upper bound on expected regret E[R(Λ)] that decomposes per arm according to the “partition” K(m) of arms that can be eliminated at fidelity m, showing that most cost is incurred at the lowest fidelity that can eliminate the arm. Compared to standard UCB at the highest fidelity, MF-UCB achieves improvement by a factor roughly λ(JkK)/λ(M) for arms that can be eliminated early, under a mild regularity condition (Assumption 1).
- A lower bound (for Bernoulli rewards) demonstrating near-optimality of MF-UCB on a large subset of arms (those not misleading at lower fidelities). The authors identify a gap for arms where lower-fidelity gaps are positive but small (K(m)7), and explain why the MF-UCB switching rule (designed to avoid over-spending at low fidelities) cannot achieve the lower bound there.
- Empirical simulations on synthetic problems show improved regret relative to UCB that ignores fidelities and cost.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on classical UCB and regret analyses (Agrawal; Auer et al.; Lai & Robbins; Bubeck & Cesa-Bianchi). It contrasts with budgeted bandits (Tran-Thanh et al.; Xia et al.) where the goal is reward-per-cost across arms, not multi-fidelity approximations within arms. It is also distinct from multi-fidelity optimization and reinforcement learning literature (Huang et al.; Cutler et al.) which typically lacks bandit-style regret analysis, and from active learning with weak/strong labelers (Zhang & Chaudhuri). The extension to GP bandits with multi-fidelity (Kandasamy et al.) is cited as follow-up.

Strengths
- Novel and well-motivated formalization of multi-fidelity bandits with biased approximations and fidelity-dependent costs.
- Clean, principled algorithm MF-UCB with intuitive design: min across fidelity-specific UCBs and a cost/bias-aware switching rule.
- Theoretical contributions: a regret upper bound that clearly delineates how and where cost is spent; identification of arms eliminated by each fidelity; lower bound establishing near-optimality on a substantial subset; discussion of the residual gap with technical insight.
- The analysis is general in ψ (beyond sub-Gaussian) and addresses the anytime-in-Λ nature by controlling the total number of plays across fidelities.
- Simulations corroborate the theory.

Weaknesses and limitations
- The regret notion is application-appropriate but somewhat specialized: weighting by cost and always valuing the high-fidelity objective even when playing at low fidelity. While justified (e.g., online ads), alternative notions (e.g., fidelity-specific rewards, or zero reward for simulations) are only briefly discussed.
- Requires known fidelity bias envelopes ζ(m). Practical guidance on estimating/validating these bounds and robustness to misspecification is missing.
- Assumption 1 (decay condition on ζ) is used to sharpen comparisons; discussion of performance when it fails is brief.
- Lower bound is restricted to Bernoulli rewards with µ? > 1/2 and ζ(1) < 1/2, plus Assumption 3. Extending to general sub-Gaussian settings would strengthen the contribution.
- Experimental evaluation is limited to synthetic data with few baselines (only “naive” UCB). Empirical sensitivity to ζ/λ, ablations for γ(m), and comparisons to elimination-style algorithms or heuristic multi-fidelity strategies are absent. No real or semi-realistic application.
- Some notational density and occasional presentation issues (e.g., stray sentence fragments, uneven constants/ρ conditions) hinder readability.

Quality
Overall technically sound with careful high-level reasoning and plausible proofs (details deferred to the appendix). The decomposition into K(m), the bounds on plays per fidelity, and the probability control to relate N to nΛ are convincing. The identification of the suboptimality on K(m)7 shows self-awareness of limitations. The lower bound’s constraints and the any-time capital handling could be discussed more fully.

Clarity
Clear motivation and high-level intuition; algorithm is simple to implement. However, notational complexity is high, and key sets (K(m)) and thresholds could be better motivated earlier, with a small running example. Some typos/reference formatting issues should be fixed. Clarify the role of ρ (why ρ > 4 in the main theorem, > 2 in lemmas), and explicitly state constants in the main bounds (at least once).

Originality
To my knowledge, this is the first formal regret analysis for stochastic bandits with multiple biased fidelities and fidelity-dependent costs. The algorithmic design and analysis are novel within the bandit literature, beyond budgeted bandits or multi-fidelity optimization without regret.

Significance
The setting is practically important (ads, algorithm selection, simulation-to-real), and the theory provides actionable insights: use cheap fidelities to eliminate many arms and save expensive evaluations for the few promising ones. The regret improvements over single-fidelity UCB can be substantial when many arms are clearly suboptimal. This can influence future work, including contextual and structured bandits.

Suggestions and questions
- Practicality: How to obtain or learn ζ(m)? Can MF-UCB be adapted to unknown or misspecified ζ(m), e.g., via data-driven calibration or robust confidence radii?
- Lower bound: Can it be extended beyond Bernoulli to general sub-Gaussian families?
- Switching: Is there a way to narrow the K(m)7 gap (e.g., occasional low-fidelity checks informed by cost-benefit estimates) without incurring high higher-fidelity spend?
- Experiments: Include baselines (Successive Rejects/Elimination variants adapted to costs; Thompson sampling variants), sensitivity analyses over λ, ζ, ψ, and at least one realistic task.
- Clarify Assumption 1’s role and provide guidance on when it holds in practice.
- Editorial: fix stray sentences (e.g., the line after Eq. 4 and Fig. 1 caption), unify notation, and complete bibliography details.

Arguments for acceptance
- New, well-motivated problem formulation with clear practical relevance.
- Simple, principled algorithm with strong, interpretable theory and near-optimality on a large class of instances.
- Clear improvement over standard UCB when many arms can be eliminated cheaply.

Arguments against acceptance
- Dependence on known bias bounds and limited discussion of robustness.
- Lower bound scope and experimental evaluation are limited; few baselines and no real data.
- Some clarity/polish issues.

Overall recommendation
I lean toward acceptance. The problem formulation and theoretical contributions are novel and impactful for the community. Strengthening the empirical section and clarifying assumptions would further improve the paper.
