This paper introduces a multi-fidelity extension to the classic multi-armed bandit (MAB) problem, motivated by scenarios where cheaper, approximate evaluations of arm rewards are available. The authors propose a Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB) algorithm and provide a theoretical analysis demonstrating improved regret bounds compared to standard UCB in appropriate settings.

The core idea – leveraging cheaper fidelities to prune suboptimal arms and focus expensive evaluations on promising candidates – is intuitively appealing and addresses a practically relevant problem. The paper is well-structured, clearly presenting the problem formulation, algorithm, theoretical results, and simulation experiments. The connections to prior work are adequately discussed, referencing relevant literature in both bandit algorithms and multi-fidelity optimization.  The introduction effectively motivates the problem and highlights the potential benefits of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty and Relevance:** The multi-fidelity bandit problem is a novel and important extension of the classical MAB framework, directly applicable to various real-world scenarios (e.g., online advertising, algorithm selection, robotics).
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The authors provide a solid theoretical analysis, including a regret bound for MF-UCB and a lower bound demonstrating its near-optimality. The assumptions made are clearly stated.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and organized. The notation is consistent, and the algorithm is presented in a straightforward manner.
* **Empirical Validation:** The simulations provide evidence supporting the theoretical claims and demonstrating the performance improvement of MF-UCB over standard UCB.
* **Well-Connected to Existing Work:**  The authors demonstrate awareness of relevant prior work, not just in bandit algorithms, but also in multi-fidelity optimization and related areas.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumption 1:** Assumption 1 (regarding the decay of ζ(m)) feels somewhat artificial and potentially restrictive. While the authors acknowledge its impact on performance, it’s not entirely clear how often this assumption would realistically hold in practice. A discussion of alternative assumptions or robustness to violations of this condition would be beneficial.
* **Regret Definition:** The chosen regret definition, while motivated, might not be universally appealing.  The authors acknowledge alternative definitions, but a more thorough discussion of the trade-offs and implications of different regret metrics would strengthen the paper.  Specifically, the choice to always reward based on the highest fidelity µ(M) could potentially mask the value of efficiently exploring with lower fidelities.
* **Lower Bound Gap:** The identified gap between the lower bound and the MF-UCB performance for specific scenarios (detailed in Section 5.2) is a legitimate concern. While the authors provide a plausible explanation, further investigation or a tighter lower bound would be desirable.
* **Simulation Details:** While simulations are provided, more detailed information on the specific synthetic problem settings (e.g., the range of µ(m)k values, the cost ratios λ(m)) would enhance reproducibility and allow for a more thorough evaluation of the algorithm's performance.
* **Computational Complexity:** The paper doesn't explicitly discuss the computational complexity of MF-UCB, particularly the overhead associated with maintaining and updating the upper confidence bounds at multiple fidelities. This could be a relevant consideration for practical applications.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper presents a significant and novel contribution to the field of bandit algorithms, addressing a practically relevant problem.
* The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides strong guarantees on the performance of the proposed algorithm.
* The empirical results support the theoretical claims and demonstrate the effectiveness of MF-UCB.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented, making it accessible to a broad audience.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* Assumption 1 may limit the applicability of the theoretical results.
* The regret definition might not be ideal for all applications.
* The gap between the lower bound and the algorithm's performance warrants further investigation.
* The computational complexity of MF-UCB is not explicitly discussed.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper with significant potential. The proposed multi-fidelity bandit framework and the MF-UCB algorithm represent a valuable contribution to the field. While the identified weaknesses are not critical, addressing them would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact.  

**Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Discuss the practical implications of Assumption 1 and explore potential alternatives or robustness analyses.
2.  Provide a more thorough discussion of the regret definition and its implications.
3.  Investigate the gap between the lower bound and the algorithm’s performance, potentially aiming for a tighter lower bound.
4.  Provide more detailed information on the simulation settings.
5.  Discuss the computational complexity of MF-UCB.
