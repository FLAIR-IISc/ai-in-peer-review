Summary
This paper formalizes a stochastic K-armed bandit setting where each arm has multiple “fidelities”: cheaper, biased proxies that approximate the highest-fidelity reward with known bias bounds ζ(m) and costs λ(m). The authors define a cost-weighted pseudo-regret under a capital budget Λ, competing (per unit cost) against an oracle that always allocates capital to the optimal arm. They propose MF-UCB, which maintains UCB-style upper bounds for each arm at each fidelity (adjusted by ζ(m)), selects the arm with the tightest upper bound on µ(M), and chooses a fidelity via thresholds γ(m) that balance bias vs. additional cost-informed information gain. They prove a gap-dependent regret upper bound scaling essentially like O(∑k Δk(M) λ(Jk)/ψ(Δk(Jk)) log(Λ/λ(M))), and provide a lower bound (for Bernoulli rewards) showing MF-UCB is near-optimal for a large subset of arms, with a remaining gap explained by fidelity-switching limitations. Synthetic experiments compare MF-UCB to “naive UCB” and show improved regret and allocation patterns across fidelities.

Relation to prior work
- Builds on classical UCB [Auer, Agrawal, Audibert/Munos/Szepesvári; summarized in Bubeck & Cesa-Bianchi].
- Distinct from budgeted bandits (reward/cost ratio focus).
- Related to multi-fidelity optimization and RL simulators; acknowledges GP-based multi-fidelity bandits [Kandasamy et al.].
- Missing discussion of closely related early-stopping/multi-fidelity selection methods emerging around that time (e.g., Successive Halving, Hyperband), which also use cheaper proxies to filter arms.

Quality
- The theoretical development is mostly sound given the assumptions. The high-level proof techniques (UCB analysis with change-of-measure for lower bounds) are standard and appropriate.
- The regret definition is clearly motivated by applications where lower fidelities are biased proxies, and the decomposition separating the “residual budget” term is reasonable.
- However, the analysis relies on several strong assumptions:
  - Known bias bounds ζ(m) and known concentration ψ (and its inverse) for all fidelities; MF-UCB and γ(m) depend on these. There is no discussion of how to estimate ζ(m) or ψ in practice, sensitivity to misspecification, or robust alternatives.
  - Assumption 1 requires ζ(m) to decay rapidly (geometric-type) for the most appealing comparisons; otherwise the practical advantage may shrink. The paper claims it is “not necessary” but then uses it to dominate terms; this should be clarified.
  - The lower bound is restricted to Bernoulli rewards with µ* > 1/2 and ζ(1) < 1/2, and includes Assumption 3 excluding strategies that play suboptimal arms more than subpolynomially often. This is standard in spirit, but the restricted distributional class and auxiliary assumption limit generality.
- The gap identified for K(m)7 arms is nontrivial; the algorithm may be suboptimal in regimes where it is cost-effective to certify suboptimality at a cheaper fidelity despite small ∆(`). The discussion is honest but leaves open an important case.
- Empirical validation is very limited: only synthetic problems and only a comparison to a naive single-fidelity UCB baseline. No evaluation against stronger baselines (e.g., treating each arm-fidelity pair as a costed arm with appropriate bias correction, cost-aware elimination schemes, or early-stopping/successive halving variants), and no real-world-inspired tasks (e.g., hyperparameter optimization with data sub-sampling or time truncation). No sensitivity analysis to mis-specified ζ(m), λ(m), or to misleading low fidelities.
- Minor technical concern: the statement “UCB achieves regret O(K log n) and is minimax optimal” is imprecise; UCB has gap-dependent O(∑ (log n)/Δk) and gap-independent O(√(Kn log n)) rates; minimax claims in stochastic bandits are more nuanced.

Clarity
- The paper is dense but mostly readable; the intuition for fidelity selection and thresholds is helpful. However:
  - Some notation/formatting is rough (e.g., figure references, broken equations, stray text around Fig. 1).
  - Theorem 2’s displayed bound appears to include two additive terms that look overlapping; the intended final scaling and when each term dominates should be stated more cleanly.
  - The role and necessity of Assumption 1 in the final bound should be made explicit.
  - The introduction uses Kg informally; the body later formalizes K(m); aligning these would help.

Originality
- The formal multi-fidelity bandit model with biased proxies and cost-weighted regret is a useful abstraction, and the MF-UCB design is a natural adaptation of UCB to this model. The lower bound and the identification of the suboptimality gap for certain arms add value.
- That said, the algorithmic core (UCB with fidelity switching thresholds) is conceptually straightforward once the model is posited.

Significance
- Potentially important for domains with clear low-/high-fidelity trade-offs, but the paper stops short of convincing empirical evidence that the approach is robust and impactful in realistic settings.
- The reliance on known ζ(m) and ψ, and the sensitivity to their decay, may limit applicability unless guidance is provided.

Pros
- Clear, principled formulation of multi-fidelity stochastic bandits with biased approximations and costs.
- MF-UCB is simple and well-motivated; the analysis is careful within the model.
- Upper and lower bounds mostly align; honest discussion of the gap.
- Intuitive behavior (cheap fidelities eliminate many arms) is borne out in synthetic plots.

Cons
- Strong modeling assumptions (known ζ(m), ψ; fast-decaying ζ) with no robustness analysis.
- Limited experiments: only synthetic, only naive UCB as baseline; no real or realistic benchmarks; no sensitivity or ablations.
- Lower bound restricted to Bernoulli and uses an auxiliary assumption; practical generality unclear.
- Minor clarity issues and a potentially confusing theorem statement.
- Missing discussion of closely related multi-fidelity selection literature (e.g., successive halving/Hyperband).

Questions for the authors
- How should practitioners obtain ζ(m) and ψ? How sensitive is MF-UCB to over-/under-estimating ζ(m)? Can you provide adaptive/robust variants?
- Can you compare against cost-aware elimination schemes (e.g., successive halving/hyperband-like) and baselines that treat (k,m) as distinct costed arms with bias correction?
- Can the lower bound be extended beyond Bernoulli and without Assumption 3?
- Can you clarify Theorem 2’s bound and precisely where Assumption 1 is used?

Recommendation: Reject
While the theoretical framework is interesting and the analysis is competent, the paper makes strong assumptions that are not addressed empirically, and the experimental validation is too limited to justify acceptance. Strengthening the empirical section with robust baselines and real-world-like tasks, and providing guidance or adaptive methods for unknown ζ(m)/ψ, would substantially improve the work. My confidence is moderate.
