## Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel framework for multi-armed bandits where each arm can be evaluated at multiple fidelities, representing varying costs and approximation qualities. The authors propose the Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB) algorithm, which leverages cheaper, lower-fidelity evaluations to efficiently eliminate suboptimal arms and focus higher-fidelity evaluations on promising candidates. They provide a theoretical analysis demonstrating regret bounds that outperform naive UCB strategies under certain conditions, and validate their approach with simulations. The work builds on classic UCB methods but tackles the new challenges introduced by the multi-fidelity setting.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Problem Formulation:** The multi-fidelity bandit problem is a relevant and practically motivated extension to the standard bandit framework. The scenarios outlined in the introduction (online advertising, algorithm selection) are compelling and highlight the potential impact of this work.
* **Sound Theoretical Analysis:** The regret analysis is rigorous and provides a clear understanding of the algorithm’s performance.  The bound (O ( (λ(1)K + λ(2)|Kg|) log(Λ/λ(2)) )) is well-defined and demonstrably better than standard UCB under the stated assumptions (|Kg| < K and λ(1) < λ(2)).
* **Intuitive Algorithm:** MF-UCB is a natural adaptation of the UCB principle to the multi-fidelity setting, making it relatively easy to understand and implement. The switching criterion based on γ(m) and the associated rationale are well-explained.
* **Empirical Validation:** The simulations confirm the theoretical results and demonstrate the advantage of MF-UCB over standard UCB in relevant scenarios.
* **Clear Writing & Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making it easy to follow the main ideas and technical details. The notation is mostly clear and consistent.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumption 1 (ζ Decay):**  The regularity assumption on the ζ(m) decay (Assumption 1) is somewhat restrictive. While the authors acknowledge its impact, the practical implications of violating this assumption aren't thoroughly discussed.  It limits the applicability of the theoretical guarantees. Is this assumption often met in practice?
* **Lower Bound Gap:** The authors acknowledge a gap between their regret bound and the lower bound (Theorem 4), particularly for arms in K(m)7. This gap, while explained, suggests potential for further improvement and limits the claim of near-optimality. Understanding and closing this gap would strengthen the paper.
* **Limited Empirical Scope:** The simulations are relatively basic.  More comprehensive experiments, especially on more realistic datasets and with varying cost and fidelity parameters, would bolster the empirical validation.  For example, a more thorough exploration of the impact of violating Assumption 1 would be valuable.
* **Discussion of qt Choice:** While the justification for setting qt = µ(M)It is reasonable, a more thorough discussion of alternative choices for qt and their implications (as briefly mentioned at the end of Section 7) would be beneficial.
* **Overly Dense Notation:** While mostly consistent, the paper occasionally suffers from overly dense notation.  A few equations could benefit from more spacing or simplification for increased readability.



**Originality & Significance:**

The work is original in its formalization of the multi-fidelity bandit problem and its theoretical analysis.  While multi-fidelity optimization has been explored in other areas (robotics, optimization), applying this concept to the bandit setting with a focus on regret minimization is novel. The potential significance is considerable.  Efficiently leveraging cheaper approximations is crucial in many real-world applications, and this work provides a solid theoretical and algorithmic foundation for tackling these problems.  The paper advances the state-of-the-art in bandit algorithms by addressing a practically relevant extension of the classic framework.

**Quality & Clarity:**

The paper is technically sound, with a well-defined algorithm and a rigorous regret analysis. The proofs appear correct, and the arguments are generally convincing.  The writing is mostly clear, though occasionally dense. The organization is logical and facilitates understanding.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a novel and practically relevant problem.
* Provides a strong theoretical guarantee with a demonstrably improved regret bound.
* Presents an intuitive and well-motivated algorithm.
* Empirically validates the approach with simulations.
* Clear and well-structured presentation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The restrictive Assumption 1 limits the generalizability of the theoretical results.
* The gap between the regret bound and lower bound weakens the claim of near-optimality.
* The empirical validation could be more comprehensive and realistic.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a strong contribution to the field of bandit algorithms. The problem formulation is insightful, the theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the empirical results are encouraging. While the assumption on ζ(m) decay and the gap in the lower bound are concerns, they do not fundamentally detract from the paper’s overall quality and significance.

**Recommendation:** **Accept** with minor revisions. 

I recommend the authors address the following in a revision:

1.  **Discuss the practical implications of violating Assumption 1.** Provide examples where it might not hold and discuss potential workarounds or extensions to the algorithm that could mitigate this issue.
2.  **Further investigate the gap between the regret bound and lower bound.** Even a discussion of potential directions for closing this gap would be valuable.
3. **Expand the empirical evaluation.** Include experiments with more realistic datasets and a wider range of parameter settings.
