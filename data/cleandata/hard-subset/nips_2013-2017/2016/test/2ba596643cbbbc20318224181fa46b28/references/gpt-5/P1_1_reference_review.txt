Summary and contributions
The paper introduces a new multi-fidelity variant of the stochastic K-armed bandit. Each arm admits M fidelities: lower fidelities are cheaper to query but provide biased approximations of the highest-fidelity reward; the bias is bounded by known fidelity-specific tolerances ζ(m) that decrease with m. The authors define a cost-weighted pseudo-regret for a policy that expends total capital Λ: the benchmark receives µ* per unit cost regardless of fidelity, while the learner receives the highest-fidelity mean µ(M)
It for the chosen arm It at any fidelity, penalized by the cost paid at that time. This leads to a decomposition where regret against arm k accumulates proportionally to the total cost allocated to that arm times ∆(M)
k.

They propose MF-UCB, an extension of UCB that maintains an upper confidence bound on µ(M)
k by combining empirical means from multiple fidelities and adding ζ(m) as a bias correction, then selects arms by the tightest upper bound. A fidelity-selection rule escalates from lower to higher fidelity when the confidence radius at fidelity m falls below a threshold γ(m) chosen to balance cost and bias: γ(m)=ψ^{-1}(λ(m)/λ(m+1)·ψ(ζ(m))). Under standard concentration (2) and a regularity assumption on the ζ(m) sequence (Assumption 1), they prove an expected regret bound (Theorem 2) showing that MF-UCB pays roughly λ(JkK)/ψ(∆(JkK)
k) per suboptimal arm k, where JkK is the lowest fidelity that can eliminate k, while investing only O(1) at higher fidelities for clearly suboptimal arms. This compares favorably to standard UCB, which must pay λ(M)/ψ(∆(M)
k) for every arm at the most expensive fidelity. A lower bound (Theorem 4), for Bernoulli rewards under a mild non-wastefulness assumption (Assumption 3), matches MF-UCB on a substantial subset of arms (K(m)
3) and identifies a gap on arms where earlier fidelities are only marginally informative (K(m)
7). Synthetic experiments illustrate improved regret slopes and reduced high-fidelity usage relative to UCB.

Relation to prior work
The work is conceptually related to budgeted/bandits-with-resource constraints (e.g., budgeted MAB, BwK), but differs by modeling multiple biased fidelities per arm with known bias bounds and distinct costs. It also relates to multi-fidelity optimization (e.g., Huang et al., Rajnarayan et al.) and to multi-fidelity RL simulations, but those do not treat online exploration-exploitation regret. The MF-UCB design draws from optimism-based UCB literature and the analysis uses standard concentration tools. It complements the authors’ later GP-based multi-fidelity bandits by addressing the discrete-arm case. Missing but relevant references include “Bandits with Knapsacks” (Badanidiyuru et al., 2013) as a neighboring framework for cost-aware bandits.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The regret definition is internally consistent and allows a clean decomposition. The arm partition K(m) and fidelity-switching thresholds are well-motivated, and the upper bound captures the key intuition: use cheap fidelities to eliminate many arms, reserving expensive plays for near-optimal arms. The lower bound is appropriate for Bernoulli rewards and explains where MF-UCB is near-optimal and where it can be loose.
- Assumptions: The need for known bias bounds ζ(m) and concentration function ψ, and Assumption 1 (roughly exponential decay of ζ(m) in the sub-Gaussian case), may be strong in practice but are clearly stated. Assumption 3 for the lower bound restricts to “reasonable” algorithms; while standard in spirit, it should be discussed more.
- Proof sketch: The main ideas (arm partitioning, high-probability control of plays at each fidelity, and bounding total plays N relative to nΛ) are clear. Some notational slips (e.g., Theorem 2 expressions and double sums) could be clarified.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and readable, with a clear algorithm description and sensible intuitions for the fidelity-switching rule. The regret definition is unusual; the discussion that motivates rewarding by µ(M) at any fidelity is helpful, but it would benefit from an explicit comparison to alternative definitions (beyond a brief note in Section 7), including what would change in the analysis. Figures illustrating partition sets K(m) and fidelity usage are helpful. A careful proofreading is needed for minor typos, reference formatting, and to streamline notation in Section 4.

Originality
Modeling multi-fidelity pulls per arm with known bias bounds and fidelity-specific costs, and designing a UCB-style policy that adaptively escalates fidelities with provable cost-weighted regret improvements, is novel to the best of my knowledge. The lower bound and the near-optimality characterization (except on K(m)
7) are also new contributions.

Significance
The setting is practically relevant (ads, algorithm selection, simulations vs. real trials). The theoretical results show meaningful improvements over single-fidelity baselines when lower fidelities can eliminate many arms cheaply. This is likely to stimulate subsequent work (indeed, it has in GP bandits). The empirical section supports the theory but is limited in scope.

Pros
- Clear, well-motivated new model for multi-fidelity bandits with costs and bias bounds.
- Simple and principled algorithm (MF-UCB) with interpretable switching rule.
- Theoretical guarantees: nontrivial upper bound versus UCB and matching lower bound on a subset of instances.
- Good intuition: high-cost plays are focused on a small set of near-optimal arms.

Cons and suggestions
- Practicality of known ζ(m): In many applications bias bounds are unknown or arm-dependent. Discuss robustness and propose adaptive estimation or conservative envelopes; analyze sensitivity to misspecification.
- Assumption 1 (rapidly decaying ζ) is strong; comment on performance when it fails, and whether alternative γ(m) choices trade off robustness vs. tightness.
- Lower bound only for Bernoulli and under Assumption 3; discuss extension to general sub-Gaussian rewards and whether the K(m)
7 gap can be narrowed with modified policies.
- Empirics are limited: only synthetic, few baselines. Include KL-UCB/Thompson variants, and a “multi-start UCB-at-top-fidelity” budget baseline; show sensitivity to λ and ζ; include experiments with mis-specified ζ; add a realistic case study (e.g., simulated ad impressions or ML algorithm selection with subsampling).
- Relation to BwK/budgeted bandits should be clarified, including why those frameworks do not directly cover multi-fidelity bias and how your regret notion compares to reward-per-cost optimization.
- The regret definition rewards by µ(M)
It for lower-fidelity pulls. While motivated, it is application-specific. A short appendix contrasting regret definitions (including cases where lower-fidelity rewards “don’t count”) would broaden applicability.

Questions for authors
- Can MF-UCB be adapted when ζ(m) are unknown and possibly arm-dependent? Could one maintain empirical cross-fidelity discrepancies to learn ζ online?
- How sensitive is performance to mis-specified ζ(m) or ψ? Any guarantees under overestimation/underestimation?
- Could the fidelity-switch rule incorporate estimated ∆(`)
k while still controlling the total high-fidelity plays, to shrink the K(m)
7 gap?
- How does MF-UCB relate to BwK if we treat λ as resource consumption and reward as µ(M)? Are there reductions either way?

Recommendation
This is a strong and timely paper with a clean algorithm, solid theory, and a well-motivated problem. With improved empirical evaluation and a more explicit discussion of assumptions (ζ, ψ, Assumption 1) and related frameworks (BwK), it would be an excellent NIPS contribution. I lean toward acceptance.
