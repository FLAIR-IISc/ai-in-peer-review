## Peer Review: The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel multi-fidelity extension to the classical K-armed bandit problem. The authors motivate this extension with real-world scenarios where obtaining precise reward estimates is costly, but cheaper, approximate estimates are available. They formally define a cost-weighted regret metric for this setting and present the Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB) algorithm, which intelligently allocates resources between different fidelities. The core idea is to use lower fidelities to quickly eliminate poorly performing arms, concentrating higher-fidelity evaluations on a promising subset.  The paper provides a theoretical analysis demonstrating the algorithm’s performance, showing it can outperform naive UCB strategies when the cost of higher fidelities is significant and lower fidelities provide useful information.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The authors correctly position their work within the extensive literature on multi-armed bandits, acknowledging the foundational work of Robbins and the development of UCB algorithms. They distinguish themselves from related work in multi-fidelity optimization, reinforcement learning, and budgeted bandits, highlighting the specific focus on the exploration-exploitation trade-off in a bandit setting *with biased fidelity approximations*.  The related work section is well-organized and relevant, citing key papers in each area.  The connection to Kandasamy et al. [8] is particularly important, as it shows awareness of recent progress in multi-fidelity bandits, while demonstrating the unique contribution of addressing the biased fidelity issue.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The multi-fidelity bandit setting with *biased* approximations is a genuinely novel contribution.  Addressing the inherent bias in lower-fidelity estimates, rather than treating them as noisy observations, is a crucial step forward.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a solid theoretical analysis, establishing regret bounds for the MF-UCB algorithm. The derivation of these bounds seems carefully done, and the comparison to the UCB baseline is insightful.
* **Clear Formalization:** The cost-weighted regret metric is well-defined and appropriate for the problem setting. The assumptions regarding fidelity accuracy (ζ(m)) and cost (λ(m)) are clearly stated.
* **Intuitive Algorithm:** The MF-UCB algorithm is logically sound and easy to understand. The switching criterion between fidelities, based on confidence bound width and cost, appears well-motivated.
* **Empirical Validation:** While simulations are relatively simple (details in appendix), they demonstrate that MF-UCB outperforms naive UCB, supporting the theoretical findings.
* **Detailed Analysis:** The extensive analysis, including the partitioning of arms into K(m) sets, provides deep insights into the algorithm’s behavior and its performance guarantees.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions:** The assumption of known ζ(m) values might be unrealistic in some applications.  While the paper acknowledges this, exploring scenarios where ζ(m) must be estimated would strengthen the work.  Assumption 1 is a bit restrictive and its practical impact could be explored further.
* **Lower Bound Gap:** The authors honestly admit a gap between their upper bound (MF-UCB's performance) and the lower bound. While they provide a plausible explanation, closing this gap would be a valuable extension. The conditions under which the gap is significant should be characterized.
* **Simulation Scope:** The simulations presented are somewhat limited in scope. Expanding the experiments to include more complex scenarios, different reward distributions, and a wider range of parameter settings would bolster the empirical evaluation.
* **Clarity of Notation:** While generally clear, some notation is dense (e.g., the definitions of K(m)). A more gradual introduction of notation and potentially a table summarizing key symbols would improve readability.
* **Discussion of Practicality:** While the paper highlights motivating applications, a deeper discussion of the practical challenges of applying MF-UCB in real-world settings (e.g., choosing appropriate fidelities, estimating ζ(m)) would be beneficial.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound contribution to the field of bandit optimization.
* The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides meaningful insights into the algorithm's performance.
* The MF-UCB algorithm is well-motivated, intuitive, and demonstrably outperforms the baseline UCB strategy.
* The paper addresses a practical problem with broad applicability.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The assumptions made regarding fidelity accuracy (ζ(m)) might limit the applicability of the work.
* The gap between the upper and lower bounds leaves room for improvement.
* The simulations are somewhat limited in scope.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of bandit optimization. While there are some minor weaknesses, the novelty, theoretical rigor, and potential impact of the work outweigh these concerns. The problem addressed is relevant, the proposed solution is well-designed, and the analysis is convincing.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Discuss the sensitivity of the results to the assumption of known ζ(m) values and potentially explore methods for estimating these values.
2.  Further investigate and potentially characterize the conditions under which the gap between the upper and lower bounds is significant.
3.  Expand the simulation studies to include more diverse scenarios and parameter settings.
4.  Improve the clarity of notation and consider adding a table summarizing key symbols.
5.  Add a discussion of the practical challenges of implementing MF-UCB in real-world applications.
