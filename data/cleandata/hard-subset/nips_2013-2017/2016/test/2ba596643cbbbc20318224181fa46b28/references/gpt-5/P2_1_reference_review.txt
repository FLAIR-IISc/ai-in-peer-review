Summary and relation to prior work
The paper introduces a stochastic K-armed bandit setting where each arm has multiple fidelities: lower-fidelity observations are cheaper but biased approximations of the highest-fidelity reward. Each pull at fidelity m incurs a cost lambda(m); lower fidelities have larger bias bounds zeta(m), assumed known and decreasing to zero at the highest fidelity. The authors define a cost-weighted pseudo-regret under a fixed budget Lambda that rewards the algorithm as if it had received the highest-fidelity reward of the chosen arm, regardless of the fidelity actually played. They propose MF-UCB, which maintains per-fidelity UCBs inflated by the bias bounds, selects the arm via the minimum of these upper bounds, and adaptively chooses which fidelity to query using a threshold gamma(m) that balances informativeness versus cost. Theoretical results include an upper bound on regret that improves over vanilla UCB by investing most of its budget on cheap fidelities for clearly suboptimal arms, and a lower bound (for Bernoulli rewards) showing MF-UCB is near-optimal except for a specific subset of arms where small positive gaps at lower fidelities suggest more exploration should happen cheaply.

This connects to and extends classical UCB-based stochastic bandits to a multi-fidelity, cost-aware setting. It differs from budgeted bandits (which optimize reward/cost per pull, not hierarchical approximations), from active learning with weak/strong labelers, and from multi-fidelity optimization in continuous domains. The closest precedents are multi-fidelity methods in optimization and RL simulators, but those generally lack regret analysis in the bandit sense. The paper’s framework and results appear to be the first principled treatment for K-armed bandits with biased, multi-cost approximations.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The algorithmic design is coherent: upper bounds incorporate bias allowances; the fidelity-selection rule via gamma(m) is motivated by a clean cost–information trade-off formalized using the concentration function psi and cost ratios. The analysis yields a regret bound that decomposes by arms into contributions largely paid at the cheapest fidelity that can eliminate the arm, and shows MF-UCB is never much worse than standard UCB and can be significantly better when many arms are clearly suboptimal at low fidelity. The lower bound is constructed via a change-of-measure argument on Bernoulli rewards and identifies an intrinsic gap where MF-UCB’s switching rule is conservative. Overall, the arguments appear careful, with proofs deferred to the appendix; the high-level proof sketches are plausible.
- Assumptions: Key assumptions are strong but reasonable for a first formalization: known and decreasing bias bounds zeta(m); per-fidelity sub-exponential concentration with known psi; and a regularity condition on the decay of zeta to avoid degenerate fidelity ladders. The lower bound additionally assumes Bernoulli rewards and specific parameter ranges. These assumptions are clearly stated and their roles discussed. However, the need to know zeta(m) and psi, and to tune rho, may limit direct applicability; robustness to misspecification is not studied.
- Empirics: Synthetic experiments show consistent improvements over UCB and qualitatively match the theory (cheap filtering of clearly suboptimal arms). The experimental section is brief; more comprehensive ablations and sensitivity analyses would strengthen the empirical story.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized and readable for experts in bandits. The problem setup and regret definition are clearly motivated; the algorithm is simple to implement given zeta and psi. Notation is heavy but mostly systematic. The partitioning K(1),…,K(M) and the role of gamma(m) are explained with intuition and an illustrative figure. Some suggestions:
- Provide more guidance for setting or estimating zeta(m) in practice; discuss consequences of misspecification.
- Briefly re-derive the form of gamma(m) in the main text (beyond the intuition) to aid readers who may not consult the appendix.
- Expand the empirical section to show sensitivity to lambda, zeta, and M; include baselines such as a naive multi-fidelity heuristic or Thompson Sampling variants.

Originality
The combination of a multi-fidelity, biased-observation model with cost-weighted regret and a principled UCB-style algorithm/analysis appears novel for K-armed bandits. The paper clearly differentiates from budgeted bandits and multi-fidelity optimization works, and situates the contribution within UCB literature. The lower bound and near-optimality characterization further enhance originality.

Significance
The formalism is compelling for applications where cheap biased proxies are available (advertising, AutoML, simulations). The main insight—use low-cost biased evaluations to prune obviously suboptimal arms—yields provable improvements over standard approaches and is likely to influence follow-on work (e.g., contextual/linear bandits, GP bandits, RL with simulators). While the reliance on known bias bounds may constrain immediate deployment, the theoretical framing and near-optimality make this a meaningful advance.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated problem formalization with cost-aware regret aligned to high-fidelity objectives.
- Simple, principled algorithm with interpretable fidelity selection.
- Solid theoretical guarantees: upper bounds improving over UCB, and a matching lower bound up to a well-characterized gap.
- Good intuition linking cost, bias, and where to spend exploration effort.

Weaknesses
- Requires known bias bounds and concentration function; no robustness analysis to misspecification.
- Regularity condition on zeta decay is somewhat ad hoc; scope when violated is not deeply explored.
- Lower bound restricted to Bernoulli rewards; generalization is not discussed.
- Empirical evaluation is limited to synthetic data; lacks comparisons to additional multi-fidelity baselines and sensitivity studies.

Questions for authors
- How sensitive is MF-UCB to over- or under-estimation of zeta(m)? Can one adaptively estimate or upper-bound zeta?
- Can the lower bound be extended to sub-Gaussian rewards with general psi?
- Would a variant that occasionally tests lower fidelities longer (to cover the K(m)7 case) close the optimality gap without incurring large cost?
- Could lambda and zeta vary per arm in practice? How would MF-UCB adapt?

Pros
- Novel setting and timely problem
- Clean algorithm with strong theory
- Intuitive and practically relevant design

Cons
- Practical deployability depends on knowing zeta(m)
- Empirics could be stronger
- Some assumptions limit generality of the lower bound

Overall evaluation
The paper meets the NeurIPS criteria well: it is technically solid, clearly written, original, and addresses an important and practical problem with rigorous analysis. I recommend acceptance, with a request to improve the empirical section and to discuss robustness to bias misspecification.
