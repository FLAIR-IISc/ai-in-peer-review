- Introduces a novel multi-fidelity extension to the multi-armed bandit problem with cheaper, biased approximations available for arm evaluation.
- Proposes the MF-UCB algorithm that dynamically allocates evaluations across fidelities to efficiently learn optimal arms.
- Theoretical analysis yields a regret bound that outperforms naive UCB when lower fidelities are much cheaper and useful for pruning.
- Core strategy: use low-fidelity evaluations for exploration and pruning, reserving high-fidelity evaluations for a small candidate set.
- Situates work within MAB/UCB literature and related multi-fidelity optimization, highlighting novelty in regret-minimization.
- References to budgeted bandits, active learning with weak/strong labelers, and prior GP-bandit work provide useful context.
- Novelty: multi-fidelity bandit framework is practical and impactful for many real-world tasks (advertising, hyperparameter tuning, etc.).
- Theoretical rigor: clear proofs and a solid mathematical foundation for MF-UCB’s performance claims.
- Intuitive algorithm: MF-UCB is a natural extension of UCB with a well-motivated dynamic fidelity selection.
- Presentation is clear and well-organized, with helpful notation and illustrative figures (e.g., arm partitioning).
- Empirical validation shows MF-UCB outperforming naive UCB, though most details are relegated to the appendix.
- Assumption of known ζ(m) (error bounds between fidelities) is restrictive and may be hard to estimate in practice.
- The practical implications and frequency of violating the regularity condition (Assumption 1) are not fully explored.
- A gap remains between the regret bound and the lower bound for arms with small post-first-fidelity gaps, weakening theoretical tightness.
- The design of the K(m) sets is cited as an explanation for the lower-bound gap but needs deeper analysis.
- Experimental details are limited; more information on problem instances, parameters, and statistical significance is needed.
- The choice and role of qt (set to µ(M)I) are justified but alternative choices and their effects are not sufficiently discussed.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel problem, rigorous theory, well-motivated algorithm, clear writing, and empirical gains.
- Arguments against acceptance: restrictive ζ(m) assumption and regularity condition, regret vs. lower-bound gap, and limited experimental detail.
- Overall assessment: reviewer recommends “strong accept” despite noted assumptions and theoretical gaps due to novelty and impact.
- Recommended improvements: analyze sensitivity to ζ(m) estimation errors and the practical effects of Assumption 1.
- Recommended improvements: attempt to close or better explain the regret vs. lower-bound gap and the impact of K(m) design.
- Recommended improvements: provide more extensive experimental results with parameter details and statistical significance.
- Recommended improvements: expand discussion of alternative qt choices and implications for regret and algorithm behavior.
