

# Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

This paper introduces a novel multi-fidelity extension to the classic multi-armed bandit problem, where each arm has access to multiple approximations (fidelities) with varying costs and accuracy levels. The authors propose MF-UCB, an algorithm that leverages cheaper lower-fidelity evaluations to eliminate suboptimal arms before investing in expensive high-fidelity evaluations. The paper makes a significant contribution to the bandit literature by formalizing this setting and providing a rigorous regret analysis.

The paper's main strengths lie in its clear problem formulation, comprehensive theoretical analysis, and empirical validation. The authors carefully define the multi-fidelity regret metric that accounts for both the cost of evaluations and the reward structure. Their partitioning of arms into sets K(m) (based on fidelity-specific confidence intervals) provides excellent intuition for how the algorithm allocates resources across fidelities. The regret bound in Theorem 2 is well-motivated and shows MF-UCB achieves O((λ(1)K + λ(2)|Kg|)log(Λ/λ(2))) regret, which can significantly outperform standard UCB when lower fidelities are cheaper and can identify promising arms.

The theoretical analysis is particularly strong. The authors derive a lower bound in Theorem 4 that demonstrates MF-UCB is near-optimal for arms in K(m)₃, and their proof sketches provide valuable insights into the algorithm's behavior. The empirical results in Section 6 corroborate the theoretical claims, showing MF-UCB outperforms UCB across multiple synthetic problems.

However, the paper has several notable weaknesses that limit its impact. First, the regularity condition in Assumption 1 (requiring ζ(m) to decay sufficiently fast) is quite strong and may not hold in many practical applications. While the authors acknowledge this, they don't provide sufficient justification for why this condition is reasonable or how common it is in real-world settings. The condition essentially requires that the improvement in fidelity is proportional to the cost increase, which may not be the case in many applications.

Second, the paper identifies a theoretical gap for arms in K(m)₇ (where arms are misidentified at lower fidelities), and the authors acknowledge MF-UCB doesn't meet the lower bound for these arms. The explanation for this gap is reasonable, but the paper doesn't propose a solution or even discuss whether this gap can be closed with a different algorithmic approach.

Third, the related work section is somewhat superficial. While the authors mention Kandasamy et al. [8] (a follow-up paper), they don't thoroughly compare with other multi-fidelity optimization methods that might be relevant to the bandit setting. The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of how their work relates to existing multi-fidelity optimization literature.

The paper also lacks discussion of practical implementation considerations. For instance, it doesn't address how to choose the parameter ρ, or what to do when ζ(m) values are unknown (which would be the case in most real applications). The assumption that ζ(m) are known is a significant limitation for practical deployment.

The paper is well-written and technically sound, but the strong assumptions and theoretical gap reduce its impact. The authors make a good case for the theoretical value of their approach, but the practical applicability is limited by the restrictive assumptions.

In terms of significance, the multi-fidelity bandit problem is highly relevant to many real-world applications (online advertising, hyperparameter tuning, algorithm selection), so the problem addressed is important. However, the paper's contribution is somewhat constrained by its assumptions, which may limit how broadly it can be applied.

To strengthen the paper, I would recommend:
1. Providing more discussion on when Assumption 1 holds in practice
2. Addressing the theoretical gap for arms in K(m)₇
3. Adding a more thorough comparison with related multi-fidelity optimization methods
4. Discussing practical implementation considerations

Overall, this is a solid theoretical contribution that extends the bandit literature in a meaningful way. While the assumptions limit its immediate practical applicability, the theoretical insights are valuable. The paper would be significantly stronger with more discussion of the assumptions' practical relevance and the theoretical gap.

**Recommendation**: Accept (with minor revisions to address the weaknesses noted above)

**Summary of strengths**: Novel problem formulation, strong theoretical analysis, clear intuition, empirical validation.

**Summary of weaknesses**: Strong assumptions (particularly Assumption 1), theoretical gap for certain arms, limited discussion of practical implementation.
