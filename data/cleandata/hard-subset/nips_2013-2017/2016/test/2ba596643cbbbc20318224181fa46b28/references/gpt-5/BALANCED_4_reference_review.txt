Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces a stochastic multi-armed bandit setting where each arm can be queried at multiple fidelities m = 1,…,M. Lower fidelities are cheaper (costs λ(m) increasing in m) but biased approximations to the highest-fidelity reward distribution θ(M) with means satisfying known deviation bounds |µ(M)k − µ(m)k| ≤ ζ(m) (ζ decreasing, ζ(M)=0). The authors define a cost-weighted pseudo-regret under a spending budget Λ in which the oracle accrues µ? per unit cost at any fidelity, and the learner is credited with µ(M)It regardless of which fidelity is played. They propose MF-UCB: maintain UCBs at each fidelity, B(m) = empirical mean + uncertainty + ζ(m), take the minimum across fidelities Bk = minm B(m) as an upper bound on µ(M)k, choose the arm with largest Bk, and then choose the lowest fidelity whose uncertainty has not yet fallen below a threshold γ(m) that trades off cost and informativeness. They analyze regret, partitioning arms into sets K(m) that can be eliminated at fidelity m, and show MF-UCB concentrates expensive queries on few near-optimal arms. A lower bound (for Bernoulli rewards under a regularity assumption) shows near-optimality on a subset of arms; a gap remains for certain “borderline” arms. Simulations on synthetic problems show improvements over UCB run only at the highest fidelity.

This framework is distinct from budgeted bandits (which optimize reward-to-cost ratios) and from multi-fidelity optimization in BO/GP settings; it formalizes multi-fidelity for cumulative regret with biased low-fidelity signals. It builds on UCB methods [Auer; Audibert et al.; Bubeck & Cesa-Bianchi] and relates to multi-fidelity optimization [Huang et al.; Rajnarayan et al.] and MF-GP bandits [Kandasamy et al.], while addressing a different objective.

Strengths
- Novel, well-motivated formulation of multi-fidelity stochastic bandits with explicit bias bounds and fidelity-dependent costs.
- Clean algorithmic idea: min over fidelity-specific UCBs plus an adaptive fidelity-selection rule with principled γ(m) derived from cost/bias tradeoffs.
- Theoretical results: a regret upper bound that improves over standard UCB by replacing λ(M) with λ(JkK) for many suboptimal arms; lower bound showing near-optimality on a broad class of arms; high-level proof techniques are sound (confidence-based elimination across fidelities, careful accounting of N vs nΛ).
- Clear intuition: lower fidelities cheaply eliminate clearly suboptimal arms; expensive plays focus on “hard” arms.
- Simulations corroborate the theory’s qualitative predictions.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Assumptions may limit applicability:
  - Known bias bounds ζ(m) and concentration function ψ for all fidelities; performance and correctness under misspecification are not addressed.
  - Assumption 1 (fast decay of ζ relative to ψ) is needed for the most appealing comparisons; its practical reasonableness is unclear and not empirically examined.
- Regret definition: crediting low-fidelity pulls with µ(M)It is appropriate for some applications (e.g., short-run ads approximating long-run click-through) but not others; while the paper discusses alternatives in the conclusion, the main results hinge on this choice.
- Lower bound holds for Bernoulli rewards with µ? > 1/2, ζ(1) < 1/2 and under Assumption 3 (suboptimal arms played sub-polynomially). The necessity and breadth of Assumption 3 are not fully justified; the gap for K(m)7 arms is acknowledged but unresolved.
- Experimental evaluation is limited to synthetic settings and a single baseline (UCB at M). Missing comparisons to other plausible strategies (e.g., UCB/TS variants that combine fidelities, hierarchical elimination, or best-arm identification style successive halving adapted to costs). No sensitivity analysis to ζ(m), λ(m), or robustness to misspecified ζ.
- Some clarity issues: notation-heavy with dense definitions (e.g., sets K(m), J(m)η); Figure references and text snippets appear truncated; references lack complete bibliographic details.

Evaluation against NeurIPS criteria
- Quality: Technically solid in the upper-bound analysis; the fidelity selection rule is well motivated. Lower bound is meaningful but restricted and leaves an open gap. Empirical section, while positive, is limited.
- Clarity: Generally clear but dense; would benefit from more intuition around γ(m) and the K(m) partition, a worked example, and tighter presentation (fix figure captions, complete references).
- Originality: High. The precise multi-fidelity cumulative regret formalism and analysis appear new.
- Significance: Potentially impactful for applications where biased, cheap proxies exist and bias bounds are known/estimable. Practical impact could be tempered by reliance on known ζ(m) and by limited experiments.

Questions for the authors
1. How sensitive is MF-UCB to misspecified ζ(m)? Can you provide robustness guarantees (e.g., if ζ̂(m) ≥ ζ(m) but loose), or adaptive estimation of ζ(m)?
2. Can your analysis extend to sub-exponential/heavy-tailed rewards (different ψ), or to unknown ψ?
3. How critical is Assumption 1 in practice? Could MF-UCB adapt its fidelity thresholds when successive fidelities are only marginally more informative?
4. For the lower bound, is Assumption 3 necessary beyond excluding pathological strategies? Can you give an instance class where the K(m)7 gap is provably unavoidable for any “practical” algorithm?
5. Can you compare against additional baselines, such as Thompson Sampling at M, or simple two-stage schemes (low-fidelity screening then high-fidelity UCB), and discuss computational overhead?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a robustness/sensitivity study to ζ(m), λ(m), and ρ; discuss practical ways to set ρ and estimate ψ.
- Provide an ablation or heuristic that inflates ζ(m) adaptively (safe but conservative) and report performance.
- Clarify the regret definition vis-à-vis other application regimes; include a short appendix showing how the algorithm or analysis would change if low-fidelity plays yield their own rewards.
- Improve exposition of the K(m) partition with a concrete toy example and a clearer figure; complete references.
- Expand experiments: more problem instances, broader baselines, and report the distribution of plays across fidelities and arms; include wall-clock/cost-normalized performance.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and well-motivated problem formulation with clear practical relevance.
- Solid theoretical contribution with interpretable improvement over standard UCB and a meaningful lower bound.
- Simple, implementable algorithm derived from principled insights.

Arguments against acceptance
- Strong assumptions (known ζ, ψ; Assumption 1) with no robustness analysis.
- Lower bound’s restrictions and unresolved optimality gap for a subset of arms.
- Limited empirical validation and baseline comparisons; clarity issues in presentation.

Recommendation: Weak accept
The paper offers a timely and original formalization with a clear algorithm and substantial analysis. Addressing robustness and expanding experiments would strengthen it further.
