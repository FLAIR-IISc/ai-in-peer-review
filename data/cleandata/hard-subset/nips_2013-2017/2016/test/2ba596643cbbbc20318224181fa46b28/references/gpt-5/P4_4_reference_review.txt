Summary and relation to prior work
This paper formulates and studies a stochastic K-armed bandit setting where each arm admits multiple “fidelities” (M levels), each cheaper but more biased relative to the highest-fidelity reward distribution. The key modeling assumptions are: (i) known fidelity-dependent cost λ(m) with λ(1) < … < λ(M), (ii) known fidelity-dependent bias bounds |µ(M)k − µ(m)k| ≤ ζ(m) with ζ(m) decreasing and ζ(M) = 0, and (iii) sub-exponential concentration with generic ψ in the tail bound. The objective is a capital-weighted cumulative pseudo-regret under a total budget Λ, defined so that the reward of any play is evaluated at the highest-fidelity mean µ(M), while costs are paid according to the fidelity queried.

The proposed algorithm, MF-UCB, maintains UCB-style indices for each arm at each fidelity:
B(m)k,t = X̄(m)k + ψ−1(ρ log t / T(m)k ) + ζ(m), and chooses the arm with the smallest across-fidelity upper bound Bk,t = minm B(m)k,t. It then selects the fidelity by escalating from low to high fidelities until the confidence radius at level m falls below a threshold γ(m) = ψ−1( (λ(m)/λ(m+1)) ψ(ζ(m)) ), or reaches m = M. Thus, cheap fidelities are used to eliminate clearly suboptimal arms; expensive fidelities are reserved for near-optimal arms.

The analysis partitions arms into sets K(m) based on how close their fidelity-m means are to µ?, and shows MF-UCB concentrates effort at the “appropriate” fidelity per arm. The regret bound (Theorem 2) scales as
E[R(Λ)] ≲ log(Λ/λ(M)) [∑k∉K? ∆(M)k λ(JkK)/ψ(∆(JkK)k) + lower-order terms],
with N, the number of plays under capital Λ, controlled so that N ≈ Λ/λ(M). Compared to UCB that operates solely at fidelity M, MF-UCB can improve regret by roughly λ(JkK)/λ(M) on arms that can be discarded at low fidelities. A lower bound (Theorem 4) for Bernoulli rewards under a mild regularity assumption shows MF-UCB is near-optimal, matching the lower bound on a broad subset of arms, with a quantified gap for a subset K(m)₇ where the optimal distinguishing fidelity can be lower than MF-UCB’s switching point. Synthetic experiments support the predicted improvements.

This work connects to classical UCB algorithms, budgeted bandits (which optimize reward/cost ratios per arm rather than exploit biased proxies), and multi-fidelity methods in optimization and RL (which usually do not formalize cumulative regret with biased surrogates). The paper’s formulation and analysis appear novel within the stochastic bandits literature.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The algorithmic design is coherent: confidence inflation by ζ(m) upper-bounds µ(M)k from lower fidelities, and γ(m) trades off cost vs. information via ψ and λ. The regret analysis is systematic, leveraging an arm partition and high-probability control to keep N ≈ Λ/λ(M). The upper bound compares favorably to single-fidelity UCB and captures when multi-fidelity pays off (small |K(M)|, low λ(1)). The lower bound uses a change-of-measure argument and identifies a principled gap set K(m)₇ that the current switching rule does not close. Assumptions are clearly stated: known ζ(m), known costs, and concentration (ψ) with a mild regularity Assumption 1 to avoid near-duplicate fidelities. The residual term r̃(Λ) is correctly shown negligible.
- Limitations: (i) The regret notion rewards only µ(M) while paying costs at all fidelities; this matches the stated motivation but other applications may prefer alternate reward models (acknowledged in the conclusion). (ii) ζ(m) must be known and correctly specified; robustness to misspecification is not analyzed. (iii) The lower bound is for Bernoulli rewards; extension to general ψ would strengthen the claims. (iv) The γ(m) schedule is fixed; adaptivity or data-driven selection is not explored. (v) Sensitivity to ρ and ψ choice is not empirically studied.
- Empirics: The synthetic experiments illustrate the qualitative behavior and regret improvements, but the empirical section is minimal. No real-world data or stress-tests of misspecified ζ/λ or varying M are provided.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The problem setting, assumptions, and regret definition are explicit. The algorithm is concise and intuitive. The analysis sketch gives insight into the role of the arm partition and the N-control. Some suggestions:
- Provide intuition for Assumption 1 (currently given) and concrete examples beyond the sub-Gaussian case.
- Discuss practical estimation or calibration of ζ(m) and costs, and sensitivity to misspecification.
- Include pseudocode with initialization policy and tie-breaking, and state default ρ.
- Expand experimental details in the main text (currently relegated to the appendix).

Originality
The formulation of multi-fidelity MAB with biased, cheaper proxies and capital-weighted regret measured against µ(M) is novel. The MF-UCB design that integrates bias-aware UCB indices across fidelities with a cost-sensitive switching threshold appears new. The lower-bound characterization identifies a subtle gap driven by switching rather than estimation of small ∆’s, which is informative for future work.

Significance
Multi-fidelity evaluation is common (ads, simulation-to-real, algorithm selection), and a principled algorithm with near-optimal guarantees is valuable. The theoretical contribution is substantial and may spur follow-up work on alternative regret models, adaptive γ(m), and robustness. The practical impact would be strengthened by empirical validation on real tasks and analysis of ζ/λ estimation.

Pros
- Clear, well-motivated problem formulation tailored to common applications.
- Simple, implementable algorithm with strong intuition.
- Near-optimal regret guarantees under explicit assumptions; clear comparison to single-fidelity UCB.
- Identification of when multi-fidelity helps most (small |K(M)|, large λ(M)/λ(1)).
- Lower bound highlights precise source of suboptimality and open directions.

Cons
- Requires known fidelity biases ζ(m); no robustness analysis to misspecification.
- Lower bound restricted to Bernoulli; empirical section limited and synthetic only.
- The regret definition may not fit all multi-fidelity applications; alternatives only briefly discussed.
- Gap for K(m)₇ remains; no adaptive switching heuristic proposed to address it.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- How sensitive is MF-UCB to over-/under-estimation of ζ(m)? Can bounds degrade gracefully?
- Can γ(m) be adapted online using observed discrepancies across fidelities?
- Can the lower bound be extended to general ψ, or at least sub-Gaussian families?
- Provide experiments varying ζ, λ, M, and with misspecified ζ(m), and include a real-world dataset if possible.
- Discuss computational overhead and constants in the bounds.

Recommendation
The paper presents a novel and well-analyzed multi-fidelity bandit framework with a simple algorithm and near-optimal guarantees. Despite limited empirical validation and some assumptions, the theoretical contribution is solid and relevant to NeurIPS. Accept.
