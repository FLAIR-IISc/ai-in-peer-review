

# Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

This paper introduces a novel framework for multi-armed bandit problems where each arm has multiple fidelities (approximations) with different costs. The authors propose a Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB) algorithm that leverages cheaper lower-fidelity approximations to eliminate suboptimal arms and reserves expensive high-fidelity evaluations for promising arms. The paper provides a formal regret definition for this setting, theoretical guarantees showing MF-UCB achieves regret O((λ(1)K + λ(2)|Kg|) log(Λ/λ(2))), and a matching lower bound. The work builds on UCB methods [3] but addresses a significant gap in the literature regarding multi-fidelity bandit problems, distinct from previous multi-fidelity optimization work [7,10] and budgeted bandit studies [13,15].

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Formalism:** The paper introduces a well-motivated regret definition that properly accounts for both the cost of fidelities and the quality of approximations. The distinction between the regret definition and the naive alternative (using the fidelity's own reward) is particularly well-explained with the online advertising example.

2. **Theoretical Contribution:** The regret bound analysis is thorough and carefully constructed. The partitioning of arms into K(m) sets and the demonstration that MF-UCB achieves near-optimal regret (matching the lower bound for most arms) represents a significant theoretical advance. The analysis handles the key challenge of balancing exploration at low fidelity with exploitation at high fidelity.

3. **Algorithm Design:** MF-UCB is a natural and elegant extension of UCB, with a clear intuition for how fidelity switching works. The choice of γ(m) is well-justified and represents a careful trade-off between exploration and exploitation.

4. **Empirical Validation:** The synthetic experiments convincingly demonstrate MF-UCB outperforms standard UCB, with the plots showing both lower regret and steeper gradients as expected from the theory. The visualization of fidelity usage across arms aligns with the theoretical predictions.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Assumption Limitations:** The analysis relies on several strong assumptions that may not hold in practice. The requirement that ζ(m) are known and decay sufficiently fast (Assumption 1) is particularly restrictive. In real applications, these parameters would typically need to be estimated, which could significantly impact performance. The paper doesn't address how to handle this estimation challenge.

2. **Parameter Sensitivity:** The algorithm depends on the choice of ρ (a parameter in the confidence bound), but the paper doesn't discuss how to select this parameter in practice or how sensitive the algorithm is to this choice. The analysis requires ρ > 4, but the paper doesn't provide guidance on choosing an appropriate value.

3. **Limited Scope of Analysis:** The paper treats M (number of fidelities) as a constant, which limits the practical applicability when M is large. The regret bound doesn't explicitly show how the algorithm scales with M, which would be important for real-world applications with many fidelity levels.

4. **Incomplete Lower Bound Analysis:** The lower bound doesn't match MF-UCB's performance for all arms (specifically for arms in K(m)7), and the authors acknowledge this gap without providing a resolution. This gap is non-trivial and could be a significant weakness for practitioners.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a well-written, technically sound paper that makes a clear contribution to the bandit literature. The problem is important, the formalism is novel, and the theoretical results are strong. The paper addresses a significant gap in the literature and provides both a new algorithm and a theoretical analysis that demonstrates its advantages over standard bandit methods.

The main limitations are the restrictive assumptions about known parameters and the focus on M as a constant. These are common challenges in bandit literature but would need to be addressed for broader applicability. The paper would be significantly strengthened by discussing how to estimate the ζ(m) parameters and how to choose the algorithm's parameters in practice.

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper makes a clear, significant contribution to the bandit literature with strong theoretical results and empirical validation. While the assumptions are restrictive, they are common in bandit analysis and the paper provides a solid foundation for future work addressing these limitations. The theoretical results are sufficiently novel and significant to warrant acceptance at NIPS.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

1. In the experimental section, include a sensitivity analysis for the parameter ρ to show how performance varies with this choice.

2. Discuss how to estimate ζ(m) in practice, perhaps with a simple adaptive method or a discussion of the consequences of estimation error.

3. Extend the regret bound analysis to show how the regret scales with M (the number of fidelities), not just treating it as a constant.

4. Address the gap in the lower bound analysis for arms in K(m)7 - either provide a more refined algorithm that matches the lower bound or discuss the practical implications of this gap.

5. Consider adding a brief discussion of how the framework might extend to cases where fidelities are not strictly ordered by cost (e.g., λ(1) > λ(2) but ζ(1) < ζ(2)), which could occur in some applications.
