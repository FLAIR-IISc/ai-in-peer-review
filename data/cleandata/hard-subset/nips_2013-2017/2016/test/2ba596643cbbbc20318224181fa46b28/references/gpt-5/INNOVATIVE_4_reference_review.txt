Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper introduces a multi-fidelity variant of the stochastic K-armed bandit where each arm admits a hierarchy of biased, increasingly accurate (and increasingly costly) fidelities. The goal is to maximize highest-fidelity reward under a capital budget, using cheaper fidelities to screen arms. The authors formalize a cost-weighted pseudo-regret that rewards all plays according to the highest-fidelity mean, regardless of the fidelity used, thereby explicitly modeling biased approximations rather than variance-reduced proxies. They propose MF-UCB, which maintains per-fidelity UCBs corrected by known bias bounds ζ(m) and selects both an arm and a fidelity via a principled thresholding rule γ(m) that trades off per-sample cost λ(m) and achievable discrimination at fidelity m.

Theoretical contributions include: (i) a regret upper bound showing MF-UCB expends most capital on near-optimal arms at high fidelity and uses low fidelities to eliminate clearly suboptimal arms, yielding regret improvements proportional to λ(m)/λ(M) when many arms can be filtered cheaply; (ii) a lower bound (for Bernoulli rewards) showing near-optimality except for a specific subset of arms where the switching rule may not attain the information-theoretic allocation. The paper positions itself against classical UCB and budgeted bandits (different objective), and distinguishes itself from multi-fidelity optimization and RL works that lack bandit regret analyses. The formulation is novel and complementary to recent multi-fidelity BO/GP-bandit settings.

Quality:
- Theory: The algorithm is clearly specified and the analysis is careful. The upper bound reflects an intuitive partition of arms (K(m)) by how early they can be eliminated; the regret allocates cost proportional to λ(JkK)/ψ(Δ(JkK)k). The lower bound uses a change-of-measure argument and clarifies where MF-UCB is tight vs. potentially suboptimal due to its conservative switching rule. Assumptions (sub-Gaussian-like concentration; known bias bounds ζ(m); a mild regularity Assumption 1 to avoid degenerate fidelity ladders) are explicit. The lower bound is given for Bernoulli but the upper bound is more general via ψ.
- Empirics: Synthetic experiments corroborate the theory qualitatively (reduced slope; most capital spent at low fidelity for bad arms). However, the empirical section is light and lacks real-world or larger-scale tests, ablations on ζ/λ schedules, and comparisons beyond vanilla UCB.

Clarity:
The paper is mostly well-written and well-structured. The regret definition and its decomposition are clearly motivated for applications where only high-fidelity reward “counts.” The set partition K(m) and switching thresholds γ(m) are central and explained, though the exposition could be streamlined (e.g., a schematic table clarifying Jη(m), Δ(m)k, K(m), and JkK would help). A brief discussion of implementation details (handling ψ, ρ, and unknown ζ) would improve reproducibility.

Originality:
High. The formalization of a multi-fidelity stochastic bandit with biased approximations, explicit per-fidelity costs, and a capital-based regret that values only the highest-fidelity reward is, to my knowledge, new in the bandit literature. It goes beyond budgeted bandits (reward/cost ratio objective) and beyond multi-fidelity optimization without regret analysis. The analysis captures how fidelity hierarchy structure and costs translate into regret, which is a nontrivial and valuable conceptual advance.

Significance:
Potentially high. Many practical settings (ads, ML algorithm selection with subsampling/proxies, simulation-to-real) exhibit precisely this structure. The results suggest that when many arms are clearly suboptimal at low fidelity, MF-UCB saves substantial high-fidelity cost relative to single-fidelity UCB. The framework opens directions for more realistic variants (unknown/learned biases, alternative regrets) and should catalyze subsequent work (as hinted by the GP extension they cite).

Strengths:
- Clear and novel problem formulation with actionable modeling choices (λ, ζ).
- Simple, principled algorithm with transparent design (min of bias-augmented UCBs; threshold-based fidelity selection).
- Nontrivial regret guarantees and matching lower bounds in broad regimes; sharp intuition on where gains arise.
- Honest discussion of gaps and when MF-UCB may not be optimal.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- The assumption of known, uniform bias bounds ζ(m) is strong in many applications; sensitivity and adaptive estimation of ζ would be valuable. Can MF-UCB be made robust to misspecified ζ or learn ζ online?
- Assumption 1 (decay of ζ) simplifies comparison to UCB; it would help to characterize performance without it, beyond the “M small” remark.
- The regret notion, while well-motivated, may not fit settings where low-fidelity plays have no intrinsic reward (as the authors note). A short appendix formalizing alternative regrets would broaden applicability.
- Experiments are limited: include comparisons against more baselines (e.g., Thompson Sampling at high fidelity only; naive two-stage screens), stress tests with different ζ/λ schedules, heavy-tailed or heteroscedastic fidelities, and larger K.
- The lower bound is Bernoulli-specific; a discussion of extension to general ψ classes would strengthen the theoretical story.
- Practical guidance: how to set ρ; computational complexity per step; any pathologies if λ(m+1)≈λ(m) and ζ(m+1)≈ζ(m).

Questions for authors:
- Can you provide a variant that adaptively calibrates ζ(m) (e.g., via cross-fidelity discrepancy estimates) with regret guarantees?
- How sensitive is MF-UCB to moderate misspecification of ζ(m) and λ(m)?
- Could you include at least one semi-realistic simulation-to-real proxy task to validate the claimed gains?
- Is there a principled way to narrow the k∈K(m)7 gap without incurring high-fidelity sampling for most arms?

Minor:
- A compact pseudocode block including initialization and handling of zero counts would aid clarity.
- Some references appear incomplete; please fix bibliographic details.

Recommendation: Accept
This paper offers a clear, novel and impactful direction for bandits with biased, multi-cost feedback, along with a simple algorithm and solid analysis. Despite relatively modest experiments and some strong modeling assumptions, the conceptual advance is significant and likely to influence follow-up work. I am inclined to accept given the originality and potential impact.
