

# Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

This paper introduces a novel framework for multi-armed bandit problems where each arm has multiple "fidelities" - cheaper, approximate versions of the arm's outcome that can be used to guide exploration before committing to more expensive, higher-fidelity measurements. The authors formalize this setting, propose the MF-UCB algorithm that adapts UCB to multi-fidelity contexts, and provide theoretical regret bounds that demonstrate significant advantages over standard UCB when lower fidelities are cheaper. The work is motivated by practical applications like online advertising (where shorter ad displays provide cheaper approximations of click-through rates) and algorithm selection (where cheaper computational approximations of algorithm performance can guide exploration).

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it introduces a new formalism for bandit problems with multiple fidelities, which is a meaningful extension beyond the standard bandit framework. Second, it proposes a novel algorithm (MF-UCB) that strategically uses low-fidelity approximations to eliminate suboptimal arms before committing to expensive high-fidelity evaluations. Third, it provides a theoretical analysis showing MF-UCB achieves regret bounds of O((λ(1)K + λ(2)|Kg|) log(Λ/λ(2))) compared to standard UCB's O(λ(2)K log(Λ/λ(2))) regret, which can be substantially better when |Kg| << K and λ(1) << λ(2). The authors also derive a matching lower bound, demonstrating near-optimality of their approach.

The paper is well-motivated with compelling real-world examples and carefully distinguishes its contributions from related work on multi-fidelity optimization (which typically doesn't address exploration-exploitation trade-offs), budgeted bandits, and active learning. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-structured, with clear definitions and careful handling of the multi-fidelity complexity. The empirical results on synthetic problems convincingly demonstrate MF-UCB's superiority over standard UCB.

This work stands out for its conceptual novelty and clear practical relevance. The multi-fidelity bandit framework addresses a significant gap in the bandit literature, as most prior work assumes uniform cost per arm pull, which is unrealistic in many applications where cheaper approximations exist. The authors have made a bold conceptual leap by recognizing that these cheaper approximations can be strategically leveraged to reduce overall cost while maintaining performance, which is not merely an incremental improvement but a fundamentally different approach to the exploration-exploitation trade-off.

While there are some minor limitations worth noting, they are outweighed by the paper's novelty. The analysis assumes known ζ(m) values (the bounds on how much lower fidelities can differ from high fidelity), which might be a practical constraint, but the authors acknowledge this as a limitation. The lower bound analysis shows a gap for certain arms (those in K(m)7), but the authors provide a thoughtful explanation for this gap and correctly identify it as an open problem. The empirical validation is limited to synthetic data, but the paper clearly demonstrates the theoretical insights through well-designed experiments.

The paper excels in clarity, with a well-organized structure, clear notation, and thorough explanations of the key concepts. The algorithm description is precise, and the regret bound analysis is accessible to bandit researchers.

In terms of significance, this work opens up an important new direction in bandit research with direct applications in fields where high-fidelity measurements are expensive. The multi-fidelity framework could be particularly valuable in resource-constrained settings like online advertising, robotics, and algorithm selection, where cost efficiency is paramount. The paper demonstrates a clear advancement over previous work, not just by providing a new algorithm but by fundamentally redefining the problem setting.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold conceptual contributions, I believe this paper makes a significant and original contribution to the field. The core idea of multi-fidelity bandits represents a meaningful extension of the bandit framework that addresses a practical gap in the literature. While the methodological details are sound, the true value here is the novel conceptual framework, which aligns perfectly with what NIPS values most.

The paper is of high quality, well-written, and makes a clear, significant contribution to the field. It is not merely an incremental improvement but a genuinely new direction that will likely inspire follow-up work. The minor limitations do not detract from the paper's overall novelty and significance.

**Recommendation: Accept**
