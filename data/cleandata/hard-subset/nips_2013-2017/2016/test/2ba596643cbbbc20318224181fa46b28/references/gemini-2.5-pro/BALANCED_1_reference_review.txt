### Summary

This paper introduces the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit, a novel extension of the classical K-armed bandit problem. The key idea is that for each arm, one can access not only the true reward distribution (the highest fidelity) but also a set of cheaper, lower-fidelity approximations. These approximations are biased, but their means are guaranteed to be within a known distance, ζ(m), of the true mean. The authors propose a new cost-aware regret metric for this setting, which considers a total capital budget Λ.

The main contribution is the Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB) algorithm. This algorithm intelligently allocates the budget by leveraging information from all fidelities. It selects arms based on the tightest upper confidence bound across all available fidelities and then uses a principled rule to decide which fidelity to query for the selected arm. The rule essentially queries cheap fidelities to gain initial information and only proceeds to more expensive ones if the uncertainty cannot be sufficiently reduced at a lower cost.

The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis of MF-UCB, including an upper bound on its regret. This analysis formalizes the intuition that MF-UCB uses cheap fidelities to quickly discard clearly suboptimal arms, reserving the expensive, high-fidelity queries for a small set of promising candidates. Furthermore, the authors derive a lower bound for the problem, demonstrating that MF-UCB is near-optimal. The theoretical claims are supported by simulations on synthetic data, which show a clear performance improvement over a standard UCB algorithm that ignores the multi-fidelity structure. This work extends the well-studied UCB framework to a practical and important new setting, differing from prior work on budgeted bandits or multi-fidelity optimization by its focus on regret minimization in the bandit exploration-exploitation context.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper formulates a novel and well-motivated problem that captures a common real-world scenario where cheap, approximate evaluations are available. This multi-fidelity setting is highly relevant for applications like hyperparameter tuning, ad placement, and scientific experimentation. The work opens up a promising new direction for bandit research.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically very strong.
    *   The proposed MF-UCB algorithm is elegant and intuitive. The mechanism for selecting both the arm (based on the tightest UCB) and the fidelity (based on a cost-aware uncertainty threshold) is well-designed and principled.
    *   The theoretical analysis is comprehensive and insightful. The regret upper bound is particularly compelling, as its structure clearly reflects how the algorithm benefits from the multi-fidelity setup. The use of arm partitions (K(m)) is an effective analytical tool to demonstrate that "bad" arms are primarily queried at cheap fidelities.
    *   The inclusion of a problem-specific lower bound is a major strength. It provides a strong benchmark for the proposed algorithm and formally establishes its near-optimality. The authors are also commendably transparent about the small gap between their upper and lower bounds for certain arm configurations (the K(m)7 sets), providing a clear direction for future work.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The problem, algorithm, and main theoretical results are presented effectively. The introduction does an excellent job of motivating the problem with concrete examples. Despite the density of the notation required for the analysis, it is consistently defined and used.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

While this is an excellent paper, a few aspects could be clarified or strengthened to increase its impact.

1.  **Assumption on Known Biases (ζ(m)):** The assumption that the bias bounds ζ(m) are known a priori is strong and may not hold in all practical settings. The performance of MF-UCB seems to rely heavily on these values. The paper would be more impactful if it included a discussion on this assumption. For instance: How sensitive is the algorithm to misspecified ζ(m) values? Could these bounds be learned online? Even a brief discussion of these practical considerations would significantly enhance the paper.

2.  **Motivation for Hyperparameter γ(m):** The choice of the threshold γ(m) in Equation (4) is critical to the algorithm's performance, as it governs the switch between fidelities. The paper states that this choice represents an "optimal tradeoff," but the motivation in the main text is brief. A more detailed intuitive explanation of how this specific form balances the cost ratio λ(m)/λ(m+1) with the information gain related to ζ(m) would be very helpful for the reader to fully grasp the mechanics of the algorithm.

3.  **Experimental Details:** The simulation section is very concise, with most details deferred to the appendix. To make the empirical results more self-contained and convincing, it would be beneficial to include a bit more detail in the main paper. Specifically, describing the parameters (K, M, λ(m), ζ(m)) and the process for generating the reward means for one of the key experiments would help readers better understand the conditions under which MF-UCB provides substantial gains.

4.  **Clarity of Arm Partitions:** The recursive definition of the arm partitions K(m) is a powerful analytical tool but is also quite complex. While Figure 1 is a useful aid, adding a sentence or two to the main text to further build intuition for this specific definition (e.g., "K(m) represents the set of arms that are indistinguishable from the best arm at all fidelities below m, but become clearly suboptimal at fidelity m") could improve readability.

### Arguments for Acceptance

*   **Pro:** The paper introduces a novel, significant, and well-motivated bandit problem with broad applicability.
*   **Pro:** The proposed MF-UCB algorithm is simple, elegant, and supported by a rigorous theoretical analysis.
*   **Pro:** The work is very complete, providing both upper and lower regret bounds that establish the near-optimality of the proposed method.
*   **Pro:** The paper is well-written and represents a foundational contribution to a new sub-field of bandit theory.

### Arguments Against Acceptance

*   **Con:** The practical applicability is somewhat limited by the strong assumption that the fidelity bias bounds `ζ(m)` are known in advance.
*   **Con:** Key design choices and experimental details are not fully elaborated in the main body of the paper, requiring the reader to consult the appendix for a complete understanding.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It presents a high-quality, original, and significant contribution to the field. The identified weaknesses are largely presentational and do not detract from the core technical contributions. This paper is an excellent fit for NeurIPS and should be accepted.
