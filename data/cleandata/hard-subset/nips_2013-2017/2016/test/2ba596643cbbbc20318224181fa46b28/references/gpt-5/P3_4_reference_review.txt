Summary
The paper introduces a multi-fidelity variant of the stochastic K-armed bandit, where each arm admits M fidelities with increasing cost λ(m) and decreasing approximation bias ζ(m) to the “true” (highest-fidelity) reward distribution. The authors define a cost-weighted pseudo-regret in which the oracle plays the best arm at any fidelity but rewards are always measured with respect to the highest-fidelity mean µ(M). They propose MF-UCB, which maintains per-fidelity UCBs for each arm using standard concentration ψ plus a bias correction ζ(m), and uses a fidelity-selection rule governed by thresholds γ(m) that balance cost and approximation error. The analysis partitions arms into sets K(m) according to how close their lower-fidelity means are to optimal, proving that MF-UCB filters clearly suboptimal arms at cheap fidelities and spends high-fidelity budget mostly on near-optimal arms. The main result yields a regret bound that improves over single-fidelity UCB by factors roughly λ(Jk)/λ(M) for arms that can be eliminated at fidelity Jk. A lower bound (via change-of-measure for Bernoulli rewards) shows near-optimality for a large subset of arms, with a quantified gap for certain “borderline” arms, which the authors discuss. Simulations on synthetic problems corroborate the theory.

Relation to prior work
The work is distinct from budgeted bandits (e.g., Tran-Thanh; Xia et al.), which optimize reward/cost ratios with one fidelity per arm; from multi-fidelity optimization (Huang; Rajnarayan) that lacks regret analysis; and from RL with simulators (Cutler et al.) or active learning with weak/strong labelers (Zhang & Chaudhuri), which have different objectives. Within bandits, the closest methodological lineage is UCB (Auer; Audibert et al.) and best-arm identification/elimination style analyses; the multi-fidelity twist with known per-fidelity bias bounds and costs appears novel and is well positioned relative to these literatures.

Strengths
- Clear modeling of multi-fidelity access with explicit, known bias envelopes ζ(m) and costs λ(m). The regret definition is principled for applications where only high-fidelity reward counts while lower fidelities guide exploration.
- Algorithmic design is simple and implementable: per-fidelity UCBs with a natural inflation by ζ(m), and a fidelity-escalation rule γ(m)=ψ^{-1}((λ(m)/λ(m+1))ψ(ζ(m))) that has intuitive form (for sub-Gaussian, γ(m)=ζ(m)√(λ(m)/λ(m+1))).
- Theoretical results are strong: a clean arm partition K(m) underpins the regret bound, which is demonstrably better than naïve UCB whenever many arms can be ruled out at low fidelities. Lower bounds match the upper bounds on a broad set K(m)3, establishing near-optimality.
- The analysis is careful about cost accounting: bounding the total number of plays N under a capital Λ and isolating the negligible residual term r̃.
- Simulations match the theory, illustrating that MF-UCB concentrates expensive pulls on promising arms.

Weaknesses and questions
- Modeling assumptions: The algorithm and guarantees require known, uniform bias bounds ζ(m) across arms and fidelities and the regularity Assumption 1 on the decay of ζ(m) relative to ψ. In practice, ζ(m) may be arm-dependent or unknown; robustness or adaptive estimation of ζ(m) is not considered. A discussion or experiment on mis-specification would strengthen the paper.
- Regret notion: While the chosen pseudo-regret is well motivated, alternative definitions (e.g., per-unit-cost regret, reward observed only at high fidelity, or different penalties for low-fidelity outcomes) are only briefly discussed. Providing one more formal alternative and how MF-UCB would adapt would broaden impact.
- Lower bound scope: The lower bound is for Bernoulli rewards with µ* > 1/2 and ζ(1) < 1/2. Although the upper bounds are stated under general ψ, it would be useful to comment on extending the lower bound to general sub-Gaussian ψ (or to present a sketch).
- Gap on K(m)7: The identified suboptimality for arms with small positive Δ at lower fidelities is important. It would be helpful to explore whether a simple, implementable tweak (e.g., allowing additional low-fidelity sampling when λ(`)/Δ(`)2 is favorable) can close the gap without requiring high-fidelity samples to estimate Δ(M).
- Empirics: Experiments are limited to synthetic data and compare only against single-fidelity UCB. Additional baselines would help: (i) a “flat” UCB on the augmented arm set {(k,m)} that treats each fidelity as a separate arm with index X̄(m)+ζ(m) and cost λ(m), (ii) successive elimination adapted to multi-fidelity costs, and (iii) a Thompson Sampling variant. Sensitivity analyses to K, M, λ, ζ and ψ would also be valuable.
- Clarity: A few notational choices (e.g., early reference to Kg vs the later K(m) partition) could be harmonized. Some proofs and constants are in the appendix; a short intuition for Assumption 1’s necessity in the main text would aid readability.

Quality
Technically solid. The proofs appear careful, with high-probability control of fidelity-wise pulls, a thoughtful arm partition, and nontrivial lower bounds. The assumptions are explicit, and the limitations (gap on K(m)7) are candidly discussed.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. The intuition behind γ(m) and the K(m) partition is clear. Minor notation inconsistencies and missing figure references could be smoothed.

Originality
The multi-fidelity bandit formulation with explicit bias envelopes and costs, the regret definition tied to the highest-fidelity reward, and the MF-UCB design and analysis are, to my knowledge, novel in the bandit literature.

Significance
High. Many applications (ads, algorithm selection, simulation-to-real transfer) admit cheaper biased proxies; a principled, near-optimal algorithmic treatment is timely. The results should spur follow-on work (e.g., context, GP priors, unknown ζ, Thompson sampling).

Suggestions for improvement
- Discuss/experiment with robustness to ζ mis-specification; consider arm-dependent ζk(m).
- Add at least one additional baseline and a sensitivity study.
- Expand on practical guidance for choosing ρ and handling general ψ.
- Briefly sketch how the framework adapts to an alternative regret (e.g., reward only at high fidelity).

Pros
- Novel, well-motivated problem formulation.
- Simple, practical algorithm with clear intuition.
- Strong theoretical guarantees; near-optimal for a broad class.
- Empirical evidence aligns with theory.

Cons
- Requires known, uniform ζ(m) and regularity Assumption 1.
- Lower bound limited to Bernoulli; gap remains for K(m)7.
- Limited empirical baselines and real-world validation.

Recommendation
Accept. This is a solid, original contribution with clear theoretical and practical merit. I would rate it 7–8/10, confidence high.
