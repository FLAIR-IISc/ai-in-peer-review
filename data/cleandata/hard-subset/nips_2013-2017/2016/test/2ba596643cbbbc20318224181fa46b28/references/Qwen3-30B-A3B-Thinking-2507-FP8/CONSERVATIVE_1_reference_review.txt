

# Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

This paper proposes an extension of the classical multi-armed bandit problem to incorporate multiple fidelities, where each arm can be evaluated at different levels of approximation (cost) with varying accuracy. The authors introduce an MF-UCB algorithm that leverages cheaper, lower-fidelity evaluations to eliminate suboptimal arms before committing to expensive high-fidelity evaluations. They claim their method achieves better regret bounds than standard UCB algorithms in the multi-fidelity setting, with a theoretical analysis supporting this claim.

The paper builds on established bandit literature (particularly UCB methods) and presents a formal framework for multi-fidelity bandits. The theoretical analysis includes regret bounds for MF-UCB and a lower bound, with the authors showing that MF-UCB is near-optimal for certain arm subsets. The paper also includes synthetic experiments demonstrating MF-UCB outperforms UCB.

**Strengths:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The theoretical analysis is technically sound, and the paper properly references related work. The multi-fidelity bandit problem is a natural extension of existing bandit literature with potential practical applications in areas like online advertising and algorithm selection. The regret bound analysis is thorough for the proposed setting.

**Weaknesses:** The paper's primary weakness is the lack of sufficient empirical validation. The experiments are limited to synthetic problems with no real-world data or applications. The authors claim MF-UCB "outperforms naive UCB on simulations," but provide no quantitative comparison of regret values or statistical significance testing. Without real-world validation, it's difficult to assess the practical significance of the claimed improvements.

The theoretical analysis contains a notable gap: the regret bound for MF-UCB does not match the lower bound for arms in the set K(m)7 (where "there exists some ` < m such that 0 < ∆ (`) k < 2γ (`)"). The authors acknowledge this gap but do not provide a convincing explanation for why this gap is acceptable or how it might be resolved. This undermines their claim that MF-UCB is "near-optimal."

The paper also fails to adequately address the practical conditions under which their approach would provide meaningful benefits. The authors assume that lower fidelities are both cheaper and provide useful information, but they don't demonstrate how often these conditions hold in practice or quantify the cost-benefit tradeoff. Without this, the paper doesn't convincingly establish the practical significance of their contribution.

The paper makes a strong theoretical claim but lacks the empirical rigor expected for a NIPS publication. The authors cite related work on multi-fidelity optimization (e.g., [7, 10]), but their algorithm is not compared to these methods, making it difficult to assess whether they provide a meaningful advancement over existing approaches.

**Recommendation:** The paper presents a theoretically interesting extension to bandit problems, but the lack of empirical validation and the theoretical gap in the lower bound analysis significantly weaken its contribution. While the problem is well-motivated, the paper fails to demonstrate that the proposed method provides a substantial practical advantage over existing approaches.

The authors should address the following before acceptance:
1. Provide more comprehensive empirical validation with real-world data or at least more detailed synthetic experiments showing statistical significance.
2. Address the gap between the upper and lower bounds for arms in K(m)7.
3. More thoroughly compare with existing multi-fidelity optimization methods.
4. Provide a clearer analysis of the practical conditions under which the method would provide meaningful benefits.

The paper is a step toward a more nuanced understanding of bandit problems with multiple evaluation options, but it falls short of providing a rigorous, validated contribution that would advance the state-of-the-art. It reads more like a theoretical exploration than a complete, well-validated scientific contribution.

**Pro and Con for Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- Well-structured theoretical analysis
- Clear motivation from practical applications
- Builds on established bandit literature
- Identifies an interesting extension to the bandit problem

*Con:*
- Limited empirical validation (only synthetic experiments)
- Theoretical gap between upper and lower bounds
- Insufficient comparison with existing multi-fidelity methods
- Unclear practical significance without real-world validation

Given the skeptical nature of this review and the lack of sufficient empirical validation and theoretical completeness, I recommend rejection. The paper would benefit from more extensive empirical validation and a more thorough analysis of the gap between the upper and lower bounds before it could be considered for publication at NIPS.
