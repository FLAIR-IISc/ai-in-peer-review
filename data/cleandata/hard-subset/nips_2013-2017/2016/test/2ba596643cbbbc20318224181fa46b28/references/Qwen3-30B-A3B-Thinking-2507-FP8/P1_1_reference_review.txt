

# Peer Review: "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

## Summary
This paper introduces a novel framework for multi-armed bandit problems where each arm has multiple "fidelities" - cheaper, lower-accuracy approximations of the arm's true value, along with more expensive, higher-accuracy evaluations. The authors propose the MF-UCB algorithm that adaptively selects both which arm to play and at which fidelity to evaluate it. The key insight is that cheaper lower-fidelity evaluations can eliminate "bad" arms, reserving expensive high-fidelity evaluations for a small subset of promising arms. The paper provides a regret bound showing MF-UCB achieves O((λ(1)K + λ(2)|Kg|) log(Λ/λ(2))) regret, which outperforms standard UCB (O(λ(2)K log(Λ/λ(2)))) when |Kg| < K and λ(1) < λ(2). The authors also establish a matching lower bound, demonstrating near-optimality. This work extends classical bandit theory [3, 9] and builds on related work in multi-fidelity optimization [7, 10], but makes the crucial distinction of formally analyzing regret in the bandit setting.

## Strengths
The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by formalizing a natural extension of the bandit problem with practical relevance. The MF-UCB algorithm is elegantly designed to leverage the multi-fidelity structure, with the intuition that lower fidelities should be used for exploration and higher fidelities for exploitation. The theoretical analysis is thorough and well-executed, with a clear regret bound that demonstrates the algorithm's advantage over standard approaches when the problem satisfies the necessary conditions.

The paper distinguishes itself well from related work. It clearly differentiates from budgeted bandits [13, 15] (which optimize reward/cost ratios) and from multi-fidelity optimization [7, 10] (which don't formalize regret in bandit settings). The authors also provide a thoughtful discussion of the motivating applications, particularly in online advertising and algorithm selection, which helps ground the theoretical contribution.

The experimental results (in the appendix) complement the theory well, showing MF-UCB outperforms UCB across multiple synthetic problems. The analysis of how MF-UCB allocates plays across fidelities (Figure 2) provides concrete evidence matching the theoretical intuition about how the algorithm should behave.

## Weaknesses
The main limitation is the assumption that ζ(m) (the error bound between fidelities) is known and satisfies the fast decay condition (Assumption 1). In many real-world applications, ζ(m) would need to be estimated, which isn't addressed in the paper. The authors mention this briefly in Section 7 but don't explore potential solutions or discuss how their method would adapt to unknown ζ(m).

The regret bound (Theorem 2) shows MF-UCB is at most a constant times worse than UCB in the worst case, but it's not clear how often this worst case would actually occur in practice. The paper could provide more concrete guidance on when the multi-fidelity advantage is most pronounced, beyond the theoretical conditions.

The paper doesn't explore how to handle cases where the lower fidelity might be misleading (e.g., an arm with low high-fidelity reward but high low-fidelity reward), though this is acknowledged in the problem statement. While the paper acknowledges this limitation, a brief discussion of potential mitigation strategies would strengthen the contribution.

## Overall Assessment
This is a high-quality theoretical contribution that makes a clear advance in bandit theory. The paper is technically sound, with rigorous theoretical analysis that is well-supported by the experimental results. The writing is clear and well-organized, with precise definitions and logical flow from problem formulation to algorithm design to analysis.

The main limitations (known ζ(m) and fast decay assumption) are common in theoretical work and don't significantly undermine the paper's contribution. The authors have clearly identified the conditions under which their method outperforms existing approaches, and the theoretical guarantees are strong.

## Recommendation

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Novel formalism for multi-fidelity bandits with clear theoretical contribution
- Well-designed algorithm (MF-UCB) with strong regret guarantees
- Near-optimal performance demonstrated by both theory and experiments
- Clear distinction from related work
- High technical quality and well-written

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Requires ζ(m) to be known (not always realistic)
- Assumes fast decay of ζ(m), which might be restrictive
- Limited discussion of practical implementation considerations

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. It makes a significant theoretical contribution to bandit literature that is both rigorous and well-motivated. The problem is important for applications in online advertising, algorithm selection, and other domains where multi-fidelity evaluations are common. The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way, providing a clear algorithm with strong theoretical guarantees that would be valuable to the NIPS community. The limitations noted are common in theoretical work and don't detract from the paper's main contribution.
