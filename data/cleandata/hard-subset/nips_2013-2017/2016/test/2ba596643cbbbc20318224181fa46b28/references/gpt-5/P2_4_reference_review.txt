Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper introduces a stochastic K-armed bandit setting in which each arm can be queried at multiple fidelities with increasing cost and decreasing bias relative to the true (highest-fidelity) reward distribution. The authors formalize a cost-weighted pseudo-regret under a fixed capital Λ, design a Multi-Fidelity UCB (MF-UCB) algorithm that (i) maintains UCBs on the highest-fidelity means using samples from all fidelities with a known bias envelope ζ(m), and (ii) adaptively chooses which fidelity to query using a threshold γ(m) that trades off cost and informativeness. They prove a regret upper bound showing that, roughly, expensive queries are concentrated on a small subset of near-optimal arms, while cheaper fidelities are used to eliminate clearly suboptimal arms. They also provide a lower bound (for Bernoulli rewards) demonstrating near-optimality on a large subset of instances and discuss a gap for a particular class of arms. Simulations on synthetic problems compare MF-UCB to a naive high-fidelity UCB baseline and show consistent gains.

Relative to prior work: classical UCB and bandit theory are well covered; budgeted bandits (reward/cost ratio optimization) are distinct. Multi-fidelity ideas exist in optimization and RL, but, to my knowledge, multi-fidelity stochastic bandit regret with biased lower fidelities and explicit cost tradeoffs was not formalized prior to this line. The paper builds on UCB methodology and concentration inequalities and is related but orthogonal to budget-constrained UCB formulations.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The UCB construction Bk,t = minm[X̄(m) + conf + ζ(m)] is a principled way to form high-probability upper bounds on µ(M). The fidelity selection rule via γ(m) = ψ^{-1}(λ(m)/λ(m+1) ψ(ζ(m))) is well-motivated: do not over-invest at fidelity m once the statistical uncertainty falls below a scale set by its irreducible bias ζ(m) and cost ratio. The main regret bound carefully decomposes pulls by fidelity via a partition of arms K(m), and the comparison to standard UCB is convincing: MF-UCB is at worst within a constant (up to log and gap-dependent terms) and often improves by factors λ(m)/λ(M) for many arms. The lower bound (under Bernoulli rewards and a mild subpolynomial-pulls assumption) captures the right λ/Δ^2 dependence and clarifies when MF-UCB is optimal and where it can be loose (the K(m)7 set). The proofs rely on standard concentration and change-of-measure techniques and appear consistent with the claims.
- Assumptions: The approach requires known bias envelopes ζ(m), monotone decreasing, and a regularity condition (Assumption 1) relating their decay to ψ. This is reasonable for theory, but may be strong in practice. The lower bound is specialized to Bernoulli and includes Assumption 3 to exclude degenerate strategies; this is typical but should be discussed more.
- Experiments: Only synthetic experiments, with UCB at highest fidelity as the sole baseline. The empirical trends match theory (cheaper fidelities screen many arms), but the evaluation is limited. No sensitivity to mis-specifying ζ(m), cost ratios, or number of fidelities is reported. No real-world or semi-realistic datasets are included.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well organized, with clear notation and a helpful figure explaining the arm partitions K(m). The regret definition and oracle comparator could be clarified: by setting qt = µ(M)It regardless of the fidelity queried, the “reward” is a pseudo-reward that reflects the long-run value per unit cost rather than the observed instantaneous outcome. This is a defensible modeling choice for the motivating applications, but should be contrasted more explicitly with alternatives (some of which the authors mention in the conclusion). The role of ψ and how to instantiate it in sub-Gaussian settings could be spelled out in the main text. Constants and the choice of ρ > 4 could be better motivated. Some editorial polish would help (typos, consistent notation), but overall the paper is readable.

Originality:
The problem formulation—multi-fidelity, biased low-cost proxies within a bandit framework—and the MF-UCB algorithm are novel and nontrivial extensions of UCB. The theoretical analysis (both upper and lower bounds) is, to my knowledge, new for this setting. The relation to budgeted bandits, multi-fidelity optimization, and RL simulators is clearly delineated.

Significance:
The setting is practically relevant (ads, subsampling ML algorithms, simulators) and the results are likely to influence follow-on work (as the authors’ own GP extension hints). The theoretical message is clean: if cheap fidelities are informative enough, one can save capital by eliminating many arms before paying for the highest fidelity, improving regret by cost ratios. The main caveats limiting immediate practical significance are (i) the need to know bias envelopes ζ(m), (ii) the lower-bound gap for certain instances, and (iii) limited empirical validation. Nonetheless, as a principled foundation, this is impactful.

Pros:
- Clear, original formulation of multi-fidelity bandits with biased approximations and costs.
- Simple, principled algorithm with transparent intuition.
- Strong theory: regret upper bound that improves over single-fidelity UCB when structure exists; matching lower bound on a large subset; explicit discussion of a gap.
- Alignment between theory and simulations.

Cons:
- Regret notion is a pseudo-regret; the oracle and reward model deserve deeper discussion and possibly alternative metrics.
- Requires known ζ(m) and ψ; robustness to misspecification not analyzed.
- Assumption 1 may be restrictive; implications when violated are not quantified.
- Lower bound limited to Bernoulli; optimality gap on K(m)7 is unresolved.
- Empirical evaluation is thin (no real data; limited baselines; no sensitivity analyses).

Questions/suggestions for authors:
- How sensitive is MF-UCB to mis-specifying ζ(m)? Can one adaptively estimate or upper bound ζ(m)?
- Can the analysis be extended to unknown variance or heavy-tailed rewards (data-dependent confidence)?
- What happens if Assumption 1 fails (e.g., very fine fidelity ladders)? Can you provide a bound quantifying the degradation?
- Provide experiments demonstrating robustness to ζ(m) and λ(m) misspecification and comparisons to reasonable heuristics (e.g., TS variants, simple elimination schemes).
- Clarify the practical meaning of the regret baseline and discuss alternative definitions in more detail.

Recommendation: Accept (borderline to weak accept). The paper addresses a timely and practically motivated problem with a clean algorithm and solid theory. While the empirical evaluation is limited and some assumptions are strong, the contribution is original and likely to spur further research. Confidence: high.
