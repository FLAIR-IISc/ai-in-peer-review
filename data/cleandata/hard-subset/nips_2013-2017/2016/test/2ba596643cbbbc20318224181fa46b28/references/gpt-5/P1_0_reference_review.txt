Summary
The paper introduces a new stochastic K-armed bandit setting where each arm can be queried at multiple fidelities with increasing cost and decreasing bias relative to the “true” (highest-fidelity) reward. The key modeling assumptions are: (i) for each arm k and fidelity m, the mean bias is bounded by a known ζ(m), decreasing with m (with ζ(M)=0), and (ii) querying at fidelity m incurs cost λ(m), increasing with m. The authors propose a cost-weighted pseudo-regret suitable for a capital-constrained setting: with total capital Λ, each play consumes λ(mt) and earns pseudo-reward equal to the highest-fidelity mean μ(M) of the chosen arm. They present MF-UCB, an adaptation of UCB that (a) maintains an upper bound on μ(M) per arm by taking the minimum across the fidelity-specific UCBs augmented with ζ(m), and (b) chooses, for the selected arm, the lowest fidelity whose uncertainty is still above a threshold γ(m) that trades off cost ratios and bias bounds. Theoretical results show that MF-UCB concentrates exploration for clearly suboptimal arms at low fidelities and reserves expensive high-fidelity plays for a small set of near-optimal arms. The main regret bound matches or improves on single-fidelity UCB depending on the problem structure, and a lower bound demonstrates near-optimality except for a well-characterized subset of problem instances. Synthetic experiments corroborate the theoretical findings.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on classical UCB and regret analysis in stochastic bandits (Auer et al.; Bubeck & Cesa-Bianchi) and differs from budgeted bandits and reward/cost ratio objectives (Tran-Thanh et al.; Xia et al.). Multi-fidelity has been studied in optimization and RL (Huang et al.; Cutler et al.), but without a rigorous regret analysis in a bandit setting. The closest conceptual relatives are multi-fidelity optimization/BO (referenced) and sequential resource allocation, but the present work appears to be the first to formalize a multi-fidelity stochastic MAB with biased approximations, costs, and regret guarantees. The authors also contrast their framework with their GP-based extension.

Strengths
- Novel and well-motivated problem formulation capturing realistic settings where cheap, biased approximations exist (ads with short exposures, restricted demographics; algorithm selection with cheaper surrogates).
- Clear algorithmic idea: combine fidelity-specific UCBs into an arm index for μ(M), and a principled fidelity-selection rule via γ(m) that balances cost and bias.
- Theoretical guarantees: a transparent regret bound showing savings proportional to cost ratios when low-fidelity signals suffice to eliminate many arms; high-probability control of the number of plays under a capital budget; a lower bound that matches the upper bound on a large subset of arms and clarifies where suboptimality can occur.
- Insightful discussion of where multi-fidelity helps (many bad arms, small set near-optimal) and where it does not (arms close to optimal still require high-fidelity).
- Simple, practical algorithm (no tuning beyond ρ and known ζ, λ); experiments qualitatively support theory and illustrate fidelity allocation behavior.

Weaknesses and questions
- Assumption of known, uniform bias bounds ζ(m) across arms is strong. In many applications these are unknown or arm-dependent. How sensitive is MF-UCB to misspecified ζ(m)? Can the algorithm be adapted to estimate or adapt to ζ(m)?
- The regret definition credits each play with μ(M) regardless of fidelity, which is appropriate for scenarios where low-fidelity evaluations provide information but no intrinsic payoff. However, in many applications lower fidelities do yield (possibly smaller) rewards. The paper notes alternative definitions in the conclusion; more discussion on how MF-UCB and the analysis would adapt to other regret notions would strengthen the contribution.
- Assumption 1 (decay of ζ(m) relative to ψ) is introduced to avoid degenerate fidelities and to highlight MF-UCB’s advantages. While the authors claim it is not needed for analysis per se, the main qualitative improvements rely on it. More explicit statements of theorems/lemmas without this assumption (even if constants worsen) would clarify the true generality.
- Lower bound covers Bernoulli rewards and assumes μ* > 1/2, ζ(1) < 1/2. These technical conditions seem inessential; a brief discussion on extending to sub-Gaussian families (beyond a remark on ψ) would be helpful.
- Experimental evaluation is limited to synthetic data. The paper would benefit from:
  - Comparisons to stronger baselines beyond single-fidelity UCB, e.g., two-phase “screen-then-UCB” heuristics, Successive Rejects-style pruning with multi-fidelity, or Thompson sampling variants using lower fidelities.
  - Sensitivity analyses: varying λ and ζ schedules, mis-specified ζ, different M, and distributions (sub-Gaussian vs. heavier tails).
  - A small real or semi-synthetic study (e.g., algorithm selection with subsampled datasets) to demonstrate practical viability.
- Some technical choices could be motivated more: the derivation and optimality of γ(m), and the role of ρ>4 (vs. >2) beyond a brief remark. Intuition on the partition K(m) and the sets Jη would benefit from more examples.

Quality
The paper is technically careful, with clear assumptions and nonasymptotic-style bounds parameterized by ψ. Proof sketches are informative and the full proofs are reportedly in the appendix. The gap highlighted by the lower bound is candidly acknowledged and posed as open. Empirical results are consistent, though limited in scope.

Clarity
Overall well organized and readable. Notation is dense (K(m), Jη(m), Δ(m)k, partitions), but definitions are precise. Figure 1 is helpful; including the exact λ/ζ settings for the simulations in the main text would aid comprehension. Minor issues: consistent use of ψ−1 and stating the sub-Gaussian specialization earlier could make the paper more accessible.

Originality
High. The multi-fidelity MAB with biased approximations, explicit cost model, and a regret framework/algorithm that exploits fidelity hierarchies appears novel. The fidelity-selection mechanism and its analysis are nontrivial.

Significance
High. The setting is broadly relevant, the algorithm is simple, and the theory demonstrates tangible gains over standard UCB when many arms are clearly suboptimal at low cost. This is likely to stimulate follow-up work (e.g., learning ζ, contextual or structured variants, Thompson sampling, best-arm identification with fidelities, adversarial settings).

Pros for acceptance
- New and relevant problem formulation with strong motivation
- Elegant, simple algorithm with principled fidelity selection
- Solid theoretical guarantees and informative lower bound
- Clear demonstration of when and why multi-fidelity helps

Cons for acceptance
- Strong knowledge assumptions (ζ(m), ψ) and limited discussion of robustness
- Experiments are synthetic only and lack comparisons to stronger multi-fidelity baselines
- Lower-bound scope restricted to Bernoulli and specific parameter ranges
- Regret definition fits only one class of applications; alternatives are deferred

Suggestions for improvement
- Add experiments with baselines that explicitly pre-screen arms via low fidelity.
- Provide sensitivity to ζ mis-specification and arm-dependent ζ.
- Discuss or sketch extensions to alternative regret definitions where low-fidelity yields intrinsic reward.
- Clarify the derivation and optimality of γ(m); give a short intuitive derivation in the main text.
- State a version of the main bound without Assumption 1 (even with weaker constants), and discuss practical guidance for choosing fidelities when Assumption 1 fails.

Overall recommendation
Weak accept to accept. The paper is original, technically solid, and addresses an important and practical variant of bandits. Strengthening the empirical section and clarifying robustness would make it even more compelling.
