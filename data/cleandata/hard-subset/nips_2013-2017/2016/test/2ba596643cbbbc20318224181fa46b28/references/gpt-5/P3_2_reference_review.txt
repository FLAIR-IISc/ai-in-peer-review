Summary and context
This paper formulates a stochastic K-armed bandit setting where each arm admits M fidelities: lower fidelities are cheaper but biased approximations of the highest-fidelity reward, with known fidelity-dependent bias bounds ζ(m); costs λ(m) increase with m. The objective is to minimize a cost-weighted pseudo-regret under a capital budget Λ, where the benchmark is an oracle that always plays an optimal arm at any fidelity but the reward is evaluated at the highest fidelity µ(M). The authors propose MF-UCB, which maintains UCBs for µ(M) per arm by combining per-fidelity empirical means, confidence bonuses, and a bias offset ζ(m), and chooses: (i) the arm with the smallest cross-fidelity upper bound, and (ii) the lowest fidelity whose statistical uncertainty remains above a threshold γ(m) tuned to λ(m), λ(m+1), ζ(m), ψ. They derive an upper bound showing MF-UCB spends most capital on promising arms at high fidelities and uses low-fidelity plays to eliminate clearly suboptimal arms; they also provide a lower bound showing near-optimality except for a specific subset of arms where lower-fidelity gaps are small but positive. Experiments on synthetic problems show improved regret over naïve high-fidelity UCB.

Relation to prior work: The setting is distinct from classical UCB (single fidelity) and from budgeted bandits where the objective is reward per cost. The paper is related to multi-fidelity optimization/BO (Huang et al., Rajnarayan et al.) and multi-fidelity RL (Cutler et al.), but those do not formalize bandit regret. It is also related in spirit to weak/strong labelers in active learning (Zhang & Chaudhuri). A closer line not discussed is bandits with knapsacks (e.g., Badanidiyuru et al., Kleinberg-Slivkins), which also model costs under resource constraints, though the objective and feedback model differ; and bandits with side observations/structured correlations, which share the idea of leveraging cheap information to reduce expensive exploration.

Strengths
- Novel and well-motivated formalization of multi-fidelity stochastic bandits with explicit cost modeling and biased low-fidelity feedback.
- Clear algorithmic idea: combine fidelity-specific UCBs with a principled fidelity-escalation rule γ(m) reflecting cost and bias.
- Theoretical contributions: (i) regret upper bound that improves over single-fidelity UCB by a factor tied to λ(m)/λ(M) for many suboptimal arms; (ii) a matching lower bound for a broad class of arms, with a transparent explanation of the remaining gap.
- Analysis exhibits a clean partition of arms into K(m) layers, neatly capturing where exploration should occur; the proof structure is reasonable given the complexities of cross-fidelity elimination.
- Synthetic experiments corroborate the predicted qualitative behavior (few high-fidelity pulls on clearly bad arms).

Weaknesses and questions
- Knowledge of ζ(m) and ψ is assumed and built into both the UCB offset (ζ) and γ(m). In practice, fidelity biases are arm-dependent and rarely known; robustness to misspecified ζ or adaptive calibration is not addressed empirically or theoretically.
- Assumption 1 (fast decay of ζ relative to ψ) is used to obtain clean improvements; its necessity and prevalence in realistic settings are only partially discussed. The paper would benefit from either weakening it, quantifying the degradation when it fails, or empirically probing regimes where ζ decays slowly.
- Regret definition uses µ(M) as the pseudo-reward even when playing at lower fidelities, which is coherent for the stated applications, but somewhat nonstandard; the paper rightly mentions alternatives in the conclusion but does not analyze them. A brief discussion of how the analysis would change for other natural choices (e.g., taking qt = µ(mt)) would strengthen the contribution.
- The lower bound requires Assumption 3 (subpolynomial pulls of suboptimal arms overall), which is reasonable but not automatic; some commentary on its necessity (does any near-optimal algorithm satisfy it?) would help.
- Empirics are limited: only synthetic benchmarks and a single baseline (naïve UCB). Missing comparisons to simple multi-stage elimination heuristics (e.g., fixed-budget low-fidelity elimination then high-fidelity UCB), to budgeted bandit variants, or to Thompson sampling adaptations. No sensitivity to ζ mis-specification, λ ratios, or M.
- Minor clarity issues: The escalation condition in Algorithm 1 could be rephrased for intuition; some inequalities drop constants and could use a small appendix table of constants to aid reproducibility.

Quality
The technical development is sound and carefully argued. The γ(m) choice is well motivated and flows through the proofs; the K(m) partition is insightful. The upper bound scaling and comparisons to single-fidelity UCB are convincing. The lower bound construction is standard (change of measure) but appropriately adapted. The gap for K(m)7 arms is acknowledged; while unresolved, the discussion is honest and informative.

Clarity
Overall well written and organized. The notation is heavy but mostly consistent. Improvements:
- State early that ψ is the CGF-based tail function and give explicit ψ for sub-Gaussians to ground γ(m).
- Provide a compact intuitive description of the fidelity selection rule alongside Eq. (4).
- Explicitly state independence assumptions across fidelities and across time.
- Summarize constants and conditions in Theorem 2 in the main text.

Originality
The problem formulation and regret notion for multi-fidelity biased feedback are novel in the bandit literature. The algorithmic idea—using fidelity-specific UCBs with bias offsets and a cost-aware escalation criterion—is a meaningful extension of UCB to this setting. The lower bound and near-optimality characterization are original.

Significance
This work opens a useful direction for settings where cheap biased proxies are available (ads, simulations, subsampling). The theoretical insights are likely to influence subsequent methods (e.g., contextual extensions, Bayesian variants, GP-based models). With stronger empirical validation and robustness analysis, the ideas could see practical adoption.

Suggestions
- Investigate robustness to misspecified ζ(m): both theory (e.g., conservative overestimation) and experiments.
- Compare against: (i) a two-phase elimination baseline; (ii) bandits with knapsacks heuristic adapted to this setting; (iii) Thompson sampling variant with fidelity-aware priors.
- Discuss/experiment with arm-dependent ζk(m) or stochastic bias models.
- Clarify how to choose ρ in practice and sensitivity to it.
- Consider adaptive estimation of ψ or using canonical sub-Gaussian bonuses.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel, well-motivated setting and algorithm.
- Strong theoretical guarantees and insight.
- Clear improvement over standard UCB in the intended regime.
- Clean, reproducible conceptual framework.

Cons for acceptance
- Reliance on known global ζ(m) and ψ; no robustness analysis.
- Limited empirical evaluation and baselines.
- Restrictive Assumption 1 for best-case improvements; unclear prevalence.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper makes a solid theoretical contribution on an important and timely variant of bandits, with a principled algorithm and near-optimal guarantees. Addressing robustness and empirical comparisons would further strengthen the work.
