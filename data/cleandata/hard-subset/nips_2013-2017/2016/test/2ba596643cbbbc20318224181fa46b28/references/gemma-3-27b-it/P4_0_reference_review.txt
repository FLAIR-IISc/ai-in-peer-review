The paper "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit" introduces a novel framework for multi-armed bandit problems where evaluating arms at different fidelities is possible, each with varying costs and accuracy. The core idea is to leverage cheaper, lower-fidelity evaluations to quickly eliminate suboptimal arms, reserving more expensive, high-fidelity evaluations for a smaller set of promising candidates. The authors propose the Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB) algorithm and provide a theoretical analysis demonstrating improved regret bounds compared to standard UCB when lower fidelities offer significant cost savings.

This work builds upon the extensive literature on multi-armed bandits (e.g., [9, 11, 12]) and UCB algorithms [2, 3]. It differentiates itself by explicitly addressing scenarios where obtaining rewards isn't instantaneous or uniform in cost. While multi-fidelity optimization has been explored in various domains [7, 10, 6], this paper specifically formalizes and analyzes regret in a multi-fidelity bandit setting, a gap identified in the related work. The connection to active learning with weak and strong labelers [16] is acknowledged, but the authors highlight the distinct focus on exploration-exploitation trade-offs inherent in bandits. The recent work by Kandasamy et al. [8] on multi-fidelity bandits with Gaussian process payoffs is also acknowledged, showing awareness of current research.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The formulation of a multi-fidelity bandit problem with a cost-weighted regret metric is a significant contribution. This extends the classical bandit framework to a more realistic setting with varying evaluation costs and fidelities.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of the MF-UCB algorithm. The regret bound is clearly stated and compared to standard UCB, highlighting the potential for improvement. The lower bound demonstrates near-optimality.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is clearly defined, and the algorithm is presented in a straightforward manner. The motivations for design choices, like the choice of γ(m), are explained.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The inclusion of simulations provides evidence supporting the effectiveness of MF-UCB compared to UCB. The visualizations of the number of plays at each fidelity offer intuition regarding algorithm behaviour.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Assumption 1:** The regularity condition on the ζ(m) values (Assumption 1) seems somewhat restrictive. While the authors acknowledge it's not strictly necessary, its absence affects the performance improvement over UCB. It's unclear how common this assumption is in practical scenarios. A discussion of its practical implications could be beneficial.
*   **Lower Bound Gap:** The authors acknowledge a gap between their upper bound (MF-UCB's performance) and the derived lower bound, particularly for certain arms (K(m)7). While they provide a reasonable explanation, closing this gap would strengthen the theoretical results.
*   **Limited Simulation Scope:** The simulation results, while supportive, are limited to synthetic problems.  Exploring the algorithm's performance on real-world datasets or more complex scenarios would enhance the practical relevance of the findings.
*   **Regret Definition:** The choice of *qt* = µ(M)It in the regret definition, while motivated, could be debated. The authors acknowledge alternative choices, but a more thorough exploration of the implications of different regret definitions would be valuable.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a novel and important problem in the bandit literature.
*   The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides strong guarantees on the algorithm's performance.
*   The empirical results support the theoretical findings.
*   The writing is clear and the paper is well-organized.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The restrictive Assumption 1 limits the generality of the results.
*   The gap between the upper and lower bounds weakens the theoretical argument.
*   The lack of real-world evaluation limits the practical impact of the research.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of multi-armed bandits. The problem formulation is novel, the theoretical analysis is sound, and the empirical results are encouraging. While some weaknesses exist, they do not significantly detract from the overall quality of the work.  I believe the paper is worthy of acceptance to NIPS, though addressing the concerns regarding Assumption 1 and the lower bound gap would further strengthen it.
