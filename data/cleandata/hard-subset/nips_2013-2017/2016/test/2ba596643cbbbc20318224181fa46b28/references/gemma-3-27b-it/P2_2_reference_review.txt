## Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a multi-fidelity extension to the classic multi-armed bandit (MAB) problem, motivated by scenarios where obtaining precise reward estimates is costly, but cheaper, approximate estimates are available. The authors formalize a cost-weighted notion of regret and propose a Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB) algorithm that intelligently balances exploration at different fidelities. They provide theoretical guarantees demonstrating that MF-UCB can outperform standard UCB algorithms, particularly when the number of arms is large and only a small subset are truly optimal.  The key idea is to use low-fidelity evaluations to quickly eliminate suboptimal arms and focus high-fidelity evaluations on the most promising candidates.

**Relationship to Prior Work:**

The paper builds directly upon the foundational work in MABs, particularly UCB algorithms (Auer et al., 2002).  It distinguishes itself from prior work on multi-fidelity optimization (e.g., Huang et al., 2006) by explicitly focusing on the exploration-exploitation trade-off inherent in the bandit setting and defining a relevant regret metric.  While related work exists on budgeted bandits (Tran-Thanh et al., 2013) and active learning with weak/strong labelers (Zhang & Chaudhuri, 2016), the authors clearly articulate how their setting differs – specifically, the emphasis on *approximations* to the true reward rather than simply cheaper evaluations.  The recent work by Kandasamy et al. (2016) on multi-fidelity bandits with Gaussian processes is also acknowledged, positioning this work as a more general, distribution-agnostic treatment.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Formulation:** The introduction of a multi-fidelity bandit framework is a valuable contribution. It accurately captures a realistic scenario that arises in many applications (e.g., online advertising, algorithm selection, robotics).
* **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of MF-UCB, demonstrating its performance bounds and showing how it can outperform standard UCB in certain regimes. The lower bound analysis is particularly strong.
* **Clear Algorithm:** The MF-UCB algorithm is well-defined and relatively easy to understand. The switching criterion based on the fidelity-specific uncertainty and cost is intuitive.
* **Empirical Validation:** The simulations provide evidence supporting the theoretical claims and demonstrate the practical benefits of MF-UCB.
* **Detailed Analysis and Proofs:** The paper provides thorough proofs and detailed analysis, building confidence in the validity of the results.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions:** The assumption that the lower fidelity means are within a known bound (ζ(m)) of the high-fidelity mean, while reasonable, might not hold in all real-world scenarios.  The sensitivity of the results to violations of this assumption is not fully explored.
* **Regularity Condition:** Assumption 1, requiring a specific decay rate for the ζ(m) values, feels somewhat restrictive.  While the authors acknowledge it isn't *necessary*, its impact on performance when violated isn’t clearly quantified.
* **Lower Bound Gap:** The authors honestly acknowledge a gap between their regret bound and the theoretical lower bound for certain scenarios, stemming from the switching criterion. While this is a nuanced point, it suggests there might be room for further improvement.
* **Clarity of γ(m) Choice:** The motivation for the specific form of γ(m) (Equation 4) is a bit hand-wavy. A more intuitive explanation of why this choice is optimal would be beneficial.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of bandit algorithms. The problem formulation is compelling, the algorithm is well-designed, and the theoretical analysis is rigorous. The strengths significantly outweigh the weaknesses. While the assumptions and regularity condition are worth considering, they do not fundamentally undermine the paper's contributions.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Novel and well-motivated problem formulation.
* Strong theoretical guarantees.
* Clear and well-explained algorithm.
* Empirical validation supports the theoretical claims.
* Thorough analysis and proofs.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*  The assumptions (especially ζ(m) bounds) might not always hold in practice.
*  The regularity condition on ζ(m) could be restrictive.
*  The gap between the regret bound and the lower bound, although acknowledged, could be a point of concern.




**Rating:**  **Strong Accept (8/10)**

I believe this paper is of high quality and deserving of acceptance to NIPS. It addresses an important problem, provides a rigorous solution, and is clearly presented.  The minor weaknesses do not detract significantly from the overall value of the work.
