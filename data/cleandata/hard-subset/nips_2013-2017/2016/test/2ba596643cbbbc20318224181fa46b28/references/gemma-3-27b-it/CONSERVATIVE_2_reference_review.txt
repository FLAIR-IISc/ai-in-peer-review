This paper introduces a multi-fidelity bandit setting where evaluating arms comes with varying costs and access to cheaper, albeit biased, approximations of the true reward. The authors propose MF-UCB, an algorithm adapting the UCB approach, and provide theoretical regret bounds demonstrating potential improvements over standard UCB when lower fidelities effectively prune suboptimal arms. The work builds on the well-established UCB framework and attempts to address a practical challenge – efficiently allocating resources when exact evaluation is expensive.

The core idea is sound and addresses a relevant problem. Many real-world applications, such as hyperparameter tuning or A/B testing, involve trade-offs between evaluation cost and accuracy. Formalizing this within the bandit framework is a natural extension. However, the paper suffers from a lack of compelling evidence that the proposed method offers substantial practical benefits over simpler baselines beyond the specific theoretical scenarios outlined. 

**Strengths:**

* **Well-motivated problem:** The multi-fidelity setting is intuitively appealing and relevant to numerous applications.
* **Theoretical analysis:** The regret bound provides a clear understanding of MF-UCB’s performance under specific assumptions. The analysis appears rigorous, and the comparison to standard UCB is insightful.
* **Clear presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and the notation is clearly defined.
* **Connection to existing work:** The authors appropriately situate their work within the broader bandit literature and acknowledge related efforts in multi-fidelity optimization.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited empirical evaluation:** The simulations presented are insufficient to demonstrate the practical advantages of MF-UCB. The figure lacks detail regarding the specific simulation setup (e.g., the magnitude of the biases introduced by lower fidelities, the cost ratios between fidelities) and the statistical significance of the observed improvements. Simply stating that MF-UCB “outperforms naive UCB” is not sufficient; quantitative results with error bars are crucial.
* **Strong assumptions:** The theoretical analysis relies on several assumptions, including the knowledge of ζ(m) (the bias bounds) and Assumption 1 regarding the decay of these biases. These assumptions may not hold in many practical scenarios. The paper doesn’t adequately discuss the sensitivity of the results to violations of these assumptions. The impact of inaccurate ζ(m) estimates is not explored.
* **Lack of comparison to strong baselines:** The paper primarily compares to a “naive UCB” which simply uses the highest fidelity. A more thorough evaluation would include comparisons to other relevant strategies, such as Thompson Sampling adapted to the multi-fidelity setting, or simpler heuristics that dynamically switch between fidelities based on uncertainty estimates.  It is not clear that the presented advantage over "naive UCB" represents a significant practical improvement.
* **Lower bound gap:** The authors acknowledge a gap between their upper bound and the derived lower bound. This gap suggests that the algorithm may not be fully optimal, and further improvements may be possible.
* **Regret definition:** While the cost-weighted regret is a natural extension, its interpretation isn’t fully clear. The choice of qt = µ(M)It, while motivated, may not be appropriate in all scenarios. The discussion of alternative choices feels underdeveloped.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The problem is well-motivated and the theoretical results are mathematically sound.
* The paper represents a novel extension of the classical bandit framework.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The empirical evaluation is weak and doesn’t convincingly demonstrate practical benefits.
* The strong assumptions limit the applicability of the theoretical results.
* The lack of comparison to strong baselines weakens the claim of improved performance.
* The gap between upper and lower bounds suggests potential for improvement.



**Overall Assessment:**

I find this paper to be a theoretically sound but ultimately underwhelming contribution. While the problem setting and proposed algorithm are interesting, the lack of strong empirical evidence and the reliance on restrictive assumptions raise serious concerns.  The theoretical results, while correct, don’t translate into a compelling case for the practical utility of MF-UCB. The paper feels incomplete – the simulations need to be significantly expanded and more robust, and the algorithm should be compared to a wider range of baselines.  I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to address the weaknesses identified above. The authors should prioritize a more thorough empirical validation and a discussion of the algorithm's robustness to realistic data distributions and parameter settings.
