- Problem: Introduces stochastic K-armed bandits with multiple fidelities per arm where lower fidelities are cheaper but biased approximations of the highest-fidelity reward.
- Regret: Defines a cost-weighted pseudo-regret under a fixed budget that rewards as if highest-fidelity outcomes were observed.
- Algorithm: Proposes MF-UCB which inflates per-fidelity UCBs by known bias bounds and picks fidelities via a cost–information threshold gamma(m).
- Theory — upper bound: Proves a regret bound improving over vanilla UCB by spending most budget on cheap fidelities to eliminate clearly suboptimal arms.
- Theory — lower bound: Provides a Bernoulli lower bound showing MF-UCB is near-optimal except for arms with small positive gaps at lower fidelities.
- Relation to prior work: Extends UCB-style bandits to multi-fidelity, cost-aware settings and differs from budgeted bandits, active learning, and continuous multi-fidelity optimization.
- Novelty: First principled regret analysis for K-armed bandits with biased, multi-cost approximations, per the reviewer.
- Technical soundness: Algorithmic design and analysis are coherent; proofs plausible though deferred to appendix.
- Key assumptions: Requires known, decreasing bias bounds zeta(m), per-fidelity concentration psi, and a regularity condition on zeta decay.
- Limitations of assumptions: Knowing zeta(m) and psi and tuning rho may limit applicability; no robustness to misspecification studied.
- Lower-bound scope: Lower bound assumes Bernoulli rewards and specific parameter ranges; generalization not addressed.
- Empirics: Synthetic experiments support theory showing cheap filtering of suboptimal arms, but empirical evaluation is brief.
- Empirical suggestions: Needs more ablations, sensitivity analyses (lambda, zeta, M), and baselines (heuristics, Thompson variants).
- Clarity: Paper is well-organized and readable for experts; notation is heavy but systematic; key ideas illustrated.
- Suggested clarifications: Provide guidance on estimating zeta(m), re-derive gamma(m) in main text, and expand empirical details.
- Strengths: Clear problem formalization, simple principled algorithm, solid theoretical guarantees, and strong intuition linking cost and bias.
- Weaknesses: Reliance on known zeta/psi, ad hoc regularity condition, limited lower-bound generality, and narrow empirical study.
- Open questions for authors: Sensitivity to zeta misspecification, extending lower bound beyond Bernoulli/sub-Gaussian, variants to close the switching-gap, and per-arm lambda/zeta heterogeneity.
- Pros: Novel timely setting, clean algorithm, strong theory, and practical relevance.
- Cons: Practical deployability hinges on knowing zeta(m), limited empirical validation, and some restrictive assumptions.
- Recommendation: Reviewer recommends acceptance, requesting improved empirical section and discussion of robustness to bias misspecification.
