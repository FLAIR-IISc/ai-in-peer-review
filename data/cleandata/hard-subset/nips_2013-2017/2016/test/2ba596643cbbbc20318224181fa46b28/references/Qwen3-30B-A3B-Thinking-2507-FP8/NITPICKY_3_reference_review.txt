

# Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

This paper introduces a multi-fidelity extension to the classical multi-armed bandit problem, where each arm has multiple approximations (fidelities) with different costs. The authors propose a new algorithm, MF-UCB, and provide theoretical regret bounds and a lower bound. While the problem is relevant to practical applications like online advertising and algorithm selection, the paper suffers from numerous technical inconsistencies, unclear notations, and minor but significant methodological issues that undermine its credibility.

**Quality Issues:**
The paper's main technical contribution is the regret bound for MF-UCB. However, several key issues prevent the analysis from being fully convincing:
- The regret definition (Equation 1) is inconsistent with the problem statement. The authors claim to define a "cost weighted notion of regret" but simply add a cost term to standard regret, which is not a truly novel concept.
- The assumption ζ(1) > ζ(2) > · · · > ζ(M) = 0 is stated without justification, and the paper later uses ζ(M) in regret bounds, which would be zero, making the bound trivial.
- The choice of γ(m) = ψ⁻¹(λ(m)/λ(m+1) ψ(ζ(m))) is not well-motivated, and the paper fails to explain why this specific choice leads to optimal performance.
- The lower bound (Theorem 4) shows a gap for arms in K(m)₇, contradicting the claim that MF-UCB is "near-optimal."
- The paper states "MF-UCB is near-optimal" but the lower bound (Equation 6) shows a gap for some arms, making the claim inaccurate.

**Clarity Issues:**
The paper contains numerous notational inconsistencies and ambiguous definitions:
- Inconsistent use of "K" for both the set of arms and the number of arms.
- Confusing notation for partitions: "JkK = m s.t. k ∈ K(m)" is poorly defined and not consistently used.
- The definition of K(m) is inconsistent with its properties (e.g., "For all k ∈ K(m), ∆(m)k > 2γ(m)" but this isn't true for k ∈ K(1)).
- The paper uses "Kg" in the abstract without defining it until later (Section 4.1), and uses inconsistent capitalization ("Kg" vs "Kg").
- Inconsistent use of "log" without specifying the base (natural log vs base-2), which matters for theoretical analysis.
- The paper claims "the ζ(m)'s decay fast enough" but doesn't provide a clear mathematical condition that would be useful for readers.

**Minor but Significant Technical Errors:**
- The regret bound in Theorem 2 states "E[R(Λ)] log(nΛ) . ..." but the log should be in the denominator, not multiplied.
- In the lower bound (Theorem 4), it states "lim inf Λ→∞ E[R(Λ)] log(nΛ) ≥ c · ..." but the log(nΛ) should be in the denominator.
- The example of exponentially decaying ζ(m) values (1/√2, 1/2, 1/2√2, ...) does not decay exponentially.
- The paper states "the performance of MF-UCB when compared to UCB is most appealing when the above holds" but "the above" refers to an assumption that is not necessary for the analysis.
- The paper claims "For sub-Gaussian distributions, the condition is satisfied" but doesn't provide a proof or reference for this claim.

**Originality and Significance:**
The multi-fidelity bandit framework is somewhat novel, but the paper fails to clearly distinguish it from prior work, particularly Kandasamy et al. [8] which is mentioned as "we extended ideas in this work." The paper doesn't adequately explain how its approach differs from this prior work. The significance claim that MF-UCB "outperforms naive UCB" is supported by simulations, but the paper lacks sufficient detail about the experimental setup or quantitative improvements.

**Conclusion:**
While the paper addresses a relevant problem and presents a theoretically interesting extension to bandit algorithms, the numerous minor technical inconsistencies and notational errors significantly undermine its credibility. As a perfectionist reviewer, I find these issues too numerous to overlook, even though the core idea is sound. The paper would benefit from a thorough rewrite to resolve these inconsistencies and provide clearer motivation for key design choices.

The paper's quality is compromised by these minor but pervasive issues, which collectively prevent it from meeting NIPS's standards for a high-quality scientific contribution. I recommend rejection.
