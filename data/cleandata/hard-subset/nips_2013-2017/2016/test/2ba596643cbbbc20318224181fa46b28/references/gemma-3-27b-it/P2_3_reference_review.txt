## Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a multi-fidelity extension to the classical K-armed bandit problem. The authors motivate this extension with practical examples like online advertising and algorithm selection, where obtaining high-fidelity reward estimates is costly, but cheaper, approximate estimates are available. They propose a novel algorithm, Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB), that leverages these multiple fidelities to reduce regret, particularly when the number of arms is large and only a subset are truly promising.  The paper provides a theoretical analysis demonstrating near-optimality and empirical validation through simulations.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty and Significance:** The multi-fidelity bandit setting is a natural and important generalization of the classic problem, with clear relevance to many real-world applications. Addressing the exploration-exploitation trade-off in the presence of varying fidelity estimates is a significant contribution.
* **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical analysis appears rigorous, with a well-defined regret metric, assumptions, and a regret bound for MF-UCB. The proof sketches provided are convincing, and the lower bound analysis offers valuable insights. The careful consideration of the bias introduced by lower fidelities is commendable.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and provides intuitive explanations. The algorithm is clearly presented, and the notation is consistent.
* **Empirical Validation:** The simulations demonstrate the performance gains of MF-UCB over a naive UCB implementation, supporting the theoretical results.
* **Detailed Analysis:** The partitioning of arms into K(m) sets and the detailed analysis of regret based on these sets provide a strong understanding of how the algorithm operates and its limitations.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions:** The assumption of known ζ(m) values (the bias between fidelities) might be restrictive in practical scenarios. While acknowledged, the impact of inaccurate ζ(m) estimates isn’t thoroughly discussed.
* **Lower Bound Gap:** The authors acknowledge a gap between their upper bound and the lower bound, particularly for arms that are initially misidentified.  While the explanation is insightful, resolving this gap would further strengthen the paper.
* **Simulation Scope:** The simulations are somewhat limited.  Exploring a wider range of parameter settings, reward distributions, and problem sizes would increase confidence in the generalizability of the results.  Specifically, how does performance degrade with increasing numbers of fidelities?
* **Discussion of Related Work:**  While the related work section is adequate, it could benefit from a more detailed discussion of work in areas like Bayesian Optimization with surrogate models, which shares similarities with the multi-fidelity approach.

**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** 8/10 - The paper presents a technically sound and complete piece of work. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the empirical validation is supportive.
* **Clarity:** 8/10 - The paper is well-written and easy to follow, despite the complexity of the problem.
* **Originality:** 9/10 - The multi-fidelity bandit setting is a novel and important generalization of the classical problem.
* **Significance:** 8/10 - The results are likely to be useful to researchers and practitioners working with costly evaluation processes.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses an important and practically relevant problem.
* Provides a novel and theoretically grounded algorithm (MF-UCB).
* Demonstrates strong empirical performance.
* Offers a clear and insightful analysis of the algorithm's behavior.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*  The assumption of known ζ(m) values is potentially limiting.
*  The gap between the upper and lower bounds, while explained, could be improved.
*  The simulation scope could be broader.



**Overall Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of bandit optimization. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and can be addressed through revisions.  I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the following points:

1. **Sensitivity Analysis:** Discuss the sensitivity of the algorithm's performance to errors in the estimation of ζ(m).
2. **Further Simulation:**  Expand the simulation experiments to include a wider range of scenarios, particularly varying the number of fidelities and reward distributions.
3. **Related Work:** Briefly discuss connections to related work in Bayesian Optimization.



I believe that addressing these points will further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact.
