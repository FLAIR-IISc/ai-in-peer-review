

# Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

This paper introduces a novel framework for multi-armed bandit problems with multiple fidelities, where each arm can be evaluated at different levels of cost and accuracy. The core insight is that cheaper, lower-fidelity approximations can be used to eliminate suboptimal arms, reserving expensive high-fidelity evaluations for a smaller subset of promising arms. This approach contrasts with standard bandit algorithms like UCB that only use the highest fidelity.

The paper makes three key contributions: (1) a formal framework for multi-fidelity bandits with a cost-weighted regret definition, (2) the MF-UCB algorithm that adapts UCB to this setting, and (3) theoretical guarantees showing MF-UCB achieves better regret than standard bandit methods when lower fidelities are cheaper and can identify a small subset of high-reward arms. The regret bound O((λ(1)K + λ(2)|Kg|)log(Λ/λ(2))) demonstrates that MF-UCB outperforms UCB when |Kg| < K and λ(1) < λ(2), which is a significant theoretical advance. The lower bound analysis shows MF-UCB is near-optimal for most arms, with a small gap only for arms that are misleading at lower fidelities.

The paper is well-motivated with clear examples from online advertising and algorithm selection, where cheaper approximations of expensive experiments exist. The theoretical analysis is thorough, with careful definitions of the problem setting, a detailed regret bound proof sketch, and a matching lower bound. The empirical results on synthetic problems convincingly demonstrate MF-UCB's superiority over standard UCB, with the number of plays at different fidelities aligning with the theoretical predictions.

This work represents a significant conceptual advance in bandit theory. While there has been prior work on multi-fidelity methods in optimization (e.g., [7, 10]) and robotics [6], none formalize regret in the bandit setting as the authors do here. The paper clearly distinguishes itself from related work in the "Related Work" section, showing the novelty of their formalism and regret definition. The problem is well-motivated, the approach is bold in its extension of bandit theory, and the results are compelling.

The only minor weaknesses are: (1) the paper assumes ζ(m) decay conditions that may not hold in all applications, though the authors acknowledge this; (2) the empirical section is limited to synthetic problems, though the authors note they plan to explore real-world applications in future work. These are minor concerns for a paper that introduces a fundamentally new problem framework.

This paper's strength lies in its conceptual novelty. It doesn't just incrementally improve bandit algorithms but reimagines the problem by incorporating multiple fidelities into the core framework. The regret bound analysis is elegant and shows how the algorithm efficiently allocates resources across fidelities. The paper successfully bridges theoretical bandit analysis with practical considerations of cost and approximation quality.

In contrast to many bandit papers that offer marginal improvements to existing methods, this work introduces a genuinely new direction for exploration-exploitation trade-offs. The multi-fidelity framework has clear applicability to many real-world problems where expensive experiments can be approximated at lower cost, such as hyperparameter tuning in machine learning or clinical trials.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides sufficient detail for replication. The theoretical results are sound, and the empirical validation supports the claims. While the problem could be seen as a natural extension of bandit problems, the authors convincingly demonstrate that it requires a fundamentally new approach to regret analysis and algorithm design.

This is the kind of paper that expands the boundaries of the field, introducing a novel framework with significant theoretical and practical implications. It represents a meaningful advance over the standard bandit setting, not just an incremental improvement. The authors have successfully created a new direction for bandit research that will likely influence future work.

Given the high novelty, clear significance, and strong theoretical foundation, I strongly recommend acceptance. This paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings as it introduces a fresh perspective on an important problem class. The authors have not only solved a specific problem but have also created a new framework that will enable future research in multi-fidelity decision-making.
