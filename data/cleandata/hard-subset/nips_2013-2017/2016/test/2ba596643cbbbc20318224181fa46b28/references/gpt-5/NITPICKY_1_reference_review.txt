Summary
The paper introduces a multi-fidelity variant of the stochastic K-armed bandit where each arm can be queried at multiple fidelities m=1,…,M with increasing cost λ(m) and decreasing bias ζ(m) relative to the true (highest-fidelity) mean μ(M)k. The authors propose MF-UCB, which maintains per-fidelity UCBs B(m)k,t for μ(M)k via B(m)k,t = X(m)k,s + ψ−1(ρ log t/s) + ζ(m) and chooses the arm with the smallest among these upper bounds, then selects the lowest fidelity whose confidence radius exceeds a threshold γ(m) = ψ−1( (λ(m)/λ(m+1)) ψ(ζ(m)) ). They define a cost-weighted pseudo-regret under a total budget Λ: R(Λ) = Λμ* − ∑t λ(mt) μ(M)It, and prove an instance-dependent upper bound (Theorem 2) showing savings when lower fidelities eliminate suboptimal arms, and a lower bound (Theorem 4) under Bernoulli rewards and an additional assumption (Assumption 3). Simulations indicate MF-UCB outperforms single-fidelity UCB.

Quality
Strengths
- Clear high-level idea: use cheap fidelities to prune arms and save expensive queries; the algorithmic design matches the insight.
- The use of per-fidelity UCBs with bias offsets and a principled switching rule γ(m) is elegant; for sub-Gaussian ψ, γ(m)=ζ(m)√(λ(m)/λ(m+1)) is intuitive.
- Nontrivial analysis: the partition K(1),…,K(M) and bounds on T(m)k,n lead to a regret decomposition that captures where cost is spent.
- Lower bound with change-of-measure highlights where MF-UCB is near-optimal and where a gap remains.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Regret definition is unusual and not fully justified. R(Λ) benchmarks against Λμ*, i.e., an oracle that accrues μ* per cost unit regardless of which fidelity is used; the algorithm’s actual observations at low fidelities do not generate true reward. This is a pseudo-regret appropriate only when every play (even at low fidelity) would earn the same true per-cost reward as a high-fidelity play. This is strong; many motivating scenarios violate it (the paper mentions this only in the conclusion). The paper needs a clearer, earlier discussion of when this notion is appropriate and comparisons to alternatives.
- Strong and somewhat opaque assumptions:
  - Known, deterministic bias bounds ζ(m) are required and Assumption 1 imposes a geometric-type decay (∑i≤m 1/ψ(ζ(i)) ≤ 1/ψ(ζ(m+1))). This is restrictive and not validated empirically; the “not necessary” claim is contradicted by its use to dominate terms in proofs.
  - Lower bound requires Bernoulli rewards, μ*∈(1/2,1), ζ(1)<1/2, and Assumption 3 (suboptimal arms are played o(n^a)). These materially limit generality.
- Theorem statements and notation are error-prone and at times inconsistent:
  - Theorem 2 as written contains two sums; it is unclear if both contribute or if one is meant to dominate. The subsequent narrative suggests the main term is λ(JkK)/ψ(Δ(JkK)k), but the theorem does not cleanly state this.
  - The definition of K(1) via J(1)ζ(1)+2γ(1) is inconsistent: J(m)η = {k: μ*−μ(m)k ≤ η}, but K(1) is then claimed to equal {k: Δ(1)k > 2γ(1)}; these sets are not equal (sign inversion). This undermines the partitioning logic used in the analysis.
  - Several quantities are introduced without rigorous handling of edge cases (e.g., B(m)k,t when T(m)k,t−1=0; X(m)k,0 is undefined; the text sets 1/s=∞ but does not define the corresponding bound value rigorously).
  - The .,.,& notation “ignoring constants” appears in explanatory prose but also leaks into theorem statements, which is inappropriate.
- Conceptual accuracy issues:
  - The introduction claims “UCB achieves O(K log n) regret and is minimax optimal,” which is inaccurate (instance-dependent bounds scale with ∑ Δ−1 log n; minimax is Θ(√(Kn)) in the stochastic setting).
  - The baseline “UCB on highest fidelity” is described with gap-free O(λ(2)K log(Λ/λ(2))) style bounds; a fair instance-dependent comparison should be presented.
- Algorithmic details:
  - The fidelity selection rule uses mt = min{m : ψ−1(ρ log t/T(m)It,t−1) ≥ γ(m) ∨ m=M}. The “∨ m=M” inside the min is odd; clarify the control flow. Behavior when T=0 and the numerical stability of ψ−1(·) at large arguments need to be spelled out.
  - Complexity per round is O(KM) due to computing all B(m)k,t; no discussion of scalability.
  - Sensitivity to ρ and ζ(m) mis-specification is not analyzed.
- Lower bound gap: The paper acknowledges a gap for K(m)7 arms and leaves resolution open; this is precisely where cost-aware switching could matter most.
- Experiments are too thin:
  - No details in the main text (Appendix C only), no error bars, unclear K, M, λ, ζ, reward distributions, or parameter ρ; no ablations.
  - Only a single baseline (vanilla UCB). Missing comparisons to other cost-aware or budgeted methods (e.g., budgeted bandits, variance-aware UCB, Thompson sampling variants).
  - Claims like “smaller gradient corroborating theory” are qualitative; slopes/scaling with Λ should be quantified.

Clarity
- Numerous formatting issues, typos, and incomplete sentences (e.g., the isolated fragment “ζ(m)+2γ(m) are indicated…”, the “th Conference…” line), inconsistent equation numbering, and incomplete references (missing years/venues). These hinder readability and reproducibility.
- Several crucial definitions (e.g., qt choice, role of μ*, N vs nΛ) could be presented more cleanly and earlier, with a schematic.

Originality
- The multi-fidelity bandit model with biased, costed fidelities and an optimistic strategy that aggregates per-fidelity UCBs appears novel and timely (related to multi-fidelity optimization and weak/strong labelers). The paper positions itself reasonably in related work.

Significance
- If clarified and tightened, the approach is potentially impactful for applications where reliable bias bounds and cost ratios are available. However, the current theoretical claims lean on restrictive assumptions, the lower bound is not fully general, and experimental evidence is insufficient to establish practical significance.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and relevant problem formulation.
- Principled algorithm with clear intuition.
- Nontrivial theoretical analysis including (partial) lower bound.
- Promising empirical trends.

Cons for acceptance
- Significant clarity/notation/formatting problems, including apparent inconsistencies in core definitions.
- Strong, insufficiently justified assumptions (known ζ, Assumption 1; lower bound conditions).
- Regret notion applicability is narrow and not adequately defended.
- Experimental section is too weak for NIPS standards; missing baselines and details.
- Some technically inaccurate or imprecise statements about classical bandits.

Questions for authors
1. Can you correct and clarify the partition definitions (J(m)η, K(m)) and restate Theorem 2 cleanly with explicit constants/big-O and dominant terms?
2. How sensitive is MF-UCB to mis-specified ζ(m)? Can you provide robustness guarantees or adaptive estimation?
3. Can you provide experiments with varying ζ/λ schedules, larger K, and additional baselines (e.g., budgeted bandits, Thompson sampling), with quantitative scaling plots?
4. Under what applications is your regret metric the right one? Can you formalize and analyze variants where low-fidelity plays yield no reward?
5. Can Assumption 1 be relaxed or removed in the main bound?

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). The paper presents a compelling idea with substantial potential, but the current submission has too many technical and presentation issues—some possibly substantive (partition inconsistency)—and insufficient experimental validation for acceptance at NIPS. With rigorous fixes, clearer theorems, and stronger empirical evaluation, this work could become a solid contribution.

Confidence: High.
