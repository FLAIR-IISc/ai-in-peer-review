Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces a stochastic K-armed bandit setting where each arm admits M fidelities: lower fidelities are cheaper but biased approximations of the highest-fidelity reward, with known bias bounds |µ(M)k − µ(m)k| ≤ ζ(m) and increasing costs λ(1) < … < λ(M). The authors define a cost-weighted pseudo-regret for a capital budget Λ, where the instantaneous reward is the highest-fidelity mean µ(M) of the played arm regardless of the fidelity queried, thereby comparing against an oracle that always plays an optimal arm at any fidelity.

They propose MF-UCB, which maintains per-fidelity UCBs B(m)k,t = X̄(m)k + ψ−1(ρ log t / s) + ζ(m), takes Bk,t = minm B(m)k,t as an upper bound on µ(M)k, selects arms by maxk Bk,t, and chooses the fidelity by escalating from low to high until the uncertainty at fidelity m drops below a threshold γ(m) = ψ−1( (λ(m)/λ(m+1)) ψ(ζ(m)) ), otherwise querying at m. The central idea is to use cheap fidelities to eliminate clearly suboptimal arms and reserve expensive queries for a small set of promising arms.

Theoretically, they derive a finite-capital regret bound (Theorem 2) showing MF-UCB’s regret scales as O((λ(1)K + λ(2)|Kg| + …) log(Λ/λ(M))), where Kg collects near-optimal arms that survive lower-fidelity elimination. They show MF-UCB is never much worse than running UCB solely at the highest fidelity and can be substantially better when many arms are clearly suboptimal at low fidelities and λ(m) increases. They also present a lower bound (Theorem 4) for Bernoulli rewards under a mild subpolynomial-pulls assumption, and identify a gap on a particular subset of arms (K(m)7) where MF-UCB can be suboptimal by a constant factor due to its switching rule. Synthetic experiments corroborate the improvements.

This work differs from budgeted bandits (which optimize reward-to-cost ratios), multi-fidelity optimization in engineering (which typically lacks regret analyses), and reinforcement learning with simulators (multi-fidelity MDPs). It is complementary to Gaussian process multi-fidelity bandits (which the authors cite as a later extension).

Quality
- Theory: The algorithm is well specified, with a clean UCB adaptation to multiple fidelities. Assumptions include concentration inequalities (generic ψ), known bias bounds ζ(m), and a regularity condition on the decay of ζ(m) (Assumption 1). The high-level proof strategy (arm partition, per-fidelity pull bounds, control of total plays N, and comparison to UCB) is standard and convincing, and the lower bound uses an appropriate change-of-measure argument à la Lai–Robbins. The identification of the K(m)7 gap is honest and insightful.
- Definitions: The regret definition is thoughtful and consistent with applications where long-run high-fidelity utility is the objective and we can cheaply probe with biased proxies. The decomposition into residual capital term and the main term is reasonable, and the residual is suitably bounded.
- Limitations: Practical reliance on known ζ(m) and ψ may be strong; robustness to misspecification is not investigated. The lower bound is specialized to Bernoulli and requires Assumption 3. Experiments are limited to synthetic settings; details are deferred and no ablations or sensitivity analyses (e.g., to ζ misspecification) are shown. Comparisons are only to naive UCB; stronger baselines (e.g., two-phase schemes, Thompson sampling, UCB-V, or “always low-fidelity until confidence then switch”) would strengthen the empirical case.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and mathematically clear. The MF-UCB rule and the role of γ(m) are well motivated. The partitioning K(1), …, K(M) is conceptually important; while a figure is referenced, the description is dense and could be streamlined with an early intuitive example. Some notational choices (e.g., mixing of indices and sets) could be tightened. The regret notion is nonstandard; more discussion of alternative objectives (as in the conclusion) earlier would help readers position the contribution.

Originality
To my knowledge, this is the first rigorous treatment of stochastic multi-armed bandits with multiple, biased fidelities per arm and per-fidelity costs under a regret notion aimed at high-fidelity utility. The algorithmic design (UCB with per-fidelity confidence + bias inflation and cost-informed switching) and the accompanying instance-dependent regret analysis and lower bound appear novel and nontrivial. The relation to budgeted bandits and multi-fidelity optimization is well discussed.

Significance
The setting is practically relevant (ads, simulators, algorithm selection) and the results provide both conceptual clarity and provable gains over standard bandits when cheap proxies are informative. The lower bound and near-optimality lend weight. If adopted, the ideas could influence how bandit algorithms leverage approximate evaluations. The main caveats are the need for known bias bounds and the limited empirical validation.

Pros
- Novel and timely problem formulation with clear motivation.
- Elegant algorithm extending UCB to multi-fidelity via bias-aware confidence intervals and cost-aware switching.
- Solid theoretical guarantees: instance-dependent upper bound, meaningful comparison to UCB, and a complementary lower bound with an insightful identified gap.
- Clear explanation of when and why multi-fidelity helps (many bad arms + cheap low fidelities).

Cons
- Requires known fidelity bias bounds ζ(m) and ψ; no robustness analysis.
- Lower bound restricted to Bernoulli and relies on a subpolynomial-pulls assumption.
- Experiments are synthetic-only and minimal; lack of stronger baselines and sensitivity studies.
- The regret notion, while motivated, may not fit all multi-fidelity applications; limited empirical discussion of alternatives.

Questions and suggestions
- Robustness: How sensitive is MF-UCB to misspecified ζ(m) (overly optimistic or pessimistic)? Can one estimate effective ζ(m) online or design a safe variant that adapts γ(m) without prior ζ?
- Baselines: Please compare against a simple two-phase policy (screen all arms at fidelity 1 to a target confidence, then restrict to a shortlist at higher fidelities) and against Thompson sampling variants with costs.
- Experiments: Include ablations varying K, M, λ ratios, ζ decay, and report per-fidelity pulls and confidence intervals. A small real or semi-synthetic case (e.g., algorithm selection with subsampling as low fidelity) would strengthen the case.
- Assumption 1: Clarify behavior when it fails (e.g., redundant fidelities). Does MF-UCB gracefully degrade to UCB’s regret, and can one merge near-redundant fidelities automatically?
- Regret objective: Consider adding an appendix with alternative regret definitions (e.g., when low-fidelity actions yield intrinsic utility) and whether MF-UCB or a variant applies.

Recommendation: Weak accept
This is a well-motivated, technically solid, and novel contribution on an important variant of bandits. The theory is strong and the insights are valuable. The main weaknesses are empirical (limited evaluation) and practical (knowledge of ζ). With additional experiments and some discussion on robustness, this would be an easy accept.
