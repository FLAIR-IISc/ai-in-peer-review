- Introduces a stochastic multi-armed bandit where each arm has multiple fidelities: cheaper biased approximations with known bias bounds ζ(m) and an expensive unbiased fidelity M under a total capital budget Λ.  
- Defines a cost-weighted pseudo-regret R(Λ)=Λµ* − Σt λ(mt) q_t with q_t equal to the high-fidelity mean of the arm played, making the comparator an oracle spending all capital on the best arm.  
- Proposes MF-UCB: fidelity-aware indices B(m)=empirical mean + confidence radius + ζ(m), taking the min across fidelities and using switching thresholds γ(m) to decide fidelity.  
- The fidelity switching rule γ(m)=ψ^{-1}((λ(m)/λ(m+1)) ψ(ζ(m))) is motivated by trading off confidence improvement cost vs bias floor.  
- Upper bound analysis shows MF-UCB spends cheap pulls to eliminate clearly suboptimal arms and reserves high-fidelity pulls for near-optimal arms, yielding cost savings vs standard UCB.  
- Decomposes arms into partitions K(m) based on how early they can be excluded using low fidelities and quantifies capital spent per partition.  
- Provides a lower bound for Bernoulli rewards that matches the upper bound on a large subset but leaves a gap for a hard subset K(m)7 due to conservative switching.  
- Theoretical development is solid and nontrivial, with principled UCB-style indices and partition-based analysis.  
- Assumes known monotone bias bounds ζ(m), known costs λ(m), and a concentration function ψ; Assumption 1 requires sufficiently fast decay of ζ(m) for large gains.  
- Limitations: lower bound is restricted to Bernoulli rewards and some proof details (high-probability control of N and Λ dependence) are delicate and in the appendix.  
- Paper is generally clear and well-structured; intuition for partitions, ζ(m) inflation, and γ(m) is well-explained despite heavy notation.  
- Regret definition is nonstandard but motivated; the paper could more explicitly contrast alternative reward/regret choices and applicability.  
- Novelty: formalizes multi-fidelity MAB with biased approximations and capital-based regret, and introduces MF-UCB with an explicit switching criterion.  
- Significance: practically relevant setting (e.g., advertising, simulation-to-real) and offers reusable theoretical insights on cost-bias-confidence tradeoffs.  
- Empirical evaluation is minimal: synthetic tasks only, single baseline (standard UCB), no KL-UCB/TS comparisons or real-data experiments.  
- Key strength: principled formalization and a simple, well-motivated algorithm with provable savings over naïve high-fidelity UCB in favorable regimes.  
- Key strength: nontrivial upper bound and a matching lower bound on many instances, with honest discussion of remaining gaps.  
- Major weakness: requires known and valid ζ(m) with no analysis of robustness to misspecification or online estimation.  
- Major weakness: regret notion credits low-fidelity pulls with high-fidelity value, limiting applicability when low fidelities provide no usable reward.  
- Major weakness: reliance on Assumption 1 for appealing gains and limited discussion/experiments when ζ decays slowly or costs are similar.  
- Major weakness: limited experiments and baselines; no ablations or real-world validation provided.  
- Questions for authors include sensitivity to misspecified ζ(m), potential adaptive γ(m) to handle K(m)7, behavior when Assumption 1 fails, choices of ψ and ρ for sub-Gaussian rewards, and formalizing alternative regret notions.  
- Minor suggestions: specify constants for γ(m) under sub-Gaussian ψ, clarify that partition K(m) is analysis-only, include ablations varying K, M, ζ decay and cost ratios, and add additional baselines (KL-UCB, TS, screen-then-UCB).  
- Recommendation: strong theoretical contribution and likely accept with the expectation of strengthened empirical evaluation and clarification of robustness; reviewer leans toward acceptance.
