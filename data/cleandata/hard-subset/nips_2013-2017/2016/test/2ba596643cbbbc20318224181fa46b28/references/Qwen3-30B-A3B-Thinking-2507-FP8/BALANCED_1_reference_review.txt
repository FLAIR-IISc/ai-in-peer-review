

# Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

This paper introduces a novel multi-fidelity extension to the classic multi-armed bandit problem, where each arm has access to multiple approximations (fidelities) with different costs and accuracy levels. The authors formalize a cost-weighted regret measure for this setting and propose MF-UCB, an algorithm that uses lower-fidelity (cheaper) evaluations to eliminate suboptimal arms while reserving higher-fidelity (more expensive) evaluations for promising arms. The paper provides theoretical guarantees showing that MF-UCB achieves better regret than standard UCB when the number of high-reward arms is small and lower fidelities are significantly cheaper, and establishes a matching lower bound demonstrating near-optimality.

The work builds on the classic bandit literature (e.g., [3, 9, 11]) and extends recent multi-fidelity optimization work [7, 10, 16]. While Kandasamy et al. [8] previously studied multi-fidelity bandits with Gaussian process payoffs, this paper provides a more general framework applicable to a broader class of problems with discrete arms and a clear cost structure. The paper's main contributions—formalizing the multi-fidelity bandit problem, proposing MF-UCB, and providing theoretical guarantees—are well-motivated and address a practical gap in the bandit literature.

**Strengths:**

1. **Quality:** The theoretical analysis is thorough and carefully constructed. The paper provides a detailed regret bound for MF-UCB and a matching lower bound, demonstrating the algorithm's near-optimality. The assumptions are clearly stated, and the analysis correctly accounts for the trade-offs between fidelity costs and accuracy. The empirical results on synthetic data support the theoretical claims.

2. **Originality:** The paper introduces a novel framework for multi-fidelity bandit problems that extends beyond previous work. The specific regret formulation that accounts for both the cost of fidelity and the quality of approximation is well-motivated by practical applications. The distinction between this work and prior multi-fidelity optimization studies is clearly articulated.

3. **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The problem formulation, algorithm description, and theoretical results are presented with sufficient mathematical rigor. The figures help illustrate the key concepts, particularly the partitioning of arms by fidelity.

4. **Significance:** The problem is highly relevant to real-world applications where expensive evaluations can be approximated with cheaper alternatives (e.g., online advertising, algorithm selection). The theoretical and empirical results provide a clear understanding of when multi-fidelity approaches yield advantages, which is valuable for practitioners.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Practical limitations:** The paper assumes that the ζ(m) parameters (the error bounds between fidelities) are known, which is a strong assumption in practice. The authors do not address how these parameters would be estimated or how their estimation might affect performance. This is a significant gap for real-world applications.

2. **Limited empirical evaluation:** The experiments are restricted to synthetic data. The paper would benefit from testing on real-world datasets to demonstrate the practical advantages of MF-UCB over UCB. The synthetic experiments also lack statistical significance tests.

3. **Comparison with related work:** While the authors mention Kandasamy et al. [8], they don't clearly articulate how their approach differs from this prior work on multi-fidelity bandits. A more detailed comparison would strengthen the paper's positioning.

4. **Computational complexity:** The paper doesn't analyze the computational complexity of MF-UCB compared to UCB, which is important for practical implementation. The cost of maintaining and updating the upper confidence bounds across multiple fidelities could be significant.

**Constructive Suggestions:**

1. Address the practical limitation of assuming known ζ(m) parameters. Consider adding a section on how these parameters might be estimated from data, or analyze the robustness of MF-UCB to estimation errors.

2. Expand the empirical evaluation to include real-world datasets that naturally exhibit multi-fidelity characteristics (e.g., online advertising click-through rate data with different observation periods).

3. Provide a more detailed comparison with Kandasamy et al. [8], clarifying the specific differences in problem formulation, assumptions, and algorithmic approach.

4. Include a discussion of the computational complexity of MF-UCB and compare it to UCB, particularly in terms of the number of fidelities M.

**Recommendation:**

This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the bandit literature by formalizing a novel multi-fidelity framework and providing theoretical guarantees. The main weaknesses are related to practical implementation considerations and limited empirical validation. With the suggested improvements, this paper would be an excellent fit for NIPS.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper's theoretical contributions are significant, and the practical relevance is clear. The suggested improvements are relatively straightforward and would substantially strengthen the paper's impact. The work advances the state of the art in bandit problems by providing a principled approach to incorporating multiple fidelity evaluations, which is valuable for both theoretical understanding and practical applications.
