The paper "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit" introduces a novel framework for multi-armed bandits where evaluating each arm has varying fidelities with associated costs. The core idea is to leverage cheaper, lower-fidelity evaluations to quickly eliminate suboptimal arms and focus expensive, high-fidelity evaluations on a smaller, promising subset. The authors propose the Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB) algorithm and provide a theoretical analysis demonstrating a regret bound of O((λ(1)K + λ(2)|Kg|) log(Λ/λ(2))), where λ(i) is the cost of fidelity i, K is the number of arms, Kg is the set of optimal arms, and Λ is the total budget.

This work builds upon the extensive literature on multi-armed bandits, particularly UCB algorithms [3, 9]. It differentiates itself by explicitly addressing the scenario of multiple fidelities, a setting not thoroughly explored in classical bandit theory. While related work acknowledges multi-fidelity methods in optimization [7, 10] and reinforcement learning [6], it lacks a formal regret analysis tailored to the bandit setting. Connections to budgeted bandits [13, 15] are noted, but the authors clearly articulate the difference—budgeted bandits focus on reward/cost ratios, while this work focuses on approximating the reward itself at varying costs. Recent work by Kandasamy et al. [8] on multi-fidelity bandits with Gaussian process payoffs is also acknowledged, positioning the current work as a contribution to the broader understanding of this emerging area.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The formalization of the multi-fidelity bandit problem with a cost-weighted regret definition is a significant contribution. This is not a trivial extension of existing bandit frameworks.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a clear theoretical analysis of MF-UCB, establishing a regret bound and demonstrating its near-optimality. The proof sketches are included which adds to the transparency and reproducibility.
* **Clear Algorithm:** The MF-UCB algorithm is well-defined and intuitively appealing. The switching criterion based on confidence bounds and cost considerations seems reasonable.
* **Empirical Validation:** The simulations presented, while not extensive, support the theoretical findings and demonstrate the algorithm’s practical effectiveness.
* **Well-written and organized:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly structured, making it easy to follow the authors’ line of reasoning. The notation is consistent.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumption 1:** The regularity condition on the ζ(m) values (Assumption 1) feels somewhat restrictive and its practical implications aren’t fully explored. The authors note it is not *necessary* for analysis, but its impact on performance when violated isn't discussed.
* **Lower Bound Gap:** The authors acknowledge a gap between their theoretical regret bound and the established lower bound (Theorem 4), particularly for arms that can be quickly identified as suboptimal.  While an explanation is offered, resolving this gap would strengthen the theoretical contribution.
* **Limited Simulations:** The simulation section is brief. More comprehensive experiments with varying parameters (e.g., different cost ratios, number of arms, distribution of rewards) would bolster confidence in the algorithm’s robustness.
* **Choice of qt:** The motivation for setting qt = µ(M)It is sound, but the discussion regarding alternative choices for qt feels somewhat cursory. Expanding on the implications of different regret definitions would be valuable.
* **Bernoulli Assumption in Lower Bound:** The lower bound proof relies on Bernoulli reward distributions, limiting its generality.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper introduces a novel and well-motivated problem.
* The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides valuable insights.
* The algorithm is clearly defined and performs well in simulations.
* The work has the potential to impact applications where multi-fidelity evaluations are common (e.g., online advertising, algorithm selection, robotics).

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The regularity assumption (Assumption 1) could be a limitation.
* The gap between the theoretical regret bound and the lower bound raises some concerns.
* The simulation section could be more extensive.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of multi-armed bandits. The novelty of the problem, the rigor of the analysis, and the clear presentation of the algorithm outweigh the minor weaknesses. While addressing the limitations regarding the regularity assumption and the lower bound gap would further strengthen the paper, it is currently of sufficient quality for acceptance to NIPS.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
