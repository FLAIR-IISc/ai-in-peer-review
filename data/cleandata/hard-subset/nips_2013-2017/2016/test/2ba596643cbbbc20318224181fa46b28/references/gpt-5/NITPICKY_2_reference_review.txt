Summary
The paper introduces a stochastic K-armed bandit setting where each arm admits M fidelities: lower fidelities are cheaper but biased approximations of the highest-fidelity (true) reward. Costs λ(m) increase with fidelity, and the bias is upper bounded by known ζ(m), decreasing with m. The authors define a capital-based pseudo-regret R(Λ) that credits the algorithm per unit capital spent using the highest-fidelity mean of the played arm (qt = μ(M)It), and penalizes unused capital. They propose MF-UCB: maintain per-arm, per-fidelity UCBs B(m),k,t(s) = X̄(m)k,s + ψ−1(ρ log t / s) + ζ(m), take the minimum across fidelities as the arm’s index, pick the best arm, then select the lowest fidelity whose uncertainty is above a threshold γ(m) = ψ−1( (λ(m)/λ(m+1)) ψ(ζ(m)) ); otherwise advance to higher fidelities. Intuition: use cheap, biased fidelities to eliminate clearly suboptimal arms; reserve expensive plays for near-optimal arms. Main results: (i) an upper bound on regret (Theorem 2) showing a decomposition by fidelity tiers that (roughly) recovers UCB-style log factors but with cost savings when many arms can be eliminated at low fidelities; (ii) a lower bound (Theorem 4) for Bernoulli rewards under an assumption that suboptimal arms are played sub-polynomially often, showing near-optimality except on a subset K(m)7 where MF-UCB may switch “too late.” Experiments on synthetic data suggest improved regret slopes vs. a baseline UCB that uses only the highest fidelity.

Relation to prior work
- Classical stochastic MAB and UCB: correctly connected to [Auer02, Bubeck & Cesa-Bianchi], but some claims are loose (see below).
- Budgeted/bandits-with-resource constraints: the paper cites budgeted bandits in online advertising, but omits closely related “Bandits with Knapsacks” and resource-constrained MAB literature, which formalize capital/budget-aware regret and are highly relevant to the Λ-budget setup and cost-weighted regret.
- Multi-fidelity optimization/GP bandits: the paper positions itself relative to multi-fidelity optimization and its own later GP bandit work.
- Weak/strong labeler active learning: related in spirit but different objective.

Strengths
- Problem formulation is interesting and practically motivated: biased, cheaper approximations occur frequently (simulators, short-horizon proxies).
- Algorithmic idea is natural and well-motivated: nested UCBs with fidelity-aware switching thresholds tied to cost and bias.
- Theoretical contributions: nontrivial regret upper bound that decomposes arms by “elimination fidelity,” and a lower bound identifying where MF-UCB can be tight vs. loose; explicit discussion of the K(m)7 gap is honest.
- The regret definition based on capital rather than time horizon is sensible for cost-asymmetric settings and avoids per-play oracle ambiguities.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Quality/technical soundness
- Heavy reliance on appendices: Key lemmas (5, 6) and proof details for the claimed N . nΛ property are not in the main text. Given the nonstandard regret model and switching rule, more main-body intuition or proof sketches would help assess correctness.
- Assumption 1 (∑i≤m 1/ψ(ζ(i)) ≤ 1/ψ(ζ(m+1))) is strong and central to the claimed advantage. The paper says it is “not necessary,” yet much of the clean comparison to UCB appears to hinge on it. Please clarify precisely where it is used and provide bounds without it (beyond “M constant”).
- Known ζ(m): The algorithm and guarantees require known bias radii. This is a significant practical limitation; there is no discussion of robustness to misspecification (over-/under-estimating ζ), or how to estimate ζ online.
- Regret model: While the capital-based pseudo-regret is coherent, the paper’s oracle interpretation is confusing. You state the oracle “plays an optimal arm at any fidelity all the time,” but the benchmark Λμ* effectively assumes optimal per-unit-capital yield. Please tighten the exposition and contrast with alternatives (e.g., fidelity-dependent rewards, as briefly mentioned in the conclusion).
- Baseline and empirical support: Only a single “UCB at highest fidelity” baseline is reported, with no details in the main text (figures not shown here). Stronger baselines (e.g., naïve multi-fidelity elimination, Thompson-based variants, or cost-aware knapsack UCB) and ablations (sensitivity to λ, ζ, ψ, ρ; mis-specified ζ) are needed. Current evidence is light for NeurIPS standards.
- Initialization and complexity: The algorithm does not specify initialization/tie-breaking when s=0; the O(MK) per-step overhead is not discussed; practical tuning of ρ and γ(m) thresholds is unclear.
- Lower bound: Restricted to Bernoulli, μ* ∈ (1/2,1), ζ(1) < 1/2, and under Assumption 3; generality is limited. While such assumptions are common, the paper should better justify Assumption 3 or derive a version without it.

Clarity/presentation
- Numerous formatting/typographical issues:
  - Stray line “th Conference on NIPS 2016…” in the middle of Section 1.
  - References are incomplete or malformed (missing years/venues/pages; broken titles; misspellings, e.g., “Schenider”).
  - Use of “.,.&” in prose to denote inequalities “ignoring constants” is non-standard and distracting.
  - Figure references and captions appear mangled (e.g., “ζ(m)+2γ(m) are indicated…”, but the figure is absent; color references without color-safe alternatives).
  - Notational glitches: the UCB comparison line around Theorem 2 reads unclearly; some sums appear duplicated or mis-typed.
- Some claims are inaccurate or oversimplified: “UCB achieves O(K log n) regret and is minimax optimal” is not correct in general; minimax regret is Θ(√(Kn)) and UCB1 is not minimax-optimal (MOSS/KL-UCB achieve closer rates). Please correct and cite appropriately.

Originality
- The multi-fidelity-with-bias model in a pure stochastic MAB setting is novel and well-distinguished from budgeted/cost-ratio bandits and from GP-based multi-fidelity BO. However, the paper should more thoroughly position itself relative to “bandits with knapsacks” and other budget-aware bandit models that also optimize under capital constraints.

Significance
- The setting is compelling, and the upper/lower bounds suggest a meaningful algorithmic insight (cheap fidelities can provably reduce cost on clearly suboptimal arms). If polished (both theory and experiments) and better positioned, this could influence follow-up work on structured bandits with biased proxies. As is, the impact is limited by presentation issues, restrictive assumptions, and sparse empirical validation.

Questions for authors
1. How sensitive is MF-UCB to misspecified ζ(m)? Can you provide robustness bounds or heuristics to estimate ζ online?
2. Can you compare against a knapsack-aware bandit baseline and a simple multi-fidelity elimination heuristic?
3. Can Assumption 1 be relaxed with controlled degradation?
4. Provide explicit, non-asymptotic constants in Theorem 2 for a sub-Gaussian ψ(x)=Θ(x^2) case.
5. Clarify the oracle/benchmark for the proposed regret and contrast with alternatives discussed in Section 7.

Minor issues/typos (non-exhaustive)
- Many references missing years/venues; inconsistent formatting; broken titles (e.g., Thompson).
- Spelling: “Schenider” → “Schneider.”
- Notation: define ψ−1 domain carefully when s=0; specify tie-breaking and initialization.
- Clean up Figure references/captions; avoid color-only distinctions.

Recommendation: Weak reject
The core idea is interesting and the theory has merit, but the paper in its current form has too many presentation issues, incomplete related work positioning, restrictive assumptions (known ζ, Assumption 1), and limited experiments. With substantial revisions—tightened exposition, corrected claims/citations, stronger empirical section, and robustness analysis—I would be inclined to reconsider.

Confidence: High
