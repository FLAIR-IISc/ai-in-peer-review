- Paper introduces a multi-fidelity extension to multi-armed bandits, formalizes cost-weighted regret, and proposes MF-UCB to allocate evaluations across fidelities.  
- MF-UCB leverages cheap, noisy low-fidelity evaluations to prune arms and focus high-fidelity budget on promising arms.  
- Situates work relative to UCB, multi-fidelity optimization, budgeted bandits, and multi-fidelity RL; cites relevant GP multi-fidelity bandit work.  
- Novel and well-motivated formulation that explicitly accounts for fidelity costs in regret.  
- MF-UCB is a conceptually sound algorithm with an intuitive adaptive fidelity-allocation strategy.  
- Strong theoretical analysis: regret bounds showing clear advantage over standard UCB under realistic conditions and near-optimality in identified regimes.  
- Empirical results support theory; visualization of fidelity allocation is informative.  
- Paper is clearly written and well organized.  
- Strong assumption that low-fidelity means are within known ζ(m) of high-fidelity means; robustness to misleading low-fidelity estimates is not explored.  
- Assumption 1 on ζ(m) decay feels arbitrary and would benefit from justification or sensitivity analysis.  
- Gap remains between the algorithm’s regret bound and the lower bound, particularly for arms with small gaps ∆(m)k.  
- Empirical evaluation is limited in scope; needs more parameter settings, problem sizes, reward types, and stronger baselines than standard UCB.  
- Choice of reward definition (qt = µ(M)k) is justified but alternative choices are insufficiently discussed.  
- Proof sketches are high-level; more detailed proofs or appendix references would improve reproducibility.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel problem, solid theory, effective algorithm, and potential application impact.  
- Arguments against acceptance: strong fidelity assumptions, regret-vs-lower-bound gap, and limited empirical validation.  
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions to address assumptions, expand experiments, and deepen discussion of reward choices.  
- Reviewer confidence: 8/10.
