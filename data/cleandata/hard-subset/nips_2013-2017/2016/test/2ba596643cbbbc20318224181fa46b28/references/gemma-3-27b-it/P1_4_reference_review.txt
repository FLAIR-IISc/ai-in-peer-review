## NIPS 2016 Review: The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel framework for the multi-armed bandit (MAB) problem where evaluating each arm comes with varying fidelities (and associated costs). The authors motivate this setting with examples from online advertising and algorithm selection, where cheaper approximations to true reward can be obtained. They propose a Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB) algorithm that leverages lower-fidelity evaluations to efficiently eliminate suboptimal arms and focuses higher-fidelity evaluations on promising candidates.  The core contribution lies in a theoretical analysis demonstrating that MF-UCB achieves a regret bound superior to naive UCB when lower fidelities are significantly cheaper and provide reasonably accurate approximations. 

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon the well-established UCB framework for MABs [Auer, 2002]. It distinguishes itself from existing work by explicitly addressing the multi-fidelity aspect, which hasn't been formally analyzed in the bandit setting. The authors acknowledge related work in multi-fidelity optimization [Huang et al., 2006], reinforcement learning with multi-fidelity simulators [Cutler et al., 2016], and budgeted bandits [Tran-Thanh et al., 2015], highlighting how their problem differs—specifically, the focus on exploration-exploitation in the presence of biased approximations, rather than pure optimization or cost-reward tradeoffs.  The paper builds upon prior work from the authors, Kandasamy et al. [2016], extending ideas to this setting.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The multi-fidelity bandit framework is a genuinely new perspective on the classic MAB problem, opening up possibilities for more efficient exploration in scenarios with varying evaluation costs.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The authors provide a formal analysis of MF-UCB, deriving a regret bound that demonstrates its improvement over standard UCB under realistic conditions (cheaper lower fidelities, bounded bias).  The analysis is comprehensive, including a lower bound to showcase the near-optimality of the approach.
* **Clear Intuition:** The core idea of using lower fidelities for initial screening and reserving higher fidelities for promising arms is intuitively appealing and well-justified by the analysis.
* **Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem definition to algorithm description and theoretical analysis.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The simulations, while not extensive, provide initial evidence supporting the theoretical findings and demonstrating the practical benefits of MF-UCB.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions:** The theoretical analysis relies on assumptions about the fidelity bias (ζ(m)) and the concentration properties of the reward distributions (ψ). While reasonable, these assumptions might not hold in all practical scenarios.  The impact of violating these assumptions is not fully discussed.
* **Regularity Condition (Assumption 1):** While the authors acknowledge that Assumption 1 isn't *necessary* for the analysis, it appears crucial for the improved performance over UCB to be realized. This condition, relating the decay of bias to the cost, feels somewhat restrictive and could limit the applicability of the algorithm.
* **Lower Bound Gap:** The authors identify a gap between their upper bound for MF-UCB and the derived lower bound, particularly for arms that are initially identified as potentially promising based on low-fidelity evaluations.  Addressing this gap would strengthen the theoretical contribution.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The simulations are somewhat basic.  More extensive experiments, including comparisons on real-world datasets or more diverse synthetic scenarios, would provide stronger evidence of the algorithm’s effectiveness.
* **Discussion of Other Regret Definitions:** The paper focuses on a specific definition of regret.  While justified, briefly discussing alternative choices and their implications would enhance the discussion.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper introduces a novel and well-motivated problem setting with potential applications in various domains.
* The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides a clear understanding of the algorithm's performance.
* The algorithm is relatively simple to implement and has the potential to significantly improve efficiency in multi-fidelity evaluation scenarios.
* The paper is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The assumptions underlying the theoretical analysis might be restrictive in some applications.
* The gap between the upper and lower bounds leaves room for improvement in the theoretical understanding.
* The empirical evaluation is somewhat limited and could be strengthened with more comprehensive experiments.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of multi-armed bandits. While the assumptions and the gap in the lower bound are weaknesses, the novelty of the problem setting, the rigor of the analysis, and the clear presentation outweigh these concerns.  I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance to NIPS, pending minor revisions to address the reviewer's comments.

**Recommendation:** Accept with minor revisions.

**Specific Suggestions for Revision:**

* **Clarify the impact of violating the assumptions.** Discuss how the performance of MF-UCB might degrade when the bias assumptions are not met or when the reward distributions don't satisfy the concentration inequalities.
* **Further explore the implications of Assumption 1.** Provide more intuition about why this condition is important and discuss potential strategies for dealing with scenarios where it doesn't hold.
* **Address the lower bound gap.**  Investigate the reasons for the discrepancy between the upper and lower bounds and consider whether a tighter lower bound can be derived.
* **Expand the empirical evaluation.**  Include more diverse synthetic scenarios and consider evaluating the algorithm on real-world datasets.
