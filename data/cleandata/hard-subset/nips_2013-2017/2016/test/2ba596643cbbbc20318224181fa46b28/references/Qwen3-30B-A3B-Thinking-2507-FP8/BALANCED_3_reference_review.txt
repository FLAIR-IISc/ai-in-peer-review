

# Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

This paper introduces a novel extension of the classical multi-armed bandit problem where each arm can be evaluated at multiple fidelities with varying costs and accuracy levels. The authors propose MF-UCB, an algorithm that leverages cheaper low-fidelity evaluations to eliminate suboptimal arms while reserving expensive high-fidelity evaluations for promising arms. The paper provides a formal regret analysis showing that MF-UCB can achieve significantly better regret than standard UCB when the number of arms with high expected reward is small and the cost difference between fidelities is substantial.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with a complete theoretical framework. The regret bounds for MF-UCB and the matching lower bound are well-developed and provide clear insights into when the multi-fidelity approach is beneficial. The proofs appear rigorous, with detailed arguments in the appendix. The empirical results on synthetic problems support the theoretical claims. However, a significant limitation is the assumption that the bias parameters ζ(m) are known. In practice, these parameters would need to be estimated, which could affect the algorithm's performance. The paper would benefit from discussing how to estimate these parameters in a practical setting.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and well-organized. The problem statement, algorithm description, and theoretical analysis flow logically. The key concepts are clearly explained, and the figures effectively illustrate the partitioning of arms and the algorithm's behavior. The notation is consistent throughout, and the appendix provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.

**Originality:** The multi-fidelity bandit problem is a meaningful extension of the classical bandit problem with clear motivation from practical applications like online advertising, algorithm selection, and experimental design. The paper distinguishes itself well from related work on budgeted bandits, multi-fidelity optimization, and active learning. The combination of the problem formulation and the MF-UCB algorithm represents a significant advancement in bandit theory.

**Significance:** The problem has clear practical relevance, as many real-world applications involve cheaper approximations to expensive experiments. The results advance the state of the art by providing a formal framework for multi-fidelity settings and identifying precisely when the multi-fidelity approach provides benefits. The regret bounds clarify the conditions under which practitioners should adopt this approach.

**Strengths:** The paper's most compelling strengths are its clear problem motivation, natural algorithmic extension of UCB, clean theoretical analysis with matching lower bound, and empirical validation. The key insight that MF-UCB can achieve better regret by using low-fidelity evaluations to eliminate arms is well-explained and supported by both theory and experiments.

**Weaknesses:** The assumption of known ζ(m) parameters is a significant practical limitation that the paper does not adequately address. The empirical section is limited to synthetic problems; results on real-world data would strengthen the practical relevance. The paper acknowledges a gap between the upper and lower bounds for certain arms (K(m)7), but doesn't propose how to close this gap, which represents an opportunity for future work.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- Novel problem extension with clear practical motivation
- Clean theoretical analysis with matching lower bound
- Well-designed algorithm that naturally extends UCB
- Strong empirical validation on synthetic problems
- Clear characterization of when multi-fidelity approach is beneficial

*Con:*
- Assumption of known bias parameters ζ(m) is unrealistic for practical applications
- Limited empirical validation to synthetic problems only
- Gap between upper and lower bounds for certain arms not resolved
- Limited discussion of parameter selection in practice

**Recommendation:** This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to bandit theory with clear practical relevance. The main limitation is the assumption of known ζ(m) parameters, which is a significant barrier to practical adoption. However, this is a common issue in theoretical bandit work, and the paper provides a solid foundation for future work addressing this limitation. The paper is well-suited for NeurIPS as it presents a novel theoretical contribution with clear practical implications.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
1. Discuss how to estimate the bias parameters ζ(m) in practice, perhaps through initial exploration at higher fidelity.
2. Include experiments on real-world datasets to demonstrate practical utility.
3. Explore how the algorithm might be modified to handle the gap between the upper and lower bounds for arms in K(m)7.
4. Consider discussing the impact of large numbers of fidelities (e.g., M=100) on the algorithm's performance.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that advances the state of the art in bandit theory. With minor revisions addressing the practical limitations, it would be an excellent contribution to NeurIPS.
