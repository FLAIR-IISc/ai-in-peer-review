

# Peer Review: "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

## Summary

This paper introduces a novel framework for multi-armed bandit problems where each arm has multiple "fidelities" - cheaper approximations to the true arm reward that can be used to guide more expensive high-fidelity evaluations. The authors formalize this setting, propose the MF-UCB algorithm, and provide theoretical regret bounds showing that their approach can achieve significantly better regret than standard UCB when lower fidelities are sufficiently cheaper and when only a small subset of arms are high-performing. The paper builds on the UCB framework but extends it to handle the multi-fidelity structure, with applications in online advertising, algorithm selection, and other domains where approximate evaluations are available. The work is well-motivated and relates appropriately to related work in bandit theory and multi-fidelity optimization.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors carefully define their problem setting, make reasonable assumptions (including the regularity condition in Assumption 1), and provide a comprehensive theoretical analysis with both upper and lower bounds. The regret bound (Theorem 2) is carefully derived, and the lower bound (Theorem 4) demonstrates that MF-UCB is near-optimal for most arms. The empirical results on synthetic data (Section 6) corroborate the theoretical claims, showing MF-UCB outperforms standard UCB in terms of regret. The authors acknowledge the limitation in the lower bound for certain arms (those in K(m)7), showing appropriate honesty in their evaluation.

**Originality:** This work significantly extends the standard bandit framework by introducing the multi-fidelity perspective. While there has been some work on multi-fidelity optimization (e.g., [7, 10]), this paper is the first to formalize the regret notion and provide theoretical guarantees for the bandit setting. The idea of using cheaper approximations for exploration and reserving expensive evaluations for promising arms is novel and well-motivated. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work, particularly from budgeted bandits [13, 15] and multi-fidelity optimization in other domains.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The introduction effectively motivates the problem with concrete examples (online advertising, algorithm selection). The formalism is well-explained with appropriate notation, and the key concepts (like the partitions K(m)) are clearly illustrated in Figure 1. The technical content is presented at an appropriate level for the NIPS audience, and the proofs are detailed enough to be convincing (with full proofs in the appendix).

**Significance:** The problem addressed is highly relevant to practical applications where evaluations are costly. The theoretical results provide clear guidelines for when multi-fidelity bandits will be beneficial (when λ(1) < λ(2) and |Kg| < K), and the empirical results demonstrate this advantage. The framework could be applied to many domains beyond those mentioned (e.g., hyperparameter tuning, robotics), making it likely to be influential in both theory and practice.

## Weaknesses

**Minor clarity issues:** The paper would benefit from slightly more concise writing in some sections. For example, the motivation in Section 1 could be tightened to immediately highlight the key novelty. The connection between the theoretical results and practical implementation details (e.g., how to set the parameter ρ) is somewhat vague.

**Limited empirical scope:** While the synthetic experiments are convincing, the paper lacks real-world applications to demonstrate the framework's practical utility. The authors mention applications in online advertising and algorithm selection, but don't provide empirical evidence on real data.

**Theoretical gap:** The lower bound (Theorem 4) shows MF-UCB is near-optimal for arms in K(m)3 but may be loose for arms in K(m)7. The authors provide a good explanation for this gap but don't propose how to address it. This is a limitation that should be acknowledged as a direction for future work.

## Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to bandit theory. The problem is well-motivated, the theoretical analysis is rigorous and comprehensive, and the empirical results support the claims. The paper advances the state-of-the-art in bandit algorithms by addressing a natural extension of the standard model that has important practical implications.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Novel framework extending bandit theory to multi-fidelity settings
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with upper and lower bounds
- Clear demonstration of theoretical advantages over standard UCB
- Well-motivated with concrete applications
- Strong empirical validation on synthetic data

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Limited to synthetic experiments (though this is common in theoretical bandit papers)
- Minor issues with presentation clarity in some sections
- Theoretical gap for certain arms (but this is acknowledged and is a natural limitation)

## Final Recommendation

I strongly recommend acceptance. This paper makes a clear, significant contribution to the bandit literature with a novel framework that has both theoretical and practical relevance. The quality of the theoretical analysis is high, the writing is clear, and the work is well-positioned to influence future research in bandit algorithms and multi-fidelity optimization. The minor weaknesses noted do not detract from the paper's overall strength and contribution.

The paper is clearly within the scope of NIPS as it addresses a fundamental problem in online decision making with clear theoretical and practical significance. The work represents a meaningful advancement beyond previous NIPS bandit papers, particularly those on UCB methods [2, 3] and budgeted bandits [13, 15].

I believe this paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings, providing both theoretical insights and practical guidance for researchers and practitioners working with expensive evaluation problems.
