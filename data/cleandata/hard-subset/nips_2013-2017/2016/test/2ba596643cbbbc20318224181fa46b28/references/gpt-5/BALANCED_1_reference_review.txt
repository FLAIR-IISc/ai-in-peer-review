Summary and relation to prior work
The paper introduces a multi-fidelity variant of the stochastic K-armed bandit. Each arm admits M fidelities: lower fidelities are cheaper but yield biased approximations to the highest-fidelity reward, with known fidelity-wise bias bounds |µ(M)k − µ(m)k| ≤ ζ(m), and costs λ(1) < … < λ(M). The authors define a cost-weighted pseudo-regret when a policy expends total capital Λ: R(Λ) = Λµ* − ∑t λ(mt)µ(M)It, i.e., the benchmark is an oracle that always plays an optimal arm at any fidelity while paying the corresponding costs. They propose MF-UCB, which maintains per-fidelity UCBs for each arm and chooses the arm with the tightest (minimum) upper bound; it then selects the lowest fidelity whose current confidence radius exceeds a threshold γ(m) that balances fidelity cost and bias. Theoretical analysis shows an O(log(Λ/λ(M))) regret with constants that decompose across fidelities, and a lower bound (for Bernoulli rewards and a mild class of “reasonable” strategies) that MF-UCB matches up to constants on a broad subset of arms. Empirically, on synthetic problems MF-UCB improves over vanilla UCB that only plays at the highest fidelity.

This work builds on classical UCB and Lai–Robbins theory, and relates to budgeted bandits (e.g., rewards and costs per pull) and to multi-fidelity optimization/BO and RL simulators. Unlike budgeted bandits that target reward-per-cost ratios, this paper exploits a hierarchical approximation structure with known uniform bias bounds. It is also distinct from weak/strong labeler active learning in that it addresses online reward maximization with exploration–exploitation.

Strengths
- Novel problem formulation: A clear, principled multi-fidelity bandit model with explicit cost and bias trade-offs; the regret notion is carefully tailored to “capital-limited” settings.
- Algorithmic design: MF-UCB is a natural, simple, and implementable extension of UCB with an elegant fidelity-switching rule via γ(m) = ψ^{-1}(λ(m)/λ(m+1) ψ(ζ(m))).
- Theory: Nontrivial analysis. The upper bound shows that low fidelities filter bad arms, concentrating expensive evaluation on promising ones. The lower bound elucidates when this is information-theoretically necessary; the paper is transparent about a gap on a particular subset of arms and explains why closing it is hard.
- Insight: The partitioning K(1), …, K(M) clarifies how costs and approximations shape exploration; the results recover standard UCB behavior on “hard” arms while gaining on many others.

Weaknesses and detailed evaluation
Quality
- Assumptions: The known uniform fidelity bias bounds ζ(m) and known ψ (concentration) are strong. In many applications, ζ(m) is unknown, arm-dependent, or only approximately bounded. The analysis and the switching thresholds crucially depend on ζ(m) and λ(m); robustness to misspecification is not explored.
- Lower bound scope: The lower bound is presented for Bernoulli rewards and strategies satisfying Assumption 3 (sub-polynomial pulls of suboptimal arms). While reasonable, it narrows generality. The constant c is not characterized, and the gap on K(m)7 arms is unresolved.
- Empirics: Experiments are limited to synthetic tasks and compare only to vanilla UCB at the highest fidelity. Stronger baselines are missing, e.g., (i) treating each (arm,fidelity) pair as an arm with cost-aware UCB, (ii) heuristics that run low-fidelity elimination followed by high-fidelity UCB, (iii) budgeted bandit methods tuned to this regret notion, or Thompson Sampling variants. There is no sensitivity analysis to ζ mis-specification or to violations of Assumption 1.
- Regret definition: While well-motivated, it is nonstandard. The benchmark assumes the oracle receives µ* per unit capital regardless of fidelity; a discussion of units and interpretation (e.g., when µ(M) is “per unit cost/time” and λ multiplies “duration”) could be clearer. The residual term r̃ is small but the dependence on Λ through N and nΛ is subtle.

Clarity
- Generally well-written with clear intuition and proof sketches. However, notation is heavy; some statements reference figures or tables that are not fully visible in the main text. A more intuitive explanation of γ(m) and the stopping rule would help readers less familiar with ψ-based UCBs.
- The choice ρ > 4 and its role could be motivated more; can one attain ρ > 2 as hinted, and what are the practical implications?

Originality
- The multi-fidelity MAB model with biased fidelities and cost-weighted regret is, to my knowledge, novel in the bandit literature. The MF-UCB construction and analysis are not mere reductions to known settings.

Significance
- The framework is potentially impactful in domains with simulators or cheap proxies (ads, A/B testing with short windows, ML algorithm selection with subsampling). Theoretical results show meaningful gains when low fidelities can filter many arms cheaply. However, practical significance depends on availability/estimation of ζ(m) and on robustness; stronger empirical validation would raise confidence.

Suggestions for improvement
- Robustness: Develop MF-UCB variants that learn or adaptively calibrate ζ(m), e.g., via cross-fidelity discrepancy estimates with confidence bounds; analyze regret under misspecified ζ.
- Baselines: Compare against cost-aware multi-armed reductions (treating each (k,m) as an arm with adjusted indices), successive halving/Successive Rejects with low-fidelity filters, and Thompson Sampling counterparts. Include scenarios where ζ is large (little benefit) and where ζ is small (big benefit).
- Regret discussion: Clarify the oracle’s action space and units; contrast with alternative regret choices (e.g., fidelity-dependent rewards) and, if possible, provide results for at least one alternative to illustrate generality.
- Lower bound: Extend beyond Bernoulli to sub-Gaussian families, or clarify what changes are needed. Explore partial closing of the K(m)7 gap, perhaps with an oracle-tuned variant to shed light on limits.
- Practical guidance: Sensitivity to λ ratios, choice of ρ, and computational overhead; ablate the contribution of each fidelity.

Arguments for acceptance
- Introduces a timely, well-motivated bandit model with multi-fidelity structure.
- Clean, principled algorithm with insightful analysis and near-optimality guarantees.
- Clear separation of when and why multi-fidelity saves capital compared to UCB.

Arguments against acceptance
- Empirical evaluation is thin and lacks strong baselines and robustness studies.
- Strong assumptions (known ζ(m), Assumption 1) limit practical applicability.
- Lower bound scope and the unresolved gap on a subset of arms.

Recommendation: Weak accept
I find the theoretical contribution and problem formulation sufficiently novel and insightful for NeurIPS. Strengthening the empirical section and discussing robustness would make the paper substantially stronger.
