Summary and context
This paper introduces a stochastic K-armed bandit model with multiple fidelities per arm, where each lower fidelity provides a biased, cheaper approximation to the highest-fidelity reward. The authors formalize a cost-weighted pseudo-regret over a capital budget Λ that values each play according to the highest-fidelity mean µ(M) of the selected arm, irrespective of the fidelity used, while accounting for the cost λ(m) of the chosen fidelity. They assume known uniform bias bounds |µ(M)k − µ(m)k| ≤ ζ(m) with ζ(m) decreasing to zero, and concentration of empirical means via a common function ψ.

They propose MF-UCB, which maintains per-fidelity UCBs for µ(M)k using empirical means plus an exploration term and the bias bound ζ(m), and selects an arm via the minimum of these UCBs across fidelities. A fidelity-selection rule escalates from low to high fidelity when the current confidence radius at fidelity m drops below a threshold γ(m) that trades off cost and bias: γ(m) = ψ−1( (λ(m)/λ(m+1)) ψ(ζ(m)) ). Intuitively, cheap fidelities are used to eliminate clearly suboptimal arms; expensive fidelities are reserved for hard-to-distinguish arms near-optimal at lower fidelities.

Theoretical results include:
- An upper bound on expected regret E[R(Λ)] that, up to log factors, scales like ∑k∉K* Δ(M)k λ(Jk)/ψ(Δ(Jk)k), where Jk is the highest fidelity at which arm k can be eliminated, contrasting with the UCB baseline that would pay λ(M) uniformly.
- A lower bound (for Bernoulli rewards) showing that any “reasonable” strategy must incur, up to constants, ∑k Δ(M)k min` λ(`)/Δ(`)2. MF-UCB is shown to be near-optimal for a large subclass of arms (those not “nearly optimal” at any lower fidelity), with a known gap for arms that are just slightly suboptimal at some lower fidelity.

Relation to prior work
The work sits at the intersection of classical UCB bandits and multi-fidelity optimization. It is distinct from budgeted/knapsack bandits (e.g., bandits with knapsacks, budgeted MAB), where costs and rewards are realized jointly and the objective is to maximize cumulative reward under resource constraints; here, lower-fidelity observations are biased proxies for the high-fidelity reward, and the objective values always refer to µ(M). It also differs from settings with multiple labelers/weak supervision, and from GP-based multi-fidelity BO (including the authors’ later work), as it gives finite-time, frequentist guarantees in a nonparametric, per-arm setting. It would be useful to cite and contrast explicitly with BwK/knapsack bandits (e.g., Badanidiyuru et al., NIPS 2013), structured/correlated bandits with side observations, and cascade/linked bandits in ad-click models.

Quality
- Soundness: The algorithmic design is principled, and the analysis is careful. The per-fidelity UCB construction with ζ(m) offsets gives valid high-probability bounds on µ(M). The fidelity-switching rule derived from a cost–bias tradeoff is plausible and justified by the subsequent regret decomposition.
- Upper bound: The partition of arms K(m) and the resulting bounds match the intuition: most regret mass concentrates at the highest fidelity only for arms that remain near-optimal at lower fidelities; cheaper fidelities eliminate clearly suboptimal arms. The dependence on ψ allows general sub-exponential tails; specialization to sub-Gaussian is straightforward.
- Lower bound: The change-of-measure lower bound is standard and well-adapted to this setting. Assumption 3 excludes pathological strategies and seems benign. The paper is transparent about the gap for the K(m)7 subset and explains why closing it is nontrivial.
- Assumptions: The model assumes known, arm-independent bias bounds ζ(m) and a common ψ with Assumption 1 (roughly, sufficiently fast decay of ζ). These are strong but clearly stated; the paper discusses when Assumption 1 is needed for the most attractive comparisons.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized, with a clear algorithm description and intuitive explanations. Some suggestions to improve clarity:
- The regret definition is unconventional; motivate it more explicitly and contrast with alternative objectives (e.g., actual realized reward at played fidelity vs. opportunity-cost-style pseudo-reward). Section 7 hints at variants; a short paragraph earlier would help.
- The sets J(·) and K(·) are intricate; a small example or table in the main text (beyond Fig. 1) would aid readability.
- Explicitly state the union bound/high-probability event under which Bk,t upper-bounds µ(M)k, and the role of ν in (2).
- Discuss sensitivity to mis-specification of ζ(m). Is the algorithm robust if only upper bounds on ζ(m) are known, or if ζ varies by arm?

Originality
The multi-fidelity bandit formulation with biased, costed proxies and a cost-weighted regret is, to my knowledge, novel in the stochastic MAB literature. The MF-UCB design—min over per-fidelity UCBs for the target mean, coupled with a derived switching threshold—is an elegant adaptation of optimism to this hierarchical setting. The upper/lower bounds and the partition-based analysis appear new and insightful.

Significance
The paper provides a clean framework and near-optimal guarantees for an important and practical setting (e.g., ad testing, algorithm selection, simulation vs. real-world trials). The theoretical insights are likely to influence follow-up work (as evidenced by later GP-based multi-fidelity bandits). Empirically, results are promising but limited; broader experiments (including robustness to ζ mis-specification and varying λ, M) would strengthen practical impact.

Pros
- Clear, principled formulation of multi-fidelity bandits with costs and bias bounds.
- Simple, implementable algorithm with interpretable fidelity-switching.
- Strong theoretical guarantees: regret improvements over UCB, near-matching lower bound, transparent characterization of when gains occur.
- Good discussion of assumptions and limitations.

Cons
- Regret notion may not align with applications where low-fidelity plays do not yield any immediate reward; more discussion and empirical justification would help.
- Strong assumptions: known, arm-independent ζ(m); Assumption 1 on decay; common ψ across fidelities.
- Lower bound gap for K(m)7 arms remains open.
- Experiments are synthetic and brief; no sensitivity analysis or real-data validation.
- Missing discussion of closely related resource-constrained bandit models (BwK/knapsack), and structured/side-observation bandits.

Questions/suggestions for authors
- How sensitive is MF-UCB to over/underestimation of ζ(m)? Can you provide bounds that degrade gracefully with ζ mis-specification, or an adaptive variant that estimates effective bias?
- Could the fidelity-selection rule incorporate rough estimates of Δ(m)k without incurring the full higher-fidelity sampling cost (e.g., via occasional probes)?
- Please discuss relations and differences to bandits with knapsacks and side-observation/correlated bandits.
- Include additional experiments: varying λ ratios and ζ schedules; robustness; larger K; and, if possible, a semi-synthetic or real application.
- Clarify whether ψ and ν can vary across m,k and how the analysis would change.

Overall recommendation
This is a strong theoretical paper with a novel formulation and solid analysis. Despite some strong assumptions and limited experiments, it advances the state of the art and is well-suited for NeurIPS. I recommend acceptance after addressing clarity and related-work gaps, and, if space allows, expanding empirical validation.
