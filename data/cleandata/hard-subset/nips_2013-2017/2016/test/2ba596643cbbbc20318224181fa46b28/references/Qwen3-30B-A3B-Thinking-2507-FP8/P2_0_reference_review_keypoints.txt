- Introduces multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit and MF-UCB algorithm that uses cheap lower-fidelity evaluations to eliminate arms and reserves expensive high-fidelity evaluations for promising arms.  
- Formalizes a new regret framework for the multi-fidelity setting and provides rigorous theoretical guarantees.  
- Provides thorough theoretical analysis: an upper bound (Theorem 2) and a matching lower bound (Theorem 4), with careful justification of design choices (γ(m)).  
- Explains the performance gap for arms in K(m)7 and why MF-UCB does not meet the lower bound for those arms.  
- Shows MF-UCB can significantly outperform standard UCB when few arms are promising and lower fidelities are cheaper.  
- Synthetic experiments in Section 6 corroborate theoretical claims and show consistently better performance than UCB.  
- Paper is technically sound, exceptionally well-written, and well-organized with clear notation and intuitive explanations; Figure 1 aids understanding.  
- Work is novel within bandit literature and clearly distinguished from related multi-fidelity, budgeted bandits, and weak-label active learning.  
- The problem and results are significant for real applications where high-fidelity evaluations are expensive (e.g., advertising, robotics, clinical trials).  
- Weakness: gap between MF-UCB performance and the lower bound for arms in K(m)7 without a proposed fix.  
- Weakness: experiments are limited to synthetic problems and lack a real-world application demonstration.  
- Weakness: assumes known ζ(m) fidelity-bias parameters and does not analyze algorithm performance with estimated parameters.  
- Overall assessment: high-quality, significant contribution to bandit theory; recommended for acceptance at NIPS.  
- Pro/Con summary — Pros: novel formulation, strong theoretical guarantees, clear writing, supportive experiments; Cons: synthetic-only experiments, known ζ(m) assumption, performance gap for certain arms.
