

# Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

## Summary
This paper introduces a novel multi-fidelity extension of the classical K-armed bandit problem, where each arm has multiple fidelities (approximations) with different costs and accuracy levels. The authors formalize a cost-weighted regret measure and propose the MF-UCB algorithm that leverages cheaper lower-fidelity approximations to eliminate suboptimal arms and reserves expensive high-fidelity evaluations for promising arms. The paper establishes theoretical regret bounds for MF-UCB and demonstrates that it achieves better regret than standard UCB algorithms when lower fidelities are sufficiently cheaper and informative. The authors also provide a lower bound showing that MF-UCB is near-optimal under certain conditions. This work extends the UCB framework to a more realistic setting where multiple fidelity levels of information are available at different costs, with applications in online advertising, algorithm selection, and other domains where approximate evaluations are cheaper than exact ones.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound theoretical contribution. The formalism for multi-fidelity bandits is well-motivated and carefully constructed, with appropriate assumptions (including the regularity condition in Assumption 1). The regret analysis is thorough, with clear theorems establishing both upper and lower bounds. The proof sketches provide sufficient detail to understand the key technical arguments, and the empirical results on synthetic problems support the theoretical claims. The paper acknowledges limitations, particularly the gap between MF-UCB and the lower bound for certain arms (K(m)7), which demonstrates careful scholarship.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem statement is clear from the outset with relevant examples (online advertising, algorithm selection). The formalism is well-defined with appropriate notation, and the algorithm description is straightforward. The theoretical results are presented logically, with the regret bound comparison to UCB providing valuable intuition. The figures (e.g., Fig. 1) effectively illustrate the partitioning of arms across fidelities. The paper provides sufficient technical details for an expert reader to understand and potentially reproduce the results.

**Originality:** The paper makes a clear contribution to the bandit literature by formalizing the multi-fidelity bandit problem, which has not been systematically studied in the NIPS literature. While previous work has considered multi-fidelity optimization in other contexts (e.g., [7, 10, 16]), this paper provides the first regret analysis for the bandit setting with multiple fidelities. The authors carefully distinguish their work from related approaches (budgeted bandits, multi-fidelity optimization, active learning), demonstrating a clear understanding of the literature. The problem is novel and addresses a practical gap in bandit theory.

**Significance:** The multi-fidelity setting is highly relevant to real-world applications where cheaper approximations are available (e.g., online advertising with shorter ad displays, algorithm selection with cheaper data subsets). The theoretical and empirical results demonstrate that MF-UCB can significantly outperform standard UCB methods when lower fidelities are sufficiently cheap and informative. This work advances the state of the art in bandit theory by addressing a practically important extension of the problem and provides a foundation for future work in multi-fidelity decision-making.

## Weaknesses

**Limited Discussion of Practical Implementation:** While the theoretical analysis is strong, the paper could benefit from more discussion about how to choose the fidelity parameters (ζ(m), λ(m)) in practice. The paper assumes these are known, but in many applications they might need to be estimated, which could impact performance.

**Narrow Scope of Empirical Evaluation:** The empirical results are limited to synthetic problems. While the synthetic examples are well-chosen to illustrate the theoretical insights, the paper would be stronger with experiments on real-world datasets or applications to demonstrate practical utility.

**Assumption 1 Restriction:** The regularity condition in Assumption 1 is necessary for the most appealing comparison to UCB, but the paper does not sufficiently explore the consequences when this condition is violated. The authors note it's not necessary for algorithm analysis, but the regret comparison becomes less compelling without it.

## Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments

**Pro-acceptance:**
- Novel formalism for multi-fidelity bandits that extends classical bandit theory to a practically important setting
- Strong theoretical contribution with tight regret bounds and near-optimal algorithm
- Clear, well-structured presentation with sufficient technical detail
- Empirical results corroborate theoretical insights
- Addresses a well-motivated problem with clear applications in online advertising, algorithm selection, and other domains

**Con-acceptance:**
- Limited empirical validation beyond synthetic problems
- Assumption 1 restricts the practical applicability of the most compelling regret comparison
- Some aspects of the algorithm design (choice of γ(m)) could benefit from more intuitive explanation

## Overall Assessment
This is a high-quality theoretical contribution that advances bandit theory by formalizing and solving the multi-fidelity bandit problem. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a clear novel contribution to the literature. The theoretical results are significant and the practical motivation is strong. While the empirical evaluation could be expanded, the theoretical contribution stands strongly on its own. The paper meets all four criteria for NIPS publication: it is of high quality, clear, original, and significant. The minor weaknesses do not detract from the paper's substantial contribution to the field.
