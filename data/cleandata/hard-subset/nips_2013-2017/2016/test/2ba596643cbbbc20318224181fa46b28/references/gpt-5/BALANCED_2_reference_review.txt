Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper introduces a multi-fidelity extension of the stochastic K-armed bandit where each arm admits M fidelities: cheaper, biased approximations (lower fidelities) with known bias radii ζ(m), and a most expensive, target fidelity M with mean μ(M)k. Each pull at fidelity m incurs a known cost λ(m), increasing in m. The authors define a cost-weighted pseudo-regret for a capital budget Λ: R(Λ)=Λμ*−∑t λ(mt) μ(M)It, i.e., the comparator plays an optimal arm at any fidelity continuously, and rewards are always measured w.r.t. the highest-fidelity means. They propose MF-UCB, which maintains UCB-style upper bounds B(m)k,t(s)=X̄(m)k,s+ψ^{-1}(ρ log t/s)+ζ(m), and selects arm It maximizing minm B(m)k,t. A fidelity-gating rule chooses the smallest m such that the uncertainty ψ^{-1}(ρ log t/T(m)It,t−1) exceeds a threshold γ(m)=ψ^{-1}(λ(m)/λ(m+1) ψ(ζ(m))), else escalates to higher fidelities. Intuitively, low fidelities eliminate clearly suboptimal arms; high fidelities are reserved for near-optimal arms.

Under concentration assumptions (2) and a regularity condition on the decay of ζ(m) (Assumption 1), the paper proves an upper bound on expected regret scaling like log(nΛ) times a sum over arms of Δ(M)k multiplied by cost-over-information terms. In two-fidelity problems this translates to savings roughly O((λ(1)K+λ(2)|Kg|) log(Λ/λ(2))) vs the λ(2)K log baseline of standard UCB at fidelity M. A lower bound for Bernoulli rewards (with a mild subpolynomial-play assumption) shows near-optimality, up to a gap for certain arms where low-fidelity gaps are small but positive. Experiments on synthetic data support the theory.

This work is related to classical stochastic bandits and UCB (Auer 2002; Agrawal 1995; Lai and Robbins 1985; Bubeck & Cesa-Bianchi 2012), multi-fidelity optimization (Huang et al. 2006; Rajnarayan et al. 2008), and cost/budgeted bandits (e.g., budgeted MAB and closely related “bandits with knapsacks”). It also connects to multi-fidelity RL (Cutler et al. 2014) and weak/strong labeler active learning (Zhang & Chaudhuri 2015), though the objective here is regret with exploration–exploitation trade-offs.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The algorithmic design and proofs are well-motivated. The partitioning K(m) and gating thresholds γ(m) yield interpretable bounds, and the comparison to standard UCB is careful. The lower bound via change-of-measure for Bernoulli arms is standard and informative. The regret notion is coherent with the “cost per unit time” view.
- Assumptions: The need for known bias radii ζ(m), monotone in m, and for Assumption 1 on ζ-decay may be restrictive in practice. The analysis assumes sub-Gaussian-like concentration with known ψ and independence across fidelities; correlation across fidelities and misspecification of ζ(m) are not addressed. Assumption 3 in the lower bound precludes pathological strategies, but seems reasonable for competent algorithms.
- Completeness: Some technical details (e.g., handling of the residual r̃ term, dependence of δ on Λ in the N control) are deferred; the high-level argument is convincing.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clear and well organized, with helpful intuition. A few notational choices (e.g., ∆(m)k and the partition definitions) are dense and could benefit from a small illustrative example. The motivation for the regret definition is good, but lingering alternative choices merit more discussion.

Originality:
The formalization of multi-fidelity, biased approximations within the stochastic bandit framework, with a cost-aware regret and a principled UCB-style algorithm, is novel and interesting. The tight coupling of the gating thresholds to costs and bias radii is elegant. While related literatures exist (budgeted bandits; bandits with knapsacks; multi-fidelity optimization), the specific problem setting and results here are distinct.

Significance:
The contributions advance our understanding of how to exploit cheap biased proxies to reduce exploration costs—a practically important scenario (ads, ML algorithm selection, simulation vs. real trials). The upper/lower bounds and the algorithm should spur follow-up work, including extensions to structured settings (e.g., GP bandits, combinatorial arms). Empirical support is limited but suggestive.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, well-motivated problem formulation of practical relevance.
- Simple, principled algorithm with strong intuition.
- Nontrivial theory: instance-dependent upper bounds and matching lower bounds (up to a gap in a specific regime).
- Insight that most budget is spent at the fidelity where the arm is “borderline,” with only promising arms escalated—mirrors applied practice.

Cons against acceptance:
- Practicality of assumptions: known ζ(m), independence/concentration across fidelities, and Assumption 1 on ζ-decay.
- Regret definition, while justified, may not match all application goals; alternative comparators are only briefly discussed.
- Lower bound restricted to Bernoulli rewards and assumes subpolynomial play; near-optimality gap remains open.
- Experiments are synthetic and limited in detail; no sensitivity analysis to ζ mis-specification or correlation across fidelities; missing comparisons to cost-aware baselines (e.g., bandits with knapsacks).

Questions for the authors:
1. How sensitive is MF-UCB to misspecification of ζ(m)? Can you provide an adaptive variant that estimates or upper-bounds ζ(m) online with guarantees?
2. Do the results extend to heavy-tailed or correlated fidelity noise? Can ψ be learned or robustified?
3. How does MF-UCB compare to a “bandits with knapsacks” baseline tuned to cost λ(m), or to simple two-phase screening heuristics?
4. Could you quantify the regime where Assumption 1 fails (slowly decaying ζ) and characterize MF-UCB’s degradation?
5. Can the lower-bound technique be extended beyond Bernoulli to ψ-sub-Gaussian families?

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add experiments: (i) sensitivity to ζ(m) over/underestimation; (ii) correlated fidelity noise; (iii) comparisons to knapsack/budgeted bandit baselines; (iv) real or semi-synthetic tasks (e.g., ML model selection with subsampled data vs full training).
- Clarify the regret comparator further and discuss practical variants where low-fidelity rewards differ (e.g., zero reward in simulation). A brief appendix with alternative regret notions and how MF-UCB would adapt would be valuable.
- Discuss how to set ρ in practice and provide ablations on γ(m). A small worked example illustrating the K(m) partition would help readability.
- Note related work on bandits with knapsacks (e.g., Badanidiyuru et al., NIPS 2013) and contextual variants, and contrast objectives/formal differences.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The formulation and analysis are novel and technically solid, with clear potential impact. Strengthening the empirical section and discussing robustness to ζ(m) misspecification would further improve the paper.
