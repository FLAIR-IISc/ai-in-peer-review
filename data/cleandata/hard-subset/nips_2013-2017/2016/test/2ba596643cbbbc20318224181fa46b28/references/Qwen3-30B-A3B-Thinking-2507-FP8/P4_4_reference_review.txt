

# Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

This paper introduces a novel extension of the classic multi-armed bandit problem by incorporating multiple fidelities (approximations) for each arm, where each fidelity provides a progressively better but more expensive approximation of the true reward distribution. The authors formalize a cost-weighted regret measure that accounts for both the cost of playing at different fidelities and the reward structure. The main contribution is the Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB) algorithm, which leverages cheaper lower-fidelity approximations to eliminate suboptimal arms before committing to expensive high-fidelity evaluations. The paper provides theoretical analysis showing MF-UCB achieves better regret bounds than standard UCB when lower fidelities are cheaper and the number of arms with high expected reward is small.

The paper relates to previous work by building on UCB methods (Auer et al., 2002) but extending them to a multi-fidelity setting. It distinguishes itself from related work on multi-fidelity optimization (Huang et al., 2006; Cutler et al., 2015) by formally analyzing regret in the bandit setting, and from budgeted bandits (Tran-Thanh et al., 2010) by addressing multi-fidelity evaluations rather than cost-per-play. The authors also clarify how their work differs from active learning with weak/strong labelers (Zhang & Chaudhuri, 2015), noting that their focus is on exploration-exploitation trade-offs rather than label acquisition.

**Strengths:**
1. **Technical soundness:** The paper provides a rigorous formalism with clear assumptions and a well-motivated regret definition. The theoretical analysis is comprehensive, including both an upper bound for MF-UCB and a matching lower bound that demonstrates near-optimality. The proofs are well-structured, with key insights sketched in the main text and detailed in the appendix.

2. **Novelty:** The multi-fidelity bandit problem represents a meaningful extension to the bandit literature. Previous work on multi-fidelity methods focused on optimization rather than bandits, and this paper fills a significant gap by providing the first formal analysis of regret in this setting. The MF-UCB algorithm is a novel adaptation of UCB that properly accounts for the multi-fidelity structure.

3. **Clarity:** The paper is well-written with a logical flow from problem formulation to algorithm design to theoretical analysis. The motivating examples (online advertising, algorithm selection) effectively illustrate the practical relevance. The partitioning of arms across fidelities (Fig. 1) is particularly helpful for understanding the algorithm's behavior.

4. **Significance:** The problem has clear applications where cheaper approximations exist (e.g., online advertising, algorithm selection). The theoretical results demonstrate that MF-UCB can significantly outperform standard UCB when lower fidelities are sufficiently cheap and the number of near-optimal arms is small. The empirical results corroborate the theoretical insights.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Assumption of known ζ(m):** The paper assumes that the bias between fidelities (ζ(m)) is known, which may not be realistic in many applications. While this is a common simplification in bandit analysis, the paper could discuss how to estimate these quantities in practice.

2. **Limited regret definition scope:** The regret definition focuses on comparing against an oracle that plays the optimal arm at any fidelity, which may not capture all application scenarios (e.g., the gold mining robot example in the conclusion where lower fidelity plays have no rewards). While the authors acknowledge this limitation, a more general regret definition might strengthen the paper's applicability.

3. **Gap in lower bound analysis:** The lower bound shows a gap for arms in K(m)_7 (those where some lower fidelity suggests the arm might be optimal), which the authors acknowledge but cannot fully resolve. While this is a reasonable limitation given the problem structure, it would be beneficial to discuss potential approaches to address this gap.

**Recommendation:**

This paper makes a significant contribution to the bandit literature by formalizing a multi-fidelity extension of the classic problem and providing a theoretically sound algorithm with provable regret bounds. The technical content is rigorous, the problem is well-motivated, and the results represent a meaningful advancement over previous work. While there are some limitations in the assumptions and regret definition, these are reasonable for the theoretical contribution and do not undermine the core novelty and technical soundness of the work.

The paper satisfies all four NIPS review criteria:
- Quality: Technically sound with complete theoretical analysis
- Clarity: Well-written with clear explanations and figures
- Originality: Novel problem formulation and algorithmic approach
- Significance: Addresses a meaningful practical problem with strong theoretical results

The paper is clearly within the scope of NIPS, as it makes a theoretical contribution to the bandit problem, a core area in machine learning. The work builds on but significantly extends previous NIPS papers on bandits and multi-fidelity methods.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Novel problem formulation extending the bandit literature
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with upper and lower bounds
- Clear motivation and practical relevance
- Strong empirical validation
- Well-written and accessible to the bandit community

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Assumption of known ζ(m) may limit practical applicability
- Regret definition does not cover all potential applications
- Gap in lower bound for certain arms

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field that advances the state of the art in bandit theory. The paper is technically sound, novel, and significant, and the minor limitations do not detract from its value as a scientific contribution. I recommend acceptance.
