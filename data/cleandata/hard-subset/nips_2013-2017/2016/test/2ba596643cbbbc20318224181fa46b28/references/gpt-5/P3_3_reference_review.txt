Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces a stochastic multi-armed bandit setting where each arm admits multiple fidelities: cheaper, biased approximations (with known bias bounds ζ(m)) culminating in a most expensive, unbiased fidelity M. Each pull at fidelity m incurs cost λ(m), and the agent operates under a total capital budget Λ. The paper proposes a cost-weighted pseudo-regret R(Λ)=Λµ* − Σt λ(mt) qt with qt set to the high-fidelity mean of the arm played (independent of the fidelity), so the comparator is an oracle that always plays an optimal arm (at any fidelity) and uses all capital. The authors propose MF-UCB, which maintains upper bounds B(m) using observations at each fidelity plus a bias inflation ζ(m), takes the min across fidelities for an arm’s index, and decides which fidelity to play via a switching threshold γ(m) that trades off cost and bias. They derive an upper bound showing that MF-UCB spends most capital at low fidelities to eliminate clearly suboptimal arms and reserves expensive high-fidelity pulls for near-optimal arms. A lower bound (for Bernoulli rewards) shows near-optimality except in a specific subset of instances. Synthetic experiments compare MF-UCB with standard UCB that only uses the highest fidelity.

This work is distinct from budgeted bandits (reward/cost trade-offs per pull) and from multi-fidelity optimization in GP settings; it formalizes the multi-fidelity MAB with biased approximations and derives regret guarantees. It connects to UCB-style optimism and to works on multi-fidelity simulation in RL/optimization, but differs by explicitly handling fidelity bias and a capital budget with a new regret notion.

Quality
- Theory: The algorithm and analyses are technically solid and thoughtfully designed. The index B(m)k,t = empirical mean + confidence radius + ζ(m) is a valid high-probability upper bound on the true high-fidelity mean; taking the min across fidelities is principled. The fidelity selection rule via γ(m)=ψ^{-1}(λ(m)/λ(m+1) ψ(ζ(m))) is well-motivated: you stop at m when further confidence shrinkage is too costly relative to the bias floor ζ(m) and the next-fidelity cost. The upper bound decomposes arms into partitions K(m) by how early they can be excluded using low fidelities, and shows that MF-UCB pays λ(JkK)/ψ(Δ(JkK)k) (up to logs) rather than λ(M)/ψ(Δ(M)k) as in UCB, yielding savings whenever many arms can be eliminated cheaply. The lower bound (Bernoulli, with a regularity assumption excluding pathological policies that pull suboptimal arms polynomially many times) matches the upper bound on a substantial subset K(m)3 and isolates the gap on K(m)7 due to the switching rule’s conservatism.
- Assumptions: The concentration assumption (ψ), and known, monotone ζ(m) and costs λ(m) are standard within this stylized model. Assumption 1 (fast enough decay of ζ(m)) clarifies when multi-fidelity helps most.
- Completeness and gaps: The lower bound is restricted to Bernoulli rewards and leaves an open gap on K(m)7 instances (authors discuss why adapting switching on estimates might defeat the purpose). The high-probability control of N and the dependency on Λ are acknowledged as delicate; full proofs are in the appendix.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-structured. The intuition for the partition K(m), the role of ζ(m) in inflating UCBs, and the γ(m)-based switching rule are well-explained. Notation is heavy but consistent. The regret definition is nonstandard; the paper motivates it appropriately (qt equals high-fidelity value to reflect long-run utility), but a more explicit discussion contrasting alternative choices (e.g., fidelity-specific reward qt=µ(m)It or goal-dependent utilities) would help readers assess applicability.

Originality
The formalization of multi-fidelity bandits with known fidelity bias bounds, cost-weighted capital-based regret, and the MF-UCB algorithm with fidelity-aware indices and an explicit switching criterion is novel. The theoretical development—especially the partition-based analysis and the lower bound isolating the hard subset—goes beyond straightforward adaptations of UCB.

Significance
The setting is practically relevant (advertising, algorithm selection, simulation-to-real), and the work provides a clear algorithmic template with provable gains over UCB in regimes where many arms can be screened at low cost. The theoretical insights (tradeoffs between cost, bias, and confidence) are likely to be reused. However, the empirical section is limited (synthetic tasks only, one baseline), and the practicality hinges on knowing ζ(m), which may be unrealistic.

Strengths
- Clear, principled formalization of multi-fidelity MAB with biased approximations and costs.
- Simple MF-UCB algorithm with a compelling switching rule derived from cost-bias tradeoffs.
- Nontrivial regret upper bound showing tangible savings vs UCB; careful decomposition via K(m).
- Lower bound and near-optimality characterization; honest discussion of the remaining gap.
- Intuitive and consistent story: cheap fidelities prune, expensive fidelity focuses on near-optimal arms.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Requires known and valid bias bounds ζ(m). No analysis of robustness to misspecification or ways to learn ζ(m) online.
- The regret notion credits low-fidelity pulls with high-fidelity value; while motivated, it limits applicability (e.g., when only high fidelity produces value). Section 7 mentions alternatives but does not formalize them.
- Assumption 1 (fast decay) is important for appealing gains; limited discussion of performance when it fails.
- Experiments are minimal: only synthetic, only UCB as a baseline. No comparison to KL-UCB, TS adaptations, or simple multi-fidelity heuristics; no real-data study.
- Lower bound restricted to Bernoulli; open gap for K(m)7.

Questions for authors
1) How sensitive is MF-UCB to misspecified ζ(m)? Can you provide robustness bounds or an adaptive scheme to estimate or upper-bound ζ(m) from data?
2) Can the switching threshold be adjusted to better handle K(m)7 without incurring high-fidelity overuse (e.g., adaptive γ(m) using empirical gaps)?
3) How does the algorithm behave when Assumption 1 is violated (e.g., ζ decays slowly or λ(m+1)≈λ(m))? Any empirical stress tests?
4) Can you clarify practical choices of ψ and ρ for sub-Gaussian rewards and report empirical sensitivity to ρ?
5) Could you formalize and analyze alternative regret notions mentioned in Section 7 (e.g., no reward at low fidelity), or at least provide experiments in that setting?

Minor comments and suggestions
- Specify constants in γ(m) for sub-Gaussian ψ(x)=c x^2 to aid implementation.
- Clarify in Section 3 that the partition K(m) is used only for analysis (depends on unknown µ).
- Provide ablations varying K, M, ζ decay, and cost ratios, and include additional baselines (KL-UCB, TS, and a naive “screen-then-UCB” heuristic).
- Tighten some notation around Theorem 2 where two sum terms appear; ensure the final bound is stated unambiguously.

Recommendation
This is a strong theoretical paper with a clear algorithmic contribution and well-articulated insights. Despite limited experiments and the ζ(m) knowledge assumption, the work advances the study of multi-fidelity bandits in a meaningful way. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on strengthening the empirical evaluation and clarifying robustness. Confidence: high.
