

This paper proposes a novel extension to the multi-armed bandit problem by introducing multiple fidelities for each arm, where each fidelity provides a cheaper but potentially biased approximation of the arm's true reward. The authors formalize a new notion of regret that accounts for the cost of different fidelities and propose a Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB) algorithm that leverages lower fidelity evaluations to eliminate suboptimal arms while reserving higher fidelity evaluations for promising arms. The paper provides theoretical analysis showing that MF-UCB achieves regret bounds that can be significantly better than standard UCB algorithms when the number of arms with high expected reward is small and the cost of lower fidelity is substantially less than higher fidelity.

The paper builds on previous work in bandit algorithms (e.g., UCB [3]), multi-fidelity optimization [7, 10], and budgeted bandits [13, 15]. It clearly distinguishes itself from related work by formalizing a regret notion appropriate for the multi-fidelity setting and providing a theoretical analysis that demonstrates the benefits of exploiting multi-fidelity information.

Strengths:
1. The problem formulation is novel and well-motivated with clear practical applications in online advertising, algorithm selection, and robotics.
2. The theoretical analysis is comprehensive, providing both upper and lower bounds that demonstrate the near-optimality of MF-UCB.
3. The algorithm design is well-justified by the theoretical analysis, with clear intuition about how the multi-fidelity structure is exploited.
4. The paper is well-written with clear notation, logical flow, and helpful illustrations (e.g., Figure 1).
5. The empirical results on synthetic problems corroborate the theoretical insights.

Weaknesses:
1. The analysis relies on Assumption 1, which may not hold in all practical settings. The paper does not discuss how to handle cases where this assumption fails.
2. The paper does not address how to estimate the ζ(m) parameters in practice, which are assumed to be known. This is a significant limitation since these parameters would likely be unknown in most applications.
3. The lower bound has a gap for certain arms (K(m)7), which the authors acknowledge but do not fully resolve.
4. The empirical evaluation is limited to synthetic problems; more realistic applications would strengthen the practical relevance.
5. The computational complexity of MF-UCB is not discussed in detail, which could be important for large-scale applications.

Pro/Con for Acceptance:
Pro:
- Novel problem formulation with clear practical motivation
- Strong theoretical analysis with both upper and lower bounds
- Well-designed algorithm that achieves better regret bounds in the multi-fidelity setting
- Clear writing and good organization

Con:
- Reliance on Assumption 1 that may not hold in practice
- Lack of discussion on handling unknown ζ(m) parameters
- Gap in the lower bound for certain arms
- Limited empirical evaluation to synthetic problems

The paper makes a significant contribution to the multi-armed bandit literature by formalizing a new setting and providing a theoretically sound algorithm that achieves better regret bounds when the multi-fidelity structure is exploited. The novelty of the problem formulation and the theoretical analysis are strong points. While the paper has some limitations regarding practical implementation, the theoretical contribution is substantial and well-supported. The paper meets all four criteria: it is technically sound with well-supported claims, clearly written and organized, novel in its problem formulation, and significant in addressing a practical problem with theoretical rigor.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses related to Assumption 1 and the handling of unknown ζ(m) parameters.
