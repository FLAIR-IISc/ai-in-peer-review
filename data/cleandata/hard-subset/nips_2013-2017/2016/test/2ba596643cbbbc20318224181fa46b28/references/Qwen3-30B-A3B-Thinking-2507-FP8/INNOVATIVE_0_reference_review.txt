

# Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

This paper introduces a novel extension to the classic multi-armed bandit problem by incorporating multiple fidelities for each arm, where each fidelity represents a cheaper approximation to the true (highest fidelity) reward distribution. The authors formalize this setting with a cost-weighted regret definition that accounts for the varying costs of playing arms at different fidelities. Their key insight is that using cheaper low-fidelity observations to eliminate suboptimal arms before investing in expensive high-fidelity evaluations can significantly reduce regret compared to standard bandit algorithms that only use the highest fidelity.

The paper makes three main contributions: (1) a novel formalism for multi-fidelity bandits, (2) the MF-UCB algorithm that extends UCB to this setting, and (3) a theoretical analysis showing MF-UCB achieves regret O((λ(1)K + λ(2)|Kg|) log(Λ/λ(2))), which is better than standard UCB when |Kg| < K and λ(1) < λ(2). The authors provide a matching lower bound demonstrating near-optimality.

The paper is well-motivated with concrete applications in online advertising (where low-fidelity ad display periods can estimate click-through rates) and algorithm selection (where cheaper approximations of algorithm performance can be used). The formalism is clean, the algorithm is well-described, and the theoretical analysis is comprehensive, with careful handling of the bias introduced by lower fidelities (not treating them as merely higher-variance observations).

The paper's main strength is its novelty - while multi-fidelity methods have been used in optimization and robotics, this is the first to formalize and analyze the regret-based bandit problem with multiple fidelities. This represents a significant conceptual advance in bandit theory with clear practical implications. The theoretical contribution is substantial, with both a tight upper bound and lower bound showing near-optimality.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with good figures illustrating the arm partitioning across fidelities. The empirical validation on synthetic problems demonstrates MF-UCB's advantage over standard UCB, as predicted by the theory. While the experiments are limited to synthetic data, the theoretical results are strong enough to make a compelling case.

Minor weaknesses include: (1) the assumption that ζ(m) decays sufficiently fast (Assumption 1) might be restrictive in some applications, though the paper notes this is not necessary for small M; (2) the lower bound has a gap for certain arms (K(m)7), which the authors acknowledge but don't resolve; (3) the paper doesn't discuss how to choose parameters like ρ in practice.

However, these are relatively minor concerns in the context of the paper's significant contribution. The novelty and theoretical depth of this work make it a strong candidate for NIPS. The problem is clearly defined, the solution is elegant, and the analysis is rigorous. The paper opens up an important new direction in bandit research that will likely influence future work in both theory and applications.

The authors appropriately situate their work in relation to related literature, including budgeted bandits and multi-fidelity optimization, while clearly distinguishing their novel approach. The comparison to standard UCB is particularly compelling, showing how the multi-fidelity structure can be leveraged to achieve better regret bounds.

This paper exemplifies the kind of bold, novel theoretical contribution that NIPS values. While the methodological details are sound, the true strength lies in the new conceptual framework it introduces. The authors have identified a meaningful gap in the bandit literature and provided a well-motivated, theoretically grounded solution that significantly advances the state of the art.

I recommend acceptance. The paper is a valuable contribution that will likely inspire follow-up work on multi-fidelity bandits and related settings. The novelty of the problem formulation and the strength of the theoretical results outweigh the minor weaknesses in the empirical validation and theoretical assumptions.
