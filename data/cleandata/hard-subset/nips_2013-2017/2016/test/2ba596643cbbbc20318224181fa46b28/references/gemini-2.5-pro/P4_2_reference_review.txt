**Summary**

This paper introduces the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit (MF-MAB) problem, a novel extension of the classical K-armed stochastic bandit framework. In this setting, each of the K arms can be evaluated at M different "fidelities." Pulling an arm at a lower fidelity is cheaper but provides a more biased or noisy estimate of the true (highest fidelity) reward. The authors formalize this by assuming that for each arm `k` and fidelity `m`, the cost of a pull is `λ(m)` and the mean reward `µ(m)k` is within a known bound `ζ(m)` of the true mean `µ(M)k`. The costs are increasing with fidelity (`λ(1) < ... < λ(M)`) while the bias bounds are decreasing (`ζ(1) > ... > ζ(M) = 0`). The goal is to minimize a cost-weighted pseudo-regret given a total capital budget `Λ`.

The primary contribution is a new algorithm, the Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB). This algorithm adapts the optimism-in-the-face-of-uncertainty principle from classic UCB methods. At each step, MF-UCB selects the arm with the tightest upper confidence bound on its true mean, calculated by taking the minimum of the bounds derived from each fidelity. It then employs a sequential decision rule to select which fidelity to play for the chosen arm, starting from the cheapest. It plays at the first fidelity level `m` where the statistical uncertainty is larger than a carefully chosen threshold `γ(m)`, effectively deciding that more information can be gained at that cost level.

The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis of MF-UCB. It presents a regret upper bound which demonstrates that the algorithm leverages cheap fidelities to quickly eliminate highly suboptimal arms, reserving expensive high-fidelity pulls for a smaller set of promising candidates. This leads to a regret that, for many arms, scales with the cheaper cost `λ(m)` instead of the most expensive cost `λ(M)`. The authors complement this result with a problem-specific lower bound, showing that MF-UCB is near-optimal for a significant class of problem instances. The theoretical findings are supported by simulations on synthetic problems, where MF-UCB is shown to outperform a standard UCB algorithm that operates only at the highest fidelity. This work builds upon the extensive literature on UCB methods for bandits but carves out a new and practical direction, distinct from related areas like budgeted bandits or multi-fidelity optimization which do not focus on regret minimization.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed MF-MAB framework is well-defined, and the cost-weighted regret notion is appropriately motivated for the problem setting. The MF-UCB algorithm is intuitive and elegantly designed. The theoretical analysis is the main strength of the paper; it is rigorous and provides deep insights into the algorithm's behavior. The proof strategy, involving a partitioning of the arms based on their suboptimality at different fidelities, is effective. The inclusion of a lower bound analysis is a significant strength, as it provides a benchmark against which the proposed algorithm can be judged and demonstrates its near-optimality. The authors are careful to state their assumptions (e.g., known `ζ(m)` bounds, regularity on the decay of `ζ(m)`) and are transparent about the gap between the upper and lower bounds for a specific subset of arms (`K(m)7`).

**Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem with practical examples and situates the work within the existing literature. The problem formulation, algorithm description, and main theoretical results are presented logically and are easy to follow. The choice of notation is consistent and generally helpful. Figure 1 provides a useful visualization of the arm partitioning scheme used in the analysis. The core ideas behind the proofs are sketched in the main body, with full details in the appendix, which is an appropriate structure for a conference paper.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While multi-fidelity evaluation is a concept used in optimization and reinforcement learning, this paper appears to be the first to formally introduce and analyze it within the stochastic multi-armed bandit framework with a focus on regret minimization. The problem formulation, the MF-UCB algorithm, and the corresponding theoretical analysis are all novel contributions. The paper successfully carves out a new and meaningful variant of the MAB problem, differing significantly from prior work on budgeted or variable-cost bandits.

**Significance:** The results presented are significant and likely to be influential. The multi-fidelity setting is highly relevant to many real-world sequential decision-making problems where preliminary, cheaper evaluations are possible, such as hyperparameter tuning for machine learning models (using subsets of data), A/B testing (short-term vs. long-term exposure), and scientific simulation. The paper provides a principled and theoretically-grounded algorithm for this setting, which practitioners and researchers can build upon. The near-optimal theoretical guarantees suggest that the proposed approach is fundamentally sound. The framework opens up several avenues for future research, such as relaxing the assumptions or extending the setting to more complex bandit models.

A potential weakness lies in the assumption that the bias bounds `ζ(m)` for each fidelity are known in advance. In many practical scenarios, these may be difficult to specify accurately. A discussion on the robustness of the algorithm to misspecification of these parameters, or potential methods for learning them online, would strengthen the paper's practical implications. Furthermore, while the gap in the lower bound is acknowledged, it does leave a small part of the theoretical analysis incomplete. However, these are minor points that do not detract from the paper's core contributions.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Introduces a novel, well-motivated, and practical extension of the multi-armed bandit problem.
*   Proposes an elegant and intuitive algorithm, MF-UCB, for this new setting.
*   Provides a strong and rigorous theoretical analysis, including a regret upper bound that highlights the benefits of the multi-fidelity approach.
*   Derives a nearly-matching lower bound, establishing the near-optimality of MF-UCB.
*   The work is technically sound, clearly written, and addresses a problem of significant practical and theoretical interest.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The assumption of known fidelity bias bounds `ζ(m)` may be restrictive for some applications.
*   A small theoretical gap remains between the upper and lower bounds for certain arm configurations.
