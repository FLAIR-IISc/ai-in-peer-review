Summary and relation to prior work
The paper formalizes a stochastic K-armed bandit setting where each arm admits M fidelities: θ(1)k,…,θ(M−1)k are progressively cheaper, biased approximations to the target θ(M)k, with known bias bounds |µ(M)k − µ(m)k| ≤ ζ(m), where ζ(1) > … > ζ(M)=0. Each fidelity m has a cost λ(m), with λ(1) < … < λ(M). The paper proposes a cost-weighted pseudo-regret for a fixed capital Λ: R(Λ) = Λµ* − ∑t λ(mt)µ(M)It, which decomposes into an unused-capital term and a cost-weighted sum of instantaneous pseudo-regrets measured against the highest-fidelity mean µ(M). This captures applications where lower fidelity plays incur cost but do not yield the “true” reward one ultimately cares about (e.g., short ad displays as biased proxies for long-term CTR; simulators for real-world experiments).

The main algorithm, MF-UCB, adapts optimism in the face of uncertainty to the multi-fidelity setting. For each arm k and fidelity m, it maintains an upper bound B(m)k,t(s) = X̄(m)k,s + ψ−1(ρ log t / s) + ζ(m), where ψ comes from assumed concentration for each fidelity. The per-arm bound is the minimum across fidelities, Bk,t = minm B(m)k,t, and the arm with maximal Bk,t is chosen. The fidelity to play is selected by a stopping rule: starting at m=1, if the current uncertainty ψ−1(ρ log t / T(m)It,t−1) is ≥ γ(m) (or if m=M), play at m; otherwise escalate to higher fidelities. The thresholds are γ(m) = ψ−1( (λ(m)/λ(m+1)) ψ(ζ(m)) ), chosen to balance the cost of higher fidelities against the information cap implied by ζ(m).

Theoretical analysis proceeds by partitioning arms into sets K(m) determined by how close their fidelity-m means are to optimal, showing that MF-UCB (a) eliminates arms with clearly suboptimal lower-fidelity means using mostly low-cost plays, and (b) reserves expensive plays for arms whose high-fidelity means are near-optimal. The main upper bound shows E[R(Λ)] is O(log(Λ/λ(M))) times a sum in which each suboptimal arm contributes roughly λ(JkK)/ψ(Δ(JkK)k), with JkK the fidelity level needed to eliminate arm k and Δ(m)k = µ* − µ(m)k − ζ(m). This is contrasted with UCB run only at fidelity M, which pays λ(M)/ψ(Δ(M)k) for all arms. A lower bound (via change-of-measure for Bernoulli rewards) matches MF-UCB up to constants for a large subset of arms K(m)3, with a gap identified for a subset K(m)7 where MF-UCB’s conservative escalation rule can be suboptimal by a fidelity-dependent factor. Simulations on synthetic problems indicate improved cost-regret scaling over baseline UCB and fidelity usage consistent with the theory.

This work builds on classical UCB methods (Auer; Audibert et al.; Lai & Robbins) and is distinct from budgeted bandits (cost/reward ratio objectives), multi-fidelity optimization in engineering (which typically lacks bandit-style regret analysis), and RL with multi-fidelity simulators. It is related to, but different from, multi-fidelity Gaussian process bandits, as it treats independent arms with general concentration and explicit bias/cost tradeoffs.

Quality (technical soundness)
- Model and assumptions: The paper states clear stochastic assumptions (uniform concentration with function ψ and constant ν), known fidelity biases ζ(m) and costs λ(m), and a regularity condition (Assumption 1) ensuring ζ(m) decays sufficiently fast. These are used transparently in the algorithm (γ(m)) and analysis.
- Algorithm: The UCB construction with bias inflation and fidelity selection via uncertainty thresholds is technically well-motivated and simple to implement for sub-Gaussian ψ. The partitioning K(m) clarifies how effort is allocated.
- Theory: The regret bound carefully controls counts at each fidelity, the total number of plays under a capital budget, and shows that low-fidelity effort is dominated by the “right” fidelity cost term under Assumption 1. The lower bound captures the fundamental cost-information tradeoff and pinpoints a gap set K(m)7 where MF-UCB is conservative. Proof sketches are coherent; full proofs are deferred to the appendix.
- Limitations: The analysis requires known ζ(m) and ψ (or at least sub-Gaussian proxy), and ρ>4. Assumption 1 may be strong in practice; the paper notes it is not strictly necessary when M is small, but it is important for strongest comparisons. The lower bound’s distributional restriction (Bernoulli) is standard but narrower than the upper bound’s general ψ.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized: problem setup, regret definition, algorithm, main theorems, proof sketches, and experiments. Notation is dense but mostly consistent; explaining the oracle underlying the regret and the practical meaning of qt helps. Some minor presentational issues (e.g., citation formatting, defining ψ−1 and ν usage, clarifying the ∨ in the fidelity rule) could be improved. Experimental details are in the appendix; a brief in-body description would aid readability.

Originality
The multi-fidelity bandit formalism with biased, costed fidelities and a principled cost-regret notion, along with MF-UCB and matching lower bounds for a large subset of arms, appears novel relative to prior NIPS work on UCB, budgeted bandits, and multi-fidelity optimization.

Significance
The results can materially reduce cost-regret when many arms are clearly suboptimal at low fidelities, while avoiding worse-than-UCB performance in the worst case (up to constants). This has practical relevance in domains with cheap biased proxies (ads, simulators, subsampled training). The lower bound and near-optimality strengthen the contribution. Current empirical validation is limited to synthetic setups; broader experiments and comparisons would strengthen impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear and useful formalization of multi-fidelity bandits with an appropriate cost-weighted regret.
- Simple, interpretable algorithm with principled fidelity selection.
- Theoretical guarantees: near-optimal upper bound, informative lower bound, and clear identification of when gains occur.
- General analysis framework accommodating ψ beyond sub-Gaussian.
- Empirical results consistent with theory.

Cons against acceptance
- Strong assumption of known fidelity bias bounds ζ(m) and decay condition; no method or analysis for misspecification/estimation.
- Gap to the lower bound on K(m)7; no proposed remedy beyond leaving it open.
- Experimental evaluation is limited (synthetic only, few baselines; no comparison to other cost-aware or multi-fidelity strategies such as TS variants or heuristic escalations).
- Regret notion, while motivated, may not match all applications; alternative formulations are only briefly discussed.
- Some clarity/presentation issues (citation formatting, limited experimental detail in the main text).

Suggestions for improvement
- Discuss how ζ(m) could be estimated or upper-bounded in practice, and analyze robustness to misspecification.
- Consider fidelity-dependent concentration (ψm) and unequal variances across fidelities.
- Explore adaptive or data-driven γ(m), possibly with safe escalation rules that close the K(m)7 gap.
- Expand experiments: ablations over ζ and λ, robustness to mis-specified ζ, comparisons to stronger baselines (e.g., TS with fidelity-aware priors, heuristic bandit-with-proxies), and a real or semi-synthetic task.
- Clarify the oracle implicit in the regret and emphasize when this definition is appropriate versus alternatives.

Overall, this is a solid and timely contribution with strong theoretical underpinnings and practical motivation, with room to broaden empirical validation and relax assumptions.
