## Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel multi-fidelity extension to the classic multi-armed bandit (MAB) problem. The authors motivate this extension with practical scenarios where obtaining rewards at higher fidelities (e.g., longer ad displays, more extensive algorithm training) is costly, but cheaper approximations are available. They propose the Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB) algorithm, which leverages lower fidelity evaluations to efficiently eliminate suboptimal arms and focuses higher fidelity evaluations on promising candidates. The paper provides a theoretical analysis demonstrating a regret bound that outperforms naive UCB strategies under certain conditions, and empirical results validating this improvement.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds directly on the extensive literature surrounding UCB algorithms for MABs (Auer, 2002; Bubeck & Cesa-Bianchi, 2006). It differentiates itself by addressing the multi-fidelity aspect, which, while explored in optimization and reinforcement learning contexts (Cutler et al., 2016; Huang et al., 2006), has not been formally analyzed within the regret minimization framework of the MAB problem.  The authors correctly position their work relative to related areas like budgeted bandits (Tran-Thanh et al., 2013) and active learning (Zhang & Chaudhuri, 2016), highlighting the distinct focus on the exploration-exploitation trade-off inherent in the bandit setting. The reference to Kandasamy et al. (2016) is apt, showing awareness of very recent work extending the concepts to Gaussian process bandits.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The multi-fidelity MAB formulation is a genuinely new and practically relevant extension of the classic problem.  The motivation is clear and compelling.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a solid theoretical analysis with a regret bound for MF-UCB.  The proof sketch in the paper provides insight into how the algorithm leverages the fidelity structure. The lower bound analysis further strengthens the theoretical contributions.
*   **Clear Algorithm:** MF-UCB is clearly described and the intuition behind the algorithm (using lower fidelities for exploration and higher fidelities for exploitation) is well-articulated.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The simulation results demonstrate the practical benefits of MF-UCB compared to a standard UCB baseline.
*   **Well-Written & Organized:**  The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a logical flow of ideas. The notation is clearly defined, and the introduction effectively motivates the problem.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Assumptions:** The assumption that lower fidelity means are within a known bound (ζ(m)) of the true mean, while simplifying analysis, might be unrealistic in some applications.  The impact of violating this assumption is not discussed.
*   **Regularity Condition (Assumption 1):**  The necessity and practicality of Assumption 1 on ζ(m) decay isn’t fully compelling. While it improves performance, the paper doesn't demonstrate a scenario where *not* satisfying it leads to significantly degraded performance.
*   **Lower Bound Gap:** The authors acknowledge a gap between their regret bound and the derived lower bound for certain arms (K(m)7). Addressing this gap, even with discussion of potential future work, would significantly strengthen the theoretical contribution.
*   **Simulation Scope:** While the simulations are helpful, they seem limited. Exploring scenarios with more arms and varying cost/fidelity tradeoffs would further validate the algorithm's performance.
*   **Discussion of qt:** The choice of `qt = µ(M)It` is justified, but a deeper discussion of alternative choices for `qt` and their implications on the regret definition would be beneficial.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles a relevant and novel problem with a sound theoretical foundation.
*   The proposed algorithm, MF-UCB, is well-designed and empirically shown to outperform a standard baseline.
*   The theoretical analysis provides valuable insights into the algorithm's behavior and limitations.
*   The paper is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The strong assumptions (especially on the fidelity approximations) and the gap in the lower bound could be considered significant weaknesses.
*   The simulation results, while positive, could be more comprehensive.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper with significant potential. The novelty of the problem formulation, the rigor of the analysis, and the empirical validation make it a valuable contribution to the MAB literature.  While the assumptions and minor gaps in the theoretical results are concerns, they do not outweigh the overall quality of the work.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting the paper with the following revisions:

*   **Discuss the impact of violating the assumption |µ(M)k - µ(m)k| ≤ ζ(m).** What happens if the fidelity approximations are less accurate than assumed?
*   **Provide further justification for Assumption 1** or provide an example where it is significantly violated and demonstrate its impact on performance.
*   **Expand the discussion of the gap between the regret bound and the lower bound.**  Acknowledge the limitations and suggest directions for future research to close this gap.
*   **Consider adding a brief discussion of other potential choices for `qt` and their implications.**
*   **Include more varied simulation scenarios** to further demonstrate the robustness of the algorithm.
