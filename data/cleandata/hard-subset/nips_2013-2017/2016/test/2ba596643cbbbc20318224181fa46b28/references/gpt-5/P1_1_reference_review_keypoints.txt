- Introduces a multi-fidelity stochastic K-armed bandit where each arm has M fidelities with known bias bounds ζ(m) and fidelity-specific costs.  
- Defines a cost-weighted pseudo-regret rewarding µ(M) per unit cost and decomposes regret by cost allocated to each arm.  
- Proposes MF-UCB: a UCB-style algorithm that aggregates multi-fidelity samples with ζ(m) bias correction and escalates fidelity using thresholds γ(m).  
- Proves an upper bound (Theorem 2) showing MF-UCB often pays only the cost up to the lowest fidelity that can eliminate an arm, saving high-fidelity cost on many arms.  
- Gives a lower bound (Theorem 4) for Bernoulli rewards under a non-wastefulness assumption that matches MF-UCB on many arms but reveals a gap for marginally informative fidelities.  
- Synthetic experiments show improved regret slopes and reduced high-fidelity usage compared to standard UCB.  
- Related to budgeted/bandits-with-resource frameworks but distinct due to biased multi-fidelity observations and known bias bounds.  
- Complements prior multi-fidelity optimization and GP bandits by treating discrete-arm online regret.  
- Technical soundness: regret definition, arm partitioning, and fidelity-switching rules are well-motivated and analysis uses standard concentration tools.  
- Assumptions: reliance on known ζ(m), known concentration ψ, and Assumption 1 (rapid decay of ζ) may be strong and limit practicality.  
- Lower bound assumption (Assumption 3) restricts to reasonable algorithms and should be discussed further.  
- Proofs: main ideas are clear though some notational slips and clarifications are needed in statements (e.g., Theorem 2).  
- Clarity: paper is well organized and readable, but would benefit from explicit comparison to alternative regret definitions and careful proofreading.  
- Originality: novel model and UCB-style policy for multi-fidelity per-arm pulls with provable cost-weighted regret improvements.  
- Significance: practically relevant setting with meaningful theoretical improvements; empirical section supportive but narrow.  
- Pros: clear model, simple principled algorithm, nontrivial upper bound with matching lower bound on many instances, and focused high-cost plays on near-optimal arms.  
- Cons: practicality concerns about known ζ(m); strong Assumption 1; lower bound limited to Bernoulli; empirical evaluation limited in baselines and robustness checks.  
- Suggestions: discuss robustness or adaptive estimation of ζ, analyze performance when ζ decay fails, extend lower bound beyond Bernoulli, and expand experiments (KL-UCB/Thompson baselines, mis-specified ζ, realistic case study).  
- Suggest clarifying relation to BwK and contrasting regret notions where lower-fidelity rewards might not count.  
- Questions for authors: can MF-UCB adapt to unknown/arm-dependent ζ(m); sensitivity to mis-specified ζ or ψ; can fidelity-switching use estimated gaps to reduce the K(m)7 gap; connections/reductions to BwK?  
- Recommendation: strong, timely paper likely acceptable with improved empirical evaluation and clearer discussion of assumptions and related frameworks.
