

# Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

This paper proposes a multi-fidelity extension to the classic multi-armed bandit problem, introducing a new algorithm called MF-UCB that leverages cheaper lower-fidelity approximations to improve regret performance compared to standard UCB algorithms. The authors formalize a multi-fidelity setting where each arm has M approximations (fidelities) with increasing cost and accuracy, and propose a regret metric that accounts for both reward and fidelity costs. The theoretical analysis claims MF-UCB achieves O((λ(1)K + λ(2)|Kg|) log(Λ/λ(2))) regret, with a lower bound showing near-optimality.

## Strengths

The paper makes a clear contribution to formalizing the multi-fidelity bandit problem, which is an important extension to the standard bandit framework. The mathematical formulation is sound, and the theoretical analysis is thorough with a regret bound and lower bound. The intuition that lower-fidelity approximations can help eliminate suboptimal arms before expensive high-fidelity evaluations is reasonable and aligns with existing multi-fidelity optimization literature.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of substantial empirical validation**: The paper claims MF-UCB outperforms UCB on synthetic problems, but the experimental section is extremely limited (only referenced to Appendix C due to space constraints). There is no analysis of how the performance gain scales with problem size, how sensitive the algorithm is to parameter choices, or how it compares to other multi-fidelity methods. Without concrete experiments on meaningful problem instances, the claimed advantage over UCB remains theoretical.

2. **Questionable practical significance**: The theoretical advantage (O((λ(1)K + λ(2)|Kg|) log(Λ/λ(2))) vs. O(λ(2)K log(Λ/λ(2))) for UCB) only materializes when |Kg| << K. However, the paper does not provide evidence that this condition holds in practical scenarios where multi-fidelity approximations are available. In many real-world applications (e.g., algorithm selection), the number of promising arms may not be significantly smaller than the total number of arms.

3. **Insufficient comparison to related work**: The paper cites several works on multi-fidelity methods [7, 10, 16], but fails to adequately distinguish their approach from these. For instance, the work of Kandasamy et al. [8] (which the authors themselves cite) also studies multi-fidelity bandits with Gaussian process payoffs. The paper should clarify how MF-UCB differs from and improves upon this prior work.

4. **Unjustified claims about "near-optimality"**: The lower bound analysis reveals a gap between MF-UCB's performance and the theoretical lower bound for certain arms (K(m)7). The paper acknowledges this gap but fails to explain why this gap matters in practice or how it might be addressed. The claim that MF-UCB is "near-optimal" seems overstated given this gap.

5. **Minimal discussion of implementation challenges**: The algorithm requires careful tuning of parameters like γ(m) and ρ. The paper provides no guidance on how to select these in practice, which would be critical for adoption. The theoretical analysis assumes knowledge of ζ(m), which is unrealistic in most applications where these biases are unknown.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper presents a theoretically interesting extension to the multi-armed bandit framework, it falls short of demonstrating a meaningful practical contribution. The claimed improvement over standard UCB algorithms is conditional on assumptions that may not hold in practice (|Kg| << K), and the empirical validation is insufficient to support the theoretical claims. The paper makes a modest contribution to the literature on multi-fidelity optimization but does not provide compelling evidence that this approach would be preferred over simpler methods in real-world applications.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1) More detailed experimental validation on synthetic and potentially real-world problems
2) Clearer comparison to related multi-fidelity bandit approaches
3) Discussion of practical implementation challenges and parameter sensitivity
4) A more nuanced assessment of when the multi-fidelity approach is actually beneficial

## Recommendation

This paper presents an interesting theoretical extension to the multi-armed bandit problem but lacks sufficient empirical validation to support its claims. The improvement over standard UCB is only significant in specific, potentially rare scenarios, and the paper does not convincingly demonstrate the practical value of the proposed approach. While the theoretical analysis is sound, the lack of thorough experimental validation and discussion of practical implementation challenges makes it difficult to assess the true significance of the contribution.

I recommend rejection. The paper would be a stronger contribution with more extensive empirical validation, clearer differentiation from prior work, and a more realistic assessment of the conditions under which the proposed method would be advantageous.
