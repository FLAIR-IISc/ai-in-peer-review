- Introduces a multi-fidelity MAB framework where arm evaluations have varying fidelities and costs.  
- Proposes MF-UCB, which uses low-fidelity evaluations to eliminate arms and reserves high-fidelity pulls for promising candidates.  
- Theoretical analysis shows MF-UCB has improved regret over naive UCB when low fidelities are cheap and biases are bounded, with a derived lower bound.  
- Builds on UCB literature and differentiates from multi-fidelity optimization, RL simulators, and budgeted bandits by handling biased approximations in exploration-exploitation.  
- Extends the authors’ prior multi-fidelity GP bandit work to the bandit setting.  
- Novelty: presents a genuinely new perspective on MABs enabling cost-aware exploration.  
- Theoretical rigor: provides formal regret bounds and near-optimality arguments.  
- Intuition: using cheaper fidelities for screening and expensive ones for verification is clear and well-justified.  
- Presentation: paper is well-structured and clearly written.  
- Empirics: simulations support theory but are limited and not extensive.  
- Assumptions: analysis depends on fidelity bias (ζ(m)) and concentration (ψ) assumptions that may not hold in practice.  
- Regularity Condition (Assumption 1) is somewhat restrictive and appears crucial for improved performance over UCB.  
- Lower-bound gap: there is a gap between the MF-UCB upper bound and the derived lower bound for certain arms.  
- Limited experiments: needs more diverse synthetic scenarios and real-world dataset evaluations.  
- Regret definition: focuses on a specific regret notion and should discuss alternative definitions and implications.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel setting, rigorous analysis, simple implementable algorithm, and clear exposition.  
- Arguments against acceptance: restrictive assumptions, theoretical gap, and limited empirical validation.  
- Overall assessment: strong paper deserving acceptance pending minor revisions to address assumptions, the lower-bound gap, and experiments.  
- Suggested revisions: clarify impact of assumption violations, further explore Assumption 1, investigate the lower-bound gap, and expand empirical evaluation.
