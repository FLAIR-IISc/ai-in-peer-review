

# Review: The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit

This paper introduces a multi-fidelity extension to the standard multi-armed bandit problem, where each arm has multiple approximations (fidelities) with different costs. The authors formalize a cost-weighted notion of regret for this setting, propose the Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB) algorithm, and provide theoretical guarantees showing that MF-UCB achieves better regret bounds than standard UCB when the number of "good" arms is small and lower fidelities are cheaper. The work builds on standard UCB approaches [2,3,5] but extends them to the multi-fidelity setting, which has been previously studied in optimization contexts [7,10] but without formal regret analysis.

**Quality:** The paper demonstrates strong technical soundness. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with careful definition of assumptions (particularly Assumption 1 regarding the decay of ζ(m)) and well-structured proofs for both upper and lower bounds. The regret bound (Theorem 2) is carefully derived, and the lower bound (Theorem 4) demonstrates that MF-UCB is near-optimal for most arms. The paper correctly acknowledges limitations (e.g., the gap in the lower bound for certain arms in K(m)7) and provides a clear explanation for why this gap exists. The experimental validation on synthetic problems supports the theoretical claims, though it would be strengthened by comparisons to alternative multi-fidelity bandit approaches.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly articulated with practical examples (online advertising, algorithm selection), and the formalism is logically developed. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is precise, and the figures effectively illustrate the partitioning of arms across fidelities. The paper provides sufficient detail for an expert to reproduce the results, with clear definitions of all key terms and notation. The exposition of the regret formulation (equation 1) and the motivation for choosing qt = µ(M)It is particularly well-justified.

**Originality:** The paper makes a clear and significant contribution by formalizing the multi-fidelity bandit problem with a cost-weighted regret metric and providing theoretical guarantees. While related work exists on multi-fidelity methods in optimization [7,10] and active learning [16], these works do not formalize or analyze regret in the bandit setting. The authors correctly distinguish their contribution from previous work and provide a novel algorithm (MF-UCB) that adapts UCB to this setting. The specific regret formulation and theoretical analysis represent a meaningful extension to the bandit literature.

**Significance:** The problem addressed has clear practical importance, as many real-world applications involve expensive evaluations that can be approximated with cheaper alternatives (e.g., online advertising, algorithm selection). The theoretical results demonstrate that MF-UCB can achieve significantly better regret than standard UCB when the number of promising arms is small, which is a common scenario in practice. This represents a demonstrable advance in the state of the art for bandit problems with multi-fidelity evaluations. The paper also identifies promising directions for future work in different regret formulations for other application domains.

**Strengths:** The paper provides a complete theoretical analysis with both upper and lower bounds, demonstrating near-optimality. The motivation is strong with practical examples, and the algorithm design is well-justified by the theory. The writing is clear and well-organized, making complex concepts accessible.

**Weaknesses:** The experimental validation is limited to synthetic problems without comparison to alternative multi-fidelity bandit approaches. The paper relies on specific assumptions (particularly Assumption 1), and the robustness of the algorithm to violations of these assumptions is not discussed. The practical considerations for parameter selection (e.g., ρ and γ(m)) are not addressed.

**Arguments for acceptance:** This paper presents a novel formalism for multi-fidelity bandits with rigorous theoretical guarantees, including a tight regret bound and matching lower bound for most arms. The problem is well-motivated, the analysis is sound, and the results represent a significant advance over standard bandit approaches in scenarios with multiple fidelities. The paper is clearly written and makes a strong contribution to the bandit literature.

**Arguments against acceptance:** The limited experimental validation on synthetic data only partially supports the theoretical claims. The reliance on specific assumptions (like Assumption 1) may limit applicability in some practical scenarios. The paper does not address how to choose parameters in practice, which could be important for real-world implementation.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the multi-armed bandit literature. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the problem is well-motivated, and the results represent a clear advance over standard approaches. While the experimental validation could be strengthened, the theoretical contribution is substantial and the paper meets all four review criteria at a high level. I recommend acceptance.
