Summary and relation to prior work
The paper introduces a stochastic K-armed bandit setting with M fidelities per arm. Each fidelity m provides a biased approximation to the highest-fidelity reward with known bias bounds |µ(M)k − µ(m)k| ≤ ζ(m), and incurs an increasing cost λ(m). The objective is to minimise cost-weighted pseudo-regret under a capital budget Λ, where the pseudo-reward qt is defined as the highest-fidelity mean µ(M) of the played arm, irrespective of which fidelity was queried. The proposed algorithm, MF-UCB, extends UCB by constructing fidelity-specific upper bounds B(m) using empirical means, concentration terms ψ−1(ρ log t / s), and bias ζ(m), and taking Bk,t = minm B(m). After selecting the arm with maximal Bk,t, MF-UCB chooses the least expensive fidelity whose current uncertainty exceeds a designed threshold γ(m) = ψ−1( (λ(m)/λ(m+1)) ψ(ζ(m)) ), thereby using cheap fidelities to eliminate clearly suboptimal arms and reserving expensive queries for near-optimal arms.

The analysis partitions arms according to how close their fidelity-m means are to µ?, and shows an upper bound on regret that (informally) scales as ∑k ∆(M)k λ(JkK)/ψ(∆(JkK)k ) up to log factors, where JkK is the fidelity at which arm k is effectively eliminated. This is contrasted with standard UCB, which pays λ(M) for all arms. A lower bound (for Bernoulli rewards) shows MF-UCB is near-optimal except for a specific subset of arms where small gaps at low fidelities make an early switch suboptimal. Related work in classic bandits (UCB), budgeted bandits, multi-fidelity optimization (e.g., multi-fidelity BO/kriging), and multi-fidelity RL is discussed; the bandit-with-costs-and-biases formulation appears novel.

Strengths
- Problem formulation: Multi-fidelity bandits with biased approximations and explicit cost trade-offs is well-motivated and addresses realistic scenarios (advertising, algorithm selection).
- Algorithmic design: MF-UCB is simple, principled, and computationally light. The fidelity-switching rule γ(m) captures a clear cost–accuracy trade-off.
- Theory: The upper bound cleanly decomposes cost spent across fidelities, showing how low fidelities eliminate many suboptimal arms and yielding improvements when |K(M)| ≪ K. The lower bound matches the upper bound on a broad class of arms, clarifying where the multi-fidelity structure helps and where it cannot.
- Conceptual clarity: The use of optimism with bias corrections (ζ(m)) is natural, and the partitioning K(m) gives good intuition about fidelity allocation.
- Empirical evidence: Synthetic experiments corroborate the theory, showing reduced regret slope and fidelity usage as predicted.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Modeling choice for regret: Defining qt = µ(M)It regardless of the fidelity is a strong choice. In many applications, low-fidelity actions do produce actual utility (e.g., shortened ads yield real clicks), and stakeholders may care about reward-per-cost or actual realized reward at the played fidelity. While you discuss alternatives in the conclusion, it would strengthen the paper to: (a) formalize at least one alternative regret notion (e.g., realized reward-per-cost or a two-objective formulation), and (b) analyze whether MF-UCB or a variant maintains guarantees under that metric.
- Known bias bounds ζ(m): The analysis assumes fidelity bias envelopes are known and uniform across arms, which is rarely true in practice. The paper would benefit from: (a) a robustness discussion and sensitivity experiments where ζ(m) is mis-specified, (b) a data-driven calibration (e.g., occasional paired high/low-fidelity evaluations to estimate ζ(m) with confidence bounds), and (c) an extension using learned or arm-dependent bias bounds without breaking optimism.
- Assumption 1 (fast decay of ζ): This regularity condition (sum of inverse ψ terms) is fairly strong; it essentially requires exponential decay for sub-Gaussian ψ. What happens when fidelities are dense or improvements are marginal (a plausible practical regime)? It would help to present a bound without Assumption 1 (possibly with worse constants) or empirically show performance degradation when the assumption fails.
- Lower bound gap and switching policy: The gap for arms in K(m)7 highlights that the myopic switch rule can be suboptimal when small positive gaps at low fidelities could be certified cheaply. While you correctly point out that estimating ∆(m) precisely can itself be costly, it would be valuable to discuss heuristic remedies (e.g., occasional “cheap certification” tests at low fidelities for arms near switching thresholds) and quantify when such tests pay off.
- Relation to other lines: There are additional relevant literatures that could be cited and contrasted: side-observation/graph-structured bandits (where one action gives information about others), bandits with knapsacks (resource constraints and costs), and best-arm identification with heterogeneous costs. This would help position the work more broadly.
- Empirics: The experiments are limited to synthetic data and UCB as the sole baseline. Including: (a) a simple hierarchical elimination baseline that uses low-fidelity screening plus high-fidelity UCB, (b) a Thompson Sampling variant with multi-fidelity posteriors, and (c) sensitivity to ζ, λ, M, and misleading lower fidelities would increase confidence. A real-world case study (even small-scale) would be impactful.

Quality
- The technical development is sound and careful. Proof sketches align with standard UCB and change-of-measure techniques, with clear identification of where multi-fidelity structure enters. Some statements (e.g., Theorem 2 presenting two summations) would benefit from clearer presentation; the authors later argue the first-term cost dominates, but cleaning up the bound statement would improve readability.

Clarity
- Generally clear and well-organized, with helpful intuition for γ(m) and the K(m) partition. Suggestions: explicitly state that K(m) is used for analysis only; add guidance on choosing ψ and ρ in practice; provide more detail on how to compute ψ−1 for common sub-Gaussian cases.

Originality
- The formulation and algorithm are novel in the bandit regret setting with biased approximations and per-fidelity costs. The work is clearly distinct from budgeted bandits and multi-fidelity BO.

Significance
- The setting is practically relevant and the theoretical insights are likely to influence follow-up work on bandits with proxies/simulators. The paper advances understanding of how to exploit cheap biased information to reduce regret.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and relevant problem formulation
- Simple, principled algorithm with strong theory
- Clear intuition; near-optimality for a large class
- Empirical results consistent with theory

Cons for acceptance
- Strong assumptions (known ζ, Assumption 1); limited robustness discussion
- Narrow empirical evaluation; few baselines and no real data
- Regret definition may misalign with some applications; alternatives not analyzed
- Lower bound gap unresolved (though well-explained)

Overall recommendation
I lean toward acceptance. The work is technically solid, novel, and addresses an important class of problems; the limitations are primarily in assumptions and empirical breadth, which are clearly acknowledged and suggest fruitful future work.
