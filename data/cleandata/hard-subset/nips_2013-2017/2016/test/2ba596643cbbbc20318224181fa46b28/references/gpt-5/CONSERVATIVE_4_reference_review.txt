Summary
The paper introduces a multi-fidelity variant of the stochastic K-armed bandit where each arm can be queried at multiple fidelities that trade off cost and bias. Lower fidelities are cheaper but biased approximations of the highest-fidelity reward; the biases are upper-bounded by known quantities ζ(m) that decrease with fidelity. The authors propose a cost-weighted pseudo-regret under a capital budget Λ that rewards (in pseudo-regret accounting) the highest-fidelity mean regardless of the fidelity queried. They present MF-UCB, which maintains upper confidence bounds for each arm-fidelity using concentration functions ψ and adds ζ(m) to account for bias; the algorithm picks the arm with the tightest UCB on the high-fidelity mean and chooses the lowest fidelity whose confidence radius exceeds a threshold γ(m) depending on λ(m), λ(m+1), and ζ(m). They prove an upper bound showing MF-UCB expends most effort on promising arms at higher fidelities and eliminates clearly suboptimal arms using cheaper fidelities; they also give a lower bound (for Bernoulli rewards) and identify a gap for a subset of instances. Synthetic experiments show improvements over standard UCB that ignores low-fidelity evaluations.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on UCB-style algorithms and the theory of stochastic bandits, as well as budgeted bandits (reward/cost tradeoffs) and applications of multi-fidelity modeling in optimization and RL. Unlike budgeted bandits, the objective here is to compete with the best high-fidelity arm while using inexpensive proxies to reduce exploration cost. The paper appears to be one of the earlier formal treatments of biased multi-fidelity observations in bandits with regret guarantees, complementing empirical multi-fidelity heuristics.

Strengths
- Clear formalization of a practically motivated setting where biased, cheaper proxies are available.
- Algorithmic design is principled: UCB with bias-aware upper bounds and a fidelity-switching rule tuned to λ and ζ.
- Nontrivial theoretical analysis: instance-dependent upper bound and a lower bound (under Bernoulli), with a crisp interpretation that multi-fidelity helps most on “easy” arms far from optimality.
- Intuitive partitioning of arms by which fidelity can eliminate them; the results explain when and by how much MF-UCB improves over single-fidelity UCB.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Novelty vs. validation: While the theory is interesting, empirical evaluation is minimal and entirely synthetic. There are no comparisons to stronger bandit baselines (e.g., KL-UCB, Thompson sampling) adapted to the cost model, nor to elimination/racing strategies that naturally fit multi-fidelity screening. The only baseline is “UCB at highest fidelity,” which is a rather weak comparator in this setting. Without broader experiments, the practical impact is unclear.
- Assumption of known bias bounds ζ(m): MF-UCB heavily relies on accurate ζ(m) to construct UCBs and choose γ(m); the analysis and purported gains hinge on this. In realistic applications, ζ(m) is unknown or instance-specific. The paper neither proposes a way to estimate ζ(m) nor evaluates robustness to mis-specification.
- Regret definition: The choice to credit pseudo-reward as the high-fidelity mean regardless of the queried fidelity is reasonable for some applications but nonstandard. It strongly favors algorithms that spend more “plays” at cheap fidelities. The residual term r̃ and the reliance on capital conversion complicate comparison to standard regret, and make the fairness of the UCB baseline less clear. Alternative choices for qt are mentioned but not explored.
- Regularity condition (Assumption 1): The key assumption relating ζ(m) through ψ appears tailored to the analysis and to guarantee appealing improvements. Its practical validity and necessity are not convincingly justified. Empirical sensitivity to violations is not studied.
- Lower bound limitations: The lower bound is restricted to Bernoulli rewards and requires Assumption 3 (ruling out strategies that play suboptimal arms polynomially often at any fidelity). The constant c is unspecified, and the authors acknowledge a gap for K(m)7 instances. Claiming near-optimality is thus stronger than what is rigorously established.
- Missing baselines and related work: No comparison to successive elimination/racing-type methods adapted to multi-fidelity costs, nor to more refined UCB variants (KL-UCB) that could change constant factors. The relationship to budget-limited bandits is discussed, but empirical comparisons are absent.

Quality
The theoretical development appears sound at a high level; the partition-based proof strategy and fidelity-switching rule are well motivated. However, key practical aspects (setting/estimating ζ, robustness, parameter sensitivity) are untested. The lower bound is narrower in scope than the upper bound.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written, though dense. The regret formulation and the role of qt could be clarified further with concrete examples. The dependence of constants and the role of ψ in practice (beyond sub-Gaussian) should be better explained. Experimental details are deferred to the appendix; more in-paper detail would help.

Originality
The formulation and algorithmic adaptation of UCB to biased multi-fidelity observations are novel and timely. The fidelity-switching rule coupled with bias-aware UCBs is a clean idea. The theoretical framework seems original relative to prior multi-fidelity heuristics.

Significance
Potentially significant if robust and practically applicable; many applications have proxy evaluations. However, the current empirical evidence and assumptions (known ζ) limit immediate impact. The lower bound gap reduces the strength of optimality claims.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- How sensitive is MF-UCB to misspecified ζ(m)? Can you propose/assess a data-driven way to upper bound ζ(m)?
- Compare against stronger baselines: KL-UCB, Thompson sampling (with cost), and simple two-phase/racing heuristics that use low fidelity to screen arms.
- Provide experiments with real or semi-realistic setups (e.g., simulator vs. real task) and varying λ, ζ schedules, including cases where Assumption 1 fails.
- Clarify how to set ρ in practice and the effect on performance.
- Can the lower bound be extended beyond Bernoulli and without Assumption 3?
- Discuss arm-dependent costs or fidelity-dependent noise variances.

Pros
- Clear and interesting formalization of multi-fidelity bandits with biased proxies.
- Principled algorithm with interpretable design and instance-dependent regret bound.
- Lower bound and honest discussion of a gap.

Cons
- Very limited empirical validation and weak baseline comparison.
- Strong reliance on known ζ(m); no robustness study.
- Assumptions (Assumption 1, Bernoulli for lower bound) constrain generality.
- Regret notion is nonstandard; fairness of comparisons to single-fidelity UCB is debatable.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). The theoretical contribution is solid, but given the heavy reliance on unverified assumptions and the limited, synthetic-only experiments against a weak baseline, I am not convinced of practical soundness yet. Strengthening empirical validation, adding robust baselines, and addressing ζ(m) estimation/robustness would substantially improve the paper.
