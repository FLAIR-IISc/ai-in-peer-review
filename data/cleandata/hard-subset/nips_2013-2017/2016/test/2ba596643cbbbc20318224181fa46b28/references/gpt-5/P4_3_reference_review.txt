Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper introduces a stochastic K-armed bandit setting where each arm admits M fidelities: lower fidelities are cheaper but biased approximations to the highest-fidelity reward distribution. The bias between fidelity m and the true (Mth) fidelity is bounded by known ζ(m), with ζ(m) decreasing in m. Each fidelity has an associated cost λ(m), increasing in m. The authors define a cost-weighted pseudo-regret appropriate for this setting: regardless of which fidelity is played, the instantaneous pseudo-reward is taken to be the highest-fidelity mean of the played arm, and regret accounts for both the suboptimality of the arm and the cost incurred, plus a residual term for unused budget.

They propose MF-UCB, an upper-confidence-bound algorithm that maintains per-fidelity UCBs on μ(M)k by adding ζ(m) to the empirical mean at fidelity m, and taking the minimum across fidelities. MF-UCB selects the arm with the largest bound, and determines the fidelity adaptively: it stays at a lower fidelity m while the statistical uncertainty at that fidelity exceeds a threshold γ(m) (chosen as a function of λ and ζ), and escalates otherwise. Intuitively, lower fidelities are used to cheaply exclude clearly suboptimal arms; expensive higher fidelities are reserved for a small set of promising arms.

Under standard concentration assumptions (sub-Gaussian-like, via a generic ψ), an additional regularity condition on the decay of ζ(m), and for sufficiently large budget, the paper proves an expected regret bound (Theorem 2) scaling like:
- For each suboptimal arm k, roughly λ(JkK)/ψ(Δ(JkK)k) times log budget, where JkK is the highest fidelity at which k can be eliminated based on the thresholds and biases; plus smaller terms from lower fidelities.
This improves over a naive single-fidelity UCB that always pays λ(M), by replacing λ(M) with λ(JkK) for many arms. The authors also prove a lower bound (Theorem 4) for Bernoulli rewards showing MF-UCB is near-optimal: it matches the lower bound for a broad subset of arms K(m)3, and is off by at most a constant factor or a fidelity-choice factor for arms K(m)7, where distinguishing suboptimality at a cheaper fidelity would be information-theoretically optimal but is avoided by the algorithm’s switching rule.

Related work: The setting differs from budgeted bandits [13, 15], where the objective relates to reward/cost ratios per arm, and from multi-fidelity RL/simulator work [6] and multi-fidelity optimization [7, 10], which do not analyze exploration–exploitation regret. It complements UCB-classic analyses [2, 3, 5, 9] by introducing biased, cost-tiered observations and establishing new regret and lower bounds. The multi-fidelity GP bandit work [8] extends this line to structured (continuous) domains, while this paper treats the finite-armed case.

Strengths:
- Clear, well-motivated formalization of multi-fidelity bandits with biased lower fidelities and explicit cost model.
- Algorithmic contribution (MF-UCB) is principled and simple to implement: per-fidelity UCBs with bias correction and an interpretable escalation rule.
- Theoretical guarantees: a regret upper bound showing substantial savings when many arms can be eliminated at low fidelity; matching or near-matching lower bound, clarifying optimality and where gaps arise.
- Insightful partitioning of arms K(1), …, K(M) according to eliminability at each fidelity, clarifying how cost savings are realized.
- Simulations corroborate theory: MF-UCB achieves lower regret and allocates most expensive plays to near-optimal arms.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Assumption of known bias bounds ζ(m) is strong. Many applications may not have certified bias envelopes. Discussion on estimating or adaptively calibrating ζ(m), or robustness to mis-specification, would strengthen applicability.
- Assumption 1 (fast decay of ζ) is used to sharpen comparisons to UCB. Empirical sensitivity to slower ζ decay could be explored; an experiment varying ζ decay would clarify robustness.
- Lower bound is given for Bernoulli rewards. While ψ-general upper bounds are provided, a ψ- or KL-based lower bound (e.g., via information-theoretic inequalities) would better align upper/lower analyses under general sub-Gaussian conditions.
- Baselines: comparisons are only to a naïve single-fidelity UCB. It would be informative to compare to simple heuristic multi-fidelity baselines (e.g., fixed-stage elimination at low fidelity, Thompson sampling adaptations, or KL-UCB variants).
- Regret definition: adopting qt = μ(M)It is motivated and consistent with long-horizon objectives (e.g., advertising), but alternatives exist (as noted in Sec. 7). A brief experimental or theoretical comparison to alternatives (e.g., fidelity-dependent reward) would contextualize the choice.
- Some notation is dense; a compact pseudocode block and a short example illustrating the switching condition would aid clarity.

Quality:
- Technical soundness appears solid. The bias-adjusted UCB construction and switching thresholds are well motivated, and the proofs (as sketched) use standard concentration and change-of-measure tools. The partition-based analysis is coherent, and the near-optimality discussion is honest about the gap for K(m)7 arms. Assumptions are stated; the dependence on ρ and Λ0 is explained.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally clear but notation-heavy. The regret decomposition and the role of r̃ vs. R̃ are well explained. Figures illustrating partitions and fidelity allocation help. Providing more intuition around γ(m) beyond the ψ-based expression, and adding a small running example, could further improve readability.

Originality:
- The formalization of multi-fidelity, biased observations per arm with costs, and the associated MF-UCB design and analyses, are novel relative to prior bandit work and to multi-fidelity optimization/RL literature cited. The lower bound tailored to this setting is also original.

Significance:
- The work addresses a practical and recurring scenario (cheap but biased proxies), and demonstrates potentially large cost/regret savings when many arms are easily eliminated at low fidelity. The theory clarifies when savings are expected. The contribution is likely to stimulate follow-up on adaptive bias estimation, TS/KL-UCB variants, and structured settings (as in [8]).

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel and well-scoped problem formulation with clear practical relevance.
- Simple, principled algorithm with strong theoretical guarantees and near-optimality.
- Clear improvement over single-fidelity UCB under realistic conditions.
- Empirical results consistent with theory.

Cons for acceptance:
- Reliance on known ζ(m); limited discussion/experiments on robustness and estimation.
- Lower bound restricted to Bernoulli; lack of stronger empirical baselines.
- Assumption 1 narrows best-case comparisons; limited empirical exploration of adverse cases.

Questions for authors:
- How sensitive is MF-UCB to misspecified ζ(m)? Can one adaptively inflate ζ(m) from data to maintain validity?
- Could the switching rule be modified to close the K(m)7 gap without incurring high-fidelity sampling for most arms?
- Do TS or KL-UCB-style multi-fidelity variants exhibit similar improvements?

Overall: This is a solid, theoretically grounded contribution introducing a compelling multi-fidelity bandit framework and near-optimal algorithm. With minor improvements in empirical evaluation and discussion of robustness, it is a strong candidate for acceptance.
