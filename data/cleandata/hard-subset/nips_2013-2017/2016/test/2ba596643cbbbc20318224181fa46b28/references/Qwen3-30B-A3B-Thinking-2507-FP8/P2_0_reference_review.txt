

# Review: "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

This paper introduces a novel extension to the classical multi-armed bandit problem where each arm has access to multiple approximations (fidelities) of its reward distribution at different costs. The authors propose MF-UCB, an algorithm that strategically uses cheaper lower-fidelity evaluations to eliminate suboptimal arms while reserving expensive high-fidelity evaluations for promising arms. The paper makes a significant contribution to bandit theory by formalizing a new regret framework for this multi-fidelity setting and providing rigorous theoretical guarantees.

**Quality** (Strong): The paper is technically sound with a clear problem formalization and well-constructed algorithm. The theoretical analysis is thorough, featuring both an upper bound on MF-UCB's regret (Theorem 2) and a matching lower bound (Theorem 4). The authors carefully justify their design choices, particularly the choice of γ(m) in equation (4), and provide a clear explanation for the gap between MF-UCB's performance and the lower bound for certain arms (K(m)7). The regret bound demonstrates that MF-UCB can significantly outperform standard UCB when the number of promising arms is small and lower fidelities are cheaper. The synthetic experiments in Section 6 corroborate the theoretical claims, showing MF-UCB consistently outperforms UCB with a smaller gradient in regret curves.

**Clarity** (Strong): The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem with relevant examples (online advertising, algorithm selection) that make the multi-fidelity concept immediately accessible. The notation is consistent and well-defined, and the key insights are explained intuitively before diving into technical details. Figure 1 effectively visualizes the partitioning of arms across fidelities, aiding in understanding the algorithm's behavior. The paper is structured logically: problem statement, algorithm, analysis, experiments, and discussion.

**Originality** (Strong): This work is genuinely novel in the bandit literature. While there is related work on multi-fidelity optimization in other domains (e.g., [7, 10]), these papers do not formalize or analyze regret in the bandit setting. The authors clearly distinguish their work from budgeted bandits [13, 15] and active learning with weak labelers [16]. The core insight—using lower fidelities to eliminate arms and reserving high fidelity for promising arms—is a fresh perspective on exploration-exploitation trade-offs. The paper significantly extends the bandit literature by introducing a new framework that is both theoretically sound and practically relevant.

**Significance** (Strong): The problem addressed is highly significant for applications where high-fidelity evaluations are expensive (e.g., online advertising, robotics, clinical trials). The results demonstrate that incorporating multi-fidelity information can lead to substantial improvements in regret bounds, especially when the number of promising arms is small. This represents a meaningful advancement over standard bandit approaches that ignore fidelity information. The paper advances the state of the art in bandit theory and provides a framework with clear potential for real-world impact.

**Weaknesses**:
1. The lower bound analysis reveals a gap for arms in K(m)7 where MF-UCB does not meet the lower bound. While the authors provide a reasonable explanation, it would strengthen the paper to propose a way to close this gap.
2. The experimental results are limited to synthetic problems. Including a real-world application would better demonstrate the practical relevance of the approach.
3. The paper assumes ζ(m) parameters (the bias between fidelities) are known, which may not be realistic in all applications. The authors briefly mention this limitation but don't explore how the algorithm would perform with estimated parameters.

**Overall Assessment**:
This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to bandit theory. The problem is well-motivated, the approach is novel and technically sound, and the results are well-supported by both theory and experiments. The paper advances the state of the art in bandit theory and has clear practical implications for applications where high-fidelity evaluations are expensive.

The paper meets all four NIPS criteria at a high level. It is well-suited for NIPS as it addresses a fundamental problem in online learning with clear theoretical contributions and practical relevance. The authors have carefully considered related work and clearly articulated how their approach differs from previous contributions.

**Pro/Con Summary**:
- *Pro*: Novel problem formulation in bandit literature, strong theoretical guarantees, well-motivated algorithm, clear writing, experimental results supporting theoretical claims
- *Con*: Limited to synthetic experiments (could include real-world application), assumes known ζ(m) parameters, gap between MF-UCB performance and lower bound for certain arms

The paper is a strong contribution to the field and deserves acceptance at NIPS. The minor weaknesses noted do not detract from the overall quality and significance of the work. The authors have made a meaningful advancement in bandit theory that will likely influence future research in exploration-exploitation trade-offs.
