

# Review: The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper introduces a novel multi-fidelity extension to the classical multi-armed bandit problem, where each arm has access to multiple approximations (fidelities) of its reward distribution with varying costs and accuracy. The authors formalize this setting by introducing a cost-weighted regret measure that accounts for both the regret incurred and the cost of playing at different fidelities. They propose the Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB) algorithm, which strategically uses cheaper lower-fidelity approximations to eliminate suboptimal arms before committing to expensive high-fidelity evaluations. The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis showing MF-UCB achieves regret O((λ(1)K + λ(2)|Kg|) log(Λ/λ(2))), which is superior to standard UCB when the cost of low fidelity (λ(1)) is significantly cheaper than high fidelity (λ(2)) and |Kg| < K. The authors also establish a matching lower bound, demonstrating near-optimality.

This work builds on the UCB framework but addresses a significant gap in the bandit literature: while multi-fidelity methods exist in other optimization contexts (e.g., Bayesian optimization), this is the first formal treatment of multi-fidelity bandits with regret analysis. The paper carefully distinguishes itself from related work on budgeted bandits and multi-fidelity optimization, acknowledging prior work on Gaussian process bandits (Kandasamy et al. [8]).

## Strengths

**Quality (High):** The paper is technically sound with a well-structured theoretical analysis. The authors clearly define the problem, make reasonable assumptions (including the regularity condition Assumption 1), and rigorously prove both upper and lower bounds. The regret bound for MF-UCB is derived with appropriate attention to the trade-off between exploration at low fidelity and exploitation at high fidelity. The empirical validation on synthetic problems convincingly demonstrates MF-UCB's superiority over standard UCB, with results aligning with the theoretical predictions.

**Clarity (High):** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem formulation is motivated with clear real-world examples (online advertising, algorithm selection), and the key concepts (like the partitioning of arms into K(m)) are well-explained with a helpful illustration (Figure 1). The mathematical notation is carefully defined, and the authors provide sufficient intuition for each technical component.

**Originality (High):** This is a genuinely novel contribution to bandit theory. The multi-fidelity bandit framework has not been previously formalized with regret analysis in the bandit literature. The MF-UCB algorithm represents a meaningful extension of UCB to handle multiple fidelities, and the authors clearly articulate how their work differs from related approaches. The paper makes a clear, significant contribution to the bandit literature.

**Significance (High):** The problem addressed is highly relevant to many practical applications where high-cost experiments have cheaper approximations (e.g., online advertising, algorithm selection, robotics). The theoretical results provide principled guidance on when multi-fidelity bandits can yield substantial improvements over standard bandits, and the empirical results validate this in practice. The paper identifies important future directions, including handling cases where low-fidelity evaluations don't yield rewards (e.g., simulations in robotics).

## Weaknesses

**Minor Theoretical Limitation:** The lower bound is proven only for Bernoulli distributions, which may limit the generalizability of the lower bound to other distribution classes. While the upper bound is more general, the gap between the lower bound and MF-UCB performance for certain arms (K(m)7) is noted as an open problem, which is a reasonable acknowledgment of limitations.

**Practical Assumption:** The analysis assumes known ζ(m) values (the bias between fidelities), which may not be realistic in practice. The paper could have briefly discussed how to estimate ζ(m) from data or acknowledged this as a limitation.

## Overall Assessment

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to bandit theory. The multi-fidelity bandit framework is both novel and well-motivated, the theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-executed, and the empirical results confirm the theoretical insights. The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly positions itself within the broader literature.

The paper meets all NIPS criteria at a high level:
- **Quality:** Strong theoretical foundation with well-supported claims
- **Clarity:** Exceptional organization and writing
- **Originality:** Truly novel contribution to bandit theory
- **Significance:** Addresses a practical problem with meaningful theoretical and empirical results

While the assumption of known ζ(m) is a minor limitation, it's a reasonable starting point for the theoretical analysis, and the authors acknowledge the practical challenges in the conclusion. The paper's contributions are substantial enough that this limitation doesn't detract from the overall quality.

## Recommendation

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. It makes a clear, important contribution to the bandit literature that advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way. The paper is well-written, theoretically sound, and addresses a relevant problem with significant practical implications. The work is clearly distinguished from previous contributions, and the analysis provides valuable theoretical insights that will be useful to both researchers and practitioners working with bandit problems in settings with multiple fidelity evaluations.

The paper's contribution is not just a technical extension but represents a meaningful conceptual advance in how we think about exploration-exploitation trade-offs when cheaper approximations are available. This is exactly the kind of work that NIPS should be highlighting.
