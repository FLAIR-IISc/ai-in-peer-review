Summary
This paper introduces a novel multi-fidelity stochastic K-armed bandit setting where each arm has M fidelities: lower fidelities are cheaper but biased approximations of the highest-fidelity reward. The authors propose a cost-weighted regret notion for a capital budget Λ, comparing to an oracle that always plays an optimal arm (at any fidelity). They develop MF-UCB, which maintains UCBs at each fidelity augmented by the known bias bounds ζ(m), takes the minimum across fidelities for arm selection, and chooses which fidelity to query via a thresholding rule γ(m) that trades off cost and informativeness. They prove upper bounds on cost-weighted regret showing that lower fidelities can eliminate many suboptimal arms so that expensive plays are concentrated on a small subset near-optimal arms. They also provide a lower bound (for Bernoulli rewards) and show MF-UCB is near-optimal on a broad subset of arms, with a characterized gap on a specific class. Synthetic experiments show gains over naïve UCB that always plays at the highest fidelity.

Relation to prior work
- Builds on UCB and minimax regret analyses (Auer et al.; Bubeck & Cesa-Bianchi).
- Different from budgeted/bandits-with-knapsacks/budgeted MAB (e.g., Tran-Thanh et al.; Xia et al.), where each arm has reward and cost per pull and the aim is reward-per-cost; here multiple fidelities of the same arm provide biased signals about a single target reward.
- Related in spirit to multi-fidelity optimization and BO (Huang et al.; Kandasamy et al.) and to weak/strong labelers in active learning (Zhang & Chaudhuri), but those do not analyze bandit-style regret with exploration-exploitation under cost and bias.
- Distinct from side-observation or correlated-arm bandits: lower fidelities are biased proxies with known bias envelopes ζ(m), not unbiased side observations.

Strengths
- Novel and timely formalization of multi-fidelity bandits with a clear, principled cost-weighted regret definition. This is a meaningful new direction with many applications (ads, algorithm selection, simulators).
- Elegant algorithmic idea: combine per-fidelity UCBs with bias corrections and a fidelity-switch rule derived from cost/information tradeoffs; the Bk,t = minm B(m)k,t construction is clean and intuitive.
- Theoretical analysis is substantial: upper bounds that reveal how arms are partitioned by fidelity-dependent difficulty; high-probability control of plays across fidelities; lower bound that matches the upper bound for a large subset of arms and explicitly articulates where a gap remains.
- Clear qualitative message: low fidelities eliminate many arms cheaply; expensive plays are focused on near-optimal arms; regret scales with λ(JkK) at the “last informative” fidelity. This is both insightful and practically useful.

Weaknesses and questions
- Assumes known bias envelopes ζ(m) and concentration function ψ. In many applications ζ(m) is unknown or only approximately bounded. How sensitive is MF-UCB to misspecification? Can ζ(m) be learned on-the-fly or handled robustly (e.g., via inflated confidence radii)?
- Assumption 1 (regularity on ζ decay) is important for the clean comparisons; while discussed, more guidance on when it holds and what happens if it fails (beyond “less appealing”) would help. Empirical ablations varying ζ and λ would be valuable.
- Experiments are limited to synthetic settings and compare only to highest-fidelity UCB. Stronger empirical validation (e.g., simulated ad-click or algorithm-selection tasks) and comparisons to reasonable heuristics (e.g., pre-screening via lower fidelity then UCB at the top fidelity; successive elimination adapted to costs; simple budget splitting) would strengthen the case.
- The regret notion rewards plays at low fidelity as if they achieved µ(M), scaled by cost. While the paper explains the rationale, some applications may prefer alternative objectives (e.g., no immediate reward from low-fidelity plays). A brief empirical study of alternate regret definitions (mentioned in the conclusion) would increase applicability.
- The lower bound is proved for Bernoulli rewards. It would be good to comment on extension to general sub-Gaussian or to the ψ-based setting assumed in the upper bound.
- Some dependence on parameters (ρ, γ(m)) could be clarified, and guidance on their practical tuning would help.

Quality
- Generally technically sound with careful high-level proofs and reasonable assumptions for theory. The upper bound proof strategy (arm partitioning, fidelity-wise control of play counts, controlling N vs nΛ) is convincing. The characterization of the gap in the lower bound is refreshingly honest. The experiments support the claims but are relatively light; additional baselines and sensitivity analyses would improve confidence.

Clarity
- Writing is clear overall and well organized; the intuition for the algorithm and partition K(m) is helpful. Some parts (e.g., Assumption 1 motivation, choice and interpretation of γ(m), the residual term r̃) could be further clarified. A short pseudocode box would be welcome, though the algorithm steps are already outlined. Minor typos exist.

Originality
- High. The formulation, regret definition, and algorithmic treatment for multi-fidelity bandits with biased proxies and costs seem new, distinct from budgeted MAB and multi-fidelity BO, and go beyond simple combinations of known techniques.

Significance
- High. This opens a line of work on structured, cost-aware bandits with biased proxies, relevant across ML systems and experimental design. The theory offers actionable insights and near-optimality guarantees. I expect follow-on work (e.g., learning ζ(m), contextual variants, GP-based settings) to build on this.

Pros
- Novel problem formulation and regret notion
- Clean, principled algorithm with strong intuition
- Nontrivial theoretical results (upper and lower bounds), near-optimal on a broad class
- Practical insight: cheap fidelities to prune, expensive plays on few arms

Cons
- Requires known bias envelopes; sensitivity not studied
- Limited empirical evaluation and baselines
- Lower bound limited to Bernoulli; gap for K(m)7 not closed
- Assumption 1 restrictiveness could be discussed more

Minor comments
- Fix typos (e.g., “th Conference…”, “Schenider” → “Schneider”). Ensure all references are complete.
- Provide guidance on choosing ρ and handling ψ in practice (e.g., sub-Gaussian case).

Recommendation
Accept. This is a bold, original contribution that opens an important new direction in bandit research with a well-motivated algorithm and solid theory. Despite some experimental limitations and assumptions (known ζ), the conceptual advance and analysis merit inclusion.
