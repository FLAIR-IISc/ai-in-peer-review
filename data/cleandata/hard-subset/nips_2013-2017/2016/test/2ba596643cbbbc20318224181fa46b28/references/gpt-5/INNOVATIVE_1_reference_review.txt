Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper introduces a novel formalization of stochastic K-armed bandits with multiple fidelities per arm. Each fidelity m provides a biased, cheaper proxy for the highest-fidelity reward, with known bias bounds ζ(m) and costs λ(m) increasing with fidelity. The authors define a cost-weighted regret under a capital budget Λ that evaluates instantaneous reward at the highest fidelity regardless of which fidelity is sampled—motivated by settings like advertising where the long-term outcome is what ultimately matters. They propose MF-UCB, which maintains per-fidelity UCBs corrected by ζ(m) and uses the minimum across fidelities as the arm index. Upon selecting an arm, the algorithm chooses the fidelity via a threshold rule γ(m) that trades off cost and the informativeness/bias of lower fidelities. Theoretical results include an upper bound showing MF-UCB is near-optimal and improves over plain UCB by using cheaper fidelities to eliminate obviously suboptimal arms, plus a lower bound (for Bernoulli rewards and under a mild regularity assumption) that MF-UCB matches up to a gap on a particular subset of arms. Simulations on synthetic problems support the theory.

Relative to prior work: it is distinct from budgeted/knapsack bandits (reward-cost ratios), from multi-fidelity optimization in BO (e.g., multi-fidelity GP bandits), and from active learning with weak/strong labelers. It builds on classical UCB analyses (Auer et al., Bubeck & Cesa-Bianchi) but the multi-fidelity structure, biased proxies, and cost-sensitive regret are substantively new for the K-armed setting. The lower/upper bounds are in the spirit of standard bandit theory but adapted to this new structure.

Strengths:
- Novel and timely problem formulation: biased, cost-tiered proxies are common in practice (ads, AutoML, sims vs. real-world).
- Clean, interpretable algorithm: taking the min across bias-corrected UCBs is simple and principled; fidelity selection via γ(m) is intuitive and cost-aware.
- Solid theory: the regret bound shows a clear advantage over UCB when a small subset of arms survives low-fidelity screening and when λ(1) ≪ λ(M). The control of total plays under budget is handled carefully. The lower bound clarifies where near-optimality holds and explicitly identifies a gap subset.
- Honest discussion of limitations and open problems (e.g., K(m)7 gap), and clear conditions (concentration via ψ, regularity Assumption 1).
- Simulations align with the predicted behavior (cheap filtering of bad arms, expensive plays concentrated on near-optimal arms).

Weaknesses and questions:
- Knowledge of ζ(m): The algorithm and guarantees rely on known, fidelity-specific bias envelopes. In many real applications, ζ(m) is unknown or arm-dependent. How sensitive is MF-UCB to mis-specified ζ(m)? Can ζ(m) be learned or estimated online with guarantees (e.g., robust worst-case bounds, meta-level confidence sets for cross-fidelity coupling)?
- Assumption 1 (regularity on ζ(m) decay vs ψ): The performance gains are “most appealing” under this assumption. What degrades when it fails? Please include a discussion or example illustrating behavior when ζ(m) decays slowly and λ(m+1)≈λ(m).
- Lower bound scope: Proven for Bernoulli rewards and strategies satisfying Assumption 3; while common in bandit lower bounds, the specialization could be discussed more. Is there an extension beyond Bernoulli (e.g., sub-Gaussian via KL or ψ)?
- Regret definition: Counting reward at the highest fidelity even when sampling low fidelity is appropriate for some domains (ads), but others may prefer fidelity-specific utility or partial credit. While the conclusion mentions alternatives, an ablation or short appendix analyzing the algorithm’s behavior under µ(mt)It as qt would strengthen the paper.
- Experimental evaluation: Currently only synthetic, with limited baselines. Missing:
  - Comparisons against more baselines: (i) UCB on the top fidelity with pre-screening heuristics; (ii) Thompson sampling variants; (iii) budgeted/knapsack bandit baselines where applicable; (iv) multi-fidelity heuristics (e.g., fixed schedule).
  - Stress tests on K, λ-ratios, ζ-profiles, and mis-specification of ζ(m).
  - A realistic case study (ads-like click model, AutoML/algorithm selection, or robotics sim-to-real) to showcase impact.
- Algorithmic details: MF-UCB uses ψ and ψ−1, requiring knowledge of concentration. In practice, do you instantiate with Hoeffding (ψ(x)∝x^2) and set ρ? Please clarify initialization, tie-breaking, and computational cost. A brief pseudocode with a concrete ψ would aid reproducibility.
- Gap on K(m)7: The paper explains why the current switching rule can miss a cheaper disambiguation at a lower fidelity when gaps are small but costs differ sharply. It would be valuable to propose and test a heuristic that occasionally “peeks” longer at lower fidelities when λ(`)/λ(m) is large, while keeping theoretical guarantees as an open problem.

Quality:
- Theoretical development appears technically sound and well-motivated. Proof sketches are plausible; key lemmas align with UCB-style analyses adapted to multiple fidelities. Assumptions are explicit, and the open gap is clearly stated.

Clarity:
- Generally clear and well-organized. Some notation is heavy; a compact algorithm box with a concrete ψ would help. Minor typos and formatting glitches (e.g., “th Conference” header) should be fixed.

Originality:
- High. The multi-fidelity K-armed abstraction with biased proxies and a cost-weighted regret, plus MF-UCB and associated bounds, represent a meaningful new direction beyond standard bandits and budgeted variants.

Significance:
- High potential impact. The conceptual framework and algorithm are likely to spur follow-up work (e.g., learning ζ, TS variants, contextual extensions, nonstationary fidelities) and have practical relevance in domains with cheap proxies.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add robustness experiments for ζ mis-specification and ablations over λ, ζ profiles.
- Include at least one real or realistic case study and additional baselines.
- Provide a concrete instantiation with sub-Gaussian Hoeffding bounds, giving full algorithmic details.
- Discuss avenues to estimate ζ(m) online and heuristics to mitigate the K(m)7 gap.

Recommendation: Accept (clear novelty and a compelling new direction outweigh limitations in experiments and assumptions). 

Confidence: High.
