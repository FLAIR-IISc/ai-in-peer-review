Summary
The paper introduces a novel stochastic K-armed bandit setting where each arm can be queried at multiple fidelities, each with its own cost and a known bias bound relative to the highest-fidelity reward. The authors propose a cost-aware notion of pseudo-regret that values performance with respect to the highest-fidelity reward while accounting for the cost of each play. They design MF-UCB, an adaptation of UCB that maintains upper confidence bounds at each fidelity per arm and chooses both an arm and a fidelity via a principled switching rule governed by thresholds γ(m) derived from costs, bias bounds ζ(m), and concentration ψ. The theory shows that MF-UCB uses cheap fidelities to eliminate clearly suboptimal arms and reserves expensive queries for a small set near the optimum. They derive an instance-dependent regret bound that is better than standard UCB when lower fidelities are informative and cheaper, and a lower bound that matches the upper bound on a large subset of instances (with a precisely characterized gap). Simulations on synthetic problems corroborate the main qualitative claims.

Relation to prior work
The setup differs from budgeted MAB (which optimizes reward/cost ratio per arm) and from multi-fidelity RL/active learning in that lower fidelities are biased approximations to the same underlying arm with known bias bounds. The work is also distinct from multi-fidelity Bayesian optimization (co-kriging) as it focuses on bandit regret and arm selection rather than continuous optimization. The algorithm builds on classical UCB but the fidelity-selection mechanism and the cost-weighted regret are new.

Strengths
- Novel problem formulation: a crisp, practically motivated multi-fidelity bandit model with a clear cost-aware regret definition that privileges long-run (highest-fidelity) value.
- Simple, principled algorithm: MF-UCB’s min-over-fidelities UCB and the γ(m) switching rule are intuitive and easy to implement; the design aligns with the bounded-bias structure.
- Strong theory: instance-dependent upper bound that cleanly decomposes cost spent per fidelity and shows improvements over single-fidelity UCB when most arms are clearly suboptimal at lower fidelities. The lower bound (for Bernoulli) matches on a broad class (K(m)3) and carefully explains the remaining gap (K(m)7), with a thoughtful discussion of why closing it is nontrivial.
- Clarity of intuition: the partition K(1),…,K(M) gives a compelling picture of how MF-UCB prunes arms fidelity-by-fidelity.
- Empirical confirmation that the slope of regret vs. capital improves, consistent with theory.

Weaknesses and points for improvement
- Reliance on known bias bounds ζ(m): In many applications ζ(m) may be unknown or hard to calibrate. The paper would benefit from a discussion or experiments on misspecification (robustness when ζ is over/under-estimated) and possible adaptive estimation schemes.
- Assumption 1 (decay condition) is somewhat ad hoc; the algorithm is analyzed without it but the sharpest benefits rely on it. Clarify practical regimes where it holds and what happens when it is violated.
- Limited baselines and experiments: comparisons are only to standard UCB. Stronger baselines include (i) a two-stage elimination that uses low fidelity with bias-aware CIs then runs high-fidelity UCB on survivors, (ii) UCB-V/Thompson Sampling variants adapted to cost, and (iii) simple rules that always use low-fidelity until a fixed CI width. Real-data or semi-synthetic studies would strengthen the case.
- Regret definition: while well-motivated for ads/long-run CTR, it is nonstandard and may be controversial in other domains; a more extensive discussion of alternative definitions and when they are appropriate would help. The paper briefly mentions alternatives in the conclusion; expanding this would be useful.
- Lower bound scope: restricted to Bernoulli with µ* > 1/2 and requires Assumption 3. It is fine as a first step, but discussing how the lower bound extends to general sub-Gaussian (or general ψ) would be valuable.
- Exposition: some notational density and minor clarity issues (e.g., references to Table 1/Fig. 1 without full context, constants elided in main text). A short, worked example illustrating γ(m) and K(m) construction would aid readability.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is MF-UCB to mis-specification of ζ(m)? Can you provide bounds or experiments where ζ(m) is only approximately known or learned online?
- Could γ(m) be adapted from data (e.g., via empirical variance and empirical bias estimates) while preserving guarantees?
- What is the behavior when M is large or fidelities are near-continuous? Can the switching rule be made continuous in cost?
- Can the lower bound be generalized beyond Bernoulli to general ψ with similar matching (up to constants)?
- Implementation details: guidance on choosing ρ; complexity and memory for large K and M.

Pros for acceptance
- Introduces a timely, novel formalization of multi-fidelity bandits with a clear, useful regret notion.
- Elegant algorithm with strong, near-matching theory and clear intuitions.
- Potentially impactful for domains with cheap proxies (ads, simulation-to-real), opening a direction for further work.

Cons for acceptance
- Empirical section is thin and lacks stronger baselines/realistic scenarios.
- Practical reliance on known bias bounds; robustness not demonstrated.
- Some theoretical assumptions (Assumption 1, Bernoulli lower bound scope) limit generality.

Overall evaluation
This paper proposes a bold and genuinely new direction for bandits, with a clean algorithm and substantive theory. Despite limited experiments and some practical assumptions (ζ known), the conceptual contribution is significant and likely to spur follow-on work (including the cited GP extension). I lean to accept.
