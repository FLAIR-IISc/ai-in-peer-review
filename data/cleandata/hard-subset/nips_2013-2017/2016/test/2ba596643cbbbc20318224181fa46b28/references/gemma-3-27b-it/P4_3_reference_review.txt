The paper introduces a multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit problem, where obtaining rewards for arms comes with varying costs and accuracies. The authors propose a Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB) algorithm and provide a theoretical analysis of its performance, demonstrating regret bounds better than a naive UCB approach when lower fidelities are cheaper and provide useful information. The contributions are a novel formalism, a new algorithm, and a corresponding theoretical analysis including a lower bound.

**Summary & Relation to Prior Work:**

The work builds upon the well-established K-armed bandit framework (Lai & Robbins, 1985; Auer, 2002) and UCB algorithms (Auer, 2002; Bubeck & Cesa-Bianchi, 2012). It extends this framework by introducing multiple fidelities for each arm, acknowledging that cheaper, lower-fidelity evaluations can inform the exploration-exploitation trade-off. This concept relates to work in multi-fidelity optimization (Huang et al., 2006; Rajnarayan et al., 2006) but distinguishes itself by providing a regret-based analysis – a core aspect of bandit problems – absent in those optimization settings.  Connections to active learning with weak and strong labelers (Zhang & Chaudhuri, 2016) are noted, but the authors emphasize the difference in objective – exploring exploration-exploitation versus labeling efficiency. The work also differentiates itself from budgeted bandit problems (Tran-Thanh et al., 2013; Xia et al., 2015) where the focus is on reward/cost ratios rather than fidelity levels as approximations.  Recent work by Kandasamy et al. (2016) on multi-fidelity bandits with Gaussian process payoffs is also acknowledged.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clear Problem Formulation:** The problem of multi-fidelity bandits is well-motivated and clearly defined, with a realistic scenario presented (e.g., online advertising). The cost-weighted regret notion is appropriate for this setting.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a theoretical analysis with a regret bound for the MF-UCB algorithm. The proof sketches are reasonably detailed, indicating a solid understanding of the underlying mathematical concepts. The inclusion of a lower bound strengthens the theoretical contribution.
*   **Algorithmic Insight:** The MF-UCB algorithm provides a practical approach to leveraging multiple fidelities, balancing exploration and exploitation by intelligently allocating resources to different fidelity levels. The switching criterion based on γ(m) appears well-reasoned.
*   **Empirical Validation:** Simulations demonstrate the performance improvement of MF-UCB over the naive UCB approach, bolstering the theoretical findings.
* **Connections to existing work:** The paper provides good and thorough connections to the existing literature.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Assumptions:** The assumptions, particularly Assumption 1 regarding the decay of ζ(m), could be restrictive. While the authors acknowledge this, the impact of violating this assumption on the algorithm's performance isn't fully explored.
*   **Lower Bound Gap:** The authors acknowledge a gap between their regret bound and the proposed lower bound. While they provide insights into the reason for this gap, closing it would significantly strengthen the theoretical contribution.
*   **Clarity in Proof Sketches:** While reasonably detailed, the proof sketches could benefit from more clarity and precision in certain areas, especially regarding the probabilistic bounds and the handling of the capital constraint (Λ).  More detail regarding the constant factors hidden in the asymptotic notation would be useful.
* **Limited Simulation scope:** While the simulations demonstrate the benefits of the MF-UCB algorithm, they are limited to synthetic problems. Experiments on real-world datasets would provide a more compelling validation.
* **γ(m) choice justification**: While the authors offer a motivation for the choice of γ(m), a more rigorous justification relating it to the specific problem parameters and the desired trade-off would enhance understanding.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novelty: The multi-fidelity bandit problem is a new and interesting extension of the classic bandit framework.
*   Theoretical Soundness: The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis with a regret bound and a lower bound.
*   Potential Impact: The proposed algorithm has the potential to improve performance in various real-world applications where multi-fidelity evaluations are available.
*   Clarity: The paper is generally well-written and organized, making it accessible to researchers in the field.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Restrictive Assumptions: The assumptions may limit the applicability of the algorithm in certain scenarios.
*   Gap in Lower Bound: The gap between the regret bound and the lower bound weakens the theoretical strength of the paper.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of bandit optimization. The problem formulation is novel and well-motivated, and the proposed algorithm is supported by a solid theoretical analysis and empirical validation. While the assumptions and the gap in the lower bound are weaknesses, the overall strengths of the paper outweigh these concerns. The work is significant, and the ideas presented are likely to be of interest to the NIPS community.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** The authors should address the weaknesses related to the assumptions and the lower bound gap, and provide more detailed explanations in the proof sketches. Expanding the simulation study with real-world datasets would also be beneficial.
