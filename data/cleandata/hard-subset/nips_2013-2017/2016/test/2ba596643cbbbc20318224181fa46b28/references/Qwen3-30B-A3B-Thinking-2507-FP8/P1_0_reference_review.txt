

# Peer Review: "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

## Summary

This paper introduces a novel extension to the classical multi-armed bandit problem by incorporating multiple fidelities (approximations) for each arm, where each fidelity has a different cost and accuracy level. The authors formalize a new notion of regret that accounts for the cost of different fidelities, propose the Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB) algorithm, and provide theoretical guarantees showing that MF-UCB achieves better regret than standard UCB algorithms. The paper builds on the classic UCB framework [3,9] and extends it to handle multi-fidelity settings, distinguishing itself from related work on budgeted bandits [13,15] and multi-fidelity optimization [7,10]. The authors also establish a lower bound showing MF-UCB is near-optimal in this setting.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Soundness and Novelty**: The paper makes a clear theoretical contribution by formalizing the multi-fidelity bandit problem and providing a rigorous analysis of MF-UCB. The problem formulation is well-motivated with practical examples (online advertising, algorithm selection), and the theoretical results (Theorems 2 and 4) are carefully developed with proofs in the appendix. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work, particularly by formalizing regret in the multi-fidelity setting.

2. **Theoretical Contribution**: The regret bound in Theorem 2 demonstrates that MF-UCB can achieve significantly better performance than UCB when lower fidelities are sufficiently cheaper and the set of promising arms (K(M)) is small. The lower bound in Theorem 4 shows that MF-UCB is near-optimal, with the only gap being for arms that are "misleading" at lower fidelities (K(m)₇).

3. **Clarity and Organization**: The paper is well-written, logically structured, and clearly explains the intuition behind the algorithm. The introduction effectively motivates the problem, the formalism is carefully developed, and the empirical results (Figure 2) support the theoretical claims.

4. **Practical Relevance**: The work has clear applications in domains where expensive experiments have cheaper approximations (e.g., online advertising, algorithm selection, robotics), making it valuable for practitioners.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Experimental Validation**: The empirical results are confined to synthetic problems, which is understandable for a theoretical paper, but real-world experiments would strengthen the significance. The paper mentions "other settings" (gold mining robots, clinical trials) but doesn't provide any empirical evidence for these applications.

2. **Assumption Limitations**: The analysis assumes ζ(m) (the bias between fidelities) is known, which may be restrictive in practice. The paper doesn't discuss how to estimate these parameters or handle unknown ζ(m), which is a significant practical concern.

3. **Clarity of Algorithm Design**: While the algorithm is well-described, the motivation for the specific choice of γ(m) (Equation 4) could be more intuitive. The connection between γ(m) and the regret tradeoff could be made clearer.

4. **Limited Discussion of Practical Implementation**: The paper doesn't address how the algorithm would be implemented in practice, particularly how to handle the transition between fidelities when the optimal fidelity isn't known in advance.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis supported by proofs in the appendix. The claims are well-supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical results on synthetic data. The authors are careful to discuss limitations (e.g., the gap between MF-UCB and the lower bound for K(m)₇ arms) and provide conditions under which the theoretical guarantees hold. The paper is a complete piece of work rather than a position paper.

## Clarity

The paper is well-written and organized, making the technical content accessible. The notation is consistent, and the key concepts (fidelities, regret definition, partitioning of arms) are clearly explained. The figures (particularly Figure 1) help illustrate the partitioning of arms across fidelities. Some minor improvements could be made to the motivation for γ(m) and the connection between theory and practice.

## Originality

This work is highly original in its formalization of the multi-fidelity bandit problem and development of MF-UCB. While there has been work on multi-fidelity optimization in other contexts [7,10,16], this is the first to formalize regret in the bandit setting and provide theoretical guarantees. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work, particularly budgeted bandits [13,15] and the robotics work [6], by focusing on exploration-exploitation trade-offs in a multi-fidelity setting.

## Significance

The problem addressed is important for applications where experiments have multiple fidelity levels at different costs. The theoretical results show that MF-UCB can achieve significantly better regret than standard UCB, with the potential for large gains when lower fidelities are substantially cheaper. The paper advances the state of the art in bandit theory and provides a practical framework for using multiple fidelities. While the paper is limited to synthetic experiments, the theoretical insights are valuable for practitioners working in domains with multi-fidelity evaluations.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The work makes a significant theoretical contribution to the bandit literature with a novel problem formulation, a well-designed algorithm, and rigorous theoretical analysis. The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and addresses an important problem with practical relevance. The minor weaknesses (limited experimental validation, assumption limitations) are common in theoretical papers and do not detract from the overall contribution.

**Pro acceptance:**
- Novel problem formulation for multi-fidelity bandits
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with regret bound and lower bound
- Clear algorithm design with intuitive motivation
- Empirical results supporting theoretical claims
- Strong connection to practical applications

**Con acceptance:**
- Limited to synthetic experiments (would benefit from real-world applications)
- Assumes known ζ(m) parameters (practical limitation)
- Could improve motivation for γ(m) choice
- Limited discussion of practical implementation details

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the bandit literature and would be a strong addition to NIPS. The theoretical insights are significant and could guide future work in multi-fidelity optimization.
