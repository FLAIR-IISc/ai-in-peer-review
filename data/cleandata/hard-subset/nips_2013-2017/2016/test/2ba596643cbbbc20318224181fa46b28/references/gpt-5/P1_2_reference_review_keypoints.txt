- Formalizes multi-fidelity stochastic K-armed bandits where fidelities have different costs and known bias bounds ζ(m).
- Defines a capital-constrained pseudo-regret that credits the high-fidelity mean µ(M) regardless of queried fidelity and subtracts fidelity costs.
- Proposes MF-UCB: adds ζ(m) to UCBs to bound µ(M) and adaptively selects fidelity via thresholds γ(m) trading cost vs. informativeness.
- Proves an upper bound scaling like O((λ(1)K + Σm λ(m)|K(m)|) log(Λ/λ(M))) with arms partitioned by fidelity informativeness.
- Shows MF-UCB can cheaply eliminate many bad arms and reserve expensive evaluations for a small set of promising arms.
- Provides a Bernoulli lower bound matching the upper bound for most arms and identifies a gap for arms where low fidelities are only mildly informative.
- Theoretical analysis and algorithm construction (confidence bounds and γ(m) choices) are technically sound and well motivated.
- Requires known ζ(m), concentration ψ (e.g., sub-Gaussian), monotone costs, and a regularity condition (Assumption 1) that may be strong in practice.
- Assumption 1 (fast-decaying ζ) is important for strongest gains; its realism varies by domain.
- Lower bound is currently restricted to Bernoulli rewards; extension to general sub-Gaussian families is missing.
- Experiments are synthetic and qualitatively support theory but are limited in scope, baselines, sensitivity analyses, and lack real-world data.
- Paper is well organized with clear intuition and proof sketches, though notation is heavy and some parts need clarification.
- Needs more discussion on why the regret credits µ(M) regardless of fidelity and comparisons to alternative regret definitions.
- Practical considerations (estimating ζ(m), ψ, cost robustness, and robustness to misspecification) need more emphasis and analysis.
- Novelty: new finite-armed multi-fidelity bandit formulation, MF-UCB algorithm, and near-optimal lower bound characterization.
- Significance: addresses realistic settings with cheaper proxies and provides theory likely to influence follow-up methods.
- Pros: principled formulation, simple implementable algorithm, insightful arm partitioning, and near-optimal guarantees.
- Cons: reliance on known ζ/ψ, strong Assumption 1, Bernoulli-only lower bound, limited experiments, and potentially limited applicability of the regret notion.
- Open questions: sensitivity to misspecified ζ/ψ, online adaptation of γ(m), comparisons to UCB-V/Thompson/racing methods, extending lower bound to sub-Gaussian, and real-world case studies.
- Suggestions: run experiments varying ζ decay and cost ratios, add stronger baselines and sensitivity analyses, provide guidance for estimating ζ(m), include full pseudo-code and constants, and expand discussion of alternative regret notions.
- Recommendation: Weak accept—timely, elegant, and theoretically solid but empirically limited and reliant on known ζ(m).
- Reviewer confidence: High.
