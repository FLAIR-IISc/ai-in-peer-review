Summary and relation to prior work
The paper introduces a stochastic K-armed bandit setting where each arm admits M fidelities: lower fidelities are cheaper but provide biased approximations of the highest-fidelity reward. The authors formalize a cost-weighted notion of regret under a total capital budget Λ, where the instantaneous “pseudo-reward” is always taken to be the highest-fidelity mean µ(M) of the played arm, while the incurred cost depends on the fidelity chosen. They assume known fidelity-specific bias bounds ζ(m) and costs λ(m) with λ(1) < … < λ(M), and sub-exponential concentration governed by a function ψ.

They propose MF-UCB, which maintains per-fidelity UCBs on µ(M) by adding ζ(m) to the empirical mean confidence radius, and uses the minimum across fidelities as the arm index. After choosing the arm with maximal index, the algorithm selects the lowest fidelity whose uncertainty is still above a threshold γ(m) that balances the cost ratio λ(m)/λ(m+1) and bias ζ(m). The analysis gives an upper bound on regret showing that, roughly, suboptimal arms are mostly eliminated at cheaper fidelities, reserving expensive evaluations for a small set of near-optimal arms. A lower bound (for Bernoulli rewards) shows MF-UCB is near-optimal on a large subset of arms, with a gap identified for a certain class where small positive gaps at lower fidelities make it information-theoretically preferable to eliminate at a cheaper level.

This work builds on classical UCB [Auer et al., Agrawal; Bubeck & Cesa-Bianchi tutorial] and lower-bound techniques (à la Lai & Robbins). It differs from budgeted/bandits-with-knapsacks settings, where the goal is to optimize reward per cost or respect resource constraints; here, multiple fidelities serve as biased proxies for a single ground-truth reward. It also complements multi-fidelity surrogate modeling (e.g., co-kriging, engineering design) and recent RL work using simulators, which do not provide bandit-style regret analyses.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The algorithmic design is principled: the per-fidelity UCBs with bias offsets yield valid upper bounds on µ(M) and the fidelity-switching threshold γ(m) is derived to trade off cost and bias. The regret upper bound decomposes over arms via a partition K(m) defined by fidelity-specific proximity to optimality, aligning with the algorithm’s behavior. The lower bound identifies when elimination at cheaper fidelities is information-theoretically preferable and pinpoints the subcases where MF-UCB is suboptimal due to its conservative switching rule. Assumptions and concentration are handled via a general ψ, with concrete instantiations for sub-Gaussian/Bernoulli.
- Completeness: The upper bound scales as O(((λ(1)K + λ(M)|Kg|) log(Λ/λ(M))) in the idealized case, and a lower bound is provided under Assumption 3. The proofs are outlined with lemmas controlling per-fidelity pulls and total plays under capital. The analysis is careful about the residual term and anytime nature.
- Empirics: Synthetic experiments corroborate theory, showing fewer expensive pulls on clearly suboptimal arms. However, baselines are limited to top-fidelity UCB; missing ablations and comparisons reduce empirical persuasiveness.

Clarity
The problem formulation, notation, and algorithm are mostly clear, though the exposition is dense. The K(m) partition and the role of ζ(m), γ(m), and ψ could benefit from more intuition and a running example. Some editorial issues and figure references suggest polishing is needed. The choice of pseudo-reward qt = µ(M)It is discussed but deserves further justification and alternatives.

Originality
The multi-fidelity, biased-proxy bandit formulation with a capital-weighted regret is novel and timely. The fidelity selection rule derived from costs and bias bounds is an original contribution, and the lower bound analysis highlights a nontrivial optimality gap in a clear subclass.

Significance
The framework is relevant to domains where cheap but biased proxies exist (ads, algorithm selection, simulators). The theory suggests substantial savings when many arms are suboptimal and only a small subset is near-optimal. The requirement of known per-fidelity bias bounds is a strong practical limitation; addressing this would greatly increase impact. Still, the conceptual and theoretical contributions are significant and likely to spur follow-up work.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Clear and compelling formalization of multi-fidelity bandits with biased proxies and cost-aware regret.
- Principled algorithm with interpretable fidelity-switching criterion.
- Nontrivial regret upper bound showing cost savings and arm-wise behavior; near-optimal lower bound with well-explained gap.
- General concentration framework via ψ; covers sub-Gaussian/Bernoulli.
- Simulations align with theoretical predictions.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Assumes known global bias bounds ζ(m) shared across arms; practicality questionable. No method to estimate/adapt ζ(m).
- Lower bound optimality gap for K(m)7 cases remains open; MF-UCB may misallocate effort at higher fidelities.
- Limited empirical evaluation: only synthetic data, and baseline restricted to top-fidelity UCB. No ablations on γ(m) or sensitivity to misspecified ζ(m).
- The regret notion qt = µ(M)It, while motivated, may not match some applications (e.g., simulations with no “real” reward); broader discussion is brief.

Suggestions for improvement
- Discuss and/or propose an adaptive variant that estimates ζ(m) online (confidence sets for bias) or uses arm-specific bias bounds; analyze robustness to misspecification.
- Provide KL-based bounds where possible (e.g., for Bernoulli) to tighten constants and align with Lai–Robbins style lower bounds; clarify the role and necessity of Assumption 3.
- Expand empirical section: include heuristic multi-fidelity baselines (e.g., successive elimination using low fidelities; budget-aware elimination), Thompson Sampling variants with multiple fidelities, and sensitivity analyses to ζ(m) misspecification and cost ratios. Report per-fidelity pull counts and arm partitions.
- Clarify the applicability of the regret definition and contrast with alternative objectives (e.g., reward-at-fidelity, best-arm identification with multi-fidelity).
- Improve exposition of the K(m) partition and the intuition behind Assumption 1; add a concrete numerical example tracing decisions across fidelities.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers a well-motivated problem formulation, a principled algorithm, and solid theory with insightful lower bounds. The practicality of known bias bounds and the limited experiments are drawbacks, but the contribution is novel and likely to influence future work on multi-fidelity bandits.
