This paper introduces a multi-fidelity bandit framework, addressing a practical challenge in scenarios where evaluating arms is costly and cheaper approximations are available. The core idea – leveraging lower-fidelity data to prune suboptimal arms and focus expensive evaluations – is compelling and represents a novel perspective on the classic bandit problem. The authors formulate a cost-weighted regret and present an algorithm, MF-UCB, with a theoretical regret bound demonstrating its advantage over standard UCB in certain regimes. This work builds naturally upon existing UCB methods but introduces significant new algorithmic and theoretical challenges, successfully addressed by the authors.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The multi-fidelity bandit setting is a genuinely new framing of the problem, with direct relevance to numerous applications like online advertising, algorithm selection, and robotics. This isn’t merely an incremental improvement; it’s a conceptual shift.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The regret analysis is well-developed, providing a clear understanding of the algorithm’s performance and demonstrating its potential benefits over naive UCB. The assumptions, while requiring careful consideration, are clearly stated.
*   **Intuitive Algorithm:** MF-UCB is a logical extension of the UCB algorithm, making it relatively easy to understand and implement. The adaptive fidelity selection strategy based on uncertainty thresholds is well-motivated.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The simulations, while not extensive, provide preliminary evidence supporting the theoretical findings.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The motivation for the multi-fidelity approach is clearly articulated.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Assumptions:** The assumption that lower-fidelity means are within a known bound (ζ(m)) of the highest fidelity mean, while reasonable, might be restrictive in some applications. The impact of violating this assumption isn’t thoroughly discussed.
*   **Regularity Condition:** Assumption 1 (ζ(m) decay) feels somewhat ad-hoc. While not critical for the analysis, its role in achieving the stated performance gains isn’t fully convincing. The impact of violating this assumption warrants more discussion.
*   **Simulation Scope:** The simulations are limited. A more comprehensive evaluation across diverse problem settings (varying K, λ(m), and ζ(m)) would strengthen the empirical support. Demonstrating the scaling behaviour would also be valuable.
*   **Lower Bound Gap:** The authors acknowledge a gap between the theoretical lower bound and their algorithm's performance, particularly for arms close to optimal. Addressing this gap, even conceptually, would be desirable.
*   **Discussion of Alternative Regret Definitions:** While the authors justify their chosen regret metric, a more thorough exploration of alternative definitions and their implications could enrich the discussion.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a fundamentally novel and important problem framing.
*   The theoretical analysis is sound and provides valuable insights into the algorithm’s performance.
*   The algorithm is intuitively appealing and well-motivated.
*   The work has the potential to significantly impact several application domains.
*   Minor flaws (e.g., restrictive assumptions) are outweighed by the originality and significance of the contribution.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The simulations are limited in scope.
*   The gap between the lower bound and the algorithm's performance could raise concerns.
*   Some assumptions may be restrictive in practical settings.
*   The regularity condition feels somewhat arbitrary.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that introduces a significant new idea to the multi-armed bandit literature. While there are minor weaknesses, the novelty, theoretical rigor, and potential impact of the work are compelling. I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field and warrants acceptance, despite the limited empirical validation and the gap in the lower bound. I would encourage the authors to expand their simulations and address the remaining concerns in a future revision. I am willing to overlook these minor issues given the importance and novelty of the proposed framework.



**Rating:** Accept.
