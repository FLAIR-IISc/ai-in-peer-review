### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces the "stochastic K-armed multi-fidelity bandit," a novel extension of the classical multi-armed bandit problem. The key idea is that for each of the K arms, an agent can pull from one of M "fidelities." Lower fidelities are cheaper but provide biased and/or noisy approximations of the true reward, while the highest fidelity gives the true reward but is the most expensive. The authors formalize this setting by assuming the mean reward at fidelity `m`, `µ(m)k`, is within a known bound `ζ(m)` of the true mean `µ(M)k`.

To address this problem, the paper makes three main contributions:
1.  **A new problem formulation:** It proposes a cost-weighted regret formulation that accounts for a total capital budget `Λ`. The goal is to minimize the opportunity cost relative to an oracle that always plays the best arm, considering the costs of pulls.
2.  **A new algorithm, MF-UCB:** This algorithm adapts the UCB principle. It selects an arm based on the tightest confidence bound across all fidelities. Crucially, it then employs a novel rule for selecting which fidelity to play, starting with the cheapest and moving to more expensive ones only when the uncertainty at the current level is below a carefully chosen threshold.
3.  **A rigorous theoretical analysis:** The authors provide an upper bound on the regret of MF-UCB, demonstrating that it effectively uses cheap fidelities to eliminate clearly suboptimal arms, saving expensive high-fidelity pulls for a smaller set of promising candidates. This leads to significantly lower regret than a standard UCB algorithm (which only uses the highest fidelity) when `K` is large and low fidelities are informative. They complement this with a nearly matching lower bound for the problem, showing their algorithm is near-optimal.

The claims are supported by simulations on synthetic data, which corroborate the theoretical findings.

### Review and Justification

This is a strong, well-executed paper that introduces a compelling new problem and provides a thorough theoretical and empirical treatment.

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality.
*   The theoretical analysis is the main strength. The derivation of the regret bound in Theorem 2 is non-trivial and clearly demonstrates the advantage of the multi-fidelity approach. The key insight of partitioning the arms into sets `K(m)` based on which fidelity is most effective for their elimination is elegant and powerful.
*   The inclusion of a lower bound (Theorem 4) significantly strengthens the paper. It not only establishes the near-optimality of MF-UCB but also provides deeper insight into the problem's fundamental difficulty. The authors are honest about the small gap between their upper and lower bounds for arms in `K(m)7` and provide a clear explanation for its source, which shows a careful and complete analysis.
*   The assumptions are clearly stated. The core assumption `|µ(M)k − µ(m)k| ≤ ζ(m)` is reasonable and necessary to connect the fidelities. Assumption 1, while perhaps strong, is well-justified as a condition under which the multi-fidelity approach is most beneficial, and the authors note it is not strictly necessary for the analysis.
*   The experimental results, while on synthetic data, are well-designed to validate the core claims of the theory. The plots clearly show MF-UCB outperforming UCB and, importantly, illustrate the mechanism by showing how play counts are distributed across fidelities.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized.
*   The introduction provides excellent motivation using relatable examples from online advertising and algorithm selection, making the problem's relevance immediately clear.
*   The problem formulation is precise. The new cost-weighted regret definition is introduced and justified carefully, which is crucial as it underpins the entire paper.
*   The MF-UCB algorithm is presented clearly, and the intuition behind both the arm selection and, critically, the fidelity selection rule is well-explained. The choice of the threshold `γ(m)` is motivated before being used in the analysis.
*   A minor suggestion for improvement: The analysis section (Section 4), particularly the description of the arm partitions `K(m)`, is quite dense. While the figure is helpful, a brief intuitive example walking through how a specific arm might be classified and handled by the algorithm could further improve readability for a broader audience.

**Originality:** The work is highly original.
*   While multi-fidelity optimization and bandits are established fields, their combination in this specific regret-minimization framework is novel and significant. It differs clearly from related work in budgeted bandits (which typically have a cost per arm, not per fidelity) and active learning with different labelers (which do not focus on the exploration-exploitation trade-off).
*   The proposed MF-UCB algorithm, particularly its fidelity selection mechanism, is a novel contribution tailored to the problem structure. It is not a straightforward application of UCB.
*   The formulation of cost-weighted regret for a capital budget `Λ` is a thoughtful and important conceptual contribution that properly frames the multi-fidelity problem.

**Significance:** The paper's contributions are highly significant and likely to have a strong impact.
*   The problem setting is practical and broadly applicable. Many real-world sequential decision problems involve trade-offs between cheap, approximate evaluations and expensive, accurate ones (e.g., hyperparameter tuning with partial training epochs, material science experiments using simulations vs. physical tests, drug discovery). This paper provides the first strong theoretical foundation for tackling such problems in a bandit setting.
*   The results are strong. The analysis shows that MF-UCB can offer substantial improvements over standard approaches that ignore multi-fidelity information, especially in the common "large K, small `K*`" regime.
*   The paper opens up numerous avenues for future research. It lays the groundwork for extensions to more complex settings like linear or generalized bandits, contextual bandits, and Bayesian approaches (e.g., Multi-fidelity Thompson Sampling). The discussion of alternative regret definitions in the conclusion is insightful and points to interesting future challenges.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Introduces a novel, important, and practical problem setting (multi-fidelity bandits).
2.  Proposes an elegant and intuitive algorithm (MF-UCB) that is a non-trivial extension of UCB.
3.  Provides a comprehensive and high-quality theoretical analysis, including both an upper regret bound and a nearly-matching lower bound, establishing near-optimality.
4.  The paper is very well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to follow.
5.  The work is likely to inspire significant follow-up research in the community.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
1.  The experimental validation is limited to synthetic problems. While sufficient for a primarily theoretical paper, experiments on a semi-synthetic or real-world application (e.g., hyperparameter tuning) could have further strengthened the impact.
2.  Assumption 1, which relates the decay of `ζ(m)` to the concentration function `ψ`, might be difficult to verify or satisfy in some practical settings. However, the authors correctly note it is not essential to the core analysis, but rather a condition for an appealing performance comparison.

In conclusion, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It is a complete and impressive piece of work that makes a foundational contribution to the multi-armed bandit literature. I strongly recommend acceptance.
