

# Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

## Summary
This paper introduces a novel multi-fidelity extension to the classic multi-armed bandit problem, where each arm has access to multiple approximations (fidelities) with varying costs and accuracy. The authors formalize a cost-weighted regret notion that accounts for the different costs of using different fidelities and propose the MF-UCB algorithm that adaptively selects fidelities for arm exploration. The paper builds on UCB bandit methods [3,9] while addressing the new challenges introduced by the multi-fidelity setting. It distinguishes itself from related work on budgeted bandits [13,15] and active learning with weak/strong labelers [16] by focusing on the exploration-exploitation trade-off in multi-fidelity contexts. The paper also references recent work by Kandasamy et al. [8] on Gaussian process bandits with multi-fidelity evaluations.

## Strengths
1. **Novel Problem Formulation**: The paper successfully formalizes a practical multi-fidelity bandit setting with clear motivation from applications like online advertising and algorithm selection. The cost-weighted regret notion is well-motivated and necessary for this setting.

2. **Theoretical Contributions**: The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis with a regret bound for MF-UCB and a matching lower bound. The analysis carefully characterizes how the algorithm uses lower fidelities to eliminate suboptimal arms and reserves higher fidelities for promising arms.

3. **Algorithm Design**: MF-UCB elegantly extends UCB by maintaining confidence bounds across fidelities and adaptively selecting the appropriate fidelity. The choice of γ(m) is well-justified through the analysis.

4. **Empirical Validation**: The synthetic experiments demonstrate MF-UCB outperforming UCB, with results aligning with theoretical insights about fidelity usage patterns.

## Weaknesses
1. **Assumption of Known Bias**: The analysis assumes ζ(m) (the known bias between fidelities) is known, which is unrealistic in most practical applications. The paper doesn't address how to estimate ζ(m) from data or handle uncertainty in this parameter.

2. **Dependence on Assumption 1**: The regret bound comparison to UCB relies on Assumption 1 (fast decay of ζ(m)), which may not hold in practice. The paper doesn't discuss how violations of this assumption affect performance.

3. **Lack of Real-World Validation**: The empirical evaluation is limited to synthetic problems. The paper would benefit from demonstrating the approach on real-world datasets or applications.

4. **Parameter Sensitivity**: The algorithm's performance depends on the choice of ρ, but the paper doesn't provide guidance on selecting this parameter in practice.

5. **Limited Connection to [8]**: The paper cites Kandasamy et al. [8] as related work but doesn't sufficiently discuss how this paper differs from or extends their Gaussian process bandit approach.

## Quality Assessment
The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis. The regret bounds appear correct, and the lower bound analysis complements the upper bound. However, the assumption that ζ(m) is known significantly limits practical applicability. The experimental validation is adequate for synthetic problems but insufficient for demonstrating real-world utility.

## Clarity Assessment
The paper is well-written and clearly structured, with good notation and helpful figures (Fig. 1). The motivation and problem statement are clear. Some technical details (e.g., the choice of γ(m) and ρ) could be better motivated, and the discussion of Assumption 1 could be more concrete.

## Originality Assessment
This is a novel contribution to bandit literature, extending the classic problem to the multi-fidelity setting with a new regret formulation. While building on UCB, the multi-fidelity framework and regret notion represent a significant extension. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work.

## Significance Assessment
The problem has clear practical relevance in applications where cheaper approximations exist (online advertising, algorithm selection). The theoretical and empirical results demonstrate that multi-fidelity bandits can substantially improve regret compared to standard approaches. This could be impactful for practitioners in these domains. However, the assumption of known bias limits the immediate applicability.

## Arguments Pro and Con
**Pro acceptance**: Novel problem formulation with clear practical motivation, thorough theoretical analysis with matching upper and lower bounds, elegant algorithm design that extends UCB, and empirical results supporting the theoretical claims.

**Con acceptance**: Assumption of known bias (ζ(m)) is unrealistic for most applications, reliance on Assumption 1 for meaningful regret improvements, limited empirical validation on synthetic data only, and insufficient discussion of connection to recent related work [8].

## Recommendations
1. Address the assumption that ζ(m) is known. Consider how to estimate ζ(m) from data or develop a version of the algorithm that doesn't require this knowledge.

2. Discuss how violations of Assumption 1 affect performance, and provide empirical results showing behavior when this assumption is relaxed.

3. Include experiments on real-world data or a more diverse set of synthetic problems to strengthen the empirical claims.

4. Provide more guidance on selecting the parameter ρ in practice.

5. Expand the discussion of how this work relates to and extends Kandasamy et al. [8], particularly regarding the Gaussian process setting.

This paper makes a valuable contribution to bandit literature by formalizing and solving a practically relevant extension of the classic problem. With the suggested improvements addressing the assumption of known bias and expanding the empirical validation, it would be an excellent contribution to NeurIPS. As currently written, the assumptions limit the practical impact, but the theoretical framework is strong and well-motivated.
