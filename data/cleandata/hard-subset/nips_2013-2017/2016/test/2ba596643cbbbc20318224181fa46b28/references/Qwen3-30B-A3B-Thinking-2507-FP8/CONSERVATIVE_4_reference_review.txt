

# Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

This paper introduces a multi-fidelity extension to the classical multi-armed bandit problem, where each arm has multiple fidelities with different costs and approximations to the true reward. The authors propose MF-UCB, an algorithm that uses cheaper lower-fidelity evaluations to eliminate suboptimal arms, reserving expensive high-fidelity evaluations for a small subset of promising arms. They claim their approach achieves better regret bounds than standard UCB algorithms under specific conditions.

## Strengths

The paper presents a well-structured formalism for multi-fidelity bandits, clearly defining the problem, regret notion, and theoretical guarantees. The theoretical analysis appears sound, with a proper regret bound for MF-UCB and a matching lower bound for most cases. The algorithm is a natural extension of UCB, leveraging the same optimism principle in a multi-fidelity context. The paper properly acknowledges related work in multi-fidelity optimization and budgeted bandits, though the distinction from these works could be clearer.

## Weaknesses

While the theoretical contribution is interesting, the paper has significant weaknesses that prevent it from meeting NIPS standards for rigorous scientific contribution:

1. **Lack of thorough empirical validation**: The paper only provides synthetic experiments (Figure 2) without any real-world validation. The authors claim MF-UCB "outperforms naive UCB on simulations," but the experiments are limited to a few synthetic problems without demonstrating robustness across diverse settings. Crucially, they don't provide evidence of the practical impact of their method in real applications where multi-fidelity approximations exist (e.g., online advertising, algorithm selection).

2. **Incomplete comparison against relevant baselines**: The paper compares only to UCB, ignoring other relevant approaches. For instance, they cite Kandasamy et al. [8] (which also studies multi-fidelity bandits with Gaussian process payoffs) but don't compare to their methods. The budgeted bandit work by Tran-Thanh et al. [13] and others (which addresses cost constraints in bandits) isn't properly contrasted with their approach.

3. **Theoretical gap**: The lower bound analysis reveals a gap for arms in K(m)7, which the authors acknowledge but don't resolve. They suggest this is "an open problem," but this undermines the claim that MF-UCB is "near-optimal." The paper doesn't provide any experimental evidence to show how significant this gap is in practice.

4. **Overstated practical significance**: The paper claims "when |Kg| < K and λ(1) < λ(2), such a strategy will outperform the more standard UCB algorithms," but doesn't quantify how frequently this condition holds in practice or how much better the performance is. The synthetic experiments don't demonstrate the claimed performance improvements in a convincing way.

5. **Superficial application examples**: The motivating applications (online advertising, algorithm selection) are mentioned but not deeply explored. The paper doesn't provide evidence that the multi-fidelity framework actually solves meaningful problems in these domains, which is crucial for demonstrating significance.

6. **Assumption limitations**: The paper relies on Assumption 1 (ζ(m) decays fast enough), which may be restrictive in real-world settings. The paper doesn't discuss how sensitive their approach is to violations of this assumption or how to handle cases where it doesn't hold.

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents an interesting theoretical extension to the bandit problem, but it falls short of NIPS standards for a rigorous scientific contribution. While the theoretical analysis is sound, the lack of thorough empirical validation in realistic settings, incomplete comparison against relevant baselines, and unquantified practical significance limit its impact. The paper reads more like a theoretical exploration than a complete contribution that advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way.

The authors have made a novel contribution to the multi-fidelity bandit framework, but the paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence to support the practical relevance of their approach. Without compelling empirical evidence in realistic settings or a clear demonstration of how much better their method performs in practice, the theoretical contribution doesn't meet the bar for acceptance at NIPS.

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection. The paper presents an interesting theoretical idea, but the lack of thorough empirical validation, incomplete comparison with relevant baselines, and insufficient demonstration of practical significance prevent it from meeting NIPS standards for a high-quality scientific contribution. The authors should either provide more comprehensive empirical validation in realistic settings or substantially strengthen their theoretical claims to address the identified gaps before reconsidering for publication.
