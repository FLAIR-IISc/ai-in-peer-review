Summary
The paper introduces a stochastic multi-armed bandit setting with multiple fidelities per arm. Lower fidelities provide biased but cheaper approximations of the highest-fidelity reward, with known uniform bias bounds ζ(m) and cost λ(m) increasing with fidelity. The authors propose a cost-weighted regret for a fixed capital Λ, rewarding plays by the highest-fidelity mean µ(M) of the chosen arm regardless of fidelity. They design MF-UCB, which maintains upper confidence bounds per fidelity and selects both arm and fidelity by checking whether the uncertainty at a lower fidelity is still large relative to a threshold γ(m) that trades off bias and cost. They analyze regret, proving an upper bound showing that cheap fidelities eliminate many suboptimal arms, with expensive plays concentrated on promising arms. A lower bound (for Bernoulli rewards) shows MF-UCB is near-optimal, tight on a broad subset of arms. Synthetic experiments indicate gains over single-fidelity UCB.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on UCB algorithms and Lai–Robbins lower bounds, and distinguishes itself from budgeted bandits (reward/cost ratios) and multi-fidelity optimization (which typically lacks exploration–exploitation/regret analyses). It is complementary to multi-fidelity RL simulators and to the authors’ GP-based multi-fidelity bandits work. To my knowledge (for the NIPS 2016 timeframe), a formal multi-fidelity MAB model with biased approximations, explicit cost structure, and regret analysis is new.

Quality
- Modeling: The assumptions are clearly stated: known bias bounds |µ(M)k−µ(m)k|≤ζ(m), sub-exponential concentration with generic ψ, monotone costs, and a regularity condition on ζ decay. The regret notion explicitly accounts for cost and unused capital, and aligns with applications where only highest-fidelity performance counts.
- Algorithm: MF-UCB is a principled extension of UCB, using per-fidelity UCBs augmented by ζ(m), and a fidelity-selection rule via thresholds γ(m)=ψ−1(λ(m)/λ(m+1) ψ(ζ(m))). This choice is well-motivated as the optimal tradeoff in their analysis.
- Theory: The upper bound shows E[R(Λ)] scales like log(Λ/λ(M)) times a sum where each suboptimal arm’s main contribution is paid at the lowest fidelity that can eliminate it, yielding potential improvements by factors λ(JkK)/λ(M) relative to UCB. The analysis controls the number of plays N within capital Λ and shows high-probability sublinearity of low-fidelity plays beyond what’s needed. The lower bound (Bernoulli, ψ(·)≈·2) matches the upper bound on arms whose lower fidelities do not misleadingly suggest optimality, and explains the remaining gap for arms where small positive gaps at low fidelities fall below γ thresholds. The mismatch is discussed candidly.
- Empirics: Synthetic experiments consistently show improved regret slopes and the expected allocation of plays across fidelities. However, experimental details are relegated to the appendix, baselines are limited, and there is no real-data evaluation. Overall, the theoretical development is careful and sound; the empirical section is adequate but not strong.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear, with precise notation and a helpful partitioning K(m) that conveys the algorithm’s behavior. The regret definition and the choice of qt are explained with application-based justification. Some presentation issues remain: the heavy notation burden could be eased (e.g., summarizing symbols in a table), constants are often hidden in the main text, and the assumptions’ practical implications (e.g., sensitivity to ζ mis-specification) could be discussed more. The figure illustrating partitions is helpful; more intuition/examples would aid accessibility.

Originality
The formalization of multi-fidelity MAB with biased approximations and costs, the cost-aware regret, and the MF-UCB algorithm/fidelity-switching rule are novel. The lower-bound analysis tailored to this setting is also original. While the algorithmic core inherits from UCB, the fidelity-selection criterion and analysis are nontrivial and new.

Significance
The setting is practically relevant (ads, algorithm selection, simulation vs. real-world evaluation). The results can yield real savings when many arms are clearly suboptimal at low fidelities, with improvements scaling with λ(M)/λ(low). The analysis should stimulate further work (e.g., adaptive estimation of bias bounds, nonuniform/arm-dependent biases, alternate regret notions). The main limitation is empirical: broader baselines and real datasets would improve the case for impact.

Strengths
- Clear and principled problem formalization with appropriate regret for cost-constrained settings.
- MF-UCB design is simple, implementable, and theoretically justified.
- Strong theoretical results: upper bounds with clear improvement regimes; informative lower bound and discussion of the gap.
- Good intuitions (K(m) partitions) that match empirical behavior.

Weaknesses
- Strong assumptions: known, arm-independent bias bounds ζ(m); known ψ; regularity on ζ decay. Practicality and robustness to mis-specification are not evaluated.
- Lower bound limited to Bernoulli and does not fully close the gap on K(m)7 arms.
- Experimental evaluation is limited to synthetic data, with few baselines (no KL-UCB, Thompson sampling adaptations, or elimination-style multi-fidelity heuristics).
- The regret definition (qt=µ(M) regardless of fidelity) is reasonable for some applications, but alternative choices are only briefly discussed.

Suggestions and questions for authors
- Discuss robustness: how sensitive is performance to over/under-estimation of ζ(m)? Can γ(m) be tuned adaptively?
- Consider arm-dependent bias bounds or learned biases; could the analysis extend if ζ(m) varies by arm or is only known up to confidence?
- Provide stronger empirical evaluation: additional baselines (KL-UCB, Thompson sampling variants), ablations on γ(m), and a real-world or semi-synthetic task.
- Clarify the range of ψ beyond sub-Gaussian (e.g., sub-exponential) and how constants affect γ(m).
- Expand on alternate regret notions sketched in the conclusion; when would they be preferable?

Overall recommendation
Lean accept. The paper is technically solid, original, and addresses an important and practical extension of bandits with a clear algorithm and near-optimal theory. The main shortcomings are empirical breadth and strong modeling assumptions; these are acceptable for NIPS if acknowledged and positioned as avenues for future work.
