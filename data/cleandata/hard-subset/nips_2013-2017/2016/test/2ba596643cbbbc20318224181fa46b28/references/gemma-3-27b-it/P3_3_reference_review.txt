## Review of "The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a multi-fidelity extension to the classic multi-armed bandit (MAB) problem, motivated by scenarios where cheaper, noisier approximations of arm rewards are available. The authors formalize a cost-weighted notion of regret in this setting and propose a new algorithm, Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB), which adaptively allocates resources across different fidelities.  They demonstrate, both theoretically and empirically, that MF-UCB outperforms standard UCB strategies when lower fidelities provide useful information for eliminating suboptimal arms. The key idea is to leverage cheaper evaluations to quickly prune unpromising arms, focusing expensive, high-fidelity evaluations on a smaller subset.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds naturally on the extensive body of work surrounding the K-armed bandit problem, particularly UCB algorithms (Auer et al., 2002).  It correctly situates itself within related areas like multi-fidelity optimization (Huang et al., 2006) and active learning (Zhang & Chaudhuri, 2016), acknowledging the differences in objectives (regret minimization vs. optimization/label efficiency) and problem formulations. The authors are also cognizant of work on budgeted bandits (Tran-Thanh et al., 2013) and multi-fidelity reinforcement learning (Cutler et al., 2016), distinguishing their setting where fidelity levels represent approximations of the *same* underlying reward distribution, rather than different tasks.  The recent work by Kandasamy et al. (2016) extending multi-fidelity ideas to bandits with Gaussian process payoffs is also appropriately cited.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Formulation:**  The paper presents a clear and well-motivated framework for multi-fidelity bandits, formalizing a regret measure that explicitly considers both reward and fidelity costs. This is a valuable contribution.
* **Algorithmic Contribution:** MF-UCB is a conceptually sound algorithm that intuitively addresses the key challenges of the multi-fidelity setting. The adaptive fidelity allocation strategy seems well-designed.
* **Theoretical Analysis:**  The theoretical results are a significant strength. The regret bound demonstrates a clear advantage over standard UCB under realistic conditions (lower fidelity cost, informative low-fidelity estimates, a limited number of strong arms). The authors successfully identify conditions under which MF-UCB is near-optimal.
* **Empirical Validation:** The simulation results, though brief, provide evidence supporting the theoretical claims and demonstrating the practical benefits of MF-UCB.  The visualization of fidelity allocation is particularly helpful.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the concepts and analysis accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions:** The assumption that lower fidelity means are within a known ζ(m) of the highest fidelity mean ( |µ(M)k − µ (m) k | ≤ ζ(m) ) is strong. While realistic in some settings, it may not hold in others.  The paper acknowledges the possibility of misleading low-fidelity estimates but doesn't explore the robustness of MF-UCB to such scenarios. The Assumption 1 regarding the decay of ζ(m) feels somewhat arbitrary and could benefit from further justification or investigation of its impact.
* **Lower Bound Gap:** The gap between the derived regret bound and the lower bound (Theorem 4) is acknowledged and attributed to the algorithm's handling of arms with small ∆(m)k. While the explanation is reasonable, closing this gap would significantly strengthen the theoretical contribution.
* **Simulation Scope:** The empirical evaluation is somewhat limited. More comprehensive experiments with different parameter settings, problem sizes, and types of reward distributions would bolster confidence in the algorithm’s performance. A comparison to other relevant baselines beyond standard UCB would also be valuable.
* **Choice of qt:** While the justification for using µ(M)k as the reward (qt) is reasonable, a more thorough discussion of alternative choices and their implications would be beneficial. The brief mention at the end of Section 7 feels a bit abrupt.
* **Proof Sketches:** While the authors mention proof sketches, they’re very high-level. Adding more details to the sketch, or referencing more comprehensive proofs in the appendix, would increase the reproducibility of the results.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* This paper presents a novel and well-motivated problem formulation with a solid theoretical foundation.
* The proposed MF-UCB algorithm is a conceptually sound and practically effective solution.
* The theoretical analysis provides valuable insights into the algorithm's performance and establishes a clear advantage over existing approaches under reasonable conditions.
* The work has the potential to impact several application areas where multi-fidelity evaluations are common.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The strong assumptions regarding fidelity accuracy and the gap between the regret bound and lower bound raise some concerns.
* The empirical evaluation is limited and could benefit from more comprehensive testing.
* The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of alternative reward definitions and the robustness of the algorithm to violated assumptions.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of bandit optimization. The problem is well-motivated, the proposed solution is elegant and effective, and the theoretical analysis is rigorous. While there are some weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.  I **recommend acceptance** with minor revisions to address the concerns raised above, particularly regarding a more detailed discussion of the assumptions and a slightly expanded empirical evaluation.

**Confidence:** 8/10
