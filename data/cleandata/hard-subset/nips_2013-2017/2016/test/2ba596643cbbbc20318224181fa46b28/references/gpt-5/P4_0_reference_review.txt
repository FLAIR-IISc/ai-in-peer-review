Summary and relation to prior work
The paper introduces a multi-fidelity variant of the stochastic K-armed bandit where each arm can be queried at multiple fidelities with increasing cost and decreasing bias. Lower fidelities provide biased but cheaper observations of the true (highest-fidelity) reward. The authors define a cost-weighted regret under a capital budget Λ: the oracle plays optimal high-fidelity arms, and each action incurs the chosen fidelity’s cost while being credited according to the highest-fidelity mean. They propose MF-UCB, an Upper Confidence Bound algorithm that maintains per-fidelity UCBs adjusted by known bias envelopes ζ(m) and selects both an arm and fidelity using thresholds γ(m) that trade off the marginal information gain versus cost. The analysis shows that MF-UCB uses cheaper fidelities to eliminate clearly suboptimal arms and reserves expensive evaluations for near-optimal arms.

Technically, the paper derives a regret upper bound showing E[R(Λ)] scales like log(Λ/λ(M)) times a sum over arms of Δ(M) times λ(JkK)/ψ(Δ(JkK)), where JkK is the highest fidelity needed to eliminate arm k, ψ is the concentration rate, and Δ(M) is the highest-fidelity gap. Compared to a standard single-fidelity UCB baseline, this can yield savings proportional to λ(JkK)/λ(M) for many arms. A lower bound (for Bernoulli rewards and under a mild “no polynomial over-pulling” assumption) matches the upper bound on a large subclass of arms, leaving a gap only for arms that are borderline at some lower fidelity; the paper explains why MF-UCB’s switching rule is conservative in that case. Synthetic experiments corroborate that MF-UCB achieves lower capital-normalized regret and invests most high-fidelity plays on near-optimal arms.

This setting is distinct from budgeted bandits (which optimize reward/cost ratios per pull) and from weak/strong labeler active learning; it formalizes a biased multi-fidelity approximation hierarchy for bandits. Prior work on multi-fidelity has appeared in optimization and RL simulators, but without bandit-style regret analysis. The paper’s formulation and analysis appear novel for K-armed bandits; a related GP-based multi-fidelity bandit (Kandasamy et al., NIPS 2016) addresses a different function-optimization setting.

Quality (technical soundness)
- Assumptions: The model assumes known bias envelopes ζ(m) with ζ(m) decreasing to 0 and per-fidelity costs λ(m) increasing. Rewards satisfy a concentration inequality with generic ψ (sub-Gaussian is a special case). A regularity condition on ζ (Assumption 1) avoids pathological incremental fidelities. These are clearly stated.
- Algorithm: MF-UCB’s selection rule is natural: choose the arm with the tightest upper bound across fidelities and play at the lowest fidelity whose statistical uncertainty remains above a bias-informed threshold γ(m) = ψ^{-1}( (λ(m)/λ(m+1)) ψ(ζ(m)) ). This embodies an information-per-cost tradeoff.
- Analysis: The regret decomposition, high-probability control of per-fidelity pulls, and handling of the random number of plays N under a budget are standard but carefully adapted. The upper bound matches intuitive behavior: most capital on arms requiring higher fidelities, with low-fidelity exploration bounded sublinearly. The lower bound uses a change-of-measure argument for Bernoulli rewards and highlights a principled gap for borderline arms. Proof sketches are coherent; full details are deferred to the appendix.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-organized: problem setup, algorithm, regret definition, analysis, and simulations flow logically. The partition K(1), …, K(M) and the role of Δ(m) are helpful, though notation is dense and could benefit from a brief intuition box.
- The definition of regret is justified with concrete applications; alternative choices are acknowledged in the conclusion.
- Some assumptions (e.g., known ζ and the effect of Assumption 1) could be foregrounded earlier with a brief discussion of practicality.

Originality
- The formalization of multi-fidelity stochastic bandits with biased approximations and a cost-weighted regret is novel in the K-armed setting.
- The MF-UCB algorithm and its analysis (upper bound and partial lower bound) are new. The use of a general ψ-function keeps the results broadly applicable beyond sub-Gaussian cases.

Significance
- The framework captures realistic scenarios (ads, algorithm selection, simulators) where cheap proxies can prune arms. Theoretically, the results show provable capital savings relative to single-fidelity UCB whenever many arms are clearly suboptimal at low fidelities.
- The lower bound and near-optimality results enhance the contribution’s credibility. This work is likely to stimulate further research on multi-fidelity bandits, regret notions, and practical algorithms.

Strengths
- Clear and principled multi-fidelity formulation with cost-aware regret.
- Simple, implementable algorithm with a transparent switching rule.
- Solid theoretical guarantees: regret upper bound improving over UCB in broad regimes; lower bound explaining where improvements are and are not possible.
- General concentration framework and costs; not tied to a single noise model.
- Empirical support consistent with theory.

Weaknesses
- Practicality of known ζ(m) bounds: many applications may not provide reliable bias envelopes; sensitivity to mis-specification is not studied.
- Assumption 1 (on ζ decay) is important for the most appealing comparisons; behavior without it is less clear beyond constants/M small.
- Lower bound is restricted to Bernoulli rewards, μ* > 1/2, ζ(1) < 1/2, and requires Assumption 3; constants are unspecified.
- Experimental evaluation is limited: only compares to naive UCB; omits comparisons to plausible baselines (e.g., heuristics that estimate and screen using low fidelity, UCB-V, budgeted bandits adapted to this setting, or Thompson Sampling variants).
- The regret definition may not fit domains where low-fidelity actions yield no real reward; although acknowledged, no alternative analysis is provided.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and well-motivated problem formulation with practical relevance.
- Technically sound algorithm and analysis with meaningful improvements over standard baselines.
- Near-optimality on a large class of instances, with transparent discussion of the residual gap.
- Clear potential to influence subsequent work on multi-fidelity decision-making.

Arguments for rejection
- Dependence on known bias envelopes limits immediate practical applicability; robustness is not evaluated.
- Experimental section is relatively thin and lacks comparisons to stronger or tailored baselines.
- Lower bound’s scope and additional assumptions reduce its generality; constants are opaque.

Suggestions for improvement
- Discuss how to estimate or adapt to unknown ζ(m), and provide robustness experiments when ζ(m) is mis-specified.
- Add baselines: (i) a two-stage screen-then-UCB using only low fidelity for elimination; (ii) cost-aware Thompson Sampling; (iii) UCB-V; and ablations of γ(m).
- Explore sensitivity to the number of fidelities and to violating Assumption 1.
- Provide concrete guidelines for choosing ρ and empirical sensitivity.
- Consider an appendix with alternative regret notions (e.g., no reward at low fidelity) and preliminary results or impossibility statements.

Scope
The paper is in scope for NIPS (bandits, online learning, resource-aware decision-making).
