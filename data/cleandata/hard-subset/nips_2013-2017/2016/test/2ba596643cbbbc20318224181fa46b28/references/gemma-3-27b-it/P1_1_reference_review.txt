## Peer Review: The Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel framework for multi-armed bandits where each arm has multiple fidelity levels representing varying costs and approximation qualities. The authors propose the Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB) algorithm, which leverages lower-fidelity evaluations to quickly eliminate suboptimal arms and focuses higher-fidelity evaluations on the most promising candidates. They provide a theoretical analysis demonstrating improved regret bounds compared to standard UCB when lower fidelities are significantly cheaper and offer reasonable approximations. The work bridges the gap between classic bandit problems and practical scenarios where obtaining accurate rewards is costly or time-consuming.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds upon the well-established foundation of multi-armed bandit theory, particularly UCB algorithms [Auer, 2002]. It acknowledges and distinguishes itself from related areas like multi-fidelity optimization [Huang et al., 2006] and reinforcement learning with simulators [Cutler et al., 2016], which do not explicitly address the exploration-exploitation trade-off inherent in the bandit setting.  It also differentiates itself from budgeted bandit problems [Tran-Thanh et al., 2014] by focusing on fidelity as an approximation rather than cost, and acknowledges its extension in their previous work [Kandasamy et al., 2016] with Gaussian processes. The authors do a good job of situating their work within the existing literature.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Framework:** The multi-fidelity bandit setting is a valuable generalization of the classic problem, representing a significant step towards more realistic applications.
* **Clear Algorithm:** MF-UCB is clearly described and intuitively appealing. The mechanism for switching between fidelities based on confidence bounds is well-motivated.
* **Strong Theoretical Analysis:**  The regret bound presented is rigorous and demonstrates the benefits of using multiple fidelities under reasonable assumptions.  The derivation provides insight into how MF-UCB leverages lower fidelities for initial exploration and focuses high-fidelity evaluations efficiently.
* **Lower Bound:**  The inclusion of a lower bound strengthens the claim that MF-UCB is near-optimal and provides further theoretical justification for the approach.
* **Empirical Validation:** The simulations presented provide evidence that MF-UCB outperforms standard UCB in the proposed setting.
* **Well-written and Organized:** The paper is generally well-structured and the mathematical notation is clear.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions:** The assumption that lower fidelity means are within a known quantity (ζ(m)) of the high-fidelity mean may be strong in some real-world applications.  The impact of inaccurate ζ(m) values isn’t thoroughly explored.
* **Regularity Condition (Assumption 1):** While the authors acknowledge that Assumption 1 isn't *necessary*, its impact on performance isn't fully elucidated.  It's unclear how sensitive MF-UCB is to violations of this condition.
* **Lower Bound Gap:** The authors identify a gap between the lower bound and the achieved regret for certain arms, particularly those with small differences in fidelity-dependent rewards. This suggests potential for further optimization.
* **Simulation Scope:**  While the simulations are helpful, they are relatively limited in scope.  More diverse and complex scenarios would strengthen the empirical validation.  The specific details on the simulation setup are relegated to the appendix, making them less accessible.
* **Discussion of Alternative Regret Definitions:** The authors mention other possible regret definitions but do not deeply explore their implications or justify their chosen definition beyond the motivating example.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:**  The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The analysis is rigorous, and the claims are well-supported. However, the reliance on specific assumptions and the identified gap in the lower bound prevent a perfect score.
* **Clarity:** The paper is mostly clear and well-organized. The mathematical notation is consistent. The inclusion of illustrative figures (e.g., Fig. 1) is helpful.  Some details, like the simulation setup, could be brought forward from the appendix.
* **Originality:** The multi-fidelity bandit framework is a genuinely novel contribution. While building on UCB, the adaptation to this new setting requires significant insight and the resulting algorithm and analysis are original.
* **Significance:**  This work has the potential to be highly significant, particularly in applications where obtaining accurate data is expensive, time-consuming, or computationally intensive (e.g., hyperparameter optimization, robotics, clinical trials).

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Introduces a new and relevant problem setting (multi-fidelity bandits).
* Proposes a theoretically sound and intuitively appealing algorithm (MF-UCB).
* Provides a rigorous regret analysis demonstrating the advantages of the approach.
* Offers a complementary lower bound, further validating the algorithm’s performance.
*  Demonstrates empirical improvements over standard UCB.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*  Relies on potentially strong assumptions about fidelity approximations.
*  The regularity condition (Assumption 1) requires further investigation regarding its impact.
*  A gap exists between the theoretical lower bound and the achieved regret.
*  The simulations could be more comprehensive.



**Overall Recommendation:**

**Strong Accept.** Despite a few minor weaknesses, this paper presents a significant contribution to the field of multi-armed bandits. The novelty of the problem setting, the soundness of the proposed algorithm, and the strength of the theoretical analysis outweigh the limitations. The potential impact of this work is substantial, and it is likely to stimulate further research in this important area. I recommend acceptance to NIPS 2016.
