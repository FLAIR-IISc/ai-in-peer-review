

The paper introduces a multi-fidelity bandit framework where each arm can be pulled at multiple fidelity levels, with each fidelity providing a biased estimate of the true reward (within known ζ(m) of the highest fidelity mean) and associated with a different cost (λ(m) increasing with fidelity). The authors formalize a regret measure that accounts for both the reward gap and the cost of fidelity choices, propose the MF-UCB algorithm that leverages lower fidelities to eliminate suboptimal arms before investing in high-fidelity evaluations, and provide theoretical guarantees showing MF-UCB achieves better regret than standard UCB when using only the highest fidelity. The paper also establishes a lower bound demonstrating MF-UCB is nearly optimal under certain conditions.

Strengths: The problem formulation addresses a meaningful tradeoff between information cost and reward in bandit settings, with clear relevance to applications like online advertising and algorithm selection. The framework extends classical bandit literature by incorporating multiple fidelity levels, and the theoretical analysis provides valuable insights into how to leverage cheaper approximations to improve efficiency. The empirical results on synthetic problems corroborate the theoretical claims.

Weaknesses: The regret definition appears unnatural as it multiplies the cost with the regret rather than adding it, which seems to be motivated only by the advertising example. A more general regret formulation that adds cost to the regret would be preferable. Assumption 1 (on the decay rate of ζ(m)) is problematic as it appears arbitrary and lacks justification for general applications; the paper does not address whether this assumption is necessary or how to handle cases where it fails. The gap between the lower bound and MF-UCB's performance for certain arms (K^(m)_7) requires further investigation, and the algorithm's design seems tailored to specific ζ(m) sequences without clear justification for why these sequences are "right." The main result (Theorem 2) is difficult to interpret due to complex dependencies on the partitioned sets K^(m) and Δ^(m)_k, which would benefit from clearer exposition. The paper's presentation of the results becomes unnecessarily technical, obscuring the main contributions despite a strong theoretical foundation.

Pro: Interesting problem formulation addressing a meaningful tradeoff between cost and information in bandit settings; novel extension of bandit literature to multi-fidelity evaluations; theoretical guarantees showing improved regret over naive strategies.

Con: Unnatural regret definition that multiplies cost with regret rather than adding it; Assumption 1 appears arbitrary without justification for general learning problems; gap in lower bound requires further investigation; main result is difficult to interpret due to complex notation and dependencies; paper loses clarity in presenting results despite strong theoretical foundation.
