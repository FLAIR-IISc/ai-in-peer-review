

This paper introduces a novel multi-armed bandit framework where each arm's pull incurs a cost proportional to its fidelity (accuracy), with regret defined as the product of cost and standard pseudo-regret. The authors propose MF-UCB, an algorithm for this setting, and establish matching upper and lower bounds on cumulative pseudo-regret. The main text is well-written and clearly presents the theoretical contributions, offering valuable insights into the interplay between fidelity costs and regret minimization.

However, significant issues require attention. The supplementary materials contain numerous typographical errors and minor inconsistencies that impair readability and undermine confidence in the proofs. These include inconsistent notation (e.g., $H^{(m)}$ vs. $K^{(m)}$), missing symbols (e.g., $\hat{\gamma}$ vs. $\gamma^{(m)}$), incorrect indices (e.g., $k$ vs. $k$), and logical gaps (e.g., an unjustified implication on page 13 requiring $\rho > 4$). While these are easily correctable, their presence detracts from the paper's rigor. The authors should thoroughly proofread the supplementary material to eliminate these distractions.

The use of "up-to-a-multiplicative-constant" notation also causes ambiguity. For instance, Theorem 2 hides the algorithm parameter $\rho$ within the constant factor. Explicitly stating the dependence on $\rho$ (as done in the supplementary proofs) would clarify the results and avoid potential misinterpretation of the algorithm's behavior. Theorems and bounds should be presented with precise constants where feasible.

Practically, the applicability of MF-UCB to real-world problems is unclear. The algorithm relies on low-fidelity pulls to eliminate suboptimal arms, but this strategy may be sensitive to inaccurate estimation of $\zeta$ (the fidelity parameter). The authors should discuss practical limitations—such as sensitivity to $\zeta$ estimation errors—and propose mitigation strategies (e.g., adaptive fidelity scheduling or robustness analysis). Without this, the algorithm's real-world utility remains speculative.

Theoretical contributions are strong: the problem formulation is novel, the proofs are generally sound (despite supplementary errors), and the bounds are tight. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to bandit theory, particularly for cost-aware decision-making. However, the current presentation of results and supplementary materials necessitates revisions before acceptance.

**Required revisions:**
1. **Correct all typos and notation errors** in the supplementary materials (e.g., page 10, Eq. 8: $k$; page 11, Eq. (10): $\gamma^{(m)}$; page 13, Eq. (15): $x_{n,\delta}$; page 15: $p$/$m$ interchange; page 17: "distribtuions" → "distributions").
2. **Replace asymptotic notation** with precise constants where possible (e.g., explicitly handle $\rho$ in Theorem 2).
3. **Add a discussion** on practical implementation challenges, especially regarding $\zeta$ estimation and real-world applicability.

This work is promising and theoretically significant. With careful attention to the above points, it will be a strong contribution to the literature. I recommend **acceptance pending minor revisions**.
