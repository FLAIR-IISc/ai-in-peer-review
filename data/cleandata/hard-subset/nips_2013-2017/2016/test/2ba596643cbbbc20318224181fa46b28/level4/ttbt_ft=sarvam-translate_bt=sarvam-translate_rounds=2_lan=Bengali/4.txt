The author has provided a new framework called the Multi-Fidelity Multi-Armed Bandit. In this framework, weapons can be sampled with varying levels of fidelity, and a cost can be associated with each weapon. Low-cost, low-fidelity samples are taken from a distribution close to the highest fidelity distribution. They provide an algorithm that fits the sequence of available estimates and costs, its regret analysis, and a lower bound of the problem. They provide numerical simulations in synthetic problems where the proposed algorithm exceeds the UCB. The proposed framework is interesting and could be influential if further developed. However, in this state, I found the paper unsuitable for publication.

1/ The role of signals is quite confusing, making them very scattered and difficult to define. For example, I did not find what $s$ is (after the protest, it seems $s$ was defined 1 page after the first use and then rarely used in the rest of the paper). 2/ The use of advertisements as examples is confusing in some cases. In many places, the display time and non-stability of an advertisement are related. Non-stability is irrelevant in this paper. Using another example might be a good idea (see 4). 3/ I find it difficult to justify regret analysis in this situation. A regret analysis involves maximizing a profit in the long run and thus finding the weapon with the best ratio reward/cost. Here, the best policy is always the weapon with the highest average, regardless of the associated cost. Therefore, the best policy is weakened by the obligation to sample the best weapon at the lowest fidelity level. This can mask the cost of sampling at a lower level when it is not needed. 4/ A sample complexity analysis (another cost complexity here) might be useful in the context of Multi-Fidelity Multi-Armed Bandits. This could be inspired by the testing phase of prototypes for new product concepts. The goal is to find the best product with the highest probability, reducing the accumulated cost of the testing phase before mass production. Since companies are limited by regulations, contracts, and existing benefits, they are forced to use low-fidelity estimates before accessing advanced and expensive testing. 5/ I also found some parts of the text confusing. The paper could be reviewed to make it more streamlined.
