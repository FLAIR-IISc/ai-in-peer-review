This paper introduces a multi-fidelity extension to the multi-armed bandit (MAB) problem. In this formulation, each of the *K* arms can be sampled at *M* different fidelity levels. Lower fidelity samples are less costly but introduce a bias in the reward estimate – the expected value of the observed reward deviates from the arm’s true expected reward, with the magnitude of this deviation bounded by the fidelity level. The authors analyze a UCB-based algorithm adapted to this setting, widening the confidence intervals to account for the bias inherent in lower fidelity sampling.

The resulting theoretical analysis yields regret bounds that, while mathematically sound, are complex and difficult to interpret. The paper argues that these bounds will frequently outperform those obtained by consistently using the highest fidelity level and a standard UCB approach. Simulation results are presented to support this claim.

Initially, I questioned the modeling choice of lower fidelity as introducing bias rather than increased variance, as the latter seems equally plausible and intuitively aligned with the notion of ‘low fidelity’. However, the provided motivating examples, particularly in the context of algorithm selection for machine learning, partially justified this formulation. I remain skeptical of the advertising example, where shorter ad display times are posited to yield biased estimates of long-term effectiveness. Furthermore, in the algorithm selection scenario, the paper’s focus on minimizing regret doesn’t fully align with the typical objectives of an algorithm designer. Overall, the connection between the proposed model and its motivating applications feels somewhat tenuous, though not critically flawed.

The primary contribution of this work appears to be the formulation of the multi-fidelity MAB problem itself. The proposed algorithm represents a standard application of the “optimism in the face of uncertainty” principle. As noted previously, the theoretical performance guarantees are difficult to interpret, hindering a clear understanding of the algorithm’s strengths. 

The simulation results, while demonstrating potential improvements, are challenging to evaluate. Appendix C details the parameter values used, but lacks justification for these choices. The observed performance improvement of the MF-UCB algorithm, a factor of 3 to 4 over standard UCB in many simulations, raises the question of whether this is representative of expected performance in practice, or a consequence of the specific parameter configuration (e.g., values of ζ<sup>(m)</sup>). Determining the robustness of this improvement requires further investigation and justification of the simulation setup.
