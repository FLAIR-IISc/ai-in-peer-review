The paper studies multi-armed bandits where, for each pull, the learner also chooses a fidelity level from a finite set. Pulling an arm at fidelity m yields an i.i.d. stochastic reward whose mean lies within ζm of the true mean; the ζm parameters are known in advance, and the highest fidelity has ζ = 0. Lower fidelities are cheaper than higher ones. The authors formulate the objective of sequentially selecting arms and fidelities to maximize a notion of net reward, propose a new algorithm for this setting, and argue it can outperform UCB run solely at the highest fidelity. The paper also provides a lower bound for the regret of the proposed algorithm. The problem is interesting and relevant—it captures the online tradeoff between information, cost, and reward (cheap, low-information observations versus expensive, high-information ones) in stochastic bandits—and could serve as a useful benchmark.

However, the paper lacks sufficient explanation of its results and assumptions. The adopted regret definition seems unnatural from at least one perspective: why penalize resource consumption multiplicatively rather than additively? The ad-display example motivates the authors’ choice, but it does not appear to be the most general. A more natural model might incorporate the cost of fidelity selection additively into the cumulative reward (and hence regret). It would be valuable to understand what happens under this additive formulation.

Assumption 1 appears artificial/opaque, imposing constraints on the fidelity parameters known to the learner. Why should it hold in general learning problems? More importantly, if the ζm do not decay as rapidly as required by Assumption 1, is there a principled way to select a subsequence of fidelities that satisfies the assumption while preserving the results? Providing such a method would significantly strengthen the paper’s message. Although the authors state the assumption is not crucial, it seems to be used in the proofs. My main concern is whether it critically influences the algorithm’s design and performance. The algorithm appears tailored to perform well for certain ζ configurations, but the justification for why these are the “right” ζ sequences is missing.

The gap in the lower bound also merits further investigation and may require a redesign of the proposed strategy.

Interpreting the main result (Theorem 2, the regret bound for MF-UCB) and contrasting it with UCB (presumably run at the highest fidelity, though this is not explicitly stated) is difficult due to the technical presentation. The relative gain or loss of MF-UCB seems to depend on Δ and [[k]], which in turn depend on the complicated sequence of sets K^(m). A more explicit explanation—ideally with clear definitions and intuition for these quantities, or concrete examples—would substantially clarify the comparison.

Overall, the paper starts from an appealing and timely learning model, but it quickly loses clarity when presenting the main results.
