This paper introduces a novel multi-armed bandit setting where each arm can be sampled at different levels of accuracy, or "fidelities." In this framework, pulling an arm at a higher fidelity provides a more accurate reward estimate but also incurs a greater cost. The authors define a cost-weighted pseudo-regret to formalize the trade-off between exploration, exploitation, and budget constraints. They propose an algorithm for this setting, MF-UCB, and provide a theoretical analysis that includes both upper and lower bounds on its cumulative regret.

The main paper is clear and well-written. From a theoretical perspective, this paper provides valuable insight into an interesting problem, supported by strong theorems and solid proofs. The formulation of the multi-fidelity bandit problem is a significant and original contribution.

However, there are several areas where the paper could be improved. While the main body is clear, the same cannot be said for the supplementary material, which contains the majority of the proofs. The supplement has many typos and minor, easily correctable errors that can confuse the reader. Addressing these would greatly increase the paper's readability. A non-exhaustive list of these issues is provided at the end of this review.

Additionally, the use of asymptotic notation (e.g., proportionality signs) in the main paper can be slightly misleading, as the hidden "constants" can depend on algorithm parameters. For instance, in Theorem 2, a factor of ρ (an algorithm parameter) is hidden in the constant. Stating the exact inequalities from the supplementary material directly in the main text would improve the clarity and precision of the results.

From a practical standpoint, it is not clear how easily this algorithm can be applied to real-world problems. The MF-UCB strategy of using low-fidelity pulls to eliminate highly suboptimal arms could be sensitive to inaccurate estimations of the ζ parameters (the fidelity accuracy). The paper would benefit from a discussion on these practical limitations and potential methods to overcome them, such as the algorithm's robustness to misspecified ζ values.

Overall, despite some issues with the presentation in the appendix and a lack of discussion on practical limitations, I believe this work is a significant and interesting contribution to the field.
