This paper introduces the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit (MF-MAB), a novel and practical extension of the classic stochastic K-armed bandit problem. In this setting, each arm can be evaluated at one of M different "fidelities". Lower fidelities are cheaper to sample but provide a more biased estimate of the true reward, while higher fidelities are more expensive but more accurate. The core idea is to leverage cheap, low-fidelity evaluations to quickly identify and discard suboptimal arms, thereby reserving the expensive, high-fidelity evaluations for a smaller set of promising candidates. This framework, which builds upon the UCB family of algorithms, is well-motivated by applications such as online advertising or hyperparameter tuning. The authors formalize this problem with a cost-aware notion of regret, propose an elegant algorithm called Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB), and provide a thorough theoretical analysis of its performance, including both an upper regret bound and a corresponding lower bound demonstrating its near-optimality. The theoretical results are further supported by simulations.

**Strengths of the paper:**

*   **Novel and Significant Problem Formulation:** The paper formalizes a practical and important problem that has received limited attention in the bandit literature. The multi-fidelity setting captures a crucial cost-versus-accuracy trade-off present in many real-world sequential decision-making tasks, representing a significant conceptual contribution.
*   **Intuitive and Effective Algorithm:** The proposed MF-UCB algorithm is a natural and clever extension of the classic UCB framework. Its mechanism for deciding which fidelity to use based on confidence intervals and fidelity-switching thresholds is well-justified and elegantly adapts to the problem structure.
*   **Rigorous Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis, deriving regret upper bounds that clearly demonstrate how MF-UCB benefits from the multi-fidelity structure compared to a naive UCB approach. The inclusion of a lower bound further strengthens the contribution by establishing the near-optimality of the algorithm.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Clarification:**

The primary theoretical assumption that may limit practical application is that the bias bounds, $\zeta^{(m)}$, for each fidelity are known in advance. For the motivating examples, like online advertising, it is not immediately clear how one would specify these values accurately. Could the authors comment on the sensitivity of the algorithm to the choice of these parameters, or suggest practical methods for estimating them? An extension of the work to unknown $\zeta^{(m)}$ values would be a highly significant follow-up.
