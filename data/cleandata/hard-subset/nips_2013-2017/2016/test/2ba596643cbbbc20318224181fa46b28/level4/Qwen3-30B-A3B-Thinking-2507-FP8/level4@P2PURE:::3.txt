

The paper introduces a "multi-fidelity" variant of the multi-armed bandit problem, featuring K arms each playable at M fidelity levels. Lower fidelity reduces cost but increases potential bias in reward estimation, as pulling an arm at fidelity m draws a sample from a distribution whose expectation deviates from the arm's true reward by at most a value bounded by fidelity. The authors analyze a UCB-based algorithm where confidence interval widths are expanded to account for bias-induced uncertainty from low-fidelity sampling. The algorithm's analysis yields complex regret bounds (summarized in theorems) that are difficult to interpret; the paper contends these bounds typically outperform the regret of always using highest fidelity with standard UCB. A brief simulation section supports this claim. I initially questioned the bias-based low-fidelity model versus a variance-based one (which also seems plausible). While the motivating examples (e.g., machine learning algorithm selection) ultimately convinced me that bias modeling is well-motivated, I remain unconvinced by one application (the ad display example claiming shorter intervals yield biased effectiveness estimates over longer intervals) and by the relevance of regret minimization in machine learning algorithm selection. Thus, while the model-application connection is disappointingly weak, it does not constitute a disqualifying flaw. The paper's main contribution is the multi-fidelity bandit formulation itself; the algorithm is a standard application of "optimism in the face of uncertainty." The performance guarantee remains hard to interpret, as noted. The synthetic simulation results are difficult to evaluate: Appendix C details simulation parameters but omits justification for their choices. The regret of MF-UCB improves upon UCB by a factor of 3–4 in many cases; it is unclear whether this relative performance is typical in practice or an artifact of specific parameter selections (e.g., ζ^(m) values).
