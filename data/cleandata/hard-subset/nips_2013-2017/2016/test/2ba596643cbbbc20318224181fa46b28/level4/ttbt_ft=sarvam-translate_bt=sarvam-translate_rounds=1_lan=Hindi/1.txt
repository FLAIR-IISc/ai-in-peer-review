This paper illustrates multi-armed bandit problems, where there is a 'loyalty' component (from a limited set of loyalties) for the arm being drawn by the learner. An arm drawn at a certain loyalty gives a stochastic iid reward with a mean within a certain zetam of the true mean, where the zetam parameter (errors) are known a priori, and where the highest loyalty level corresponds to zeta = 0. Furthermore, arms with smaller loyalties are cheaper (i.e., less costly) than arms with higher loyalties. The authors have formulated the problem of sequentially drawing arms with loyalties in such a way as to maximize the notion of pure reward, provide a new algorithm for the problem, and argue that it can outperform UCBs that only work at high loyalty levels. The paper also provides a lower bound for the regret of the proposed algorithm. In my opinion, this paper studies an interesting and relevant problem – modeling the tradeoffs between information, cost, and reward in online learning (whether to choose less information that is cheaper or more information that is expensive) – specifically in stochastic banditry. In this sense, it could be useful as a benchmark for improvement. Although the paper seems technically sound, a significant drawback is the lack of sufficient explanation regarding its results and assumptions. The adopted regret definition seems unnatural from at least one angle – why not add resource consumption (or 'cost') as a penalty instead of the qualitative one used here? The authors' example of advertisement display motivates their definition, but it may not be the most common. What happens when the cost paid for suitable loyalty observations is added as a penalty to the total reward (or regret), perhaps this is a more natural question, and it would be good to know. The introduction to assumption 1 seems somewhat artificial/ambiguous, including constraints on the loyalty parameters given to the learner. Why would the authors expect this to be applicable in a general learning problem? More importantly, if the zeta_m assumptions do not decay as rapidly as required by assumption 1, is there a general way to select a sub-sequence of loyalties to satisfy the assumption and therefore the results (this would greatly help strengthen the paper's message)? Although the authors mention that the assumption is not critical, it seems to be used in the proofs, and my main concern is whether the assumption significantly affects the design and performance of the algorithm. It seems that the algorithm's design is prepared to perform well for certain types of zeta configurations, but the justification for why these are the 'right' zeta sequences is missing. Again, the difference in the lower bound also requires more investigation and perhaps a redesign of the proposed strategy. Interpreting the main result (Thm. 2, the regret bound for MF-UCB) and contrasting it with UCBs (at the highest loyalty? (this is not explicitly stated in the discussion)) is not easy to understand due to the highly technical nature of the presentation. The relative advantage/disadvantage of MF-UCB depends on delta and [[k]], which in turn depends on the relatively complex sequence of sets \cal{K}^(m). A more explicit explanation would be very helpful in the authors' case. Overall, I think the paper makes a good start in terms of considering an interesting learning model, but loses clarity rapidly when it comes to presenting the results.
