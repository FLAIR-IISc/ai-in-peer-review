The authors present a novel framework termed the Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit, addressing a scenario where evaluating arms incurs varying costs and cheaper, albeit biased, approximations of the true reward are available. They propose an algorithm, MF-UCB, that adapts to the sequence of available approximations and costs, accompanied by a regret analysis and a corresponding lower bound. Empirical evaluation on synthetic problems demonstrates MF-UCB's superior performance compared to the standard UCB algorithm. This framework holds promise and, with further development, could be impactful. However, in its current state, I find the paper unsuitable for publication.

1/ The introduction of notation is often confusing. Definitions are dispersed, making them difficult to locate. For example, the variable *s* is introduced without immediate definition (it appears one page later and is subsequently rarely used). Clarity in notation is crucial for reproducibility and understanding.

2/ The use of online advertising as a motivating example occasionally introduces misleading concepts. The discussion sometimes draws parallels between display time and non-stationarity, which is outside the scope of the presented problem. A more focused example might strengthen the presentation (e.g., consider algorithm selection for machine learning, as briefly mentioned in the introduction).

3/ The justification for the regret analysis in this setting is somewhat unclear. A standard regret analysis aims to maximize long-term gain, identifying the arm with the best reward-to-cost ratio. However, the proposed framework appears to prioritize identifying the arm with the highest *mean* reward, irrespective of cost. This approach potentially diminishes the importance of minimizing low-fidelity sampling when it’s not beneficial. The imposed obligation to sample the optimal arm at lower fidelities may obscure the true cost of such sampling when unnecessary.

4/ A sample complexity analysis, or more accurately a cost complexity analysis, would be highly valuable in the context of multi-fidelity bandits. This could be motivated by scenarios like prototype testing in product design, where the goal is to identify the best product with high probability while minimizing the cumulative cost of the testing phase before mass production. Constraints from regulations, contracts, and existing facilities often necessitate the use of low-fidelity approximations before resorting to advanced, costly tests.

5/ Parts of the text are unwieldy and could benefit from streamlining. A thorough revision for conciseness and clarity would improve readability.

**Arguments for rejection:**

*   Confusing notation and dispersed definitions hinder understanding.
*   The motivating example introduces irrelevant concepts (non-stationarity).
*   The justification for the chosen regret analysis is questionable.
*   The absence of a cost complexity analysis limits the practical relevance.
*   Overall writing quality requires improvement.

**Arguments for acceptance (minor):**

*   The multi-fidelity bandit framework is a novel and potentially valuable extension of the classical bandit problem.
*   MF-UCB demonstrates empirical superiority over UCB in synthetic experiments.
*   The theoretical analysis provides a regret bound and a corresponding lower bound.
