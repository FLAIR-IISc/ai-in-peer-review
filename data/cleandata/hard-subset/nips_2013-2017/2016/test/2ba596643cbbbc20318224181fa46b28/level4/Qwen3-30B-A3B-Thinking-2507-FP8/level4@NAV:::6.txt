

This paper introduces a novel multi-armed bandit framework incorporating $M$ fidelities, where the learner selects an arm at a specific fidelity level. Each higher fidelity yields a more accurate reward approximation but incurs greater cost. A strategy leveraging cheaper fidelities to eliminate suboptimal arms early, reserving expensive fidelities for a refined set of promising arms, may surpass standard UCB by exploiting fidelity characteristics. This setting is applicable to online advertising, where brief ad displays (low fidelity) approximate long-term click behavior at reduced cost. The authors propose a new regret definition, present an algorithm with theoretical guarantees (including a regret lower bound), and validate via simulations. The theoretical analysis is novel. However, the assumption that deviations $\varepsilon^{(m)}$ (from the highest-fidelity mean) are known a priori—critical for the algorithm—raises practical concerns: how are these values determined in real applications (e.g., online advertising)? Can this assumption be relaxed?
