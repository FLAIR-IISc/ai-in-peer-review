This paper addresses a novel variant of the classical stochastic K-armed bandit problem where cheap, biased approximations for the reward of each arm are available. The authors formalize this setting as the "multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit" problem, where each arm can be played at different fidelities, with lower fidelities incurring less cost but providing less accurate reward estimates. The central contribution is the Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB) algorithm, an extension of the classic UCB framework. MF-UCB intelligently selects an arm based on an aggregated confidence bound and then chooses a fidelity level based on a cost-aware switching criterion. The core idea is to leverage inexpensive low-fidelity pulls to quickly discard clearly suboptimal arms, thereby reserving the more expensive, high-fidelity evaluations for a small set of promising candidates. The paper supports this algorithmic contribution with a thorough theoretical analysis, providing an upper bound on the regret of MF-UCB and a corresponding lower bound for the problem itself. This analysis demonstrates that MF-UCB is nearly optimal and achieves significantly better regret than standard UCB strategies that ignore the availability of low-cost approximations.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed MF-UCB algorithm is technically sound, and its design is well-justified by clear intuitions. The theoretical analysis is comprehensive and rigorous, providing both an upper bound for the algorithm's regret and a lower bound for the problem class. This duality allows the authors to compellingly demonstrate the near-optimality of their method. The regret bound clearly quantifies the benefit of using multiple fidelities, showing how savings in cost translate to reduced regret. Furthermore, the authors are intellectually honest, acknowledging a small gap between their upper and lower bounds for certain arms and leaving its resolution as an open problem.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation for the multi-fidelity problem is presented clearly with compelling real-world examples. The problem is formalized precisely, with a well-defined, cost-weighted notion of regret that is appropriate for the setting. The algorithm is described simply, and the logic behind key design choices, such as the fidelity switching threshold `γ(m)`, is well-explained. The analysis is made more accessible through the helpful partitioning of the arm set (K(m)), which clarifies the behavior of the algorithm.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. The formulation of the multi-fidelity bandit problem addresses a practical and important aspect of sequential decision-making—the trade-off between evaluation cost and accuracy—that has been largely overlooked in the standard bandit framework. While the algorithm builds upon the established UCB principle, its adaptation to the multi-fidelity setting introduces significant new ideas, particularly in the fidelity selection mechanism. The work is well-positioned relative to prior literature and clearly distinguishes its contributions from related concepts like budgeted bandits.

**Significance:** The contributions of this paper are significant and likely to have a considerable impact on both research and practice. The multi-fidelity abstraction is broadly applicable to numerous domains where expensive experiments can be approximated by cheaper surrogates, such as hyperparameter tuning, A/B testing in online advertising, and scientific simulations. The MF-UCB algorithm provides a practical and provably effective tool for practitioners in these fields. The paper also opens up a promising new direction for bandit research, and its ideas could inspire extensions to more complex settings like contextual or linear bandits.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Practicality of Assumptions:** The theoretical analysis relies on the assumption that the approximation error bounds `ζ(m)` are known in advance. In many practical scenarios, these values would be unknown and would need to be estimated, which could impact the algorithm's performance and guarantees. A discussion of the algorithm's robustness to misspecified `ζ(m)` values would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are conducted on synthetic data. While these simulations effectively validate the theoretical claims and illustrate the algorithm's behavior, the paper's impact would be further enhanced by a demonstration on a real-world or more complex semi-synthetic problem (e.g., a hyperparameter optimization benchmark).

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Pro:**
    *   Introduces a novel, well-motivated, and practical problem formulation.
    *   Proposes an intuitive and elegant algorithm (MF-UCB) to solve the problem.
    *   Provides strong and comprehensive theoretical guarantees, including both upper and lower regret bounds, which establish the algorithm's near-optimality.
    *   Clearly demonstrates a significant performance improvement over standard bandit methods in the appropriate setting.
    *   The paper is exceptionally clear and opens up a promising new area of research.

*   **Con:**
    *   The assumption of known error bounds `ζ(m)` may be limiting in some applications.
    *   The empirical validation is restricted to synthetic problems.

**Conclusion:**

This is an excellent paper that makes a novel and significant contribution to the field. The weaknesses identified are minor compared to the strengths of the new problem formulation, the elegant algorithm, and the rigorous theoretical analysis. The work is of high quality, originality, and clarity. I strongly recommend acceptance.
