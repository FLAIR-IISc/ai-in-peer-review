This paper introduces a novel “multi-fidelity” extension to the classical multi-armed bandit problem. The core idea is to allow for varying levels of fidelity when evaluating each of the *K* arms, where lower fidelities are cheaper to obtain but introduce potentially biased reward estimates. Specifically, playing an arm at fidelity *m* draws a sample from a distribution with an expected value that may deviate from the true reward by a quantity bounded by *ζ<sup>(m)</sup>*. The authors propose and analyze a UCB-based algorithm adapted to this setting, adjusting confidence bounds to account for fidelity-induced bias. The theoretical analysis yields regret bounds, which the authors argue are superior to those achieved by standard UCB applied solely at the highest fidelity. Simulation results are presented to support this claim.

Initially, the modeling choice of representing lower fidelity as introducing *bias* rather than *variance* was puzzling. While a higher-variance interpretation seems intuitively appealing, the provided motivating examples – particularly algorithm selection for machine learning – demonstrate the relevance of the bias formulation. However, the application to online advertising, where shorter ad display times are claimed to yield biased effectiveness estimates, is less convincing. Furthermore, the connection between minimizing regret and the objective of an algorithm designer in the machine learning context remains unclear. Despite these minor disconnects, the primary contribution lies in the formulation of this multi-fidelity bandit problem.

The proposed algorithm is a natural extension of the “optimism in the face of uncertainty” principle, adapting UCB to the new fidelity dimension. However, the resulting theoretical performance guarantees, while rigorously derived, are complex and challenging to interpret. The simulation results, while demonstrating improvement over standard UCB (often by a factor of 3-4), lack sufficient justification for the chosen parameter values. It remains unclear whether this performance gain is representative or an artifact of the specific parameter settings.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Problem Formulation:** The multi-fidelity bandit framework is a significant and potentially impactful contribution, recognizing a common scenario in many real-world applications.
*   **Sound Algorithm:**  MF-UCB is a sensible adaptation of the well-established UCB algorithm to the new problem setting.
*   **Theoretical Analysis:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, offering regret bounds for the proposed algorithm.
*   **Empirical Validation:** Simulations demonstrate the potential benefits of MF-UCB over standard UCB.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Motivating Examples:** The connection between the model and some of the motivating applications (specifically, online advertising) feels tenuous.
*   **Regret Bound Complexity:** The derived regret bounds are difficult to interpret and provide limited practical insight.
*   **Simulation Parameter Selection:** The choices of parameters in the simulation experiments are not adequately justified, raising concerns about the generalizability of the results.
*   **Bias vs. Variance:** The rationale for modeling lower fidelity as introducing *bias* rather than *variance* isn’t completely persuasive, although the examples help.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a novel and potentially important problem with clear applications.
*   The proposed algorithm is a logical and well-motivated extension of existing bandit techniques.
*   The theoretical analysis provides a solid foundation for understanding the algorithm’s performance.
*   The simulation results, though imperfect, offer initial evidence of the algorithm’s benefits.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The connection between the model and some motivating applications could be stronger.
*   The complexity of the regret bounds limits their practical usefulness.
*   The lack of justification for simulation parameters raises concerns about the robustness of the results.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of bandit optimization by introducing a new, relevant, and well-motivated problem. While the theoretical results are complex and the empirical validation could be strengthened, the novelty and potential impact of the work warrant acceptance. I recommend acceptance, with encouragement to the authors to provide a more intuitive explanation of their regret bounds and to better justify the parameter choices in their simulations.
