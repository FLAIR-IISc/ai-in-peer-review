The authors provide a new framework called Multi-fidelity Multi-armed bandit. In this problem, arms can be sampled with different levels of fidelity, and a cost is associated with each one. While having a lower cost, low-fidelity samples are drawn from a distribution approximating the highest fidelity distribution. They provide an algorithm adapting to the sequences of available approximations and cost, its regret analysis, and a lower bound of the problem. They also provide numerical simulations on synthetic problems where the proposed algorithm outperforms UCB.

The proposed framework is interesting and, if more developed, can be impactful. However, in its current state, I found the paper unfit for publication.

1. The introduction of notations is quite confusing, as they are very dispersed and make their definitions hard to find. For example, I did not find what $s$ is (after the rebuttal, it appears that $s$ is defined 1 page after its first use and is then barely used in the rest of the paper).

2. The use of advertising as an example is misleading at some points. At many places, correlations with the display time of an ad and non-stationarity are made. Non-stationarity is out of context in this paper. It may be a good idea to use another example (see point 4).

3. I find it hard to justify the regret analysis in this setting. A regret analysis involves maximizing a gain in the long run and thus finding the arm with the best ratio reward/cost. Here, the optimal policy is always the arm with the highest mean, whatever the associated cost is. Thus, the optimal policy is weakened by the obligation of sampling the best arm at lower fidelity levels. This may hide the cost of sampling low levels when it is not needed.

4. A sample complexity analysis (here more a cost complexity) may be useful in the context of Multi-fidelity Multi-armed bandits. It can be motivated by the testing phase of prototypes for the conception of new products. The goal is to find with high probability the best product while minimizing the cumulated cost of the test phase before mass production. As companies are bounded by regulations, contracts, and existing facilities, they are obligated to use low-fidelity approximations before accessing advanced and costly tests.

5. I also found some parts of the text unwieldy. It may be worth making a pass on the paper to make it more streamlined.

Strengths:

* The authors propose a new and interesting framework for multi-fidelity multi-armed bandits.
* They provide an algorithm adapting to the sequences of available approximations and cost, its regret analysis, and a lower bound of the problem.
* They provide numerical simulations on synthetic problems where the proposed algorithm outperforms UCB.

Weaknesses:

* The introduction of notations is confusing, and their definitions are hard to find.
* The use of advertising as an example is misleading at some points.
* The regret analysis is not well justified in this setting.
* The paper lacks a sample complexity analysis (or cost complexity) that could be useful in the context of multi-fidelity multi-armed bandits.
* Some parts of the text are unwieldy and need to be streamlined.

* The authors propose a new framework for multi-fidelity multi-armed bandits, which is an original contribution to the field.
* They provide an algorithm adapting to the sequences of available approximations and cost, its regret analysis, and a lower bound of the problem.
