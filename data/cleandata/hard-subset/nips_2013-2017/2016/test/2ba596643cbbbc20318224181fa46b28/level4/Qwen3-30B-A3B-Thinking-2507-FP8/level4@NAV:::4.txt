

The authors propose a novel framework termed Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandits, wherein arms can be sampled at varying fidelity levels associated with distinct costs. Lower-fidelity samples are drawn from a distribution approximating the highest-fidelity distribution but incur reduced cost. The paper presents an adaptive algorithm for sequences of available approximations and costs, accompanied by regret analysis and a problem-specific lower bound. Numerical experiments on synthetic problems demonstrate that the proposed algorithm outperforms UCB. While the framework is potentially impactful with further development, the current submission is unsuitable for publication. Specific concerns are:  
1. Notation definitions are scattered and confusing; for instance, $s$ is introduced without definition and later defined one page afterward, after which it is scarcely used.  
2. The advertising example is misleading, as it incorrectly invokes non-stationarity (e.g., correlations between ad display time and non-stationarity), which is irrelevant to the problem context. A more appropriate example should be provided.  
3. The regret analysis lacks justification: regret minimization should prioritize the arm with the optimal reward/cost ratio, yet the optimal policy here is solely the arm with the highest mean reward regardless of cost. This obscures the cost of sampling low-fidelity arms when unnecessary.  
4. A cost complexity analysis (replacing sample complexity) would be highly relevant. It could be motivated by product prototyping scenarios: identifying the best product with high probability while minimizing cumulative testing costs before mass production, where regulatory or infrastructural constraints necessitate low-fidelity testing prior to costly high-fidelity validation.  
5. The text contains unwieldy sections requiring streamlining for clarity and conciseness.
