

This paper addresses a novel variant of the classical stochastic K-armed bandit problem where cheaper approximations to arm outcomes are available alongside the expensive high-fidelity observations. The authors formalize this as a multi-fidelity bandit problem, where each arm can be observed at M different fidelities with increasing cost and decreasing bias. The paper introduces MF-UCB, a novel upper confidence bound algorithm that strategically uses lower-fidelity observations to eliminate suboptimal arms before investing in expensive high-fidelity evaluations. The authors prove that MF-UCB achieves regret bounds that are superior to standard UCB when the cost of lower fidelities is significantly less than the highest fidelity, and they establish a matching lower bound showing near-optimality under certain conditions.

The paper builds on classical bandit work (e.g., [3, 9, 11]) and extends it to the multi-fidelity setting, which differs from related work on budgeted bandits [13, 15] and multi-fidelity optimization [7, 10] by incorporating exploration-exploitation trade-offs within the bandit framework. The paper also relates to Kandasamy et al. [8] which considered Gaussian process payoffs in multi-fidelity bandits, but this work provides a more general theoretical framework.

**Quality**: The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-executed. The authors provide both upper and lower bounds on regret, demonstrating that MF-UCB achieves nearly optimal performance under reasonable assumptions. The regret analysis properly accounts for the cost structure of different fidelities and the bias introduced by lower-fidelity observations. The proofs are technically sound, with careful attention to the trade-offs between fidelity cost, bias, and information gain. The experimental results complement the theoretical findings, showing clear improvements over standard UCB in synthetic settings. The paper is technically complete and well-supported by both theory and experiments.

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem formulation is clear from the outset, with the multi-fidelity framework and cost structure well-motivated by practical examples (e.g., online advertising). The algorithm description is precise and the theoretical results are presented with appropriate mathematical notation. The figures (particularly Figure 1) effectively illustrate the partitioning of arms by fidelity and the algorithm's behavior. The paper provides sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the results, meeting the high standard of clarity expected at NIPS.

**Originality**: The paper makes a clear contribution by formalizing the multi-fidelity bandit problem and developing a principled algorithm that adapts to the cost-bias structure. While multi-fidelity methods have been studied in optimization [7, 10], this work is the first to properly integrate them into the bandit framework with exploration-exploitation trade-offs. The key insight that lower fidelities can be used to eliminate arms before expensive high-fidelity evaluations is novel and well-supported. The paper's approach differs significantly from related work on budgeted bandits [13, 15], which focuses on reward-to-cost ratios rather than fidelity-based approximations. The authors provide sufficient context to establish the novelty of their contribution.

**Significance**: This work has significant potential impact for applications where high-fidelity observations are expensive but cheaper approximations are available (e.g., online advertising, algorithm selection, clinical trials). By reducing the number of expensive high-fidelity evaluations needed, the approach could substantially lower costs in practical bandit applications. The theoretical results provide a clear framework for understanding the trade-offs between fidelity costs and regret, which is valuable for both theoretical and applied researchers. The paper advances the state of the art in bandit theory by addressing a practically relevant extension to the classical problem.

**Arguments for acceptance**:
- The problem is well-motivated by practical applications where high-fidelity observations are expensive.
- The theoretical results are rigorous and provide clear improvements over standard bandit methods.
- The algorithm is well-designed and the analysis correctly accounts for the multi-fidelity structure.
- The experimental results support the theoretical claims.
- The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written.

**Arguments against acceptance**:
- The analysis assumes that the bias parameters ζ(m) are known, which may be restrictive in some applications.
- The regret bounds require the regularity condition in Assumption 1, which may not always hold in practice.
- The experimental evaluation is limited to synthetic problems, though the authors note this is due to space constraints.

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to bandit theory that addresses a practically important extension to the classical problem. The technical quality is excellent, the presentation is clear, and the significance is high for both theoretical and applied researchers. The paper meets all NIPS quality criteria and advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way. The minor limitations noted above do not detract from the paper's strong contribution. I recommend acceptance.
