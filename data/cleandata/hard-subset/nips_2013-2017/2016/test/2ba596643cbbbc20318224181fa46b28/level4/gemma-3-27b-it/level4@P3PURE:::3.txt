The paper introduces a “multi-fidelity” extension to the multi-armed bandit (MAB) problem, featuring K arms each playable at M distinct fidelity levels. Lower fidelity samples are less costly but introduce potentially greater bias in reward estimation – specifically, the expected value of the observed reward distribution at fidelity *m* deviates from the arm’s true expected reward by an amount bounded by a fidelity-dependent quantity. The authors analyze a natural UCB-based algorithm adapted to this setting, increasing the confidence interval width to account for the bias inherent in low-fidelity sampling. The algorithm’s performance is characterized through theorems yielding regret bounds that, while technically correct, are complex and difficult to interpret. The paper argues these bounds will often represent a significant improvement over the regret incurred by consistently playing arms at the highest fidelity using standard UCB. Supporting simulation results are presented in a concise section.

My initial reaction was to question the modeling choice of lower fidelity as introducing bias rather than variance, as the latter seems intuitively well-motivated. However, the provided motivating examples – particularly those concerning algorithm selection for machine learning – justified this approach, though I remain unconvinced by the ad display duration example. Furthermore, within the algorithm selection scenario, the objective of minimizing regret may not align with the typical goals of an algorithm designer. Overall, the connection between the model and its motivating applications feels somewhat tenuous, though not to a degree that disqualifies the work. 

The primary innovation appears to be the formulation of the multi-fidelity MAB problem itself. The proposed algorithm represents a standard application of the “optimism in the face of uncertainty” principle. As previously noted, the theoretical performance guarantees are difficult to interpret. The simulation results, presented on synthetic problems, are challenging to assess fully. While Appendix C provides detailed parameter settings, it lacks justification for these choices. The observed improvement of MF-UCB over UCB – a factor of 3 to 4 in many simulations – raises the question of generalizability. Is this performance gain typical, or is it sensitive to the specific parameter values (e.g., those of ζ<sup>(m)</sup>) used in the experimental setup?
