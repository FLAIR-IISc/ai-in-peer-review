

This paper proposes a novel multi-fidelity bandit framework where each arm can be evaluated at multiple cost levels (fidelities), with lower fidelities providing cheaper but biased approximations of the true reward. The authors formalize this as a cost-weighted regret minimization problem and introduce MF-UCB, a principled algorithm that dynamically allocates fidelities to balance exploration and exploitation. The theoretical contribution includes a tight regret bound for MF-UCB that matches a derived lower bound for most arms, demonstrating that the algorithm strategically uses low-fidelity evaluations to eliminate suboptimal arms early and reserves high-fidelity queries for promising candidates. Empirical results on synthetic data confirm MF-UCB’s superiority over standard UCB, particularly when low-fidelity costs are significantly lower than high-fidelity costs.

The work builds meaningfully on prior bandit literature while addressing a critical gap in cost-aware exploration. Unlike budgeted bandits [13,15], which optimize reward/cost ratios, this paper models *sequential* fidelity choices with biased approximations—a setting with clear practical relevance in online advertising (e.g., testing ads on smaller audiences first) and algorithm selection (e.g., using smaller datasets for preliminary model evaluation). The authors correctly situate their contribution against related work: multi-fidelity optimization [7,10] lacks regret analysis, robotics applications [6] focus on MDPs without bandit exploration, and active learning with labelers [16] ignores the bandit’s exploration-exploitation trade-off. The connection to Kandasamy et al. [8] (Gaussian process multi-fidelity bandits) is appropriately noted as a complementary extension.

**Strengths:**  
1. **Quality & Originality (High):** The multi-fidelity formalism is novel and well-motivated. The regret bound (Theorem 2) is technically rigorous, with a tight lower bound (Theorem 4) showing near-optimality for most arms. The algorithm’s design—using fidelity-specific confidence bounds and a natural switching criterion (γ(m))—logically follows from the problem structure. Crucially, the regret definition (qt = μ^(M)_k) correctly models real-world applications where low-fidelity outcomes (e.g., short-term ad clicks) do not reflect long-term value, a nuance missed in prior work.  
2. **Clarity (High):** The paper is exceptionally well-organized. The intuition behind K(m) partitions (Fig. 1) and the MF-UCB algorithm (Algorithm 1) are clearly explained. The regret decomposition (Equation 5) and regret bound analysis (Theorem 2) are accessible to experts, with sufficient detail for reproducibility.  
3. **Significance (High):** The results address a pervasive practical challenge—reducing costly evaluations in sequential decision-making. The bound shows MF-UCB achieves regret scaling as λ^(J_kK)/ψ(Δ^(J_kK)_k) instead of λ^(M)/ψ(Δ^(M)_k), yielding substantial gains when |K^(m)| ≪ K and λ^(m) ≪ λ^(M). Simulations (Fig. 2) empirically validate this, demonstrating lower regret and steeper convergence than UCB.  

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Assumption Restriction (Moderate):** Assumption 1 (ζ^(m) decay rate) is necessary for the regret comparison with UCB to be appealing. While the paper notes it’s avoidable for small M, the analysis of MF-UCB’s performance *relative to UCB* relies on it. For applications where ζ^(m) decays slowly (e.g., ζ^(1)=0.5, ζ^(2)=0.49), the regret advantage may vanish. The authors should discuss this limitation and explore whether a weaker condition suffices for the core algorithmic guarantee.  
2. **Empirical Scope (Minor):** The synthetic experiments are convincing but limited to Bernoulli rewards. The theoretical analysis assumes concentration inequalities (Equation 2), yet the empirical validation does not test non-sub-Gaussian cases (e.g., heavy-tailed rewards). Including one such test would strengthen the practical relevance.  
3. **Lower Bound Gap (Minor):** The lower bound (Theorem 4) is tight for arms in K^(m)_3 but loose for K^(m)_7 (where Δ^(m)_k > 0 for some m < M). While the paper correctly explains this gap (MF-UCB avoids over-investing in low fidelities for arms with small Δ^(m)_k), a brief discussion of how to close this gap in future work would add depth.  

**Pro/Con Acceptance Summary:**  
*Pros:* Novel problem formalism with strong theoretical guarantees; clear algorithm design; rigorous regret analysis matching a lower bound; compelling empirical validation; high practical relevance.  
*Cons:* Assumption 1 restricts applicability to scenarios with rapidly decaying ζ^(m); empirical tests limited to Bernoulli rewards.  

**Verdict:** This is a strong, high-quality contribution to NIPS. It advances the state of the art in bandit theory by solving a well-motivated practical problem with a technically sound approach. The paper’s clarity, theoretical depth, and empirical validation make it a significant step forward. The weaknesses are minor and addressable in future work; they do not undermine the core contribution. The work is clearly within NIPS scope and represents a substantial improvement over prior multi-fidelity bandit research. I recommend acceptance.
