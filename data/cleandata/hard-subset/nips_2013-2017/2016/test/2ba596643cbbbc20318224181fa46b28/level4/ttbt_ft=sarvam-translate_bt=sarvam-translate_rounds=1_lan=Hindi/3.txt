This paper presents a "multi-fidelity" version of the multi-armed bandit problem with K arms, each of which can be played at M different levels of "fidelity." Lower fidelity is less expensive, but it potentially introduces more bias in the estimation of the arm's reward. (Drawing an arm at fidelity m yields a sample from a distribution whose expected value deviates from the arm's actual expected reward by an amount whose upper bound depends on the fidelity.) This paper analyzes a natural UCB-based algorithm for this problem, where the privacy width is increased to compensate for the uncertainty arising from the bias of sampling at lower fidelity. The analysis of the algorithm is summarized by a theorem with perturbed regret bounds that are difficult to understand; the paper argues that this regret bound will often be significantly better than the regret bound obtained by always playing arms at the highest fidelity and using the standard UCB algorithm to choose which arm to play. A brief section near the end of the paper presents simulation results to validate this claim. I was initially surprised by why the paper chose to model lower fidelity as introducing more bias rather than more variance. ("Lower fidelity" is also well-motivated by the latter interpretation.) The motivating examples given in the paper (such as algorithm selection for machine learning problems) convinced me that modeling lower fidelity as more bias is well-motivated, although I am still not convinced by one of the motivations (namely, the argument that displaying an advertisement for a short interval yields a biased estimate of the advertisement's effectiveness over a longer interval) and in the example of algorithm selection for machine learning, it is not clear why the algorithm designer's goal in that application would be to minimize regret. So in summary, I thought this paper had a disappointingly weak connection between the model and the motivating applications, but not so weak as to constitute a disqualifying flaw. The main innovation in the paper appears to be the construction of the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit problem. The algorithm is a fairly specific application of the "optimism in the face of uncertainty" principle. The performance guarantee, as mentioned earlier in this review, seems disappointingly difficult to interpret. It is difficult for me to assess the simulation results on synthetic problems: Appendix C details in more detail the set of parameter values that were used in the simulation, but does not attempt to justify these choices of parameter values. In several simulations, the regret of MF-UCB seems to outperform the regret of UCB by a factor of 3 to 4; should we expect this to be specific to the relative performance of the two algorithms in practice, or is it an artifact of the parameter method (such as the values of \zeta^{(m)}) chosen)?
