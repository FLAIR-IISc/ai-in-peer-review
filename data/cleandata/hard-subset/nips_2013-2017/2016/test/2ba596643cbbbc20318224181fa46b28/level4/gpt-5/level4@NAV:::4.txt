The authors present a new framework termed Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit. In this setting, arms can be queried at multiple fidelity levels, each incurring a different cost. Lower-fidelity observations are cheaper and drawn from distributions that approximate the highest-fidelity distribution. The paper proposes an algorithm that adapts to the sequence of available approximations and their costs, provides a regret analysis and a lower bound for the problem, and reports numerical experiments on synthetic tasks where the method outperforms UCB. The framework is interesting and, with further development, could be impactful. However, in its current form I find the paper unsuitable for publication.

1/ The notation is introduced in a confusing and fragmented manner, making definitions hard to locate. For example, I could not find the definition of s (after the rebuttal, it appears s is defined one page after its first use and is then barely used elsewhere).

2/ The advertising example is occasionally misleading. The text repeatedly links ad display time to non-stationarity, which is out of scope for this paper. It may be better to use a different motivating example (see 4/).

3/ I find the regret analysis difficult to justify in this setting. Regret typically reflects long-run gain maximization, hence the best reward-to-cost ratio. Here, however, the benchmark policy is the arm with the highest mean regardless of cost. Moreover, the optimal policy is constrained to sample the best arm at lower fidelity levels, which can obscure the cost of unnecessary low-fidelity sampling.

4/ A sample complexity (here, cost complexity) analysis may be more suitable for Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandits. A motivating scenario is prototype testing in product development: the goal is to identify the best product with high probability while minimizing cumulative testing cost before mass production. Due to regulations, contracts, and facility limitations, companies are often required to rely on low-fidelity approximations before accessing advanced, expensive tests.

5/ Several parts of the text are unwieldy. A thorough editing pass to streamline and clarify the presentation would be beneficial.
