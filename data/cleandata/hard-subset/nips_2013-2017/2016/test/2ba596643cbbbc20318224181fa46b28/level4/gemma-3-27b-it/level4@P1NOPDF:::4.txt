The authors present a new framework called Multi-fidelity Multi-armed bandit, addressing a problem where arms can be sampled with varying levels of fidelity, each associated with a distinct cost. Low-fidelity samples are drawn from a distribution approximating the higher-fidelity one, and the authors provide an algorithm that adapts to sequences of available approximations and costs. They include a regret analysis and a lower bound for the problem, and demonstrate through numerical simulations on synthetic problems that their algorithm outperforms UCB. The proposed framework is interesting and has the potential to be impactful with further development. However, in its current state, I do not believe the paper is suitable for publication.

1/ The introduction of notation is confusing. Definitions are dispersed throughout the text, making them difficult to locate. For example, the variable *s* is not defined until a page after its first use, and is then only sparingly used thereafter.

2/ The use of advertising as an illustrative example is occasionally misleading. The paper frequently draws connections between ad display time and non-stationarity, which feels out of context within the presented framework. A different example might be more appropriate (see point 4).

3/ I find the justification for the regret analysis in this setting questionable. Typically, regret analysis aims to maximize long-term gain and identify the arm with the best reward/cost ratio. Here, the optimal policy consistently favors the arm with the highest mean reward, irrespective of its associated cost. Therefore, the requirement to sample the best arm at lower fidelity levels potentially obscures the true cost of employing these low-fidelity samples when they are unnecessary.

4/ A sample complexity analysis (or, more accurately, a cost complexity analysis) would be highly valuable in the context of Multi-fidelity Multi-armed bandits. This could be motivated by considering the prototype testing phase in new product development. The goal would be to identify the best product with high probability while minimizing the cumulative cost of the testing phase before mass production. Companies are often constrained by regulations, contracts, and existing infrastructure, obligating them to utilize low-fidelity approximations before accessing more advanced and costly tests.

5/ Finally, some sections of the text feel unwieldy and could benefit from streamlining. A dedicated revision pass focused on clarity and conciseness would improve the overall readability of the paper.
