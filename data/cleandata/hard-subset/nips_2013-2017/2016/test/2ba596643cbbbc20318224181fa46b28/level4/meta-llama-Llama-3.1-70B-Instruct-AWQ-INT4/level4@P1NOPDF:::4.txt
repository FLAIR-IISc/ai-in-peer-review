The authors propose a new framework called Multi-fidelity Multi-armed bandit. In this problem, arms can be sampled with different levels of fidelity, each associated with a cost. While lower-cost, low-fidelity samples are drawn from a distribution approximating the highest fidelity distribution, the authors provide an algorithm that adapts to the sequences of available approximations and cost, its regret analysis, and a lower bound of the problem. They also provide numerical simulations on synthetic problems where the proposed algorithm outperforms UCB. The proposed framework is interesting and has the potential to be impactful if further developed. However, in its current state, I found the paper unfit for publication.

Several issues detract from the paper's overall quality. Firstly, the introduction of notations is confusing, as they are dispersed throughout the text, making their definitions difficult to find. For instance, I did not find a clear definition of $s$ (which, after the rebuttal, appears to be defined one page after its first use and is then barely used in the rest of the paper).

Secondly, the use of advertising as an example is misleading at some points. The authors draw correlations between display time and non-stationarity, which is out of context in this paper. Non-stationarity is not relevant to the problem at hand. It may be beneficial to use an alternative example (see point 4).

Thirdly, I find it challenging to justify the regret analysis in this setting. A regret analysis typically involves maximizing gain in the long run and finding the arm with the best reward-to-cost ratio. However, in this case, the optimal policy always chooses the arm with the highest mean, regardless of the associated cost. This weakens the optimal policy by obligating the sampling of the best arm at lower fidelity levels, potentially hiding the cost of sampling low levels when it is not necessary.

Fourthly, a sample complexity analysis (or rather, a cost complexity analysis) would be useful in the context of Multi-fidelity Multi-armed bandits. This could be motivated by the testing phase of prototypes for the conception of new products. The goal is to find, with high probability, the best product while minimizing the cumulative cost of the test phase before mass production. Companies are often bound by regulations, contracts, and existing facilities, obligating them to use low-fidelity approximations before accessing advanced and costly tests.

Lastly, some parts of the text are unwieldy and could benefit from revision. It may be worth revising the paper to make it more streamlined and easier to follow.
