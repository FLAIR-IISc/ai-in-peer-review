This paper addresses the multi-armed bandit problem where, for each arm pull, the learner must also choose a "fidelity" level from a finite set. Pulling an arm at a given fidelity yields a stochastic reward whose mean is guaranteed to be within a known error bound, `zeta_m`, of the true mean. Higher fidelity levels have lower error (with `zeta = 0` for the highest fidelity) but incur a greater cost. The authors frame this as a problem of maximizing a net reward, propose a new algorithm called MF-UCB, and argue that it can outperform standard UCB algorithms that operate only at the highest fidelity. The paper also provides a lower bound on the regret for this problem.

The paper studies an interesting and relevant problem: modeling the trade-off between the cost and quality of information in online learning. In this respect, the work could serve as a useful benchmark for future research. However, while the paper appears technically solid, a key shortcoming is the inadequate explanation of its core assumptions and results.

Several aspects of the paper lack sufficient justification. First, the proposed regret definition, which penalizes resource consumption multiplicatively, seems unnatural. The ad-display example used for motivation may not be general enough, and an analysis of the more standard additive cost model—where cost is subtracted from the total reward—would be a more natural and valuable contribution.

Second, Assumption 1, which places constraints on the fidelity error parameters `zeta_m`, appears artificial and lacks clear motivation. It is unclear why this assumption would hold in a general learning problem. The authors mention that the assumption is not crucial, yet it is used in the proofs and appears to critically influence the algorithm's design and performance. The paper would be significantly strengthened if the authors could provide a general method for selecting a subsequence of fidelities that satisfies the assumption when the original set does not. As it stands, the algorithm seems tailored to specific `zeta` configurations without justifying why these are the right ones to consider.

Third, the interpretation of the main result (Theorem 2, the regret bound for MF-UCB) is difficult. The comparison to standard UCB is not straightforward, partly because the baseline (presumably UCB at the highest fidelity) is not explicitly stated in the discussion. The relative performance of MF-UCB depends on `Delta` and `[[k]]`, which in turn depend on a complicated sequence of sets, `\cal{K}^(m)`. A more explicit explanation of these terms and their implications is needed to make the case for the proposed algorithm's benefits. Furthermore, the noted gap in the lower bound warrants more investigation and might suggest that a re-design of the proposed strategy is necessary.

Overall, while the paper introduces a compelling learning model, the presentation rapidly loses clarity when presenting the assumptions, the algorithm's design choices, and the final results.
