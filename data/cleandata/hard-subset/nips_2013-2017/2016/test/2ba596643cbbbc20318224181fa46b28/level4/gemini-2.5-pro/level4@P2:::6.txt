This paper introduces the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit, a novel and practical variant of the classical stochastic K-armed bandit problem. The key idea is that for each arm, the learner can choose from one of M "fidelities" for each pull. Lower fidelities are cheaper to sample but provide a more biased or noisy estimate of the true reward, while higher fidelities are more expensive but more accurate. The authors propose that an effective strategy can leverage these cheap, lower-fidelity samples to quickly eliminate highly suboptimal arms, reserving the expensive, high-fidelity evaluations for a smaller set of promising candidates. This setting is well-motivated by real-world applications such as online advertising or automated algorithm selection, where one can often run cheaper, shorter, or smaller-scale experiments to approximate the outcome of a full-scale evaluation.

The main contributions of the paper are: (1) a formal definition of the multi-fidelity bandit problem, including a cost-aware notion of regret; (2) a new algorithm, MF-UCB, which extends the classic UCB approach to this new setting; (3) a thorough theoretical analysis providing a regret upper bound for MF-UCB and a corresponding lower bound for the problem, demonstrating the near-optimality of the algorithm under certain conditions; and (4) simulation results that validate the theoretical claims and show that MF-UCB outperforms a standard UCB baseline.

**Strengths / Arguments for Acceptance:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper formulates a novel and well-motivated problem that captures a practical aspect of sequential decision-making often overlooked in standard bandit literature. The idea of trading off evaluation cost and accuracy is highly relevant, and this work provides the first rigorous, regret-based analysis for it in the bandit setting. The proposed framework is likely to be influential and could inspire further research in this direction.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is comprehensive and technically sound. The authors provide a detailed regret analysis for the proposed MF-UCB algorithm, which clearly demonstrates how the algorithm benefits from lower fidelities. Crucially, they also derive a problem-specific lower bound, which shows that their algorithm's performance is nearly optimal. This constitutes a complete and significant theoretical contribution.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The motivation for the problem is well-explained with concrete examples. The algorithm is presented intuitively, and the analysis, though complex, is structured in a way that highlights the key ideas. The empirical results in the simulations effectively support the theoretical findings.

**Weaknesses / Areas for Improvement:**

The primary concern revolves around a key assumption required for the algorithm and its analysis. The algorithm assumes that the bias parameters, ζ(m) (the maximum deviation of the m-th fidelity's mean from the highest fidelity's mean), are known in advance. While this assumption is necessary for the theoretical guarantees, its practical feasibility is not immediately obvious. For instance, in the online advertising example, how would one determine a priori the precise maximum bias introduced by displaying an ad for a shorter duration?

It would strengthen the paper significantly if the authors could discuss the practicality of this assumption. For example:
*   Could they provide guidance on how these parameters might be estimated or bounded in real-world scenarios?
*   How sensitive is the MF-UCB algorithm to a mis-specification of the ζ(m) values? A discussion on the robustness of the algorithm would be valuable.
*   Is it possible to relax this assumption, perhaps by learning these bias terms online?

This is the main point that could limit the direct applicability of the proposed method, and addressing it would enhance the paper's impact.

**Detailed Comments and Minor Corrections:**

The paper is of high quality, but I found several typos, mostly in the appendix/proofs, which should be corrected.

1.  Line 326, 357, and 386: In the expression "... arm k ∈ H^(m)", the set 'H' should be 'K'.
2.  Line 343: In the last indicator term of the inequality, the argument "B_{k*,t} ≤ s" should likely be "B_{k*,t} ≤ μ*".
3.  Line 345: γ should be γ^(m).
4.  Line 346-347: In the phrase "... and the last step uses ψ(Δk^(m) - γ^(m)) ≤ ψ(γ^(m)) when ψ(Δk^(m)) ≤ 2γ^(m)", both instances of '≤' should be '≥'. The argument holds when Δk^(m) ≥ 2γ^(m), which implies ψ(Δk^(m) - γ^(m)) ≥ ψ(γ^(m)).
5.  Line 384: In the first inequality, the inner sum on the left side is over "all k ∈ those 'above' K^(m)". This seems incorrect; it should probably be a sum over partitions "below or equal to" K^(m), i.e., ∪_{j=1 to m} K^(j). Please clarify.
6.  Line 407: On the right side of the final inequality, δ^(m) should be δ^(M). Additionally, the last term μ*κλ^(m) appears to be missing a μ* based on the definition of regret; it should likely be κλ^(m)μ*.
7.  Line 431: "for l ≥ m" should be "for l ≥ p".
8.  Line 440: In Equation (17), the condition "l ≥ m" should be "l ≥ p".
9.  Line 450: P(A_{n,3}) should be P(A_{n,2}).
