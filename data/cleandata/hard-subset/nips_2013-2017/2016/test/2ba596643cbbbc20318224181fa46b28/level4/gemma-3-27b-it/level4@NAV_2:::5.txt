This paper investigates a multi-fidelity variant of the classic K-armed bandit problem, motivated by scenarios where evaluating an arm's performance is costly but cheaper approximations are readily available. The authors formalize this as a multi-fidelity bandit, introducing a cost λ for high-fidelity evaluations and λ < λ for approximations, which provide biased estimates. They propose MF-UCB, a novel upper confidence bound algorithm tailored to this setting, and prove it achieves improved regret compared to naive strategies that disregard approximations. A lower bound is also established, demonstrating near-optimality under certain conditions. 

The paper clearly articulates the problem and its relevance to real-world applications like online advertising and algorithm selection, building upon the established foundation of bandit algorithms, notably the UCB approach [3, 9, 12]. The introduction effectively motivates the multi-fidelity setting and highlights how it differs from existing work on budgeted bandits [13, 15] and multi-fidelity optimization [7, 10], as well as active learning with weak and strong labelers [16].  The authors appropriately reference foundational work by Robbins [11] and related UCB developments [2, 5].

A key strength of the paper lies in its rigorous theoretical analysis. The regret bound for MF-UCB, presented in Theorem 2, is well-defined and supported by detailed arguments in the appendix. The comparison to the standard UCB algorithm's performance is insightful. The accompanying lower bound (Theorem 4) strengthens the claims of near-optimality and provides a valuable benchmark. The inclusion of Assumption 1 and a discussion of its impact on performance demonstrate careful consideration of the algorithm’s limitations. The presentation of the proof sketches provides useful insights into the technical details.

However, the paper could benefit from further clarity in a few areas. The notation, while generally consistent, can be dense.  Expanding on the intuition behind the choice of γ(m) in Algorithm 1 beyond its current justification would be helpful.  While the simulations demonstrate the efficacy of MF-UCB, more detailed parameter settings and a wider range of problem instances would further strengthen the empirical validation. It is also unclear how sensitive the results are to the accuracy of the ζ(m) values (representing the bias of the lower fidelities). A sensitivity analysis could be added.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The multi-fidelity bandit framework is a novel extension of the classical bandit problem with clear practical relevance.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides strong theoretical guarantees for the performance of MF-UCB, including a regret bound and a lower bound demonstrating near-optimality.
*   **Sound Methodology:** The proposed algorithm, MF-UCB, is a logical adaptation of UCB principles to the multi-fidelity setting.
*   **Empirical Validation:** Simulations provide supporting evidence for the algorithm's effectiveness.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   **Notational Density:** The notation can be challenging for readers unfamiliar with the field.
*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The simulations could be expanded to provide a more comprehensive assessment of the algorithm’s performance.
*   **Sensitivity Analysis:** The sensitivity to the assumed bias (ζ(m)) could be explored in more detail.



Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of bandit optimization. The novelty of the problem formulation, the rigor of the theoretical analysis, and the promising empirical results outweigh the minor areas for improvement. I recommend acceptance.
