This paper addresses a novel and practical variant of the classical stochastic K-armed bandit problem, where cheap, biased approximations for the reward of each arm are available. The authors formalize this setting as a "multi-fidelity bandit" problem, where each arm can be played at one of several fidelities, with lower fidelities being less expensive but providing less accurate reward estimates. The core idea is to use the cheap fidelities to quickly eliminate clearly suboptimal arms and reserve expensive, high-fidelity evaluations for a small set of promising candidates. The authors propose a new algorithm, the Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB), and provide a rigorous theoretical analysis of its performance, including a regret upper bound for the algorithm and a corresponding lower bound for the problem itself. The theoretical findings are supported by simulations on synthetic problems.

**Strengths**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The multi-fidelity bandit formulation is a novel and significant contribution. It elegantly models a common feature of real-world applications like online advertising, clinical trials, or large-scale simulations, where the cost of gathering information is a critical constraint and cheap approximations are often available. This work opens a new and promising research direction by formally incorporating variable evaluation costs and accuracies into the bandit framework. The ideas presented are likely to be built upon by other researchers and used by practitioners.

*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically strong. It provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis of the proposed MF-UCB algorithm. The derivation of the regret upper bound clearly shows how the algorithm leverages the multi-fidelity structure to achieve better performance than naive strategies that ignore this information. A major strength is the inclusion of a problem-specific lower bound, which is used to demonstrate that MF-UCB is nearly optimal under certain conditions. The analysis appears sound and carefully considers the new challenges introduced by the multi-fidelity setting.

*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is very well-written, well-organized, and easy to understand. The motivation for the problem is clearly articulated, the algorithm is presented with strong intuition, and the theoretical results are explained thoroughly. The use of arm partitions (K(m)) to explain the algorithm's behavior and structure the proof is particularly effective and aids in reader comprehension.

*   **Empirical Validation:** The theoretical claims are well-supported by experimental results. The simulations on synthetic data clearly demonstrate that MF-UCB outperforms the standard UCB algorithm, corroborating the central claim that intelligently using lower fidelities leads to lower overall regret. The plots showing the distribution of plays across fidelities provide compelling insight into the algorithm's practical behavior, confirming that it successfully screens out poor arms at low cost.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

The paper is a strong submission, and the following points are minor suggestions rather than significant weaknesses.

*   The authors are commendably transparent about the gap between their upper bound and the lower bound for a specific subset of arms (those in K(m)7), correctly identifying this as an open problem. This honesty strengthens the paper. While the algorithm is already shown to be nearly optimal for a large class of arms, this gap highlights an interesting direction for future theoretical refinement.

*   Assumption 1, which governs the decay rate of the approximation error ζ(m), is well-motivated for simplifying the analysis and making the comparison to standard UCB more direct. A brief discussion on how the algorithm's performance or the theoretical bounds might change if this assumption were relaxed could be a valuable addition for the reader.

In summary, this is an excellent paper that introduces a novel problem, proposes an effective and intuitive algorithm, and provides a thorough theoretical and empirical validation. The work is a valuable contribution to the multi-armed bandit literature.
