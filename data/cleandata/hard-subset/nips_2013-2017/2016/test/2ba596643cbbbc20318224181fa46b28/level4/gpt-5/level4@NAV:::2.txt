This paper studies a novel multi-armed bandit setting in which each arm can be pulled at multiple accuracies (fidelities). The cost of a pull increases with the desired accuracy, and the regret is defined as the product of the sampling cost and the standard pseudo-regret. The authors introduce an algorithm tailored to this setting, MF-UCB, and establish both upper and lower bounds on its cumulative pseudo-regret. The main manuscript is clear and well written. This is less true of the supplementary material, where most proofs appear: it contains numerous typos and minor, easily fixable mistakes that can confuse readers (see a non-exhaustive list below). Correcting these would greatly enhance readability. In addition, the frequent use of “up to a multiplicative constant” notation can be somewhat misleading, since some of the so-called constants depend on algorithmic parameters; for example, in Theorem 2, a factor ρ is absorbed into the constant. Using the exact inequalities (as presented in the supplement) would improve the clarity of the results. From a practical perspective, it is not obvious that the algorithm can be readily deployed in real-world applications. For instance, MF-UCB’s reliance on low-fidelity pulls to eliminate clearly suboptimal arms could be brittle if the fidelity calibration ζ (the accuracy parameter) is misspecified. A discussion of these practical limitations and possible mitigations would strengthen the paper. Theoretically, the work offers a clear view of the problem, with interesting theorems and solid proofs. Overall, I consider this a significant and compelling contribution.
