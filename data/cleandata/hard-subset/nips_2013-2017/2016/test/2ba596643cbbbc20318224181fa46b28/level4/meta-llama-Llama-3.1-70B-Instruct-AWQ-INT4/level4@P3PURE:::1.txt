The paper models multi-armed bandit problems with a fidelity component, where the learner chooses an arm's fidelity from a finite set. An arm pulled at a certain fidelity yields a stochastic, iid reward with a mean within zeta_m of the true mean, where zeta_m parameters (errors) are known a priori, and the highest fidelity level corresponds to zeta = 0. Furthermore, smaller fidelity pulls are cheaper than higher fidelity pulls. The authors frame the problem of sequentially pulling arms with fidelities to maximize a notion of net reward, propose a new algorithm for the problem, and argue that it can outperform UCB operating at only high fidelity levels. The paper also provides a lower bound for the regret of the proposed algorithm.

In my opinion, the paper studies an interesting and relevant problem - modeling the tradeoff between information, cost, and reward (whether to choose low information that is cheap or high information that is expensive) - in online learning, specifically stochastic bandits. This problem may serve as a useful benchmark to improve upon. Although the paper appears to be technically solid, a key shortcoming is the lack of adequate explanation about its results and assumptions.

The regret definition adopted seems unnatural from at least one angle - why not penalize resource consumption (or 'cost') additively instead of multiplicatively, as done here? The authors' example of ad display motivates their definition, but may not be the most general. It would be beneficial to know what happens when the cost paid for suitable fidelity observations is additively factored into the total reward (or regret). The introduction of Assumption 1 seems rather artificial and opaque, involving constraints on the fidelity parameters given to the learner. It is unclear why the authors would expect this assumption to hold in a general learning problem. More importantly, if the zeta_m's do not decay as fast as required by Assumption 1, is there a general way of selecting a subsequence of fidelities to satisfy the assumption and hence the results? This would significantly help strengthen the paper's message.

Although the authors mention that the assumption is not crucial, it appears to be used in the proofs, and my main concern is whether the assumption influences the algorithm's design and performance in a critical fashion. The algorithm's design seems tailored towards performing well for specific kinds of zeta configurations, but the justification for why these are the 'right' zeta sequences is missing. The gap in the lower bound also demands more investigation and perhaps a re-design of the proposed strategy. Interpreting the main result (Theorem 2, the regret bound for MF-UCB) and contrasting it with UCB (at the highest fidelity, although this is not explicitly stated in the discussion) is not easy to grasp due to the highly technical nature of the presentation. The relative gain/loss of MF-UCB appears to depend on Delta and [[k]], which in turn depend on the rather complicated sequence of sets \cal{K}^(m). A more explicit explanation would greatly help the authors' case.

Overall, I feel that the paper gets off to a good start in terms of considering an interesting learning model, but rapidly loses clarity when presenting the results. Minor corrections include: line 56, "near-optimal"; line 126, unnecessary period before "for all m < M"; and line 179, using set notation (curly braces) to define [[k]] would be more suitable.
