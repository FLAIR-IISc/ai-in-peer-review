

This paper introduces a novel multi-armed bandit framework where each arm can be observed at multiple fidelities, each providing a progressively better approximation of the true reward at increasing cost. The authors formalize this as a multi-fidelity bandit problem, where a strategy can choose to play an arm at any of M fidelities, with higher fidelities providing better reward estimates but at greater cost. They propose MF-UCB, an algorithm that leverages cheaper lower fidelities to quickly eliminate suboptimal arms and reserves expensive higher fidelities for a small subset of promising arms, thereby achieving better regret than standard UCB that ignores fidelity information. The authors develop a new regret definition accounting for cost, provide theoretical analysis including a regret upper bound and lower bound, and demonstrate empirical superiority over UCB in simulations. The work extends the classical bandit literature to settings where cheaper approximations of outcomes are available, with potential applications in online advertising (where shorter ad displays can approximate long-term click-through rates) and algorithm selection (where cheaper model evaluations can approximate full training performance). The theoretical analysis is sound and addresses the new challenges posed by the multi-fidelity setting, building upon UCB methods while introducing significant algorithmic and theoretical innovations. The main concern is the assumption that the deviation ε^(m) between the m-th fidelity mean and the highest fidelity mean is known in advance - this is a strong assumption that requires justification in practical applications like online advertising where such deviations would typically need to be estimated. Addressing this assumption would strengthen the paper's applicability. The paper makes a significant contribution to bandit literature with well-motivated problem formulation, sound theoretical analysis, and empirical validation. The main concern about the assumption of known ε^(m) is the only substantial weakness, but the work is otherwise technically sound and makes a valuable contribution to the field.
