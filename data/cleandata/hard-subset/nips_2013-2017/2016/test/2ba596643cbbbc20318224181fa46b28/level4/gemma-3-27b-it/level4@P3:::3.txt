This paper introduces a novel multi-fidelity extension to the classic multi-armed bandit (MAB) problem. The key idea is to allow for cheaper, lower-fidelity evaluations of each arm, which introduce bias but reduce cost. The authors develop MF-UCB, a UCB-based algorithm adapted to this setting, and provide theoretical regret bounds demonstrating its improved performance over a naive UCB strategy that only utilizes the highest fidelity. 

This work builds upon the extensive literature on MABs [11, 9, 12] and UCB algorithms [3, 2]. The concept of leveraging cheaper approximations connects to related work in multi-fidelity optimization [7, 10] and reinforcement learning [6], but importantly, this paper formalizes and analyzes regret in this multi-fidelity bandit setting, a contribution missing from those areas. While budgeted bandit problems [13, 15] consider cost constraints, they differ from this work’s focus on *approximations* to the reward itself. Recent work by Kandasamy et al. [8] on multi-fidelity bandits with Gaussian processes complements this paper, though the focus and theoretical results differ.

The strengths of the paper lie in the clear formulation of the multi-fidelity bandit problem and the development of MF-UCB, a natural extension of UCB. The theoretical analysis, including the regret bound and the associated lower bound, demonstrates a strong understanding of the problem’s challenges and the algorithm’s performance. The simulations, while synthetic, offer initial evidence supporting the theoretical findings. 

However, the paper also has weaknesses. The regret bound (Theorem 2) is complex and difficult to interpret intuitively. While the authors state conditions under which MF-UCB outperforms standard UCB, translating this into practical expectations is challenging. The connection between the model and the motivating applications feels somewhat weak. For example, the claim regarding biased estimates from shorter ad displays feels debatable. Although the authors acknowledge potential alternative interpretations (e.g., increased variance), they don’t fully explore these. Furthermore, the simulations lack justification for the chosen parameter values. The improvement of 3-4x observed in the simulations could be sensitive to these choices, and more comprehensive sensitivity analysis or real-world experiments would strengthen the findings. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The multi-fidelity bandit formulation is a new and potentially impactful contribution.
*   **Soundness:** The algorithm is well-motivated, and the theoretical analysis appears rigorous.
*   **Potential Significance:**  The problem is relevant to many practical applications where cheap approximations are available, and the improved regret bounds suggest a tangible benefit.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Complexity:** The regret bound is difficult to interpret and limits the practical utility of the theoretical results.
*   **Weak Motivation:** The connection to motivating applications is not entirely convincing.
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The simulations, while supportive, are synthetic and lack parameter justification.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid scientific contribution. While the complex regret bound and somewhat weak connection to motivating examples are concerns, the novelty and theoretical rigor of the work are significant. I recommend **acceptance**, but with the caveat that the authors consider simplifying the presentation of their results and providing a more thorough justification for the simulation parameters. Future work exploring the practical implications and robustness of MF-UCB would be valuable.
