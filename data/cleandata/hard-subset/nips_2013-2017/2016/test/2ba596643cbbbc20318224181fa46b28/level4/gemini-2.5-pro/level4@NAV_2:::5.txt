This paper introduces a novel and practical variant of the stochastic K-armed bandit problem, termed the multi-fidelity bandit. The key idea is that for each arm, in addition to an expensive, high-fidelity observation, one has access to a hierarchy of cheaper, but biased, lower-fidelity approximations. The authors formalize this setting with a cost-weighted notion of regret. The primary contribution is the Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB) algorithm, which intelligently leverages the different fidelities. The intuition is to use cheap approximations to quickly identify and discard clearly suboptimal arms, thereby reserving expensive, high-fidelity pulls for a smaller set of promising candidates. This work builds upon the classical UCB framework for bandits but addresses a new problem structure with significant algorithmic and theoretical challenges. It is distinguished from related work in budgeted bandits, which typically considers a reward/cost ratio, and from active learning with weak/strong labelers, which does not focus on the cumulative regret setting.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The problem formulation is novel and well-motivated, capturing a common scenario in many real-world applications such as online advertising, hyperparameter tuning, and scientific experimentation, where preliminary or simulated results can guide more costly, definitive tests. The formalization of the multi-fidelity setting and the corresponding cost-aware regret is a significant contribution to the bandit literature.
*   **Technical Quality:** The proposed MF-UCB algorithm is an elegant and intuitive extension of the UCB principle. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides a clear characterization of the algorithm's performance. The regret bound insightfully shows how the cost incurred for a suboptimal arm scales with the cost of the lowest fidelity at which it can be identified as poor, which is the core of the method's advantage. The inclusion of a lower bound that demonstrates the near-optimality of MF-UCB under specific conditions is a strong theoretical result.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly articulated through practical examples. The problem setup, algorithm, and theoretical claims are presented in a logical and organized manner. The graphical illustration (Fig. 1) and the partitioning of the arm set (the `K(m)` sets) are particularly effective in conveying the intuition behind the analysis.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Practicality of Assumptions:** The analysis requires that the bias bounds `ζ(m)` for each fidelity level are known in advance. In practice, estimating or bounding these biases might be a challenging problem in itself. It would be beneficial for the authors to briefly discuss the sensitivity of the algorithm to misspecification of these parameters or potential ways to learn them online.
*   **Gap in Theoretical Bounds:** As the authors correctly identify, there is a gap between the upper and lower regret bounds for a particular class of arms (`K(m)7`). While the explanation for this gap is clear, it suggests that the proposed fidelity-switching criterion may not be optimal in all cases. This is a minor point that does not detract from the main contribution but highlights an interesting avenue for future research.
*   **Synthetic Experiments:** The simulations, while effectively corroborating the theoretical analysis, are performed on synthetic datasets. While this is standard for this type of theoretical work, the paper's impact could be further amplified by demonstrating its performance on a semi-synthetic or real-world problem that exemplifies the multi-fidelity structure.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that addresses an important and practical problem with a well-designed algorithm and strong theoretical backing.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Introduces a novel and significant extension to the multi-armed bandit framework with clear real-world relevance.
*   Presents an intuitive and effective algorithm, MF-UCB.
*   Provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis, including regret upper bounds and a near-matching lower bound, which clearly explains the benefits of the multi-fidelity approach.
*   The paper is very well-written and the claims are well-supported.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The assumption of known bias bounds `ζ(m)` might limit direct applicability in some domains.
*   The experiments are limited to synthetic data.
