This paper introduces a multi-fidelity extension to the classic multi-armed bandit problem. The framework considers *K* arms, each playable at *M* distinct fidelity levels, where lower fidelity pulls are less expensive but introduce potential bias in reward estimation – specifically, the expected value of the observed reward deviates from the true arm reward, with the magnitude of this deviation bounded by the fidelity level. The authors analyze a UCB-based algorithm adapted for this setting, increasing the confidence width to account for fidelity-induced bias. The resulting regret bounds, while formally presented in theorems, are complex and difficult to readily interpret. The paper argues that this algorithm achieves improved regret performance compared to a standard UCB approach applied with only high-fidelity samples. Supporting simulation results are provided.

My initial reaction questioned the modeling choice of lower fidelity as introducing bias rather than variance, as the latter seems intuitively plausible. However, the provided motivating examples – particularly those concerning algorithm selection – justified this formulation, although the rationale for the advertising example remains less convincing. Furthermore, the objective of regret minimization in the algorithm selection context isn’t entirely clear from a practical algorithm design perspective. Overall, the connection between the model and its motivating applications feels somewhat tenuous, though not critically flawed. 

The primary contribution appears to be the problem formulation itself. The proposed algorithm represents a standard application of the optimism principle. As previously noted, the theoretical performance guarantees are difficult to interpret. The simulation results, while demonstrating potential improvements, are challenging to evaluate fully; the parameter choices in Appendix C are detailed but lack justification. The observed 3-4x improvement of MF-UCB over UCB in the simulations raises the question of whether this performance gain is representative of typical behavior or a consequence of the specific parameter settings (e.g., the values of ζ<sup>(m)</sup>).
