This paper proposes a “multi-fidelity” extension of the stochastic K-armed bandit in which each arm can be queried at one of M fidelity levels. Lower fidelities cost less but yield observations with bounded bias relative to the highest-fidelity reward. Concretely, pulling arm k at fidelity m draws from a distribution whose mean differs from the true high-fidelity mean by at most a fidelity-dependent bound. The authors analyze a natural UCB-style approach tailored to this setting, in which the confidence bounds are adjusted to account for fidelity-induced bias (not just statistical variance). Theoretical results provide regret guarantees that, while algebraically involved and not easy to parse at a glance, are argued to be strictly better than those of a strategy that ignores low fidelities and runs standard UCB exclusively at the highest fidelity. A short empirical section with synthetic experiments supports this claim.

At first, I was unsure why the model treats low fidelity primarily as introducing bias rather than additional variance. While I can imagine scenarios where variance inflation is a plausible abstraction, the paper’s motivating use cases (e.g., algorithm selection with reduced data/computation) make a bias-centric formulation reasonable. That said, I remain unconvinced by one of the examples (the claim that short-time ad displays systematically bias long-term effectiveness), and for algorithm selection it is not obvious that regret minimization is the most relevant objective for a practitioner. In sum, the connection between the model and its applications feels thinner than ideal, though not to the extent of undermining the contribution.

The main novelty lies in formalizing the multi-fidelity bandit problem itself. The algorithmic instantiation is a straightforward application of optimism under uncertainty adapted to the multi-fidelity structure. As noted above, the performance bounds are difficult to interpret directly. The simulation study on synthetic data is similarly challenging to evaluate: Appendix C specifies the parameter settings but does not justify them, and thus it is hard to gauge how representative they are. The reported improvements—often a 3–4× reduction in regret for MF-UCB over UCB—are encouraging, but it is unclear whether this gap is typical in practice or a consequence of particular choices (e.g., the values of ζ(m)).
