- Paper introduces a multi-fidelity stochastic K-armed bandit where each arm can be played at M fidelities with cheaper biased approximations and defines a cost-weighted regret for this setting.

- Proposes MF-UCB, an adaptive UCB-based algorithm that uses low fidelities to eliminate bad arms and reserves high fidelities for promising ones.

- Provides regret upper bounds showing MF-UCB can outperform standard UCB and a lower bound demonstrating near-optimality in many cases.

- Experimental results on synthetic data corroborate theory and show MF-UCB concentrates high-fidelity plays on near-optimal arms.

- Novel problem formulation capturing practical scenarios with biased cheap approximations (e.g., online advertising, algorithm selection).

- Principled regret definition aligned with applications where only highest-fidelity outcomes ultimately matter.

- Intuitive, well-motivated algorithm that adaptively chooses arm and fidelity using confidence bands and thresholds.

- Strong theoretical analysis including matching upper and (in many cases) lower bounds clarifying when multi-fidelity helps.

- Empirical validation supports theoretical claims and practical effectiveness.

- Main weakness: analysis assumes known fidelity bias bounds ε^(m) that decrease with fidelity; practical estimation or online learning of these is not addressed.

- Requests for discussion or methods to estimate/learn ε^(m) online with guarantees, or to relax analysis to data-driven/distribution-free bounds.

- Suggests adding robustness analysis for misspecified ε^(m) (overly optimistic/pessimistic) and impact on switching thresholds γ^(m) and regret.

- Quality: technical development and regret analysis are sound with clear intuition for switching rules and a revealing lower bound.

- Clarity: generally well written and organized but contains several minor notational and typographical errors to fix.

- Originality: multi-fidelity bandit setting and regret formulation are novel and extend UCB techniques nontrivially.

- Significance: setting is realistic and results offer both conceptual and practical insights for leveraging cheap approximations.

- Pros for acceptance: clear timely formulation, new algorithm with strong theory, empirical alignment with theory, and broad applicability.

- Cons for acceptance: strong reliance on known ε^(m) without practical estimation/robustness discussion; minor clarity/notation issues.
