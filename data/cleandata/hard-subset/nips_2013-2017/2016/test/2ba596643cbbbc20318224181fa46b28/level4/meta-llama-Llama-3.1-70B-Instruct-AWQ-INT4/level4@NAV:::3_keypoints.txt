- Proposes a multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit model: K arms, M fidelity levels where lower fidelity is cheaper but introduces fidelity-dependent bias in reward estimates.
- Adapts a UCB-style algorithm by inflating confidence widths to account for low-fidelity bias.
- Theoretical analysis yields complex regret bounds that are hard to interpret.
- Authors claim the bounds often outperform always-playing-highest-fidelity UCB; simulations are presented to support this.
- Modeling low fidelity as bias (not variance) initially seemed odd but is justified by some motivating examples (e.g., algorithm selection).
- The ad-display example as a motivation for bias is unconvincing.
- In the machine-learning algorithm-selection motivation, it’s unclear why minimizing regret is the primary objective.
- Overall connection between model and applications is somewhat weak but not a fatal flaw.
- Main novelty is the problem formulation; the algorithm itself is a fairly standard application of optimism/UCB.
- Performance guarantees are disappointingly opaque and difficult to interpret.
- Simulation section lacks justification for chosen parameters, making results hard to assess.
- Reported 3–4× improvement of MF-UCB over UCB may be an artifact of parameter choices rather than typical performance.
