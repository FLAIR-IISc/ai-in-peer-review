This paper introduces and studies a novel multi-armed bandit setting in which the learner can play any arm at one of $M$ different fidelity levels. Each successive fidelity level provides a more accurate approximation of the true rewards but at a greater cost. The core idea is that an effective strategy can leverage low-cost, low-fidelity pulls to quickly eliminate sub-optimal arms, reserving the more expensive, high-fidelity pulls for a small subset of promising arms. Such a strategy could outperform standard algorithms like UCB, which do not account for this cost-fidelity trade-off.

The authors motivate this setting with a practical application in online advertising, where displaying an ad for a short period can approximate long-term click rates more cost-effectively. For this problem, the paper's contributions include a new regret definition tailored to the setting, a novel algorithm with a corresponding theoretical analysis (including a regret lower bound), and supporting simulations. The analysis appears to be novel and is a key contribution.

My main concern relates to a key assumption in the theoretical analysis. It is assumed that $\varepsilon^{(m)}$, the deviation of the $m$-th fidelity's mean reward from that of the highest fidelity, is known in advance. This raises a practical question: how would these values be known in real-world applications, such as the online advertising example provided? The paper would be significantly strengthened if the authors could address the practical implications of this assumption or discuss whether it is possible to relax it.
