- Introduces a novel multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit framework where each arm can be pulled at multiple fidelity levels with different costs and accuracies.  
- Formalizes the problem with a cost-weighted regret measure that accounts for observation costs and incurred regret.  
- Proposes MF-UCB, an algorithm that adaptively selects fidelity levels, using cheap low-fidelity pulls to eliminate suboptimal arms and reserving high-fidelity evaluations for promising arms.  
- Proves near-optimal regret bounds for MF-UCB and provides a matching lower bound under specified conditions.  
- Demonstrates theoretical improvement over standard UCB, showing gains proportional to the cost ratio of lower to highest fidelity for certain arms.  
- Builds on prior bandit and multi-fidelity literature while filling a gap by providing rigorous regret analysis for the multi-fidelity setting.  
- Theoretical analysis carefully examines interplay between fidelity levels, costs, and regret and is presented thoroughly.  
- Paper is well-structured, clearly written, and uses practical examples (online advertising, algorithm selection) to motivate the problem.  
- Empirical validation on synthetic problems is strong and supports the theoretical claims.  
- Supplementary materials contain numerous typos, notation inconsistencies, and errors that hinder readability and make proof verification difficult.  
- Use of "up to a multiplicative constant" notation is misleading because constants hide dependence on algorithm parameters (e.g., ρ); exact inequalities should be shown.  
- Practical issues are under-addressed: the algorithm assumes knowledge of fidelity biases ζ(m) and does not discuss estimation or robustness to misspecification.  
- Strengths: novel framework, rigorous/tight theory, clear presentation, strong empirical validation, and high practical relevance.  
- Weaknesses: supplementary errors, obscured parameter dependence in asymptotic notation, limited discussion of practical implementation and bias assumption failures.  
- Overall assessment: strong, theoretically sound contribution with practical relevance that requires minor revisions (fix supplementary errors and add discussion of practical limitations).
