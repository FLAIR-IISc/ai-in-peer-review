The authors present a new framework, the Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit. In this setting, each arm can be queried at multiple fidelity levels, each incurring a distinct cost; lower-fidelity queries are cheaper but provide biased approximations of the highest-fidelity outcome. The paper proposes an algorithm that adapts to the sequence of available approximations and costs (MF-UCB), together with a regret analysis and a problem-specific lower bound. It also includes simulations on synthetic tasks showing the proposed method outperforming UCB. The framework is interesting and, with further development, could be impactful. However, in its current form, I do not find the paper suitable for publication.

1/ The introduction of notation is confusing: symbols are scattered throughout the text and their definitions are difficult to locate. For example, I could not find what s denotes (after the rebuttal, it appears that s is defined one page after its first use and is then scarcely used in the rest of the paper).

2/ The advertising example is at times misleading. Several places suggest correlations with ad display time and invoke non-stationarity, which is outside the scope of this paper. It may be preferable to choose a different motivating example (see 4/).

3/ I find the chosen regret notion hard to justify in this setting. Regret analyses typically optimize long-run gain, which naturally leads to selecting the arm with the best reward/cost ratio. Here, the optimal policy is defined as always playing the arm with the highest mean, regardless of cost. Consequently, the benchmark is weakened by the obligation to sample the best arm at lower fidelities, potentially obscuring the cost of unnecessary low-fidelity measurements.

4/ A sample-complexity (more precisely, cost-complexity) analysis would be valuable for multi-fidelity bandits. A compelling motivation is the prototype testing phase in new product development: the goal is to identify, with high probability, the best product while minimizing the cumulative testing cost prior to mass production. Because companies are constrained by regulations, contracts, and existing facilities, they may be required to use low-fidelity approximations before accessing advanced and expensive tests.

5/ Some portions of the text are unwieldy. A thorough editorial pass to streamline the presentation and improve clarity would strengthen the manuscript.
