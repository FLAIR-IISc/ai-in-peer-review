This paper introduces a novel multi-armed bandit setting where the learner can choose to play an arm at one of $M$ available fidelities. Each successive fidelity provides a better approximation of the rewards but incurs a larger cost. A strategy that effectively leverages lower and cheaper fidelities to eliminate sub-optimal arms, reserving higher and more expensive fidelities for a small subset of promising arms, may outperform the standard UCB algorithm, which neglects these fidelity characteristics. This setting has practical applications, such as online advertising, where displaying ads for a short duration can approximate long-term click-through rates while minimizing costs.

The authors propose a new regret definition, develop an algorithm with theoretical analysis, including a regret lower bound, and conduct simulations to evaluate their approach. The analysis is novel and contributes to the field. However, the assumption that the deviations of the $m$th fidelity mean from the highest fidelity mean, denoted as $\varepsilon^{(m)}$, are known in advance, raises questions about their practical estimation. For instance, in online advertising, how can these values be determined? Is it possible to relax this assumption?
