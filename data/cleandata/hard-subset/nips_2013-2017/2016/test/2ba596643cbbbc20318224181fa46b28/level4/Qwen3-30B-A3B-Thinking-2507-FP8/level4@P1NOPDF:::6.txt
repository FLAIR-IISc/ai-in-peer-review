

This paper introduces a novel multi-armed bandit framework with multiple fidelity levels, where each successive fidelity provides a more accurate reward estimate at a higher cost. A key insight is that leveraging cheaper, lower-fidelity approximations to eliminate suboptimal arms early—while reserving higher-cost fidelities for a refined set of promising arms—can yield better performance than standard UCB, which ignores fidelity trade-offs. The setting is well-motivated by applications like online advertising, where brief ad displays (low fidelity) can estimate long-term click-through rates (high fidelity) while balancing cost constraints. The authors propose a new regret formulation, a corresponding algorithm with theoretical guarantees (including a regret lower bound), and supporting simulations. The analysis is technically sound and novel.

However, a critical assumption requires clarification: the deviations $\varepsilon^{(m)}$ (between the $m$-th fidelity's mean reward and the highest-fidelity mean) are assumed known a priori. The paper does not address how these values would be obtained in practice—e.g., in the online advertising example, how would one reliably estimate $\varepsilon^{(m)}$ without costly high-fidelity trials? This assumption significantly impacts applicability, and the authors should discuss whether it can be relaxed (e.g., via adaptive estimation or confidence intervals) or whether the framework remains viable under uncertainty.
