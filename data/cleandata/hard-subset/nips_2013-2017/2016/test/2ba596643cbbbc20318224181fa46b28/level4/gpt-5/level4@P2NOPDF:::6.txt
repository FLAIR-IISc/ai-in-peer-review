This paper studies a multi-armed bandit problem with M fidelity levels. At each round, the learner selects both an arm and a fidelity. Higher fidelities yield more accurate reward estimates but incur higher costs; lower fidelities are cheaper but biased. An effective strategy can use low-cost, low-fidelity feedback to rule out clearly suboptimal arms, reserving high-cost, high-fidelity pulls for a small set of promising arms. In such settings, this approach can outperform standard UCB-style algorithms that ignore fidelity structure.

A natural application is online advertising, where short-run engagement (e.g., clicks over a few minutes) can serve as a low-cost proxy for long-run performance.

The paper introduces a regret notion tailored to this setting (accounting for fidelity-specific costs and bias), proposes an algorithm with theoretical guarantees, including a regret lower bound, and presents simulations. The analysis appears novel and technically sound.

Assumption and practicality. The theory assumes that for each fidelity m, the bias epsilon^(m) between the m-th fidelity mean and the highest-fidelity mean is known in advance. How would these quantities be specified in practice (e.g., in the advertising example)? Can this assumption be relaxed—for instance, by:
- estimating or upper-bounding epsilon^(m) online,
- using data-driven calibration phases,
- or constructing confidence sets that adapt to unknown fidelity biases—
while retaining comparable regret guarantees?
