This paper introduces a multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit framework in which each arm can be sampled at different fidelities, each with an associated cost; low-fidelity samples are cheaper but only approximate the highest-fidelity distribution. The authors propose an algorithm that adapts to the available fidelities and costs, provide a regret analysis along with a lower bound for the problem, and present simulations on synthetic tasks showing improvements over UCB. The framework is interesting and has the potential to be impactful if further developed. However, in its current form I do not recommend acceptance.

Strengths
- Novel problem formulation addressing practical scenarios where cheap, approximate evaluations precede expensive, high-fidelity ones.
- An adaptive algorithm with theoretical analysis (regret upper bounds and a lower bound).
- Empirical results on synthetic problems indicating gains over a standard baseline.

Major concerns
1) Notation and definitions are hard to follow.
- Key symbols are introduced late or only used sparsely. For instance, s is first used before it is defined (and then barely used thereafter), which makes reading and cross-referencing difficult.
- Definitions are dispersed across sections, forcing the reader to search for assumptions and symbol meanings.
- Recommendation: Consolidate the formal problem statement in one place. Define all notation at first use; state assumptions about the relationship between fidelities (e.g., bias/variance trade-offs, stochastic dominance), stationarity, and cost model. A table of symbols would help.

2) The motivating example and discussion of non-stationarity are misleading.
- The advertising example intermittently associates display time with non-stationarity. Non-stationarity is not modeled or analyzed in this paper and distracts from the core contribution.
- Recommendation: Either remove claims that rely on non-stationarity or make it explicit that the model is stationary. Consider a more faithful motivating example, such as engineering design/prototyping, physics simulations with varying mesh granularity, or hyperparameter tuning with cheap proxies—settings that naturally fit multi-fidelity sampling without invoking non-stationarity.

3) Objective and regret notion appear misaligned with costs.
- In a cost-sensitive setting, the benchmark for regret typically accounts for cost, e.g., maximizing reward per unit cost or maximizing cumulative reward under a budget. In the present formulation, the optimal policy is defined as the arm with the highest mean at the highest fidelity, independent of cost. This makes the regret notion difficult to justify in a setting where costs are central.
- Furthermore, the algorithm is effectively obliged to collect low-fidelity samples, which can obscure or understate the cost of unnecessary low-fidelity exploration when the best arm could be identified more directly.
- Recommendation: Clearly specify the objective—cumulative reward subject to costs, reward per cost, or a budgeted setting. If the aim is identification rather than reward maximization, a fixed-confidence (or fixed-budget) best-arm identification objective with cost-sensitive sample complexity may be more appropriate. Alternatively, define a cost-aware regret (e.g., pseudo-regret normalized by cumulative cost, or regret under a budget constraint) and compare against a cost-aware oracle.

4) Consider a cost-complexity (sample-complexity) analysis tailored to multi-fidelity settings.
- A fixed-confidence best-arm identification analysis that minimizes total cost to identify the best arm with high probability fits many realistic workflows where low-fidelity gates access to high-fidelity tests. This is especially compelling in product development, where regulations, contracts, and facility access impose mandatory low-fidelity testing before expensive trials.
- Such an analysis would complement or, in some contexts, better reflect the practical value of the proposed framework than the current regret metric.

5) Writing and structure need streamlining.
- Several sections are unwieldy, and the flow makes it hard to track assumptions, definitions, and the logical progression of results.
- Recommendation: Tighten the exposition; front-load the formal setting and assumptions; separate motivation from technical development; and ensure consistency of notation throughout.

Empirical evaluation
- The reported gains over UCB are encouraging, but the experimental setup needs more clarity and breadth to be convincing in a cost-sensitive, multi-fidelity context.
  - Specify whether low-fidelity observations are biased approximations, how bias and variance scale across fidelities, and how costs are set.
  - Include sensitivity analyses to approximation quality, number of fidelity levels, and cost ratios.
  - Compare against cost-aware or multi-fidelity baselines where applicable (e.g., budgeted bandits, reward-per-cost UCB variants), not only standard UCB at a single fidelity.
  - Report how performance scales with a fixed total budget and with different budget regimes.

Minor comments
- Correct language issues: “The authors provide…”, “synthetic problems” (not “synthetical”), “outperforms UCB.”
- Define s at first use and maintain consistent notation throughout.
- Avoid references to non-stationarity unless it is explicitly modeled and analyzed.
- Clarify what is meant by “adapting to sequences of available approximations and costs”: Are these sequences known in advance, adversarial, or stochastic? Are availability and costs stationary?
- It would help to state upfront whether the lower bound matches the upper bound in order or where gaps remain.

Overall assessment
The paper tackles a timely and practically relevant problem and offers an interesting algorithm with initial theory and experiments. However, the current regret formulation does not properly reflect the role of costs, the exposition is difficult to follow, and the empirical evaluation lacks breadth relative to the stated goals. I encourage the authors to (i) clarify and streamline the problem setup and notation, (ii) adopt a cost-aware objective (either regret or best-arm identification with cost complexity), and (iii) expand the experimental evaluation accordingly. With these revisions, the work could become a strong contribution to the study of multi-fidelity bandits. At this stage, I do not recommend acceptance.
