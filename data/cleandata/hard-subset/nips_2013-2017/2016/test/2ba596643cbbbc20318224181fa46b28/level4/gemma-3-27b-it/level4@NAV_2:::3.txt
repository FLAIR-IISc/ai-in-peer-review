The paper introduces a multi-fidelity variant of the multi-armed bandit problem, where each of the K arms can be played at M different fidelities, balancing cost and bias in reward estimation. Lower fidelities are less expensive but yield potentially biased estimates of the true reward. The authors analyze a UCB-based algorithm adapted for this setting, incorporating fidelity-dependent uncertainty bounds. The core result is a regret bound demonstrating potential improvements over standard UCB when always playing at the highest fidelity. Simulation results support these theoretical findings.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a practically relevant trade-off between exploration cost and estimation accuracy. The multi-fidelity bandit framework itself is a valuable contribution, offering a novel perspective on exploration-exploitation dilemmas. The proposed MF-UCB algorithm is a natural extension of UCB, applying the optimism principle appropriately to this new setting. The theoretical analysis, while resulting in complex regret bounds, establishes a foundation for understanding the algorithm’s performance.

However, the connection between the model and its motivating applications feels somewhat tenuous. While examples like algorithm selection are presented, the rationale for framing these as regret minimization problems isn’t fully compelling. The modeling choice of lower fidelity introducing *bias* rather than *variance* is initially counterintuitive, though the explanations provided are reasonable, even if not entirely convincing in all cases (e.g., the advertising example). The resulting regret bounds are also difficult to interpret concisely, hindering quick assessment of practical gains.

The simulations, while demonstrating performance improvements, lack sufficient justification for the chosen parameter values. It remains unclear whether the observed 3-4x improvement in regret is representative of typical performance or an artifact of specific parameter settings. More robust sensitivity analysis or discussion of parameter selection would strengthen the empirical validation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and well-motivated problem formulation.
*   Natural and reasonable algorithm design (MF-UCB).
*   Theoretical analysis providing performance guarantees.
*   Empirical results supporting theoretical findings.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Weak connection between the model and motivating applications.
*   Complex and difficult-to-interpret regret bounds.
*   Limited justification for simulation parameter choices.
*   Modeling choice of bias over variance might not resonate with all readers.

Overall, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the bandit literature, introducing a novel framework and algorithm with solid theoretical grounding. While some aspects of the presentation and justification could be improved, the strengths of the work outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend acceptance, with minor revisions to clarify the connection to applications and provide more detailed justification for the simulation setup.
