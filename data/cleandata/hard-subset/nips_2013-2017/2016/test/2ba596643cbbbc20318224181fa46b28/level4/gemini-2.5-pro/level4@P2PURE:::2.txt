This paper proposes a novel multi-armed bandit setting where arms can be pulled with varying levels of accuracy, or fidelity. In this framework, the cost of pulling an arm is a function of its fidelity, and the regret is defined as the product of this cost and the standard pseudo-regret. The authors introduce a new algorithm, MF-UCB, to address this problem and provide corresponding upper and lower bounds on its cumulative pseudo-regregret.

The main paper is clear and well-written. However, the supplementary material, which contains the majority of the proofs, unfortunately does not meet the same standard. It suffers from numerous typos and minor, albeit easily correctable, errors that can confuse the reader (a non-exhaustive list is provided below). Correcting these issues is essential and would significantly improve the paper's overall readability.

Additionally, the use of "up-to-a-multiplicative-constant" notation in the main paper can be misleading, as these "constants" conceal dependencies on important algorithm parameters. For instance, the bound in Theorem 2 hides a factor of `\rho`. Presenting the full, exact inequalities from the supplementary material directly in the main paper would offer greater clarity and precision.

From a practical standpoint, the direct applicability of the proposed algorithm to real-world problems is not immediately apparent. The core strategy of MF-UCB—using low-fidelity pulls to quickly discard suboptimal arms—seems potentially sensitive to the accurate estimation of the fidelity parameter `\zeta`. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of such practical limitations and potential strategies to mitigate them.

Despite these points, the paper makes a strong theoretical contribution. It offers valuable insight into the multi-fidelity bandit problem, supported by interesting theorems and solid proofs. Overall, this is a significant and interesting contribution to the field.
