This paper studies a variant of the classic stochastic K-armed bandit problem where obtaining outcomes for each arm is expensive, but cheaper approximations are available. The authors formalize this as a multi-fidelity bandit problem, and propose a novel algorithm, MF-UCB, with accompanying theoretical regret bounds. They also provide simulations demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach. The presentation is clear and well-organized, making the core ideas easy to follow. The simulations effectively illustrate the benefits of the multi-fidelity bandit method, and the thorough theoretical analysis, including a lower bound, convincingly demonstrates the algorithm’s potential improvements over standard approaches.

The paper builds upon the well-established foundation of multi-armed bandit research, referencing seminal work by Robbins [11] and subsequent advancements like UCB [3, 12]. It correctly identifies a practical limitation of the standard bandit framework – the cost associated with obtaining rewards – and addresses it by leveraging cheaper, though potentially biased, approximations. This approach is motivated by real-world applications like online advertising and algorithm selection [4], where obtaining precise performance estimates can be resource-intensive. The formalization of the problem as a multi-fidelity bandit is a significant contribution, and the introduction of the cost-weighted regret metric appropriately captures the trade-offs involved.

The core strength of this work lies in its theoretical rigor. The development of MF-UCB is intuitive – intelligently allocating resources to promising arms while using cheaper approximations to rapidly eliminate suboptimal options – and the regret analysis formally validates this intuition. The bound of O((λ(1)K + λ(2)|Kg|) log(Λ/λ(2))), where Kg represents a small set of potentially optimal arms, is particularly compelling, demonstrating the potential for significant improvements over the standard UCB regret of O(λ(2)K log(Λ/λ(2))). The accompanying lower bound further strengthens this claim by showing that MF-UCB is near-optimal under certain conditions. The assumptions made (Assumption 1) are clearly stated and justified.

However, there are a few areas where the paper could be strengthened. While the connection to existing multi-fidelity optimization methods [7, 10] and reinforcement learning approaches [6] is acknowledged, a more detailed comparison of MF-UCB with these techniques would be valuable. Specifically, discussing how the exploration-exploitation trade-off is handled differently in these settings would provide further context. The discussion of the choice of *qt* in the regret definition is also insightful, but briefly mentioning alternative choices and their implications could enhance the paper. Finally, the practical implications of Assumption 1 deserve further discussion. While the authors acknowledge it isn’t essential, clarifying under what conditions it’s likely to hold and how violations might affect performance would be helpful.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Strong theoretical foundation:** The regret analysis is rigorous and provides compelling evidence of the algorithm’s performance.
*   **Novelty:** The multi-fidelity bandit framework is a novel extension of the classical bandit problem.
*   **Practical relevance:** The problem addressed is relevant to a wide range of applications.
*   **Clear presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to understand.
*   **Empirical validation:** The simulations support the theoretical findings.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The connection to related work in multi-fidelity optimization could be more detailed.
*   A more thorough discussion of the practical implications of Assumption 1 would be beneficial.



**Overall:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of multi-armed bandits. The theoretical results are strong, the algorithm is well-motivated, and the presentation is clear. While there are minor areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
