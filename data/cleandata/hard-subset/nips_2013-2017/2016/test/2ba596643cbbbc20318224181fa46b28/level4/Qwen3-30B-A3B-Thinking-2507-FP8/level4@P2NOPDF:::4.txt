

The authors propose a novel framework for multi-fidelity multi-armed bandits, where arms may be sampled at varying fidelity levels with associated costs. Lower-fidelity samples approximate the highest-fidelity distribution at reduced cost. They present an adaptive algorithm, its regret analysis, and a problem-specific lower bound, supported by synthetic experiments demonstrating superior performance over UCB. While the framework holds potential for impact, the current submission requires significant revision to meet publication standards.  

Major concerns include:  
1. **Confusing notation**: Key symbols (e.g., $s$) are introduced without clear definitions, appearing one page after first use and rarely utilized thereafter. This disorganization impedes comprehension.  
2. **Misleading example**: The advertising use case incorrectly invokes non-stationarity (e.g., correlating ad display time with non-stationary behavior), which is irrelevant to the stationary setting. A more suitable example—such as product prototyping where low-fidelity testing precedes costly validation—would strengthen the motivation.  
3. **Flawed regret formulation**: The regret analysis is fundamentally unsound. Regret requires maximizing long-term reward *per unit cost*, yet the paper incorrectly assumes the optimal policy always selects the arm with the highest mean reward, irrespective of cost. This overlooks how cost constraints should shape the optimal policy, thereby obscuring the true trade-off between fidelity and cost.  
4. **Missing cost complexity analysis**: The paper should include a cost-complexity perspective, critical for applications like product development. Here, the goal is to identify the optimal prototype with high probability while minimizing *cumulative testing cost* before mass production. Companies often face regulatory or infrastructure constraints mandating low-fidelity testing before high-cost validation—a context where cost complexity directly aligns with the problem’s practical value.  
5. **Unpolished exposition**: The text requires substantial editing for clarity and flow. Redundant phrasing, inconsistent terminology, and abrupt transitions hinder readability. A thorough revision to streamline arguments and unify notation is essential.  

The core idea is promising but currently underdeveloped. The regret analysis must be reworked to properly model cost-aware optimization, and the advertising example should be replaced with a domain where fidelity-cost trade-offs are inherently relevant (e.g., engineering prototyping). Incorporating cost complexity analysis would also significantly strengthen the contribution. Without these revisions, the paper fails to convincingly establish its theoretical and practical significance.  

In summary, the framework is conceptually interesting but suffers from critical flaws in formulation, motivation, and exposition. The authors must address all points above—particularly the regret analysis and example—before the work can be considered for publication. As submitted, it does not meet NIPS’s standards for technical rigor and clarity.
