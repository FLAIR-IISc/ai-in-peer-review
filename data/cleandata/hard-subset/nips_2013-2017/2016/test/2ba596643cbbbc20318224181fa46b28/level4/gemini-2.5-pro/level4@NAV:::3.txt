This paper presents a "multi-fidelity" extension of the multi-armed bandit problem, where K arms can be evaluated at M different fidelity levels. In this formulation, lower fidelity is less costly but introduces greater potential bias in the reward estimate. Specifically, pulling an arm at fidelity *m* draws from a distribution whose expected value deviates from the arm's true mean by an amount upper-bounded by a function of that fidelity level. The authors propose a UCB-based algorithm that accommodates this setting by widening the confidence intervals to account for the uncertainty arising from low-fidelity bias.

The paper's theoretical analysis culminates in complex regret bounds that are difficult to parse. The authors argue that this bound is often superior to that of a standard UCB algorithm restricted to the highest fidelity level, a claim substantiated by a brief set of simulations.

Initially, the choice to model lower fidelity as a source of bias rather than increased variance seems questionable, as the latter is also a plausible interpretation. However, the motivating examples, particularly algorithm selection for machine learning, provide a reasonable justification for the bias-centric model. That said, some motivations are less compelling; for instance, the claim that shorter ad display times yield biased estimates of long-term effectiveness is not entirely convincing. Furthermore, in the algorithm selection example, it is not clear that regret minimization is the most appropriate objective. In summary, the connection between the proposed model and its motivating applications is somewhat tenuous, though this is not a disqualifying flaw.

The primary innovation of this work appears to be the formulation of the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit problem itself. The proposed algorithm is a standard application of the "optimism in the face of uncertainty" principle. As noted, the theoretical performance guarantees are disappointingly abstruse.

The empirical evaluation on synthetic problems is difficult to assess. While Appendix C details the parameters used in the simulations, it provides no justification for these choices. The results show that MF-UCB improves upon UCB's regret by a factor of 3 to 4 in many scenarios. However, it remains unclear whether this level of improvement is typical of the algorithms' relative performance in practice or is an artifact of the specific parameter selections (e.g., the values of ζ⁽ᵐ⁾).
