The paper introduces a multi-fidelity version of the multi-armed bandit problem, featuring K arms each playable at M different fidelity levels. Lower fidelity pulls are less costly but introduce potentially greater bias in the estimated reward of an arm – specifically, sampling from a distribution whose expected value deviates from the true reward, with the deviation bounded by the fidelity level. The authors analyze a natural UCB-based algorithm for this problem, widening the confidence bounds to account for the uncertainty introduced by low-fidelity bias. The algorithm’s analysis results in regret bounds that, while technically correct, are complex and difficult to interpret. The paper argues these bounds will often be significantly better than those achieved by consistently playing arms at the highest fidelity using standard UCB. A brief section presents simulation results to support this claim. 

Initially, I questioned the modeling choice of lower fidelity as introducing *bias* rather than *variance*. The latter interpretation of “low fidelity” also seems intuitively strong. However, the motivating examples—particularly algorithm selection for machine learning—convinced me that modeling lower fidelity as increased bias is reasonable. I remain unconvinced by the advertising example (where shorter ad display times are posited to yield a biased estimate of long-term effectiveness), and in the algorithm selection scenario, it’s unclear why minimizing regret would be the primary objective of an algorithm designer. Overall, the connection between the model and its motivating applications feels somewhat weak, though not critically so.

The paper’s main contribution lies in formulating the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit problem itself. The proposed algorithm is a fairly standard application of the “optimism in the face of uncertainty” principle. As noted earlier, the performance guarantee’s complexity hinders its interpretability. The simulation results, based on synthetic problems, are difficult to assess fully. Appendix C details the parameter values used, but lacks justification for these choices. MF-UCB appears to improve upon UCB’s regret by a factor of 3 to 4 in many simulations. It’s crucial to determine whether this performance gain is typical in practice, or merely an artifact of the selected parameter values (e.g., the values of ζ<sup>(m)</sup>).
