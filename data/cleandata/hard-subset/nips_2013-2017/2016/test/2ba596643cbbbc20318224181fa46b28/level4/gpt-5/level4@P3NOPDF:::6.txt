Summary and contributions
The paper studies a multi-fidelity stochastic bandit setting in which each arm can be queried at one of M fidelity levels. Higher fidelities offer more accurate reward information but incur higher costs; lower fidelities are cheaper but biased. The goal is to minimize cumulative regret under a cost budget by judiciously using cheap fidelities to prune clearly suboptimal arms and reserving expensive fidelities for a small set of promising arms. The paper proposes a new regret notion that incorporates the costs of fidelity choices, develops a UCB-type algorithm tailored to this setting, proves upper and lower bounds (including a problem-dependent lower bound), and presents simulations.

The formulation is timely and relevant for applications such as online advertising, simulation-based optimization, and A/B testing, where low-cost proxies can accelerate exploration. The analysis appears novel, and the algorithmic idea is simple and appealing.

Strengths
- Well-motivated problem: The multi-fidelity view captures realistic cost–accuracy trade-offs and offers a principled alternative to standard bandits that ignore fidelity structure.
- Novel regret formulation: The cost-aware regret notion is appropriate for this setting and highlights a nontrivial design space (choosing both arms and fidelities).
- Theoretical rigor: The paper provides instance-dependent upper bounds and a matching (or near-matching) lower bound under the proposed model, lending credibility to the algorithmic design.
- Practical intuition: The strategy of using cheap fidelities for elimination and reserving expensive queries for a few good candidates is intuitive and well-justified by the theory.

Weaknesses and questions
- Assumption of known fidelity biases: A central modeling assumption is that, for each fidelity m, the deviation epsilon^(m) between the mean at fidelity m and the highest-fidelity mean is known a priori. This may be difficult to justify in many applications (e.g., online advertising), where the fidelity relationship is uncertain and may vary across arms or over time. Clarifications and suggestions:
  - Please explain how epsilon^(m) would be obtained in practice. Are they per-fidelity global bounds (uniform across arms) or arm-specific? Are they absolute biases or high-probability envelopes?
  - Can the analysis accommodate known upper bounds on epsilon^(m) rather than exact values? If the algorithm uses conservative overestimates of epsilon^(m), how do the regret guarantees degrade?
  - Is it possible to learn epsilon^(m) online? For instance, one could devote a small exploration budget to co-sampling at successive fidelities to estimate or upper-bound the bias, and then plug these estimates into the decision rule with appropriate confidence adjustments. A short discussion (or an appendix sketch) on how to relax the assumption would strengthen the work.
- Modeling scope: The paper appears to assume a monotone improvement in fidelity (bias bounded by epsilon^(m) and costs increasing with m). Please clarify whether the results require monotonicity and whether they extend to settings with heteroskedastic noise or arm-dependent biases.
- Practical guidance: The algorithm involves thresholds/parameters (e.g., gamma^(m), choice of exploration coefficients). More guidance on how to set them from known quantities (costs, noise level, risk tolerance) would help practitioners.
- Relation to prior work: Please better position the paper with respect to successive elimination methods and other structured bandits (e.g., hierarchical testing, budgeted bandits), and clarify conceptual differences from related multi-fidelity Bayesian optimization approaches (e.g., GP-based methods) even if assumptions differ.

Empirical evaluation
The simulations illustrate the expected gains when low fidelities are informative and cheap. To strengthen the empirical section:
- Include sensitivity analyses to misspecification of epsilon^(m) (e.g., over- and under-estimation).
- Compare against stronger baselines that use elimination under a cost budget (e.g., cost-aware successive rejects/elimination, Thompson sampling variants with fidelity costs).
- If possible, add one semi-synthetic or real-world experiment reflecting a plausible multi-fidelity pipeline.

Clarity and presentation
Overall the paper is readable and the high-level ideas are clear. The proofs are dense but appear sound; a brief proof roadmap and a table of main symbols would improve accessibility.

Overall assessment
This paper addresses an important and practically motivated variant of stochastic bandits and provides a clean algorithm with supporting theory, including lower bounds. The main caveat is the reliance on known fidelity biases, which seems strong for many applications; discussing how to obtain or relax this assumption would significantly improve the work. Subject to clarifying this point and fixing the minor issues above, I recommend acceptance.
