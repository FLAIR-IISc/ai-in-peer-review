

This paper introduces a novel multi-fidelity bandit framework where each arm can be evaluated at multiple fidelities, with lower fidelities providing cheaper but biased approximations to the highest-fidelity reward. The authors formalize a new regret definition that accounts for both the cost of playing at different fidelities and the bias introduced by lower-fidelity approximations. They propose MF-UCB, an algorithm that adaptively uses lower fidelities to eliminate suboptimal arms while reserving higher fidelities for promising candidates, and provide theoretical guarantees demonstrating its near-optimality. The work extends classical bandit literature [3, 9, 11] by addressing a practical scenario where resource constraints make high-fidelity evaluations expensive, as seen in online advertising (where short-term ad displays approximate long-term click-through rates) and machine learning algorithm selection (where cheaper data subsets estimate full-data performance). Unlike prior work on budgeted bandits [13, 15] or multi-fidelity optimization [7, 10], this paper specifically formalizes regret in the bandit setting with multiple fidelities per arm.

The paper's main strengths are its clear problem formulation, rigorous theoretical analysis, and compelling empirical validation. The authors successfully bridge theoretical bandit analysis with practical applications by providing a regret bound that demonstrates MF-UCB's advantage over standard UCB strategies when lower fidelities are significantly cheaper. Their analysis carefully accounts for the bias introduced by lower-fidelity approximations (|μ^(M)_k - μ^(m)_k| ≤ ζ^(m)), which distinguishes their work from naive approaches that treat low-fidelity observations as simply noisier high-fidelity ones. The regret bound in Theorem 2 shows that MF-UCB achieves regret proportional to λ^(m)/ψ(Δ^(m)_k) for arms played at fidelity m, which is superior to UCB's λ^(M)/ψ(Δ^(M)_k) when λ^(m) < λ^(M) and |K^(m)| < |K| for m < M. The lower bound in Theorem 4 further demonstrates that MF-UCB is nearly optimal under the given assumptions.

The empirical results in Section 6 provide strong support for the theoretical claims, showing MF-UCB consistently outperforms UCB across synthetic problems. The authors' visualization of arm play distribution across fidelities (Figure 2) effectively illustrates how MF-UCB allocates cheaper fidelities to eliminate suboptimal arms while reserving expensive fidelities for promising candidates, aligning perfectly with their theoretical analysis.

However, the paper's main limitation concerns the assumption that ζ^(m) (the deviation bounds between fidelities) is known in advance. While this assumption simplifies the theoretical analysis, it may be restrictive for real-world applications like online advertising, where the relationship between short-term and long-term performance is typically unknown. The authors do not address how to estimate ζ^(m) in practice or whether the algorithm can adapt to unknown ζ^(m) values. This is a significant practical concern that deserves more thorough discussion. The paper mentions this as an open problem in Section 7, but it would be strengthened by at least a brief exploration of potential approaches or empirical validation of how robust MF-UCB is to estimation errors in ζ^(m).

The paper also contains several minor technical issues that would benefit from correction:
1. In lines 326, 357, and 386: "H^(m)" should be "K^(m)"
2. In line 343: The argument "β^(k*,t) ≤ s" should be "β^(k*,t) ≤ μ*"
3. In line 345: "γ" should be "γ^(m)"
4. In lines 346-347: The inequalities should be "≥" instead of "≤"
5. In line 384: The summation should be over k ∈ K^(m) (not "above" K^(m)")
6. In line 407: "δ^(m)" should be "δ^(M)" and "μ*κλ^(m)" should be "κλ^(m)"
7. In line 431: "for l ≥ m" should be "for l ≥ p"
8. In line 440: "l ≥ m" should be "l ≥ p" in Equation (17)
9. In line 450: "P(A_{n,3})" should be "P(A_{n,2})"

These corrections would improve the paper's technical accuracy and readability. The writing is generally clear, but some phrases could be more natural (e.g., "a novel upper confidence bound procedure for this setting" could be "a new upper confidence bound algorithm tailored to this setting").

Overall, this is a strong contribution that advances the bandit literature by addressing a practical resource-constrained setting. The theoretical analysis is sound, the empirical results are convincing, and the application examples are well-chosen. While the assumption about known ζ^(m) warrants further discussion, the paper makes a significant and well-executed contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the technical corrections noted above and to elaborate on the practical estimation of ζ^(m).
