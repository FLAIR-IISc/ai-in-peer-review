This paper introduces a “multi-fidelity” extension to the classic multi-armed bandit (MAB) problem. In this setting, each of the *K* arms can be played at *M* different fidelity levels. Lower fidelity pulls are less costly but introduce a bias in the reward estimate, with the magnitude of the bias bounded by the fidelity level. The authors analyze a natural UCB-based algorithm adapted to this setting, increasing the confidence interval width to account for the bias introduced by lower fidelity samples.

The core contribution of the paper lies in formulating this multi-fidelity MAB problem and providing a theoretical analysis of the proposed algorithm. The resulting regret bounds, however, are complex and difficult to readily interpret. The authors argue that, despite this complexity, the proposed algorithm achieves significantly better regret performance than consistently using the highest fidelity level with standard UCB. Supporting this claim, the paper presents simulation results demonstrating performance gains.

My initial reaction focused on the modeling choice to represent lower fidelity as increased *bias* rather than increased *variance*. While increased variance seems a natural interpretation of “low fidelity,” the paper's motivating examples—particularly algorithm selection for machine learning—ultimately justified this design choice. However, I remain unconvinced by the example relating shorter ad display intervals to biased effectiveness estimates. Furthermore, in the context of algorithm selection, the paper’s focus on minimizing regret as the primary goal feels somewhat disconnected from the typical objectives of an algorithm designer. Overall, the connection between the model and its motivating applications, while not disqualifying, feels weaker than desired.

The algorithm itself represents a straightforward application of the “optimism in the face of uncertainty” principle. Its theoretical performance guarantee, as previously noted, is difficult to parse and extract practical insights from. The simulation results, presented on synthetic problems, are challenging to evaluate thoroughly. While Appendix C details the specific parameter values used, it lacks justification for these choices. A crucial question remains: is the observed 3-4x improvement in regret over standard UCB a typical expectation in practice, or is it an artifact of the specific parameter settings (e.g., values of ζ<sup>(m)</sup>)? Without a clearer understanding of parameter sensitivity and the rationale behind the chosen values, the empirical validation is less compelling.

**Strengths:**

*   The formulation of the multi-fidelity MAB problem is a novel and potentially valuable extension to the standard MAB framework.
*   The proposed algorithm is a reasonable and intuitive adaptation of the UCB approach.
*   The simulation results, while requiring further scrutiny, suggest potential performance benefits.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The regret bounds are complex and lack interpretability.
*   The connection between the model and motivating applications is not consistently strong.
*   The simulation setup lacks justification for parameter choices, raising concerns about the generalizability of the observed results.
*   The paper does not fully explore the implications of modeling low fidelity as bias versus variance.



**Overall:**

This paper presents an interesting contribution to the MAB literature, but falls short of being truly impactful. While the problem formulation is a valuable contribution, the complexity of the analysis and the somewhat tenuous connection to applications limit the paper’s overall strength. The simulation results provide preliminary evidence of performance gains, but further investigation is needed to establish the algorithm’s practical effectiveness and robustness. I would recommend acceptance with minor revisions, contingent on the authors addressing the concerns raised regarding parameter selection in the simulations and providing a clearer discussion of the model's limitations and potential extensions. Specifically, exploring a sensitivity analysis of the simulation parameters would significantly strengthen the empirical evaluation.
