This paper introduces the "multi-fidelity" multi-armed bandit problem, in which K arms can be played at M different fidelity levels. In this model, playing an arm at a lower fidelity is less costly but introduces greater potential bias in the reward estimate; specifically, the expected value of a sample deviates from the arm's true mean by an amount upper-bounded by a function of the fidelity level. The paper proposes a natural UCB-based algorithm for this problem that inflates the confidence bounds to account for the uncertainty introduced by the bias from low-fidelity samples.

The theoretical analysis culminates in complex regret bounds that are difficult to interpret. The authors argue that these bounds are often significantly better than the standard regret achieved by playing all arms at the highest fidelity with a conventional UCB algorithm. This claim is supported by a brief experimental section with simulations on synthetic data.

While the formulation of the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit problem is a novel contribution, the proposed algorithm is a relatively straightforward application of the "optimism in the face of uncertainty" principle. The paper's primary weaknesses lie in its motivation and evaluation.

The connection between the proposed model and its motivating applications is tenuous. I was initially puzzled by the choice to model lower fidelity as increased bias rather than increased variance, though the example of machine learning algorithm selection provides some justification for this choice. However, other motivations are less convincing. For example, the claim that displaying an ad for a shorter duration yields a biased estimate of its long-term effectiveness is questionable. Moreover, for the machine learning algorithm selection scenario, it is not clear that regret minimization is the appropriate objective. While these motivational weaknesses are not a disqualifying flaw, they do diminish the paper's impact.

The empirical evaluation is also difficult to assess. The simulations are conducted on synthetic problems, and while Appendix C specifies the parameter values used, it offers no justification for these choices. Consequently, it is unclear whether the reported 3-4x improvement of the proposed MF-UCB algorithm over standard UCB is representative of typical performance or an artifact of a favorable parameterization (e.g., the choice of bias-bound values, ζ^(m)).
