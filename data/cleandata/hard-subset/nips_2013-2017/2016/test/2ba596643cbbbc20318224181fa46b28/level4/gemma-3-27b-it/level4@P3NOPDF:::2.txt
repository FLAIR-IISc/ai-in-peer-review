This work introduces a novel multi-armed bandit (MAB) setting where each arm offers varying levels of accuracy, or fidelity, at an increasing cost. The regret is defined as the product of this cost and the standard pseudo-regret. The authors propose an algorithm, MF-UCB, specifically designed for this setting and provide both upper and lower bounds on its cumulative pseudo-regret.

The paper is generally clear and well-written, presenting a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of MABs. However, the supplementary material, which contains the majority of the proofs, requires significant improvement. Numerous typos and minor, correctable errors detract from readability and have the potential to confuse the reader. A detailed list of these issues is provided at the end of this review. Addressing these errors would substantially enhance the paper’s accessibility.

Furthermore, the consistent use of “up-to-a-multiplicative-constant” bounds, while common in theoretical computer science, feels somewhat misleading in this context. The constants involved are not truly constant; for example, Theorem 2 includes the parameter ρ, inherent to the algorithm, within the constant factor. Presenting the more precise inequalities – as found in the supplementary material – would improve the clarity and impact of the theoretical results.

From a practical perspective, the immediate applicability of MF-UCB to real-world problems is not immediately obvious. The algorithm’s strategy of utilizing low-accuracy pulls to efficiently eliminate suboptimal arms relies heavily on accurate estimation of the fidelity parameter ζ. Inaccurate estimates could severely hamper performance. A discussion regarding these practical limitations, along with potential strategies to mitigate them (e.g., robust estimation techniques for ζ, adaptive accuracy selection), would significantly strengthen the work.

Despite these concerns, the paper provides a compelling theoretical insight into this interesting and potentially important problem. The theorems are well-motivated, and the proofs appear solid, once the identified errors are corrected. Overall, this work represents a significant and interesting contribution to the literature on multi-armed bandits.
