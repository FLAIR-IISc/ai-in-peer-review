This paper introduces a novel multi-armed bandit framework where each arm can be sampled at varying accuracy levels. The cost associated with pulling an arm increases in tandem with the desired accuracy, and the regret is quantified as the product of the cost and the standard pseudo-regret. The authors propose an algorithm, MF-UCB, to address this setting and establish both an upper and a lower bound on the cumulative pseudo-regret of this algorithm. The main manuscript is well-structured and clearly written. However, the supplementary materials, which contain the majority of the proofs, are marred by numerous typos and minor, easily correctable errors that may confuse the reader (a non-exhaustive list is provided below). Correcting these errors would significantly enhance the paper's readability. Furthermore, the use of up-to-a-multiplicative-constant notation in the paper may be somewhat misleading, as constants are not truly constant; for example, in Theorem 2, there is a factor \rho (an algorithm parameter) that is concealed within the constant. Utilizing the exact inequalities (presented in the supplementary materials) could improve the clarity of the results. From a practical perspective, it is unclear whether this algorithm can be readily applied to real-world problems. For instance, the strategy employed by MF-UCB (leveraging low-accuracy pulls to eliminate highly suboptimal arms) may be vulnerable to inaccurate estimation of the \zeta (fidelity accuracy). A discussion on the practical limitations and potential solutions would be beneficial to this work. Theoretically, this paper offers valuable insights into the problem, along with interesting theorems and robust proofs. Overall, this work constitutes a significant and intriguing contribution.
