This paper introduces and analyzes a novel multi-armed bandit setting, termed the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit problem. In this framework, a learner can play any arm at one of $M$ different "fidelities." Each successive fidelity level provides a more accurate approximation of the true reward but incurs a higher cost. The core idea is that an intelligent strategy can leverage the cheaper, lower-fidelity options to quickly discard suboptimal arms, thereby reserving the more expensive, high-fidelity evaluations for a small set of promising candidates. This approach could significantly outperform standard bandit algorithms like UCB, which do not account for variable evaluation costs. The authors motivate this setting with practical examples, such as online advertising, where a short ad display can serve as a cheap proxy for its long-term performance.

For this new problem, the authors make several contributions. They propose a cost-weighted notion of regret tailored to the multi-fidelity setting. They develop a new algorithm, the Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB), and provide a thorough theoretical analysis, including an upper bound on its regret. This is complemented by a lower bound for the problem, which shows that MF-UCB is nearly optimal under certain conditions. The theoretical claims are supported by simulations comparing MF-UCB to a standard UCB baseline.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper formulates a novel and well-motivated extension of the stochastic multi-armed bandit problem. The concept of trading off evaluation cost and accuracy is highly relevant to many real-world applications, including online advertising, clinical trials, and large-scale system configuration, where the cost of experimentation is a primary concern. This work opens up a promising direction for research.

2.  **Theoretical Depth:** The paper provides a comprehensive and rigorous theoretical analysis. The development of both an upper regret bound for the proposed MF-UCB algorithm and a problem-specific lower bound is a key strength, as it demonstrates that the algorithm's performance is close to the best possible. The analysis appears sound and provides valuable insights into the problem's structure.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation for the multi-fidelity setting, the new regret definition, and the intuition behind the MF-UCB algorithm are explained clearly.

**Weaknesses**

The primary weakness of the paper lies in a key assumption that may limit its immediate practical application. The analysis assumes that the quantities $\zeta^{(m)}$—the maximum deviation between the mean of the $m$-th fidelity and the mean of the highest fidelity—are known in advance. In many practical scenarios, such as the online advertising example, it is unclear how one would acquire these precise bounds. It would significantly strengthen the paper if the authors could discuss the sensitivity of their algorithm to inaccuracies in these parameters or suggest methods for learning or estimating them from data. Is it possible to relax this assumption and develop an algorithm that is robust to unknown or misspecified $\zeta^{(m)}$ values?
