This paper investigates a novel multi-armed bandit setting featuring multiple fidelities for each arm. The learner can choose to sample arms at $M$ different fidelities, with higher fidelities providing more accurate reward approximations at increasing cost. The authors argue that a strategy leveraging lower fidelities to efficiently eliminate suboptimal arms, while reserving higher fidelities for a promising subset, can outperform standard algorithms like UCB which do not account for these fidelity characteristics. A compelling application is online advertising, where initial low-cost ad displays can provide early estimates of long-term click-through rates.  The authors define a new regret measure tailored to this setting, propose an algorithm, provide a theoretical analysis including a regret lower bound, and support their claims with simulations. The analysis presented is novel. However, a key assumption is that the deviation $\varepsilon^{(m)}$ between the mean reward at the $m$th fidelity and the highest fidelity is known *a priori*.  It is unclear how these values would be determined in practice, particularly in applications like online advertising; exploring relaxation of this assumption would strengthen the work.
