Summary
The paper studies a stochastic K-armed bandit setting in which each arm can be queried at one of M fidelity levels. Lower fidelities are cheaper but provide biased observations whose means are within a known tolerance ζ(m) of the highest-fidelity mean (with ζ decreasing in m and ζ(M)=0). The authors define a cost-weighted notion of pseudo-regret, propose MF-UCB—an upper-confidence-bound algorithm that adaptively chooses both arm and fidelity—and prove that it can achieve better regret than naive strategies that operate only at the highest fidelity. They provide a regret upper bound, a matching lower bound under certain conditions, and simulations showing MF-UCB’s empirical advantage. The work fits within the UCB literature on stochastic bandits while explicitly modeling information–cost trade-offs via multiple biased, cheaper proxies; it differs from budgeted bandits and from multi-fidelity RL/MBO works that do not analyze bandit regret with biased fidelities.

Strengths
- Addresses a timely and practical setting where one trades off evaluation cost against information quality.
- Provides a clear algorithmic contribution (MF-UCB) with nontrivial analysis.
- Presents both upper and lower bounds, and simulations supporting the theory.
- Offers a potentially useful benchmark formulation for multi-fidelity decision-making in bandits.

Main concerns and suggestions
1) Regret definition and modeling choice:
- The chosen pseudo-regret multiplies rewards by the cost spent and benchmarks against always playing an optimal arm at any fidelity. While the ad-display example motivates this, an additive cost model (reward minus cost) feels more natural in many applications.
- Suggestion: Discuss how results and algorithms would change under an additive cost objective, or at least justify more fully why the multiplicative form is preferred and when it is appropriate. A short analysis or proposition for the additive case would broaden the paper’s impact.

2) Assumption 1 (decay of ζ(m)):
- The assumption appears opaque and restrictive, effectively imposing structure on fidelity quality. The paper says it is not essential, but it is used in proofs and seems to influence both algorithm design (choice of γ(m)) and performance guarantees.
- Suggestions:
  - Clarify precisely where Assumption 1 is used and whether bounds qualitatively degrade or simply pick up constants when it fails.
  - Provide a principled procedure for selecting a subsequence of fidelities that satisfies the assumption (e.g., skipping near-duplicate fidelities with similar cost/ζ), and show that MF-UCB retains its guarantees on that subsequence.
  - Include a sensitivity analysis (theoretical or empirical) showing how regret scales with slower ζ-decay.

3) Interpreting Theorem 2 and comparison to UCB:
- The advantage of MF-UCB is stated via sets K(m), the partition index JkK, and gaps Δ(m)k, which makes the result difficult to parse.
- Suggestions:
  - Explicitly state the UCB baseline as “UCB operating only at the highest fidelity” and present a simplified corollary comparing terms side-by-side, highlighting the λ(JkK)/λ(M) savings and when they dominate.
  - Add an intuitive explanation of the partition K(1),…,K(M) with a small numerical example and a diagram mapping arms to their dominant fidelity.
  - Summarize in prose: “Arms far from optimal are eliminated at low fidelities; only near-optimal arms consume high-fidelity budget.”

4) Lower bound gap and algorithm design:
- The lower bound is tight for arms in K(m)3 but can be loose for K(m)7, reflecting cases where distinguishing suboptimality is cheaper at a lower fidelity yet MF-UCB escalates prematurely due to its switching criterion.
- Suggestions:
  - Discuss whether a modified switching rule using confidence on Δ(`)k at cheaper fidelities (without requiring many high-fidelity pulls) could close the gap, or provide a formal impossibility showing the trade-off.
  - Provide guidance on parameter choices (e.g., γ(m)) or practical heuristics that reduce unnecessary escalation.

Evaluation along NIPS criteria
- Quality: The analysis appears technically sound, with meaningful upper and lower bounds; however, some assumptions and design choices need clearer justification, and the interpretability of the main bound could be improved.
- Clarity: The problem setup is clear. The presentation of the main results is technically dense, and the role of Assumption 1 and the K(m) partition needs more intuition and exposition. A clearer baseline comparison would help.
- Originality: The formulation of multi-fidelity stochastic bandits with biased proxies and a cost-aware UCB-style method is novel relative to standard bandits and to prior multi-fidelity works without regret analysis.
- Significance: The framework is relevant to applications with evaluative proxies (ads, algorithm selection, simulations) and could serve as a reference model; the empirical and theoretical results suggest practical gains.

Pros for acceptance
- Interesting and relevant problem formulation capturing information–cost trade-offs.
- Concrete algorithm with theoretical guarantees and empirical support.
- Provides both upper and lower bounds; near-optimal under conditions.

Cons for acceptance
- Regret model choice may be too narrow; additive-cost formulations are not analyzed.
- Assumption 1 feels ad hoc; its necessity and practical handling need clarification.
- Interpretation of main bounds is difficult; baseline comparison should be more explicit.
- Lower bound gap raises questions about the switching strategy.
