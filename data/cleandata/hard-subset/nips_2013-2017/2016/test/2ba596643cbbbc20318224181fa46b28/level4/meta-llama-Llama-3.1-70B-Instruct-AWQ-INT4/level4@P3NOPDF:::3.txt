The paper introduces a "multi-fidelity" version of the multi-armed bandit problem, where each of the K arms can be played at M different levels of "fidelity". Lower fidelity is less costly but leads to potentially greater bias in the estimate of the arm's reward. Specifically, pulling an arm at fidelity m draws a sample from a distribution whose expected value deviates from the arm's true expected reward by an amount whose upper bound depends on the fidelity.

The paper analyzes the natural UCB-based algorithm for this problem, where the width of the confidence interval is increased to compensate for the uncertainty stemming from the bias of sampling at low fidelity. The analysis of the algorithm is summarized by theorems containing regret bounds that, while mathematically rigorous, are somewhat difficult to interpret. The paper argues that this regret bound will often be significantly better than the regret bound attained by always playing arms at the highest fidelity and using the standard UCB algorithm to select which arm to play.

A brief section near the end of the paper presents simulation results to substantiate this claim. These results show that the proposed algorithm, MF-UCB, can achieve lower regret than the standard UCB algorithm in certain scenarios. However, the choice of parameter values used in the simulations, as detailed in Appendix C, is not thoroughly justified, making it challenging to assess the generality of these findings.

Initially, I was puzzled as to why the paper chooses to model lower fidelity as introducing more bias, rather than introducing more variance. Upon further consideration, the motivating examples provided in the paper, such as algorithm selection for machine learning problems, convinced me that modeling low fidelity as greater bias is well-motivated. However, I still have some reservations regarding the applicability of this model to certain scenarios, such as the example of displaying ads for shorter time intervals.

In summary, while I found the connection between the model and the motivating applications to be somewhat weak, it is not a fatal flaw. The main innovation in the paper appears to be the formulation of the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit problem itself. The proposed algorithm is a fairly typical application of the "optimism in the face of uncertainty" principle. The performance guarantee, while mathematically rigorous, is somewhat difficult to interpret. Overall, the paper provides an interesting contribution to the field of multi-armed bandits, but some aspects of the analysis and simulation results could be improved for greater clarity and impact.

Strengths:

* The paper introduces a novel formulation of the multi-armed bandit problem, which could be useful in various applications.
* The analysis of the proposed algorithm is mathematically rigorous, providing a solid foundation for further research.

Weaknesses:

* The connection between the model and the motivating applications could be stronger.
* The simulation results, while promising, are not thoroughly justified and may not be generalizable to other scenarios.
* The performance guarantee is somewhat difficult to interpret, which could limit its practical impact.

Recommendation:

I recommend accepting the paper, but with revisions to address the weaknesses mentioned above. Specifically, the authors could provide more justification for the choice of parameter values used in the simulations and explore ways to make the performance guarantee more interpretable. Additionally, the authors could consider providing more discussion on the applicability of the model to various scenarios, including those mentioned in the paper.
