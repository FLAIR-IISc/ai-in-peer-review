Here's a paraphrased version of the review while maintaining the original structure, meaning, and technical content:

The authors introduce a novel framework called Multi-fidelity Multi-armed bandit, where arms can be sampled at varying levels of fidelity, each associated with a cost. Low-fidelity samples are drawn from a distribution approximating the highest fidelity distribution, albeit at a lower cost. The authors propose an algorithm that adapts to sequences of available approximations and costs, provide a regret analysis, and establish a lower bound for the problem. Numerical simulations on synthetic problems demonstrate the proposed algorithm's superiority over UCB. Although the framework is intriguing and potentially impactful with further development, the paper is not yet suitable for publication.

Several concerns need to be addressed: 

1. The introduction of notations is disorganized, making their definitions difficult to locate. For instance, the definition of $s$ is not provided until a page after its initial use, and it is barely utilized in the remainder of the paper.

2. The use of advertising as an example is misleading in certain contexts, particularly when correlations with display time and non-stationarity are drawn. Non-stationarity is not relevant to this paper, and an alternative example might be more suitable (see point 4).

3. The regret analysis in this setting is challenging to justify. Regret analysis typically involves maximizing long-term gains and identifying the arm with the optimal reward-to-cost ratio. However, in this case, the optimal policy always favors the arm with the highest mean, regardless of the associated cost. This may obscure the cost of sampling low-fidelity levels when unnecessary.

4. A sample complexity analysis (or cost complexity analysis) could be valuable in the context of Multi-fidelity Multi-armed bandits. This could be motivated by the testing phase of prototype development for new products, where the goal is to identify the best product with high probability while minimizing the cumulative cost of the testing phase before mass production. Companies often rely on low-fidelity approximations due to regulatory, contractual, and facility constraints.

5. Certain sections of the text are unwieldy and could benefit from revision to improve the overall flow of the paper.
