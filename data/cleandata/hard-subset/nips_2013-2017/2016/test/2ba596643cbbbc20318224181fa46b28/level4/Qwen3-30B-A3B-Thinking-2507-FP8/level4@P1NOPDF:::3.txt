

The paper introduces a "multi-fidelity" variant of the multi-armed bandit problem, where each of K arms can be pulled at M fidelity levels. Lower fidelity incurs reduced cost but introduces greater bias in reward estimation, as sampling at fidelity level m draws from a distribution whose mean deviates from the true arm reward by an amount bounded by a fidelity-dependent term. The authors analyze a UCB-based algorithm where confidence intervals are widened to account for this bias-induced uncertainty. The theoretical analysis yields complex regret bounds that are difficult to interpret, though the paper argues these bounds often substantially outperform the standard high-fidelity UCB approach. A brief simulation section supports this claim, but the connection between the model and applications requires closer scrutiny.

I initially questioned why the model treats low fidelity as introducing bias rather than variance (a more common interpretation). While the motivating examples—particularly algorithm selection for machine learning—convince me that bias-based modeling is plausible, the specific ad display example (shorter intervals yielding biased effectiveness estimates) appears unpersuasive. Furthermore, in the machine learning context, it remains unclear why regret minimization would be the primary objective for an algorithm designer. Consequently, the link between the model and its applications, while not disqualifying, feels somewhat tenuous.

The paper's main contribution lies in formalizing the multi-fidelity bandit problem itself. The proposed algorithm is a standard application of "optimism in the face of uncertainty," lacking significant novelty in methodology. The theoretical guarantee, as noted, suffers from interpretability issues due to its complex regret bound. Regarding the simulations: Appendix C details the parameter choices but provides no justification for them. The results show MF-UCB outperforming UCB by a factor of 3–4 in many synthetic cases. However, without justification for the parameter values (e.g., specific \zeta^{(m)} settings), it is unclear whether this performance gap is typical or an artifact of the chosen parameters. The reader cannot assess whether these results generalize to practical scenarios.
