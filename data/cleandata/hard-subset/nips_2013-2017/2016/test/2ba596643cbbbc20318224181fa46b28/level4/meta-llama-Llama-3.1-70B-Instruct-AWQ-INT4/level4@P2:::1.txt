The paper proposes a novel framework for studying exploration-exploitation trade-offs in multi-armed bandit problems, where cheaper approximations to a desired experiment are available. The authors introduce the concept of "fidelity" and develop an algorithm, MF-UCB, that uses the cheap lower fidelity plays to eliminate several bad arms and reserves the expensive high fidelity queries for a small set of arms with high expected reward.

The paper is technically solid, and the problem studied is interesting and relevant to online learning. The authors provide a lower bound for the regret of the proposed algorithm, which demonstrates that MF-UCB is near-optimal. However, there are some concerns regarding the clarity of the presentation and the assumptions made.

The regret definition adopted in the paper seems unnatural, as it penalizes resource consumption multiplicatively rather than additively. The authors' example of ad-display motivates their definition, but it may not be the most general. It would be interesting to know what happens when the cost paid for suitable fidelity observations is additively factored in total reward (or regret).

The introduction of Assumption 1 seems rather artificial and opaque, involving constraints on the fidelity parameters given to the learner. It is unclear why the authors expect this assumption to hold in a general learning problem. If the zeta_m's don't decay as fast as required by Assumption 1, is there a general way of selecting a subsequence of fidelities to satisfy the assumption and hence the results?

The algorithm's design appears to be tailored towards performing well for some kinds of zeta configurations, but the justification of why these are the 'right' zeta sequences is missing. The gap in the lower bound also demands more investigation and perhaps a re-design of the proposed strategy.

Interpreting the main result (Thm. 2, the regret bound for MF-UCB) and contrasting with UCB (at highest fidelity) is not easy to grasp due to the highly technical nature of the presentation. The relative gain/loss of MF-UCB seems to depend on Delta and [[k]], which in turn depend on the rather complicated sequence of sets \cal{K}^(m). A more explicit explanation would greatly help the authors' case.

Strengths:

* The paper proposes a novel framework for studying exploration-exploitation trade-offs in multi-armed bandit problems.
* The authors develop an algorithm, MF-UCB, that uses the cheap lower fidelity plays to eliminate several bad arms and reserves the expensive high fidelity queries for a small set of arms with high expected reward.
* The paper provides a lower bound for the regret of the proposed algorithm, which demonstrates that MF-UCB is near-optimal.

Weaknesses:

* The regret definition adopted in the paper seems unnatural.
* The introduction of Assumption 1 seems rather artificial and opaque.
* The algorithm's design appears to be tailored towards performing well for some kinds of zeta configurations, but the justification of why these are the 'right' zeta sequences is missing.
* The gap in the lower bound demands more investigation and perhaps a re-design of the proposed strategy.
* The presentation is highly technical, making it difficult to interpret the main result and contrast with UCB.

Suggestions for improvement:

* Provide a more explicit explanation of the regret definition and its motivation.
* Clarify the role of Assumption 1 and provide justification for its introduction.
* Investigate the gap in the lower bound and consider re-designing the proposed strategy.
* Provide a more intuitive explanation of the main result (Thm. 2) and its implications for MF-UCB.
* Consider simplifying the presentation to make it more accessible to a broader audience.
