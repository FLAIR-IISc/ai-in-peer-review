

The paper introduces a novel multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit framework where each arm can be sampled at M distinct fidelity levels, with lower fidelities offering cheaper but biased estimates of the true reward (characterized by known bias bounds ζ(m) with ζ(1) > ... > ζ(M)=0). The authors formalize a cost-weighted regret metric accounting for both the sampling cost λ(m) and the bias, and propose MF-UCB—a UCB-based algorithm that dynamically selects fidelities to minimize regret. The theoretical contribution includes a regret bound showing MF-UCB achieves O(∑_k ∆^(M)_k λ(J_k^K)/ψ(∆^(J_k^K)_k)) regret, which can be significantly better than standard UCB (O(∑_k ∆^(M)_k λ(M)/ψ(∆^(M)_k))) when lower fidelities are sufficiently cheaper and accurate enough to eliminate suboptimal arms. The analysis is complemented by a matching lower bound for certain arm classes. Empirical results on synthetic problems demonstrate MF-UCB's superior regret compared to UCB, with the algorithm predominantly using low fidelities for suboptimal arms and reserving high fidelities for near-optimal arms.

Strengths: The problem formulation itself is a clear original contribution to bandit literature, addressing a practical gap in scenarios where cheap approximations exist (e.g., shorter ad display periods, cheaper algorithm evaluations). The theoretical analysis rigorously establishes near-optimality under the proposed assumptions, and the empirical results validate the theoretical insights. The paper properly situates itself against related work on budgeted bandits [13,15] and multi-fidelity optimization [7,10], distinguishing its regret-centric framework.

Weaknesses: The regret bound (Theorem 2) is technically intricate and difficult to interpret without deep engagement with the proof, making it challenging to assess the practical magnitude of improvement. The application motivation for modeling low fidelity as bias (rather than variance) requires stronger justification; while the ad example (shorter display periods yielding biased long-term estimates) is plausible, the claim about algorithm selection (minimizing regret as a goal for ML practitioners) lacks convincing context. The simulation parameters (e.g., ζ^(m) values) in Appendix C are not adequately justified, raising questions about whether the 3-4x regret improvement over UCB is typical or sensitive to parameter choices. The connection to NIPS topics is strong (bandit algorithms, optimization), but the significance of the improvement in real-world settings could be more convincingly argued.

Overall, the paper makes a technically sound contribution to bandit theory with a well-motivated problem extension. While the regret analysis is complex and the application links could be strengthened, the core ideas are novel and the theoretical results are compelling. The work would be strengthened by clearer interpretation of the regret bound and more detailed justification of simulation parameters.
