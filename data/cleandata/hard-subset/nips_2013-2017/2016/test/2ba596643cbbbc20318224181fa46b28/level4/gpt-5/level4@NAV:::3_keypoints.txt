- Proposes a multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit: K arms, M fidelities where lower fidelities are cheaper but potentially biased.
- Model: pulling an arm at fidelity m yields samples whose expectation can deviate from the true mean by a fidelity-dependent upper bound.
- Algorithm: natural UCB-based method with confidence intervals widened to account for low-fidelity bias.
- Theoretical analysis gives complicated regret bounds that are hard to interpret.
- Authors claim these bounds often beat always-using-highest-fidelity UCB.
- Short simulation section supports the claim; MF-UCB often improves over UCB by ~3–4× in experiments.
- Reviewer initially puzzled by modeling low fidelity as bias rather than increased variance.
- Motivating examples largely justify the bias model, but the ad-short-interval rationale is unconvincing.
- In the algorithm-selection example it is unclear why minimizing regret aligns with the designer’s objective.
- Overall connection between the formal model and motivating applications is weaker than desirable but not disqualifying.
- Main contribution is the problem formulation; the algorithm is a fairly standard optimism/UCB application.
- Performance guarantees are disappointingly opaque; synthetic experiments are hard to assess because parameter choices (Appendix C) lack justification.
- Raises concern that reported empirical improvements may be artifacts of chosen parameters (e.g., ζ^(m)) rather than representative.
