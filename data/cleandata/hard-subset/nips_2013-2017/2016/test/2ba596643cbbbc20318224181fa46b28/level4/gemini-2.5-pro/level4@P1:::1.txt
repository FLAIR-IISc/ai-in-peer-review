This paper introduces a multi-fidelity variant of the stochastic multi-armed bandit problem. In this setting, when pulling an arm, the learner can also select from a finite set of 'fidelities'. Lower fidelities are cheaper but provide biased estimates of the true (highest-fidelity) reward mean, with known error bounds `zeta_m`. Higher fidelities are more expensive but more accurate, with the highest fidelity having zero bias (`zeta_M = 0`). The authors propose a cost-weighted regret metric for this problem, introduce a new algorithm called MF-UCB, and provide a regret analysis demonstrating that it can outperform a standard UCB algorithm that operates only at the highest fidelity. The paper also includes a lower bound for the problem to show that the proposed algorithm is near-optimal under certain conditions.

The primary strength of this paper is its formulation of an interesting and practical problem. Modeling the trade-off between the cost and quality of information is highly relevant for online learning and sequential decision-making, with clear applications in areas like ad placement, hyperparameter tuning, and scientific experimentation. The proposed multi-fidelity bandit framework is a valuable contribution and could serve as a foundation for future work. The paper appears to be technically solid.

Despite these strengths, the paper's impact is hindered by a lack of clarity in its presentation of key results and assumptions, which makes it difficult to fully appreciate the contributions. My main concerns are as follows:

1.  **Regret Definition:** The proposed multiplicative, cost-weighted regret definition, while motivated by the online advertising example, may not be the most natural formulation for all applications. It would be valuable for the authors to discuss why this is preferable to a more standard additive penalty for resource consumption. Addressing how the problem changes with an additive cost in the regret would broaden the paper's applicability and strengthen its framing.

2.  **Assumption 1:** The introduction of Assumption 1, which constrains the decay rate of the fidelity error bounds (`zeta_m`), seems artificial and its motivation is not well-explained. It is unclear why this condition should be expected to hold in general learning problems. While the authors mention that the assumption is not crucial, it appears to be used in the proofs, and it is unclear if the algorithm's design and performance critically depend on it. The paper would be significantly strengthened by either providing a better justification for this assumption or, more compellingly, by discussing how to handle cases where it is violated. For instance, could a practitioner select an appropriate subsequence of available fidelities to ensure the condition is met?

3.  **Clarity of Main Result:** The presentation of the main result, Theorem 2, is highly technical, making it difficult to interpret the regret bound and to clearly understand the comparison with the standard UCB baseline (which I assume operates at the highest fidelity, though this should be stated explicitly). The relative performance of MF-UCB depends on the partition of arms into complex sets `K^(m)` and on the quantity `J_k^K`, but the intuition behind this partitioning and its implications for the regret are not clearly conveyed. A more explicit, high-level explanation of *when* and by *how much* MF-UCB can be expected to outperform UCB would greatly help the reader.

4.  **Lower Bound Gap:** The analysis reveals a gap between the algorithm's upper bound and the derived lower bound. This gap deserves more investigation and discussion. A deeper analysis could potentially motivate a redesign of the proposed strategy to close this gap, which would be a significant contribution.

In summary, the paper introduces a compelling learning model and makes a good start toward addressing it. However, its potential is currently limited by the opacity of its core assumptions and theoretical results. Improving the clarity of the presentation and providing stronger justification for the modeling choices would make the paper much stronger.
