The paper proposes a novel framework for studying exploration-exploitation trade-offs in multi-armed bandit problems where cheaper approximations to a desired experiment are available. The authors introduce the concept of "fidelity" and model the problem as a multi-fidelity bandit, where each arm can be played at different fidelities, each with a corresponding cost and reward distribution. The goal is to design a strategy that balances the trade-off between information, cost, and reward.

The authors propose a new algorithm, Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB), which adapts the classical Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) strategy to the multi-fidelity setting. They prove that MF-UCB achieves better regret than naive strategies that ignore the approximations. The paper also provides a lower bound on the regret, which demonstrates that MF-UCB is near-optimal under certain conditions.

The paper is technically solid, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the algorithm's performance. However, there are some concerns regarding the clarity of the presentation and the assumptions made in the paper.

Firstly, the regret definition adopted in the paper seems unnatural. The authors penalize resource consumption multiplicatively, which may not be the most general or natural approach. It would be interesting to explore alternative regret definitions, such as additive penalization of resource consumption.

Secondly, the introduction of Assumption 1, which constrains the fidelity parameters, seems artificial and opaque. The authors claim that this assumption is not crucial, but it is used in the proofs, and it is unclear whether it influences the algorithm's design and performance in a critical fashion. It would be helpful to provide more justification for this assumption and explore ways to relax or eliminate it.

Lastly, the gap in the lower bound demands more investigation and perhaps a re-design of the proposed strategy. The authors mention that MF-UCB may not meet the lower bound on certain arms, and it is unclear whether this is a fundamental limitation of the algorithm or a consequence of the specific design choices made.

In terms of presentation, the paper could benefit from more explicit explanations and examples to illustrate the main results. The technical nature of the presentation makes it challenging to grasp the relative gain/loss of MF-UCB compared to UCB.

Minor typos and suggestions for improvement:

* Line 56: "near-optimal" should be hyphenated.
* Line 126: The period before "for all m < M" is unnecessary.
* Line 179: Set notation (curly braces) would be better to define [[k]].

Overall, the paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of multi-armed bandits and provides a new perspective on the exploration-exploitation trade-off. However, it would benefit from more clarity and justification in the presentation and assumptions.
