Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces a multi-fidelity variant of the stochastic K-armed bandit. Each arm can be queried at one of M fidelities: higher fidelities are more accurate but more expensive, and lower fidelities yield biased yet informative approximations whose bias is bounded by known ζ(m). The authors define a cost-weighted pseudo-regret that measures performance against the best arm at the highest fidelity while accounting for the cost of each query. They propose MF-UCB, an upper-confidence-bound algorithm that adaptively allocates plays across fidelities: cheaper fidelities are used to eliminate clearly suboptimal arms, while expensive plays are reserved for a smaller set of promising arms. Theoretical results include a regret upper bound showing improved scaling relative to naive UCB that ignores low-fidelity approximations, and a lower bound showing MF-UCB is nearly optimal under certain conditions. Simulations on synthetic data support the theory.

This work builds on classical UCB methods (Auer et al.; Bubeck & Cesa-Bianchi) and is related to budgeted bandits and multi-fidelity optimization/simulation (e.g., reinforcement learning with simulators, multifidelity kriging), but differs in its explicit modeling of multiple biased fidelities, known bias envelopes ζ(m), and cost-weighted regret. The lower bound analysis, though specialized to Bernoulli rewards, clarifies when multi-fidelity information provably helps and where gaps remain.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation with a principled cost-aware regret notion tied to the highest fidelity.
- Algorithmic design is intuitive and well-motivated: thresholds γ(m) balance cost versus informativeness.
- Solid theoretical analysis: upper bounds that recover UCB behavior on “hard” arms but save cost on “easy” arms; a complementary lower bound with a sharp characterization for many arms.
- Empirical results match the theory (reduced slope and reduced high-fidelity queries).
- The paper connects to multiple application domains where multi-fidelity evaluations are natural.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Practicality: MF-UCB requires known ζ(m) and concentration ψ. In real applications, ζ(m) may be unknown or misspecified; robustness to such misspecification is not discussed. Similarly, guidance for choosing ρ and handling unknown ψ (beyond sub-Gaussian) would help.
- Assumption 1 (on decay of ζ(m)) is used to obtain the most appealing comparisons to UCB; discussion of performance when it is violated would strengthen the work.
- The lower bound applies to Bernoulli rewards and leaves a gap for arms in K(m)7. It would be useful to discuss whether adaptive switching rules could close this gap without incurring excessive high-fidelity pulls.
- Experiments are limited to synthetic settings; inclusion of a realistic case study (e.g., simulated ad allocation or algorithm selection) and stronger baselines would improve significance.
- Clarity: the main paper is clear, but the supplementary material contains numerous typographical errors and some notational inconsistencies that hinder readability (see list below). Several constants dependent on ρ are hidden under asymptotic symbols in the main text, which can be misleading.

Quality
The technical development appears sound. The regret decomposition and fidelity-partition analysis are careful, and the proofs (once typos are fixed) are convincing. The sensitivity to ζ(m) and the specialized lower bound assumptions are the main caveats.

Clarity
The main paper is well-organized and readable. The supplement needs proofreading and minor corrections. Revealing some constants (especially ρ-dependence) in the main statements would improve clarity.

Originality
The multi-fidelity bandit formalization, regret definition, and MF-UCB design are novel relative to prior bandit, budgeted bandit, and multi-fidelity optimization work.

Significance
The contributions are significant for problems where low-cost biased proxies are available. The theoretical insights are likely to influence follow-on work in cost-aware and structured bandits.

Reasons to accept
- Well-motivated new setting with clear theoretical contributions (upper and lower bounds).
- Simple, interpretable algorithm with provable gains over naive UCB in relevant regimes.
- Connections to multiple applied domains with multi-fidelity structure.

Reasons to reject
- Practical applicability is under-discussed (unknown or misspecified ζ, choice of ρ).
- Experimental validation is limited to synthetic data with modest baselines.
- Supplementary material has many errors that impede verification.

Suggestions and questions for authors
- Discuss robustness to misspecified ζ(m) and whether ζ can be estimated online with confidence bounds; consider adaptive or conservative settings of γ(m) under uncertainty.
- Provide guidance for selecting ρ and handling unknown ψ (e.g., default sub-Gaussian choices).
- Clarify performance when Assumption 1 is violated, and whether merging or skipping nearly redundant fidelities helps.
- Add a realistic case study and compare against stronger baselines (e.g., heuristic elimination using low fidelities, budgeted bandit methods adapted to this setting).
- Consider exposing key constants (especially ρ) in Theorem 2.
