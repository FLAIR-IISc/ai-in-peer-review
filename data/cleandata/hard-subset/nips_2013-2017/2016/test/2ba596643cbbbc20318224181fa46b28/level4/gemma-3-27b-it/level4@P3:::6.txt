This paper studies a novel multi-armed bandit (MAB) setting where evaluating an arm’s reward comes with varying fidelities, each offering a different trade-off between cost and accuracy. This “multi-fidelity bandit” problem is well-motivated, particularly in applications like online advertising where approximating long-term performance can be done cheaply but with inherent bias. The authors propose a Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB) algorithm, provide a theoretical analysis including a regret bound and a matching lower bound, and demonstrate its effectiveness through simulations.

The core idea of MF-UCB – leveraging lower fidelities to quickly eliminate suboptimal arms and focusing higher-fidelity evaluations on promising candidates – is intuitive and potentially impactful. The paper effectively formalizes this intuition and provides a rigorous analysis. The introduction clearly articulates the problem and its relevance, and the connection to existing bandit literature (Robbins’ work, UCB algorithms) is well-established. The regret definition is appropriately adapted to account for the cost associated with each fidelity level. The theoretical results, including the nearly optimal regret bound, are strong and demonstrate a clear advantage over naive approaches that ignore the fidelity structure. The simulation results provide further evidence of the algorithm’s performance.

However, a significant practical limitation is the assumption that the deviation between fidelity levels (ζ(m)) is *known* in advance. In many real-world scenarios, accurately estimating these deviations could be challenging, if not impossible. Exploring relaxations of this assumption, or developing methods to learn these deviations online, would significantly enhance the applicability of this work. Furthermore, while the paper establishes a lower bound, the discussion around its tightness and the discrepancies in certain cases (mentioned in Section 5.2) could be expanded. The explanation of how the lower bound is achieved is somewhat terse and could benefit from greater clarity.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The multi-fidelity bandit setting is a worthwhile generalization of the classical MAB problem, with clear relevance to real-world applications.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The algorithm is accompanied by a rigorous theoretical analysis, including a regret bound and a matching lower bound.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the problem setup, algorithm, and analysis.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The simulations demonstrate the practical benefits of MF-UCB over a standard UCB approach.
*   **Well-Motivated Problem:** The paper effectively motivates the problem and highlights its practical importance with the online advertising example.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Strong Assumption:** The assumption of known fidelity deviations (ζ(m)) is a significant limitation.
*   **Lower Bound Discussion:** The discussion of the lower bound’s tightness and potential discrepancies could be more thorough.
*   **Limited Scope of Simulations:** While the simulations are helpful, more diverse experimental setups might further validate the algorithm’s robustness.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a novel and well-motivated problem in the bandit literature.
*   The theoretical results are strong and demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods.
*   The algorithm is well-designed and the analysis is rigorous.
*   The simulations support the theoretical findings.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The strong assumption of known fidelity deviations limits the practical applicability of the work.
*   The lower bound discussion could be improved for greater clarity and insight.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of multi-armed bandits. The introduction of the multi-fidelity setting and the corresponding theoretical analysis are valuable additions to the literature. While the assumption of known fidelity deviations is a limitation, the potential benefits of this approach outweigh this drawback, particularly given the clear theoretical guarantees.
