- Introduces a multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit where each arm has M fidelity levels: lower fidelity costs less but gives biased reward estimates with fidelity-dependent bias bounds.  
- Proposes a UCB-based algorithm that widens confidence intervals to account for bias from low-fidelity sampling.  
- The theoretical regret analysis produces complex, hard-to-interpret bounds, though claims they typically beat always-using-highest-fidelity UCB.  
- Provides a brief simulation section that supports the algorithm’s advantage.  
- Modeling low fidelity as introducing bias (instead of variance) is initially puzzling and seems not uniquely justified.  
- Some motivating examples (e.g., ML algorithm selection) support the bias model, but other examples (ad display duration) make the bias interpretation questionable.  
- The suitability of regret minimization as an objective for ML algorithm selection is unclear, weakening the application link.  
- The model-to-application connection is notably weak, though not a fatal flaw.  
- Primary contribution is the new multi-fidelity bandit formulation rather than novel algorithmic ideas.  
- The algorithm is a standard optimism-in-the-face-of-uncertainty application; the regret guarantee is disappointingly opaque.  
- Simulation details are on synthetic data; Appendix C lists parameters but doesn’t justify their choices.  
- Empirically MF-UCB outperforms UCB by ~3–4× in experiments, but it’s unclear if this gap is representative or due to specific parameter (e.g., ζ^{(m)}) settings.
