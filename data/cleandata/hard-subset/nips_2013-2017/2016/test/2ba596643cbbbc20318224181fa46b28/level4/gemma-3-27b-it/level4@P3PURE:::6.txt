This paper studies a novel multi-armed bandit (MAB) setting featuring multiple fidelities for evaluating arm rewards. The learner can choose between $M$ fidelities, where higher fidelities provide more accurate reward approximations at a greater cost. The core idea is that a strategy leveraging lower fidelities to quickly eliminate suboptimal arms, and reserving expensive, high-fidelity evaluations for a promising subset, can outperform standard algorithms like UCB which do not account for these fidelity characteristics. A motivating application is online advertising, where initial ad display times can be short and inexpensive to estimate long-run click-through rates. The authors introduce a new regret definition tailored to this setting, propose an algorithm, and provide a theoretical analysis including a regret lower bound, supported by simulation results. The analysis appears novel and contributes to the theoretical understanding of MABs with variable fidelity.

However, a key assumption is that the deviation $\varepsilon^{(m)}$ of the $m$-th fidelity mean from the highest fidelity mean is known in advance. It is unclear how these values would be known in practice, particularly in applications like online advertising. Investigating the possibility of relaxing this assumption would strengthen the work considerably.
