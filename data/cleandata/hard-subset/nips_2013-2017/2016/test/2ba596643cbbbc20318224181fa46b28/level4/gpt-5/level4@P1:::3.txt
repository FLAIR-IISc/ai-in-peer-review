Summary
This paper introduces a multi-fidelity version of the stochastic K-armed bandit. Each arm can be played at one of M fidelities, where lower fidelities are cheaper but yield biased estimates of the arm’s highest-fidelity mean. The bias at fidelity m is upper-bounded by a known ζ(m) that decreases with m, and the cost per play increases with fidelity (λ(1) < … < λ(M)). The authors define a cost-weighted notion of regret that rewards actions by the highest-fidelity mean, regardless of the fidelity actually played.

They propose MF-UCB, an upper-confidence-bound algorithm that maintains a set of bias-adjusted UCBs per arm across fidelities. The algorithm escalates from lower to higher fidelities when the confidence radius at the current fidelity drops below a threshold γ(m), chosen to balance the cost of moving up in fidelity against the information gain possible at the current (biased) fidelity. Theoretical results show that MF-UCB uses cheaper fidelities to eliminate suboptimal arms and reserves expensive high-fidelity plays for a small set of promising arms. The paper provides regret upper bounds and a lower bound, arguing near-optimality under certain conditions. Simulations on synthetic problems indicate that MF-UCB often outperforms running standard UCB exclusively at the highest fidelity.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on classical UCB methods and differs from budgeted bandits and weak/strong labeler models by explicitly modeling multiple biased approximations per arm with known bias envelopes ζ(m) and costs λ(m). The multi-fidelity framing is distinct from prior multi-simulator RL work and from settings that treat “low fidelity” as higher variance rather than bias.

Strengths
- A clear and timely problem formulation: multiple biased approximations with costs per fidelity are common in practice.
- The algorithm is natural and principled: bias-aware confidence bounds and a fidelity-escalation rule that reflects cost–information trade-offs.
- Theoretical analysis includes nontrivial ideas (partitioning arms by fidelity-level gaps, a tailored choice of γ(m), and a lower bound), and the upper/lower bounds together provide evidence of near-optimality for a subset of arms.
- Simulations support the claim that cheaper fidelities can accelerate elimination and reduce regret relative to naive high-fidelity-only UCB.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Interpretability of the regret bounds: the main theorems are technically heavy and the resulting bounds are difficult to parse. While the narrative explains qualitative behavior, it would help to distill clean corollaries for simple cases (e.g., two fidelities with sub-Gaussian noise) showing explicit dependence on K, λ(·), and ζ(·).
- Modeling choice (bias vs variance): The paper convincingly motivates bias in some settings (e.g., subsampling data or compute), but the advertising example is less persuasive. It is not obvious that short display windows produce systematic bias in the long-run click rate rather than primarily inducing higher variance; more empirical or citation-based support would help.
- Objective in applications: In the algorithm-selection example, it is unclear that the regret definition used here (reward at the highest fidelity regardless of chosen fidelity) matches the practitioner’s goal. The paper could better justify why this regret notion is the right target for such applications.
- Experiments: The simulations are synthetic, and while Appendix C details parameter choices, it does not justify them or probe sensitivity. The reported 3–4× improvement is promising, but it is unclear whether this is typical or an artifact of specific ζ(m) and cost schedules. A sensitivity analysis over ζ(m), cost ratios λ(m)/λ(m+1), and noise classes (ψ) would make the empirical case stronger.

Quality
The algorithm and analysis appear technically sound. The use of bias-adjusted UCBs and fidelity-dependent thresholds is well reasoned, and the lower bound clarifies where MF-UCB is tight and where there may be room for improvement. However, the regret bounds are hard to interpret without additional simplifications or instantiated constants. The empirical section demonstrates the basic effect but lacks justification of parameter regimes and broader sensitivity exploration.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written, but the core bounds are difficult to digest. I recommend adding:
- A pedagogical two-fidelity corollary with explicit big-O constants for sub-Gaussian rewards, illustrating the improvement over high-fidelity UCB as a function of λ(1), λ(2), and ζ(1).
- A clearer explanation of the choice of γ(m) and its dependence on costs and biases, with intuition for when MF-UCB prefers to stop at a given fidelity.
- A more careful motivation of the regret definition for different applications, and a brief discussion of alternative regret notions.

Originality
The main novelty is the multi-fidelity bandit formulation with known bias envelopes and fidelity costs. The algorithm follows the optimism principle, but the fidelity-switching design and analysis are nontrivial. The lower bound is a valuable addition.

Significance
If cheaper biased proxies are common and ζ(m) is known or estimable, the setting is important and the approach could be influential. The theoretical results suggest clear advantages when many arms can be eliminated cheaply. The current empirical evidence is suggestive but limited to synthetic data; broader experimental validation would increase the paper’s impact.

Pros
- Clear and relevant new problem setting.
- Principled algorithm leveraging low fidelities for elimination.
- Theoretical upper and lower bounds with near-optimality in important cases.
- Empirical gains over high-fidelity UCB.

Cons
- Regret bounds are difficult to interpret without simplified corollaries.
- Some application motivations (e.g., advertising) are unconvincing; objective alignment is unclear for algorithm selection.
- Simulations lack parameter justification and sensitivity analysis; improvements may depend on chosen ζ(m) and λ(m).

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Provide a simple two-fidelity corollary highlighting concrete dependence on K, λ(1), λ(2), ζ(1), and ψ for sub-Gaussian rewards.
- Justify the advertising example with empirical evidence or references that short display windows produce systematic bias, not primarily higher variance.
- Clarify how your regret definition maps to goals in algorithm selection and other applications; consider discussing alternative regret notions.
- Add sensitivity analyses over ζ(·) and λ(·), and clarify whether the 3–4× improvement should be expected broadly or only in particular regimes.
