The authors present a novel framework for Multi-fidelity Multi-armed bandits, where arms can be sampled at different levels of fidelity, each associated with a cost. Low-fidelity samples are drawn from a distribution approximating the highest fidelity distribution, but at a lower cost. The authors propose an algorithm that adapts to the sequences of available approximations and costs, provide a regret analysis, and establish a lower bound for the problem. Numerical simulations on synthetic problems demonstrate the superiority of the proposed algorithm over UCB. Although the framework is intriguing and has the potential to be impactful with further development, its current state makes it unsuitable for publication.

Several issues detract from the paper's clarity and coherence. Firstly, the introduction of notations is confusing, with definitions scattered throughout the text, making them difficult to find. For instance, the definition of $s$ is not provided until a page after its initial use and is barely utilized in the remainder of the paper. A more organized and concise presentation of notations would improve the readability of the paper.

The use of advertising as an example is misleading in some instances. The authors draw correlations between display time and non-stationarity, which is outside the scope of this paper. Non-stationarity is not relevant to the problem at hand, and its inclusion may cause confusion. Consider replacing this example with a more suitable one, such as the testing phase of prototypes for new product conception (discussed in point 4).

The regret analysis in this setting is difficult to justify. A typical regret analysis aims to maximize gain in the long run by finding the arm with the best reward-to-cost ratio. However, in this case, the optimal policy always chooses the arm with the highest mean, regardless of the associated cost. This may obscure the cost of sampling low-fidelity levels when not necessary. A more nuanced analysis that accounts for the trade-off between reward and cost would strengthen the paper.

A sample complexity analysis (or cost complexity analysis) would be valuable in the context of Multi-fidelity Multi-armed bandits. This could be motivated by the testing phase of prototypes, where the goal is to identify the best product with high probability while minimizing the cumulative cost of the testing phase before mass production. Companies often rely on low-fidelity approximations due to regulatory, contractual, or infrastructural constraints, making this analysis relevant.

Finally, some sections of the text are unwieldy and could benefit from a thorough revision to improve clarity and flow. A streamlined presentation would enhance the overall quality of the paper.
