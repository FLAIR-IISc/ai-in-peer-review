The paper studies a multi-armed bandit setting in which, for each pull, the learner also selects a fidelity level from a finite set. Pulling an arm at a given fidelity yields a stochastic i.i.d. reward whose mean is within ζm of the arm’s true mean; the ζm parameters (errors) are known a priori, and the highest fidelity corresponds to ζ = 0. Lower fidelities are cheaper than higher ones. The authors formulate the problem of sequentially choosing arms and fidelities to maximize a notion of net reward, propose a new algorithm, and argue that it can outperform UCB run exclusively at the highest fidelity. The paper also provides a lower bound on the regret of the proposed algorithm.

The problem is interesting and relevant: it models the trade-off between information, cost, and reward (choosing low-information/cheap versus high-information/expensive observations) in online learning, specifically stochastic bandits. In this sense, it could serve as a useful benchmark to build upon. Although the paper appears technically solid, a key shortcoming is the lack of sufficient explanation of its results and assumptions.

The adopted regret definition appears somewhat unnatural from one perspective: why not penalize resource consumption (cost) additively rather than multiplicatively, as done here? The ad-display example motivates the chosen definition, but it may not be the most general. Understanding what happens when the cost of fidelity is incorporated additively into total reward (or regret) seems a more natural question and would be valuable to know.

Assumption 1 feels artificial/opaque, imposing constraints on the fidelity parameters provided to the learner. Why should this hold in general learning problems? More importantly, if the ζm do not decay as fast as required by Assumption 1, is there a general way to select a subsequence of fidelities that satisfies the assumption and thereby preserves the results? This would significantly strengthen the paper’s message. Although the authors note that the assumption is not crucial, it seems to be used in the proofs; my main concern is whether it critically influences the algorithm’s design and performance. The algorithm appears tailored to perform well for certain configurations of ζ, but the justification for why these are the “right” ζ sequences is missing. The gap in the lower bound also warrants further investigation and perhaps a redesign of the proposed strategy.

Interpreting the main result (Thm. 2, the regret bound for MF-UCB) and contrasting it with UCB (run at the highest fidelity? this is not explicitly stated) is difficult due to the highly technical presentation. The relative gain or loss of MF-UCB seems to depend on Δ and [[k]], which in turn depend on the rather complicated sequence of sets K^(m). A more explicit, high-level explanation would substantially strengthen the authors’ case.

Overall, the paper starts from an interesting learning model but rapidly loses clarity in presenting and interpreting the results.
