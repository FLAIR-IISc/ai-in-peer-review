

This paper introduces a novel multi-armed bandit framework incorporating multiple fidelities. In this setting, the learner may select an arm at $M$ distinct fidelity levels, where each higher fidelity provides a more accurate reward approximation but incurs greater cost. A strategy that leverages lower-cost fidelities to eliminate suboptimal arms, reserving higher-cost fidelities for a small subset of promising arms, may outperform the standard UCB algorithm, which ignores fidelity-specific cost structures. This setting is applicable to online advertising, where displaying ads for brief periods can approximate long-term click behavior under cost constraints. The authors propose a new regret definition, present a novel algorithm with theoretical guarantees (including a regret lower bound), and validate their approach via simulations. The analysis is technically sound and novel. However, the assumption that $\varepsilon^{(m)}$, the deviation of the $m$-th fidelity's mean reward from the highest fidelity's mean, is known a priori raises a practical concern: how are these values obtained in real applications (e.g., online advertising)? Is it possible to relax this assumption?
