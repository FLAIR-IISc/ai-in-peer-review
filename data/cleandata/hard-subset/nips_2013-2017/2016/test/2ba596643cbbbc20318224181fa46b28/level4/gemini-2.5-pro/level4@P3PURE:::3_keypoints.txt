- Introduces a multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit problem where each arm can be pulled at M fidelity levels with lower fidelity being cheaper but inducing bounded bias in samples.
- Models lower fidelity as increased bias (bounded deviation from true mean) rather than increased variance, with some motivating examples offered.
- Proposes a natural UCB-based algorithm that widens confidence bounds to account for low-fidelity bias.
- Theoretical analysis yields complex regret bounds that are argued to improve over a baseline that runs standard UCB only at the highest fidelity.
- Primary contribution is the problem formulation; modeling choice (bias vs variance) is plausible but not definitively justified.
- Motivations feel tenuous in places (e.g., short ad displays causing biased long-term effectiveness; algorithm selection where regret may not be the main objective).
- Technical novelty is modest—the algorithm is a straightforward application of optimism in the face of uncertainty.
- Performance guarantees are difficult to interpret, limiting practical insight.
- Empirical validation is limited to synthetic simulations with parameter choices listed but not justified.
- Reported 3–4× improvement over standard UCB may be an artifact of specific parameter settings (e.g., ζ^(m)) and its generality is unclear.
