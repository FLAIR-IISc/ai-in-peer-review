

The paper addresses a multi-armed bandit problem incorporating a "fidelity" parameter, where the learner selects a fidelity level (from a finite set) for the pulled arm. Each arm pulled at fidelity level $m$ yields a stochastic iid reward with mean within $\zeta_m$ of the true mean, where $\zeta_m$ (the error tolerance) is known a priori and $\zeta_M = 0$ for the highest fidelity level $M$. Lower fidelity levels incur lower costs than higher ones. The authors formulate the sequential decision problem to maximize net reward, propose a new algorithm (MF-UCB), and claim it outperforms UCB operating solely at high fidelity. A regret lower bound is also provided.  

The problem formulation—modeling the tradeoff between information, cost, and reward—is interesting and relevant to online learning, particularly stochastic bandits, and could serve as a useful benchmark. However, the paper suffers from insufficient justification of key assumptions and results. The regret definition, which penalizes cost multiplicatively (e.g., $\text{net reward} = \text{reward} \times (1 - \text{cost})$), appears unnatural. A more standard approach would penalize cost additively (e.g., $\text{net reward} = \text{reward} - \text{cost}$), and the authors' ad-display example does not generalize this motivation. The authors should clarify whether additive cost incorporation would yield a more natural problem formulation and how it impacts their results.  

Assumption 1, which imposes constraints on the fidelity parameters $\{\zeta_m\}$, seems artificial and lacks justification. Why would such constraints hold in general learning settings? Crucially, if $\zeta_m$ decays slower than required by Assumption 1, the paper does not address how to select a subsequence of fidelities satisfying the assumption to preserve the results. While the authors state the assumption is "not crucial," it is used in proofs, and its influence on algorithm design and performance is unclear. The algorithm appears tailored for specific $\zeta_m$ sequences without justification for why these sequences are "correct."  

The lower bound analysis contains a gap requiring further investigation, which may necessitate re-designing the proposed strategy. The interpretation of Theorem 2 (the MF-UCB regret bound) and its comparison to UCB (which operates at the highest fidelity level, though this is not explicitly stated) is difficult due to the technical presentation. The relative performance of MF-UCB depends on $\Delta$ and $[k]$, which themselves depend on the complex sequence of sets $\mathcal{K}^{(m)}$. A more explicit explanation of the key dependencies and relative gains would strengthen the argument.  

Overall, the paper begins promisingly by introducing a relevant model but loses clarity in presenting results and justifying assumptions. Minor corrections:  
- Line 56: "near-optimal" (correct as written, but ensure consistency)  
- Line 126: Remove the period before "for all $m < M$"  
- Line 179: Use curly braces for set notation, e.g., $\{k\}$ instead of $[k]$
