The paper models multi-armed bandit problems with a novel “fidelity” component, allowing the learner to choose, from a finite set, the precision with which an arm’s reward is estimated. Pulling an arm at a given fidelity yields a stochastic, i.i.d. reward with a mean within ζ<sub>m</sub> of the true mean, where the ζ<sub>m</sub> (errors) are known a priori, and the highest fidelity corresponds to ζ<sub>m</sub> = 0. Furthermore, lower fidelity pulls are less costly. The authors formulate the problem as sequential arm and fidelity selection to maximize net reward, propose a new algorithm for this problem, and demonstrate its potential to outperform UCB operating solely at the highest fidelity level. A lower bound on the proposed algorithm’s regret is also provided. 

This work addresses an interesting and relevant problem – modeling the tradeoff between information, cost, and reward (i.e., balancing low-cost, low-information observations with high-cost, high-information ones) in online learning, specifically within the stochastic bandit framework. As such, it may serve as a useful benchmark for future research. While the paper appears technically sound, a key weakness lies in the insufficient explanation of its results and underlying assumptions.

The adopted regret definition feels somewhat unnatural. Why not penalize resource consumption (cost) additively, rather than multiplicatively as done here? The authors motivate this choice with an ad-display example, but this may not generalize well. A more natural question, and one whose answer would be valuable, is how the results change when cost is incorporated additively into the total reward (or regret).

The introduction of Assumption 1 seems artificial and opaque, imposing constraints on the fidelity parameters known to the learner. What justification is there to expect this assumption to hold in a general learning problem? Critically, if the ζ<sub>m</sub> values do not decay as rapidly as required by Assumption 1, is there a principled method for selecting a subsequence of fidelities to satisfy the assumption and, consequently, the paper’s results? Addressing this would significantly strengthen the paper’s core message. Though the authors state that the assumption is not crucial, it appears integral to the proofs, and my primary concern is whether it fundamentally influences the algorithm’s design and performance. The algorithm's design appears tailored to specific ζ<sub>m</sub> configurations, but lacks a clear justification for why these configurations are preferred. Furthermore, the gap in the lower bound warrants further investigation and potentially a re-evaluation of the proposed strategy.

Interpreting the main result (Theorem 2, the regret bound for MF-UCB) and comparing it with UCB (presumably at the highest fidelity, though this isn't explicitly stated) is challenging due to the technical presentation. The relative gain or loss of MF-UCB appears to depend on Δ and |K|, which themselves are determined by the complex sequence of sets K<sup>(m)</sup>. A more explicit explanation would considerably strengthen the authors' argument.

Overall, the paper demonstrates a promising start by considering an intriguing learning model, but rapidly loses clarity in the presentation of its results.
