### **Summary**

This paper introduces and analyzes a novel multi-armed bandit (MAB) setting with variable "fidelities." In this framework, a learner can pull an arm at one of $M$ different fidelity levels. Each successive fidelity level provides a more accurate approximation of the true reward but incurs a greater cost. The central idea is to design a strategy that leverages cheap, low-fidelity pulls to quickly eliminate suboptimal arms, thereby reserving the more expensive, high-fidelity pulls for a small set of promising candidates. This approach could significantly outperform standard MAB algorithms, like UCB, that do not account for such a cost-accuracy trade-off. The authors motivate this problem with a practical application in online advertising, where brief, inexpensive ad displays can be used to estimate long-term click-through rates.

The paper's contributions include a new regret definition tailored to this setting, an algorithm based on successive elimination, a thorough theoretical analysis featuring both regret upper and lower bounds, and simulation results to validate the approach.

### **Strengths**

The problem formulation is well-motivated, elegant, and addresses a practical challenge in resource-constrained sequential decision-making. The trade-off between the cost and quality of information is a crucial aspect of many real-world problems, and this paper provides a principled framework for navigating it.

The proposed algorithm is intuitive and theoretically sound. The theoretical analysis is a key strength of the paper; by providing both an upper bound for the proposed algorithm and a matching lower bound for the problem, the authors convincingly demonstrate the near-optimality of their approach. The novelty of the analysis and the problem setting represent a solid contribution to the bandit literature.

### **Main Weakness / Question for Authors**

A primary concern is the assumption that the deviation parameters, $\varepsilon^{(m)}$, are known in advance. These parameters represent the maximum deviation of the mean reward at fidelity $m$ from the mean reward at the highest fidelity, $M$. This assumption seems quite strong and may limit the direct applicability of the proposed algorithm in many real-world scenarios.

For instance, in the motivating online advertising example, how would one determine a priori the precise deviation between click-through rates estimated from a few minutes of exposure versus those estimated over a full day? This value would likely depend on many external factors.

It would significantly strengthen the paper if the authors could:
1.  Discuss the sensitivity of the algorithm and its theoretical guarantees to the mis-specification of these $\varepsilon^{(m)}$ parameters.
2.  Comment on how these parameters might be estimated or bounded in practice.
3.  Elaborate on whether it is possible to relax this assumption. For example, could an algorithm be developed that learns or adapts to these deviation parameters online? An exploration of this possibility would be a valuable extension.

### **Minor Comments and Typos**

The paper is generally well-written, but there are a few minor errors in the proofs that should be corrected:

1.  **Line 326, 357, and 386:** In "... arm k $\in H^{(m)}$", the set 'H' should be 'K'.
2.  **Line 343:** For the last indicator term in the inequality, the argument "$\beta_{k^*,t} \leq s$" should be "$\beta_{k^*,t} \leq \mu_*$".
3.  **Line 345:** $\gamma$ should be $\gamma^{(m)}$.
4.  **Lines 346-347:** In the phrase "... and the last step uses $\psi( \Delta_k^{(m)} - \gamma^{(m)} ) \leq \psi( \gamma^{(m)} )$ when $\psi( \Delta_k^{(m)} ) \leq 2 \gamma^{(m)}$", both instances of '$\leq$' should be '$\geq$'.
5.  **Line 384:** For the inner summand on the left side of the first inequality, the summation appears to be over the incorrect set of arms. The summation should be over arms eliminated at the current level, not those that survive it.
6.  **Line 407:** On the right side of the last inequality, $\delta^{(m)}$ should be $\delta^{(M)}$, and the last term $\mu_* \kappa \lambda^{(m)}$ should be $\kappa \lambda^{(m)}$.
7.  **Line 431:** The condition "for $l \ge m$" should be "for $l \ge p$".
8.  **Line 440:** In Equation (17), the condition "$l \geq m$" should be "$l \geq p$".
9.  **Line 450:** $P(A_{n,3})$ should be $P(A_{n,2})$.
