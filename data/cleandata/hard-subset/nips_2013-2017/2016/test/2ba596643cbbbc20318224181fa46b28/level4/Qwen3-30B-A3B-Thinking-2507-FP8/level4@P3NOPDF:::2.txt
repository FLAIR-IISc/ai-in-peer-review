

This paper introduces a novel multi-armed bandit framework where each arm's pull incurs a cost proportional to its fidelity (accuracy), defining regret as the product of this cost and the standard pseudo-regret. The authors propose the MF-UCB algorithm, establishing both an upper bound and a matching lower bound on cumulative pseudo-regret. The core contribution is theoretically significant, offering fresh insights into cost-aware bandit problems with well-structured proofs and clear exposition in the main text. The paper represents a meaningful advancement in the field.

However, two critical issues require attention before acceptance. First, the supplementary materials contain numerous typographical and notational errors that undermine clarity and readability. For instance: page 10, Eq. (8) has a misplaced index; Section A.1 uses inconsistent notation (H^(m) vs. K^(m)); page 10, line 328, contains a missing denominator; and page 13, Eq. (15), mislabels variables. A comprehensive proofreading is essential to ensure the mathematical rigor of the theoretical claims is fully accessible. These errors, while individually minor, collectively distract from the paper's strengths.

Second, the paper's asymptotic notation—particularly the use of "up to multiplicative constants"—risks misleading readers. As noted in Theorem 2, the implicit constant absorbs the algorithm parameter ρ, which is not fixed but varies with the problem. Explicitly stating the exact inequalities (as provided in the supplementary materials) would enhance precision and avoid potential misinterpretation of the bounds' dependence on ρ. The authors should replace asymptotic phrasing with concrete expressions where feasible.

Furthermore, the practical applicability of MF-UCB warrants deeper discussion. The algorithm’s strategy of using low-fidelity pulls to eliminate suboptimal arms hinges on accurate estimation of ζ (the fidelity parameter). If ζ is misestimated—common in real-world scenarios where fidelity costs are uncertain—the arm elimination process could fail catastrophically. The authors should address this vulnerability, discussing sensitivity to ζ estimation errors and proposing mitigation strategies (e.g., adaptive fidelity scheduling or robustness guarantees). Without this, the algorithm’s real-world utility remains speculative.

Theoretical strengths are clear: the problem formulation is novel, the bounds are tight, and the proofs (despite supplementary errors) demonstrate sound methodology. The paper effectively bridges cost-aware bandit theory with practical constraints, though the connection to application domains needs strengthening. The core contribution is substantial, and the work would be a valuable addition to the literature with the proposed revisions.

In summary, this is a strong theoretical contribution with clear potential impact. The main text is well-written and compelling, but the supplementary materials must be meticulously corrected. The asymptotic notation should be refined for precision, and the practical limitations of the algorithm require explicit discussion. Addressing these points would significantly elevate the paper’s quality and readiness for publication.

**Recommendation**: Accept (with minor revisions). The paper’s theoretical merits are clear, and the requested revisions are straightforward and essential for clarity. The supplementary errors are fixable, and the practical concerns can be addressed in a brief addition. This work deserves publication in NIPS 2016.
