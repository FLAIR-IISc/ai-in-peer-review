This paper studies a new multi-armed bandit setting where the learner can choose to play an arm at multiple fidelities, each providing a better approximation to the rewards but expending larger cost. The authors propose a novel algorithm, MF-UCB, which adapts the classical Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) strategy to this multi-fidelity setting. They provide a theoretical analysis, including a regret lower bound, and conduct simulations to demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach.

The paper is well-written, and the authors do a good job of explaining the new setting and their algorithm. The theoretical analysis is thorough, and the simulations provide useful insights into the performance of MF-UCB.

One of the main strengths of the paper is its ability to provide a new perspective on the multi-armed bandit problem. By considering multiple fidelities, the authors are able to develop an algorithm that can take advantage of cheaper approximations to eliminate suboptimal arms, while reserving more expensive fidelities for a small subset of good arms.

The authors also provide a new regret definition that is suitable for this multi-fidelity setting. This definition takes into account the cost of playing an arm at different fidelities and provides a more nuanced measure of regret.

The theoretical analysis is novel and provides a thorough understanding of the performance of MF-UCB. The authors provide a regret bound for MF-UCB and show that it is near-optimal under certain conditions. They also provide a lower bound on the regret, which demonstrates that MF-UCB is competitive with other algorithms in this setting.

The simulations provide useful insights into the performance of MF-UCB. The authors compare MF-UCB to the standard UCB algorithm and show that MF-UCB outperforms UCB in several scenarios. They also provide a visualization of the number of plays at each fidelity, which helps to illustrate the behavior of MF-UCB.

One potential weakness of the paper is the assumption that the deviations of the mth fidelity mean from the one of the highest fidelity are known in advance. In practice, it may be difficult to determine these values, especially in applications such as online advertising. It would be useful to relax this assumption or provide guidance on how to estimate these values in practice.
