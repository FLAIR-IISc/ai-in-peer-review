- Paper introduces a multi-fidelity stochastic K-armed bandit where each arm has M fidelities with increasing accuracy and cost, and lower fidelities give biased but cheaper estimates of the high-fidelity reward.  
- Proposes MF-UCB, an algorithm that adaptively selects arm and fidelity to prune clearly suboptimal arms using cheap fidelities and reserve expensive plays for promising arms.  
- Provides upper and lower bounds on cost-weighted pseudo-regret and shows near-optimality in certain regimes.  
- Builds on standard UCB literature but addresses a distinct setting from budgeted bandits, multi-fidelity optimization, and multi-fidelity GP bandits.  
- Strength: clear formalization of a practically motivated, cost-and-bias-aware bandit setting.  
- Strength: principled MF-UCB algorithm integrating fidelity selection with arm selection.  
- Strength: theoretical guarantees showing how low fidelities reduce exploration cost and matching lower bounds up to constants in key cases.  
- Strength: empirical (synthetic) results qualitatively corroborate theory by showing fewer high-fidelity pulls on poor arms.  
- Weakness: supplementary material contains numerous typos and minor errors that hinder readability and should be fixed.  
- Weakness: frequent use of "up-to-constants" inequalities hides dependence on parameters (notably ρ), reducing interpretability.  
- Weakness: practical applicability relies on known fidelity-bias bounds ζ(m) and cost ratios; robustness to misspecification and guidance for choosing thresholds γ(m) and ρ are missing.  
- Evaluation — Quality: technically solid analysis distinguishing reduced exploration for far-from-optimal arms and matching standard UCB for near-optimal arms; appendix typos must be fixed.  
- Evaluation — Clarity: main text is clear; supplementary material needs careful proofreading and clearer exposition of constants like ρ.  
- Evaluation — Originality: novel multi-fidelity K-armed bandit formulation with explicit bias bounds and original fidelity-switching criterion and analysis.  
- Evaluation — Significance: practically relevant setting likely to stimulate further work; theoretical insights are of independent interest.  
- Pros for acceptance: well-motivated setting, sound algorithmic design, meaningful theoretical guarantees, and supportive empirical evidence.  
- Cons for acceptance: heavy reliance on known ζ(m) without robustness analysis, hidden constants (including ρ) obscuring practical use, and numerous typos in the supplementary material.  
- Overall recommendation: acceptance after careful proofreading of the supplement, clearer presentation of constants/parameter dependencies, and a brief discussion on robustness to misspecified ζ(m) and practical parameter selection.
