

The paper introduces a novel multi-fidelity variant of the stochastic multi-armed bandit problem, where each arm can be pulled at M different fidelity levels. Each fidelity m (1 ≤ m ≤ M) provides a biased estimate of the true reward with known error bound ζ(m), where ζ(1) > ζ(2) > ... > ζ(M) = 0. Lower fidelities (smaller m) are cheaper to use (cost λ(m) < λ(m+1)), while higher fidelities provide more accurate estimates. The authors define a cost-weighted regret measure that accounts for both the reward difference from the optimal arm and the cost of observations, and propose MF-UCB, a multi-fidelity extension of the UCB algorithm. They prove that MF-UCB achieves regret bounds that can be significantly better than standard UCB when the sets K(m) are small, and provide a matching lower bound.

This work builds on the classical bandit literature [3,9,11] and extends it to settings with multiple fidelity options. While there has been some prior work on multi-fidelity optimization [7,10,16], the authors are the first to formalize regret in this context with theoretical guarantees, making their contribution novel in the bandit literature.

**Strengths:**
The paper addresses an important practical problem where high-fidelity observations are expensive but lower-fidelity approximations are available. The problem formulation is well-motivated by applications like online advertising and algorithm selection, where the authors convincingly argue that multi-fidelity approaches can significantly reduce costs. The MF-UCB algorithm is well-designed and the theoretical analysis is technically sound, with the regret bounds demonstrating the expected improvement over UCB. The lower bound analysis provides strong theoretical support for the near-optimality of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**
The regret definition, while motivated by the advertising example, is somewhat unnatural from a theoretical perspective. The multiplicative cost term λ(m)rt in the regret expression (equation 1) seems less intuitive than an additive cost penalty would be. The authors should clarify why this formulation is preferable to a more standard additive cost term and discuss how their results would change with different regret formulations.

Assumption 1, which requires ζ(m) to decay sufficiently fast, appears arbitrary and lacks justification. The authors state it's not necessary for the algorithm's analysis but is crucial for comparing MF-UCB to UCB. It's unclear why this assumption would hold in general learning problems, and the paper doesn't address how to handle cases where it's violated. A more general approach that automatically selects an appropriate subset of fidelities to satisfy the assumption would significantly strengthen the paper.

The main regret bound (Theorem 2) is technically complex and difficult to interpret. The dependence on the partition sets K(m) and the indices JkK makes it challenging to see the practical implications of the bound. The paper would benefit from a more intuitive explanation of how the regret improvement scales with the problem parameters and when MF-UCB would provide substantial advantages over UCB.

The gap between the lower bound and MF-UCB's performance (for arms in K(m)7) is not fully explained. The authors note this is an open problem but don't clearly articulate how this gap affects the practical utility of their algorithm.

**Quality:** The theoretical results are technically sound and the analysis is thorough. However, the paper would benefit from more discussion about the practical applicability of Assumption 1 and how to handle cases where it's violated.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-structured but the presentation of the main results is overly technical. The significance of the regret bound and the conditions under which MF-UCB outperforms UCB are not clearly communicated to readers outside the immediate technical niche.

**Originality:** The multi-fidelity bandit framework is novel to the bandit literature, extending the standard bandit problem to incorporate multiple fidelity options. This is a meaningful contribution that addresses a practical limitation of existing bandit methods.

**Significance:** The problem addressed is important for applications where high-fidelity observations are expensive. The paper provides a theoretically grounded approach that could significantly reduce costs in such settings. However, the practical significance would be stronger if the authors could demonstrate the algorithm's performance in real-world scenarios beyond synthetic experiments.

**Recommendation:** The paper presents a technically sound contribution to the bandit literature with clear practical motivation. However, the lack of clarity in explaining the results, the arbitrary nature of Assumption 1, and the complexity of the regret bound presentation significantly reduce the paper's impact. I recommend rejection, but with the strong suggestion that the authors revise the paper to address these issues before resubmission. Specifically, they should:

1. Reconsider the regret formulation to use a more intuitive additive cost term
2. Provide a justification for Assumption 1 or develop a more general approach that handles cases where it's violated
3. Simplify the presentation of the main regret bound to make the practical implications clearer
4. Discuss the implications of the gap between the lower bound and MF-UCB's performance

The paper has the potential to be a strong contribution to NIPS with these revisions, but as written, it fails to clearly communicate the significance of its results to a broader audience.
