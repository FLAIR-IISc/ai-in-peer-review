This paper introduces the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit, a novel extension of the classic stochastic K-armed bandit problem. The key idea is that for each arm, a decision-maker can choose to sample from one of M "fidelities." Higher fidelities are more expensive but provide rewards from distributions that are closer to the true, desired distribution. Lower fidelities are cheaper but return a biased estimate of the true reward. The authors formalize this setting with a cost-aware notion of regret and propose a new algorithm, the Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB). The algorithm's intuition is to use cheap, low-fidelity samples to quickly eliminate clearly suboptimal arms, reserving expensive, high-fidelity samples for a smaller set of promising candidates. The paper provides a theoretical regret analysis for MF-UCB, comparing it to standard UCB, and establishes a lower bound for the problem, demonstrating that MF-UCB is nearly optimal under certain conditions. The theoretical claims are supported by simulations on synthetic data.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The problem formulation is novel and addresses a practical and important gap in the bandit literature. Many real-world applications, from hyperparameter tuning and A/B testing to physical prototype design, involve a trade-off between the cost of an experiment and the quality of the information it provides. Formalizing this as a multi-fidelity bandit problem is a valuable conceptual contribution.
2.  **Technical Soundness:** The paper provides a complete theoretical treatment of the proposed problem. The development of the MF-UCB algorithm is well-motivated, and the accompanying regret analysis and lower bound provide strong theoretical support for the approach.
3.  **Clear Intuition:** The core strategy of the algorithm—using cheap information to prune the search space before committing expensive resources—is intuitive and compelling.

**Weaknesses**

While the core idea is promising, I have several concerns regarding the problem formulation, clarity, and overall impact that lead me to recommend rejection in its current form.

1.  **Justification of the Regret Formulation:** A central concern lies with the justification for the chosen regret metric. The regret is defined relative to an oracle that always plays the optimal arm *at the highest fidelity*. This formulation defines the "best" arm based solely on its highest-fidelity mean `µ(M)`, irrespective of the costs associated with any fidelity level. In a setting explicitly motivated by cost, one might expect the objective to relate to maximizing the reward-to-cost ratio. The current framework seems to weaken the problem, as the optimal policy is pre-defined as finding the arm with the highest `µ(M)`, which may not be the most cost-effective arm to play in the long run. This choice appears to obscure the trade-offs that make the problem interesting.

2.  **Alternative Problem Framing:** The multi-fidelity concept might be better suited to a different online learning protocol than regret minimization. For instance, a "cost complexity" or "best arm identification" (BAI) framework seems more natural. Consider the motivating example of testing prototypes for a new product. The goal is not to minimize cumulative regret during the testing phase but to identify the single best prototype with high confidence, while minimizing the total testing cost before mass production. In such a scenario, companies are often constrained by budgets and required to use cheaper, lower-fidelity tests (e.g., simulations) before proceeding to expensive, high-fidelity ones (e.g., physical trials). Framing the problem in this way could lead to a more impactful contribution.

3.  **Clarity and Presentation:**
    *   The paper's clarity could be significantly improved. The introduction of notation is dispersed, making it difficult for the reader to follow the technical development. For example, the variable `s` (for the number of samples) is used in Section 2 before being formally defined later in the same section, and is then used sparingly. A centralized notation table or more careful organization would be beneficial.
    *   The motivating example of online advertising is occasionally confusing. The paper alludes to non-stationarity effects (e.g., "displaying an ad for longer intervals changes user behaviour"), but the underlying bandit model is stationary. This mismatch can mislead the reader and distracts from the core problem. The prototype testing example suggested above might be a more fitting and clearer motivation.
    *   Some sections of the text are unwieldy and could benefit from a thorough revision to improve flow and readability.

**Conclusion**

The paper introduces an interesting and potentially significant problem. The authors' initiative to formalize the trade-off between experimental cost and information quality is commendable. However, the current manuscript suffers from a questionable regret formulation that seems to sidestep the core reward/cost trade-off. Furthermore, the presentation has clarity issues that hinder readability. While the core idea has merit, I believe the work would be substantially stronger if it were reframed, perhaps as a best arm identification problem with a cost budget, and if the manuscript were revised for greater clarity. For these reasons, I cannot recommend acceptance for NIPS 2016 at this time.
