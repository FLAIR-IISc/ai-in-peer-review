

The paper introduces a multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit framework, where each arm can be sampled at multiple fidelity levels with associated costs, with lower fidelity samples being cheaper but biased approximations of the highest fidelity outcome. The authors propose MF-UCB, an algorithm that adaptively selects both arms and fidelity levels, and provide theoretical regret bounds along with a matching lower bound. This work extends the classical UCB framework [3] to handle multiple fidelity levels, distinguishing it from related work on budgeted bandits [13, 15] and multi-fidelity optimization [7, 10], which do not formalize or analyze regret in this setting.

The paper's strengths include a novel problem formulation addressing a practical scenario where cheaper approximations are available, a theoretically grounded algorithm with a regret bound demonstrating near-optimality, and empirical results showing MF-UCB outperforms standard UCB on synthetic problems.

However, I have several significant concerns that prevent me from recommending acceptance:

First, the paper's notation is confusingly dispersed throughout the text. For example, $s$ is first used on page 2 without definition and is only defined one page later, with minimal subsequent usage. Similarly, key notations like $T^{(m)}_{k,t}$ and $Q^{(m)}_{k,t}$ are introduced without clear contextual explanation, making the paper difficult to follow for readers unfamiliar with these specific symbols.

Second, the advertising example is misleading due to unnecessary references to non-stationarity. The paper discusses how "displaying an ad for longer intervals changes user behaviour," which introduces a non-stationarity element not addressed in the theoretical framework. A more appropriate example would focus solely on the fidelity-cost relationship without introducing extraneous concepts.

Third, the regret formulation is conceptually flawed. The paper defines regret as $R(\Lambda) = \Lambda\mu^* - \sum\lambda(m_t)q_t$, where $q_t = \mu^{(M)}_{I_t}$. However, this formulation fails to properly capture the cost-exploration trade-off. The optimal policy should simply select the arm with the highest mean reward ($\mu^{(M)}_k$), regardless of cost. The regret analysis does not account for the fact that lower fidelity samples provide cheaper exploration, which is the core contribution of the paper. A more appropriate regret metric would compare against an oracle that can choose both arm and fidelity levels optimally, rather than fixing fidelity at the highest level.

Fourth, the paper would benefit from a cost complexity analysis that better aligns with the motivating applications. In practical scenarios like product development, where low-fidelity prototypes are used before high-fidelity testing, the goal is to find the best product while minimizing cumulative testing costs. This is a more natural formulation than the reward-based regret analysis provided, which focuses on maximizing reward rather than minimizing cost.

Finally, the writing contains several unwieldy sections that could be streamlined. For instance, the formal problem definition could be presented more clearly with all symbols introduced before their first use, and the relationship between the theoretical results and practical applications could be better articulated.

To improve the paper, the authors should:
1. Clarify and consistently use notation with all symbols properly defined before first use
2. Replace the advertising example with a more appropriate one that focuses solely on fidelity-cost relationships
3. Reconsider the regret formulation to properly capture the cost-exploration trade-off
4. Provide a cost complexity analysis that better aligns with practical applications
5. Streamline the writing for improved clarity

While the paper's core idea is promising and has potential impact, the current formulation has significant issues that need to be addressed. The theoretical contribution is not adequately justified, and the application context is poorly explained. Given these concerns, I recommend rejection, but would be happy to reconsider if the authors address these issues in a revision.
