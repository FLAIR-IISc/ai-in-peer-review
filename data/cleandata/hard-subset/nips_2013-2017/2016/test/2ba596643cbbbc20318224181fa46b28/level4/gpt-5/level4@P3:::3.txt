Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper introduces a multi-fidelity extension of the stochastic K-armed bandit. Each arm can be queried at one of M fidelities with increasing cost λ(m) and decreasing bias relative to the desired highest-fidelity distribution. The key modeling assumption is a known fidelity-dependent bias bound |µ(M)
k − µ(m)
k| ≤ ζ(m), with ζ(1) > … > ζ(M) = 0, and standard concentration for each fidelity. The authors define a cost-weighted pseudo-regret for a fixed capital Λ that rewards plays according to the highest-fidelity mean µ(M), regardless of which fidelity was used. They propose MF-UCB, which maintains upper confidence bounds B(m)
k,t that add ζ(m) to account for bias and selects both an arm and a fidelity by comparing uncertainty to thresholds γ(m) chosen to balance elimination at low fidelities against the cost of escalating to higher fidelities. The main results show that MF-UCB concentrates exploration at low fidelity for clearly suboptimal arms and reserves expensive high-fidelity queries for near-optimal arms. The regret bound is compared to “naive” UCB that only plays at the highest fidelity, and a lower bound is given; MF-UCB is near-optimal under certain conditions (notably an assumption controlling how fast ζ(m) decays).

The work builds on UCB methods (Auer et al.; Bubeck & Cesa-Bianchi), and is distinct from budgeted bandits (e.g., Tran-Thanh et al.; Xia et al.), multi-fidelity simulators in RL (Cutler et al.), and multi-fidelity optimization (Huang et al.; Rajnarayan et al.) by explicitly modeling fidelity-specific biases and exploring/exploiting under a cost constraint. It is complementary to the authors’ GP-based multi-fidelity bandits (Kandasamy et al., NIPS 2016), which target a different payoff model.

Strengths:
- Clear and timely problem formulation: formalizes multi-fidelity bandits with biased approximations and costs, a setting relevant to many applications.
- Algorithmic approach is natural yet carefully designed: the fidelity-switching rule γ(m) captures cost–information trade-offs and is well-motivated by the analysis.
- Theoretical results include both an upper bound and a lower bound, with a transparent explanation of when MF-UCB improves over high-fidelity-only UCB (especially when many arms are obviously suboptimal at low fidelity).
- Honest discussion of limitations (e.g., the gap on the K(m)7 set) and conditions (Assumption 1 on ζ decay).
- Synthetic experiments show consistent gains over UCB, aligning with the theory’s qualitative predictions.

Weaknesses:
- Interpretability of the main regret bound is limited: the general form with ψ and multiple partitions obscures practical implications. Simple corollaries (e.g., sub-Gaussian, M=2) would help.
- The modeling assumption that ζ(m) are known and correctly bounded may be unrealistic; there is no guidance on estimating or being robust to misspecification.
- The choice of regret (rewarding µ(M) regardless of fidelity) is justified for some domains but not universally; alternatives are briefly mentioned but not explored.
- Empirical section lacks realism and ablations: parameter choices (e.g., ζ(m), costs) are not justified or stress-tested, and there is no sensitivity analysis or robustness to wrong ζ(m).
- Some motivating examples (e.g., short vs long ad display) are debatable, and the mapping to regret-minimization in algorithm selection could be clarified.
- Practical details (how to set ρ, γ(m) in practice; what if Assumption 1 fails; computational overhead) are not discussed.

Quality:
The analysis appears technically sound under the stated assumptions. The upper bound carefully tracks plays across fidelities via the K(m) partition and shows that MF-UCB incurs most cost at the “right” fidelity. The lower bound uses an informative change-of-measure argument and clarifies where the algorithm may be suboptimal. However, the results rely on (i) known bias bounds ζ(m) and (ii) a regularity condition on their decay. More discussion on whether these hold in practice, and robustness if they do not, would strengthen the paper.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written but heavy in notation. It would benefit from:
- A distilled, interpretable theorem specialized to sub-Gaussian rewards with M=2 (and perhaps M=3), including explicit constants and a short narrative summary.
- A figure or table mapping how γ(m), ζ(m), λ(m) interact in the switching rule, with an illustrative numerical example.
- A short section on how a practitioner might specify or estimate ζ(m) and λ(m), and what happens if ζ(m) are over/under-estimated.

Originality:
The problem formulation and regret notion are, to my knowledge, new in the bandit literature. The algorithm is a natural extension of UCB with a nontrivial fidelity-selection mechanism. The lower-bound analysis identifying the K(m)3 vs K(m)7 distinction is insightful.

Significance:
Multi-fidelity evaluation is common in practice, and the framework has the potential to influence both theory and applications. While the algorithm is incremental, the formalization and near-optimal guarantees are valuable. With stronger empirical validation and guidance on modeling ζ(m), the impact could be high.

Specific questions and suggestions for the authors:
- Provide a simplified bound and discussion for sub-Gaussian rewards with M=2, explicitly quantifying the improvement factor in terms of λ(1)/λ(2), ζ(1), and gaps ∆.
- Discuss how one might obtain or validate ζ(m) in practice. Can MF-UCB be made robust to misspecified ζ(m) (e.g., via inflation or online calibration)?
- Add experiments varying ζ(m), λ(m), K, and the gap distribution to show sensitivity and to indicate when gains are largest/smallest. Include a baseline that treats low fidelity as “higher variance” rather than biased, to empirically justify your modeling choice.
- Clarify the application mapping: in online ads, can you provide evidence or citations that short-horizon CTR is systematically biased relative to long-horizon CTR? For algorithm selection, clarify why regret minimization (as defined here) is the appropriate objective.
- Report the typical improvement factor and its variance across many random instances; explain whether the 3–4× gain seen is typical or due to a specific regime of parameters.
- Discuss how to set ρ and the practical sensitivity to this parameter.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel and well-motivated problem formulation with a principled algorithm and tight analysis in many regimes.
- Clear qualitative insight: use cheap fidelities to cull obviously suboptimal arms; reserve expense for near-optimal ones.
- Lower bound and near-optimality under explicit conditions.
- Empirical results align with theory.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Main bounds are hard to interpret; limited practical guidance and robustness to misspecified ζ(m).
- Empirical evaluation is synthetic and lacks ablations or real/semi-synthetic datasets; parameter choices are not justified.
- Some motivating examples and the regret definition may not fit all target domains.

Overall, this is a solid theoretical paper with a compelling formulation and promising results. With improved interpretability and stronger empirical support, it would make a valuable contribution.
