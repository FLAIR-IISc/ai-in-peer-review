This work introduces a novel multi-armed bandit setting in which each arm can be sampled with varying levels of accuracy, or fidelity. The cost of sampling an arm increases with the desired accuracy, and the regret is defined as the product of this cost and the standard pseudo-regret. The authors propose an algorithm, MF-UCB, to address this setting and provide both upper and lower bounds on its cumulative pseudo-regret. The main paper is clearly written and well-organized. However, the supplementary material, which contains the majority of the proofs, suffers from numerous typos and minor, correctable errors that hinder readability (a non-exhaustive list is provided below). Rectifying these errors would significantly improve the paper's clarity. Furthermore, the use of “up-to-a-multiplicative-constant” bounds throughout the paper can be potentially misleading, as these “constants” are not truly constant; for example, Theorem 2 includes a factor ρ (a parameter of the algorithm) hidden within the constant. Presenting the exact inequalities, as found in the supplementary material, would enhance the transparency of the results. From a practical standpoint, it is unclear how readily this algorithm can be applied to real-world problems. The MF-UCB strategy, which relies on low-accuracy pulls to quickly eliminate suboptimal arms, may be vulnerable to inaccurate estimation of ζ (the fidelity accuracy). A discussion of these practical limitations and potential mitigation strategies would strengthen the work. Theoretically, this paper offers valuable insights into the problem, accompanied by interesting theorems and rigorous proofs. Overall, I consider this a significant and interesting contribution to the field.
