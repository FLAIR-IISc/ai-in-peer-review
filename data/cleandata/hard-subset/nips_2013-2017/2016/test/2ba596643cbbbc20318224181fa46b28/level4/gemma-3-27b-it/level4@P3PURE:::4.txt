The authors present a new framework called Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit, addressing a problem where arms can be sampled with varying levels of fidelity, each associated with a specific cost. Low-fidelity samples are drawn from a distribution approximating the high-fidelity distribution. They provide an algorithm that adapts to sequences of available approximations and costs, along with a regret analysis and a lower bound for the problem. Numerical simulations on synthetic problems demonstrate that the proposed algorithm outperforms UCB. The framework is interesting and, with further development, could be impactful. However, in its current state, I do not believe the paper is suitable for publication.

1/ The introduction of notation is confusing. Definitions are dispersed throughout the text, making them difficult to locate. For example, the variable *s* is not defined until a page after its initial use, and is subsequently used sparingly. 

2/ The use of advertising as a motivating example is, at times, misleading. The paper frequently draws parallels between ad display time and non-stationarity, a concept that is outside the scope of the current work. Choosing a different illustrative example would improve clarity (see point 4).

3/ The justification for the regret analysis in this setting is unclear. A typical regret analysis aims to maximize long-term gain and identify the arm with the best reward/cost ratio. However, in this formulation, the optimal policy consistently favors the arm with the highest mean reward, regardless of its associated cost. This prioritization obscures the cost of sampling at lower fidelities when such sampling is not necessary.

4/ A sample complexity analysis (more accurately, a cost complexity analysis) would be a valuable addition, particularly in the context of Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandits. This could be motivated by the prototype testing phase in new product development, where the goal is to identify the best product with high probability while minimizing the cumulative cost of testing before mass production. The need for low-fidelity approximations is often dictated by regulatory constraints, contractual obligations, and limitations of existing facilities.

5/ Several sections of the text are unwieldy and could benefit from streamlining. A focused revision to improve readability is recommended.
