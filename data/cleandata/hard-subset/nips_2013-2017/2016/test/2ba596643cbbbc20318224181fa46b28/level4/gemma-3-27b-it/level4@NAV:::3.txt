This paper introduces a multi-fidelity extension to the classic multi-armed bandit (MAB) problem. The authors consider a setting with *K* arms, each playable at *M* different fidelity levels, where lower fidelity pulls are cheaper but introduce potential bias in reward estimation – specifically, the expected value of the observed reward at fidelity *m* may deviate from the true arm reward, with a bounded error dependent on *m*. The work analyzes a UCB-based algorithm adapted for this setting, increasing the confidence width to account for fidelity-induced bias. The resulting regret bounds, while formally presented in theorems, are complex and challenging to interpret. The authors argue that, despite this complexity, their approach offers significant improvements over standard UCB applied to arms always played at the highest fidelity. Simulation results are provided to support this claim.

Initially, I questioned the modeling choice of lower fidelity as increased bias rather than increased variance, as variance seems a natural interpretation. However, the paper’s motivating examples, particularly in the context of algorithm selection, provided sufficient justification for the bias formulation. I remain skeptical of the advertising example provided as motivation, and question the relevance of a regret minimization objective in the algorithm selection scenario. Overall, the connection between the model and its motivating applications felt somewhat tenuous, though not fatally flawed. 

The primary contribution appears to be the formulation of the multi-fidelity MAB problem itself. The proposed algorithm represents a standard application of optimistic exploration. However, the theoretical performance guarantees are difficult to readily interpret. The simulation results, conducted on synthetic data, are hard to fully evaluate; while Appendix C details the parameter settings used, it lacks justification for these choices. The observed 3-4x improvement of MF-UCB over standard UCB in the simulations raises the question of whether this performance gain is typical, or a consequence of the specific parameterization (e.g., values of ζ<sup>(m)</sup>) employed.
