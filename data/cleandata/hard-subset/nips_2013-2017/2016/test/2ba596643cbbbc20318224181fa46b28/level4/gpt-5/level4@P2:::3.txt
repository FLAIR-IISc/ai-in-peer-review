This paper formulates and studies a multi-fidelity version of the stochastic K-armed bandit. Each arm can be pulled at one of M fidelities with increasing cost (lambda(1) < ... < lambda(M)). Lower fidelities provide cheaper but biased proxies for the true (highest-fidelity) reward: the mean at fidelity m differs from the true mean by at most zeta(m), with zeta(m) decreasing to 0 at the highest fidelity. The authors propose MF-UCB, an upper-confidence-bound method that (i) builds per-fidelity UCBs adjusted by the bias envelope zeta(m), and (ii) adaptively chooses the fidelity at which to sample via thresholds gamma(m). The algorithm uses low fidelities to eliminate clearly suboptimal arms and reserves high-fidelity evaluations for arms whose high-fidelity means are near-optimal. The theoretical results include a regret upper bound showing improvements over running standard UCB at the highest fidelity alone, and a lower bound indicating near-optimality under certain conditions. A brief set of simulations on synthetic problems suggests MF-UCB often reduces regret by roughly 3–4x versus high-fidelity-only UCB.

Relation to prior work: The formulation extends classical stochastic bandits and UCB methods to a setting where observations can be biased proxies of the target reward, differing from budgeted bandits (which optimize reward/cost ratios) and from multi-fidelity optimization work that typically lacks bandit-style regret analysis. The setting also differs from weak/strong labeler active learning, as the objective here is cumulative regret under biased, cost-varying feedback.

Strengths
- Clear and timely problem formulation capturing practical scenarios with proxy evaluations of varying fidelity and cost.
- Conceptually simple algorithm derived from “optimism under uncertainty,” with a principled correction for fidelity-induced bias.
- Theoretical analysis that (a) explains how low fidelities prune suboptimal arms and (b) matches a lower bound on a broad subset of arms, making a compelling case for the approach.
- Simulations show meaningful empirical gains over standard UCB on synthetic instances.

Weaknesses
- The regret bounds, while comprehensive, are difficult to parse and interpret; key takeaways are not highlighted through simplified corollaries or canonical special cases (e.g., M=2, sub-Gaussian, simple zeta/cost schedules).
- The fidelity-selection thresholds gamma(m) are motivated but still feel opaque; more intuition and sensitivity analysis would help.
- The application motivation is uneven. Modeling low fidelity as bias is plausible in several settings, but the short-ad-duration example is debatable, and in algorithm selection it is not obvious that cumulative regret is the right operational objective.
- The simulations use particular parameter choices (as detailed in Appendix C) without justification or sensitivity analysis, making it hard to judge how representative the reported 3–4x improvement is.

Detailed evaluation
- Quality: The problem is well posed, and the analysis appears technically sound. The upper bound, lower bound, and partitioning of arms by fidelity provide a coherent story, though the presentation of the main bound is hard to digest. Assumptions (e.g., concentration, decay of zeta, and regularity) are reasonable for sub-Gaussian settings, but discussion of their necessity and practical verification could be expanded.
- Clarity: The paper is generally well written but notation-heavy. The definitions of K(m), J-sets, zeta(m), and gamma(m) could be accompanied by a running, concrete example (e.g., M=2) and a one-page “interpretation” subsection explaining the bound term-by-term. A brief table summarizing regimes where MF-UCB strictly improves over UCB would be valuable.
- Originality: The multi-fidelity bandit formulation with biased proxies is novel and distinct from budgeted bandits and multi-fidelity optimization without regret. The algorithmic idea is a natural UCB extension, but the fidelity-switching rule and its analysis are new.
- Significance: If multi-fidelity proxies are common in practice, the results are potentially impactful, offering both insight and a principled algorithm. The near-optimality results strengthen the case. That said, stronger empirical evidence and clearer interpretability of the bounds would increase practical significance.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Modeling: Could you compare your “bias” model to a “variance-only” low-fidelity model on the same tasks, or discuss hybrid models where low fidelities have both bias and reduced variance? This would help contextualize your design choice.
- Applications: For online ads, please substantiate the claim that short displays produce biased long-horizon estimates (e.g., empirical evidence, known effects). For algorithm selection, clarify why minimizing cumulative regret over a capital budget is the right operational target.
- Bounds: Provide interpretable special cases (e.g., M=2, geometric zeta(m), geometric costs) and plot the bound components vs. gaps to illustrate when improvements over high-fidelity UCB are largest. A theorem corollary highlighting the exact multiplicative factor vs. UCB under simple schedules would help.
- Fidelity policy: Expand intuition for the choice of gamma(m) and include a sensitivity analysis showing how performance varies with alternative thresholds. Can the gap to the lower bound on K(m)7-type arms be narrowed by mild modifications?
- Experiments: Justify the parameter choices in Appendix C, add sensitivity analyses over zeta schedules, costs, and M, and report performance under mismatched zeta (e.g., mis-specified bias envelopes). Including additional baselines (e.g., Thompson sampling variants, naive multi-fidelity heuristics) would strengthen the empirical section.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and well-motivated bandit formulation with biased, cost-varying proxies.
- Solid theoretical development with upper and lower bounds and clear insights about when multi-fidelity helps.
- Simple, implementable algorithm with promising empirical results.

Cons for acceptance
- Main regret bounds are hard to interpret without substantial effort; clearer corollaries and examples are needed.
- Application motivations are not uniformly convincing; stronger empirical linkage would help.
- Experimental evaluation is limited and parameter choices are unmotivated; lack of sensitivity studies makes the magnitude of gains hard to generalize.

Overall, the paper’s core idea and analysis are interesting and potentially impactful, but the exposition and empirical validation could be strengthened to make the contributions more accessible and convincing.
