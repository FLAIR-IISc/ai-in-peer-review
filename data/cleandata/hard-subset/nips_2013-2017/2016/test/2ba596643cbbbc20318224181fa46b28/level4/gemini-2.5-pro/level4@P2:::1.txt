This paper introduces a variant of the stochastic K-armed bandit problem where, in addition to selecting an arm to pull, the agent must also select a "fidelity" for that pull. Each arm can be evaluated at one of M fidelities. Lower fidelities are cheaper but provide rewards from a distribution whose mean is only guaranteed to be within a known bound, ζ(m), of the true mean (i.e., the mean at the highest fidelity). The highest fidelity is the most expensive but is unbiased (ζ(M) = 0). The authors formalize this problem with a cost-weighted notion of regret. They propose a new algorithm, the Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB) algorithm, which extends the classic UCB framework. The core idea is to use cheap, low-fidelity pulls to quickly eliminate clearly suboptimal arms, reserving expensive, high-fidelity pulls for a smaller set of promising candidates. The authors provide a regret bound for MF-UCB, arguing that it can significantly outperform a standard UCB strategy that only operates at the highest fidelity. They also derive a lower bound for the problem, showing their algorithm is near-optimal under certain conditions.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper addresses an interesting and practically relevant problem. The trade-off between the cost of acquiring information and the quality of that information is a fundamental challenge in many sequential decision-making tasks, such as online advertising, clinical trials, or algorithm configuration. Formalizing this within the multi-armed bandit framework is a novel and valuable contribution. The proposed model could serve as a benchmark for future work in this area.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper appears technically solid. The authors provide a complete theoretical treatment, including the development of a new algorithm (MF-UCB), an upper bound on its regret, and a corresponding lower bound for the problem itself. The analysis, though complex, follows a logical progression and builds upon established techniques in bandit theory.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Clarity of Main Results:** While the problem setup is well-motivated, the presentation of the main theoretical result, Theorem 2, is difficult to parse. The regret bound's dependence on the complex partitioning of the arms into sets K(m) makes it challenging to build intuition about when and by how much MF-UCB outperforms a standard UCB baseline. A more explicit discussion, perhaps with a simplified corollary or illustrative examples, would greatly help in understanding the practical implications of the bound. For instance, a clearer characterization of the conditions under which the gain is substantial would strengthen the paper's message.

2.  **Justification of Regret Definition:** The proposed multiplicative, cost-weighted regret definition is motivated by an online advertising example. However, this formulation may not be the most natural for all applications. A more standard approach might be to treat the cost of each pull as an additive penalty to the overall reward (or regret). It would be beneficial for the authors to discuss the implications of their choice and how the problem might change under an additive cost model. This would help readers understand the generality of the proposed framework.

3.  **The Role of Assumption 1:** Assumption 1, which requires the error bounds ζ(m) to decay sufficiently fast, feels somewhat artificial and may not hold in all practical scenarios. While the authors state it is not strictly necessary, it appears to play a role in the analysis and potentially in the design of the algorithm's switching criterion (γ(m)). My main concerns are:
    *   Does this assumption critically influence the algorithm's design and performance? The choice of γ(m) seems tailored to settings where this assumption holds, but the justification for why these are the "right" configurations of ζ(m) is not fully clear.
    *   Could the result be made more general? For instance, if a given set of fidelities does not satisfy Assumption 1, could one simply select an optimal subsequence of fidelities that does? A discussion on this point would significantly strengthen the paper's applicability.

4.  **Gap in the Lower Bound:** The authors acknowledge a gap between their upper and lower bounds for arms in the set K(m)7. This gap arises because MF-UCB might continue to a higher, more expensive fidelity when it could have been more cost-effective to identify an arm as suboptimal at a cheaper fidelity. While identifying this gap is honest and valuable, the paper would be stronger with more discussion on potential ways to close it, perhaps through a refined switching criterion or a different algorithmic approach. This is left as an open problem, but more insight would be welcome.

**Summary of Evaluation:**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound, presenting a complete piece of work with an algorithm and matching upper/lower bounds. However, the strength of the claims is somewhat limited by strong assumptions and a theoretical gap in the analysis.
*   **Clarity:** The problem motivation and algorithm description are clear. The theoretical results and their interpretation, however, are dense and could be made much more accessible.
*   **Originality:** High. The multi-fidelity bandit setting is a novel and well-motivated problem formulation that extends the classical bandit framework in a meaningful way.
*   **Significance:** Potentially high. The problem is relevant to many domains, and this paper provides the first formal analysis, establishing a strong baseline for future research.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Introduces and formalizes a novel, interesting, and practical bandit problem.
*   Provides a complete theoretical analysis, including a new algorithm (MF-UCB), an upper bound, and a nearly matching lower bound.
*   The work is a solid contribution to the bandit literature and is likely to inspire follow-up research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The clarity of the main theoretical results is low, making it difficult to appreciate the magnitude and conditions of the claimed improvement over UCB.
*   The generality of the results is constrained by a strong assumption on the fidelity structure (Assumption 1) and a specialized regret definition.
*   The acknowledged gap in the lower bound analysis suggests the proposed algorithm may not be fully optimal.

Overall, I believe the paper's strengths in originality and significance outweigh its weaknesses in clarity and generality. It introduces an important new problem to the community. I would be inclined to accept the paper, but I strongly urge the authors to improve the clarity of their results and to discuss the limitations imposed by their assumptions and regret model in the final version.
