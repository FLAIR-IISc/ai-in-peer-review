Summary and contributions
The paper studies stochastic multi-armed bandits in which the learner can choose, at each pull, one of several fidelity levels. Pulling arm k at fidelity m yields an i.i.d. reward whose mean differs from the arm’s true mean by at most ζm (with ζM = 0 at the highest fidelity). Lower fidelities are cheaper. The goal is to design a policy that adaptively selects both arms and fidelities to optimize a notion of net performance that accounts for information–cost tradeoffs. The paper proposes MF-UCB, provides an upper bound on its regret, and gives a lower bound that suggests regimes in which MF-UCB can outperform running UCB solely at the highest fidelity. The problem is interesting and relevant for cost-aware online learning, and the technical results appear sound.

Strengths
- Formulates a meaningful and practically motivated extension of stochastic bandits with fidelity–cost tradeoffs.
- Presents a concrete algorithm (MF-UCB) with provable guarantees and a nontrivial analysis.
- Provides a lower bound and a comparison that suggests advantages over high-fidelity-only baselines in certain regimes.
- Potential to serve as a benchmark problem and algorithm for multi-fidelity decision making.

Main concerns and questions
1) Objective and regret definition. The paper adopts a multiplicative penalization of resource consumption in the regret definition. This choice is motivated by an ad-display example, but it is not clear that this objective is the most natural or general. An additive accounting of cost (i.e., reward minus cost) is more standard in cost-sensitive bandits and seems easier to interpret and compare across settings. Please:
- Justify more thoroughly why multiplicative penalization is appropriate beyond the motivating example.
- Discuss how the analysis and results would change under an additive regret objective, or at least provide intuition or a proposition for this variant.
- Clarify precisely the baseline used in comparisons (e.g., UCB at highest fidelity with the same regret objective) and state this explicitly near Theorem 2.

2) Assumption 1 on the fidelity parameters. The assumption imposing constraints on the ζm sequence feels opaque and potentially restrictive.
- Provide intuition for why such an assumption is reasonable in practical multi-fidelity settings. What classes of ζm sequences satisfy it?
- Describe the algorithm’s sensitivity to violations or misspecification of Assumption 1. Which parts of the proof depend critically on it, and which are robust?
- If ζm do not decay “fast enough,” is there a principled way to select a subsequence of fidelities (a pruned ladder) that satisfies the assumption and preserves guarantees? Even a constructive heuristic with a supporting lemma would strengthen the message.
- Clarify whether MF-UCB requires exact knowledge of ζm and costs or only upper bounds, and discuss robustness to errors in these quantities.

3) Interpreting Theorem 2 and comparison to UCB. The presentation of the main bound is difficult to parse.
- Provide a plain-language corollary or summary that makes explicit how regret scales with key quantities: gaps Δk, fidelity errors ζm, and costs. For example, a statement of the form “for arm k, MF-UCB pays roughly O(minm f(Δk, ζm, costm)) pulls at fidelity m” would help.
- Define the sets K(m) and the quantity [[k]] intuitively: what arms fall into K(m), and how does [[k]] choose the fidelity that balances bias ζm, variance, and cost relative to Δk?
- Explicitly state the baseline (e.g., UCB at highest fidelity M) and summarize when MF-UCB strictly improves and when it does not. A compact table or figure comparing regimes would be valuable.

4) Lower bound and optimality gap. The paper mentions a gap between the lower bound and the algorithm’s upper bound.
- Quantify this gap and pinpoint its source (analysis looseness vs. inherent suboptimality of MF-UCB).
- Indicate whether tightening is plausible with refined analysis, or if a different strategy is needed in certain ζm/cost configurations.

5) Scope and generality.
- Discuss extensions to unknown or partially known ζm and costs, nonuniform variances across arms, and non-Gaussian rewards. Which parts of the analysis require sub-Gaussianity?
- Comment on computational complexity per round and any practical tuning parameters beyond standard UCB constants.

Clarity and presentation
- The exposition becomes highly technical quickly, making it hard to build intuition. Adding an early, running example with two or three fidelities that walks through MF-UCB’s decisions would greatly improve readability.
- Before Theorem 2, include a short “intuition box” explaining the roles of Δk, ζm, and costs, and how MF-UCB adaptively picks fidelities as a function of estimated gaps.
- When introducing Assumption 1, provide examples of ζm sequences that satisfy and violate it, and explain the consequences in each case.
- State explicitly whether comparisons to UCB are performed at the highest fidelity and under the same regret objective.
- Consider including empirical illustrations (even small-scale) contrasting MF-UCB with: (i) high-fidelity UCB, (ii) a naive low-fidelity-only policy, and (iii) a policy optimized for an additive cost objective. Experiments where ζm violates Assumption 1, with and without fidelity pruning, would be especially informative.

Minor comments and typos
- Line 56: “near-optimal.”
- Line 126: Remove the period before “for all m < M.”
- Line 179: Use set notation (curly braces) when defining [[k]].
- Define all symbols (e.g., K(m), [[k]], Δk) at first use and keep notation consistent.
- Clarify whether ζm are absolute biases or upper bounds on approximation error and whether they can vary by arm.

Overall assessment
The paper tackles an important and timely variant of stochastic bandits and provides solid algorithmic and analytical contributions. However, key modeling choices (the regret objective, Assumption 1) and the interpretation of the main results are not explained with sufficient clarity. With a clearer justification of the objective, a more transparent treatment of Assumption 1 (including guidance on fidelity pruning), and a reader-friendly summary of Theorem 2 and its implications relative to standard UCB, the paper’s impact would be significantly strengthened.
