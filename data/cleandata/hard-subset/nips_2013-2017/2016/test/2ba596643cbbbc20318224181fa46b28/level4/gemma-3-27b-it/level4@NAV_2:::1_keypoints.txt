- Paper models multi- fidelity stochastic multi-armed bandits where each pull has a chosen fidelity with known bias ζ_m and different costs, and aims to maximize net reward.  
- Authors propose MF-UCB, give regret bounds, provide a lower bound, and show MF-UCB can outperform high-fidelity-only UCB empirically.  
- Problem is interesting and relevant as a benchmark for cost vs. information tradeoffs in online learning.  
- Regret definition multiplies reward by fidelity-related factor and seems unconventional; additive cost penalization would be more natural and is not analyzed.  
- Assumption 1 (constraints on fidelity parameters ζ_m) appears artificial, opaque, and its generality is not justified.  
- It’s unclear how to proceed if ζ_m do not decay as required—selecting a subsequence of fidelities to satisfy the assumption is suggested but not developed.  
- Concern that the algorithm is tailored to specific ζ configurations; unclear if assumption critically affects algorithm design or performance.  
- The lower bound has a gap relative to the algorithm’s bound and requires further investigation or strategy redesign.  
- Theorem 2 and its comparison to (high-fidelity) UCB are hard to interpret due to technical presentation and complex dependence on Δ and sets K^(m).  
- The relative gain of MF-UCB depends on Δ and |K_g| via complicated K^(m) sequences; more explicit explanation and intuition are needed.  
- Overall paper starts well conceptually but loses clarity in presenting assumptions, results, and their implications.  
- Paper is technically sound and well-written mathematically, and the multi-fidelity bandit problem is a novel extension.  
- Major weaknesses: unconventional regret metric, artificial Assumption 1, opaque presentation of main results, and unexplained lower-bound gap.  
- Minor typos: l56 "near-optimal"; l126 extra period before "for all m < M"; l179 use curly braces to define K_g.
