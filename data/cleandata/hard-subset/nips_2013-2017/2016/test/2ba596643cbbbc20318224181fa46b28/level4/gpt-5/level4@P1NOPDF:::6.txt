This paper studies a multi-armed bandit problem with M fidelity levels. At each round, the learner can query any arm at one of these fidelities: higher fidelities yield more accurate reward information but incur higher cost. A strategy that leverages cheap, lower-fidelity queries to eliminate clearly suboptimal arms, reserving expensive, high-fidelity queries for a small set of promising arms, can outperform standard UCB-style approaches that ignore the fidelity structure.

The setting is motivated by applications such as online advertising, where one might run an ad for only a brief period to obtain a low-cost proxy for its long-run click-through performance.

The paper proposes a new regret notion tailored to cost-aware, multi-fidelity feedback, presents an algorithm with theoretical guarantees (including a regret lower bound), and provides simulation results. The analysis appears novel and technically interesting.

A key assumption is that each ε^{(m)}—the deviation between the mean reward at fidelity m and the true (highest-fidelity) mean—is known a priori. In practice (e.g., in online advertising), how can these ε^{(m)} values be obtained? Are they assumed to come from domain knowledge, prior calibration, or side data? Can the algorithm be made robust to misspecification or extended to estimate ε^{(m)} online (e.g., via confidence bounds or adaptive calibration), and how would this affect the guarantees? Clarifying this assumption and its practical implications would significantly strengthen the paper.
