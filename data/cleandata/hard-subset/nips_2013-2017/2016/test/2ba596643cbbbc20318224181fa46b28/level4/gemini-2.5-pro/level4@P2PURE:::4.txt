The paper introduces a new framework called Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandits, where arms can be sampled at different fidelity levels, each with an associated cost. Lower fidelity samples are cheaper but are drawn from a distribution that only approximates the true (highest-fidelity) distribution. The authors provide an algorithm that adapts to the available sequences of approximations and costs, along with a regret analysis and a problem-specific lower bound. Numerical simulations on synthetic problems show the proposed algorithm outperforms UCB.

While the proposed framework is interesting and could be impactful if further developed, I find the paper in its current state to be unfit for publication due to the following reasons:

1.  The introduction of notation is confusing. Definitions are scattered throughout the text, making them difficult to locate and track. For example, the variable $s$ is used a full page before it is defined, and is then barely used again, which exemplifies this issue.

2.  The use of online advertising as a motivating example is sometimes misleading. The paper frequently draws connections to ad display times and the concept of non-stationarity. However, non-stationarity is outside the scope of the problem setting presented in this work, and introducing it may confuse the reader. A different motivating example, such as the one suggested in point 4, might be more appropriate.

3.  The justification for the chosen regret analysis is unclear. In a setting with explicit costs, one might expect the goal to be optimizing a reward-to-cost ratio. Instead, the paper defines the optimal policy as simply identifying the arm with the highest mean reward, irrespective of the costs associated with sampling it. This framing seems to de-emphasize the role of cost in the final objective and may obscure the expense of sampling low-fidelity levels when it is not strictly necessary for identifying the best arm.

4.  The multi-fidelity setting seems better suited for a cost complexity analysis (i.e., a PAC or best-arm identification framework) than for a regret minimization framework. This alternative framing can be motivated by scenarios like prototype testing for new product development. In such cases, the objective is to identify the best option with high confidence while minimizing the total testing cost, rather than maximizing cumulative reward over a long horizon. For instance, companies are often constrained by budgets or regulations, forcing them to use cheaper, lower-fidelity approximations (e.g., simulations) before committing to expensive, high-fidelity tests (e.g., physical prototypes).

5.  Finally, several sections of the paper are unwieldy and difficult to follow. The manuscript would benefit significantly from a thorough revision to improve its overall clarity and readability.
