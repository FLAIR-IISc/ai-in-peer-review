The paper introduces a Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit (MF-MAB) framework in which each arm can be sampled at different fidelity levels, each with an associated cost. Lower-fidelity samples are cheaper but drawn from distributions that approximate the highest-fidelity distribution. The authors propose an algorithm that adapts to the available approximations and costs, provide a regret analysis along with a lower bound, and present simulations on synthetic tasks showing that their method outperforms UCB. The framework is interesting and potentially impactful with further development. However, in its current form, I do not recommend acceptance.

Major concerns:
1) Notation and presentation: The introduction of notation is scattered and difficult to follow, making key definitions hard to locate when first needed. For example, the variable s is first used before it is defined (apparently defined a page later after rebuttal) and is then rarely used thereafter. A clearer, centralized notation table or an earlier, consolidated definitions section would help substantially.

2) Motivating example: The advertising example is sometimes misleading. The text repeatedly invokes correlations with display time and introduces notions of non-stationarity, which are out of scope for the (apparently) stationary setting analyzed in the paper. This creates confusion about the assumptions and applicability of the results. It would be clearer to switch to a different motivating scenario that better matches the model’s assumptions (see point 4).

3) Regret formulation: I find the regret analysis difficult to justify for this setting. Regret typically captures long-run performance and, with costs, should reflect reward per unit cost. In the paper’s setup, however, the benchmark seems to be the arm with the highest mean at the highest fidelity, irrespective of cost. Moreover, the algorithm is constrained to sample lower-fidelity levels of the best arm, even when such sampling may be unnecessary, which can mask the true cost of low-fidelity exploration. The paper should more clearly justify the regret notion used, or consider a cost-aware benchmark that aligns with the objective of maximizing reward per cost.

4) Alternative objective: A cost (sample) complexity analysis may be more natural for MF-MAB, especially for best-arm identification under resource constraints. A compelling application is product prototyping: the goal is to identify the best product with high probability while minimizing total testing cost before mass production. Companies are often constrained by regulations, contracts, or facility limitations that require low-fidelity tests before expensive, high-fidelity evaluations. Framing the problem this way could better capture the benefits of multi-fidelity sampling and yield more actionable guarantees.

5) Writing quality: Several parts of the text are unwieldy. A thorough editing pass to streamline the presentation, reduce redundancy, and ensure that assumptions and notation are introduced before use would improve readability and impact.

In summary, the proposed framework is promising and the technical components (algorithm, regret analysis, lower bound) are interesting, but the paper currently suffers from unclear notation, a potentially misleading example, and a regret formulation that seems misaligned with cost-sensitive objectives. I encourage the authors to (i) reorganize and clarify the notation and assumptions, (ii) replace the advertising example with one consistent with stationarity, and (iii) reconsider the performance criterion—either by justifying the current regret definition more convincingly or by adopting a cost-aware regret or cost-complexity objective. With these revisions, the work could be substantially strengthened.
