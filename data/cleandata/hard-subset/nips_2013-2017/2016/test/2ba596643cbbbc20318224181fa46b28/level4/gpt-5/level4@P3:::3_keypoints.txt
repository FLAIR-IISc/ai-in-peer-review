- Introduces multi-fidelity stochastic K-armed bandits with fidelity-dependent costs and known bias bounds ζ(m).
- Defines cost-weighted pseudo-regret measuring reward by highest-fidelity mean µ(M) regardless of fidelity used.
- Proposes MF-UCB, which adds ζ(m) to UCBs and uses thresholds γ(m) to decide fidelity escalation.
- Main theoretical claim: MF-UCB concentrates cheap-fidelity exploration on clearly suboptimal arms and reserves high-fidelity queries for near-optimal arms.
- Provides upper-bound regret analysis and a matching lower bound; MF-UCB is near-optimal under a ζ-decay assumption.
- Builds on classical UCB work and complements prior multi-fidelity GP/optimization and budgeted bandit literature.
- Strength: clear, timely problem formulation addressing biased approximations with costs.
- Strength: natural algorithmic design with a well-motivated fidelity-switching rule and careful analysis.
- Strength: honest discussion of limitations and conditions (e.g., ζ-decay Assumption 1).
- Strength: synthetic experiments show consistent gains aligning qualitatively with theory.
- Weakness: main regret bound is hard to interpret due to complex notation and ψ/partitioning; needs simple corollaries.
- Weakness: assumption that ζ(m) are known and correct is unrealistic; no guidance on estimating or robustness to misspecification.
- Weakness: regret definition (rewarding µ(M) for all plays) may not fit all domains and alternatives are not explored.
- Weakness: empirical section is synthetic, lacks ablations, sensitivity analyses, and justification of parameter choices.
- Weakness: some motivating examples are debatable and mapping to regret-minimization (e.g., algorithm selection) is unclear.
- Weakness: practical details (setting ρ, γ(m); failure of Assumption 1; computational overhead) are not discussed.
- Quality: analysis appears technically sound under assumptions but heavily relies on known ζ(m) and regularity of their decay.
- Clarity: well written but notation-heavy; would benefit from specialized sub-Gaussian/M=2 theorem, illustrative figures, and guidance for practitioners.
- Originality: formulation and regret notion are novel; fidelity-selection mechanism and lower-bound insights are useful.
- Significance: potentially high impact for multi-fidelity evaluation if empirical validation and modeling guidance are strengthened.
- Suggestions: provide simplified bounds (e.g., sub-Gaussian, M=2), discuss estimating/robustifying ζ(m), add sensitivity experiments, and clarify application mappings.
- Acceptance pro: novel, principled formulation with near-optimal guarantees and insightful lower-bound distinctions.
- Acceptance con: hard-to-interpret bounds, unrealistic modeling assumptions, and weak empirical validation may limit practical impact.
