The paper proposes a model for multi-armed bandit problems with a fidelity component, where the learner must choose an arm's fidelity from a finite set. The fidelity affects the arm's reward, which is a stochastic, iid variable with a mean within a known error margin, denoted by the zeta parameter. The highest fidelity level corresponds to a zeta value of 0. Additionally, lower fidelity pulls are less expensive than higher fidelity pulls. The authors frame the problem as maximizing a notion of net reward by sequentially pulling arms with chosen fidelities and present a new algorithm to solve it. They argue that this algorithm can outperform the Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) algorithm operating at high fidelity levels. The paper also provides a lower bound for the regret of the proposed algorithm.

In my opinion, the paper explores an interesting and relevant problem - modeling the tradeoff between information, cost, and reward in online learning, specifically stochastic bandits. This problem is useful as a benchmark to improve upon. However, the paper seems to lack adequate explanation of its results and assumptions. The definition of regret adopted in the paper appears unnatural, as it penalizes resource consumption multiplicatively rather than additively. Although the authors provide an example of ad display to motivate their definition, it may not be the most general scenario. It would be beneficial to investigate what happens when the cost of suitable fidelity observations is additively factored into the total reward or regret.

The introduction of Assumption 1 seems artificial and opaque, as it involves constraints on the fidelity parameters given to the learner. It is unclear why the authors expect this assumption to hold in general learning problems. Furthermore, if the zeta values do not decay as fast as required by Assumption 1, it is unclear whether there is a general way to select a subsequence of fidelities to satisfy the assumption and hence the results. Although the authors mention that the assumption is not crucial, it appears to be used in the proofs, and my main concern is whether the assumption influences the algorithm's design and performance critically.

The algorithm's design seems tailored to perform well for specific zeta configurations, but the justification for these configurations is missing. The gap in the lower bound also requires further investigation and possibly a re-design of the proposed strategy. Interpreting the main result (Theorem 2, the regret bound for MF-UCB) and contrasting it with UCB (presumably at the highest fidelity, although this is not explicitly stated) is challenging due to the technical nature of the presentation. The relative gain or loss of MF-UCB appears to depend on Delta and [[k]], which in turn depend on the complicated sequence of sets \cal{K}^(m). A more explicit explanation would greatly help the authors' case.

Overall, the paper makes a good start by considering an interesting learning model, but it rapidly loses clarity when presenting the results. Minor corrections are needed: "near-optimal" (line 56), an unnecessary period before "for all m < M" (line 126), and using set notation (curly braces) to define [[k]] (line 179).
