This paper studies a novel multi-armed bandit setting where observing the outcome of each arm is expensive, but cheaper approximations are available – a “multi-fidelity bandit.” The core idea is that lower fidelities can be used to efficiently eliminate suboptimal arms, reserving the more expensive, high-fidelity evaluations for a smaller set of promising candidates. This is formally modeled, and the authors present a new algorithm, MF-UCB, along with theoretical guarantees, including a regret bound and a lower bound demonstrating near-optimality under certain conditions. The application to online advertising, where short-term displays approximate long-term performance, is a compelling motivation. 

The paper builds directly upon the classical UCB algorithm [3] and extends it to this multi-fidelity setting. It introduces a cost-weighted notion of regret appropriate for the problem and rigorously analyzes the performance of MF-UCB, showing its advantage over naive strategies that ignore the fidelity information. The connection to related work, including multi-fidelity optimization [7, 10] and budgeted bandits [13, 15], is well-described, highlighting the novelty of this approach within the bandit framework. The experimental results further support the theoretical findings, demonstrating the practical benefits of MF-UCB.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The multi-fidelity bandit problem is a well-motivated and interesting extension of the classical bandit setting, with clear practical relevance.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The analysis is thorough, with a regret bound and a matching lower bound, establishing the near-optimality of MF-UCB. The assumptions are clearly stated.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the ideas accessible. The algorithm is presented in a straightforward manner.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The online advertising example provides a concrete application that grounds the theoretical work. The simulations help support the claims.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Assumption of Known ζ(m):** The assumption that the deviation between fidelities, ζ(m), is known in advance is a significant limitation. In many real-world applications (such as the online advertising example), this information may not be readily available. Exploring ways to relax this assumption, perhaps by estimating ζ(m) online, would strengthen the paper.
*   **Complexity of Analysis:** While rigorous, the analysis is quite involved, potentially making it difficult for readers to fully grasp the key insights. A more intuitive explanation of the regret bound, focusing on the trade-offs between exploration at different fidelities, would be beneficial.
*   **Limited Scope of Simulations:** The simulations are somewhat limited in scope. Expanding the experiments to include more diverse scenarios and comparing MF-UCB to a wider range of baseline algorithms would increase confidence in its robustness.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and well-motivated problem with a significant potential impact on the field of reinforcement learning and bandit algorithms.
*   The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides strong guarantees on the performance of the proposed algorithm.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the key concepts and contributions.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The assumption of known ζ(m) is a significant limitation that restricts the applicability of the algorithm.
*   The complexity of the analysis may hinder understanding and adoption of the proposed approach.
