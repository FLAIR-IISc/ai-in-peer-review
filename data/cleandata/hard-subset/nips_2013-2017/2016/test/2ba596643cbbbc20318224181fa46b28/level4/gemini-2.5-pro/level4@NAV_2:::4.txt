This paper introduces the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit, a novel extension of the classical stochastic K-armed bandit problem. In this setting, each arm can be evaluated at one of M different "fidelities." Lower fidelities are cheaper to sample but provide biased approximations of the true reward, which is only observable at the highest, most expensive fidelity. The authors formalize this setting by assuming the bias of each fidelity is bounded by a known quantity. The main contribution is the Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB) algorithm, an adaptation of the UCB principle that leverages cheap, low-fidelity evaluations to quickly eliminate suboptimal arms, thereby reserving expensive, high-fidelity pulls for a smaller set of promising candidates. The paper provides a theoretical analysis of MF-UCB, proving a regret bound that demonstrates its advantage over naive strategies that ignore the multi-fidelity structure. This is complemented by a lower bound for the problem, showing that MF-UCB is nearly optimal under certain conditions. The proposed framework is distinguished from related work in budgeted bandits and active learning by its specific focus on the exploration-exploitation tradeoff with successively refined, biased approximations.

**Strengths of the paper:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The multi-fidelity formulation is novel and well-motivated. It captures a practical and important structure present in many real-world sequential decision-making problems, such as hyperparameter tuning, clinical trials, or engineering design, where cheaper but less accurate evaluations are often available. This is a strong conceptual contribution to the bandit literature.
*   **Technical Quality:** The proposed MF-UCB algorithm is intuitive and elegantly extends the optimistic principle of UCB to the multi-fidelity setting. The theoretical analysis is solid, providing a regret bound that formalizes the algorithm's key advantage: adapting its exploration strategy across fidelities to minimize cost. The inclusion of a lower bound further strengthens the paper's theoretical contributions.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The synthetic experiments effectively demonstrate the practical benefits of MF-UCB, corroborating the theoretical findings. The results clearly show that the proposed algorithm outperforms a standard UCB baseline that operates only at the highest fidelity.

**Weaknesses of the paper:**

*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper's readability could be significantly improved. Notations are introduced in a dispersed manner, making them difficult to track. For example, the variable 's' in Equation (3) is used before it is formally defined in the notation section on the following page. A centralized notation table and a thorough editorial pass to streamline the exposition would make the paper more accessible.
*   **Problem Formulation and Regret Definition:** The choice of the cost-weighted pseudo-regret metric is debatable and may not be the most natural fit for the motivating applications. The objective is to be competitive with an oracle that always plays the arm with the highest mean (µ*), making the optimal arm independent of the cost structure. This framework seems to undervalue the costs, as they do not influence which arm is considered optimal. An alternative formulation, such as best-arm identification with a fixed budget (i.e., identifying the arm with the highest µ* with high probability, given a total cost budget Λ) or fixed confidence, might be more compelling and directly applicable to scenarios like prototype testing, where the goal is identification rather than minimizing regret during the testing phase itself.
*   **Motivating Example:** While the online advertising example is intuitive at first, it introduces potential confusion by alluding to non-stationary dynamics (e.g., "displaying an ad for longer intervals changes user behaviour"). This is at odds with the stationary stochastic setting assumed in the paper's model. Using a different primary example, such as algorithm selection (where lower fidelities correspond to running an algorithm on a subset of data) or material science (where computer simulations are cheap approximations of expensive physical experiments), would align better with the formal problem definition and avoid potential misinterpretation.

In summary, the paper introduces an interesting and significant problem setting with a well-designed algorithm and sound theoretical analysis. However, the presentation needs improvement, and the justification for the specific regret formulation could be stronger. Reframing the problem in the context of best-arm identification might provide a more natural and impactful contribution.
