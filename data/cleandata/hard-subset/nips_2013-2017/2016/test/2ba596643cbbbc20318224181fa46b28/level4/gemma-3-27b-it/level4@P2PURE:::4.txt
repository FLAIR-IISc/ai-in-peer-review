The authors present a novel framework called Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit, addressing a problem where arms can be sampled at varying levels of fidelity, each associated with a distinct cost. Low-fidelity samples are drawn from a distribution that approximates the high-fidelity distribution. The paper introduces an algorithm that adapts to sequences of available approximations and costs, provides a regret analysis, and establishes a lower bound for the problem. Numerical simulations on synthetic problems demonstrate that the proposed algorithm outperforms UCB. While the framework is interesting and holds potential for significant impact with further development, I currently find the paper unsuitable for publication.

My concerns are as follows: 1/ The introduction of notation is confusing; definitions are dispersed throughout the text, making them difficult to locate. For instance, the variable *s* is not defined until a page after its first use and is subsequently rarely utilized. 2/ The use of advertising as a motivating example is occasionally misleading. The paper frequently draws connections between ad display time and non-stationarity, which is outside the scope of the presented work. Revisiting this example, or substituting it with a more relevant one (see 4/), would be beneficial. 3/ The regret analysis is difficult to justify in this setting. Standard regret analysis aims to maximize long-term gain, thereby identifying the arm with the best reward/cost ratio. However, in this framework, the optimal policy consistently favors the arm with the highest mean reward, irrespective of its cost. This prioritization obscures the cost incurred by sampling at lower fidelity levels when it may not be necessary. 4/ A sample complexity analysis—or more appropriately, a cost complexity analysis—would be valuable in the context of multi-fidelity bandit problems. This could be motivated by the prototype testing phase in new product development, where the goal is to identify the best product with high probability while minimizing the cumulative cost of testing before mass production. Given constraints such as regulations, contracts, and existing facilities, companies often rely on low-fidelity approximations before conducting advanced, costly tests. 5/ Finally, portions of the text are unwieldy and could benefit from a revision focused on streamlining the presentation.
