This paper discusses the multi-armed bandit problem, which involves a 'trustworthy' element that students must choose from (a limited set of trustworthies) for the arm being pulled. Pulling an arm with a specific trustworthy value yields a stochastic ID reward, with an average within the true mean of the zetas. Here, the zetas parameter (error) is known beforehand, and the maximum trustworthy level corresponds to zeta = 0. Furthermore, pulling a lower trustworthy value is cheaper (i.e., less expensive) than pulling a higher trustworthy value. The authors have designed the problem of pulling arms sequentially with trustworthies in such a way that the concept of the net reward is maximized, providing a new algorithm for the problem, and they argue that it can only outperform the UCB at higher trustworthy levels. This paper also provides a lower bound for the regret of the proposed algorithm. In my opinion, this paper discusses an interesting and relevant problem – modeling the trade-offs between information, cost, and reward in online learning (whether choosing less information is cheaper or more information is more expensive), especially in the case of stochastic bandits. In this sense, it could be useful as a benchmark for improvement. Although the paper seems technically challenging, a major flaw is the lack of adequate explanation regarding its results and assumptions. The accepted definition of regret seems unusual from at least one perspective – why not penalize the use of resources (or 'cost') positively instead of doing it multiplicatively? The authors' example of ad-display encourages their definition, but it may not be the most common. What happens if the cost of observing a suitable trustworthy value is added positively to the total reward (or regret)? This is probably a more natural question, and it is good to know. The introduction of assumption 1 seems rather artificial/vague, involving limitations on the trustworthy parameters given to the student. Why would the authors expect it to apply to a general learning problem? More importantly, if the value of zeta_m does not decrease faster than required by assumption 1, is there a general way to choose a subset of trustworthy values to satisfy the assumption and therefore the results (this would help significantly strengthen the paper's message)? Although the authors mention that the assumption is not critical, it seems to have been used in the proof, and my main concern is whether the assumption critically affects the algorithm's design and performance. It seems that the algorithm's design was made to perform well for some type of zeta configuration, but the reason why these are the 'right' zeta sequences is missing. Again, the gap within the lower bound requires further investigation and possibly redesigning of the proposed strategy. The main results (theorem 2, the bound for regret for MF-UCB) are not easy to understand due to the highly technical nature of the presentation and comparing it to UCB (at the maximum trustworthy level? (this is not explicitly stated in the discussion)). The relative gain/loss delta for MF-UCB seems to depend on [[k]], which again depends on the complex order of the set \cal{K}^(m). A clearer explanation would greatly help the authors' argument. Overall, I think the paper makes a good start in considering an interesting learning model, but loses clarity quickly in presenting the results.
