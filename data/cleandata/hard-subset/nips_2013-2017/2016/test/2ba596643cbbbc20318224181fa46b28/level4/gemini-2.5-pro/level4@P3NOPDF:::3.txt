This paper introduces the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit (MF-MAB) problem, a novel extension of the classic MAB setting. In this formulation, there are K arms, each of which can be evaluated at M different fidelity levels. Lower fidelity evaluations are less costly but yield reward estimates with greater bias. Specifically, the expected value of a sample drawn at a given fidelity deviates from the arm's true mean reward by an amount upper-bounded by a known, fidelity-dependent parameter.

The authors propose MF-UCB, an algorithm based on the "optimism in the face of uncertainty" principle. It adapts the standard UCB algorithm by expanding the confidence bounds to account for the additional uncertainty introduced by the bias from low-fidelity samples. The paper provides a theoretical analysis of MF-UCB, culminating in an upper bound on its cumulative regret, and argues that this bound will often be significantly better than that achieved by a standard UCB algorithm restricted to the highest fidelity level. This claim is supported by a brief set of simulations.

The primary contribution of this work is the formulation of the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit problem itself. This is an interesting and potentially valuable extension of the MAB framework. The modeling choice to associate lower fidelity with increased bias, rather than increased variance, is well-justified for certain applications, such as the machine learning algorithm selection problem mentioned by the authors.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that temper its overall contribution.

First, while the paper provides a theoretical regret analysis for the proposed MF-UCB algorithm, the resulting bounds are complex and difficult to interpret. This complexity makes it challenging to gain intuition about the key factors driving performance or to directly compare the bound to that of standard UCB without significant effort. A simplified or asymptotic version of the bound, or a more detailed discussion interpreting its dominant terms, would greatly enhance the paper's theoretical contribution. As it stands, the main theoretical result is not very illuminating.

Second, the connection between the proposed model and some of the motivating applications feels tenuous. For instance, the claim that displaying an ad for a shorter duration provides a *biased* (as opposed to a more noisy) estimate of its long-term effectiveness is not immediately convincing and would benefit from further justification. Furthermore, in the otherwise compelling example of machine learning algorithm selection, it is not clear that minimizing cumulative regret is the most appropriate objective. Often, the goal in such settings is simple regret minimization (i.e., identifying the best arm with high probability), rather than optimizing performance throughout the entire learning process. This slight mismatch between the problem formulation and its motivating applications weakens the paper's impact.

Third, the empirical evaluation is difficult to fully assess. The proposed MF-UCB algorithm is a natural and straightforward extension of UCB, so its practical benefit over standard approaches is a key point of interest. The appendix details the parameter settings used in the simulations but provides no justification for these choices. Consequently, it is unclear whether the reported performance improvements of MF-UCB over standard UCB (often by a factor of 3-4) are representative of typical scenarios or are an artifact of a parameterization particularly favorable to the proposed method. A more thorough ablation study or a discussion on the sensitivity to these parameters (e.g., the bias terms \zeta^{(m)}) would be necessary to substantiate the claims of practical superiority.

In summary, the paper introduces a novel and interesting problem formulation, which is its main strength. The proposed algorithm is a reasonable, if not highly innovative, application of the UCB principle. However, the paper is weakened by a theoretical analysis that is hard to interpret, a motivational section that is not entirely convincing, and an empirical evaluation that lacks the necessary rigor to fully support its claims.
