- Paper studies multi-armed bandits with a finite set of fidelities per arm, fidelity m giving iid rewards within known error ζ_m and lower fidelities costing less; highest fidelity has ζ=0.  
- Authors formulate sequential fidelity-and-arm selection to maximize net reward, propose MF-UCB, and prove a regret upper bound plus a lower bound.  
- The problem is interesting and relevant and could serve as a benchmark for future work.  
- Paper appears technically sound overall.  
- Main shortcoming: explanations of results and assumptions are insufficient and overly technical, making interpretation difficult.  
- The adopted multiplicative regret definition (penalizing resource consumption multiplicatively) seems unnatural; an additive cost penalty would be more general and intuitive.  
- The ad-display motivation for the multiplicative penalty is limited and not broadly convincing.  
- Assumption 1 (constraints on provided ζ_m) appears arbitrary and opaque; authors do not justify why it would hold in general.  
- It is unclear whether one can select a subsequence of fidelities to satisfy Assumption 1 if the ζ_m decay violates it, and this is not addressed.  
- Although authors claim Assumption 1 is not crucial, it is used in proofs, raising concerns it may affect algorithm design and performance.  
- The algorithm seems tailored to specific ζ sequences without justification that those sequences are “correct.”  
- The gap in the lower bound needs more investigation and might require redesigning the strategy.  
- Theorem 2 (MF-UCB regret bound) is hard to interpret and not clearly contrasted with standard high-fidelity UCB; comparisons should be explicit.  
- MF-UCB’s relative performance depends on Δ and the index sets K^(m), which are defined through a complex sequence; clearer explanation of these dependencies is needed.  
- Paper loses clarity in presenting results and would benefit from more intuition, clearer comparisons, and justification of key modeling choices.  
- Minor typos: remove extra period before “for all m < M” (line 126); use curly braces for [k] (line 179).
