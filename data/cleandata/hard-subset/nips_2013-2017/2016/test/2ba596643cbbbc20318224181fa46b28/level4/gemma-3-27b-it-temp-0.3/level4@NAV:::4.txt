The authors present a novel framework, the Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit, addressing the problem of sampling arms with varying fidelity levels at associated costs, where lower fidelity samples approximate the highest fidelity distribution. They propose an algorithm that adapts to sequences of approximations and costs, accompanied by a regret analysis and a problem-specific lower bound. Numerical simulations on synthetic data demonstrate performance gains over UCB. While the framework holds promise and potential for significant impact with further development, I currently assess the paper as unsuitable for publication. 

My concerns are detailed below:

1/ The notation is presented in a disorganized manner, hindering clarity and making definitions difficult to locate. For instance, the variable *s* is defined only after its initial use (specifically, one page later) and subsequently receives limited attention.

2/ The advertising example used to illustrate the framework is, at times, misleading. The connection drawn between ad display time and non-stationarity feels extraneous to the core problem; a different illustrative example might be more appropriate.

3/ The justification for the regret analysis in this context is unclear. Given that the optimal policy appears to consistently favor the arm with the highest mean reward, irrespective of cost, the regret metric may not fully capture the cost implications of sampling lower fidelity levels when unnecessary. This could obscure the true cost of utilizing low-fidelity samples.

4/ A sample (or, more accurately, cost) complexity analysis would be a valuable addition. This could be motivated by practical applications such as prototype testing in product development, where regulatory constraints and existing infrastructure often necessitate the use of low-fidelity approximations before more advanced (and costly) testing can be performed. The goal would be to identify the best product with high probability while minimizing the cumulative testing cost prior to mass production.

5/ Finally, certain sections of the manuscript are somewhat cumbersome. A revision focused on streamlining the writing would improve overall readability.
