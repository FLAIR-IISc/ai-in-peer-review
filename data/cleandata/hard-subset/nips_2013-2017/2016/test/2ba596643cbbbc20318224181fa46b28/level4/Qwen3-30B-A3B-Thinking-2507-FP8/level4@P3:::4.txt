

The paper introduces a novel multi-fidelity bandit framework where each arm can be sampled at multiple fidelity levels, with lower fidelities providing cheaper but biased approximations to the true arm reward distribution. The authors propose MF-UCB, an algorithm that adaptively selects both arms and fidelity levels to minimize regret under a constrained budget. The paper includes theoretical analysis with regret bounds and a lower bound demonstrating near-optimality under certain conditions, as well as empirical validation on synthetic data showing MF-UCB outperforms standard UCB.

The work builds on the classical multi-armed bandit literature (e.g., [3, 9, 11]) and UCB methods [2, 3] while addressing a practical gap in the literature where lower-fidelity approximations are available for cheaper exploration. Related work on multi-fidelity methods in optimization [7, 10] and active learning [16] do not formalize regret in this bandit setting, making this contribution distinct from prior approaches. The paper also relates to budgeted bandits [13, 15] but differs fundamentally by treating fidelities as approximations rather than cost-reward ratios.

**Strengths:**
1. **Originality:** The multi-fidelity bandit framework is novel, addressing a practical gap where cheaper approximations exist but are biased. The problem formulation is well-motivated by real-world scenarios like online advertising and algorithm selection, where lower-fidelity approximations (e.g., shorter ad display times, smaller datasets) are commonly used for exploration.
2. **Theoretical Contribution:** The regret analysis is technically sound, with a well-constructed algorithm and tight bounds demonstrating MF-UCB's advantage over UCB when lower fidelities are sufficiently cheaper and informative. The lower bound shows MF-UCB is nearly optimal under the proposed assumptions.
3. **Empirical Validation:** The synthetic experiments effectively illustrate MF-UCB's advantage, showing both better regret and strategic use of lower fidelities for exploration.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Clarity and Notation:** The paper suffers from inconsistent and poorly defined notation. Key terms like "s" (number of samples) are introduced without clear definition and are rarely used afterward. Notation for fidelity levels (m, M), cost parameters (λ(m)), and regret components (R̃, r̃) are scattered throughout the text, making the paper difficult to follow. For instance, the definition of s appears only one page after its first use, and the relationship between different fidelity levels is obscured by inconsistent notation.
   
2. **Regret Formulation Justification:** The regret definition (Equation 1) is problematic. The authors claim the optimal policy should always select the arm with the highest mean reward, regardless of cost, but their regret formulation (which incorporates cost) suggests otherwise. This creates a conceptual inconsistency: if the optimal arm is always the one with the highest mean, then cost should not factor into the optimal policy, making the regret analysis misleading. The paper would benefit from a clearer justification of why cost should be incorporated into the regret metric rather than treated as a budget constraint.

3. **Example Misalignment:** The online advertising example is frequently used but contains misleading references to non-stationarity (e.g., "displaying an ad for longer intervals changes user behaviour"), which is irrelevant to the stationary bandit setting. This distracts from the core contribution and suggests the example was not carefully vetted for consistency with the model.

4. **Missing Context for Practical Relevance:** While the paper mentions applications like product testing and clinical trials, it fails to provide a cost complexity analysis that would be crucial for practitioners. A more detailed discussion of how the framework applies to real-world constraints (e.g., regulatory requirements for low-fidelity testing before high-fidelity validation) would strengthen the significance argument.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Novel problem formulation addressing a practical gap in bandit literature.
- Strong theoretical results with tight regret bounds.
- Clear empirical validation demonstrating the algorithm's advantage.
- Well-motivated by relevant applications.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
- Significant clarity issues due to inconsistent notation and scattered definitions.
- Regret formulation contains a conceptual inconsistency that undermines the theoretical contribution.
- Example references non-stationarity, which is irrelevant to the model.
- Lacks a cost complexity analysis that would better establish practical significance.

While the paper's core idea is promising, the current presentation has critical flaws that prevent it from meeting NIPS's standards for clarity and conceptual precision. The regret formulation's inconsistency is particularly problematic, as it misrepresents the fundamental trade-off the paper claims to address. The notation issues also make the technical contribution difficult to evaluate. With substantial revisions to clarify the regret definition, streamline the notation, and better align the examples with the model, this paper could make a valuable contribution. As written, however, the weaknesses outweigh the strengths.

**Recommendation:** Reject. The paper presents a novel and potentially impactful idea but suffers from critical clarity issues and a fundamental flaw in the regret formulation that undermines its theoretical contribution. These issues need to be resolved before the paper can be considered for publication.
