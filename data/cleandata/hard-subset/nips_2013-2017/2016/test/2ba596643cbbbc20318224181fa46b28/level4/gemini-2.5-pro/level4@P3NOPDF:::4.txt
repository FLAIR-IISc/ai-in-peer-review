This paper introduces a novel framework named the Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit (MF-MAB). In this setting, each arm can be sampled at various "fidelity" levels, where each level has an associated cost. Lower-fidelity samples are cheaper but are drawn from a distribution that only approximates the highest-fidelity (ground truth) distribution. The authors propose an algorithm designed to adapt to the available fidelity levels and their costs, provide a corresponding regret analysis, and establish a problem-specific lower bound. Numerical simulations on synthetic data show that the proposed algorithm outperforms a standard UCB baseline.

While the proposed framework is interesting and has the potential for significant impact, the paper in its current state has several major issues that prevent me from recommending publication.

**Major Concerns:**

1.  **Clarity of Presentation and Notation:** The paper is difficult to follow due to unclear and dispersed notation. Key variables are often used before they are formally defined, forcing the reader to hunt for their definitions. For example, the variable `$s$` appears in the text a full page before its definition is provided, and it is then used sparingly, which adds to the confusion. The overall writing is unwieldy in several sections and would benefit significantly from a thorough revision to improve clarity and streamline the presentation of the core ideas.

2.  **Misleading Motivating Example:** The use of online advertising as the primary motivating example is problematic. The authors repeatedly draw parallels between fidelity levels and the display time of an ad, which naturally introduces the concept of non-stationarity (e.g., user interest waning over time). However, non-stationarity is explicitly outside the scope of the stationary bandit setting considered in this paper. This creates a disconnect between the motivation and the formal problem setting, potentially confusing the reader about the paper's actual contributions. A more suitable motivating example would strengthen the paper considerably (see point 4).

3.  **Weak Justification for the Regret Metric:** The choice of regret analysis as the primary performance metric seems ill-suited for the problem's core trade-offs. In a typical regret analysis, the goal is to maximize cumulative reward over the long run. However, in this cost-sensitive framework, a more natural objective would be to optimize a reward-to-cost ratio. The current definition of the optimal policy is to always play the arm with the highest mean reward, regardless of the cost associated with sampling it. The regret is then measured against a policy that is obligated to sample this best arm, even at low-fidelity levels. This formulation seems to obscure the central challenge of the problem: deciding *when* the cost of obtaining a high-fidelity sample is justified. It potentially penalizes an algorithm for choosing not to sample a costly arm, even if that is the economically rational decision.

4.  **Alternative Formulation: Cost Complexity:** A "cost complexity" or "best-arm identification with a budget" analysis would be a more natural and compelling framework for this problem. This perspective can be motivated by a different, and arguably more fitting, application: the prototype testing phase for new product development. In this scenario, the goal is not long-term reward accumulation but to identify the best product design with high confidence while minimizing the total testing cost before committing to mass production. Companies are often constrained by regulations, contracts, or facilities, forcing them to use a sequence of low-fidelity approximations (e.g., simulations, scale models) before conducting expensive, high-fidelity tests (e.g., full-scale prototypes). Framing the problem this way—finding the best arm with high probability for the lowest possible cumulative cost—would align the theoretical analysis more closely with the practical utility of the multi-fidelity setting and provide a clearer benchmark for algorithmic performance.

In summary, the paper introduces a promising direction, but its execution is hampered by significant issues in presentation, motivation, and the choice of theoretical analysis. Reframing the problem around a more appropriate motivating example and adopting a cost-complexity analysis instead of a standard regret metric would substantially improve the paper's clarity and impact. For these reasons, I recommend rejecting the paper at this time but would be very interested in seeing a revised version that addresses these fundamental concerns.
