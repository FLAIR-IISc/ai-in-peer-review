The authors propose a novel framework called Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit, where arms can be sampled at various levels of fidelity, each associated with a cost. Low-fidelity samples are drawn from a distribution approximating the highest fidelity distribution, offering a trade-off between cost and accuracy. The authors provide an algorithm that adapts to sequences of available approximations and costs, along with a regret analysis and a lower bound for the problem. They also present numerical simulations on synthetic problems, demonstrating the proposed algorithm's superior performance compared to UCB. Although the framework is intriguing and has potential for impact with further development, I find the paper in its current state unfit for publication.

Several issues detract from the paper's clarity and coherence. Firstly, the introduction of notations is confusing, with definitions dispersed throughout the text, making them difficult to locate. For instance, the definition of $s$ is not provided until a page after its initial use and is subsequently underutilized in the remainder of the paper. Secondly, the use of advertising as an example is misleading, as it introduces correlations with display time and non-stationarity, which are outside the scope of this paper. Consider replacing this example with a more relevant one, such as the testing phase of prototypes for new product conception (as mentioned below).

Furthermore, I find the regret analysis in this setting challenging to justify. A regret analysis typically aims to maximize long-term gain by identifying the arm with the optimal reward-to-cost ratio. However, in this scenario, the optimal policy always favors the arm with the highest mean, regardless of the associated cost. This approach may obscure the cost of sampling low-fidelity levels when unnecessary. A more nuanced analysis, such as a sample complexity (or cost complexity) analysis, would be beneficial in the context of Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandits. This type of analysis could be motivated by the product development process, where companies must balance the need to identify the best product with the cost of testing, often relying on low-fidelity approximations due to regulatory, contractual, and infrastructural constraints.

Lastly, certain sections of the text are unwieldy and could benefit from revision to improve the overall flow and readability of the paper.
