Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper introduces a multi-fidelity variant of the stochastic K-armed bandit in which each arm can be queried at one of M fidelities with increasing cost and decreasing bias relative to the highest-fidelity outcome. The authors assume known fidelity-wise bias bounds ζ(m) (monotone decreasing with ζ(M)=0) and per-fidelity costs λ(m) (monotone increasing). They define a cost-weighted pseudo-regret where the forecaster is always evaluated against the highest-fidelity mean µ(M), regardless of which fidelity was played, and propose MF-UCB, an upper confidence bound algorithm that simultaneously selects an arm and a fidelity. The arm selection uses a min over fidelity-specific UCBs (inflated by ζ(m)), and the fidelity selection is governed by thresholds γ(m) that compare estimation uncertainty to the fidelity’s bias-cost tradeoff. Theoretical analysis yields an upper bound showing that MF-UCB spends most of its budget on promising arms at high fidelity and prunes clearly suboptimal arms at low fidelities. A lower bound (for Bernoulli rewards) shows near-optimality under certain conditions, with a gap identified for a subset of arms. Simulations on synthetic data corroborate improved regret over naive UCB that ignores fidelities.

This work builds on UCB methods (Auer; Bubeck & Cesa-Bianchi) while differing from budgeted bandits (e.g., Tran-Thanh; Xia et al.), where reward/cost ratios are optimized rather than exploiting multiple biased approximations per arm. It is related to multi-fidelity optimization in engineering (Huang et al.) and reinforcement learning with simulators (Cutler et al.), and complements work on weak/strong labelers (Zhang & Chaudhuri). The authors’ Gaussian process extension (Kandasamy et al., NIPS 2016) is consistent with the framework but targets a different payoff model.

Strengths:
- Clear and well-motivated formalization of multi-fidelity bandits with explicit bias and cost models. The regret definition is consistent with applications where evaluation is judged by long-term/high-fidelity performance.
- Algorithmic novelty: MF-UCB integrates fidelity-specific UCBs and a principled fidelity-switching rule γ(m)=ψ−1(λ(m)/λ(m+1)·ψ(ζ(m))) that balances bias against estimation error and cost.
- Strong theory: an interpretable regret bound via a partition of arms K(m) showing that cheap fidelities eliminate many arms, with high-fidelity budget concentrated on near-optimal arms. A lower bound demonstrates near-optimality for a broad subset of arms, and the paper is explicit about where a gap remains.
- General concentration framework via ψ, covering sub-Gaussian and beyond; anytime behavior without knowing the capital Λ.
- Simulations show consistent improvements and align with the theoretical intuition about fidelity usage.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Practicality of assumptions: The bias bounds ζ(m) are assumed known and uniform across arms. Realistically, ζ(m) may be unknown, arm-dependent, or only approximately bounded. Discussion or experiments on misspecified ζ(m), adaptive estimation of ζ(m), or robust variants would improve applicability.
- Assumption 1 (on the decay of ζ(m) relative to ψ) is key for the most appealing comparisons; the paper should show sensitivity when fidelities are dense (small cost/accuracy gaps) or when M is large, and clarify how guarantees degrade if the assumption fails.
- Lower bound gap for K(m)7: The algorithm can be suboptimal when small positive gaps at lower fidelities would allow cheap rejection, but MF-UCB escalates to higher fidelity. A brief discussion of possible fixes (e.g., adaptive thresholds, occasional cheap fidelity re-checks, or arm-dependent γ) and an empirical illustration would strengthen the contribution.
- Regret model: Evaluating all plays by µ(M) is appropriate for many settings (e.g., ads), but the paper already notes other regimes (e.g., simulations without intrinsic reward). It would help to add a short subsection formalizing alternative regret choices and how MF-UCB could be adapted.
- Parameterization and implementation details: Guidance on selecting ρ, handling unknown ψ (or choosing ψ for sub-Gaussian rewards), computational overhead, and sensitivity analyses would aid reproducibility.
- Empirics: Comparisons are limited to standard UCB at high fidelity. Including additional baselines (e.g., Thompson sampling adapted to multi-fidelity, cost-aware successive elimination, UCB-V), ablations on γ(m), and experiments with misleading lower fidelities or ζ/λ misspecification would give a fuller picture. A real-data case study would be valuable.

Evaluation by criteria:
- Quality: The analysis appears technically sound, with clear decomposition of regret and careful use of concentration. The lower-bound construction is standard and well-argued.
- Clarity: Generally well organized and easy to follow. Consider adding a short two-fidelity walkthrough, enhancing intuition for the choice of qt and γ(m), and summarizing the K(m) partition with a small numerical example.
- Originality: The multi-fidelity bandit formalism with biased approximations and explicit costs, the new regret notion, and the MF-UCB design are novel relative to prior bandit literature.
- Significance: Likely to influence practical algorithms in domains with surrogate evaluations (ads, AutoML, simulation-to-real). The results offer a useful blueprint for leveraging cheap proxies in online decision-making.

Pros for acceptance:
- Well-motivated problem setting with clear real-world relevance.
- Elegant algorithm with strong, mostly tight theory.
- Insightful analysis showing how and why cheaper fidelities help.
- Consistent empirical gains.

Cons against acceptance:
- Reliance on known ζ(m) and Assumption 1; limited discussion of robustness.
- Identified optimality gap not addressed algorithmically.
- Empirical evaluation could be broader and more realistic.

Overall recommendation:
A solid and timely contribution. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying assumptions, expanding empirical comparisons, and discussing robustness to misspecification.
