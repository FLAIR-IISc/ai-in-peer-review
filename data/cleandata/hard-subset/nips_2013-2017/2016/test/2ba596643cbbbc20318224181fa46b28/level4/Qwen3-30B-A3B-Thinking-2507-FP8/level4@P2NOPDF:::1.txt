

The paper introduces a multi-armed bandit framework where each arm pull can be executed at varying fidelity levels (from a finite set), with higher fidelity yielding rewards closer to the true mean (error parameter ζₘ, known a priori, with ζₘ=0 for highest fidelity) and lower fidelity being cheaper. The goal is to maximize net reward by balancing cost, information, and reward. The authors propose an algorithm (MF-UCB) and claim it outperforms UCB operating only at high fidelity, supported by a regret lower bound. The problem formulation—addressing the tradeoff between cost, information quality, and reward—is novel and relevant for online learning, potentially serving as a useful benchmark.

However, the paper’s technical contribution is undermined by significant clarity and justification gaps. The regret definition (multiplicative cost penalty) appears unnatural; an additive cost penalty would better align with standard optimization goals and general problem settings, not just the ad-display example provided. The authors should clarify why multiplicative cost is preferable and explore the additive case, as this is a critical design choice.

Assumption 1 (on ζₘ decay rates) is particularly problematic. It imposes arbitrary constraints on the fidelity parameters, yet the paper offers no justification for why these constraints hold in general learning scenarios. If ζₘ decays slower than required, the authors provide no method to select a valid subsequence of fidelities to satisfy the assumption and retain theoretical guarantees. While the authors state the assumption is "not crucial," it is explicitly used in proofs, raising concern that the algorithm’s design and performance are overly tailored to specific ζₘ sequences. The paper lacks argument for why the assumed sequences represent "natural" or common cases, weakening the general applicability of the results.

The main result (Theorem 2, MF-UCB’s regret bound) is obscured by excessive technicality. The regret depends on complex parameters (Δ, [[k]], and nested sets 𝒦^(m)), making it difficult to interpret the algorithm’s relative performance versus UCB (which is not explicitly specified as operating at highest fidelity). A clearer, more intuitive comparison—e.g., how gains depend on problem structure—would strengthen the claim of superiority. The lower bound gap also requires deeper analysis; resolving this might necessitate revising the proposed strategy.

Minor issues include:
- Line 56: "near-optimal" (typo: should be "near-optimal")
- Line 126: Unnecessary period before "for all m < M"
- Line 179: Use curly braces (e.g., {{k}}) for set notation.

In summary, the paper addresses an interesting problem but fails to adequately justify its key assumptions, clarify its regret formulation, or present results accessibly. The algorithm’s dependence on Assumption 1 and the unnatural regret definition limit its perceived contribution. While the core idea is promising, the current presentation obscures the practical insights and theoretical value. To strengthen the paper, the authors should: (1) justify Assumption 1’s generality or provide a fallback for violated cases; (2) compare additive vs. multiplicative cost penalties; (3) simplify the presentation of Theorem 2 and the UCB comparison; and (4) clarify the role of fidelity sequences in the algorithm design. Without these, the paper’s impact is diminished despite a solid technical foundation.
