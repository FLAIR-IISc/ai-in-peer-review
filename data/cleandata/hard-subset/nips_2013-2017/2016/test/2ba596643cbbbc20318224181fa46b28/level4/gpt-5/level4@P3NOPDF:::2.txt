This paper introduces a multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit setting in which each arm can be sampled at different accuracies (fidelities), with higher accuracy incurring higher cost. Regret is defined in a cost-aware manner, effectively weighting suboptimal pulls by their sampling cost. The authors propose MF-UCB, an algorithm tailored to this setting, and provide both upper and lower bounds on its cumulative pseudo-regret.

Overall assessment
- The main paper is clear, well-structured, and articulates the problem and approach convincingly. The core idea—using inexpensive, low-fidelity pulls to quickly discard clearly suboptimal arms before investing in high-fidelity evaluations—is both natural and insightful.
- The theoretical contributions are solid. The upper and lower bounds provide a meaningful characterization of performance and give insight into how fidelity, cost, and confidence interact in this setting.
- The supplementary material, where most proofs reside, currently contains many typos and small errors that hinder readability and verification. Correcting these would significantly improve the paper’s accessibility and impact.

Strengths
- Novel and well-motivated problem formulation that reflects realistic settings where evaluations have tunable accuracy and cost.
- A principled algorithm (MF-UCB) that operationalizes fidelity choices within a UCB-style framework.
- Theoretical results include regret upper bounds and matching (up to constants) lower bounds, giving a near-complete picture of difficulty in this setting.
- Clear exposition in the main text, with good intuition for the multi-fidelity strategy.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Hidden constants in the “up to a multiplicative constant” notation sometimes obscure important parameter dependencies. In particular, Theorem 2 appears to hide the dependence on ρ (an algorithm parameter) inside the constant. Since ρ can materially affect the algorithm’s behavior, masking it under asymptotic notation weakens interpretability and practical guidance.
- Practical applicability is not fully addressed. MF-UCB relies on knowledge of fidelity accuracies (e.g., bias/error parameters ζ at each fidelity). If these are misspecified or poorly estimated, the early elimination of arms at low fidelity may be unreliable, potentially leading to irreversible errors.
- Many typos and minor inconsistencies in the supplementary material impede careful checking of the proofs and may confuse readers.

Suggestions for improvement
- Surface constants in the main statements whenever they involve key algorithmic or problem parameters (e.g., ρ, fidelity accuracies, and cost ratios). When space is limited, add a short remark after each theorem summarizing the dominant dependencies, and defer full inequalities to the appendix.
- Add a discussion on robustness to misspecification of fidelity accuracies ζ. For example:
  - Analyze or at least discuss how estimation errors in ζ affect elimination guarantees and regret.
  - Consider simple robust variants (e.g., conservative confidence adjustments, validation at higher fidelity before final elimination).
  - Include sensitivity analyses in experiments showing performance under moderately misspecified ζ.
- Provide guidance on selecting ρ and on setting or learning fidelity thresholds/cost schedules in practice.
- Revise and thoroughly proofread the supplementary material. Consolidating the core proof ideas (with a brief roadmap) would help readers navigate the technical sections.

Clarity and presentation
- The main body is clear and easy to follow. Replacing some “≲” style statements with exact inequalities (or adding a remark with explicit constants and parameter dependencies) would improve precision without sacrificing readability.
- Ensure notation is consistent across the main text and appendix (e.g., consistent use of indices, fidelity superscripts, and parameters such as γ, K, ρ).

Overall recommendation
This work provides a significant and timely contribution to bandits with variable-accuracy observations, with compelling theoretical results and a clear algorithmic design. I recommend acceptance after addressing the clarity issues in the supplementary material and adding a brief discussion on practical limitations and robustness.
