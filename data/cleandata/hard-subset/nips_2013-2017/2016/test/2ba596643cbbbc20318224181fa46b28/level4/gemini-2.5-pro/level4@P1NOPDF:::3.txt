This paper introduces a "multi-fidelity" version of the multi-armed bandit problem, where each of K arms can be evaluated at M different levels of fidelity. In this formulation, lower fidelity evaluations are less costly but introduce greater bias into the reward estimate. Specifically, pulling an arm at a given fidelity level draws a sample from a distribution whose mean deviates from the arm's true expected reward by a fidelity-dependent, bounded amount.

The authors propose a natural UCB-based algorithm for this problem, which inflates the confidence bounds to account for the additional uncertainty introduced by low-fidelity, biased samples. The theoretical analysis culminates in complex regret bounds that are difficult to interpret. The authors argue that these bounds are often significantly tighter than those achieved by a standard UCB algorithm restricted to the highest fidelity level. A brief experimental section provides simulations intended to substantiate this claim.

A core modeling choice is to associate lower fidelity with increased bias rather than increased variance. While the latter also seems like a plausible model for "low fidelity" scenarios, the paper's motivating examples (such as algorithm selection) provide a reasonable justification for the bias-based approach.

However, the connection to the motivating applications is not entirely convincing. For instance, the argument that displaying an ad for a shorter duration yields a *biased* estimate of its long-term effectiveness is not fully substantiated. Furthermore, in the context of machine learning algorithm selection, it is unclear why regret minimization is the appropriate objective for an algorithm designer. Overall, the link between the proposed model and its motivating applications feels tenuous, though this is not a disqualifying flaw.

The paper's primary contribution appears to be the novel formulation of the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit problem itself. The proposed algorithm is a relatively straightforward application of the "optimism in the face of uncertainty" principle. As noted earlier, the theoretical performance guarantees are disappointingly difficult to interpret.

The simulation results on synthetic data are also difficult to assess. While Appendix C details the parameter values used, it offers no justification for these choices. In many simulations, the proposed MF-UCB algorithm outperforms standard UCB by a factor of 3 to 4. However, it is unclear whether this significant improvement is representative of performance in practical scenarios or an artifact of the specific parameter choices (e.g., the values of ζ⁽ᵐ⁾).
