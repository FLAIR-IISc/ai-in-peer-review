This paper introduces a novel and practical variant of the stochastic K-armed bandit problem, termed the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit. The key idea is that for each arm, the agent can choose to play it at one of M different "fidelities." Lower fidelities are cheaper to sample but provide a more biased or noisy estimate of the true reward, while higher fidelities are more expensive but more accurate. The highest fidelity corresponds to the true reward distribution. This setting is motivated by practical applications like online advertising, where an ad's long-term performance might be cheaply approximated by displaying it for shorter periods.

The main contributions of the paper are threefold:
1.  It formalizes the multi-fidelity bandit setting and proposes a suitable cost-weighted notion of regret that accounts for the capital expended.
2.  It presents the Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB) algorithm, an elegant extension of the classic UCB algorithm. MF-UCB uses the cheaper, lower fidelities to quickly eliminate clearly suboptimal arms, thereby reserving expensive, high-fidelity plays for a smaller set of promising candidates.
3.  It provides a thorough theoretical analysis of MF-UCB, including a regret upper bound that demonstrates its adaptive behavior and a corresponding lower bound for the problem, showing that MF-UCB is near-optimal under certain conditions.

The proposed framework is novel and distinct from prior work. While it builds on the well-established UCB principle, it addresses a different problem structure than budgeted bandits (which typically associate a single random cost with each arm) or active learning with weak and strong labelers.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The multi-fidelity formulation is a significant and well-motivated contribution. It captures a practical aspect of sequential decision-making—the availability of cheap, approximate evaluations—that is largely overlooked in the standard bandit literature. The potential applications are numerous, from hyperparameter tuning and algorithm selection to scientific experiment design, making the problem setting highly relevant.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper presents a complete and technically sound piece of work. The proposed MF-UCB algorithm is intuitive and its design, particularly the criterion for switching between fidelities, is clever. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, providing both an upper bound for the algorithm and a lower bound for the problem itself. This comprehensive analysis strongly supports the paper's claims.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem, explaining the intuition behind their algorithm, and outlining the key ideas of their theoretical results. The partitioning of the arm set in the analysis (the `K(m)` sets) is a particularly effective tool for clarifying how the algorithm performs.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Practicality of Assumptions:** The primary weakness lies in the assumption that the bias bounds, `ζ(m)` (the maximum deviation of the m-th fidelity's mean reward from the highest fidelity's mean), are known in advance. In many of the motivating applications, such as online advertising, quantifying these biases a priori would be very difficult. The algorithm's performance, especially the fidelity-switching mechanism (`γ(m)`), depends on these values. This assumption may limit the direct applicability of the proposed method in its current form. It would strengthen the paper significantly to discuss the sensitivity of the algorithm to misspecification of `ζ(m)` or to propose methods for learning these parameters online.

**Recommendation:**

Overall, this is a strong paper that introduces a novel and interesting problem with a well-designed algorithm and thorough theoretical analysis. The strengths in originality and technical quality are substantial. The main weakness concerning the assumption of known bias bounds is a valid concern for practical deployment but represents an excellent direction for future work rather than a fatal flaw in this foundational contribution. The paper is a clear advancement of the state of the art in bandit theory and is likely to inspire follow-up research. I recommend acceptance.
