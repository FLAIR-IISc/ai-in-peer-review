This paper introduces a novel multi-armed bandit (MAB) setting incorporating variable fidelity rewards, where each arm can be evaluated at $M$ different fidelities, trading off cost and accuracy. Higher fidelities provide more precise reward estimates but at a greater expense. The authors argue, and convincingly demonstrate, that leveraging lower fidelities for initial arm elimination and reserving higher fidelities for promising candidates can outperform standard algorithms like UCB, particularly in scenarios where evaluation cost is a significant factor â€“ online advertising being a compelling example. The paper presents a new regret definition tailored to this setting, proposes an algorithm designed to exploit the fidelity structure, provides a theoretical analysis including a regret lower bound, and validates the approach through simulations.

The core contribution lies in the analysis of this new setting and the algorithm's theoretical guarantees. The regret analysis appears novel and carefully constructed, effectively capturing the interplay between exploration, exploitation, and fidelity level. The presented algorithm is intuitive: it intelligently allocates resources to higher fidelities for arms that appear promising based on lower-fidelity observations, aiming to minimize the overall cost of learning the optimal arm.

However, a critical practical limitation arises from the assumption that the deviation $\varepsilon^{(m)}$ of the $m$-th fidelity mean from the highest fidelity mean is known *a priori*. This is a strong assumption, and its applicability to real-world scenarios like online advertising is unclear. Knowing the precise distribution of the difference between short-term and long-term click-through rates is challenging. The paper would benefit significantly from a discussion of this limitation and potential avenues for relaxation, such as learning the $\varepsilon^{(m)}$ values online or introducing robustness to estimation errors. Investigating the performance sensitivity to inaccuracies in these estimates would be valuable.

The experimental results provide support for the theoretical analysis, but it would be beneficial to see comparisons against more sophisticated bandit algorithms tailored for constrained optimization or exploration-exploitation trade-offs. Additionally, exploring a wider range of parameter settings and fidelity cost ratios could provide a more comprehensive understanding of the algorithm's performance characteristics.

Overall, this is a well-written and theoretically sound paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of multi-armed bandits. The proposed setting and algorithm have the potential to be highly relevant in practical applications where fidelity and cost trade-offs are important. However, the strong assumption regarding prior knowledge of fidelity deviations is a significant limitation that needs to be addressed, ideally through further theoretical analysis or empirical investigation.
