This paper introduces the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit, a novel variant of the classical stochastic K-armed bandit problem. In this setting, each of the K arms can be evaluated at one of M different fidelities. Playing an arm at a lower fidelity is less costly but yields a reward estimate that is biased relative to the true reward observed at the highest fidelity. The paper formalizes this by assuming the mean reward at fidelity *m* deviates from the true mean by at most a known bound ζ(*m*). The authors propose MF-UCB, an algorithm based on the upper confidence bound (UCB) principle, which adapts to this setting by incorporating the fidelity-dependent bias into its confidence intervals. The algorithm aims to use cheaper, lower-fidelity pulls to quickly eliminate suboptimal arms, reserving more expensive, high-fidelity evaluations for a smaller set of promising candidates. The work provides a theoretical regret analysis for MF-UCB, arguing its superiority over a naive UCB strategy that only uses the highest fidelity, and complements this with a lower bound to show the near-optimality of their approach. The core idea is related to the extensive literature on UCB methods but extends it to a setting where cost and information quality are explicitly traded off through biased approximations, a distinction from prior work on budgeted bandits or bandits with random costs.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The primary contribution is the novel and interesting formulation of the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit problem. The concept of leveraging cheaper, biased approximations to manage the exploration-exploitation tradeoff is well-motivated and could be significant for applications where evaluation costs vary, such as hyperparameter tuning, simulation-based optimization, or clinical trials.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, including an upper bound on the regret for the proposed MF-UCB algorithm and a corresponding lower bound on the problem. This establishes a solid theoretical foundation for this new bandit variant and demonstrates that the proposed algorithm is nearly optimal under certain conditions.
*   **Clarity of Algorithm:** The MF-UCB algorithm is an intuitive and natural extension of the well-known UCB framework. The mechanism for choosing which arm to play and at which fidelity is clearly explained and well-justified by the "optimism in the face of uncertainty" principle.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**
*   **Clarity of Theoretical Results:** While the regret analysis is a key contribution, the main regret bound presented in Theorem 2 is complex and difficult to interpret directly. It is challenging for the reader to immediately grasp the conditions under which MF-UCB provides a substantial improvement over standard UCB, and by what magnitude. A simplified corollary or a more detailed discussion breaking down the terms in the bound would greatly enhance the paper's clarity and impact.
*   **Connection to Motivating Applications:** The link between the proposed model and the motivating examples could be strengthened. For instance, the assumption that shorter ad display times lead to a *biased* (rather than a higher-variance) estimate of long-term performance is not fully justified. Similarly, in the context of algorithm selection, minimizing cumulative regret may be a less natural objective than best-arm identification with a fixed budget. A more thorough discussion of how the model's assumptions (specifically, known bias bounds ζ(*m*)) map to these practical scenarios would improve the paper's significance.
*   **Experimental Evaluation:** The empirical results, while promising, are conducted on synthetic problems. The paper does not provide a strong justification for the choice of parameters used in the simulations (e.g., the costs λ(*m*) and bias bounds ζ(*m*)). This makes it difficult to ascertain if the reported performance gains of 3-4x are representative of what might be expected in practice, or if they are an artifact of a parameterization that is particularly favorable to MF-UCB. The empirical claims would be more convincing with a sensitivity analysis or experiments driven by parameter ranges from real-world applications.
