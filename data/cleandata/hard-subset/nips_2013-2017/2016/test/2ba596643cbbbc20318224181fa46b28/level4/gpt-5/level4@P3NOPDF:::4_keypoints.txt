- Novelty: Proposes a multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit framework where arms can be sampled at multiple costs/fidelities with cheaper, approximate low-fidelity samples.  
- Algorithm and theory: Presents an adaptive algorithm with regret upper bounds and a problem lower bound.  
- Empirics (positive): Synthetic experiments show improvements over standard UCB.  

- Notation/definitions confusing: Key symbols introduced late or sparsely, definitions dispersed, and a consolidated formal problem statement and symbol table are needed.  
- Misleading non-stationarity discussion: Motivating example and references to non-stationarity are unsupported by the stationary model and should be removed or clarified.  
- Objective/regret misalignment with costs: The regret target ignores costs (best high-fidelity mean only), making the cost-sensitive setting and low-fidelity sampling decisions unjustified; recommend cost-aware objectives or best-arm identification formulations.  
- Missing cost-complexity analysis: A fixed-confidence, cost-minimizing best-arm identification analysis would better capture practical multi-fidelity workflows.  
- Writing and structure need work: Exposition is unwieldy, assumptions and notation are not front-loaded, and the logical flow should be tightened.  

- Empirical evaluation lacks clarity and breadth: Must specify bias/variance and costs across fidelities, include sensitivity analyses (approximation quality, fidelity count, cost ratios), compare to cost-aware/multi-fidelity baselines, and report performance under fixed budgets and varying budget regimes.  

- Minor fixes: Language corrections, define symbol s at first use and maintain notation, avoid unsupported non-stationarity claims, clarify whether fidelity availabilities/costs are known/adversarial/stationary, and state whether lower and upper bounds match in order.  

- Overall assessment: Timely and relevant problem with promising initial results, but current regret formulation, exposition, and experiments are insufficient—recommend revisions for cost-aware objectives, clearer setup, and expanded evaluation; do not recommend acceptance in current form.
