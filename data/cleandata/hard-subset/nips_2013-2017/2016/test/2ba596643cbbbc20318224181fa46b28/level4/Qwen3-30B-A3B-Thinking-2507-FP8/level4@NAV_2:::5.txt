

This paper introduces a novel multi-fidelity bandit framework that extends the classical K-armed bandit problem by incorporating multiple levels of fidelity for each arm, where lower-fidelity approximations are cheaper but biased, while higher-fidelity evaluations are more expensive but accurate. The authors formalize this problem with a cost-weighted regret definition, develop the MF-UCB algorithm that adapts UCB to this setting, and provide theoretical regret bounds along with a matching lower bound. The work builds upon UCB methods [3, 5] and relates to multi-fidelity optimization work [7, 10], but makes a significant contribution by addressing the exploration-exploitation trade-off central to bandit problems, which differs from prior multi-fidelity methods that focus on optimization rather than bandit settings. The authors also reference recent NIPS work on multi-fidelity bandits [8], demonstrating awareness of related developments.

The paper demonstrates strong quality with rigorous theoretical analysis. The regret bounds for MF-UCB are well-motivated and proven, and the matching lower bound demonstrates near-optimality under certain conditions. The authors carefully address the biases introduced by lower-fidelity approximations, which is a significant theoretical contribution. The clarity of the paper is excellent: it is well-organized, the formalism is carefully developed, and the intuition behind MF-UCB is effectively explained. The figures (particularly Figure 1) clearly illustrate the partitioning of arms by fidelity, and the theoretical results are presented logically. The originality is strong, as the paper formalizes a multi-fidelity bandit setting that has not been previously analyzed in the context of bandit problems. While there is related work on multi-fidelity methods in optimization [7, 10], the paper clearly distinguishes its contribution by focusing on the exploration-exploitation trade-off central to bandit problems. The significance is high, as the problem addressed has direct relevance to practical applications like online advertising (where lower-fidelity approximations can be obtained through shorter ad displays or narrower audience targeting) and algorithm selection in machine learning (where cheaper approximations can be obtained with less data or computation). The theoretical results demonstrate that MF-UCB can achieve better regret than standard UCB methods when lower fidelities are sufficiently cheaper and informative, and the empirical results corroborate these theoretical insights.

The main weaknesses are that the theoretical analysis relies on specific assumptions (e.g., Assumption 1 on bias parameter decay) that may not hold in all practical applications, though the authors acknowledge this limitation. The paper could provide a more intuitive explanation for the choice of γ(m) in Equation 4 to enhance clarity. Additionally, while the authors reference Kandasamy et al. [8], a more explicit comparison of problem formulations and theoretical results would better establish the novelty of this work relative to recent NIPS contributions.

Pro arguments for acceptance: The paper addresses a relevant problem with significant practical applications; the theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides meaningful insights; the MF-UCB algorithm is well-motivated with empirical results supporting theoretical claims; the paper clearly distinguishes itself from related work.

Con arguments against acceptance: The theoretical analysis depends on specific assumptions that may limit practical applicability; empirical validation is limited to synthetic data; the paper could better differentiate its contribution from recent related work [8].

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the bandit literature that makes a significant theoretical advance. The multi-fidelity framework is well-motivated, the algorithm is well-designed, and the theoretical analysis is rigorous. The minor weaknesses do not detract from the paper's core contribution. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the clarity concerns regarding γ(m) and the comparison with [8].
