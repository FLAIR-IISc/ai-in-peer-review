

This paper introduces a novel multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit framework, where each arm can be sampled at different fidelities (approximations) with varying costs. The authors formalize a cost-weighted regret measure that accounts for both the quality of the estimate and the cost of sampling at each fidelity level. They propose MF-UCB, an adaptive algorithm that leverages cheaper lower-fidelity approximations to eliminate suboptimal arms before investing in expensive high-fidelity evaluations. This extends the classical UCB framework [3, 9] to incorporate multi-fidelity structures, addressing an important practical consideration in applications like online advertising (where short display periods can approximate long-term performance) and algorithm selection (where cheaper data subsets can approximate full-data performance).

The paper makes significant contributions across all four NIPS evaluation criteria. For quality, the authors provide a rigorous regret analysis establishing that MF-UCB achieves near-optimal performance under reasonable assumptions, with a matching lower bound demonstrating the tightness of their upper bound. The theoretical results are well-supported with careful mathematical analysis. For clarity, the main paper is exceptionally well-written and accessible, clearly explaining the problem formulation, algorithmic intuition, and key theoretical results. However, the supplementary materials contain numerous typos and minor errors that undermine their readability (e.g., inconsistent notation like H^(m) vs. K^(m), missing parentheses, incorrect variable subscripts, and typographical errors in equations). A thorough proofreading would significantly improve the paper's presentation.

The work demonstrates strong originality by formalizing a multi-fidelity bandit framework that differs from related work. While budgeted bandits [13, 15] consider cost per arm, and multi-fidelity optimization [7, 10] focuses on optimization rather than exploration-exploitation, this paper uniquely addresses the regret minimization trade-off across multiple fidelity levels. The authors properly contextualize their work against recent NIPS papers, including their own prior work [8] on multi-fidelity bandits.

The significance of this work is substantial, as it addresses a critical gap in applying bandit methods to real-world scenarios where high-fidelity evaluations are expensive. The theoretical results demonstrate that MF-UCB can achieve better regret than standard UCB algorithms by leveraging lower-fidelity approximations, with the practical benefit being particularly pronounced when the set of promising arms is small relative to the total number of arms. The empirical results in Figure 2 corroborate the theoretical insights, showing MF-UCB consistently outperforms UCB across synthetic problems.

However, the paper could be strengthened in two key areas. First, the authors use "up to a multiplicative constant" notation throughout, which obscures the dependence on algorithm parameters like ρ (a design parameter in MF-UCB). For instance, the regret bound in Theorem 2 hides a factor of ρ, which is not constant but a tunable parameter. Explicitly stating the exact inequalities (as provided in the supplementary materials) would improve precision and clarity. Second, the practical applicability of the method depends on accurate knowledge of ζ(m), the bias between fidelities. In real applications, ζ(m) would need to be estimated, potentially introducing additional challenges. A discussion of how to handle estimation errors in ζ(m) and their impact on performance would greatly enhance the paper's practical relevance.

The strengths of this work clearly outweigh the weaknesses. The theoretical contributions are sound, the problem formulation is novel and well-motivated, and the empirical results support the theoretical claims. The minor issues with the supplementary materials are easily fixable, and the theoretical precision concerns can be addressed with minor revisions to the main text. The paper represents a significant contribution to the bandit literature, with clear potential for practical impact in applications where multi-fidelity evaluations are common.

In summary, this paper presents a novel and well-motivated extension of the multi-armed bandit framework with strong theoretical results and practical relevance. The main paper is exceptionally clear, and while the supplementary materials require proofreading, the core contributions are compelling. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the theoretical precision concerns and include a brief discussion of practical implementation considerations.
