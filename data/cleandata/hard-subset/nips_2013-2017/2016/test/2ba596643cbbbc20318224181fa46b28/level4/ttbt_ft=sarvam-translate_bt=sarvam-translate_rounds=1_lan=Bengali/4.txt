The author has provided a new framework called Multi-Fidelity Multi-Armed Bandits. In this framework, weapons can be sampled with varying levels of fidelity, and a cost is associated with each weapon. At a lower cost, lower-fidelity samples are taken from a distribution close to the highest fidelity distribution. They provide an algorithm that adapts to the available estimates and cost order, its regret analysis, and a lower bound of the problem. They provide numerical simulations in synthetic problems where the proposed algorithm exceeds the UCB. The proposed framework is interesting and could be impactful if further developed. However, in this state, I found the paper unsuitable for publication.

1/ The role of signals is quite confusing, making them very scattered and difficult to define. For example, I couldn't find what $s$ is (after protests, it seems $s$ was defined 1 page after the first use and then rarely used in the rest of the paper). 2/ The use of advertisements as examples is confusing in some cases. In many places, the display time and non-stability of an advertisement are correlated. Non-stability is irrelevant in this paper. Using another example might be a good idea (see 4). 3/ I find it difficult to justify regret analysis in this situation. A regret analysis involves maximizing a profit in the long run and thus finding the weapon with the best ratio reward/cost. Here, the best policy is always the weapon with the highest average, regardless of the associated cost. Therefore, the best policy is weakened by the obligation to sample the best weapon at a low fidelity level. This can mask the cost of sampling at a low level when it is not needed. 4/ A sample complexity analysis (another cost complexity here) might be useful in the context of Multi-Fidelity Multi-Armed Bandits. It can be inspired by the testing phase of prototypes for new product concepts. The goal is to find the best product with the highest probability, reducing the accumulated cost of the testing phase before mass production. Since companies are limited by regulations, contracts, and existing benefits, they are forced to use lower-fidelity estimates before accessing advanced and expensive testing. 5/ I also found some parts of the text confusing. It might be worth taking a look at the paper to make it more streamlined.
