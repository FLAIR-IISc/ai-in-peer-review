This paper presents a "multi-reliability" version of the multi-armed bandit problem, where there are K weapons, each of which can be used at different levels of "reliability." Lower reliability is less expensive, but leads to potentially greater bias in estimating the expected reward of the weapon. (Using a weapon at reliability m results in a sample taken from a distribution whose expected value deviates from the actual expected reward of the weapon to such an extent that the upper bound depends on the reliability.) This paper analyzes a natural UCB-based algorithm for this problem, where the privacy margin is increased to compensate for the uncertainty arising from the bias of taking a sample at lower reliability. The algorithm's analysis is summarized by a theorem that has complex regret bounds that are difficult to explain; the paper argues that these regret bounds will often be significantly better than the bounds obtained using the standard UCB algorithm for choosing which weapon to use at maximum reliability and choosing which weapon to use. A brief section near the end of the paper presents simulation results to support this claim. Initially, I was puzzled as to why the paper chose to model lower reliability as introducing more bias rather than introducing more variance. ("The subsequent explanation for lower reliability also seems quite reasonable to me.") The motivating examples given in the paper (such as algorithm selection for machine learning problems) convinced me that modeling lower reliability as greater bias is quite reasonable, although I am still not convinced by one motivation (e.g., a biased estimate of the effectiveness of an advertisement for a longer period when an advertisement is shown for a shorter period), and in the example of algorithm selection for machine learning, it is not clear why reducing regret would be an algorithm designer's goal in that application. So in summary, I thought this paper had a frustratingly weak connection between models and motivating applications, but it is not weak enough to create an untrustworthy flaw. The paper's main innovation is probably the origin of the multi-reliability multi-armed bandit problem. The algorithm is a fairly general application of the "optimism in the face of uncertainty" principle. The performance guarantee mentioned before this review seems frustratingly difficult to explain to me. Simulation results on synthetic problems are difficult for me to evaluate: Appendix C describes in more detail the set of parameter values used in the simulations, but does not attempt to justify these choices of parameter values. In many simulations, the MF-UCB regret seems to be 3 to 4 times greater than the UCB regret; should we expect this to be a typical example of the relative performance of the two algorithms in practice, or is it an artifact of the choice of parameters (such as the value of \ zeta^{(m)}$-)?
