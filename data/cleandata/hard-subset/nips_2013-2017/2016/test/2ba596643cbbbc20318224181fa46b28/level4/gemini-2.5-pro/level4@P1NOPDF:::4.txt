The authors propose a new framework called Multi-fidelity Multi-armed bandits. In this setting, arms can be sampled at different fidelity levels, each with an associated cost. Low-fidelity samples, while cheaper, are drawn from a distribution that approximates the true distribution of the highest fidelity level. The authors present an algorithm that adapts to the available sequences of approximations and their costs, provide a regret analysis, and derive a lower bound for the problem. They also conduct numerical simulations on synthetic problems, showing that their proposed algorithm outperforms UCB.

The proposed framework is interesting and has the potential to be impactful with further development. However, in its current state, I find the paper is not ready for publication for the following reasons:

1.  **Clarity of Notation:** The introduction of notation is confusing. Definitions are dispersed throughout the text, making them difficult to locate and follow. For instance, the variable $s$ is used a full page before it is formally defined, and then it is seldom used thereafter. This lack of clarity hinders the reader's ability to understand the technical contributions.

2.  **Misleading Motivating Example:** The motivating example of online advertising is, at times, misleading. The paper frequently draws connections between ad display time and non-stationarity. However, non-stationarity is outside the scope of the problem formulation presented in this paper, which assumes stationary distributions. It might be beneficial to use a different motivating example that aligns more closely with the stationary setting, such as the one suggested in point 4.

3.  **Justification of the Regret Analysis:** The choice of regret as the performance metric is not well-justified for this problem setting. In this paper's formulation, the optimal policy is defined as always pulling the arm with the highest mean, regardless of the costs associated with its different fidelity levels. This definition decouples reward from cost. Consequently, the regret analysis seems to penalize the algorithm for not sampling the best arm (even at a low fidelity/cost), while in many practical applications, the true goal might be to identify the best arm with high confidence while minimizing the total accumulated cost. The current regret formulation may obscure this more practical objective and does not seem to fully capture the trade-off between cost and reward, which is central to the problem.

4.  **Alternative Formulation:** A cost complexity analysis would be a more natural fit for the multi-fidelity bandit problem than a regret analysis. This alternative formulation could be motivated by applications such as prototype testing in new product development. In such a scenario, the goal is to identify the best product with high probability while minimizing the total cost of the testing phase before committing to mass production. This example aligns well with the proposed framework, as companies are often required (due to regulations, contracts, or resource constraints) to use low-fidelity approximations (e.g., simulations, small-scale models) before proceeding to more advanced and costly tests (e.g., full-scale prototypes).

5.  **General Readability:** Finally, the overall writing could be improved. Several sections of the paper are unwieldy and would benefit from a thorough revision to make the presentation more streamlined and easier to read.
