This paper introduces a "multi-fidelity" extension of the multi-armed bandit problem. In this setting, each of K arms can be pulled at one of M fidelity levels. Pulling at a lower fidelity is less costly but introduces greater bias into the reward estimate. Specifically, the expected value of a sample drawn at fidelity level *m* deviates from the arm's true mean reward by an amount that is bounded as a function of the fidelity.

The authors propose and analyze a natural UCB-based algorithm for this problem. The algorithm adapts the standard UCB approach by widening the confidence bounds to account for the additional uncertainty introduced by the low-fidelity bias. The theoretical analysis culminates in regret bounds that, while complex and difficult to interpret, are argued to be a significant improvement over a baseline strategy that uses a standard UCB algorithm on the highest fidelity level alone. A brief experimental section presents simulation results to substantiate this claim.

A key contribution of the paper is the problem formulation itself. The choice to model lower fidelity as increased bias, rather than increased variance (which would also be a plausible model), is reasonably justified by some of the motivating examples, such as algorithm selection for machine learning. However, the connection between the proposed model and its motivating applications feels tenuous at times. For instance, the argument that displaying an ad for a shorter duration yields a biased estimate of its long-term effectiveness is not entirely convincing. Furthermore, in the algorithm selection setting, it is not clear that minimizing regret is the primary objective for an algorithm designer. While this motivational weakness is notable, it does not constitute a disqualifying flaw.

Beyond the problem formulation, the primary technical contributions appear to be modest. The proposed algorithm is a straightforward application of the "optimism in the face of uncertainty" principle. The corresponding performance guarantees, as noted, are difficult to interpret, which limits their utility in providing practical insight.

The empirical validation also has limitations. The simulation results, presented on synthetic problems, are difficult to assess. While Appendix C details the parameter values used, it provides no justification for these choices. Consequently, it is unclear whether the reported 3-4x improvement of the proposed MF-UCB algorithm over standard UCB is representative of typical performance or an artifact of the specific parameter settings (e.g., the values of ζ^(m)).
