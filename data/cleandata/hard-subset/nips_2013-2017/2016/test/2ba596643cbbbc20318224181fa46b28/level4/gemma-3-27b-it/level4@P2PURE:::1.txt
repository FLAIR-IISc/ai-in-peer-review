This paper addresses multi-armed bandit problems with a fidelity component, allowing the learner to select from a finite set of fidelities for each arm pull. Pulling an arm at a given fidelity yields a stochastic, i.i.d. reward with a mean within a known error bound, ζ<sub>m</sub>, of the true mean, with the highest fidelity corresponding to ζ<sub>m</sub> = 0. Crucially, lower fidelities are less expensive than higher ones. The authors formulate this problem as maximizing net reward, propose a novel algorithm, and demonstrate its potential to outperform standard UCB algorithms operating solely at high fidelities. They also provide a lower bound on the algorithm’s regret.

The problem studied is both interesting and relevant, effectively modeling the fundamental tradeoff between information, cost, and reward in online learning – specifically, the decision of whether to prioritize cheap, low-information samples or expensive, high-information ones. As such, it offers a valuable benchmark for future research. While the paper appears technically sound, a significant weakness lies in the insufficient explanation of its results and underlying assumptions. 

The adopted regret definition feels somewhat unnatural; penalizing resource consumption (cost) additively, rather than multiplicatively as is done here, might be more intuitive. Although the authors motivate their choice with an ad-display example, this may not generalize well. Exploring the implications of incorporating fidelity costs additively into the total reward (or regret) would be beneficial.

Furthermore, Assumption 1 – imposing constraints on the fidelity parameters known to the learner – appears artificial and its justification is unclear. It is not obvious why this assumption should hold generally in a learning problem. More importantly, if the ζ<sub>m</sub> values do not decay at the rate specified in Assumption 1, is there a principled method for selecting a subset of fidelities that *would* satisfy the assumption, thereby preserving the validity of the results? Addressing this point would substantially strengthen the paper. Despite the authors’ claim that the assumption is not crucial, it seems integral to the proofs, and a primary concern is whether it fundamentally influences the algorithm’s design and performance. The algorithm appears tailored to specific ζ<sub>m</sub> configurations, but the rationale for why these configurations are considered appropriate is missing. The gap in the lower bound also warrants further investigation and potentially a refinement of the proposed strategy.

Interpreting the main result (Theorem 2, the regret bound for MF-UCB) and comparing it to UCB (presumably at the highest fidelity, though this is not explicitly stated) is challenging due to the technical complexity of the presentation. The relative performance gain or loss of MF-UCB seems dependent on Δ and ||k||, which in turn are determined by the intricate sequence of sets 𝒦<sup>(m)</sup>. A more transparent explanation would significantly bolster the authors’ argument.

Overall, the paper presents a promising starting point by introducing an interesting learning model, but it quickly loses clarity when detailing the results. Minor issues include: line 56, “near-optimal”; line 126, an unnecessary period before “for all m < M”; and line 179, where curly braces would be preferable for defining ||k||.
