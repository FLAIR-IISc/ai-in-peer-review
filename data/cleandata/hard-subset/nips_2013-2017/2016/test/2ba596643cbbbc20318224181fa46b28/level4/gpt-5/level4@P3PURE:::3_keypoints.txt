- Introduces a multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit (K arms, M fidelities) where lower fidelities are cheaper but biased.  
- Proposes a UCB-style algorithm that inflates confidence intervals to account for fidelity-dependent bias.  
- Provides theoretical regret bounds that are messy and difficult to interpret.  
- Claims the regret guarantees can often outperform standard high-fidelity UCB, sometimes substantially.  
- Includes a brief synthetic simulation section that empirically supports MF-UCB’s improvements.  
- Modeling lower fidelity as increased bias (rather than variance) is initially puzzling but plausible for some applications.  
- The ad example (shorter display windows causing bias) is unconvincing as a motivation for the bias model.  
- For the algorithm selection example, it is unclear why regret minimization is the appropriate objective.  
- Main novelty is the problem formulation; the algorithm itself is a fairly standard optimism-in-the-face-of-uncertainty application.  
- Theoretical guarantees lack clear, interpretable conditions specifying when MF-UCB outperforms high-fidelity UCB.  
- Experiments use synthetic parameters (e.g., ζ^(m)) with no justification; observed 3–4x gains may be parameter-specific.  
- Strengths: clear problem statement, plausible bias motivation in some cases, natural algorithmic approach, some empirical gains.  
- Weaknesses: weak/occasional connection to real applications, hard-to-parse theoretical bounds, unjustified experimental choices, unclear generalizability.  
- Suggested revisions: clarify bias vs variance modeling and strengthen the ad example; justify regret as the objective for algorithm selection.  
- Suggested revisions (cont.): provide more interpretable corollaries or conditions for the regret bounds and justify/sensitivity-test simulation parameter choices.
