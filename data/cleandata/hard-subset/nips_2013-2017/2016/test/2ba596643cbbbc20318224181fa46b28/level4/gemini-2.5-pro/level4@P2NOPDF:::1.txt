This paper introduces a multi-armed bandit framework where the learner must choose not only an arm to pull but also a 'fidelity' level for that pull. Each fidelity level is associated with a cost and an error bound on the observed reward. Specifically, pulling an arm at fidelity `m` incurs a cost `c_m` and yields a stochastic reward whose mean is guaranteed to be within `zeta_m` of the true mean. Higher fidelities are more expensive but provide more accurate reward estimates, with the highest fidelity having zero error (`zeta=0`). The authors formulate the objective as maximizing the net reward (total reward minus total cost), propose a new algorithm called MF-UCB, and provide an analysis of its regret. They argue that this approach can outperform standard UCB algorithms that operate only at the highest fidelity.

The problem formulation is both interesting and practically relevant. It effectively models the fundamental trade-off between the cost of acquiring information and the value of that information in an online learning setting. The stochastic bandit problem is a natural place to study this trade-off, and this work could serve as a valuable benchmark for future research in this direction.

Despite the promising setup, the paper suffers from a lack of clarity regarding its core assumptions and results, which significantly weakens its impact. My main concerns are as follows:

**1. Justification of the Regret Formulation:**
The paper defines regret with a multiplicative cost structure. While the authors' ad-display example provides some motivation for this choice, it is not clear that this formulation is broadly applicable. A more standard and arguably more natural approach would be to incorporate costs additively, where the goal is to maximize `(total reward) - (total cost)`. The implications of this alternative formulation are not discussed. A more thorough justification for the multiplicative model, or a comparison with the additive cost model, would strengthen the paper by situating the work within a broader context.

**2. The Role and Necessity of Assumption 1:**
A significant concern is the introduction of Assumption 1, which places constraints on the decay rate of the fidelity error parameters (`zeta_m`). This assumption appears artificial and its motivation is not explained. Why should a practitioner expect these parameters to follow such a specific structure in a general learning problem?

The authors state that this assumption is not crucial, yet it appears to be used in the proofs. This raises a critical question: does the assumption heavily influence the design and performance of the MF-UCB algorithm? It seems the algorithm may be specifically tailored to problem instances that satisfy this condition. A key point of clarification would be to address what happens if the `zeta_m` values do not decay as required. For example, could a learner select a subset of the available fidelities to satisfy the assumption? Answering this would significantly strengthen the paper's practical message. Without a better justification, the assumption makes the setting feel narrow and the algorithm's utility less general.

**3. Clarity and Interpretation of Theoretical Results:**
The presentation of the main theoretical results, particularly the regret bound in Theorem 2, is difficult to interpret. The comparison with the standard UCB baseline is not explicitly detailed—is the baseline a UCB algorithm operating only at the highest fidelity?

Furthermore, the complexity of the regret bound, with its dependence on `Delta`, `[[k]]`, and the sequence of sets `K^(m)`, obscures the practical takeaways. It is not immediately clear from the theorem under what conditions MF-UCB offers a substantial improvement over simpler strategies. The authors' case would be much more compelling if they provided a more intuitive explanation of the result. For instance, a simplified corollary or an illustrative example showing how problem parameters (`Delta`, costs, and `zeta_m` values) affect the relative performance of MF-UCB would greatly enhance the reader's understanding. Additionally, the noted gap between the upper and lower regret bounds warrants further discussion, as it may suggest opportunities for either a tighter analysis or algorithmic improvements.

In summary, the paper introduces a compelling and well-motivated learning model. However, its contribution is undermined by a lack of clarity in its presentation. The justification for the regret model is thin, a key assumption about the problem structure is not well-motivated, and the main theoretical results are presented in a way that is difficult to parse and appreciate. I would encourage the authors to substantially revise the paper to address these issues of clarity and justification.
