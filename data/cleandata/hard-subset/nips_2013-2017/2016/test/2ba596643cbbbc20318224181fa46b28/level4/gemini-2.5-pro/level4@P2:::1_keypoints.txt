- Introduces a multi-fidelity stochastic K-armed bandit where each arm can be pulled at M fidelities with cheaper, biased low fidelities and an unbiased highest fidelity, and defines a cost-weighted regret.  
- Proposes MF-UCB, a UCB-style algorithm that uses low-fidelity pulls to quickly eliminate clearly suboptimal arms and reserves high-fidelity pulls for promising arms.  
- Provides an upper bound on MF-UCB’s regret and derives a lower bound for the problem, claiming near-optimality under certain conditions.  
- Strength: Addresses a novel, practically relevant trade-off between information quality and cost, offering a useful new benchmark for sequential decision problems.  
- Strength: Technically solid overall, with complete theoretical treatment, algorithm design, and regret analyses building on established bandit techniques.  
- Weakness: Theorem 2 and the regret bound are hard to parse due to complex arm partitioning into K(m), limiting intuition about when MF-UCB outperforms standard UCB.  
- Weakness: The multiplicative cost-weighted regret choice is motivated by one example (online advertising) but may be nonstandard; additive-cost formulations are not discussed thoroughly.  
- Weakness: Assumption 1 (rapid decay of fidelity error bounds ζ(m)) seems artificial, may not hold broadly, and its role in algorithm design (e.g., switching thresholds γ(m)) is insufficiently justified.  
- Question: Could results/general applicability be recovered by selecting an optimal subsequence of fidelities if Assumption 1 fails—this is not addressed.  
- Weakness: There is a gap between upper and lower bounds for arms in K(m) (MF-UCB may use higher-cost fidelities unnecessarily); the paper notes this but offers little direction to close it.  
- Suggestion: The paper would benefit from a simplified corollary, illustrative examples, or clearer conditions characterizing when MF-UCB yields substantial gains over single-fidelity UCB.  
- Summary evaluation: Technically strong and original, but clarity and generality are limited by dense theoretical presentation, strong assumptions, and a theoretical gap.  
- Argument for acceptance: Novel problem formulation, complete analysis (algorithm + upper/lower bounds), and high potential impact for follow-up research.  
- Argument against acceptance: Low clarity of main theoretical claims, reliance on restrictive fidelity assumptions and specialized regret model, and unresolved optimality gap.  
- Minor typos: suggested edits include changing "near-optimal" to "nearly optimal" (Line 56), removing an unnecessary period (Line 126), and using set notation for JkK for clarity (Line 179).
