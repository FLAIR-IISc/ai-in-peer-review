This paper introduces a multi-armed bandit setting that incorporates a "fidelity" component. For each arm pull, the learner must select a fidelity level from a finite set. A pull at a given fidelity yields a stochastic i.i.d. reward whose mean is guaranteed to be within a known error, `zeta_m`, of the true mean. The highest fidelity level corresponds to zero error (`zeta=0`), and lower fidelities are associated with lower costs. The authors frame the objective as maximizing a notion of net reward through a sequential strategy of choosing both arms and fidelities. They propose a new algorithm, MF-UCB, and argue that it can outperform standard UCB algorithms that operate only at the highest fidelity. The paper also provides a lower bound on the regret.

The problem studied in this paper is both interesting and relevant. It addresses the fundamental trade-off between the quality of information, its associated cost, and the resulting reward in an online learning context, specifically for stochastic bandits. As such, the proposed model and algorithm could serve as a valuable benchmark for future research in this area.

While the paper appears to be technically sound, its primary weakness is a lack of clear explanation for its core assumptions and results. A key concern is the definition of regret. The authors penalize resource consumption (cost) multiplicatively, which seems unnatural. While the provided ad-display example motivates this choice, it may not be broadly applicable. A more standard and arguably more natural formulation would be to incorporate costs additively into the total reward or regret. It would be valuable for the authors to discuss this alternative formulation and how their results might change under such a model.

Furthermore, Assumption 1, which imposes constraints on the fidelity parameters `zeta_m`, seems artificial and is not well-justified. It is unclear why this assumption should hold in a general learning problem. A crucial question is whether the results can be extended to cases where the `zeta_m` values do not satisfy the required decay rate. For instance, could a learner select a subsequence of fidelities that *do* satisfy the assumption? Addressing this possibility would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution. The authors state that this assumption is not critical, yet it appears to be used in the proofs. This raises the concern that the assumption may fundamentally influence both the algorithm's design and its performance. The current algorithm seems tailored to specific `zeta` configurations, but the paper lacks a convincing argument as to why these are the most relevant or important configurations to consider.

The gap between the regret bound and the provided lower bound also warrants further investigation and may suggest that a refined algorithm is needed. Finally, the presentation of the main result (Theorem 2) makes it difficult to interpret. The regret bound for MF-UCB is highly technical, and a clear, intuitive comparison with a baseline like standard UCB (presumably operating at the highest fidelity, though this is not explicitly stated) is missing. The relative performance of MF-UCB depends on complex terms such as `Delta`, `[[k]]`, and the sequence of sets `\cal{K}^(m)`. The authors' case would be much stronger if they provided a more explicit and intuitive explanation of this result, perhaps with illustrative examples or a simplified corollary that highlights the key dependencies.

In summary, the paper introduces a compelling learning model and gets off to a strong start. However, the clarity of the presentation diminishes significantly when discussing the theoretical results, making it difficult to fully appreciate the contributions. Improving the justification for the modeling choices and the exposition of the main theorems would greatly enhance the paper's impact.
