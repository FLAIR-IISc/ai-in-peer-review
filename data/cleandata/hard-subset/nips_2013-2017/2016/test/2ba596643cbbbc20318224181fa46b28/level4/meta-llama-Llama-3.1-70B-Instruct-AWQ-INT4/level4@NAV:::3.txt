This paper presents a "multi-fidelity" extension of the multi-armed bandit problem, where each of the K arms can be played at M different levels of fidelity. A lower fidelity level incurs lower costs but may result in greater bias in estimating the arm's reward, with the upper bound of the bias depending on the fidelity level. The expected value of the distribution from which a sample is drawn when an arm is pulled at fidelity m deviates from the arm's true expected reward by an amount that depends on the fidelity level.

The paper analyzes a natural UCB-based algorithm adapted for this problem, where the confidence interval width is increased to account for the uncertainty caused by the bias of low-fidelity sampling. The analysis of this algorithm is summarized in theorems that provide complex regret bounds that are difficult to interpret. However, the paper argues that these regret bounds often significantly outperform those achieved by consistently playing arms at the highest fidelity and using the standard UCB algorithm for arm selection. Simulation results supporting this claim are presented in a brief section near the end of the paper.

Initially, I found it puzzling that the paper models lower fidelity as increased bias rather than increased variance. However, the provided motivating examples, such as algorithm selection for machine learning problems, convinced me that modeling low fidelity as greater bias is well-motivated. Nevertheless, one of the motivations, specifically the claim that displaying an ad for a shorter time interval yields a biased estimate of the ad's effectiveness over longer intervals, is not entirely convincing. Furthermore, in the context of algorithm selection for machine learning, it is unclear why minimizing regret would be the primary goal of an algorithm designer.

In summary, I found the connection between the model and the motivating applications to be somewhat weak, although not weak enough to be a major flaw. The main innovation of the paper appears to be the formulation of the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit problem itself. The proposed algorithm is a relatively standard application of the "optimism in the face of uncertainty" principle. However, the performance guarantee is disappointingly difficult to interpret. The simulation results on synthetic problems are challenging to assess due to the lack of justification for the chosen parameter values. The results show that the regret of MF-UCB improves upon that of UCB by a factor of 3 to 4 in many simulations, but it is unclear whether this improvement is typical of the relative performance of the two algorithms in practice or an artifact of the chosen parameter values.
