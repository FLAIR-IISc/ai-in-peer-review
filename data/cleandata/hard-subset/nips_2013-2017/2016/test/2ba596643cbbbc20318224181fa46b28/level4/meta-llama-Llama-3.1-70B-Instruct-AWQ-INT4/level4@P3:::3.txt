The paper introduces a "multi-fidelity" version of the multi-armed bandit problem, where each of the K arms can be played at M different levels of fidelity. The lower fidelity is less costly but leads to potentially greater bias in the estimate of the arm's reward. The paper analyzes the natural UCB-based algorithm for this problem, where the width of the confidence interval is increased to compensate for the uncertainty stemming from the bias of sampling at low fidelity.

The paper provides a clear and concise introduction to the multi-fidelity bandit problem, including a detailed explanation of the problem formulation, the assumptions, and the objectives. The authors also provide a thorough review of the related work, highlighting the key differences between their approach and existing methods.

The main contribution of the paper is the development of the Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB) algorithm, which adapts the classical Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) strategies to the multi-fidelity setting. The authors provide a detailed analysis of the algorithm's performance, including a regret bound and a lower bound. The regret bound is somewhat complex and difficult to interpret, but the authors provide a clear explanation of the key factors that influence the bound.

The paper also presents simulation results on synthetic problems, which demonstrate the effectiveness of the MF-UCB algorithm in reducing regret compared to the standard UCB algorithm. However, the choice of parameter values used in the simulations is not fully justified, and it is unclear whether the results are representative of the typical performance of the two algorithms in practice.

Overall, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of multi-armed bandits by introducing a new problem formulation and developing an effective algorithm for solving it. While the paper could benefit from a clearer explanation of the regret bound and a more detailed analysis of the simulation results, it is well-written and provides a thorough treatment of the subject matter.
