This paper discusses a new multi-armed bandit scenario. The scenario involves $M$ loyalties, where a student can choose an armed bandit. Each successive loyalty reward provides a good approximation but costs more. If a strategy uses fewer and cheaper loyalties to eliminate a few sub-optimal armed bandits, where a small subset of good armed bandits can be saved for a larger and more expensive loyalty, it could surpass the standard UCBC algorithm, which ignores these loyalty characteristics. This scenario can be used in online advertising, where advertisements can be shown for a few minutes to estimate click numbers in the long run, considering the cost. For this problem, the authors consider a new regret definition, provide an algorithm with theoretical analysis (with a lower bound on regret), and conduct simulations. The analysis is novel. Since it is assumed that the average deviation of the maximum loyalty from each \varepsilon^{(m)}, i.e., the m-th loyalty, is already known, how can we actually know these values (e.g., its application in online advertising)? Is it possible to relax this assumption?
