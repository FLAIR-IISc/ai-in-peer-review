This paper introduces a novel multi-armed bandit (MAB) setting where each arm provides feedback with varying accuracy, or fidelity. The cost of sampling an arm increases with the desired accuracy level, and the regret is defined as the product of this cost and the standard pseudo-regret. The authors propose an algorithm, MF-UCB, to address this setting and provide both upper and lower bounds on its cumulative pseudo-regret.

The main body of the paper is exceptionally clear and well-written. However, the supplementary material, which contains the majority of the proofs, suffers from numerous typos and minor errors that detract from readability and potentially introduce confusion. (A detailed list of these issues is provided at the end of this review.) Correcting these errors would significantly improve the clarity and accessibility of the technical details. 

Furthermore, the paper’s frequent use of “up-to-a-multiplicative-constant” bounds feels somewhat misleading. While mathematically correct, the constants involved are not truly constant; they depend on parameters of the algorithm itself (e.g., ρ in Theorem 2). Presenting the more precise inequalities, as found in the supplementary material, would enhance the transparency and understanding of the results.

From a practical standpoint, the immediate applicability of this algorithm to real-world problems is not readily apparent. The MF-UCB strategy relies on low-accuracy pulls to rapidly eliminate suboptimal arms, but the accuracy of this elimination hinges on accurate estimation of ζ (the fidelity parameter). I believe a discussion of the potential practical limitations of the algorithm, and possible strategies for mitigating them, would substantially strengthen the work. 

Despite these concerns, the paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution. It offers a compelling analysis of this new MAB problem, supported by interesting theorems and rigorous proofs. Overall, I consider this a significant and interesting contribution to the field.
