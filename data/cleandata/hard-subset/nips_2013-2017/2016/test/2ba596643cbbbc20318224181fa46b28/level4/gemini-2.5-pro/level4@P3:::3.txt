This paper introduces the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit (MF-MAB) problem, a novel variant of the classical stochastic K-armed bandit setting. The core idea is that for each of the K arms, a decision-maker can choose to pull it at one of M different "fidelities." Pulling an arm at a lower fidelity is cheaper but provides a reward signal that is a more biased approximation of the "true" reward, which is only observed at the highest fidelity. The bias for each fidelity level `m` is assumed to be bounded by a known value `ζ(m)`. The paper's main contributions are: 1) the formalization of this MF-MAB problem and a corresponding cost-aware notion of regret; 2) a new algorithm, Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB), which adapts the UCB principle to this setting; and 3) a theoretical analysis providing an upper regret bound for MF-UCB and a corresponding lower bound, showing the algorithm is nearly optimal. The algorithm intelligently uses cheap, low-fidelity pulls to quickly eliminate clearly suboptimal arms, reserving expensive, high-fidelity pulls for a smaller set of promising candidates.

### Strengths:

1.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Problem Formulation:** The primary strength of this paper is the introduction and formalization of the MF-MAB problem. This is an original and practical extension of the standard bandit framework that captures a realistic trade-off between cost and information quality present in many real-world applications, such as hyperparameter tuning, clinical trials, or online advertising. The modeling choice of representing lower fidelity as a bounded bias in the mean reward is well-argued and plausible for several of the motivating examples.

2.  **Intuitive and Sound Algorithmic Design:** The proposed MF-UCB algorithm is a natural and elegant extension of the UCB framework. The "optimism in the face of uncertainty" principle is applied by constructing confidence bounds that account for both statistical uncertainty (from a finite number of samples) and systemic bias (from the fidelity level `ζ(m)`). The criterion for switching between fidelities is clever and well-justified by the subsequent theoretical analysis.

3.  **Comprehensive Theoretical Analysis:** The paper provides a rigorous regret analysis for MF-UCB, demonstrating that its performance can be significantly better than a naive UCB strategy that only uses the highest fidelity. This is complemented by a problem-specific lower bound, which establishes that MF-UCB is nearly optimal on a substantial subset of problem instances. This provides a strong theoretical foundation for the proposed approach.

### Weaknesses:

1.  **Interpretability of the Regret Bounds:** While technically sound, the main regret bound presented in Theorem 2 is complex and difficult to parse. The performance guarantee depends on a partitioning of the arms into sets `K(m)`, which, while necessary for the proof, obscures a clear and intuitive understanding of how the regret scales with the core problem parameters (`λ(m)`, `ζ(m)`, `Δ(m)_k`). The paper would be strengthened by a simplified corollary or a more detailed discussion that walks the reader through the implications of the bound in a simplified, concrete setting.

2.  **Connection to Motivating Applications:** The link between the formal model and some of the motivating examples could be stronger. For instance, the claim that displaying an ad for a shorter time yields a *biased* estimate, rather than simply a higher-variance one, is asserted but not fully substantiated. For the algorithm selection example, it is not immediately clear that minimizing cumulative regret is the most natural objective for a practitioner, as opposed to a pure-exploration goal like best-arm identification. While the bias model is interesting, exploring the alternative model where lower fidelity introduces higher variance would also be a valuable contribution.

3.  **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The simulation results, while promising, are presented on synthetic problems with a specific set of parameters detailed in the appendix. There is little justification for why these parameter choices (e.g., the specific values for costs `λ(m)` and bias bounds `ζ(m)`) were made. Consequently, it is difficult to assess whether the reported 3x-4x improvement over standard UCB is representative of the typical gains one might expect in practice, or if it is an artifact of a parameter regime particularly favorable to MF-UCB.

### Overall Assessment:

This is a well-written paper that introduces an interesting and important new problem setting. The main contribution—the MF-MAB formulation—is significant and likely to inspire follow-up work. The proposed algorithm is intuitive, and the theoretical analysis is solid. The primary weaknesses lie in the clarity and interpretation of the main theoretical result and the limited scope of the empirical validation. Despite these points, the novelty of the problem and the quality of the technical contributions make this a strong paper for acceptance.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Introduces a novel, practical, and well-formalized extension of the multi-armed bandit problem.
*   Proposes an elegant and natural algorithm (MF-UCB) for this new setting.
*   Provides a strong theoretical analysis with both upper and lower regret bounds, showing near-optimality.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The main regret bound is complex and its practical implications are difficult to interpret.
*   The connection to some motivating applications could be more rigorously established.
*   The experimental results lack justification for parameter choices, making it hard to gauge the generality of the performance improvements.
