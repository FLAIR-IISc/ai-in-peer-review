- Introduces a multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit model where lower fidelities are cheaper but introduce fidelity-dependent bias in rewards.  
- Proposes a UCB-style algorithm that inflates confidence intervals to account for both statistical noise and fidelity-induced bias.  
- Provides technically involved regret bounds that are difficult to interpret in their current form.  
- Claims the bounds often improve substantially over always-sampling-at-highest-fidelity UCB in many regimes.  
- Simulation results (synthetic) support performance improvements but are limited and hard to generalize.  
- Questions modeling low fidelity primarily as bias rather than increased variance, though algorithm-selection examples justify the bias model.  
- Finds some motivating examples (e.g., short ad exposure) unconvincing and questions whether cumulative regret is the right objective for algorithm selection.  
- Judges the connection between the formal model and motivating applications weaker than desirable but not disqualifying.  
- Views the main contribution as the problem formulation; the algorithm itself is a straightforward optimism-under-uncertainty extension.  
- Identifies the key weakness as lack of interpretability of the theoretical guarantees and absence of clear corollaries isolating important regimes.  
- Criticizes the empirical section for using synthetic parameters without justification or sensitivity analysis; reported 3–4× gains may depend on specific choices.  
- Recommends clearer, more interpretable bounds, principled empirical design with sensitivity analyses, and stronger application narratives.
