This paper introduces a multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit problem, where observing the outcome of each arm is expensive, but cheaper approximations are available. The authors formalize this as a multi-fidelity bandit, allowing the forecaster to choose an arm at any of *M* fidelities, each with a cost and a potentially biased estimate of the true reward. They propose MF-UCB, a novel upper confidence bound algorithm, and provide theoretical guarantees demonstrating its near-optimality and improved regret compared to naive strategies that ignore the available approximations. The problem addressed is relevant to numerous applications, including online advertising and algorithm selection, where balancing exploration and exploitation with cost constraints is crucial.

The paper builds upon the established foundation of multi-armed bandit research, notably referencing seminal work by Robbins [11] and the UCB algorithm [3]. It positions itself within a growing body of work considering cost-sensitive bandit problems [13, 15], but distinguishes itself by explicitly modeling and analyzing the use of multiple fidelity approximations. The connection to related work in multi-fidelity optimization [7, 10] and reinforcement learning [6] is also acknowledged, highlighting the novelty of applying regret minimization techniques to this setting.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The multi-fidelity bandit formulation is a valuable contribution, providing a framework for addressing a practical trade-off between cost and information.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a theoretical analysis of MF-UCB, including a regret bound and a lower bound, demonstrating a strong understanding of the algorithm's performance.
*   **Potential for Impact:** The proposed algorithm has the potential to be useful in various real-world applications where cheaper approximations of expensive evaluations are available.
*   **Clear Motivation:** The introduction effectively motivates the problem with the example of online advertising, making the research accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Regret Definition:** The adopted regret definition, incorporating cost multiplicatively, feels somewhat unnatural and lacks clear justification beyond the motivating example. An additive cost penalty might be more intuitive and generally applicable.
*   **Assumption 1:** The regularity assumption on the fidelity parameters (zeta_m) appears artificial and limits the generality of the results. The paper doesn’t fully explore the implications of this assumption, or discuss how to handle cases where it doesn't hold. Is there a principled way to select a subset of fidelities satisfying this assumption?
*   **Presentation & Clarity:** The presentation is highly technical, making it challenging to grasp the practical implications of the theoretical results. The contrast between MF-UCB and standard UCB is difficult to interpret due to the complexity of the regret bound and the reliance on the defined sets *K*(m).
*   **Lower Bound Gap:** The gap between the lower bound and the upper bound suggests potential for further optimization of the proposed strategy. This discrepancy merits further investigation.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles a novel and relevant problem with a sound theoretical foundation.
*   The proposed algorithm, MF-UCB, is well-motivated and demonstrates promising performance.
*   The theoretical analysis, including the regret bound and lower bound, provides valuable insights into the algorithm's behavior.
*   The work could serve as a useful benchmark for future research in multi-fidelity bandit problems.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The unnatural regret definition and the restrictive Assumption 1 limit the generality and practical applicability of the results.
*   The highly technical presentation hinders understanding and makes it difficult to assess the significance of the findings.
*   The gap between the upper and lower bounds suggests room for improvement and raises questions about the optimality of MF-UCB.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper presents a worthwhile contribution to the field of multi-armed bandit research. While the technical presentation and certain assumptions limit its immediate impact, the novel problem formulation and theoretical results are promising. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  **Justify the regret definition more thoroughly**, and perhaps explore the implications of an alternative, additive cost penalty.
2.  **Provide a more detailed discussion of Assumption 1**, including its impact on the algorithm's performance and potential strategies for handling cases where it doesn't hold.
3.  **Improve the clarity of the presentation**, focusing on explaining the practical implications of the theoretical results and simplifying the explanation of the regret bound.
4. **Investigate the gap between the upper and lower bounds**, to understand if a tighter bound or an improved algorithm is possible.
