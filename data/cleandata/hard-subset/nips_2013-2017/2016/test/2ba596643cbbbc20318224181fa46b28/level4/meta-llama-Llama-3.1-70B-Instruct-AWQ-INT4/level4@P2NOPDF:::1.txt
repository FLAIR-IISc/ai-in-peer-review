The paper presents a multi-armed bandit problem with a novel 'fidelity' component, where the learner must choose the fidelity level for each arm pull from a finite set. The fidelity level affects the reward, which is a stochastic, iid variable with a mean within a known error margin (zetam) of the true mean. The highest fidelity level corresponds to zero error (zeta = 0), while lower fidelity levels are less expensive but provide less accurate rewards. The authors formulate the problem as maximizing a notion of net reward and propose a new algorithm, MF-UCB, which they claim can outperform UCB operating at high fidelity levels. They also provide a lower bound for the regret of MF-UCB.

In my opinion, the paper explores an interesting and relevant problem in online learning, specifically stochastic bandits, by modeling the tradeoff between information, cost, and reward. This problem has the potential to serve as a useful benchmark for future improvements. However, the paper's technical soundness is overshadowed by a lack of clear explanations for its results and assumptions.

One major concern is the regret definition, which seems unnatural. The authors penalize resource consumption multiplicatively, whereas an additive penalty might be more intuitive. The example of ad display motivates their definition, but it may not be generalizable. It would be interesting to explore the scenario where the cost of fidelity observations is factored into the total reward or regret additively.

The introduction of Assumption 1 appears artificial and opaque, imposing constraints on the fidelity parameters given to the learner. It is unclear why the authors expect this assumption to hold in general learning problems. Furthermore, if the zeta_m's do not decay as required by Assumption 1, it is unclear how to select a subsequence of fidelities to satisfy the assumption and thus the results. Although the authors claim that the assumption is not crucial, it is used in the proofs, and its influence on the algorithm's design and performance is unclear.

The algorithm's design seems tailored to perform well for specific zeta configurations, but the justification for these configurations is lacking. The gap in the lower bound also requires further investigation and possibly a re-design of the proposed strategy. Interpreting the main result (Theorem 2, the regret bound for MF-UCB) and comparing it to UCB is challenging due to the technical presentation. The relative gain/loss of MF-UCB depends on Delta and [[k]], which in turn depend on the complicated sequence of sets \cal{K}^(m). A more explicit explanation would greatly improve the clarity of the results.

In conclusion, while the paper explores an interesting learning model, it rapidly loses clarity in presenting the results. Minor corrections include: line 56 ("near-optimal"), line 126 (unnecessary period before "for all m < M"), and line 179 (using set notation with curly braces to define [[k]]).
