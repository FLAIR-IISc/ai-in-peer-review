The authors present a new framework called Multi-fidelity Multi-armed bandit, which allows arms to be sampled at different levels of fidelity, each associated with a cost. Low-fidelity samples are drawn from a distribution approximating the highest fidelity distribution, offering a trade-off between cost and accuracy. The authors propose an algorithm that adapts to the sequences of available approximations and costs, provide a regret analysis, and establish a lower bound for the problem. Numerical simulations on synthetic problems demonstrate the proposed algorithm's superior performance compared to UCB.

Although the proposed framework is interesting and has the potential to be impactful with further development, the paper in its current state is not suitable for publication. Several issues need to be addressed:

Firstly, the introduction of notations is confusing, with definitions dispersed throughout the text, making them difficult to find. For instance, the definition of $s$ is not immediately apparent, and it is only defined a page after its first use, with minimal subsequent usage.

Secondly, the use of advertising as an example is misleading at times. The authors draw correlations between display time and non-stationarity, which is outside the scope of this paper. It may be beneficial to consider an alternative example that better aligns with the problem.

Thirdly, the regret analysis in this setting is difficult to justify. Regret analysis typically involves maximizing gain in the long run by identifying the arm with the best reward-to-cost ratio. However, in this framework, the optimal policy always favors the arm with the highest mean, regardless of the associated cost. This may obscure the cost of sampling low-fidelity levels when unnecessary.

Fourthly, a sample complexity analysis (or cost complexity analysis) would be valuable in the context of Multi-fidelity Multi-armed bandits. This could be motivated by the testing phase of prototype development for new products, where the goal is to identify the best product with high probability while minimizing the cumulative cost of the testing phase before mass production. Companies often face regulatory, contractual, and facility constraints, necessitating the use of low-fidelity approximations before accessing more advanced and costly tests.

Lastly, some sections of the text are unwieldy and could benefit from streamlining to improve clarity and readability. A thorough review of the paper would help to address these issues and enhance the overall quality of the manuscript.
