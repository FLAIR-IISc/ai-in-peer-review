The paper introduces a “multi-fidelity” variant of the multi-armed bandit problem with K arms, each playable at M levels of fidelity. Lower fidelities are cheaper but can introduce bias in the reward estimate: pulling an arm at fidelity m yields a sample from a distribution whose expectation deviates from the arm’s true mean reward by an amount bounded in a way that depends on m.

The paper analyzes a natural UCB-style algorithm for this setting, in which the confidence interval is widened to account for the uncertainty induced by low-fidelity bias. The resulting theorems provide regret bounds that are cumbersome and difficult to interpret. The authors argue, however, that these bounds will often be substantially better than those obtained by always sampling at the highest fidelity and applying standard UCB to choose arms. A brief section near the end offers simulations to support this claim.

I was initially unsure why the paper models low fidelity as increased bias rather than increased variance, since the latter also seems well motivated. The examples (e.g., algorithm selection for machine learning tasks) largely persuaded me that modeling low fidelity as bias is reasonable. That said, I remain unconvinced by one specific motivation: the claim that showing an ad for a shorter interval yields a biased estimate of its effectiveness over longer intervals. Moreover, in the algorithm-selection setting, it is not clear why minimizing regret would be the algorithm designer’s objective.

Overall, I find the link between the model and the motivating applications disappointingly weak, though not so weak as to be a fatal flaw. The main contribution is the formulation of the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit problem itself. The algorithm is a fairly standard application of the “optimism in the face of uncertainty” principle. As noted above, the performance guarantees are hard to interpret.

The synthetic experiments are also difficult to assess. Appendix C provides detailed parameter settings but does not justify these choices. In many simulations, MF-UCB’s regret improves over UCB by a factor of 3–4. Should we expect this to be representative in practice, or is it driven by the specific parameterization (e.g., the chosen values of ζ^(m))?
