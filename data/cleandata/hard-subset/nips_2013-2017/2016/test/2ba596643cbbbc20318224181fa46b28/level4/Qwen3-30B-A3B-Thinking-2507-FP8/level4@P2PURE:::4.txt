

The authors propose a new framework termed Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandits, in which arms can be sampled at varying fidelity levels, each associated with a distinct cost. Lower-cost, low-fidelity samples are drawn from a distribution approximating the highest-fidelity distribution. They introduce an algorithm adaptive to sequences of available approximations and costs, provide a regret analysis, and establish a problem-specific lower bound. Numerical experiments on synthetic problems demonstrate that the proposed algorithm outperforms UCB. While the framework is potentially impactful if further developed, I find the current version unsuitable for publication.  

1.  Notation definitions are dispersed and confusing, making key symbols difficult to locate. For example, the meaning of $s$ was unclear until it was defined one page after its first appearance (and subsequently used minimally).  
2.  The advertising example is misleading in parts, particularly where correlations between ad display time and non-stationarity are discussed. Non-stationarity is irrelevant to this work; a different example would be preferable.  
3.  The regret analysis is difficult to justify. Regret minimization typically aims to maximize long-term gain by identifying the arm with the best reward-to-cost ratio. However, the optimal policy here is always the arm with the highest mean reward, irrespective of cost. The requirement to sample the optimal arm at low fidelity weakens this policy and obscures the cost of low-fidelity sampling when it is unnecessary.  
4.  A sample complexity analysis (more precisely, a cost complexity analysis) would be valuable in this context. This could be motivated by the testing phase of product prototypes, where the goal is to identify the best product with high probability while minimizing cumulative test costs before mass production. Companies often face constraints (regulatory, contractual, or infrastructural) requiring the use of low-fidelity approximations before advancing to costly tests.  
5.  Certain sections of the text are awkwardly phrased. A thorough revision to improve flow and clarity is recommended.
