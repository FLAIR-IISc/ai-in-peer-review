This paper investigates a multi-fidelity extension to the classical K-armed bandit problem, motivated by scenarios where evaluating an arm’s performance is costly but cheaper approximations are available – for example, testing ad creatives with limited exposure. The authors formalize this as a multi-fidelity bandit, where each arm can be played at varying “fidelities” representing different levels of cost and accuracy. They introduce MF-UCB, a novel algorithm building upon the Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) approach, and provide a theoretical analysis of its regret bound, demonstrating improved performance over naive strategies that ignore fidelity levels. The paper is well-written and clearly presents its contributions.

The core idea—leveraging cheaper approximations to efficiently eliminate suboptimal arms and focus expensive evaluations on promising candidates—is intuitively appealing and addresses a practically relevant problem. The authors successfully formalize this concept and provide a solid theoretical foundation for their proposed algorithm. The regret analysis is a key strength, showing near-optimality under certain conditions and quantifying the benefits of using multi-fidelity evaluations. Complementing this, the inclusion of a lower bound further strengthens the theoretical contributions. The simulation results provide empirical support for the theoretical findings, demonstrating the effectiveness of MF-UCB in synthetic settings.  The presentation is clear, well-organized, and generally easy to follow.

However, a few points could be addressed. While the related work section acknowledges prior work on UCB methods and multi-fidelity optimization, it could benefit from a more detailed discussion of recent advancements in contextual bandits or other related areas that might inform the development of MF-UCB.  Specifically, the connection to active learning with weak and strong labelers [Zhang and Chaudhuri, 2016] is well noted but could be expanded.  Furthermore, the assumption that the lower fidelity means are within a known quantity ζ(m) of the highest fidelity mean might be restrictive in some applications. It would be helpful to discuss the sensitivity of the algorithm’s performance to inaccuracies in estimating these biases or potential approaches for adapting the algorithm when these values are unknown. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The multi-fidelity bandit framework is a novel extension of the classical bandit problem, addressing a practically relevant scenario.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The regret analysis is rigorous and demonstrates the algorithm's near-optimality. The inclusion of a lower bound provides a strong theoretical justification.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the problem, proposed solution, and theoretical results.
*   **Empirical Validation:** Simulations support the theoretical findings, demonstrating the effectiveness of MF-UCB.
*   **Significance:** The approach has the potential to improve the efficiency of online decision-making in various applications, such as online advertising and algorithm selection.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Assumption Limitations:** The assumption of known ζ(m) values might limit the applicability of the algorithm in certain scenarios.
*   **Related Work Depth:** The related work section could be expanded to include a more comprehensive discussion of relevant advancements in bandit algorithms and active learning.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of bandit algorithms. The proposed multi-fidelity framework is novel and well-motivated, and the theoretical and empirical results are compelling. Despite minor limitations, the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised above, particularly regarding the discussion of the ζ(m) assumption and a more thorough review of related work.
