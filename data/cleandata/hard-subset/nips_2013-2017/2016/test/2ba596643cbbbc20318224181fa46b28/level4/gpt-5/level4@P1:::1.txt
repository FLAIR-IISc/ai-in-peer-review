Summary
The paper studies a stochastic K-armed bandit setting where each arm can be evaluated at one of M fidelities. Lower fidelities are cheaper but yield biased observations whose means are within a known tolerance ζ(m) of the highest-fidelity mean (ζ(M) = 0, ζ(1) > ··· > ζ(M)). Each fidelity m incurs cost λ(m), with λ(1) < ··· < λ(M). The authors propose MF-UCB, an upper-confidence-bound strategy that uses low-fidelity plays to eliminate clearly suboptimal arms and reserves expensive high-fidelity plays for promising arms. They define a cost-weighted pseudo-regret that rewards the algorithm according to the highest-fidelity mean µ(M) while charging the cost of the fidelity used. They provide a regret upper bound for MF-UCB, a matching lower bound under certain conditions (showing near-optimality), and simulations showing improvement over a baseline that runs UCB only at the highest fidelity. The work relates to UCB-based bandits, budgeted bandits, multi-fidelity optimization, and weak/strong labelers in active learning, but addresses a distinct exploration–exploitation trade-off due to fidelity-dependent bias and cost.

Strengths
- Interesting and relevant problem: explicitly models the trade-off among information quality, cost, and reward in online learning with biased but cheaper proxies.
- Algorithmic novelty: a natural UCB-style method that adapts to multiple fidelities via fidelity-specific confidence bounds and switching thresholds γ(m).
- Theoretical results: regret bounds that capture how low-fidelity evaluations reduce cost by eliminating many suboptimal arms; a lower bound showing MF-UCB is nearly optimal under certain conditions.
- Empirical evidence: simulations consistently show MF-UCB outperforms a highest-fidelity UCB baseline, with steeper cost savings where many arms are clearly suboptimal.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Regret definition: The adopted pseudo-regret multiplies the highest-fidelity mean µ(M) by the total cost spent (and assigns zero reward to unused budget), while charging the actual fidelity costs. While the advertising example motivates this, an additive cost penalty in the objective (e.g., total reward minus total cost) seems at least as natural in many applications. It would be valuable to analyze or at least discuss how the results (algorithm and bounds) change under an additive-cost regret model, which arguably corresponds to a more general utility framework.
- Assumption 1 (ζ(m) decay): This regularity condition on the ζ(m) sequence feels opaque and tailored to the analysis. Why should this hold in general? More importantly, if the ζ(m) do not decay as required, is there a principled way to select a subsequence of fidelities to satisfy the assumption (e.g., by skipping near-redundant fidelities where λ(m+1) ≈ λ(m) and ζ(m+1) ≈ ζ(m))? A constructive subsequence selection procedure would substantially strengthen the contribution and clarify the algorithm’s robustness.
- Dependence on ζ: The algorithm and thresholds γ(m) critically depend on the ζ(m) values being known and appropriately scaled. The paper states Assumption 1 is not crucial, yet it is used in the proofs and appears to influence both design and performance. A clearer discussion of sensitivity to misspecified ζ(m) and to slow decay would help.
- Lower bound gap: The gap for arms in K(m)7 indicates MF-UCB can spend at higher fidelities even when it would be cheaper to distinguish suboptimality at a lower fidelity. This suggests that revisiting the switching criterion (perhaps by partial cost-rate comparisons) could reduce the gap; at minimum, more discussion of whether a redesign could close it would be useful.
- Clarity of main results: The interpretation of Theorem 2 and its comparison to UCB are hard to parse due to the layered dependence on Δ and the partition K(m). It would help to:
  - Explicitly state that the UCB comparator runs at the highest fidelity only.
  - Provide a small illustrative example with concrete ζ, λ, and means showing how K(m) partitions are formed and how cost is saved relative to UCB.
  - Intuitively explain why most cost for arm k is incurred at fidelity JkK and how this leads to savings λ(JkK)/λ(M) when Δ(JkK)k is not much larger than Δ(M)k.

Quality, clarity, originality, significance
- Quality: The paper appears technically sound; bounds and proof structure are reasonable. However, some key assumptions and choices (regret definition, Assumption 1, switching rule) need clearer justification.
- Clarity: The presentation becomes dense quickly. More intuition, a worked example for K(m), and an explicit comparison point to highest-fidelity UCB would aid readers.
- Originality: The multi-fidelity bandit formalization with biased lower-fidelity observations and costs is novel relative to budgeted bandits and prior multi-fidelity optimization works that lack regret analysis.
- Significance: The framework is practically relevant and could serve as a benchmark for future methods; the near-optimality result is valuable, though the lower-bound gap highlights room for improvement.

Pros
- Addresses a timely and practical setting with multiple biased, cheaper proxies.
- Clear algorithmic idea with nontrivial analysis and near-optimality results.
- Demonstrated empirical advantage over a natural baseline.

Cons
- Regret notion may be application-specific; additive-cost formulations are not addressed.
- Assumption 1 and reliance on known ζ(m) reduce generality; robustness is unclear.
- Clarity issues hinder accessibility of the main theorems and comparisons.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Clarify the comparator: confirm that the UCB baseline uses only the highest fidelity.
- Discuss an additive-cost regret formulation and whether MF-UCB or a variant would extend with similar guarantees.
- Provide a method to prune or subsample fidelities to satisfy Assumption 1 when ζ(m) decays slowly; analyze performance loss.
- Add a small, concrete example illustrating the K(m) partitioning and the resulting savings.
- Comment on robustness to misspecified ζ(m) and to violations of Assumption 1; include a sensitivity experiment.
- Consider whether a modified switching rule could better align with the lower bound for K(m)7 arms.
