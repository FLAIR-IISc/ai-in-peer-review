Summary and contributions:
The paper introduces a new framework for multi-fidelity multi-armed bandits, where each arm can be sampled at different fidelity levels with associated costs. Lower-fidelity samples are cheaper but come from distributions that approximate the highest-fidelity distribution. The authors propose an algorithm that adapts to sequences of available approximations and costs, provide a regret analysis together with a lower bound, and present numerical simulations on synthetic problems showing that the proposed algorithm outperforms UCB. The framework is interesting and, if further developed, could be impactful.

However, in its current form I do not find the paper suitable for publication.

Major concerns:
1) Notation and presentation:
- The introduction and use of notation are confusing and dispersed, making definitions hard to locate. For example, I could not find the definition of s where it is first used; after the rebuttal, it appears that s is defined a page after its first occurrence and is then barely used in the rest of the paper. This significantly hinders readability and verification of the results.

2) Misleading example and scope:
- The advertising example is, at several points, misleading. The paper repeatedly draws connections between display time for ads and non-stationarity. Non-stationarity is out of scope for this paper and these references distract from the core setting. It would be better to use a different motivating example that aligns more closely with the assumptions of the model (see point 4).

3) Regret formulation and interpretation:
- I find it hard to justify the chosen regret analysis in this setting. Regret in bandit problems typically reflects the goal of maximizing long-run gain, which in cost-sensitive settings suggests benchmarking against the best reward-to-cost ratio. In the present formulation, however, the optimal policy is taken to be the arm with the highest mean reward, regardless of its sampling cost. Moreover, the analysis effectively weakens this optimal policy by imposing the obligation to sample the best arm at lower fidelity levels. This can obscure the true cost of unnecessary low-fidelity samples and may not capture the decision-making trade-offs one would expect in a cost-aware framework.

4) Alternative evaluation: cost/sample complexity:
- A cost complexity (sample complexity) analysis would be particularly appropriate for multi-fidelity multi-armed bandits. A natural motivation comes from prototype testing in product development, where the goal is to identify, with high probability, the best product while minimizing the total testing cost before mass production. Because companies are constrained by regulations, contracts, and existing facilities, they may be required to use low-fidelity approximations before accessing advanced and costly tests. An analysis along these lines would better reflect the practical value of multi-fidelity sampling.

5) Writing quality:
- Several parts of the text are unwieldy. A careful editing pass to streamline the exposition, consolidate notation, and improve the flow would substantially improve clarity.

Overall assessment:
- The problem setting is interesting and the results (algorithm, regret bound, lower bound, and simulations showing improvements over UCB on synthetic tasks) are promising. Nevertheless, issues with notation and presentation, a potentially misaligned regret benchmark for a cost-sensitive setting, the confusing use of a non-stationary advertising example, and the absence of a cost complexity perspective lead me to recommend rejection in the paper’s current form. I encourage the authors to (i) tighten and consolidate the notation, (ii) replace the advertising example with one consistent with stationarity (e.g., prototype testing), (iii) reconsider the regret benchmark to account explicitly for costs or add a complementary cost complexity analysis, and (iv) revise the writing for clarity and concision.
