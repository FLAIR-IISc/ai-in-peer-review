This paper studies stochastic multi-armed bandits where the learner also selects a fidelity level from a finite set when pulling an arm. Pulling arm k at fidelity m yields i.i.d. rewards whose mean lies within ζm of the arm’s true mean; the ζm values are known beforehand, with the highest fidelity satisfying ζ = 0. Lower fidelities are cheaper than higher ones. The goal is to adaptively choose arms and fidelities to maximize a notion of net reward that trades off accuracy and cost. The authors propose MF-UCB, provide regret guarantees and a lower bound, and argue that it can outperform running standard UCB solely at the highest fidelity.

The problem is interesting and relevant: it captures a central trade-off among information, cost, and reward in online learning. The work could serve as a benchmark for future research. The technical development appears sound. However, the paper suffers from clarity issues regarding assumptions, modeling choices, and interpretation of the results.

- Regret definition. The regret notion penalizes resource consumption multiplicatively rather than additively. This choice feels nonstandard. While the ad-display example motivates it, the formulation may not generalize. An additive-cost formulation (i.e., subtracting incurred costs from rewards in the cumulative objective) seems more natural in many settings. How would the analysis and algorithm change under additive costs? Would MF-UCB or its regret bounds extend?

- Assumption 1. The constraints imposed on the fidelity parameters ζm appear opaque and somewhat artificial. Why should such conditions hold in general applications? If the ζm sequence does not decay as required, can one systematically select a subsequence of fidelities to satisfy the assumption while preserving performance guarantees? Clear guidance here would significantly strengthen the message. Although the text claims the assumption is not crucial, it is used in the proofs, and it is unclear whether it critically shapes the algorithm’s design and performance. The method seems tailored to particular ζ configurations; the paper should justify why these are the “right” regimes to optimize for.

- Lower bound gap. The gap between the upper and lower bounds merits deeper discussion. Can the algorithm be refined to close the gap, or is the gap intrinsic? Clarifying whether the lower bound is instance- or class-dependent, and how it scales with fidelity costs and ζm, would help.

- Interpreting Theorem 2. The main bound for MF-UCB is difficult to parse and to compare against UCB. Please make explicit which UCB baseline is used (e.g., UCB operating only at the highest fidelity). The relative advantage of MF-UCB depends on Δ and [[k]], which in turn depend on the sequences of sets K^(m). The paper would benefit from:
  - A high-level explanation of how K^(m) is constructed and how it influences exploration.
  - Intuition for Δ and [[k]] in simple canonical instances (e.g., two fidelities with geometric ζ decay; uniform costs; a few illustrative arm gaps).
  - A concise statement of when MF-UCB is provably better, ties, or worse than high-fidelity UCB.

Overall, the paper tackles a compelling model with solid technical ingredients, but the presentation needs clearer motivation for the modeling choices, more transparent assumptions, and more accessible interpretation of the results. Adding intuition, examples, and a brief discussion of the additive-cost variant would make the contribution more broadly applicable and easier to evaluate.
