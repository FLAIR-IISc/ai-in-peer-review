This paper studies multi-armed bandits in which the learner must also choose a fidelity level (from a finite set) for each arm pull. Pulling an arm at fidelity m yields an i.i.d. stochastic reward with mean within zeta_m of the arm’s true mean, where the zeta_m parameters are known a priori, and the highest fidelity satisfies zeta = 0. Lower fidelities are cheaper than higher fidelities. The authors formalize an objective of maximizing a notion of net reward under these fidelity-dependent costs, propose a new algorithm for this setting, and argue that it can outperform UCB when UCB is run only at the highest fidelity. They also prove a lower bound on the regret of their proposed algorithm.

The problem is interesting and relevant: it explicitly models the trade-off among information, cost, and reward—choosing between low-information/low-cost and high-information/high-cost observations—in online learning, specifically stochastic bandits. In this sense, the work could serve as a useful benchmark to improve upon. While the paper appears technically solid, a key shortcoming is the lack of clear explanation of the results and assumptions.

First, the regret definition seems unnatural from at least one perspective: why penalize resource consumption (cost) multiplicatively rather than additively? The ad-display example motivates the authors’ choice, but it is not obviously the most general. It would be valuable to know what happens when the cost of fidelity is incorporated additively into the total reward (or regret), which arguably is a more natural formulation.

Second, Assumption 1 appears artificial and opaque, imposing constraints on the fidelity parameters available to the learner. Why should one expect this to hold in general learning problems? More importantly, if the zeta_m’s do not decay as quickly as required by Assumption 1, is there a principled way to select a subsequence of fidelities that satisfies the assumption and thereby preserves the results? Such a construction would significantly strengthen the paper’s message. Although the authors state that the assumption is not crucial, it is used in the proofs; my main concern is whether this assumption critically shapes the algorithm’s design and performance. The algorithm seems tailored to particular zeta configurations, but the paper does not justify why these are the “right” sequences to target.

Relatedly, the gap in the lower bound warrants further investigation and may even call for a redesign of the proposed strategy.

Finally, interpreting the main result (Theorem 2, the regret bound for MF-UCB) and contrasting it with UCB (presumably at the highest fidelity, though this is not stated explicitly) is difficult due to the technical presentation. The relative gain or loss of MF-UCB appears to depend on Delta and [[k]], which themselves depend on the complicated sequence of sets K^(m). A more explicit, high-level explanation and a clearer comparison to standard UCB would substantially strengthen the exposition.

Overall, the paper starts from an appealing and timely modeling choice but loses clarity when presenting and interpreting its main results.
