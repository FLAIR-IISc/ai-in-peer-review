- Paper introduces multi-fidelity stochastic K-armed bandits with biased, cheaper fidelities and a capital-aware regret measuring high-fidelity reward minus sampling cost.  
- Each arm has M fidelities with known bias bounds ζ(m) and costs λ(m); low fidelities give biased observations within ζ(m) of high-fidelity mean.  
- Proposes MF-UCB: bias-aware UCB that adds ζ(m) to low-fidelity confidence bounds and adaptively selects fidelity via thresholds γ(m).  
- Analysis shows MF-UCB concentrates low-fidelity exploration to eliminate clearly suboptimal arms and reserves high-fidelity plays for near-optimal arms.  
- Derived regret bound improves over naive high-fidelity UCB by a factor depending on cost ratios and number of arms filtered at low fidelities.  
- Complementary lower bound shows near-optimality of MF-UCB, with a small gap for arms where low-fidelity signals are marginally informative.  
- Simulations on synthetic problems consistently demonstrate improvements over standard UCB.  
- Novelty: costed, biased, multi-fidelity regret formulation and MF-UCB’s analysis appear new relative to prior UCB, budgeted bandits, and multi-fidelity optimization work.  
- Strength: clear formalization of a realistic bandit variant with explicit costs and biased proxies.  
- Strength: elegant, conservative algorithm design using bias-aware optimism and theoretically motivated fidelity thresholds.  
- Strength: solid theory—upper bound that recovers UCB behavior on hard arms and a tight lower bound with explained gap.  
- Strength: good intuition via arm partition K(1)…K(M) and analysis matching simulation behavior.  
- Weakness: assumes known bias bounds ζ(m); lacks discussion of misspecification and adaptive/data-driven estimation.  
- Weakness: reliance on Regularity Assumption 1; needs guidance on practical conditions (e.g., bias decay vs. ψ).  
- Weakness: concentration assumptions and ψ (and knowledge of ν, ψ) need clearer practical guidance and tuning advice beyond ρ.  
- Weakness: practical impact of the lower-bound gap for K(m)7 is unclear; suggest quantification and heuristics (e.g., occasional low-fidelity rechecks).  
- Weakness: regret notion ties reward to high-fidelity only; should discuss variants where low-fidelity plays have different reward semantics.  
- Weakness: experiments limited—compare only to high-fidelity UCB; needs stronger baselines, sensitivity analyses, ablations, larger K, and semi-synthetic/real tasks.  
- Evaluation: technically sound with clear proofs; some assumptions limit deployability but are transparent for a first formal treatment.  
- Clarity: generally well written; suggestions include defining regret oracle and qt earlier, more intuition for γ(m), restating partition in proofs, and a notation table.  
- Originality: high—addresses an underexplored, realistic setting with novel formalization and algorithm.  
- Significance: high—captures many practical scenarios and suggests meaningful cost savings; needs more empirical validation and robustness discussion.  
- Pros for acceptance: novel problem, principled algorithm with strong guarantees, clear insights, and supporting empirical evidence.  
- Cons against acceptance: dependence on known ζ(m) and regularity, limited empirical evaluation, and open lower-bound gap for some arms.  
- Recommendation: lean toward acceptance pending minor revisions to expand empirical validation and robustness discussion about ζ(m) misspecification.
