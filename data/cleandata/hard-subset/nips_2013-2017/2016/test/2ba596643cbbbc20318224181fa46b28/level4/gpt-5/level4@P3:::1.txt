Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies a stochastic K-armed bandit setting where each arm can be evaluated at one of M fidelities. Lower fidelities are cheaper but yield biased, noisy observations whose expectations are within a known deviation ζ(m) of the highest-fidelity mean (with ζ(1) > … > ζ(M) = 0), and each fidelity m has a known cost λ(m) with λ(1) < … < λ(M). The goal is to design an anytime strategy that, under a fixed total budget Λ, selects arm–fidelity pairs to minimize a cost-weighted pseudo-regret defined against the highest-fidelity rewards.

The authors propose MF-UCB, an upper-confidence-bound algorithm that maintains fidelity-specific UCBs inflated by ζ(m) and picks the arm with the smallest high-probability upper bound on the unknown highest-fidelity mean. It adaptively chooses the fidelity via thresholds γ(m) that balance the information obtainable at fidelity m against its cost and the bias ζ(m). The paper gives a regret upper bound showing that MF-UCB concentrates most expensive (high-fidelity) pulls on a small set of near-optimal arms, while using low fidelities to eliminate clearly suboptimal arms. A lower bound shows near-optimality on a subset of instances and identifies a remaining gap for cases where low-fidelity means are only slightly informative.

This work builds on UCB-style methods (Auer et al.; Audibert et al.; Bubeck & Cesa-Bianchi) and is related to budgeted/bandits-with-costs (Tran-Thanh et al.; Xia et al.), weak/strong labelers in active learning (Zhang & Chaudhuri), and multi-fidelity optimization in engineering and RL. Relative to these, the paper formalizes a multi-fidelity bandit with biased approximations and provides algorithmic and theoretical analysis tailored to this setting.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The algorithm design is principled, and the analysis (upper bound, instance-dependent and cost-aware; and a complementary lower bound) appears careful. The partition of arms into sets K(m) clarifies where cost is spent. The residual gap in the lower bound is acknowledged and explained.
- Assumptions: The concentration assumption is standard. Assumption 1 (on the decay of ζ(m)) plays a notable role in the cleanest comparison to UCB; the paper states it is not necessary for all parts, but its interpretability and practical plausibility could be explained more clearly (see below).
- Empirics: Synthetic experiments are consistent with the theory but are limited in scope; more ablations would strengthen the empirical case.

Clarity
The setting is interesting and the main ideas are sound, but presentation could be clearer:
- The regret definition and oracle should be contrasted explicitly with alternatives (e.g., additive cost penalties) and with standard budgeted/bandits-with-costs objectives.
- The construction and interpretation of the sets K(m) could be explained via an extended two- or three-fidelity example, with a small table mapping arms to the fidelity at which they are eliminated.
- The baseline for comparison should be stated precisely as “UCB at the highest fidelity only.”
- The role of Assumption 1 should be highlighted where it is used, and the consequences if it fails should be discussed concretely.

Originality
The formalization of biased, multi-fidelity observations within bandits, the MF-UCB design with ζ-adjusted UCBs and fidelity-thresholding, and the regret analysis are, to my knowledge, novel and timely. The lower-bound discussion identifies a meaningful open issue.

Significance
The setting is practically relevant (advertising, algorithm selection, simulation-to-real), and the results suggest tangible gains when cheap but informative approximations exist. The work can serve as a foundation for subsequent extensions (e.g., contextual, non-stationary, or GP-based, as noted by the authors).

Strengths
- Well-motivated multi-fidelity bandit formalism with biased approximations and costs.
- Simple, scalable algorithm with intuitive fidelity selection via γ(m).
- Instance- and cost-dependent regret bound showing savings from low-fidelity elimination.
- Lower bound identifying near-optimality on a broad class of instances.
- Clear articulation of when multi-fidelity helps (many clearly suboptimal arms) and when it cannot (near-optimal arms).

Weaknesses and questions
- Regret definition: The choice R(Λ) = Λµ* − Σt λ(mt) q_t, with q_t pegged to the highest-fidelity mean of the arm played, is reasonable for some applications (e.g., long-term ad revenue). However, it departs from additive cost-penalty objectives more common in budgeted bandits. It would help to (i) justify this choice more broadly, (ii) discuss how the analysis would change under an additive penalty or under an objective that values low-fidelity outcomes directly, and (iii) connect more explicitly to prior “cost-aware” bandit formulations.
- Assumption 1: This constraint on ζ(m) (fast decay relative to ψ) deserves more intuition. Are there natural criteria on the pair (ζ, λ) under which the assumption is expected to hold? If it fails, can one systematically prune or coarsen fidelities (e.g., discard adjacent levels with poor cost-to-bias improvement) to restore the condition without hurting performance? A simple “subsequence selection” heuristic would strengthen practicality.
- Known ζ(m): The algorithm and analysis assume known bias envelopes ζ(m). In many applications these are estimated. How sensitive is MF-UCB to misspecification, and can it be made robust (e.g., by inflating ζ adaptively)?
- Lower-bound gap: The identified gap for arms with small positive low-fidelity gaps suggests possible improvements via selective early elimination when low-fidelity distinctions are cheap. Even a heuristic variant or a theoretical discussion of when such switching is provably safe would be valuable.
- Empirics: Consider including (i) sensitivity to (ζ, λ), (ii) comparisons to a two-phase baseline (low-fidelity screening followed by high-fidelity UCB), and (iii) non-sub-Gaussian noise.

Suggestions to improve clarity and presentation
- Provide a worked two-fidelity example tracing K(1), K(2), and the associated cost terms in the regret bound.
- State explicitly that the comparison baseline is “UCB using only the highest fidelity.”
- Add a brief paragraph interpreting Theorem 2 in plain language, translating the partition-based bound into: cost at low fidelities is O(λ(m) log n / ψ(·)) only for arms eliminated at fidelity m; only near-optimal arms are played expensively.
- Explain Assumption 1 with a rule-of-thumb: e.g., keep only fidelities where ψ(ζ(m+1)) ≥ Σi≤m ψ(ζ(i)) to avoid dominated levels, and note that geometric decay of ζ suffices for sub-Gaussian noise.
- Discuss robustness to ζ misestimation.
- Minor notation: define JkK when first used; prefer curly braces for set definitions; ensure consistent use of ∆(m) and K(m).

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Addresses a practically important and underexplored variant of bandits.
- Conceptually clean algorithm; analysis yields clear intuition about when/why MF helps.
- Provides both upper and lower bounds and identifies open theoretical questions.
- Could serve as a benchmark for multi-fidelity online decision-making.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Regret notion and assumptions may limit applicability; stronger discussion and experiments would help.
- Presentation is dense; key ideas and comparisons could be clearer.
- Lower-bound gap suggests that MF-UCB may be suboptimal on some natural instances, with no remedy proposed.

Minor comments/typos
- l.56: “near-optimal” → “near optimal” (or ensure consistent hyphenation).
- l.126: Remove the period before “for all m < M.”
- l.179: Use curly braces to define JkK and related sets; define JkK on first use.

Overall, the paper proposes an interesting and potentially impactful framework and algorithm. With clearer exposition, a more thorough justification of the regret objective and Assumption 1, and modest additional experiments, it would make a strong contribution.
