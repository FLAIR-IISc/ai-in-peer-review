The paper studies a stochastic K-armed bandit setting in which the learner, at each round, selects both an arm and one of several fidelity levels. Pulling an arm at fidelity m yields an i.i.d. stochastic reward whose mean is within zeta_m of the true (highest-fidelity) mean; the zeta_m values are known in advance and decrease with fidelity, with zeta_M = 0. Lower fidelities are cheaper than higher ones. The authors formalize a budgeted objective that trades off information and cost, propose MF-UCB (a UCB-style algorithm adapted to multiple fidelities), and argue it can outperform a baseline that runs UCB only at the top fidelity. They also provide a regret lower bound. The problem is interesting and timely: it explicitly models the information–cost–reward trade-offs in online learning (stochastic bandits) and could serve as a useful benchmark.

While the work appears technically sound, a major limitation is insufficient explanation of several key choices and results. The adopted regret notion feels somewhat unnatural from at least one perspective: why couple reward with resource expenditure multiplicatively rather than penalizing cost additively? The ad-display example motivates the authors’ definition, but it may not be the most general formulation. It would be valuable to clarify what changes when the cost of fidelity is incorporated additively into total reward (or regret), which arguably is a more standard setup.

Assumption 1 also seems opaque and somewhat ad hoc, as it imposes constraints on the fidelity parameters available to the learner. Why should one expect this to hold in general? More importantly, if the zeta_m sequence does not decay at the prescribed rate, is there a principled way to select a subsequence of fidelities that satisfies the assumption, thereby recovering the guarantees? Although the authors state the assumption is not crucial, it appears in the proofs, and it is unclear to what extent it critically shapes the algorithm’s design and performance. The current scheme seems tuned to certain zeta configurations, but the justification that these are the “right” sequences is not provided. Relatedly, the gap in the lower bound suggests more investigation is needed, potentially motivating a refinement of the strategy.

Interpreting the main result (Theorem 2) and comparing it against UCB (presumably run only at the highest fidelity, though this is not stated explicitly in the discussion) is difficult because of the technical presentation. The advantage or disadvantage of MF-UCB depends on Delta and [[k]], which in turn depend on the nested sets K^(m). A more direct, reader-friendly explanation of when and by how much MF-UCB improves over the high-fidelity-only UCB would strengthen the paper.

Overall, the paper begins with a compelling model but quickly loses clarity in presenting and interpreting its theoretical results.
