This paper introduces a novel multi-armed bandit setting where a learner can play an arm at $M$ different fidelities. Each successive fidelity provides a better approximation of the rewards, but at a higher cost. A strategy that effectively utilizes lower and cheaper fidelities to eliminate sub-optimal arms, while reserving higher and more expensive fidelities for a small subset of good arms, may outperform the standard UCB algorithm, which neglects these fidelity characteristics. This setting has potential applications in online advertising, where displaying ads for a short period can approximate long-term clicks while considering cost constraints.

The authors propose a new regret definition, develop an algorithm, and provide theoretical analysis, including a regret lower bound. They also conduct simulations to support their claims. The analysis is novel and contributes to the field. However, a critical assumption is that the deviations of the $m$th fidelity mean from the highest fidelity mean, denoted as $\varepsilon^{(m)}$, are known in advance. It is unclear how to obtain these values in practice, such as in the online advertising application. Relaxing this assumption would strengthen the results.
