

The paper introduces a novel multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit framework where each arm can be sampled at multiple fidelity levels with corresponding costs. The authors develop MF-UCB, an algorithm that adaptively leverages the sequence of available approximations and costs, and provide a theoretical regret analysis along with a matching lower bound. The paper demonstrates empirically that MF-UCB outperforms standard UCB algorithms on synthetic problems.

This work extends the classical multi-armed bandit literature [3, 9, 11] by introducing a new dimension to the problem: multiple fidelity levels for each arm. While related work has explored multi-fidelity methods for optimization [7, 10], this paper is the first to formally analyze regret in this setting with theoretical guarantees. The authors extend UCB strategies to incorporate fidelity information, which represents a significant contribution to the bandit literature.

Strengths:
1. The paper formalizes a novel problem setting with clear practical relevance in domains like online advertising and algorithm selection.
2. The MF-UCB algorithm is well-motivated and effectively leverages lower fidelity information to reduce regret.
3. The theoretical analysis is comprehensive, providing both an upper bound on regret and a matching lower bound.
4. The experimental results support the theoretical claims.

Weaknesses:
1. The notation is confusingly introduced and scattered throughout the paper. For example, the variable 's' is first used without definition and is only defined one page later, where it's barely used for the remainder of the paper.
2. The advertising example is problematic because it introduces non-stationarity (e.g., "displaying an ad for longer intervals changes user behavior"), which is not relevant to the bandit problem being studied. The authors should consider a different example that doesn't introduce irrelevant complications.
3. The regret analysis appears to misunderstand the core problem. The optimal policy should select the arm with the highest reward-to-cost ratio, not just the highest reward. The paper's regret definition fixes the optimal arm selection (based on highest fidelity reward) without properly accounting for the cost of sampling, which weakens the analysis.
4. The paper could benefit from a cost complexity analysis (rather than just sample complexity), which would be more relevant to the multi-fidelity setting. This is particularly important for applications like product development where the goal is to identify the best product with minimal testing cost before mass production.
5. The paper contains some awkward phrasing and could benefit from a more streamlined presentation.

The paper's core contribution is promising, but the current presentation has significant issues that need to be addressed. The theoretical results are interesting but the analysis of the regret definition is problematic, and the example used to motivate the problem introduces irrelevant complications. The notation issues also make the paper difficult to follow.

Pro:
- Novel problem formulation
- Theoretical guarantees with upper and lower bounds
- Empirical results showing improvement over UCB

Con:
- Confusing notation that's scattered throughout the paper
- Misleading advertising example that introduces non-stationarity
- Flawed regret analysis that doesn't properly account for cost in the optimal policy
- Lacks cost complexity analysis that would better motivate the problem
- Awkward phrasing that makes the paper difficult to follow

Given these issues, I recommend rejection. The paper has a promising idea but the current presentation and analysis contain significant flaws that undermine the contribution. The authors would need to substantially revise the paper to address these concerns before it could be considered for publication.
