- Addresses a multi-armed bandit variant where each arm pull has a chosen fidelity affecting mean reward and cost, with error margins decreasing with fidelity.
- Proposes a new algorithm that purportedly balances information, cost, and reward, and provides a regret lower bound.
- The problem formulation is interesting and practically relevant as a benchmark for cheap low-information vs expensive high-information pulls.
- Technical soundness is undermined by unclear explanations of results and assumptions.
- The regret definition is counterintuitive: it penalizes resource consumption multiplicatively rather than additively, and alternative additive formulations should be explored.
- Assumption 1 appears artificial and unjustified, especially the constraints on fidelity parameters and their decay.
- It is unclear how to choose a subsequence of fidelities to satisfy Assumption 1 when error margins do not decay as required.
- Although claimed non-crucial, Assumption 1 is used in proofs, leaving its impact on algorithm design and performance ambiguous.
- The algorithm seems tailored to specific error-margin configurations without justification for those configurations.
- The gap in the provided lower bound needs further investigation and may require redesigning the proposed strategy.
- Theorem 2 is technically dense and hard to interpret; a clearer, more explicit explanation is needed.
- The algorithm’s relative gain or loss versus UCB depends on complex parameters/sequences, making practical advantages unclear.
- Overall: interesting model but presentation lacks clarity; minor corrections needed (typos on lines 56, 126, and 179).
