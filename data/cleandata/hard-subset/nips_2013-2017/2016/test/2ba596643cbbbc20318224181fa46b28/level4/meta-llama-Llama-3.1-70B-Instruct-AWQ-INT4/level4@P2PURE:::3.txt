The paper introduces a multi-fidelity version of the multi-armed bandit problem, where each of the K arms can be played at M different levels of fidelity. Playing at a lower fidelity is less costly but results in a potentially greater bias in estimating the arm's reward, as the expected value of the drawn sample deviates from the arm's true expected reward by an amount whose upper bound depends on the fidelity. The authors analyze a natural UCB-based algorithm for this problem, which compensates for the uncertainty stemming from the bias of sampling at low fidelity by increasing the width of the confidence interval. The analysis is summarized in theorems with complex regret bounds that are difficult to interpret. However, the paper argues that these regret bounds are often significantly better than those attained by always playing arms at the highest fidelity and using the standard UCB algorithm to select which arm to play. Simulation results presented in a brief section near the end of the paper support this claim.

Initially, I found it puzzling that the paper models lower fidelity as introducing more bias rather than more variance, as the latter interpretation also seems well-motivated. However, the motivating examples provided, such as algorithm selection for machine learning problems, convinced me that modeling low fidelity as greater bias is well-motivated. Nevertheless, I remain unconvinced by one of the motivations, specifically the contention that displaying an ad for a shorter time interval yields a biased estimate of the ad's effectiveness over longer intervals. Furthermore, in the example of algorithm selection for machine learning, it is unclear why minimizing regret would be the goal of an algorithm designer in that application. In summary, I found the connection between the model and the motivating applications to be somewhat weak, although not weak enough to be a disqualifying flaw.

The main innovation in the paper appears to be the formulation of the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit problem itself. The proposed algorithm is a fairly typical application of the "optimism in the face of uncertainty" principle. However, the performance guarantee, as noted earlier, is disappointingly hard to interpret. I also found the simulation results on synthetic problems to be difficult to assess. Although Appendix C provides the set of parameter values used in the simulations, it does not attempt to justify these choices. The results suggest that the regret of MF-UCB improves upon that of UCB by a factor of 3 to 4 in many simulations, but it is unclear whether this is a typical outcome in practice or an artifact of the chosen parameter values, such as the values of ζ(m).
