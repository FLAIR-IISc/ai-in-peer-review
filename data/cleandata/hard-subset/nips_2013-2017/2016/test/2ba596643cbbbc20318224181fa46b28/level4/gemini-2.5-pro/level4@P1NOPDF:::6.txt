This paper introduces and studies a new multi-armed bandit setting in which a learner can play any arm at one of $M$ different fidelities. Each successive fidelity provides a better approximation of the true reward, but at a progressively higher cost. The central idea is that an effective strategy can leverage the cheaper, lower fidelities to quickly eliminate suboptimal arms, while reserving the more expensive, higher fidelities for a small subset of promising candidates. Such an approach has the potential to outperform standard algorithms like UCB, which do not account for this multi-fidelity structure. The setting is well-motivated by applications such as online advertising, where one might display an ad for a short period (a low-fidelity, low-cost action) to approximate its long-term click-through rate before committing to a longer, more expensive campaign.

To address this problem, the authors propose a new regret definition tailored to the multi-fidelity setting. They introduce an algorithm accompanied by a novel theoretical analysis, which includes both upper and lower regret bounds. The claims are validated through simulations.

My main concern relates to a key assumption in the theoretical analysis: that $\varepsilon^{(m)}$, the deviation of the $m$-th fidelity's mean reward from that of the highest fidelity, is known in advance. This raises a practical question: how would these values be determined in a real-world application like the one described for online advertising? This assumption appears quite strong and may limit the algorithm's direct applicability. It would strengthen the paper if the authors could discuss the possibility of relaxing this requirement.
