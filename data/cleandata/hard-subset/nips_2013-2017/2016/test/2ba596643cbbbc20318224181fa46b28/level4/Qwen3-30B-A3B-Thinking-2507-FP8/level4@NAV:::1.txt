

The paper models multi-armed bandit problems incorporating a 'fidelity' choice (from a finite set) for each pulled arm. An arm pulled at fidelity level \(m\) yields a stochastic iid reward with mean within \(\zeta_m\) of the true mean, where \(\zeta_m\) (error parameters) are known a priori, and the highest fidelity level satisfies \(\zeta = 0\). Lower fidelity pulls incur lower cost than higher fidelity pulls. The authors formulate the sequential arm-pulling problem to maximize net reward, propose a novel algorithm (MF-UCB), and claim it outperforms UCB operating only at high fidelity. They also establish a lower bound on the regret of their algorithm. The study addresses an interesting and relevant tradeoff between information, cost, and reward (low-information/cheap vs. high-information/expensive) in stochastic bandits, potentially serving as a useful benchmark. While technically sound, the paper suffers from insufficient explanation of results and assumptions. The adopted regret definition—penalizing cost multiplicatively rather than additively—appears unnatural; additive cost penalties are arguably more general than the ad-display motivation provided. The necessity of Assumption 1 (constraining fidelity parameters) is unclear; why would this hold generally? If \(\zeta_m\) decays too slowly to satisfy Assumption 1, a method to select a valid fidelity subsequence (to preserve results) would strengthen the paper. Though the authors state Assumption 1 is not crucial, it appears essential in proofs, raising concern about whether it critically shapes the algorithm's design and performance. The justification for selecting the specific \(\zeta_m\) sequences used in the algorithm is missing. The lower bound gap requires further investigation, possibly necessitating a strategy redesign. The main result (Theorem 2, MF-UCB's regret bound) is difficult to interpret relative to UCB (at highest fidelity? not explicitly stated), as the relative performance depends on complex parameters \(\Delta\) and \(\mathcal{K}^{(m)}\); a clearer exposition would significantly aid the argument. Overall, the paper initiates a promising model but loses clarity in presenting results. Minor typos: Line 56: "near-optimal"; Line 126: unnecessary period before "for all \(m < M\)"; Line 179: use curly braces for set notation defining \(\mathcal{K}\).
