This paper introduces and analyzes a novel multi-armed bandit problem featuring multiple fidelities for reward observation. The core idea is that each arm can be played at varying fidelities, with higher fidelities providing more accurate reward estimates at a greater cost. The authors argue, and intuitively, that an algorithm leveraging lower fidelities for initial arm elimination and reserving higher fidelities for promising arms can outperform standard approaches like UCB, which do not account for these fidelity characteristics. A compelling application is highlighted in online advertising, where initial ad displays can serve as low-cost approximations of long-term click-through rates.  

The authors define a specific regret measure tailored to this setting, propose an algorithm, and provide a theoretical analysis including a regret lower bound, complemented by simulation results. The analysis appears to be a novel contribution. 

A key assumption, however, is that the deviation of each fidelity’s mean reward from the highest fidelity’s mean is known *a priori*.  The practicality of this assumption is unclear – how would these deviations be estimated in real-world applications like online advertising? Exploring the possibility of relaxing this assumption would strengthen the work.
