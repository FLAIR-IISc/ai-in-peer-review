

The paper models multi-armed bandit problems with a "fidelity" parameter chosen by the learner from a finite set for each pulled arm. An arm pulled at fidelity level $m$ yields a stochastic iid reward with mean within $\zeta_m$ of the true mean, where the $\zeta_m$ parameters (error bounds) are known a priori, and the highest fidelity level corresponds to $\zeta = 0$. Lower-fidelity pulls incur lower costs than higher-fidelity pulls. The authors formulate the problem of sequentially selecting arms with fidelities to maximize net reward, propose a new algorithm (MF-UCB), and argue it can outperform UCB operating exclusively at high fidelity. They also establish a lower bound for the regret of their algorithm. The problem studied—modeling the tradeoff between information quality, cost, and reward in online learning (specifically stochastic bandits)—is interesting and relevant, potentially serving as a benchmark for future work. However, while the paper appears technically sound, a key shortcoming is insufficient explanation of results and assumptions. The adopted regret definition, which penalizes resource consumption multiplicatively, seems unnatural; an additive cost penalty (e.g., factoring cost into total reward or regret) would be more general and intuitive, though the authors' ad-display motivation is limited in scope. The introduction of Assumption 1—which imposes constraints on fidelity parameters provided to the learner—appears arbitrary and opaque. Why would this hold in general? Crucially, if the $\zeta_m$ decay rates violate Assumption 1, is there a general method to select a subsequence of fidelities satisfying it (thereby preserving the results)? Though the authors claim the assumption is not crucial, it is used in proofs, raising concern about whether it critically influences the algorithm's design and performance. The algorithm appears tailored for specific $\zeta$ sequences, but the justification for these being "correct" is missing. The lower bound's gap also warrants further investigation, potentially necessitating a strategy redesign. Interpreting the main result (Theorem 2, MF-UCB's regret bound) and contrasting it with UCB (at highest fidelity—this is not explicitly stated in the discussion) is difficult due to excessive technicality. The relative performance of MF-UCB depends on $\Delta$ and $[k]$, which themselves depend on the complex sequence of sets $\mathcal{K}^{(m)}$; a clearer explanation would strengthen the argument. Overall, the paper initiates a promising line of inquiry but loses clarity in presenting results.
