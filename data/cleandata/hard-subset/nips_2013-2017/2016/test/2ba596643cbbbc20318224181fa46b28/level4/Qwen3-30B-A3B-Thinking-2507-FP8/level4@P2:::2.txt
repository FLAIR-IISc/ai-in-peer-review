

This paper introduces a multi-fidelity bandit framework that extends the classical stochastic K-armed bandit problem to settings where each arm can be observed at multiple fidelity levels, with higher fidelities providing more accurate but more expensive observations. The authors formalize this as a bandit problem where each arm can be pulled at M fidelities, with fidelity m having cost λ(m) and providing a biased estimate of the highest fidelity's reward. They propose MF-UCB, an algorithm that leverages lower-fidelity observations to quickly eliminate suboptimal arms before investing in more expensive high-fidelity evaluations. The paper provides theoretical guarantees showing MF-UCB achieves better regret than standard UCB algorithms that ignore the multi-fidelity structure.

The work builds on the extensive literature on multi-armed bandits [9, 11, 12] and upper confidence bound methods [2, 3], while addressing a novel setting where observations come at different costs and accuracies. It relates to recent work on multi-fidelity optimization [7, 10] and budgeted bandits [13, 15], but makes a significant contribution by specifically formalizing and analyzing the exploration-exploitation trade-off in the bandit setting with multiple fidelities.

Strengths:
- The problem formulation is well-motivated with clear applications in online advertising and algorithm selection, where displaying ads for shorter durations or using less data provides cheaper approximations of performance.
- The theoretical analysis is strong, providing both an upper bound for MF-UCB and a matching lower bound showing near-optimality under certain conditions.
- The main paper is clearly written and well-organized, making the key insights accessible to readers.
- The algorithm design intuitively captures the trade-off between using cheap, low-fidelity observations for exploration and reserving expensive, high-fidelity observations for exploitation.

Weaknesses:
- The supplementary materials contain numerous typos and errors that significantly reduce readability (e.g., page 10 equation (8) has a missing subscript, Section A1 uses H^(m) instead of K^(m), page 13 has incorrect exponents). Correcting these would greatly improve the paper's quality.
- The use of "up-to-a-multiplicative-constant" notation is somewhat misleading, as constants are not truly constant (e.g., the parameter ρ in Theorem 2 is hidden in the constant). Using exact inequalities (as provided in the supplementary materials) would improve clarity.
- Practical applicability concerns: The algorithm's performance is sensitive to the estimation of ζ(m), which characterizes the bias between fidelities. The paper lacks discussion of how to estimate ζ(m) in practice or how the algorithm behaves when ζ(m) is misspecified.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of practical limitations and potential solutions, especially for real-world applications where fidelity bias estimation might be challenging.

Quality: The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims. The proofs are mostly solid, though the supplementary materials require careful correction of numerous errors. The authors are careful in discussing both strengths and weaknesses of their approach.

Clarity: The main paper is clear and well-written, with good organization. The supplementary materials, however, suffer from many typos and errors that detract from the clarity of the theoretical results.

Originality: The multi-fidelity bandit setting is novel and extends the standard bandit problem in a meaningful way. The authors clearly differentiate their work from related literature on multi-fidelity optimization and budgeted bandits.

Significance: The results address an important practical problem in resource-constrained learning settings. The insights about efficiently allocating resources across different fidelity levels could be valuable for practitioners in fields like online advertising, algorithm selection, and experimental design.

Pro: Strong theoretical contribution, well-motivated problem, clear main paper, solid empirical results.
Con: Supplementary materials need correction, practical applicability concerns need more discussion, "up-to-constant" notation could be improved.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the bandit literature. The problem is important, the theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the practical insights are valuable. With minor revisions to correct the typos in the supplementary materials and address the practical applicability concerns, it would be an excellent fit for NIPS. The authors' work provides a solid foundation for future research on bandit problems with multiple fidelity evaluations, and the insights about resource allocation across different fidelity levels have clear practical implications.
