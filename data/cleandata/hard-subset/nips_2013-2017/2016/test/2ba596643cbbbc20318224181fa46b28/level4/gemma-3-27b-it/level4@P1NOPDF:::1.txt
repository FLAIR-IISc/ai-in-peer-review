The paper models multi-armed bandit problems with a ‘fidelity’ component selectable by the learner for each arm pull, chosen from a finite set of fidelities. Pulling an arm at a given fidelity yields a stochastic, i.i.d. reward with a mean within ζ<sub>m</sub> of the true mean, where the ζ<sub>m</sub> parameters (errors) are known a priori, and the highest fidelity corresponds to ζ = 0. Furthermore, lower fidelity pulls are cheaper than higher fidelity pulls. The authors formulate the problem of sequentially selecting arms and fidelities to maximize a notion of net reward, introduce a novel algorithm for this problem, and demonstrate that it can outperform UCB operating only at the highest fidelity level. The paper also provides a lower bound on the regret of the proposed algorithm.

In my opinion, the paper addresses an interesting and relevant problem: modeling the trade-off between information, cost, and reward – specifically, the choice between low-information, cheap options and high-information, expensive ones – in online learning, particularly stochastic bandits. In this regard, it could serve as a valuable benchmark for future work. While the paper appears technically sound, a key weakness is the insufficient explanation of its results and underlying assumptions. 

The adopted regret definition feels unnatural from at least one perspective – why not penalize resource consumption (or ‘cost’) additively rather than multiplicatively, as is done here? The authors motivate this definition with an ad-display example, but this may not be universally applicable. A more natural question might be how the inclusion of cost, factored additively into the total reward (or regret), impacts performance, and exploring this would strengthen the paper. 

The introduction of Assumption 1 appears somewhat artificial and opaque, involving constraints on the fidelity parameters known to the learner. It’s unclear why the authors expect this to hold in a general learning problem. More importantly, if the ζ<sub>m</sub> values do not decay as quickly as required by Assumption 1, is there a general method for selecting a subsequence of fidelities to satisfy the assumption and thus maintain the validity of the results? This would significantly bolster the paper's message. Although the authors claim the assumption is not crucial, it appears to be used in the proofs, and my primary concern is whether it fundamentally influences the algorithm's design and performance. The algorithm appears tailored to perform well with specific ζ configurations, but the justification for why these are the ‘right’ configurations is missing. The gap in the lower bound also warrants further investigation and potentially a refinement of the proposed strategy.

Interpreting the main result (Theorem 2, the regret bound for MF-UCB) and contrasting it with UCB (at the highest fidelity? This isn't explicitly stated in the discussion) is difficult due to the technical complexity of the presentation. The relative gain or loss of MF-UCB seems to depend on Δ and [[k]], which in turn depend on the rather intricate sequence of sets 𝒦<sup>(m)</sup>. A more explicit explanation would significantly strengthen the authors' argument. 

Overall, the paper begins well by considering an interesting learning model but quickly loses clarity in its presentation of results.
