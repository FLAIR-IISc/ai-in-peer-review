This paper introduces a novel framework, the Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit, addressing scenarios where evaluating arms incurs varying costs and cheaper, approximate evaluations are available. The authors present MF-UCB, an algorithm adapting to these costs and fidelities, alongside a regret analysis and a corresponding lower bound. Empirical evaluation on synthetic data demonstrates performance exceeding that of standard UCB. While the proposed framework holds promise, I currently believe the paper, in its present state, is not suitable for publication.

My primary concern lies with clarity and presentation. The notation is introduced in a fragmented manner, making definitions difficult to locate. For instance, the variable ‘s’ appears before its definition on the following page, and is then sparsely utilized. A more organized presentation of notation would significantly improve readability.

The illustrative example of online advertising, while intended to motivate the problem, introduces potentially distracting elements. The connection drawn between display time and non-stationarity feels misplaced within the core focus of the paper. A different example, perhaps centered around algorithm selection for machine learning (as briefly mentioned in the introduction) or engineering design (elaborated upon in point 4), might offer a cleaner analogy. 

The rationale for the regret analysis warrants further justification. In a cost-sensitive setting, maximizing long-term gains typically equates to identifying the arm with the highest reward-to-cost ratio. However, the current formulation prioritizes the arm with the highest mean reward regardless of cost, potentially penalizing exploration at lower fidelities even when unnecessary. This approach may obscure the true cost associated with sampling low-fidelity approximations when higher-fidelity information could be more valuable.

A cost (or sample) complexity analysis would be a valuable addition. This could be motivated by scenarios like prototype testing for product development, where regulations, contracts, and existing infrastructure necessitate utilizing low-fidelity approximations before more expensive, high-fidelity testing. The goal becomes identifying the best product with high confidence while minimizing the cumulative cost of the testing phase prior to mass production.

Finally, sections of the text feel unwieldy. A thorough revision to streamline the writing and enhance conciseness would benefit the paper.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novelty: The multi-fidelity bandit framework is a valuable extension to the classical bandit problem, addressing a practical challenge in many real-world applications.
*   Theoretical Contribution: The development of MF-UCB, along with its associated regret analysis and lower bound, provides a solid theoretical foundation for tackling this new problem.
*   Empirical Validation: The synthetic simulations demonstrate that MF-UCB outperforms standard UCB, suggesting its practical effectiveness.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Clarity and Presentation: The disorganized notation and convoluted writing hinder understanding.
*   Justification of Regret Analysis: The current regret formulation may not fully capture the cost-sensitive nature of the problem.
*   Missing Analysis: A cost/sample complexity analysis would strengthen the paper's theoretical contributions and practical relevance.
*   Misleading Example: The online advertising example introduces irrelevant concepts, potentially diverting attention from the core contribution.
