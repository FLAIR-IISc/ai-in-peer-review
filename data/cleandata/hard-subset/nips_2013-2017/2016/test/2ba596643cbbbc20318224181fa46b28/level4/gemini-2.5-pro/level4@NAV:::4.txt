This paper introduces a novel framework termed the Multi-fidelity Multi-armed bandit, where each arm can be sampled at various fidelity levels, with each level having an associated sampling cost. Lower fidelity samples, while less expensive, are drawn from distributions that approximate the true, highest-fidelity distribution. The authors propose an algorithm designed to adapt to the available sequences of approximations and costs, providing a corresponding regret analysis and a problem-specific lower bound. Numerical simulations on synthetic datasets demonstrate that the proposed algorithm shows superior performance over standard UCB.

The framework presented is novel and holds the potential for significant impact with further development. However, in its current form, the paper has several shortcomings that prevent me from recommending it for publication.

1.  The presentation of the notation is a significant weakness. Definitions are scattered throughout the text, making them difficult to locate and reference. For instance, the variable $s$ is used a full page before its definition appears, and is subsequently used sparingly, which adds to the confusion.

2.  The choice of online advertising as a motivating example is problematic and at times misleading. The discussion frequently invokes concepts such as non-stationarity and correlations with display time. These concepts, particularly non-stationarity, are outside the scope of the stationary bandit problem addressed in the paper and detract from the core contribution. A more fitting motivating example would strengthen the paper (see point 4).

3.  The justification for the chosen regret analysis framework is unclear. Typically, regret in a cost-sensitive setting would be defined relative to an optimal policy that balances reward and cost. In this work, the optimal policy is defined solely by the arm with the highest mean reward, irrespective of the costs associated with sampling it. This definition seems to weaken the problem's premise; it implicitly penalizes the algorithm for sampling the best arm at lower fidelities, potentially masking the true cost trade-offs, especially in scenarios where sampling low-fidelity levels is inefficient.

4.  An analysis based on cost complexity, rather than regret, might be a more natural fit for the multi-fidelity problem. This perspective could be strongly motivated by applications such as prototype testing in product design. The objective in such a scenario is to identify the best product (or arm) with high confidence while minimizing the total financial cost of the testing phase. This aligns well with real-world constraints where companies must progress through a sequence of increasingly expensive, higher-fidelity tests due to regulations or available infrastructure.

5.  Finally, the manuscript would benefit from a thorough revision for clarity and conciseness. Certain sections are difficult to follow, and streamlining the presentation would significantly improve the paper's readability.
