This paper introduces a "multi-fidelity" version of the multi-armed bandit problem. In this setting, there are K arms, and each can be evaluated at one of M different fidelity levels. Lower fidelity evaluations are less costly but introduce a greater potential bias in the reward estimate. Specifically, pulling an arm at fidelity *m* draws a sample from a distribution whose mean deviates from the arm's true mean reward by a bounded amount that depends on the fidelity level.

The authors propose a natural UCB-based algorithm for this problem, MF-UCB, which modifies the standard UCB index by widening the confidence interval to account for the additional uncertainty introduced by low-fidelity sampling bias. The paper's theoretical analysis culminates in a regret bound that is complex and difficult to interpret directly. The authors argue that this bound is often significantly better than the regret achieved by a standard UCB algorithm restricted to playing arms only at the highest fidelity. This claim is supported by a brief experimental section with simulation results.

The choice to model lower fidelity as increased bias, rather than increased variance, is an interesting one. While the latter is also a plausible model for "low fidelity," the motivating examples provided, such as hyperparameter tuning for machine learning algorithms, lend support to the bias-based formulation. However, the connection between the model and its motivating applications could be strengthened. For instance, the argument that displaying an advertisement for a shorter duration yields a biased estimate of its long-term effectiveness is not entirely convincing. Furthermore, in the context of ML algorithm selection, it is not immediately clear that minimizing cumulative regret is the most appropriate objective for an algorithm designer, who may be more concerned with final model performance. While these points weaken the motivation, they do not constitute a disqualifying flaw in the work.

The primary innovation of this paper appears to be the formulation of the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit problem itself. The proposed algorithm is a relatively straightforward application of the "optimism in the face of uncertainty" principle, which is standard for this class of problems. As noted above, the theoretical performance guarantee is disappointingly difficult to interpret, making it hard to gain intuition about the factors that govern performance.

The simulation results are also challenging to assess fully. While Appendix C provides the parameter values used in the experiments, it offers no justification for these specific choices. The results show that MF-UCB improves upon the standard UCB baseline by a factor of 3 to 4 in many scenarios. However, it is unclear whether this level of improvement is representative of what one should expect in practice, or if it is an artifact of the specific parameter values chosen for the simulations (e.g., the bias decay function values, ζ^(m)). Further analysis connecting the experimental parameters to realistic scenarios would significantly strengthen the paper's empirical claims.
