

This paper introduces a multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit framework where arms can be sampled at varying fidelity levels, each associated with a cost. Lower-fidelity samples (less costly) approximate the highest-fidelity distribution. The authors propose an adaptive algorithm, provide regret analysis, a lower bound, and synthetic experiments demonstrating superior performance over UCB. While the framework is conceptually interesting and holds potential for impact, the current submission requires substantial revision before it can be considered for publication. The following concerns must be addressed:

**1. Notation Clarity and Presentation:**  
The paper’s notation is scattered and poorly defined, disrupting readability. Key symbols like $s$ (defined one page after first use, then rarely referenced) are introduced without clear context. This forces readers to hunt for definitions, undermining the paper’s accessibility. A centralized notation table early in the paper, with precise definitions and consistent usage, is essential. The current presentation of $s$ exemplifies a broader issue: symbols are introduced opportunistically rather than systematically, making the technical arguments hard to follow.

**2. Misleading Motivating Example:**  
The advertising example is problematic. The paper repeatedly links ad display time to non-stationarity, yet non-stationarity is entirely absent from the problem formulation or analysis. This creates confusion and distracts from the core multi-fidelity cost structure. The example should be replaced with a more relevant scenario where fidelity levels and costs are naturally aligned (e.g., simulating product prototypes before physical testing, as suggested in point 4). The current example undermines the paper’s technical coherence.

**3. Flawed Regret Analysis Justification:**  
The regret analysis is conceptually unsound. Regret in bandits measures suboptimality relative to the *optimal arm*—here, the arm with the *highest reward-to-cost ratio* (not merely the highest mean reward). However, the paper’s analysis incorrectly assumes the optimal policy is simply "select the arm with the highest mean reward," ignoring cost. This misalignment renders the regret objective invalid: the optimal policy *must* account for cost, and the regret bound should reflect this. The current formulation fails to justify why the algorithm’s objective (minimizing cost-adjusted regret) is meaningful under the stated problem setup. Without a clear cost-aware optimization goal, the regret analysis lacks theoretical grounding.

**4. Missing Cost Complexity Analysis:**  
A critical omission is the absence of a *cost complexity* analysis. The problem’s practical motivation (e.g., minimizing testing costs before product launch) demands a guarantee on *expected total cost* to identify the best arm with high probability—distinct from regret. The authors should provide:  
- A cost-complexity bound (e.g., "the algorithm identifies the best arm with probability $1-\delta$ using total cost $O(\text{poly}(1/\delta))$").  
- A justification for why cost complexity is more relevant than regret in multi-fidelity settings (e.g., "Companies must minimize cumulative testing cost before mass production, not long-term reward").  
This analysis would directly address the real-world applicability the paper hints at and strengthen the contribution.

**5. Writing and Structure:**  
The paper suffers from verbose, disorganized prose. Sentences like "arms can be sampled with different level of fidelity" contain grammatical errors ("level" → "levels"). Sections lack logical flow; for instance, the advertising example’s non-stationarity tangents disrupt the narrative. A thorough edit to streamline arguments, eliminate redundancies, and ensure technical precision is required. Specific examples: the transition from problem setup to algorithm description is abrupt, and the lower bound derivation lacks clarity on its relation to the regret analysis.

**Recommendation:**  
The core idea—multi-fidelity bandits with cost-aware sampling—is promising and addresses a meaningful gap. However, the current paper’s theoretical flaws (points 3–4), presentation issues (point 1), and distracting example (point 2) prevent it from meeting NIPS’s standards. The authors should:  
(i) Redefine the regret objective to explicitly optimize reward/cost ratio, re-derive the regret bound, and clarify the optimal policy.  
(ii) Replace the advertising example with a cost-driven application (e.g., product testing).  
(iii) Add a cost complexity analysis motivated by industrial constraints.  
(iv) Revise notation and prose for clarity and precision.  

With these revisions, the paper could make a valuable contribution. As written, it is **not suitable for publication**. I recommend *major revision* after addressing these points. The authors have a strong foundation but must correct fundamental theoretical and presentational issues to align with the paper’s stated goals.
