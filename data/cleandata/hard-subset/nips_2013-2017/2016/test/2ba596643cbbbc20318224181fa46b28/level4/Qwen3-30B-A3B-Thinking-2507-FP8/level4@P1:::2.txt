

This paper introduces a novel multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit framework, where each arm can be pulled at multiple fidelity levels with varying costs and accuracy. The authors formalize the problem by introducing a cost-weighted regret measure that accounts for both the cost of observations and the regret incurred. They propose MF-UCB, an algorithm that dynamically selects the appropriate fidelity level for each arm, using lower-fidelity observations to efficiently eliminate suboptimal arms while reserving high-fidelity evaluations for promising candidates. The authors prove that MF-UCB achieves near-optimal regret bounds under certain conditions, demonstrating significant improvements over standard UCB algorithms when lower fidelities are substantially cheaper.

The paper builds upon the rich literature on multi-armed bandits, including seminal works by Robbins [11] and Auer et al. [3], while addressing a practical gap in the literature where cheaper approximations to expensive experiments are available. Unlike previous work on budgeted bandits [13, 15] or multi-fidelity optimization [7, 10], this paper provides a rigorous regret analysis for the multi-fidelity setting, making it a valuable contribution to the bandit literature.

The paper excels in theoretical contributions, providing clear regret bounds for MF-UCB and a matching lower bound. The regret analysis demonstrates how the algorithm effectively leverages lower-fidelity observations to reduce the overall cost while maintaining good performance. The authors show that MF-UCB outperforms standard UCB by a factor of λ(m)/λ(M) for arms in K(m), where lower fidelities are significantly cheaper. The theoretical analysis is thorough, with careful consideration of the interplay between fidelity levels, costs, and regret.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, making the technical content accessible to readers familiar with bandit algorithms. The examples provided (online advertising, algorithm selection) effectively illustrate the practical relevance of the problem. The authors also provide a convincing theoretical justification for their regret definition, arguing that using the highest fidelity as the target reward makes sense for applications where short-term performance doesn't reflect long-term value.

However, the supplementary materials contain numerous typos and minor errors that significantly hinder readability and may confuse readers. For example, in Appendix A, there are inconsistencies in notation (H^(m) vs K^(m)), missing parentheses, and incorrect symbols (e.g., \nu/(rho-2) instead of \nu/2). These errors, while easily correctable, detract from the paper's professionalism and make verification of the proofs difficult.

The paper's use of "up to a multiplicative constant" notation can be misleading, as the constants depend on algorithm parameters like ρ. Presenting the exact inequalities (which are available in the supplementary materials) would enhance clarity. Specifically, Theorem 2's bound contains ρ, which is a parameter of the algorithm, yet it's hidden within the constant notation.

Practically, the algorithm's performance depends on accurate estimation of ζ(m), the bias between fidelity levels. The paper doesn't discuss how to handle cases where ζ(m) is unknown or inaccurately estimated—a limitation that could affect real-world applicability. A brief discussion of practical considerations, such as how to estimate ζ(m) or handle model misspecification, would strengthen the paper.

The paper's significance is clear: it provides a theoretically sound approach to a practical problem in online learning where expensive experiments can be supplemented with cheaper approximations. The results are particularly relevant for applications like online advertising, where the cost of evaluating ad performance at full scale is high, but partial evaluations (e.g., shorter display times) can provide useful preliminary insights.

Strengths:
- Novel multi-fidelity bandit framework addressing an important practical gap
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with tight regret bounds
- Clear presentation of the problem and solution
- Strong empirical validation on synthetic problems
- Practical relevance to real-world applications

Weaknesses:
- Supplementary materials contain numerous typos and notation inconsistencies
- The use of asymptotic notation obscures the dependence on algorithm parameters
- Limited discussion of practical implementation challenges (e.g., estimating ζ(m))
- The paper doesn't address how to handle cases where the bias assumptions might not hold

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the bandit literature with clear theoretical insights and practical relevance. The paper makes a significant step toward incorporating multi-fidelity evaluations into bandit algorithms, which is increasingly important as machine learning systems face resource constraints. With minor revisions to correct the typos in the supplementary materials and a brief discussion of practical limitations, this paper would be an excellent fit for NIPS. The theoretical results are sound, the problem is well-motivated, and the contributions advance the state of the art in bandit algorithms.
