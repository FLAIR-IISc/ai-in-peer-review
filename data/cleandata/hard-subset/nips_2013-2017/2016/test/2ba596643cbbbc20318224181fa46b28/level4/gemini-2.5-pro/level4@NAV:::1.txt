This paper introduces a multi-armed bandit framework where the learner must select not only an arm to pull but also a 'fidelity' level from a finite set. Pulling an arm at a given fidelity produces a stochastic i.i.d. reward whose mean is guaranteed to be within a known error parameter, zetam, of the true mean. The highest fidelity corresponds to zero error (zeta = 0), and lower fidelity pulls incur a smaller cost. The authors formulate this as a problem of sequentially selecting arms and fidelities to maximize a defined net reward. They propose a novel algorithm, MF-UCB, and argue that it can surpass the performance of a standard UCB algorithm restricted to the highest fidelity. The work also includes a regret lower bound for the proposed method.

The problem studied in this paper is both interesting and relevant, as it formally models the fundamental tradeoff between information accuracy, cost, and reward in the context of online learning with stochastic bandits. As such, this work could serve as a valuable benchmark for future research. While the technical contributions appear sound, a significant weakness of the paper is the insufficient justification and explanation of its core assumptions and results.

A key concern is the definition of regret, which seems unnatural. It is unclear why resource consumption (cost) is penalized multiplicatively rather than additively, a more conventional approach. While the ad-display example provides some motivation, its generality is limited. A more natural formulation would involve factoring the cost of observations additively into the total reward or regret, and an analysis of this alternative setting would be insightful.

Furthermore, Assumption 1, which places constraints on the fidelity error parameters, comes across as artificial and lacks clear motivation. It is not obvious why this assumption should hold in general learning scenarios. The paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing whether its results can be recovered when this assumption is violated—for instance, by selecting a subset of fidelities that satisfies the required decay rate. Although the authors state that this assumption is not essential, it appears in the proofs, raising concerns about whether it critically impacts the algorithm's design and performance. The algorithm seems specifically tailored to perform well under certain configurations of the zeta parameters, but the paper fails to justify why these are the most relevant ones to consider.

Additionally, the gap between the regret upper bound and the provided lower bound requires further investigation and may suggest that a redesign of the proposed strategy is necessary to achieve optimality.

Finally, the presentation of the main result (Theorem 2) is difficult to interpret. The comparison to standard UCB is also ambiguous; it is not explicitly stated whether the baseline is UCB operating at the highest fidelity. The performance of MF-UCB is expressed in terms of complex parameters such as Delta, [[k]], and the sequence of sets \cal{K}^(m). A more intuitive explanation of how these factors influence the relative gains and losses would substantially strengthen the authors' claims.

In summary, the paper introduces a compelling learning model but suffers from a lack of clarity in the presentation of its theoretical results and assumptions, which obscures the impact of its contributions.
