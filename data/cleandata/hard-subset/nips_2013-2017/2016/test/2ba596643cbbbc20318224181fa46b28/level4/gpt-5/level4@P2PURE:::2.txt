This paper introduces a new multi-armed bandit setting in which each arm can be pulled at multiple accuracy (fidelity) levels. Pull cost increases with the desired accuracy, and regret is defined as cost-weighted pseudo-regret (i.e., cost multiplied by the standard pseudo-regret). The authors propose an algorithm, MF-UCB, for this setting and provide both upper and lower bounds on its cumulative pseudo-regret. The main paper is clear and well written.

However, the supplementary material—where most proofs appear—is much less polished. It contains many typos and minor, easily correctable errors that can confuse readers (see the non-exhaustive list below). Fixing these would substantially improve readability. In addition, the frequent use of “up to a multiplicative constant” notation can be misleading because some of these “constants” depend on algorithm parameters; for example, Theorem 2 hides a factor of ρ (an algorithm parameter) in the constant. Stating the exact inequalities (as given in the supplement) would improve clarity.

From a practical standpoint, it is not obvious that the algorithm is immediately applicable to real-world problems. For instance, MF-UCB’s strategy of using low-accuracy pulls to eliminate clearly suboptimal arms could be sensitive to inaccurate estimates of ζ (the fidelity accuracy). A discussion of such practical limitations and possible remedies would strengthen the paper.

From a theoretical perspective, the paper offers valuable insights into the problem along with interesting theorems and solid proofs. Overall, I view this as a significant and interesting contribution.
