This work introduces a novel multi-armed bandit (MAB) setting where each arm can be evaluated with varying levels of fidelity, incurring different costs. The core idea is that cheaper, lower-fidelity evaluations can inform exploration and quickly eliminate suboptimal arms, reserving expensive, high-fidelity evaluations for promising candidates. The authors propose the MF-UCB algorithm, and provide theoretical guarantees with both upper and lower regret bounds. The paper is generally well-written and presents a clear exploration of this new problem setting. However, the supplementary material contains numerous typographical errors and minor inconsistencies that detract from readability. Addressing these would significantly improve the paper’s clarity. Additionally, while the theoretical analysis is solid, a more in-depth discussion of practical limitations and potential mitigation strategies would strengthen the work.

The paper begins by formally defining the multi-fidelity bandit problem, introducing the concept of cost-weighted regret, and outlining the contributions. The proposed MF-UCB algorithm builds upon the classic UCB approach, adapting it to the multi-fidelity setting by strategically choosing the fidelity level for each arm pull. The theoretical analysis establishes a regret bound for MF-UCB and demonstrates near-optimality under certain conditions. The connection to existing work in bandit algorithms (e.g., UCB) and related areas like multi-fidelity optimization and active learning is well-established.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The multi-fidelity bandit setting is a valuable extension to the classic MAB framework, addressing a common scenario in many real-world applications where evaluating actions at different levels of precision and cost is possible.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a strong theoretical foundation for MF-UCB, including both upper and lower regret bounds. The proofs, while lengthy, appear sound (pending verification of corrections – see below).
* **Clear Presentation:** The core concepts and the proposed algorithm are presented clearly and concisely. The motivation for the approach is intuitive and well-explained.
* **Potential Impact:** The work has the potential to be influential in fields where efficient exploration-exploitation trade-offs are critical, such as online advertising, experimental design, and resource allocation.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Supplementary Material Quality:** The supplementary material contains a significant number of typographical errors and minor inconsistencies (detailed list provided at the end of the original review). These errors hinder readability and could potentially lead to confusion.
* **Hidden Constants:** The use of “up-to-a-multiplicative-constant” signs can obscure the practical implications of the theoretical results. Providing more precise bounds, or at least acknowledging the dependence on algorithm parameters (e.g., ρ), would be beneficial.
* **Practical Considerations:** The paper lacks a thorough discussion of practical challenges. The algorithm’s sensitivity to the accuracy of the fidelity accuracy estimate (ζ) is not fully explored, and potential strategies for handling inaccuracies are not addressed.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** While simulations demonstrate the algorithm's performance on synthetic problems, a more extensive evaluation on real-world datasets or more complex scenarios would strengthen the empirical validation.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper introduces a novel and theoretically well-grounded framework for a challenging problem.
* The theoretical analysis provides strong guarantees on the algorithm's performance.
* The work has the potential to impact several important application areas.
* The authors demonstrate a good understanding of the relevant literature.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The numerous errors in the supplementary material detract from the overall quality of the paper.
* The lack of a detailed discussion of practical limitations and potential mitigation strategies.
* The limited empirical evaluation makes it difficult to assess the algorithm’s performance in real-world scenarios.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this work represents a significant and interesting contribution to the field of bandit algorithms. The novelty of the problem setting, the theoretical rigor of the analysis, and the potential impact of the results make it a worthwhile submission to NIPS. However, addressing the issues related to the supplementary material and providing a more thorough discussion of practical considerations are crucial for strengthening the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should carefully review and correct all errors in the supplementary material, provide more precise bounds where possible, and expand the discussion of practical limitations and potential solutions.
