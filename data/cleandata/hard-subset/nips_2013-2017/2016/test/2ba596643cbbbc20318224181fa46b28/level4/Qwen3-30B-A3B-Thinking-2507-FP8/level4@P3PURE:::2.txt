

This paper introduces a novel multi-armed bandit framework where each arm can be pulled at varying accuracies (or fidelities), with the cost of pulling an arm increasing with the desired accuracy. The regret is defined as the product of this cost and the standard pseudo-regret. The authors propose the MF-UCB algorithm for this setting, establishing both an upper bound and a lower bound on the cumulative pseudo-regret. The main paper is clear and well-written, though the supplementary materials suffer from numerous typos and minor errors that hinder readability. Additionally, the use of "up to a multiplicative constant" throughout the paper is potentially misleading, as the constants implicitly depend on algorithm parameters (e.g., $\rho$ in Theorem 2). Explicit inequalities (as provided in the supplementary materials) would enhance clarity. From a practical perspective, the applicability of MF-UCB to real-world problems is unclear. Specifically, its strategy of using low-accuracy pulls to eliminate suboptimal arms may be sensitive to inaccurate estimation of $\zeta$ (the fidelity accuracy). A discussion of practical limitations and mitigation strategies would strengthen the work.  

Theoretically, the paper offers valuable insights, with well-motivated theorems and rigorous proofs. Overall, this is a significant and interesting contribution to the bandit literature.
