This paper explores a new multi-armed bandit setting. The setting is that there are $M$ loyalties that a learner can choose to play the weapon. Each successive loyalty provides a better estimate of rewards but costs more. If a strategy can eliminate many sub-optimal weapons using fewer and cheaper loyalties, while reserving higher and more expensive loyalties for a smaller subset of good weapons, it may outperform the standard UCB algorithm, which ignores these characteristics of loyalties. This setting can be applied to online advertising, where one might want to display ads for a few minutes to estimate clicks over the long term for any cost concern. For this problem, the authors consider a new regret definition, provide an algorithm with theoretical analysis (including a regret lower bound), and conduct simulations. The analysis is novel. Since \varepsilon^{(m)} is considered the deviation of the mean of the mth loyalty from one of the highest loyalties, we know the values in practice (as in applications in online advertising). Is it possible to relax this assumption?
