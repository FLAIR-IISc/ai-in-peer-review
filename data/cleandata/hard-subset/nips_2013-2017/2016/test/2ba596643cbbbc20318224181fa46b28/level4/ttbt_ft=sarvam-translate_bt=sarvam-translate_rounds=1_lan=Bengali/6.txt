This paper discusses a new multi-armed bandit scenario. The scenario involves $M$ loyalties where a student can choose an armed bandit. Each consecutive loyalty provides a good approximation of the reward but costs more. If a strategy can eliminate a few sub-optimal armed bandits using fewer and cheaper loyalties, where a smaller subset of good armed bandits can be saved for a larger and more expensive loyalty, it could surpass the standard UCBC algorithm, which ignores these loyalty characteristics. This scenario can be used in online advertising, where advertisements can be shown for a few minutes to estimate click numbers in the long term, considering the cost. For this problem, the authors consider a new regret definition, provide an algorithm with theoretical analysis (including a lower bound on regret), and conduct simulations. The analysis is novel. Since each \varepsilon^{(m)}, i.e., the deviation of the mean of the m-th loyalty from the mean of the maximum loyalty, is assumed to be known in advance, how do we actually know these values (e.g., its application in online advertising)? Is it possible to relax this assumption?
