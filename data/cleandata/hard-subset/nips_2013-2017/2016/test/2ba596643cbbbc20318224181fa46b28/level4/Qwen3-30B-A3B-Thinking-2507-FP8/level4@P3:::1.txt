

The paper introduces a novel multi-fidelity bandit framework that extends the classical multi-armed bandit problem by incorporating multiple levels of fidelity for each arm, where lower fidelity observations are cheaper but less accurate approximations of the highest fidelity outcome. This addresses a practical consideration in many real-world applications (e.g., online advertising, algorithm selection) where obtaining high-fidelity information is expensive, but cheaper, lower-fidelity approximations are available. The authors formalize this as a multi-fidelity bandit problem with a cost-weighted regret definition that accounts for both the quality of information and the cost of acquisition. They propose MF-UCB, a novel algorithm that adapts UCB strategies to this setting, and provide theoretical guarantees demonstrating that MF-UCB achieves better regret than standard UCB approaches that ignore fidelity information. The paper complements this with a lower bound showing MF-UCB is near-optimal under certain conditions.

The work builds on the rich bandit literature (e.g., [3, 9, 11]) and related multi-fidelity optimization approaches (e.g., [7, 10, 16]), but makes a significant contribution by formalizing the problem and providing regret guarantees for the multi-fidelity setting, which has not been previously analyzed in the bandit context. While previous work has explored multi-fidelity methods in optimization and active learning, the authors correctly note that these do not address the exploration-exploitation trade-off inherent to bandit problems.

**Strengths:**
The paper's primary strength lies in its clear problem formulation and practical motivation. The multi-fidelity framework is well-motivated by real-world scenarios where information acquisition has a cost and fidelity trade-off. The MF-UCB algorithm is well-designed, leveraging the intuition that lower fidelities should be used for exploration to eliminate suboptimal arms, while reserving higher fidelities for promising candidates. The theoretical analysis is thorough, with careful construction of the regret bound and lower bound. The empirical results on synthetic problems convincingly demonstrate MF-UCB's superiority over standard UCB.

**Weaknesses:**
The most significant weakness concerns the regret definition. The authors adopt a multiplicative cost formulation (Equation 1), which seems unnatural compared to the standard additive cost formulation where regret would be defined as the sum of the cost-weighted regret. While the authors justify this with an advertising example, it's unclear why this formulation is more appropriate for the general case. A more natural formulation would additively factor cost into the regret, which would make the paper's contribution more broadly applicable.

Assumption 1 (on the decay rate of ζ(m)) appears arbitrary and lacks justification. The authors claim it's not necessary for the algorithm to work, but it's crucial for demonstrating MF-UCB's advantage over UCB. Without clear justification for why ζ(m) would decay in this specific manner in practice, the theoretical results seem limited to a narrow class of problems. The paper should address whether the assumption can be relaxed or how to select a subsequence of fidelities to satisfy it when it doesn't hold.

The connection between the regret bound and practical performance is obscured by the complex notation and partitioning of arms into K(m) sets. The paper would benefit from a more intuitive explanation of how the regret bound translates to practical performance improvements, especially for readers unfamiliar with the technical details.

**Pro/Con for Acceptance:**
*Pro:* The problem is well-motivated, the algorithm is novel and well-designed, the theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the empirical results support the claims. The multi-fidelity bandit framework addresses an important practical consideration in bandit problems that has been overlooked in the literature.

*Con:* The regret definition is unnatural, Assumption 1 lacks justification and limits the applicability of the results, and the theoretical contribution is somewhat obscured by complex notation. The gap in the lower bound analysis (where MF-UCB may not be optimal for certain arms) is not adequately addressed.

**Overall Assessment:**
This paper addresses an important practical problem in bandit learning that has been overlooked in the literature. The theoretical contribution is significant, but the presentation and some technical choices (particularly the regret definition and Assumption 1) weaken the paper's impact. The authors could substantially strengthen their contribution by:
1. Adopting a more standard additive cost formulation for regret
2. Providing justification for Assumption 1 or relaxing it
3. Offering clearer intuition about how the regret bound translates to practical performance

The paper is technically sound and makes a valuable contribution to bandit literature, but the current presentation and assumptions limit its broader applicability. With the suggested revisions, this would be a strong candidate for acceptance. As it stands, it's a solid contribution that could be improved with more careful consideration of the problem formulation and assumptions. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the concerns about the regret definition and Assumption 1.
