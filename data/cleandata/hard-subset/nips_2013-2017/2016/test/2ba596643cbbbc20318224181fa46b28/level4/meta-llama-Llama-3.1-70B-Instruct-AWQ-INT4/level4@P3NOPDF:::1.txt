This paper proposes a novel approach to modeling multi-armed bandit problems, incorporating a 'fidelity' component that allows the learner to choose from a finite set of fidelities for each arm. The learner's goal is to maximize a notion of net reward, which is a function of the reward and the fidelity-dependent cost. The authors present a new algorithm, MF-UCB, and argue that it can outperform the traditional UCB algorithm operating at only high fidelity levels. They also provide a lower bound for the regret of the proposed algorithm.

The problem studied in this paper is interesting and relevant, as it models the tradeoff between information, cost, and reward in online learning, specifically stochastic bandits. This tradeoff is a crucial aspect of many real-world applications, and the paper's contribution may serve as a useful benchmark for future improvements.

However, the paper's technical solidity is somewhat overshadowed by the lack of clear explanations about its results and assumptions. The regret definition adopted in the paper seems unnatural, as it penalizes resource consumption multiplicatively rather than additively. While the authors provide an example of ad display to motivate their definition, it may not be the most general or intuitive approach. A more natural question might be to consider the cost paid for suitable fidelity observations as an additive factor in the total reward or regret.

The introduction of Assumption 1 appears artificial and opaque, as it involves constraints on the fidelity parameters given to the learner. It is unclear why the authors expect this assumption to hold in general learning problems. Furthermore, if the zeta_m's do not decay as fast as required by Assumption 1, it is unclear whether there is a general way to select a subsequence of fidelities to satisfy the assumption and hence the results. This would significantly strengthen the paper's message.

Although the authors mention that Assumption 1 is not crucial, it seems to be used in the proofs, and it is unclear whether the assumption influences the algorithm's design and performance in a critical fashion. The algorithm's design appears to be tailored towards performing well for specific zeta configurations, but the justification for these configurations is missing.

The gap in the lower bound also demands more investigation and perhaps a re-design of the proposed strategy. Interpreting the main result (Theorem 2, the regret bound for MF-UCB) and contrasting it with UCB (at the highest fidelity) is not easy to grasp due to the highly technical nature of the presentation. The relative gain/loss of MF-UCB seems to depend on Delta and [[k]], which in turn depend on the complicated sequence of sets \cal{K}^(m). A more explicit explanation would greatly help the authors' case.

In conclusion, while the paper makes a good start in considering an interesting learning model, it rapidly loses clarity when presenting the results. To strengthen the paper, the authors should provide more intuitive explanations for their assumptions and results, and address the concerns mentioned above.
