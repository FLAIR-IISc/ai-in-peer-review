This paper addresses a compelling and relevant problem: balancing the trade-off between information acquisition, cost, and reward in multi-armed bandit settings. The authors introduce a novel framework where each arm can be sampled at varying levels of ‘fidelity,’ each yielding a stochastic reward with accuracy dependent on the fidelity level and associated cost. They propose a new algorithm, MF-UCB, and demonstrate its potential to outperform traditional UCB approaches operating at the highest fidelity, alongside providing a lower bound on its regret. This work could serve as a valuable benchmark for future research in online learning and stochastic bandits.

However, despite the technical soundness of the paper, its presentation hinders a clear understanding of the results and underlying assumptions. A significant concern lies with the chosen regret definition, which employs a multiplicative penalty for resource consumption. While the authors motivate this choice with an ad-display example, an additive penalty – directly incorporating cost into the total reward or regret – might offer a more general and intuitive approach. Exploring the algorithm’s performance under an additive cost structure would strengthen the analysis.

Furthermore, Assumption 1, constraining the fidelity parameters, appears somewhat artificial. The rationale for expecting this assumption to hold in general learning scenarios is unclear. More critically, it’s important to address the consequences when the decay rate of the zeta values doesn’t meet the requirements of Assumption 1. Could a strategic selection of fidelities be employed to satisfy the assumption, and thereby maintain the validity of the results? A discussion of this would significantly enhance the paper’s robustness. Although the authors claim the assumption isn't crucial, its presence in the proofs raises concerns about its influence on both the algorithm’s design and its performance characteristics. The algorithm seems tailored for specific zeta configurations, but the justification for these configurations remains absent.

The gap in the lower bound also warrants further investigation and potentially a refinement of the proposed strategy. Interpreting the main result, Theorem 2 (the regret bound for MF-UCB), and comparing it to UCB at the highest fidelity is challenging due to the complex notation and presentation. The performance gain or loss of MF-UCB appears heavily dependent on Delta and [[k]], which are themselves tied to the complicated sequence of sets  \cal{K}^(m). A more explicit and intuitive explanation of these dependencies is crucial to support the authors’ claims. 

Overall, the paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable model for online learning. However, its clarity suffers when detailing the results and justifying the assumptions. Addressing the concerns regarding the regret definition, Assumption 1, and the interpretability of the regret bound would substantially improve the paper.
