This paper studies a novel multi-armed bandit setting where observing the outcome of each arm is costly, but cheaper approximations to the outcome are available at varying fidelities. The core idea is that by leveraging these lower-fidelity, lower-cost approximations, one can efficiently eliminate suboptimal arms and focus expensive, high-fidelity evaluations on a smaller set of promising candidates, potentially outperforming standard UCB algorithms that do not exploit this structure. The authors formalize this as a multi-fidelity bandit problem, propose a novel algorithm (MF-UCB), provide a theoretical analysis including a regret lower bound, and validate their approach through simulations. The problem setting and analysis are both novel and represent a valuable contribution to the bandit literature. The application to online advertising, where short-term ad displays can approximate long-term click-through rates, is a compelling example.

The paper is well-structured, and the motivations are clearly explained. The theoretical analysis appears rigorous and provides valuable insights into the performance of MF-UCB. The simulations further support the algorithm’s effectiveness. The connections to related work, including multi-fidelity optimization and budgeted bandits, are appropriately discussed. 

However, a key assumption underlying the analysis is that the bias (deviation) of each fidelity from the true reward is *known* in advance. This raises a practical question: how can these bias values be reliably estimated in real-world applications, such as online advertising? Relaxing this assumption would strengthen the work.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The multi-fidelity bandit setting is a novel extension of the classical bandit problem with clear practical relevance.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The proposed algorithm (MF-UCB) is accompanied by a rigorous theoretical analysis, including a regret bound and a lower bound demonstrating near-optimality.
*   **Potential Impact:** The algorithm has the potential to improve exploration-exploitation strategies in various applications where cheaper approximations of expensive rewards are available.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the problem, the proposed solution, and the theoretical results.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Strong Assumption:** The assumption of known fidelity biases limits the applicability of the algorithm in real-world scenarios. Addressing this limitation would significantly strengthen the work.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of multi-armed bandits. While the assumption of known fidelity biases is a limitation, the novelty of the setting, the rigor of the analysis, and the potential impact of the algorithm outweigh this concern. I recommend acceptance, with the caveat that the authors consider addressing the issue of unknown fidelity biases in future work.
