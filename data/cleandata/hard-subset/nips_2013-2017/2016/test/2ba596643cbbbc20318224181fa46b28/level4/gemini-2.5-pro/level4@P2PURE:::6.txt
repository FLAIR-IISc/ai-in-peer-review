This paper introduces a novel multi-armed bandit setting where each arm can be played at one of $M$ different fidelities. Higher fidelities provide more accurate reward estimates but incur greater costs. The central idea is that an effective strategy can leverage low-cost, low-fidelity pulls to quickly eliminate suboptimal arms, thereby reserving expensive, high-fidelity pulls for a small subset of promising ones. Such an approach could outperform standard algorithms like UCB that do not account for this fidelity-cost trade-off. The authors motivate the problem with applications like online advertising, where short ad displays (low fidelity) might be used to approximate long-term click rates (high fidelity) more cost-effectively.

To address this problem, the paper proposes a new, setting-specific regret definition, develops a novel algorithm, and provides a thorough theoretical analysis that includes both regret upper and lower bounds. The theoretical findings are supported by simulations.

The analysis is novel and represents a significant contribution. However, a key assumption is that $\varepsilon^{(m)}$, the deviation of the $m$-th fidelity's mean reward from that of the highest fidelity, is known in advance. This raises a practical concern: how would these bias values be determined in real-world applications, such as the proposed online advertising scenario? The paper would be strengthened by a discussion on whether this assumption can be relaxed or how these parameters could be estimated in practice.
