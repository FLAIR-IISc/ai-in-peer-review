This paper introduces and investigates a novel multi-armed bandit framework featuring $M$ distinct fidelities at which each arm can be sampled. These fidelities present a trade-off, where higher levels offer more precise reward approximations at a greater cost. The core premise is that an efficient strategy can leverage the cheaper, lower fidelities to rapidly eliminate sub-optimal arms, reserving the more expensive, high-fidelity samples for a smaller set of promising candidates. This approach has the potential to substantially outperform standard methods like UCB, which do not exploit such a fidelity structure. The authors motivate this setting with an application in online advertising, where brief ad displays could serve as a low-cost proxy for long-term user engagement.

To tackle this problem, the authors' contributions are threefold: they propose a new regret definition tailored to this setting, develop an algorithm accompanied by a rigorous theoretical analysis (which includes a regret lower bound), and validate their findings with simulations. The provided analysis is novel and constitutes a significant contribution.

A key assumption is that the deviation of the $m$-th fidelity's mean reward from that of the highest fidelity, $\varepsilon^{(m)}$, is known a priori. This raises a practical question: how would these values be determined in a real-world application like online advertising? It would be valuable if the authors discussed whether this assumption could be relaxed, perhaps by estimating these parameters concurrently.
