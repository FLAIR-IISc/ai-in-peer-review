This paper introduces a multi-fidelity bandit problem, a compelling extension of the classical K-armed bandit setting where evaluating arms is costly but cheaper approximations are available. The authors formalize this scenario and present MF-UCB, a novel algorithm based on upper confidence bounds, designed to leverage these approximations effectively. The paper provides a strong theoretical analysis, including a regret bound for MF-UCB and a matching lower bound, demonstrating its near-optimality under certain conditions. Empirical validation through simulations further supports the algorithm’s effectiveness.

The core idea – utilizing cheaper, albeit noisy, evaluations to efficiently explore the action space and focus expensive evaluations on promising arms – is well-motivated and relevant to numerous real-world applications, such as online advertising and algorithm selection, as highlighted in the introduction. The connection to existing bandit literature (e.g., Robbins’ work, UCB algorithms [Auer, 2002]) is clearly established. The paper also appropriately references related work in multi-fidelity optimization [Huang et al., 2006] and active learning [Zhang & Chaudhuri, 2016], distinguishing its focus on the exploration-exploitation dilemma specific to the bandit setting. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The multi-fidelity bandit formulation is a valuable contribution, expanding the traditional bandit framework to address a practical and important scenario.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The regret analysis is comprehensive and provides strong guarantees on the algorithm's performance. The derivation of a matching lower bound strengthens the claim of near-optimality.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The notation is generally clear, and the algorithm is easy to understand.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The simulations provide compelling evidence of MF-UCB’s effectiveness compared to a naive UCB approach.
*   **Detailed Related Work:** The authors demonstrate awareness of the relevant literature, carefully positioning their work within the existing research landscape.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Assumptions:** The analysis relies on several assumptions, including the regularity condition (Assumption 1) regarding the decay of ζ(m) and the concentration inequalities (Equation 2). While reasonable, the impact of violating these assumptions isn't fully explored. It would be beneficial to discuss the robustness of MF-UCB under weaker conditions.
*   **Lower Bound Gap:** The authors acknowledge a gap between their regret bound and the lower bound, particularly for certain arms (K(m)7). A more detailed discussion of the reasons for this gap and potential avenues for closing it would be valuable.
*   **γ(m) Choice:** While the justification for the choice of γ(m) is provided, the intuition behind it isn't entirely clear. A more detailed explanation, perhaps with an example, would enhance understanding.
*   **Simulation Details:** The simulation setup is briefly mentioned, but more details regarding the specific parameters used, the number of runs, and the statistical significance of the results would strengthen the empirical validation.
*  **Discussion on qt:** The discussion around choosing qt, specifically alternatives to µ(M)It, feels somewhat abrupt. Expanding this and discussing potential implications of different choices would enrich the paper.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound solution to a practically relevant problem.
*   The regret analysis and lower bound demonstrate the algorithm’s effectiveness and near-optimality.
*   The simulations support the theoretical findings.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The assumptions made in the analysis may limit the applicability of the results.
*   The gap between the regret bound and the lower bound warrants further investigation.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of bandit algorithms. The multi-fidelity bandit formulation is insightful, and the proposed algorithm, MF-UCB, is well-justified both theoretically and empirically. While some minor weaknesses exist regarding the assumptions and the gap in the lower bound, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend acceptance.

**Confidence:** 8/10
