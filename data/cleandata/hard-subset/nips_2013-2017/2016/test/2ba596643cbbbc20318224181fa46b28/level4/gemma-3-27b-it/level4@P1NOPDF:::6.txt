This paper studies a novel multi-armed bandit (MAB) setting with multiple fidelities. The learner can choose to play an arm at $M$ different fidelity levels, where each higher fidelity provides a more accurate estimate of the reward but at a greater cost. The authors argue that a strategy leveraging lower, cheaper fidelities to quickly eliminate suboptimal arms, and reserving higher, more expensive fidelities for a promising subset, can outperform standard algorithms like UCB that do not account for these fidelity characteristics. A compelling application is online advertising, where initial ad displays for a short duration can provide a cost-effective approximation of long-term click-through rates. 

For this problem, the authors introduce a new regret definition, propose an algorithm, and provide a theoretical analysis – including a regret lower bound – supported by simulation results. The analysis appears novel and represents a significant contribution. However, a key assumption is that the deviation, $\varepsilon^{(m)}$, of the mean reward at the $m$th fidelity from the highest fidelity is known in advance. This raises a practical concern: how would these values be known *a priori* in applications like online advertising? Exploring the possibility of relaxing this assumption would strengthen the paper.
