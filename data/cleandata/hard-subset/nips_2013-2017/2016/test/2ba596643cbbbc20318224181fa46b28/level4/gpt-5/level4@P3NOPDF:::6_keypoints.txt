- Paper studies multi-fidelity stochastic bandits with cost–accuracy trade-offs, proposes a cost-aware regret notion, a UCB-type algorithm, instance-dependent upper and lower bounds, and simulations.  
- Problem formulation is timely and relevant to applications like online advertising, simulation-based optimization, and A/B testing.  
- Novel regret formulation that jointly handles arm and fidelity selection.  
- Theoretical contributions are strong: instance-dependent upper bounds and matching/near-matching lower bounds.  
- Algorithmic idea is intuitive: cheap fidelities for elimination and expensive fidelities for promising arms.  
- Major weakness: central assumption that fidelity biases ε^(m) are known a priori is strong and may be unrealistic in practice.  
- Reviewer requests clarification on how ε^(m) would be obtained (global vs arm-specific, absolute vs high-probability) and how sensitive results are to misspecification.  
- Ask whether analysis can work with only upper bounds on ε^(m), conservative overestimates, or online learning of biases (e.g., co-sampling schemes).  
- Modeling scope unclear: need clarification on monotonicity assumptions and extensions to heteroskedastic noise or arm-dependent biases.  
- Practical guidance is limited: more detail needed on setting thresholds/parameters (γ^(m), exploration coefficients) from costs, noise levels, and risk tolerance.  
- Positioning versus prior work is insufficient: clarify differences from successive elimination, budgeted/structured bandits, and multi-fidelity Bayesian optimization.  
- Empirical suggestions: include sensitivity analyses to ε^(m) misspecification, compare to stronger cost-aware baselines (cost-aware successive rejects, Thompson sampling variants), and add a semi-synthetic or real-world experiment.  
- Presentation: overall readable but proofs are dense; recommend a brief proof roadmap and a table of main symbols to improve accessibility.  
- Minor typos and formula errors listed (specific line-numbered corrections provided).  
- Overall assessment: solid and practically motivated work with good theory; main caveat is reliance on known fidelity biases—recommend weak accept subject to clarifications and fixes.
