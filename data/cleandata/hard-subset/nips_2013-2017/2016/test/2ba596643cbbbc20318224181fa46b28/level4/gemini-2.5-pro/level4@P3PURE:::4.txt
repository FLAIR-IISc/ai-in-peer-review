The authors introduce a novel framework called the Multi-fidelity Multi-armed bandit. In this problem, each arm can be sampled at various fidelity levels, with a specific cost associated with each level. Lower fidelity samples are less costly but are drawn from a distribution that only approximates the true, highest-fidelity distribution. The paper presents an algorithm designed to adapt to the available fidelities and their associated costs, accompanied by a regret analysis and a problem-specific lower bound. Numerical simulations on synthetic data demonstrate that the proposed algorithm outperforms a standard UCB baseline.

While the proposed framework is interesting and has the potential for significant impact, the paper in its current state is not ready for publication for several reasons.

1.  The presentation suffers from unclear and poorly organized notation. Definitions are scattered throughout the text, making them difficult to locate and follow. For instance, the variable $s$ is used on one page, but its definition does not appear until the following page, after which it is barely used again. This makes the paper unnecessarily difficult to read.

2.  The motivating example of online advertising is potentially misleading. The authors draw connections to factors like ad display time, which introduces the concept of non-stationarity. However, non-stationarity is outside the scope of the stationary bandit problem addressed in this work, which creates a disconnect between the motivation and the technical contribution. A more fitting example might better motivate the problem setting (as suggested in point 4).

3.  The choice of a standard regret analysis framework seems questionable for this problem. Typically, regret analysis in a cost-sensitive setting would measure performance against an oracle that optimizes a long-term reward-to-cost trade-off. In this paper, however, the optimal policy is defined as simply identifying the arm with the highest mean reward, irrespective of sampling costs. This definition of the optimal policy weakens the benchmark by obligating it to sample the best arm at various fidelities. Consequently, the regret formulation may not properly account for the cost of sampling at lower fidelity levels, especially when such exploration is no longer necessary.

4.  A cost complexity analysis, rather than regret minimization, may be a more natural fit for the multi-fidelity bandit problem. This perspective can be motivated by a compelling real-world scenario, such as prototype testing in product design. In this setting, the objective is to identify the best product with high probability while minimizing the total cost incurred during the testing phase before committing to mass production. This scenario naturally models the constraints faced by companies, which often must use inexpensive low-fidelity approximations (e.g., simulations) before they can proceed to more advanced and costly high-fidelity tests (e.g., physical prototypes).

5.  Finally, the overall writing could be improved. Several sections are unwieldy, and a thorough revision for clarity and conciseness would significantly strengthen the paper.
