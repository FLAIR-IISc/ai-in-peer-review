This paper introduces a multi-fidelity version of the multi-armed bandit problem with K arms and M fidelity levels per arm. Lower fidelities are cheaper to sample but introduce bias: pulling an arm at fidelity m yields a reward whose expected value can deviate from the arm’s true mean by a fidelity-dependent bound. The paper analyzes a natural UCB-style policy that inflates the confidence interval to account for both statistical noise and the bias induced by low-fidelity sampling.

The theory culminates in regret bounds that are technically involved and, in their current form, difficult to interpret. The authors argue that, in many regimes, these bounds substantially improve over the regret of always sampling at the highest fidelity and running standard UCB. A short simulation section supports this claim.

I initially questioned the choice to model lower fidelity primarily as introducing bias rather than additional variance, since “low fidelity” could plausibly be interpreted either way. The paper’s motivating examples (e.g., algorithm selection where low-fidelity evaluations use fewer training iterations or reduced data) make the bias model reasonable: truncated training or subsampled data can systematically shift performance estimates. That said, I remain unconvinced by one stated motivation—the idea that observing an ad for a shorter time yields a biased estimate of long-horizon effectiveness—and, in the algorithm-selection example, it is not clear why minimizing cumulative regret is the designer’s objective as opposed to, say, identifying the best algorithm under a fixed evaluation budget.

Overall, the connection between the formal model and the motivating applications feels weaker than I would like, though not so weak as to be disqualifying. The paper’s primary contribution is the formulation of the multi-fidelity bandit problem itself. The proposed algorithm is a straightforward application of optimism under uncertainty, augmented to cover fidelity-induced bias. The main weakness is the interpretability of the performance guarantees: the theorems are heavy and do not yield immediate intuition about when and by how much multi-fidelity sampling helps. Clearer corollaries that isolate key regimes—e.g., in terms of the fidelity costs, bias budgets zeta^(m), and arm gaps—would strengthen the paper.

The empirical section, based on synthetic problems, is also hard to assess. Appendix C lists the parameter settings but does not justify them or explore sensitivity. In many runs, MF-UCB improves regret by a factor of roughly 3–4 over standard UCB. It would help to clarify whether such gains are typical across a broad range of cost and bias parameters, or whether they hinge on specific choices (e.g., particular zeta^(m) values). Without such context, it is unclear how to extrapolate the reported improvements to practical settings.

In summary, the problem formulation is interesting and timely, and the algorithmic approach is sensible. However, the theoretical guarantees are hard to parse, the empirical validation is limited, and the link to real applications could be sharper. Strengthening the interpretability of the bounds, providing more principled empirical design (including sensitivity analyses), and refining the application narratives would substantially improve the paper.
