- Paper studies multi-fidelity stochastic K-armed bandits where cheaper fidelities give biased proxies with known bias envelopes zeta(m).
- Proposes MF-UCB: per-fidelity UCBs corrected for bias and adaptive fidelity selection via thresholds gamma(m).
- MF-UCB uses low fidelities to cheaply eliminate clearly suboptimal arms and reserves high-fidelity pulls for near-optimal arms.
- Theoretical results include a regret upper bound showing improvements over single high-fidelity UCB and a matching lower bound under certain conditions.
- Simulations on synthetic problems report roughly 3–4x regret reduction vs high-fidelity-only UCB.
- Novelty: extends classic bandits to biased multi-fidelity feedback, distinct from budgeted bandits and most multi-fidelity optimization work.
- Strength: clear, timely problem formulation reflecting practical proxy evaluations and costs.
- Strength: conceptually simple, principled algorithm based on optimism with fidelity-bias correction.
- Strength: solid theoretical analysis explaining pruning behavior and showing near-optimality for many arms.
- Strength: empirical results indicate meaningful gains in synthetic settings.
- Weakness: main regret bounds are dense and hard to interpret; lacking simplified corollaries or canonical special cases.
- Weakness: fidelity-selection thresholds gamma(m) are under-explained and need more intuition and sensitivity analysis.
- Weakness: application motivation is uneven—some examples (short-ad duration, algorithm selection) are not convincingly argued.
- Weakness: experiments lack justification for parameter choices and sensitivity analyses, so reported gains may not generalize.
- Quality: technically sound analysis and reasonable assumptions, but necessity and verification of assumptions could be expanded.
- Clarity: generally well written but notation-heavy; needs a concrete running example and interpretation subsection.
- Originality: formulation and fidelity-switching analysis are novel despite a natural algorithmic idea.
- Significance: potentially impactful if multi-fidelity proxies are common, but practical impact limited by weak empirical support and interpretability.
- Suggestions: compare bias-only model to variance/hybrid models, substantiate application claims, provide interpretable special cases and plots, expand gamma(m) intuition and sensitivity, justify experimental choices and add baselines/robustness tests.
- Pros for acceptance: novel formulation, solid theory, simple implementable algorithm with promising empirical signs.
- Cons for acceptance: opaque main bounds, uneven application motivation, limited and unmotivated experimental evaluation.
- Overall: interesting and potentially impactful work that requires clearer exposition and stronger empirical validation to be fully convincing.
