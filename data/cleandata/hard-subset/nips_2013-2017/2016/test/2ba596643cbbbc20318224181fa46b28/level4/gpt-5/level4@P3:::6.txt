Summary and relation to prior work
The paper introduces a finite-armed, stochastic multi-armed bandit (MAB) model in which each arm can be queried at one of M fidelities. Higher fidelities are more accurate (unbiased target is the highest fidelity) but costlier; lower fidelities are cheaper but biased, with known uniform bias bounds ζ(m), decreasing with m. The goal is to maximize cumulative high-fidelity reward under a total capital budget Λ, leading to a cost-weighted pseudo-regret that accounts for (i) the reward of the high-fidelity outcome and (ii) the cost of the chosen fidelity at each play.

The authors propose MF-UCB, which maintains per-fidelity UCBs on each arm’s high-fidelity mean by adding the bias bound ζ(m) to the empirical mean plus a confidence radius. At each round, the algorithm selects the arm with the smallest upper envelope across fidelities, then chooses the lowest fidelity whose uncertainty is still above a threshold γ(m) (chosen as a function of the cost ratio and ζ(m)). Intuitively, low fidelities are used to eliminate obviously suboptimal arms; expensive fidelities are reserved for promising arms.

The paper provides regret upper bounds that compare favorably to naive UCB that ignores fidelities, especially when many arms can be discarded cheaply, and a lower bound showing near-optimality except for a specific class of instances where the switching criterion may be conservative. Empirical simulations corroborate the theory.

This work builds on classical UCB analyses (Auer et al.; Bubeck & Cesa-Bianchi) and differs from budgeted bandits (where reward/cost tradeoffs drive the objective) and from multi-fidelity optimization/GP bandits, as it focuses on finite arms with explicit, known bias bounds and a new cost-weighted regret. It is also related to settings with weak/strong labelers and multi-fidelity RL, but targets the exploration–exploitation trade-off under biased, tiered-cost feedback.

Assessment by review criteria

Quality
- The modeling is clear, and the regret analysis is technically solid. The upper bound shows how MF-UCB concentrates spending at the lowest fidelity capable of eliminating an arm; only the hardest-to-distinguish arms use the top fidelity. The lower bound uses a change-of-measure argument and matches the upper bound up to constants for a broad subset of arms, with a carefully explained gap for cases where the algorithm opts to escalate fidelity rather than resolve tiny gaps at a cheaper level.
- Assumptions: (i) known bias bounds ζ(m); (ii) sub-Gaussian–type concentration via ψ; (iii) a regularity condition (Assumption 1) preventing near-duplicate fidelities. These are reasonable for analysis but raise practical questions (see below).
- Experiments are suggestive but limited to synthetic problems.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written, with good intuition for the fidelity-selection rule and the regret definition. The notation is heavy; a short table of main symbols would help. The choice of qt (always the high-fidelity mean) is well motivated and distinguishes this work from alternatives that would ignore the multi-fidelity structure.
- I list concrete minor issues and typos below.

Originality
- The formalization of multi-fidelity finite-armed bandits with explicit bias bounds and a budgeted pseudo-regret is novel. The MF-UCB design (min over fidelity-specific UCBs with a cost-aware switching rule) is an elegant adaptation not present in prior UCB or budgeted-bandit work.

Significance
- The approach is potentially impactful in domains where early, cheap proxies are available (e.g., ads, A/B testing, AutoML, simulators vs. real-world trials). Theoretical improvements over standard UCB are meaningful when many arms can be screened out at low cost. The main limitation for immediate deployment is the need to know ζ(m) and ψ, and the sensitivity to their mis-specification.

Strengths
- Clear, principled formalization of multi-fidelity bandits and an appropriate regret notion aligned with long-run performance.
- Simple, interpretable algorithm with a strong theoretical guarantee and an accompanying lower bound.
- Insightful explanation of when and why multi-fidelity helps (screening many obviously bad arms cheaply).

Weaknesses and questions
- Practical availability of ζ(m): The algorithm and analysis assume known uniform bias bounds between fidelities. In many applications (e.g., ads), these are not known a priori. How can ζ(m) be estimated in practice? Suggestions:
  - Pilot sampling to estimate fidelity biases per arm or globally, with high-probability upper bounds that feed into γ(m).
  - Adaptive/robust variants that inflate ζ(m) online until empirical cross-fidelity discrepancies stabilize.
  - Sensitivity analysis showing performance degradation under over/under-estimation of ζ(m).
- Assumption 1 (fast decay of ζ): While analytically convenient, it rules out near-duplicate fidelities. Empirically, show how MF-UCB behaves when this is mildly violated and whether a simple merging/selection of fidelities mitigates the issue.
- Lower-bound gap for K(m)7: Could the fidelity-switching rule be refined to allow cheap resolution when lower-fidelity gaps are small but still informative (e.g., via arm-specific, data-driven γ(m)) without incurring high-fidelity sampling?
- Experiments: Results are solely synthetic. A real or semi-synthetic study (e.g., simulators vs. “truth”) and sensitivity to ζ(m), λ(m), and ψ would strengthen the case. Also compare against heuristic baselines that use low-fidelity screening with fixed thresholds.
- Parameter choices: Guidance for choosing ρ and the practical impact of ψ-mis-specification would be valuable.

Arguments for acceptance
- Well-motivated problem with clear practical relevance.
- Novel and principled algorithmic design with near-optimal theory.
- Clean insights into where multi-fidelity provides gains.

Arguments against acceptance
- Reliance on known ζ(m); limited guidance and validation for the practical setting where ζ(m) is unknown or heterogeneous across arms.
- Experimental evaluation is limited and does not test robustness to assumption violations.
- The lower-bound gap highlights a regime where the policy can be conservative.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a compelling and timely formulation, a simple algorithm with strong theory, and promising empirical behavior. Addressing the practicality of unknown ζ(m) and expanding the experimental evaluation would significantly strengthen the contribution.
