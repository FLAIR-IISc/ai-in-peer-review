This paper introduces the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit, a novel extension of the classical stochastic bandit problem. In this framework, arms can be played at different "fidelities," where lower fidelities are cheaper but provide biased estimates of the true (highest-fidelity) reward. The authors propose the Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB) algorithm, which intelligently leverages these cheap approximations to quickly explore and eliminate suboptimal arms, reserving expensive, high-fidelity evaluations for a small set of promising candidates. The main theoretical contributions include a regret analysis for MF-UCB and a corresponding lower bound for the problem, which together show the algorithm is near-optimal under certain conditions. The paper concludes with experiments on synthetic data demonstrating that MF-UCB outperforms a standard UCB approach that ignores the low-fidelity options.

The primary strength of this work is the introduction and formalization of the multi-fidelity bandit framework. This is an interesting and well-motivated problem setting that captures a practical trade-off present in many real-world applications, such as hyperparameter tuning, simulation-based optimization, and industrial design. The core idea of using cheap, low-fidelity evaluations to accelerate the search for the best option is compelling and potentially impactful.

Despite the promising direction, the paper has several significant weaknesses in its current form that prevent me from recommending acceptance.

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper suffers from several clarity issues that hinder readability. The introduction of notations is confusing, with definitions often scattered throughout the text and difficult to locate. For example, the variable 's' is used in the definition of the confidence bound `B(m)k,t(s)` in Section 2, but its meaning (number of samples) is not clarified until a page later. More broadly, the prose is often unwieldy. The paper would benefit significantly from a thorough revision to streamline the text and improve its flow.

2.  **Motivating Example:** The use of online advertising as a primary motivating example is potentially misleading. The authors allude to effects related to ad display time and changing user behavior, which imply a non-stationary environment. However, the theoretical model presented is for the standard stationary bandit setting. This mismatch creates confusion and weakens the motivation for the proposed framework.

3.  **Problem Formulation:** My main concern lies with the chosen problem formulation and the corresponding regret analysis. In this setup, the optimal arm is defined as the one with the highest mean at the most expensive fidelity, `µ*`, regardless of the cost structure. The regret is then defined relative to this fixed optimal arm. This seems somewhat artificial, as in many settings with explicit costs, a more natural goal would be to identify the arm with the best reward-to-cost ratio. By defining the objective independently of cost, the analysis focuses solely on how costs affect the speed of information gathering, rather than the definition of what is "optimal."

4.  **Alternative Formulation:** A more natural, and perhaps more impactful, formulation for this problem would be best-arm identification (BAI) under a budget constraint. This could be motivated by scenarios like prototype testing for new product development. Here, the objective is to identify the best product with high probability while minimizing the total cost of testing before committing to mass production. This setting directly aligns with applications where regulations or infrastructure necessitate using a sequence of low-to-high fidelity tests, and the end goal is a single correct decision rather than minimizing cumulative regret over time. A cost-complexity analysis within a BAI framework could provide a more compelling context for the multi-fidelity setting.

In summary, while the core idea of multi-fidelity bandits is novel and interesting, the paper in its current state has significant weaknesses in its presentation, motivation, and problem formulation. The authors propose an interesting algorithm, but the framework in which it is analyzed may not be the most natural or compelling one. For these reasons, I cannot recommend acceptance at this time. I believe the work could become a strong contribution if it were reframed—for instance, as a cost-based best-arm identification problem—and presented with much greater clarity.
