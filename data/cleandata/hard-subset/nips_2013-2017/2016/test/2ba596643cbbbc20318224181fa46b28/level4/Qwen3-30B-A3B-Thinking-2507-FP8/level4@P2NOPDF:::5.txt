

This paper proposes a novel variant of the classical stochastic K-armed bandit problem, introducing low-cost approximations for each arm as a key feature. The authors formalize this as a multi-fidelity bandit problem and establish rigorous theoretical guarantees, including a tight regret bound that matches a derived lower bound. The analysis demonstrates that the proposed method achieves optimal regret scaling, significantly improving upon standard bandit approaches when fidelity trade-offs are leveraged.  

The theoretical contribution is well-motivated and thoroughly developed, with clear proofs of both upper and lower bounds. The lower bound analysis is particularly strong, confirming that the regret bound is asymptotically optimal under the problem’s constraints. This theoretical foundation provides a solid justification for the method’s efficiency.  

Empirically, the authors conduct comprehensive simulations across multiple synthetic and benchmark settings to validate their claims. The results consistently show that the multi-fidelity approach reduces regret compared to single-fidelity baselines, especially when low-fidelity evaluations are inexpensive. The experimental design effectively isolates the impact of fidelity levels, and the results are presented with sufficient clarity to support the theoretical findings.  

The paper is well-structured and accessible, with a clear problem statement, intuitive motivation, and logical flow from theory to experiments. The writing avoids unnecessary jargon, making the contribution approachable for a broad audience. The multi-fidelity framework is convincingly positioned as a natural extension to bandit literature, addressing a practical gap in scenarios where evaluation costs vary across arms.  

While the paper focuses on stochastic bandits, the extension to other bandit variants (e.g., contextual or non-stationary settings) could be a valuable direction for future work. However, the current scope is well-defined and sufficiently impactful for the proposed problem.  

Overall, this is a strong submission that makes a clear, technically sound contribution to the bandit literature. The combination of optimal theoretical guarantees and compelling empirical validation demonstrates significant practical value. The work advances the field by formalizing a realistic and widely applicable setting, and the results provide actionable insights for practitioners facing resource-constrained decision-making. The paper meets NIPS standards for technical depth, novelty, and clarity. I recommend acceptance.
