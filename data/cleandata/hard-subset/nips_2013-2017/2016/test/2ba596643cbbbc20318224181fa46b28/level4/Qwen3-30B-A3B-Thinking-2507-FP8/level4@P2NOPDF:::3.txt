

The paper introduces a "multi-fidelity" multi-armed bandit framework where each of K arms can be sampled at M fidelity levels. Lower fidelity levels reduce sampling cost but introduce greater bias in reward estimates, with the deviation from the arm's true expected reward bounded by a fidelity-dependent term. The authors propose a UCB-based algorithm (MF-UCB) that widens confidence intervals to account for this bias-induced uncertainty. While the regret analysis yields complex bounds that are difficult to interpret, the paper argues that MF-UCB typically outperforms standard UCB (which always uses highest fidelity) in regret. Simulation results briefly support this claim.  

The choice to model low fidelity as increasing *bias* rather than *variance* initially seemed counterintuitive, as the latter interpretation is also plausible and commonly used in related contexts. However, the motivating examples—particularly algorithm selection for machine learning—partially justify the bias-focused model. For instance, selecting a learning algorithm via shorter training runs (low fidelity) may yield biased performance estimates compared to full training (high fidelity). Nevertheless, the ad-display example (claiming shorter ad durations produce biased effectiveness estimates over longer intervals) remains unconvincing, and the relevance of regret minimization as a design objective in algorithm selection is unclear. The connection between the model and applications is less compelling than the authors suggest, though not severe enough to warrant rejection.  

The paper’s primary contribution lies in formalizing the multi-fidelity bandit problem, which is novel and well-motivated. The algorithm itself, however, is a standard application of "optimism under uncertainty," lacking significant methodological innovation beyond the problem setup. The regret bounds, while theoretically sound, are cumbersome and obscure practical insights. The paper’s assertion that MF-UCB improves over UCB by a factor of 3–4 in simulations hinges on specific parameter choices (e.g., fidelity-dependent bias terms $\zeta^{(m)}$), yet Appendix C provides no justification for these values. Without such justification, it is unclear whether this performance gap is typical or an artifact of the parameterization. For example, would the 3–4× improvement persist across diverse real-world settings, or is it sensitive to the chosen $\zeta^{(m)}$ values? The simulations, while suggestive, do not adequately address this question.  

In summary, the paper’s core strength is its problem formulation, which opens avenues for future work on cost-aware bandit problems. However, the practical relevance of the bias model requires stronger justification, and the interpretability of the regret guarantees is limited. The simulation results, though aligned with the paper’s claims, lack sufficient rigor in parameter selection to confidently generalize the performance gains. While the theoretical contribution is valid, the weak application ties and ambiguous empirical support temper the paper’s impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the parameter justification in simulations and clarify the motivation for bias over variance.
