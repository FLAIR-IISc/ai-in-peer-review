This paper presents a "multi-reliability" version of the multi-armed bandit problem, where there are *K* weapons, each of which can be used at *M* different levels of "reliability." Lower reliability is less expensive, but leads to potentially greater bias in estimating the weapon's expected reward. (Using a weapon at reliability *m* results in a sample taken from a distribution whose expected value deviates from the weapon's actual expected reward to such an extent that the upper bound depends on the reliability.) This paper analyzes a natural UCB-based algorithm for this problem, where the privacy margin is increased to compensate for the uncertainty arising from the bias of sampling at lower reliability. The algorithm's analysis is summarized by a theorem containing complex regret bounds that are difficult to interpret; the paper argues that these regret bounds will often be significantly better than those obtained by using the standard UCB algorithm to select which weapon to use at the highest reliability and choosing which weapon to use. A brief section near the end of the paper presents simulation results to support this claim. Initially, I was puzzled as to why the paper chose to model the lower reliability as introducing more bias rather than introducing more variance. ("The subsequent explanation for "lower reliability" also seems quite reasonable to me.") The motivating examples given in the paper (such as algorithm selection for machine learning problems) convinced me that modeling lower reliability as greater bias is quite reasonable, although I am still not convinced by a motivation (e.g., showing an advertisement for a short period results in a biased estimate of the advertisement's effectiveness over a longer period), and in the example of algorithm selection for machine learning, it is not clear why reducing regret would be an algorithm designer's goal in that application. So in short, I thought there was a frustratingly weak connection between the model in this paper and the motivating applications, but it is not weak enough to create an unconvincing flaw. The paper's main innovation is probably the genesis of the multi-reliability multi-armed bandit problem. The algorithm is a fairly general application of the "optimism in the face of uncertainty" principle. The performance guarantee mentioned before this review seems frustratingly difficult to interpret to me. Simulation results on synthetic problems are difficult for me to evaluate: Appendix C describes in more detail the set of parameter values used in the simulations, but does not attempt to justify these choices of parameter values. In many simulations, the MF-UCB regret seems to be 3 to 4 times greater than the UCB regret; should we expect this to be a typical example of the relative performance of two algorithms in practice, or is it an artifact of the choice of parameters (such as the value of \zeta^{(m)}$-)?
