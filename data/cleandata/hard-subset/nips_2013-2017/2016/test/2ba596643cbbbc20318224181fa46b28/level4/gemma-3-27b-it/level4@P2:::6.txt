This paper introduces a novel multi-armed bandit (MAB) setting where evaluating an arm’s reward comes with varying fidelities, each offering a different trade-off between cost and accuracy. Unlike the standard MAB problem, here observing the outcome of each arm can be expensive, but cheaper, albeit biased, approximations are available – a situation common in applications like online advertising where shorter ad displays offer quicker, less precise performance estimates. The authors formalize this as a multi-fidelity bandit problem and propose MF-UCB, a novel upper confidence bound algorithm tailored to this setting. They prove regret bounds for MF-UCB, demonstrating its adaptability to the sequence of available approximations and costs, and establish near-optimality under certain conditions. This work builds upon the well-established UCB framework [Auer, 2002] and extends it to a practical and increasingly relevant scenario.

The core idea – leveraging cheaper approximations to efficiently eliminate suboptimal arms and focus expensive evaluations on promising candidates – is intuitive and compelling. The formalization of the multi-fidelity setting and the associated regret definition are well-motivated and clearly presented. The theoretical analysis, including the lower bound, is a significant contribution and provides a strong justification for the algorithm’s performance. The simulations further support the theoretical findings, showcasing MF-UCB’s superiority over a naive UCB approach. This work is related to existing research on budgeted bandits [Tran-Thanh et al., 2016] and multi-fidelity optimization [Huang et al., 2006], but distinguishes itself by specifically addressing the exploration-exploitation trade-off inherent in the bandit setting with a focus on biased approximations.  Recent work by Kandasamy et al. [2016] extends ideas in this work to Gaussian process payoffs, further highlighting the active research in this area.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The multi-fidelity bandit setting is a valuable and practical extension of the classical MAB problem.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The regret analysis and lower bound provide a strong theoretical foundation for MF-UCB.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the problem, algorithm, and analysis.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The application to online advertising and similar scenarios demonstrates the practical utility of the work.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** Simulations validate the theoretical findings and show improved performance compared to baseline algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Assumption of Known Fidelity Deviation:** The assumption that the deviation between fidelity means (ζ(m)) is known *a priori* is a significant limitation. In many real-world scenarios, accurately estimating these deviations would be challenging. Relaxing this assumption would be a valuable extension.
*   **Complexity of Analysis:** While theoretically sound, the analysis is quite dense and may be difficult for readers unfamiliar with advanced bandit theory to fully grasp.
*   **Limited Scope of Simulations:** The simulations are conducted on synthetic problems. Evaluating MF-UCB on real-world datasets would further strengthen the paper.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a novel and important problem with clear practical applications.
*   The theoretical contributions are significant and well-supported.
*   The empirical results validate the theoretical findings.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The strong assumption of known fidelity deviations limits the applicability of the algorithm in certain scenarios.
*   The complexity of the analysis may hinder its accessibility to a broader audience.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of multi-armed bandits. The novel setting, rigorous theoretical analysis, and promising empirical results warrant acceptance. While the assumption of known fidelity deviations is a limitation, it does not negate the overall significance of the work. Addressing this limitation in future research would further enhance the impact of this contribution.
