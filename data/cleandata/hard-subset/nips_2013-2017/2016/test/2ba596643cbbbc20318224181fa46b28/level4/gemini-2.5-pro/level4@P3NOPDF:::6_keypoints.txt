- Introduces a multi-armed bandit setting with M fidelities where higher fidelities give more accurate but costlier rewards, motivated by online advertising.  
- Central idea: use cheap, low-fidelity pulls to eliminate suboptimal arms and reserve expensive, high-fidelity pulls for promising arms.  
- Contributions: new regret definition for the fidelity setting, a successive-elimination algorithm, theoretical upper and lower regret bounds, and simulation validation.  
- Strength: problem formulation is well-motivated, elegant, and addresses resource-constrained sequential decision-making.  
- Strength: the proposed algorithm is intuitive and appears theoretically sound.  
- Strength: provides both an upper bound for the algorithm and a matching lower bound, demonstrating near-optimality.  
- Strength/novelty: the setting and analysis add a substantive contribution to bandit literature.  
- Main weakness: strong assumption that deviation parameters ε^(m) (max deviation of fidelity m from highest fidelity) are known a priori, which limits applicability.  
- Practical concern: in real-world examples (e.g., short vs. full-day CTR), these ε^(m) values are hard to specify and depend on external factors.  
- Requested analysis: evaluate sensitivity of algorithm and guarantees to mis-specification of ε^(m).  
- Requested addition: discuss practical ways to estimate or bound the ε^(m) parameters.  
- Requested extension: consider relaxing the assumption—e.g., algorithms that learn/adapt ε^(m) online.
