

The paper introduces a multi-fidelity bandit framework where each arm can be pulled at various fidelities, with higher fidelities providing more accurate reward estimates but at higher cost. The authors formalize this as a multi-fidelity bandit problem with known error bounds ζ(m) between fidelities, and propose an algorithm called MF-UCB that adaptively chooses fidelities to minimize regret. They prove that MF-UCB achieves regret bounds that can significantly outperform standard UCB when low-fidelity observations are much cheaper than high-fidelity ones. The paper also provides a lower bound showing MF-UCB is nearly optimal under certain conditions.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to bandit literature by addressing a practical consideration often ignored in theoretical work: the cost of obtaining reward information. This is particularly relevant to applications like online advertising, where lower-fidelity experiments (e.g., showing ads for shorter periods or to smaller audiences) can quickly eliminate poor-performing options before committing to expensive high-fidelity experiments. The problem formulation is well-motivated, and the connection to existing work on bandits and multi-fidelity optimization is clear.

The paper's main strengths include:
- A novel and well-motivated problem formulation addressing an important practical consideration
- A technically sound algorithm (MF-UCB) with a clear theoretical analysis
- A lower bound demonstrating MF-UCB's near-optimality under certain conditions
- Empirical results showing MF-UCB's superiority over UCB in synthetic experiments

However, the paper has several significant weaknesses that need to be addressed:

1. The regret definition is problematic. The authors define regret as R(Λ,A) = Λμ* - Σλ(mt)qt, treating cost as a multiplicative factor rather than an additive component. This is unnatural because cost should directly reduce the total reward, not multiply the reward. For example, in online advertising, the regret should be (total expected clicks if using the best ad) minus (actual clicks obtained minus cost of experiments), not multiplied by cost. The authors' justification using the ad example is insufficient, as the cost should be subtracted from the total reward, not multiplied. A more natural definition would have regret as (total reward at optimal arm) - (actual reward) - (total cost).

2. Assumption 1, requiring ζ(m) to decay sufficiently fast, appears artificial and lacks justification. The authors state this assumption is "not necessary to analyze our algorithm" but is "most appealing" for comparing MF-UCB to UCB. However, the algorithm's design seems heavily dependent on this assumption, and it's unclear why this would hold in practical scenarios. The paper should either provide stronger justification for this assumption or show how to select a subset of fidelities that satisfies it when the full sequence doesn't.

3. The main regret bound (Theorem 2) is difficult to interpret due to its complexity. The bound depends on complicated sets K(m) and parameters like Δ(JkK)k that aren't clearly explained. A more intuitive explanation of how MF-UCB's regret compares to UCB would help readers understand the practical implications. The paper should explicitly state how much better MF-UCB performs under typical conditions.

4. The gap between the lower bound (Theorem 4) and MF-UCB's performance for certain arms (K(m)7) is not adequately addressed. The authors note that MF-UCB may not meet the lower bound for these arms but don't discuss whether this gap is fundamental or could be closed with a different algorithm design.

5. The paper lacks a clear discussion of when the multi-fidelity approach would be most beneficial. The regret bound suggests MF-UCB outperforms UCB when the sets K(m) for m < M are non-trivial and the cost ratio λ(1)/λ(2) is large, but it's not clear how often these conditions hold in practice.

Minor issues include:
- "near-optimal" on line 56 should be "nearly optimal"
- An unnecessary period before "for all m < M" on line 126
- The notation [[k]] should be defined with proper set notation

Overall, the paper addresses an important practical extension to bandit problems, but the theoretical analysis is not as clear or compelling as it could be. The regret definition is particularly problematic, as it doesn't align with standard practice in bandit literature or the motivating applications. The technical arguments are sound but require better explanation to convince readers of the practical value of the approach.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel problem formulation addressing a practical concern in bandit problems
- Clear theoretical analysis showing improved regret under certain conditions
- Strong empirical results demonstrating MF-UCB's superiority over UCB

Cons for acceptance:
- Problematic regret definition that doesn't align with standard practice or motivating applications
- Unjustified Assumption 1 that appears critical to the results
- Overly technical presentation of the main result that makes it difficult to understand practical implications
- Unexplained gap between the lower bound and MF-UCB's performance

The paper has potential but needs significant revisions to address these issues before it could be accepted at NIPS. The core idea is valuable, but the presentation and theoretical justification need strengthening.
