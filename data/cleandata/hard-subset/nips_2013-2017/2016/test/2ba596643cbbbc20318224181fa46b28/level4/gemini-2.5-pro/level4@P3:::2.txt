This paper introduces a novel multi-armed bandit setting where each arm can be evaluated at multiple fidelities. Lower fidelities are cheaper to sample but provide biased approximations of the true reward, while higher fidelities are more expensive but more accurate. The authors formalize this problem with a cost-weighted notion of pseudo-regret. They propose the Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB) algorithm, which intelligently allocates budget by using cheap, low-fidelity pulls to quickly eliminate highly suboptimal arms, reserving expensive, high-fidelity pulls for a smaller set of promising candidates. The primary contributions include the problem formulation, the MF-UCB algorithm, a regret upper bound for the algorithm, and a nearly matching lower bound that demonstrates the near-optimality of the approach. This work builds upon the extensive literature on UCB methods for stochastic bandits by incorporating a practical and challenging new dimension of cost and approximation.

### Strengths

*   **Originality and Significance:** The multi-fidelity bandit formulation is a novel and well-motivated extension of the classical multi-armed bandit problem. It captures a practical trade-off present in many real-world applications, such as online advertising, clinical trials, and algorithm selection, where preliminary, cheaper experiments can inform more expensive ones. This is a significant contribution that could inspire further research in this area.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper provides a solid theoretical analysis of the problem. The MF-UCB algorithm is intuitive and elegant. The analysis, which includes both an upper bound on the algorithm's regret and a corresponding lower bound for the problem, is rigorous and establishes the near-optimality of the proposed method under certain conditions. The proofs, while containing some minor errors in the appendix, appear to be fundamentally sound.
*   **Clarity of the Main Paper:** The main body of the paper is well-written, clear, and effectively organized. It successfully conveys the core intuition behind the problem, the algorithm, and the theoretical results. The conceptual partitioning of the arms (the `K(m)` sets) is a particularly effective device for explaining the behavior of the algorithm and the structure of the regret bound.

### Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement

*   **Clarity of Supplementary Material:** While the main paper is clear, the supplementary material, which contains the bulk of the proofs, has numerous typos and minor errors that significantly hinder readability and the process of verifying the technical claims. A thorough proofreading of the appendix is essential and would greatly improve the overall quality of the submission. A non-exhaustive list of issues is provided below.
*   **Clarity of Theoretical Results:** The use of asymptotic notation (`.` and `&`) in the main paper's theorems, while concise, can be somewhat misleading as it hides dependencies on important constants and algorithm parameters (e.g., the parameter `ρ` in Theorem 2). Including the full, explicit inequalities from the appendix in the main paper would offer greater transparency and provide a more complete understanding of the performance guarantees.
*   **Discussion of Practical Limitations:** The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of the practical challenges and limitations of the proposed method. The algorithm assumes that the fidelity bias bounds `ζ(m)` are known a priori. In many real-world settings, these values would be unknown and need to be estimated. The performance of MF-UCB could be sensitive to the accuracy of these estimates. A discussion on the robustness of the algorithm to misspecified `ζ` values, or potential strategies to address this, would enhance the paper's practical impact.

### Summary

Overall, this paper presents a significant and interesting contribution to the bandit literature. The multi-fidelity setting is novel and practical, the proposed MF-UCB algorithm is intuitive, and the theoretical analysis is solid. Despite the need for careful proofreading of the appendix and a more detailed discussion of practical considerations, the core ideas are strong and well-executed. This work is a valuable contribution that is likely to be of high interest to the NIPS community.
