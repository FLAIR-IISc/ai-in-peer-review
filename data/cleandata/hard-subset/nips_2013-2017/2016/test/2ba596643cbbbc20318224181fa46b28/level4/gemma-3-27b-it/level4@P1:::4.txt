The authors present a novel framework called the Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit. In this problem, arms can be sampled with different levels of fidelity, each associated with a cost. While lower cost, low-fidelity samples are drawn from a distribution approximating the highest fidelity distribution. They propose an algorithm that adapts to the sequence of available approximations and costs, provide a regret analysis, and establish a lower bound for the problem. They also present numerical simulations on synthetic problems demonstrating that the proposed algorithm outperforms UCB. The proposed framework is interesting and has the potential to be impactful if further developed. However, in its current state, I find the paper unsuitable for publication.

1.  The introduction of notations is somewhat confusing. They are dispersed throughout the text, making their definitions difficult to locate. For example, the variable *s* is defined on a page after its first use and is then barely used in the remainder of the paper.
2.  The use of online advertising as an example is occasionally misleading. The paper sometimes draws connections between display time and non-stationarity, which is outside the scope of this work. A different example might be more appropriate (see point 4).
3.  I find it difficult to fully justify the regret analysis in this setting. A standard regret analysis aims to maximize long-term gain and therefore identify the arm with the best reward/cost ratio. Here, the optimal policy always prioritizes the arm with the highest mean reward, regardless of its associated cost. This focus potentially weakens the concept of optimality by requiring sampling the best arm at lower fidelity levels even when not strictly necessary, potentially obscuring the cost of sampling at low fidelities when they are not beneficial.
4.  A sample complexity analysis (or, more appropriately, a cost complexity analysis) might be more relevant in the context of Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandits. This could be motivated by the testing phase of prototypes during new product development. The goal would be to identify the best product with high probability while minimizing the cumulative cost of the testing phase before mass production. Given that companies are often constrained by regulations, contracts, and existing facilities, they are often obligated to utilize low-fidelity approximations before accessing advanced and costly tests.
5.  I also found parts of the text to be unwieldy and could benefit from streamlining. A final pass to improve clarity and conciseness is recommended.

**Arguments Pro Acceptance:**

*   Novel framework and problem formulation.
*   Algorithm with theoretical guarantees (regret bound).
*   Demonstrated empirical improvement over a baseline (UCB).

**Arguments Con Acceptance:**

*   Confusing notation and organization.
*   Questionable relevance of the motivating example.
*   Justification of the regret formulation is not entirely convincing.
*   Lack of a cost-complexity analysis, which would be highly relevant.
*   Overall writing quality could be improved.
