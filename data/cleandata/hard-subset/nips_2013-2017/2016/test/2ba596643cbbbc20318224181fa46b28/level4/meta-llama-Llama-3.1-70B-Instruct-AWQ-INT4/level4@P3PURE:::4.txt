The authors propose a new framework called Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit, which allows arms to be sampled at different levels of fidelity, each associated with a cost. While lower-cost, low-fidelity samples are drawn from a distribution approximating the highest fidelity distribution, the authors provide an algorithm that adapts to the sequence of available approximations and costs, along with its regret analysis and a lower bound for the problem. They also present numerical simulations on synthetic problems, where the proposed algorithm outperforms UCB. The proposed framework is interesting and has the potential to be impactful if further developed. However, in its current state, I found the paper unfit for publication.

Firstly, the introduction of notations is quite confusing, as they are dispersed throughout the paper, making their definitions hard to find. For instance, I did not find a clear definition of $s$ until after the rebuttal, where it was revealed that $s$ is defined one page after its first use and is then barely used in the rest of the paper.

Secondly, the use of advertising as an example is misleading at some points. The authors draw correlations between the display time of an ad and non-stationarity, which is out of context in this paper. Non-stationarity is not relevant to the problem at hand, and it may be beneficial to use a different example, such as the testing phase of prototypes for the conception of new products.

Thirdly, I find it challenging to justify the regret analysis in this setting. A regret analysis typically involves maximizing gain in the long run and finding the arm with the best reward-to-cost ratio. However, in this case, the optimal policy is always the arm with the highest mean, regardless of the associated cost. This weakens the optimal policy by obligating the sampling of the best arm at a lower fidelity level, potentially hiding the cost of sampling low levels when it is not needed.

Fourthly, a sample complexity analysis (or more accurately, a cost complexity analysis) may be useful in the context of Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandits. This can be motivated by the testing phase of prototypes for the conception of new products, where the goal is to find the best product with high probability while minimizing the cumulative cost of the test phase before mass production. Companies are often bound by regulations, contracts, and existing facilities, which obligate them to use low-fidelity approximations before accessing advanced and costly tests.

Lastly, I found some parts of the text unwieldy, and it may be worth revising the paper to make it more streamlined.
