

The authors propose a new framework, Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandits, where arms can be sampled at varying fidelity levels with associated costs. Lower-fidelity samples approximate the highest-fidelity distribution but incur lower costs. They present an adaptive algorithm, a regret analysis, and a problem-specific lower bound, supported by synthetic experiments showing performance gains over UCB. While the framework is promising and potentially impactful, the current paper is unsuitable for publication. Key concerns are:

1.  **Confusing Notation:** Notation definitions are scattered and difficult to locate. For instance, the variable $s$ is introduced without definition and only defined one page later, after its first appearance; it is then rarely used thereafter. This significantly impedes readability.

2.  **Misleading Advertising Example:** The use of advertising as a motivating example is problematic. The paper repeatedly draws connections between ad display time, non-stationarity, and the framework. Non-stationarity is entirely outside the scope of the paper's stationary setting, making these references misleading. A more appropriate example (e.g., product prototyping, as suggested in point 4) should be used.

3.  **Flawed Regret Analysis:** The regret analysis is difficult to justify. Regret minimization inherently aims to maximize long-term gain, requiring the optimal arm to be identified based on the *reward-to-cost ratio*. However, the paper's formulation implicitly assumes the optimal policy is simply the arm with the highest *mean reward*, regardless of cost. This is inconsistent with the cost structure. Crucially, the requirement to sample the optimal arm at lower fidelity *weakens* the optimal policy definition, potentially obscuring the true cost of sampling low-fidelity levels when higher fidelity would be unnecessary. The regret metric does not adequately incorporate the cost dimension.

4.  **Need for Cost-Aware Sample Complexity:** A cost-aware sample complexity analysis would be highly valuable and better suited to the problem's context than regret. For instance, in product development, the goal is often to identify the best prototype with high probability while minimizing the *cumulative testing cost* before mass production. Companies are typically constrained by regulatory, contractual, and infrastructure limitations, forcing them to use low-fidelity approximations before costly high-fidelity tests. Framing the problem around minimizing cost to achieve a target confidence level (e.g., "find the best product with 95% confidence at minimal total test cost") would align more naturally with the multi-fidelity setting and provide clearer practical motivation than regret minimization.

5.  **Unpolished Writing:** Several sections are awkwardly phrased and lack flow. A thorough revision to streamline the exposition and improve clarity is necessary.

The core idea has merit, but the current presentation contains critical flaws in both the technical formulation (particularly the regret analysis) and the supporting narrative (the advertising example). The paper fails to properly integrate cost into the optimization objective, and the motivating example distracts from the technical contribution. Addressing the notation issues, replacing the misleading example, fundamentally rethinking the analysis to incorporate cost appropriately (e.g., via sample complexity), and polishing the prose are essential before the work can be considered for publication. As presented, the paper does not meet the standards of NIPS.
