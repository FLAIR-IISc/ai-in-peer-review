This paper discusses the multi-armed bandit problem, where there is a 'honest' element that students must choose from (a limited set of honest options) to pull a hand. Pulling a hand with a certain honest value yields a stochastic ID reward, whose average lies within the true value of the zetas. Here, the zeta parameter (error) is known beforehand, and the maximum honest level corresponds to zeta = 0. Furthermore, pulling a lower honest value is cheaper (i.e., less expensive) than pulling a higher honest value. The authors have designed the problem of pulling hands sequentially with honest options in such a way that the concept of the net reward is maximized, providing a new algorithm for the problem, and they argue that it can surpass the UCB only at the higher honest level. This paper provides a lower bound for the regret of the proposed algorithm. In my opinion, this paper discusses an interesting and relevant problem – modeling the trade-off between information, cost, and reward in online learning (whether choosing less information is cheaper or more information is more expensive), especially in the case of stochastic bandits. In this sense, it can be useful as a benchmark for improvement. Although the paper seems technically challenging, a major drawback is the lack of sufficient explanation regarding its results and assumptions. The accepted definition of regret seems unusual from at least one perspective – why not penalize resource (or 'cost') usage positively rather than exponentially? The authors of the advertisement-display example encourage their definition, but it may not be the most common. What if the cost of observing the appropriate honest value is positively added to the total reward (or regret)? This is probably a more natural question, and it is good to know. The introduction of assumption 1 seems rather artificial/vague, involving the limitations of the honest parameters given to the student. Why would the authors expect it to be applicable to a general learning problem? More importantly, if the value of zeta_m does not decrease faster than required by assumption 1, is there a general way to choose a subset of honest values to satisfy the assumption and therefore the results (which would help significantly strengthen the paper's message)? Although the authors mention that the assumption is not critical, it seems to have been used in the proof, and my main concern is whether the assumption critically affects the design and performance of the algorithm. It seems that the algorithm's design was made to perform well for any type of zeta configuration, but why these are the 'right' zeta sequences is missing. Again, the gap within the lower bound requires further investigation and redesigning of the proposed strategy. The main results (article 2, the bound for regret for MF-UCB) are not easy to understand due to the highly technical nature of the presentation and because of the comparison with UCB (at the maximum honest level? (This is not explicitly stated in the discussion)). It seems that the relative gain/loss delta for MF-UCB depends on [[k]], which again depends on the complex order of the set \cal{K}^(m). A clear explanation would greatly aid the authors' argument. Overall, I think the paper makes a good start in considering an interesting learning model, but loses clarity quickly when presenting the results.
