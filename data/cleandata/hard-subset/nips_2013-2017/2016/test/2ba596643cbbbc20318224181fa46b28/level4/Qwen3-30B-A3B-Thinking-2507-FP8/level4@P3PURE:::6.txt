

This paper introduces a novel multi-armed bandit framework with multiple fidelity levels, where each higher fidelity provides a more accurate reward approximation at increased cost. The key insight is that leveraging cheaper lower-fidelity evaluations to eliminate suboptimal arms, while reserving expensive higher-fidelity evaluations for promising arms, may yield better performance than standard UCB, which ignores fidelity costs. The setting is motivated by applications such as online advertising, where short-duration ad displays (low fidelity) approximate long-term click behavior at lower cost. The authors propose a new regret definition, a corresponding algorithm with theoretical guarantees (including a regret lower bound), and empirical validation. The theoretical analysis is novel and well-executed. However, the assumption that all $\varepsilon^{(m)}$ (the deviations of the $m$-th fidelity mean from the highest fidelity mean) are known a priori is questionable in practice (e.g., in online advertising). The authors should address how these values can be obtained or whether the assumption can be relaxed.
