Summary
The paper introduces a multi-fidelity extension of the stochastic K-armed bandit, motivated by settings where high-fidelity evaluations (true outcomes) are costly but cheaper, biased approximations are available. Each arm admits M fidelities with costs λ(1) < ... < λ(M), and lower fidelities m < M produce biased observations whose means differ from the high-fidelity mean by at most a known ζ(m). The authors propose a capital-aware notion of regret that rewards actions by the high-fidelity value of the chosen arm while accounting for the costs of the fidelity used.

The main contribution is MF-UCB, an upper-confidence-bound algorithm that (i) constructs for each arm a set of fidelity-specific optimism bounds on the high-fidelity mean by adding the bias offsets ζ(m), and (ii) adaptively chooses the fidelity at which to sample via thresholds γ(m) that trade off the cost of higher fidelities against the informativeness of lower ones. The analysis shows that MF-UCB concentrates exploration at low fidelities to eliminate clearly suboptimal arms and reserves high-fidelity plays for a small set of near-optimal candidates.

The authors derive a regret bound that improves over naive UCB running solely at the highest fidelity by a factor that reflects the cost ratio and the number of arms filtered out at lower fidelities. A complementary lower bound shows that MF-UCB is nearly optimal, with a small gap for a subset of arms where lower-fidelity signals are only marginally informative relative to the switching thresholds. Simulations on synthetic problems illustrate consistent improvements over standard UCB.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on classical UCB methods (Auer et al., Bubeck & Cesa-Bianchi) and is distinct from budgeted bandits (where reward/cost ratios per arm are optimized) and from multi-fidelity optimization in engineering and RL (e.g., Cutler et al.) that do not formalize regret in this way. The weak/strong labeler setting in active learning (Zhang & Chaudhuri) is conceptually related but addresses a different objective. The authors’ own follow-on work on GP bandits extends these ideas to structured payoffs. To my knowledge, the costed, biased, multi-fidelity regret formulation and MF-UCB’s analysis are novel.

Strengths
- Clear formalization of a realistic variant of bandits with biased, cheaper proxies and explicit costs.
- Elegant algorithmic design: a conservative, bias-aware UCB with fidelity selection guided by theoretically motivated thresholds γ(m).
- Solid theory: an upper bound that recovers UCB behavior on near-optimal arms while saving cost on clearly suboptimal arms, and a lower bound that is tight up to a well-explained gap.
- Good intuition: the partition K(1), …, K(M) makes clear which arms are filtered at which fidelity; the analysis aligns with the qualitative behavior seen in simulations.
- Simulation results consistently support the claimed advantages.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Assumption of known bias bounds ζ(m): In many applications, tight bounds on fidelity bias are unknown or only partially known. Please discuss how MF-UCB performs when ζ(m) are misspecified (over- or underestimates) and whether adaptive or data-driven estimates could be used without breaking guarantees.
- Regularity Assumption 1: The appeal of MF-UCB over UCB is strongest under this condition. While the paper notes this, more guidance on when the condition is likely to hold in practice (e.g., how fast the bias must decay relative to ψ) would help readers assess applicability.
- Concentration assumptions and ψ: The results are presented for general ψ with examples for sub-Gaussian rewards. Clarify what is required in practice (e.g., knowledge of ν, ψ), and whether any tuning is needed beyond ρ.
- Lower bound gap: The identified gap for K(m)7 is insightful. It would be useful to quantify its practical impact (e.g., how often do such arms arise under natural parameter regimes?) and to comment on whether simple heuristics (e.g., occasional low-fidelity rechecks) could reduce the gap without sacrificing overall savings.
- Regret notion: The choice to evaluate reward at the highest fidelity while paying the cost of the sampled fidelity is reasonable for many applications (e.g., ads), but alternative notions may be appropriate in others (e.g., when low-fidelity plays have zero or different reward semantics). The paper briefly mentions this; adding a short discussion on how MF-UCB might adapt to those variants would broaden the impact.
- Experiments: Comparisons are only to high-fidelity UCB. It would strengthen the empirical section to include:
  - Stronger baselines (e.g., KL-UCB at high fidelity, Thompson sampling variants, simple multi-fidelity heuristics such as two-stage elimination).
  - Sensitivity to cost ratios and ζ(m); robustness to misspecified ζ(m).
  - Ablations on γ(m) and ρ; number of fidelities M; and a larger K regime demonstrating scalability.
  - If possible, a semi-synthetic or real task illustrating the practical workflow (e.g., ads, algorithm selection, or simulation-to-reality).

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: The paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs for the main claims and a clear identification of where the algorithm can be suboptimal. Some assumptions (known ζ(m), regularity) limit direct deployability, but are transparent and reasonable for a first formal treatment.
- Clarity: Generally well written and organized. Suggestions: define the regret oracle and the choice of qt more prominently; provide more intuition for the γ(m) threshold; briefly restate the partition K(m) when used in proofs; and collect key notation in a table.
- Originality: High. The multi-fidelity regret formulation with biased proxies and explicit fidelity selection via MF-UCB appears novel and addresses a realistic and underexplored setting.
- Significance: High. The framework captures common scenarios (ads, algorithm selection, simulation-based evaluation) and the results suggest meaningful cost savings. With additional empirical validation and robustness discussion, this work could influence practice and spur follow-on research.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and well-motivated problem formulation.
- Principled algorithm with strong theoretical guarantees.
- Clear insights into when and why multi-fidelity helps.
- Empirical evidence consistent with theory.

Cons against acceptance
- Dependence on known ζ(m) and regularity condition; limited robustness discussion.
- Empirical evaluation is limited to synthetic data and a narrow set of baselines.
- Lower bound gap for a subset of arms remains open.

Recommendation
I lean toward acceptance. The paper makes a substantial and timely contribution by formalizing and addressing multi-fidelity bandits with biased, costed observations. With minor revisions to broaden and deepen the empirical section and to discuss robustness to ζ(m) misspecification, this work would be a strong addition to the program.
