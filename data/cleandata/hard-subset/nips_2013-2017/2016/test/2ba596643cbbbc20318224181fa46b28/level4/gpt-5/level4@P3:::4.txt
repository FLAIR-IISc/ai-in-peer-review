Summary
This paper introduces the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit (MF-MAB) setting, where each arm can be queried at one of M fidelities with increasing cost and decreasing bias with respect to the highest-fidelity reward. The authors assume known fidelity-dependent bias bounds ζ(m) and costs λ(m), and define a cost-weighted pseudo-regret that evaluates rewards at the highest fidelity while accounting for the cost actually incurred at the fidelity played. They propose MF-UCB, an upper-confidence-bound algorithm that maintains per-fidelity confidence bounds, chooses the arm with the tightest optimistic upper bound on the high-fidelity mean, and selects the lowest fidelity whose uncertainty is still above a prescribed threshold γ(m) (chosen to balance cost and informativeness). The analysis shows that MF-UCB uses cheap fidelities to eliminate clearly suboptimal arms and invests expensive high-fidelity pulls only on near-optimal arms. The authors derive a regret upper bound that improves over standard high-fidelity-only UCB by a factor reflecting the use of cheap fidelities, and provide a lower bound showing near-optimality under assumptions. Simulations on synthetic problems corroborate the theoretical trends. The work relates to UCB methods (Auer et al.), budgeted bandits, multi-fidelity optimization in engineering design, reinforcement learning with multi-fidelity simulators, and weak/strong labeling in active learning.

Strengths
- Novel and well-motivated formulation of bandits with biased, cheaper approximations, distinct from budgeted bandits (reward/cost ratio) and from variance-only modeling.
- A simple, principled algorithmic adaptation of UCB to the multi-fidelity setting, with a clear design of fidelity-switching thresholds that trade off cost and information.
- Nontrivial analysis: partition of arms by fidelity, regret upper bound that reflects where cost is spent, and a lower bound that clarifies when the algorithm is near-optimal and where a gap remains.
- Empirical results, albeit synthetic, align with the theoretical intuition: the algorithm concentrates high-fidelity budget on a small set of promising arms.

Weaknesses and suggestions
1) Clarity and organization
- Notation is introduced piecemeal, which makes the paper hard to follow. For example, the variable s (sample count in Xk,s) is used before it is defined, and later appears only sporadically. I recommend a consolidated notation table or an upfront subsection that defines all symbols (arms, fidelities, costs, ζ(m), γ(m), ψ, s, T(m)k,t, etc.) before they are used.
- The paper would benefit from tightening the exposition of the regret definition (equation (1)) and the role of qt. Make explicit early that rewards are evaluated at the highest fidelity regardless of mt, and why this matches the target applications considered.

2) Examples and motivation
- The online advertising example sometimes conflates fidelity with display duration and references possible non-stationarity (time-varying click-through rates). Non-stationarity is not modeled in the theory, which assumes fixed distributions per fidelity. Either revise the example to avoid implying non-stationarity or add a clear caveat. Alternative examples such as engineering product testing (prototype tests of increasing fidelity and cost) or multi-fidelity simulators in robotics better match the assumptions.

3) Objective and regret notion
- The choice of pseudo-regret that always evaluates qt = μ(M)It deserves a more thorough justification and contrast to alternatives. Under the proposed metric, the oracle always plays an optimal arm (at any fidelity) and achieves Λμ*. The only role of lower fidelities is to reduce exploration cost. This is sensible for applications where only high-fidelity outcomes matter, but it differs from budgeted bandits (reward/cost optimization) and from pure-exploration objectives.
- I encourage the authors to (a) clearly position their objective versus budgeted MAB and best-arm identification, (b) discuss settings where their objective is the right one, and (c) at least sketch an alternative “cost complexity” analysis for identification (e.g., minimal capital to identify an ε-optimal arm with high probability), which seems natural for multi-fidelity testing pipelines in industry.

4) Assumptions and practicality
- The algorithm and guarantees rely on known bias bounds ζ(m) and Assumption 1 (fast decay). Please discuss how ζ(m) could be estimated or calibrated in practice, the robustness of MF-UCB if ζ(m) are misspecified, and what happens when Assumption 1 does not hold.
- Provide guidance on setting ρ and the γ(m) thresholds in practice, and report sensitivity analyses in the experiments.

5) Empirical evaluation
- The simulations are helpful but limited. Strengthen the empirical section by (i) including ablations on ζ(m), λ(m), and M; (ii) comparing against stronger baselines (e.g., naïve policies that use a fixed number of low-fidelity pulls per arm before switching); and (iii) showing the distribution of plays per fidelity per arm group to mirror the theoretical partitions K(m).

Quality
- Technical development is careful overall, with a meaningful new regret analysis. The lower bound is informative, and the identified gap (for arms in K(m)7) is discussed candidly. However, clarity issues (notation, examples) and limited empirical breadth detract from the presentation.

Clarity
- The paper is readable in parts but currently unwieldy. Consolidate notation, streamline the introduction (remove or qualify non-stationary remarks), and add a brief intuition box for the choice of γ(m) and the arm partitions.

Originality
- The setting and analysis are original relative to UCB and budgeted MAB, and distinct from multi-fidelity optimization in engineering and RL. The explicit modeling of biased approximations with known bounds is a useful contribution.

Significance
- The ideas have potential impact in domains with staged, cost-tiered evaluations. The theoretical results are solid, and the approach could influence subsequent work (e.g., GP-based multi-fidelity bandits). Additional empirical validation would strengthen the case.

Arguments for acceptance
- Introduces a timely and practically motivated bandit formulation with rigorous analysis.
- Simple, implementable algorithm with interpretable fidelity-switching logic.
- Clear theoretical improvement over naïve high-fidelity-only UCB under reasonable conditions.

Arguments against acceptance
- Clarity/organization issues (notation, examples) hinder accessibility.
- Objective function requires stronger justification and differentiation from alternative, arguably natural, objectives (ratio-based or identification).
- Practical aspects (estimating ζ, parameter tuning, robustness) are under-discussed.
- Empirical evaluation is limited to synthetic data and lacks comprehensive baselines/ablations.

Overall assessment
This is an interesting and potentially impactful paper with solid theory. However, in its current form I am not convinced it meets the bar for NIPS due to clarity issues, limited empirical support, and insufficient justification of the chosen regret notion relative to alternatives. I would lean toward rejection at this stage, with a strong encouragement to revise: streamline the exposition, strengthen and broaden experiments, clarify assumptions and practical tuning, and better position the objective (possibly adding a cost-aware identification analysis). I would be enthusiastic about a revised version addressing these points.
