

This paper introduces a novel multi-armed bandit framework with multiple fidelity levels, where each arm can be evaluated at varying costs (fidelities) to approximate rewards. The core insight is that leveraging cheaper, lower-fidelity evaluations to eliminate suboptimal arms early—reserving higher-fidelity evaluations for promising arms—could yield significant regret improvements over standard UCB, which ignores fidelity costs. The authors formalize this with a new regret definition, propose a fidelity-aware algorithm, provide theoretical guarantees (including a regret lower bound), and validate via simulations. The technical contributions are well-motivated and the analysis demonstrates clear novelty in adapting bandit theory to fidelity-dependent cost structures.

The setting is compelling for applications like online advertising, where low-cost short-term ad displays (low fidelity) can approximate long-term click behavior (high fidelity), enabling cost-efficient exploration. However, a critical practical limitation arises from the assumption that the deviations $\varepsilon^{(m)}$ (the gap between the reward mean at fidelity $m$ and the highest fidelity) are known *a priori*. In real-world scenarios (e.g., online advertising), $\varepsilon^{(m)}$ would typically require historical data or pilot studies to estimate, which may not be feasible during online operation. The authors should address how this assumption could be relaxed—e.g., through adaptive estimation of $\varepsilon^{(m)}$ or by designing algorithms robust to estimation errors. Without such discussion, the practical applicability of the proposed method remains questionable, as the theoretical guarantees rely entirely on known $\varepsilon^{(m)}$.

The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with a well-constructed regret upper bound and matching lower bound. The algorithm design effectively balances exploration at low fidelity and exploitation at high fidelity, and the simulation results align with the theoretical predictions. However, the manuscript would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the trade-offs between fidelity cost and regret reduction in practice, including a brief comparison of computational overhead versus standard UCB.

These corrections are essential for clarity and correctness. The paper’s conceptual contribution is strong, but the assumption of known $\varepsilon^{(m)}$ warrants deeper scrutiny. If the authors can address this limitation—perhaps by proposing a variant that estimates $\varepsilon^{(m)}$ online—the work would significantly strengthen its practical relevance. With minor revisions to the assumption and notation, it would make a valuable contribution to the literature. The analysis is solid, the problem is well-motivated, and the empirical results support the claims. I recommend acceptance pending resolution of the $\varepsilon^{(m)}$ concern and the listed technical corrections.
