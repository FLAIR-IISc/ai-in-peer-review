This paper introduces and investigates a novel multi-armed bandit (MAB) framework incorporating the concept of 'fidelities'. In this setting, a learner can pull an arm at one of $M$ different fidelity levels. Higher fidelities yield reward estimates that are closer to the true mean rewards but incur a greater cost. Conversely, lower fidelities are cheaper but provide noisier approximations.

The central motivation is that an intelligent strategy can leverage this trade-off by using inexpensive, low-fidelity pulls to quickly discard clearly suboptimal arms, reserving the costly, high-fidelity pulls for a smaller, more promising set of candidates. Such an approach has the potential to significantly outperform standard MAB algorithms like UCB, which do not account for this multi-fidelity structure. The authors motivate this problem with practical applications such as online advertising, where short ad displays could serve as a low-cost, low-fidelity proxy for long-term click-through rates.

The paper's contributions are threefold: (1) a new definition of regret tailored to this cost-versus-accuracy setting, (2) a novel algorithm designed to exploit the fidelity structure, accompanied by a rigorous theoretical analysis of its regret bound, and (3) a matching lower bound that establishes the near-optimality of the proposed algorithm. The theoretical findings are further supported by simulation experiments.

The primary strength of this work lies in its formalization of the compelling and practical multi-fidelity MAB problem. The proposed algorithm is elegant and well-motivated, and the theoretical analysis is comprehensive and novel. Providing both an upper regret bound and a problem-specific lower bound is a significant contribution that demonstrates the near-optimality of the approach.

A key assumption in the theoretical analysis is that the fidelity biases, $\varepsilon^{(m)}$ (the deviation of the $m$-th fidelity's mean reward from the true mean), are known to the learner a priori. While this is a reasonable starting point for theoretical analysis, the practical applicability of the algorithm hinges on this knowledge. For instance, in the online advertising example, how would one determine these bias values in advance? It would significantly strengthen the paper if the authors could discuss the practical estimation of these parameters or comment on the robustness of their algorithm to misspecified $\varepsilon^{(m)}$ values. Furthermore, an important direction for future work would be to relax this assumption entirely and develop algorithms that can learn these biases online. Could the authors comment on the potential challenges of such an extension?
