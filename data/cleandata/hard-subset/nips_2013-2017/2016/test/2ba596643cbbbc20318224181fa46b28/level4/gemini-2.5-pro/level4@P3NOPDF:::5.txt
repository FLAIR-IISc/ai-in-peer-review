This paper addresses a compelling variant of the classical stochastic K-armed bandit problem, introducing a setting where low-cost, lower-fidelity approximations of each arm's reward are available. The authors formalize this setting as a multi-fidelity (MF) bandit problem, proposing an algorithm to efficiently leverage these approximations to minimize cumulative regret. The work provides strong theoretical results, including regret bounds, and supports these findings with simulations.

**Strengths**

1.  **Problem Formulation:** The formalization of the multi-fidelity bandit problem is a notable contribution. It elegantly captures a practical and important scenario frequently encountered in real-world applications such as hyperparameter optimization, simulation-based science, and clinical trials, where expensive, high-accuracy evaluations can be supplemented by cheaper, less precise ones. This provides a valuable framework for a problem that has been handled more heuristically in the past.

2.  **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper is grounded in solid theoretical analysis. The authors provide a regret bound for their proposed algorithm that clearly quantifies the benefit of using multiple fidelities. The analysis convincingly demonstrates a theoretical improvement over standard bandit algorithms that only have access to a single, high-cost fidelity. Furthermore, the inclusion of a lower bound analysis is a significant strength, showing that the proposed approach is nearly optimal under the specified assumptions.

3.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The problem motivation is clear, the algorithm is described intuitively, and the main theoretical results are presented in an understandable manner. This clarity makes the paper's contributions accessible to a broad audience within the field.

4.  **Empirical Validation:** The simulation results effectively support the theoretical claims. The experiments clearly demonstrate that the proposed MF bandit method outperforms relevant baselines, especially in regimes where high-fidelity evaluations are significantly more costly than low-fidelity ones. The results validate the core thesis that intelligently querying cheap approximations can lead to substantial gains in learning efficiency.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

While the paper is strong overall, a few points could be clarified or expanded upon to further strengthen the contribution:

1.  **Assumptions on Fidelity Relationship:** The model's performance relies on the assumed relationship between the rewards at different fidelity levels (e.g., shared mean, known bias structure). It would be beneficial for the authors to discuss the sensitivity of the algorithm and its theoretical guarantees to these assumptions. How would the method perform if the relationship were misspecified or more complex? A discussion on the robustness of the approach would be a valuable addition.

2.  **Practical Parameter Estimation:** The proposed algorithm appears to require knowledge of certain parameters related to the costs and statistical properties of the different fidelities. Could the authors comment on the practical application of the method when these parameters are unknown and must be estimated online? This would enhance the paper's practical relevance.

3.  **Comparison to Related Areas:** The paper could be strengthened by positioning itself more clearly with respect to related literature in areas like Bayesian optimization, which has also explored multi-fidelity modeling (e.g., using Gaussian processes). A brief discussion comparing and contrasting the bandit-based approach with these alternative frameworks would provide valuable context for the reader.

**Conclusion**

This is a well-executed paper that tackles an interesting and practical problem. The combination of a novel problem formulation, rigorous theoretical analysis, and clear empirical validation makes it a strong contribution to the bandit literature. The weaknesses identified are minor and represent opportunities for future work or clarification in a final version. I recommend acceptance.
