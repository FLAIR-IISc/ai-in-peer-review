The authors propose a new framework, the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit, in which each arm can be sampled at multiple fidelity levels, each with an associated cost. Lower-fidelity samples are cheaper but come from distributions that approximate the highest-fidelity distribution. They present an algorithm that adapts to the sequence of available approximations and costs, provide a regret analysis and a lower bound, and report numerical simulations on synthetic problems where the proposed algorithm outperforms UCB. The framework is interesting and could be impactful if further developed. However, in its current form I find the paper unfit for publication.

1/ The notation is confusing: definitions are scattered, making them hard to find. For example, I could not locate the definition of s; after the rebuttal, it appears that s is defined one page after its first use and is then barely used in the rest of the paper.

2/ The advertising example is, at points, misleading. The paper repeatedly links ad display time with non-stationarity, but non-stationarity is out of scope here. It may be better to use a different example (see 4/).

3/ I find the regret formulation difficult to justify in this setting. Regret analysis typically aims to maximize long-run gain and thus to identify the arm with the best reward-to-cost ratio. Here, however, the optimal policy is always the arm with the highest mean, regardless of cost. This optimal policy is then weakened by the requirement to sample the best arm at lower fidelity levels, which can obscure the cost of unnecessary low-fidelity sampling.

4/ A sample-complexity (here, cost-complexity) analysis may be more appropriate for multi-fidelity bandits. One motivation is prototype testing in product development: the goal is to identify the best product with high probability while minimizing the cumulative cost of the testing phase before mass production. Because companies are constrained by regulations, contracts, and existing facilities, they are often required to use low-fidelity approximations before accessing advanced and costly tests.

5/ Several parts of the text are unwieldy. A careful editing pass to streamline the presentation would help.
