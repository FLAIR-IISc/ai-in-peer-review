The authors propose a new framework, the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit, in which each arm can be sampled at different fidelity levels with associated costs. Lower-fidelity samples are cheaper but are drawn from distributions that only approximate the highest-fidelity distribution. The paper presents an algorithm that adapts to sequences of available approximations and costs, provides a regret analysis and a lower bound, and reports numerical simulations on synthetic problems where the proposed algorithm outperforms UCB. The framework is interesting and, with further development, could be impactful. However, in its current form I find the paper unfit for publication, for the following reasons.

1/ The notation is introduced in a confusing and scattered way, making definitions hard to locate and follow. For example, I could not find the definition of s when it was first used; after the rebuttal, it appears that s is defined one page after its first appearance and is then barely used in the remainder of the paper. This hinders readability and verification.

2/ The advertising example is, at points, misleading. The paper repeatedly invokes correlations with the display time of ads and non-stationarity, but non-stationarity is outside the scope of this work. This conflation distracts from the core setting studied here. It may be better to use a different motivating example (see 4/).

3/ I find the regret analysis hard to justify in this setting. Standard regret compares cumulative gain to that of an optimal policy, which, under costs, should reflect reward per unit cost (i.e., the best reward/cost ratio in the long run). In the paper’s formulation, however, the optimal policy is always the arm with the highest mean, irrespective of its cost. Moreover, the algorithm is obligated to sample the best arm at lower fidelity levels. This weakens the benchmark and can obscure the true cost of unnecessary low-fidelity sampling. As a result, the regret notion used may fail to capture the fundamental trade-off that motivates multi-fidelity sampling.

4/ A sample-complexity (here, cost-complexity) analysis would be more natural and useful for multi-fidelity bandits. A compelling application is the prototyping phase in product development: the goal is to identify the best product with high probability while minimizing the total testing cost before mass production. Because companies are constrained by regulations, contracts, and existing facilities, they are often required to use low-fidelity approximations before accessing advanced, costly tests. In such settings, high-probability identification under a cost budget (or minimizing cost to achieve a target confidence) aligns directly with practice and would better showcase the value of the framework.

5/ Some parts of the text are unwieldy and difficult to follow. A thorough editing pass to streamline the presentation, consolidate notation, and remove extraneous references (e.g., to non-stationarity) would significantly improve clarity.

In summary, the problem is interesting and the proposed framework has potential, but the current version suffers from unclear notation, a misleading motivating example, a regret formulation that does not reflect the core cost trade-offs of multi-fidelity sampling, and presentation issues. I encourage the authors to consider a cost-complexity perspective (with an appropriate motivating example), tighten the notation and exposition, and realign the performance criteria with the decision-making goals inherent to multi-fidelity bandits.
