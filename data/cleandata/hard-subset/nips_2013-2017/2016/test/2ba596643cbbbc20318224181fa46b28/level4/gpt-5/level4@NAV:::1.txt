The paper studies multi-armed bandits in which the learner, in addition to choosing an arm, selects a fidelity level from a finite set. Pulling an arm at fidelity m yields an i.i.d. stochastic reward whose expectation lies within zeta_m of the true mean; the zeta_m parameters are known beforehand, and the highest fidelity corresponds to zeta = 0. Lower fidelities are cheaper than higher ones. The authors formulate the problem of sequential arm–fidelity selection to maximize a notion of net reward, introduce a new algorithm for this setting, and argue it can outperform UCB run only at the highest fidelity. The paper also provides a lower bound on the regret of the proposed algorithm.

I find the problem interesting and relevant—explicitly capturing the trade-off among information, cost, and reward (choosing low-information but cheap vs high-information but expensive) in online learning, specifically stochastic bandits. In this sense it could serve as a useful benchmark to build upon.

Despite technical soundness, a major shortcoming is the lack of clear explanation of results and assumptions. The chosen regret definition feels somewhat unnatural from one viewpoint—why penalize resource usage multiplicatively rather than additively? The ad-display example supports their choice but may not generalize. It would be helpful to know what changes when the cost of a chosen fidelity is incorporated additively into total reward (or regret), which arguably seems more natural.

Assumption 1 appears somewhat artificial/opaque, imposing constraints on the fidelity parameters available to the learner. Why should one expect it to hold in general? More importantly, if the zeta_m do not decay as quickly as Assumption 1 requires, is there a general procedure for selecting a subsequence of fidelities that satisfies the assumption and hence preserves the results? This would significantly strengthen the paper’s message. Although the authors claim the assumption is not crucial, it is used in the proofs; my main concern is whether it critically affects the algorithm’s design and performance. The algorithm seems tuned to particular zeta configurations, yet a justification for why these are the “right” zeta sequences is missing.

The gap in the lower bound also merits further investigation and possibly a redesign of the proposed strategy.

Interpreting the main result (Thm. 2, the regret bound for MF-UCB) and contrasting it with UCB (presumably at the highest fidelity—this is not stated explicitly) is difficult given the technical presentation. The relative gain/loss of MF-UCB appears to depend on Delta and [[k]], which in turn depend on the rather intricate sequence of sets K^(m). A more explicit and accessible explanation would materially help the authors’ case.

Overall, the paper starts from a compelling learning model but quickly loses clarity in presenting and interpreting the results.
