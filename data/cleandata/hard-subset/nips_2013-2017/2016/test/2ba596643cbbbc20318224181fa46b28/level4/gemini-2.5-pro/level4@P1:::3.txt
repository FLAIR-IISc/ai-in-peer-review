This paper introduces a multi-fidelity version of the stochastic multi-armed bandit problem. In this setting, each of the K arms can be played at M different fidelity levels. Playing at a lower fidelity is less costly but yields a reward estimate with a potentially larger bias; specifically, the expected reward at a given fidelity is guaranteed to be within a known, fidelity-dependent bound of the true (highest-fidelity) expected reward. The authors propose MF-UCB, a natural UCB-based algorithm for this problem, which adjusts the confidence bounds to account for the uncertainty introduced by the bias from lower-fidelity samples. The paper provides a theoretical analysis with regret bounds, arguing that this approach can significantly outperform a standard UCB algorithm that only ever plays arms at the highest fidelity. This claim is supported by simulation results on synthetic problems.

The primary contribution and main strength of the paper is the formulation of the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit problem itself. This is an interesting and well-motivated extension of the classical bandit setting, capturing a practical trade-off between the cost of an action and the quality of the information it provides.

However, the paper has several weaknesses. The connection between the theoretical model and the motivating applications feels somewhat tenuous. While the choice to model lower fidelity as increased bias rather than increased variance is plausible, some of the specific examples are not fully convincing. For instance, the contention that displaying an ad for a shorter time interval yields a biased (as opposed to simply noisier) estimate of its long-term effectiveness is debatable. Furthermore, for the application of algorithm selection, the appropriateness of regret minimization as the primary objective is not well-justified.

From a technical standpoint, the proposed MF-UCB algorithm is a fairly straightforward application of the "optimism in the face of uncertainty" principle to the new problem formulation. The theoretical performance guarantees are presented as complex regret bounds that are difficult to interpret, which makes it challenging to extract clear insights into how the algorithm's performance scales with key problem parameters. Finally, the experimental validation is difficult to assess. While the appendix provides the parameter values used in the simulations, it offers no justification for these choices. Consequently, it is unclear whether the reported performance improvement of MF-UCB over standard UCB (by a factor of 3 to 4) is typical of what one might expect in practice, or if it is an artifact of a parameterization that is particularly favorable to the proposed method.
