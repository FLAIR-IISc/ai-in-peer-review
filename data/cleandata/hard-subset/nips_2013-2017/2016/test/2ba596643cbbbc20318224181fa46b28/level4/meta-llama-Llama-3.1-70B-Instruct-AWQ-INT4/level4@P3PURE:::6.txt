This paper introduces a novel multi-armed bandit setting where the learner can choose to play an arm at one of $M$ available fidelities. Each successive fidelity provides a better approximation of the rewards but incurs a larger cost. A strategy that effectively utilizes lower and cheaper fidelities to eliminate sub-optimal arms, while reserving higher and more expensive fidelities for a small subset of good arms, may outperform the standard UCB algorithm that neglects these fidelity characteristics. This setting has potential applications in online advertising, where displaying ads for a short duration can approximate long-term clicks while considering cost constraints.

The authors propose a new regret definition for this problem and present an algorithm with theoretical analysis, including a regret lower bound. They also conduct simulations to demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach. The analysis is novel and contributes to the field.

However, there are some concerns regarding the practicality of the proposed approach. Specifically, the analysis assumes that the deviations of the $m$th fidelity mean from the highest fidelity mean, denoted as $\varepsilon^{(m)}$, are known in advance. It is unclear how these values can be obtained in practice, such as in the context of online advertising. Relaxing this assumption would strengthen the results.
