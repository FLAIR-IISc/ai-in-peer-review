Summary
The paper introduces a multi-armed bandit setting with M fidelities. Each higher fidelity offers a more accurate approximation to the true (highest-fidelity) rewards but comes at a higher cost. A strategy that uses cheaper, lower fidelities to eliminate many suboptimal arms and reserves the expensive, higher fidelities for a small set of promising arms can outperform standard UCB algorithms that ignore these fidelity-specific characteristics. The setting is motivated by applications such as online advertising, where one might display ads briefly to approximate long-run click performance for cost reasons. The authors define a new regret notion tailored to this setting, propose an algorithm with theoretical analysis (including a regret lower bound), and provide simulation results. The analysis is novel.

Main concern
The analysis assumes that each ε^(m), the deviation between the mean reward at fidelity m and the highest-fidelity mean, is known in advance. In practice (e.g., in the online advertising application), how would one obtain these values? Is it possible to relax this assumption?
