Summary
This paper introduces a multi-fidelity variant of the stochastic K-armed bandit. Each arm can be queried at one of M fidelity levels, with higher fidelities being more accurate but more expensive. Lower-fidelity observations are biased approximations of the highest-fidelity reward, with known bias bounds |µ(M)k − µ(m)k| ≤ ζ(m) that shrink with m. The authors define a cost-weighted notion of pseudo-regret under a total capital Λ, where, importantly, the instantaneous “reward” is always measured with respect to the highest-fidelity mean of the chosen arm, regardless of the fidelity actually used. They propose MF-UCB, which maintains per-fidelity upper confidence bounds B(m)k,t that include the bias offset ζ(m), chooses the arm with the tightest bound, and selects the fidelity via thresholds γ(m) that trade off cost and information gain. They prove an upper bound showing MF-UCB uses low fidelities to eliminate clearly suboptimal arms and reserves expensive high-fidelity plays for a small set of promising arms; they also provide a lower bound and argue near-optimality under certain conditions (e.g., their regularity assumption on ζ(m)). Simulations on synthetic problems show MF-UCB outperforms vanilla UCB in cumulative pseudo-regret.

Strengths
- Timely and interesting problem formulation that explicitly models biased, cheaper approximations with costs.
- Simple, implementable algorithm extending UCB with fidelity-aware confidence bounds and a principled fidelity-switching rule.
- Nontrivial theory: an upper bound that improves over standard UCB when many arms can be screened at low cost, plus a lower bound and a discussion of where the bound is tight or loose.
- Empirical evidence on synthetic tasks aligns with the theory and illustrates the intended behavior (aggressive pruning at low fidelities).

Weaknesses and suggestions
1) Notation and clarity: The notation is dispersed and hard to follow, and several definitions appear after their first use. For example, the definition of s (the number of samples) appears a page after it is first used and is then rarely used subsequently. Please collect key notation in a single table or subsection and ensure each symbol is defined at first use. Streamlining these choices would greatly improve readability.

2) Misleading application example: The online advertising example repeatedly invokes correlations with display time and nonstationarity, which are out of scope for the stationary stochastic setting analyzed here. This distracts from the core contribution. Consider replacing or de-emphasizing this example in favor of one where stationarity is plausible (see item 4).

3) Objective and regret definition: I find it hard to justify the regret criterion in this setting. In long-run decision-making with costs, the natural objective optimizes reward per unit cost (or revenue under a budget), which leads to reward/cost trade-offs. In this paper, however, the optimal policy under the authors’ pseudo-regret is always to play the arm with the highest mean, regardless of cost, and the algorithm is effectively required to sample the best arm at lower fidelities as part of its exploration strategy. This can hide the cost of low-fidelity sampling when it is not needed and conflates exploration with a cost-aware objective. While the analysis is internally consistent, the chosen regret notion feels misaligned with the motivating applications. At minimum, a stronger justification and discussion of alternatives (e.g., reward-per-cost regret, budgeted bandits) would help.

4) Alternative evaluation: A fixed-confidence, cost-complexity (sample-complexity) objective seems especially natural here. For example, in prototyping and product design, the goal is to identify the best product with high probability while minimizing total testing cost before deployment. Companies are often constrained to use low-fidelity tests before accessing expensive facilities, which matches your multi-fidelity structure. An analysis of the cost to identify the best arm with high confidence would fit this use case better than the current cumulative pseudo-regret.

5) Writing and organization: Some passages are unwieldy. Beyond consolidating notation, I recommend: clarifying the stationarity assumptions and removing references that imply nonstationarity; explicitly motivating the choice qt = µ(M)It and contrasting it with alternatives; and tightening the presentation of assumptions (e.g., Assumption 1) and their practical implications.

Quality
- The theoretical analysis appears careful and technically sound within the chosen framework, with clear use of concentration inequalities and fidelity-dependent confidence bounds. However, the central modeling choice for regret is weakly justified and at odds with the stated applications, which undermines the perceived quality of the contribution.

Clarity
- Currently below NIPS standards due to dispersed notation and confusing examples. A thorough editorial pass and a reorganized notation section are needed.

Originality
- The multi-fidelity bandit formalism with known bias envelopes and fidelity costs is novel and distinct from budgeted bandits and weak/strong labeler settings. The fidelity-aware UCB design is a natural but nontrivial extension.

Significance
- Potentially impactful, as many real-world settings rely on low-fidelity proxies. However, the impact is limited by the mismatch between the regret objective and realistic goals in such applications. A cost-complexity identification analysis would substantially strengthen the paper.

Pros for acceptance
- Interesting and timely problem setting.
- Clean algorithm with intuitive behavior.
- Nontrivial upper and lower bounds; empirical support.

Cons against acceptance
- Regret definition and optimality notion are weakly justified relative to applications; cost/benefit trade-off is not modeled in the objective.
- Clarity issues: dispersed notation; confusing example invoking nonstationarity.
- Missing analysis for arguably more relevant objectives (fixed-confidence identification/cost complexity).

Recommendation
- Reject in its current form. The framework is promising and could be impactful with clearer exposition and a better-aligned objective (e.g., reward-per-cost regret or fixed-confidence cost complexity). I encourage the authors to (i) reorganize and streamline notation, (ii) replace the advertising example, (iii) substantially strengthen the motivation for their regret choice or adopt a cost-aware objective, and (iv) add a cost-complexity identification analysis motivated by prototyping or similar applications.
