This paper introduces a novel framework for Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandits (MF-MAB), addressing scenarios where arms can be sampled at varying fidelity levels with associated costs. The authors present an algorithm that adapts to sequences of approximations and costs, accompanied by a regret analysis and a problem-specific lower bound. Empirical evaluation on synthetic problems demonstrates performance gains over UCB. While the framework shows promise and has the potential for significant impact with further development, I currently find the paper unsuitable for publication. My concerns fall into several categories detailed below.

First, the notation is presented in a confusing and disjointed manner. Definitions are scattered throughout the text, making them difficult to locate. For example, the variable ‘s’ is introduced without definition and only appears defined a page after its initial use, after which it is scarcely utilized. A more consistent and centralized presentation of notation is crucial for clarity.

Second, the paper’s use of advertising as a motivating example is occasionally misleading. The connection drawn between ad display time and non-stationarity feels out of place and detracts from the core MF-MAB problem. The concept of non-stationarity is not central to the analysis and introduces unnecessary complexity to the motivating scenario. A different, more focused example might better illustrate the framework’s benefits. Consider, for instance, a scenario involving iterative prototyping and testing, as discussed in point 4.

Third, the justification for the regret analysis within this setting is not entirely convincing. In standard MAB problems, regret analysis focuses on maximizing long-term cumulative reward. However, in this MF-MAB formulation, the optimal policy appears to consistently favor the arm with the highest mean reward, irrespective of its associated cost. This inherently prioritizes reward and potentially obscures the cost incurred by sampling lower-fidelity arms, even when such sampling is not strategically beneficial. The regret metric, as currently presented, may not accurately reflect the trade-off between reward and cost central to the problem.

Fourth, a sample (or, more appropriately, *cost*) complexity analysis would be highly valuable. This could be motivated by practical applications such as product development, where the goal is to identify the best design with high probability while minimizing the cumulative testing cost before mass production. This is particularly relevant as organizations frequently operate under constraints—regulatory, contractual, or logistical—that necessitate the use of lower-fidelity approximations before committing to expensive, high-fidelity evaluations. Analyzing the cost complexity directly addresses this real-world limitation. 

Finally, several sections of the text are awkwardly worded and could benefit from revision. A thorough editing pass focused on streamlining the prose would significantly improve the paper’s readability and impact.
