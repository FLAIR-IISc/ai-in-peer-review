- Introduces a multi-fidelity stochastic K-armed bandit where each arm has M biased fidelities with known bias bounds ζ(m) and defines a cost-weighted pseudo-regret relative to the highest fidelity.  
- Proposes MF-UCB, an adaptive UCB-style algorithm that uses cheap fidelities to eliminate clearly suboptimal arms and reserves expensive plays for promising arms.  
- Provides theoretical upper bounds showing improved scaling over naive UCB that ignores low-fidelity information.  
- Gives a complementary lower bound (specialized to Bernoulli rewards) showing MF-UCB is nearly optimal under certain conditions.  
- Empirical simulations on synthetic data match the theoretical predictions (reduced regret slope and fewer high-fidelity queries).  
- Builds on classical UCB and relates to budgeted bandits and multi-fidelity optimization, but differs by explicitly modeling biased fidelities with known ζ(m) and cost-weighted regret.  
- Clear problem formulation and principled, cost-aware regret measure tied to the highest fidelity.  
- Algorithm design is intuitive and well-motivated, with thresholds γ(m) balancing cost vs. informativeness.  
- Solid theoretical analysis: recovers UCB behavior on hard arms while saving cost on easy arms; thoughtful fidelity-partition regret decomposition.  
- Lower bound analysis clarifies when multi-fidelity information helps and where gaps remain.  
- Practicality concern: MF-UCB requires known ζ(m) and concentration ψ; robustness to misspecification or unknown ψ is not addressed.  
- Dependence on Assumption 1 (decay of ζ(m)) is critical for comparisons to UCB; performance when violated is not discussed.  
- Lower bound limitations: specialized to Bernoulli rewards and leaves gaps for certain arm classes (K(m)7); unclear if adaptive switching could close gaps without extra high-fidelity pulls.  
- Experimental limitations: only synthetic evaluations and modest baselines; lacks realistic case studies (e.g., ad allocation or algorithm selection).  
- Clarity issues: main paper is readable but the supplement contains many typographical and notational errors that impede verification; some important constants (notably ρ-dependence) are hidden in asymptotic notation.  
- Technical quality: development appears sound and proofs convincing once typos are fixed, but sensitivity to ζ(m) and specialized lower-bound assumptions are caveats.  
- Originality: the multi-fidelity bandit formalization, regret definition, and MF-UCB algorithm are novel relative to prior work.  
- Significance: relevant and impactful for settings with low-cost biased proxies; likely to influence cost-aware and structured bandit research.  
- Reasons to accept: well-motivated new setting, clear theoretical contributions (upper and lower bounds), and a simple, interpretable algorithm with provable gains.  
- Reasons to reject: under-discussed practical applicability (unknown/misspecified ζ, choice of ρ), limited empirical validation, and numerous supplement errors.  
- Key suggestions: discuss robustness to misspecified ζ and online estimation, provide guidance for selecting ρ and handling unknown ψ, clarify behavior when Assumption 1 fails, add realistic case studies and stronger baselines, and expose key constants (especially ρ) in main theorems.  
- Supplement errata: numerous specific typos and notational inconsistencies listed (equation/label fixes, symbol replacements, logical implications, and section headings) that require correction.
