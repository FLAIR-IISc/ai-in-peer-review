This paper introduces the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit (MF-MAB) problem, a novel extension of the classic stochastic bandit setting. In this formulation, each of K arms can be pulled at one of M fidelities. Lower fidelities are cheaper but yield reward estimates that are biased relative to the true, highest-fidelity reward. The degree of bias is assumed to be bounded by a known, fidelity-dependent quantity. The authors propose MF-UCB, an algorithm that adapts the UCB principle to this setting. It incorporates the bias bounds into the confidence intervals to guide exploration, using cheaper, low-fidelity pulls to quickly eliminate suboptimal arms and reserving expensive, high-fidelity pulls for a smaller set of promising candidates. The paper provides a theoretical analysis with upper and lower regret bounds, arguing that MF-UCB can significantly outperform standard UCB strategies that only use the highest fidelity. This claim is supported by experiments on synthetic data.

### Strengths

*   The primary contribution of this work is the novel and well-motivated formulation of the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit problem. The framework for trading evaluation cost against known observational bias is relevant to many practical applications where approximate or preliminary measurements are available.
*   The proposed MF-UCB algorithm is an elegant and intuitive extension of the classic UCB algorithm. It correctly applies the "optimism in the face of uncertainty" principle to the multi-fidelity context.
*   The paper presents a complete piece of work, including the problem formulation, an algorithm, a detailed theoretical analysis of regret, and an empirical evaluation.

### Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement

*   **Connection to Applications:** The connection between the proposed model and the motivating applications could be strengthened. The choice to model lower fidelity as introducing bias rather than higher variance is interesting, and some examples (like algorithm selection) are convincing. However, other examples are less so. For instance, the claim that shorter ad display times lead to biased (rather than simply noisier) estimates of long-term performance is not fully substantiated. Similarly, for the application of algorithm selection, it is not immediately clear that regret minimization is the most appropriate objective. Clarifying these connections or providing more concrete, compelling examples would improve the paper's impact.
*   **Clarity of Theoretical Results:** While the paper provides a detailed theoretical analysis, the resulting regret bounds are complex. This complexity makes it difficult to extract a clear, intuitive understanding of when and by how much MF-UCB is expected to outperform standard UCB. The authors could improve clarity by providing a simplified corollary or a more thorough discussion that isolates the key interplay between costs (λ), bias bounds (ζ), and reward gaps (Δ) that determines the magnitude of the performance gain.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The simulation results are promising, but their interpretation is challenging without more context on the parameter choices. The paper demonstrates a 3-4x improvement in regret over UCB, but it is unclear if this is representative of typical scenarios or an artifact of the specific parameter settings (e.g., the decay rate of ζ(m), the cost increase of λ(m)). To make the results more convincing, the authors could either justify why their chosen parameters reflect realistic problem instances or provide a sensitivity analysis showing how the relative performance of the algorithms varies with these parameters. Without this, it is difficult to assess whether the reported gains are typical or specific to a favorable experimental setup.

In summary, the paper introduces an interesting and original problem formulation and proposes a natural UCB-style algorithm. The main weaknesses lie in the justification for the model's applicability and the interpretability of the theoretical and empirical results. While the core idea is valuable, the paper would be stronger if it provided a clearer link to practice and more accessible insights into the algorithm's performance.
