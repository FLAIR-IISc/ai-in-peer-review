The paper introduces a "multi-fidelity" version of the multi-armed bandit problem in which there are K arms, each of which can be played at M different levels of "fidelity". Lower fidelity is less costly, but leads to potentially greater bias in the estimate of the arm's reward. The paper analyzes the natural UCB-based algorithm for this problem, where the width of the confidence interval is increased to compensate for the uncertainty stemming from the bias of sampling at low fidelity.

The analysis of the algorithm is summarized by theorems containing regret bounds that provide insight into the algorithm's performance. The paper argues that this regret bound will often be significantly better than the regret bound attained by always playing arms at the highest fidelity and using the standard UCB algorithm to select which arm to play. A brief section near the end of the paper presents simulation results to substantiate this claim.

The authors provide a clear and well-motivated formulation of the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit problem, which is the main innovation in the paper. The algorithm, MF-UCB, is a fairly typical application of the "optimism in the face of uncertainty" principle, but its analysis is thorough and well-executed.

One potential weakness of the paper is the connection between the model and the motivating applications. While the authors provide some examples, such as algorithm selection for machine learning problems, the connection between the model and these applications could be stronger. Additionally, the simulation results on synthetic problems are somewhat difficult to assess, as the choice of parameter values is not thoroughly justified.

Overall, the paper provides a solid contribution to the field of multi-armed bandits, and the authors have made a good effort to analyze and evaluate their algorithm. However, some further work on justifying the choice of parameter values in the simulations and strengthening the connection to motivating applications would be beneficial.
