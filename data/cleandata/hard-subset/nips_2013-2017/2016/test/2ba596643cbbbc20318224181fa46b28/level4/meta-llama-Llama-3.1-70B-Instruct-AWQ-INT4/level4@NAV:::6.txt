This paper introduces a novel multi-armed bandit framework. In this setting, the learner can choose from $M$ distinct fidelities to play an arm, with each successive fidelity offering a more accurate reward approximation at the cost of increased expense. By leveraging lower-cost fidelities to eliminate suboptimal arms and reserving higher-cost fidelities for a subset of promising arms, a strategy can potentially outperform the standard UCB algorithm, which disregards these fidelity characteristics. This framework has practical applications, such as online advertising, where displaying ads for a short duration can approximate long-term click-through rates while minimizing costs. The authors propose a new regret definition, develop an algorithm, and provide theoretical analysis, including a regret lower bound, accompanied by simulation results. The analysis presents a fresh perspective on the subject.

However, a critical assumption is that each $\varepsilon^{(m)}$, representing the deviation of the $m$th fidelity mean from the highest fidelity mean, is known in advance. In practical applications, such as online advertising, it is unclear how these values would be determined. Is it possible to relax this assumption?
