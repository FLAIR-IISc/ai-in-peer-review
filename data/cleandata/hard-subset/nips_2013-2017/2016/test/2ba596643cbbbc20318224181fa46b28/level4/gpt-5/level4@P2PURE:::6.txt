This paper introduces a new multi-armed bandit setting with M fidelity levels at which the learner can pull an arm. Higher fidelities yield more accurate approximations to the true rewards but incur higher costs. A strategy that uses cheaper, lower fidelities to quickly eliminate many suboptimal arms, while reserving the expensive, higher fidelities for a small set of promising arms, can potentially outperform standard UCB algorithms that ignore this fidelity structure. The setting is motivated by applications such as online advertising, where one might display an ad for only a few minutes to estimate its long-run click-through behavior in a cost-effective way. The authors propose a new regret notion, present an algorithm with theoretical guarantees (including a regret lower bound), and provide simulations. The analysis appears novel.

A key assumption is that each ε^(m), the deviation of the m-th fidelity mean from the highest-fidelity mean, is known a priori. How would these values be obtained in practice (e.g., in the online advertising application)? Can this assumption be relaxed?
