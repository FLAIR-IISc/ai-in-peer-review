- Paper introduces MF-MAB where arms have M fidelities with increasing cost and decreasing bias, and proposes MF-UCB to use cheap fidelities to screen arms and expensive ones for promising arms.  
- Defines a cost-weighted pseudo-regret benchmarked against the highest-fidelity mean and provides upper and near-matching lower bounds; synthetic experiments show improvements over vanilla UCB.  
- Builds on classical stochastic bandits/UCB literature and differs from budgeted bandits, weak/strong supervision, and prior multi-fidelity works by modeling biased lower-fidelity observations in an online bandit regret setting.  
- Strength: novel, practical problem formalization capturing low-cost biased proxies guiding exploration before expensive evaluations.  
- Strength: simple, interpretable MF-UCB algorithm consistent with UCB principles.  
- Strength: theoretical analysis offers a nontrivial upper bound explaining fidelity filtering and a lower bound showing near-optimality for many arms.  
- Strength: empirical results show fewer expensive pulls on clearly suboptimal arms and improved cost-adjusted regret in synthetic tasks.  
- Weakness: scattered notation and presentation; many symbols introduced after use and should be consolidated (e.g., ζ(m), γ(m), ψ, T(m)k,t).  
- Weakness: running example (online advertising) conflates bias with non-stationarity/context, risking confusion; recommend replacing with more stationary examples.  
- Weakness: objective choice qt = µ(M)It is conceptually problematic because it benchmarks against highest-fidelity mean and ignores reward-per-cost trade-offs; alternative objectives may be more appropriate.  
- Weakness: suggests an alternative pure-exploration best-arm identification (cost-complexity) formulation would better match many practical scenarios.  
- Weakness: writing and organization need restructuring—introduce setup and notation before algorithm, give intuition before formal rules, and separate lemmas from sketches.  
- Technical concern: Assumption 1 on ζ(m) decay may be tailored for analysis—need discussion of necessity, plausibility, and sensitivity when it fails.  
- Technical concern: experiments limited to synthetic data; need robustness checks across ζ/λ schedules and realistic proxy-vs-target benchmarks.  
- Technical concern: clarify how qt maps to application semantics (e.g., why short-horizon ad metrics are biased yet informative).  
- Suggestions: consolidate notation, revise the running example, expand discussion of objective choices, consider adding a cost-complexity result, and run ablations varying ζ(m), λ(m), M and additional baselines.  
- Pros for acceptance: timely, interesting formulation; clean algorithm; insightful analysis with near-optimal guarantees; consistent empirical gains.  
- Cons for acceptance: insufficient justification of objective qt; presentation and notation issues; examples may conflate bias with non-stationarity; limited experimental validation.  
- Overall recommendation: promising but not ready for acceptance—needs stronger objective justification or identification angle, improved clarity/notation, refined examples, and expanded experiments.
