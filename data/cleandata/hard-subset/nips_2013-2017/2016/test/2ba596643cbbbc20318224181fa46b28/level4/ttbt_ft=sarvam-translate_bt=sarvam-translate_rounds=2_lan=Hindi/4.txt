The author has provided a new framework called Multi-Fidelity Multi-Armed Bandit. In this framework, weapons can be sampled with varying levels of fidelity, and each has a cost associated with it. While weapons with lower costs are sampled with lower fidelity, weapons with lower fidelity are sampled from the expected distribution of the highest fidelity distribution. This provides an algorithm adapted to the available estimates and cost sequences, an analysis of its regret, and a lower bound of the problem. They provide numerical simulations on synthetic problems where the proposed algorithm performs better than UCB. The proposed framework is interesting and could be impactful if further developed. However, in this state, I found this paper unsuitable for publication.

1/ The introduction to the notation is quite confusing; they are very scattered, and it is difficult to find their definitions. For example, I could not find what $s$ is (after fragmentation, it seems that $s$ is defined after 1 page of its first use and then used hardly in the rest of the paper).
2/ Using advertising as an example is misleading in some points. In many places, performance time and the non-stability of advertisements have been correlated. Non-stability is out of context in this paper. Using another example might be a good idea (see 4).
3/ I find it difficult to justify the regret analysis in this setting. A regret analysis involves maximizing profit in the long run and thus finding the best-ratio reward/cost. Here, the optimal policy is always the one with the highest mean, regardless of the associated cost. Thus, the optimal policy is weakened by the constraint of sampling the best hand at a lower fidelity level. This can hide the cost of sampling at lower levels when it is not needed.
4/ A sample complexity analysis (here more a cost complexity) might be useful in the context of Multi-Fidelity Multi-Armed Bandits. This could be inspired by the prototype testing phase for new product concepts. The goal is to find the best product with the highest probability, reducing the accumulated cost of the testing phase before mass production. Since companies are bound by regulations, contracts, and existing facilities, they are obligated to use lower fidelity estimates before reaching advanced and expensive testing.
5/ I also found some parts of the text strange. It might be appropriate to take a look at the paper to make it more streamlined.
