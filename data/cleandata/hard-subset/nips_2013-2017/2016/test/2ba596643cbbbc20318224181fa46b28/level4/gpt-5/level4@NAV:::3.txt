The paper proposes a “multi-fidelity” variant of the multi-armed bandit problem with K arms, each playable at M levels of fidelity. Lower fidelities are cheaper but can introduce greater bias in estimating an arm’s reward. Specifically, pulling an arm at fidelity m yields a sample from a distribution whose expected value can deviate from the arm’s true mean by a fidelity-dependent upper bound. The paper analyzes a natural UCB-based algorithm for this setting, where the confidence intervals are widened to account for the uncertainty introduced by low-fidelity bias. The analysis is summarized by theorems with complicated regret bounds that are hard to interpret; the authors argue that these bounds will often be substantially better than those obtained by always using the highest fidelity with standard UCB to choose arms. A short section near the end presents simulations to support this claim.

I was initially puzzled by the choice to model lower fidelity as inducing bias rather than increased variance (the latter also seems well motivated). The motivating examples (e.g., algorithm selection for machine learning problems) largely convinced me that treating low fidelity as added bias is reasonable, though I remain unconvinced by one rationale in particular—namely, the claim that showing an ad for a shorter interval yields a biased estimate of its effectiveness over longer intervals—and in the algorithm-selection example, it is not clear why minimizing regret would be the algorithm designer’s objective.

In sum, I found the connection between the model and the motivating applications weaker than I would like, but not so weak as to be disqualifying. The main contribution appears to be the formulation of the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit problem itself. The algorithm is a fairly standard application of the “optimism in the face of uncertainty” principle. The performance guarantees, as noted above, are disappointingly hard to interpret. The synthetic experiments are difficult to assess: Appendix C details the parameter settings used, but does not justify these choices. MF-UCB often improves over UCB by a factor of 3–4 in the simulations; should we expect this to be representative of their relative performance in practice, or is it an artifact of the chosen parameters (e.g., the values of ζ^(m))?
