This work investigates a new stochastic K-armed bandit formulation in which the learner can query any arm at one of M fidelities. Higher fidelities yield more accurate estimates of the true reward but incur greater cost, while lower fidelities are cheaper and biased. The key idea is to exploit inexpensive, biased evaluations to prune clearly suboptimal arms early, reserving costly, high-fidelity pulls for a small set of promising arms—potentially outperforming standard UCB methods that disregard fidelity structure. A motivating example is online advertising, where short displays or restricted audiences approximate long-run click behavior under tight budgets. The paper introduces a tailored notion of regret for this multi-fidelity setting, proposes an MF-UCB algorithm with theoretical guarantees (including a lower bound), and presents simulations. The analysis appears original.

A central modeling assumption is that each ε^{(m)} (the bias between the mth-fidelity mean and the highest-fidelity mean) is known a priori. How would these quantities be obtained in practice (e.g., in the online advertising example)? Can the approach be extended to learn or adapt to ε^{(m)} online, or to proceed with weaker assumptions?
