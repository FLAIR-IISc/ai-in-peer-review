

This paper introduces a novel multi-fidelity bandit framework where each arm can be evaluated at multiple fidelities with varying costs and accuracy. The authors formalize this setting by introducing a cost-weighted regret measure that accounts for both the cost of evaluations and the quality of approximations. They propose MF-UCB, a novel algorithm that adapts UCB principles to the multi-fidelity setting, and provide theoretical guarantees including a regret upper bound and a matching lower bound. The paper is well-motivated by practical applications like online advertising, where lower-fidelity approximations (e.g., shorter ad displays) can be used to quickly eliminate poor-performing ads before investing in expensive high-fidelity evaluations.

The paper makes significant contributions to the bandit literature by formalizing a new problem setting that extends beyond the standard single-fidelity framework. Previous work on bandits (e.g., UCB algorithms [3]) assumes a single fidelity, while related work on multi-fidelity methods [7, 10, 16] focuses on optimization without regret analysis. The authors properly contextualize their work against these prior contributions and demonstrate how their approach differs fundamentally from existing methods.

The strengths of this paper are substantial. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with well-structured proofs that clearly demonstrate the algorithm's advantages over naive UCB strategies. The regret upper bound shows MF-UCB achieves better performance than standard UCB by leveraging lower-fidelity evaluations to eliminate suboptimal arms early, while the lower bound demonstrates near-optimality under certain conditions. The empirical results on synthetic problems corroborate the theoretical findings, showing consistent improvements over UCB across various settings. The paper is well-organized, with clear definitions and a logical flow from problem formulation to analysis to experiments.

The main weakness concerns the assumption that the deviations ζ(m) between fidelities are known in advance. While this is a reasonable technical assumption for the analysis, it may be restrictive in practical applications like online advertising where these deviations are typically unknown. The paper does not discuss how to estimate these parameters from data or handle cases where they are unknown, which would be valuable for real-world implementation. The authors should address this limitation and discuss potential extensions.

Minor issues include several notational inconsistencies (e.g., "H" instead of "K" in lines 326, 357, 386; "s" instead of "μ*" in line 343; γ vs. γ^(m) in line 345; incorrect inequalities in line 346-347; summation direction in line 384; δ^(m) vs. δ^(M) in line 407; "l ≥ m" vs. "l ≥ p" in lines 431, 440; P(A_{n,3}) vs. P(A_{n,2}) in line 450). These should be carefully corrected in the final version.

In terms of the four key criteria:
- Quality: The paper is technically sound with strong theoretical analysis and empirical validation. The authors are careful to evaluate both strengths (MF-UCB's regret bound) and limitations (assumption about ζ(m)).
- Clarity: The paper is well-written and organized, with clear definitions and theorems. The figures effectively illustrate the partitioning of arms across fidelities.
- Originality: The multi-fidelity bandit framework represents a meaningful extension of existing bandit literature, distinct from prior work on budgeted bandits [13, 15] and multi-fidelity optimization [7, 10].
- Significance: The problem is practically important in domains like online advertising, and the results provide a theoretically grounded approach to resource-constrained exploration-exploitation trade-offs.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel problem formulation extending bandit literature
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with upper and lower bounds
- Clear practical motivation and empirical validation
- Well-structured and well-written paper

Arguments against acceptance:
- Assumption that ζ(m) are known in advance may limit practical applicability
- Limited discussion of how to estimate ζ(m) in practice

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the bandit literature that advances both theoretical understanding and practical applications. The paper would benefit from addressing the ζ(m) assumption and correcting the minor notational issues, but these are not fundamental flaws. The theoretical results are significant and the framework has clear potential for practical implementation. The paper meets all the criteria for a high-quality NIPS contribution. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the noted issues.
