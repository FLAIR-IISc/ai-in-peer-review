This paper introduces a multi-armed bandit problem where the learner must choose not only an arm to pull but also a 'fidelity' level for that pull, selected from a finite set. An arm pulled at a given fidelity yields a stochastic IID reward whose mean is within a known error bound, `zeta_m`, of the true mean. Higher fidelities are more accurate (with the highest fidelity having `zeta = 0`) but also more costly. The authors frame the problem as sequentially selecting arms and fidelities to maximize a notion of net reward. They propose a new algorithm, MF-UCB, provide a lower bound on regret, and argue that their approach can outperform standard UCB that operates only at the highest fidelity.

The paper addresses an interesting and relevant problem in online learning: modeling the trade-off between the quality of information, its acquisition cost, and the resulting reward. The formulation of the multi-fidelity bandit problem is a valuable contribution and could serve as a useful benchmark for future research. While the paper appears technically solid, its primary weakness is the lack of adequate explanation for its assumptions and results.

**Major Concerns:**

1.  **Regret Formulation:** The definition of regret, which incorporates cost multiplicatively, seems questionable. While motivated by an ad-display example, this may not be the most general formulation. A more natural and widely applicable approach would be to penalize resource consumption additively in the total reward or regret. It would be valuable to know how the analysis changes under an additive cost model.

2.  **Assumption 1:** Assumption 1, which places constraints on the decay rate of the fidelity error parameters (`zeta_m`), appears artificial and lacks clear justification. It is not obvious why this assumption would hold in general learning scenarios. This raises a crucial question: if a given set of fidelities does not satisfy this assumption, can a subset be selected to meet the condition and thereby make the results applicable? Addressing this would significantly broaden the impact of the work.

3.  **Influence of Assumption 1 on Algorithm Design:** Although the authors state that Assumption 1 is not crucial, it is used in the proofs. My main concern is that the assumption critically influences the algorithm's design and performance. The MF-UCB algorithm appears to be tailored to specific configurations of `zeta_m` that satisfy this assumption, but the paper lacks a justification for why these are the "right" configurations to focus on.

4.  **Gap Between Bounds:** The gap between the derived upper and lower regret bounds is notable. This gap warrants further investigation and may indicate that a redesign of the proposed strategy could lead to a tighter, potentially optimal, algorithm.

**Clarity and Presentation:**

A key shortcoming of the paper is the lack of clarity in the presentation of its main results. The regret bound in Theorem 2 is highly technical, making it difficult to interpret and compare against a baseline like standard UCB. The paper should explicitly state which variant of UCB (e.g., UCB operating only at the highest fidelity) is used for comparison. The performance gains of MF-UCB depend on complex terms such as `Delta`, `[[k]]`, and the sequence of sets `\cal{K}^(m)`. A more intuitive explanation of these dependencies and their implications would be invaluable for understanding the practical benefits of the proposed algorithm. Overall, while the paper introduces a compelling problem, its core theoretical contributions are obscured by an overly technical presentation.
