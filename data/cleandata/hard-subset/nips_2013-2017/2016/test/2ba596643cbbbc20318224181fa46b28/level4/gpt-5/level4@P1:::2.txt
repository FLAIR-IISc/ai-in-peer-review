Summary
This paper introduces a multi-fidelity variant of the stochastic K-armed bandit, where each arm can be evaluated at M fidelities of increasing accuracy and cost. Lower fidelities provide biased but cheaper estimates of the highest-fidelity reward. The authors propose MF-UCB, an upper-confidence-bound algorithm that adaptively selects both the arm and the fidelity, using low-cost fidelities to prune clearly suboptimal arms and reserving expensive, high-fidelity plays for a small set of promising arms. They provide upper and lower bounds on the cost-weighted pseudo-regret and show near-optimality under certain conditions. This work builds on the UCB literature (e.g., Auer et al.; Audibert et al.; summarized by Bubeck & Cesa-Bianchi) but addresses a distinct setting from budgeted bandits (Tran-Thanh et al.; Xia et al.), multi-fidelity optimization (Huang et al.; Rajnarayan et al.), and multi-fidelity RL (Cutler et al.). It is related to, but different from, multi-fidelity GP bandits (Kandasamy et al., NIPS), as it focuses on stochastic K-armed bandits with explicit cost and bias trade-offs.

Strengths
- Clear formalization of a practically motivated variant of bandits where evaluations incur different costs and biases.
- A principled algorithm (MF-UCB) that integrates fidelity selection with arm selection and provably improves over naive single-fidelity UCB when lower fidelities are informative and cheaper.
- Theoretical results include an upper bound that reveals how low fidelities reduce exploration cost for clearly suboptimal arms, and a matching (up to constants and in specific cases) lower bound that clarifies when MF-UCB is near-optimal.
- The empirical results (on synthetic data) qualitatively corroborate the theory by showing fewer high-fidelity pulls on poor arms.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Clarity: The main paper is clearly written, but the supplementary material, where most proofs reside, contains numerous typos and minor errors that hinder readability (see detailed list below). Correcting these would substantially improve the manuscript.
- Use of “up-to-constants” notation: The frequent use of inequalities that hide constants can be misleading, especially when algorithm parameters (e.g., ρ in Theorem 2) are absorbed into “constants.” Presenting the exact inequalities (as in the supplement) or explicitly exposing the dependence on ρ in the main statements would improve interpretability.
- Practical applicability: The approach relies on known fidelity-bias bounds ζ(m) and cost ratios. In real applications these may be uncertain or misspecified. A discussion of robustness to misspecified ζ(m), or procedures to estimate/validate ζ(m) online, would strengthen the practical narrative. Similarly, guidance on choosing the thresholds γ(m) and parameter ρ in practice would be helpful.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: The technical development is solid overall. The analysis clearly distinguishes how MF-UCB reduces high-fidelity exploration for arms that are far from optimal, while matching standard UCB rates for near-optimal arms. The lower bound highlights conditions where MF-UCB is tight and explains the gap in cases K(m)7, which is a thoughtful limitation. The many typos in the appendix should be fixed.
- Clarity: The main text is well-structured and easy to follow; the supplementary material needs careful proofreading. Exposing constants (especially ρ) in key results would aid clarity.
- Originality: The multi-fidelity bandit formulation with explicit bias bounds and costs is novel within the K-armed bandit setting. The fidelity-switching criterion and its analysis are original and nontrivial.
- Significance: The setting is practically relevant (e.g., online advertising, algorithm selection) and the results are likely to stimulate further work on cost-aware, biased observations in bandits. The theoretical insights are of independent interest.

Pros for acceptance
- Well-motivated new setting with clear practical relevance.
- Sound algorithmic design integrating fidelity selection.
- Meaningful theoretical guarantees (upper and lower bounds), near-optimal in important regimes.
- Empirical evidence consistent with theory.

Cons for acceptance
- Heavy reliance on known ζ(m); sensitivity to misspecification is not evaluated.
- Main results hide constants (including ρ), which obscures practical guidance.
- Numerous typos and minor errors in the supplementary material.

Overall assessment
This is a significant and interesting contribution that advances our understanding of bandits with biased, cost-varying observations. I recommend acceptance after careful proofreading of the supplementary material and with a clearer presentation of constants and parameter dependencies. A brief discussion on robustness to misspecified ζ(m) and practical parameter selection would further strengthen the paper.
