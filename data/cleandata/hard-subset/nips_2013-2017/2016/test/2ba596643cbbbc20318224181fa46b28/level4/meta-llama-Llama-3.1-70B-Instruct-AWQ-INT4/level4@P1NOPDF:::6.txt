This paper introduces a novel multi-armed bandit setting, where a learner can choose to play an arm at one of $M$ fidelities. Each successive fidelity provides a better approximation of the rewards but incurs a larger cost. A strategy that effectively utilizes lower and cheaper fidelities to eliminate sub-optimal arms, while reserving higher and more expensive fidelities for a small subset of good arms, may outperform the standard UCB algorithm that neglects these characteristics of the fidelities. This setting has potential applications in online advertising, where one may want to display ads for a few minutes to approximate long-run clicks while being mindful of costs.

The authors tackle this problem by introducing a new regret definition, proposing an algorithm, and providing theoretical analysis, including a regret lower bound. They also conduct simulations to validate their approach. The analysis is novel and contributes to the field. However, a critical assumption is that the deviation of the $m$th fidelity mean from the highest fidelity mean, denoted as $\varepsilon^{(m)}$, is known in advance. In practice, such as in online advertising applications, it is unclear how to obtain these values. It would be beneficial to discuss the possibility of relaxing this assumption.
