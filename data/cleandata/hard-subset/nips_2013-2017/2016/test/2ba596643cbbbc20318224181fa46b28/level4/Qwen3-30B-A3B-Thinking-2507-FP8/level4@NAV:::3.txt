

The paper introduces a "multi-fidelity" extension of the multi-armed bandit problem, featuring K arms each accessible at M fidelity levels. Lower fidelity reduces sampling cost but increases bias in reward estimation (pulling an arm at fidelity m yields a sample with expected value deviating from the true reward by a bound dependent on fidelity). The authors analyze a UCB-based algorithm where confidence interval widths are expanded to account for bias-induced uncertainty. Their regret bounds, presented in theorems, are complex and difficult to interpret, though the paper contends these bounds typically outperform high-fidelity UCB. A brief simulation section supports this claim. I was initially puzzled why the model uses bias rather than variance to characterize low fidelity (the latter interpretation also seems plausible), though the motivating examples (e.g., machine learning algorithm selection) partially justify the bias approach. However, one motivation (shorter ad display intervals yielding biased effectiveness estimates) remains unconvincing, and it is unclear why regret minimization would be the goal for algorithm designers in machine learning contexts. Thus, while the model-application connection is weak, it is not a disqualifying flaw. The paper's main contribution is the multi-fidelity bandit formulation itself; the algorithm is a standard application of "optimism in the face of uncertainty." The regret guarantee's complexity is disappointing, and the simulation results (with MF-UCB outperforming UCB by 3- to 4-fold in many cases) are hard to assess due to unexplained parameter choices in Appendix C—should this performance gap be typical, or is it sensitive to the selected values of $\zeta^{(m)}$?
