This paper studies a variant of the classical stochastic K-armed bandit problem in which inexpensive, lower-fidelity approximations are available for each arm. The authors formalize this setting as a multi-fidelity bandit problem and analyze its implications for exploration–exploitation.

The paper provides theoretical results on regret, including upper bounds for the proposed multi-fidelity approach and lower-bound analyses that clarify when and how multi-fidelity information yields improvements over single-fidelity baselines. The theoretical development appears sound and indicates meaningful gains under appropriate conditions.

The empirical evaluation uses simulations to demonstrate the practical benefits of exploiting multiple fidelities. The experiments clearly illustrate how incorporating low-cost approximations can reduce regret and improve sample efficiency relative to standard bandit strategies.

The presentation is clear and easy to follow. Definitions, assumptions, and results are stated in a way that facilitates understanding, and the connection between theory and experiments is well articulated.

Overall, the paper makes a compelling case for the multi-fidelity bandit framework, with clear problem formulation, rigorous regret analysis—including informative lower bounds—and experiments that substantiate the theoretical advantages.
