This paper introduces the Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit (MF-MAB) framework, a novel extension of the classic MAB problem. In this setting, each arm can be sampled at various fidelity levels, with each level having an associated cost. Low-fidelity samples are cheaper but are drawn from a distribution that only approximates the true, highest-fidelity distribution. The authors propose an algorithm designed to adapt to a given sequence of fidelity approximations and their costs. The paper includes a regret analysis for this algorithm, a corresponding problem-specific lower bound, and numerical simulations on synthetic data where the proposed algorithm outperforms a standard UCB baseline.

While the proposed framework is interesting and addresses a potentially impactful problem, the manuscript in its current form has several significant weaknesses that prevent me from recommending its acceptance. My main concerns are detailed below.

1.  **Questionable Regret Formulation:** A primary concern lies with the justification for the regret analysis in this specific setting. Regret is typically defined as the cumulative loss against an optimal policy that always plays the single best arm. In this paper, the "best" arm is defined as the one with the highest mean reward at the highest fidelity, irrespective of its sampling cost. This definition seems to misalign with the core motivation of the multi-fidelity problem, which is to efficiently balance reward exploration with sampling costs. A more natural objective in a cost-sensitive setting would be to identify the arm with the best reward-to-cost ratio or to maximize the total reward within a given budget. By defining the optimal policy solely based on mean reward, the current regret formulation is weakened; it forces the optimal policy to sample the best arm even at low fidelities, which may not be the most cost-effective strategy. This choice may obscure the true cost of exploration, particularly the cost incurred by sampling low-fidelity levels when it is not economically beneficial.

2.  **Alternative Formulation May Be More Suitable:** Following the point above, the paper would be strengthened by considering an alternative performance metric. Instead of regret minimization over a long horizon, a fixed-confidence (best-arm identification) or fixed-budget formulation might be more natural for the multi-fidelity setting. This could be framed as a cost complexity analysis: what is the minimum cumulative cost required to identify the best arm with a certain probability? This perspective is well-motivated by practical applications such as prototype testing in product development. In such scenarios, the goal is not long-term reward accumulation but rather efficient identification of the best option before a final decision (e.g., committing to mass production), all while minimizing the total testing budget. This framing also naturally incorporates real-world constraints, such as the procedural necessity of performing cheaper, low-fidelity tests before advancing to more expensive, high-fidelity evaluations.

3.  **Lack of Clarity in Presentation and Notation:** The manuscript suffers from significant clarity issues that hinder readability. A major problem is the introduction of notation, where definitions are often scattered throughout the text, making them difficult to locate and track. For example, the variable $s$ is used on one page but is not defined until a page later, and is then used so sparingly that its purpose is unclear. Beyond notation, several sections of the text are unwieldy and difficult to follow. The paper would benefit substantially from a thorough revision to streamline the writing and improve the overall flow and conciseness.

4.  **Misleading Motivating Example:** The motivating example of online advertising is, at times, confusing. The authors draw connections to factors like ad display time, which naturally introduce the concept of non-stationarity. However, the problem formulation and theoretical analysis in this paper are strictly for a stationary bandit setting. This disconnect between the example and the underlying model can confuse the reader about the scope and applicability of the proposed method. A different motivating example, such as the prototype testing scenario mentioned in point 2, might better align with the paper's stationary framework and cost-centric goals.
