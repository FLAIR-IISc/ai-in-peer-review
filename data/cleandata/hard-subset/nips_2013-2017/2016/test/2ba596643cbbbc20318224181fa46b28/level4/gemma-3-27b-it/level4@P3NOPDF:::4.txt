## Review of NIPS 2016 Submission: Multi-fidelity Multi-armed Bandit

This paper introduces a novel framework for multi-fidelity multi-armed bandits, where arms can be sampled at varying levels of fidelity with associated costs, and low-fidelity samples approximate the highest-fidelity distribution. The authors present an algorithm designed to adapt to sequences of approximations and costs, along with a regret analysis and a lower bound for this problem. Empirical evaluation on synthetic data demonstrates performance exceeding that of UCB. While the proposed framework is potentially interesting and could be impactful with further development, I currently find the paper unsuitable for publication. My concerns relate to clarity of presentation, questionable motivation in the regret analysis, and a missed opportunity to connect the framework to a relevant application domain.

My primary concern is the presentation and notation. The introduction of symbols is often dispersed throughout the text, making definitions difficult to locate. For example, the variable *s* is introduced without clear definition and only appears defined on a subsequent page, and even then, it sees limited use. A dedicated section outlining all notations before the formal algorithm description would significantly improve readability. The current approach forces the reader to continually backtrack, hindering comprehension.

The illustrative example utilizing advertising is, in some respects, misleading. The authors repeatedly draw parallels between ad display time and non-stationarity, but non-stationarity is largely irrelevant within the context of this paper’s defined problem. The focus on time-varying reward distributions distracts from the core contribution of balancing fidelity and cost. A more appropriate example, perhaps rooted in engineering design or experimentation (see point 4), would more effectively highlight the problem’s strengths.

I question the validity of the regret analysis in this specific setting. Standard regret analysis in bandit problems aims to maximize cumulative reward in the long run, guiding exploration towards the arm offering the highest expected reward. However, in this formulation, the optimal policy consistently favors the arm with the highest *mean* reward, irrespective of its associated cost. The constraint of sampling the best arm at lower fidelity levels, therefore, appears to artificially deflate the apparent regret, potentially obscuring the true cost of utilizing low-fidelity samples when high-fidelity information is accessible. The analysis seems to implicitly penalize exploration for higher fidelity information, which is counterintuitive. 

A cost complexity, or perhaps more accurately, a sample complexity analysis would be a valuable addition. This could be motivated by a scenario like the iterative testing of prototypes during product development. In this context, the goal is to identify the optimal product design with high confidence while minimizing the cumulative cost of the testing phase before mass production. Many companies face practical constraints – regulations, contractual obligations, or existing infrastructure – that necessitate utilizing low-fidelity approximations before progressing to more advanced and expensive tests. Framing the analysis within this context would strengthen the paper's appeal and highlight its practical relevance. 

Finally, the writing style in places feels cumbersome. A thorough revision to streamline the prose and improve overall clarity is recommended. The paper would benefit from more concise phrasing and a more logical flow of ideas.



**Overall Score:** 4/10. The idea is interesting, but the current presentation and theoretical justification need significant work before publication is warranted. Addressing the concerns outlined above, particularly regarding notation, the motivation of the regret analysis, and the inclusion of a more compelling application example, would substantially improve the paper.
