

This paper introduces a novel multi-armed bandit framework incorporating multiple fidelities, where the learner selects an arm at one of $M$ fidelity levels. Each fidelity provides a progressively more accurate reward approximation at increasing cost. The key insight is that leveraging lower-cost fidelities for early elimination of suboptimal arms, while reserving higher-cost fidelities for refining promising arms, can yield superior regret performance compared to standard UCB, which ignores fidelity costs. The setting is motivated by online advertising, where brief ad displays (low fidelity) can approximate long-term click behavior (high fidelity) while minimizing cost. The authors propose a new regret definition, a corresponding algorithm with theoretical guarantees (including a regret lower bound), and empirical validation. The theoretical analysis is novel and well-executed.  

A critical limitation concerns the assumption that $\varepsilon^{(m)}$ (the deviation of the $m$-th fidelity's mean reward from the highest fidelity) is known a priori. The paper does not address how these values would be obtained in practice, such as in the online advertising application. Without a practical mechanism to estimate $\varepsilon^{(m)}$, the algorithm's real-world applicability is questionable. The authors should discuss whether this assumption can be relaxed (e.g., via adaptive estimation) or provide justification for its feasibility in target domains.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to bandit theory by integrating fidelity costs into regret minimization. The theoretical results are sound, and the application context is relevant. However, the assumption of known $\varepsilon^{(m)}$ represents a significant practical hurdle. Addressing this—either by relaxing the assumption or demonstrating its feasibility—would substantially strengthen the work. The minor corrections above are essential for clarity and correctness.  

Overall, the paper presents an interesting and timely extension of bandit problems with clear potential impact. With minor revisions to address the fidelity assumption and notational issues, it would be suitable for publication. The technical contribution is sufficiently novel and well-supported.
