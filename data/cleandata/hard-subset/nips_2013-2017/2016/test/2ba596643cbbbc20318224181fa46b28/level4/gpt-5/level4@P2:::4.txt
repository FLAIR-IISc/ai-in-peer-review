Summary
This paper introduces the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit (MF-MAB), where each arm can be queried at one of M fidelities with increasing cost and decreasing bias relative to the highest-fidelity reward. The authors propose MF-UCB, an upper-confidence-bound strategy that adaptively uses cheaper fidelities to screen arms and reserves expensive queries for promising arms. They define a cost-weighted pseudo-regret that benchmarks against always playing an optimal arm, provide a regret upper bound, and complement it with a lower bound showing near-optimality under certain conditions. Simulations on synthetic problems show improvements over standard UCB that ignores fidelity structure.

Relation to prior work
The work is rooted in classical stochastic bandits and UCB (Auer et al.; Lai & Robbins; Bubeck & Cesa-Bianchi). It differs from budgeted bandits (e.g., Tran-Thanh et al.; Xia et al.), which focus on reward-per-cost trade-offs per arm, and from weak/strong supervision models in active learning (Zhang & Chaudhuri). It is also distinct from multi-fidelity optimization and RL simulators (Huang et al.; Cutler et al.) by explicitly modeling biased lower-fidelity observations and analyzing regret in an online bandit setting. The framework is related to the authors’ own subsequent GP multi-fidelity bandits (Kandasamy et al.).

Strengths
- Novel problem formalization capturing a practical setting where low-cost biased proxies guide exploration before committing expensive evaluations.
- Algorithmic contribution (MF-UCB) that is simple, interpretable, and consistent with UCB principles.
- Theoretical analysis includes a nontrivial upper bound that explains how low fidelities filter arms, and a lower bound establishing near-optimality for a broad subset of arms.
- Empirical results (synthetic) demonstrate the expected advantage: fewer expensive pulls on clearly suboptimal arms and better cost-adjusted regret.

Weaknesses and clarity issues
1) Notation and presentation: The notation is dispersed and sometimes introduced after first use. For example, the variable s appears before it is defined (it is defined roughly a page later) and then plays a minimal role thereafter. Important quantities (e.g., ζ(m), γ(m), ψ and ψ−1, T(m)k,t) should be collected and defined once, before they are used, ideally summarized in a notation table. Several arguments would benefit from clearer signposting and a brief “roadmap” of the proof ideas before diving into technicalities.

2) Examples and scope: The recurring online advertising example conflates display duration/audience restrictions with non-stationarity and context effects. Since non-stationarity is out of scope here, the example risks being misleading. Consider replacing or supplementing it with a context where stationarity is more plausible (e.g., algorithm selection with truncated training, simulation-vs-reality in engineering tests), and clearly distinguish bias from temporal drift or covariate shift.

3) Objective definition and regret: The chosen pseudo-reward qt = µ(M)It regardless of fidelity raises conceptual questions. Because the oracle’s benchmark is the highest-fidelity mean, the “optimal” policy inherently ignores reward-per-cost trade-offs and always favors the arm with highest µ(M). This can obscure the true cost of unnecessary low-fidelity evaluations and weakens the motivation for regret as the primary objective in this setting. Alternative objectives seem natural:
- Reward-per-cost regret (more aligned with budget constraints).
- Pure-exploration best-arm identification with cost complexity (see below).
At minimum, the paper should better justify qt, discuss implications, and delineate when this notion is appropriate.

4) Alternative problem statement (cost complexity): A pure-exploration, best-arm identification formulation with capital constraints would be compelling for MF-MAB. For example, in product prototyping, regulations and facilities often require cheaper low-fidelity tests before costly advanced tests; the goal is to identify the best product with high confidence while minimizing total test cost. A cost-complexity analysis (sample complexity weighted by λ(m)) would directly address this use case and avoid the ambiguities of the current regret notion.

5) Writing and organization: Several sections are unwieldy. The flow would improve by:
- Introducing the full setup, assumptions (including Assumption 1), and notation before the algorithm.
- Providing intuition for γ(m) and the switching rule, then giving the formal definition.
- Separating technical lemmas from main proof sketches.
- Tightening related work to highlight precise distinctions from budgeted bandits and multi-fidelity optimization.

Additional technical concerns
- Assumption 1 (on ζ(m) decay) appears tailored to obtain clean comparisons with UCB; discuss its necessity, practical plausibility, and how performance degrades when it fails.
- Experiments are limited to synthetic data; demonstrating robustness across different ζ/λ schedules and including a realistic proxy-vs-target setting would strengthen the empirical case.
- Clarify how qt aligns with application semantics; for advertising, explain why short-horizon metrics are biased yet still informative and how that maps to the model’s bias bounds ζ(m).

Suggestions for improvement
- Consolidate and front-load notation; add a table of symbols.
- Rework the running example to avoid non-stationarity/confounding; emphasize stationary proxy biases.
- Expand the discussion of objective design, contrasting cost-adjusted regret, reward-per-cost, and cost complexity for identification; consider adding a pure-exploration result or leaving it as a concrete future direction.
- Provide ablations varying ζ(m), λ(m), and M, and compare to budgeted-bandit baselines and naive reward-per-cost heuristics.
- Clarify the scope and implications of Assumption 1 and sensitivity to ψ.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Interesting and timely problem formulation with a clear practical motivation.
- Clean algorithm with insightful analysis and near-optimal guarantees for many cases.
- Empirical gains over UCB consistent with theory.

Cons:
- Objective choice (qt) is insufficiently justified and may misrepresent cost-benefit trade-offs.
- Presentation and notation hamper readability.
- Examples risk conflating bias with non-stationarity.
- Limited experimental validation; no real or semi-real benchmarks.

Overall recommendation
Promising idea and analysis, but in its current form I do not recommend acceptance. Strengthening the objective justification (or adding a cost-complexity identification angle), improving clarity/notation, refining examples, and expanding experiments would significantly increase the paper’s impact.
