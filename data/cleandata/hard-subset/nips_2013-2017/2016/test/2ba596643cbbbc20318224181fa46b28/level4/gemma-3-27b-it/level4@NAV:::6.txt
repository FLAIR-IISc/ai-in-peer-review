This paper introduces and analyzes a novel multi-armed bandit problem featuring a spectrum of $M$ fidelity levels for each arm. Higher fidelities yield more accurate reward approximations but incur greater costs. The core idea is that an algorithm leveraging lower fidelities to efficiently eliminate poor-performing arms, and reserving expensive, high-fidelity evaluations for promising candidates, can surpass the performance of standard algorithms like UCB which do not account for these fidelity characteristics. A compelling application area is online advertising, where initial, low-cost ad displays can provide early estimates of long-term click-through rates. The authors define a suitable regret metric for this setting, propose a corresponding algorithm, and provide a theoretical analysis – including a regret lower bound – supported by simulation results. The analysis presents a novel contribution to the field. 

A key assumption is the advance knowledge of $\varepsilon^{(m)}$, representing the deviation of the mean reward at the $m$-th fidelity level from that of the highest fidelity. A practical concern is how these values would be known *a priori* in real-world applications (e.g., in the aforementioned online advertising scenario). Exploring the possibility of relaxing this assumption would be a valuable direction for future work.
