

This work introduces a multi-fidelity bandit framework where each arm can be sampled at varying fidelities with associated costs, formalizing the trade-off between approximation quality and resource expenditure. The authors generalize the classical UCB approach to this setting via MF-UCB, proving near-optimal regret bounds that scale with lower-fidelity costs and leverage cheaper exploratory pulls to eliminate suboptimal arms before committing to high-cost evaluations. This extends prior NIPS work on bandits (e.g., Agrawal [1], Lai and Robbins [9]) by addressing a practically relevant cost-constrained exploration-exploitation scenario not covered in standard formulations. The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a compelling motivation from online advertising and algorithm selection.  

**Strengths:**  
- Theoretical contribution is significant: MF-UCB achieves regret scaling as $O\left(\sum_{m=1}^M \sum_{k \in K^{(m)}} \frac{\lambda^{(m)} \Delta_k^{(M)}}{\psi(\Delta_k^{(m)})}\right)$, which strictly improves upon naive UCB ($O(\lambda^{(M)} K \log(\Lambda/\lambda^{(M)}))$) when $\lambda^{(1)} \ll \lambda^{(M)}$ and $|K^{(M)}| \ll K$.  
- The lower bound (Theorem 4) demonstrates near-optimality of MF-UCB for arms in $K^{(m)}_3$, providing a rigorous foundation.  
- Empirical validation on synthetic problems confirms superior regret performance over UCB (Figure 2), with clear visualization of fidelity-specific arm play patterns.  

**Weaknesses:**  
- Supplementary material contains multiple technical errors that undermine reproducibility: e.g., page 10 equation (8) uses $k$ instead of $k'$, Section A1 mislabels $H^{(m)}$ as $K^{(m)}$, page 10 line 328 has $\nu/(\rho-2)$ instead of $\nu/2$, and page 15 interchanges $p$ and $m$ repeatedly. These require correction for clarity.  
- Asymptotic notation ($\lesssim$) obscures critical dependencies: Theorem 2 hides the parameter $\rho$ (which must satisfy $\rho > 4$), and the regret bound’s dependence on $\rho$ is not explicitly discussed, potentially misleading readers about algorithmic sensitivity.  
- Practical applicability is underdeveloped: The algorithm’s performance hinges on accurate knowledge of $\zeta^{(m)}$ (the fidelity bias bounds). In real applications, $\zeta^{(m)}$ is typically unknown and must be estimated, which could destabilize the arm elimination process (e.g., if $\zeta^{(m)}$ is underestimated, suboptimal arms may not be eliminated). A discussion of robustness to $\zeta^{(m)}$ estimation errors or adaptive strategies would strengthen the contribution.  

**Pros and Cons for Acceptance:**  
*Pro:* High theoretical novelty, strong empirical validation, clear connection to practical problems (e.g., advertising, algorithm selection), and near-optimal regret guarantees.  
*Con:* Supplementary material errors require significant correction; practical limitations regarding $\zeta^{(m)}$ estimation need addressing to ensure real-world utility.  

Overall, this is a technically sound and significant contribution to bandit theory, aligning with NIPS’ focus on rigorous scientific advances. The theoretical insights are compelling, though minor revisions to the supplementary material and expanded discussion of practical implementation would elevate the work further. The paper advances the state of the art in multi-fidelity optimization and merits acceptance.
