This paper introduces a formal framework for the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit problem, a novel variant of the classical stochastic bandit setting. In this formulation, each arm can be evaluated at different "fidelity" levels, where higher fidelities provide more accurate reward estimates but incur greater costs. The authors propose the Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB) algorithm, which extends the optimism principle of UCB to this new setting. The core idea is to leverage cheap, low-fidelity observations to rapidly eliminate clearly suboptimal arms, thereby reserving the more expensive, high-fidelity evaluations for a smaller set of promising candidates. The paper supports this algorithmic contribution with a rigorous theoretical analysis, providing an upper bound on the cost-weighted pseudo-regret of MF-UCB and a corresponding lower bound that establishes the algorithm's near-optimality under specific conditions. This work builds upon the extensive literature on UCB methods and connects to related areas like budgeted bandits and multi-fidelity optimization, while addressing a new and practically relevant exploration-exploitation challenge.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles a well-motivated and practical extension of the multi-armed bandit problem. The proposed multi-fidelity framework is applicable to numerous real-world scenarios, such as online advertising or algorithm configuration, where the cost and accuracy of evaluations can vary. This work provides a principled and insightful formalization of this problem.
*   **Technical Quality:** The theoretical contributions are strong and provide a solid foundation for the proposed algorithm. The derivation of both an upper regret bound for MF-UCB and a problem-specific lower bound offers a comprehensive analysis of performance, rigorously demonstrating the algorithm's effectiveness and near-optimality.
*   **Clarity:** The main body of the paper is very clear and well-written. The problem, the algorithm's intuition, and the main theoretical results are presented in a well-organized and accessible manner.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Clarity of Supplementary Material:** The appendix, which contains the bulk of the proofs, would benefit from substantial proofreading. There are numerous typos and minor, easily correctable errors that can confuse the reader and disrupt the flow of the technical arguments. Addressing these issues would greatly enhance the overall quality and readability of the paper. A non-exhaustive list of suggested corrections is provided below.
*   **Precision in Theoretical Results:** The use of "up-to-a-multiplicative-constant" notation in the main theorems can be somewhat misleading, as it hides dependencies on important parameters. For example, the regret bound in Theorem 2 conceals a dependency on the algorithm parameter ρ. While the full inequalities are available in the supplement, making these dependencies explicit in the main paper's theorem statements would improve the clarity and precision of the results.
*   **Discussion of Practical Limitations:** From a practical standpoint, the performance of MF-UCB relies on prior knowledge of the fidelity accuracy parameters, ζ. The paper could be strengthened by a discussion on the algorithm's robustness to inaccurate estimates of these parameters. Addressing the practical challenges of setting or learning these values would make the work more readily applicable to real-world problems.

Overall, this paper presents a significant and interesting contribution to the bandit literature. The problem formulation is novel, the algorithm is intuitive, and the theoretical analysis is solid. The work would be even stronger if the authors address the clarity issues in the supplementary material and include a discussion on the practical challenges and assumptions of the model.
