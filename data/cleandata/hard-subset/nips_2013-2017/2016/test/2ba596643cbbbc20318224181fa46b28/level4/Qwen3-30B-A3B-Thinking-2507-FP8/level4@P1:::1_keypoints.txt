- Paper introduces a multi-fidelity bandit problem with fidelity-dependent costs and proposes MF-UCB to adaptively choose fidelities to reduce regret.  
- MF-UCB is backed by theoretical regret bounds showing potential large gains over standard UCB when low-fidelity observations are much cheaper.  
- The authors provide a lower bound claiming MF-UCB is nearly optimal under certain conditions.  
- Strength: novel, well-motivated problem formulation addressing practical cost of observations.  
- Strength: technically sound MF-UCB algorithm with clear theoretical analysis.  
- Strength: lower bound supports near-optimality in some regimes.  
- Strength: empirical synthetic results show MF-UCB outperforming UCB.  
- Major weakness: regret definition treats cost multiplicatively (R = Λμ* - Σλ(mt)qt), which is unnatural vs. standard additive cost formulations.  
- Major weakness: Assumption 1 (fast decay of ζ(m)) seems artificial, lacks justification, and appears to be critical to the algorithm’s design.  
- Major weakness: Theorem 2 regret bound is complex and hard to interpret due to opaque sets K(m) and parameters, lacking intuitive comparison to UCB.  
- Major weakness: gap between the lower bound and MF-UCB’s performance for certain arms (K(m)7) is not explained or addressed.  
- Major weakness: paper lacks clear guidance on when multi-fidelity methods are practically beneficial (when conditions for gains actually hold).  
- Minor issues: wording/typos ("near-optimal" → "nearly optimal"), extraneous punctuation, and undefined/unclear notation ([[k]]).  
- Overall: promising and valuable idea but requires significant revisions to regret definition, justification of assumptions, clarity of theoretical results, and discussion of practical applicability.  
- Pros for acceptance: novel problem, solid theory under conditions, and strong empirical evidence.  
- Cons for acceptance: problematic regret formulation, unjustified key assumption, unclear main result presentation, and unexplained lower-bound gap.
