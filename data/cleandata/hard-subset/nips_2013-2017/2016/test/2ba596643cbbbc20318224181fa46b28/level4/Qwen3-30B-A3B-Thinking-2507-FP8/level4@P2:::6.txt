

This paper introduces a novel multi-fidelity bandit framework where each arm can be played at multiple fidelities with varying costs and approximation qualities. The authors formalize the problem by considering M successively better approximations to the highest fidelity (most expensive) reward, with each fidelity m having cost λ(m) and mean deviation ζ(m) from the highest fidelity. The main contribution is the MF-UCB algorithm, which intelligently allocates resources across fidelities to reduce regret compared to standard UCB that ignores fidelity information. This extends classical bandit work [3, 9, 11] and builds on UCB methods [2, 3] while addressing a significant gap in the literature regarding multi-fidelity settings, distinct from related work on budgeted bandits [13, 15] and multi-fidelity optimization [7, 10].

The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution with a novel regret definition accounting for fidelity costs, a comprehensive regret upper bound demonstrating MF-UCB's superiority over UCB when |Kg| < K and λ(1) < λ(2), and a matching lower bound showing near-optimality under certain conditions. The analysis is thorough and well-structured, with clear theorems and proofs. The authors also provide compelling empirical results on synthetic problems showing MF-UCB outperforms UCB with smaller regret gradients, consistent with their theoretical insights.

The problem formulation is well-motivated by practical applications like online advertising (where short ad displays approximate long-term click-through rates) and algorithm selection (where cheaper data subsets provide performance approximations). The paper effectively demonstrates how MF-UCB uses lower fidelities to eliminate suboptimal arms while reserving high-fidelity evaluations for promising candidates, achieving better cost efficiency.

A significant question arises regarding the assumption that the bias parameters ζ(m) must be known in advance. The paper states "the ζ(m)'s are known," but the practical applicability of this assumption is unclear. In the online advertising example, how would one determine the exact bias between a 5-minute ad display and a full hour? The authors should address whether ζ(m) could be estimated in practice or how the algorithm might be adapted to handle unknown biases, as this limitation could impact real-world deployment.

The paper is well-written and organized, with clear notation and sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the results. The figures effectively illustrate the key concepts, particularly Figure 1 showing how MF-UCB allocates plays across fidelities. The related work section properly contextualizes the contribution within recent NIPS literature, including [8] on multi-fidelity bandits with Gaussian process payoffs.

The main limitation is the known-bias assumption, which requires further discussion. However, the paper's theoretical and empirical results are strong, and the problem is clearly significant for resource-constrained bandit applications. The work advances the field by providing a formal framework and efficient algorithm for multi-fidelity bandit problems, with clear potential for practical impact in domains where high-fidelity experiments are expensive.
