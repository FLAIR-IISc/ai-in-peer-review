This paper addresses a variant of the classical stochastic K-armed bandit problem, specifically considering scenarios where cheap approximations of each arm’s reward are available. The authors formalize this as a multi-fidelity bandit problem, and provide theoretical regret bounds for their approach. Simulations are presented to demonstrate the benefits of leveraging multi-fidelity information. The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The simulations clearly illustrate the effectiveness of the proposed multi-fidelity bandit method. Importantly, the authors include sufficient theoretical analysis, establishing a lower bound for their method and demonstrating a theoretical improvement over existing approaches. 

The key idea – exploiting cheaper, lower-fidelity evaluations to guide exploration and reduce the cost of obtaining high-fidelity rewards – is a valuable contribution. The formalization is clean and well-motivated. The regret analysis appears sound, and the provided bounds are reasonably tight, given the problem setting. The simulations corroborate the theoretical findings, showing a clear performance advantage in scenarios where the cost of high-fidelity evaluations is substantial.

However, a few points could be strengthened. While the paper demonstrates improvement *over* a baseline, it would be useful to compare performance against other relevant bandit algorithms that might also be adapted to this multi-fidelity setting (e.g., UCB, Thompson Sampling with appropriate modifications). This would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the proposed method’s relative performance. 

Furthermore, the practical considerations of choosing the fidelity levels (i.e., the trade-off between cost and accuracy of the approximations) are not fully explored. The paper could benefit from a discussion of how these levels should be set in practice, perhaps with some guidance based on the characteristics of the underlying reward distributions. A more detailed analysis of the impact of noisy low-fidelity observations would also be beneficial. Specifically, how does the performance degrade as the noise in the lower fidelities increases?

Despite these minor suggestions, this is a solid contribution to the bandit literature. The problem formulation is insightful, the theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the empirical results are convincing. The paper addresses an important practical challenge – balancing exploration and exploitation in the presence of costly evaluations – and offers a promising solution. I recommend acceptance.
