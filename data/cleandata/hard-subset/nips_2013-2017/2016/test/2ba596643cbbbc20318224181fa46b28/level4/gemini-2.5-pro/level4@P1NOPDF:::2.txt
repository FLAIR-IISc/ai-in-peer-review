This paper introduces a novel multi-armed bandit setting where arms can be pulled at varying levels of accuracy (or fidelity). In this framework, the cost of an arm pull increases with its fidelity, and the regret is defined as a cost-weighted version of the standard pseudo-regret. The authors propose an algorithm, MF-UCB, to address this setting and provide both upper and lower bounds on its cumulative pseudo-regret.

The main paper is clear and well-written. However, the supplementary materials, which contain most of the proofs, are not of the same quality. They contain numerous typos and minor errors which, while easily correctable, can be confusing for the reader. Correcting these issues would significantly improve the paper's overall readability. A non-exhaustive list of these errors is provided at the end of this review.

Additionally, the use of asymptotic notation that hides multiplicative constants can be misleading, as these "constants" sometimes conceal important dependencies on algorithm parameters. For instance, in Theorem 2, the constant hides a factor of \rho, a key parameter of the algorithm. Stating the exact inequalities from the supplementary materials directly in the main paper would improve the clarity and precision of the results.

From a practical perspective, the applicability of the MF-UCB algorithm to real-world problems is not entirely clear. The core strategy of using low-fidelity pulls to quickly eliminate suboptimal arms appears to be sensitive to the accurate estimation of the fidelity-accuracy parameter, \zeta. The paper would benefit from a discussion of such practical limitations and potential strategies for overcoming them.

Theoretically, this paper provides valuable insights into the multi-fidelity bandit problem, supported by interesting theorems and solid proofs. Overall, this is a significant and interesting contribution.
