Summary
The paper introduces a multi-fidelity variant of the multi-armed bandit problem with K arms and M fidelity levels per arm. Lower-fidelity pulls are cheaper but introduce bounded bias in the mean reward estimate; the bias bound depends on the fidelity. The authors propose an optimism-based algorithm (MF-UCB) that widens confidence intervals to account for both statistical uncertainty and fidelity-induced bias. They provide regret bounds and argue that, for a wide range of instances, MF-UCB improves substantially over always sampling at the highest fidelity and running standard UCB. Simulations on synthetic instances support this claim.

Strengths
- The problem formulation is timely and natural. Many practical settings offer cheaper, biased proxies (e.g., truncated training, fewer data, coarser simulations), and it is valuable to formalize how to leverage them.
- The algorithm is conceptually simple, principled, and easy to implement: it augments UCB with fidelity-aware confidence adjustments.
- The paper provides theoretical guarantees and empirical evidence that lower-fidelity information can reduce regret under appropriate conditions.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Modeling low fidelity solely as increased bias (rather than variance) is a strong choice. Some motivating examples fit this view, but others are less convincing. In particular, the claim that showing an ad for a shorter interval yields a biased estimate of long-term effectiveness is not well substantiated. More discussion (or a crisper example) would help.
- In the algorithm-selection example, it is not clear that cumulative regret minimization is the right objective; best-arm identification or fixed-budget settings may be more appropriate for practitioners. The connection between the formal model and the stated applications feels weak, though not fatally so.
- The regret bounds are difficult to interpret. They are technically sound but messy, making it hard to extract simple insights or compare regimes. The paper would benefit from interpretable corollaries under stylized assumptions (e.g., two fidelities, simple gap structure, specific cost ratios) that transparently show when and by how much MF-UCB improves over high-fidelity-only UCB.
- The experimental evaluation relies on synthetic problems. While Appendix C lists parameter settings, it does not justify them or explore sensitivity. The observed 3–4x regret improvements may depend on particular choices (e.g., values of zeta^(m)); it is unclear whether this is typical in practice. Realistic case studies or at least semi-realistic simulations with principled parameterization would strengthen the claims.

Clarity and presentation
- The high-level intuition is appealing—use cheap, biased samples to eliminate suboptimal arms, then escalate fidelity as needed—but this message is somewhat buried under technical detail. A short, self-contained explanation early on, with a small, fully worked two-arm, two-fidelity example and a corresponding simplified bound, would improve readability.
- It would help to explicitly define how the confidence intervals are adjusted for bias and to summarize the algorithm’s decision rule in a way that highlights when to switch fidelities.

Questions for the authors
- How are the bias bounds zeta^(m) obtained in practice? Are they assumed known, estimated online, or provided by domain knowledge? What happens when they are misspecified (over- or underestimation)?
- Can the analysis accommodate fidelity-dependent variances in addition to bias, or modest violations of the bias-only assumption?
- What guidance can you offer on the number of fidelities to include and how to set their costs and target bias levels? Are there regimes where additional fidelities yield diminishing returns?
- Are there simple instance conditions under which MF-UCB is provably no better than high-fidelity UCB (e.g., when bias dominates gaps), and can these be stated in an interpretable way?
- How does computational overhead scale with M, and how should budgets be accounted for to ensure fair comparisons with high-fidelity UCB?

Comments on experiments
- Please justify the parameter choices in Appendix C, especially the bias schedules zeta^(m) and cost ratios. Include sensitivity analyses varying these quantities and the reward gaps.
- Report results under misspecified bias bounds and under fidelity-dependent variances to assess robustness.
- Consider adding a real or semi-real benchmark (e.g., hyperparameter tuning with early stopping as a low fidelity) and clarifying the performance metric (wall-clock cost vs. regret).
- Beyond UCB, compare to elimination-style methods tailored to cost constraints or to best-arm identification baselines, which may better reflect certain application goals.

Overall assessment
The paper’s main contribution is the formalization of the multi-fidelity MAB setting and a clean, UCB-style approach to exploit it. The idea is interesting and relevant, and the theory appears technically careful. However, the practical import is obscured by hard-to-parse regret expressions, a relatively weak link to motivating applications, and limited empirical validation. With clearer, interpretable theoretical summaries and more convincing experiments (including parameter justification and robustness), the paper would be significantly strengthened.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide simple, interpretable corollaries of the regret bounds highlighting dependence on gaps, costs, and bias levels; include rules of thumb summarizing when MF-UCB helps.
- Clarify and, if possible, empirically validate the bias-only fidelity model; alternatively, discuss extensions that handle variance changes.
- Strengthen the experimental section with justified parameter choices, sensitivity analyses, and at least one realistic case study.
- Discuss the choice of objective (regret vs. fixed-budget identification) for the presented applications and, if appropriate, relate the approach to those settings.
- Offer practical guidance on estimating or setting zeta^(m), tuning confidence inflation, and selecting fidelities in real deployments.
