Summary
The paper introduces a multi-fidelity variant of the stochastic K-armed bandit. Each arm can be played at one of M fidelities: lower fidelities are cheaper but yield biased, approximate rewards; higher fidelities are more accurate and costly. The authors formalize a cost-weighted regret notion tailored to this setting and propose MF-UCB, an upper-confidence-bound algorithm that adaptively uses cheaper fidelities to eliminate clearly suboptimal arms and reserves expensive evaluations for a small set of promising arms. They derive regret upper bounds showing that MF-UCB can outperform standard UCB (which uses only the highest fidelity), provide a regret lower bound demonstrating near-optimality under certain conditions, and validate the approach on synthetic experiments. The formulation and analysis build on classical UCB techniques while addressing new challenges from fidelity-dependent bias and cost.

Strengths
- Novel problem formulation that captures an important practical scenario where biased, cheaper approximations are available (e.g., online advertising, algorithm selection).
- A principled regret definition that aligns with applications where only the highest-fidelity outcome is ultimately valuable.
- An intuitive and well-motivated algorithm (MF-UCB) that chooses both the arm and fidelity adaptively via confidence bands and thresholds.
- Theoretical analysis includes both upper bounds and a matching lower bound in many cases, clarifying when multi-fidelity information yields gains over UCB.
- Simulations corroborate the theory, showing improved regret and demonstrating that MF-UCB allocates most high-fidelity plays to near-optimal arms.

Weaknesses / Questions
- The analysis assumes that, for each fidelity m, the bias bound between the m-th and highest fidelity means (denoted ε^(m), i.e., |μ^(M) − μ^(m)| ≤ ε^(m); called ζ(m) in the paper) is known a priori and decreases with m. In practice (e.g., the advertising example), how would one obtain these ε^(m) values? Are there realistic procedures to estimate or learn them online with guarantees? Can the analysis be relaxed to data-driven or distribution-free bounds on ε^(m), possibly with adaptive calibration?
- The paper would benefit from a brief discussion of robustness when ε^(m) are misspecified (overly optimistic or pessimistic), and how this impacts the switching thresholds γ^(m) and regret.

Evaluation by NIPS criteria
- Quality: The technical development appears sound. The regret analysis is careful, with clear intuition for the switching rule and a lower bound that highlights where MF-UCB is tight and where a gap remains.
- Clarity: Generally well written and organized, with helpful intuition for the algorithm and partitioning of arms. However, there are several minor notational/typo issues (listed below) that should be fixed.
- Originality: The multi-fidelity bandit setting and regret formulation are novel relative to standard bandits and budgeted bandits. The analysis extends UCB ideas in a nontrivial way to handle fidelity-dependent bias and cost.
- Significance: The setting is motivated by realistic applications, and the results provide both conceptual and practical insights into leveraging cheap approximations to reduce regret.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Clear and timely problem formulation.
- New algorithm with strong theoretical backing (upper and lower bounds).
- Empirical results align with theory.
- Potentially broad applicability.

Cons:
- Reliance on known ε^(m) (bias bounds) is strong; practical estimation and robustness are not addressed.
- Minor clarity and notation issues.

Minor comments and typos

2) Line 343: In the last indicator term of the inequality, the argument “B_{k*,t} ≤ s” should be “B_{k*,t} ≤ μ*”.
3) Line 345: γ should be γ^(m).
4) Lines 346–347: In “… and the last step uses ψ(Δ_k^(m) − γ^(m)) ≤ ψ(γ^(m)) when ψ(Δ_k^(m)) ≤ 2γ^(m)”, both “≤” should be “≥”.

6) Line 407: On the right side of the last inequality, δ^(m) should be δ^(M), and the last term μ* κ λ^(m) should be κ λ^(m).

Overall assessment
This is a strong paper with a well-motivated setting, a principled algorithm, and solid analysis. Clarifying how to obtain or relax the ε^(m) assumptions would further strengthen the work. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on addressing the minor issues and adding a brief discussion of practicality and robustness regarding ε^(m).
