

The paper addresses a multi-armed bandit problem incorporating a "fidelity" choice for each arm pull, where the learner selects from a finite set of fidelity levels. Each arm pulled at fidelity level \(m\) yields a stochastic reward with mean within \(\zeta_m\) of the true mean (with known \(\zeta_m\), where \(\zeta_0 = 0\) corresponds to the highest fidelity). Lower-fidelity pulls incur lower costs. The authors formulate the sequential decision problem to maximize net reward, propose a new algorithm (MF-UCB), and claim it outperforms UCB operating only at high fidelity. They also establish a regret lower bound. The problem—modeling the tradeoff between information quality, cost, and reward—is timely and relevant for stochastic bandits, potentially serving as a useful benchmark.  

While technically sound, the paper suffers from significant clarity issues regarding its core assumptions and results. The regret formulation, which penalizes cost multiplicatively (e.g., \(\text{reward} \times \text{cost}^{-1}\)), appears unnatural. Why not penalize cost additively (e.g., \(\text{reward} - \text{cost}\))? The ad-display example motivating this choice lacks general applicability; a more natural formulation would incorporate cost directly into the regret (e.g., \(\text{regret} = \sum (\text{optimal reward} - \text{observed reward} + \text{cost})\)), and the authors should clarify why their choice is preferable.  

Assumption 1 (constraining \(\zeta_m\) decay rates) is particularly problematic. It appears ad hoc, with no justification for why such constraints should hold in general learning settings. Crucially, if the \(\zeta_m\) decay rates fall short of Assumption 1’s requirements, how can one select a subsequence of fidelity levels to satisfy the assumption and retain the theoretical guarantees? The authors note the assumption is "not crucial," yet it underpins key proofs. I question whether the algorithm’s design and performance critically depend on this specific \(\zeta_m\) structure. The justification for why the assumed \(\zeta_m\) sequences are "right" is missing, suggesting the algorithm may only perform well for narrow parameter regimes.  

The lower bound’s looseness also warrants deeper investigation. If the bound is loose, the proposed algorithm may not achieve near-optimality, necessitating a revised strategy. More critically, the interpretation of the main result (Theorem 2, MF-UCB’s regret bound) and its comparison to UCB (at highest fidelity, though this is not explicitly stated) remains opaque. The regret bound depends on complex parameters \(\Delta\) and \([k]\), which themselves rely on the intricate sequence of sets \(\mathcal{K}^{(m)}\). A clearer, more explicit explanation of how MF-UCB’s performance scales relative to UCB—especially under varying \(\zeta_m\) configurations—would strengthen the paper’s contribution.  

The paper begins promisingly by modeling a meaningful tradeoff but loses clarity in technical execution. Key questions about the regret formulation, Assumption 1’s necessity, and the interpretation of results remain inadequately addressed.
