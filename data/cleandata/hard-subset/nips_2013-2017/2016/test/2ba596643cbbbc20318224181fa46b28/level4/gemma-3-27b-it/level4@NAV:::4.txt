The authors present a novel framework, the Multi-fidelity Multi-armed bandit, addressing a scenario where arms can be sampled at varying fidelity levels, each incurring a specific cost. Lower fidelity samples approximate the highest fidelity distribution, and the authors introduce an algorithm that adapts to sequences of available approximations and associated costs, accompanied by a regret analysis and a problem-specific lower bound. Numerical results on synthetic datasets demonstrate performance gains over UCB. While the proposed framework holds promise and potential for significant impact with further development, I currently assess the paper as unsuitable for publication.

My concerns are detailed below:

1/ The presentation of notation is unclear and fragmented, hindering the comprehension of definitions. For example, the variable *s* is initially used without definition (appearing only on a subsequent page after its first use) and then receives limited further attention. 

2/ The illustrative example utilizing advertising is, at times, misleading. The paper draws connections between ad display time and non-stationarity, a concept largely irrelevant to the core problem being addressed. Revisiting this example with a more pertinent illustration would be beneficial.

3/ The justification for the regret analysis in this multi-fidelity context is questionable. Standard regret analysis aims to maximize long-term gain, identifying the arm with the optimal reward/cost ratio. However, in this framework, the optimal policy consistently favors the arm with the highest mean reward, irrespective of cost. This prioritization potentially obscures the cost associated with unnecessary sampling at lower fidelity levels.

4/ A sample (or more accurately, cost) complexity analysis would be a valuable addition. This could be motivated by practical applications, such as prototype testing in product development, where the objective is to identify the best design with high probability while minimizing the cumulative cost of testing before mass production. Real-world constraints, including regulations and existing infrastructure, often necessitate the use of low-fidelity approximations before investing in costly, high-fidelity evaluations.

5/ Finally, portions of the text are cumbersome and could benefit from a revision focused on clarity and streamlining.
