The paper investigates multi-armed bandit problems with a fidelity dimension, allowing the learner to select from a finite set of fidelities for each arm pull. Each fidelity level yields a stochastic, i.i.d. reward with a mean within a known error bound (zeta_m) of the true mean, with higher fidelities corresponding to zero error. Importantly, lower fidelities are less costly. The authors formulate this as a sequential decision problem aiming to maximize net reward, propose a novel algorithm, and demonstrate its potential to outperform standard UCB approaches operating at only the highest fidelity. A lower bound on the algorithm’s regret is also provided.

This work addresses a compelling and relevant problem: modeling the fundamental tradeoff between information gain, cost, and reward in online learning, specifically within the stochastic bandit framework. As such, it offers a valuable benchmark for future research. While the paper appears technically sound, a significant weakness lies in the insufficient explanation of its results and underlying assumptions. 

The adopted regret definition feels somewhat unnatural; an additive penalty for resource consumption (cost) might be more intuitive than the current multiplicative approach. While the authors motivate their definition with an ad-display example, its generality is questionable. Investigating the impact of incorporating cost additively into the total reward (or regret) would be beneficial. 

Furthermore, Assumption 1, which imposes constraints on the fidelity parameters, appears artificial and its justification within a general learning context is unclear. It’s crucial to understand whether the algorithm’s performance is critically dependent on this assumption, and if so, whether a method exists to select a suitable subsequence of fidelities when the zeta_m values do not decay at the rate specified. Although the authors claim the assumption is not crucial, it appears to be utilized in the proofs, raising concerns about its influence on both algorithm design and performance. The algorithm seems optimized for specific zeta configurations, but the rationale for these configurations remains unexplained. The gap in the lower bound also warrants further investigation and potentially a refinement of the proposed strategy.

Interpreting the main result (Theorem 2, the regret bound for MF-UCB) and comparing it to UCB (presumably at the highest fidelity, though this isn’t explicitly stated) is challenging due to the highly technical presentation. The relative performance of MF-UCB appears sensitive to Delta and [[k]], which are themselves dependent on the complex sequence of sets \cal{K}^(m). A more transparent explanation would significantly strengthen the authors’ argument. 

In summary, the paper begins promisingly by introducing an interesting learning model, but clarity diminishes when presenting and interpreting the results. Minor issues include a typo on line 56 ("near-optimal"), an unnecessary period on line 126 before "for all m < M", and a suggestion to use curly braces for set notation on line 179 to define [[k]].
