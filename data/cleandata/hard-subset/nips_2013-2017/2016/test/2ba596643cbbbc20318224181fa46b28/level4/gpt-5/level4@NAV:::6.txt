This paper investigates a new multi-armed bandit setting with multiple fidelity levels. There are M fidelities at which the learner can pull an arm, where each higher fidelity yields a more accurate approximation of the true rewards but incurs a higher cost. A policy that exploits cheaper low-fidelity feedback to prune many suboptimal arms, while reserving expensive high-fidelity evaluations for a small set of promising arms, can outperform the standard UCB algorithm that ignores the fidelity structure. The framework is relevant to online advertising, where one might display ads briefly to approximate long-run clicks under cost constraints. For this problem, the authors introduce a new regret notion, propose an algorithm with theoretical guarantees (including a regret lower bound), and present simulations. The analysis is original. Since each \varepsilon^{(m)}—the deviation of the m-th fidelity mean from the highest-fidelity mean—is assumed known a priori, how are these values determined in practice (e.g., in the advertising setting)? Can this assumption be relaxed?
