

The paper introduces a "multi-fidelity" extension to the multi-armed bandit problem, where each arm can be observed at M different fidelity levels with increasing cost and decreasing bias. At fidelity m, the expected reward estimate deviates from the true reward by at most ζ(m), with ζ(1) > ζ(2) > ... > ζ(M) = 0. The authors propose MF-UCB, a modified UCB algorithm that adaptively selects fidelity levels to minimize regret. The regret is defined to account for the cost of using lower fidelities, and the authors provide theoretical guarantees showing that MF-UCB can achieve better regret than standard UCB when the number of high-reward arms is small.

This work extends the classical bandit framework to incorporate multiple levels of observation quality, addressing a gap in the literature. While related to budgeted bandits [13, 15] and multi-fidelity optimization [7, 10], this paper is distinct in formalizing regret in the multi-fidelity setting and providing algorithmic and theoretical analysis specific to bandit problems. The paper builds upon UCB methods [2, 3] but addresses new challenges in the multi-fidelity context, making it a meaningful contribution to the bandit literature.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with a clear regret bound for MF-UCB and a matching lower bound under certain conditions. The assumptions (e.g., Assumption 1) are reasonable and well-justified. The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and includes helpful figures illustrating the algorithm's behavior. The empirical results demonstrate consistent improvements over standard UCB across synthetic problems.

The main strength of this paper is its novel formalization of the multi-fidelity bandit problem, which is well-motivated by practical applications like online advertising (where shorter ad displays can estimate click-through rates) and algorithm selection (where cheaper approximations of algorithm performance can be used). The theoretical analysis is sound, and the empirical results substantiate the theoretical claims.

However, the paper could strengthen its motivation by more clearly justifying why low fidelity should be modeled as introducing bias rather than variance. While the paper provides examples, the claim that shorter ad displays yield a biased estimate of long-term performance is questionable (shorter displays might simply have higher variance due to fewer impressions). The authors should address this distinction more thoroughly. The regret bound, while technically sound, is complex and challenging to interpret for practitioners; the paper could better highlight when and by how much the improvement over standard UCB is expected.

The simulation results are convincing but lack sufficient justification for parameter choices. Appendix C provides parameter values but doesn't explain why these specific values were selected or how they relate to real-world scenarios. The paper claims MF-UCB outperforms UCB by 3-4x in simulations, but it's unclear whether this represents typical performance or is an artifact of the parameter choices.

Despite these weaknesses, the paper makes a clear contribution to bandit theory. The problem is significant for applications where high-fidelity observations are costly, and the algorithm provides a principled way to leverage cheaper approximations. The theoretical and empirical results are strong, and the paper is well-written with clear mathematical exposition.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel formalization of multi-fidelity bandits
- Sound theoretical analysis with matching lower bound
- Clear empirical demonstration of improvement over standard UCB
- Well-motivated by practical applications

Arguments against acceptance:
- Insufficient justification for bias modeling approach
- Complex regret bound that's difficult to interpret
- Lack of justification for simulation parameters
- Unclear practical significance of the magnitude of improvement

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution to the bandit literature with clear theoretical and empirical merits. The minor weaknesses in motivation and interpretation do not undermine the paper's technical contributions. The work advances the state of the art in bandit theory and has potential practical applications in domains where observation costs are a concern. I recommend acceptance.
