The paper introduces a "multi-fidelity" version of the multi-armed bandit problem, where each of the K arms can be played at M different levels of "fidelity". The fidelity levels affect the trade-off between cost and accuracy of the arm's reward estimate. Specifically, lower fidelity levels are less costly but may lead to greater bias in the estimate, with the upper bound of the bias depending on the fidelity level. The paper analyzes a natural extension of the Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) algorithm to this problem, where the confidence interval width is adjusted to account for the uncertainty introduced by the bias of low-fidelity sampling.

The paper provides theorems with regret bounds for this algorithm, which are somewhat complex and difficult to interpret. However, the authors argue that these bounds are often significantly better than the regret bounds obtained by always playing arms at the highest fidelity and using the standard UCB algorithm. Simulation results are presented to support this claim, showing that the proposed algorithm, MF-UCB, can achieve a 3-4 fold improvement in regret compared to UCB in certain scenarios.

Initially, I found the modeling choice of lower fidelity introducing more bias to be counterintuitive, as it seemed equally plausible that lower fidelity could introduce more variance instead. However, the paper's motivating examples, such as algorithm selection for machine learning problems, helped to justify this choice. Nevertheless, I remain unconvinced by one of the provided motivations, namely that displaying an ad for a shorter time interval yields a biased estimate of its effectiveness over longer intervals. Furthermore, in the context of algorithm selection for machine learning, it is unclear why minimizing regret would be the primary goal of an algorithm designer.

In summary, while I found the connection between the model and the motivating applications to be somewhat weak, it is not a fatal flaw. The main contribution of the paper appears to be the formulation of the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit problem itself. The proposed algorithm is a fairly standard application of the "optimism in the face of uncertainty" principle, and the performance guarantee, although difficult to interpret, suggests potential benefits over the standard UCB algorithm. However, the simulation results are challenging to assess, and it is unclear whether the observed improvement in regret is typical of the relative performance of the two algorithms in practice or an artifact of the chosen parameter values.
