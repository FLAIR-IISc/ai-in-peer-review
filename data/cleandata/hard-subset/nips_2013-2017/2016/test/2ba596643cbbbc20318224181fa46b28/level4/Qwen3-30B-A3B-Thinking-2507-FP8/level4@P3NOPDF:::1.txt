

The paper introduces a compelling extension to multi-armed bandits by incorporating a *fidelity* parameter for each arm, where selecting a lower fidelity (e.g., cheaper, noisier observations) yields rewards with known error bounds (ζₘ) and reduced cost, while higher fidelity (ζₘ = 0) provides exact rewards at greater expense. The authors formalize a net reward objective, propose the MF-UCB algorithm, and claim it outperforms standard UCB operating solely at high fidelity. The problem is relevant for cost-aware online learning, and the technical content is sound. However, significant gaps in justification and presentation undermine the contribution’s impact.

The primary concern lies in the regret formulation. The paper defines regret as *multiplicatively* scaled by cost (e.g., net reward = total reward × cost factor), which appears arbitrary. A more natural alternative—penalizing cost *additively* in regret (e.g., regret = (expected reward) − (observed reward + cost))—would better align with standard cost-sensitive learning. While the ad-display example motivates the current definition, it lacks generality. The authors should explicitly justify why multiplicative scaling is preferable and analyze how results would change under additive cost, as this choice critically shapes the problem’s interpretation and applicability.

Assumption 1, which constrains the decay rate of ζₘ (e.g., ζₘ ≤ C/m^α), is particularly problematic. It is introduced without motivation, and its necessity is unclear: *Why would this hold in general learning scenarios?* Crucially, the paper states the assumption is "not crucial" yet relies on it for regret bounds. If ζₘ decays slower than required (violating Assumption 1), the regret bound becomes invalid. The authors should address this: (1) provide conditions under which Assumption 1 *must* hold for practical fidelity sequences, or (2) propose a method to select a subsequence of fidelities satisfying the assumption (e.g., skipping slow-decaying ζₘ). Without this, the algorithm’s performance guarantee is restricted to artificial settings, weakening the paper’s core claim.

The algorithm design (MF-UCB) appears tailored to specific ζₘ sequences satisfying Assumption 1, but the paper fails to justify *why* these sequences are "natural" or how they arise in practice. For instance, if ζₘ decays slowly (e.g., ζₘ ∼ 1/√m), does MF-UCB still outperform UCB? The authors should discuss robustness to non-conforming sequences. Additionally, the lower bound (Theorem 1) has a gap relative to the upper bound (Theorem 2), and the paper does not investigate whether this gap stems from suboptimal analysis or inherent limitations of the model. A tighter lower bound or discussion of the gap’s implications would strengthen the theoretical contribution.

The presentation of results is overly technical, obscuring key insights. The regret bound in Theorem 2 depends on complex parameters (Δ, [[k]], ℳ^(m)), making it difficult to compare MF-UCB’s performance to UCB (which operates at *high fidelity*, though this is not explicitly stated). The authors should provide: (1) a concrete example (e.g., with specific ζₘ sequences) showing when MF-UCB *improves* regret over UCB, and (2) a clear explanation of how Δ and [[k]] relate to the fidelity hierarchy. For instance, does MF-UCB excel when ζₘ decays rapidly, and how does this trade-off with cost? A high-level interpretation would clarify the practical value.

Minor issues include:
- Line 56: "near-optimal" → "near-optimal" (typo fixed).
- Line 126: Remove unnecessary period before "for all m < M".
- Line 179: Use curly braces for set notation (e.g., {k} instead of [[k]]).

In summary, the paper tackles an important problem (cost-aware bandits) with a technically solid approach. However, the regret formulation, reliance on Assumption 1 without justification, lack of robustness analysis, and opaque presentation of results prevent it from making a clear, generalizable contribution. Addressing these would significantly elevate the work. As stated, the paper is not sufficiently convincing for publication.
