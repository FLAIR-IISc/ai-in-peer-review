This paper investigates a variation of the classic stochastic K-armed bandit problem, specifically addressing scenarios where inexpensive approximations of each arm’s reward distribution are readily available. The authors formalize this setting as a multi-fidelity bandit problem, leveraging these cheaper evaluations to accelerate learning. 

The primary contribution of this work lies in the derivation of theoretical regret bounds for the proposed multi-fidelity bandit approach. These bounds provide a formal guarantee on the algorithm’s performance and quantify the benefit of incorporating lower-fidelity data. The paper supports these theoretical findings with simulations that effectively demonstrate the practical impact of utilizing multi-fidelity information. 

The presentation is clear and accessible, making the core ideas of the paper easy to grasp. The simulations convincingly illustrate the performance gains achieved by the multi-fidelity bandit method, particularly in situations where high-fidelity evaluations are costly. Importantly, the authors include a detailed analysis establishing a lower bound on performance, showcasing a theoretical improvement over existing methods. This analysis strengthens the claim that the proposed approach is fundamentally more efficient in these settings.

Overall, the paper presents a well-defined problem, a sound theoretical analysis, and compelling empirical validation. The formalization of the multi-fidelity bandit problem is a valuable contribution, and the regret bounds provide a strong guarantee on performance. The simulations clearly demonstrate the advantages of the proposed method, and the lower bound analysis solidifies its theoretical significance.
