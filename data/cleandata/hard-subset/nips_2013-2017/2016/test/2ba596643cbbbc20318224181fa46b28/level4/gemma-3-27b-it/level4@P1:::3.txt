The paper introduces a multi-fidelity extension to the classic multi-armed bandit problem. In this setting, each of the *K* arms can be played at *M* different fidelities, where lower fidelity evaluations are cheaper but provide potentially biased estimates of the true reward. The bias increases with decreasing fidelity. The authors analyze a UCB-based algorithm adapted to this setting, modifying the confidence bounds to account for the uncertainty introduced by the fidelity-dependent bias. The analysis yields regret bounds, which, while complex, are argued to be significantly better than those achieved by standard UCB when always using the highest fidelity. Simulation results are presented to support this claim.

Initially, I questioned the modeling choice of representing lower fidelity as introducing *bias* rather than *variance*. While increased variance seems intuitively connected to lower fidelity evaluations, the motivating examples – such as algorithm selection for machine learning – demonstrate the validity of modeling bias. However, the justification for one example, concerning the impact of shorter ad display times on click-through rate, felt less convincing. Furthermore, in the algorithm selection context, it’s unclear why regret minimization would be the primary objective for a designer. Despite these reservations, the connection between the model and its motivations is not a fatal flaw.

The primary contribution of this work appears to be the formulation of the multi-fidelity bandit problem itself. The proposed algorithm is a natural application of the "optimism in the face of uncertainty" principle. The resulting regret bound, however, is difficult to interpret. The simulation results, conducted on synthetic problems, demonstrate improvements of a factor of 3-4 in regret compared to standard UCB. It remains unclear whether this performance improvement is representative of typical scenarios or an artifact of the chosen parameter values, which lack detailed justification in Appendix C.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Problem Formulation:** The multi-fidelity bandit problem is a valuable extension of the classical setting, with potential applications in various domains.
*   **Natural Algorithm:** The proposed MF-UCB algorithm is a straightforward adaptation of the well-established UCB approach.
*   **Theoretical Analysis:** The paper provides a theoretical analysis of the algorithm’s performance, establishing regret bounds.
*   **Empirical Validation:** Simulation results demonstrate the potential benefits of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Complex Regret Bound:** The derived regret bound is difficult to interpret and assess practically.
*   **Motivating Examples:** While generally sound, the connection between the model and some of the motivating examples is weak.
*   **Simulation Parameter Selection:** The parameter choices in the simulations are not fully justified, hindering the interpretability of the results.
*   **Bias vs. Variance:** The initial modeling choice of bias rather than variance in lower-fidelity evaluations requires some justification, though the authors provide adequate explanations.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a novel and potentially impactful problem formulation.
*   The proposed algorithm is a reasonable approach to solving the problem.
*   The theoretical analysis provides insights into the algorithm’s performance.
*   The simulation results, while requiring further scrutiny, support the claim of improved performance over standard UCB.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The regret bound is complex and difficult to interpret.
*   The connection to some of the motivating examples is tenuous.
*   The simulation setup lacks sufficient justification for the chosen parameter values.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a worthwhile contribution to the field of bandit algorithms. The multi-fidelity setting is a natural and potentially useful generalization of the classical problem. While the technical details are somewhat complex and the simulation setup could be strengthened, the overall approach is sound and the results are promising. I recommend acceptance, with the caveat that the authors should consider clarifying the regret bound and providing more justification for the simulation parameters in a future revision.
