- Introduces the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit: each arm has cheap, low-fidelity approximations plus an expensive high-fidelity reward.
- Motivating examples include hyperparameter tuning, A/B testing, and simulations where evaluations vary in cost and accuracy.
- Proposes MF-UCB, an extension of UCB that selects arms using the tightest confidence across fidelities and adaptively chooses fidelities to play.
- Fidelity selection uses cheap evaluations to rapidly eliminate clearly suboptimal arms and reserves expensive pulls for promising candidates.
- Derives an upper bound on cost-weighted regret showing MF-UCB can substantially outperform a naive high-fidelity-only UCB, especially when many arms are far from optimal.
- Provides a problem-specific lower bound and shows MF-UCB is near-optimal under certain conditions.
- Uses a partitioning of arms (K(m)) in the analysis to clarify which arms are eliminated at each fidelity level.
- Empirical validation via synthetic simulations supports the theoretical benefits of the multi-fidelity approach.
- Strength: novel and well-motivated problem with clear practical relevance.
- Strength: strong theoretical analysis with both upper and lower bounds explaining the source of improvement.
- Strength: clear, well-written presentation and intuitive algorithm design with justified switching criteria.
- Weakness: relies on known bias bounds ζ(m), which may be unavailable in practice and could affect performance and guarantees.
- Weakness: experiments are only on synthetic data; no real-world multi-fidelity evaluation provided.
- Weakness: minor theoretical looseness—gap between upper and lower bounds for a subset of arms (K^(m)), noted but unresolved.
- Overall: high-quality, significant work that warrants acceptance and is likely to inspire follow-up research.
