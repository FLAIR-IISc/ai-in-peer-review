Summary
The paper introduces a multi-fidelity variant of the stochastic K-armed bandit. Each arm can be evaluated at one of M fidelities: higher fidelities are more accurate but more expensive, while lower fidelities yield biased approximations at reduced cost. The model assumes known fidelity-specific bias bounds ζ(m) and costs λ(m), with ζ(1) > … > ζ(M) = 0 and λ(1) < … < λ(M). The authors define a cost-aware regret under a capital budget Λ, where the pseudo-reward is always the highest-fidelity mean, regardless of the fidelity played. They propose MF-UCB, which maintains per-fidelity upper confidence bounds on the highest-fidelity mean via bias-corrected UCBs, picks the arm with the smallest such bound, and selects a fidelity using thresholds γ(m) that trade off bias and cost. Theoretical results show that MF-UCB concentrates expensive queries on a small set of near-optimal arms, yielding regret improvements over single-fidelity UCB when cheap fidelities can eliminate clearly suboptimal arms. A lower bound shows near-optimality under certain conditions. Simulations on synthetic problems corroborate the theory.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on UCB ideas and connects to multi-fidelity optimization and budgeted bandits, but differs from budgeted bandits by leveraging structured, fidelity-specific approximations rather than random costs. It also relates to works using weak/strong labelers in active learning and multi-fidelity reinforcement learning, but focuses on exploration–exploitation with biased approximations and cost-aware regret.

Strengths
- Originality: A clear, well-motivated formalization of multi-fidelity bandits with biased, costed approximations and a principled regret notion tied to the highest-fidelity objective.
- Quality: MF-UCB is natural and well-analyzed. The upper bound decomposes regret by fidelity and shows how cheap fidelities filter arms; the lower bound isolates regimes where this is provably necessary. The partition K(m) and threshold design γ(m) are insightful.
- Significance: The framework captures common practice in domains like online advertising and algorithm selection, and suggests concrete gains over single-fidelity bandits when many arms can be cheaply discarded.
- Clarity: Overall presentation is organized, with a clear algorithmic description and intuition (e.g., the cap on information from fidelity m via ζ(m)).

Weaknesses and questions
- Knowledge of ζ(m): The analysis assumes known, fidelity-specific bias bounds. How are these obtained in practice (e.g., in advertising or simulations)? What happens if they are misspecified? Can ζ(m) be learned online or handled via robust bounds? Some sensitivity analysis or an adaptive variant would strengthen the practical relevance.
- Assumption 1: This regularity ensures the benefit of fidelity staging. It would help to discuss how often it holds in realistic settings and the consequences when it fails (e.g., closely spaced fidelities with similar costs and biases).
- Regret definition: Always crediting the highest-fidelity mean aligns with the long-term objective but may not fit all applications. The paper briefly mentions alternatives; more discussion on when the proposed notion is appropriate, and how the algorithm or analysis changes under other definitions, would be valuable.
- Parameter sensitivity: The choice γ(m) = ψ^{-1}( λ(m)/λ(m+1) · ψ(ζ(m)) ) is theoretically motivated. Empirical sensitivity to mis-specified costs or ψ, and to the tuning parameter ρ, would be informative.
- Empirical evaluation: Experiments are synthetic only. Even a small semi-synthetic study (e.g., simulated fidelities from real clickstream statistics or ML model selection with subsampling as low fidelity) would strengthen the empirical case. Comparisons to alternative baselines (e.g., Thompson sampling variants adapted to multi-fidelity) would also be useful.
- Gap to lower bound for K(m)7: The discussion notes a gap when some lower-fidelity gaps are small but still positive. A brief exploration of heuristics that occasionally validate suboptimality at a cheaper lower fidelity (without incurring worst-case high-fidelity costs) could be interesting future work.

Pro/Con arguments for acceptance
Pros:
- Timely and well-scoped problem formulation with clear practical relevance.
- Strong theoretical contributions: bias-aware UCB design, regret upper bounds, and a meaningful lower bound.
- Clean intuition: use low-cost fidelities to eliminate many arms; reserve expensive queries for promising arms.
- Improves over standard UCB under plausible conditions; simulations align with theory.

Cons:
- Reliance on known ζ(m) and Assumption 1; limited guidance on estimation or robustness.
- Empirical validation limited to synthetic data; no real or semi-real use case.
- Some notational heaviness and minor presentation issues (listed below).

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide guidance or methods to estimate ζ(m) from pilot data, or design an adaptive/robust MF-UCB that learns or upper-bounds ζ(m) online.
- Add sensitivity experiments for mis-specified ζ(m), λ(m), and ρ, and report empirical robustness.
- Include a semi-synthetic or real-case study and comparisons to additional baselines.
- Expand the discussion of regret notions for settings where low-fidelity outcomes have different utility or risk profiles.
