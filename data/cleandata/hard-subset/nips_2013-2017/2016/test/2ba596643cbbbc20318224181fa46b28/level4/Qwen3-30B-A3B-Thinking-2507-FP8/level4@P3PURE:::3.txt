

The paper introduces a "multi-fidelity" variant of the multi-armed bandit problem, where each of K arms can be played at M fidelity levels. Lower fidelity incurs lower cost but yields reward estimates with potentially higher bias, as sampling at fidelity m draws from a distribution whose mean deviates from the arm's true expected reward by an amount bounded by a fidelity-dependent term. The authors analyze a UCB-based algorithm that adjusts confidence interval widths to account for bias from low-fidelity sampling. The analysis yields complex regret bounds that are difficult to interpret, though the paper argues these bounds typically outperform the regret of always using the highest fidelity with standard UCB. A brief simulation section supports this claim.  

The choice to model low fidelity as introducing bias (rather than variance) is initially puzzling, as the latter interpretation seems equally plausible. While the motivating examples (e.g., machine learning algorithm selection) justify the bias model, the application to ad display duration—where shorter intervals supposedly yield biased effectiveness estimates—remains questionable. Additionally, in machine learning algorithm selection, the relevance of regret minimization as a design goal is unclear. Thus, while the model-application connection is notably weak, it does not constitute a disqualifying flaw.  

The primary contribution lies in formulating the multi-fidelity bandit problem itself. The algorithm is a standard application of "optimism in the face of uncertainty," and the regret guarantee appears disappointingly complex and opaque. The simulation results on synthetic data are hard to assess: Appendix C details parameter choices but fails to justify them. While MF-UCB’s regret consistently outperforms UCB’s by a factor of 3–4 in the experiments, it remains unclear whether this gap is typical in practice or an artifact of specific parameter selections (e.g., ζ^{(m)} values).
