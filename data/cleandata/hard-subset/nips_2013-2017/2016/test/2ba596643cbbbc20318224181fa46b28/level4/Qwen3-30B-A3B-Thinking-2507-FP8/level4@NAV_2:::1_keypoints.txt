- Paper introduces a multi-fidelity bandit setting where each arm has multiple fidelity levels with known bias bounds ζ(m) and increasing costs λ(m).
- Authors define a regret measure that multiplies cost with reward gap rather than adding cost.
- Propose MF-UCB, an algorithm that uses cheaper low-fidelity pulls to eliminate suboptimal arms before high-fidelity evaluation.
- Theoretical guarantees show MF-UCB achieves better regret than standard UCB that uses only the highest fidelity.
- A lower bound is established indicating MF-UCB is nearly optimal under certain conditions.
- Strength: Problem formulation addresses a meaningful tradeoff between information cost and reward with clear application relevance (e.g., advertising, algorithm selection).
- Strength: Framework extends classical bandit literature to incorporate multiple fidelity levels.
- Strength: Theoretical analysis provides insights on leveraging cheaper approximations to improve efficiency.
- Strength: Empirical results on synthetic problems corroborate theoretical claims.
- Weakness: Regret definition is unnatural—multiplicative cost-regret coupling seems unjustified and motivated mainly by one example.
- Weakness: Assumption 1 on the decay rate of ζ(m) appears arbitrary and lacks justification or discussion of necessity.
- Weakness: Paper does not address how to handle cases where Assumption 1 fails.
- Weakness: Gap between the lower bound and MF-UCB’s performance for some arms (e.g., K^(m)_7) needs further investigation.
- Weakness: Algorithm design seems tailored to specific ζ(m) sequences without clear justification that those sequences are appropriate.
- Weakness: Main theorem (Theorem 2) is hard to interpret due to complex dependencies on partitioned sets and notation.
- Weakness: Presentation becomes overly technical and obscures the main contributions despite a strong theoretical foundation.
