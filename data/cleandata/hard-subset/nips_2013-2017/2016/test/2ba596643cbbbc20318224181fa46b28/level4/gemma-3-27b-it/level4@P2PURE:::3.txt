The paper introduces a “multi-fidelity” extension to the multi-armed bandit problem, featuring K arms each playable at M different fidelity levels. Lower fidelity pulls are less expensive but introduce potential bias into the reward estimate – specifically, the expected value of the observed reward distribution at fidelity *m* may deviate from the arm’s true expected reward, with an upper bound on this deviation dependent on *m*. The authors analyze a UCB-based algorithm adapted to this setting, increasing the confidence interval width to account for the bias inherent in lower fidelity samples. The algorithm’s performance is characterized by regret bounds, which, while mathematically rigorous, are complex and difficult to interpret. The paper argues these bounds often represent a significant improvement over the performance of standard UCB applied to arms always played at the highest fidelity. Simulation results are provided to support this claim. Initially, I questioned the modeling choice of lower fidelity as introducing bias rather than increased variance, a representation that also appears intuitively plausible. However, the paper’s motivating examples – such as algorithm selection for machine learning – demonstrated the validity of the bias-focused approach, though I remain unconvinced by the advertising example (where shorter ad display times are posited to yield biased estimates of long-term effectiveness). Furthermore, within the context of algorithm selection, the objective of minimizing regret for an algorithm designer isn’t immediately clear. Overall, the connection between the proposed model and its motivating applications feels somewhat tenuous, although not critically flawed. The primary contribution appears to be the formulation of the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit problem itself. The algorithm represents a standard application of the “optimism in the face of uncertainty” principle. As previously noted, the resulting performance guarantee is difficult to interpret. The simulation results, performed on synthetic problems, are challenging to evaluate; while Appendix C details the parameter settings used, it offers no justification for these choices. The simulations suggest MF-UCB achieves a 3-4x improvement in regret over standard UCB in many cases. It is unclear whether this performance gap is representative of the algorithms’ typical relative behavior in practice, or a consequence of the specific parameter values (e.g., \zeta^{(m)}) selected for evaluation.
