The paper addresses a variant of the multi-armed bandit problem, where the learner must choose an 'fidelity' level from a finite set for each arm, with the fidelity affecting the mean reward and cost. Specifically, pulling an arm at a given fidelity yields a stochastic iid reward with a mean within a known error margin of the true mean, where the error margins decrease with increasing fidelity. The authors propose a new algorithm for this problem, which balances the trade-off between information, cost, and reward, and provide a lower bound for its regret. The paper explores an interesting problem, modeling the trade-off between cheap, low-information pulls and expensive, high-information pulls in online learning. This problem is relevant and could serve as a useful benchmark for future improvements. However, the paper's technical soundness is undermined by a lack of clear explanations for its results and assumptions. The definition of regret used in the paper seems counterintuitive, as it penalizes resource consumption multiplicatively rather than additively. While the authors provide an example to motivate their definition, it may not be the most general or natural approach. It would be beneficial to explore the impact of additively factoring in the cost of fidelity observations on the total reward or regret. The introduction of Assumption 1 appears artificial and lacks clear justification, particularly regarding the constraints on the fidelity parameters. It is unclear why the authors expect this assumption to hold in general learning problems or how to select a subsequence of fidelities to satisfy the assumption when the error margins do not decay as required. Although the authors claim that the assumption is not crucial, it is used in the proofs, and its influence on the algorithm's design and performance is unclear. The algorithm's design seems tailored to specific error margin configurations, but the justification for these configurations is missing. Furthermore, the gap in the lower bound requires more investigation and potentially a re-design of the proposed strategy. The main result, Theorem 2, is difficult to interpret due to the technical nature of the presentation, and a more explicit explanation would be beneficial. The relative gain or loss of the proposed algorithm depends on complex parameters and sequences, making it challenging to understand its advantages over UCB. Overall, the paper presents an interesting learning model but lacks clarity in presenting its results. Minor corrections are needed, including typos on lines 56, 126, and 179.
