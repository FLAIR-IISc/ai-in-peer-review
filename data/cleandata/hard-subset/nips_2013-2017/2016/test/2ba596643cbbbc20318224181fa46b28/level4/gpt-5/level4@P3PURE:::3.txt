This paper introduces a multi-fidelity variant of the multi-armed bandit problem with K arms and M fidelity levels per arm. Lower fidelities are cheaper but introduce bias: pulling an arm at fidelity m yields a sample whose expectation may deviate from the arm’s true expected reward by a fidelity-dependent bound. The paper analyzes a natural UCB-style algorithm that inflates confidence intervals to account for the additional uncertainty due to this bias. The theoretical analysis yields regret bounds that are difficult to interpret; the authors argue these bounds can often be substantially better than those obtained by always sampling at the highest fidelity with a standard UCB algorithm. A brief simulation section supports this claim.

I was initially puzzled by the decision to model lower fidelity as increased bias rather than increased variance; the latter also seems well motivated. The examples given (e.g., algorithm selection for machine learning problems) make the bias model plausible overall, though one motivation remains unconvincing: the claim that showing an ad for a shorter time window yields a biased estimate of its effectiveness over longer windows. Moreover, in the algorithm selection example, it is unclear why regret minimization is the natural objective for an algorithm designer in that application. In short, the connection between the formal model and the motivating applications feels weak, though not so weak as to be a disqualifying flaw.

The main innovation appears to be the formulation of the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit problem itself. The algorithm is a fairly standard application of optimism in the face of uncertainty. The performance guarantees, while nontrivial, are disappointingly hard to parse; the regret bounds are messy and their practical implications are not immediately clear. The paper contends that these guarantees are often significantly better than always using the highest fidelity with standard UCB, but the results would be more compelling if the conditions under which improvements occur were clearer.

The experimental section is hard to assess. The simulations are synthetic; Appendix C details the parameter settings but provides no justification for these choices. Empirically, MF-UCB often improves over UCB by a factor of 3–4. Should we expect this to be typical in practice, or is it an artifact of the specific parameterization (e.g., the chosen values of ζ^(m))?

Overall assessment:
- Strengths: Clear and interesting problem formulation; plausible motivation for modeling low fidelity as bias in some settings; a natural UCB-based approach; some empirical gains.
- Weaknesses: Limited and occasionally unconvincing connection to applications; theoretical bounds are difficult to interpret; lack of justification for experimental parameter choices; unclear whether observed empirical gains generalize.

Questions and suggestions for the authors:
- Clarify when and why the bias-based fidelity model is preferable to (or more realistic than) a variance-based model, and address the ad example more convincingly.
- Explain the appropriateness of regret as the objective in the algorithm selection application.
- Provide more interpretable statements or corollaries of the regret bounds, and specify conditions under which MF-UCB improves over always using the highest fidelity.
- Justify the simulation parameters (including ζ^(m)) and discuss how sensitive the relative performance is to these choices, to help readers judge whether the observed 3–4x improvement is typical or scenario-specific.
