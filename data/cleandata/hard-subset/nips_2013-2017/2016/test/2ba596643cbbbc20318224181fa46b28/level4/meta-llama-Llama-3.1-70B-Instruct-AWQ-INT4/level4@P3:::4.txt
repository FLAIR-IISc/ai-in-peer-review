The authors provide a new framework called Multi-fidelity Multi-armed bandit, which is an interesting and impactful contribution to the field. In this problem, arms can be sampled with different levels of fidelity, and a cost is associated with each one. While having a lower cost, low fidelity samples are drawn from a distribution approximating the highest fidelity distribution.

The authors provide an algorithm, MF-UCB, that adapts to the sequences of available approximations and costs, along with its regret analysis and a lower bound of the problem. They also provide numerical simulations on synthetic problems where the proposed algorithm outperforms UCB.

However, there are some areas that need improvement. The introduction of notations is quite confusing, and they are very dispersed, making their definitions hard to find. For example, the definition of 's' is not clear until it is used again on the next page, and it is barely used in the rest of the paper.

The use of the advertising example is misleading at some points. At many places, correlations with the display time of an ad and non-stationarity are made. Non-stationarity is out of context in this paper. It may be a good idea to use another example that better fits the problem.

The regret analysis in this setting needs more justification. A regret analysis typically involves maximizing a gain in the long run and finding the arm with the best ratio of reward to cost. However, in this paper, the optimal policy is always the arm with the highest mean, regardless of the associated cost. This may hide the cost of sampling low levels when it is not needed.

A sample complexity analysis (or cost complexity analysis) may be useful in the context of Multi-fidelity Multi-armed bandits. This can be motivated by the testing phase of prototypes for the conception of new products. The goal is to find with high probability the best product while minimizing the cumulated cost of the test phase before mass production.

Some parts of the text are unwieldy and need to be rewritten to make the paper more streamlined. With some revisions to address these issues, the paper has the potential to be a valuable contribution to the field.

Strengths:
- The authors provide a new framework for studying exploration-exploitation trade-offs when cheaper approximations to a desired experiment are available.
- The algorithm, MF-UCB, adapts to the sequences of available approximations and costs, and its regret analysis and lower bound are provided.
- The numerical simulations on synthetic problems show that the proposed algorithm outperforms UCB.

Weaknesses:
- The introduction of notations is confusing, and their definitions are hard to find.
- The use of the advertising example is misleading at some points.
- The regret analysis needs more justification.
- A sample complexity analysis (or cost complexity analysis) may be useful in this context.
- Some parts of the text are unwieldy and need to be rewritten.

Recommendation:
The paper has the potential to be a valuable contribution to the field, but it needs some revisions to address the issues mentioned above. With clearer notation, a more fitting example, and a more justified regret analysis, the paper can be improved. Additionally, considering a sample complexity analysis (or cost complexity analysis) may add more value to the paper.
