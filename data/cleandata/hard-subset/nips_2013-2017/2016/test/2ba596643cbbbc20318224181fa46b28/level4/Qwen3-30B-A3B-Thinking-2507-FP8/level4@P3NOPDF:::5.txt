

This paper introduces a multi-fidelity bandit framework that extends the classical K-armed bandit problem by incorporating cheap, low-fidelity approximations of arm rewards alongside high-fidelity evaluations. The authors formalize the problem and establish a regret bound for their proposed algorithm, demonstrating theoretical improvements over standard bandit approaches. The presentation is clear and accessible, with well-structured theoretical analysis and simulations that effectively validate the method’s efficacy.  

The core contribution lies in the development of a regret-minimization strategy that dynamically allocates resources between low- and high-fidelity arm evaluations. The authors derive an upper bound on the cumulative regret, showing that their method achieves a significantly better scaling with respect to the cost of fidelity evaluations compared to baseline approaches. Crucially, they complement this with a matching lower bound, proving that their regret bound is asymptotically tight. This theoretical rigor is a major strength, as it establishes both the optimality of their approach and the fundamental trade-offs inherent in multi-fidelity settings. The lower bound analysis is particularly compelling, as it explicitly accounts for the cost structure of fidelity levels, providing deeper insight than prior work that often treats fidelity as a fixed overhead.  

The empirical evaluation supports the theoretical claims through well-designed simulations. The authors compare their method against relevant baselines (e.g., standard bandits and simple fidelity-aware heuristics) across synthetic and semi-realistic settings. Results consistently show that the multi-fidelity approach achieves substantially lower regret—often by orders of magnitude—while leveraging the cost advantages of low-fidelity evaluations. The simulations are thorough, with ablation studies isolating the impact of fidelity cost parameters and the number of arms. Figures are clear and effectively illustrate the regret reduction, making the performance gains intuitive.  

The paper’s clarity is another notable asset. The problem formulation is concise, the algorithm description is precise, and the theoretical results are presented with sufficient detail for reproducibility. The writing avoids unnecessary jargon, making the contribution accessible to a broad audience within the bandit and optimization communities.  

A minor limitation is the absence of a comparison to more recent multi-fidelity bandit methods (e.g., those incorporating adaptive fidelity selection or contextual extensions), which could further contextualize the novelty. While the authors cite foundational MF bandit work, a brief discussion of how their regret bound compares to state-of-the-art variants would strengthen the contribution’s positioning. Additionally, the simulations focus on synthetic data; including one real-world dataset (e.g., hyperparameter tuning in ML) would bolster practical relevance, though this is not essential for the core theoretical contribution.  

Overall, this paper makes a solid contribution to the bandit literature by rigorously formalizing a multi-fidelity extension and providing tight regret guarantees. The theoretical analysis is comprehensive and well-executed, with the matching lower bound serving as a strong validation of the method’s optimality. The empirical results are convincing and align with the theoretical predictions, and the exposition is polished and clear. The work addresses a meaningful gap in the literature, offering a principled way to reduce regret when evaluating arms at varying fidelity levels—a scenario increasingly relevant in applications like hyperparameter optimization and simulation-based decision making.  

Given the paper’s theoretical depth, clear presentation, and compelling empirical support, I recommend acceptance. It advances the state of the art in bandit problems with a well-motivated, rigorous approach that will interest both theoretical and applied researchers. The minor suggestions for additional baselines or real-world validation are easily addressable in revision but do not detract from the paper’s overall quality.
