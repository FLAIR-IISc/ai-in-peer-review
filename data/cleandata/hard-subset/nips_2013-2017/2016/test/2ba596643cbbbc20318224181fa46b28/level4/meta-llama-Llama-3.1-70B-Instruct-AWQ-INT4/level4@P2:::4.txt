The authors propose a new framework called the Multi-fidelity Multi-armed bandit, which is an extension of the classical stochastic K-armed bandit problem. In this setting, observing the outcome of each arm is expensive, but cheap approximations to this outcome are available. The authors formalize this task as a multi-fidelity bandit, where at each time step, the forecaster may choose to play an arm at any one of M fidelities. The highest fidelity (desired outcome) expends cost λ, while the m fidelity (an approximation) expends λ < λ and returns a biased estimate of the highest fidelity.

The authors develop a novel upper confidence bound procedure, MF-UCB, for this setting and prove that it naturally adapts to the sequence of available approximations and costs, thus attaining better regret than naive strategies that ignore the approximations. They also complement this result with a lower bound and show that MF-UCB is nearly optimal under certain conditions.

The paper provides a thorough analysis of the problem, including a regret analysis and a lower bound. The authors also provide numerical simulations on synthetic problems, where the proposed algorithm outperforms UCB.

However, there are some issues with the paper. The introduction of notations is confusing, and they are dispersed throughout the text, making their definitions hard to find. For example, the variable "s" is not clearly defined until much later in the text. Additionally, the use of advertising as an example is misleading at some points, as it implies non-stationarity, which is not relevant to the problem.

Furthermore, the regret analysis in this setting is not entirely convincing. A regret analysis typically involves maximizing a gain in the long run and finding the arm with the best ratio of reward to cost. However, in this setting, the optimal policy is always the arm with the highest mean, regardless of the associated cost. This may hide the cost of sampling low levels when it is not needed.

A sample complexity analysis (or cost complexity analysis) may be more relevant in the context of Multi-fidelity Multi-armed bandits. This could be motivated by the testing phase of prototypes for the conception of new products, where the goal is to find with high probability the best product while minimizing the cumulated cost of the test phase before mass production.

Some parts of the text are also unwieldy and could benefit from a more streamlined presentation.
