This paper introduces a multi-fidelity variant of the classic stochastic K-armed bandit problem. In this setting, pulling an arm can be done at one of M different "fidelity" levels, each with an associated cost. Lower fidelities are cheaper but provide rewards that are biased approximations of the true (highest fidelity) reward. The authors formalize this problem with a cost-weighted regret notion, propose a new algorithm called Multi-Fidelity Upper Confidence Bound (MF-UCB), and provide a theoretical analysis of its performance. The key idea is that MF-UCB uses cheap, low-fidelity pulls to quickly discard highly suboptimal arms, reserving expensive, high-fidelity pulls for a smaller set of promising candidates. This work extends the substantial body of literature on UCB-style algorithms by incorporating a practical trade-off between the cost and quality of information.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper addresses a novel and practical extension of the multi-armed bandit framework. The problem of balancing exploration-exploitation with varying information costs is highly relevant to many real-world applications, such as online advertising, clinical trials, and algorithm configuration, where preliminary or approximate evaluations are available. The formulation itself is a valuable contribution.
*   **Technical Quality:** The proposed MF-UCB algorithm is an intuitive and sensible extension of the UCB principle to the multi-fidelity setting. The theoretical analysis, which includes both an upper regret bound for the algorithm and a corresponding lower bound for the problem, appears technically solid.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**
*   **Clarity of Main Results:** While the paper starts with a clear motivation, the presentation of the main theoretical results could be significantly improved. Interpreting the regret bound in Theorem 2 and understanding its comparison to a standard UCB baseline is challenging due to the technical density and reliance on the complex definitions of the sets `K(m)`. A more intuitive walkthrough of the result, perhaps with a simplified concrete example, would greatly enhance the paper's impact and make the claimed benefits of MF-UCB more transparent.
*   **Justification of Assumptions and Definitions:**
    *   The cost-weighted regret definition in Equation (1) is central to the paper, but its motivation could be strengthened. The choice of a multiplicative cost factor is justified through an online advertising example, but this may not be the most natural formulation for all scenarios. A discussion of alternatives, such as an additive cost penalty, and how that would change the problem and the proposed solution, would be beneficial.
    *   Assumption 1, which constrains the decay rate of the fidelity approximation errors (`ζ(m)`), feels somewhat artificial. The paper should provide more intuition as to why this structure is expected to hold in practice. More importantly, the authors should clarify how critical this assumption is to the algorithm's design and performance. If it does not hold, can the method be adapted, for instance, by algorithmically selecting a subset of fidelities that satisfy the condition? This would significantly strengthen the generality of the contribution.
*   **Optimality Gap:** The analysis reveals a gap between the algorithm's upper bound and the derived lower bound for certain classes of arms (`K(m)7`). This is an important finding that deserves more discussion. While the authors provide a technical explanation, elaborating on the implications of this gap and whether it suggests a fundamental limitation of the current algorithmic approach or points toward a more sophisticated strategy would be valuable.

**Summary of Arguments:**
*   **Pro-acceptance:** The paper introduces a novel and significant problem setting with clear practical relevance. The proposed MF-UCB algorithm is a non-trivial and well-motivated contribution, and the work appears to be technically sound. It establishes a strong benchmark for future research in this area.
*   **Con-acceptance:** The paper's clarity suffers in its presentation of the main results, making the contributions difficult to fully appreciate. Furthermore, key modeling choices and assumptions (the regret definition, Assumption 1) could be better justified to broaden the perceived applicability of the work.

Overall, this paper tackles an interesting and important problem. While the technical core seems strong, its potential impact is currently hindered by a lack of clarity in the presentation of its key theoretical results and the justification for certain modeling assumptions. Addressing these points would substantially improve the manuscript.
