- Introduces a novel multi-armed bandit setting with M fidelity levels per arm trading off cost and accuracy.  
- Shows using low fidelities for initial elimination and high fidelities for promising arms can outperform standard algorithms (e.g., UCB) in cost-sensitive settings like online advertising.  
- Proposes a new regret definition for the fidelity setting, an algorithm exploiting fidelity structure, and provides theoretical analysis including a regret lower bound.  
- Core contribution: novel, careful regret analysis capturing exploration–exploitation–fidelity interplay and theoretical guarantees for the proposed algorithm.  
- Algorithm intuition: allocate higher-fidelity (costly) evaluations only to arms that look promising from lower-fidelity observations to minimize overall cost.  
- Major practical limitation: assumes known a priori deviations ε^{(m)} between fidelity means and the highest-fidelity mean—a strong, potentially unrealistic assumption.  
- Recommended: discuss/relax the ε^{(m)} assumption (e.g., learn online or add robustness) and analyze sensitivity to estimation errors.  
- Experiments support theory but should compare to more sophisticated constrained/optimization-aware bandit algorithms and explore wider parameter and cost-ratio regimes.
