Summary:
The paper studies a variant of the classical stochastic K-armed bandit problem in which each arm admits cheaper, lower-fidelity approximations. The authors formalize this setting as a multi-fidelity (MF) bandit problem, propose algorithms that leverage low-cost evaluations to guide exploration, and provide theoretical regret guarantees. They complement the theory with simulations demonstrating the benefits of using multiple fidelities. The presentation is generally clear and easy to follow.

Strengths:
- Clear problem formulation: The MF bandit framework is well-motivated and intuitively extends the standard bandit setup to scenarios where approximate, inexpensive feedback is available. This setting is practically relevant to many domains (e.g., simulation-based optimization, hyperparameter tuning with proxies).
- Theoretical contributions: The paper provides regret bounds for the proposed methods and includes lower-bound analysis. The results help quantify how exploiting low-fidelity information can reduce regret relative to single-fidelity baselines, under appropriate assumptions. The theoretical development appears sufficiently thorough and highlights where improvements arise.
- Empirical support: Simulations illustrate the effect of incorporating multiple fidelities and show performance gains consistent with the theory. The experiments are easy to interpret and align with the claimed advantages.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Assumptions on fidelity relationships: The guarantees hinge on how high- and low-fidelity observations relate (e.g., bias, variance, or stochastic dominance). It would help to more explicitly state and discuss these assumptions, provide intuition for when they hold in practice, and analyze sensitivity when they are violated. How robust are the algorithms if low-fidelity signals are weakly correlated or occasionally misleading?
- Knowledge of problem parameters: Do the algorithms require prior knowledge of fidelity biases, noise levels, or costs, or do they estimate them online? Clarifying what is known a priori versus learned adaptively would strengthen the practical relevance and interpretability of the guarantees.
- Tightness and scaling: While the presence of both upper and lower bounds is valuable, it would be useful to comment on the gap between them and whether it is intrinsic or an artifact of analysis. In particular, how do the bounds scale with the number of fidelities, the cost ratios, and the quality of approximations? Are there regimes where the method essentially reduces to single-fidelity exploration because low fidelities are too noisy or biased?
- Experimental breadth: The simulations demonstrate the MF effect, but the empirical section could be expanded to include comparisons against strong single-fidelity and naive multi-fidelity baselines (e.g., uniform allocations across fidelities or heuristics that query low-fidelity first). Including at least one semi-realistic setting or a sensitivity study over fidelity quality and cost parameters would make the case more compelling. Reporting wall-clock or cost-normalized metrics would also clarify practical gains.
- Algorithmic details: More detail on how the method decides when to escalate from low to high fidelity would aid reproducibility. Are there hyperparameters governing switching thresholds, and how sensitive is performance to them?

Clarity and presentation:
The paper is clearly written overall, and the main ideas are easy to understand. A few suggestions for further clarity:
- Provide a concise summary early on of the regret definition used (e.g., cost-aware regret) and how it differs from the standard K-armed bandit regret.
- Clearly separate the statements of upper and lower bounds, including the precise conditions under which each holds, and summarize the main qualitative takeaway (e.g., dependence on costs and approximation errors).
- Include a brief discussion of computational complexity and memory overhead relative to standard UCB/Thompson sampling.
- Minor: standardize notation for fidelities and arms and ensure all symbols are defined when first used.

Overall assessment:
This paper addresses an important and timely generalization of stochastic bandits and provides solid theoretical and empirical evidence that multi-fidelity information can yield meaningful improvements. The formulation is natural, the analysis is careful, and the writing is clear. While broader experiments and additional discussion of the assumptions and robustness would strengthen the work, the current version already offers substantive contributions. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying assumptions, elaborating on parameter knowledge and robustness, and modestly enhancing the empirical evaluation to include stronger baselines and cost-aware metrics.
