The paper introduces a multi-fidelity variant of the multi-armed bandit problem with K arms, each playable at M levels of fidelity. Lower fidelities are cheaper but introduce bias in the observed reward. Specifically, pulling an arm at fidelity m yields a sample whose expectation can deviate from the arm’s true mean by an amount bounded as a function of the fidelity. The paper analyzes a natural UCB-style algorithm for this setting, in which the confidence interval is widened to account for the additional uncertainty due to low-fidelity bias. The theoretical results provide regret bounds for this algorithm and argue that, in many regimes, these bounds are substantially better than those achieved by always pulling at the highest fidelity and running standard UCB over arms. A short simulation section aims to support this claim.

I was initially puzzled by the modeling choice to treat low fidelity as inducing bias rather than increased variance; the latter also seems like a plausible and practically relevant interpretation of “low fidelity.” The motivating examples (e.g., algorithm selection for machine learning tasks) largely persuaded me that modeling low fidelity as bias is reasonable. However, I remain unconvinced by one specific justification: the claim that showing an ad for a shorter time window produces a biased estimate of its performance over longer horizons. Moreover, in the algorithm-selection example, it is unclear why minimizing cumulative regret would be the primary objective for a designer in that context. In sum, the connection between the proposed model and the motivating applications feels weaker than I would like, though not so weak as to be disqualifying.

Methodologically, the main innovation appears to be the formulation of the multi-fidelity multi-armed bandit problem itself. The algorithmic approach is a fairly standard application of the “optimism in the face of uncertainty” principle, adapted to incorporate bias through enlarged confidence intervals. The performance guarantees, while nontrivial, are presented via regret bounds that are complicated and difficult to interpret. The paper asserts that these bounds often improve substantially over those obtained by always using the highest fidelity with standard UCB, but the theorems do not make it easy to see when and by how much.

The empirical evaluation is limited and difficult to assess. Appendix C provides detailed parameter settings for the synthetic experiments but does not justify those choices. In many runs, MF-UCB reduces regret by a factor of 3–4 compared to UCB. It is unclear whether this gap is representative in practice or primarily a consequence of the particular parameterization (e.g., the values of ζ^(m) and related cost/bias trade-offs). Without a more systematic exploration or principled rationale for parameter choices, it is hard to gauge the practical relevance of the reported gains.

Overall, I appreciate the clarity of the problem formulation and the natural extension of UCB to account for bias under multi-fidelity sampling. However, the regret bounds are hard to parse, the empirical section is too thin to substantiate the claimed advantages, and the link to motivating applications remains tentative in places. Strengthening the interpretability of the theory, justifying experimental parameters, and sharpening the application-driven motivations would significantly improve the paper.
