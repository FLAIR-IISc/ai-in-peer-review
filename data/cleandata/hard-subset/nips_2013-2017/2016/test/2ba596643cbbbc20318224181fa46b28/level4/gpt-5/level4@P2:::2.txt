Summary
The paper introduces a stochastic K-armed bandit setting in which each arm can be evaluated at one of M fidelities, with higher fidelities yielding less biased estimates of the true reward but at higher cost. The authors propose a cost-weighted notion of regret that accounts for the spent budget and the long-run (highest-fidelity) reward, and present MF-UCB, an upper-confidence-bound algorithm that adaptively selects both the arm and the fidelity. MF-UCB uses lower fidelities to screen out clearly suboptimal arms and reserves expensive evaluations for promising arms. The paper provides an upper bound on regret showing improved performance over single-fidelity UCB when low fidelities are informative and cheaper, as well as a lower bound demonstrating near-optimality under certain conditions. This work builds on classical UCB methods and is distinct from budgeted MABs and multifidelity optimization in that it explicitly models biased low-fidelity observations, provides a cost-aware regret, and analyzes exploration–exploitation trade-offs across fidelities.

Strengths
- Clear and compelling problem formulation of multi-fidelity bandits with biased approximations and explicit costs.
- A simple, intuitive algorithm (MF-UCB) that leverages low-cost information for arm elimination.
- Theoretical analysis includes both upper and lower bounds; the partitioning into fidelity-dependent arm sets provides insight into where the budget is spent.
- Simulations support the theory, showing reduced regret and appropriate allocation of high-fidelity pulls.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Practicality: The algorithm assumes known fidelity biases ζ(m) and uses these to set thresholds γ(m). In real applications, ζ(m) may be unknown or misspecified. A discussion and/or methods for estimating or adaptively calibrating ζ(m) (e.g., via conservative bounds, sensitivity analysis, pilot studies, or jointly learning per-fidelity bias bounds) would strengthen applicability.
- Constants hidden in “up-to-constants” notation: In the main text, several results hide dependence on algorithmic parameters (e.g., ρ) in constants, which can be misleading in practice. Stating explicit constants or at least highlighting key dependences (on ρ, λ(m), ζ(m), ψ) in the main paper would improve clarity.
- Lower-bound gap: The paper openly notes a gap for certain arms (those in K(m)_7). Briefly discussing whether a variant with more aggressive early-fidelity testing (or data-driven switching rules) could narrow this gap without sacrificing budget efficiency would be helpful.
- Supplement clarity: The main paper is well written, but the supplement contains numerous typos and minor errors that impede readability. Correcting these would significantly improve the presentation.

Quality
- The technical development is sound and carefully argued. The assumptions (concentration, decay of ζ(m), and the budget-aware regret) are clearly stated. Proofs appear solid, though the supplement’s typos should be fixed.

Clarity
- The main exposition is clear and well structured. However, the reliance on asymptotic “≈”/constant-hiding notation in the main text can obscure parameter dependence. Bringing key constants into the main statements or prominently referencing the exact inequalities would help.

Originality
- The formalization of multi-fidelity bandits with biased lower-fidelity observations, a cost-weighted regret targeting highest-fidelity utility, and an algorithm that couples arm and fidelity selection appears novel relative to prior UCB, budgeted bandits, and multifidelity optimization literatures.

Significance
- The setting is relevant to domains like online advertising, algorithm selection, and simulation-based evaluation. The results provide actionable insights on how to structure exploration across fidelities and when multifidelity structure helps. The theory suggests substantial gains when many arms are easily eliminated at low cost.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and well-motivated problem formulation.
- Conceptually simple algorithm with strong theoretical guarantees.
- Clear insight into when and why multifidelity information yields gains.
- Solid empirical support.

Cons for acceptance
- Practical deployment may be hindered by unknown ζ(m) and sensitivity to misspecification.
- Some dependence on parameters (e.g., ρ) is obscured in constants in the main text.
- Numerous minor errors in the supplement; presentation needs polishing.
- Lower-bound gap for certain cases remains open.
