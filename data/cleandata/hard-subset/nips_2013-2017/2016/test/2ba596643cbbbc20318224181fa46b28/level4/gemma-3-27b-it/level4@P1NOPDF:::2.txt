This work introduces a novel multi-armed bandit setting where each arm can be sampled with varying levels of accuracy, or fidelity. The cost of pulling an arm increases with the desired accuracy, and the regret is defined as the product of this cost and the usual pseudo-regret. The authors propose an algorithm to address this setting, MF-UCB, and provide both an upper and a lower bound on its cumulative pseudo-regret. The main paper is clear and well-written. However, the supplementary materials, which contain most of the proofs, are less polished and include numerous typos and minor, easily correctable errors that could confuse the reader (see the list below). Correcting these errors would significantly improve the paper’s readability. 

Additionally, I believe the “up-to-a-multiplicative-constant” notation used throughout the paper can be slightly misleading, as these “constants” are not truly constant. For instance, Theorem 2 includes a factor ρ (a parameter of the algorithm) that is hidden within the constant. Presenting the exact inequalities, as they are written in the supplementary materials, could enhance the clarity of the results.

From a practical perspective, it’s not immediately clear how easily this algorithm could be applied to real-world problems. For example, MF-UCB’s strategy of using low-accuracy pulls to quickly eliminate clearly suboptimal arms may be vulnerable to inaccurate estimation of ζ (the fidelity accuracy). A discussion of the practical limitations and potential approaches to overcome them would strengthen this work.

From a theoretical standpoint, this paper offers valuable insight into the problem, along with interesting theorems and sound proofs. Overall, I consider this a significant and interesting contribution.
