- Introduces a novel information-theoretic framework mapping crowdsourcing to a communication system (source, encoder, noisy channels, decoder).  
- Derives rate-distortion style lower bounds on the number of queries needed to reach a target error, for known and unknown worker skill levels.  
- Proposes and analyzes a concrete query scheme, k-ary Incidence Coding (kIC), with performance bounds under a spammer-hammer worker model.  
- Technically sound application of joint source-channel coding theorems with rigorous derivations.  
- Presents a complete contribution combining general theoretical limits and analysis of a specific coding scheme.  
- Authors clearly state assumptions, including iid data and specific worker channel models (MSC and SHC).  
- Well-written and organized; the communication analogy is explained clearly and the flow from theory to kIC is logical.  
- Results are clearly presented (Figure 2 effectively compares theoretical limits and kIC performance).  
- Original framing of crowdsourcing via rate-distortion theory is novel and distinguishes the work from prior algorithm-focused literature.  
- Derived bounds provide a significant benchmark ("gold standard") for evaluating practical crowdsourcing algorithms.  
- Main limitation: reliance on asymptotic assumptions and simplified worker models; needs discussion of implications for finite, practical scenarios.
