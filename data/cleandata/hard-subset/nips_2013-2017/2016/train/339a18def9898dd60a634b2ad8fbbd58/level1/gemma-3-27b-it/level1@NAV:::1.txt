## Review of “Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing”

This paper investigates the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing through an information-theoretic lens, modeling it as a joint source-channel coding problem. The core idea is to determine the ultimate fidelity achievable given a budget (number of queries) and worker skill levels. The authors establish bounds for both scenarios: unknown skill levels (SL-UK) and perfectly estimated skill levels (SL-CS). They also propose and analyze a *k*-ary incidence coding (kIC) scheme, offering insights into query design and pricing strategies.

The work builds upon existing research in crowdsourcing, particularly focusing on aggregation schemes like majority voting and the Dawid-Skene algorithm, and connects it to fundamental limits derived from information theory. The authors appropriately cite relevant work in both areas, referencing key papers like Dawid & Skene (1999) and Cover & Thomas (2006).  The framing of crowdsourcing as a communication system with noisy channels (workers) is a valuable and novel contribution. It enables the application of well-established information-theoretic tools to a practical problem. The introduction of kIC is also interesting, providing a concrete example of how to translate the theoretical framework into a specific coding scheme.

A key strength of the paper is its rigorous mathematical treatment. Theorems 2 and 3 establish clear bounds on achievable performance.  However, the paper’s clarity suffers somewhat from dense notation and a lack of intuitive explanation for some of the derived equations. While mathematically sound, the connection between the theorems and practical implications for crowdsourcing system design could be strengthened. A weakness is the limited empirical validation. The results are largely theoretical and rely on asymptotic analysis.  Demonstrating the practical relevance of these bounds through simulations or experiments would significantly enhance the impact of the work. Furthermore, the discussion of query pricing (Appendix E) feels somewhat tacked on and lacks detailed analysis.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of information theory to crowdsourcing, providing fundamental limits.
*   Rigorous mathematical analysis and clear theoretical bounds.
*   Introduction of a potentially useful coding scheme (kIC).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited empirical validation; lacks practical demonstration.
*   Clarity could be improved through more intuitive explanations and less dense notation.
*   Query pricing discussion is underdeveloped.



**Overall:** This paper presents a significant theoretical contribution to understanding the limits of crowdsourcing. While it would benefit from more empirical validation and improved clarity, the rigorous analysis and novel framing make it a worthwhile contribution to the field.  I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions** to address the clarity and empirical validation concerns.
