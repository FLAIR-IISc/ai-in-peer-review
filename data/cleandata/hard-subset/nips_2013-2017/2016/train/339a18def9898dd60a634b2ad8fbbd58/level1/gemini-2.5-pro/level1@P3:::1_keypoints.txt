- Proposes an information-theoretic framework modeling crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding problem to analyze budget-fidelity trade-offs.  
- Derives fundamental rate-distortion lower bounds on query rate required to achieve target fidelity for general M-ary labeling.  
- Analyzes two worker-knowledge scenarios (SL-UK unknown skills, SL-CS known skills) and shows knowing skills changes the effective channel capacity.  
- Introduces k-ary Incidence Coding (kIC) as a practical query scheme where workers compare k items at once.  
- Provides a performance bound for kIC under a spammer-hammer worker model and compares it numerically to the theoretical limits.  
- Highlights originality and significance: a novel, principled perspective that sets universal benchmarks for crowdsourcing systems.  
- Praises theoretical quality: sound application of rate-distortion and capacity theory, with insightful SL-UK vs SL-CS distinction.  
- Values the bridge to practice: connects abstract limits to a concrete coding scheme and quantifies the gap between them.  
- Criticizes the standard n → ∞ assumption: finite-length (finite-L) settings common in practice may invalidate or weaken the main theorems.  
- Notes simplistic worker models: MSC and SHC ignore item-dependent difficulty, adversarial or biased behaviors that could affect limits.  
- Flags an oversimplified cost model: using queries-per-item as budget ignores cognitive/monetary cost scaling with k and uses ad-hoc pricing.  
- Points out kIC analysis uses an Oracle decoder that unrealistically identifies hammers/spammers, so its bound is not practically achievable.  
- Requests clearer definitions linking block length n, total items L, and rate R to avoid ambiguity between theoretical and operational rates.  
- Recommends acceptance for conceptual novelty and solid theory but urges revisions to discuss finite-length effects, richer worker/cost models, and practical decoder performance.
