- Models crowdsourcing as a communication system (source, noisy channels, encoder, decoder) to apply information-theoretic tools.
- Main contribution: algorithm-agnostic lower bounds on queries per item for desired accuracy using rate-distortion and channel capacity.
- Bounds derived for workers as M-ary Symmetric Channels in two settings: unknown skill levels (SL-UK) and known skills (SL-CS) — Theorems 2 and 3.
- Proposes k-ary Incidence Coding (kIC): present k items, ask workers to group by label, exploiting invalid response patterns for error correction.
- Analyzes kIC under the Spammer-Hammer Channel model and provides scheme-specific performance lower bounds.
- Distinguishes itself from prior work by focusing on fundamental limits rather than specific inference algorithms or pairwise queries.
- Technical soundness: correct application of rate-distortion theory and joint source-channel coding; solid probabilistic analysis of kIC.
- Main theoretical weakness: relies on simplified worker models (MSC, SHC) that may not capture realistic, task-dependent, or adversarial behaviors.
- Results are asymptotic (large number of items/block lengths); tightness and relevance for practical finite-scale crowdsourcing are not established.
- Writing and organization are clear; the communication-system analogy is well explained and illustrated.
- Presentation of kIC could use a concrete example for k≥3 to better illustrate the error-correction mechanism from invalid patterns.
- Originality: novel application of information theory to derive algorithm-agnostic crowdsourcing limits and generalization to kIC.
- Significance: provides theoretical benchmarks to evaluate how close practical systems are to optimal performance.
- Practical limitations: no experiments or simulations to validate bounds or assess kIC empirically; limited guidance for designing practical, near-optimal systems.
