Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper models label crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding problem and studies fundamental budget–fidelity trade-offs. Items are drawn from a discrete source B(X); workers are modeled as noisy channels. The authors derive information-theoretic lower bounds on the minimum number of queries per item (rate) required to achieve a target average error (distortion) under two scenarios: (i) worker skill levels unknown to both taskmaster and crowdsourcer (SL-UK), and (ii) worker skills perfectly known to the crowdsourcer (SL-CS). Workers are modeled as M-ary symmetric channels (MSC), and bounds express Rmin in terms of source entropy and channel capacities. The paper also proposes k-ary incidence coding (kIC), where each query jointly relates k items, noting its built-in error-correction via invalid configurations. For a spammer–hammer channel (SHC) and binary labeling, it derives a necessary condition on rate for target error (Theorem 4) assuming an oracle decoder, and discusses a simple pricing heuristic. The work positions itself relative to majority voting, Dawid–Skene and EM-based models, spectral/convex inference methods, and bandit-based task allocation (Karger–Oh–Shah; Zhang et al.; Whitehill et al.), adding an information-theoretic perspective that is not tied to a specific inference algorithm.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The rate-distortion framing and use of separation are standard and appropriate for studying ultimate limits. The SL-UK and SL-CS bounds (Theorems 2–3) are plausible and neatly distinguish “average worker” capacity vs. “average of capacities.” The kIC analysis for SHC uses an oracle decoder to deliver a necessary condition; the calculations for spammer error in kIC (Lemma 1) are reasonable and consistent with the intuition that larger k filters invalid responses and improves robustness.
- Assumptions and limitations: The main bounds rely on memoryless MSC/SHC models, independence across workers and items, non-adaptive querying, and infinite blocklength; they ignore item difficulty and non-symmetric confusions common in crowdsourcing (e.g., annotator confusion matrices). The kIC result is only a converse bound under an oracle that perfectly identifies spammers vs. hammers; there is no constructive decoder or achievability result approaching the bounds. Empirical validation on real datasets or AMT is absent; numerical illustrations are limited to plotting the converse vs. an information-theoretic lower bound.
- Completeness: The paper develops lower bounds and some analytical insights but stops short of demonstrating tightness, robustness to model misspecification, or practical gains in realistic settings. The pricing heuristic based on k is a back-of-the-envelope approximation without economic or behavioral validation.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well-organized: modeling (source/channel/code), formulation (rate–distortion), main bounds, then kIC analysis. The intuition behind “invalid configurations” as error-correction in kIC is clearly explained with k=3 examples.
- Notational clarity could be improved. The roles of N (number of source classes), M (number of response options), U and V, and the mapping between them (often U=V and M=N) should be made explicit early and used consistently. Some formulas (e.g., Theorems 2–3) use HN(.) and HM(.) without reminding the reader how these entropies are parameterized; a brief recap would help. The non-adaptive “rate per item” for kIC where each query covers C(k,2) pairwise relations is discussed in Appendix E; a concise accounting in the main text would make cross-method budget comparisons clearer.
- Proofs are deferred to appendices; that is fine for NIPS, but a brief proof sketch for the core theorems would aid readability.

Originality:
- Bringing a clean rate–distortion lens to crowdsourcing and articulating SL-UK vs. SL-CS capacity interpretations are novel and conceptually valuable. Although prior work has derived budget–accuracy trade-offs for specific algorithms and models (e.g., Karger–Oh–Shah; spectral + EM), an explicit separation-based characterization across arbitrary querying/inference schemes is new in this community.
- The kIC idea has predecessors in pairwise/ternary incidence queries for clustering, but the framing of kIC as a code with inherent error-correction and deriving necessary rates for SHC is a useful twist.

Significance:
- If the community adopts these bounds as benchmarks, they can guide the design of adaptive query schemes, worker selection, and pricing. The SL-UK vs. SL-CS gap quantifies the potential value of skill estimation, informing when annotator modeling is worth the extra budget.
- However, the practical impact is limited by strong modeling assumptions and the lack of constructive, near-optimal schemes or empirical evidence. The kIC converse under oracle decoding sets a bar but does not show how close practical decoders can get under realistic worker models.

Strengths:
- Clear information-theoretic formulation of crowdsourcing limits.
- Distinguishes unknown vs. known skills with interpretable bounds.
- Insightful view of k-ary incidence queries as error-correcting.
- Derivation of spammer error under kIC and a necessary rate condition.

Weaknesses:
- Heavy reliance on MSC/SHC; no treatment of general confusion matrices or item difficulty (e.g., GLAD-style models).
- Only converse bounds for kIC with oracle; no achievable constructions or tightness results.
- Non-adaptive setting; no analysis of adaptive querying or worker selection, despite their prevalence in practice.
- Limited experiments; no real-world validation or comparison to DS/EM/majority vote under matched budgets.
- Some notational ambiguities and budget accounting for kIC left to appendices.

Questions and suggestions:
- How sensitive are the bounds to mild asymmetries in worker confusions? Could Theorems 2–3 extend to per-worker confusion matrices (as in DS)?
- Can you provide any achievability result (even for simplified cases) showing a concrete scheme approaching Theorems 2–3?
- For kIC, can you analyze non-oracle decoders that estimate worker types and quantify the performance gap to Theorem 4?
- Discuss adaptivity: does allowing feedback change the bounds (strictly) in SL-UK/SL-CS? Any separation violations for finite blocklength?
- Clarify rate accounting for kIC in the main text, and report simulations comparing kIC vs. majority vote/DS at equal total time/cost.
- Explore N>2 and unknown N for kIC, since clustering is a key motivation.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Provides a clean, novel theoretical framework and interpretable bounds for a widely studied problem.
- Offers fresh insight into query design via kIC and quantifies the value of skill estimation.
- Could serve as a benchmark for future algorithmic work.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Results are primarily converse bounds under restrictive models; limited constructive or empirical contributions.
- Practical relevance is undermined by strong assumptions and lack of validation on real crowdsourcing data.
- Some clarity and notation issues and limited comparison to state-of-the-art theoretical bounds in crowdsourcing.

Overall assessment:
Borderline. The conceptual contribution of casting crowdsourcing limits in a rate–distortion framework is interesting and relevant to NIPS. However, the lack of achievability/constructive results and empirical validation reduces the impact. Strengthening the paper with concrete schemes approaching the bounds and experiments under realistic annotator models would significantly improve its case.
