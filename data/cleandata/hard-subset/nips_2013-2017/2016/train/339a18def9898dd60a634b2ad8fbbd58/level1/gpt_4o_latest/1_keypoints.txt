- Paper proposes an information-theoretic framework for analyzing and optimizing crowdsourcing fidelity–budget tradeoffs.  
- Models workers as noisy communication channels and frames crowdsourcing as human-in-the-loop computation.  
- Uses rate-distortion theory and joint source-channel coding to derive fundamental performance bounds.  
- Analyzes two scenarios: unknown worker skills (SL-UK) and known worker skills (SL-CS), deriving minimum query rates.  
- Introduces k-ary incidence coding (kIC) as a novel query scheme and analyzes its error-correction and efficiency properties.  
- Explores query pricing strategies within the proposed information-theoretic model.  
- Builds on prior crowdsourcing work (majority voting, Dawid–Skene, probabilistic skill estimation) and provides a rigorous theoretical foundation.  
- Strength: strong theoretical rigor leveraging established information-theoretic concepts.  
- Strength: novel and potentially practical query design in kIC.  
- Strength: comprehensive treatment including bounds, pricing, and worker variability.  
- Strength: clear derivation of minimum query rates for SL-UK and SL-CS scenarios.  
- Weakness: lacks empirical experiments or simulations to validate practical applicability.  
- Weakness: reliance on specific worker models (MSC, SHC) may limit real-world generalizability.  
- Weakness: scalability and computational feasibility of kIC for large-scale/high-dimensional data are not thoroughly addressed.  
- Weakness: mathematical derivations are dense and may be hard for readers unfamiliar with information theory.  
- Argument for acceptance: addresses a fundamental problem with novel, rigorous contributions and actionable insights.  
- Argument against acceptance: insufficient empirical validation and reliance on model assumptions without real-world datasets.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to improve clarity and address practical implications.
