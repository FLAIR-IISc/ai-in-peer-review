

# Review of "Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing"

This paper proposes an information-theoretic framework for analyzing crowdsourcing systems, modeling the problem as a joint source-channel coding problem over parallel noisy channels. The authors establish fundamental limits on the trade-off between budget (number of queries per item) and fidelity (accuracy of the final label) in crowdsourcing, considering two scenarios: when worker skill levels are unknown (SL-UK) and when they are known (SL-CS). They introduce a novel query scheme called k-ary incidence coding (kIC) and derive theoretical bounds on its performance.

The work builds on the established crowdsourcing literature that has primarily focused on algorithmic approaches like majority voting and the Dawid-Skene algorithm [5, 8, 12]. While previous work has examined specific inference algorithms, this paper takes a fundamental approach by characterizing the ultimate performance limits achievable by any query scheme and inference algorithm. The paper's information-theoretic perspective is a significant departure from the algorithmic focus of most prior work, and the authors properly contextualize their contributions relative to previous NIPS papers on crowdsourcing (e.g., [1, 8, 11, 13]).

**Quality:** The paper presents technically sound theoretical analysis with well-established information-theoretic foundations. The authors successfully derive fundamental bounds for the SL-UK and SL-CS scenarios (Theorems 2 and 3), and provide a rigorous analysis of the k-ary incidence coding scheme (Theorem 4). The proofs are provided in the appendix, demonstrating thoroughness. The paper acknowledges limitations, such as the assumption of large datasets and the need for future work on adaptive coding. However, the experimental validation is limited to theoretical bounds and numerical comparisons without real-world crowdsourcing data, which would strengthen the practical relevance of the results. The comparison between kIC and simpler approaches (e.g., k=1) is useful but could be expanded.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The communication system model (Figure 1a) effectively frames the crowdsourcing problem. The explanation of k-ary incidence coding (Figures 1b, 1c) is particularly helpful for understanding the novel query scheme. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined throughout. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how to implement the theoretical results in practice, particularly regarding the pricing strategy mentioned in Appendix E. The writing is accessible to NIPS readers with a background in information theory or machine learning.

**Originality:** The information-theoretic framework for crowdsourcing is a significant original contribution to the field. While previous work has focused on specific algorithms, this paper establishes fundamental limits that apply to any query scheme and inference algorithm. The k-ary incidence coding scheme represents a novel combination of familiar techniques (incidence coding and error correction) applied to crowdsourcing. The paper clearly distinguishes its approach from previous work, noting that most prior research has focused on algorithmic improvements rather than fundamental limits. The authors appropriately cite related work from NIPS and other venues, showing awareness of the field's evolution.

**Significance:** The results have substantial significance for both researchers and practitioners in crowdsourcing. Understanding the fundamental trade-off between budget and fidelity is crucial for designing efficient crowdsourcing systems, especially given the high costs of human labeling. The derived bounds provide a theoretical benchmark against which new algorithms can be evaluated. The kIC scheme could inspire practical improvements in crowdsourcing platforms, and the pricing rule of thumb (π(k1)/π(k2) ≈ k1/k2) has direct implications for platform design. The framework could also be extended to other human-in-the-loop systems beyond traditional crowdsourcing.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- Establishes fundamental information-theoretic limits for crowdsourcing, which is a major contribution to the field
- Introduces a novel query scheme (k-ary incidence coding) with theoretical analysis
- Provides a rigorous framework that generalizes previous algorithmic approaches
- Clear writing and well-structured exposition

*Con:*
- Limited experimental validation with real data
- Some practical implementation details could be expanded
- The scope of the framework (limited to discrete labeling problems) may not cover all crowdsourcing applications

This paper makes a strong contribution to the theoretical understanding of crowdsourcing systems. While it lacks extensive empirical validation, its theoretical rigor and novel perspective justify its significance for NIPS. The information-theoretic approach provides a valuable new lens for analyzing crowdsourcing, and the derived bounds serve as an important benchmark for future work. The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it advances the fundamental understanding of a problem with broad practical relevance.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the limited empirical validation (e.g., adding a brief discussion of how these bounds could be verified in practice) and to expand on the practical implications of the pricing rule. The theoretical contribution is substantial and represents a meaningful advance in the field.
