This paper models label crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding problem and studies the fundamental budget–fidelity trade-off via rate–distortion theory. Workers are modeled as parallel noisy channels, with two populations: an M-ary symmetric channel (MSC) mixture and a spammer–hammer channel (SHC). The authors derive converse-style lower bounds on the minimum number of queries per item to attain a target error under two regimes: (i) unknown worker skills (SL-UK) and (ii) skills known to the crowdsourcer (SL-CS). They also introduce k-ary incidence coding (kIC), which generalizes pairwise/binary incidence queries to k items per query, show its inherent error-correction via invalid response pruning, and provide an oracle lower bound under SHC along with a pricing rule-of-thumb. The work connects to classical aggregation (majority vote, Dawid–Skene) and to NeurIPS literature on probabilistic inference and spectral/EM methods for heterogeneous workers (e.g., Whitehill et al.; Zhang et al.; Karger–Oh–Shah), and to clustering from pairwise data (Vinayak et al.). The main novelty is a clean information-theoretic formulation yielding general, algorithm-agnostic limits.

Quality: The theoretical development is mostly sound and well-motivated, with clear statements of the separation-based bounds for MSC mixtures and the oracle lower bounds for kIC under SHC. However, several assumptions (iid items, uniform labels in parts, memoryless errors, non-adaptive tasking) narrow applicability, and there is limited validation: no synthetic or real experiments beyond numerical illustrations of bounds. The pricing result is heuristic.

Clarity: The paper is generally well-organized, but notation is dense and some editorial issues remain (incomplete citations/years, typos). A clearer discussion of how M, N, and k interact and a consolidated notation table would help. Proof sketches or intuition for Theorems 2–3 would aid readability.

Originality: Casting crowdsourcing limits via rate–distortion and explicitly contrasting SL-UK vs SL-CS is novel and insightful. kIC formalization builds on known pairwise/ternary incidence ideas but the error-correction and lower bounds are a useful addition.

Significance: The bounds can guide system design (e.g., when skill estimation materially reduces budget; how k affects budgets). That said, restriction to non-adaptive schemes and SHC/MSC limits the immediate practical impact; demonstrating tightness with concrete algorithms would strengthen significance.

Pros for acceptance:
- Conceptually clean information-theoretic framework yielding interpretable bounds.
- Clear comparison of SL-UK vs SL-CS regimes.
- Practical query design (kIC) with error-correction intuition and analysis.
- Potential to inform pricing and task design.

Cons for acceptance:
- Lack of empirical validation or constructive algorithms approaching the bounds.
- Strong simplifying assumptions (iid labels, memoryless MSC/SHC, non-adaptive).
- kIC analysis limited to N=2 and oracle decoders.
- Presentation and references need polishing; related NeurIPS work on adaptive assignment and minimax rates could be discussed more fully.
