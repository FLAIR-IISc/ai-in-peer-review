This paper introduces a novel information-theoretic framework to analyze the fundamental limits of the budget-fidelity trade-off in label crowdsourcing. The authors model the crowdsourcing system as a communication problem: the true labels are the source, the query design is the encoder, workers are parallel noisy channels, and the label aggregation algorithm is the decoder. This approach contrasts with much of the existing literature (e.g., Dawid & Skene, 1979; Karger et al., 2011), which typically focuses on designing and analyzing specific aggregation algorithms rather than establishing ultimate performance bounds. The core contributions are the derivation of rate-distortion style lower bounds on the number of queries required to achieve a target error rate, considering cases where worker skill levels are known or unknown. Furthermore, the paper introduces and analyzes a specific query scheme, k-ary Incidence Coding (kIC), providing performance bounds for it under a spammer-hammer worker model.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The application of joint source-channel coding theorems to the crowdsourcing problem is appropriate and the derivations in the main text appear rigorous. The work is a complete contribution, presenting both general theoretical bounds and an analysis of a concrete coding scheme. The authors are careful to state their assumptions, such as the iid nature of the data and the specific worker channel models (MSC and SHC).

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The analogy to a communication system is explained clearly in Section 2 and effectively frames the problem for readers familiar with information theory. The progression from general theorems to the specific analysis of kIC is logical. The results are presented clearly, especially in Figure 2, which effectively compares the theoretical limits with the kIC performance bounds.

**Originality:** The primary strength of this paper is its originality. Framing crowdsourcing through the lens of rate-distortion theory is a novel and insightful approach. While components like the spammer-hammer model have appeared before, their integration into this comprehensive theoretical framework is new. This work clearly distinguishes itself from prior art by shifting the focus from algorithmic performance to fundamental limits.

**Significance:** The results are highly significant for the research community. The derived bounds provide a crucial benchmark against which any practical crowdsourcing algorithm can be evaluated, helping researchers understand how close their methods are to the theoretical optimum. The ideas presented are likely to inspire further theoretical work on crowdsourcing system design and analysis.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Pro:** Introduces a novel, powerful, and highly original theoretical framework for a fundamental problem in machine learning.
*   **Pro:** The derived performance bounds are a significant contribution, providing a "gold standard" for the field.
*   **Pro:** The paper is technically solid, well-written, and clearly contextualized within existing literature.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   **Con:** The analysis relies on asymptotic assumptions (e.g., infinite data) and simplified worker models, and a brief discussion on the implications for finite, practical scenarios would strengthen the work.
