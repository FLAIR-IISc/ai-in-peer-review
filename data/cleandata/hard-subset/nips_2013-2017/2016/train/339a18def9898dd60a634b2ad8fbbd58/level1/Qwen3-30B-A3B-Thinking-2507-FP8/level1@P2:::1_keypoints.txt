- Proposes an information-theoretic framework modeling crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding problem over parallel noisy channels.  
- Establishes fundamental budget–fidelity trade-off limits for crowdsourcing under unknown (SL-UK) and known (SL-CS) worker skill scenarios.  
- Introduces a novel query scheme, k-ary incidence coding (kIC), and analyzes its performance theoretically.  
- Derives rigorous bounds (Theorems 2–4) with proofs provided in the appendix.  
- Builds on and contrasts with prior algorithmic crowdsourcing work by offering fundamental performance limits applicable to any scheme/algorithm.  
- Analysis assumes large datasets and non-adaptive coding, which the authors acknowledge as limitations.  
- Experimental validation is limited to theoretical/numerical comparisons and lacks real-world crowdsourcing data.  
- Comparison between kIC and simpler approaches (e.g., k=1) is included but could be expanded.  
- Paper is well-organized and clearly written, with helpful figures and consistent mathematical notation.  
- kIC explanation (figures) aids understanding, but practical implementation details—especially pricing strategy—need more discussion.  
- The information-theoretic perspective and kIC represent a significant and original contribution to the field.  
- Results are significant: provide benchmarks for algorithm evaluation and potential practical implications (e.g., pricing rule π(k1)/π(k2) ≈ k1/k2).  
- Con: limited empirical validation, some practical implementation gaps, and framework scope restricted to discrete labeling problems.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to address empirical validation and expand on practical/pricing implications.
