## Review of "Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing"

This paper investigates the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing through an information-theoretic lens, modeling it as a joint source-channel coding problem. The authors aim to identify the ultimate fidelity achievable given a budget (number of queries) and analyze the performance of a specific query scheme, k-ary incidence coding (kIC). This work builds upon existing research in crowdsourcing, particularly focusing on aggregation schemes like majority voting and the Dawid-Skene algorithm, and connects it to foundational concepts in information theory like rate-distortion theory and channel capacity. The approach of framing crowdsourcing as a communication problem with noisy channels representing workers is a novel and potentially powerful perspective.

The paper’s strengths lie in its rigorous mathematical formulation and attempt to establish fundamental limits. The derivation of the bounds in Theorems 2 and 3, while complex, provides valuable theoretical insights into the trade-offs inherent in crowdsourcing. The analysis of kIC, including the performance bound in Theorem 4 and the discussion of query pricing, is a concrete contribution. The inclusion of numerical results, even if limited, helps to illustrate the theoretical findings. The paper is well-structured, with a clear progression from problem definition to modeling, analysis, and results.

However, the paper also has weaknesses. The assumptions, particularly the large dataset size (L → ∞) and the focus on binary labeling in the kIC analysis, limit the immediate applicability of the results. While the theoretical framework is strong, the connection to practical crowdsourcing systems could be more explicit. The discussion of query pricing, while interesting, feels somewhat tacked on and lacks a detailed comparison to existing pricing strategies. The notation is dense and could benefit from more intuitive explanations. Clarity is occasionally hampered by the mathematical complexity, making it challenging to grasp the key takeaways without careful study. While related work is cited, a more thorough discussion of recent advances in active learning and query selection strategies within crowdsourcing would strengthen the paper.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel application of information theory to crowdsourcing.
*   Rigorous mathematical analysis and derivation of fundamental limits.
*   Concrete contribution with the analysis of k-ary incidence coding.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Strong assumptions limit practical applicability.
*   Limited connection to real-world crowdsourcing systems.
*   Dense notation and mathematical complexity hinder clarity.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable theoretical contribution to the understanding of crowdsourcing. While the assumptions and complexity may limit its immediate impact, the rigorous analysis and fundamental insights offered are significant. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to improve clarity and strengthen the connection to practical considerations. Specifically, the authors should consider expanding the discussion of limitations and potential future work to address the assumptions made and explore the applicability of the results to more complex scenarios.
