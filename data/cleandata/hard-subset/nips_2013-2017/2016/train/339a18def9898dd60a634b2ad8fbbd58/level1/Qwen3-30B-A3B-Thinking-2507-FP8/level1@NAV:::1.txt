

This paper models crowdsourcing as an information-theoretic rate-distortion problem, establishing fundamental performance limits for the budget-fidelity trade-off in label crowdsourcing. The authors propose a communication system framework where the dataset is the source, workers are modeled as parallel noisy channels (MSC and SHC models), query schemes serve as encoding, and inference algorithms function as decoding. The paper extends previous work on crowdsourcing (e.g., Dawid-Skene, majority voting, and recent probabilistic models) by providing information-theoretic limits rather than heuristic algorithms. It introduces two key theorems establishing minimum query rates for desired fidelity under unknown (SL-UK) and known (SL-CS) worker skill levels, and proposes k-ary incidence coding (kIC) as a novel query scheme with error-correcting properties.

The paper's strengths include strong theoretical foundations, clear articulation of the problem within information theory, and thoughtful comparison to prior work (citing NIPS proceedings appropriately). The kIC scheme represents a novel approach to query design that could improve crowdsourcing efficiency. The framework is well-structured, with clear definitions and logical progression from problem formulation to theoretical results.

However, the paper has notable weaknesses. The quality is limited by the lack of empirical validation - the theoretical bounds are not tested against real crowdsourcing data or compared to practical algorithms. The significance is somewhat diminished as the paper doesn't demonstrate how these fundamental limits would change practical crowdsourcing system design beyond theoretical interest. While the clarity is generally good, some technical details (e.g., the SHC model) could be better motivated for non-information-theoretic readers.

Pro: Strong theoretical contribution establishing fundamental limits; novel kIC query scheme; clear relation to previous work.

Con: No empirical validation; limited discussion of practical implementation challenges; the significance of the bounds for real-world applications isn't sufficiently demonstrated.

Overall, this is a technically sound theoretical contribution that establishes important information-theoretic limits in crowdsourcing. While the paper makes a significant theoretical advance, the lack of empirical validation and limited discussion of practical implications slightly reduces its impact for an NIPS audience. The work is highly original and well-executed theoretically, but would benefit from additional practical considerations to strengthen its significance. The paper is within scope for NIPS as it addresses a fundamental computational problem with information-theoretic methods.
