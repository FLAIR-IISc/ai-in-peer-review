- Paper models crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding problem and derives information-theoretic lower bounds on queries-per-item for target error under unknown (SL-UK) and known (SL-CS) worker skills.  
- Workers are modeled as M-ary symmetric channels (MSC) and a special spammer–hammer channel (SHC); bounds express minimum rate in terms of source entropy and channel capacities.  
- Introduces k-ary incidence coding (kIC), leveraging invalid configurations as built-in error correction, and derives a necessary rate condition for SHC with an oracle decoder (Theorem 4).  
- Positions the work relative to majority voting, Dawid–Skene/EM, spectral/convex inference, and bandit-based allocation, offering a separation-based, algorithm-independent perspective.  
- Technical soundness: rate–distortion framing and separation use are appropriate; SL-UK vs SL-CS bounds are plausible and interpret average-worker vs average-capacity distinctions.  
- kIC analysis calculations (e.g., spammer error in Lemma 1) are reasonable and align with intuition that larger k improves robustness.  
- Major assumptions: memoryless MSC/SHC models, worker/item independence, non-adaptive querying, infinite blocklength; ignores item difficulty and non-symmetric confusion matrices.  
- kIC results are only converse bounds under an oracle that perfectly identifies spammers/hammers; no constructive decoders or achievability results are provided.  
- Empirical validation is absent: no real AMT/dataset experiments and limited numerical illustrations beyond converse plots.  
- Completeness: develops lower bounds and insights but lacks tightness proofs, robustness to model misspecification, and demonstrations of practical gains.  
- Clarity: paper is generally well-organized and explains kIC intuition clearly, but has notational ambiguities (N, M, U, V) and important budget accounting relegated to appendices.  
- Proofs deferred to appendices; a brief in-text proof sketch would improve readability.  
- Originality: novel application of rate–distortion to crowdsourcing and a useful framing of SL-UK vs SL-CS; kIC reframes known incidence queries as error-correcting codes.  
- Significance: provides benchmarks and quantifies value of skill estimation, but practical impact is limited by restrictive models and lack of constructive/empirical results.  
- Strengths: clear information-theoretic formulation, interpretable SL-UK/SL-CS bounds, insightful kIC perspective, and derivation of spammer error and necessary rate conditions.  
- Weaknesses: heavy reliance on MSC/SHC, no achievability or tightness for kIC, non-adaptive setting only, limited experiments, and some notation/budget-accounting issues.  
- Key suggested extensions: analyze asymmetric confusion matrices, provide any achievability schemes, study non-oracle decoders for kIC, consider adaptivity/finite-blocklength effects, clarify kIC rate accounting, and run simulations vs DS/EM/majority vote.  
- Arguments for acceptance: clean, novel theoretical framework with interpretable bounds that can serve as benchmarks for future work.  
- Arguments against acceptance: mainly converse results under restrictive assumptions with little constructive or empirical support, reducing practical relevance.  
- Overall assessment: Borderline — conceptually interesting and relevant but needs achievability results, robustness analysis, and empirical validation to increase impact.
