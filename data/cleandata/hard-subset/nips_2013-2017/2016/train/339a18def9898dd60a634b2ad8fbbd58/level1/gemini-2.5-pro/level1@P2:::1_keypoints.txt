- Introduces an information-theoretic framework mapping crowdsourcing to a communication system (source, channels, encoder, decoder) to study budget–fidelity trade-offs.
- Derives fundamental lower bounds on queries required for given error using joint source-channel coding theory for M-ary Symmetric Channel (MSC) worker models.
- Distinguishes two scenarios: unknown worker skills (SL-UK) limited by average worker capacity, and known skills (SL-CS) limited by average of worker capacities, quantifying the value of skill estimation.
- Proposes k-ary Incidence Coding (kIC) where workers group k items, analyzes it under a spammer-hammer channel (SHC) model, and derives a performance lower bound.
- Uses kIC analysis to suggest a simple query pricing model based on query complexity.
- Emphasizes novelty by asking what performance any algorithm can achieve rather than designing specific inference methods.
- Paper is technically sound; application of source-channel separation theorem and rate-distortion derivations appear correct.
- Theoretical results show insightful difference between E[C(ε)] and C(E[ε]), highlighting benefits of skill knowledge.
- Analysis is asymptotic and relies on strong assumptions (infinite block lengths, i.i.d. data, specific channel models), limiting direct practical applicability.
- Lacks empirical validation—no simulations or experiments to show bounds’ tightness or comparison to practical algorithms (e.g., EM, majority voting).
- kIC analysis assumes an Oracle decoder, making its lower bound potentially loose.
- Writing and organization are clear; analogy and figures effectively communicate the framework.
- Minor clarity issues: entropy notation HN(ε) should be defined explicitly and Figure 2’s interpretation could be elaborated (gap between general limit and kIC bound).
- Originality is high: novel application of rate-distortion theory to crowdsourcing and introduction of kIC as a practical coding scheme.
- Significance is high: establishes a benchmark ("gold standard") for crowdsourcing performance and opens new research directions (finite-blocklength analysis, practical code design).
- Pros summarized: high originality, significant theoretical contribution, deep insight on skill estimation, constructive kIC proposal.
- Cons summarized: absence of experiments and reliance on strong/asymptotic modeling assumptions.
- Overall recommendation: strongly recommend acceptance despite lack of experiments due to outstanding novelty and significance.
