Summary
The paper models crowdsourcing for labeling and clustering as a joint source–channel coding problem and derives information-theoretic budget–fidelity limits. Items have latent labels B(X) in N classes; workers are modeled as noisy channels, primarily M-ary symmetric channels (MSC) and spammer–hammer channels (SHC). For Hamming distortion and non-adaptive querying, the authors derive lower bounds on the minimum number of queries per item Rmin to achieve a target error rate ε̂:

- SL-UK (worker skill levels unknown to decoder): the effective channel is the mixture over skills; the per-query “capacity” term is log M − HM(E[ε]).
- SL-CS (skill levels known to decoder): the capacity term averages the per-skill capacities log M − E[HM(ε)].

In both cases, the numerator is the rate–distortion function for an N-ary discrete source under Hamming distortion, H(B) − HN(ε̂), yielding Rmin ≈ (H(B) − HN(ε̂)) / (effective capacity). The paper also introduces k-ary incidence coding (kIC), where a query presents k items and asks for partitioning them into same/different labels; invalid response patterns are removed, providing error-correction. For SHC(q), the paper derives a lower bound on achievable error for kIC via an oracle decoder that distinguishes spammers from hammers. A practical implication is a pricing heuristic: price per query should scale roughly proportionally to k/log benefit.

Relation to prior work
The work connects to NIPS literature on crowdsourcing aggregation and budget–accuracy tradeoffs (e.g., Karger-Oh-Shah; Whitehill et al.; Zhang et al. “Spectral methods meet EM”; convex clustering with pairwise queries). Most prior work focuses on algorithmic estimators (majority vote, EM/DS, spectral/graphical methods) and task assignment; this paper instead focuses on fundamental limits via rate–distortion and channel capacity and introduces a structured query code (kIC), complementing algorithmic studies. The SHC analysis extends classical spammer–hammer models (e.g., Karger et al.) to k-ary comparative queries.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The use of the separation theorem is appropriate under the stated assumptions (iid sources, DMC channels, large blocklength, non-adaptive coding). The SL-UK vs SL-CS capacity terms align with known results: mixture channel capacity with decoder side information equals average capacity, while without side information it reduces to the capacity of the averaged channel. The N-ary Hamming rate–distortion expression is standard.
- Rigor: The main theorems are clearly stated, and proofs are said to be in appendices. The assumptions (iid labels, non-adaptive, large populations in each skill bin, memoryless workers) are explicit.
- kIC analysis: The combinatorial derivation of the spammer error for kIC (Lemma 1) is reasonable and highlights the error-correction from invalid partitions. Theorem 4 gives a necessary condition on rate for SHC with oracle decoding; it is a useful bound but depends on strong oracle assumptions and uniform binary labels.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized, with a clean mapping from crowdsourcing to source/channel coding. Explanations of kIC with examples (N=2, k=3) are helpful.
- Some notation is heavy and could be streamlined (N for classes vs M for response options vs k for arity). Explicitly define R when each kIC query touches k items; e.g., R queries per item means each item appears in R’ queries with R = R’/k. Clarify this early and consistently.
- Some references lack years and precise venues; related work coverage on alternative worker/item models could be expanded.

Originality
- Applying rate–distortion and separation to derive budget–fidelity limits for crowdsourcing with multi-class labels and contrasting SL-UK vs SL-CS is a novel and useful framing. The kIC scheme as an error-correcting comparative query code, and its analysis under SHC(q), adds originality beyond pairwise comparisons and majority vote settings.
- The pricing heuristic derived from the bound is a nice byproduct, though heuristic.

Significance
- The bounds provide principled targets and can inform system design (how many queries are needed, value of estimating skills, benefits of comparative queries). The result that decoder-side knowledge of skills changes the effective capacity from HM(E[ε]) to E[HM(ε]) is an important, actionable insight that justifies investing in skill estimation.
- Practical impact would be strengthened by empirical validation (synthetic and real AMT datasets), comparisons with standard decoders (majority, DS, spectral), and exploration of non-uniform priors and multi-class kIC.

Pros
- Clear, principled information-theoretic framework yielding interpretable bounds.
- Distinct analysis for SL-UK vs SL-CS highlighting value of skill estimation.
- Introduction and analysis of kIC, showing structural error-correction from invalid partitions.
- Bridges theory and practice via a pricing heuristic and discussions of query costs.

Cons
- Strong assumptions: iid labels, memoryless and independent workers, non-adaptive querying, large-blocklength limits. No item difficulty or dependence (e.g., IRT) or adversaries beyond SHC spammers.
- kIC analysis limited to N=2, uniform labels, and oracle skill detection; no results for N>2 or non-uniform priors where kIC’s invalid-pattern advantage is more nuanced.
- No empirical experiments; only a figure comparing bounds. Lacks comparisons with practical inference algorithms approaching the bounds.
- Ambiguity in rate normalization for kIC and the practical cost model; pricing rule is approximate.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify and formalize the definition of “queries per item” for kIC, including how R relates to the number of k-ary prompts each item appears in and how budget scales with k in real platforms. Move Appendix E cost normalization into the main text.
- Provide simulations comparing kIC vs simple labeling (k=1) under MSC and SHC for various q, ε distributions, and priors (balanced vs imbalanced), with majority vote, DS, and spectral EM decoders. Show gaps to the information-theoretic limits.
- Extend kIC analysis beyond N=2; characterize the count of valid partitions for general N and its impact on effective error rates and capacity.
- Discuss adaptive task assignment and its potential to close the gap to the SL-CS bound when skills are unknown initially; relate to bandit survey/task routing literature.
- Consider richer worker/item models (heterogeneous skills, item difficulty/IRT, adversarial workers) and discuss how the bounds might change (e.g., channels with input-dependent state).
- Tighten and complete citations (years/venues) and relate to works on minimax rates in crowdsourcing.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper offers a clean and insightful information-theoretic treatment of crowdsourcing budget–fidelity trade-offs and introduces a promising query scheme (kIC). While assumptions are strong and empirical validation is lacking, the conceptual contributions and bounds are likely to be of interest to the NIPS community and can stimulate follow-up work bridging theory and practice. Strengthening with experiments and clarifications would solidify the contribution.
