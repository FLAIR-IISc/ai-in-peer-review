- Proposes modeling crowdsourcing systems with an information-theoretic rate-distortion framework to characterize ultimate achievable fidelity under a budget.
- Models crowd workers as parallel noisy channels and formulates query design and decoding as joint source-channel coding.
- Main contributions: a joint source-channel coding scheme and information-theoretic bounds on minimum queries per item for a target fidelity.
- Introduces and analyzes a specific query scheme, k-ary incidence coding, evaluated under a spammer-hammer worker pool model.
- Derivations of the information-theoretic bounds are rigorous and well-supported by references.
- Technical analysis of k-ary incidence coding is thorough and yields useful performance insights.
- Novelty lies in applying information-theoretic techniques to crowdsourcing to study fundamental performance limits.
- Results provide practical insights for designing optimized crowdsourcing systems and trade-offs between budget and fidelity.
- Paper is well-organized with generally clear explanations, though contains minor writing errors and awkward phrasings.
- Assumes a relatively simple worker model, which may not capture complexities of real-world crowdsourcing.
- Results are demonstrated for a specific query scheme and may not generalize to all query designs or systems.
- Recommendation: accept pending minor revisions to fix writing and add discussion on model limitations and generalization potential.
