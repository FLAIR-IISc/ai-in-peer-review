- Tackles budget-fidelity trade-off in crowdsourcing using an information-theoretic joint source–channel coding framework, deriving bounds and analyzing k-ary incidence coding with pricing.  
- Novel application of rate-distortion theory to establish fundamental limits beyond algorithmic aggregation approaches.  
- Provides theoretical bounds for known vs. unknown worker skill scenarios, offering benchmarks for achievable fidelity per query budget.  
- Introduces k-ary incidence coding and analyzes its performance, including under a spammer-hammer channel model.  
- Connects theoretical results to practical concerns via optimized query pricing analysis (Appendix E).  
- Presentation is clear and well-organized for a theoretical paper; figures help illustrate k-ary coding.  
- Relies on strong simplifying assumptions (i.i.d. data, memoryless/independent worker channels, perfect skill estimation) that limit practical applicability.  
- Practical implementation concerns: increasing k may make queries harder for workers and decoding complexity is not addressed.  
- Lacks empirical validation or simulations on real-world datasets to demonstrate practical relevance.  
- Analysis is largely restricted to binary labeling (N=2); multi-class generalization is not explicitly shown.  
- Theorem 4 uses an “oracle” decoder with perfect skill estimates, reducing applicability to realistic systems.  
- Arguments for acceptance: rigorous, novel theoretical framework, significant bounds, promising k-ary scheme, good literature connection.  
- Arguments against acceptance: heavy assumptions, no empirical validation, and unaddressed practicality of k-ary coding.  
- Overall recommendation: strong accept for novelty and rigor, with suggested future work to relax assumptions, evaluate practicality, and add empirical validation.
