- Models crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding problem and studies budget–fidelity trade-off via rate–distortion theory.
- Workers modeled as parallel noisy channels with two populations: MSC (M-ary symmetric channel) mixture and SHC (spammer–hammer channel).
- Derives converse-style lower bounds on minimum queries per item for target error in two regimes: SL-UK (skills unknown) and SL-CS (skills known).
- Introduces k-ary incidence coding (kIC), generalizing pairwise incidence queries to k items per query.
- Shows kIC has inherent error-correction through invalid-response pruning and provides an oracle lower bound under SHC.
- Presents a heuristic pricing rule-of-thumb informed by the theoretical bounds.
- Connects to classical aggregation methods (majority vote, Dawid–Skene) and NeurIPS literature on heterogeneous workers and pairwise clustering.
- Main novelty: clean information-theoretic, algorithm-agnostic formulation yielding general limits and explicit SL-UK vs SL-CS comparison.
- Theoretical development is mostly sound with clear separation-based bounds and oracle lower bounds for kIC under SHC.
- Strong assumptions narrow applicability: iid items, uniform labels in parts, memoryless worker errors, and non-adaptive tasking.
- Limited empirical validation: no synthetic or real experiments beyond numerical illustrations of bounds.
- Pricing result and some analyses are heuristic rather than rigorously validated.
- Clarity issues: dense notation, editorial typos/incomplete citations, and need for clearer discussion of interactions among M, N, and k.
- Suggestion: add a consolidated notation table and provide proof sketches or intuition for Theorems 2–3 to aid readability.
- kIC analysis is limited (focus on N=2 and oracle decoders), reducing immediate practical impact.
- Significance: bounds can guide system design (when skill estimation reduces budget, effect of k) but practical impact limited without adaptive schemes or constructive algorithms.
- Pros: interpretable information-theoretic framework, clear SL-UK vs SL-CS comparison, practical query-design insights, potential to inform pricing/task design.
- Cons: lack of empirical/algorithmic validation, strong simplifying assumptions, limited kIC analysis scope, and presentation/related-work polishing needed.
