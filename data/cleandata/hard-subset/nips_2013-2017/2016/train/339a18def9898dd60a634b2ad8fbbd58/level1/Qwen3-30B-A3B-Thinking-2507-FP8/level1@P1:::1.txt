

# Peer Review: Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing

This paper models crowdsourcing as a communication problem within an information-theoretic rate-distortion framework, aiming to identify the ultimate fidelity achievable for a given budget (number of queries per item). The authors establish fundamental limits for two scenarios: when workers' skill levels are unknown (SL-UK) and when they are known (SL-CS). They also introduce and analyze a query scheme called k-ary incidence coding (kIC), which involves showing multiple items to workers and asking them to identify which belong to the same category. The work builds upon previous crowdsourcing research (e.g., Dawid-Skene algorithm, majority voting) but approaches the problem from an information-theoretic perspective rather than algorithmic design.

**Strengths:**

1. **Quality:** The paper presents a theoretically sound framework for crowdsourcing. The authors derive fundamental limits using information theory (Theorems 2 and 3), providing clear bounds on the budget-fidelity trade-off. The proofs are well-structured and appear rigorous. The kIC analysis (Theorems 4, Lemma 1) provides valuable insights into a specific query scheme, with a clear connection between the coding scheme and the worker model. The paper also provides a practical query pricing rule of thumb (π(k₁)/π(k₂) ≈ k₁/k₂), which is a useful contribution for practitioners.

2. **Originality:** This is the first paper to apply information-theoretic rate-distortion theory to crowdsourcing in this specific way. While previous work has focused on algorithmic approaches to aggregation (e.g., Dawid-Skene, majority voting), this paper provides fundamental limits that are independent of specific algorithms. The k-ary incidence coding scheme is a novel contribution that leverages error correction properties of the query design.

3. **Significance:** The results provide a theoretical foundation for understanding the fundamental trade-offs in crowdsourcing, which can guide the design of more efficient systems. The practical query pricing rule has immediate applicability to crowdsourcing platforms like Amazon Mechanical Turk. The paper also connects to broader information theory concepts (source-channel separation theorem), making it relevant to the broader NIPS community.

4. **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The introduction effectively sets up the problem and relates it to previous work. The figures (1b, 1c) help illustrate the kIC scheme. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Assumptions:** The paper makes several simplifying assumptions that may limit its practical applicability. For example, the assumption of a large number of workers with skill levels drawn from a fixed distribution may not reflect real-world crowdsourcing scenarios where workers' skills can vary widely and are not necessarily drawn from a known distribution. The paper also assumes a uniform distribution of the dataset (in Theorem 4 and Corollary 1), which may not hold in practice.

2. **Limited empirical validation:** While the paper provides theoretical bounds, there is limited empirical validation of the proposed kIC scheme on real crowdsourcing data. The numerical results (Figure 2) are based on theoretical bounds rather than actual crowdsourcing experiments, which makes it difficult to assess the practical relevance of the findings.

3. **Connection to recent NIPS work:** The paper could better distinguish itself from more recent NIPS work on crowdsourcing. For example, "Spectral methods meet EM: A provably optimal algorithm for crowdsourcing" (Zhang et al., NIPS 2014) and "Learning from crowds" (Raykar et al., JMLR 2010) have explored similar problems but from algorithmic perspectives. The paper should more clearly articulate how its information-theoretic approach differs from and complements these works.

4. **Practical considerations:** The paper discusses query pricing in Appendix E, but the practical implementation details of kIC (e.g., how to design the queries, how to handle varying worker skill levels) are not sufficiently explored. The claim that kIC provides "error correction capability" needs more detailed explanation.

**Pro and Con for Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- Provides fundamental theoretical limits for crowdsourcing that are independent of specific algorithms
- Introduces a novel query scheme (kIC) with clear theoretical analysis
- Connects crowdsourcing to broader information theory concepts
- Offers a practical query pricing rule of thumb

*Con:*
- Makes simplifying assumptions that may limit real-world applicability
- Limited empirical validation on real crowdsourcing data
- Could better distinguish itself from recent NIPS work on similar problems
- Insufficient discussion of practical implementation challenges

**Overall Recommendation:**

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of crowdsourcing systems. The information-theoretic approach provides fundamental insights that complement existing algorithmic approaches. While the paper has some limitations in terms of assumptions and empirical validation, the theoretical results are sound and significant. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses noted above, particularly by strengthening the connection to recent NIPS work and providing more discussion of practical implementation considerations.

The paper should be accepted as it makes a clear and important theoretical contribution to the field, advancing our understanding of the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing systems. The practical implications of the findings, particularly the query pricing rule, make this work valuable to both researchers and practitioners in the field.
