- The paper frames crowdsourcing as a rate-distortion communication problem and derives rigorous fundamental budget–fidelity bounds (Theorems 2 and 3).  
- Introduces k-ary incidence coding (kIC) and analyzes its performance, linking the coding scheme to the worker error model (Theorem 4, Lemma 1).  
- Provides a practical query pricing heuristic (π(k1)/π(k2) ≈ k1/k2) useful for practitioners.  
- Novel application of information-theoretic rate-distortion theory to crowdsourcing, independent of specific aggregation algorithms.  
- Connects crowdsourcing limits to broader information theory concepts (e.g., source–channel separation), increasing relevance to the community.  
- Paper is well-written, organized, and mathematically rigorous with clear figures and notation.  
- Relies on simplifying assumptions (e.g., many workers drawn from a fixed skill distribution, uniform data distribution) that may limit real-world applicability.  
- Limited empirical validation: numerical results are theoretical bounds without experiments on real crowdsourcing datasets.  
- Needs clearer distinction and connection to recent algorithmic crowdsourcing work (e.g., spectral/EM methods, learning-from-crowds literature).  
- Practical implementation aspects of kIC (query design, handling variable worker skills, and claimed error-correction capabilities) are underexplored.  
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions to strengthen empirical validation, practical implementation discussion, and connections to recent related work.
