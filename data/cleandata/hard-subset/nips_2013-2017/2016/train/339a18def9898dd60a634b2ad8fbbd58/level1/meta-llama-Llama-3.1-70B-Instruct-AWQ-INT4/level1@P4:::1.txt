This paper presents a novel approach to modeling and analyzing crowdsourcing systems using an information-theoretic rate-distortion framework. The authors aim to identify the ultimate fidelity that can be achieved by any form of query from the crowd and any decoding algorithm with a given budget. The paper provides a comprehensive overview of the crowdsourcing problem, including the modeling of crowd workers as parallel noisy channels, the design of query schemes and inference algorithms, and the analysis of the fundamental performance limits of crowdsourcing.

The paper's main contributions are the development of a joint source-channel coding scheme for crowdsourcing and the derivation of information-theoretic bounds on the minimum number of queries per item required to achieve a target fidelity. The authors also present a specific query scheme, k-ary incidence coding, and analyze its performance using a spammer-hammer worker pool model.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the problem, the proposed approach, and the results. The writing is generally good, although there are some minor errors and awkward phrasings throughout the text.

The technical soundness of the paper is strong, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the crowdsourcing problem using information-theoretic tools. The derivation of the information-theoretic bounds is rigorous and well-supported by the references provided. The analysis of the k-ary incidence coding scheme is also thorough and provides valuable insights into the performance of this specific query scheme.

The novelty of the paper lies in its application of information-theoretic tools to the crowdsourcing problem. While crowdsourcing has been studied extensively in the literature, the use of information-theoretic techniques to analyze the fundamental performance limits of crowdsourcing is a new and interesting approach. The paper's results provide valuable insights into the design of optimized crowdsourcing systems and the trade-offs between query budget and fidelity.

In terms of significance, the paper's results have important implications for the design of crowdsourcing systems. The information-theoretic bounds provide a fundamental limit on the performance of any crowdsourcing system, and the analysis of the k-ary incidence coding scheme provides a specific example of how these bounds can be achieved in practice. The paper's results are likely to be of interest to researchers and practitioners in the field of crowdsourcing and human-computer interaction.
