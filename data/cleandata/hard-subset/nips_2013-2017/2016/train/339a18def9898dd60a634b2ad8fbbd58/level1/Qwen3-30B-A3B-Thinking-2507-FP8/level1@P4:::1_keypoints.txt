- Introduces an information-theoretic framework modeling crowdsourcing as communication over noisy channels (workers as imperfect channels).
- Derives fundamental bounds on queries per item to achieve target fidelity for unknown (SL-UK) and known (SL-CS) worker skills.
- Proposes and analyzes k-ary incidence coding (kIC) with theoretical and numerical performance characterization.
- Extends prior work by providing theoretical limits rather than algorithmic inference methods like Dawid–Skene or EM.
- Technical novelty and soundness: rigorous derivations (Theorems 2,3) using source-channel separation offer a novel approach.
- Clear presentation: model, rate-distortion formulation, and kIC analysis (Lemma 1, Theorem 4) are well motivated and logically presented.
- Significant contribution: provides a benchmark for evaluating crowdsourcing systems and discusses query pricing for practical relevance.
- Rigorous comparison of scenarios: SL-UK vs SL-CS distinction and resulting insights on the value of skill knowledge.
- Limited empirical validation: lacks experiments on real crowdsourcing datasets to support theoretical bounds.
- Worker-model assumptions (MSC, SHC) may not capture real-world behavior; extension to more complex models is needed.
- Practical implementation guidance is limited; handling violated assumptions and platform-specific details (e.g., MTurk) is under-discussed.
- kIC analysis focuses on binary labeling (N=2); numerical results for non-binary cases are missing.
- Pro acceptance: establishes fundamental limits, introduces a rigorous new framework, and presents innovative kIC error-correction insights.
- Con acceptance: insufficient real-data validation, potentially unrealistic model assumptions, and superficial practical implementation discussion.
- Overall: technically strong and novel theoretical contribution that sets useful benchmarks; recommended for acceptance despite empirical/implementation gaps.
