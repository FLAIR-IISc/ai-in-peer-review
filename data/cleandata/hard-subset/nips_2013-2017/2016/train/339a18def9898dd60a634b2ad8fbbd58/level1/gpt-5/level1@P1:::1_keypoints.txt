- Paper frames crowdsourcing labeling/clustering as a joint source–channel coding problem and derives information-theoretic budget–fidelity limits.
- Items have latent N-class labels; workers modeled as noisy channels (MSC and SHC).
- For Hamming distortion and non-adaptive querying, authors derive lower bounds on queries per item Rmin to achieve target error ε̂.
- SL-UK (unknown worker skills) effective capacity uses the mixture channel: log M − H_M(E[ε]).
- SL-CS (known worker skills) effective capacity averages per-skill capacities: log M − E[H_M(ε)].
- Numerator of Rmin is the N-ary rate–distortion H(B) − H_N(ε̂), yielding Rmin ≈ (H(B) − H_N(ε̂)) / (effective capacity).
- Introduces k-ary incidence coding (kIC): queries asking to partition k items into same/different labels, removing invalid response patterns for error correction.
- For SHC(q) the paper derives a lower bound on achievable error for kIC using an oracle decoder that separates spammers and hammers.
- Practical implication: a pricing heuristic suggesting price per query scales roughly with k divided by log benefit.
- Relates to prior crowdsourcing work (Karger-Oh-Shah, Whitehill, Zhang et al.) but focuses on fundamental limits rather than algorithmic estimators.
- SHC analysis extends classical spammer–hammer models to k-ary comparative queries.
- Technical approach is sound: appropriate use of separation theorem under stated iid, DMC, large-blocklength, non-adaptive assumptions.
- SL-UK vs SL-CS capacity results align with known information-theoretic properties of mixture channels and side information.
- Rate–distortion expression for N-ary Hamming distortion is standard and correctly applied.
- kIC combinatorial derivation (Lemma 1) is reasonable and highlights error-correction from invalid partitions.
- Theorem 4 provides a useful necessary condition on rate for SHC with oracle decoding but relies on strong oracle and uniform binary label assumptions.
- Paper is generally well organized with helpful kIC examples, but notation (N, M, k) is heavy and needs streamlining.
- Ambiguity in defining queries-per-item for kIC; recommend clarifying R and mapping of item appearances to queries.
- Some references lack complete bibliographic details and related work on alternative worker/item models is under-covered.
- Original framing using rate–distortion/separation and contrasting SL-UK vs SL-CS is novel and useful.
- kIC as an error-correcting comparative query scheme provides originality beyond pairwise comparisons.
- Significance: bounds give principled targets for system design and quantify value of skill estimation (HM(E[ε]) vs E[HM(ε)]).
- Practical impact limited by lack of empirical validation, comparisons to standard decoders, and exploration of non-uniform priors/multi-class kIC.
- Cons: strong assumptions (iid labels, memoryless/independent workers, non-adaptive queries, large-blocklength) and limited worker/item models.
- Cons: kIC analysis restricted to N=2, uniform labels, and oracle skill detection; no N>2 results or non-uniform priors.
- Cons: no experiments, only a figure comparing bounds, and no evaluation against practical inference algorithms.
- Suggestions: formalize queries-per-item for kIC and move cost normalization to main text.
- Suggestions: provide simulations comparing kIC vs k=1 under MSC/SHC and practical decoders (majority, DS, spectral EM).
- Suggestions: extend kIC analysis to N>2 and non-uniform priors; discuss adaptive task assignment and richer worker/item models.
- Tighten citations and relate to minimax rates in crowdsourcing literature.
- Recommendation: Borderline accept — conceptually strong and stimulating but needs experiments and clarifications to solidify contribution.
