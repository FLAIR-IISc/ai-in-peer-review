This paper investigates the fundamental limits of budget-fidelity trade-offs in label crowdsourcing, framing it as a human-in-the-loop computation problem and applying an information-theoretic approach. The authors model the problem using a joint source-channel coding scheme, with workers represented as parallel noisy channels. They derive bounds on achievable fidelity given a budget and analyze a specific query scheme (k-ary incidence coding) with associated query pricing considerations. This builds on prior NIPS work on crowdsourcing, such as Dawid and Skene’s algorithm [5] and recent investigations into worker skill estimation [6, 8, 9, 12, 10, 13], by shifting the focus to fundamental limits rather than algorithmic design. It also draws from classical information theory concepts like rate-distortion theory [4] and joint source-channel coding [7].

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The modeling of crowdsourcing as a communication system is well-motivated, and the application of information-theoretic tools appears appropriate. The derivation of the bounds (Theorems 2 and 3) seems mathematically rigorous, though detailed verification requires expertise in information theory. The presentation of k-ary incidence coding is clear, and the analysis of its performance (Theorem 4) is a valuable contribution. However, the paper could benefit from more extensive empirical validation of the theoretical bounds. While the authors acknowledge the limitations of the bounds, demonstrating their tightness even with simulations would strengthen the results. The discussion of query pricing, while intriguing, feels somewhat cursory and relies heavily on the derived bounds without concrete examples or comparisons to existing pricing strategies. The assumption of i.i.d. sources, while standard in information theory, might be too restrictive for many real-world crowdsourcing scenarios. The authors acknowledge this implicitly but a discussion on relaxing this assumption would be beneficial. The distinction between SL-UK and SL-CS is insightful, and the derived bounds highlight the value of worker skill estimation.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction provides adequate context and motivation. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the key concepts are explained clearly. Figure 1a is helpful in illustrating the system model. However, some sections could benefit from more detailed explanations. For instance, the transition to k-ary incidence coding (Section 2.3) could be smoother, with a clearer connection to the general system model. The discussion around the invalid responses in kIC (Figure 1b and 1c) is useful but could be expanded upon.  The jump between Lemma 1 and Theorem 4 in section 4.2 feels abrupt; a more step-by-step derivation or explanation would be beneficial. Some of the equations (e.g., Theorem 2) are quite dense and might be difficult for readers less familiar with information theory to fully grasp.

**Originality:** The application of information-theoretic techniques to analyze the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing is novel. While information theory has been used to analyze communication systems for decades, its application to crowdsourcing is relatively recent. The proposed k-ary incidence coding scheme and the associated analysis represent a new contribution.  The paper’s strength lies in its theoretical approach, providing bounds that are independent of specific algorithmic solutions.  However, the authors adequately reference prior work in both crowdsourcing and information theory, demonstrating awareness of the existing literature. It’s worth noting that while the application of information theory *to* crowdsourcing is novel, the underlying theoretical tools are not. The novelty stems from the specific modeling and insightful application of these tools to the unique challenges of crowdsourcing.

**Significance:** The results are significant because they provide a theoretical benchmark for evaluating the performance of crowdsourcing systems. The derived bounds offer valuable insights into the trade-offs between budget and fidelity, which can guide the design of more efficient and reliable crowdsourcing solutions.  The analysis of k-ary incidence coding could lead to practical improvements in query design. The paper addresses a challenging problem in a systematic and rigorous manner. The results are likely to be of interest to researchers and practitioners working on crowdsourcing, machine learning, and information theory. The identification of the performance gains achieved through worker skill level estimation (SL-CS vs. SL-UK) is particularly relevant in practice. The connection to query pricing, though not fully fleshed out, has the potential to influence real-world crowdsourcing platforms.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of information theory to crowdsourcing.
*   Technically sound and mathematically rigorous.
*   Provides fundamental limits and insights into budget-fidelity trade-offs.
*   Introduces a new query scheme (k-ary incidence coding) with performance analysis.
*   Addresses a significant problem with potential practical impact.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited empirical validation of the theoretical bounds.
*   Query pricing discussion is somewhat superficial.
*   Assumption of i.i.d. sources may be restrictive.
*   Some sections could benefit from more detailed explanations.



Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of fundamental limits in crowdsourcing. While some improvements could be made (particularly in terms of empirical validation and elaboration of the pricing strategy), the theoretical rigor, novelty, and potential significance of the work justify acceptance.
