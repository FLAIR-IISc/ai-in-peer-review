- Presents a novel information-theoretic framework for modeling and analyzing crowdsourcing systems using a joint source-channel coding perspective.  
- Aims to identify fundamental performance bounds relating budget (queries per item) and fidelity (accuracy).  
- Builds on prior work including Dawid and Skene and provides a comprehensive related-literature overview.  
- Main theoretical contribution: two theorems establishing minimum queries per item for desired fidelity in unknown-worker-skill (SL-UK) and known-skill (SL-CS) scenarios.  
- Introduces a new query scheme, k-ary incidence coding (kIC), and derives a lower bound for its performance with a spammer-hammer worker model.  
- Provides numerical results demonstrating kIC’s effectiveness compared to traditional schemes.  
- Paper is well-written, clear, and concise; framework and components are easy to follow for readers with relevant background.  
- Innovative use of information-theoretic tools constitutes a significant contribution to crowdsourcing analysis.  
- Strengths: clear explanation, strong literature coverage, novel theoretical limits, and introduction of kIC with improved performance.  
- Weaknesses: assumes uniform dataset distribution which may be unrealistic in practice.  
- Weaknesses: analysis restricted to binary labeling tasks; generalization to multi-class or complex tasks is unclear.  
- Weaknesses: lacks a detailed empirical or theoretical comparison of kIC against other query schemes.  
- Evaluation scores: Quality 9/10, Clarity 9/10, Originality 9/10, Significance 9/10.  
- Arguments for acceptance: significant contribution, innovative methodology, useful benchmark limits, and a promising new query scheme.  
- Arguments against acceptance: restrictive assumptions (uniformity, binary labels) and insufficient comparison of kIC with alternatives.  
- Recommendation: accept for NIPS 2016 despite limitations because contributions are substantial and novel.
