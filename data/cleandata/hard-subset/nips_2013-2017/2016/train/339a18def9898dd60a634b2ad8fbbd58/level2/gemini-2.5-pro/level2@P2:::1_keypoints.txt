- Paper introduces an information-theoretic framework modeling crowdsourcing as communication over noisy channels to derive rate (queries per item) vs. distortion (accuracy) limits.  
- Uses joint source-channel coding to derive minimum rate for target distortion under M-ary Symmetric Channel (MSC) worker models.  
- Analyzes two scenarios: unknown worker skills (SL-UK) and known skills (SL-CS), showing E[Capacity(worker)] ≥ Capacity(E[worker]).  
- Proposes k-ary Incidence Coding (kIC) — querying workers on k-item comparisons/clusters — and derives a lower bound under a spammer-hammer (SHC) model.  
- Strength: novel, significant reframing that provides fundamental performance benchmarks beyond algorithmic tweaks.  
- Strength: insightful theoretical rate-distortion results and clear formalization of the benefit of skill estimation.  
- Strength: introduction and analysis of a concrete, non-trivial query scheme (kIC) linking theory to practice.  
- Strength: clear writing and consistent communication-system analogy.  
- Weakness: entirely theoretical with no empirical validation or comparisons to practical algorithms like Dawid-Skene or majority voting.  
- Weakness: relies on strong assumptions (L→∞, i.i.d. labels, memoryless workers) without discussion of finite/real-world implications.  
- Weakness: inconsistent worker models — general bounds use MSC while kIC analysis uses SHC — with limited justification.  
- Weakness: kIC performance bound assumes an oracle decoder that knows worker types, limiting practical applicability.  
- Overall: highly original and important theoretical contribution that justifies acceptance despite empirical and modeling gaps.
