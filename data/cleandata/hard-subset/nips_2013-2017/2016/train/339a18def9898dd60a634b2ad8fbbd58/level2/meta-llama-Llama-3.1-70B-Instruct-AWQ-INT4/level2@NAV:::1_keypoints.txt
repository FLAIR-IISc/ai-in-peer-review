- Proposes an information-theoretic crowdsourcing model: crowd as parallel noisy channels, taskmaster as encoder, crowdsourcer as decoder.  
- Claims a fundamental, code-agnostic limit on the minimum queries per item to achieve a target fidelity.  
- Introduces k-ary incidence coding (kIC): queries ask workers to identify same-label items among k items.  
- Analyzes kIC under a spammer-hammer worker pool and provides a lower bound assuming an Oracle decoder.  
- Contribution: novel information-theoretic framework for modeling and analyzing crowdsourcing.  
- Contribution: derives a fundamental budget–fidelity trade-off (minimum queries per item).  
- Contribution: proposes kIC query scheme that can improve crowdsourcing performance.  
- Support: theoretical analysis using joint source–channel coding and numerical results; proofs in appendices.  
- Usefulness: provides a benchmark for evaluating crowdsourcing algorithms and guidance for system design.  
- Practical impact: kIC may help when worker skill levels are unknown.  
- Originality: novel modeling approach and new kIC scheme not previously studied in crowdsourcing.  
- Technical soundness: well-written, coherent, and mathematically justified with organized results.  
- Clarity: paper is well-organized and easy to follow.  
- Significance: potential to influence crowdsourcing design and machine learning/data-science practices.  
- Criticisms: assumes a simplistic worker-skill model that may be unrealistic.  
- Criticisms: kIC may not suit all task types and the paper lacks experimental/real-world validation.  
- Recommendation: reviewer recommends acceptance.
