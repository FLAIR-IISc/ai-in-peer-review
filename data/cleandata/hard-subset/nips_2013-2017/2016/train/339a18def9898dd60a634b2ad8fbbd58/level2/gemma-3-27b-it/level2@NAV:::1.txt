## Review of "Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing"

This paper investigates the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing through an information-theoretic lens, modeling it as a joint source-channel coding problem. The authors aim to identify the ultimate fidelity achievable given a certain budget (number of queries) and analyze the performance of a specific query scheme – k-ary incidence coding (kIC). The work is motivated by the increasing use of crowdsourcing for tasks like data labeling and the need to understand the trade-offs between cost and accuracy.

The paper’s strength lies in its novel approach. Framing crowdsourcing within the well-established information-theoretic framework provides a rigorous foundation for analyzing performance limits.  The derivation of lower bounds on the required query rate for achieving a desired fidelity, both with and without knowledge of worker skill levels (Theorems 2 & 3), is a significant contribution. Furthermore, the analysis of kIC and the derived bounds for its performance (Theorem 4) offers valuable insights into the potential of this specific query strategy.  The connection made to query pricing based on the kIC analysis is a practical addition. The paper appropriately references relevant work in crowdsourcing and information theory, demonstrating awareness of the existing literature.

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. The presentation is quite dense and theoretical, making it challenging to grasp the practical implications of the derived results. While the theoretical framework is sound, the limited discussion of the assumptions made (e.g., i.i.d. sources, specific channel models) and their impact on the results detracts from the overall impact.  Specifically, the assumption of uniformly distributed data feels restrictive and its impact on the bounds isn’t clearly discussed. The numerical results, while present, are limited in scope and don’t thoroughly explore the parameter space. A more detailed simulation study showcasing the performance of kIC compared to simpler strategies (e.g., majority voting) would greatly strengthen the paper. Additionally, the notation is occasionally cumbersome, hindering readability.

**Arguments for Acceptance:** The paper presents a novel and rigorous theoretical analysis of crowdsourcing using established information-theoretic tools. The derived bounds provide fundamental limits on performance and offer insights into the design of efficient crowdsourcing systems.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**  The highly theoretical nature and limited discussion of practical implications, along with a lack of comprehensive numerical validation, limit the immediate impact of the work. The dense presentation and occasional notational complexity also make the paper challenging to follow.

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a valuable theoretical contribution to the field. While the current presentation requires improvement, the underlying ideas are strong and warrant consideration. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The authors should focus on improving the clarity of the presentation, providing a more thorough discussion of the assumptions made, and expanding the numerical results to demonstrate the practical relevance of their findings.
