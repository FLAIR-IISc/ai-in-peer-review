- Models crowdsourcing as an information-theoretic rate-distortion problem to quantify budget-fidelity trade-offs.  
- Frames crowdsourcer as transmitter, workers as parallel noisy channels, and inference as receiver minimizing distortion under a query budget.  
- Derives two fundamental bounds: SL-UK (unknown worker skills) and SL-CS (perfectly estimated skills) via joint source-channel coding.  
- Introduces k-ary incidence coding (kIC) and analyzes its performance under a spammer-hammer channel model.  
- Provides theoretical guarantees for kIC in Corollary 1 and Theorem 4, linking query design to error performance.  
- Connects to prior crowdsourcing work (e.g., Dawid–Skene, majority voting) but frames the problem in a novel information-theoretic context.  
- Theoretical development is rigorous with complete derivations for Theorems 2 and 3 and clear proofs for Lemma 1 and Theorem 4.  
- Numerical results (Figure 2) effectively compare theoretical bounds and illustrate trade-offs.  
- Model assumptions (independent workers, no feedback, specific channel models) are well-motivated for the theoretical setting.  
- Presentation is generally clear and well-structured; the kIC explanation and examples are particularly accessible.  
- Originality is a major strength: first NIPS work to establish information-theoretic fundamental limits for this trade-off and novel combinatorial query scheme.  
- Significance is substantial: bounds provide benchmarks for practical systems and shift focus from aggregation to fundamental fidelity limits.  
- Provides actionable insight with a query pricing rule (π(k1)/π(k2) ≈ k1k2) for system designers.  
- Key weaknesses: limited empirical validation and no experiments on real crowdsourcing data to test practical utility.  
- Analysis often assumes uniform datasets and binary classification (N=2), limiting applicability to multi-class or non-uniform scenarios.  
- SL-CS assumes perfect skill estimation, which is strong; handling imperfect estimation is not addressed.  
- Scalability concerns: reliance on asymptotic (n→∞) results without discussion of finite-block effects relevant to real systems.  
- Lacks explicit empirical comparison to state-of-the-art algorithms (e.g., spectral methods, EM) to contextualize theoretical limits.  
- Reviewer recommendations: add discussion of practical implications, examine assumption impacts, and include brief empirical (even synthetic) comparisons.  
- Overall verdict: technically sound, novel, and significant; accept with minor revisions to improve practical relevance and discussion of assumptions.
