- Paper frames crowdsourcing as a source-channel coding problem and measures budget as code rate and fidelity as distortion.
- Main contribution: uses source-channel separation to derive minimum budget bounds for target fidelity in two cases (worker skills unknown SL-UK, known SL-CS).
- Theoretical bounds (Theorems 2 and 3) provide a fundamental benchmark for crowdsourcing performance.
- The distinction between SL-UK and SL-CS quantifies the gain from estimating worker skill levels.
- Introduces k-ary Incidence Coding (kIC) as a novel query scheme that embeds error-correction via structural constraints.
- Provides a performance bound for kIC under a spammer-hammer worker model and compares it to the information-theoretic limit.
- Strength: original, significant reframing that shifts focus from algorithms to fundamental limits.
- Strength: sound application of joint source-channel coding theory and insightful formal distinctions.
- Weakness: asymptotic results (n → ∞) lack discussion of applicability to typical finite-size crowdsourcing projects.
- Weakness: no empirical comparison to practical algorithms (e.g., Dawid-Skene EM, majority voting) to show gap to limits.
- Weakness: kIC description could be clearer about how invalid/transitive-violating responses are detected or corrected.
- Weakness: simplifying assumptions (i.i.d. item difficulty, memoryless worker behavior) limit realism and need discussion of extensions.
