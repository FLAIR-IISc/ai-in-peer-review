- Proposes an information-theoretic framework modeling crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding problem to derive budget–fidelity trade-off limits.
- Derives theoretical bounds for unknown worker skill (SL-UK) and known skill (SL-CS) scenarios and introduces k-ary incidence coding (kIC).
- Strength: rigorous, novel information-theoretic foundation that provides meaningful theoretical bounds (Theorems 2 and 3).
- Strength: clear motivation for SL-UK vs SL-CS distinction and useful analysis of kIC under a spammer–hammer model.
- Weakness: insufficient connection to existing crowdsourcing literature and unclear positioning relative to prior aggregation methods (e.g., Dawid–Skene, EM).
- Weakness: no empirical validation—no experiments on real or simulated data comparing theoretical bounds to practical methods.
- Weakness: practical relevance underdeveloped; vague query-pricing rule and superficial connection to platforms like Amazon Mechanical Turk.
- Weakness: claims of “ultimate performance bounds” are unsupported by achievable schemes that match the provided lower bounds.
- Presentation: well-structured and clear, but main text lacks sufficient technical detail (proofs mainly in appendices) and key concepts need more elaboration for a broad audience.
- Significance: interesting theoretically but fails to convincingly demonstrate substantial impact on real-world crowdsourcing practice.
- Suggested improvements: add detailed comparisons with prior methods, include empirical validation, develop concrete cost/practical models, and provide or discuss achievable schemes.
