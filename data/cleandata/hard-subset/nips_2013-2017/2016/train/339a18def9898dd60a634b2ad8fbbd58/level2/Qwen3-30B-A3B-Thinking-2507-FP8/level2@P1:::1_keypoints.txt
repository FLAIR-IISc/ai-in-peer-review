- Presents an information-theoretic framework linking budget (queries/item) and fidelity (error probability) in crowdsourcing via joint source-channel coding.  
- Models workers as parallel noisy channels and derives distortion-rate functions for unknown-skill (SL-UK) and known-skill (SL-CS) scenarios.  
- Analyzes k-ary incidence coding (kIC) and provides a lower bound for kIC under spammer-hammer channels (Theorem 4).  
- Theorems 2 and 3 rigorously establish fundamental budget–fidelity bounds, generalizing prior work.  
- kIC is a novel, creative query design leveraging valid-response constraints for error correction, with theoretical insights (Theorem 4, Corollary 1).  
- Numerical results (Figure 2) validate the theoretical bounds and framework.  
- Offers practical implications (e.g., pricing rule π(k₁)/π(k₂) ≈ k₁/k₂) that can guide real-world crowdsourcing design.  
- Strength: original information-theoretic perspective moving beyond heuristic models like Dawid–Skene or majority voting.  
- Strength: high technical quality with clear distinction between SL-UK and SL-CS and rigorous derivations.  
- Strength: significant practical relevance—provides actionable bounds to avoid suboptimal schemes and improve fidelity.  
- Weakness: SL-CS scenario lacks discussion of how perfect skill knowledge might be obtained in practice (e.g., calibration).  
- Weakness: notation and concepts (e.g., Hₙ(ε) and role of M) need clearer explanation for readability.  
- Weakness: kIC analysis is limited to N=2 (binary); extension or discussion of scaling to general N is needed.  
- Weakness: pricing insight in Appendix E feels disconnected and should be integrated into the main text (e.g., after Theorem 3).  
- Overall assessment: strong, original, technically rigorous contribution that establishes fundamental limits for crowdsourcing; minor clarifications would further strengthen the paper.  
- Recommendation: Accept.
