This paper investigates the fundamental limits of budget-fidelity trade-offs in label crowdsourcing, modeling it as a human-in-the-loop computation problem and analyzing it through an information-theoretic lens. The authors establish bounds on achievable fidelity given a budget, considering scenarios with unknown and known worker skill levels. They also introduce and analyze a k-ary incidence coding scheme and discuss query pricing strategies.

The paper is a valuable contribution as it attempts to move beyond the purely algorithmic and system-design aspects of crowdsourcing (which dominate the NIPS literature) and address fundamental theoretical limits. Previous NIPS work on crowdsourcing, such as [8, 13, 11], focuses on inference and aggregation algorithms (e.g., Dawid-Skene) or specific applications like clustering, often relying on empirical evaluations. This paper's information-theoretic approach provides a complementary perspective, offering ultimate performance bounds and insights into optimal query design.  The connection to rate-distortion theory is well-motivated and appropriately applied. The introduction effectively positions the work within the broader context of crowdsourcing and human-in-the-loop computation.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The application of information theory to crowdsourcing is a novel and promising approach. Establishing fundamental limits provides a theoretical foundation for evaluating existing and designing new crowdsourcing systems.
* **Rigorous Analysis:** The paper provides a rigorous mathematical analysis of the problem, deriving information-theoretic bounds under various assumptions.
* **Clear Modeling:** The communication system model using noisy channels to represent workers is intuitive and provides a solid framework for analysis.
* **Interesting Coding Scheme:** The k-ary incidence coding scheme is a potentially useful contribution, particularly in scenarios where reducing the number of queries is crucial.  The discussion of query pricing is a practical extension.
* **Well-written (mostly):**  The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the complex concepts relatively accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions:** The model relies on several simplifying assumptions, such as independence of workers and memoryless channels, which may not hold in real-world crowdsourcing scenarios. The impact of these assumptions on the tightness of the bounds is not thoroughly discussed. For instance, the assumption of independent workers seems strong given potential social influence or shared knowledge.
* **Practicality of kIC:** While theoretically interesting, the practicality of k-ary incidence coding in high-dimensional or complex labeling tasks is not fully explored.  The computational cost of formulating and solving the inference problem for kIC could be substantial.  The discussion of query pricing felt somewhat tacked on and lacked detailed justification.
* **Limited Empirical Validation:** The paper lacks empirical validation. While deriving theoretical bounds is valuable, demonstrating their relevance to real-world crowdsourcing platforms (like AMT) would significantly strengthen the contribution. Even simulations would be helpful.
* **Notation:** While generally clear, the notation could be improved in places. For example, the distinction between different types of 'R' (rate, budget) is not always consistently maintained.
* **Discussion of Related Work:**  While the introduction cites relevant papers, it could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of how this work differs from, and builds upon, existing literature.  Specifically, connections to active learning, where query selection is also a key concern, could be explored.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a fundamental question in crowdsourcing with a novel and theoretically sound approach.
* The information-theoretic bounds provide valuable insights into the limits of achievable performance.
* The k-ary incidence coding scheme offers a potentially useful contribution to query design.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The simplifying assumptions may limit the practical applicability of the results.
* The lack of empirical validation weakens the impact of the theoretical findings.
* The discussion of practical considerations (e.g., kIC complexity, query pricing) is somewhat superficial.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a solid contribution that warrants acceptance, despite its limitations. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and the insights gained are valuable.  However, I strongly encourage the authors to address the weaknesses identified above, particularly by providing some empirical validation (even simulations) and expanding the discussion of practical considerations.  A more thorough exploration of the impact of the simplifying assumptions would also be beneficial.

**Recommendation:** Accept (with minor revisions to address the weaknesses)
