- Main contribution: Proposes an information-theoretic framework for crowdsourcing using rate-distortion theory, models human-in-the-loop computation, derives performance bounds, and introduces k-ary incidence coding (kIC) analyzed under worker models including SHC.

- Novelty: Applies a novel information-theoretic perspective to crowdsourcing, departing from traditional approaches.

- Theoretical rigor: Provides mathematically rigorous derivations and proofs for bounds under multiple worker models (MSC, SHC).

- Practical relevance: Introduces kIC with analysis of error correction and query pricing that could inform real CS system design.

- Clarity of objectives: Clearly states goals around performance limits and query scheme optimization.

- Limited experimental validation: Lacks simulations or real-world experiments to demonstrate practical utility of kIC.

- Simplified worker models: Relies on MSC and SHC which may not capture dynamic, heterogeneous real-world worker behavior.

- Dense presentation: Mathematical sections are heavy and may be hard to follow for readers without strong information-theory background.

- Limited discussion of limitations: Does not sufficiently address scalability or adaptability to dynamic worker populations.

- Argument for acceptance: Fills a literature gap by applying information theory to crowdsourcing and offers novel theoretical insights.

- Argument for acceptance: Theoretical contributions (bounds and kIC) are original and could advance the field.

- Argument for acceptance: Work is relevant to both researchers and practitioners in crowdsourcing optimization.

- Argument against acceptance: Practical feasibility is uncertain due to lack of empirical validation.

- Argument against acceptance: Worker-model assumptions may oversimplify and limit generalizability.

- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions, requesting experimental validation and a more detailed discussion of limitations and extensions.
