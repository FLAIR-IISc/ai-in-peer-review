- Paper frames crowdsourcing as a rate–distortion/source–channel coding problem and derives lower bounds on queries per item for target accuracy under two scenarios: SL-UK and SL-CS.
- Workers are modeled as heterogeneous M-ary symmetric channels (MSC) or spammer–hammer channels (SHC); main theorems give Rmin for Hamming distortion as functions of priors and reliabilities.
- Introduces k-ary incidence coding (kIC) where each query clusters k items, removing invalid responses and providing implicit error-correction.
- For SHC with binary labels, derives spammer error under kIC (Lemma 1) and a necessary rate condition (Theorem 4); provides an SL-CS benchmark corollary.
- Offers a heuristic pricing rule for kIC proportional to k and compares kIC bounds to information-theoretic limits numerically.
- Differentiates from prior algorithmic work (majority, Dawid–Skene, spectral methods) by providing algorithm-agnostic, coding-theoretic lower bounds.
- kIC relates to prior same–different pair/triad queries, but its SHC error-correcting analysis and necessary rate condition are presented as novel.
- Strength: clear, principled formulation of budget–fidelity trade-offs via information theory, yielding algorithm-independent bounds.
- Strength: separate SL-UK vs SL-CS analysis clarifies value of skill information (average-worker capacity vs. average of capacities).
- Strength: kIC is simple and practical; analysis shows increasing k improves robustness and informs pricing.
- Strength: results align with coding-theory intuitions, unifying redundancy and reliability perspectives.
- Weakness: modeling is restrictive—MSC assumes symmetric errors and SHC is an extreme model, unlike realistic DS-type confusion matrices.
- Weakness: separation-based bounds rely on large blocklengths, i.i.d. items, and non-adaptive querying, which may not hold in practice.
- Weakness: kIC analysis limited to binary labels, uniform priors, non-adaptive assignment, and oracle decoder knowledge, making bounds potentially loose.
- Weakness: notational inconsistencies, ambiguous symbols, and incomplete/imprecise references harm readability.
- Weakness: minimal empirical validation—no simulations to assess bound tightness, finite-sample effects, or comparisons to common decoders under kIC.
- Quality: technical core is sound and consistent with information theory; proofs are plausible though deferred to appendices.
- Quality caveat: practical relevance depends on deviation from MSC/SHC in real data and on finite-sample behavior, which are unassessed.
- Clarity: high-level narrative and assumptions are stated, but formatting, notation reuse, missing references, and underdescribed figures need fixes.
- Originality: novel framing of crowdsourcing via rate–distortion and new SHC/kIC analysis, though kIC is related to existing query primitives.
- Significance: provides conceptual lower bounds useful for system design and pricing, but practical impact requires extensions to realistic models and experiments.
- Essential suggestions: unify and clarify notation, state theorem assumptions clearly, contrast MSC/SHC with DS-type models, and add finite-sample simulations against standard decoders.
- Nice-to-have suggestions: extend kIC beyond binary/uniform priors, analyze simple adaptive querying, adopt a two-parameter confusion model, and empirically validate pricing heuristics.
- Arguments for acceptance: fills a theoretical gap with principled bounds, offers interpretable kIC insights, and connects well to NIPS literature.
- Arguments against acceptance: strong assumptions limit applicability, lack of empirical evidence and clarity issues reduce confidence in practical utility.
- Overall: a valuable theoretical contribution that needs clearer exposition and empirical support to strengthen practical relevance.
