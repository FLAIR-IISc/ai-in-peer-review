- Models crowdsourcing as a rate-distortion communication problem to establish fundamental budget–fidelity limits.  
- Theorem 2: derives minimum queries per item when workers’ skill levels are unknown (SL-UK).  
- Theorem 3: provides the corresponding bound when workers’ skill levels are known (SL-CS).  
- Introduces k-ary incidence coding (kIC): workers pick items with the same label among k randomly selected items.  
- Analyzes kIC performance under a spammer–hammer channel model; Theorem 4 gives analytic results.  
- Novel information-theoretic approach complements algorithmic aggregation work (e.g., Dawid–Skene, majority voting).  
- Proofs for Theorems 2–3 are well-constructed; kIC analysis yields practical insights and Figure 2 shows kIC approaches bounds as k increases.  
- Analysis depends on specific channel models (MSC and SHC) that may not capture real-world worker behavior.  
- Theoretical bounds assume large datasets/infinite block lengths, limiting direct practical applicability.  
- Query-pricing rule in Appendix E (π(k1)/π(k2) ≈ k1/k2) lacks experimental validation.  
- Significant contribution by establishing fundamental performance limits that can guide future algorithm design.  
- Writing is clear and organized; practical relevance could be improved by addressing implementation challenges.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to address the gap between theoretical bounds and practice.
