- Presents a novel information-theoretic framework modeling crowdsourcing as human-in-the-loop computation and applies rate-distortion theory to bound queries per item.  
- Paper is well-written with clear explanations and a thorough related-work review that positions the contribution well.  
- Main technical contributions: two theorems providing lower bounds on the minimum queries per item for a target fidelity.  
- Theorems cover both unknown worker skill levels and perfectly known skill levels.  
- Analyzes k-ary incidence coding and derives a lower bound on its performance.  
- Provides numerical results illustrating the information-theoretic bounds and trade-offs between query count and fidelity.  
- Weakness: uses a relatively simple model that may not capture real-world crowdsourcing complexities.  
- Weakness: analysis is focused on k-ary incidence coding, leaving unclear generalization to other query schemes.  
- Weakness: lacks a clear discussion of practical implications for crowdsourcing system design.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, significant, technically sound, well-presented, and well-situated in literature.  
- Arguments for rejection: limited model realism, narrow scope of query schemes analyzed, and insufficient design guidance.  
- Overall recommendation: accept, because technical contributions are significant despite noted limitations.
