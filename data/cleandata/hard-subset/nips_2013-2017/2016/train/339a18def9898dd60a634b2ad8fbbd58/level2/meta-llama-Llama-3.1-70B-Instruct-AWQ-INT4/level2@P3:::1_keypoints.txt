- Models crowdsourcing as a communication system to study budget (queries/item) vs. fidelity trade-offs using information theory.  
- Derives information-theoretic bounds on minimum queries/item for target fidelity in unknown-skill (SL-UK) and known-skill (SL-CS) settings.  
- Introduces and analyzes k-ary incidence coding (kIC) as a query scheme and studies its performance.  
- Novel framework applying information-theoretic concepts to crowdsourcing.  
- Provides tight bounds that can benchmark crowdsourcing system performance.  
- kIC is a valuable and original query-scheme contribution with useful budget–fidelity insights.  
- Assumes a simplistic worker model (spammers vs. hammers) that may not reflect real-world behavior.  
- Analysis is restricted to binary labeling tasks, leaving multi-class or more complex tasks unaddressed.  
- Lacks clear comparison with existing crowdsourcing algorithms or empirical systems, limiting practical assessment.  
- Significant implications for designing and optimizing crowdsourcing systems based on theoretical trade-offs.  
- Paper is generally well-written and clear, though some technical proofs may be hard for non-experts.  
- Theoretical results are technically sound and supported by analysis and simulations.  
- For acceptance: novel, significant contribution with practical design implications and clear presentation.  
- For acceptance: theoretical benchmarks and kIC add value to the field.  
- Against acceptance: oversimplified worker assumptions may limit applicability.  
- Against acceptance: restriction to binary tasks raises questions about generalizability.  
- Against acceptance: missing empirical or comparative evaluation against existing methods.  
- Recommend extending analysis to multi-class or more complex tasks.  
- Recommend providing clear comparisons with existing crowdsourcing algorithms/systems.  
- Recommend relaxing worker-behavior assumptions to increase realism.
