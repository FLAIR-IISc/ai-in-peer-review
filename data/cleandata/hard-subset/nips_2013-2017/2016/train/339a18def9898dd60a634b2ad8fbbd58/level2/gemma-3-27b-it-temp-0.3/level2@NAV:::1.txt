## Review of "Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing"

This paper investigates the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing through an information-theoretic lens, modeling it as a joint source-channel coding problem. The core idea is to establish the ultimate fidelity achievable given a budget (number of queries) and analyze the trade-off. The authors derive lower bounds on the required query rate under different scenarios – unknown vs. known worker skill levels – and propose a k-ary incidence coding scheme with analysis, including a discussion of query pricing.

The paper’s strength lies in its novel application of information theory to a practical problem like crowdsourcing. Framing the problem within a rate-distortion framework provides a rigorous theoretical foundation, moving beyond empirical studies common in this area. The derivation of the fundamental limits (Theorems 2 & 3) is a significant contribution, offering a benchmark for evaluating existing and future crowdsourcing algorithms. The introduction of k-ary incidence coding is also a valuable addition, providing a concrete coding scheme with performance analysis. The connection to query pricing, while brief, hints at practical implications.

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. While the theoretical framework is strong, the practical relevance of some assumptions is questionable. For example, assuming a uniformly distributed source (B(X)) might not hold in many real-world crowdsourcing scenarios. The analysis of k-ary incidence coding, while mathematically sound, lacks extensive numerical validation beyond the presented figures. A more comprehensive comparison with existing aggregation algorithms (like DS) in terms of rate-distortion performance would strengthen the results. The discussion on query pricing is also very brief and could be expanded.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel application of information theory to crowdsourcing.
*   Derivation of fundamental limits provides a valuable theoretical benchmark.
*   Introduction of a new coding scheme (k-ary incidence coding) with analysis.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Strong assumptions (e.g., uniform source distribution) may limit practical applicability.
*   Limited numerical validation of the proposed coding scheme.
*   Brief discussion of query pricing and lack of comparison with existing algorithms.

**Overall:**

This paper presents a solid theoretical contribution to the understanding of fundamental limits in crowdsourcing. While some assumptions and a lack of extensive empirical validation are concerns, the novelty and rigor of the work are commendable. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. Specifically, the authors should address the limitations of the uniform source assumption, provide more extensive numerical results comparing k-ary incidence coding with existing methods, and expand the discussion on query pricing. Encouraging the authors to explore the implications of non-uniform source distributions and more realistic worker models would further enhance the impact of this work.
