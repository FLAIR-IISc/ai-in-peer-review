

This paper models crowdsourcing as an information-theoretic rate-distortion problem to establish fundamental performance limits for the budget-fidelity trade-off. The authors frame the crowdsourcer as a transmitter sending queries through parallel noisy channels (workers) to a receiver performing inference, with the goal of minimizing distortion (e.g., classification error) under a query budget constraint. The work derives two key theoretical bounds: one for unknown worker skill levels (SL-UK) and another for perfectly estimated skill levels (SL-CS), using a joint source-channel coding framework. It also introduces k-ary incidence coding (kIC), analyzes its performance under a spammer-hammer channel model, and provides a theoretical bound for kIC in Corollary 1 and Theorem 4. The paper connects to prior crowdsourcing work (e.g., Dawid-Skene, majority voting) but frames the problem in a novel information-theoretic context, distinguishing it from existing algorithmic approaches.

The work demonstrates strong theoretical quality. The proofs of Theorems 2 and 3 are rigorous and follow information-theoretic conventions, with appendices providing complete derivations. The kIC analysis (Lemma 1, Theorem 4) offers a clear connection between query design and error performance, and the numerical results in Figure 2 effectively compare the theoretical bounds. The model's assumptions (independent workers, no feedback, specific channel models) are well-motivated for the theoretical setting. The clarity is generally high, with well-structured sections and helpful examples (e.g., Figure 1c illustrating kIC). The kIC explanation is particularly accessible, though the transition from channel models to crowdsourcing could be slightly smoother for non-information-theory readers.

Originality is a major strength. While crowdsourcing has been extensively studied algorithmically, this paper is the first to establish information-theoretic fundamental limits for the budget-fidelity trade-off at NIPS. The kIC query scheme represents a novel combinatorial approach to error correction in crowdsourcing, distinct from prior work on redundant assignment or EM-based inference. The comparison to prior work (e.g., [5, 8, 11]) is thorough, and the paper explicitly positions its contribution as complementary to existing algorithmic designs.

Significance is substantial. The derived bounds provide a theoretical benchmark for evaluating practical crowdsourcing systems, addressing a critical gap between empirical studies and foundational understanding. The query pricing rule in Appendix E (π(k₁)/π(k₂) ≈ k₁k₂) offers a concrete, actionable insight for system designers. The work advances the field by shifting the focus from "how to aggregate labels" to "what is the best possible fidelity achievable for a given budget," which could guide future algorithm development. However, the significance would be enhanced by more explicit discussion of how these bounds translate to real-world platforms (e.g., AMT cost structures) and limitations of the theoretical assumptions.

Key weaknesses include limited empirical validation beyond theoretical bounds. While Figure 2 compares bounds, there are no experiments on real crowdsourcing data to demonstrate the gap between theory and practice or to validate the kIC scheme's practical utility. The paper also assumes a uniform dataset (Corollary 1) and binary classification (N=2) for much of the analysis, which may limit applicability to more complex scenarios like multi-class labeling or non-uniform data distributions. The SL-CS assumption (perfect skill estimation) is strong and may not hold in practice; a discussion of how to handle imperfect skill estimation would strengthen the SL-CS analysis.

Theoretical depth is excellent, but the paper could better address scalability concerns. The proofs assume large block lengths (n→∞), which is standard for rate-distortion theory, but practical crowdsourcing systems operate on finite datasets. A brief discussion of finite-block effects would improve the work's applicability. Additionally, while the paper cites recent NIPS work (e.g., Zhang et al. 2016), it does not explicitly compare its bounds to the performance of state-of-the-art algorithms like spectral methods [11] or EM-based approaches [5, 9], which would clarify the practical relevance of the theoretical limits.

The review should focus on these points constructively: the theoretical contribution is strong and novel, but the paper would benefit from (1) more discussion of practical implications of the bounds, (2) exploration of how the assumptions (e.g., uniform data, perfect skill estimation) affect real-world applicability, and (3) a brief empirical comparison (even synthetic) to contextualize the theoretical bounds against existing methods. These revisions are reasonable and would significantly enhance the paper's impact without requiring major rework.

The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to crowdsourcing, establishing fundamental limits that have not been explored in prior NIPS work. It is technically sound, well-motivated, and presents a novel perspective on a well-studied problem. While the empirical validation is limited, the theoretical rigor and originality justify acceptance, with minor revisions to strengthen the practical relevance and discussion of assumptions. The work provides a solid foundation for future algorithm design and should be of interest to the NIPS community.
