- Novel framing: applies information-theoretic rate-distortion framework to crowdsourcing, a new and promising approach.  
- Theoretical contribution: derives bounds on fidelity given budget and vice-versa for known and unknown worker skill levels.  
- Concrete scheme: analyzes k-ary incidence coding (kIC) as a practical query strategy beyond abstract bounds.  
- Motivation and relevance: problem is well-motivated and connected to real systems (AMT, Dawid–Skene).  
- Notation and exposition: notation is dense and many variables lack clear explanation; mathematical introduction should be more gradual.  
- Joint source-channel link: mentions joint source-channel coding but fails to detail how Shannon’s results specifically apply to the crowdsourcing model.  
- Worker model limits: reliance on MSC and SHC worker models is restrictive and their practical relevance isn’t well defended.  
- Pricing analysis weak: query pricing section feels tacked on and the pricing rule (π(k1) π(k2) ≈ k1k2) is not well motivated.  
- Proof presentation: some proofs omit steps and lack intuitive explanations, reducing accessibility and rigor.  
- Tightness of bounds unclear: claims of lower bounds are not shown to be tight or practical without further evidence.  
- Lack of empirical validation: no simulations or experiments provided to support theoretical claims or assess bounds in practice.  
- Practical connection: theoretical results are not strongly connected to implementation details or real-world applicability.  
- Originality: novel combination of established information-theory tools applied to crowdsourcing; kIC is an original contribution.  
- Significance contingent: results could significantly impact crowdsourcing design, but depend on model validity and bound tightness.  
- Overall recommendation: recommend rejection but encourage revision addressing clarity, assumptions, proofs, and adding empirical results.
