This paper introduces an information-theoretic framework to analyze the fundamental limits of the trade-off between budget and fidelity in crowdsourcing. The authors model the crowdsourcing problem as a communication system where the true labels of items are a source, the workers are parallel noisy channels, and the query/inference schemes constitute a joint source-channel code. The budget is represented by the code rate (queries per item) and the fidelity is measured by distortion (probability of error).

The main contribution is the application of the source-channel separation theorem to derive the minimum budget required to achieve a target fidelity. The paper establishes these fundamental bounds for two scenarios: 1) when worker skill levels are unknown (SL-UK), and 2) when they are known to the crowdsourcer (SL-CS). These bounds serve as an ultimate benchmark for any crowdsourcing system. The paper also introduces and analyzes a specific query scheme, k-ary Incidence Coding (kIC), where workers are asked to compare k items simultaneously. A performance bound for kIC under a spammer-hammer worker model is derived and compared to the general information-theoretic limit. This work distinguishes itself from most prior research in crowdsourcing, which has primarily focused on developing specific inference algorithms (like those based on EM or majority voting), by instead asking what the best possible performance is for a given budget.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of this paper is its novel and powerful framing of the crowdsourcing problem. By abstracting away from specific algorithms and using the lens of information theory, the authors provide a new perspective and address a fundamental question about the inherent limits of crowdsourcing. The derived bounds (Theorems 2 and 3) are significant because they provide a theoretical "gold standard" against which any practical crowdsourcing algorithm can be measured. This can help researchers and practitioners understand the potential for improvement in their systems.

2.  **Theoretical Soundness:** The application of joint source-channel coding theory is well-founded and appropriate for the problem. The distinction between the SL-UK and SL-CS cases is particularly insightful, as it formally quantifies the performance gain achievable by accurately estimating worker skill levels (the difference between the capacity of an average worker and the average of worker capacities).

3.  **Introduction of a Novel Query Scheme:** The proposal of k-ary Incidence Coding (kIC) is an interesting contribution. It represents a concrete query design that attempts to build error correction into the query process itself by leveraging structural constraints (e.g., transitivity in clustering). The analysis of this scheme provides a valuable link between the abstract information-theoretic limits and a more practical coding strategy.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Connection to Practice:** While the theoretical results are strong, the paper could be improved by better connecting the asymptotic bounds to practical, finite-sized crowdsourcing scenarios. The information-theoretic limits hold for large block lengths (n → ∞), and it is not immediately clear how they apply to typical projects with finite datasets and budgets. A discussion on the implications for non-asymptotic settings would be valuable.

2.  **Lack of Empirical Comparison:** The numerical results in Figure 2 compare the theoretical limit to the derived lower bound for kIC. While useful, the paper's impact would be substantially greater if these bounds were also compared with the performance of well-established, practical crowdsourcing algorithms like the Dawid-Skene (EM) algorithm or simple majority voting. Simulating these algorithms under the same SHC worker model and plotting their budget-fidelity curves would provide a clear picture of how far current state-of-the-art methods are from the fundamental limit, thereby better motivating the search for improved schemes.

3.  **Clarity of kIC:** The description of k-ary Incidence Coding could be expanded for clarity. While the core idea is understandable, the mechanism by which it provides "error correction capability" could be explained more intuitively. For example, showing how a worker selecting an "invalid" response (e.g., one that violates transitivity, like in Figure 1c) can be detected or corrected by the decoder would make the concept more concrete for the reader.

4.  **Model Assumptions:** The analysis relies on standard but simplifying assumptions, such as i.i.d. sources (all items are equally difficult) and memoryless channels (worker performance is constant). In reality, item difficulty can vary, and workers can exhibit fatigue or learning effects. A brief discussion acknowledging these limitations and suggesting potential directions for extending the model to more complex scenarios would strengthen the paper.

In summary, this paper presents a novel, theoretically sound, and significant contribution to the understanding of crowdsourcing. By establishing fundamental limits, it provides a valuable new benchmark for the field. The weaknesses identified are primarily related to bridging the gap between the elegant theory and messy practice, but they do not detract from the core value of the work. The paper is well-suited for NIPS and is likely to inspire future research in this direction.
