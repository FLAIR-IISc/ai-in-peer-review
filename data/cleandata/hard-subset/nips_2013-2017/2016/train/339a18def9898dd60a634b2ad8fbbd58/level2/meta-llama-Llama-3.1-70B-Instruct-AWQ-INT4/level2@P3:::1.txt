**Summary of the Main Ideas**

This paper presents a framework for modeling and analyzing crowdsourcing problems in an information-theoretic setting. The authors aim to identify the fundamental performance limits of crowdsourcing, specifically the trade-off between the budget (number of queries per item) and the fidelity (accuracy) of the results. They model the crowdsourcing process as a communication system, where the taskmaster (encoder) sends queries to workers (channels) who respond with answers, and the crowdsourcer (decoder) infers the correct labels from the responses.

The authors consider two scenarios: when the workers' skill levels are unknown (SL-UK) and when they are perfectly known by the crowdsourcer (SL-CS). They derive information-theoretic bounds on the minimum number of queries per item required to achieve a certain fidelity in both scenarios. They also introduce a query scheme called k-ary incidence coding (kIC) and analyze its performance.

**Strengths**

1. The paper provides a novel framework for modeling and analyzing crowdsourcing problems using information-theoretic concepts.
2. The authors derive tight bounds on the minimum number of queries per item required to achieve a certain fidelity, which can serve as a benchmark for evaluating the performance of crowdsourcing systems.
3. The introduction of kIC as a query scheme is a valuable contribution, and the analysis of its performance provides insights into the trade-offs between budget and fidelity.

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper assumes a simplistic model of worker behavior, where workers are either spammers or hammers, which may not accurately capture the complexity of real-world crowdsourcing scenarios.
2. The analysis is limited to binary labeling tasks, and it is unclear how the results generalize to more complex tasks or multi-class labeling problems.
3. The paper does not provide a clear comparison with existing crowdsourcing algorithms or systems, making it difficult to assess the practical implications of the theoretical results.

**Originality**

The paper presents a novel application of information-theoretic concepts to crowdsourcing problems, which is a significant contribution to the field. The introduction of kIC as a query scheme is also an original contribution.

**Significance**

The paper's results have significant implications for the design of crowdsourcing systems, as they provide a theoretical foundation for understanding the trade-offs between budget and fidelity. The results can be used to optimize the design of crowdsourcing systems, leading to more efficient and accurate results.

**Clarity**

The paper is well-written and clear in its presentation of the main ideas and results. However, some of the technical details and proofs may be challenging for non-experts to follow.

**Quality**

The paper is technically sound, and the results are well-supported by theoretical analysis and numerical simulations.
