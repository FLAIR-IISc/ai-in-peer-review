This paper presents an information-theoretic framework for analyzing the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing systems. The authors model the crowdsourcing problem as a human-in-the-loop computation problem and apply rate-distortion theory to derive bounds on the minimum number of queries per item required to achieve a certain level of fidelity.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the underlying concepts and models. The authors provide a thorough review of related work and clearly position their contribution within the existing literature.

The technical contributions of the paper are significant. The authors derive two theorems that provide lower bounds on the minimum number of queries per item required to achieve a certain level of fidelity in crowdsourcing systems. The first theorem assumes that the workers' skill levels are unknown, while the second theorem assumes that the skill levels are perfectly known by the crowdsourcer. The authors also analyze the performance of a specific query scheme, k-ary incidence coding, and derive a lower bound on its performance.

The paper also includes numerical results that illustrate the information-theoretic bounds and the performance of k-ary incidence coding. The results are interesting and provide insight into the trade-offs between the number of queries and the level of fidelity in crowdsourcing systems.

* The paper presents a novel information-theoretic framework for analyzing crowdsourcing systems.
* The technical contributions are significant, including the derivation of two theorems that provide lower bounds on the minimum number of queries per item required to achieve a certain level of fidelity.

Weaknesses:

* The analysis is limited to a specific type of query scheme, k-ary incidence coding, and it is not clear how the results generalize to other query schemes.
* The paper does not provide a clear discussion of the implications of the results for the design of crowdsourcing systems.
