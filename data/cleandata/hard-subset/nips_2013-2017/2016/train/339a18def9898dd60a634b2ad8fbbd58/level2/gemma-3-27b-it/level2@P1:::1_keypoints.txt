- Paper studies budget–fidelity trade-offs in crowdsourcing using an information-theoretic framework and models workers as noisy channels.
- Derives fundamental bounds on achievable fidelity for given budgets under known and unknown worker skill levels.
- Introduces and analyzes a k-ary incidence coding (kIC) scheme and discusses query pricing strategies.
- Novel application of information theory to crowdsourcing, offering theoretical foundations beyond algorithmic/system work.
- Rigorous mathematical analysis with well-motivated connection to rate-distortion theory.
- Communication-system-style modeling of workers (noisy channels) is clear and intuitive.
- kIC is an interesting coding/query design contribution, potentially useful for reducing query counts.
- Paper is generally well-written and organized, making complex concepts accessible.
- Strong simplifying assumptions (independent workers, memoryless channels) may not hold in practice and impact bound tightness.
- Practicality and computational cost of kIC for high-dimensional or complex tasks are insufficiently explored.
- Discussion of query pricing feels underdeveloped and lacks detailed justification.
- Lack of empirical validation or simulations weakens claims about real-world relevance.
- Notation inconsistencies (e.g., multiple meanings of 'R') could be clarified.
- Related-work discussion could be deeper, especially connections to active learning and prior crowdsourcing inference methods.
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses a fundamental question, provides novel theoretical limits, and proposes a useful coding scheme.
- Arguments against acceptance: restrictive assumptions, no empirical validation, and superficial practical discussion.
- Overall assessment: solid contribution meriting acceptance with minor revisions to add empirical validation, clarify assumptions, and expand practical discussion.
- Recommendation: Accept (with minor revisions).
