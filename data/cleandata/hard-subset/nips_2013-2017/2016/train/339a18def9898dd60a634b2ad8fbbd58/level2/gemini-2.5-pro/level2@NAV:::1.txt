This paper presents a novel information-theoretic framework for analyzing the fundamental trade-off between budget and fidelity in crowdsourcing. The authors model the crowdsourcing pipeline as a joint source-channel communication system: the underlying true labels are the "source," the crowd workers are "parallel noisy channels," and the query and inference mechanisms constitute the "coding" and "decoding" schemes. The main contribution is the derivation of ultimate performance bounds, expressed as a rate-distortion function, which characterizes the minimum budget (queries per item) required to achieve a target fidelity (error rate). This approach departs from much of the existing literature (e.g., Dawid & Skene, 1979; Karger et al., 2014), which focuses on designing specific aggregation algorithms rather than establishing problem-agnostic limits. The paper further applies this framework to analyze a specific query scheme, k-ary Incidence Coding (kIC), and derives its performance bounds.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The application of rate-distortion theory to crowdsourcing is a highly original and significant contribution. It provides a principled way to understand the inherent limits of the problem, offering a new benchmark against which all crowdsourcing algorithms can be measured. This theoretical lens could guide the design of more efficient systems by quantifying the value of information, such as knowing worker skill levels (as shown by comparing Theorems 2 and 3).
*   **Technical Quality:** The theoretical formulation is rigorous and well-grounded in information theory. The modeling choices are justified, and the derived theorems provide clear, interpretable results about the system's capacity under different assumptions about worker knowledge.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The analogy to a communication system is effectively used to make complex theoretical concepts accessible to the broader machine learning community.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While establishing fundamental limits is valuable, the work would be significantly strengthened by simulations comparing the performance of well-known practical algorithms (e.g., Majority Voting, Dawid-Skene's EM) against the derived theoretical bounds. Without this, it is difficult to assess how tight these bounds are or how much room for improvement exists over current state-of-the-art methods in practical, non-asymptotic settings.
*   **Reliance on Asymptotic Assumptions:** The bounds are derived under asymptotic assumptions (e.g., large datasets, large block lengths), which may not hold in many real-world crowdsourcing tasks. A discussion on the implications for finite-sized problems would be beneficial.

**Recommendation:**

This paper introduces a powerful and original theoretical perspective on a fundamental problem. While the lack of empirical validation is a notable weakness, the novelty and significance of the theoretical contribution are high. I recommend acceptance, but strongly encourage the authors to include simulation-based experiments in a future version to bridge the gap between their theory and existing practice.
