- Proposes an information-theoretic framework modeling crowdsourcing as joint source-channel coding and defines a fidelity–budget function analogous to rate–distortion.  
- Derives lower bounds on required queries per item to achieve target accuracy for two scenarios: skills unknown (SL-UK) and skills known (SL-CS).  
- Introduces k-ary Incidence Coding (kIC) and analyzes its performance under a spammer–hammer worker model to demonstrate practical applicability.  
- Theorems 2 and 3 present rigorous, fundamental performance limits that serve as benchmarks for any crowdsourcing algorithm.  
- Highlights a key distinction: with known skills performance depends on the average of worker capacities, while with unknown skills it depends on the capacity of an average worker.  
- Analysis is technically sound but relies on i.i.d. sources and memoryless channels, which may not capture real-world factors like variable item difficulty, fatigue, or learning.  
- Paper is well-written and organized; the communication-system analogy and figures (e.g., 3IC) effectively build intuition.  
- Potential minor confusion: treating "rate" directly as "budget" without formally incorporating differing query costs into the main model.  
- Highly original approach shifting focus from algorithm design to fundamental limits, complementing prior EM/spectral method work.  
- kIC is a novel, structured query scheme introduced within the theoretical framework.  
- Results are significant: provide Shannon-like bounds to guide system design, minimum budget questions, and theoretical justification for reputation/skill estimation.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel framework, fundamental limits, insights on skill estimation benefits, technical rigor, and connection to kIC.  
- Suggestions for improvement: discuss implications of asymptotic bounds for finite tasks; analyze kIC performance with practical (non-oracle) decoders; integrate query cost considerations into the main budget formulation.
