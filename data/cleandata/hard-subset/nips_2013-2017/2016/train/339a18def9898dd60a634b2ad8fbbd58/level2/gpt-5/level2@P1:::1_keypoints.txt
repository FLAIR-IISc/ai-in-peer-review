- Models crowdsourcing as joint source–channel coding, deriving rate–distortion trade-offs with workers as DMCs and considering MSC and SHC worker models (SL-UK vs SL-CS bounds).  
- Introduces k-ary incidence coding (kIC), gives a lower bound under an oracle decoder for SHC and a pricing heuristic for different k.  
- Frames results as information-theoretic performance benchmarks independent of specific aggregation algorithms.  
- Novelty: adapts classical rate–distortion/separation ideas to crowdsourcing, quantifies value of skill estimation and structured queries, and connects to triplet/incidence query literature.  
- Strength: clear, unifying coding perspective with interpretable theorems (Theorems 2–3) and useful intuition on SL-UK vs SL-CS.  
- Strength: kIC analysis highlights self-consistency/error-correction of structured queries and yields an interpretable SHC-based bound (Theorem 4) and pricing rule-of-thumb.  
- Strength: careful separation of information-theoretic limits from algorithmic choices and explicit non-adaptivity assumptions.  
- Key soundness concern: need rigorous justification that separation and DMC assumptions hold under random/unknown worker skill states (memorylessness, stationarity, mixture/compound channel issues).  
- Key modeling issue: kIC causes query-dependent worker error distributions and “static feedforward,” which technically violate standard DMC assumptions and may affect bounds.  
- Theorem 4 uses an oracle that perfectly identifies hammers/spammers; tightness is unclear without complementary achievability or simulation results.  
- Asymptotic focus: no finite-sample or finite-blocklength analysis, limiting practical relevance and applicability.  
- Clarity issues: ambiguous notation (M vs N, E, HM/HN, R′, p_max), unstated priors and worker-selection assumptions, and cost model details buried in appendix.  
- Originality assessment: high-level mapping is classical, but the SL-UK/SL-CS framing and kIC analysis under SHC are novel and useful as benchmarks.  
- Significance: useful for benchmarking and design guidance (skill estimation, query structure), but limited near-term practical impact without achievability/validation.  
- Suggestions: justify capacity terms via mixed/compound channel literature; add achievability baselines and simulations (repetition, majority, DS/spectral EM); extend kIC beyond N=2 and nonuniform priors; discuss adaptivity; provide finite-sample bounds; clarify and validate cost/pricing model.  
- Pros for acceptance: principled formulation, informative limits, and potential theoretical/practical influence.  
- Cons for acceptance: heavy reliance on asymptotic separation with limited justification, no achievability/empirical validation, narrow worker models and limited kIC scope.  
- Overall: a promising theoretical contribution that needs stronger rigor on channel/separation assumptions and empirical/algorithmic comparisons to demonstrate tightness and practical value.
