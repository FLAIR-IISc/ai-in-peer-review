Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper models label crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding problem and derives information-theoretic budget–fidelity limits. The main claims are: (1) fundamental lower bounds on the number of queries per item needed to achieve target error under two settings—unknown worker skill (SL-UK) and perfectly known skill at the crowdsourcer (SL-CS)—for MSC worker models; (2) analysis of k-ary incidence coding (kIC) under a spammer–hammer (SHC) model, yielding lower bounds and a pricing heuristic. This connects to classic rate–distortion and channel capacity (Cover & Thomas; El Gamal & Kim) and to NeurIPS-canonical crowdsourcing work on aggregation and budget/reliability trade-offs (Dawid–Skene EM, Karger–Oh–Shah, spectral/EM hybrids). The kIC idea relates to pairwise/graph-based clustering constraints (Vinayak et al.) but emphasizes the error-correction implicit in ruling out invalid incidence patterns.

Strengths:
- Quality: The theorems for Rmin under MSC (SL-UK vs SL-CS) and the kIC lower bound under SHC are technically sound within stated assumptions; proofs are outlined in appendices. The use of separation to obtain clean limits is principled.
- Originality: Framing crowdsourcing end-to-end via rate–distortion and separation is fresh and unifying, clarifying when “average worker” vs “average capacity” governs performance. The kIC lens and its combinatorial redundancy are insightful, and the pricing rule-of-thumb is a practical byproduct.
- Significance: Establishing ultimate limits is valuable for experimental design and platform pricing, and provides a baseline to evaluate inference/assignment algorithms.

Weaknesses:
- Assumptions: MSC/SHC, iid items, non-adaptive coding, independence across workers, and SL-CS “perfect skill estimation” are restrictive. Difficulty-dependent noise and adversarial/coordinated behaviors are not modeled.
- Validation: No simulations or experiments to compare tightness of bounds or illustrate finite-sample regimes; the pricing heuristic lacks empirical support.
- Completeness/clarity: Some notation (M vs N, U=V) is easy to confuse; references have missing years; related NeurIPS results on minimax sample complexity and optimal allocations (e.g., KOS) could be discussed more directly vis-à-vis these bounds.

Constructive suggestions:
- Add simulations comparing kIC vs binary queries under varying q, k, and costs; report finite-length gaps to the information-theoretic limit.
- Explore adaptive/query-dependent designs (e.g., active kIC, difficulty-aware channels) and partial skill estimation between SL-UK and SL-CS.
- Clarify notation early (N classes, M choices) and complete citations; discuss how bounds change for non-uniform class priors and multiclass kIC.
- Compare to Karger–Oh–Shah bounds and recent spectral/EM theory to position novelty and tightness.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear, principled information-theoretic framing; novel insights on kIC and pricing; potential to influence practice.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited empirical/finite-length evidence; strong modeling assumptions; incomplete related-work positioning.

Overall: A solid theoretical contribution with clear potential; I recommend acceptance if the authors add simulations and strengthen the discussion of assumptions and prior theory.
