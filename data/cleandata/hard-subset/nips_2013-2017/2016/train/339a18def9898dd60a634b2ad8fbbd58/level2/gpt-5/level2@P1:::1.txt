Summary
The paper models label crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding problem and studies the fundamental trade-off between query budget (rate) and achievable fidelity (distortion). The dataset label function B(X) is treated as a source to be reconstructed from noisy crowd responses, with workers modeled as parallel discrete memoryless channels. Two worker models are considered: (i) M-ary symmetric channels (MSC) with a distribution over skill levels; and (ii) a spammer–hammer channel (SHC) with probability q of a perfect worker. Two information-theoretic lower bounds on the minimum queries-per-item R needed to achieve average error ≤ ε are derived: one when worker skill levels are unknown to both taskmaster and crowdsourcer (SL-UK), and one when skill levels are perfectly known to the crowdsourcer (SL-CS). The paper also introduces k-ary incidence coding (kIC), where each query presents k items and asks for their within-class incidences; it derives a lower bound on the rate–fidelity trade-off for kIC under an oracle decoder and an SHC(q) population, and states a rule-of-thumb for relative pricing of different k based on this analysis. The results are positioned as ultimate (information-theoretic) performance benchmarks, independent of specific aggregation algorithms.

Relation to prior work
There is a rich literature on aggregation/inference (Dawid–Skene EM, spectral/graphical EM, convex relaxations, and the Karger–Oh–Shah line on budget-optimal allocation and reliability), as well as adaptive selection/bandit formulations. This paper differs by providing rate–distortion-style limits under explicit channel models, complementing algorithmic analyses such as majority vote/EM optimality gaps and adaptivity. The use of incidence/triplet-style queries connects to clustering with pairwise/triplet constraints studied via convex and spectral methods; here the novelty is to quantify their error-correction value within a coding-theoretic framework.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: casting crowdsourcing as joint source–channel coding yields a unifying lens and clean statements of limits (Theorems 2–3).
- Two regimes (SL-UK vs SL-CS) are contrasted, giving intuition about the value of estimating worker skills (average worker capacity vs average of capacities).
- k-ary incidence coding is natural and the analysis highlights why structured queries can be self-consistent and error-correcting.
- The SHC-based bound for kIC (Theorem 4) is interpretable; the dependence on k and q matches intuition, and the resulting pricing heuristic is potentially useful in practice.
- The paper is careful to separate information-theoretic achievability/converse from algorithmic choices, and calls out non-adaptivity assumptions.

Weaknesses and questions
Quality (soundness)
- The application of source–channel separation to the SL-UK case assumes an effective channel formed by random, unknown skill levels. The capacity terms in Theorems 2–3 involve expressions like log2 M − H_M(·). It would help to justify rigorously: (a) whether the effective channel is memoryless and stationary under random worker sampling with unknown state at the decoder; (b) when separation continues to hold under channel state uncertainty (mixture/compound channels); and (c) how the denominator is derived (average conditional entropy vs entropy of mixture), including the role of input distribution over query symbols U.
- kIC induces query-dependent worker error distributions (workers face different combinatorial choice sets as k varies). The paper notes “static feedforward,” which technically violates the no-feedforward assumption required for a clean DMC model. The implications for the separation-based lower bounds deserve more explicit treatment.
- The kIC bound (Theorem 4) assumes an oracle that perfectly identifies hammers vs spammers. This is fine for a lower bound, but its tightness is unclear without complementary achievability (e.g., DS-like estimators) or simulation to show a small gap.
- Finite-sample behavior is not addressed. As blocklength n → ∞ is central to separation, some finite-length or concentration bounds (even coarse) would improve practical relevance.

Clarity
- Several notational points would benefit from careful disambiguation: M (number of response choices) vs N (number of true labels), E (set of skill levels) and HM(·), HN(·), and their arguments; R′ in kIC; and p_max in Theorems 2–3. A compact notation table would help.
- Assumptions on priors (uniform vs non-uniform labels), worker selection (random, independent draws), and independence across items should be stated upfront for each theorem.
- The cost model matters for comparing different k; Appendix E is referenced but not summarized in the main text. The pricing heuristic π(k1)/π(k2) ≈ k1/k2 would be stronger with a brief derivation and discussion of cognitive/time costs.

Originality
- The high-level mapping to rate–distortion and separation is classical. The originality lies in adapting it to crowdsourcing models (SL-UK vs SL-CS) and in analyzing kIC under SHC. This seems novel and useful as a benchmark, though the algorithmic implications are indirect.

Significance
- As a benchmarking framework, the results can guide system design, e.g., whether to invest in skill estimation, and whether to prefer higher-arity incidence queries. The pricing rule-of-thumb is a nice byproduct. However, without achievability constructions or empirical validation, it is hard to assess tightness; this may limit near-term impact on practice.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a more explicit derivation and justification of the capacity terms in Theorems 2–3 under unknown/known channel states, including references to information theory of mixed/compound channels and conditions under which separation applies.
- Add achievability baselines: show how simple schemes (repetition + majority vote; DS/spectral EM with estimated worker skills) perform relative to the bounds on synthetic data for both MSC and SHC, across varying q, M, N, and k.
- Extend kIC analysis beyond N = 2 and uniform priors; quantify the number of invalid patterns and resultant error-correction margin as a function of N and k.
- Discuss adaptivity: how would allowing feedback (e.g., routing more queries to likely hammers) change the bounds? Even upper/lower bounds would be informative.
- Include finite-length/sample complexity insights, even if via normal approximations or simple Chernoff-type bounds, to bridge theory to practice.
- Clarify the cost model for kIC, including human effort/time, and validate the pricing heuristic with pilot measurements or references.

Pros for acceptance
- Clean, principled formulation with informative, interpretable limits.
- Highlights the benefit of worker-skill estimation and of structured queries.
- Potential to influence both theory (benchmarks) and practice (pricing/design).

Cons for acceptance
- Heavy reliance on asymptotic separation with limited justification under state uncertainty and kIC feedforward effects.
- No achievability algorithms or empirical validation; tightness of bounds unclear.
- Limited scope of worker models (MSC, SHC) and N = 2 in kIC analysis; practical heterogeneities (item difficulty, adaptivity) are not treated.

Overall assessment
A thoughtful theoretical contribution offering useful benchmarks and a compelling coding perspective on crowdsourcing. I encourage the authors to strengthen the rigor around channel modeling/separation assumptions and to add empirical comparisons to demonstrate tightness and practical value. At present, I view it as a promising but incomplete step toward fundamental understanding of budget–fidelity trade-offs.
