

This paper models crowdsourcing as a communication problem within an information-theoretic rate-distortion framework, establishing fundamental limits on the budget-fidelity trade-off. The authors present two key theoretical results: Theorem 2 derives the minimum queries per item required when workers' skill levels are unknown (SL-UK), and Theorem 3 provides the corresponding bound when skill levels are known (SL-CS). They also introduce k-ary incidence coding (kIC), a query scheme where workers are asked to identify items with the same label among k randomly selected items, and analyze its performance under a spammer-hammer channel model.

The paper's main strength lies in its novel theoretical approach to crowdsourcing, which has been previously addressed through algorithmic optimization rather than fundamental limits. The information-theoretic framework provides a rigorous foundation that complements existing work on crowdsourcing aggregation (e.g., Dawid-Skene, majority voting). The proofs of Theorems 2 and 3 are well-constructed, and the kIC analysis with Theorem 4 offers practical insights into query design. The numerical results in Figure 2 effectively demonstrate how kIC performance approaches the theoretical bounds as k increases.

However, the paper has notable limitations. The analysis relies on specific channel models (MSC and SHC) that may not capture the full complexity of real-world crowdsourcing scenarios. The theoretical bounds assume large datasets and infinite block lengths, which may not translate directly to practical implementations. Additionally, while the paper mentions query pricing in Appendix E, it lacks concrete experimental validation of the proposed pricing rule (π(k1)/π(k2) ≈ k1/k2).

The paper makes a significant contribution by establishing fundamental performance limits in crowdsourcing, which could guide future algorithm design. The writing is clear and well-organized, though the practical relevance could be strengthened by addressing implementation challenges. The theoretical results are sound and properly contextualized within existing crowdsourcing literature, making this a valuable contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the gap between theoretical bounds and practical implementation.
