This paper investigates the fundamental limits of budget-fidelity trade-offs in label crowdsourcing, modeling the problem as a communication system and leveraging information theory. The authors aim to determine the ultimate performance bounds achievable by any query scheme and inference algorithm, considering scenarios with both unknown and perfectly estimated worker skill levels. They also introduce and analyze k-ary incidence coding as a specific query scheme. The work builds upon existing research in crowdsourcing, particularly regarding aggregation methods like majority voting and the Dawid-Skene algorithm, and extends it by focusing on information-theoretic limitations rather than algorithmic optimization.

**Strengths:**

*   **Rigorous Framework:** The paper provides a well-defined information-theoretic framework for analyzing crowdsourcing, offering a principled approach to understanding fundamental limits.
*   **Novelty of Approach:** Applying information theory to the crowdsourcing problem, specifically to determine achievable bounds on fidelity given a budget, is a novel contribution.  Most existing work focuses on algorithmic improvements *within* a certain budget, rather than characterizing the budget needed to achieve a given fidelity.
*   **Two Skill Level Scenarios:** The analysis of both unknown and perfectly estimated worker skill levels provides valuable insights into the impact of worker knowledge on performance.
*   **k-ary Incidence Coding:** The introduction and preliminary analysis of k-ary incidence coding offer a potentially useful query scheme with error correction capabilities.
*   **Clear Mathematical Formulation:** The problem is clearly formalized, using concepts from information theory such as rate-distortion functions and mutual information.
* **Potential Practical Implications:** The discussion of query pricing strategy based on the proposed kIC coding is a welcome addition.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Assumptions & Simplifications:** The model relies on several simplifying assumptions (e.g., i.i.d. source, memoryless channels, perfect skill estimation in one scenario) that may not hold in real-world crowdsourcing applications. The impact of these assumptions is not fully discussed.
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The paper lacks empirical validation. While the theoretical analysis is strong, demonstrating the practical relevance of the derived bounds through simulations or real-world experiments would significantly strengthen the work.
*   **kIC Analysis Depth:** The analysis of k-ary incidence coding, while promising, feels somewhat superficial. The theorems regarding kIC provide bounds, but lack a detailed discussion on the practical implications of choosing *k*. A more thorough exploration of the trade-offs would be beneficial.
*   **Clarity of Notation:** While the mathematical formulation is generally clear, the extensive use of symbols and abbreviations requires careful attention, and could potentially hinder readability for some readers. More descriptive variable names could improve clarity.
* **Connection to Existing Literature:** The discussion of related work, while present, could be more thorough. Specifically, connections to active learning and experimental design in the machine learning literature could be beneficial. These areas also address the problem of efficiently querying for labels.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to understanding the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing.
*   The application of information theory provides a strong foundation for analyzing the budget-fidelity trade-off.
*   The introduction of k-ary incidence coding is a potentially valuable contribution.
*   The mathematical formulation is rigorous and well-defined.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The lack of empirical validation limits the practical relevance of the theoretical results.
*   The simplifying assumptions may restrict the applicability of the findings to real-world scenarios.
*   The analysis of k-ary incidence coding could be more comprehensive.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the understanding of crowdsourcing.  The information-theoretic approach is novel and provides important insights into the fundamental limits of the problem. However, the lack of empirical validation and the simplifying assumptions raise concerns about the practical relevance of the results. The analysis of k-ary incidence coding, while promising, could be more detailed.

**Recommendation:**

Weak Accept. The theoretical novelty and rigor are commendable, but the absence of empirical support prevents a strong recommendation.  I suggest the authors address the empirical validation concerns in future work to further solidify their contributions.  A more detailed analysis of the kIC scheme, including a discussion of optimal k selection strategies, would also strengthen the paper.
