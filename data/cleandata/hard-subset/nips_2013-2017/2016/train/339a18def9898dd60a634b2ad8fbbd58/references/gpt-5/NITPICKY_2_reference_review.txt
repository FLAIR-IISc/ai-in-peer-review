Summary
The paper proposes an information-theoretic framework for crowdsourcing and studies fundamental budget–fidelity trade-offs. The model maps items and their labels to a source, workers to noisy channels (with M-ary symmetric channel, MSC, and spammer–hammer channel, SHC), and query/inference schemes to joint source–channel coding. The main theoretical results are:

- Lower bounds on the required queries per item (Rmin) to achieve target error rate under Hamming distortion via a source–channel separation argument:
  - SL-UK (skill levels unknown at the crowdsourcer): denominator uses capacity of the averaged MSC.
  - SL-CS (skill levels known at the crowdsourcer): denominator uses the expectation of per-state capacities.
- Introduction of k-ary incidence coding (kIC), where a query bundles k items and invalid response patterns are disallowed, yielding an “implicit” error-correcting effect.
- For SHC(q) worker pools, a lemma gives the probability a spammer induces error under kIC, and Theorem 4 gives a necessary condition on R for achieving a target error. A corollary provides an information-theoretic lower bound for general coding under SHC with skill known to the crowdsourcer. The paper also suggests a rule-of-thumb for query pricing across different k.

Relation to prior work: The paper connects to Dawid–Skene, bandit survey models, EM-based and spectral methods for aggregation, and convex formulations for clustering with pairwise queries. The novelty is the explicit use of rate–distortion and channel coding bounds to derive fundamental limits independent of any specific inference algorithm, plus the analysis of kIC within this lens.

Strengths
- Ambitious and timely attempt to place crowdsourcing reliability–cost trade-offs on firm information-theoretic footing.
- Separation-based lower bounds for SL-UK vs SL-CS are conceptually clean; using average capacity (unknown state) vs expected capacity (state known at receiver) is reasonable for symmetric channels.
- kIC is a natural and practical query design; the observation that restricting to valid partitions prunes error patterns is intuitive and potentially impactful.
- The SHC analysis yielding necessary conditions is informative as a baseline and aligns with common practical concerns (spammers vs hammers).
- The work could unify disparate results (aggregation algorithms, task allocation, redundancy) under a common limit.

Weaknesses and issues (many minor but numerous; some substantial)
Technical correctness and completeness:
- Missing or malformed definitions/notation throughout. Examples: MSC parameter “ε” not consistently introduced; “∈ E , ∼ P( )” placeholders; W vs W′; HN(·) and HM(·) not precisely defined with bases and arguments; U,V, M,N used inconsistently (M = response alphabet size vs N = number of classes) yet sometimes conflated. Eq. (1) is malformed: P(v|u) = { ? } with missing cases and symbols.
- Theorems 2–3: Rmin formulae use log2 M − HM(E(ε)) and log2 M − E[HM(ε)]. For an M-ary symmetric channel, capacity is log2 M − H([1−ε, ε/(M−1), …]), but this should be stated explicitly and justified. For SL-UK (unknown skill), the “averaged channel” argument needs a proof that the mixture remains MSC with ε̄, and that capacity equals log2 M − HM(ε̄). For SL-CS (state known at receiver), capacity being log2 M − E[HM(ε)] needs the standard result (maximization over p(u) independent of state) and the symmetry argument.
- Rate–distortion term H(B) − HN(δ) is asserted but not derived; constraints on δ (min{1−pmax, 1−1/N}) should be justified with a citation (e.g., Cover–Thomas, discrete memoryless source with Hamming distortion). Please ensure the exact form (multinomial entropy) and base are consistent.
- kIC analysis: Lemma 1’s expression for “probability of error of a spammer” is opaque, dimensionally suspicious (sums involving i × binomial terms), and lacks derivation. It is unclear whether this is per-item mislabel probability or expected number of mislabeled edges; clarity is needed. The dependence on k and the role of uniform random choice among valid partitions should be made explicit.
- Theorem 4: R < (1/k) ln(1−q)/ln(δ̄S) as a necessary condition: signs/monotonicity and domain constraints are unclear (δ̄S ∈ (0,1), ln(1−q) ≤ 0, so the ratio is positive only if ln(δ̄S) < 0; this should be stated). The link R ↔ R′/k is hand-wavy and uses “nature of the code” without a formal argument. Assumptions of independence across queries and uniform subsampling of items need to be stated and justified.
- Pricing rule π(k1)/π(k2) ≈ k1/k2 appears in the text as “≈ k1 k2” (missing division). In any case, pricing should depend on human time/effort and response set size, not just k; otherwise, it is speculative.

Clarity and organization:
- Numerous grammatical errors, typos, and broken LaTeX undermine readability (e.g., “th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016)” appears mid-paragraph; broken equations; mismatched parentheses; “Wiely”; “Oymak”).
- Figures 1a–c and Figure 2 are referenced but not provided, and Appendices A–E (with the main proofs) are not included. This prevents verification of key claims.
- The paper promises design elaboration in Section 3 but mostly recapitulates standard separation theorems rather than presenting concrete code designs or achievability schemes tailored to crowdsourcing.
- Ambiguity in symbols (N vs M, SL-UK vs SL-CS, E used for both expectation and set of skill levels) pervades the draft.

Positioning and related work:
- The literature on sample complexity/fundamental limits in crowdsourcing is richer than cited (e.g., Karger–Oh–Shah’s series; Shah and Wainwright; Zhang et al. 2014 on spectral-EM; recent information-theoretic analyses of adaptive vs non-adaptive task assignment). A clearer discussion of how the presented bounds compare to known order-wise tight bounds would help.
- Same-cluster queries/k-ary comparisons relate to correlation clustering and active clustering literature; more precise connections would strengthen the contribution.

Empirical/constructive aspects:
- No empirical validation, simulations, or finite-blocklength analysis. Only asymptotic necessary conditions are provided. Even synthetic experiments comparing kIC vs pairwise vs direct labeling under SHC/MSC would improve significance.

Pros
- Clear high-level vision of unifying crowdsourcing budget–fidelity trade-offs via information theory.
- Separation-based bounds that distinguish SL-UK vs SL-CS are conceptually interesting.
- kIC idea is practical and the invalid-response pruning insight is appealing.

Cons
- Severe clarity/notation/formatting issues; missing figures and appendices.
- Key derivations are either standard without precise adaptation to this setting, or nonstandard but insufficiently justified.
- kIC analysis appears error-prone/incomplete; pricing claim is speculative.
- Limited novelty relative to existing sample complexity results; no algorithms or experiments.

Questions for authors
- Provide the full, consistent definitions of MSC parameter ε, HM(·), HN(·), and clarify M vs N throughout. Can you include proofs (or at least sketches) for Theorems 2–3 that properly handle state uncertainty/knowledge?
- Precisely define the error metric for kIC (per-item vs per-edge), and derive Lemma 1 rigorously. Can you validate Theorem 4 with simulations?
- How do your lower bounds compare numerically and order-wise to Karger–Oh–Shah style bounds under similar worker models?
- Can you show an achievable scheme approaching the lower bounds in any special case?

Typos and minor comments (non-exhaustive)
- Eq. (1) is malformed; please fix case structure and variables. Many equations have missing symbols or bases for logarithms/entropies.
- “Wiely” → Wiley. Many references are incomplete (years, venues, pages).
- “π(k1)/π(k2) ≈ k1 k2” likely intended as k1/k2.
- Random lines like “th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016), Barcelona, Spain.” appear mid-text.
- Consistently denote probabilities (P(B(Xn)) vs P(B(X)n)) and expectations (E vs set E).

Recommendation
Reject. While the overarching direction is promising, the submission in its current form contains numerous notational/formatting errors, missing content (figures/appendices), and insufficiently rigorous derivations, especially for the kIC part. A substantially revised version with complete proofs, consistent notation, comparisons to existing limits, and at least some empirical validation could be much stronger.
