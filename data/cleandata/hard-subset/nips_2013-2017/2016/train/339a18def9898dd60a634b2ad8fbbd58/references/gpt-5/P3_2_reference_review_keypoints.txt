- Models crowdsourcing with information theory: workers as discrete memoryless channels, tasks as source symbols, deriving budget–fidelity limits independent of aggregation algorithms.
- Analyzes two worker models: M-ary symmetric channels (MSC) with error-rate distribution and spammer–hammer channel (SHC); two scenarios SL-UK (skill unknown) and SL-CS (skill known).
- Uses source–channel separation to derive lower bounds on queries-per-item R for target error ϵ̂; SL-UK replaces capacity with log M − HM(E[ε]) while SL-CS uses E[log M − HM(ε)], exposing a Jensen gap.
- Introduces k-ary Incidence Coding (kIC) that asks same-cluster partitions of k items, exploiting invalid response patterns for error screening.
- Provides SHC binary-labeling analysis with an oracle decoder and a necessary condition on R as function of k and q; proposes a heuristic pricing rule π(k) ∝ k.
- Connects to Dawid–Skene, EM/spectral methods, budget-optimal task allocation, and pairwise/incidence-query clustering; omits some related work on same-cluster queries and minimax analyses.
- Strength: clear, unifying information-theoretic framework separating source complexity from worker reliability.
- Strength: insightful SL-UK vs SL-CS comparison quantifying value of estimating worker reliabilities.
- Strength: kIC is practically relevant and the paper identifies its inherent invalid-answer filtering benefit.
- Strength: bounds are simple and interpretable (source compressibility over average per-query capacity).
- Weakness: Assumptions and notation need tightening; the R(D) simplification holds only under specific symmetry/constraint conditions and needs justification or citation.
- Weakness: Reliance on infinite-blocklength separation is problematic for typically single-shot/small-block crowdsourcing; finite-sample gaps and achievability schemes are not discussed.
- Weakness: SL-UK justification (averaging channel capacity) is unclear—needs careful treatment of channels with random state and decoder/encoder CSI assumptions.
- Weakness: kIC analysis is narrow (N=2, SHC, non-adaptive uniform sampling, oracle decoder); Theorem 4 is only a loose necessary condition with no achievability/upper bounds.
- Weakness: Claim that each item appears in exactly R′ queries contradicts random sampling unless explicitly enforced.
- Weakness: Pricing rule π(k)∝k is speculative and ignores cognitive load, UI, and platform factors; must be clearly labeled heuristic or empirically justified.
- Weakness: Important practical factors (item difficulty, worker–item interactions like GLAD) are deferred without outlining how they affect converses.
- Weakness: Writing and presentation have numerous formatting/typo issues, missing figures, broken references, and inconsistent notation that hinder verification.
- Quality: Core ideas are conceptually sound but many derivations are deferred and key modeling choices are not justified in the main text.
- Clarity: Exposition requires substantial polishing—overloaded/undefined notation and missing figures impede understanding.
- Originality: Framing is a useful application of separation-based converses to crowdsourcing; kIC angle is interesting but technical novelty is limited by scope.
- Significance: Could be influential if extended (DS/GLAD, adaptive tasking, achievability), but current practical impact is limited by strong assumptions and lack of constructive/empirical results.
- Suggestions: Precisely state assumptions and symbols, provide proof sketches, clarify channel-state/CSI issues, give kIC achievability or simulations, qualify pricing, discuss adaptive/item-difficulty extensions, and fix notation/refs/figures.
- Pros for acceptance: clean information-theoretic framing, insightful SL-UK vs SL-CS comparison, highlights k-ary query benefits.
- Cons for acceptance: narrow technical scope, strong asymptotic assumptions, no constructive algorithms or empirical validation, clarity issues.
- Recommendation: Weak reject pending stronger technical development, clearer exposition, and at least one concrete achievability result or empirical validation.
