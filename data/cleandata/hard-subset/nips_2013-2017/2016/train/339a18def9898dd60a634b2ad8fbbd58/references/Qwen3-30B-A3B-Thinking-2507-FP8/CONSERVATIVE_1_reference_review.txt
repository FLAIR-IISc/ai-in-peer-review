

# Review of "Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing"

This paper proposes an information-theoretic framework for analyzing crowdsourcing systems, modeling crowdsourcing as a communication channel problem with source (dataset), channels (workers), and decoder (crowdsourcer). The authors derive theoretical bounds on the budget-fidelity trade-off (queries per item vs. error probability) under two channel models (M-ary symmetric channel and spammer-hammer channel) and propose a k-ary incidence coding (kIC) scheme.

The paper builds on prior crowdsourcing research that has primarily focused on practical aggregation methods like majority voting and Dawid-Skene (DS) algorithm [5, 8, 9, 10, 12]. While the information-theoretic perspective is theoretically interesting, the paper's contribution is limited by its disconnect from practical crowdsourcing challenges.

**Quality:** The theoretical derivations (Theorems 2 and 3) are technically sound, but the paper lacks empirical validation. The authors derive bounds under highly idealized assumptions (uniform dataset distribution, discrete symmetric channels, workers as either "spammers" or "hammers") that don't reflect real-world crowdsourcing. Crucially, they fail to demonstrate that their theoretical bounds are achievable in practice or that their proposed kIC scheme outperforms established methods like DS or majority voting on real or synthetic data. Without experimental validation, the theoretical claims remain ungrounded. The paper also doesn't properly address the practical costs of different query types (e.g., showing k images vs. a single image), despite mentioning this as an important consideration in Appendix E.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem formulation to theoretical results. The mathematical derivations are presented carefully. However, the paper fails to adequately contextualize its contribution relative to existing crowdsourcing literature. For example, the paper's "fundamental limits" are derived under assumptions that don't match the practical challenges addressed in papers like [1, 6, 8, 13], which have already studied budget-fidelity trade-offs in more realistic settings.

**Originality:** The information-theoretic framing is somewhat novel, but the paper doesn't clearly articulate how it differs from or improves upon existing theoretical work on crowdsourcing. The k-ary incidence coding (kIC) concept is interesting, but the paper doesn't adequately explain why it's superior to existing approaches or how it would be implemented in practice. The theoretical results don't seem to offer new insights for practitioners, as they're derived under assumptions that don't reflect real crowdsourcing dynamics.

**Significance:** The paper's significance is limited by its lack of connection to real-world crowdsourcing challenges. It doesn't address key practical issues like worker motivation, non-stationary skill levels, or the prevalence of spammers in real platforms like AMT. The claimed "fundamental limits" are derived under highly restrictive conditions that don't match the complexity of actual crowdsourcing systems. Without empirical validation showing that the theoretical bounds are achievable or that kIC provides practical benefits, the paper doesn't advance the state of the art in a demonstrable way.

The most significant weakness is the complete absence of empirical results. The paper claims to establish "fundamental limits," but without showing that these limits are achievable or that their proposed kIC scheme outperforms existing methods, the theoretical results remain abstract and disconnected from practical crowdsourcing needs. This is particularly concerning given the paper's claim to provide guidance for "optimized crowdsourcing systems" in the introduction.

The paper also fails to adequately address the practical implementation challenges of kIC. For instance, it doesn't discuss how to select the optimal k value for a given dataset or how to handle cases where the number of clusters N is unknown, despite mentioning this as a consideration in Section 2.1.

**Recommendation:** I recommend rejection. While the theoretical approach is interesting, the paper fails to provide sufficient evidence that the proposed framework offers practical value for crowdsourcing systems. The lack of empirical validation and disconnect from real-world crowdsourcing challenges significantly undermine the paper's contribution. The theoretical bounds are derived under assumptions that don't reflect practical crowdsourcing scenarios, and the paper doesn't demonstrate how these bounds translate to improved performance in real applications.

The authors would need to substantially strengthen the paper by providing empirical validation on real or realistic synthetic datasets, comparing their approach to established methods like DS algorithm, and addressing the practical implementation challenges of their proposed kIC scheme. Without these elements, the paper doesn't meet NIPS' standards for a scientifically rigorous contribution that advances the field.
