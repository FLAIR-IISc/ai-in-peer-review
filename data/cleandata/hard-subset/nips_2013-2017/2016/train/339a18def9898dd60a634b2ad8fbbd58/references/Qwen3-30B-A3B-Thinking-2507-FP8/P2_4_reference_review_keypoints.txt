- Novel framing: applies information theory to crowdsourcing to derive fundamental budget–fidelity limits.  
- Theoretical contributions: rigorous derivation of two main bounds for unknown-skill (SL-UK) and known-skill (SL-CS) worker settings (Theorems 2 and 3).  
- New scheme: introduces k-ary incidence coding (kIC) and provides a lower bound on its performance (Theorem 4).  
- Rigorous proofs: derivations build on source–channel separation and are mathematically sound with detailed appendices.  
- Connection to prior work: situates results relative to Dawid–Skene, majority voting, and information-theoretic literature.  
- Clarity and organization: well-written, consistent notation, clear modeling framework, and helpful figures illustrating kIC.  
- Assumptions: relies on simplified settings (iid datasets, memoryless channels) which limit real-world applicability.  
- Limited experiments: lacks empirical validation of theoretical bounds; a few numerical results would strengthen practical relevance.  
- Practical impact: provides theoretical benchmarks for query/budget design but practical implications are constrained by simplifying assumptions.  
- Originality: takes a distinct, foundational approach (fundamental limits) rather than proposing algorithms, adding a new perspective to crowdsourcing research.  
- Significance: addresses an important question (minimum queries for target fidelity) and offers a framework for future system design.  
- Audience fit: appropriate for NIPS given reliance on information-theoretic concepts and theoretical focus.  
- Recommendation: accept—strong theoretical contribution despite limitations in empirical validation.
