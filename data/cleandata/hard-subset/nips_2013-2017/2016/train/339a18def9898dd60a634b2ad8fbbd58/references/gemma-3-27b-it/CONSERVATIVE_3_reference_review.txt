This paper attempts to establish fundamental limits on the performance of crowdsourcing using an information-theoretic approach, specifically examining the trade-off between budget (number of queries) and fidelity (accuracy). While the motivation is sound – understanding the inherent limitations of crowdsourcing is crucial – the paper suffers from a significant disconnect between the theoretical framework presented and its practical relevance. The extensive use of information-theoretic concepts feels largely detached from the realities of crowdsourcing systems like AMT, and the assumptions made throughout severely limit the applicability of the derived bounds.

The paper begins with a reasonable overview of crowdsourcing, identifying the challenges of worker skill variability and potential for malicious behavior. However, the modeling choices quickly become problematic. Representing the crowd as a set of parallel noisy channels, while mathematically convenient, abstracts away critical aspects of crowdsourcing. The assumption of independence between workers, for example, is questionable given the potential for shared knowledge or coordinated spamming efforts. Furthermore, the focus on binary labeling and simple clustering scenarios feels restrictive. While useful as a starting point, it limits the generalizability of the findings.

The introduction of k-ary incidence coding is a potentially interesting idea, aiming to exploit redundancy for error correction. However, the analysis feels incomplete. The discussion of query pricing, while briefly mentioned, lacks depth and relies on a somewhat hand-wavy justification. The numerical results, while presented, don’t sufficiently bridge the gap between the theoretical bounds and practical performance. A comparison against existing crowdsourcing algorithms (like DS or more recent Bayesian approaches) is entirely absent, making it difficult to assess the practical value of the proposed kIC scheme. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Formalization of the Problem:** The paper attempts a rigorous, information-theoretic analysis of a problem that is often treated heuristically.
*   **Identification of Fundamental Limits:** Establishing theoretical bounds, even if restrictive, can provide valuable insights into the inherent capabilities of crowdsourcing.
*   **Introduction of kIC:** The k-ary incidence coding scheme presents a potentially novel approach to query design.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Oversimplification:** The model is overly simplistic and ignores critical aspects of real-world crowdsourcing platforms.
*   **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper lacks any experimental evaluation on real or simulated crowdsourcing data. The theoretical results are not grounded in practical observations.
*   **Missing Baseline Comparisons:** No comparison is made to existing crowdsourcing algorithms, making it difficult to assess the advantages of the proposed approach.
*   **Limited Scope:** The focus on binary labeling and simple clustering limits the generalizability of the results.
*   **Detached Theory:** The extensive use of information theory doesn’t translate into actionable insights for practitioners. The derived bounds are likely too loose to be useful in practice.
*   **Unclear Practical Implications:** The discussion of query pricing feels superficial and lacks concrete guidance.

**Arguments for and against acceptance:**

**Against:**

*   The paper presents largely theoretical results with limited practical relevance.
*   The model is overly simplistic and makes unrealistic assumptions.
*   The lack of empirical validation and baseline comparisons is a significant weakness.
*   The paper doesn’t offer a substantial advancement over existing knowledge in crowdsourcing.

**For:**

*   The formalization of the problem using information theory is a novel approach.
*   The identification of fundamental limits, however restrictive, is a valuable contribution.
*   The k-ary incidence coding scheme shows some promise, although it needs further investigation.

**Overall Assessment:**

I am inclined to **reject** this paper. While the authors attempt a rigorous analysis of crowdsourcing, the theoretical framework feels too disconnected from reality. The lack of empirical validation and comparison to existing methods leaves the reader unconvinced of the practical value of the proposed approach. The paper reads more like a preliminary investigation of an information-theoretic model than a substantial contribution to the field of crowdsourcing. The derived bounds are likely too loose to be useful in practice, and the kIC scheme requires much more thorough evaluation before its potential can be assessed. The paper, as it stands, doesn’t meet the NIPS standards for originality, significance, or practical impact. I suggest the authors focus on validating their theoretical findings with extensive simulations and comparisons to existing crowdsourcing algorithms before resubmitting.
