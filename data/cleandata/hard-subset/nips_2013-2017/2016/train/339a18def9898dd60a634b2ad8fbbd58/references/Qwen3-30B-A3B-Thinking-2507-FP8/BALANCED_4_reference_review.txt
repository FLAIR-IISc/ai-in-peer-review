

# Review of "Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing"

This paper presents an information-theoretic framework for analyzing crowdsourcing systems, modeling crowdsourcing as a communication channel problem to derive fundamental limits on the budget-fidelity trade-off. The authors consider two scenarios: when worker skill levels are unknown (SL-UK) and when they are perfectly known (SL-CS), and derive theoretical bounds for these cases. They also analyze a specific query scheme called k-ary incidence coding (kIC) and provide performance bounds for it.

## Strengths

1. **Novel theoretical perspective**: The paper makes a significant contribution by applying information theory to crowdsourcing, a novel approach compared to most existing work that focuses on algorithmic improvements. This provides a fresh theoretical foundation for understanding crowdsourcing systems.

2. **Sound theoretical derivations**: The paper presents rigorous information-theoretic derivations for the fundamental limits, with proofs provided in appendices. Theorems 2 and 3 establish clear bounds for the budget-fidelity trade-off, which is a valuable theoretical contribution.

3. **Clear distinction from prior work**: The paper effectively differentiates itself from previous crowdsourcing literature (e.g., [5, 8, 12]) by focusing on fundamental limits rather than specific algorithms, and provides appropriate citations to related work.

4. **Interesting analysis of kIC**: The analysis of k-ary incidence coding provides concrete insights into how query design affects performance, with Theorem 4 establishing meaningful performance bounds for this specific scheme.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited practical relevance**: While the theoretical framework is sound, the paper lacks concrete examples of how these bounds would be used in practice. The connection between theoretical results and real-world crowdsourcing system design is tenuous, making it difficult to assess the practical impact of the work.

2. **Overly simplified assumptions**: The paper assumes a large dataset (L → ∞) and fixed worker skill distributions, which may not reflect real-world crowdsourcing scenarios where datasets are finite and worker skill levels can vary dynamically.

3. **Limited numerical validation**: The numerical results (Figure 2) are minimal and don't adequately compare the theoretical bounds with practical crowdsourcing algorithms. The paper would benefit from more extensive numerical experiments showing how the bounds relate to real-world performance.

4. **Insufficient discussion of limitations**: The paper doesn't adequately address how the theoretical results might change in more complex scenarios (e.g., with adaptive query schemes, worker feedback, or varying skill levels over time), which are common in practical crowdsourcing.

5. **Underdeveloped pricing strategy**: The brief mention of query pricing (Appendix E) is not sufficiently developed, lacking concrete examples or justification for the proposed rule of thumb.

## Specific Concerns

1. The paper uses Hamming distortion as the primary metric, but doesn't adequately explain how this maps to practical crowdsourcing metrics. In many real-world scenarios, the distortion metric would be more complex than simple Hamming distance.

2. The claim that kIC provides "error correction capability" is not fully substantiated with empirical evidence or detailed explanation of how this works in practice.

3. The paper doesn't address how the theoretical limits would change if the number of clusters (N) is unknown, which is a common practical scenario in crowdsourcing.

## Suggestions for Improvement

1. Include more concrete examples demonstrating how the theoretical bounds could be used to design practical crowdsourcing systems. For instance, provide a case study showing how a crowdsourcer might use these bounds to determine query budget allocation.

2. Extend the analysis to finite dataset sizes to better reflect practical scenarios. This would make the results more applicable to real-world crowdsourcing projects.

3. Add more numerical experiments comparing the theoretical bounds with practical crowdsourcing algorithms (e.g., majority voting, Dawid-Skene), showing where the theoretical limits are achievable and where there's a gap.

4. Provide a more detailed discussion of the practical implications of the results, including how the bounds might change in more complex real-world scenarios.

5. Expand the discussion of the pricing strategy with concrete examples and justification, showing how the proposed rule of thumb would work in practice.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution by establishing information-theoretic bounds for crowdsourcing systems. The framework is novel and the derivations are sound, providing a useful foundation for understanding fundamental performance limits. However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by better connecting these theoretical results to practical crowdsourcing applications, providing more extensive numerical validation, and addressing the limitations of the assumptions.

While the theoretical contribution is strong, the limited discussion of practical implications and the lack of empirical validation reduce the paper's significance for the NeurIPS community. With the suggested improvements, particularly in demonstrating practical relevance and adding numerical validation, this paper would be a strong candidate for acceptance.

**Decision**: Weak Reject. The paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution that is well-executed, but the limited practical relevance and lack of empirical validation prevent it from meeting NeurIPS's standard for significance. The authors should address the weaknesses outlined above before resubmission.
