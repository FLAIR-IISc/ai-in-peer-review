This paper attempts to establish fundamental limits on the performance of crowdsourcing via an information-theoretic approach, modeling the problem as a communication system with noisy channels representing workers. While the motivation is sound – understanding the inherent limitations of crowdsourcing is crucial – the paper suffers from a significant disconnect between the theoretical framework presented and its practical relevance, and lacks sufficient grounding in existing crowdsourcing literature beyond basic references.

The modeling of crowdsourcing as a communication system is a reasonable starting point, and the investigation of two scenarios – unknown vs. known worker skill levels – is logical. However, the assumptions underpinning the analysis, particularly the idealized channel models (MSC and SHC) and the focus on Hamming distortion, feel overly simplistic and divorced from the complexities of real-world crowdsourcing scenarios. While these models provide a tractable analytical framework, their applicability is questionable. For instance, the SHC model, while mentioned in prior work [8], feels contrived and doesn’t capture the nuances of worker behavior – most workers aren’t simply spammers or perfect responders. The discussion of k-ary incidence coding is interesting, but the leap to a query pricing strategy in the appendix feels underdeveloped and unjustified.

The theoretical results (Theorems 2 & 3) provide fundamental limits, but these limits are expressed in terms of information-theoretic quantities (e.g., entropy) that are difficult to interpret in a practical crowdsourcing setting. The paper doesn’t adequately translate these theoretical bounds into concrete guidelines for system design. Furthermore, the numerical results (Figure 2) largely serve to confirm the theoretical bounds, without providing insights into the achievable performance of *realistic* crowdsourcing algorithms. The comparison to the bound from [8] is brief and lacks depth.

**Strengths:**

*   The attempt to apply information theory to crowdsourcing is a novel direction.
*   The formulation of the problem as a communication system provides a structured framework for analysis.
*   The consideration of both unknown and known worker skill levels is a reasonable approach.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Overly simplistic and idealized models that lack real-world grounding. The MSC and SHC channel models, while mathematically convenient, do not fully capture the complexities of human worker behavior.
*   Limited connection to existing crowdsourcing literature beyond basic references on aggregation and inference schemes. The paper fails to adequately discuss how the theoretical results relate to – and potentially improve upon – existing practical approaches like Dawid-Skene or majority voting, beyond stating they are suboptimal.
*   Theoretical results are difficult to interpret in a practical setting and lack concrete design guidelines.
*   The analysis feels incomplete. The discussion of query pricing is cursory.
*   The assumption of i.i.d. data source is overly restrictive.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel application of information theory to a relevant problem.
*   The theoretical framework is mathematically sound.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The idealized assumptions significantly limit the practical relevance of the results.
*   The lack of connection to existing crowdsourcing literature and practical algorithms weakens the contribution.
*   The paper doesn’t provide concrete guidance for system design or implementation.
*   The paper feels more like a theoretical exploration than a complete piece of work.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper, while presenting a theoretically sound approach, falls short of making a significant contribution to the field due to its limited practical relevance. The idealized assumptions and lack of connection to existing crowdsourcing algorithms prevent the results from being readily applicable to real-world problems. The paper leans heavily towards theoretical exploration and lacks the empirical validation or concrete design guidelines expected of a NIPS publication. 

**Recommendation:** Reject. The paper requires substantial revisions, including a more realistic modeling of worker behavior, a more thorough comparison to existing methods, and a stronger emphasis on translating the theoretical results into practical guidelines. I am skeptical that these revisions can sufficiently address the fundamental limitations of the current work.
