- Proposes an information-theoretic framework modeling crowdsourcing as communication channels and derives budget–fidelity limits for unknown and known worker-skill scenarios (SL-UK, SL-CS).
- Introduces k-ary incidence coding (kIC), a novel query scheme that asks workers to compare multiple items to exploit error-correction capabilities.
- Provides strong theoretical results (Theorems 2, 3; Lemma 1; Theorem 4) with proofs, showing kIC can approach the derived limits.
- Lacks empirical validation: numerical results are theoretical only and the paper would benefit from experiments on real crowdsourcing datasets.
- Well-organized and clearly written overall, but the information-theoretic channel model may challenge readers without that background.
- Exposition of kIC and the transition from theory to kIC analysis could be clearer—recommend adding a concrete example and smoother transitions.
- Original contribution: shifts crowdsourcing research from algorithmic methods to fundamental limits, a novel perspective for the field.
- kIC is an innovative application of coding-theory principles to reduce worker error via multi-item comparisons.
- Significant practical implications: distinguishes SL-UK vs. SL-CS for worker selection and proposes a pricing rule of thumb (π(k1)/π(k2) ≈ k1k2).
- Overall assessment: strong theoretical paper deserving acceptance despite minor weaknesses.
- Suggested minor revisions: include an intuitive kIC example, discuss how results scale with dataset size, and add a small empirical validation.
