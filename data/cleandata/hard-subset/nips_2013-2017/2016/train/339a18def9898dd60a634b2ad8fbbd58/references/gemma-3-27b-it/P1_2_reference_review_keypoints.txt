- Overall: The paper offers rigorous information-theoretic bounds on the budget–fidelity trade-off in crowdsourcing and is a candidate for weak accept with major revisions.  
- Framing: Models crowdsourcing as a communication problem with workers as noisy channels, applying rate-distortion and channel capacity concepts.  
- Contribution: Derives fundamental limits for both unknown and known worker skill scenarios.  
- Novelty: Introduces k-ary incidence coding (kIC) as a new query scheme for potential cost–robustness trade-offs.  
- Rigor: Mathematical derivations are clear and well-presented for readers with an information-theory background.  
- Realism: Uses a spammer–hammer worker model which is a useful simplification but may not capture all worker behaviors.  
- Assumptions: Relies on strong assumptions (i.i.d. data, memoryless channels, perfect channel estimation) that limit practical applicability.  
- Empirical validation: Lacks simulations or real-data experiments to demonstrate the practical relevance of the theoretical bounds.  
- Query cost: Acknowledges differing query costs but does not fully integrate cost differences into the analysis.  
- kIC clarity: Practical benefits, cost–complexity trade-offs, and implementation implications of kIC are not sufficiently clarified.  
- Related work: Insufficiently connects theoretical bounds to the performance of existing algorithms like Dawid–Skene or adaptive querying.  
- Section 3.1: Joint source–channel coding discussion is abstract and needs a concrete crowdsourcing example.  
- Theorems 2 & 3: Require more intuition on how bounds scale with dataset size, worker count, and error tolerance.  
- Section 4.2: Numerical results need more detailed explanation of parameter settings and implications.  
- kIC pricing: The pricing rule-of-thumb in Appendix E appears ad-hoc and needs more rigorous justification.  
- Future work: Lists extensions (feedback, variable difficulty) but should prioritize them by impact.  
- Pro-accept: Offers novel, rigorous theoretical insights and a promising new query scheme.  
- Con-accept: Strong assumptions, lack of empirical support, and limited guidance for practical system design weigh against acceptance.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept conditional on major revisions including empirical validation, practical implications, comparisons to existing algorithms, expanded kIC analysis, and a clearer pricing model.
