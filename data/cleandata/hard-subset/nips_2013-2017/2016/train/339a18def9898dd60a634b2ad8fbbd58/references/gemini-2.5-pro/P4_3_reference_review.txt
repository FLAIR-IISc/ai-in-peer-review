### Summary

This paper frames the problem of label crowdsourcing within an information-theoretic communication framework to derive fundamental limits on the trade-off between budget and fidelity. The core idea is to model the set of true labels as a source, the crowd workers as a collection of parallel noisy channels, and the querying and inference processes as a joint source-channel coding scheme. The budget is equated with the code rate (queries per item), and fidelity is measured by the average distortion (e.g., probability of error).

The authors derive information-theoretic lower bounds on the minimum rate required to achieve a target distortion level. These bounds are presented for two key scenarios: 1) when worker skill levels are unknown to the system (SL-UK), and 2) when skill levels are perfectly known to the crowdsourcer/decoder (SL-CS). The analysis, based on classic rate-distortion and channel capacity results, shows that knowing worker skills allows the system to achieve performance corresponding to the average of worker capacities, whereas ignorance of skill levels degrades performance to that of an average worker's capacity, a strictly worse outcome.

Furthermore, the paper introduces and analyzes a specific query scheme called *k-ary Incidence Coding* (kIC), where workers are asked to judge the cluster relationships of *k* items simultaneously. A performance lower bound for kIC is derived under a spammer-hammer worker model. This work distinguishes itself from prior research on crowdsourcing algorithms (e.g., Dawid & Skene, Karger et al. [8]), which focuses on the performance of specific inference methods, by aiming to establish ultimate, algorithm-agnostic performance benchmarks.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

#### Quality

The paper is of high technical quality. The application of information theory to the crowdsourcing problem is executed rigorously. The derivations for the main theoretical results (Theorems 2 and 3) correctly apply fundamental concepts from joint source-channel coding theory to the proposed crowdsourcing model. The modeling assumptions, such as the M-ary Symmetric Channel (MSC) and Spammer-Hammer Channel (SHC) for workers, are standard in the literature and facilitate a clean, analytical treatment. The distinction between the SL-UK and SL-CS scenarios is insightful and leads to a clear, quantifiable conclusion about the value of estimating worker expertise.

However, the analysis has some limitations. The primary results are asymptotic, holding for large datasets and coding block lengths, which is a standard assumption in information theory but may limit their direct applicability to finite, practical crowdsourcing tasks. The analysis of kIC in Theorem 4 is for a specific oracle decoder that perfectly identifies worker types, which provides a lower bound on the required rate for this specific setup, but not necessarily the tightest possible bound for the kIC scheme in general.

#### Clarity

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The analogy between a crowdsourcing system and a communication system is compelling and well-articulated in Section 2 and Figure 1. This framework provides an intuitive lens for understanding the problem. The theorems are stated precisely, and their implications are discussed.

A point of minor confusion is the switch between the MSC model used for the general bounds in Theorems 2 and 3, and the SHC model used for the analysis of kIC in Section 4. While both are valid channel models, the motivation for using different models in different sections is not explicitly justified, making a direct comparison of the results slightly less straightforward. Additionally, the critical issue of query cost for kIC is relegated to an appendix. Since a k-ary query is cognitively more complex than a simple binary query, its cost is central to the "budget-fidelity" trade-off and would benefit from a more prominent discussion in the main text.

#### Originality

The originality of this work is its primary strength. Framing the crowdsourcing problem in the language of information theory is a novel and powerful contribution. While prior work has focused on designing and analyzing specific algorithms for aggregation or task assignment, this paper elevates the discussion to the level of fundamental limits. This new perspective has the potential to unify disparate results and provide a common benchmark for the entire field. The introduction of kIC as a concrete example of a coding scheme is also a novel, though less developed, contribution.

#### Significance

The paper's results are significant from a theoretical standpoint. The derived bounds establish the best possible performance any crowdsourcing system can achieve, given a specific worker pool and budget. This provides a valuable "gold standard" against which practical algorithms can be measured, helping researchers understand how far their methods are from optimal. The quantitative result showing the performance gain from knowing worker skills (Theorem 3 vs. Theorem 2) provides a firm theoretical justification for the large body of work focused on worker skill estimation.

The practical significance is less immediate. The work is purely theoretical and lacks any experimental validation, even with synthetic data. It would have been highly beneficial to show how the performance of existing algorithms (e.g., majority voting, Dawid-Skene EM) compares to the derived fundamental limits under various conditions. This would help bridge the gap between theory and practice and demonstrate the tightness (or looseness) of the bounds in realistic settings.

### Arguments for Acceptance

1.  **Novel Framework:** The information-theoretic formulation of the crowdsourcing problem is highly original and provides a new and principled perspective on the fundamental trade-offs involved.
2.  **Fundamental Limits:** The paper provides the first (to my knowledge) algorithm-agnostic performance bounds for crowdsourcing, offering a valuable benchmark for the community.
3.  **Key Insights:** The theoretical results yield clear, interpretable insights, such as quantifying the performance benefit of accurately estimating worker skill levels.
4.  **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical claims are well-supported by rigorous proofs leveraging established tools from information theory.

### Arguments Against Acceptance

1.  **Limited Practical Connection:** The results are asymptotic and the paper lacks any empirical validation or simulation to connect the theoretical bounds to the performance of real-world algorithms.
2.  **Incomplete Analysis of kIC:** The discussion of kIC, a central practical example, feels incomplete. The performance bound is for a specific decoder, and the crucial aspect of query cost is not fully integrated into the main analysis.
3.  **Unjustified Modeling Choices:** The paper switches between MSC and SHC worker models for different analyses without clear justification, which could be confusing for the reader.

Overall, the paper presents a highly novel and theoretically significant contribution. Its weaknesses are primarily related to a lack of connection to practice and a less-developed analysis of the proposed kIC scheme, rather than fundamental flaws in the core theoretical results. The conceptual contribution of the information-theoretic framework is strong enough to warrant acceptance.
