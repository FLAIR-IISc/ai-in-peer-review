This paper tackles a fundamental, and often overlooked, problem in crowdsourcing: the inherent trade-off between budget (number of queries) and fidelity (accuracy of the result). The authors model crowdsourcing as a communication system and use information-theoretic tools to derive lower bounds on the required budget for a given fidelity. The introduction of k-ary incidence coding is a particularly interesting and potentially impactful contribution. While the paper is dense and could benefit from more intuitive explanations, the core idea – applying information theory to rigorously analyze crowdsourcing performance – is highly novel and presents a significant step forward.

**Summary:** The authors present an information-theoretic analysis of crowdsourcing, deriving fundamental limits on the budget-fidelity trade-off. They model workers as noisy channels and explore the performance of k-ary incidence coding, demonstrating potential error correction capabilities. The work considers scenarios with both unknown and perfectly known worker skill levels.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The application of information theory to analyze crowdsourcing is a genuinely novel approach. Most existing work focuses on algorithmic improvements to aggregation or worker selection; this paper steps back and asks *what is fundamentally possible*.
* **Rigorous Analysis:** The use of information-theoretic bounds provides a strong theoretical foundation. Theorems 2, 3, and 4 establish clear limits on performance.
* **k-ary Incidence Coding:** The introduction and preliminary analysis of kIC is a compelling idea. The potential for error correction and improved efficiency is promising.
* **Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the fundamental problem of balancing budget and fidelity in crowdsourcing.
* **Addresses Important Practical Concerns:** The final discussion connecting the theoretical bounds to query pricing is a nice touch, demonstrating potential real-world applicability.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Accessibility:** The paper is highly technical and mathematically dense. The presentation could be improved with more intuitive explanations of the concepts and results. A reader unfamiliar with information theory might struggle to follow the derivations.
* **Limited Empirical Validation:** While the theoretical bounds are strong, there is a lack of empirical validation. Demonstrating the performance of kIC on real-world crowdsourcing platforms (e.g., AMT) would significantly strengthen the paper. A simulation study, even with simplified worker models, would be valuable.
* **Assumptions:** The analysis relies on several assumptions, such as i.i.d. data and specific channel models (MSC, SHC). The sensitivity of the results to these assumptions is not fully explored.
* **Practicality of kIC:** The authors briefly touch upon the complexities of implementing kIC in practice. Further discussion of the challenges and potential solutions (e.g., handling subjective tasks, designing appropriate queries) would be beneficial.
* **Limited discussion of feedback:** The paper predominantly focuses on non-adaptive codes. Briefly discussing the potential benefits of adaptive coding schemes would enrich the analysis.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a fundamentally new perspective on crowdsourcing, moving beyond algorithmic tweaks to a rigorous information-theoretic analysis.
* The derived bounds are valuable for understanding the inherent limits of crowdsourcing performance.
* The introduction of k-ary incidence coding offers a promising new approach to query design.
* The potential impact on the field is high, as it could lead to more efficient and reliable crowdsourcing systems.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The paper is highly theoretical and lacks strong empirical validation.
* The complex mathematical presentation might limit its accessibility to a broader audience.
* The practicality of kIC and the sensitivity of the results to underlying assumptions are not fully addressed.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the weaknesses, I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field. The novelty and theoretical rigor outweigh the lack of extensive empirical validation. The paper introduces a compelling new framework for analyzing crowdsourcing, and the potential impact of k-ary incidence coding is substantial. I recommend **acceptance**, with the caveat that the authors should consider addressing the accessibility and empirical validation concerns in future work. A minor revision request would be appropriate, encouraging more intuitive explanations and a discussion of potential experimental validation strategies. This paper embodies the spirit of NIPS by presenting a bold and innovative approach to a challenging problem.
