- Proposes an information-theoretic framework for budget–fidelity trade-offs in crowdsourcing, modeling workers as noisy channels and queries/inference as joint source–channel coding.  
- Considers two worker models: M-ary symmetric channels (MSC) with heterogeneous error rates, and spammer–hammer channel (SHC).  
- Analyzes two scenarios: SL-UK (worker skills unknown to crowdsourcer) and SL-CS (skills known to crowdsourcer), yielding different capacity expressions (averaged channel vs. average of capacities).  
- Uses source–channel separation and rate–distortion (Hamming distortion) to derive necessary budget (queries per item) lower bounds for target error.  
- Introduces k-ary incidence coding (kIC), a constrained query scheme argued to have “error-correction” via invalid response patterns.  
- For SHC, derives spammer error under kIC and a converse: R ≥ (1/k) ln(ε/εS)/ln(1−q) for non-adaptive kIC with an oracle decoder.  
- Provides SL-CS corollary for SHC: benchmark R ≥ (1 − H_b(ε))/(q log M).  
- Separation-based lower bounds are technically sound and consistent with standard information-theory results.  
- Much of the theory repackages textbook results (Cover & Thomas; El Gamal & Kim); novelty is mainly in applying them to crowdsourcing.  
- kIC converse is simple and intuitive (counts items never seen by a hammer times spammer error) but not deep.  
- Proofs are deferred to missing appendices; some definitions (e.g., cost model, queries-per-item mapping) are not explicit.  
- Claims of kIC “error-correction” lack achievability constructions or constructive decoders approaching the bounds.  
- No experiments or empirical validation are provided; figures and appendices are missing in the draft.  
- Presentation issues: notational ambiguities (M vs N), typos, incomplete references, and unclear quantities (R′, item-inclusion relation).  
- Overlong Shannon theory overview that could be shortened to focus on contributions.  
- Limited originality relative to prior IT-style crowdsourcing/sample-complexity literature; needs better positioning against related work.  
- Potential practical value in helping practitioners assess feasibility and in illustrating benefit of estimating worker reliability (SL-UK vs SL-CS).  
- Practical impact limited by absence of achievability results, algorithms, or experimental comparisons to standard methods (majority vote, DS, EM).  
- Suggestions: include proofs or clear converse labeling, define cost mapping for kIC, add simulations/algorithms to show achievability, and improve positioning and notation.  
- Pros: principled IT framework, correct SL-UK vs SL-CS comparison, simple interpretable kIC converse.  
- Cons: limited novelty, no achievability/algorithms/experiments, presentation gaps, and unsubstantiated pricing/error-correction claims.  
- Recommendation: Reject (borderline if substantially revised with proofs and experiments).
