This paper attempts to establish fundamental limits on the budget-fidelity tradeoff in label crowdsourcing using an information-theoretic approach. The core idea is to model crowdsourcing as a communication system, deriving bounds on the necessary query rate (budget) to achieve a given level of fidelity (accuracy). The authors consider both scenarios: unknown worker skill levels (SL-UK) and perfectly known skill levels (SL-CS), and investigate a specific query scheme—k-ary incidence coding (kIC).

While the application of information theory to crowdsourcing is an interesting direction, this paper suffers from a significant disconnect between theoretical rigor and practical relevance. The modeling choices, particularly the assumption of a large number of workers and idealized channel models (MSC and SHC), feel detached from the realities of platforms like AMT. The extensive use of asymptotic analysis (L → ∞) further diminishes the practical applicability of the derived bounds. The kIC scheme, while mathematically analyzed, doesn't appear convincingly superior to simpler, well-established aggregation methods like Dawid-Skene or even majority voting in realistic scenarios. 

The paper's strengths lie in the mathematical formulation and derivation of bounds, demonstrating a solid understanding of information theory. Theorems 2 and 3 provide theoretical limits, and the analysis of kIC offers some insight into potential coding strategies. However, these strengths are overshadowed by several weaknesses. First, the paper lacks empirical validation. The authors present theoretical bounds but do not evaluate them against existing crowdsourcing algorithms on real or simulated data. Without such validation, the practical significance of these bounds remains unclear. Second, the assumed channel models (MSC and SHC) are overly simplistic. Real-world worker performance is far more complex than these models capture. Third, the analysis relies heavily on asymptotic assumptions, making the results less useful for finite-sized datasets, which are typical in practice. Fourth, the discussion of query pricing (in the context of kIC) feels tacked on and lacks a detailed cost analysis. The claim of a rule-of-thumb pricing based on k1 and k2 seems unsubstantiated. Finally, while the related work section is comprehensive, it doesn't adequately discuss the limitations of the proposed approach compared to existing, well-performing methods.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of information theory to crowdsourcing.
*   Mathematically sound derivation of theoretical bounds.
*   Exploration of a novel query scheme (kIC).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of empirical validation.
*   Oversimplified channel models and unrealistic assumptions.
*   Limited practical relevance due to asymptotic analysis.
*   Insufficient comparison to established crowdsourcing methods.
*   Weak justification for the proposed query pricing strategy.

**Overall Assessment:**

I find this paper to be technically sound but lacking in practical significance. The theoretical framework is interesting, but the absence of empirical validation and the reliance on unrealistic assumptions weaken its contribution. The bounds derived are unlikely to be useful in real-world crowdsourcing applications without significant modification and calibration. While the application of information theory is commendable, the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that this approach offers a substantial advantage over existing methods. Therefore, I recommend **rejection**. The paper needs significant revisions, including extensive experimental evaluation and a more nuanced discussion of its limitations, before it can be considered a valuable contribution to the field. It feels like a preliminary exploration of an idea rather than a mature research contribution.
