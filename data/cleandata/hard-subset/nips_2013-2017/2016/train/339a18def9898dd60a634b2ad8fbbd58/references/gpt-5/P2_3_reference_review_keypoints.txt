- Proposes an information-theoretic framework treating workers as noisy channels and crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding problem.
- Main contributions: lower bounds on queries per item for unknown vs known worker skills (SL-UK vs SL-CS), k-ary incidence coding (kIC) converse under the spammer–hammer channel (SHC), and a heuristic pricing implication for kIC.
- The separation-based approach yields clean necessary R ≥ R*(D)/C-type inequalities and formalizes that knowing worker reliabilities increases effective capacity.
- kIC analysis under SHC provides a converse bound with an oracle decoder and a closed-form spammer error expression.
- Technical presentation issues prevent verification: key expressions are hard to parse and potentially inconsistent (undefined HM, overloaded symbols M/N, unclear U/V roles).
- Theorems 2/3 contain an unclear condition on ε̂ using min{1 − pmax, 1 − 1/N} with no interpretation or tightness discussion.
- The kIC lemma has notation/typo errors (e.g., ε̄S, misplaced k^4 factor, bracket problems), hindering reproducibility.
- The pricing rule is typeset ambiguously (π(k1) π(k2) ≈ k1k2) and likely intends a ratio (π(k1)/π(k2) ≈ k1/k2) but lacks justification.
- Main text lacks proofs or proof sketches; everything is deferred to appendices, reducing accessibility and intuition.
- No achievability constructions or empirical simulations are provided to show how algorithms approach the derived bounds.
- kIC converse is narrowly scoped to binary labels (N=2), SHC, and an oracle decoder; practical decoders and broader settings are not analyzed.
- Manuscript has numerous typographical and formatting issues, missing/undefined notation, and poorly formatted references, which hinder understanding.
- Definitions and notation for alphabets, rates, distortion, and worker models need tightening and consistent use of M vs N.
- The connection between the general separation statement and specific MSC/SHC models (e.g., effective channel per query under kIC) should be made explicit.
- The framing of casting crowdsourcing as source–channel coding is original and conceptually appealing, and the SL-UK vs SL-CS contrast is useful.
- Paper should better position itself relative to prior theoretical crowdsourcing limits (e.g., Karger–Oh–Shah, spectral+EM results) and clarify comparisons.
- Potential significance is high if accompanied by achievability or empirical validation; as-is impact is limited by presentation and narrow empirical scope.
- Pros: clean conceptual framework, general converses for MSC, analysis illustrating kIC’s error-correction flavor, potential unifying benchmark.
- Cons: clarity/notation problems, no main-text proofs or validation, limited kIC applicability, incomplete related-work positioning, many typos.
- Suggestions: rigorously fix notation, include proof sketches/intuitions, add numeric instantiations and simulations, provide achievability or practical decoder analysis, and broaden kIC results or discuss oracle gap.
- Recommendation: lean reject in current form; promising concept but needs substantial polishing, clearer statements, and empirical or constructive support.
- Reviewer confidence: high on clarity/positioning assessment; moderate on technical correctness due to presentation errors.
