This paper tackles a fundamental and important problem: understanding the trade-offs between budget and fidelity in crowdsourcing. The authors model crowdsourcing as a communication system and derive information-theoretic limits on performance, considering both scenarios where worker skill is known and unknown. The introduction of k-ary incidence coding as a potential query scheme is a particularly interesting idea, offering a potential pathway to improved efficiency. This is a bold approach, framing a practical problem within a rigorous theoretical framework – exactly the kind of work NIPS should showcase.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The information-theoretic treatment of crowdsourcing is a significant departure from the typical empirical analyses dominating the field. It provides a fundamental understanding of what’s achievable, independent of specific algorithms.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The mathematical formulation and derivation of bounds are solid. The theorems presented are clear and well-defined.
* **Interesting Coding Scheme:** k-ary incidence coding offers a potentially practical way to improve efficiency, and the analysis of its performance is a valuable contribution.
* **Clear Problem Definition:** The authors precisely define the problem, the assumptions, and the metrics used for evaluation.
* **Potential for Impact:** Establishing fundamental limits provides a benchmark for evaluating existing and future crowdsourcing algorithms and systems. The connection to query pricing is also a pragmatic and welcome addition.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Empirical Validation:** While the theoretical results are strong, the paper lacks any empirical validation of the k-ary incidence coding scheme. It’s unclear how well it performs in practice, given real-world worker behavior and noise characteristics. The theoretical benefit might be offset by implementation complexities.
* **Assumptions:** The model relies on several assumptions (e.g., i.i.d. source, discrete memoryless channels) that might not hold in realistic crowdsourcing scenarios. The robustness of the bounds to deviations from these assumptions is not discussed. Specifically, the assumption of a uniform source distribution is a strong one.
* **Practicality of kIC:** The description of kIC focuses heavily on the theoretical advantage.  A more thorough discussion of the practical limitations (e.g., the cognitive load on workers for higher *k* values) would be beneficial.
* **Limited Discussion of Other Query Schemes:** While kIC is a compelling idea, the paper doesn’t delve deeply into a comparison with other potential query schemes beyond mentioning existing methods briefly.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a genuinely novel and theoretically sound approach to a significant problem.
* The information-theoretic bounds provide a valuable benchmark for the field.
* The k-ary incidence coding scheme is a promising direction for future research.
* The clarity of the presentation allows for easy understanding of complex concepts.
* The potential impact on the design and analysis of crowdsourcing systems is substantial.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of empirical validation is a significant drawback.
* The strong assumptions might limit the applicability of the results.
* The limited discussion of practical considerations and alternative approaches weakens the overall impact.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the lack of empirical validation and some simplifying assumptions, I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field. The novelty and theoretical depth of the work are compelling, and the established bounds are likely to guide future research. The k-ary incidence coding scheme, while needing further investigation, is a genuinely interesting idea.  I am willing to overlook the lack of experiments given the strength of the theoretical contribution.  

**Recommendation:** Accept. I encourage the authors to prioritize empirical validation and a more in-depth discussion of practical considerations in future work.  A brief discussion of how worker incentives might affect the channel model would also be valuable.
