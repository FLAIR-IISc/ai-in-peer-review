

# Review of "Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing"

This paper attempts to analyze crowdsourcing from an information-theoretic perspective, modeling it as a communication system with a source (dataset), channels (workers), and coding (query schemes). The authors derive theoretical bounds for the minimum number of queries per item needed to achieve a target error probability in two scenarios: when worker skill levels are unknown (SL-UK) and when they are known (SL-CS). They also analyze a query scheme called k-ary incidence coding (kIC) and provide theoretical performance bounds.

The paper's main contribution is the derivation of information-theoretic bounds (Theorems 2 and 3) that claim to represent fundamental limits for crowdsourcing. The authors also introduce kIC as a query scheme with potential error-correction capabilities, analyzing its performance theoretically.

**Strengths:** The paper provides a clear and mathematically rigorous framework for analyzing crowdsourcing through information theory. The theoretical development is sound, and the paper properly cites relevant prior work in crowdsourcing (e.g., Dawid-Skene algorithm, majority voting). The connection to information theory is interesting and potentially valuable for understanding fundamental limits.

**Weaknesses:** The most significant weakness is the lack of empirical validation. The paper makes strong claims about fundamental limits and the superiority of kIC, but provides no experimental results to support these claims. The authors derive theoretical bounds (Theorems 2 and 3) but fail to demonstrate how these bounds compare to practical crowdsourcing methods like majority voting or Dawid-Skene (DS) algorithm, which are standard baselines in the field. Figure 2 (mentioned in the paper) is supposed to show comparisons between theoretical bounds and kIC performance, but without seeing the figure, it's impossible to assess whether these comparisons are meaningful.

The paper also fails to address how their theoretical framework translates to practical crowdsourcing systems. For instance, the paper claims kIC has error-correction capabilities, but doesn't provide evidence that this translates to better performance in real-world scenarios with imperfect workers. The paper's claim that "the CS system provides an overall work quality that pertains to the average of the work quality of the workers" (when skill levels are known) remains theoretical without empirical backing.

The authors present a query pricing strategy (π(k1)/π(k2) ≈ k1/k2) but provide no justification for why this pricing rule would be effective in practice or how it would compare to existing pricing strategies. Without experimental validation, this claim remains unsubstantiated.

**Comparison to Established Methods:** The paper makes no comparison to established crowdsourcing methods in practice. In the introduction, the authors correctly note that majority voting and Dawid-Skene are standard approaches, but they don't show how their theoretical bounds compare to these methods' actual performance in real crowdsourcing scenarios. This is a critical omission, as the value of theoretical bounds depends on their relevance to practical implementations.

**Originality:** While the information-theoretic approach is novel for crowdsourcing analysis, the paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate its contribution from prior work. The authors cite [8] which discusses similar concepts, but don't clearly articulate how their work advances beyond this prior research. The kIC scheme is presented as a new approach, but without evidence of its practical superiority, it's hard to assess its novelty.

**Significance:** The paper's significance is limited by the lack of empirical validation. Fundamental limits are only meaningful if they're both achievable and practically relevant. The paper fails to demonstrate that the theoretical bounds it derives are achievable in practice or that they provide meaningful guidance for real crowdsourcing systems. Without this connection to practice, the paper contributes little to the field of crowdsourcing as it's applied in machine learning and human-in-the-loop systems.

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents an interesting theoretical framework for analyzing crowdsourcing, but it falls short of being a significant contribution to the field due to the lack of empirical validation and comparison to established methods. The theoretical bounds are derived with mathematical rigor, but without evidence that they're tight or achievable in practice, they remain abstract. The paper would benefit from experiments comparing their theoretical bounds to practical crowdsourcing methods, showing how their framework can guide real-world system design.

As a reviewer who prefers established methods and is skeptical of unproven approaches, I find the paper's theoretical claims compelling but insufficiently validated. The authors make strong assertions about fundamental limits without demonstrating that these limits are both achievable and practically useful. Without empirical evidence showing how these theoretical bounds translate to real-world crowdsourcing improvements, the paper's contribution is limited.

**Recommendation:** Reject. While the theoretical approach is interesting, the lack of empirical validation against established baselines and the failure to demonstrate practical relevance make this paper unsuitable for NIPS. The authors should provide experimental results comparing their theoretical bounds to standard crowdsourcing methods before claiming these represent fundamental limits.
