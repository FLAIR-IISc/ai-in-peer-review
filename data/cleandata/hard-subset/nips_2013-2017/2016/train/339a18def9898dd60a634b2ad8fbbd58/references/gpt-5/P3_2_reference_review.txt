Summary
The paper studies crowdsourcing through an information-theoretic lens. It models workers as discrete memoryless channels and tasks as source symbols, and seeks fundamental budget–fidelity limits (queries per item vs. achievable error) independent of any specific aggregation algorithm. Two worker models are analyzed: (i) M-ary symmetric channels (MSC) with a distribution over error rates; (ii) a spammer–hammer channel (SHC) where with probability q a worker is a perfect “hammer” and otherwise chooses uniformly at random among allowed responses. Two scenarios are considered: SL-UK (worker skill levels unknown to the taskmaster and decoder) and SL-CS (skill levels known to the decoder). Using source–channel separation, the paper derives lower bounds on the minimum queries per item R needed to achieve a target average error ϵ̂ for an N-ary source with Hamming distortion. The SL-UK bound replaces the channel capacity with that of an “average worker” log M − HM(E[ε]), whereas SL-CS replaces it with the average of capacities E[log M − HM(ε)], highlighting a Jensen gap. The paper also introduces k-ary Incidence Coding (kIC), where a query presents k items and asks for their same-cluster partition. Because some response patterns are invalid, kIC has an inherent error-screening effect. For binary labeling with SHC and an oracle decoder, a necessary condition on R as a function of k and q is derived; a heuristic pricing rule π(k) ∝ k is proposed. Numerical plots compare the kIC lower bound against the SL-CS information-theoretic limit.

Relation to prior work
The paper connects to Dawid–Skene and subsequent EM/spectral methods (DS, GLAD/Whitehill et al., Zhang et al.), to budget-optimal task allocation (Karger–Oh–Shah), and to pairwise/incidence-query clustering (e.g., convex approaches). Its novelty is to formalize budget–fidelity tradeoffs via rate–distortion and capacity and to analyze kIC’s combinatorial structure through this lens. Related lines not cited or under-emphasized include semi-supervised clustering with same-cluster queries (e.g., Ashtiani et al., NIPS 2016) and minimax/statistical-complexity analyses for DS-type models that derive query complexity lower/upper bounds.

Strengths
- Clear articulation of a unifying information-theoretic framework for crowdsourcing, separating source complexity from channel (worker) reliability.
- The SL-UK vs. SL-CS comparison is insightful; the swap of HM(E[ε]) vs. E[HM(ε]) quantifies the value of estimating worker reliabilities.
- kIC is a natural, practically used query primitive; identifying its inherent invalid-answer filtering is valuable. The SHC analysis isolates how k modulates spammer-induced error.
- The bounds are simple and interpretable: numerator ≈ source compressibility H(B) − HN(ϵ̂), denominator ≈ average per-query capacity.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Assumptions and notation need tightening. The expressions Rmin = [H(B) − HN(ϵ̂)] / [log M − HM(…)] are stated without precise conditions under which the N-ary Hamming rate–distortion simplifies to H(B) − HN(ϵ̂). For general non-uniform sources, this holds only under specific symmetry conditions and for ϵ̂ ≤ min{1 − pmax, 1 − 1/N}. The paper mentions this constraint but should justify the form of R(D) or cite a result.
- Infinite-blocklength separation is used to claim fundamental limits, but the CS setting is typically single-shot or small-block, non-adaptive, and heterogeneous across items. The gap between asymptotic limits and finite-sample performance is not discussed; no achievability schemes are provided (beyond existence via separation).
- The SL-UK bound appears to replace the channel capacity with log M − HM(E[ε]); this corresponds to pretending that the worker index (channel state) is averaged out before transmission. A careful justification is needed (e.g., coding without CSI at encoder/decoder vs. composite channels). In contrast, SL-CS uses E[log M − HM(ε)], consistent with per-state coding when the decoder knows the state. Clarify decoder-side state knowledge assumptions and whether converse tools for channels with random states are used.
- kIC analysis is limited to N = 2 uniform sources, SHC, non-adaptive uniform sampling, and an oracle decoder. The necessary condition in Theorem 4 is quite loose; no achievability or finite-sample upper bounds for kIC are presented, making it hard to judge tightness. The claim that each item appears in exactly R′ queries contradicts the stated random sampling (this is at best in expectation unless enforced).
- The “pricing” rule π(k1)/π(k2) ≈ k1/k2 is speculative; it ignores cognitive load, UI effects, completion time distributions, and platform acceptance. It should be clearly labeled as a heuristic and ideally supported with user-time models or empirical data.
- Important practical factors such as item difficulty and worker–item interactions (e.g., GLAD) are acknowledged but deferred; the framework could at least outline how such feedforward modifies the converse (e.g., state-dependent channels).
- The writing has numerous formatting/typo issues (missing figure captions, broken references with missing years, undefined symbols, mismatched M vs. N, inconsistent notation for rates and blocklengths), making it difficult to verify details.

Quality
The core ideas are technically sound at a high level, and the use of source–channel separation to obtain converses is appropriate. However, several derivations are deferred to appendices (not provided), and key modeling choices (composite channels, R(D) for general N-ary Hamming) are not justified in the main text. The kIC result is only a necessary condition under strong assumptions.

Clarity
The exposition needs significant polishing. Notation is overloaded (R used in multiple roles), variables are sometimes undefined (ε for MSC not clearly introduced), and many references lack years. Figures are referenced but not shown. The presentation of kIC valid/invalid patterns would benefit from a precise combinatorial description.

Originality
Applying separation-based converses to crowdsourcing and contrasting SL-UK vs. SL-CS in this clean way is a useful framing, though related abstractions have appeared. The kIC angle is interesting; however, the technical novelty is somewhat limited by scope (binary, SHC, oracle decoder, necessary-only).

Significance
If tightened and extended (e.g., to DS/GLAD, adaptive tasking, achievability for kIC), the framework could influence how we think about fundamental query complexity in CS. As is, the practical impact is limited by strong assumptions and lack of constructive algorithms or empirical validation.

Suggestions for improvement
- Precisely state assumptions for Theorems 2–3; define ε for MSC, M for each query type, and conditions for the R(D) expression. Provide proof sketches in the main text.
- Clarify SL-UK vs. SL-CS as special cases of channels with random state; cite relevant converses and discuss encoder/decoder CSI.
- For kIC, provide an achievability result (even with a simple decoder) or finite-sample simulations to quantify the gap to the converse. Generalize beyond N = 2 if possible.
- Either drop or clearly qualify the pricing claim; if retained, justify with a task-time model.
- Discuss implications for adaptive assignment and item difficulty; outline how the framework could extend (e.g., state known at encoder/decoder, compound channels).
- Fix notation, references, and figures; ensure reproducibility.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually clean information-theoretic framing of CS budget–fidelity.
- Insightful comparison between unknown vs. known worker reliabilities.
- Highlights structural benefits of k-ary queries.

Cons for acceptance
- Technical scope is narrow; converses rely on strong asymptotic assumptions and special cases.
- No constructive algorithms or empirical validation; kIC result is only a necessary condition.
- Clarity/notation issues hinder verification.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The paper has a promising angle but needs stronger technical development, clearer exposition, and at least one concrete achievability or empirical validation to meet NeurIPS standards.
