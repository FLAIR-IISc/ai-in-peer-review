

# Review of "Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing"

This paper attempts to model crowdsourcing as an information-theoretic communication system, seeking fundamental limits on the budget-fidelity trade-off. The authors propose a framework where workers are modeled as noisy channels, and analyze two scenarios: when worker skill levels are unknown (SL-UK) and when they are known (SL-CS). They introduce k-ary incidence coding (kIC) and derive theoretical bounds on its performance.

## Strengths
The paper makes a novel attempt to frame crowdsourcing within an information-theoretic framework, which is a valuable contribution to the field. The distinction between SL-UK and SL-CS scenarios is conceptually interesting, and the introduction of k-ary incidence coding provides a concrete coding scheme to explore. The paper also makes an effort to connect to related work in crowdsourcing literature.

## Weaknesses

### Technical Errors
The paper contains several critical technical errors that undermine its theoretical contributions:

1. In Equation (2), the spammer-hammer channel model is incorrectly defined. The transition probabilities do not sum to 1: "1 if C = H and v = u, 1 if C = H and v ≠ u" would sum to 2, not 1. This is a fundamental error that invalidates the channel model.

2. Theorem 4 contains a dimensionally inconsistent expression: "R < 1/k ln(1−q) ln ̄S". The term ln(1-q) has no units while ln(̄S) is dimensionless, making the entire expression dimensionally inconsistent. Rate (queries per item) cannot be expressed as a dimensionless quantity.

3. The paper claims "kIC bounds for k = 1 is equivalent to the analysis leading to Lemma 1 of [8]" but k=1 is invalid for k-ary incidence coding (which requires k≥2 as stated in the paper), and Lemma 1 of [8] is about Venus image labeling, not crowdsourcing.

4. The distortion function is incorrectly defined as D(B(X), B̂(X)) = P(B̂(X) = B(X)), but the paper previously defined D as an average distortion (Equation 4), creating a fundamental inconsistency.

### Notation and Formatting Issues
The paper suffers from numerous inconsistent notations and formatting problems:

1. Inconsistent use of "̂" versus "ε" for error probability (Equations 11-12)

2. Inconsistent capitalization of "Crowdsourcing" versus "crowdsourcing"

3. The paper states "We have B(X1, . . . , Xn) = B(Xn) = (B(X1), . . . , B(Xn))" which is mathematically incorrect notation

4. The paper refers to "Figure 1a" but provides no figures, making it impossible to understand the proposed system model

5. Inconsistent use of "CS" to mean both "crowdsourcing" and "crowdsourcer"

### Reference and Citation Issues
1. References contain formatting errors (e.g., "th Conference on Learning Theory (COLT), , " with extra commas)

2. The paper claims "kIC bounds for k = 1 is equivalent to Lemma 1 of [8]" but Lemma 1 of [8] is about Venus image labeling, not crowdsourcing

3. The paper cites "Elements of Information Theory" as "T.M. Cover, J.A. Thomas, Wiely, New Jersey, USA, ," with missing year and incorrect publisher name ("Wiely" instead of "Wiley")

### Conceptual Flaws
The paper makes several conceptual errors:

1. The claim that "the average distortion D(B(X), B̂(X)) reflects the average probability of error" is only true for binary classification with Hamming distance, not for general discrete sources as the paper claims.

2. The paper states "the taskmaster does not know the workers' skill levels" but then later assumes that the crowdsourcer knows them in the SL-CS scenario, creating confusion about who knows what.

3. The paper fails to distinguish between the source (B(X)) and the estimated labels (B̂(X)) when discussing distortion.

## Overall Assessment
While the paper presents an interesting high-level idea, the technical errors, inconsistent notation, and formatting issues are substantial and undermine the paper's credibility. The dimensionally inconsistent expression in Theorem 4 alone is a critical flaw that invalidates a key result. The paper's claim to provide "fundamental limits" is not supported by the flawed theoretical analysis.

The paper would benefit from a complete rewrite to address these fundamental issues. As it stands, the paper does not meet the technical standards expected for NIPS, and the flaws are too numerous and substantive to be overlooked.

I recommend rejection. The paper's core idea has potential, but the current version contains too many fundamental technical errors and inconsistencies to be considered for publication.
