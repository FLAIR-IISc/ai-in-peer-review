This paper attempts to establish fundamental limits on the performance of crowdsourcing, modeling it as a communication system and analyzing the trade-off between budget (number of queries) and fidelity (accuracy). While the motivation is sound and the application of information theory to crowdsourcing is a potentially valuable direction, the paper suffers from a multitude of shortcomings, ranging from imprecise definitions and unsubstantiated claims to a lack of concrete results and inconsistencies in notation and presentation.

The introduction is overly verbose and contains unnecessary background information. Statements like "Most research reported so far is devoted to…" are vague and lack specific citations to support them. The transition between different subtopics within the introduction feels disjointed. Furthermore, the claim that the paper seeks “ultimate performance bounds” is a strong assertion that the subsequent analysis fails to fully deliver on.

Section 2, while attempting to establish a formal model, introduces several issues. The notation is inconsistent; for instance, *L* is used to denote both the number of items and, implicitly, the length of a sequence. The definition of *B(X)* as a function of items is unclear – does it represent a label for each item, or a collective function over the dataset?  The discussion of the M-ary symmetric channel (MSC) and spammer-hammer channel (SHC) models is adequate, but feels disconnected from the broader goals of the paper. The rationale behind choosing these specific channel models is not fully explained. The introduction of k-ary incidence coding (kIC) is poorly motivated; the explanation of error correction capability feels hand-wavy and lacks rigorous justification. The notion of 'valid' responses in kIC is only loosely defined.

Section 3 presents the core theoretical results. The theorems (2 and 3) introduce rate bounds, but these bounds are often expressed in terms of quantities like *p<sub>max</sub>* and *H<sub>N</sub>(.)* which, while standard information-theoretic measures, are not clearly related to practical parameters of a crowdsourcing system.  The assumption of a uniformly distributed source *B(X)* is a significant simplification, and its impact on the generality of the results is not discussed. The connection between these theoretical limits and the practical constraints of crowdsourcing platforms (e.g., worker costs, task difficulty) is entirely missing.  The derivation of the bounds appears to lack sufficient detail, making it difficult to verify the correctness of the results.

The discussion of kIC in Section 3.2 is particularly problematic. Theorem 4 states a necessary condition for the rate, but the connection to Corollary 1 (which builds on Theorem 3) is not clearly established. The use of "Oracle decoder" is a significant caveat – a perfect decoder is unrealistic and undermines the practical relevance of the bound. The "approximate rule of thumb for pricing" presented at the end of this section is trivial and lacks any economic justification. The numerical results are presented without sufficient context or comparison to existing crowdsourcing algorithms.  Figure 2 lacks clear labeling and a description of the parameters used. The claim that the kIC bounds for k=1 are equivalent to Lemma 1 of [8] is incorrect; the two are not directly comparable.

Throughout the paper, there are numerous minor errors and inconsistencies. The writing style is often convoluted and repetitive. The figures are of low quality and do not effectively illustrate the concepts being discussed.  The citations are occasionally incomplete or inaccurate.

**Arguments for rejection:**

* **Lack of Practical Relevance:** The theoretical results are too abstract and divorced from the realities of crowdsourcing systems. The assumptions made (e.g., uniformly distributed source, perfect channel estimation) are unrealistic.
* **Insufficient Rigor:** The derivations are often incomplete and lack sufficient justification. The connection between theoretical bounds and practical parameters is missing.
* **Poor Presentation:** The paper is poorly written, disorganized, and contains numerous minor errors. The figures are of low quality.
* **Incremental Contribution:** While the application of information theory to crowdsourcing is a promising direction, this paper does not make a significant contribution to the field. It primarily presents known information-theoretic results applied to a new context without substantial novel insights.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* **Interesting Problem:** The problem of establishing fundamental limits on crowdsourcing performance is an important and challenging one.
* **Novel Application:** The application of information theory to crowdsourcing is a novel approach.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the interesting motivation, this paper is fundamentally flawed. The lack of rigor, poor presentation, and limited practical relevance outweigh any potential novelty. I strongly recommend **rejection**. The authors need to significantly revise the paper, providing more detailed derivations, connecting the theoretical results to practical parameters, and improving the clarity and organization of the presentation. A more focused analysis, perhaps concentrating on a specific aspect of crowdsourcing (e.g., kIC with a specific worker model), would also be beneficial.
