- Overall: Important, well-framed information-theoretic study of crowdsourcing limits with interesting results but dense presentation and limited practical illustration.

- Problem framing: Models crowdsourcing as a communication system to study budget (queries) vs. fidelity (accuracy) trade-offs.

- Scenarios analyzed: Derives lower bounds for unknown worker skills (SL-UK) and perfectly known skills (SL-CS).

- Novel method: Introduces and analyzes k-ary incidence coding (kIC) and proposes a k-based query pricing strategy.

- Relation to prior work: Correctly cites aggregation and worker-modeling literature and distinguishes itself by using information theory.

- Gap to practice: Weak connection between theoretical bounds and practical algorithms (e.g., Dawid-Skene) and real systems.

- Strength — theory: Strong theoretical and mathematical rigor underpinning the derived bounds.

- Strength — coding: kIC is a novel scheme with potential for improved efficiency and robustness.

- Strength — trade-offs: Clearly identifies key trade-offs between budget, fidelity, and worker-skill knowledge.

- Strength — pricing: The idea of linking query price to k is a practical and insightful extension.

- Weakness — clarity: Paper is notation-heavy, lacks intuitive explanations, and needs concrete examples to aid understanding.

- Weakness — experiments: Numerical evaluation is sparse and does not convincingly show kIC benefits or bound tightness.

- Weakness — realism: Assumptions like perfect skill knowledge (SL-CS) and simplified worker models (MSC/SHC) are unrealistic and under-discussed.

- Weakness — presentation: Overly dense and crammed; flow and digestibility need improvement.

- For acceptance: Addresses a fundamental problem with a rigorous, novel information-theoretic approach and promising kIC idea.

- Against acceptance: Hard to understand, limited empirical support, weak practical relevance, and theoretical results may not translate to design guidance.

- Recommendation: Reject with major revisions.

- Required revisions: Add intuitive explanations and concrete examples; expand numerical comparisons (including DS and majority voting); connect bounds to real-world system design; discuss and relax unrealistic assumptions; improve clarity and organization.
