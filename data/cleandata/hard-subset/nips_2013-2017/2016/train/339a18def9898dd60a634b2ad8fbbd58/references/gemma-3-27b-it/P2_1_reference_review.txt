## Review of "Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing (CS) in terms of the trade-off between budget (number of queries) and fidelity (accuracy). The authors model CS as a communication system and derive information-theoretic bounds on performance for two scenarios: unknown worker skill levels (SL-UK) and perfectly estimated skill levels (SL-CS). They also analyze a specific query scheme, k-ary incidence coding (kIC), and discuss its implications for query pricing. The work aims to provide theoretical guarantees on the performance achievable by *any* CS system, regardless of the specific aggregation or inference algorithm used.

**Strengths:**

* **Rigorous Theoretical Framework:** The paper employs a strong information-theoretic approach, providing a solid foundation for analyzing the fundamental limits of CS. This is a valuable contribution, as much of the existing CS literature focuses on algorithmic improvements rather than fundamental limits.
* **Clear Problem Formulation:**  The modeling of CS as a communication system is well-motivated and clearly presented, allowing for a formal analysis of the budget-fidelity trade-off.
* **Novel k-ary Incidence Coding Analysis:** The introduction and analysis of kIC is a notable contribution. The derived bounds for kIC performance, particularly in the context of spammer-hammer workers, are insightful. The connection to query pricing is a practical consideration.
* **Comprehensive Discussion of Scenarios:** The paper considers two distinct scenarios (SL-UK and SL-CS), providing a nuanced understanding of how knowledge of worker skill impacts achievable performance.
* **Connections to Existing Work:** The paper appropriately situates itself within the existing CS literature, referencing relevant work on aggregation, inference, and worker modeling.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Practical Guidance:** While the theoretical bounds are valuable, the paper offers limited practical guidance for designing real-world CS systems.  The assumptions underlying the bounds (e.g., large block length, perfect channel estimation in SL-CS) may not hold in practice. The gap between the theoretical bounds and achievable performance in real systems isn't thoroughly discussed.
* **Assumptions about Source Distribution:** The analysis relies on assumptions about the source distribution (e.g., uniformly distributed dataset). The sensitivity of the results to deviations from these assumptions is not fully explored.  How do the bounds change for non-uniform datasets?
* **Complexity of kIC:** While kIC is theoretically interesting, its practical implementation and computational complexity (especially for large *k*) are not thoroughly addressed. The benefits of kIC need to be weighed against its potential overhead.
* **Lack of Empirical Validation:**  The paper lacks experimental validation of the derived bounds.  While a purely theoretical paper isn't *required* to have experiments, some simulations demonstrating the tightness of the bounds would significantly strengthen the contribution.
* **Clarity in some sections:** Sections 3.1 and 3.2 contain a large amount of information theory background. While necessary to some degree, the presentation feels dense and may be difficult for readers less familiar with the field to follow.



**Addressing Review Criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The mathematical derivations appear correct, and the arguments are logically presented. However, the lack of empirical validation weakens the claims somewhat. It's a *complete* piece of work in the sense of presenting a thorough theoretical analysis, but it doesn’t translate directly into immediate practical implications.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, but some sections (as noted above) are dense and require a strong background in information theory. The figures are helpful, and the overall structure is logical.
* **Originality:** The combination of information-theoretic analysis with crowdsourcing is novel and represents a significant contribution. The kIC scheme and its analysis are also original.  The authors demonstrate a clear understanding of related work and adequately differentiate their contribution.
* **Significance:** The results are important in that they establish fundamental limits on CS performance. While not immediately applicable to all scenarios, the theoretical bounds provide a benchmark for evaluating existing and future CS algorithms. The insights gained from analyzing kIC could influence the design of more efficient query schemes.



**Arguments Pro and Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Provides valuable theoretical insights into the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing.
* Introduces and analyzes a novel query scheme (kIC) with potential benefits.
* Rigorous mathematical framework and clear problem formulation.
* Addresses a challenging and important problem in the context of growing reliance on crowdsourcing.

**Con:**

* Limited practical guidance and applicability to real-world systems.
* Lack of empirical validation.
* Assumptions about source distribution and channel estimation may be unrealistic.
* Potentially high complexity of kIC.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a **strong accept** for NIPS, despite its limitations. The theoretical contribution is significant, the analysis is rigorous, and the problem addressed is important. While the lack of empirical validation and practical guidance are weaknesses, the paper’s value lies in establishing fundamental limits, which is a crucial step towards designing better CS systems. The authors have clearly demonstrated a deep understanding of the problem and have presented a thoughtful and well-written analysis.  I encourage the authors to consider adding some simulations to future versions to demonstrate the tightness of their bounds and explore the practical implications of their results.



**Confidence:** 8/10
