- Summary: Models crowdsourcing as a communication system, deriving information-theoretic budget–fidelity limits for unknown-skill (SL-UK) and known-skill (SL-CS) scenarios and introducing k-ary incidence coding (kIC) with bounds under a spammer-hammer model.

- Technical quality: Rigorous theoretical derivations with well-structured proofs (Theorems 2–4, Lemma 1) and complete technical appendices.

- Model & analysis: Clear communication-system formulation and use of source–channel separation theorems to establish fundamental limits rather than algorithmic solutions.

- kIC contribution: Proposes a novel k-ary incidence coding scheme that provides error-correction capabilities and analytically derived performance bounds.

- Relation to prior work: Builds on and differentiates from Dawid–Skene, majority voting, and recent NIPS crowdsourcing papers by providing foundational limits instead of inference algorithms.

- Clarity: Well-organized presentation and effective figures, but some information-theoretic derivations and the SL-UK vs SL-CS distinction need more intuitive explanation and earlier clarification.

- Numerical results: Section 4.2 presents useful numerical analysis but needs better contextualization with practical crowdsourcing scenarios.

- Originality: Highly novel information-theoretic perspective and conceptual contributions, especially kIC, that advance the field’s theoretical foundations.

- Significance: Provides benchmarks for budget vs fidelity trade-offs with implications for designing cost-effective crowdsourcing systems and clustering applications.

- Strengths: Novel perspective, rigorous limits derivation, kIC introduction, clear connection to information theory, and complete proofs.

- Weaknesses: Technical accessibility issues for non-experts, limited discussion of practical kIC implementation, weak real-world linkage of numerical results, and lack of guidance for approaching limits in finite-data settings.

- Recommendation: Recommend acceptance—high-quality, original, significant theoretical contribution meeting conference standards.

- Acceptance arguments: Novel theoretical framework, rigorous mathematics, clear distinction from prior algorithmic work, and strong implications for system design.

- Rejection counterpoints: Some sections may be too technical for a general audience and practical implementation/real-world applicability could be better addressed.
