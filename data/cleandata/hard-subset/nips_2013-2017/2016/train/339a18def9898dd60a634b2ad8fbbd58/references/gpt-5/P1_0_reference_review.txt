Summary and relation to prior work
The paper models crowdsourcing as a communication system and studies fundamental budget–fidelity limits using information theory. Items’ labels (or a function B(X)) are the source; workers are modeled as noisy channels; the crowdsourcer’s aggregation is a decoder. Two worker models are considered: an M-ary symmetric channel (MSC) with per-worker error parameter, and a spammer–hammer channel (SHC) with hammer probability q. The paper derives lower bounds on the number of queries per item (rate) needed to achieve a target Hamming distortion (error) via separation: R(D) for the source and channel capacity for the worker model. It distinguishes two scenarios: SL-UK (worker skill levels unknown to the crowdsourcer) and SL-CS (skill levels known to the crowdsourcer), yielding different denominators: capacity of the average channel vs average of capacities. The paper also introduces k-ary incidence coding (kIC), where each query presents k items and asks for within-query clustering; because only a subset of 2^(k choose 2) responses are valid, kIC has an inherent error-correcting effect. For SHC workers, the paper derives a necessary condition on rate for kIC to achieve a target error, based on the probability that an item is never seen by a hammer and the spammer-induced error within a kIC query. A corollary gives the information-theoretic lower bound on rate under SHC with known worker types, and the paper sketches a pricing heuristic for kIC.

This framing connects to classic results in information theory (Shannon separation, rate–distortion, channel capacity) and to crowdsourcing/label aggregation work (Dawid–Skene EM, Karger–Oh–Shah on budget-optimal allocation, spectral/EM hybrids). The kIC idea relates to pairwise/triplet-query clustering and crowdsourced similarity learning.

Strengths
- Clear, principled information-theoretic formulation of budget vs fidelity that separates source complexity (via R(D)) from worker noise (via capacity).
- Useful conceptual distinction between SL-UK and SL-CS scenarios; the “capacity of the average channel” vs “average of capacities” comparison is insightful and practically relevant for when estimating worker reliabilities helps.
- kIC is a natural and appealing query primitive; showing how its combinatorial constraints provide “built-in” error correction is interesting.
- The SHC-based necessary condition for kIC is intuitive and clean; the lemma giving spammer error for kIC is a nice closed-form piece.
- Potential applicability to pricing/market design is a plus.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Technical depth vs novelty: The main bounds follow almost directly from standard separation, with fairly strong simplifying assumptions (iid source; MSC/SHC; memoryless, independent channels; non-adaptive querying; asymptotics). The paper does not provide achievability constructions tailored to crowdsourcing beyond the existence guarantees by separation, nor finite-sample analysis.
- Modeling realism: MSC assumes class-symmetric confusion; SHC is extreme. Important factors such as per-item difficulty, adversarial behavior beyond uniform spamming, and per-class asymmetries are only mentioned as future work. Many modern models (e.g., DS with item difficulty, confusion matrices) could be incorporated to sharpen the results.
- kIC analysis is partial: The main theorem is a necessary condition using an oracle that perfectly identifies hammers; no sufficient conditions or achievable algorithms for kIC are analyzed, and only N=2 is treated. The dependence on k is only through the spammer error term and the 1/k factor; empirical validation of tightness is missing.
- Lack of empirical evaluation: No simulations to calibrate tightness of the bounds, compare kIC to 2IC/majority/DS under realistic worker mixtures, or to examine finite L effects.
- Clarity and presentation issues: The manuscript has many typographical and formatting problems (missing figure content, inconsistent notation for M/N, dangling symbols, incomplete references without years/pages, e.g., “Wiely,” “th Conference”). Some equations are hard to parse (e.g., Theorems 2–3 notational overload; the pricing ratio expression seems malformed; base of logarithms not always specified). The definitions of U,V,M, and their relation to kIC’s valid-choice alphabet could be clarified.
- Limited relation to prior theoretical limits in crowdsourcing: The paper cites Karger–Oh–Shah but does not compare its bounds to theirs (e.g., budget vs error scaling under DS/majority, adaptive allocation gains), nor to more recent information-theoretic analyses of noisy label aggregation.

Quality
Technically sound at the level claimed; the information-theoretic derivations are standard and appear correct under the stated assumptions. The kIC necessary condition is reasonable. However, the work is largely lower-bound/converse; achievability and empirical support are minimal. Many details are deferred to appendices (not included), and clarity issues hinder verification.

Clarity
Currently below NIPS standards. The exposition is conceptually clear, but the manuscript has numerous typos, incomplete references, ambiguous notation, and missing figure details. The pricing heuristic formula needs correction and justification. Suggestions:
- Clearly define all alphabets and parameters (N classes vs M response options; for kIC, size of the valid-response alphabet).
- State base of logarithms consistently and domains where the R(D) expressions apply (e.g., D ≤ min{1−pmax,1−1/N}).
- Provide proof sketches for key theorems in the main text.
- Tighten the presentation of kIC, including the invalid/valid response counting for general N.

Originality
Moderate. The application of classical separation to crowdsourcing is neat but not entirely new in spirit. The SL-UK vs SL-CS capacity comparison and the kIC error-correction angle for SHC are the more original aspects. A stronger case would come from broader worker models, adaptive querying, or constructive schemes approaching the bounds.

Significance
Conceptually valuable for framing budget–fidelity trade-offs and for motivating richer query primitives like kIC. As written, impact may be limited by strong assumptions, lack of algorithms/experiments, and partial kIC treatment. With improved scope and validation, this line could influence the design of cost-effective crowdsourcing systems.

Pros for acceptance
- Clean, principled information-theoretic framework and bounds.
- Insightful distinction between unknown/known worker skills.
- Interesting kIC perspective with a simple necessary condition.
- Potential to inform pricing and query design.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty beyond applying separation; no constructive/achievable algorithms.
- Strong modeling simplifications; no finite-sample or adaptive results.
- No empirical validation; figures and appendices not provided.
- Significant clarity and referencing issues.

Suggestions for improvement
- Compare quantitatively to existing theoretical results (e.g., KOS) and to DS/majority under similar worker models.
- Extend to more realistic worker models (confusion matrices, item difficulty) and discuss how the capacity terms change.
- Provide achievability via explicit schemes (e.g., coding/aggregation strategies) and finite-length bounds, or at least simulations demonstrating tightness.
- Generalize kIC analysis beyond N=2; provide sufficient conditions or practical decoders; include experiments on synthetic/AMT data.
- Fix notation, references, and provide complete proofs/figures; clarify the pricing rule and its assumptions.

Recommendation
Borderline weak reject in current form due to presentation issues and limited empirical/constructive content, despite a sound and potentially useful theoretical framing. With revisions addressing clarity, comparison, and validation, this could be a strong contribution. Confidence: medium.
