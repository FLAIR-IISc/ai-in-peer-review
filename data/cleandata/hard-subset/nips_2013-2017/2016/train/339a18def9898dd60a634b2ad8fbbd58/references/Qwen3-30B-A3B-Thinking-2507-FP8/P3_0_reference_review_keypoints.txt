- Presents an information-theoretic framework modeling crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding problem and derives fundamental budget-fidelity limits.
- Distinguishes two scenarios: unknown worker skills (SL-UK) vs. known skills (SL-CS) and characterizes their differing capacities.
- Introduces k-ary incidence coding (kIC) as a query scheme with error-correction properties and analyzes its performance.
- Provides theoretical bounds (including Theorem 4 for the spammer-hammer model) with correct derivations tying kIC to coding theory.
- Strong rigorous theoretical foundation that is independent of specific inference algorithms.
- Insightful capacity distinction showing practical implications for system design.
- Thorough related-work discussion situating the paper among Dawid–Skene, bandit surveys, and clustering-based crowdsourcing.
- Major weakness: no empirical validation—no experiments on real crowdsourcing data or simulations testing kIC against baselines.
- Restrictive assumptions (iid data, fixed worker pool, known skill distributions) limit real-world applicability.
- Weak connection to practice: pricing “rule of thumb” (π(k₁)/π(k₂) ≈ k₁/k₂) is unvalidated and implementation guidance is lacking.
- Fails to address finite-sample behavior and scaling (analysis assumes L → ∞).
- Recommendation: reject with strong revision; request simulations/experiments, practical application discussion, concrete budget/implementation guidance, and finite-sample analysis.
