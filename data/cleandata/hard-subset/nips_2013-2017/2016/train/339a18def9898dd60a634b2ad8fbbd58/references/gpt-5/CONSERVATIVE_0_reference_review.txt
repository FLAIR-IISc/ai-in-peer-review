Summary
This paper models crowdsourcing as a communication system and studies fundamental budget–fidelity trade-offs via information-theoretic tools. The source is the latent labels B(X); workers are modeled as noisy channels. Two worker models are considered: (i) M-ary symmetric channels (MSC) with a discrete distribution over skill levels; (ii) a spammer–hammer channel (SHC(q)). The main theoretical results are (a) lower bounds on the minimum number of queries per item Rmin to achieve a target error rate under two regimes: worker skills unknown to the crowdsourcer (SL-UK) vs perfectly known (SL-CS), derived by invoking rate–distortion and channel capacity/separation arguments; and (b) a lower bound on the performance of k-ary incidence coding (kIC) under SHC with an oracle decoder, plus a heuristic pricing rule. A corollary specializes the information-theoretic limit to SHC.

Strengths
- Clear, high-level formulation linking crowdsourcing to joint source–channel coding, which helps separate source complexity (label entropy and allowed distortion) from channel quality (worker reliability).
- The comparison between SL-UK and SL-CS aligns with known information-theoretic distinctions (capacity of averaged channel vs average capacity with receiver-side state), and is conceptually useful for understanding the value of estimating worker skills.
- kIC is a natural class of queries; the observation that restricting to consistent responses provides error-correction intuition is reasonable.
- The paper strives to provide universal, code-independent lower bounds that can serve as benchmarks.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: Theorems 2 and 3 essentially restate Shannon’s separation theorem applied to a mixture of MSCs, yielding Rmin ≈ (rate–distortion of the source)/(effective channel capacity). The SL-UK vs SL-CS distinction mirrors standard results for random channels with/without receiver side information. The paper does not appear to derive new information-theoretic results beyond these specializations.
- Unrealistic assumptions and weak connection to practice: iid items, large-block asymptotics (L,n→∞), memoryless/independent worker errors, no task difficulty, no adversaries beyond random spammers, non-adaptive querying, and in kIC an oracle decoder. These idealizations make the bounds optimistic and limit their prescriptive value for real systems.
- Lack of empirical validation or finite-sample analysis: No simulations comparing the derived bounds to established aggregation methods (majority vote, DS/EM, spectral/convex methods) under realistic settings (e.g., GLAD or other difficulty/expertise models). No assessment of how tight the bounds are at practical scales.
- kIC analysis is very narrow: binary labels (N=2), SHC only, lower bounds with an oracle. No decoding algorithm, no finite-length performance, and no comparison to graph-based/convex methods for pairwise/cluster queries. The pricing rule-of-thumb is heuristic and not validated.
- Clarity/notation issues: Several notational inconsistencies and typos (e.g., H_M(E(ε)) vs E(H_M(ε)); roles of N vs M; missing equation punctuation). Some references lack years or full details. Figures are referenced but not sufficiently explained in text; proofs are only in appendices.
- Relation to prior work is incomplete. There is a substantial literature on budget-optimal crowdsourcing, sample complexity, and inference under heterogeneous workers (e.g., Karger–Oh–Shah; analyses of DS and spectral methods; GLAD/task difficulty models; triplet/comparison-based clustering). The positioning vis-à-vis these works is not sufficiently precise, and the added value over known sample-complexity bounds is unclear.

Quality
Technically, the high-level arguments are sound but largely standard. The MSC capacity terms and rate–distortion forms appear consistent, and the SL-UK vs SL-CS comparison is plausible. However, the kIC bound relies on an oracle and SHC, limiting its informativeness. The paper lacks finite-sample or non-asymptotic guarantees and any empirical validation. Overall, this reads more like a position/benchmarking note than a complete contribution.

Clarity
The manuscript is readable but needs careful editing: fix notation, clearly define all alphabets and entropies, ensure equations are unambiguous, and provide full bibliographic info. The organization is acceptable, but key results would benefit from intuition, concrete instantiations (e.g., plug-in numbers), and a clearer explanation of where the derivations depart from textbook separation theorems.

Originality
Moderate to low. The core results are direct consequences of established information theory with standard channel-state variants. The kIC component is conceptually interesting but not developed into new theory or algorithms.

Significance
Limited in current form. Asymptotic, code-independent lower bounds can be useful as sanity checks, but without tighter, practically relevant finite-sample bounds, algorithms, or empirical validation against established baselines, the impact on the NeurIPS community is likely small.

Suggestions for improvement
- Tighten the novelty: derive non-asymptotic bounds (finite L) and/or account for adaptive querying. Quantify the gain from estimating worker skills (SL-UK→SL-CS) under realistic models (e.g., GLAD).
- Extend beyond MSC/SHC to more realistic confusion matrices and task difficulty; compare to DS/EM, spectral, and KOS-style algorithms through simulations and show proximity to the IT limits.
- For kIC, provide an explicit decoding/inference method, finite-sample analysis, and empirical comparison to pairwise/graph clustering methods. Validate the pricing rule on synthetic/AMT data with time-on-task measurements.
- Clarify notation, fix typos, complete references, and include proof sketches highlighting the nontrivial steps.

Pros
- Clean formalization; connects CS to established IT principles.
- Useful conceptual comparison of unknown vs known worker skills.
- kIC is a natural, practically used query type.

Cons
- Largely a repackaging of known IT results; limited novelty.
- Strong assumptions; oracle bounds; no experiments or finite-sample analysis.
- Weak positioning vs established crowdsourcing theory and practice.

Recommendation
Reject. While the paper frames an appealing information-theoretic perspective, the technical novelty beyond standard separation-based arguments is limited, assumptions are restrictive, and there is no empirical or algorithmic contribution to ground the theory against established methods. A revised version with finite-sample results, realistic models, algorithms, and thorough comparisons could be much more compelling. Confidence: high.
