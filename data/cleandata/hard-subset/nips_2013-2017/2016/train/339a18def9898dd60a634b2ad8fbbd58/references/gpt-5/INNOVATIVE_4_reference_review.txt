Summary
This paper proposes an information-theoretic formulation of label crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding problem. The dataset labels B(X) are treated as a source, the workers are modeled as discrete memoryless channels (either M-ary symmetric channels or spammer–hammer mixtures), and the query/inference pipeline is a code with rate equal to queries per item. The main results are (i) lower bounds on the minimum query rate to achieve a target error (Hamming distortion) when worker skill levels are unknown (SL-UK) vs known to the crowdsourcer (SL-CS), and (ii) an analysis of k-ary Incidence Coding (kIC), showing that restricting answers to combinatorially valid responses yields “error-correction,” and deriving necessary conditions on budget under a SHC(q) worker pool with an oracle decoder. A simple pricing heuristic for different k is also suggested. The paper positions these bounds as fundamental budget–fidelity tradeoffs that are algorithm-agnostic, and relates them to classical rate–distortion and channel capacity via the source–channel separation theorem.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: A clean, bold synthesis that recasts crowdsourcing as joint source–channel coding and directly brings rate–distortion and capacity tools to bear. This framing is likely to stimulate new lines of work.
- General lower bounds: Theorems 2 and 3 provide explicit necessary rates for achieving a given error under MSC worker models, teasing apart SL-UK vs SL-CS. The observation that SL-UK behaves like capacity of the “average worker” while SL-CS corresponds to the average of capacities is insightful.
- k-ary incidence coding: Casting kIC as a code with built-in invalid-answer pruning is elegant. The necessary condition derived under SHC(q) with an oracle decoder gives interpretable scaling in k and q, and the comparison to the information-theoretic limit is helpful.
- Potential practical implications: The budget–fidelity lens and the rule-of-thumb pricing ratio π(k1)/π(k2) ≈ k1/k2 could influence marketplace design and task structuring.

Weaknesses
- Limited achievability/constructive side: The paper focuses on converse-style lower bounds. There is no achievability scheme shown to approach the bounds (even asymptotically), no finite-sample analysis, and no discussion of the gap. Without a sense of tightness, practical import is blunted.
- Strong idealizations: Assumes iid items and large L; non-adaptive querying; independence across workers and items; oracle knowledge of worker states for the kIC result; SHC and MSC may miss realistic correlations and adversarial behaviors. These choices are reasonable for first principles but should be explicitly scoped and stress-tested.
- Clarity and notation: The manuscript has many notational inconsistencies and typographical issues (e.g., M vs N for alphabet size; undefined or overloaded symbols like ε, E(·), HM(.); formatting in Eq. (1); missing years in references). Theorems 2–3 formulas are hard to parse and need careful restatement with clear definitions of all terms and constraints.
- Empirical validation: Aside from a single figure contrasting the kIC lower bound with the separation-based limit, there are no simulations or AMT studies. Even synthetic experiments testing how close standard decoders (e.g., DS/EM, spectral) get to the bounds would strengthen the case.
- Cost model: The pricing heuristic is appealing but simplistic; cognitive load and response latency often scale sub/super-linearly with k. Some discussion or empirical calibration would help.

Quality
Technically sound at a high level, leveraging standard information-theoretic tools appropriately. However, the paper is converse-heavy with missing proof details in the main text, and the presentation issues hinder verification. The kIC SHC result assumes an oracle decoder; stating this as a necessary condition is fine, but readers would benefit from a discussion of tightness and potential achievable schemes.

Clarity
The organization is reasonable, but notation and copyediting need significant attention. Please unify symbol usage (M vs N), define all quantities when introduced, fix equation formatting, provide units for rates (“queries per item”), and include years and complete citations.

Originality
Moderate-to-high. While worker/channel models (MSC, SHC) and kIC have appeared before, the systematic source–channel view with explicit rate–distortion style lower bounds for SL-UK vs SL-CS, and the necessary conditions for kIC under SHC, constitute a novel synthesis and push the field toward principled limits.

Significance
Potentially impactful as a conceptual foundation for budget planning, code design, and marketplace pricing. The practical significance will be higher if the authors add achievability results or empirical evidence showing that commonly used algorithms approach the derived limits.

Questions and suggestions
- Provide a clean, self-contained statement of Theorems 2–3 with all symbols defined, and discuss the tightness gap. Are there achievability results (e.g., random coding arguments, or existing decoders under redundancy) that approach these rates?
- Clarify the roles of N (number of label classes) vs M (response alphabet), and the relationship between them in different settings.
- Discuss how adaptivity (active querying, bandit selection) might move the limits; can your framework incorporate feedback-capacity style results?
- Extend the kIC analysis beyond N=2; what changes for multi-class?
- Validate the pricing heuristic with user studies or historical AMT logs; at least discuss cognitive cost scaling with k.
- Compare more directly to minimax/crowdsourcing limits (e.g., Karger–Oh–Shah and subsequent works) and delineate when your bounds are tighter/looser.

Pros for acceptance
- Bold, unifying formulation; likely to catalyze new research directions.
- Clear, interpretable lower bounds and insightful SL-UK vs SL-CS comparison.
- Elegant treatment of kIC as a code with error-correction flavor.

Cons for acceptance
- Converse-only; lack of achievability and limited empirical validation.
- Presentation/notation issues impede accessibility.
- Some assumptions (oracle decoder, iid items) limit immediate applicability.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The conceptual novelty and framing outweigh the current limitations. With improved clarity and at least some empirical or achievability evidence, this could be a valuable contribution to the NeurIPS community.

Reviewer confidence: Medium.
