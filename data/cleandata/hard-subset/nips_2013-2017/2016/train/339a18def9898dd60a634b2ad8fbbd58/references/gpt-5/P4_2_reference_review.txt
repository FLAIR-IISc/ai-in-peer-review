Summary and relation to prior work
The paper frames label crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding problem and studies the fundamental budget–fidelity trade-off. The dataset labels B(X) are modeled as a discrete memoryless source, queries as channel uses, and workers as noisy channels. Two worker models are considered: (i) M-ary symmetric channels (MSC) with heterogeneous error parameters ε drawn from a known distribution across workers, and (ii) a spammer–hammer channel (SHC) with hammer probability q. Two information-theoretic lower bounds on the minimum number of queries per item R are derived for achieving target Hamming distortion (error rate) ˆε: (a) SL-UK, where worker skill levels are unknown to both taskmaster and crowdsourcer; the bound uses the capacity of the averaged MSC, yielding a denominator log2 M − H_M(E[ε]). (b) SL-CS, where the crowdsourcer can perfectly infer worker skills; the capacity denominator becomes log2 M − E[H_M(ε)]. These follow from the source–channel separation theorem, with source rate-distortion for a discrete source R*(ˆε) = H(B) − H_N(ˆε) and channel capacity of (mixture) MSCs.

The paper also studies k-ary incidence coding (kIC), in which each query presents k items and asks workers to indicate which belong to the same class (N = 2 in the analysis). kIC constrains workers to valid incidence patterns, which introduces an implicit error-correction effect. Under the SHC model, the paper provides: (i) a combinatorial expression for a spammer’s per-item error probability under kIC (Lemma 1), and (ii) a converse-style necessary condition on R for any decoder (even oracle knowledge of worker types) to achieve error ≤ ˆε (Theorem 4). A corollary specializes the information-theoretic lower bound in the SHC setting. The paper also sketches a pricing rule-of-thumb suggesting kIC queries should be priced approximately proportional to k.

This work connects to a broad literature on aggregation/inference in crowdsourcing (Dawid–Skene, EM/spectral methods, majority voting) and budget–reliability optimization (e.g., Karger–Oh–Shah). The framing via rate–distortion and channel capacity is less common in the crowdsourcing literature and offers a clean benchmark.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual contribution: casts crowdsourcing as joint source–channel coding and derives explicit, interpretable lower bounds that separate source complexity (H(B) − H_N(ˆε)) from channel quality (capacity of worker pool).
- Distinguishes two regimes (skills unknown vs known to decoder) and quantifies the gain from estimating worker skills via the gap between log2 M − H_M(E[ε]) and log2 M − E[H_M(ε]).
- kIC analysis highlights how structured queries can induce error-correction through constrained answer sets; the SHC-based converse is intuitive and practically informative.
- Provides a normalization of “budget” as queries per item, facilitating cross-scheme comparisons, and hints at pricing implications.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is moderate: the lower bounds largely follow directly from classical separation theorems; the capacity terms for mixed MSCs are standard due to symmetry. The paper does not provide achievability or tightness results beyond these converses, nor algorithmic constructions approaching the bounds.
- Realism of assumptions is limited: iid labels, non-adaptive querying, infinite blocklength coding across items, and independent worker channels. Adaptive task assignment, heterogeneous item difficulty, and worker–task interactions are not modeled, yet are central to practical systems.
- kIC analysis, while insightful, focuses only on N = 2 and SHC. The necessary condition appears to rely on an assumption that each item is included in exactly R′ k-ary queries (R = R′/k); this should be stated precisely. The displayed threshold in Theorem 4 seems to miss its dependence on ˆε (likely R ≥ (1/k) ln(ˆε/ε̄S)/ln(1 − q)); the current form is hard to parse.
- Clarity and presentation: several typographical/notation issues (e.g., inconsistent use of M vs N, undefined symbols like E(ε) before explanation, misformatted equations), incomplete references (missing years/venues), and missing figures/appendices in the main text hinder reproducibility and verification.
- Limited empirical or simulation evidence: apart from a qualitative plot, there is no evaluation on synthetic or real data to demonstrate how close practical algorithms can get to the bounds or to assess kIC’s practical cost/benefit.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The use of rate–distortion and capacity for MSC mixtures is sound as converse bounds under the stated assumptions. The kIC SHC converse is plausible but needs clearer derivation and explicit dependence on ˆε. No achievability or finite-blocklength analysis is provided.
- Completeness: Focused on converses; lacks constructive schemes or tighter bounds in realistic finite regimes.

Clarity
- Writing is generally understandable but marred by formatting and notation issues. Proofs are deferred; main text would benefit from clearer definitions of M and N, the precise rate accounting for kIC (items-per-query vs queries-per-item), and a corrected statement of Theorem 4.

Originality
- Conceptual reframing is useful, but core bounds are direct consequences of classic results. kIC analysis for SHC adds incremental insight.

Significance
- Provides clean baselines for what any crowdsourcing system cannot beat, and quantifies potential gains from skill estimation and structured queries. Practical impact depends on extending to adaptive/finite regimes and validating with data.

Arguments for acceptance
- Establishes clear, interpretable information-theoretic lower bounds for crowdsourcing budgets under well-defined models.
- Highlights the benefit of estimating worker skills and of structured queries such as kIC.
- Offers a unifying perspective linking crowdsourcing design to joint source–channel coding.

Arguments against acceptance
- Primarily a converse paper with limited novelty beyond applying standard information-theoretic results; lacks constructive algorithms or tight achievability.
- Assumptions are restrictive for practical crowdsourcing; no treatment of adaptivity, item difficulty, or realistic worker behavior beyond MSC/SHC.
- Presentation issues (typos, missing details, unclear theorem statements) reduce clarity and confidence.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify and correct Theorem 4 (state all assumptions, show explicit dependence on ˆε, and define R vs R′ precisely). Improve notation consistency and complete references (including relevant theoretical works on sample complexity/minimax rates for crowdsourcing and adaptive task allocation).
- Provide achievability analyses (even heuristic) approaching the bounds, or finite-blocklength converses/normal approximations to assess realistic budgets.
- Extend kIC beyond N = 2 and beyond SHC, and compare against pairwise incidence (k = 2) baselines under matched budget.
- Add experiments (synthetic with controlled worker reliabilities and a real dataset) to assess tightness and practical relevance.
