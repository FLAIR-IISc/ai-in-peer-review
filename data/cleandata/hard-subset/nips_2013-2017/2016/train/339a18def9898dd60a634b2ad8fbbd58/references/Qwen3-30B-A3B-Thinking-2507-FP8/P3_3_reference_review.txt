

# Peer Review: "Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing"

This paper presents an information-theoretic framework for analyzing crowdsourcing systems, focusing on the fundamental trade-off between budget (number of queries) and fidelity (error probability). The authors model crowdsourcing as a communication system where the "source" is the ground truth labels, the "channel" represents worker responses (modeled as noisy channels), and the "decoder" is the crowdsourcer's inference algorithm. The paper makes two main contributions: (1) deriving information-theoretic bounds for two key scenarios (skill levels unknown vs. known), and (2) analyzing the performance of k-ary incidence coding (kIC).

The paper is well-organized and technically sound. The authors provide a clear motivation for their information-theoretic approach, which fills an important gap in the literature where most previous work focuses on practical algorithms rather than fundamental limits. The derivation of Theorems 2 and 3 is rigorous, and the comparison between the SL-UK and SL-CS scenarios provides valuable insights about the value of knowing worker skill levels. The analysis of kIC in Section 4 is particularly strong, with Theorem 4 establishing a useful lower bound on the performance of this coding scheme.

The paper's main strength is its theoretical contribution, which establishes fundamental limits that can serve as benchmarks for future crowdsourcing system design. The authors' approach to modeling crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding problem (via Theorem 1) is elegant and well-motivated. The distinction between the SL-UK and SL-CS scenarios is particularly insightful, revealing that when workers' skill levels are unknown, the system's effective capacity is determined by an average worker's quality, whereas when skill levels are known, it's determined by the average of all workers' qualities.

However, the paper has some notable limitations. First, it's purely theoretical without empirical validation or comparison to existing practical crowdsourcing systems. While this is understandable for a theoretical paper, it would have been helpful to include a brief discussion of how the derived bounds might be interpreted in practical scenarios. Second, the assumptions made (e.g., iid items, large dataset, specific channel models) may not hold in all real-world crowdsourcing applications. For instance, the paper doesn't address how worker fatigue or varying task difficulty might affect the channel model.

The related work section is somewhat superficial. While the authors mention several key papers in the crowdsourcing literature (e.g., Dawid-Skene, EM algorithms), they don't thoroughly compare their theoretical approach to these practical methods. A more comprehensive discussion of how their fundamental limits relate to the performance of existing algorithms would strengthen the paper.

The kIC analysis is promising, but the paper could have explored more practical aspects of this coding scheme. For example, how would kIC perform with more complex clustering tasks (beyond binary labeling)? How would it handle cases where the number of clusters is unknown (which is common in real applications)? The paper's focus on the SHC model is understandable, but extending the analysis to more general channel models would be valuable.

The paper's significance is high in the context of theoretical machine learning. Understanding the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing systems is crucial for designing efficient and reliable crowdsourcing platforms. The derived bounds provide a theoretical foundation that can guide future algorithm development. The paper also offers a potential pricing strategy for query schemes (π(k₁)/π(k₂) ≈ k₁/k₂), which could have practical implications for crowdsourcing platforms.

In conclusion, this is a strong theoretical contribution that addresses an important gap in the crowdsourcing literature. The information-theoretic approach is novel and well-executed, and the derived bounds provide valuable insights into the budget-fidelity trade-off. The main limitations are the lack of empirical validation and the somewhat narrow focus on specific channel models. However, these are common trade-offs in theoretical papers, and the paper's theoretical contribution is substantial.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should consider expanding the related work section to more thoroughly compare their theoretical approach to existing practical methods, and they might want to briefly discuss how their bounds could inform practical system design.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of crowdsourcing systems, and its results would be valuable to both researchers studying crowdsourcing and practitioners designing crowdsourcing platforms. The information-theoretic framework presented here could serve as a foundation for future work exploring the fundamental limits of other human-in-the-loop learning systems.
