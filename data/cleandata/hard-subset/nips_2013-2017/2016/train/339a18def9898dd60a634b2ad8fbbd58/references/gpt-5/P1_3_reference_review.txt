Summary
The paper studies crowdsourcing through an information-theoretic lens. It models each worker as a noisy channel and a labeling/clustering task as communicating a discrete source B(X) over a collection of channels with unknown states (worker skills). The main contributions are: (i) fundamental lower bounds on the minimum number of queries per item (budget) required to achieve a target error rate (fidelity) under two scenarios: worker skills unknown to the crowdsourcer (SL-UK) vs known at the crowdsourcer (SL-CS), with workers modeled as M-ary symmetric channels (MSC); (ii) analysis of a specific non-adaptive query design, k-ary incidence coding (kIC), which shows error-correcting structure by constraining workers’ response sets; and (iii) bounds for kIC under a spammer–hammer channel (SHC), including a necessary condition on rate and a back-of-the-envelope pricing rule.

Theorems 2 and 3 follow the source–channel separation principle: the rate needed per item is the source rate-distortion R(D) (Hamming distortion) divided by the effective channel capacity per query. With worker skill unknown at the decoder, the channel reduces to the mixture MSC, so capacity corresponds to I for the averaged channel; with skill known at the decoder, capacity is the expectation of I(U;V|skill). For kIC under SHC with N=2, the paper derives the spammer’s per-query error probability and shows that if the budget per item is below a threshold depending on k, q, and the spammer error, no decoder (even an oracle that knows who is a hammer) can attain the target error; it also specializes the information-theoretic bound for SHC, showing that only hammers contribute capacity. The paper argues that kIC’s constrained response set induces error correction and briefly discusses pricing proportional to k.

Strengths
- Clear, principled use of information theory to articulate budget–fidelity trade-offs in crowdsourcing, with intuitively satisfying comparisons between “unknown” vs “known at decoder” skill scenarios.
- The separation-based derivation neatly connects R(D) of the label source to per-query capacity, giving interpretable lower bounds and a clean comparison across worker models.
- kIC is a concrete and practically motivated query scheme; the SHC bounds emphasize the benefit of packing multiple items into a query to filter spammer noise.
- The SL-UK vs SL-CS contrast is insightful: capacity of the averaged channel vs average capacity, matching classical state-known-at-decoder results.

Weaknesses
- Clarity/notation issues are substantial: multiple typos, inconsistent use of M (response alphabet) vs N (number of labels), missing years in references, and some misrendered formulas (e.g., HN(·), HM(·), and the denominator in Eq. (11)–(12)). As written, several definitions are hard to parse and would impede reproducibility.
- The core “fundamental limits” mostly follow directly from standard rate–distortion and channel coding theorems; the technical novelty beyond applying separation to this setting is limited. Tight achievability for realistic non-asymptotic regimes is not addressed.
- The kIC analysis is restricted to N=2 and SHC; the practical value would be higher with MSC or more general worker models, and with finite-sample performance and concrete decoders.
- No empirical or simulation validation: there is no comparison of kIC against majority voting, DS/EM, or spectral/EM methods on synthetic/real data to illustrate the gap to the bounds.
- The pricing discussion is heuristic and lacks a concrete cost model; cognitive/time costs of k-ary queries and platform-specific pricing are not rigorously handled.

Quality
- The overall line of argument is technically sound and consistent with standard information-theoretic results (mixture channel capacity vs state-known-at-decoder). The SL-UK/SL-CS contrast is correct and meaningful.
- However, the exposition of theorems is hard to verify due to notation glitches. Proofs are deferred to appendices; adding proof sketches in the main text would help assess correctness.
- The kIC necessary condition under SHC uses an oracle decoder; as a converse, this is appropriate, but the practical tightness remains unclear without achievability or experiments.

Clarity
- The paper needs significant editing. Specific issues:
  - Clearly separate N (number of labels) from M (response options) and state when M=N.
  - Define HN(·), HM(·), and E(·) precisely and consistently; fix equation formatting in (11)–(12).
  - Ensure all references have complete bibliographic info; fix typos (e.g., “Wiely”, “th Conference”).
  - State assumptions explicitly (iid items, non-adaptive querying, known P(B), worker independence).
  - Provide figure captions and make sure figures are included and legible.
- A small proof sketch for Theorems 2–3 would aid readability.

Originality
- Modeling crowdsourcing as communication over worker channels has appeared in various forms (e.g., Karger–Oh–Shah on task allocation and necessary/sufficient redundancy; spectral/EM works on unknown expertise). The application of separation to derive budget–fidelity bounds is a reasonable but incremental step.
- The kIC device is related to pairwise/tuplewise queries used in clustering; positioning it relative to prior “error-correcting” query designs and pairwise-constraint clustering would help.

Significance
- The bounds offer a useful benchmark for budget planning and highlight the value of modeling worker heterogeneity. The distinction between averaged channel capacity vs average capacity under state knowledge is practically relevant (e.g., using gold questions to estimate skills).
- Impact would be strengthened by empirical studies showing how close practical schemes get to these limits and by extending kIC analysis to more realistic worker models.

Questions and suggestions
- Clarify whether P(B(X)) is known or estimated and whether non-uniform priors are used in decoding.
- Discuss finite-length effects and whether near-optimal concatenated source/channel codes or practical decoders exist for moderate L.
- Extend kIC analysis beyond N=2 and beyond SHC to MSC; provide simulations comparing kIC with DS/EM and majority voting under matched worker models.
- Formalize the pricing model: account for cognitive cost scaling with k and platform constraints; justify π(k) beyond the heuristic ratio.
- Cite and compare to closely related theory on information limits in crowdsourcing and coding-based task design (e.g., other works on error-correcting query designs and necessary/sufficient redundancy).

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually clean framework connecting crowdsourcing to rate–distortion/capacity.
- Insightful SL-UK vs SL-CS comparison; interpretable kIC converse under SHC.
- Potential to guide system design and budgeting.

Cons for acceptance
- Clarity/notation problems and incomplete references.
- Limited novelty beyond applying separation; lack of achievability/experiments.
- kIC analysis narrow (binary, SHC) and pricing discussion heuristic.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper has a solid and potentially useful conceptual contribution, but clarity, scope of analysis, and lack of empirical validation limit its impact in current form. Strengthening exposition, broadening the kIC analysis, and adding experiments would substantially improve it.
