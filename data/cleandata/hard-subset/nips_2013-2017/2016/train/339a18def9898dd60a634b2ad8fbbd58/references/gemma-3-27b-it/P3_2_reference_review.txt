## Review of "Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a theoretically grounded exploration of fundamental limits in crowdsourcing, framing it as a communication system problem. The authors successfully leverage information theory to derive bounds on the trade-off between budget (number of queries) and fidelity (accuracy) under different scenarios regarding worker skill level knowledge. While the theoretical contribution is significant and the analysis is rigorous, the practical implications and connection to existing empirical work in crowdsourcing could be strengthened. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, contingent on addressing the concerns outlined below.

**Summary of Main Ideas:** The paper models crowdsourcing as a communication system, where workers are noisy channels and the goal is to reliably infer a ground truth (e.g., image label, cluster assignment) given a limited budget for querying workers. They analyze two scenarios: unknown worker skills (SL-UK) and perfectly known worker skills (SL-CS). The authors derive fundamental limits on the query budget needed to achieve a certain fidelity, and investigate the performance of k-ary incidence coding (kIC) as a specific query scheme, providing both theoretical bounds and potential pricing strategies.

**Relation to Previous Work:** The paper builds on a well-established body of work in crowdsourcing, acknowledging key contributions from Dawid & Skene, majority voting, and more recent probabilistic models. It distinguishes itself by shifting the focus from algorithmic improvements (aggregation, inference) to *fundamental limits* using an information-theoretic approach, a relatively unexplored avenue in this field. The comparison to related work is generally thorough, although a deeper discussion of the connections between the derived bounds and empirical observations from AMT-based studies (e.g., the observed cost per reliable label) would be valuable.

**Strengths:**

* **Rigorous Theoretical Framework:** The use of information theory provides a solid foundation for analyzing the inherent limitations of crowdsourcing systems. The derivations are well-structured and clearly presented.
* **Novel Perspective:** Framing crowdsourcing as a communication problem and applying tools from coding theory is a fresh and insightful approach.
* **Clear Identification of Trade-offs:** The paper clearly articulates the trade-off between budget and fidelity and demonstrates how knowledge of worker skills impacts this trade-off.
* **k-ary Incidence Coding Analysis:**  The exploration of kIC is a valuable contribution, offering a potential strategy for improving efficiency and incorporating error correction.
* **Potential for Practical Implications:**  The suggested query pricing strategy based on kIC offers a potentially useful guideline for designing cost-effective crowdsourcing systems.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Discussion of Practical Considerations:** While the theoretical bounds are interesting, the paper lacks a detailed discussion of how these bounds translate into practical guidelines for real-world crowdsourcing platforms like AMT. For example, how do worker costs, task complexity, and worker availability factor into the optimal query strategy?
* **Strong Assumptions:** The model relies on several simplifying assumptions (e.g., i.i.d. data, perfect channel state estimation in SL-CS). The impact of relaxing these assumptions is not thoroughly explored.
* **Limited Empirical Validation:**  The paper primarily focuses on theoretical analysis. While providing numerical results for kIC performance, it lacks empirical validation of the derived bounds on real-world crowdsourcing data. Demonstrating the tightness of the bounds with experiments would significantly strengthen the paper.
* **kIC Complexity:** The practical implementation of kIC, especially for large datasets and complex tasks, might be challenging. A discussion of the computational complexity and scalability of this approach is missing.
* **Connection to Existing Crowdsourcing Algorithms:** How do the proposed bounds relate to the performance of commonly used aggregation algorithms like DS or more sophisticated Bayesian approaches? Providing some comparative analysis would be beneficial.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a fundamental question about the limits of crowdsourcing performance.
* The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides valuable insights.
* The exploration of kIC offers a novel approach to query design.
* The work has the potential to influence the design of more efficient and cost-effective crowdsourcing systems.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of empirical validation limits the practical impact of the theoretical results.
* The strong assumptions and limited discussion of real-world considerations might reduce the applicability of the findings.
* The connection to existing crowdsourcing algorithms could be stronger.

**Specific Suggestions for Improvement:**

* **Empirical Validation:** Include a small-scale experiment on a real-world crowdsourcing platform (e.g., AMT) to validate the derived bounds and assess the performance of kIC.
* **Discussion of Assumptions:**  Discuss the sensitivity of the results to the simplifying assumptions made in the model.
* **Practical Considerations:**  Expand the discussion of practical considerations, such as worker costs, task complexity, and worker availability.
* **Comparison to Existing Algorithms:**  Provide a comparative analysis of the derived bounds and the performance of commonly used crowdsourcing algorithms.
* **Scalability of kIC:** Discuss the computational complexity and scalability of kIC, and suggest potential optimizations for large datasets.




**Overall Score:** 7.5/10

**Recommendation:** Accept with minor revisions.  Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly the lack of empirical validation and the limited discussion of practical considerations, would significantly strengthen the paper and enhance its impact.
