- Paper models crowdsourcing as communicating a discrete source over noisy worker channels and studies budget–fidelity trade-offs information-theoretically.
- Main contributions: lower bounds on queries per item under unknown vs known worker skills, analysis of k-ary incidence coding (kIC), and bounds/pricing for kIC under a spammer–hammer channel (SHC).
- Theorems 2–3 apply source–channel separation: required rate = R(D) / effective per-query channel capacity for SL-UK and SL-CS scenarios.
- With unknown skills (SL-UK) capacity is that of the averaged MSC channel; with skills known at decoder (SL-CS) capacity is the expected conditional capacity.
- For kIC under SHC (binary labels), the paper derives the spammer’s per-query error and a necessary budget threshold below which no decoder can achieve the target error.
- The SHC specialization shows only hammers contribute to capacity and yields a heuristic pricing rule proportional to k.
- Strength: principled, interpretable use of information theory connecting R(D) to per-query capacity and contrasting SL-UK vs SL-CS.
- Strength: kIC is a concrete, practically motivated scheme that leverages constrained responses to induce error correction.
- Weakness: substantial clarity and notation problems (typos, inconsistent M vs N, misrendered formulas, incomplete references) that impede reproducibility.
- Weakness: technical novelty is limited since results largely follow standard rate–distortion and channel coding separation; achievability in finite, realistic regimes is not shown.
- Weakness: kIC analysis is narrow (restricted to N=2 and SHC) and lacks analysis for MSC or general worker models.
- Weakness: no empirical or simulation validation comparing kIC to majority voting, DS/EM, or spectral/EM methods.
- Weakness: pricing discussion is heuristic and omits cognitive/time cost and platform constraints.
- Quality: arguments are technically sound and consistent with known information-theoretic results, but theorem exposition and proofs need clearer presentation.
- Clarity: paper requires major editing—define notation precisely, fix equations, state assumptions, and include figure captions and proof sketches.
- Originality: framing is incremental relative to prior work on crowdsourcing channel models and error-correcting queries; kIC relates to existing tuplewise/pairwise query designs.
- Significance: bounds provide useful budgeting benchmarks and highlight the value of skill estimation, but impact is limited without experiments and broader kIC analysis.
- Suggestions: clarify prior distribution assumptions, address finite-length effects, extend kIC beyond binary/SHC, run simulations vs standard methods, and formalize pricing.
- Pros for acceptance: clean conceptual framework, insightful SL-UK vs SL-CS contrast, and informative kIC converse under SHC.
- Cons for acceptance: clarity issues, limited novelty, narrow kIC scope, and lack of empirical validation.
- Recommendation: borderline reject pending improved exposition, broader analysis, and experimental validation.
