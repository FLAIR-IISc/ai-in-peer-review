## Review of "Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing, specifically the trade-off between budget (number of queries) and fidelity (accuracy). It models crowdsourcing as a communication system and derives information-theoretic bounds on the achievable performance, considering both scenarios where worker skill levels are unknown and perfectly known.  The authors also introduce and analyze a k-ary incidence coding (kIC) scheme as a concrete example, aiming for improved error correction capabilities.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon existing research in crowdsourcing, particularly work on aggregation schemes like majority voting and the Dawid-Skene algorithm (DS) [5].  It moves beyond algorithmic approaches to focus on fundamental *limits*, drawing connections to information theory and channel coding. It references and positions itself appropriately within the existing literature on worker modeling [8, 6, 13] and optimization techniques [1, 11].  The kIC scheme has similarities to approaches leveraging pairwise comparisons [11] but generalizes it to k-ary comparisons.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper’s strength lies in its information-theoretic approach.  Framing crowdsourcing as a communication problem and deriving fundamental limits provides valuable insights beyond empirical optimization. The theorems (2, 3, and 4) establish clear boundaries on achievable performance.
* **Rigorous Analysis:** The mathematical derivations appear sound (though a detailed check requires significant effort). The proofs are referenced to appendices, which is good practice.
* **Novel Perspective:**  The k-ary incidence coding scheme presents a new approach to query design and offers a potential improvement over simpler methods, particularly in noisy environments.
* **Clear Motivation:** The introduction clearly articulates the problem of crowdsourcing and the motivation for studying its fundamental limits.
* **Consideration of Worker Skill:** The analysis of both unknown and known worker skill levels provides a more nuanced understanding of the problem.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Practicality & Assumptions:** While theoretically sound, the assumptions underlying the models (e.g., i.i.d. source, perfectly known skill levels in one scenario, MSC/SHC channel models) are strong and may not hold in real-world crowdsourcing platforms like AMT. The relevance of these theoretical bounds to practical scenarios needs further discussion.
* **kIC Analysis Complexity:** The analysis of kIC, while a valuable contribution, seems complex and relies on an 'oracle decoder' that perfectly identifies spammer/hammer workers. This significantly simplifies the analysis and may not reflect real-world decoding challenges.  The query pricing discussion (Appendix E) feels somewhat tacked on and could be more developed.
* **Limited Empirical Validation:** The paper lacks any empirical validation. While the authors focus on theoretical bounds, providing even a small-scale simulation to demonstrate the benefits of kIC compared to simpler schemes would strengthen the work.
* **Clarity of Notation:** Some of the notation (e.g., HN( )) can be a bit dense and requires careful reading.  More explanation within the text would be helpful.
* **Presentation:** The paper feels a bit dry and could benefit from more intuitive explanations of the information-theoretic concepts.

**Addressing the Review Criteria:**

* **Quality:**  Technically sound, with well-supported claims. However, the strength of the analysis is diminished by the strong assumptions and lack of empirical validation. The paper presents a complete theoretical work, but it feels somewhat disconnected from practical realities.
* **Clarity:**  Generally well-written, but some sections (particularly involving the mathematical derivations) could be more accessible. The notation could be improved.
* **Originality:** The application of information theory to crowdsourcing and the introduction of kIC are novel contributions. The paper adequately references related work.
* **Significance:** The results are potentially significant in providing fundamental limits for crowdsourcing.  However, the practical impact is unclear without further investigation and empirical validation.  If the bounds are very far from achievable in practice, the significance is reduced.



**Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For:**

* Provides a rigorous theoretical framework for understanding crowdsourcing limitations.
* Introduces a novel coding scheme (kIC) with potential benefits.
* Offers valuable insights into the impact of worker skill levels.

**Against:**

* Strong assumptions may limit the applicability of the results.
* Lack of empirical validation.
* Complexity of kIC analysis and reliance on an oracle decoder.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid theoretical contribution to the field of crowdsourcing.  However, its lack of empirical validation and strong assumptions raise concerns about its practical relevance.  I would rate this paper as **weak accept**.  

**Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should:

1. **Discuss the limitations of their assumptions:**  A section acknowledging the differences between the theoretical model and real-world crowdsourcing platforms would be valuable.
2. **Consider adding a small-scale simulation:**  Demonstrating the potential benefits of kIC, even in a simplified setting, would strengthen the paper.
3. **Improve clarity of notation:** Provide more intuitive explanations of key concepts.
4. **Expand discussion of practical implications:**  Connect the theoretical bounds to potential applications and future research directions.
