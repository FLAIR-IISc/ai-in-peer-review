- Proposes an information-theoretic framework modeling crowdsourcing as a source-channel coding problem to derive budget-fidelity bounds.  
- Derivations of Theorems 2 and 3 are logical and appendices provide technical rigor.  
- Key assumptions limit soundness: workers assumed i.i.d., simplified channel models (MSC, SHC), and asymptotic regime (L → ∞).  
- SHC (spammer-hammer) model is overly restrictive and fails to capture varying worker reliability.  
- Lack of empirical validation; numerical results only compare theory to kIC and don't show practical utility.  
- Explanation and transition to k-ary incidence coding (kIC) is confusing, especially its relation to the binary incidence matrix.  
- Novelty: applying source-channel separation to crowdsourcing is fresh, but paper insufficiently cites/compares recent related work (e.g., NIPS 2014).  
- Significance limited by absence of demonstrated practical improvements, platform design guidance, or pricing recommendations.  
- kIC analysis is interesting and suggests error-correcting capability but lacks empirical demonstration of real-world benefit.  
- Critical weaknesses: simplistic channel models, no empirical validation, unrealistic asymptotic assumptions, and limited practical insights.  
- Strengths: conceptually elegant information-theoretic framework, useful SL-UK vs SL-CS distinction, and a potentially valuable kIC query scheme.  
- Recommendation: solid theoretical contribution but insufficient for NIPS—recommend rejection.  
- Suggested improvements: provide empirical validation, connect bounds to practical gains and pricing, model more realistic worker behavior, and compare to recent NIPS work.
