- Presents an information-theoretic framing of crowdsourcing as a communication system to derive budget–fidelity limits.
- Analyzes two scenarios: unknown worker skills (SL-UK) and perfectly known worker skills (SL-CS).
- Derives fundamental query-budget bounds needed to achieve target fidelity.
- Investigates k-ary incidence coding (kIC) as a query scheme and analyzes its performance and pricing implications.
- Theoretical contributions are rigorous and derivations are well-structured.
- Novel perspective by applying coding and information theory to crowdsourcing limits.
- Clearly identifies how knowledge of worker skills affects the budget–fidelity trade-off.
- kIC analysis offers a potential strategy for improved efficiency and error correction.
- Lacks detailed discussion of translating theoretical bounds to real-world platforms like AMT.
- Relies on strong simplifying assumptions (e.g., i.i.d. data, perfect channel state in SL-CS) without sensitivity analysis.
- Limited empirical validation; numerical results exist but no real-world experiments to test bound tightness.
- Practical implementation and scalability of kIC, especially for large/complex tasks, are not addressed.
- Weak connection/comparison to existing aggregation algorithms (e.g., Dawid & Skene, Bayesian methods).
- Recommends acceptance with minor revisions contingent on addressing empirical and practical concerns.
- Specific improvements suggested: add real-world experiments, discuss assumption sensitivity, expand practical considerations, compare to existing algorithms, and analyze kIC scalability.
- Overall score: 7.5/10; recommendation: accept with minor revisions.
