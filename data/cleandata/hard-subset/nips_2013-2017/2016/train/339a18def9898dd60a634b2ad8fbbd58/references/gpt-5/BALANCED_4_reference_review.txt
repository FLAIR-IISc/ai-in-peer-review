Summary and relation to prior work
The paper models crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding (JSCC) problem to derive fundamental budget–fidelity trade-offs. Items X are mapped via a function B(X) (e.g., labels/clusters), queries to workers are modeled as uses of noisy channels, and the decoder aggregates responses to estimate B̂(X). Two worker models are considered: (i) a mixture of M-ary symmetric channels (MSC) with heterogeneous error rates; and (ii) a spammer–hammer channel (SHC) where a worker is either perfect or random. The authors derive lower bounds on the minimum number of queries per item needed to attain a target Hamming distortion (error rate) under two knowledge regimes: SL-UK (worker skills unknown to the decoder) and SL-CS (skills known to the decoder). They invoke the source–channel separation theorem: feasibility requires channel capacity exceeding the source rate at the target distortion. They then analyze a specific non-adaptive coding scheme, k-ary incidence coding (kIC), deriving a necessary rate condition for SHC with an oracle decoder and providing a heuristic pricing rule across different k.

This conceptual mapping resonates with prior NIPS and related work on the statistics and sample complexity of crowdsourcing aggregation: Dawid–Skene EM [DS’79]; budget-optimal task allocation and reliability/sample-complexity analyses by Karger–Oh–Shah (e.g., NIPS/Operations Research); spectral/EM hybrids with guarantees (Zhang et al., NIPS’14); and minimax rate analyses for label aggregation (e.g., Gao & Zhou, ICML’16; Shah, Balakrishnan, Wainwright). The novelty here is framing these limits explicitly via information-theoretic source and channel quantities and using this to compare SL-UK vs. SL-CS and to study kIC.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: Casting budget–fidelity trade-offs in the JSCC framework is clean and unifying, and the SL-UK vs. SL-CS contrast is insightful (average capacity of an average worker vs. average of capacities).
- General lower bounds: Theorems 2–3 provide simple, closed-form necessary conditions based on entropies and capacities for the MSC mixture.
- Design insight: The kIC analysis highlights implicit error-correction via invalid response elimination and yields a simple pricing heuristic π(k1)/π(k2) ≈ (k1/k2).
- Scope: The framework can accommodate many query types, heterogeneous workers, and different distortion measures.

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Quality (soundness and evaluation)
- Separation assumptions: The mixture over workers induces a channel with random state per use. The “capacity” used in Theorems 2–3 appears to be log M − H_M(·), with averaging placed differently for SL-UK vs. SL-CS. For channels with i.i.d. state unknown to encoder and possibly known/unknown to decoder, the true fundamental limit depends on state knowledge (e.g., averaged capacity vs. capacity of a compound/mixture channel). Please formalize the assumptions (is the state revealed to the decoder in SL-CS per use? is coding across uses allowed to exploit state?) and justify the separation step rigorously for each case.
- Notation and definitions: The expressions in (11)–(12) use both N (source alphabet) and M (response alphabet) and H_N(·), H_M(·); please define all entropy terms precisely and ensure consistency (e.g., H_N(ε̂) is the N-ary entropy; E(H_M(ε)) vs. H_M(E(ε))). Currently this is hard to verify.
- Finite-sample and non-asymptotic behavior are not addressed. The achievability/converse arguments rely on n → ∞; practical guidance would benefit from finite-length or sample-complexity style bounds as in prior crowdsourcing work.
- kIC lower bound (Theorem 4) relies on an oracle that perfectly identifies spammers/hammers and uses independence approximations. Please clarify the integrality constraint (R′ ∈ Z+), how items are sampled across queries, and verify monotonicity/signs in the bound (ln(1−q), ln(ε̄S) < 0). A comparison to empirical performance or to non-oracle decoders would help gauge tightness.
- No experimental validation on synthetic or real datasets is provided; the paper includes only a qualitative plot. This limits the assessment of significance and practical impact.

Clarity
- The paper has numerous typographical/formatting issues (equations broken, missing years in references, figure references with limited captions). The presentation of kIC (valid/invalid configurations, effect of N) could be tightened.
- Some claims (e.g., pricing rule) are relegated to appendices without enough intuition in the main text.
- It would help to include a table of notation and a concise problem statement before theorems.

Originality
- While the JSCC perspective is elegant, the core insight—trade-offs between redundancy and worker noise—has been studied extensively via statistical/sample complexity analyses in the crowdsourcing literature. The paper would benefit from a deeper positioning relative to:
  - Minimax/sample complexity for label aggregation and worker confusion matrices (e.g., Gao & Zhou, 2016; Shah et al.; Ghosh et al.).
  - Adaptive vs. non-adaptive allocation (Abraham et al., COLT’13; Karger–Oh–Shah).
  - Pairwise/incidence queries and their information content (e.g., work on clustering via pairwise comparisons).
Please expand related work and clarify what is provably new beyond rephrasing with information-theoretic terms.

Significance
- The high-level bounds could guide system design, but practical uptake requires: (i) tighter, model-robust bounds; (ii) empirical studies; (iii) discussion of model misspecification (non-i.i.d. items, adversaries beyond SHC, varying task difficulties). Currently, impact on real CS system design is speculative.

Pros for acceptance
- Clean, general formulation of budget–fidelity via JSCC.
- Clear comparison of SL-UK vs. SL-CS regimes.
- Analysis of kIC illuminates structure in query design.

Cons for acceptance
- Technical gaps/ambiguities in the use of capacity for stateful channels and notation.
- Limited novelty relative to existing sample complexity/minimax literature; related work is incomplete.
- No empirical validation; bounds’ tightness unclear.
- Clarity and presentation issues.

Suggestions for improvement
- Formalize the channel model with state: specify exactly what is known to encoder/decoder per use and justify the capacity quantities used in Theorems 2–3 (e.g., cite Shannon with side information at the receiver; Gel’fand–Pinsker-type distinctions if relevant).
- Unify notation (N vs. M), define all entropy terms, and provide full proofs or clear proof sketches in the main paper for key results.
- Compare theoretically and empirically with DS, spectral-EM, and minimax-optimal algorithms; include synthetic experiments demonstrating how the bounds predict the observed accuracy vs. budget.
- Discuss finite-sample effects and provide non-asymptotic bounds or at least guidelines for practical L.
- Expand related work to include minimax/sample complexity literature on crowdsourcing and pairwise/incidence labeling.
- For kIC: explain the invalid-response “error-correction” more formally; extend beyond N=2; quantify cost/latency vs. k; and test the pricing rule against real platform data or controlled user studies.
- Consider adaptive designs and worker selection, and discuss how knowledge of skills (SL-CS) can be estimated in practice.

Recommendation
Reject. The paper offers an appealing conceptual framework and some useful bounds, but the current version lacks technical precision, novelty positioning, and empirical validation needed for NeurIPS. The above revisions could significantly strengthen the work.
