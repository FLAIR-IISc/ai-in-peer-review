Summary
The paper models label crowdsourcing as a communication problem and studies fundamental budget–fidelity trade-offs via information-theoretic tools. The dataset labels are treated as a source; workers are modeled as noisy channels (either M-ary symmetric channels or a spammer–hammer mixture); query schemes are “codes”; and the aggregator is a decoder. Using source–channel separation, the authors derive lower bounds on the minimum number of queries per item needed to achieve a target error rate under two regimes: (i) worker skill levels unknown to the crowdsourcer (SL-UK) and (ii) perfectly known to the crowdsourcer (SL-CS). They also introduce k-ary incidence coding (kIC), in which each query asks about k items jointly, argue it has “built-in error correction” by constraining valid responses, and derive a necessary rate condition for kIC under a spammer–hammer pool. A simple pricing heuristic for kIC is discussed. The work is related to prior NIPS literature on EM-based aggregation (Dawid–Skene variants), spectral/convex methods for crowdsourcing, and task allocation/budget–reliability tradeoffs (e.g., Karger–Oh–Shah).

Quality
- Technical soundness: The high-level derivations via source–channel separation are broadly plausible. For SL-UK with an MSC population, the effective channel seen by the encoder/decoder is again symmetric with crossover E[ε], yielding a capacity of log M − H_M(E[ε]); under SL-CS, the decoder-side information yields average capacity log M − E[H_M(ε)]. On the source side, the use of the Hamming distortion rate–distortion term H(B) − H_N(ε̂) with the standard distortion feasibility constraints involving p_max is consistent with classical results for N-ary Hamming distortion in the symmetric regime. The comparison between SL-UK and SL-CS (average channel vs. average of capacities) is correct and insightful.
- However, the paper provides only statements; the proofs are deferred to appendices (not included here), and several definitions are imprecise. The kIC analysis is restricted to binary labels and a spammer–hammer model, and Theorem 4 is a necessary condition under an oracle decoder. The strength (tightness) of these bounds is unclear. No empirical validation, simulations, or comparison to practical aggregation schemes (majority voting, DS, spectral/EM) are provided, which weakens the evaluation.
- Some claims (e.g., “error-correction capability” of kIC) are intuitive but would benefit from precise coding arguments (e.g., enumerating valid response sets for general N, or showing ML decoding gains).

Clarity
- The paper’s presentation is uneven. There are numerous typographical issues, missing years in references, and fragmented figures (“th Conference…”). Key notation is overloaded or undefined: N (number of classes) vs. M (number of choices in a response) vs. k (query arity) need careful separation; E(ε) is used informally; H_N(·) is defined but its applicability conditions are only partially stated; p_max constraint appears without full derivation. Some equations (e.g., 11, 12) have formatting glitches.
- The system model is helpful, but assumptions need clearer statements: iid dataset labels, channel states iid across queries, independence across workers and items, non-adaptive querying, no feedback or feedforward (except implicit dependence of spammer error on k), and whether the decoder knows worker identities per response.
- The exposition of kIC would benefit from a formal count of valid response configurations for general N and k, and a clean derivation of the spammer error probability. The pricing rule is ambiguous in notation and seems to reduce to “price ∝ k” without justification of market or cognitive costs.

Originality
- Mapping crowdsourcing to joint source–channel coding and separating SL-UK vs. SL-CS to obtain distinct capacity terms is a nice and clear articulation; to my knowledge, the particular explicit lower bounds in Theorems 2–3 are not commonly stated in this compact form in the crowdsourcing literature.
- That said, related investigations of budget–reliability limits and task allocation exist (e.g., Karger–Oh–Shah; and several works on minimax rates and optimal aggregation) and should be discussed more thoroughly. The kIC idea is closely related to pairwise/tuplewise queries used for clustering; the novelty lies more in the information-theoretic framing than in the query itself.
- The kIC necessary-condition bound under SHC is new but limited in scope (binary labels, oracle decoder).

Significance
- Establishing clean, interpretable lower bounds can guide system design, especially in contrasting unknown vs. known worker skills. The insight “capacity of the average worker” vs. “average of capacities” is practically relevant.
- The practical impact is limited by the lack of algorithmic contributions, empirical validation, and coverage of more realistic settings (non-uniform labels, multi-class, adversarial spammers beyond uniform random, item difficulty, adaptivity/active querying). The kIC results are necessary-only and do not demonstrate achievable gains.

Pros
- Clear, principled information-theoretic framing of crowdsourcing.
- Useful comparison between SL-UK and SL-CS regimes.
- Introduces k-ary incidence queries and connects them to error constraints.
- Potentially broadly applicable lower bounds that can inform budgeting.

Cons
- Lack of experiments/simulations; no comparison to standard aggregation methods.
- Limited scope of kIC analysis (N=2, SHC, oracle decoder); no achievability.
- Clarity/notation issues and many typographical errors; some formulas are hard to parse.
- Related work coverage is narrow; misses some close analyses of limits/task allocation.
- Pricing heuristic is vague and not empirically grounded.

Questions for the authors
- Under what conditions on P(B) does R(D)=H(B)−H_N(D) hold for your theorems? Please clarify the exact distortion range and provide a citation.
- For SL-UK, please justify rigorously that the mixture over MSCs yields an MSC with parameter E[ε] for capacity purposes (and clarify any assumptions about iid states per use).
- Can you provide simulations showing how close majority vote, DS, and spectral/EM methods get to your lower bounds under MSC/SHC?
- For kIC, can you extend beyond N=2 and provide achievable schemes (not just necessary conditions)? How large are the gains vs. k=1 at equal cognitive cost?
- Please clarify the pricing rule derivation and its assumptions.

Suggestions for improvement
- Tighten notation and fix formatting; carefully distinguish N (classes), M (answer options), and k (arity).
- Include complete proofs or at least proof sketches in the main text for the key theorems; make the RD applicability conditions explicit.
- Add empirical studies validating the bounds and illustrating the SL-UK vs. SL-CS gap.
- Extend kIC analysis to multi-class, non-uniform priors, and provide achievable strategies or decoding rules.
- Discuss adaptivity and item difficulty as future extensions with concrete pointers.
- Expand related work to include additional theoretical analyses of crowdsourcing limits and task allocation.

Recommendation
Lean reject. The conceptual framing is appealing and the bounds are potentially useful, but the current submission lacks clarity, completeness, and empirical or algorithmic validation. With improved exposition, tighter theory (including achievability or tighter necessary bounds), and experiments, this could become a strong contribution.
