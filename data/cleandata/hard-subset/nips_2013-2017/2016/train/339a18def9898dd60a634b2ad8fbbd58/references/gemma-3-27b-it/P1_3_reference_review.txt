## Peer Review: Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing (CS) in terms of the trade-off between budget (number of queries) and fidelity (accuracy). It models CS as a communication system and uses information-theoretic tools to derive lower bounds on the required budget for a given fidelity level, considering both scenarios where worker skill levels are known and unknown to the taskmaster. The authors also introduce and analyze a specific query scheme, k-ary incidence coding (kIC), offering potential practical benefits through error correction. The work aims to provide a theoretical foundation for designing optimized CS systems.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon existing research in CS, which largely focuses on aggregation and inference algorithms (e.g., Dawid-Skene, majority voting) and worker selection (e.g., bandit surveys).  It differentiates itself by taking a more fundamental, information-theoretic approach to define ultimate performance limits, rather than optimizing specific algorithms. The references provided (e.g., Dawid & Skene [5], Karger et al. [7]) are appropriately cited and contextualized within the broader field. The connection to classical information theory (Cover & Thomas [6], El Gamal & Kim [8]) is explicitly made, establishing a solid theoretical foundation.  The introduction of kIC relates to previous work on incidence coding for clustering [11] but extends it within this more general information-theoretic framework.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper employs a strong information-theoretic framework, providing fundamental limits on CS performance. This offers a valuable benchmark against which to evaluate practical algorithms.
* **Clear Modeling:** The communication system model effectively captures the key components of a CS system (source, workers as channels, queries as signals, and inference as decoding).
* **Novel Insights:**  The derivation of budget-fidelity bounds for both known and unknown worker skill levels provides novel theoretical insights into the performance limitations of CS.
* **Practical Relevance (kIC):** The introduction and analysis of kIC provides a concrete query scheme with potential advantages (error correction) for practical CS applications.  The discussion of query pricing based on k is interesting.
* **Comprehensive Analysis:**  The paper explores both MSC and SHC worker models, adding to the robustness of the analysis. 
* **Well-structured:** The paper is logically organized, beginning with a clear introduction, progressing through the model and theoretical development, and culminating in discussion and analysis.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions:** The analysis relies on several simplifying assumptions (e.g., independent workers, i.i.d. data, perfectly known skill levels in one scenario). While justifiable for initial analysis, the impact of relaxing these assumptions isn’t fully explored. The assumption of large block length (n -> infinity) is standard in information theory but limits the direct applicability of the bounds to realistic, finite-sized datasets.
* **Limited Empirical Validation:** The paper lacks empirical validation of the derived bounds. While theoretical results are valuable, demonstrating their tightness through simulations or real-world experiments would significantly strengthen the work.  The numerical results are limited to comparing bounds, rather than demonstrating performance on specific datasets.
* **Complexity of kIC Analysis:** The analysis of kIC, while promising, is complex and relies on the oracle decoder assumption.  The practical challenges of implementing and decoding kIC efficiently are not fully addressed.
* **Limited Discussion of Adaptive Coding:** The paper focuses on non-adaptive coding schemes. The potential benefits of adaptive query strategies, leveraging feedback from previous responses, are acknowledged but not investigated.
* **Pricing Discussion:** The query pricing strategy based on k is a little hand-wavy and could benefit from more detailed cost analysis considering the actual costs associated with queries.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of crowdsourcing. The information-theoretic framework is well-applied, and the derived bounds provide a crucial benchmark for evaluating practical CS algorithms. While the simplifying assumptions and lack of empirical validation are weaknesses, the theoretical rigor and novelty of the work outweigh these concerns.  This is a solid paper that advances the state of the art in understanding the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Provides fundamental theoretical limits on CS performance.
* Introduces a potentially valuable query scheme (kIC).
* Presents a clear and well-structured analysis.
* Builds upon and extends existing work in CS and information theory.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*  Relies on simplifying assumptions.
* Lacks empirical validation.
* Limited discussion of practical implementation challenges for kIC.
*  Focuses solely on non-adaptive coding.

**Rating:**  **Accept with Minor Revisions**

**Recommendations for Revision:**

* **Address the impact of relaxed assumptions:** Discuss how the bounds might change with more realistic assumptions (e.g., correlated workers, non-i.i.d. data).
* **Add empirical validation:** Include simulations or real-world experiments to demonstrate the tightness of the derived bounds and evaluate the performance of kIC.
* **Expand discussion of kIC:**  Elaborate on the practical challenges of implementing and decoding kIC, and potential solutions.
* **Briefly explore adaptive coding:**  Discuss the potential benefits and challenges of adaptive query strategies.
* **Refine pricing discussion:** provide a more detailed analysis of the costs associated with kIC queries for a better query pricing scheme.
