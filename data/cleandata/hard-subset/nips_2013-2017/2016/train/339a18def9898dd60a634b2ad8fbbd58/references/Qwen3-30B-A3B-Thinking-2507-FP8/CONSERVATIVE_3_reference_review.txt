

# Review of "Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing"

This paper attempts to model crowdsourcing as an information-theoretic communication problem, establishing fundamental limits on the budget-fidelity trade-off using channel coding theory. The authors propose two channel models (M-ary symmetric channel and spammer-hammer channel) and introduce a novel "k-ary incidence coding" (kIC) scheme where workers are shown k items at once to identify cluster memberships. The paper derives theoretical bounds for the minimum queries per item needed to achieve a target error rate in two scenarios: when worker skill levels are unknown (SL-UK) and when they are known (SL-CS).

**Strengths:** The theoretical foundation is sound, with clear information-theoretic derivations and proofs provided in the appendices. The paper makes a legitimate attempt to connect crowdsourcing to communication theory, which is an interesting perspective. The kIC scheme is conceptually novel, and the authors correctly identify the relationship between source coding and channel coding in this context.

**Weaknesses:** Despite the theoretical elegance, the paper suffers from critical shortcomings that undermine its contribution to the field. Most significantly, the authors provide no empirical validation of their theoretical bounds against established crowdsourcing methods. The paper claims to establish "fundamental limits" but fails to demonstrate how these limits translate to practical performance improvements over existing approaches like majority voting or the Dawid-Skene algorithm. Without such validation, the theoretical bounds remain abstract and disconnected from real-world crowdsourcing systems.

The paper's modeling assumptions are overly simplistic and do not reflect the complexity of actual crowdsourcing platforms. For instance, the assumption of iid items, uniform worker skill distribution, and no feedback from the decoder to the encoder are highly unrealistic for practical crowdsourcing. The paper cites [8] as using a similar EM formulation, yet fails to properly contrast their theoretical approach with the established empirical methods that have been successfully applied in practice.

The proposed k-ary incidence coding scheme is theoretically interesting but lacks practical justification. The paper claims "improved overall CS performance" due to "error correction capability," but provides no evidence that this translates to better accuracy in real crowdsourcing tasks. The numerical results in Figure 2 compare the information-theoretic bounds to kIC performance, but fail to show how kIC compares to existing methods like Dawid-Skene or majority voting on standard crowdsourcing benchmarks.

Perhaps most concerning is the paper's failure to address the most critical question: how do these theoretical bounds inform the design of practical crowdsourcing systems? The authors mention "query pricing strategy" in Appendix E, but this remains a vague suggestion without empirical support. For a paper claiming to establish "fundamental limits" for crowdsourcing, this is a significant oversight.

The paper also fails to adequately contextualize its contributions within the existing crowdsourcing literature. While it cites several relevant papers (e.g., [5, 8, 11]), it doesn't clearly articulate how its theoretical approach differs from or improves upon established methods. The connection to the widely used Dawid-Skene algorithm is particularly underdeveloped, despite its status as a standard baseline in the field.

**Recommendation:** While the paper presents a theoretically interesting framework, it falls short of making a meaningful contribution to the field of crowdsourcing. The lack of empirical validation against established baselines, the unrealistic modeling assumptions, and the failure to demonstrate practical relevance render the theoretical results largely disconnected from real-world crowdsourcing applications. The paper would benefit from extensive empirical evaluation comparing the proposed kIC scheme to existing methods on standard crowdsourcing datasets, as well as a more thorough discussion of how these theoretical bounds translate to practical system design.

This paper is too theoretical and lacks the empirical grounding required for NIPS. The authors have presented a novel theoretical perspective but failed to demonstrate its practical value. Without evidence that these theoretical bounds translate to improved performance in real crowdsourcing systems, the contribution remains speculative. The paper would need to be substantially revised with comprehensive empirical validation to be considered for publication at NIPS.

**Pro/Con Summary:**
- *Pro:* Theoretical framework is mathematically sound; introduces a novel kIC scheme.
- *Con:* No empirical validation against established baselines; overly simplistic assumptions; fails to demonstrate practical relevance; lacks comparison to standard crowdsourcing methods; theoretical results disconnected from real-world implementation.

Given the lack of empirical validation and the disconnect between theory and practice, I recommend rejection. The paper presents an interesting theoretical angle but fails to meet NIPS's standards for empirical rigor and practical significance.
