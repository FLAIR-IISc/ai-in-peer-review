Summary
The paper models label crowdsourcing as a communication system and studies fundamental budget–fidelity trade-offs via information theory. Items are sources, queries are encodings, workers are noisy channels (either M-ary symmetric channels or spammer–hammer channels), and the crowdsourcer is a decoder. Using Shannon’s source–channel separation, the authors derive lower bounds on the minimum number of queries per item needed to achieve a target average error under two regimes: (i) worker skill levels unknown to the crowdsourcer (SL-UK), and (ii) worker skill levels perfectly known to the crowdsourcer (SL-CS). The resulting formulas relate the source rate–distortion function with Hamming distortion to the effective channel capacity: for SL-UK the capacity corresponds to the averaged channel (entropy of the average error), and for SL-CS to the average of per-worker capacities (average of entropies), clarifying the value of estimating worker skills. The paper also studies k-ary incidence coding (kIC), where each query presents k items and only logically consistent clusterings are allowed, yielding an error-correcting effect. Under a spammer–hammer pool, it derives a lower bound on achievable error for kIC with an oracle decoder, and a simple “pricing” rule-of-thumb relating per-query cost to k.

Relation to prior work
The work connects to a rich literature on aggregation (majority vote, Dawid–Skene), spectral/EM approaches, and budget-reliability trade-offs (e.g., Karger–Oh–Shah; Abraham et al. bandit surveys). It also relates to clustering via pairwise/triplet constraints and convex formulations (e.g., Vinayak–Oymak–Hassibi). The information-theoretic framing appears less explored in the crowdsourcing literature and is a useful unifying lens; the specific bounds are standard consequences of rate–distortion and channel capacity for symmetric channels. The kIC idea overlaps with comparative judgments/triplet learning; the paper emphasizes its coding-theoretic redundancy.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The separation-based lower bounds (Theorems 2–3) for MSC workers with Hamming distortion look correct and follow standard results: R*(D)=H(B)−HN(D) and C=log M−H_M(p), with the distinction between “capacity of the averaged channel” (SL-UK) and “average capacity with decoder side information” (SL-CS). The intuition and Jensen’s inequality–based ordering are right.
- kIC analysis: The SHC-based necessary condition (Theorem 4) is plausible: the only way to err with an oracle decoder is when all R′ occurrences of an item go to spammers and those spammers collectively induce an error with probability ε̄_S(k). This yields (1−q)^{kR} ε̄_S ≤ ε, giving the stated rate bound up to log-sign conventions. The lemma for ε̄_S(k) is reasonable albeit presented without proof here.
- Completeness: Proofs are deferred to appendices; main-text proof sketches would help readers assess nuances (e.g., independence assumptions in kIC; whether mixing MSCs remains MSC; state information at decoder). No empirical validation or constructive algorithms are provided beyond a schematic figure.

Clarity
The presentation needs substantial polishing:
- Notation is heavy and sometimes inconsistent (N vs M; E(·) not clearly defined; hats/bars). Some equations are malformed in the excerpt (e.g., missing braces, garbled fractions).
- The roles of encoder/decoder/taskmaster/crowdsourcer could be clarified, especially why the encoder cannot adapt in SL-CS even when the decoder knows skills.
- The “pricing” rule appears as π(k1)/π(k2)≈k1/k2 but is written ambiguously in the text; justify assumptions behind linear-in-k costs.
- References are incomplete (missing years, typos, incorrect publisher spelling), and some related areas (triplet comparisons, learning to rank from comparisons) could be better covered.

Originality
- Framing crowdsourcing budget–fidelity trade-offs via source–channel separation is a neat and relatively novel perspective for this community, even if the resulting formulas are straightforward once the mapping is fixed.
- The kIC lower bound under SHC with oracle decoding is specific but not previously articulated (to my knowledge). Still, the novelty is incremental given the idealized assumptions.

Significance
- The clean comparison between SL-UK and SL-CS provides conceptual guidance: estimating worker skills increases “capacity,” quantifiably.
- The results could inform high-level system design (e.g., how much redundancy is needed; whether to invest in skill estimation; value of higher-arity queries).
- Practical impact is limited by strong assumptions (iid items, symmetric channels, infinite blocklengths, non-adaptive assignment, known source distribution, oracle decoding for kIC) and absence of empirical studies showing tightness or practical codes approaching the bounds.

Strengths
- Clear, principled information-theoretic treatment of CS budget–fidelity trade-offs.
- Insightful contrast between unknown vs known worker skills.
- Formalizes and analyzes k-ary incidence coding as an error-correcting mechanism.
- Potentially useful conceptual benchmarks for practitioners.

Weaknesses
- Presentation issues: notation, formatting, and references hinder readability.
- Heavy reliance on idealized assumptions; limited discussion of when they hold.
- No empirical validation or constructive schemes approaching the limits.
- kIC analysis assumes an oracle and independence that may not hold with shared queries; dependency structure not addressed.

Questions for authors
- Can you provide proof sketches in the main text for Theorems 2–4, highlighting key assumptions (especially for kIC independence)?
- How tight are the lower bounds in finite regimes? Any simulations comparing majority vote/DS/optimal decoders to your limits?
- Can the framework handle adaptive task allocation or difficulty-dependent noise (input-dependent state), beyond the brief feedforward remark?
- Clarify the cost model for kIC pricing and correct the ambiguous formula.
- How sensitive are the results to deviations from symmetry (e.g., class imbalance, asymmetric confusions)?

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually clean and informative theoretical framing.
- Provides baseline limits that can orient future algorithm design.
- Novel perspective on k-ary queries as codes.

Cons for acceptance
- Incremental technically (direct application of classical IT).
- Limited practical relevance without empirical/constructive components.
- Clarity and editorial issues are substantial.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper offers a useful perspective and some interesting bounds, but the contribution is primarily a direct instantiation of standard information-theoretic results under strong assumptions, the kIC analysis is idealized, and the presentation requires significant improvement. Strengthening clarity, adding empirical evidence and/or constructive algorithms approaching the bounds, and discussing adaptivity and more realistic worker models would substantially improve the paper.
