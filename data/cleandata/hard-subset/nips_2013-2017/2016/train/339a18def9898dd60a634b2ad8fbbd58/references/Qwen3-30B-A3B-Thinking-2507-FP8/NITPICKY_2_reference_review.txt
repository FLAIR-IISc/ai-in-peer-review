

# Review of "Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing"

This paper attempts to model crowdsourcing as an information-theoretic communication problem and establish fundamental limits for the budget-fidelity trade-off. While the problem is interesting and the paper makes a reasonable attempt to connect crowdsourcing to information theory, it contains numerous technical, notational, and formatting issues that significantly undermine its contribution.

## Major Technical Issues

The paper contains fundamental mathematical errors that invalidate key results. In Equation (1), the MSC channel model is defined as "P(v|u) = {M-1 v = u 1- v = u}", which is mathematically nonsensical. The correct definition should be "1-ε for v=u and ε/(M-1) for v≠u" (assuming ε is the error probability). This error propagates through the paper and invalidates the entire theoretical foundation.

Theorem 2 contains multiple notational inconsistencies. The expression "H_N(̂)" appears without definition, and the symbol "̂" is used inconsistently for both the error probability and the estimated label. The paper should use a consistent symbol for the error probability (e.g., δ) throughout.

In Lemma 1, the error probability for a spammer is written as "1/k^2k-1", which is mathematically incorrect. The correct expression should be "1/k * 2^(k-1)" or similar. The paper's version would evaluate to zero for k>1, which is clearly wrong.

Theorem 4 contains a critical mathematical error: "R < 1k ln(1−q) ln ̄S" is nonsensical. The correct form should involve logarithms of probabilities, not products of logarithms. This error makes the theorem's conclusion meaningless.

## Notational and Formatting Inconsistencies

The paper exhibits severe notational inconsistencies. The error probability is denoted as "̂" in some places and "δ" in others. Probability notation is inconsistently written with and without spaces (e.g., "P (v|u)" vs "P(v|u)"). The binary entropy function is denoted as "Hb(̂)" in equation (14) but as "H(1-δ, δ/(N-1),...)" earlier, creating confusion.

The paper claims to be for NIPS 2016 but cites references from 2017 and later (e.g., "Spectral methods meet EM..." from NIPS 2014, which would be the 27th conference). This inconsistency undermines the paper's credibility.

## Conceptual Weaknesses

The paper claims to establish "fundamental limits" but only analyzes specific coding schemes (kIC) rather than establishing bounds that apply to all possible coding schemes. The source-channel separation theorem is mentioned but not properly applied, as the paper does not demonstrate how source and channel coding can be jointly optimized for crowdsourcing.

The k-ary incidence coding analysis is based on a specific coding scheme rather than establishing universal bounds. The paper states "this serves as an ultimate performance bound" for Theorem 2, but the proof in Appendix A does not establish this as a fundamental limit; it only establishes a bound for a specific coding scheme.

## Clarity and Presentation Issues

The paper contains numerous grammatical errors and poorly constructed sentences that make it difficult to follow. For example, "The transitory or fleeting characteristic of workers, their unknown and imperfect skill levels and their possible motivations for spamming makes the design of crowdsourcing projects and the analysis of the obtained results particularly challenging" contains a subject-verb agreement error.

The reference list contains inconsistent formatting. Some references include years, others don't. The reference to "Elements of Information Theory" is missing the year, while others include it.

## Minor but Significant Issues

The paper refers to "Figures 1b and 1c" but does not include these figures in the provided text, making it impossible to verify the claims about the graphical representation of 3IC.

The paper claims "The rate of the code is proportional to the CS budget" but later states "different types of query have different costs," creating a contradiction that is never resolved.

## Conclusion

This paper contains fundamental mathematical errors, severe notational inconsistencies, and conceptual weaknesses that undermine its main claims. The errors are not merely typographical but invalidate key theoretical results. The paper fails to establish the "fundamental limits" it claims to find, and the results presented are either mathematically incorrect or only applicable to specific coding schemes.

While the problem of crowdsourcing is interesting and the information-theoretic approach is promising, this paper does not deliver on its promise due to these critical issues. The authors should thoroughly revise the paper, correcting all mathematical errors, standardizing notation, and properly establishing the theoretical limits they claim to find.

I recommend rejection due to the presence of multiple fundamental mathematical errors that invalidate the paper's main claims. The authors should be encouraged to resubmit after a complete revision addressing these issues.
