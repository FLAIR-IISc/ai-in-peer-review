Summary
The paper proposes an information-theoretic framework for crowdsourcing that treats task assignment and aggregation as a joint source–channel coding problem. The source is the latent label/function B(X), the channels are workers modeled as discrete memoryless channels (either M-ary symmetric channels, MSC, with heterogeneous error rates, or spammer–hammer channels, SHC), and the “coder” is the query scheme plus decoder. The main contributions are:
- Two fundamental budget–fidelity lower bounds (Theorems 2 and 3) giving the minimum queries per item R required to achieve target Hamming error ε̂ for an N-ary source, in two regimes: worker skill unknown to the crowdsourcer (SL-UK) vs known (SL-CS). The expressions reduce to R ≥ [H(B) − HN(ε̂)] / [log M − HM(·)], with appropriate averaging over worker reliabilities.
- Analysis of k-ary Incidence Coding (kIC), which shows “error-correction” because many response configurations are invalid and thus removed from worker options. For SHC workers and N=2, Lemma 1 gives the spammer-only error rate as a function of k, and Theorem 4 provides a budget lower bound for non-adaptive kIC. A corollary specializes the general bound to SHC, yielding an intuitive capacity q log M per query and R ≥ (1 − Hb(ε̂)) / (q log M). A rule-of-thumb pricing ratio for different k is suggested.

Relation to prior work
The work connects to aggregation and worker-model literature (Dawid–Skene EM; spectral/EM approaches such as Zhang et al.; reliability-aware task allocation such as Karger–Oh–Shah; bandit survey/quality–cost trade-offs). The information-theoretic viewpoint complements these by producing code-agnostic limits. However, the paper could better situate itself against sample complexity and minimax-optimality results for DS-type models (e.g., Karger–Oh–Shah (NIPS/Operations Research), Zhang–Chen–Zhou–Jordan), and work on adaptive vs non-adaptive task assignments.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: casting CS as joint source–channel coding is elegant and yields clean, interpretable expressions. The distinction between SL-UK vs SL-CS channels is insightful: “capacity of average worker” vs “average of capacities.”
- Generality of lower bounds: Theorems 2 and 3 apply to arbitrary (non-adaptive) query schemes and arbitrary N-ary sources with non-uniform priors.
- kIC perspective: makes explicit how constraining answer sets provides intrinsic error-correction; the SHC corollary reduces to an intuitive capacity q log M.
- Potential practical implications (pricing/effort vs fidelity) if further developed.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Achievability vs converse: The theorems are presented as “minimum number of queries per item.” The derivations appear to rely on source–channel separation with an averaged or state-known channel. While converse-style lower bounds are plausible, achievability in the crowdsourcing constraints is not established (e.g., encoder cannot tailor channel codewords to individual workers; the “channel input alphabet” is constrained by the query mechanism; no cross-item channel coding in standard platforms). The paper should clearly position these as information-theoretic lower bounds and specify under what coding constraints achievability would hold.
- Ambiguity/notation:
  - M vs N: N is the number of classes; M is the size of the worker response alphabet. In many labeling settings M=N, but for kIC M is the number of valid response configurations. This must be stated precisely whenever Theorem 2/3 are used.
  - HM(·) and HN(·) are introduced informally; define HM(ε) explicitly as the output entropy of an M-ary symmetric channel with crossover ε, and HN(ε̂) as the rate-distortion term for N-ary Hamming.
  - The distinction between E(HM(ε)) and HM(E(ε)) is critical; the text uses both. Theorems 2 (unknown skills) vs 3 (known skills) should emphasize why the former yields log M − HM(E[ε]) and the latter log M − E[HM(ε)].
- Potential error in Theorem 4: For SHC and non-adaptive kIC, with R′ = kR item-wise inclusions, the oracle-decoder lower bound is P(error) ≥ (1 − q)^{R′} · ε̄S. Hence to guarantee ≤ ε̂, one needs R′ ≥ ln(ε̂/ε̄S) / ln(1 − q). The stated bound “R < (1/k) ln(1 − q)/ln ε̂̄S” seems to be missing ε̂ and has sign issues. Please correct and check the constants (base of logs, strict vs non-strict).
- Scope/assumptions: i.i.d. labels, i.i.d. worker states per query, no task difficulty, no adversarial bias beyond uniform spammers, non-adaptive assignments. These limit realism and make the gap to practice unclear.
- Empirical validation: No experiments or simulations compare achievable algorithms (majority, DS, spectral/EM, convex clustering) against the bounds or quantify the kIC advantage as k varies and under non-uniform class priors/N>2. The pricing rule-of-thumb is not evaluated.
- Presentation quality: Numerous typos, missing years/pages in references, missing/unclear figures, and grammatical issues make the paper hard to follow. Some sections (e.g., Appendix references) are crucial to credibility but not summarized in the main text.

Significance and originality
- The information-theoretic formalization and SL-UK vs SL-CS comparison are valuable and, to my knowledge, not explicitly presented in prior CS papers in this generality. The kIC angle is interesting, but the analysis is restricted (binary, uniform prior, SHC, oracle decoder). Overall significance will be higher if the paper either (a) tightens the bounds and clarifies achievability under realistic constraints, or (b) demonstrates that common schemes approach these limits.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify the exact coding constraints under which Theorems 2/3 are converses only vs tight (achievable). Provide proof sketches in the main paper to build intuition.
- Fix Theorem 4 and provide a clean derivation; extend to N>2 and non-uniform priors if possible.
- Precisely define all notation (M, N, HM, HN, R, R′) and the valid response set size for kIC.
- Compare numerically to DS/spectral algorithms and majority vote under MSC/SHC to show the bound’s tightness; include adaptive vs non-adaptive discussion (e.g., Karger–Oh–Shah).
- Discuss practical implementability of kIC (UI complexity, cognitive load) and revisit the pricing rule with effort/latency models.
- Improve writing, fix references (include years, venues), and ensure all figures are present and legible.

Pros
- Clean, general lower bounds via an elegant source–channel abstraction.
- Insightful comparison between unknown vs known worker skills.
- Highlights error-correction inherent in structured queries (kIC).

Cons
- Ambiguities and likely error in a main theorem; many notation/presentation issues.
- Limited scope of kIC analysis and reliance on oracle assumptions.
- No empirical validation; practical achievability not established.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The conceptual contribution is promising, but the current version needs clarification of results, correction of technical issues (Theorem 4), stronger positioning relative to prior work, and at least some empirical or algorithmic evidence to be suitable for NeurIPS. I encourage the authors to revise accordingly.
