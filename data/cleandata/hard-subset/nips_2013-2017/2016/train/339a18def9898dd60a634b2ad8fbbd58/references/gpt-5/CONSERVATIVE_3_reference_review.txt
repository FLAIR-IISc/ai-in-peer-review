Summary
The paper proposes an information-theoretic framework for crowdsourcing, casting query design and aggregation as joint source–channel coding. Workers are modeled as noisy channels (MSC for general error, SHC for spammer/hammer). The authors derive lower bounds on the minimum number of queries per item required to achieve a target error under two regimes: unknown worker skills to both taskmaster and crowdsourcer (SL-UK), and perfectly known skills to the crowdsourcer (SL-CS). They argue that “k-ary incidence coding” (kIC)—presenting k items jointly for relational labeling—has an error-correcting effect by disallowing inconsistent responses, and analyze its necessary rate under SHC with an oracle decoder. They also sketch a pricing rule-of-thumb relating k to query cost. A small set of numerical curves compares the kIC lower bound to an information-theoretic limit specialized to SHC.

Relation to prior work
The work connects to Dawid–Skene EM and its variants for aggregating noisy labels, and to theoretical analyses of crowdsourcing reliability and budget allocation (e.g., Karger–Oh–Shah; Zhang–Chen–Zhou–Jordan). The novelty is the explicit source–channel separation view and rate–distortion style lower bounds. The kIC idea relates to pairwise/tuplewise queries used in clustering (e.g., Vinayak–Oymak–Hassibi), but here it is interpreted as a code with constrained output alphabets.

Strengths
- Clean, principled mapping of crowdsourcing to source–channel coding yields closed-form lower bounds linking target error, source entropy, response alphabet size, and worker reliability.
- Clear conceptual separation between SL-UK and SL-CS regimes; the resulting bounds succinctly capture the intuition that knowing skill levels increases effective capacity.
- The kIC construction formalizes a known practical trick (asking multi-item relational questions) as a coding mechanism with implicit error correction; the SHC-based lemma and theorem are simple and interpretable.
- Potentially useful as a benchmark for any crowdsourcing scheme: if a method operates below the bound, it must fail.

Weaknesses
- Heavy reliance on asymptotic, idealized assumptions (iid items, memoryless channels, stationarity, infinite blocklength, non-adaptive querying). Real crowdsourcing exhibits item difficulty, worker non-stationarity, and strategic/adversarial behavior; none of these are accommodated.
- The “SL-CS” regime assumes perfect skill identification and uses it in lower bounds; this is an optimistic oracle that may yield bounds too loose to guide practical design. No achievability constructions or finite-length analyses are provided to assess tightness.
- The MSC and SHC models are simplistic relative to common per-worker confusion matrices and item difficulty models (e.g., GLAD, Whitehill et al.). The paper does not discuss sensitivity of the bounds to model mismatch.
- The key theorems (2, 3, 4) appear to follow directly from source–channel separation and/or Fano-style arguments. Without achievability or converse refinements beyond separation, it is unclear what is genuinely new versus a straightforward application of textbook results to a new setting.
- The kIC result (Theorem 4) is only a necessary condition under a particularly favorable oracle decoder, so its practical value is questionable; no empirical validation of kIC versus standard baselines (majority, DS, spectral-EM) on synthetic/real data.
- Claims about pricing are speculative and unvalidated; worker effort and platform frictions are not modeled.
- Clarity issues: numerous typos and missing or inconsistent notation (e.g., definitions of HN(·), HM(·), E(·); M vs N; some equations malformed); figures/appendices are referenced but not included; many references lack years/page info.

Quality
Technically, the lower bounds appear plausible under the stated models, but the paper does not provide proofs in the submission, finite-sample analysis, or any demonstrations of tightness. The modeling assumptions are quite restrictive. The lack of empirical or simulation results against established baselines limits the evidential support.

Clarity
The exposition is uneven. Sections 2–3 mix notation (N vs M, kIC alphabets), and several formulas are typeset incorrectly. Important definitions (e.g., HN(ε)) are not properly introduced. Figures/appendices are missing. The paper would benefit from substantial editing and a self-contained proof sketch for at least one main theorem.

Originality
The angle of applying rate–distortion and separation to crowdsourcing is interesting, but much of the development is a direct instantiation of standard theorems. The kIC coding view is a useful reframing of prior multi-item query practices, but the analytical result provided is limited (necessary condition under SHC with oracle decoder).

Significance
As presented, the work’s impact is limited. Without achievability results, finite-sample guidance, adaptive designs, or empirical validation, it is hard to see practitioners or theorists adopting these bounds beyond as loose sanity checks. Tighter links to existing optimality results in crowdsourcing (e.g., Karger–Oh–Shah minimax rates, spectral-EM guarantees) are missing.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide achievability constructions approaching Theorems 2/3 (even in simplified settings), or finite-length bounds to gauge tightness?
- How do your bounds compare quantitatively to known theoretical results on budget-optimal allocation and sample complexity (e.g., Karger–Oh–Shah, Zhang et al.)?
- Can you extend the framework to incorporate item difficulty and per-worker confusion matrices beyond MSC/SHC?
- For kIC, can you provide experiments comparing k ∈ {2,3,4} against DS/spectral-EM under fixed budgets on synthetic data with controlled spammer rates?
- Please clarify notation (HN, HM, E(·)), M vs N, and include the missing appendices/figures.

Pros
- Conceptually clean framework bridging information theory and crowdsourcing.
- Explicit, interpretable lower bounds in two skill-knowledge regimes.
- Formalization of multi-item queries as codes with error-correction flavor.

Cons
- Strong, unrealistic assumptions; no achievability/tightness; no empirical validation.
- Simplistic worker models; limited practical guidance.
- Clarity/notation issues and incomplete references/appendices.

Recommendation
Reject. The paper provides an interesting perspective but lacks rigorous validation, tightness results, and empirical support against established baselines. With stronger theory (achievability/finite-length), realistic modeling (item difficulty, non-iid), and experiments demonstrating practical relevance, this line could be impactful.

Confidence
High.
