

# Review of "Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing"

This paper presents an information-theoretic framework for analyzing the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing systems, specifically focusing on the trade-off between budget (number of queries per item) and fidelity (error probability). The authors model crowdsourcing as a communication system where workers act as noisy channels, and derive theoretical bounds for two scenarios: when worker skill levels are unknown (SL-UK) and when they are perfectly known (SL-CS). The paper also analyzes k-ary incidence coding (kIC) as a specific query scheme and derives performance bounds for it.

The paper builds on previous work in crowdsourcing such as the Dawid-Skene algorithm [5] and bandit survey problems [1], but makes a significant contribution by shifting the focus from algorithmic design to fundamental theoretical limits. While prior work has focused on practical aggregation methods, this paper provides a framework to understand the absolute performance boundaries of any crowdsourcing system, which is a novel perspective for the field.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel theoretical framework:** The paper successfully frames crowdsourcing as an information-theoretic problem, providing a fresh perspective that complements existing algorithmic approaches. This is a significant contribution to the literature.

2. **Clear theoretical results:** Theorems 2 and 3 establish rigorous information-theoretic bounds for the budget-fidelity trade-off under different worker skill level assumptions. The derivation of these bounds is technically sound and well-motivated.

3. **Practical relevance of kIC analysis:** The paper's analysis of k-ary incidence coding provides concrete insights into how query design affects performance. The connection between kIC and error correction capability is well-articulated and provides a useful design principle.

4. **Thorough literature review:** The paper adequately contextualizes its contribution relative to existing work in crowdsourcing, particularly by contrasting its theoretical approach with previous algorithmic work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited empirical validation:** While the theoretical results are sound, the paper lacks concrete empirical validation showing how these bounds translate to real-world crowdsourcing performance. The numerical results in Section 4.2 only compare theoretical bounds with each other, without demonstrating how actual crowdsourcing systems approach these limits.

2. **Overly idealized assumptions:** The model assumes a large dataset (L → ∞), which may not be realistic for many practical crowdsourcing applications. The discrete memoryless channel assumption also simplifies worker behavior significantly compared to real-world scenarios.

3. **Limited discussion of practical implementation:** The paper identifies the theoretical limits but doesn't sufficiently address how these limits would impact real-world system design. The brief mention of query pricing in Appendix E lacks concrete implementation details.

4. **Narrow focus on Hamming distortion:** The analysis is restricted to Hamming distance for simplicity, but it's unclear how the results would generalize to other distortion measures relevant to different crowdsourcing tasks.

**Recommendations for Improvement:**

1. Include empirical results demonstrating how actual crowdsourcing systems perform relative to the theoretical bounds. For example, implement a simple crowdsourcing system using kIC and show how its performance compares to the derived limits.

2. Discuss how the theoretical bounds might change for smaller datasets (L finite), which would make the results more applicable to real-world scenarios.

3. Expand the discussion of practical implementation by providing more concrete guidance on how the theoretical results can inform real crowdsourcing system design.

4. Consider extending the analysis to other distortion measures that might be relevant to different crowdsourcing applications.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution by establishing fundamental limits for the budget-fidelity trade-off in crowdsourcing. The information-theoretic approach is novel and well-executed, and the results provide important insights that could guide future crowdsourcing system design. However, the lack of empirical validation and the overly idealized assumptions limit the paper's practical impact. The theoretical framework is strong, but the connection to real-world implementation needs strengthening.

The paper addresses a significant gap in the literature by providing fundamental limits that previous algorithmic approaches didn't consider. While the results are theoretically interesting, the paper would be significantly strengthened by including empirical validation that demonstrates how these limits manifest in practice.

**Recommendation:** Weak Accept. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of crowdsourcing systems. With additional empirical validation and discussion of practical implementation considerations, it would be a strong candidate for publication. The current version is technically sound and makes an important contribution to the field, but the lack of empirical support for the theoretical claims prevents a stronger recommendation.
