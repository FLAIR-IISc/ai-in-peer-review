Summary
The paper models crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding problem to derive fundamental budget–fidelity trade-offs. Items to be labeled are modeled as a source B(X); workers are noisy channels with either M-ary symmetric channel (MSC) or spammer–hammer channel (SHC) behaviors; queries are “codes,” and the decoder is the crowdsourcer’s inference procedure. Using Shannon’s separation theorem, the paper gives lower bounds on the minimum number of queries per item (rate) needed to achieve a target Hamming distortion (error probability). Two cases are treated: (i) SL-UK, where worker skill (channel state) is unknown to the crowdsourcer, and (ii) SL-CS, where worker skill is perfectly known to the crowdsourcer. The bounds resemble a source rate-distortion term divided by a channel capacity term; for SL-CS the capacity is the expectation of per-worker capacities; for SL-UK it is the capacity of the averaged channel.

The paper also studies k-ary incidence coding (kIC), where a query presents k items and asks for partition/incidence relations. Because only some of the 2^(k choose 2) adjacency patterns are valid, kIC has built-in error-correction. For SHC workers, the paper derives: (a) the error probability of a spammer under kIC, and (b) a necessary condition on the query rate R (per item) to reach a target error when the decoder is an oracle that recognizes hammers. A corollary specializes the information-theoretic limit to SHC. The authors briefly discuss pricing for kIC.

Relation to prior work
This builds on: Dawid–Skene and EM-based label aggregation; worker-selection/bandit and budget-optimal assignment; and spectral/convex aggregation (e.g., Karger–Oh–Shah; Whitehill et al.; Zhang et al.; Vinayak–Oymak–Hassibi). The information-theoretic viewpoint (capacity, R(D)) is a natural but underused lens for CS; Karger et al. and subsequent work derive minimax/sample-complexity bounds with specific aggregation schemes and worker models, while this paper gives channel-agnostic lower bounds via separation plus mixture-channel analysis. The analysis of kIC overlaps with triplet/quadruplet/comparison-based clustering literature but the SHC-based bounds and the “invalid pattern” error-correction angle seem less explored.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The use of separation is appropriate: achievability/converse characterize the asymptotic limit under large blocklength and non-adaptive encoding. For SL-CS, capacity with state known at the receiver equals E[C(ε)], which matches Theorem 3. For SL-UK, treating the mixture of MSCs as an MSC with average crossover yields capacity log M − H_M(E[ε]); this is plausible for symmetric mixtures and aligns with standard results. On the source side, R(D) = H(B) − H_N(D) is valid for Hamming distortion up to D ≤ min{1 − p_max, 1 − 1/N}; the paper states this condition. The kIC lower bound via “probability no hammer labels the item” times spammer error is a clean necessary condition.
- Completeness: Proofs are deferred to appendices; the main ideas are discernible, but some key steps (mixture-channel capacity; exact R(D) regularity conditions; derivation of Lemma 1) would benefit from clearer exposition in the main text. No empirical validation or simulations are provided to assess tightness of bounds or the practical benefit of kIC.
- Correctness concerns/typos: There are several notation/formatting issues that should be fixed to ensure correctness and reproducibility (see Clarity).

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized, but the exposition suffers from numerous typos and notation glitches that impede readability: e.g., missing ε in the MSC definition; inconsistent use of M vs N (M often denotes response alphabet size, N the number of true labels); H_M(·) vs H_N(·); “Wiely”; incomplete references (missing years); and figures/tables are referenced but not shown here.
- The bounds (11) and (12) should explicitly define H_M(·) and E(ε). Please clarify when equality holds in the R(D) expression and restate the distortion threshold condition next to the formula.
- For kIC, give an intuitive explanation of Lemma 1 and how the invalid patterns yield error-correction. The Theorem 4 expression should explicitly define R’ and the relation R’ = kR (each k-ary query contributes 1/k per item on average). The pricing rule π(k1)/π(k2) ≈ k1/k2 likely has a typographical error in the draft.
- Please include at least a small synthetic study visualizing how the lower bounds compare to performance of known decoders (majority, DS, spectral) under MSC/SHC.

Originality
- The separation-based lower bounds in this setting are natural but, to my knowledge, not laid out this cleanly for both SL-UK and SL-CS with explicit N-ary Hamming R(D) and mixture MSC capacities. The kIC analysis for SHC and the oracle-based necessary condition is a neat contribution. The pricing connection, while heuristic, is interesting.
- Prior theoretical work often focuses on specific aggregation schemes and asymptotics in items/workers; this paper’s scheme-agnostic lower bounds and the explicit comparison of unknown vs known worker skill are valuable.

Significance
- The results provide conceptual guidance: (i) differentiating limits when worker skills are known at the decoder; (ii) highlighting the gains from structured queries (kIC); and (iii) presenting budget lower bounds independent of the inference algorithm. These can inform the design and evaluation of CS systems and suggest what “headroom” remains for algorithmic advances.
- However, practical impact would be strengthened by empirical or simulated evidence showing tightness (or gaps) with common algorithms, and by a more rigorous cost model for kIC and pricing.

Strengths
- Clean information-theoretic framework that unifies budget and fidelity.
- Clear comparison between SL-UK and SL-CS via capacity with/without state knowledge.
- Insightful treatment of k-ary incidence coding and a simple necessary condition under SHC.
- Potential to inform query design and pricing.

Weaknesses
- No empirical/simulation validation; tightness of bounds is unclear.
- Modeling assumptions are idealized: iid items, non-adaptive assignment, no item difficulty, independence across workers/items.
- Presentation issues: notation inconsistencies, typos, incomplete references, missing figure details.
- Pricing suggestion is underdeveloped and contains likely typos.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add simulations under MSC/SHC comparing the lower bounds to majority vote, DS, spectral-EM, and simple kIC decoders; show how close practical schemes get to the limits.
- Clarify and fix notation, equations (especially (1), (11), (12)), and the pricing ratio.
- Discuss extensions: adaptive querying/assignment, item difficulty (channel state depending on input), partial state estimation (imperfect SL-CS), and finite-blocklength effects.
- Broaden related work to include comparison-based clustering and triplet/quadruplet query literature, and more recent CS theory results.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The information-theoretic framing and kIC lower bounds are interesting and potentially impactful, but the current manuscript needs clearer exposition and at least basic empirical validation to meet NeurIPS standards. With improved clarity and experiments demonstrating relevance/tightness, this could become a strong contribution.
