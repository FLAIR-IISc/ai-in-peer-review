This paper attempts to establish fundamental limits on the budget-fidelity trade-off in label crowdsourcing using an information-theoretic framework. The authors model crowdsourcing as a communication system and derive bounds on the achievable rate (budget) required to attain a certain fidelity, considering scenarios with unknown and perfectly known worker skill levels. They also introduce and analyze a k-ary incidence coding scheme.

While the core idea of applying information theory to crowdsourcing is intriguing, and the attempt to provide fundamental limits is commendable, the paper suffers from a multitude of issues ranging from superficiality in the modeling to a lack of practical relevance and inconsistencies in presentation.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty of Approach:** The application of an information-theoretic lens to crowdsourcing is a conceptually fresh perspective, potentially offering a rigorous framework for understanding the inherent limitations of the process.
*   **Mathematical Formalism:** The authors attempt to formalize the problem using concepts from information theory, which, in principle, could lead to deeper insights.
*   **k-ary Incidence Coding:** The introduction of k-ary incidence coding is a potentially useful contribution, though its practical benefits are not convincingly demonstrated.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Oversimplified Modeling:** The communication system model feels too abstract and detached from the realities of crowdsourcing platforms like AMT. The assumption of i.i.d. data, while common in theoretical analyses, is questionable in many real-world labeling tasks. The modeling of workers as simple discrete channels (MSC or SHC) lacks nuance, neglecting the complex factors influencing worker performance.
*   **Limited Practical Relevance:** The derived bounds, while mathematically correct, are often expressed in terms of entropy and capacity, which are difficult to translate into actionable guidelines for designing practical crowdsourcing systems. The discussion on query pricing (Appendix E) is incredibly brief and lacks detail.
*   **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper lacks any empirical evaluation or simulation studies to validate the theoretical results. It is unclear whether the derived bounds are tight or even remotely useful in predicting the performance of real-world crowdsourcing systems. The numerical results primarily serve to illustrate the bounds rather than provide meaningful insights.
*   **Inconsistent Notation and Definitions:** The notation is inconsistent throughout the paper. For example, the definition of ‘R’ varies between sections. There are also some unclear definitions, such as what exactly constitutes “sufficient redundancy” and the assumptions behind the Oracle decoder.
*   **Minor Technical Flaws:** Equation (1) for the MSC model is standard, however, its direct applicability to worker accuracy is questionable. The derivation of Lemma 1 appears to contain an arithmetic error, and the connection between Theorem 4 and Corollary 1 isn't fully explained. The reasoning for the constraints imposed within Theorem 4, i.e., "1/k ln(1−q) ln ̂̄S," is not sufficiently justified.
*   **Referential Issues:** Several references are cited but not directly discussed or integrated into the narrative.
*   **Clarity and Organization:** The paper's structure feels disjointed. Sections 3.1 and 3.2, introducing background information, are overly verbose and could be streamlined. The transitions between sections are often abrupt. The figures are not clearly referenced in the text.

**Specific Points:**

*   The discussion of “feedback” in Section 2.3 is vague and doesn’t connect to the proposed models.
*   The claim that kIC offers “error correction capability” is not well-substantiated. Removing invalid responses simply reduces the search space, it doesn’t inherently correct errors.
*   Appendix E’s query pricing discussion is based on a simplified comparison of k values and ignores the cost of launching a query on a real platform.
*   The discussion in Section 3.2 concerning jointly estimating worker skill levels is underdeveloped.

**Arguments for/against acceptance:**

**Against:**

*   The paper’s theoretical results are largely divorced from practical reality.
*   The lack of empirical validation undermines the credibility of the findings.
*   Numerous technical flaws and inconsistencies detract from the overall quality.
*   The writing is often unclear and disorganized.

**For:**

*   The application of information theory to crowdsourcing is a novel approach.
*   The k-ary incidence coding scheme, while not fully evaluated, is a potentially interesting idea.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the novelty of the approach, the paper’s numerous shortcomings outweigh its strengths. The theoretical results, while mathematically sound, lack practical relevance and are not supported by empirical evidence. The paper reads more like an initial exploration of ideas than a polished, complete research contribution. The volume of minor issues – inconsistencies, lack of clarity, incomplete explanations – suggests a lack of thoroughness. I recommend **rejection**. The paper may benefit from substantial revision and a stronger focus on validating the theoretical findings with real-world data or simulations. A more grounded and less abstract approach to modeling would also be beneficial.
