Summary
The paper models label crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding problem and seeks fundamental budget–fidelity trade-offs. The source is the ground-truth labeling B(X), workers are noisy channels (primarily M-ary symmetric channels, MSC), and queries are “codes.” Two regimes are treated: (i) SL-UK, worker skill levels unknown to the taskmaster and crowdsourcer, and (ii) SL-CS, worker skills known to the crowdsourcer. Using rate–distortion and channel capacity arguments, the paper states lower bounds on the minimum queries per item to achieve a target error. It then studies k-ary Incidence Coding (kIC), where each query groups k items and asks for within-group clustering, analyzing its error-correction-like properties. For a spammer–hammer population (SHC(q)), it gives a lower bound on the rate via an oracle decoder, derives a spammer error lemma, and suggests a pricing rule of thumb. A corollary gives an information-theoretic benchmark for SHC known to the decoder.

Strengths
- Ambitious, conceptually appealing unification of crowdsourcing design with classical rate–distortion and channel capacity tools.
- Distinguishes SL-UK vs SL-CS in a way that yields interpretable denominators (capacity-like terms).
- kIC is a natural class of structured queries; discussing its implicit “error-correction” is insightful.
- The SHC analysis (oracle lower bound) is a useful necessary condition and connects to practical intuition (hammers drive information).

Weaknesses
Major technical and presentation issues significantly limit credibility and reproducibility.

Quality
- Many equations are malformed or ambiguous. MSC definition (Eq. (1)) appears broken: it should read P(v|u)=(1−ε) if v=u, and ε/(M−1) otherwise. As written, “M−1 v=u 1− v=u” is ill-defined.
- Confusing and inconsistent use of symbols M and N. N is the number of classes (bins), M seems to denote channel alphabet size and/or number of choices; in Theorems 2–3 denominators use log2 M and H_M(·), while numerators use H(B(X)) and H_N(ε̂). Precise meanings and relationships are not consistently defined.
- Theorems 2–3: Rmin formulas involve H_N(ε̂) and H_M(·) with E(ε) or E(H_M(ε))—it is unclear whether the expectation is over the worker-skill distribution before or after the entropy function, and why. Concavity of entropy implies E[H_M(ε)] ≥ H_M(E[ε]); which one is correct here? The distinction matters.
- Constraints on ε̂ use min{1−pmax, 1−1/N}. The rationale and proof are not shown; also the typo in Eq. (6) minimizes P(E : B̂(X)=B(X)) instead of P(B̂(X)≠B(X)).
- In SL-UK, the denominator looks like capacity of an “average worker,” while in SL-CS the average of capacities appears. This is plausible, but requires a precise model of worker mixing (random channel per use) and a careful converse; neither is fully specified.
- kIC analysis: Lemma 1’s expression for spammer error ȳS is opaque; the combinatorial derivation and even the exact summation limits are unclear (e.g., “i × (k choose i)” needs justification and lacks normalization). For even k, the extra term “k/4 (k choose k/2)” appears ad hoc.
- Theorem 4: R < (1/k) ln(1−q)/ln ȳS^ as a necessary condition raises sign/monotonicity issues since ln(1−q) ≤ 0 and ln(·) negative for ȳS^∈(0,1). The inequality direction, domain of ȳS^, and handling of the integer R′ (number of appearances per item) need rigorous treatment.
- Corollary 1: Rmin = (1−Hb(ε̂)) / (q log2 M). This suggests an erasure-like effective channel with erasure prob 1−q and per-use capacity q log2 M, but the mapping from SHC to a DMC with capacity q log2 M requires assumptions about the query alphabet and prior; this should be derived, especially since for binary tasks M often differs from N and from the number of valid kIC patterns.
- No empirical validation or simulations are provided to demonstrate tightness or usefulness of the bounds, nor comparisons to existing algorithms (e.g., DS, spectral, KOS) under realistic budgets.

Clarity
- The paper is hard to follow due to numerous typos, undefined symbols, and formatting errors. Examples:
  - B(X1,…,Xn)=B(Xn) is nonsensical as written.
  - “th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016), Barcelona, Spain.” appears mid-paragraph.
  - Notation U, V, M, N, k, R, W, ε, q, pmax frequently change meanings or are not restated when context shifts.
  - Figure references lack figures; Appendices A–E are referenced but not available.
  - The “feedforward” remark contradicts earlier “no feedforward” assumption without a clear resolution.

Originality
- The high-level source–channel separation framing is elegant, but the core capacity and rate–distortion ingredients are classical. Related theoretical work on budgets and reliability in crowdsourcing (e.g., Karger–Oh–Shah; minimax lower bounds; adaptive allocation; item difficulty/GLAD) is insufficiently discussed, and some key works are missing. The kIC angle is interesting but not fully new (connections to correlation clustering and noisy link queries exist).

Significance
- If clarified and made rigorous, such limits could guide system design and pricing. In its current form, the impact is limited by ambiguity and lack of validation.

Pros
- Conceptually clean mapping to information theory.
- Distinction between unknown vs known skill regimes yields interpretable differences.
- kIC as a structured query with implicit redundancy is a valuable direction.
- Potentially useful pricing heuristic.

Cons
- Numerous technical ambiguities and typos in statements and notation.
- Missing proofs/appendices and figures; limited rigor in key steps.
- Unrealistic worker models (MSC/SHC) without discussion of item difficulty or dependence; no experiments.
- Related work incomplete; references have multiple errors.

Detailed minor issues (non-exhaustive)
- Eq. (6) should use ≠ not = inside P(·).
- Section 2.3: “only four valid responses” for 3IC with N=2 should be accompanied by the exact set U and M=|U|; reconcile with earlier M.
- Pricing rule: π(k1)/π(k2) ≈ k1/k2? The manuscript shows “≈ k1k2,” missing a division bar.
- References contain many errors: misspellings (Wiley), author names (S. Ohy), missing years/pages, truncated titles.

Questions for authors
- Provide exact, consistent definitions of N and M across theorems; clarify H_N(·), H_M(·), and where expectations sit.
- Supply complete proofs (or sufficient sketches) of Theorems 2–4 and Lemma 1 with careful treatment of worker mixing and concavity.
- Explain Theorem 4 inequality signs and domains; define R′ precisely.
- How do results change under item difficulty (GLAD-like), class imbalance, or adaptive querying?
- Empirically evaluate kIC vs standard querying under SHC/MSC and compare to DS/spectral methods.

Recommendation: Reject
While the idea is promising, the current submission has too many technical, notational, and editorial issues to assess correctness, and lacks empirical validation or adequate related work coverage. A thoroughly revised version with precise statements/proofs, corrected notation/references, and at least some simulations could be much stronger.

Confidence: High (familiar with information-theoretic analysis of crowdsourcing and coding theory).
