## Peer Review: Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a theoretically grounded exploration of fundamental limits in crowdsourcing, framing it as a communication problem and deriving information-theoretic bounds on the trade-off between budget (number of queries) and fidelity (accuracy). While the work is mathematically rigorous and tackles an important problem, its practical impact is somewhat limited by the assumptions made and the focus on theoretical limits rather than practical algorithms. I lean towards a **weak accept** with major revisions. The theoretical contributions are valuable, but the paper needs to better connect with existing empirical work and offer more concrete guidance for designing *practical* crowdsourcing systems.

**1. Summary and Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper models crowdsourcing as a communication system where workers are noisy channels, and the goal is to accurately infer information (labels, cluster assignments) from a dataset with a limited budget.  It investigates the fundamental limits of this process, deriving bounds on the required query rate (budget) to achieve a certain fidelity, considering both scenarios where worker skill is unknown and perfectly known. The paper builds upon the existing literature on crowdsourcing aggregation methods like majority voting and Dawid-Skene (DS) by shifting the focus from algorithmic design to *fundamental limits*. It also leverages concepts from information theory, such as rate-distortion theory and channel capacity. The introduction does a decent job of positioning the work within the broader context of crowdsourcing and related fields.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Rigorous Theoretical Framework:** The use of information theory to analyze crowdsourcing is a significant strength. The derivation of bounds provides a fundamental understanding of the achievable performance limits.
* **Novel Modeling:** Framing crowdsourcing as a communication system with workers as noisy channels is a novel and insightful approach.
* **Two Scenarios Considered:** Analyzing both the unknown and perfectly known worker skill levels provides a nuanced understanding of the problem.
* **k-ary Incidence Coding:** The introduction and analysis of k-ary incidence coding is an interesting contribution that potentially offers a trade-off between cost and robustness.
* **Clear Mathematical Presentation:** The mathematical derivations are generally well-presented and easy to follow, given a background in information theory.
* **Consideration of Spammer-Hammer Channel:** Modeling workers as either spammers or hammers is a realistic and useful simplification.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions & Practicality:** The model relies on strong assumptions (e.g., i.i.d. data, perfect channel estimation in one scenario, memoryless channels). These assumptions may not hold in real-world crowdsourcing scenarios, limiting the direct applicability of the results. The paper doesn't adequately address the gap between these theoretical bounds and practical system design.
* **Limited Empirical Validation:** The paper lacks any empirical validation of the derived bounds. While the focus is on theoretical limits, it would be beneficial to demonstrate the bounds’ relevance through simulations or analysis of real-world datasets.  The numerical results are limited to demonstrating the bound for k-ary Incidence Coding, and even this could be expanded.
* **Query Cost Discrepancy:** The paper acknowledges that different query types have different costs, but doesn’t fully integrate this into the analysis.  The discussion of query pricing in the kIC section is a good start, but needs to be more comprehensive.
* **Clarity of kIC Benefits:** While kIC is introduced, the practical benefits over simpler schemes aren't entirely clear, especially given the added complexity. A more detailed discussion of the trade-offs between query cost, error correction, and implementation complexity is needed.
* **Connection to Existing Work:** While the introduction mentions related work, the paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of how these theoretical bounds relate to the performance of existing crowdsourcing algorithms (e.g., DS, adaptive querying schemes).



**4. Detailed Comments & Questions:**

* **Section 3.1:** The discussion of joint source-channel coding is a bit abstract.  Could the authors provide a more concrete example of how this applies to the crowdsourcing setting?
* **Theorems 2 & 3:** The bounds presented in these theorems are quite general.  It would be helpful to provide some intuition for how these bounds scale with key parameters (e.g., dataset size, number of workers, error tolerance).
* **Section 4.2:** The explanation of the numerical results could be more detailed. What parameter settings were used?  What are the implications of the observed results?
* **kIC pricing:** The pricing rule of thumb derived in Appendix E feels somewhat ad-hoc. Can this be justified more rigorously?
* **Future Work:**  The authors mention several potential extensions (e.g., incorporating feedback, variable query difficulty).  It would be useful to prioritize these extensions based on their potential impact.



**5. Arguments for and against acceptance:**

**Pro-Acceptance:**

* Novel and rigorous theoretical analysis of a fundamental problem in crowdsourcing.
*  Provides valuable insights into the inherent limitations of crowdsourcing systems.
* Introduces a new query scheme (kIC) with potential benefits.
* Well-written and mathematically sound.

**Con-Acceptance:**

* Strong assumptions limit practical applicability.
* Lack of empirical validation.
* Limited connection to existing crowdsourcing algorithms.
*  Doesn't offer clear guidance for designing practical systems.




**6. Recommendation:**

**Weak Accept** with major revisions. I believe the paper has the potential to make a valuable contribution to the field, but it needs to be significantly strengthened. Specifically, I recommend the following revisions:

* **Add empirical validation:** Include simulations or analysis of real-world data to demonstrate the relevance of the derived bounds.
* **Discuss practical implications:**  Provide a more detailed discussion of how the theoretical results can be used to inform the design of practical crowdsourcing systems.  Address the challenges of applying the model to real-world scenarios.
* **Compare to existing algorithms:**  Explicitly compare the theoretical bounds to the performance of existing crowdsourcing algorithms.
* **Expand kIC analysis:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the trade-offs associated with kIC, including its complexity and robustness.
* **Clarify pricing model:** Provide a more rigorous justification for the kIC pricing rule of thumb.



I believe addressing these points will significantly improve the paper and make it a more valuable contribution to the NIPS community.
