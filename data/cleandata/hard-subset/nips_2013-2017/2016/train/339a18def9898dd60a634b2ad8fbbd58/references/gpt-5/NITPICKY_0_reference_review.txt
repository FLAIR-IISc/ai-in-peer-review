Summary
The paper models label crowdsourcing as a communication system and seeks fundamental budget–fidelity tradeoffs via rate–distortion and channel coding. Workers are modeled as discrete memoryless channels; two worker pools are considered: (i) mixtures of M-ary symmetric channels (MSC) with unknown skill levels (SL-UK) and with skill levels known to the crowdsourcer (SL-CS), and (ii) a spammer–hammer channel (SHC). Using source–channel separation, the authors derive lower bounds on the number of queries per item required to achieve a target Hamming distortion for an N-ary source. The bound differs in SL-UK vs SL-CS through the capacity term: log M − H_M(E[ε]) vs log M − E[H_M(ε)]. The paper also studies k-ary incidence coding (kIC), claiming inherent error-correction by pruning invalid answers. For SHC, it derives a lower bound on the labeling error for spammers under kIC (Lemma 1) and a necessary budget condition for achieving a target error (Theorem 4). A corollary specializes the information-theoretic bound for SHC with state known at the decoder, giving Rmin = (1 − Hb(ε̂))/(q log2 M) for binary uniform labels.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean information-theoretic framing of crowdsourcing, connecting source–channel separation to budget–fidelity tradeoffs.
- Clear qualitative insight: when skill is unknown, the system behaves like an averaged channel; when known at the decoder, capacity averages over worker capacities.
- kIC is a natural query class worth analyzing; the intuition that pruning invalid responses adds error-correction is appealing.
- If proofs are correct, the SL-UK vs SL-CS comparison (Theorems 2–3) is a useful benchmark.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Numerous notation and typesetting errors that obscure key definitions and raise questions about correctness:
  - MSC transition probabilities (Eq. 1) omit ε and are malformed. For MSC(ε): P(v|u)=1−ε if v=u, ε/(M−1) otherwise.
  - SHC definition (Eq. 2) is malformed (missing 0 for v≠u when C=H).
  - In Theorems 2–3, H_N(ε̂) and H_M(·) are used with undefined or inconsistently defined arguments. The notations E(·), HN(·), HM(·), and hats are inconsistent.
  - M vs N conflation: N denotes label alphabet size, M denotes the number of response options; this is inconsistently used in multiple places (e.g., Eq. 11 denominator uses log2 M but numerators use H(B) over N).
- Missing or inaccessible proofs (Appendices A–E), making it impossible to verify key claims, especially for kIC/SHC.
- kIC analysis contains unstated assumptions that appear unrealistic or inconsistent:
  - Theorem 4 asserts that each item is included in exactly R′ k-ary queries; earlier the construction samples items uniformly at random, so per-item coverage is random (Binomial/Poisson), not deterministic.
  - Lemma 1 assumes spammers select uniformly among “valid” outputs; the set of valid outputs and its cardinality depend on whether cluster labels are identifiable up to permutation, and on whether empty clusters are allowed. This is not precisely specified; the denominator k·2^{k−1} suggests implicit identification up to a global flip, but this needs justification.
  - The SHC model for kIC effectively induces a channel whose capacity depends on k; the “static feedforward” comment suggests input-dependent channel state but is not formally modeled.
- The rate–distortion side for clustering/labelling:
  - R(D)=H(B)−H_N(D) is invoked but only valid under iid source labels and Hamming distortion with N-ary alphabet; the paper mixes general clustering and labelling without clarifying when labels are iid. Real clustering typically violates iid assumptions.
  - For SL-UK, the “averaged” channel argument requires care: with workers sampled i.i.d., the composite channel is indeed a DMC with averaged transition matrix, yielding capacity C̄=log M − H_M(E[ε]). This should be explicitly proved; currently it is asserted.
- Practical relevance is limited: no algorithms or decoding procedures are developed; the paper provides only converse-type (necessary) bounds and no achievability constructions beyond separation. No empirical validation or simulations are provided.
- The “pricing rule-of-thumb” π(k1)/π(k2)≈k1/k2 is speculative and unvalidated.
- Presentation/clarity:
  - Figures 1–2 are referenced but not provided; the 3IC example is not sufficiently formalized.
  - Many grammatical errors and awkward phrasing; references are incomplete (missing years, page ranges) and in several cases incorrect publisher names (“Wiely”).
  - Equation numbering, variable scoping (X vs Xn) and capitalization are inconsistent.

Relation to prior work
- Missing important related work on information-theoretic limits and minimax rates in crowdsourcing beyond Karger–Oh–Shah, e.g.,:
  - Minimax optimal inference in crowdsourcing under Dawid–Skene and general worker heterogeneity (Shah & Zhou; Gao, Zhou, & Nee; others).
  - Adaptive task assignment and spectral/EM hybrids with theoretical guarantees (Zhang et al., Ghosh & Hummel, Bhattacharya et al.).
  - Query design and multi-item comparisons in crowdsourcing/ordinal embedding (Awasthi & Balcan; Jamieson & Nowak).
- The kIC idea relates to pairwise/triadic comparisons and transitivity constraints; connections to Bradley–Terry/Luce and consistency-based error-correction could strengthen the contribution.

Reproducibility
- No code, no data, no proof details, no experimental setup. Not reproducible as-is.

Pros
- Clean, potentially useful benchmark bounds (if formalized properly).
- Brings separation-based reasoning to a familiar ML problem.
- Highlights impact of decoder-side knowledge of worker skill.

Cons
- Significant notation, definition, and typesetting errors; important formulas malformed.
- Missing proofs and figures; unclear assumptions in kIC analysis.
- Limited novelty relative to existing literature on theoretical limits of crowdsourcing.
- No empirical validation; weak practical guidance beyond high-level bounds.
- Inadequate related work coverage.

Questions for authors
1. Provide precise, corrected statements of Theorems 2–3 with full definitions of H_N(·), H_M(·), ε, M, and N. Are these bounds tight, and are there achievability constructions?
2. For SL-UK, justify rigorously that the composite worker pool induces an averaged DMC, and that capacity equals log M − H_M(E[ε]) with uniform inputs.
3. Formalize kIC “valid outputs” for N=2: do you identify labelings up to permutation? Are empty clusters allowed? What is M(k) exactly?
4. In Theorem 4, clarify the per-item query coverage assumption. If items are sampled randomly into k-ary queries, how do your bounds change?
5. Can you provide even small-scale simulations to compare kIC vs simple majority/DS under SHC/MSC?

Recommendation
Score: 4 (reject). While the high-level direction is interesting, the submission in its current form has too many technical and presentation issues, lacks proofs and experiments, and does not sufficiently position itself relative to prior work. I encourage the authors to fix the mathematical and notational errors, include complete proofs or detailed sketches, clarify kIC assumptions, add empirical validation, and substantially improve clarity and related work.

Reviewer confidence: High.
