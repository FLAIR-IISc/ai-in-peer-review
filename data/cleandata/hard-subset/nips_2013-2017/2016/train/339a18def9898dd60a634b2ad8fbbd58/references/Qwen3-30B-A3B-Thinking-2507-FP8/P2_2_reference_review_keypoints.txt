- Introduces an information-theoretic framework for the budget–fidelity trade-off in crowdsourcing, modeling workers as noisy channels.  
- Analyzes two scenarios: unknown worker skills (SL-UK) and perfectly known skills (SL-CS).  
- Derives fundamental bounds (Theorems 2 and 3) on required budget for a target fidelity.  
- Proposes and analyzes k-ary incidence coding (kIC) as a novel query scheme with error-correction properties.  
- Approach is original, moving beyond empirical/algorithmic work to establish theoretical limits.  
- Theoretical analysis and proofs are rigorous and provided in appendices.  
- Numerical results show kIC performance improves with larger k and higher worker quality q.  
- Practical insight: query pricing scales roughly proportional to k (π(k1)/π(k2) ≈ k1/k2).  
- Significant: provides a theoretical benchmark answering “minimum budget for given fidelity.”  
- Clarity is moderate—well-structured and consistent notation but some results need more intuitive explanation for non–information-theory readers.  
- Limited empirical validation—no comparisons with real crowdsourcing experiments or algorithms (e.g., Dawid–Skene, majority voting).  
- Channel models are narrow (MSC and SHC) and may not capture real worker behavior distributions.  
- Practical implementation gap—insufficient discussion of how to implement kIC, decoding complexity, and comprehensive cost trade-offs.  
- Overall assessment: strong theoretical contribution with recommendation for acceptance (Quality 8/10, Clarity 7/10, Originality 9/10, Significance 9/10).
