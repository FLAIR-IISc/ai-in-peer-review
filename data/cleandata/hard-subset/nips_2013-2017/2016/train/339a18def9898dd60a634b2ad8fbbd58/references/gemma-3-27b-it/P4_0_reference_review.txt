This paper investigates fundamental limits of budget-fidelity trade-offs in label crowdsourcing, modeling the problem as a communication system and applying information-theoretic tools. It analyzes scenarios with unknown and perfectly estimated worker skill levels, and proposes k-ary incidence coding (kIC) as a specific query scheme, alongside a pricing strategy. The work builds on existing research in crowdsourcing, particularly the Dawid-Skene algorithm and recent probabilistic models, but seeks to provide ultimate performance bounds rather than algorithmic improvements. 

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The information-theoretic approach is appropriate for establishing fundamental limits, and the mathematical derivations appear correct (though detailed verification requires significant effort). The two theorems presented – establishing lower bounds for unknown and known skill levels – are logically structured. However, the connection between the theoretical bounds and practical implications isn't fully fleshed out. While the authors acknowledge the gap between theoretical limits and achievable performance, more discussion on the tightness of the bounds and the factors preventing practical systems from reaching them would be beneficial. The presented kIC scheme is a reasonable extension of binary incidence coding and the associated analysis provides insights. The work feels incomplete in the sense that it primarily focuses on *establishing* limits rather than *achieving* them; it doesn't propose a practical algorithm to approach these limits.

**Clarity:** The paper’s clarity is mixed. The initial sections clearly explain the motivation and background in crowdsourcing. The modeling section (Section 2) is generally well-written, although the notation could be slightly more consistent. The presentation of the mathematical derivations in Section 3 is dense and requires a strong background in information theory. The explanations accompanying the theorems and lemmas could be more intuitive. For example, the interpretation of Theorem 4 and its link to pricing (Appendix E) is somewhat abrupt.  Figures 1a, 1b and 1c are helpful in visualizing the system model and kIC. The transition between the general theoretical bounds and the specific analysis of kIC is smooth, but the practical implications of kIC (beyond the pricing suggestion) aren't well emphasized. 

**Originality:** The application of information theory to *fundamentally* limit crowdsourcing performance is a novel contribution. While information-theoretic analyses have been applied to related problems (e.g., active learning, sensor networks), applying it directly to the budget-fidelity tradeoff in crowdsourcing, with a focus on fundamental limits, is a new approach.  The kIC scheme, though conceptually simple, provides a concrete example to illustrate the theoretical framework. The paper appropriately references relevant work in crowdsourcing and information theory.  However, the originality is somewhat tempered by the fact that the derived bounds, while theoretically interesting, don't immediately translate into practical algorithms that outperform existing methods.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a significant problem – the trade-off between cost and reliability in crowdsourcing. Establishing fundamental limits provides a valuable benchmark for evaluating existing and future crowdsourcing algorithms.  The theoretical bounds can guide the design of more efficient crowdsourcing systems, even if achieving these limits is challenging. The pricing strategy derived from the kIC analysis has potential practical value. However, the impact of this work depends on how readily the theoretical insights can be translated into concrete improvements in real-world crowdsourcing platforms. The results are likely to be most valuable to researchers working on the theoretical foundations of crowdsourcing rather than practitioners seeking immediate algorithmic solutions. 



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of information theory to a fundamental problem in crowdsourcing.
*   Technically sound analysis and derivation of meaningful bounds.
*   Clear presentation of the system model and motivation.
*   Introduction of a potentially useful query scheme (kIC) with an associated pricing strategy.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The paper focuses heavily on establishing theoretical limits without proposing practical algorithms to approach them.
*   The connection between the theoretical bounds and practical implications is not fully explored.
*   The mathematical derivations are dense and may be inaccessible to readers without a strong background in information theory.
*   The impact of the work may be limited to the theoretical research community.



Overall, this is a solid theoretical contribution to the field of crowdsourcing. While it doesn't present a breakthrough algorithm, it provides valuable insights into the fundamental limits of the problem and opens up new avenues for research.  I would rate this as a **weak accept**. Addressing the clarity concerns and further discussing the practical implications of the results would strengthen the paper significantly.
