## Peer Review: Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents an information-theoretic analysis of crowdsourcing, aiming to establish fundamental limits on the trade-off between budget (number of queries) and fidelity (accuracy). It’s a valuable contribution that attempts to move beyond algorithmic improvements and focuses on *what is fundamentally achievable*. However, the paper is dense, relies heavily on information-theoretic formalism that may limit its accessibility to some NIPS attendees, and the connection to practical crowdsourcing systems could be strengthened. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**, contingent on addressing clarity issues and better contextualizing the results.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper models crowdsourcing as a communication system, with workers acting as noisy channels. It investigates the theoretical limits on performance, considering scenarios with both unknown and perfectly estimated worker skill levels. The authors introduce k-ary incidence coding (kIC) as a potentially beneficial query scheme, analyzing its performance and suggesting a query pricing strategy. The paper correctly positions itself within the existing crowdsourcing literature, acknowledging work on aggregation schemes (majority voting, Dawid-Skene) and worker selection.  It appropriately references relevant information theory concepts. The move to an information-theoretic framing is a significant departure from much of the empirical work in this area and represents a worthwhile investigation.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty & Theoretical Rigor:** The primary strength lies in the novel application of information theory to rigorously analyze fundamental limits in crowdsourcing.  This is a significant departure from the predominantly algorithmic approach in the field.  The derivations of the bounds in Theorems 2 and 3 are mathematically sound (though require careful verification).
* **Fundamental Insight:** The paper provides a clear theoretical framework for understanding the inherent limitations of crowdsourcing, independent of specific algorithms. The distinction between knowing/not knowing worker skill levels is insightful.
* **k-ary Incidence Coding:** The introduction of kIC is a promising idea, offering a potential way to incorporate error correction into the query design.
* **Query Pricing Strategy:**  The discussion of a query pricing strategy based on kIC is a practical consideration, bridging the gap between theory and application.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity & Accessibility:** The paper is dense and relies heavily on information-theoretic terminology.  While appropriate for a NIPS audience, it could be more accessible.  For example, a more intuitive explanation of the connection between the channel model and real-world worker behavior would be helpful. Equations are often presented without sufficient context or explanation.
* **Practical Relevance:** While the theoretical bounds are valuable, their practical implications are not fully explored.  How tightly do these bounds hold in real-world crowdsourcing platforms like AMT, where worker behavior is more complex than the assumed models (MSC, SHC)?  A discussion of this gap would be beneficial.
* **Model Assumptions:** The MSC and SHC models are simplifications of reality. The authors acknowledge this, but a more detailed discussion of the impact of these assumptions on the validity of the results would be useful. The assumption of i.i.d. source data (B(X)) may also be limiting.
* **Limited Empirical Validation:** The paper primarily presents theoretical results. While numerical results are provided for kIC, they are relatively limited and don't fully demonstrate the practical benefit of the proposed scheme.
* **Presentation of kIC:** While the idea of kIC is interesting, the presentation could be improved.  Figure 1b and 1c are helpful, but a more detailed explanation of why certain responses are “invalid” would be beneficial.



**4. Detailed Evaluation (based on NIPS criteria):**

* **Quality (8/10):**  The technical analysis appears sound, and the derivations are likely correct (pending detailed verification).  The completeness of the work is reasonable for a theoretical investigation, but could be enhanced with more discussion of practical limitations. The analysis of kIC could be more thorough.
* **Clarity (6/10):** The paper suffers from a lack of clarity, particularly for readers less familiar with information theory. More intuitive explanations and more detailed derivations would improve readability.
* **Originality (9/10):** The application of information theory to crowdsourcing is novel, and the kIC scheme offers a potentially new approach to query design.
* **Significance (7/10):** The theoretical bounds are significant in defining the limits of what’s achievable in crowdsourcing.  However, the practical impact remains to be fully demonstrated.  The kIC scheme has the potential to be impactful if it can be efficiently implemented and shown to outperform existing methods in real-world settings.



**5. Arguments for/against Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Provides a fundamentally new perspective on crowdsourcing performance.
* Rigorous theoretical analysis establishes important bounds.
* Introduces a potentially useful query scheme (kIC).
* Contributes to a deeper understanding of the trade-offs involved in crowdsourcing.

**Con:**

*  Density and technicality may limit accessibility.
*  Limited empirical validation and discussion of practical implications.
*  Model assumptions may not fully capture real-world complexities.



**6. Questions for the Authors:**

* Could you provide a more intuitive explanation of the channel model and its relation to real worker behavior?
* How sensitive are the bounds in Theorems 2 and 3 to deviations from the MSC and SHC assumptions?
*  What are the computational challenges associated with implementing kIC in a large-scale crowdsourcing system?
* Can you elaborate on the query pricing strategy, considering the cost of different types of queries (e.g., time required from workers)?
*  Do you have any plans to empirically evaluate the performance of kIC on a real-world crowdsourcing platform?



**7. Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**  I recommend acceptance contingent on the authors addressing the clarity and practical relevance concerns outlined above. Specifically, they should focus on providing more intuitive explanations of the technical concepts, discussing the limitations of their model assumptions, and exploring the potential for empirical validation of their results.  Addressing these issues will significantly enhance the impact and accessibility of this valuable contribution.
