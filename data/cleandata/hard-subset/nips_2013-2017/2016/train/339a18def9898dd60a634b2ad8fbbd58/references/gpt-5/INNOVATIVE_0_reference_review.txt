Summary
This paper frames label crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding problem and seeks fundamental budget–fidelity trade-offs. The dataset labels are the source; workers are modeled as noisy channels (MSC and spammer–hammer SHC); a query scheme plus inference constitutes the code; and “budget” is the code rate (queries per item). The authors define distortion (Hamming) and derive information-theoretic lower bounds on the minimum rate needed to achieve a target error under two scenarios: worker skill levels unknown to both taskmaster and crowdsourcer (SL-UK) versus known to the crowdsourcer (SL-CS). They introduce k-ary incidence coding (kIC), noting its error-correcting structure (invalid response patterns), and derive a lower bound on the rate needed for kIC under an SHC pool, plus a pricing heuristic relating k. A specialized corollary compares the kIC bound to the general information-theoretic limit.

Relation to prior work
The paper contrasts with aggregation algorithms such as Dawid–Skene (EM), majority vote, and more recent spectral/EM hybrids, and with task allocation/budget analyses (e.g., Karger–Oh–Shah). Prior theoretical works established scaling laws and minimax rates under specific worker models and allocation schemes; this paper instead gives Shannon-style limits via rate–distortion and capacity, explicitly separating SL-UK vs SL-CS. The kIC angle relates to triplet/comparison-based clustering; here it is reinterpreted as a code with error-correction.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: A clear, unified information-theoretic formulation of crowdsourcing that maps budget–fidelity to rate–distortion and channel capacity, including a separation-style statement. This is a bold perspective likely to stimulate new designs and benchmarks.
- Clean bounds: Closed-form expressions for Rmin in SL-UK and SL-CS under an MSC pool, and a simple corollary for SHC. The comparison between “capacity of average worker” (SL-UK) and “average of capacities” (SL-CS) is insightful.
- Coding viewpoint on kIC: Viewing kIC as introducing structured redundancy because some response patterns are invalid is a nice contribution; the SHC-based lower bound captures how k improves robustness and yields a concrete pricing rule-of-thumb tied to k.
- Potential impact: Provides a principled benchmark independent of specific algorithms; could guide task design, budget allocation, and the value of estimating worker skills.

Weaknesses
- Idealized modeling and limited scope: MSC/SHC are convenient but restrictive; no instance difficulty, adversaries beyond spammers, or correlations across items. Only non-adaptive querying is treated, whereas adaptive allocation is central to modern systems.
- Validation is thin: No empirical or simulation study showing how close practical algorithms (e.g., DS, spectral-EM, majority, adaptive policies) come to the bounds, or how kIC performs in practice relative to the derived limits.
- Connections to prior theory underdeveloped: The paper cites budget-optimal allocation and inference works, but does not position its bounds relative to existing lower/upper bounds (e.g., KOS phase transitions, minimax rates) under comparable assumptions. Are the proposed limits tighter, looser, or orthogonal?
- Clarity/notation issues: Several notational inconsistencies (M vs N, definitions of HN and HM, pmax), missing years in references, and equation formatting glitches make it hard to verify theorems. Some conditions (e.g., support of P(B), role of M relative to the answer set) need clearer exposition.
- kIC analysis narrow: Theorem 4 treats uniformly distributed binary labels and SHC with an oracle decoder. The practical utility of the pricing rule and its robustness to non-uniform priors, multi-class labels, or imperfect skill estimation is not analyzed.

Quality
The technical ideas are sound at a high level, and the mapping to source–channel limits is appropriate. However, the proofs are deferred to appendices and key steps are not sketched, making it difficult to assess tightness and generality. The kIC SHC bound is clearly a necessary condition under strong assumptions; more discussion of achievability gaps would help.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized but marred by notation/typo issues and missing bibliographic details. Providing proof sketches for Theorems 2–4, tightening notation (clearly define HN, HM, M vs N), and clarifying assumptions would improve readability. The pricing heuristic should be derived more transparently.

Originality
High on the conceptual side: a separation-style budget–fidelity limit for crowdsourcing, explicit contrast of SL-UK vs SL-CS, and an error-correction view of kIC. While related analogies exist, this synthesis is novel and valuable.

Significance
Promising as a theoretical benchmark and design guide. If extended to adaptive settings, richer worker/item models, and backed with empirical comparisons, it could influence both methodology and practice.

Questions for the authors
- How do your bounds compare quantitatively to existing lower/upper bounds under comparable models (e.g., KOS) for binary labeling?
- Can you provide simulations showing gaps between your limits and DS/spectral-EM/majority under MSC/SHC?
- How sensitive is the kIC bound and pricing rule to non-uniform label priors and to multi-class N>2?
- Could your framework accommodate task difficulty (input-dependent channels) or adversarial workers?

Pros
- Bold, unifying information-theoretic framing of crowdsourcing
- Clear limits for SL-UK vs SL-CS
- Insightful coding perspective on kIC and simple pricing rule
- Potential to guide future algorithm and system design

Cons
- Idealized models; non-adaptive only
- Limited empirical/quantitative validation
- Incomplete comparison to prior theoretical limits
- Notational/clarity issues

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper advances a fresh, principled viewpoint with clean bounds and a compelling re-interpretation of incidence queries. Despite modeling simplifications and limited validation, the conceptual novelty and potential impact justify acceptance, provided the authors tighten notation, relate more directly to prior limits, and add at least a small empirical study or detailed comparisons. Confidence: medium.
