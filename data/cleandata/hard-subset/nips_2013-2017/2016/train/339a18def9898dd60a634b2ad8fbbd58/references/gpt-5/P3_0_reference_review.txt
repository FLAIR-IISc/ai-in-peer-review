Summary
This paper frames crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding problem and studies the fundamental budget–fidelity trade-off for labeling tasks. The dataset’s latent labels B(X) are the source; workers are modeled as noisy channels (either M-ary symmetric channels, MSC, or a spammer–hammer channel, SHC); queries are “codes,” and the decoder aggregates responses to estimate labels. Using the source–channel separation theorem, the authors derive lower bounds on the minimum queries per item required to achieve target Hamming distortion (Theorems 2 and 3). The bounds reflect (i) unknown worker skill at decoder (SL-UK) where the effective channel is a mixture, versus (ii) known worker skill at decoder (SL-CS) where state is known at the receiver and the achievable rate is the expected capacity. They also analyze k-ary incidence coding (kIC) for clustering/labeling by grouping k items per query, derive a lower bound on achievable error with SHC workers and an oracle decoder (Theorem 4), and propose a heuristic pricing rule relating per-query price to k.

Relation to prior work
The paper is well positioned relative to aggregation/EM methods (Dawid–Skene; GLAD; spectral-EM), graph-based inference (e.g., convex clustering with pairwise queries), and task allocation and budget optimality (e.g., Karger–Oh–Shah). Its contribution differs by focusing on information-theoretic limits rather than algorithm design. Closely related are works on budget-optimality and necessary/sufficient conditions for reliable labeling in random bipartite assignment graphs; the current paper’s separation-based limits complement those by offering channel-capacity-style lower bounds that are algorithm-agnostic. The kIC analysis connects to crowdsourced clustering via pairwise/ternary constraints.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: Casting crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel problem is clean and yields immediate intuition. The denominator terms log M − H_M(·) correctly mirror MSC capacity; the numerator H(B) − H_N(D) mirrors the N-ary source R(D) with Hamming distortion (with the customary p_max admissibility range).
- Clear separation of SL-UK vs SL-CS: The difference between “capacity of the mixture” (unknown skill) and “mixture of capacities” (state known at decoder) is both correct and insightful.
- kIC insight: Treating invalid response patterns as “error-detecting structure” is a nice coding-theoretic perspective; the SHC lower bound captures the benefit of increasing k.
- Practical angle: Translating limits into a pricing heuristic is appealing (albeit heuristic).

Weaknesses
- Clarity/notation issues: Several definitions are ambiguous or missing. H_M(·) and H_N(·) need precise definitions upfront. The notation in Theorem 4 is unclear: “ln ̂̄S” seems to denote ln(ĥ/ S̄) but is not defined; the derivation likely relies on P(error) ≥ (1 − q)^{R′} S̄ with R′ the number of exposures per item, implying R′ ≥ ln(ĥ/S̄)/ln(1 − q). Also, the relationship between the global “rate” R (queries per item) and the number of times an item is included in k-ary queries (R′ ≈ kR) needs to be stated precisely; the text oscillates between “exactly” and “on average.”
- Scope of worker models: MSC and SHC are stylized. Important phenomena such as item difficulty, worker–item interactions, and adversarial spammers are relegated to “future work.” While fine for limits, this weakens the practical relevance.
- Asymptotics and achievability: Results hinge on n → ∞ and source–channel separation, whereas practical CS typically uses short-block, non-adaptive designs. No finite-block refinements (e.g., dispersion/second-order rates) or achievability schemes are provided to gauge tightness for realistic scales.
- Limited coverage of kIC: The analysis is for N = 2 and SHC with an oracle decoder; the dependence on M in response options vs true labels is not fully disentangled; no treatment of nonbinary labels or of realistic decoders.
- Empirical validation: Beyond a schematic figure, there are no experiments or simulations to assess tightness or to compare with known algorithms (majority vote, DS, spectral-EM, KOS).
- References and presentation: Bibliographic entries are incomplete (missing years; typos), and several figures are referenced but not shown. There are multiple typographical issues and some inconsistent symbol usage (M vs N).

Technical assessment
- Theorems 2–3: The forms are plausible and align with standard results. For SL-UK, treating the channel as an averaged MSC with error E[ε] yields capacity log M − H_M(E[ε]). For SL-CS, with state known at the decoder, the achievable rate is E[log M − H_M(ε)], matching the theorem. The source R(D) = H(B) − H_N(D) under Hamming distortion is valid under the stated admissible D region.
- Theorem 4: The qualitative dependence on k and q is right: larger k improves spammer error S̄ and larger q lowers the needed repetitions. However, the exact statement needs repair (signs/logs and the role of R versus R′). A careful derivation and explicit constants would strengthen the result.

Significance
The paper provides a useful baseline: algorithm-independent lower bounds that can guide system design and help interpret the gains from modeling worker skills. The coding-theoretic perspective on query design (kIC) is insightful and could influence future work on structured queries and task bundling. However, the impact is limited by the current scope (stylized channels, asymptotic regime) and presentation clarity.

Questions for the authors
1) Please define H_M(·) and H_N(·) explicitly, and state all assumptions for the R(D) formula (support of P(B), admissible D).
2) Clarify the rate definition for kIC: Is R the number of k-ary queries per item, or the total item-inclusion rate divided by k? Make R′ = kR explicit.
3) Provide the exact derivation of Theorem 4 and disambiguate “ln ̂̄S.” Should the bound be R ≥ (1/k) ln(ĥ/S̄)/ln(1 − q)?
4) Can you comment on finite-sample implications and whether simple schemes (e.g., majority with degree design) approach your limits?
5) How does kIC generalize to N > 2 and to non-SHC worker models?

Pros
- Clean, principled information-theoretic framing with correct and interpretable limits.
- Insightful contrast between unknown vs known skill at the decoder.
- Useful perspective on structured queries (kIC) and how they embed error detection.
- Potential to guide pricing and budget allocation.

Cons
- Ambiguities and notation errors in key statements; incomplete references.
- Stylized worker models; no finite-block or empirical corroboration.
- kIC analysis limited to N = 2 and oracle decoders; practical decoders not analyzed.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The conceptual contribution is solid and timely, but the paper in its current form needs substantial clarification, stronger technical presentation (especially for kIC), and at least some empirical or finite-sample evidence to assess tightness and practical relevance. With these improvements, it could become a valuable reference on information-theoretic limits for crowdsourcing. Confidence: high.
