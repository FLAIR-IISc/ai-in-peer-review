Summary
The paper proposes an information-theoretic framework for crowdsourcing, modeling workers as noisy channels and queries as codes. It defines a distortion-rate (budget-fidelity) objective and invokes source–channel separation to derive limits on the minimum number of queries per item needed to achieve a target error probability. Two regimes are analyzed: (i) SL-UK, where worker skill levels (channel parameters) are unknown to the crowdsourcer; and (ii) SL-CS, where they are perfectly known at the decoder. The authors then study k-ary incidence coding (kIC) and, under a spammer–hammer channel (SHC), derive a necessary condition on the budget for achieving a target error, along with a back-of-the-envelope pricing rule. They compare these with an information-theoretic lower bound specialized to SHC.

Strengths
- Ambitious attempt to unify crowdsourcing design via a clean information-theoretic formulation (distortion-rate) with explicit budget-fidelity trade-offs.
- The contrast between SL-UK (capacity of the averaged channel) and SL-CS (average of capacities) is insightful and pedagogically valuable.
- kIC is a practically relevant family of queries; highlighting its inherent “error-correction” via invalid response elimination is interesting.
- The SHC analysis leading to a necessary rate condition and a pricing heuristic is potentially useful.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Numerous notation errors and ambiguities seriously impede verification:
  - Eq. (1) for the M-ary symmetric channel is malformed. It must be P(v|u) = 1−ε if v=u, and ε/(M−1) if v≠u.
  - HN(·) is introduced without a proper definition/equality sign (“HN(ε) H(1−ε, …)”); similarly HM(·).
  - Theorems 2–3: denominators read “log2 M − HM(E(·))” vs “log2 M − E(HM(·))” but symbols inside HM are missing; E(ε) vs E(·)? Ambiguous.
  - M denotes channel alphabet size and N denotes number of classes; Theorems use both M and N with insufficient clarity about which applies to which variable (e.g., why N appears in HN(·) while the channel is M-ary).
  - The target error is denoted by “̂” without being defined; elsewhere Dt is used. Please define variables consistently.
- Foundational assumptions are either restrictive or unclear:
  - Source iid assumption for B(X) conflicts with clustering scenarios and kIC where constraints induce dependencies; the paper oscillates between clustering and independent labeling without pinning down the setting for each theorem.
  - The SL-UK and SL-CS bounds rely on separation and memoryless channels with i.i.d. worker selection. The crowdsourcer’s lack of knowledge at the taskmaster is said to force equal mi across workers; however, this is an implementation constraint, not an information-theoretic one, and its role in the bounds is unclear.
  - For time-varying unknown channels, using the averaged channel capacity is valid when state is i.i.d. and unknown to both ends; this needs to be stated and justified. In SL-CS, “perfect skill estimation” is assumed yet the statistical conditions for perfect identification are not addressed.
- kIC analysis has gaps:
  - Lemma 1’s spammer error formula appears without derivation; the combinatorial factors (e.g., 1/(k 2^{k−1}) ∑ i (k choose i) i) are not motivated, and dimensionality is unclear. No proof is present in the submission.
  - Theorem 4’s condition R < (1/k) ln(1−q)/ln(ĒS) uses R both as queries per item and implicitly as R′/k; the relation between R and the integer number of kIC inclusions per item R′ should be made precise. Signs are delicate since ln(1−q) < 0 and ln(ĒS) < 0; state ranges under which the bound is meaningful.
  - The “pricing rule” π(k1)/π(k2) ≈ k1/k2 follows only if the necessary condition is tight and costs scale linearly with the number of items per query; both assumptions should be discussed and empirically validated.
- Comparison to prior work is superficial:
  - There is rich literature on sample complexity and budget-optimal allocation under Dawid–Skene and variants (e.g., Karger–Oh–Shah; spectral/EM methods at NIPS). The paper cites some but does not quantitatively relate its limits to known achievable schemes or impossibility results.
  - No experimental evaluation or even numerical instantiation beyond a not-shown “Figure 2.” It is crucial to show how close practical algorithms can approach the proposed limits and where kIC helps.
- Presentation and completeness issues:
  - The submission references Appendices A–E and multiple Figures, none included. For NeurIPS, proofs must be available in the supplementary; otherwise the core claims are unverifiable.
  - Many typos, grammar, and formatting errors (see below) detract from clarity and suggest inadequate proofreading.
  - Several claims about “ultimate performance bounds” rely entirely on asymptotic separation with large blocklengths; practical finite-blocklength effects (and the intrinsic coupling in kIC) are not discussed.

Specific questions for the authors
1. Provide complete, unambiguous statements and proofs for Theorems 2–4 and Lemma 1, with consistent notation for M, N, ε, q, Dt, and hat variables.
2. Clarify the precise stochastic model in SL-UK: are worker skill parameters drawn i.i.d. per query? Does the decoder know the distribution P(ε)? Are workers identifiable across queries?
3. For kIC, formalize the rate R: is it queries per item or total item-inclusions per item divided by k? How is cost normalized across different k?
4. Show numerical comparisons of the bounds to actual algorithms (majority vote, DS/EM, spectral) under MSC/SHC, and to non-kIC baselines, across q, ε, and k.
5. Discuss finite-length effects and whether adaptive policies can outperform non-adaptive designs under the same budget.

Typos, notation, and formatting (non-exhaustive)
- Eq. (1) incorrect; fix as above.
- “suite humans” → “suit humans”; “Wiely” → “Wiley”.
- “th Conference on NIPS 2016” incomplete; multiple references missing years/venues; inconsistent styles.
- HN(·), HM(·) definitions incomplete; missing equals signs.
- “v = u” appears twice in Eq. (1); likely meant v ≠ u in the second case.
- “In case, the taskmaster…” comma misused; many subject–verb agreement issues.
- Figure and Appendix references without content.

Evaluation
- Quality: Currently not verifiable due to missing proofs and figures; several technical and notation errors.
- Clarity: Weak; pervasive notation/formatting issues.
- Originality: The high-level angle (separation-based limits plus kIC) is reasonable, but much of the theory follows standard separation; the kIC lower bound could be novel if properly derived.
- Significance: Potentially useful framing, but without solid proofs, comparisons, and empirical support, impact is limited.

Recommendation: Reject in current form. With rigorous proofs, corrected notation, complete experiments, and stronger connections to existing crowdsourcing results, this could become a valuable contribution.
