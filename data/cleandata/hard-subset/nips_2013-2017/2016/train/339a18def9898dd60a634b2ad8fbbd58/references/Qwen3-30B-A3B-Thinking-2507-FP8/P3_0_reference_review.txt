

# Peer Review: "Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing"

This paper presents an information-theoretic analysis of crowdsourcing systems, modeling them as communication channels to derive fundamental limits on the budget-fidelity trade-off. The authors establish a novel framework for understanding crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding problem, deriving theoretical bounds for two key scenarios: when worker skill levels are unknown (SL-UK) and when they are perfectly known (SL-CS). The paper introduces "k-ary incidence coding" (kIC) as a query scheme with error correction capabilities, providing theoretical performance bounds for this approach.

## Strengths

The paper's primary strength is its rigorous theoretical foundation. The information-theoretic approach is well-motivated and provides a clear framework for understanding fundamental limits that are independent of specific inference algorithms. The distinction between SL-UK and SL-CS scenarios is particularly insightful, revealing that the system capacity when skill levels are unknown corresponds to the average worker quality, while known skill levels yield a capacity based on the average of worker qualities. This distinction has important implications for system design.

The kIC scheme is a novel contribution that effectively incorporates error correction into crowdsourcing query design. The analysis of kIC with the spammer-hammer channel model (Theorem 4) provides valuable theoretical insights about how query design affects performance. The connection between kIC and coding theory is well-established, and the bound in Theorem 4 is correctly derived as a necessary condition for the error rate.

The paper properly situates itself within the existing literature, citing key works on Dawid-Skene, bandit survey problems, and clustering-based crowdsourcing. The related work discussion is thorough and appropriate.

## Weaknesses

The most significant weakness is the lack of empirical validation. The paper provides theoretical bounds but does not demonstrate how these bounds hold in practice. While Figure 2 shows a comparison between theoretical bounds, there are no experiments with real crowdsourcing data or simulations that test the actual performance of the proposed kIC scheme against existing methods. Without empirical validation, it's difficult to assess the practical relevance of the theoretical results.

The paper makes several strong assumptions that may limit its applicability to real-world crowdsourcing. The iid data assumption is particularly questionable for many practical crowdsourcing tasks where items are not independent. The assumption of a fixed number of workers with known skill distributions is also restrictive, as real crowdsourcing platforms often have dynamic worker pools with unknown characteristics.

The connection to existing crowdsourcing practice could be strengthened. The paper mentions "query pricing strategy" in the conclusion but doesn't provide concrete guidance on how to apply the theoretical bounds to real-world budget allocation decisions. The "rule of thumb" for pricing (π(k₁)/π(k₂) ≈ k₁/k₂) is mentioned but not properly justified or validated.

The paper also doesn't adequately address how the theoretical bounds scale with problem size. The information-theoretic analysis assumes large datasets (L → ∞), but doesn't discuss the finite-sample behavior that would be relevant for most practical applications.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of crowdsourcing by establishing fundamental information-theoretic limits. The framework is sound, the derivations appear correct, and the kIC analysis is novel. However, the lack of empirical validation and the restrictive assumptions significantly limit the paper's practical impact.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1. Including simulations or experiments that validate the theoretical bounds
2. Discussing how the results apply to specific crowdsourcing applications
3. Providing more concrete guidance on implementation and budget allocation
4. Addressing the finite-sample behavior of the proposed bounds

While the theoretical contribution is substantial, the paper currently falls short of demonstrating the practical significance of these bounds for crowdsourcing practitioners. The information-theoretic approach is novel and valuable, but without empirical validation or clear practical guidance, its impact is limited.

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection with a strong revision. The paper has a solid theoretical foundation but requires significant empirical validation and practical discussion to meet NIPS standards. The authors should provide experimental results demonstrating how the theoretical bounds hold in practice, discuss the limitations of their assumptions, and offer concrete guidance for applying their results to real crowdsourcing systems. With these additions, the paper would make an excellent contribution to the field.

The paper is technically sound but lacks the empirical validation and practical relevance that would make it a compelling NIPS contribution. The theoretical framework is interesting but needs to be connected more clearly to practical crowdsourcing applications.
