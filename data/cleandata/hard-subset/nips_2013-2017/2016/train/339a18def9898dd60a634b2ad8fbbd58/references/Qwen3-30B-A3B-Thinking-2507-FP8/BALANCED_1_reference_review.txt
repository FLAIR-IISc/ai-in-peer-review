

# Review of "Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing"

This paper introduces an information-theoretic framework for analyzing crowdsourcing systems, modeling the problem as a communication channel where workers' responses are subject to noise. The authors derive fundamental limits on the budget-fidelity trade-off, considering two key scenarios: when worker skill levels are unknown (SL-UK) and when they are known (SL-CS). They introduce a novel query scheme called k-ary incidence coding (kIC) and analyze its performance, providing theoretical bounds on the minimum number of queries per item required to achieve a target error probability.

## Strengths

The paper makes a significant contribution by applying information theory to crowdsourcing, a domain traditionally approached through machine learning and optimization methods. The SL-UK vs. SL-CS distinction is particularly insightful, highlighting how knowledge of worker skill levels fundamentally changes the achievable performance limits. The kIC scheme is well-motivated and effectively demonstrates how query design can incorporate error correction capabilities, which is a valuable conceptual advance. The paper's structure is logical, with clear figures (especially Figures 1b and 1c) that help readers understand the kIC concept. The information-theoretic bounds derived in Theorems 2 and 3 provide meaningful theoretical limits that could guide future crowdsourcing system design.

## Weaknesses

While the theoretical framework is promising, several weaknesses limit the paper's impact. First, the paper relies heavily on appendices for key proofs (e.g., Theorems 2 and 3), which makes it difficult to assess the technical soundness without extensive cross-referencing. A more self-contained presentation would strengthen the paper. Second, the notation (e.g., HN(ε), HM(ε)) is not sufficiently explained in the main text, creating unnecessary barriers to understanding. Third, the paper lacks empirical validation of the theoretical bounds. While Figure 2 shows a comparison between theoretical bounds and kIC performance, it doesn't demonstrate how these bounds compare to existing crowdsourcing methods or provide concrete examples of how the theory would translate to practice.

The connection to recent NIPS work is also underdeveloped. While the paper references several relevant papers, it doesn't clearly position itself against recent information-theoretic approaches to related problems (e.g., information theory in distributed learning or active learning), which would strengthen the novelty claim. Additionally, the paper's practical implications are somewhat vague—while the pricing rule of thumb (π(k1)/π(k2) ≈ k1/k2) is mentioned, the paper doesn't provide concrete guidance on how crowdsourcing platforms should implement these theoretical insights.

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a theoretically interesting approach to crowdsourcing that could inform the design of more efficient systems. The information-theoretic framework is novel and well-motivated, and the kIC scheme is a compelling example of how query design can incorporate error correction. However, the paper's impact is limited by its lack of empirical validation, insufficient explanation of key technical concepts, and underdeveloped connection to practical implementation.

## Recommendations for Improvement

1. Move key proofs and explanations from appendices to the main text to improve accessibility and allow for better assessment of the technical contributions.

2. Provide more detailed explanations of technical terms and notation in the main text, rather than requiring readers to consult appendices.

3. Include empirical results comparing the theoretical bounds to practical crowdsourcing methods, ideally with real-world data or carefully designed simulations.

4. Strengthen the connection to recent NIPS work by explicitly contrasting this information-theoretic approach with other recent machine learning approaches to crowdsourcing.

5. Provide more concrete guidance on how the theoretical results could inform practical crowdsourcing system design, including potential implementation challenges.

## Decision

This paper presents a theoretically interesting framework that advances our understanding of crowdsourcing fundamentals, but its current form lacks sufficient empirical validation and practical guidance to meet NIPS's high standards. I recommend rejection with the understanding that with substantial revisions addressing the weaknesses noted above, this could become a strong contribution to the field. The theoretical insights are valuable, but the paper needs to better demonstrate their practical relevance and provide more accessible technical details.
