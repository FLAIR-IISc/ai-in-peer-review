## Review of "Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a theoretically grounded investigation into the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing, framing it within an information-theoretic framework. While the core idea of applying information theory to analyze the budget-fidelity trade-off is valuable and offers potential for deeper understanding, the paper suffers from a significant gap between theoretical development and practical implications. The mathematical formalism is extensive, but the connection to concrete crowdsourcing scenarios and existing practical algorithms feels tenuous. I believe the paper, as it stands, is borderline acceptable, leaning towards rejection, and requires substantial revisions to demonstrate its relevance to the NIPS audience.

**Summary:** The authors model crowdsourcing as a communication system, analyzing the trade-off between the budget (number of queries) and fidelity (accuracy). They explore scenarios with unknown and perfectly known worker skill levels, and introduce k-ary incidence coding (kIC) as a potential query scheme. They derive theoretical bounds on the minimum required budget for a given fidelity, based on information-theoretic principles.  They also briefly touch upon query pricing strategies.

**Strengths:**

* **Rigorous Theoretical Framework:** The paper leverages the well-established tools of information theory to provide a formal analysis of crowdsourcing. This is a valuable approach that sets the work apart from many purely empirical studies.
* **Clear Problem Formulation:** The modeling of crowdsourcing as a communication system is a useful abstraction that allows for a principled analysis.
* **Novelty of k-ary Incidence Coding:** The introduction of kIC as a potential coding scheme is an interesting idea, offering a possible pathway to error correction in crowdsourcing.
* **Comprehensive Related Work:** The authors demonstrate a good understanding of the relevant literature in crowdsourcing and information theory.
* **Identified Fundamental Limits:** The derived bounds, even if currently abstract, represent a fundamental contribution to understanding the theoretical limits of crowdsourcing performance.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Practical Relevance:** The biggest weakness is the disconnect between the theoretical results and practical crowdsourcing systems. The derived bounds, while mathematically sound, are often expressed in terms of entropy and channel capacities, making it difficult to translate them into actionable insights for practitioners.  How do these bounds compare to the performance of commonly used algorithms like DS or majority voting *in practice*?
* **Overly Simplified Assumptions:** The model relies on several simplifying assumptions, such as the independence of workers, perfect channel estimation in the SL-CS scenario, and the focus on binary classification in the kIC analysis. These assumptions may not hold in real-world crowdsourcing scenarios.  The effect of relaxing these assumptions should be discussed.
* **kIC Analysis Lacks Depth:** The analysis of kIC, while presented, feels somewhat underdeveloped. The discussion of query pricing is particularly brief and lacks a thorough justification. More extensive simulations and comparisons to existing methods would be crucial.  The computational complexity of implementing kIC needs to be addressed.
* **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical.  There are no experiments or simulations to validate the derived bounds or demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed kIC scheme. This significantly weakens the paper’s impact.
* **Notationally Dense:** The paper is very notationally dense, making it challenging to follow the derivations. While rigor is appreciated, some streamlining would improve readability.



**Detailed Comments & Questions:**

* **Section 3.1:**  The discussion of joint source-channel coding is excellent, but it could be more directly linked to the specific challenges of crowdsourcing.
* **Theorems 2 & 3:**  These theorems provide lower bounds, but how tight are these bounds?  Is there a gap between the theoretical limits and the performance of existing algorithms?  A comparison would be highly valuable.
* **kIC:** The introduction of kIC is promising.  However, the analysis seems limited to the N=2 case. What happens when N is larger? What are the trade-offs between k, the number of workers, and the accuracy?
* **SHC Model:** While the SHC model is a common simplification, it's a strong assumption. Real-world workers likely exhibit a spectrum of skill levels.  How sensitive are the results to deviations from the SHC model?
* **Pricing Discussion:** The discussion of query pricing based on k is a very preliminary idea. A more detailed analysis of cost optimization would be needed.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel theoretical framework for analyzing crowdsourcing.
* The derived bounds provide fundamental limits on performance.
* The introduction of kIC is a potentially interesting coding scheme.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The paper lacks practical relevance and empirical validation.
* The assumptions are overly simplified.
* The analysis of kIC is underdeveloped.
* The notation is dense, hindering readability.



**Recommendation:** **Borderline Reject**.  

I recommend rejection in its current form. However, I believe the paper has potential if the authors address the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically, I suggest the following revisions:

1. **Empirical Validation:** Include simulations or experiments to validate the theoretical bounds and demonstrate the performance of kIC in realistic scenarios.
2. **Practical Connection:**  Connect the theoretical results to existing crowdsourcing algorithms (e.g., DS, majority voting) and discuss their performance in light of the derived bounds.
3. **Relax Assumptions:**  Analyze the sensitivity of the results to the simplifying assumptions made in the model.
4. **Expand kIC Analysis:**  Extend the analysis of kIC to more general cases (e.g., N > 2) and investigate its computational complexity.
5. **Improve Clarity:** Streamline the notation and improve the overall readability of the paper.
