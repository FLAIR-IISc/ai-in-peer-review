## Review of "Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a theoretically grounded analysis of fundamental limits in crowdsourcing, framing it as a communication problem and deriving information-theoretic bounds on the trade-off between budget (number of queries) and fidelity (accuracy). While the mathematical treatment is rigorous and the attempt to provide ultimate limits is commendable, the paper suffers from a disconnect between the theoretical framework and practical considerations, and lacks sufficient discussion of the implications of the derived bounds. I recommend **weak reject** with the suggestion that the authors significantly expand the discussion of practical implications and connections to existing work, and perhaps focus on a more specific and tractable application within crowdsourcing.

**Summary of Ideas:** The authors model crowdsourcing as a communication system where workers are noisy channels and the goal is to accurately determine the value of a function associated with a dataset. They analyze the fundamental limits in terms of query budget and fidelity, considering both scenarios where worker skill levels are unknown and perfectly known. They introduce k-ary incidence coding (kIC) as a potential query scheme and analyze its performance, even proposing a query pricing strategy. The core contribution lies in deriving information-theoretic bounds on the minimum query budget required to achieve a desired fidelity, and highlighting the differences between known and unknown worker skill levels.

**Relation to Previous Work:** The paper correctly positions itself within the existing literature on crowdsourcing, referencing key works on aggregation schemes (majority voting, Dawid-Skene) and task assignment strategies. It accurately notes the limitations of existing empirical work and emphasizes the need for fundamental limits. However, the connection to *information-theoretic* work in related fields (e.g., distributed sensing, active learning) is not sufficiently explored.  While the authors mention joint source-channel coding, a deeper dive into how this framework has been applied to similar problems (noisy sensor networks, for example) would strengthen the paper.  The presented work builds upon Dawid-Skene but doesn’t thoroughly discuss how it generalizes or contrasts with approaches like variational inference that are becoming prevalent in crowdsourcing.

**Strengths:**

*   **Rigorous Theoretical Framework:** The use of information theory provides a solid foundation for analyzing the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing. The mathematical derivations appear correct and the framework is well-defined.
*   **Clear Problem Formulation:**  The modeling of crowdsourcing as a communication system is insightful and allows for a formal treatment of the budget-fidelity trade-off.
*   **Introduction of k-ary Incidence Coding:** The kIC scheme is a potentially interesting query strategy with error correction capabilities, and its analysis is a valuable contribution.
*   **Identification of Key Trade-offs:**  The paper clearly demonstrates the benefit of knowing worker skill levels and its impact on the required query budget.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Practical Relevance:** The derived bounds, while theoretically sound, are very general and lack concrete values for realistic crowdsourcing scenarios (e.g., image labeling on AMT). It's difficult to assess how useful these bounds are in practice without relating them to typical error rates, costs, and dataset sizes. The assumptions of i.i.d. sources and perfectly known channel statistics (in the SL-CS case) are overly optimistic.
*   **Disconnect between Theory and Practice:** The paper spends a significant amount of time deriving bounds but offers limited discussion on how these bounds might be approached in practice. How can a real-world crowdsourcing system *approach* the theoretical limits? What are the practical challenges in estimating worker skill levels?
*   **Insufficient Discussion of kIC:** While kIC is introduced, its practical implementation and advantages over simpler schemes are not convincingly demonstrated. The proposed query pricing strategy feels tacked on and lacks a detailed justification. A more thorough comparison with existing query strategies is needed.
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** There is no empirical validation of the theoretical results.  Even a small-scale simulation or analysis on a real-world dataset would significantly strengthen the paper.
*   **Clarity in Presentation:**  While the core ideas are present, the presentation can be dense and challenging for readers not deeply familiar with information theory. Some of the notation is cumbersome.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Provides a novel theoretical framework for analyzing crowdsourcing.
*   Derives fundamental limits on the budget-fidelity trade-off.
*   Introduces a potentially useful query scheme (kIC).

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lacks practical relevance and connection to real-world crowdsourcing scenarios.
*   Insufficient empirical validation.
*   Limited discussion of how to approach theoretical limits in practice.
*   Overly general bounds that are difficult to interpret.



**Specific Comments & Suggestions:**

*   **Focus & Scope:** Consider narrowing the scope of the paper to a specific type of crowdsourcing task (e.g., image classification) and providing more concrete bounds for that task.
*   **Practical Implications:** Dedicate a section to discussing the practical implications of the derived bounds. How can they be used to optimize crowdsourcing campaigns?  What are the limitations?
*   **Worker Skill Estimation:**  Discuss realistic methods for estimating worker skill levels in practice (e.g., using control tasks, reputation systems).
*   **Comparison to Existing Work:**  Provide a more detailed comparison of kIC to existing query strategies in terms of cost, accuracy, and robustness.
*   **Empirical Validation:**  Include simulations or experiments on a real-world dataset to validate the theoretical results.
*   **Clarity:** Simplify the presentation and provide more intuition behind the mathematical derivations.




In conclusion, while the paper presents a solid theoretical contribution, its limited practical relevance and lack of empirical validation weaken its overall impact.  I believe that with significant revisions addressing the points mentioned above, this work could be a valuable contribution to the field. However, in its current form, I recommend rejection.
