This paper investigates fundamental limits of budget-fidelity trade-offs in label crowdsourcing, modeling the problem through an information-theoretic lens. The authors explore achievable rates for recovering a signal (e.g., image label) given a budget of queries to a crowd of workers, considering scenarios with unknown and perfectly known worker skill levels. They also propose and analyze a k-ary incidence coding scheme.

The paper's strength lies in its attempt to provide a theoretical underpinning for crowdsourcing system design. Framing the problem as a communication system is a reasonable approach, and the exploration of fundamental limits, even with simplifying assumptions, is valuable. The consideration of both unknown and known worker skill levels adds nuance. The k-ary incidence coding is a concrete scheme worth investigating.

However, the paper suffers from a significant disconnect between theoretical analysis and practical relevance. The information-theoretic bounds derived, while mathematically sound, rely on idealized assumptions (e.g., large block lengths, perfectly known channel statistics in the ‘known skill’ scenario) that rarely hold in real-world crowdsourcing settings. The analysis, while rigorous, feels detached from the complexities of platforms like AMT, such as worker heterogeneity beyond skill, task-specific difficulties, and the impact of incentive structures. The presented theorems provide bounds, but don't offer much actionable guidance for *designing* a practical crowdsourcing system. The practical applicability of k-ary incidence coding remains unclear. While the paper mentions cost considerations in the discussion of kIC, the analysis does not fully integrate a practical cost model for queries, which is critical for real-world deployment. 

The comparison to existing crowdsourcing literature feels superficial. The introduction briefly mentions established methods like Dawid-Skene and majority voting, but the paper doesn’t convincingly demonstrate how these approaches compare to, or are encompassed by, the information-theoretic framework. The authors don't position their work as an improvement upon these existing methodologies, rather presenting it as a fundamentally different approach, without adequately showing why this new approach is superior or complementary.  The numerical results are limited and don’t convincingly illustrate the benefits of the proposed techniques.

The writing, while generally clear, is dense and could benefit from more intuitive explanations, particularly when introducing the information-theoretic concepts. The numerous equations and symbols make the paper challenging to follow for a reader not already deeply versed in information theory.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Provides a formal, information-theoretic analysis of crowdsourcing, which is a relatively unexplored area.
*   Identifies fundamental limits on performance in different scenarios.
*   Proposes a novel coding scheme (k-ary incidence coding).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The theoretical results are highly abstract and lack clear practical implications.
*   The assumptions underlying the analysis are overly simplistic and do not reflect real-world crowdsourcing complexities.
*   The connection to existing crowdsourcing methods is weak.
*   The numerical evaluation is limited.
*   The paper does not offer a compelling demonstration of how the derived bounds can be used to design better crowdsourcing systems.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting theoretical exploration, but falls short of making a significant contribution to the field. The lack of practical grounding and the limited connection to existing work outweigh the novelty of the approach. While mathematically sound, the work feels more like a proof-of-concept exploration of applying information theory to crowdsourcing than a practical contribution to the field.  I recommend **rejection**. The authors would significantly strengthen the paper by focusing on a more constrained, realistic scenario, providing a more thorough comparison to existing methods, and demonstrating the practical value of their theoretical results.
