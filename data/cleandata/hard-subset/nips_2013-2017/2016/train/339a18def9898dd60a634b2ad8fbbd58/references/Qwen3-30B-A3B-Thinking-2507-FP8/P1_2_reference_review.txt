

# Peer Review: "Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing"

## Summary
This paper models crowdsourcing as a communication system and analyzes its fundamental performance limits using information theory. The authors formulate the crowdsourcing problem as a source-channel coding problem, deriving information-theoretic bounds for the minimum number of queries per item needed to achieve a desired fidelity (error probability) under two scenarios: when worker skill levels are unknown (SL-UK) and when they are perfectly known (SL-CS). They introduce a novel query scheme called k-ary incidence coding (kIC) and analyze its performance theoretically. The work builds upon prior crowdsourcing research (e.g., Dawid and Skene algorithm, majority voting) but provides a fundamentally new perspective by framing the problem within information theory.

## Strengths

1. **Original Theoretical Framework**: The paper's most significant contribution is establishing an information-theoretic foundation for crowdsourcing analysis, which is novel in the field. The authors successfully bridge information theory with crowdsourcing, providing fundamental limits that previous empirical and algorithmic approaches could not.

2. **Clear Problem Formulation**: The paper establishes a well-defined communication model for crowdsourcing (Figure 1a), making the problem amenable to rigorous analysis. The modeling of workers as noisy channels and the definition of distortion as error probability are particularly effective.

3. **Theoretical Contributions**: Theorems 2 and 3 provide the first fundamental limits for crowdsourcing budget-fidelity trade-offs. These results are significant because they establish ultimate performance bounds that any crowdsourcing system (query scheme and inference algorithm) must satisfy.

4. **Novel Query Scheme**: The k-ary incidence coding (kIC) scheme is well-motivated and analyzed. The paper demonstrates how kIC can provide error correction capability through its design, which is a valuable practical insight.

5. **Comprehensive Related Work**: The paper thoroughly reviews prior crowdsourcing work (Dawid and Skene, majority voting, bandit survey problem) and clearly positions its theoretical contribution within this landscape.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Worker Model**: The analysis focuses on two specific channel models (MSC and SHC) which may not capture the full complexity of real crowdsourcing scenarios. The paper acknowledges this limitation but doesn't explore how the bounds might generalize to other worker models.

2. **Non-Adaptive Coding Assumption**: The paper assumes non-adaptive coding (no feedback from decoder to encoder), which is a significant simplification. In practice, adaptive crowdsourcing systems (where query design can change based on previous responses) are common and potentially more efficient.

3. **Limited Experimental Validation**: While the theoretical bounds are compelling, the paper lacks empirical validation of the kIC scheme on real crowdsourcing data. The numerical results in Section 4.2 are theoretical bounds rather than empirical performance comparisons.

4. **Practical Implementation Gap**: The paper doesn't adequately address how the theoretical results would translate to real-world crowdsourcing platforms. For instance, it doesn't discuss how to handle worker heterogeneity beyond the SHC model or how to implement the kIC scheme in practice.

5. **Narrow Scope of Analysis**: The kIC performance analysis (Section 4.1) is limited to binary labeling (N=2), which restricts the generalizability of the results to more complex crowdsourcing tasks.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution by establishing fundamental limits for crowdsourcing systems using information theory. The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and addresses an important problem in the field. The derivation of the distortion-rate function for crowdsourcing (Theorems 2 and 3) is particularly valuable as it provides a benchmark for evaluating all possible crowdsourcing schemes.

While the paper's theoretical framework is robust, its practical implications could be strengthened by addressing the limitations noted above. The authors should consider discussing how the bounds might extend to more realistic worker models, how adaptive coding might improve upon the non-adaptive results, and how the kIC scheme could be implemented in real crowdsourcing platforms.

The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of crowdsourcing. It advances the state of the art by providing the first fundamental limits for the budget-fidelity trade-off, which previous empirical work could not.

## Recommendation

The paper makes a compelling theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of crowdsourcing fundamentals. While the non-adaptive coding assumption and limited worker models are noteworthy limitations, the theoretical results are significant and well-supported. I recommend acceptance, as the paper provides a valuable new perspective that will guide future research in crowdsourcing design.

The authors should consider addressing the practical implementation gap in future work, particularly regarding how the theoretical bounds translate to real-world systems with diverse worker populations. The paper would also benefit from a brief discussion of how their framework might extend to more complex crowdsourcing tasks beyond binary labeling.

This paper is an important contribution to the theoretical foundations of crowdsourcing and will be valuable to researchers working at the intersection of information theory and human computation.
