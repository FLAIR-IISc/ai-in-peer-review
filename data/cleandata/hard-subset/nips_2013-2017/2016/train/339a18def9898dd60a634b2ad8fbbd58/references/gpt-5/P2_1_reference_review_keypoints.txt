- Paper models crowdsourcing as a communication system and uses source–channel separation to derive lower bounds on queries per item for target error under unknown (SL-UK) and known (SL-CS) worker skills.
- Formulas relate Hamming rate–distortion to effective channel capacity: SL-UK uses averaged channel capacity, SL-CS uses average of per-worker capacities, quantifying value of skill estimation.
- Studies k-ary incidence coding (kIC) where k-item queries enforce consistent clusterings, producing an error-correcting effect; derives a lower bound under a spammer–hammer channel and a pricing rule-of-thumb.
- Connects to aggregation and budget-reliability literature (majority vote, Dawid–Skene, Karger–Oh–Shah) and to clustering via comparative constraints; IT framing is relatively novel in this space.
- The separation-based lower bounds for M-ary symmetric channels with Hamming distortion appear technically sound and follow standard rate–distortion and capacity results.
- kIC analysis and the SHC-based necessary condition (Theorem 4) are plausible, with the derived rate bound logical under the oracle-decoder assumption.
- Some lemmas (e.g., for ε̄_S(k)) are reasonable but presented without proof in the main text.
- Completeness concerns: proofs are deferred to appendices and main-text proof sketches would help clarify assumptions like independence and channel mixing.
- Presentation needs substantial polishing: heavy/inconsistent notation, malformed equations, unclear definitions (e.g., E(·), hats/bars).
- Roles of encoder/decoder/taskmaster and why encoder cannot adapt in SL-CS need clearer explanation.
- The pricing formula for kIC is ambiguous; assumptions behind linear-in-k cost should be justified.
- References contain omissions and typos; related areas (triplet comparisons, learning to rank) are under-covered.
- Originality is moderate: framing is neat and helpful, but resulting formulas are straightforward given the mapping; kIC bound is specific but incremental.
- Significance is conceptual: clarifies benefit of skill estimation and informs high-level system design, but practical impact is limited by strong idealized assumptions and lack of empirical validation.
- Strengths: principled IT treatment, clear SL-UK vs SL-CS contrast, formal kIC analysis, potential benchmarking value.
- Weaknesses: presentation and editorial issues, heavy reliance on idealized assumptions, no empirical or constructive schemes, kIC independence/oracle assumptions may not hold.
- Key questions for authors include providing main-text proof sketches, empirical tightness/finite-regime evaluation, handling adaptive allocation or input-dependent noise, clarifying kIC cost model, and sensitivity to asymmetry/class imbalance.
- Pros for acceptance: clean theoretical framing, useful baselines, novel perspective on k-ary queries.
- Cons for acceptance: technically incremental, limited practical relevance without experiments or constructions, substantial clarity issues.
- Recommendation: weak reject pending improved clarity, empirical/constructive validation, and discussion of more realistic/adaptive worker models.
