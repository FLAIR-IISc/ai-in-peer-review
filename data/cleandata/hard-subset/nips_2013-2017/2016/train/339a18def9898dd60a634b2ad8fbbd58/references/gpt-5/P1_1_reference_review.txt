Summary
This paper studies fundamental budget–fidelity trade-offs for label crowdsourcing via an information-theoretic lens. The authors model a dataset with an underlying discrete source B(X) (e.g., labels or clusters), workers as noisy channels, and queries/aggregation as a coding/decoding scheme. They derive lower bounds on the minimum number of queries per item (rate) required to achieve a target error (distortion) using Shannon’s separation theorem. Two worker models are considered: (i) an M-ary symmetric channel (MSC) with heterogeneous error rates drawn from a distribution over skill levels; and (ii) a spammer–hammer channel (SHC) with probability q of a “hammer” (perfect) worker. They analyze two scenarios: worker skill levels unknown to the crowdsourcer (SL-UK) vs known (SL-CS). The main bounds (Theorems 2–3) show that Rmin is the source rate-distortion requirement [H(B) − HN(ε̂)] divided by an effective per-query channel information term. With SL-UK this is log M − HM(E[ε]), while with SL-CS it becomes log M − E[HM(ε)], reflecting the classic gain from knowing the channel state at the decoder.

The paper also proposes k-ary incidence coding (kIC): querying k items at once and constraining the answer to valid partitionings, which implicitly provides error-correction by excluding invalid patterns. For SHC and binary labels, a lemma computes the spammer’s per-item error probability under kIC, and a converse bound (Theorem 4) gives a necessary rate condition to achieve a target error with an oracle decoder. A corollary specializes the information-theoretic bound for SHC. The authors briefly discuss a pricing heuristic relating per-query prices across kIC designs.

Relation to prior work
The work connects to Dawid–Skene and EM-style aggregation, spectral/convex approaches (e.g., Zhang et al.; Vinayak et al.), bandit-style adaptive task allocation (Abraham et al.), and budget-optimal schemes (Karger–Oh–Shah). The separation-based framing is standard in information theory; the novelty lies in applying it cleanly to crowdsourcing with explicit SL-UK vs SL-CS comparisons and analyzing kIC as a coding scheme. Related information-theoretic lower/upper bounds for DS-type models and adaptive allocation exist; a more thorough positioning versus Karger–Oh–Shah’s limits and recent minimax analyses would strengthen the paper.

Strengths
- Clear, principled information-theoretic framing, yielding intuitive bounds: “source uncertainty to be conveyed” divided by “per-query information through the crowd.”
- Useful distinction between unknown vs known worker skills: the capacity of the averaged MSC (SL-UK) vs the average of capacities with receiver state information (SL-CS).
- kIC is an appealing query design that formalizes the idea that structural constraints can provide built-in error correction; the SHC analysis gives interpretable necessary conditions and highlights how k and q trade off.
- The results are broadly applicable as benchmarks independent of specific aggregation algorithms.

Weaknesses
- Incomplete presentation and clarity issues: many typographical errors, missing years in references, inconsistent notation (N vs M), and missing figures/appendices with proofs. The kIC pricing rule appears mistyped (π(k1)/π(k2) ≈ k1k2 instead of proportional to k1/k2).
- Limited technical depth beyond standard separation: Theorems 2–3 follow directly from rate–distortion plus channel capacity for (mixtures of) MSCs. Proof sketches or rigorous handling of mixture channels and state knowledge are not shown in the main text.
- kIC analysis provides only a converse (necessary condition), relies on an oracle decoder, and does not present constructive achievability or tightness. The bound in Theorem 4 is likely derived from P(error) ≥ (1−q)R′ · pS(k); the inequality in the text appears to have sign/inversion issues. Clarification is needed.
- No empirical or synthetic validation to measure how close practical algorithms (e.g., DS, spectral-EM) get to the bounds, nor comparisons of kIC vs pairwise queries under realistic budgets and worker models.
- Scope limitations: non-adaptive only; ignores feedback/active task assignment which is known to materially improve budget fidelity; finite-length effects are not discussed.
- Practical aspects (pricing) are speculative and unvalidated.

Quality
Technically mostly sound at a high level; the separation-based bounds for MSC mixtures are correct in spirit: capacity with unknown state equals capacity of the averaged symmetric channel, and with receiver state equals expected MSC capacity. The rate–distortion expression R(D) = H(B) − HN(D) under Hamming distortion is standard. However, the kIC converse derivation is opaque in the main text, and several formulas have typos or sign issues that need correction. The paper does not provide constructive codes approaching the bounds or empirical evidence.

Clarity
The manuscript needs significant editing. Notation collisions (N vs M), missing definitions (e.g., clarify M for kIC’s answer alphabet), and many formatting/typo issues hinder readability. Figures and appendices are referenced but not provided. The pricing section is very brief and not rigorous. Clearer statements of assumptions (uniform vs nonuniform sources, how R relates to per-item inclusion count R′ ≈ kR), and a compact, self-contained proof sketch for Theorems 2–3 would help.

Originality
Moderate. The use of separation to get budget–fidelity bounds under MSC/SHC is natural but useful. The SL-UK vs SL-CS comparison is insightful. kIC as a coding lens for multi-item queries is interesting, though related ideas exist in pairwise/triwise comparison literature. Novelty would be stronger with tight achievability or adaptive designs.

Significance
As a benchmark paper, it could help position algorithmic advances against information-theoretic limits and motivate structured queries like kIC. However, without achievability results, experiments, or tighter analysis, the immediate impact is limited. Strengthening connections to existing optimality results (e.g., Karger–Oh–Shah) and demonstrating closeness in practice would raise significance.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Provide clean, self-contained proofs or at least detailed sketches for Theorems 2–3 and the kIC converse. Explicitly justify capacity expressions for mixture MSCs under SL-UK and SL-CS.
- Clarify M vs N throughout, and specify M for kIC (number of valid response patterns depends on k and N).
- Fix Theorem 4 inequality and define R′ (number of kIC queries that include a given item). If the intended bound is R ≥ (1/k) ln(ε/εS)/ln(1−q), state and derive it cleanly.
- Add achievability or near-achievability: propose a concrete non-adaptive assignment plus decoder (e.g., weighted majority with worker-skill estimation) and show performance vs bounds on synthetic data.
- Discuss finite-length effects and concentration to guide practical budgets.
- Compare kIC empirically to pairwise queries for SHC/MSC with the same cost, and justify the pricing rule quantitatively.
- Improve related work to include budget-optimal task allocation and rate results in DS-type models, and clarify how your bounds relate/tighten them.

Pros for acceptance
- Principled, general framework and clean lower bounds.
- Insightful treatment of SL-UK vs SL-CS.
- Introduces and analyzes a structured query design (kIC).

Cons for acceptance
- Presentation issues and typos; missing proofs and figures.
- Primarily converse results; no achievability or empirical validation.
- Limited novelty beyond standard separation; kIC analysis is preliminary.
- Practical claims (pricing) are not substantiated.

Recommendation
Reject in current form. With clarified statements/proofs, corrected errors, stronger positioning vs prior theory, and empirical/constructive components demonstrating tightness or practical relevance (especially for kIC), this could become a valuable contribution.
