Summary and relation to prior work
This paper models crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel communication problem to study fundamental trade-offs between labeling budget (queries per item) and fidelity (error rate). The source is the ground-truth labels B(X) of a dataset; workers are modeled as discrete memoryless channels with either an M-ary symmetric channel (MSC) error model or a spammer–hammer channel (SHC). Using the rate–distortion and source–channel separation framework, the authors derive information-theoretic lower bounds on the minimum number of queries per item required to achieve a target average error rate, under two scenarios: (i) worker skill levels unknown to the taskmaster and crowdsourcer (SL-UK), and (ii) worker skill levels known perfectly to the crowdsourcer (SL-CS). The bounds (Theorems 2 and 3) highlight a gap between operating with averaged channel quality versus the average of individual channel qualities.

The paper also introduces k-ary incidence coding (kIC), where a query presents k items and asks for within-query clustering (generalizing pairwise and triplet queries). The authors show that restricting responses to graph-consistent partitions removes invalid patterns, giving an error-correction effect. For SHC workers, they derive the per-query error of a spammer under kIC (Lemma 1) and a converse-style lower bound on the rate needed to achieve a target error (Theorem 4), assuming an oracle decoder that identifies spammers vs hammers. A corollary instantiates the information-theoretic lower bound for SHC with known q, and the paper discusses implications for query pricing.

This connects to a large body of work on aggregation and redundancy in crowdsourcing: Dawid–Skene EM estimation, spectral/EM hybrids (e.g., Zhang et al., NIPS 2014), Karger–Oh–Shah (KOS) style iterative inference and budget-optimal task allocation, and bandit survey models for worker selection. The contribution here is to recast these design questions in a clean information-theoretic language and to analyze k-ary query schemes through that lens.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity in formalizing crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel problem, making explicit the role of redundancy (channel coding) vs efficient querying (source coding).
- General lower bounds (Theorems 2 and 3) that separate unknown vs known skill regimes under MSC workers; the distinction between “capacity of the average channel” and “average of capacities” is useful.
- kIC is a natural and practical query class; the argument that invalid response patterns induce error-correction is insightful and may inspire better query design.
- The SHC analysis gives interpretable dependence on k and the spammer fraction, and the pricing rule-of-thumb is a nice byproduct.
- Scope is appropriate for NeurIPS (learning with noisy labels, information-theoretic limits, crowdsourcing design).

Weaknesses
Quality
- The paper provides converse-style lower bounds but no achievability results that approach them. Without constructions (even in idealized settings) that nearly meet the limits, it is hard to assess tightness or practical relevance.
- Several modeling assumptions are strong: i.i.d. labels with known prior, memoryless channels independent of item difficulty, random non-adaptive assignments, and in the kIC analysis an oracle decoder that perfectly identifies worker types. These can make the bounds optimistic relative to real systems.
- The role of M vs N is sometimes unclear; in Theorems 2–3, N is the number of classes in B(X), but M appears as “number of choices offered per query.” It would help to disentangle these carefully, especially when kIC effectively changes M and the induced channel.
- No empirical or simulation validation: e.g., plotting achievable error with DS/spectral decoders under MSC/SHC and comparing to the bounds, or showing the error-correcting benefit of kIC in practice.

Clarity
- The exposition is generally understandable but would benefit from tighter, consistent notation (N vs M; U vs V; pmax; HN(·) and HM(·) definitions). Some equations and figure references are hard to follow without the appendices/figures.
- Typos/formatting issues and incomplete references (years missing) detract from readability. A worked example illustrating Theorems 2–3 with concrete numbers would help.

Originality
- Framing crowdsourcing within a rate–distortion/separation theorem lens and the specific bounds under MSC are, to my knowledge, not standard in prior NeurIPS papers and are a meaningful conceptual contribution. kIC as a coding lens on multi-item queries is also interesting. That said, related theoretical limits exist in the KOS line (e.g., budget-optimal redundancy and phase transitions) and in some information-theoretic analyses of noisy crowdsourcing. Positioning relative to those (tightness, assumptions) could be strengthened.

Significance
- The work provides a principled baseline for what any algorithm could hope to achieve, and may guide pricing and query design. However, the gap between the lower bounds and implementable algorithms is not explored, limiting immediate impact. Incorporating adaptivity or worker selection (e.g., bandit surveys) into the information-theoretic framework would further increase significance.

Specific suggestions for improvement
- Provide achievability results, even under stylized conditions: e.g., show that simple random coding or practical decoders (majority, DS, spectral) with appropriate redundancy approach the SL-CS bound for MSC/SHC within a constant factor.
- Clarify and unify notation; explicitly define M and N in all theorems and connect them to concrete query types (direct labeling vs kIC).
- Quantify the looseness introduced by the oracle in Theorem 4 (e.g., compare with a realistic decoder that estimates worker types jointly with labels).
- Explore adaptive designs in the framework (feedback link), or argue formally why separation still guides optimality under certain adaptive schemes.
- Add experiments/simulations comparing kIC vs pairwise vs unary queries under MSC/SHC with standard decoders, showing the error-correction effect and validating the pricing rule.
- Discuss robustness to non-i.i.d. class priors, item difficulty (GLAD-like models), and adversarial spammers.
- Improve references (complete bibliographic info) and link more directly to KOS’s budget-optimal results and phase transitions.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Clear, principled information-theoretic framing with general lower bounds.
- Insightful analysis of k-ary incidence coding and its implicit error correction.
- Potential to influence how we design and price queries.

Cons:
- No matching achievability or empirical validation; tightness unknown.
- Strong assumptions (oracle, non-adaptive, i.i.d., memoryless channels).
- Clarity/notation and references need work.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper offers a compelling conceptual framework and useful converse bounds, but to meet NeurIPS standards it should include achievability evidence (theoretical or empirical) and clearer positioning versus existing theory, along with improved clarity and experiments demonstrating the practical implications of the proposed coding perspective. I encourage the authors to strengthen these aspects; the line of work is promising.
