This paper tackles a fundamental and important problem: understanding the theoretical limits of budget-fidelity trade-offs in crowdsourcing. The authors model crowdsourcing as a communication system and derive information-theoretic bounds on performance, considering both scenarios where worker skill levels are known and unknown. The introduction of k-ary incidence coding as a concrete query scheme is a particularly interesting and novel contribution. While the paper is dense and the presentation could be improved, the boldness of the approach and the potential for significant impact outweigh the methodological shortcomings.

**Summary:** The core idea is to cast crowdsourcing as a communication problem, allowing for the application of information theory to establish fundamental limits. The authors analyze the trade-off between the cost (budget) of querying workers and the fidelity (accuracy) of the resulting labels. They introduce k-IC as a structured query scheme aimed at improving efficiency and robustness. The paper provides lower bounds on the required query rate for achieving a given level of accuracy, distinguishing between cases where worker skills are known or unknown.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The application of information theory to crowdsourcing is a genuinely novel perspective. Most existing work focuses on algorithmic improvements *within* a given crowdsourcing framework; this paper attempts to define the ultimate limits of such frameworks. The k-IC scheme itself is a creative attempt to exploit structure in the problem to achieve better performance.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The authors employ a solid information-theoretic framework and derive meaningful bounds. The proofs (while not fully examined in detail by this reviewer) appear sound, and the results provide a valuable benchmark for evaluating practical crowdsourcing algorithms.
* **Potential Impact:**  Understanding the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing could guide the design of more efficient and reliable systems.  The query pricing strategy discussion, albeit brief, hints at practical implications.  The k-IC scheme, if shown to perform well in practice, could be a significant contribution.
* **Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of balancing budget and fidelity, and frames it in a way amenable to theoretical analysis.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity & Presentation:** The paper is quite dense and mathematically involved. The notation is heavy, and the motivation for some choices isn’t always immediately clear.  More intuitive explanations and examples would significantly improve accessibility.  Figure 1, while attempting to illustrate the model, feels cluttered.
* **Limited Empirical Validation:** The paper is almost entirely theoretical. While the authors acknowledge the need for empirical validation, it's lacking.  The numerical results are limited and serve primarily to illustrate the bounds rather than demonstrating the practical performance of k-IC.  A simulation or real-world experiment comparing k-IC to existing methods (majority voting, DS) would significantly strengthen the paper.
* **Assumptions & Simplifications:** The model relies on several simplifying assumptions (e.g., i.i.d. data, memoryless channels, perfect skill estimation in one scenario). The impact of relaxing these assumptions isn't thoroughly discussed.
* **Practical Considerations:** The discussion of query pricing feels tacked on and underdeveloped. The practical challenges of implementing k-IC in a real-world crowdsourcing platform (e.g., worker comprehension, interface design) are not addressed.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a fundamentally new way of thinking about crowdsourcing.
* The theoretical results are significant and provide a valuable benchmark.
* The k-IC scheme is a promising idea, even if its practical performance remains to be demonstrated.
* The authors tackle a challenging problem with a bold and ambitious approach.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of empirical validation is a significant weakness.
* The paper is difficult to read and understand.
* The practical implications of the work are not fully explored.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper is a high-risk, high-reward submission. It's not perfect, and the lack of empirical validation is a concern. However, the novelty of the approach, the theoretical rigor, and the potential for significant impact are compelling.  I believe the strengths outweigh the weaknesses, and the paper represents a valuable contribution to the field. I recommend **acceptance** with the caveat that the authors should prioritize empirical validation and improve the clarity of the presentation in a future version. The paper pushes the boundaries of how we think about crowdsourcing and, despite its flaws, deserves to be presented at NIPS.
