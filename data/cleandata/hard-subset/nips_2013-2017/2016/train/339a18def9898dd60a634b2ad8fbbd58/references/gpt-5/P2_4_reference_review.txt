Summary
The paper proposes an information-theoretic framework for analyzing budget–fidelity trade-offs in label crowdsourcing. Workers are modeled as noisy channels; the dataset labels are a source; a query/inference pipeline is a joint source–channel code. Two worker models are considered: (i) M-ary symmetric channels (MSC) with heterogeneous error rates, and (ii) a spammer–hammer channel (SHC), where a worker is perfect with probability q and otherwise random. Two scenarios are analyzed: SL-UK (worker skills unknown to the crowdsourcer) and SL-CS (skills known to the crowdsourcer). Using source–channel separation and rate–distortion theory with Hamming distortion, the paper derives necessary budget conditions (minimum queries per item) for a target average error. The key distinction between SL-UK and SL-CS is whether capacity is computed on the averaged channel (log M − H_M(E[ε])) or as the average of capacities (E[log M − H_M(ε)]), consistent with DMCs with receiver side information. The paper then studies k-ary incidence coding (kIC) as a structured set-valued query scheme that constrains worker responses to valid partitions, arguing it has “error-correction” due to invalid response patterns. For SHC, it derives: (a) the error rate of a spammer under kIC, and (b) a converse bound: to drive error ≤ ε with a non-adaptive kIC and an oracle decoder that perfectly identifies spammers/hammers, one needs R ≥ (1/k) ln(ε/εS)/ln(1−q), where εS is the spammer-only error rate. A corollary specializes the SL-CS bound to SHC, yielding a benchmark R ≥ (1 − H_b(ε))/(q log M).

Quality
- Technical soundness: The separation-based lower bounds are plausible and consistent with known results. For SL-UK, treating the randomly varying worker types as an averaged DMC and, for SL-CS, as a state-known-to-receiver DMC gives the standard capacities. The use of the known rate–distortion expression with Hamming distortion and the domain restriction D ≤ min{1 − pmax, 1 − 1/N} is reasonable (outside this, rate can be zero). The SHC corollary (capacity q log M under receiver side info) is correct.
- However, this section mostly repackages textbook results (Cover & Thomas; El Gamal & Kim) into the crowdsourcing setting; the novelty lies in the combination rather than new theory. The kIC converse bound essentially counts the chance that an item is never seen by a hammer and multiplies by the spammer-error floor—again a clean but simple argument. Proofs are deferred to appendices not provided; several definitions (e.g., exact cost model translating “queries per item” to number of item-inclusions under kIC) are not fully explicit. There are claims of “error correction capability” for kIC but no achievability constructions that approach the bounds, and no empirical validation.
- No experiments are provided beyond a schematic figure comparison; figures and appendices are missing in this draft.

Clarity
- The paper is readable at a high level but has many notational ambiguities and presentation issues:
  - M vs N (choices per query vs number of classes) is easy to confuse and sometimes conflated.
  - Several typos and formatting problems occur; references are incomplete; figures are referenced but not present; appendices are missing.
  - Some key quantities (e.g., R′, the exact relation between “queries per item” and number of times an item appears in a k-ary query) are not crisply defined; yet they are crucial to Theorem 4.
  - The scope of the claimed rate–distortion expression for general non-uniform sources under Hamming distortion should be clearly stated with proper caveats and citation.
- The overview of Shannon theory is long relative to the new content; space could be used to tighten the core contributions.

Originality
- Modeling crowdsourcing via source–channel separation is not new in spirit; related “limits” work in crowdsourcing studies sample complexity and redundancy vs. error (e.g., Karger–Oh–Shah and follow-ups) and heterogeneous workers. The specific separation-based derivation for SL-UK vs SL-CS is standard. The kIC analysis for SHC is modestly novel in framing and could be useful, but the main bound is a simple oracle converse. The pricing “rule of thumb” is heuristic and not substantiated.
- The paper would benefit from positioning against prior IT-style limits for crowdsourcing/aggregation (beyond [6,13]), including works on task assignment, adaptive querying, and heterogeneous annotators.

Significance
- The framework can help practitioners reason about whether target error rates are feasible given worker pools and query designs, and the SL-UK vs SL-CS comparison is intuitively insightful (benefit of estimating worker reliability). The kIC section hints that structured multi-item queries can improve effective information per annotation.
- However, the current results are primarily lower bounds without achievability or algorithms approaching them, and the practical impact is limited without experiments or design guidance. The improvements over known heuristics (majority vote, DS, spectral/EM methods) are not demonstrated.

Questions and suggestions
- Provide full proofs or at least proof sketches in the main text for central theorems, or tighten statements as “converse bounds” if achievability is not shown. Clarify the exact conditions under which R(D)=H(B) − H_N(D) holds for non-uniform sources.
- Explicitly define the mapping between “queries per item” and item-inclusions for kIC; clarify cost models (time/price) and ensure apples-to-apples comparisons across k.
- Include empirical studies or simulations that compare kIC to pairwise queries and validate the converse trends; explore achievability heuristics (e.g., simple decoders) that approach the bounds.
- Position more thoroughly relative to prior limits work (sample complexity, adaptive assignment, worker heterogeneity).
- Clean up notation, references, and missing figures/appendices.

Pros
- Clear, principled framework connecting crowdsourcing to joint source–channel coding.
- Correct and interpretable comparison between unknown vs known worker skills.
- Simple, intuitive converse bound for k-ary incidence coding under SHC, with a potential pricing implication.

Cons
- Limited novelty; many results follow directly from standard IT theorems.
- No achievability, algorithms, or experiments; practical impact unclear.
- Presentation issues (notation, missing proofs/figures, references).
- kIC “error-correction” not backed by constructive schemes; pricing claim is heuristic.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline if substantially revised with proofs and experiments)
