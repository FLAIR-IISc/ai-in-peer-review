This paper attempts to establish fundamental limits on the budget-fidelity tradeoff in label crowdsourcing using an information-theoretic framework. While the motivation is sound and the problem setting relevant, the paper suffers from a multitude of issues ranging from a lack of clarity in the modeling choices to overly optimistic assumptions, and a disconnect between the theoretical developments and their practical implications. The presentation is also frequently imprecise, and riddled with minor, yet persistent, formatting and notational inconsistencies.

**Summary:** The authors model crowdsourcing as a communication system and aim to derive theoretical bounds on the number of queries needed to achieve a desired fidelity. They consider two scenarios: unknown worker skill levels (SL-UK) and perfectly known skill levels (SL-CS). They also introduce a k-ary incidence coding scheme and analyze its performance.

**Strengths:**

*   **Problem Motivation:** The core problem of balancing cost (budget) and accuracy (fidelity) in crowdsourcing is undeniably important.
*   **Information-Theoretic Approach:** Applying information theory to crowdsourcing is a potentially fruitful direction, offering a principled way to analyze fundamental limits.
*   **k-ary Incidence Coding:** The k-ary incidence coding scheme is a potentially interesting idea, though its practical utility is questionable (discussed below).

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Modeling Choices & Assumptions:** The modeling feels unnecessarily complex and relies on numerous assumptions without adequate justification. For instance, the assumption of a discrete memoryless channel to model worker responses is a significant simplification, especially given the inherent complexities of human judgment. The independence assumption between workers is also strong and unrealistic.
*   **Lack of Practicality:** The derived bounds, while theoretically interesting, appear largely disconnected from real-world crowdsourcing scenarios. The scenarios considered (perfectly known skill levels) are rarely achievable in practice. The k-ary incidence coding scheme, while theoretically analyzed, suffers from practical limitations. Showing *k* images simultaneously and asking workers to determine if they belong to the same cluster is cumbersome and susceptible to subjective interpretation – fundamentally different from the binary labeling it’s often compared to.
*   **Clarity & Precision:** The paper frequently lacks clarity. Definitions are sometimes vague, and the notation is inconsistent. For example, ‘R’ is used to denote both rate and the number of queries. The explanation surrounding Figures 1b and 1c is particularly confusing and requires significant effort to decipher.
*   **Minor Errors & Formatting:** Numerous minor errors and inconsistencies detract from the overall polish. For example, the use of "th" before “Conference” is stylistically unusual. There are several instances of incorrect mathematical notation, and the list of references is not consistently formatted. The phrase "SL-UK" and "SL-CS" are initially defined, but then used throughout without a consistent reminder of their meaning.
*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The paper lacks any empirical validation of the theoretical results. The numerical results presented in Section 4.2 are limited and do not convincingly demonstrate the applicability of the derived bounds.
*   **Theorem 4's Applicability:** Theorem 4 is presented with limited explanation of how it would actually be *used*. It provides a lower bound, but doesn't offer practical guidance on pricing or query design beyond the stated approximate rule of thumb which is itself not well-motivated.
* **Overclaiming:** The paper frequently presents results as "fundamental limits" without fully acknowledging the simplifying assumptions made. The derived bounds are conditional on a specific model and may not hold in more realistic scenarios.

**Specific Concerns:**

*   **Equation (1):** The MSC transition probability is standard, but its relevance to modeling human workers is not compellingly argued.
*   **Section 2.3:** The explanation of k-ary incidence coding is convoluted. The claim about error correction is weak; simply removing invalid responses doesn't constitute robust error correction.
*   **Appendix A & B:** The proofs in the appendices are not provided, making it impossible to verify the validity of the derived bounds.
*   **Corollary 1:** The result in Corollary 1 seems overly simplified. The reliance on only *q* (the spammer probability) and the fidelity level seems to disregard other important factors, like the channel capacity.
* **Missing Definitions:** The definition of Hb(̂) is missing, hindering understanding.




**Arguments for/against Acceptance:**

**Against:**

*   Significant lack of clarity and precision.
*   Overly strong assumptions and limited practical relevance.
*   Numerous minor errors and inconsistencies.
*   Lack of empirical validation.
*   Proofs are not included.

**For:**

*   Interesting problem motivation.
*   Novel application of information theory to crowdsourcing.
*   Potential of the k-ary incidence coding scheme (though unrealized).

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the potentially interesting theoretical direction, the paper is fundamentally flawed due to a combination of modeling issues, lack of clarity, and practical limitations. While the authors attempt to establish fundamental limits, the derived results are heavily contingent on unrealistic assumptions and lack empirical support.  I recommend **rejection** due to the significant number of weaknesses that overshadow the limited strengths. A major revision addressing the concerns outlined above would be required for reconsideration, and even then, demonstrating practical applicability is critical. The level of polish and detail expected of a NIPS publication is significantly lacking here.
