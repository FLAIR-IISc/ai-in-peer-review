## Review of "Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper tackles a fundamental and important problem in crowdsourcing: understanding the theoretical limits on performance given budgetary constraints. The authors frame the problem within an information-theoretic framework, which is a strong and appealing approach. While the paper presents some interesting theoretical results, especially regarding the k-ary incidence coding scheme, the presentation is dense and lacks concrete examples to illustrate the practical implications of the derived bounds. The connection to the broader crowdsourcing literature feels somewhat distant, and the numerical results are limited. I believe the paper has merit but requires significant revisions to improve clarity and impact.

**Summary of Main Ideas:**

The paper models crowdsourcing as a communication system, aiming to determine the fundamental trade-off between the cost (budget, measured as the number of queries) and the fidelity (accuracy) of the obtained labels. They analyze this trade-off under two scenarios: when worker skill levels are unknown (SL-UK) and when they are perfectly estimated (SL-CS). The authors derive information-theoretic lower bounds on the required budget for a given accuracy level in both scenarios. They also introduce and analyze a *k*-ary incidence coding scheme (kIC) as a potential way to improve performance, particularly when dealing with noisy workers.  A query pricing strategy based on *k* is proposed.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the existing crowdsourcing literature, acknowledging key works on aggregation techniques like majority voting and Dawid-Skene [5], and also referencing recent studies on worker modeling and task assignment [1, 6, 8, 13]. The use of information theory to analyze crowdsourcing is a valuable contribution, setting it apart from many empirical studies. However, the connection to the *practical* implications of these bounds in the context of existing algorithms (DS, etc.) is underdeveloped. While the authors mention these methods, they don’t explicitly discuss how their bounds relate to the performance achievable by these practical algorithms.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Framework:** The application of information theory provides a rigorous foundation for analyzing the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing.
* **Novel k-ary Incidence Coding:** The introduction of kIC offers a potentially valuable coding scheme for improving efficiency and robustness in crowdsourcing.
* **Identification of Key Trade-offs:** The analysis clearly highlights the trade-off between budget and fidelity, and the impact of worker skill level knowledge.
* **Mathematical Rigor:** The proofs (referenced as appendices) appear sound and contribute to the theoretical understanding of the problem.
* **Query Pricing Idea:** The discussion of linking query price to *k* in kIC is a practical and insightful extension.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Lack of Clarity and Accessibility:** The paper is heavily laden with mathematical notation and lacks intuitive explanations. Many concepts, especially surrounding the coding schemes, are difficult to grasp without significant effort.  More concrete examples throughout the paper are *crucially* needed.
* **Limited Numerical Evaluation:** The numerical results are sparse and don't provide a clear demonstration of the practical benefits of the proposed kIC scheme or the tightness of the derived bounds. A comparison with existing algorithms (DS, majority voting) under different worker skill distributions would significantly strengthen the paper.
* **Weak Connection to Practical Systems:** While the theoretical results are valuable, the paper doesn't sufficiently address how these bounds translate into concrete design guidelines for real-world crowdsourcing systems (e.g., Amazon Mechanical Turk). How can a taskmaster practically *estimate* worker skill levels to benefit from the SL-CS scenario?
* **Assumptions & Simplifications:** The assumptions of a perfectly known channel state in SL-CS feels unrealistic. The model of workers as MSC or SHC channels is a simplification; real worker behavior is likely more complex. These assumptions should be discussed more explicitly.
* **Overly Dense Presentation:** The paper feels crammed with information, and the flow could be improved by breaking down complex concepts into smaller, more digestible pieces.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a fundamental and important problem in crowdsourcing.
* The application of information theory provides a novel and rigorous approach.
* The k-ary incidence coding scheme has the potential to improve crowdsourcing performance.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The paper is difficult to understand due to its dense presentation and lack of concrete examples.
* The numerical evaluation is limited and doesn't convincingly demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed approach.
* The connection to existing crowdsourcing systems and algorithms is weak.
* The theoretical results, while interesting, may be difficult to translate into practical design guidelines.

**Recommendation:**

**Reject with Major Revisions.**

I believe this paper has the potential to be a significant contribution to the field, but it requires substantial revisions to improve clarity, accessibility, and practical relevance. Specifically, the authors should:

1. **Provide more intuitive explanations and concrete examples.** Illustrate the concepts with simple scenarios and visualizations.
2. **Expand the numerical evaluation.** Compare kIC with existing algorithms under realistic conditions and demonstrate the tightness of the derived bounds.
3. **Strengthen the connection to practical systems.** Discuss how the theoretical results can be used to guide the design of real-world crowdsourcing applications.
4. **Address the limitations of the assumptions.** Discuss the impact of the simplifying assumptions (e.g., perfect skill level estimation) on the results.
5. **Improve the overall clarity and organization of the paper.** Break down complex concepts into smaller pieces and ensure a logical flow.
