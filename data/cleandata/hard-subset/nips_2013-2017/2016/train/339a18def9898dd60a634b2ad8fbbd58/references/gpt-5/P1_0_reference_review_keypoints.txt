- Paper frames crowdsourcing as a communication system and derives budget–fidelity limits using rate–distortion and channel capacity.  
- Models workers as M-ary symmetric channels (MSC) and spammer–hammer channels (SHC), with decoder as the crowdsourcer.  
- Distinguishes SL-UK (worker skills unknown) vs SL-CS (skills known), yielding capacity-of-average vs average-of-capacities denominators.  
- Introduces k-ary incidence coding (kIC) that asks k-item clustering queries and exploits combinatorial constraints for error correction.  
- Derives necessary rate bounds for achieving target Hamming distortion via separation (R(D) and worker channel capacity).  
- For SHC, gives a necessary condition for kIC based on probability an item is never seen by a hammer and spammer-induced kIC error.  
- Provides a closed-form lemma for spammer error in kIC and a corollary lower bound for SHC with known worker types.  
- Sketches a pricing heuristic for kIC and comments on potential market-design applications.  
- Strength: principled information-theoretic formulation separating source complexity from worker noise.  
- Strength: insightful distinction between unknown and known skill scenarios and their differing capacity implications.  
- Strength: kIC is an appealing query primitive with built-in combinatorial error-correction.  
- Strength: SHC-based necessary condition and spammer-error lemma are intuitive and clean.  
- Strength: potential applicability to pricing and market design.  
- Weakness: main bounds largely follow standard separation with strong simplifying assumptions (iid source, MSC/SHC, memoryless channels, non-adaptive, asymptotic).  
- Weakness: no constructive achievability schemes tailored to crowdsourcing and no finite-sample analysis.  
- Weakness: worker models (MSC/SHC) are unrealistic extremes; per-item difficulty, adversarial behavior, and confusion matrices are not modeled.  
- Weakness: kIC analysis is partial—relies on an oracle for hammers, gives necessary but not sufficient conditions, treats N=2 mainly, and lacks tightness/empirical validation.  
- Weakness: no simulations or experiments to assess bound tightness or compare kIC to alternatives (majority, DS).  
- Weakness: manuscript has many typos, formatting and notation inconsistencies, missing figure content, and incomplete references.  
- Weakness: limited engagement with related theoretical crowdsourcing limits (e.g., Karger–Oh–Shah) and lack of quantitative comparisons.  
- Quality: technically sound under stated assumptions, but largely converse/lower-bound work with minimal achievability and deferred appendix details.  
- Clarity: exposition conceptually clear but below conference standards due to typographical and notation issues; key definitions and log bases need clarification.  
- Originality: moderate—application of separation is straightforward, but SL-UK vs SL-CS comparison and kIC’s error-correction angle add novelty.  
- Significance: conceptually valuable for framing budget–fidelity trade-offs and motivating kIC, but impact limited by assumptions and lack of algorithms/experiments.  
- Pros for acceptance: principled IT framework, skill-knowledge distinction, and interesting kIC perspective.  
- Cons for acceptance: limited novelty beyond separation, strong modeling simplifications, no empirical validation, and poor presentation.  
- Suggestions: compare quantitatively to prior theoretical results (e.g., KOS), extend to realistic worker models, provide explicit achievability schemes or finite-length bounds, generalize kIC beyond N=2 with practical decoders, and fix notation/references/figures.  
- Recommendation: borderline weak reject as submitted due to presentation and lack of empirical/constructive content; revise and validate to strengthen contribution.
