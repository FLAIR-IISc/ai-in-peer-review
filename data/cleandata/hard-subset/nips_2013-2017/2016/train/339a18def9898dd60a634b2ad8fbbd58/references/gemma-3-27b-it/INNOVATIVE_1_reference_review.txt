This paper tackles a fundamental, and often overlooked, question in crowdsourcing: what are the inherent limits on performance as a function of budget (number of queries) and fidelity (accuracy)? The authors model crowdsourcing as a communication system and leverage information theory to derive bounds on achievable performance. This is a bold and valuable approach; most crowdsourcing research focuses on algorithmic improvements *given* a budget, while this work attempts to characterize the best possible performance *as a function of* budget. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and positions the work within the existing literature. The framing of the problem, particularly the use of information theoretic tools, is highly original and distinguishes this work from the majority of empirical studies in this area.

The paper is strongest in its theoretical development. The derivations of the bounds in Theorems 2 and 3 are well-presented, and the insights gained – particularly the distinction between known and unknown worker skill levels – are significant. The introduction of k-ary incidence coding is a clever idea, and the analysis in Section 3.2 provides a preliminary understanding of its potential benefits. The discussion of query pricing in relation to kIC is a nice touch, suggesting practical implications.

However, the paper suffers from a few weaknesses. While the theoretical framework is robust, the connection to practical crowdsourcing systems feels somewhat tenuous. The MSC and SHC channel models are relatively simple, and it's unclear how well they generalize to real-world worker populations. The numerical results, while providing some validation of the theoretical bounds, are limited in scope and don't explore a wide range of parameters. The discussion surrounding the limitations of the analysis – particularly regarding the non-adaptive code and lack of feedback – is present but could be more thoroughly explored. A more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of achieving the derived bounds would also be beneficial. Furthermore, while the paper presents bounds, it doesn’t offer a concrete algorithm for *achieving* these bounds in practice beyond mentioning kIC. How can a taskmaster actually design queries and inference schemes to approach these limits? 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* **Novelty:** The application of information theory to the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing is a genuinely novel contribution.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivations and proofs appear rigorous and well-motivated.
* **Significance:** Identifying fundamental limits has long-term implications for the design and optimization of crowdsourcing systems.
* **Bold Approach:** The paper tackles a difficult problem from a largely unexplored perspective.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* **Practicality:** The connection to real-world crowdsourcing implementations is somewhat weak.
* **Limited Validation:** The numerical results are limited and don't provide a comprehensive evaluation.
* **Algorithmic Gap:**  The paper lacks a clear pathway to translate the theoretical bounds into practical algorithms.
* **Model Simplifications:** The channel models used are relatively simplistic and may not fully capture the complexity of real-world worker behavior.

**Overall:**

Despite its limitations, I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of crowdsourcing. The novelty of the approach, the theoretical rigor, and the potential for long-term impact outweigh the shortcomings. The paper introduces a new lens through which to view crowdsourcing problems and opens up avenues for future research. While the path to practical implementation remains unclear, the fundamental insights provided are valuable. I recommend **acceptance** with the caveat that the authors address the reviewers' concerns regarding the practical implications and expand the numerical validation in a future version. The paper prioritizes pushing boundaries and exploring a new direction, which aligns well with the spirit of NIPS.
