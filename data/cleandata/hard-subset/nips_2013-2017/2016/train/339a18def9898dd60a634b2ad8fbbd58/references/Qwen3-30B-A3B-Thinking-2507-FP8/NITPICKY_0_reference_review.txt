

# Review of "Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing"

This paper attempts to model crowdsourcing as an information theoretic communication problem, deriving fundamental limits on the budget-fidelity trade-off. While the conceptual framework is interesting and the problem is relevant to the NIPS community, the paper contains numerous technical inconsistencies, formatting errors, and insufficient justification of key modeling decisions that undermine its contribution.

## Major Technical Issues

1. **Inconsistent and Incorrect Notation**: The paper contains multiple instances of inconsistent and technically incorrect notation. Equation (1) states: "P(v|u) = { M−1 v = u 1− v = u." This is not standard notation for a symmetric channel and should be properly formatted as P(v|u) = (1-ε)/(M-1) for v ≠ u and P(v|u) = ε for v = u. Similarly, equation (2) contains formatting errors in the spammer-hammer channel model description.

2. **Undefined Terminology**: The paper uses "CS" without defining it in the context of "CS budget-fidelity function" (Section 2.4). The abbreviation "CS" is used throughout without explanation, creating confusion between "crowdsourcing" and "communication system."

3. **Inconsistent Theorem Notation**: Theorems 2 and 3 use inconsistent notation for the error probability bound (̂ vs ̂). The hat symbol is not consistently applied, making the bounds difficult to interpret.

4. **Missing Justification for Key Assumptions**: The paper assumes N=2 (binary labeling) in Section 4.1 without justification, despite earlier stating "the number of clusters, |B(X)| = N, may or may not be known a priori." This abrupt shift to binary labeling without explanation weakens the generality of their analysis.

5. **Incorrect Reference**: The paper claims "kIC bounds for k = 1 is equivalent to the analysis leading to Lemma 1 of [8]" but [8] is cited as "Inferring ground truth from subjective labeling of venus images," which appears unrelated to crowdsourcing. This reference is likely incorrect.

## Formatting and Presentation Issues

1. **Missing Figures**: The paper references "Figure 1a" (Section 2.1), "Figures 1b and 1c" (Section 2.3), and "Figure 2" (Section 4.2) but provides no actual figures. This makes the paper fundamentally incomplete and impossible to evaluate properly.

2. **Inconsistent Capitalization**: The paper inconsistently capitalizes "Crowdsourcing" (sometimes capitalized, sometimes not), which is unprofessional and confusing.

3. **Reference Formatting**: References have inconsistent formatting (e.g., "Conference on Learning Theory (COLT), , ," with missing years), which suggests poor attention to detail.

4. **Typographical Errors**: The paper contains numerous typos, such as "crowdsourcer" instead of "crowdsourcer" in multiple places, and "CS" instead of "crowdsourcing" in some contexts.

## Conceptual Weaknesses

1. **Unjustified Modeling Choice**: The paper models crowdsourcing as a communication system with a channel model but fails to adequately justify why this is the appropriate framework. The connection between crowdsourcing and information theory is not clearly established, making the framework feel imposed rather than natural.

2. **Lack of Clear Novelty**: While the paper claims to provide "fundamental limits," it doesn't clearly distinguish its results from previous work in the crowdsourcing literature. The connection to the Dawid-Skene algorithm and other existing methods is superficial.

3. **Inconsistent Treatment of Skill Levels**: The paper states "the workers' skill levels are typically unknown a priori" (Section 1) but then in Theorem 3 assumes they are "known to the crowdsourcer." The transition between these two scenarios lacks proper motivation.

4. **Unclear Relationship to Practical Crowdsourcing**: The paper doesn't explain how its theoretical bounds translate to practical crowdsourcing systems. The claim that "kIC provides an error correction capability" is not substantiated with clear examples or explanations.

## Minor but Significant Issues

1. The paper states "the rate of the code is R = ∑Wi=1 mi/n queries per item" but doesn't clarify whether this is the average rate or total rate.

2. The paper claims "the workers' skill levels are unknown both to the taskmaster and the crowdsourcer" but then in the SL-CS scenario assumes "the skill levels are perfectly estimated during inference by the crowdsourcer," creating confusion about the scenario definitions.

3. The paper refers to "the crowdsourcer" as the one doing the analysis, but earlier it's referred to as "the crowdsourcer" (with a 'c' not 's'), creating inconsistency.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper addresses an important problem and presents a theoretically interesting framework, the numerous technical inconsistencies, formatting errors, and lack of justification for key modeling decisions significantly undermine its contribution. The paper reads as if it were hastily prepared with insufficient attention to detail. The missing figures are particularly problematic as they are essential for understanding the proposed models.

The paper would benefit from a complete rewrite to address these issues. The authors should provide clear explanations for all modeling choices, properly format all equations, correct all notational inconsistencies, and include the referenced figures. Until these issues are resolved, the paper does not meet the quality standards expected for NIPS.

I recommend rejection due to these substantial technical and presentation flaws that prevent the paper from making a clear contribution to the field. The issues are not merely minor typographical errors but fundamental problems that affect the paper's credibility and comprehensibility.
