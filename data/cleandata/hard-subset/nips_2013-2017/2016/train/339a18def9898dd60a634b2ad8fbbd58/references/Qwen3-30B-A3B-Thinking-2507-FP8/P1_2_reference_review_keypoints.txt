- Summary: Frames crowdsourcing as a source-channel coding problem and derives information-theoretic bounds for queries-per-item under unknown and known worker skills, introducing k-ary incidence coding (kIC).

- Strength — Original theoretical framework: Establishes a novel information-theoretic foundation for analyzing crowdsourcing limits.

- Strength — Clear problem formulation: Models workers as noisy channels and defines distortion as error probability in a rigorous communication model.

- Strength — Theoretical contributions: Theorems 2 and 3 give fundamental budget-fidelity limits that benchmark all crowdsourcing schemes.

- Strength — Novel query scheme: Proposes and analyzes kIC, showing how its design can provide error-correction benefits.

- Strength — Related work coverage: Thoroughly positions the contribution relative to Dawid & Skene, majority voting, and other prior work.

- Weakness — Limited worker models: Analysis restricted to MSC and SHC channel models, limiting applicability to more complex real-world behaviors.

- Weakness — Non-adaptive coding assumption: Assumes no feedback/adaptation, whereas practical systems often use adaptive query strategies.

- Weakness — Limited experimental validation: Lacks empirical tests of kIC on real crowdsourced data; numerical results are theoretical bounds only.

- Weakness — Practical implementation gap: Insufficient discussion on applying results to heterogeneous worker populations or implementing kIC on platforms.

- Weakness — Narrow scope (binary labels): kIC performance analysis is limited to binary labeling, reducing generalizability to multi-class tasks.

- Overall assessment: Technically sound, significant theoretical advance providing valuable benchmarks, but practical implications need strengthening.

- Recommendation: Accept — strong theoretical contribution; authors should discuss extensions to realistic worker models, adaptive coding, and practical kIC implementation.
