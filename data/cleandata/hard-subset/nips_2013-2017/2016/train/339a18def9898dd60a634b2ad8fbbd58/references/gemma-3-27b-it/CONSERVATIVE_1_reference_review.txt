This paper attempts to establish fundamental limits on the budget-fidelity tradeoff in label crowdsourcing using an information-theoretic approach. The authors model crowdsourcing as a communication system and derive bounds on the required query budget given a desired fidelity, considering both scenarios where worker skill levels are known and unknown to the crowdsourcer. They also introduce and analyze a k-ary incidence coding scheme.

While the application of information theory to crowdsourcing is a potentially interesting direction, this paper falls short of convincing me of its novelty or practical relevance. The modeling choices, while mathematically convenient, feel overly abstract and disconnected from the realities of crowdsourcing platforms like AMT. The derivations, while presented with reasonable rigor, largely reiterate established information-theoretic principles applied to a novel framing—the novelty of the framing itself feels limited. 

A major weakness lies in the lack of empirical validation. The paper extensively focuses on theoretical bounds but provides *no* experimental results to demonstrate the tightness of these bounds or the practical performance of the proposed k-ary incidence coding. Asserting that the derived bounds are "fundamental limits" without demonstrating their relevance to real-world scenarios is a significant shortcoming. Existing crowdsourcing literature (cited within the paper) demonstrates practical limitations and behaviors that are not addressed within this purely theoretical framework. For example, the analysis assumes a static worker pool with well-defined skill levels, ignoring the dynamic nature of crowdsourcing platforms and the complexities of worker motivation and spam detection – aspects that significantly influence practical performance. 

The treatment of k-ary incidence coding, while presented with some detail, lacks a compelling justification for its practical adoption. The discussion of query pricing (in Appendix E) feels tacked on and lacks a thorough economic analysis. The benefits of kIC over simpler query schemes (like direct labeling) are not convincingly demonstrated, particularly considering the potential for increased cognitive load on workers when evaluating multiple items simultaneously. 

The clarity of the paper is acceptable, though the numerous theorems and lemmas contribute to a somewhat dense presentation. The notation is consistent, but the connection between the abstract mathematical model and real-world crowdsourcing scenarios could be improved. The related work section is adequate but could be expanded to include more recent advances in crowdsourcing, particularly those addressing worker modeling and adaptive query selection.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The application of information theory to crowdsourcing represents a potentially valuable, albeit ambitious, research direction.
*   The theoretical analysis is mathematically sound.
*   The paper clearly defines the problem and provides a formal framework for analyzing the budget-fidelity tradeoff.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of empirical validation makes it difficult to assess the practical significance of the theoretical results.
*   The modeling assumptions are overly simplistic and do not capture the complexities of real-world crowdsourcing platforms.
*   The proposed k-ary incidence coding scheme lacks a compelling practical justification.
*   The paper primarily reiterates established information-theoretic principles within a novel framing without providing substantial new insights.
*   The paper feels incomplete—it establishes bounds but does not explore practical algorithms for achieving them.

**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **rejection**. While the paper is technically sound and presents a formal analysis, the lack of empirical validation and the overly abstract modeling choices severely limit its impact. The authors need to demonstrate the practical relevance of their theoretical results, perhaps by conducting simulations or experiments on a real crowdsourcing platform. The paper, as it stands, feels more like a theoretical exploration than a significant contribution to the field. It does not advance the state-of-the-art in a demonstrable way and is unlikely to be widely used by researchers or practitioners. I am skeptical of the claims of establishing "fundamental limits" without providing supporting evidence from real-world deployments.
