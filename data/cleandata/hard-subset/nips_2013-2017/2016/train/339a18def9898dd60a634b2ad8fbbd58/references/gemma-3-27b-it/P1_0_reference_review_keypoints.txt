- Paper models crowdsourcing as a communication system and uses information theory to derive lower bounds on the budget required for a target fidelity under unknown and known skill scenarios; introduces k-ary incidence coding (kIC).

- Positions work within existing crowdsourcing literature, connects to rate-distortion and channel capacity, and generalizes prior binary incidence coding.

- Strength: Applies a rigorous information-theoretic framework to provide fundamental performance limits for crowdsourcing.

- Strength: Presents a clear, intuitive problem formulation that enables theoretical analysis.

- Strength: Theorems 2 and 3 give concrete lower bounds on budget vs. fidelity, serving as benchmarks.

- Strength: Introduces kIC as a novel querying scheme with error-correction potential and discusses query pricing.

- Strength: Literature review is comprehensive and well-grounded in related fields.

- Weakness: Relies on simplifying assumptions (large datasets, i.i.d. sources, memoryless channels, perfect skill estimation) with limited discussion of practical impact.

- Weakness: Lacks empirical validation or simulations comparing kIC and existing methods.

- Weakness: kIC analysis (Theorem 4) is complex and depends on an unrealistic oracle decoder, reducing real-world applicability.

- Weakness: Neglects discussion of adaptive coding schemes and their potential benefits.

- Overall: Solid theoretical contribution that offers valuable bounds and a foundation for future work but limited immediate practical impact.

- For acceptance: Addresses a fundamental problem, uses a novel information-theoretic approach, provides significant bounds, and proposes a promising kIC direction.

- Against acceptance: Strong assumptions, absence of empirical results, and reliance on an oracle decoder for kIC analysis.

- Rating: 7 — Solid paper, likely to be accepted; recommend adding empirical evaluation to strengthen the work.
