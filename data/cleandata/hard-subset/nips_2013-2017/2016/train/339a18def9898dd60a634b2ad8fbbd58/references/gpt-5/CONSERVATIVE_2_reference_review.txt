Summary
This paper models crowdsourcing for labeling/clustering as a communication problem and seeks fundamental budget–fidelity trade-offs. Workers are modeled as noisy channels (M-ary symmetric channel, MSC, and the spammer–hammer channel, SHC). The authors define the query budget as the number of queries per item (rate) and fidelity as average Hamming distortion (error rate). Using standard rate–distortion and channel capacity arguments, they derive lower bounds on the minimum queries per item to achieve a desired error probability in two scenarios: (i) worker skill levels unknown (SL-UK) and (ii) worker skill levels known to the crowdsourcer (SL-CS). They also propose k-ary incidence coding (kIC), where queries involve k items and the worker indicates which items share labels; for SHC they derive a converse-type bound on the rate needed to achieve a given error with an oracle decoder and give a closed-form for a spammer’s error rate under kIC. A corollary specializes the general information-theoretic bound to SHC. The paper briefly discusses pricing implications.

Relation to prior work
The modeling of workers as noisy channels and the use of information-theoretic tools connects to the literature on inference from crowds (e.g., Dawid–Skene EM, Karger–Oh–Shah budget-optimal allocation, spectral/EM methods). The SHC model is standard in that line of work. The joint source–channel coding view is classical; the derived expressions align with the well-known source rate–distortion R(D) for N-ary Hamming sources and channel capacity of MSC, with a distinction between unknown versus known worker skill states. Pairwise/ternary incidence queries relate to prior work on pairwise constraints and triplet queries in clustering/metric learning.

Strengths
- Clear high-level framing of budget–fidelity as a rate–distortion vs. capacity trade-off; the separation-based bounds for SL-UK vs SL-CS are intuitive and useful as conceptual benchmarks.
- The distinction between capacity of the averaged MSC (unknown state) versus average capacity (state known at decoder) is correctly captured; the formulas reflect this.
- The kIC perspective articulates a plausible error-correction intuition (invalid response patterns eliminated), and the SHC-based converse provides a parameterized necessary condition that scales with k and spammer fraction q.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is limited: The central theorems (Theorems 2–3) are direct applications of Shannon’s separation theorem with standard rate–distortion for Hamming sources and MSC capacities. The main technical contribution appears to be translating these textbook results to the crowdsourcing setting; no new information-theoretic techniques or tightness/achievability results are provided.
- Lack of validation: There is no empirical evaluation on synthetic or real crowdsourcing data, no comparison to established algorithms (majority vote, Dawid–Skene, KOS, spectral-EM), and no demonstration of how tight the bounds are in practice. For NeurIPS, the absence of experiments or constructive achievability schemes substantially limits significance.
- kIC analysis is weakly actionable: The result is a converse under SHC with an oracle decoder; there is no achievable scheme, no analysis for general N>2 beyond brief remarks, and no adaptive query design. The practical impact (e.g., how to pick k, how to decode under realistic worker noise) is not addressed.
- Modeling assumptions are restrictive: iid labels, uniform datasets in parts, non-adaptive codes, uniform random worker selection, equal rates per worker, no task difficulty effects, no adversarial behavior beyond SHC spammers, and no dependency across queries. These limit applicability and are not stress-tested.
- Clarity/rigor issues: Notation is inconsistent and sometimes undefined (e.g., H_M(E(·)) with E(·) ambiguously introduced; M vs N used both as choices/clusters; some equations have typographical errors). Several references are incomplete (missing years), and figures/appendices are critical to understanding but not available here. The feedforward dependence of k on SHC behavior is noted but not rigorously incorporated in the information-theoretic framework.
- Related work coverage is incomplete: There is little discussion of theoretical results on minimax rates, adaptive allocation, and codes for crowdsourcing; many relevant works from the past decade are not cited or contrasted.

Questions for the authors
1. Can you provide an achievability result (even under simplifying assumptions) that approaches your separation-based lower bounds, to establish tightness?
2. How sensitive are your bounds to non-iid labels and class imbalance? Can you handle instance difficulty (e.g., mixture of MSC parameters per item) or adversarial workers?
3. For kIC, do you have a concrete decoding algorithm with performance guarantees under MSC or SHC and N>2? How does k influence total cognitive cost and latency empirically?
4. Can you compare your lower bounds to the empirical performance of DS, KOS, or spectral-EM on synthetic data matched to your models?
5. Please clarify notation in Theorems 2–3, define H_M and H_N precisely, and disambiguate M (response alphabet) vs N (number of classes).

Pros for acceptance
- Provides a clean, principled information-theoretic lens on budget–fidelity trade-offs in crowdsourcing.
- The SL-UK vs SL-CS distinction is insightful and could inform when estimating worker skills is worthwhile.
- kIC idea is interesting and may inspire more structured query designs.

Cons for acceptance
- Primarily recasts standard information-theoretic results; limited novelty beyond contextualization.
- No experimental validation or constructive algorithms; practical impact unclear.
- Restrictive assumptions and incomplete treatment of key real-world factors.
- Clarity and presentation issues (notation, missing details).

Recommendation
Weak reject. The conceptual framing is sound and potentially useful, but the contribution is largely a straightforward application of established information-theoretic results without empirical or algorithmic validation. Strengthening with clear, reproducible proofs, improved notation, achievability results or tight simulations, and comparisons to established baselines would substantially improve the paper’s value for the NeurIPS audience.

Confidence: medium.
