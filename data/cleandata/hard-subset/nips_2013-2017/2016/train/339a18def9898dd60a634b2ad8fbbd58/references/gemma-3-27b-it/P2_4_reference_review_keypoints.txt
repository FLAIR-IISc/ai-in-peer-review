- Paper studies fundamental budget–fidelity limits in crowdsourcing using an information-theoretic communication model and analyzes cases with unknown and known worker skills (SL-UK, SL-CS).  
- Introduces and analyzes k-ary incidence coding (kIC) as a novel query scheme that may improve querying strategies.  
- Positions the work as establishing fundamental performance bounds, contrasting with prior literature focused on aggregation algorithms.  
- Explicitly links the problem to classical information theory (rate-distortion, channel coding) to provide a rigorous theoretical foundation.  
- Strength: strong, rigorous information-theoretic analysis that offers a valuable perspective on crowdsourcing limits.  
- Strength: clear problem formulation and intuitive budget–fidelity trade-off model with well-defined assumptions.  
- Strength: kIC is a novel contribution and serves as an interesting case study within the theoretical framework.  
- Strength: lower bounds (Theorems 2–4) are clearly presented and useful as benchmarks.  
- Strength: discussion of query pricing connects theoretical results to potential real-world implications.  
- Weakness: limited practical guidance or algorithms for achieving the derived bounds in real systems.  
- Weakness: reliance on simplifying assumptions (i.i.d. data, memoryless channels, perfect skill estimation, uniform datasets) with limited discussion of relaxing them.  
- Weakness: kIC analysis is complex and its practical benefits are not fully demonstrated or extensively compared numerically.  
- Weakness: presentation is dense and heavy in mathematical notation; more intuition would improve accessibility.  
- Evaluation scores: Quality 8/10, Clarity 6/10, Originality 8/10, Significance 7/10.  
- Arguments for acceptance: rigorous novel analysis, establishment of fundamental limits, kIC contribution, and deeper understanding of crowdsourcing trade-offs.  
- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient practical applicability, strong simplifying assumptions, and complex kIC analysis with unclear real-world benefit.  
- Overall recommendation: strong accept, with a suggestion to add discussion on bridging theory and practical implementation.
