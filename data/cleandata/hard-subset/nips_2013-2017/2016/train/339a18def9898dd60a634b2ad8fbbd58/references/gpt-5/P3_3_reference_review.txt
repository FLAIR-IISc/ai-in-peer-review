Summary
The paper frames label crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding problem and derives information-theoretic lower bounds on the minimum query budget (rate) needed to achieve a target fidelity (distortion) under Hamming loss. Workers are modeled as discrete memoryless channels, focusing on (i) M-ary symmetric channels (MSC) with a mixture over worker skill levels, and (ii) a spammer–hammer channel (SHC) with hammer probability q. Two scenarios are treated: SL-UK (skill levels unknown to crowdsourcer/decoder) and SL-CS (skill levels perfectly known at the decoder). Using source–channel separation, Theorems 2 and 3 provide lower bounds on the rate to achieve error ≤ ε: for SL-UK, the denominator is the capacity of the composite (mixture) channel; for SL-CS, the denominator is the average capacity across known states (since uniform inputs are optimal for symmetric channels). The paper then introduces k-ary Incidence Coding (kIC), where each query presents k items and asks for their same/different relations (a constrained set of valid responses that implicitly corrects some errors). For SHC, Lemma 1 computes the error of spammers under kIC; Theorem 4 gives a necessary condition on the budget R for target error ε, assuming an oracle decoder that perfectly identifies hammers vs spammers. A corollary specializes the separation bound to SHC, and an informal pricing rule π(k1)/π(k2) ≈ k1/k2 is suggested.

Relation to prior work
The paper relates to Dawid–Skene and EM-style aggregation, spectral/convex methods for crowdsourcing (e.g., Zhang et al., Vinayak–Oymak–Hassibi), and budget-optimal allocation (Karger–Oh–Shah). The information-theoretic perspective echoes “capacity of crowdsourcing” style analyses; however, the paper should better position itself against statistical limits in Karger–Oh–Shah (redundancy versus error rate, worker reliabilities) and more recent works on minimax rates, worker confusion matrices, and adaptive task assignment. The kIC design is reminiscent of using pairwise/tuplewise constraints in clustering and can be connected to coding on hypergraphs and error-correcting codes.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: Casting crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel problem provides a clean lens to reason about budget–fidelity tradeoffs, separates source-side complexity (label entropy and allowed distortion) from channel-side reliability (worker capacity).
- Generality of the lower bounds: The SL-UK and SL-CS bounds correctly distinguish composite-channel capacity (unknown state) from average capacity (state known at decoder) for MSC mixtures, and recover intuitive dependencies on priors H(B(X)) and target error via HN(ε).
- kIC insight: Highlighting that constrained response sets induce implicit error correction is useful. The SHC analysis shows how k increases effective robustness (via the probability an item is seen by at least one hammer).
- Bridge to pricing: Even if heuristic, linking k to budget via per-item coverage is a practical consideration.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Incomplete and sometimes unclear notation: Equations (11)–(12) have ambiguous terms (e.g., HM(E(·))—is this the M-ary entropy of the averaged MSC crossover? Please define clearly). The use of M in “number of choices in responding” vs N in class cardinality becomes confusing in the kIC discussion. Several references lack years; figures are not fully described.
- Scope of theorems: The main bounds are converses (necessary conditions). There are no achievability results tailored to the crowdsourcing setting (e.g., codes/decoders that approach the bounds), beyond generic separation. For kIC, Theorem 4 assumes an oracle decoder that knows worker types; without it, the lower bound may be optimistic.
- Modeling assumptions: i.i.d. items and labels, non-adaptive querying, large-blocklength asymptotics, and independence between task difficulty and errors. In practice, worker reliability varies with item difficulty, and adaptivity (worker routing, gold tasks) is central.
- SHC and kIC: The SHC corollary treats spammers as carrying zero information. Under kIC, random valid partitions may still carry non-zero mutual information because of structural constraints; this nuance is not discussed. Also, the per-query information under state-known decoding in SHC is taken as q log2 M; this ignores that for binary labeling M=2 unless kIC is used (then M equals the count of valid partitions), which should be explicit.
- Clustering ambiguity: For kIC-based clustering, labels are identifiable only up to permutation; the distortion function and entropy terms should account for this invariance (currently only discussed informally).
- Empirical validation: No experiments compare kIC to standard strategies (majority vote, DS, spectral/convex methods) at equal budgets on synthetic or real data. The pricing rule is unsupported and may underestimate cognitive costs that scale superlinearly in k.

Quality
The theoretical direction is sound, and, for symmetric channels, the SL-UK vs SL-CS distinction is correct (uniform input remains optimal so averaging capacities is justified). However, the technical development is minimal beyond the separation-based converse and a simple SHC calculation; key proofs are relegated to appendices and the notation is muddy. The kIC bound is only a necessary condition under favorable oracle assumptions.

Clarity
The paper needs significant editing for precision and readability: define all symbols; distinguish clearly between N (classes), M (response alphabet), k (query arity), ε (target error), and the worker parameters. Fix typos and incomplete references. Provide intuition after each theorem and a consistent mapping from “queries per item” to “per-item coverages” for kIC (R vs R′ with R′=kR).

Originality
While separation-based converses are standard, applying them cleanly to crowdsourcing with MSC mixtures and introducing kIC as a code family is a useful synthesis. The specific kIC SHC bound is modestly novel but limited.

Significance
As a conceptual piece, this can influence how we think about budget allocation and query design. Without achievability, adaptivity, or empirical support, the immediate practical impact is limited.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify and tighten Theorems 2–3: explicitly derive the composite-channel capacity for SL-UK and the state-known capacity for SL-CS; justify uniform inputs. Provide examples (binary labeling, non-uniform priors).
- For kIC, formalize the response alphabet size and its impact on per-query capacity under SL-CS; discuss whether spammer responses under kIC contribute mutual information. Provide both converse and achievability (e.g., simple decoders approaching the bound).
- Address label-permutation invariance in clustering within the rate–distortion formulation.
- Add finite-sample and non-asymptotic bounds, or at least discuss finite L effects.
- Include experiments comparing kIC to standard baselines at matched budgets, and analyze the practical pricing/cognitive cost of varying k.
- Improve related work coverage (minimax/statistical limits, adaptive routing) and fix references.

Pros
- Clear conceptual framework via information theory
- Useful SL-UK vs SL-CS distinction
- Insightful kIC idea linking structure to error correction

Cons
- Only converse bounds; no practical achievability or algorithms
- Oracle assumptions in kIC; limited realism
- Clarity/notation issues and incomplete citations
- No empirical validation

Recommendation: Weak reject. Interesting direction and potential, but the current version is too preliminary and unclear for NeurIPS.
