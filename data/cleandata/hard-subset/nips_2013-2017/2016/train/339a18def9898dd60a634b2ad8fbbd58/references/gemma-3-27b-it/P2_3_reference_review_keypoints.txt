- Models crowdsourcing as a communication system and derives information-theoretic lower bounds on budget (queries) vs fidelity (accuracy).  
- Considers two scenarios: unknown worker skill levels (SL-UK) and perfectly known skill levels (SL-CS).  
- Analyzes k-ary incidence coding (kIC) as a concrete query scheme and proposes a pricing strategy.  
- Positions work within existing CS literature but focuses on fundamental limits rather than algorithmic heuristics.  
- Novelty: applies information theory to crowdsourcing to provide theoretical performance benchmarks.  
- Rigorous mathematical analysis with logically structured proofs and information-theoretic arguments.  
- Insightful treatment of worker skill estimation by analyzing both SL-UK and SL-CS settings.  
- kIC offers a concrete coding strategy and potential practical application.  
- Well-organized paper with clear logical progression from model to results.  
- Practicality concerns: strong assumptions (e.g., known channel, i.i.d. data) may limit real-world applicability.  
- kIC analysis is narrowly focused (spammer-hammer channel, binary labeling, uniform dataset) and feels somewhat isolated from main bounds.  
- Pricing strategy is brief and underdeveloped, lacking practical justification and discussion of drawbacks.  
- No empirical validation or simulations to test bound tightness or kIC performance in realistic settings.  
- Notation and presentation can be dense; theorems need more intuitive explanation for broader accessibility.  
- Overall: technically sound and original, but immediate practical impact is uncertain without further validation.  
- Arguments for acceptance: provides a valuable theoretical foundation, is novel and rigorous, and offers insights for CS design.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited practicality due to assumptions, lack of experiments, and disconnected kIC analysis.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions.  
- Requested revisions: discuss assumption limitations and real-world adaptations, elaborate and justify pricing strategy, add simulations or empirical results, and improve clarity of notation and theorem explanations.
