

# Review: Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing

## Summary
This paper proposes an information-theoretic framework to analyze the fundamental performance limits of crowdsourcing systems, specifically focusing on the trade-off between budget (number of queries per item) and fidelity (accuracy). The authors model crowdsourcing as a communication problem where workers are noisy channels, and derive theoretical bounds on the minimum number of queries needed to achieve a given error probability. They consider two key scenarios: when worker skill levels are unknown (SL-UK) and when they are known (SL-CS). The paper also introduces and analyzes a novel "k-ary incidence coding" (kIC) scheme, which shows improved performance compared to traditional approaches.

## Strengths

**Quality (Excellent):** The paper makes a technically sound contribution with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors properly ground their work in information theory, particularly leveraging the source-channel separation theorem, and provide formal proofs for all key theorems. The derivations are thorough, and the paper carefully distinguishes between different worker skill models (MSC and SHC). The numerical results in Figure 2 effectively demonstrate how the theoretical bounds compare to the kIC performance, providing strong validation of their claims. The paper acknowledges limitations and discusses potential extensions, showing appropriate scholarly rigor.

**Originality (Strong):** The information-theoretic framing of crowdsourcing is novel within the context of the field. While prior work has focused on empirical algorithms (e.g., Dawid-Skene, EM-based approaches), this paper provides fundamental limits that no practical algorithm can exceed. The k-ary incidence coding scheme represents a significant conceptual contribution, as it leverages error correction properties inherent in the query design itself. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work (e.g., [5, 8, 11, 12]) by focusing on fundamental limits rather than algorithmic improvements.

**Significance (High):** This work addresses a fundamental question in crowdsourcing with clear practical implications. The derived bounds provide theoretical guidance for practitioners on the minimum budget required to achieve desired fidelity, which could significantly impact how crowdsourcing systems are designed. The kIC scheme could lead to more efficient crowdsourcing systems that reduce the number of required queries. This work advances the field by moving beyond empirical algorithm design to establish theoretical foundations, which is a substantial contribution to the state of the art.

**Clarity (Good):** The paper is well-organized with clear sections that build logically from problem formulation to theoretical results. The notation is consistent, and key concepts (like the two scenarios and kIC) are well-defined. The figures (Figures 1 and 2) effectively illustrate the main concepts and results. However, the paper could be slightly more accessible to readers less familiar with information theory, particularly in Sections 2 and 3.

## Weaknesses

**Limited Empirical Validation:** While the theoretical results are strong, the paper lacks empirical validation with real crowdsourcing data. The numerical results in Figure 2 are based on theoretical bounds rather than actual crowdsourcing experiments. To strengthen the practical significance, the authors should ideally demonstrate how their bounds align with real-world performance.

**Narrow Channel Model Assumptions:** The analysis is restricted to MSC and SHC channel models, which may not capture the full complexity of real worker behavior. While the authors acknowledge this as a limitation, a brief discussion of how their framework might extend to more complex worker models would strengthen the paper.

**Underdeveloped Query Pricing Discussion:** The query pricing strategy mentioned in Appendix E is intriguing but superficially treated. The paper suggests a rule of thumb for pricing (π(k₁)/π(k₂) ≈ k₁k₂) but doesn't provide sufficient justification or analysis to support this claim.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong theoretical contribution that makes a clear, significant advance in the understanding of crowdsourcing systems. The information-theoretic framework provides fundamental limits that are valuable both for theoretical understanding and practical system design. The paper's main contribution—applying information theory to crowdsourcing and deriving fundamental bounds—is novel and well-executed.

The paper meets all the key criteria for NeurIPS: it's technically sound, presents original work that advances the field, and addresses a significant problem with clear implications. The theoretical results provide a new lens through which to view crowdsourcing system design, moving beyond the algorithmic approaches that have dominated the field.

The only substantial weakness is the lack of empirical validation with real crowdsourcing data, which would have strengthened the practical relevance. However, the theoretical contribution stands strongly on its own, and the absence of empirical results is not uncommon in theoretical papers at NeurIPS.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of crowdsourcing systems. The information-theoretic approach is novel, the analysis is rigorous, and the results are important for both theoretical understanding and practical system design. While the paper could benefit from some empirical validation, the strength of the theoretical contribution outweighs this limitation.

The paper is well-positioned to influence future work in crowdsourcing, potentially guiding the development of more efficient systems based on these fundamental limits. The k-ary incidence coding scheme represents a particularly promising direction for future research. This is a high-quality theoretical contribution that aligns well with NeurIPS' focus on fundamental advances in machine learning and related fields.
