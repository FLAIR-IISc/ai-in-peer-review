Summary
The paper proposes an information-theoretic framework for crowdsourcing, modeling workers as noisy channels and queries/aggregation as a joint source–channel code. It focuses on binary and multi-class labeling with two worker models: (i) an M-ary symmetric channel (MSC) with heterogeneous skill levels, and (ii) a spammer–hammer channel (SHC). Using separation, the authors derive lower bounds on the minimum number of queries per item needed to achieve a target error (rate–distortion-type bounds) in two scenarios: worker skills unknown to the crowdsourcer (SL-UK) vs. known to the crowdsourcer (SL-CS). They also study k-ary incidence coding (kIC), where each query presents k items and asks the worker to group them, claiming error-correction benefits from removing invalid response patterns. For SHC and N=2, they derive a closed-form expression for the error of a spammer under kIC (Lemma 1) and a converse-style lower bound on the rate for a target error (Theorem 4). A corollary specializes the general IT bound to SHC. They discuss practical implications (pricing) and relate to prior crowdsourcing aggregation/inference work (e.g., Dawid–Skene, bandit surveys, spectral/EM methods).

Strengths
- Ambitious and appealing framing: casting budget–fidelity tradeoffs as joint source–channel coding provides a clean lens and connects to well-established theory (separation).
- Distinguishing SL-UK vs SL-CS is insightful; the observation contrasting “average worker capacity” vs “average of capacities” is correct and useful.
- The kIC perspective highlights a real design knob (query arity) and its impact on effective reliability; the closed-form for spammer error under kIC is interesting.
- If correct and complete, the general lower bounds could serve as informative benchmarks across query schemes and worker models.

Weaknesses (technical)
- Missing proofs and appendices: Theorems 2–4 and Lemma 1 are central yet relegated to Appendices A–E, which are not included; claims cannot be verified.
- Equation correctness/notation:
  - Eq. (1) for MSC is malformed; the intended P(v|u)=1−ε if v=u and ε/(M−1) otherwise is not what is written.
  - Eq. (2) for SHC is malformed; “C=H and v=u 1” is not a valid probability expression; the spammer line should read 1/|V|.
  - Theorem 2 denominator “log2 M − HM(E(ε))” is opaque. If SL-UK, the relevant capacity is that of the averaged channel, i.e., log M − H_M(ε̄) for ε̄ the mixture parameter; HM(E(ε)) reads as H_M applied to E(ε), whereas Theorem 3 uses E(H_M(ε)). Please define H_M(·) precisely and reconcile these two forms. As written, it is unclear and likely incorrect.
  - Overloaded/conflicting symbols: C is used for codes and capacity; C also denotes channel state for SHC; M vs N (response alphabet vs number of classes) are conflated in several places. W is both the number of workers and the cardinality of a skill set W′.
  - Definitions of H_N(·), Hb(·), pmax are scattered; domains/constraints are not clearly stated.
- Separation applicability and modeling assumptions are under-specified. The use of source–channel separation assumes arbitrarily large blocklengths and flexibility in designing both source and channel codes. In practice, the “channel input alphabet” is the set of allowable queries and worker response options; constraints on M, cost per query, and adaptivity are acknowledged but not integrated into the main bounds (Appendix E is missing).
- Worker/channel model: The SL-UK capacity for mixtures with unknown state at both ends needs careful handling; it is not generally equal to the average of per-state capacities. The paper’s formulas should be derived explicitly for the mixture channel case (worker selected i.i.d. per query).
- kIC analysis: The relation between per-item rate R (queries per item), query arity k, and the number of times each item appears (R′) is not rigorously specified. Theorem 4’s bound R < (1/k) ln(1−q)/ln(ε̄S) hinges on R′ vs R; this mapping must be made explicit and integer effects handled. Also, the “oracle” decoder assumption makes the bound easier but limits practical relevance; an achievability or converse tightness discussion is missing.
- No empirical validation or comparison to existing algorithmic methods (e.g., Dawid–Skene, spectral–EM) under matched models. The figure referenced (Fig. 2) is missing; no numbers are provided.

Weaknesses (presentation/formatting)
- Numerous grammatical errors and typos: “suite” → “suit”; “Wiely” → “Wiley”; “Ohy” → “Oh”; “th Conference …” placeholders; “crowdsourcer” vs “taskmaster” inconsistent; “transitory or fleeting” redundant; NIPS vs NeurIPS naming inconsistent.
- Broken LaTeX and missing figures (Figs. 1a–1c, 2); missing appendices; inconsistent equation numbering.
- References are incomplete (years, venues, pages often missing) and in several cases inaccurate; related IT-theoretic works on fundamental limits of crowdsourcing are under-cited (e.g., sharper minimax bounds under Dawid–Skene and its variants).
- Notation is inconsistent and sometimes undefined at use (e.g., M vs N in Theorems; domain of distortion D; precise definition of H_N).

Originality
- The broad idea of information-theoretic limits for crowdsourcing and budget–error tradeoffs has prior art (e.g., Karger–Oh–Shah, Zhang–Chen–Jordan, and follow-ups deriving phase transitions/minimax rates). The SL-UK vs SL-CS comparison via mixture vs state-aware capacity is a nice angle, but not entirely new conceptually.
- kIC as a coding viewpoint is interesting but similar ideas exist (pairwise/tuplewise comparisons and constraints for clustering/labeling); the specific closed-form spammer error is incremental.

Significance
- Potentially useful as a unifying benchmark framework. However, without correct, complete derivations and empirical/constructive evidence, it is hard to judge practical impact. The current state falls short of establishing new state-of-the-art limits or actionable design principles.

Questions for the authors
- Provide complete proofs for Theorems 2–4; clarify the precise capacity expressions in SL-UK vs SL-CS and the role of the averaged channel vs average capacity.
- Define H_M(·), H_N(·) rigorously; specify all constraints where the piecewise cases (e.g., D ≤ min{1−pmax, 1−1/N}) apply.
- Precisely define R, R′, and their relation under kIC; account for integer constraints.
- How does kIC compare empirically (and theoretically) to standard repeated labeling with majority vote/DS, at equal budget and cost model?
- Incorporate cost/effort per query and the choice of M into the main bounds (not just an appendix).
- Include missing figures, appendices, and fix all broken equations and references.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear big-picture framing linking CS to JSCC.
- Insightful separation of SL-UK vs SL-CS scenarios.
- Potentially useful lower bounds and kIC analysis if made rigorous.

Cons for acceptance
- Substantial technical ambiguities/errors in key equations; missing proofs/appendices.
- Missing figures and numerous presentation/formatting issues hinder evaluation.
- Limited novelty relative to existing literature on fundamental limits; insufficient related work coverage.
- No empirical validation or constructive achievability schemes approaching the bounds.

Recommendation
Reject. The conceptual direction is promising, but the manuscript in its current form has too many technical and presentation deficiencies to assess correctness or impact. A thorough revision with corrected formulas, complete proofs, proper notation, fuller related work, and either achievability or empirical validation would substantially strengthen the contribution.
