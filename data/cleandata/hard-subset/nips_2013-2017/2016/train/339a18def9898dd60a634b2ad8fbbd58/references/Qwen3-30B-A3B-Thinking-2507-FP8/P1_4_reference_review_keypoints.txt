- Paper presents an information-theoretic framework modeling crowdsourcing as a communication channel to derive budget-fidelity limits for unknown and known worker skills (SL-UK, SL-CS).
- Connects crowdsourcing to source and channel coding theorems, offering a novel theoretical perspective distinct from prior algorithmic work.
- Introduces k-ary incidence coding (kIC) as a new query scheme with built-in error-correction properties.
- Derives rigorous fundamental limits (Theorems 2 and 3) on budget versus fidelity with sound proofs and detailed appendices.
- Provides a concrete analysis of kIC under a spammer-hammer model (Theorem 4) illustrating application of the framework.
- Offers a practical pricing rule of thumb for kIC (π(k1)/π(k2) ≈ k1/k2) with potential platform implications.
- Paper is well-organized and mostly clear, though some appendix technicalities could be moved into the main text for flow.
- Lacks empirical validation comparing theoretical bounds to existing practical crowdsourcing algorithms.
- Makes simplifying assumptions (i.i.d. workers, binary labeling focus for kIC) that limit immediate practical applicability.
- Does not sufficiently relate theoretical bounds to the empirical performance of established algorithms like Dawid–Skene or EM methods.
- Practical implications and guidance for real-world taskmaster design are underdeveloped beyond the pricing heuristic.
- Overall assessment: a high-quality, novel theoretical contribution that establishes useful benchmarks for crowdsourcing research.
- Recommendation: Accept, given the rigorous theoretical advance despite limited empirical work and some restrictive assumptions.
- Pros: first info-theoretic framework for crowdsourcing, rigorous derivations, novel kIC scheme, and practical pricing insight.
- Cons: limited experiments, restrictive assumptions, and need for stronger connections to practical system design and existing algorithms.
