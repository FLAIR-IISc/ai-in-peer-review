- Proposes an information-theoretic framework modeling crowdsourcing as a source-channel coding problem to analyze budget-fidelity trade-offs.  
- Derives fundamental limits (distortion-rate) for unknown skill (SL-UK) and known skill (SL-CS) scenarios.  
- Analyzes k-ary incidence coding (kIC) and shows its error-correction capabilities with concrete bounds (Theorem 4, Corollary 1).  
- Builds on Dawid–Skene and information theory, offering a novel formal perspective on crowdsourcing limits.  
- Theoretical framework is rigorous, elegant, and technically sound (Theorems 2 and 3).  
- Clear distinction between SL-UK and SL-CS provides important practical insight.  
- kIC analysis is valuable as a concrete query scheme and is well-illustrated numerically (Figure 2).  
- Paper is well-written, well-organized, with consistent notation and helpful figures (especially Figure 1).  
- Lacks empirical validation to show how real systems approach the theoretical limits.  
- Insufficient discussion of practical implications, implementation complexity, and cost trade-offs of optimal schemes.  
- Assumptions (L → ∞, independent workers, MSC/SHC channel models) may not hold in real crowdsourcing and are not adequately examined.  
- Limited engagement with recent NIPS/related crowdsourcing literature (e.g., spectral methods, EM-based works).  
- Overall: strong theoretical contribution but practical limitations merit only weak acceptance pending experiments and deeper practical discussion.  
- For acceptance: novel theory, rigorous limits, clear presentation, useful kIC analysis.  
- Against acceptance: no experiments, weak practical guidance, limited literature engagement, and potentially unrealistic assumptions.
