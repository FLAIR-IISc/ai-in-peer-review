- Paper frames crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding problem, modeling labels as a source, workers as noisy channels, queries as codes, and aggregator as decoder.  
- Derives information-theoretic lower bounds on queries per item for target error under two regimes: worker skills unknown (SL-UK) and known (SL-CS).  
- Uses source–channel separation to obtain capacity expressions: SL-UK yields capacity with E[ε], SL-CS with E[H_M(ε)] differences; RD term uses H(B) − H_N(ε̂).  
- Introduces k-ary incidence coding (kIC) where each query covers k items and claims built-in error correction via constrained valid responses.  
- Provides a necessary rate condition for kIC under a spammer–hammer model and discusses a simple pricing heuristic.  
- High-level derivations appear plausible, and the comparison SL-UK vs SL-CS is insightful and correct conceptually.  
- Main proofs are deferred to appendices (not included) and several definitions are imprecise, weakening rigor.  
- kIC analysis is narrowly scoped to binary labels and spammer–hammer channels; Theorem 4 is only a necessary condition under an oracle decoder.  
- Tightness and achievability of the presented bounds are unclear; no explicit coding arguments are provided for claims like kIC’s error-correction.  
- No empirical validation, simulations, or comparisons to practical aggregation methods (majority vote, Dawid–Skene, spectral/EM) are provided.  
- Presentation and notation are uneven with typographical errors, missing reference years, overloaded/undefined symbols, and formatting glitches in equations and figures.  
- System model assumptions (iid labels, iid worker states, independence across items/workers, non-adaptivity, decoder knowledge of worker IDs) need clearer statement.  
- kIC exposition needs formal counting of valid responses for general N,k and a clean derivation of spammer error probability; pricing rule is ambiguous.  
- Mapping crowdsourcing to information-theoretic limits and distinguishing SL-UK vs SL-CS is an original and useful framing.  
- Related work coverage is incomplete; prior results on budget–reliability limits and tuplewise queries should be discussed more thoroughly.  
- Practical significance is limited by lack of algorithms, experiments, multi-class/adversarial/realistic settings, and only necessary kIC results without achievability.  
- Pros: principled information-theoretic framing, useful SL-UK vs SL-CS comparison, k-ary queries introduced, potentially informative lower bounds for budgeting.  
- Cons: no experiments, limited kIC scope (N=2, SHC, oracle), unclear/tightness of bounds, clarity/notation errors, narrow related work, vague pricing heuristic.  
- Reviewer questions asking for RD applicability conditions, justification of mixture-to-MSC claim for SL-UK, simulations comparing practical methods to bounds, kIC extensions and achievable schemes, and clarification of pricing derivation.  
- Suggested improvements: fix notation/formatting, include key proof sketches, add empirical validation, extend kIC to multi-class/non-uniform priors and achievable decoding, and discuss adaptivity and item difficulty.  
- Recommendation: Lean reject pending clearer exposition, tighter/achievability results, and empirical or algorithmic validation.
