This paper tackles a fundamental and important problem: understanding the limits of budget-fidelity tradeoffs in crowdsourcing. The authors model crowdsourcing as a communication system, leveraging information theory to derive bounds on achievable performance. This is a bold and valuable approach; most crowdsourcing research focuses on algorithmic improvements *within* a given framework, while this work attempts to establish the ultimate limits of what’s possible. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and connects it to relevant applications. The formulation using information theory is rigorous, and the theorems presented (even if not fully exploitable in practice yet) represent a significant conceptual step forward. The introduction of k-ary incidence coding as a concrete example and the analysis of its performance, including a discussion of potential query pricing, adds practical relevance.

However, the paper also has weaknesses. While the theoretical framework is solid, the connection to *practical* crowdsourcing systems is somewhat loose. The assumptions (e.g., perfect channel knowledge in some scenarios, memoryless channels) are strong and may not hold in real-world deployments.  The numerical results, while present, don't offer extensive validation of the theoretical bounds against empirical data or existing algorithms. The analysis of k-ary incidence coding, while interesting, feels slightly underdeveloped—a more detailed comparison to simpler schemes (like direct querying) would be beneficial. The proofs, while referenced as being in the appendix, are not readily available for review at this stage, hindering a full assessment of their validity. The discussion of query pricing feels a bit tacked on and could be expanded. Finally, while the paper acknowledges limitations, a more thorough discussion of the challenges in applying these theoretical results to complex, real-world crowdsourcing settings would be appreciated.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The information-theoretic approach to crowdsourcing is a genuinely novel and insightful contribution.
* **Rigorous Framework:** The mathematical formulation provides a solid foundation for analyzing performance limits.
* **Conceptual Advance:** The derived bounds represent a fundamental understanding of the budget-fidelity tradeoff.
* **Practical Relevance:** The k-ary incidence coding scheme offers a concrete example and potential practical benefits.
* **Clear Motivation:** The introduction effectively explains the importance of the problem.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Practicality:** The assumptions and theoretical nature of the work limit its direct applicability to real-world crowdsourcing systems.
* **Limited Validation:** The numerical results don't provide strong validation against empirical data or existing algorithms.
* **Underdeveloped Analysis:** The k-ary incidence coding analysis could be more detailed.
* **Proof Accessibility:** The lack of access to the proofs hinders a complete assessment.
* **Pricing Discussion:** The query pricing discussion is brief and could be expanded.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a truly novel and bold approach to a fundamental problem in crowdsourcing.
* The information-theoretic framework offers a new lens for understanding performance limits.
* The work has the potential to guide future research in crowdsourcing system design.
* The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of information theory and crowdsourcing.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The practical relevance of the work is limited by strong assumptions.
* The lack of extensive empirical validation weakens the claims.
* The analysis of the proposed scheme (k-ary incidence coding) could be more thorough.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some weaknesses, I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The novelty and conceptual significance of the work outweigh the limitations. While the paper may not immediately translate into practical improvements, it opens up a new avenue for research and provides a fundamental understanding of the limits of crowdsourcing. The paper’s boldness and willingness to tackle a challenging problem from a novel perspective are highly commendable. I would encourage the authors to address the weaknesses in future work by exploring more realistic models and conducting more extensive empirical validation. The inclusion of the proofs in the final version is essential.



**Recommendation:** Accept.
