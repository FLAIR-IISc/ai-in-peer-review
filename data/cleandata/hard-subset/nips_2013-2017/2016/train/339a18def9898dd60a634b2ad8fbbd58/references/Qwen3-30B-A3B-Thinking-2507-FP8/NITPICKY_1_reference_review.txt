

# Review of "Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing"

This paper proposes an information-theoretic framework for analyzing crowdsourcing systems, focusing on the fundamental trade-off between budget (number of queries) and fidelity (accuracy). The authors model crowdsourcing as a communication system with workers as noisy channels, and analyze the performance limits using rate-distortion theory. They derive theoretical bounds for two scenarios: when worker skill levels are unknown (SL-UK) and when they are perfectly known (SL-CS). The paper also introduces "k-ary incidence coding" (kIC) as a query scheme and analyzes its performance.

**Strengths:** The paper provides a novel information-theoretic perspective on crowdsourcing, which is a significant contribution to the field. The framework unifies various crowdsourcing approaches under a single theoretical model, and the derivation of the rate-distortion limits (Theorems 2 and 3) is generally sound. The kIC analysis (Section 4) offers interesting insights into how query design can improve crowdsourcing performance.

**Weaknesses:** Despite its theoretical contributions, the paper contains several critical technical and formatting inconsistencies that undermine its credibility:

1. **Inconsistent notation and mathematical formulation:** The paper uses "R" to represent both the rate (queries per item) and the number of queries, leading to confusion. In Theorem 4, the expression "R < 1/k ln(1-q)/ln(̄S)" is mathematically problematic because R (queries per item) is dimensionless while 1/k (items per query) has units of 1/item, making the inequality dimensionally inconsistent.

2. **Undefined symbols and notation:** The paper introduces symbols like "̄S" (error probability of spammer) without clear definition when first used (Section 4.1). The notation "Hb(̂)" in Corollary 1 is undefined, and the probability "̂" is used inconsistently (sometimes as error probability, sometimes as a variable).

3. **Formatting inconsistencies:** The paper contains numerous formatting issues, including inconsistent spacing around mathematical operators (e.g., "P (v|u)" vs "P(v|u)"), inconsistent equation numbering, and missing spaces in mathematical expressions. References have inconsistent formatting (e.g., "th Conference" vs "Conference"), and the paper incorrectly references "NIPS 2016" in the header.

4. **Theoretical gaps:** The paper claims to provide "fundamental limits" but fails to rigorously establish the optimality of their bounds. The derivation of the rate-distortion function in Theorems 2 and 3 assumes independence between workers and items, but the paper doesn't justify why this assumption holds in practical crowdsourcing scenarios.

5. **Misuse of information theory:** The paper incorrectly applies the source-channel separation theorem (Theorem 1). The theorem requires the source to be iid and the channel to be memoryless with no feedback, but the crowdsourcing model described in Section 2.2 has dependencies between items (via the clustering function B(X)) that violate these assumptions.

6. **Clarity issues:** The paper's description of kIC is confusing. In Figure 1c, it claims "for N = 2, we only have four valid responses" but fails to explain why. The statement "in case we do not know the number of clusters or N ≥ 3, then this would remain a valid response" is contradictory, as the number of valid responses should increase with N, not remain the same.

7. **Reference issues:** The paper cites [8] multiple times but doesn't clearly distinguish how their work differs from the analysis in [8]. The paper also fails to adequately reference recent NIPS papers on crowdsourcing (e.g., Zhang et al., 2014; Whitehill et al., 2009).

**Overall assessment:** While the paper presents an interesting theoretical framework for crowdsourcing, the numerous technical inconsistencies, undefined notation, and mathematical errors significantly undermine its contribution. The fundamental limits claimed in Theorems 2 and 3 are not properly established, and the paper's application of information theory contains critical errors. The formatting and presentation issues further detract from the paper's quality.

Given the paper's significant technical flaws and the fact that it fails to properly establish its claimed "fundamental limits," I recommend rejection. The paper would benefit from substantial revision to address these issues before it could be considered for publication. The theoretical framework has potential, but the current presentation lacks the rigor expected for NIPS.
