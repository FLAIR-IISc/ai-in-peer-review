This paper investigates fundamental limits of budget-fidelity trade-offs in label crowdsourcing, modeling the problem through an information-theoretic lens. The authors explore bounds under scenarios with unknown and perfectly known worker skill levels, and introduce k-ary incidence coding as a potential query scheme. While the topic is relevant and the attempt to establish theoretical limits is commendable, the paper suffers from a multitude of issues ranging from presentation and notation to questionable assumptions and incomplete analysis.

The introduction is verbose and lacks focus. Several sentences are unnecessarily wordy and provide little substantive information. For example, the initial paragraphs defining crowdsourcing are overly general and could be significantly condensed. The review of related work is cursory; while mentioning several relevant papers, it fails to provide a nuanced discussion of their contributions and how this work differs. The motivation for analyzing k-ary incidence coding feels somewhat arbitrary and lacks strong justification beyond a perceived error correction capability.

The modeling section, while presenting a standard communication systems framework, suffers from inconsistencies and a lack of rigor. The notation is often unclear and not consistently applied throughout the paper (e.g., use of 'L' for both the number of items and a label). The description of the M-ary symmetric channel and spammer-hammer channel is adequate, but the discussion lacks depth. The leap from these channel models to representing worker skill levels feels simplistic and doesn't fully capture the complexity of real-world crowdsourcing scenarios. The statement that the framework "can also accommodate other more general scenarios" feels like a hand-wave, without any specific elaboration. The discussion of query schemes, particularly k-ary incidence coding, is presented in a confusing manner. The figures (1b and 1c) are helpful but not adequately explained in the text. The explanation regarding "invalid responses" in kIC is unclear and requires further clarification. The definitions of the encoder and decoder mappings (Equation 3) are unnecessarily abstract and lack practical interpretation.

The core contribution of the paper lies in Theorems 2 and 3, which establish information-theoretic lower bounds. However, these theorems are presented without sufficient context or explanation. The assumptions underlying these results (e.g., i.i.d. source, large dataset, known channel statistics) are not fully discussed, and their impact on the practical applicability of the bounds is unclear.  Furthermore, the connection between the derived bounds and existing work on crowdsourcing is not adequately established. The introduction of k-ary incidence coding in Section 3.3 feels disjointed from the theoretical foundation laid out previously. The performance analysis of kIC (Theorem 4) relies on several assumptions, including an "Oracle decoder" which makes the result impractical.  The claim about pricing based on k1 and k2 is extremely hand-wavy and lacks justification. The numerical results are limited and do not provide a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed approach. The presented corollary for SHC workers is useful, but the lack of comparison with other relevant bounds diminishes its impact.

The paper exhibits several minor flaws:

*   Numerous grammatical errors and awkward phrasing throughout the text.
*   Inconsistent use of terminology (e.g., "fidelity" vs. "distortion").
*   Missing details in the appendix proofs.
*   The formatting is occasionally inconsistent.
*   The lack of experimental validation is a significant weakness.

**Arguments for rejection:**

*   **Lack of Rigor:** The analysis lacks mathematical precision and relies on several simplifying assumptions that may not hold in real-world scenarios.
*   **Poor Presentation:** The paper is poorly written, disorganized, and difficult to follow.
*   **Limited Practical Relevance:** The theoretical results are largely disconnected from practical considerations and lack clear guidelines for implementation.
*   **Incremental Contribution:**  While the authors attempt to provide fundamental limits, the derived bounds are often loose and do not offer significant insights beyond existing work.
*   **Numerous Minor Flaws:** The accumulation of grammatical errors, inconsistencies, and missing details detracts from the overall quality of the paper.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Relevant Topic:** The problem of budget-fidelity trade-offs in crowdsourcing is important and timely.
*   **Information-Theoretic Approach:** The application of information theory to crowdsourcing is a novel and potentially fruitful direction.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the potential of the topic, this paper falls short of NIPS standards. The lack of rigor, poor presentation, and limited practical relevance outweigh any potential benefits. I recommend **rejection**. A substantial revision, addressing the issues outlined above, would be necessary for reconsideration. The authors need to significantly improve the clarity, mathematical precision, and practical grounding of their work.
