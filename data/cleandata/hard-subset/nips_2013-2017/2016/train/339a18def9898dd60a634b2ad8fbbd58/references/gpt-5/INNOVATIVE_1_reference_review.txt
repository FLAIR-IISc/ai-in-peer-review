Summary
This paper frames label crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding problem and asks for fundamental limits on the budget–fidelity trade-off. Items/labels are the source; workers are noisy channels (with either M-ary symmetric channel, MSC, or spammer–hammer, SHC, models); query design and aggregation form an encoder/decoder. The authors formalize distortion (Hamming) and define D(R) and R(D) for crowdsourcing. They derive information-theoretic lower bounds on the minimum number of queries per item to achieve a target error rate under two regimes: (i) worker skill levels unknown to the taskmaster and crowdsourcer (SL-UK) and (ii) perfectly known to the crowdsourcer (SL-CS). They then study k-ary incidence coding (kIC), where queries group k items so that invalid response patterns implicitly provide error-correction, and prove a necessary condition (lower bound) on the budget to achieve a target error under SHC, plus a simple pricing heuristic. Numerical curves compare the kIC lower bounds to the SL-CS information-theoretic limit.

Relation to prior work
The work connects to Dawid–Skene and EM-based aggregation, spectral/graphical methods, and budget-optimal task allocation (Karger–Oh–Shah). There has been substantial theory on budget–accuracy trade-offs and minimax bounds for crowdsourced classification and pairwise comparisons; the paper cites some (e.g., Karger et al.) but could better situate itself relative to information-theoretic and minimax lower bounds for crowdsourcing (e.g., Gao & Zhou 2016; Karger, Oh, Shah 2011/2013; other works on error-correcting perspectives for crowdsourcing). The novelty here is a clean source–channel separation lens and closed-form Rmin expressions for MSC pools under SL-UK vs SL-CS, plus the kIC formalization as a code with constrained response sets.

Strengths
- Bold, unifying perspective: Casting query design and aggregation as joint source–channel coding is elegant and offers a principled way to articulate D(R) and R(D) limits.
- Clear theoretical contributions: Theorems 2 and 3 provide explicit lower bounds on required queries per item for target error under MSC pools, with a compelling contrast between SL-UK and SL-CS regimes.
- kIC as coding: Formalizing k-ary incidence queries and exploiting invalid patterns as implicit error-correction is insightful; the SHC analysis (Lemma 1, Theorem 4) yields interpretable necessary conditions and shows how larger k helps.
- Practical touch: The pricing rule-of-thumb scaling with k is a nice, actionable byproduct.
- Scope: Addresses a central question (budget vs accuracy) that is of broad interest to the NeurIPS community.

Weaknesses
- Limited validation: Beyond a single figure, there is little empirical or simulation evidence to assess the tightness of the bounds or how close practical algorithms (e.g., DS, spectral, or convex methods) get to them. Even synthetic experiments comparing majority/DS/spectral vs the lower bounds would strengthen the case.
- Modeling assumptions: MSC symmetry across labels, iid items, non-adaptive querying, and difficulty-independent noise are strong; the authors note extensions, but the current results may be loose for realistic, heteroskedastic settings. The SHC oracle assumption (perfect worker type identification) in Theorem 4 also makes the kIC bound optimistic as a necessary condition.
- kIC scope: The kIC analysis focuses on binary, uniform labels; extension to N > 2 and non-uniform priors is left open. Practical decoders for kIC under unknown skills (beyond oracle) are not developed.
- Clarity/notation: Some notation is opaque or under-defined (e.g., the role of M vs N in Theorems 2–3, definitions like HM(E(·)), conditions under which Rmin takes the “otherwise” case). A careful pass to standardize symbols and explicitly define all terms would help. Typos and reference formatting issues are present.
- Related work coverage: Missing discussion of several closely related theoretical works on optimal rates/minimax bounds for crowdsourced labeling and error-correcting perspectives; a comparative analysis would contextualize contributions and sharpen claims of novelty.

Quality
The theoretical developments are plausible and technically sound at a high level, with proofs deferred to appendices. The separation-based framing is standard but well-applied. The kIC SHC analysis is careful but rests on idealizations (oracle skill identification). The lack of experiments or finite-sample analysis limits practical impact.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable but dense. Improving notation consistency (clearly distinguishing N, M, k; explicitly stating distributions/entropies used in denominators), fixing typos, and adding a small running example would improve clarity.

Originality
The overarching source–channel perspective and explicit SL-UK vs SL-CS rate formulas under MSC, plus the kIC formalization with necessary bounds, constitute a novel and bold synthesis. While related in spirit to prior budget–accuracy theory, the information-theoretic separation lens and kIC angle meaningfully extend the conversation.

Significance
If adopted, this framework could guide the principled design and pricing of queries and benchmark practical algorithms against fundamental limits. Even with assumptions, the results provide useful intuition (e.g., value of skill estimation and of larger k in kIC). Stronger empirical grounding would enhance significance.

Questions and suggestions
- Please clarify the role of M vs N in Theorems 2–3, and fully define HM(·), HN(·), and E(HM(·)). Is M the number of response options in a query or the source alphabet size?
- How tight are the bounds in finite L? Any converse/achievability gaps quantified?
- Can you provide simulations showing DS/spectral/convex decoders vs the theoretical limits under MSC/SHC?
- Discuss extensions to difficulty-dependent channels (Dawid–Skene confusion matrices per item), adaptive querying, and N > 2 for kIC.
- Add related work on minimax rates/optimal allocations for crowdsourcing classification and error-correcting designs.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel, unifying, and ambitious theoretical framing with clear takeaways.
- Closed-form information-theoretic bounds that illuminate the impact of skill knowledge.
- Interesting kIC coding perspective and practical pricing heuristic.

Cons for acceptance
- Minimal empirical validation and limited scope of kIC analysis.
- Strong modeling assumptions; unclear tightness in practical settings.
- Clarity/notation and related work coverage need strengthening.

Recommendation
Score: 7 (accept)
Confidence: 3/5

Given its conceptual novelty and the potential to influence how we think about query design and budgets in crowdsourcing, I am inclined to accept despite limited empirical validation, provided the authors improve clarity and situate the work more fully within the theoretical literature.
