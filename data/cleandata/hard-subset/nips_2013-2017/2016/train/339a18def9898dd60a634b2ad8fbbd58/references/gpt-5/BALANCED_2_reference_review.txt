Summary and relation to prior work
The paper frames crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding (JSCC) problem and studies fundamental budget–fidelity limits. The source is the latent label function B(X) over a large dataset; workers are modeled as discrete memoryless channels. Two worker models are considered: (i) M-ary symmetric channels (MSC) with heterogeneous error rates across workers, and (ii) a spammer–hammer channel (SHC) with hammers answering perfectly and spammers answering uniformly at random. Two information states are analyzed: SL-UK (worker skills unknown to the taskmaster/decoder) and SL-CS (skills known to the crowdsourcer/decoder). Using Shannon’s rate–distortion function for a discrete source with Hamming distortion and channel capacity, the paper derives lower bounds on the required number of queries per item (Theorems 2 and 3). It also introduces k-ary incidence coding (kIC) as a structured query scheme for clustering/labeling, shows that it embeds “obvious-invalid” answer elimination (error-correction-like structure), and derives a necessary condition on the rate under SHC when an oracle decoder can identify spammers vs hammers (Theorem 4). A simple pricing rule-of-thumb for kIC is suggested, and a corollary compares the kIC lower bound to an information-theoretic limit.

This work is related to (i) classical information theory (Cover & Thomas; El Gamal & Kim): rate–distortion theory, channel capacity, and source–channel separation, and (ii) crowdsourcing aggregation and sample complexity (Dawid–Skene EM; spectral/EM hybrids; majority voting analyses; Karger–Oh–Shah’s budget-optimal task allocation and label complexity). The mapping of unknown vs known skill states to channels with state unknown/known at the decoder is conceptually aligned with information-theoretic models of channels with random states.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: The JSCC framing yields clean, interpretable expressions for minimum query rate as a function of source entropy, target error, and channel quality. The distinction between SL-UK and SL-CS nicely mirrors channels with state unknown vs known at the receiver, explaining the “capacity of the average channel” vs “average of capacities” phenomenon.
- Generality: Theorems 2–3, while asymptotic, apply to general N-ary sources (with nonuniform P(B(X))) and arbitrary query alphabets M.
- Structured querying: kIC is a natural class of queries for clustering/label similarity; the paper formalizes its implicit error-correction via removal of invalid patterns and derives a closed-form spammer error probability and a simple necessary rate condition under SHC.
- Potential practical implications: The analysis suggests how worker-quality knowledge can substantially reduce budget, and sketches a pricing rule-of-thumb for kIC.

Weaknesses
Quality/technical soundness
- Reliance on asymptotic separation without achievability: The main bounds come directly from source–channel separation in the n→∞ regime. No finite-length analysis, no achievability constructions, and no discussion of tightness for realistic, non-asymptotic crowdsourcing regimes.
- SL-UK treatment: Theorem 2 effectively reduces to the capacity of the averaged MSC (log M − H_M(E[ε])). For channels with random state unknown at the receiver, capacity is indeed I(U;V) under the averaged channel in symmetric cases; however, the paper should justify this reduction carefully (and state assumptions that ensure the averaged channel remains symmetric).
- kIC bound (Theorem 4): The necessary condition appears to follow from P(error) ≥ (1−q)^{R′} · p_S with R′ the number of times an item appears in k-ary queries and p_S the spammer-only error. The stated formula is hard to parse; signs and the dependence on δ/p_S should be made explicit. As written, it is ambiguous (ln(1−q)/ln(δ̄_Ŝ)). Moreover, the “oracle decoder” assumption makes the bound optimistic; tightness and practical relevance are unclear.
- Missing comparisons to known minimax/sample-complexity results: Prior work (e.g., Karger–Oh–Shah) provides sharp label complexity bounds under spammer–hammer and related models. The paper does not compare its limits to these, nor clarify regimes in which it tightens known results.

Clarity
- Notation inconsistencies and typos: N (classes) vs M (query alphabet) vs k (query arity) are sometimes conflated; H_M(·) and H_N(·) are not consistently defined; symbols like ̂ and terms like E(ε) appear without careful definition. Several equations and the pricing ratio are garbled (missing fraction bars). References are incomplete (missing years, venues) and numbering appears inconsistent.
- Appendices are essential for proofs but not provided in the body; many claims hinge on them.

Originality
- The application of classical JSCC bounds to crowdsourcing is elegant but largely a direct application of known theorems; novelty lies more in the modeling choices (SL-UK vs SL-CS, kIC formalization) than in new theory. The kIC analysis under SHC is incremental and provides only necessary conditions under an oracle.

Significance
- The insights (value of skill estimation; structural gains from kIC) are potentially useful, but without achievability, empirical validation, or comparison to existing label-complexity results, it is difficult to assess practical impact. The pricing suggestion is interesting but underdeveloped.

Arguments for acceptance
- Provides a unifying, principled information-theoretic lens on budget–fidelity trade-offs.
- Clarifies the benefit of worker-skill estimation in a crisp way.
- Suggests structured query designs (kIC) and derives interpretable necessary conditions.

Arguments against acceptance
- Largely repackages known information-theoretic results; limited novelty and no achievability/simulations.
- Clarity issues, ambiguous equations, and incomplete references.
- Missing comparison to established crowdsourcing sample-complexity literature; kIC evaluation is minimal.

Recommendation: Reject

Suggestions for improvement
1) Clarify and fix notation, equations, and references. Explicitly define H_M(ε), H_N(D), E[ε], and the conditions under which R(D)=H(B)−H_N(D) applies. Correct Theorem 4’s expression and show the derivation.
2) Compare rigorously to prior sample-complexity bounds (e.g., Karger–Oh–Shah and follow-ups). Show where your lower bounds coincide, improve, or differ, for both direct labeling and kIC queries.
3) Provide achievability results or practical schemes approaching the bounds: e.g., non-adaptive random assignment plus DS/spectral-EM under MSC/SHC; quantify the gap to your limits.
4) Evaluate kIC empirically (synthetic and real datasets), including cost/time per query, and compare to pairwise (2IC) and direct labeling. Validate the pricing rule.
5) Discuss finite-length effects, adaptivity (feedback), and task difficulty as channel state affected by inputs; outline when separation-based limits are good predictors of realistic performance.
6) Move essential proof ideas to the main text or detailed supplement, and ensure all results are reproducible by an expert reader.

With these improvements, the work could provide a more compelling and rigorous contribution to the theory and practice of crowdsourcing.
