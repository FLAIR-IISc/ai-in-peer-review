

# Review of "Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing"

This paper attempts to model crowdsourcing as a communication system and analyze its fundamental performance limits using information theory. The authors propose a channel model for workers, define a crowdsourcing budget-fidelity trade-off, and derive theoretical bounds for two scenarios (unknown skill levels vs. known skill levels). They also introduce a "k-ary incidence coding" scheme and analyze its performance.

**Strengths:**
The paper identifies an interesting connection between crowdsourcing and information theory, which has not been extensively explored in the literature. The authors correctly reference prior work on crowdsourcing (e.g., Dawid-Skene algorithm, spammer-hammer model) and attempt to establish a theoretical foundation for crowdsourcing design.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Formatting and Typographical Errors:** The paper contains numerous formatting errors that undermine professionalism. The header states "th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016)" instead of "the Conference" or "NeurIPS 2016" (NIPS was renamed to NeurIPS in 2018). The reference section contains multiple instances of "th Conference" instead of "the Conference" (e.g., "th Conference on Learning Theory (COLT)"). The paper also uses inconsistent spacing ("th Conference" vs. "the Conference") throughout.

2. **Technical Inconsistencies:** The paper claims to model crowdsourcing as a communication system but fails to establish a clear connection between the channel model and the crowdsourcing problem. For instance, in Section 2.2, it states "the number of clusters, |B(X)| = N, may or may not be known a priori," yet the numerical results in Section 4.2 assume N=2 without justification.

3. **Unverified Claims:** The paper references "Appendix A" and "Appendix B" for proofs of Theorems 2 and 3, but these appendices are not provided in the submission, making it impossible to verify the claims.

4. **Notational Inconsistencies:** The paper uses inconsistent notation throughout. For example, "̂" is used as a variable for error probability but is not properly defined, and "HN(̂)" should be "H_N(ε)" or similar. In Lemma 1, "k2k−1" should be "k·2^{k-1}" but is written as a single term.

5. **Misleading Claims:** The paper claims to provide "ultimate performance bounds" but fails to demonstrate that these bounds are achievable. The paper states "the minimum number of queries per item" but doesn't clarify how this relates to practical implementation.

6. **Reference Issues:** The reference section contains incomplete citations (e.g., "Adaptive crowdsourcing algorithms for the bandit survey problem, I. Abraham, O. Alonso, V. Kandylas, A. Slivkins, th Conference on Learning Theory (COLT), , ") with missing information and the "th" typo.

7. **Lack of Practical Connection:** While the paper presents theoretical bounds, it doesn't clearly explain how these limits translate to practical crowdsourcing systems. The connection between the theoretical analysis and real-world applications is tenuous at best.

8. **Inconsistent Terminology:** The paper uses inconsistent terms like "taskmaster," "crowdsourcer," and "decoder" without clear definitions, which confuses the reader.

**Major Concerns:**

The paper's main contribution is the information-theoretic analysis of crowdsourcing, but it fails to properly establish the connection between information theory and crowdsourcing. The channel model (MSC and SHC) is not convincingly linked to the crowdsourcing problem. For example, the paper states that "the channel output is what a user perceives or responds to a query," but doesn't explain how this relates to the actual crowdsourcing task (e.g., labeling images).

The paper also makes claims about "fundamental limits" without adequately demonstrating that these limits are indeed fundamental or achievable. The theoretical bounds in Theorems 2 and 3 are presented without clear justification for their tightness or achievability.

**Recommendation:**

Despite the interesting theoretical angle, the paper's numerous technical and formatting errors, inconsistent notation, unverified claims, and lack of clear connection between theory and practice make it unsuitable for publication at NIPS. The paper would benefit from substantial revisions to address these issues, including proper formatting, verified proofs, clearer notation, and a stronger connection between the theoretical analysis and practical crowdsourcing applications.

The paper's minor flaws are too numerous and significant to overlook, especially for a venue as selective as NIPS. While the concept is promising, the execution is flawed and the presentation is unprofessional. I recommend rejection.
