This paper investigates a crowdsourcing system where a taskmaster decomposes a complex task—such as an inference problem—into smaller subtasks distributed to a crowd of workers. The crowdsourcer then aggregates the workers’ responses to reach a final conclusion. The system accounts for worker heterogeneity in skill and potential unreliability in reporting information. The core objective is to maintain a desired level of fidelity in the final outcome despite potentially inaccurate worker responses, requiring careful design of the subtasks (queries) allocated by the taskmaster. The total number of queries represents the system's budget, and the paper explores the fundamental trade-off between this budget and the achievable fidelity. This trade-off is analyzed by establishing a connection to the joint source-channel coding problem in information theory, mapping the taskmaster to a transmitter and the crowdsourcer to a receiver/decoder. The communication channel is modeled as the unreliable link between workers and the crowdsourcer. Leveraging existing information-theoretic results, the authors derive a lower bound on the budget needed to achieve a given fidelity level, a bound independent of the specific query design and aggregation algorithm. They then introduce a specific query design scheme, k-ary incidence coding, and analyze its performance, comparing it to the derived information-theoretic lower bound under a specific channel model. 

The reviewer finds the analogy between the rate-distortion framework and the crowdsourcing problem with unreliable workers to be insightful and believes this work may encourage further exploration of crowdsourcing using information-theoretic tools. However, the realism of the proposed model in representing actual crowdsourcing systems is questionable. This may stem from the effort to directly map the crowdsourcing problem to a standard point-to-point communication problem to readily apply existing information theory results. 

The authors should address the following points: 1) The model currently conflates worker reliability and skill level into a single channel representation. The assumption of equal query allocation to all workers, regardless of their skill—potentially represented by a rating system—may not hold in practice. 2) The realism of the channel model in capturing worker unreliability warrants discussion, particularly regarding the possibility of adversarial workers who intentionally degrade fidelity. Furthermore, the explanation of the numerical results lacks clarity, and the labeling of curves in Fig.~2 could be improved; specifically, what does the $k=1$ case represent? (Does it imply workers directly providing correct labels, precluding queries involving shared label identification?) In page 6, line 246, the assumption of knowledge of $P(B(X))$ should have been stated earlier in the paper. Additionally, there are minor errors on page 4, line 135: “Figure 1.a” should be “Figure 1.b” and “Figure 1.b” should be “Figure 1.c”. Finally, the authors repeatedly use the terms 'Theorem', 'Lemma', and 'Corollary' without accompanying numbers; using 'theorem', 'lemma', and 'corollary' (lowercase) would be more appropriate in these instances.
