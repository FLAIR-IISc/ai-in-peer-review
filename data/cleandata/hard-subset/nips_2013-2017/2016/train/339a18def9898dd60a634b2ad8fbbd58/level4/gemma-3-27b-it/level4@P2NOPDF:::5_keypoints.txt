- Paper provides an information-theoretic analysis of digital crowdsourcing, deriving lower bounds on queries per task to reach a target error probability.
- Workers are modeled as parallel independent channels using symmetric discrete memoryless channels (DMCs) for error and a spammer-hammer channel (SHC(q)) for random responders.
- Analysis assumes non-adaptive coding: all queries are fixed before any responses are observed.
- Two scenarios studied: crowdsourcer knows individual worker skills versus no prior knowledge of skills.
- Taskmaster is assumed unaware of worker skills and assigns an equal number of tasks to each worker.
- Main theoretical result: a necessary condition (minimum query rate) for achieving desired performance with k-ary incidence coding, under the SHC(q) worker model.
- Paper is well-organized and clearly written overall.
- Major drawback: no experimental validation or simulations to demonstrate practical relevance or gap to theory.
- Recommendation: compare existing crowdsourcing schemes (e.g., Mechanical Turk) to the theoretical limits to assess practical gaps.
- Presentation issues: section 2.4 is overly long and should be condensed.
- Presentation issues: section 3.1 seems unnecessary or should be expanded for clarity/utility.
- Need more detailed proof sketches for core theorems in the main text; mathematical detail currently insufficient without the appendix.
- Work appears novel and valuable; the derived bounds could help system designers determine minimum query rates and inform pricing.
- Mathematical proofs were not assessed by the reviewer, but the overall approach and results are promising.
