This paper presents an information-theoretic analysis of crowdsourcing, establishing fundamental limits on the number of queries required per item in an asymptotic setting. The authors cleverly model the crowdsourcing process as a joint source-channel coding (JSCC) problem with random channel states. Specifically, items to be labeled are treated as an i.i.d. source, the taskmaster as an encoder, crowd workers as channel uses, worker skill levels as random channel states, and the crowdsourcer as the decoder. By applying existing JSCC results, the authors derive theoretical bounds on the asymptotic query complexity. They also provide a lower bound on the error probability for k-ary incidence coding (kIC) within this modeled framework. Overall, the paper is well-written and represents a potentially valuable initial step towards formally modeling crowdsourcing using JSCC principles.

However, several points warrant further consideration. The derivation of results in Theorems 2 and 3 relies on the source-channel separation theorem. While likely valid, the authors do not explicitly justify its application in the context of a JSCC problem with random, encoder-unknown channel states. A proof or citation of existing proof demonstrating the continued validity of the separation theorem under these conditions would strengthen the argument.

Furthermore, the current model may be overly restrictive. The taskmaster and crowdsourcer are assumed to have access only to item labels, whereas, in practice, both may have access to the original item data itself. This data constitutes potential side information that could significantly impact the efficiency of the JSCC process. While side information available only to the encoder might offer limited benefit, its presence at both the encoder and decoder, or even at the decoder alone, could lead to improved coding strategies and tighter bounds. Incorporating side information into the model would likely enhance its realism and persuasiveness.

Regarding the discussion of Fig. 2 in Sec. 4.2, the statement that the lower bounds for kIC “improve” as *k* and *q* increase is potentially misleading. The intended meaning – that the lower bound *value* decreases with increasing *k* and *q*, potentially indicating better code performance – should be clarified. Improvement implies comparison to an upper bound, which is not provided in this context.

Following review of the authors’ response, I acknowledge the potential value of the JSCC modeling approach. However, a non-asymptotic characterization of the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing would be significantly more practical and impactful. The authors should consider recent advancements in non-asymptotic JSCC analysis, such as the work by Kostina and Verdu ("Lossy Joint Source-Channel Coding in the Finite Blocklength Regime," IEEE Trans. on Information Theory, vol. 59, no. 5, pp. 2545-2575, May 2013), to explore avenues for obtaining such a characterization.
