The authors provide an information-theoretic treatment of the crowdsourcing problem, establishing fundamental limits on the number of queries per item in an asymptotic sense. More specifically, the crowdsourcing problem is modeled as a joint source-channel coding (JSCC) problem with random channel state, where the channel state represents worker skill levels. The authors leverage existing results on JSCC to derive bounds on the achievable rate-distortion trade-off. They consider scenarios where worker skill levels are unknown or known to the crowdsourcer. Furthermore, they analyze a specific query scheme, k-ary incidence coding, and provide a lower bound on its error probability under a specific channel model. Overall, this is a well-written and technically sound paper that represents a first step toward a rigorous information-theoretic understanding of crowdsourcing.

Several technical points warrant further consideration. First, the paper relies on applying the source-channel separation theorem to a JSCC problem with random channel state unknown at the encoder. While the theorem’s applicability is intuitively reasonable, the authors should explicitly justify its use in this context, either by providing a proof or citing relevant work demonstrating its validity when the channel state is unknown to the encoder.

Second, the model assumes the taskmaster and crowdsourcer only observe item labels, neglecting potential access to the original data. Including the original data as side information at the encoder and/or decoder could significantly impact performance, potentially leading to tighter bounds. A discussion of the implications of side information would strengthen the analysis.

Third, in Section 4.2, the authors state that the lower bounds for k-ary incidence coding improve as *k* and *q* grow. This statement would be more precise by clarifying that the lower bound decreases with increasing *k* and *q*, indicating potentially improved performance, rather than a direct improvement of the bound itself. 

Finally, while the asymptotic analysis provides valuable insights, a nonasymptotic characterization of the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing would be more practically relevant. The authors should consider recent advances in finite-blocklength JSCC, such as the work by Kostina and Verdu [Kostina & Verdu, 2013], to explore the feasibility of such a nonasymptotic analysis.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and principled information-theoretic framework for analyzing crowdsourcing.
*   The technical analysis is sound and leverages established results from coding theory.
*   The insights gained from the model can guide the design of more efficient crowdsourcing systems.
*   The analysis of k-ary incidence coding provides a concrete example and demonstrates the potential benefits of specific query schemes.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The model is somewhat restrictive by excluding potential side information available to the taskmaster and crowdsourcer.
*   The asymptotic analysis, while theoretically insightful, may have limited practical applicability.
*   The justification for directly applying the source-channel separation theorem to the presented setting is not fully articulated.



**Overall assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the understanding of crowdsourcing. While there are some limitations, the strengths of the work outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance, contingent upon the authors addressing the points raised regarding the source-channel separation theorem, the inclusion of side information in the model, and clarification of the discussion of kIC performance.
