The paper models digital crowdsourcing in an information theoretic framework to provide lower bounds on the needed number of queries per task to achieve a given probability of error. The workers are modeled as parallel independent channels, specifically symmetric discrete memoryless channels (MSC) to capture errors made by workers, and the spammer-hammer channel model (SHC) to account for workers who may be spammers with a certain probability. The code is assumed to be non-adaptive, meaning that it does not change based on the feedback from the decoder.

Two cases are considered in the analysis: one where the crowdsourcer, who decides the value of each task after collecting the results from the workers, knows the skill level of all the workers, and another where the crowdsourcer does not know anything about the workers' skills. In both cases, the taskmaster, who distributes the tasks to the workers, is assumed to not know anything about the workers' skills, so every worker is assigned the same number of tasks.

The paper provides a necessary condition on the minimum needed rate - number of queries per task - for k-ary incidence coding, where the worker is shown k items and asked if they belong to similar or different breeds, with an SHC(q) worker pool. The paper is well-organized and well-written, and the authors have done a good job of explaining the problem and their approach.

However, there are a few areas that could be improved. Firstly, the paper would benefit from some experimental or simulation results to show how far current used schemes are from the limits, for example in Amazon Mechanical Turk (AMT). This would help to provide some context and make the theoretical results more concrete.

Secondly, some sections could be shortened or removed. For example, Section 2.4 could be condensed, and Section 3.1 could be removed or shortened, as it provides a general background on information theory that is not specific to the problem at hand. Instead, the authors could provide proof sketches for the theorems, which would be more useful for readers who want to understand the technical details.

Overall, the paper seems novel and provides a useful analysis of the crowdsourcing problem from an information theoretic point of view. The results could be very useful for designers of crowdsourcing projects, as they provide a way to determine the minimum rate needed to accomplish a task with a given probability of error, which can help in pricing such projects.
