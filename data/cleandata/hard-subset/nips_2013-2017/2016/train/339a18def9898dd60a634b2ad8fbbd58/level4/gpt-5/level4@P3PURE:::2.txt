This paper develops an information-theoretic formulation of crowdsourcing and derives fundamental limits on the asymptotic number of queries per item. The authors model the labels of items as an i.i.d. source; the taskmaster as an encoder; the crowd workers as multiple uses of a channel; workers’ skill levels as random channel states; and the crowdsourcer as the decoder. Within this joint source–channel coding (JSCC) framework with random channel state, they invoke existing results on coding rates to characterize the asymptotic query complexity. They also present a lower bound on the error probability of k-ary incidence coding under a particular crowdsourcing/channel model. Overall, the paper is well written and appears to be a first step toward JSCC-based modeling of crowdsourcing.

Technical comments:

1) Use of source–channel separation with random channel state. The paper models crowdsourcing as JSCC with a random channel state that is unknown at the encoder and may or may not be known at the decoder. The main results (Theorems 2 and 3) rely on the source–channel separation theorem. However, the paper should justify that separation continues to hold under this state model. I believe separation remains valid in these settings, but the authors should either prove it within their framework or cite an existing proof that explicitly covers channels with random state unknown at the encoder and possibly known at the decoder.

2) Role of side information from the original data. The model assumes the taskmaster/encoder sees only the item labels and the crowdsourcer/decoder observes only workers’ responses. This seems overly restrictive, because in practice both the taskmaster and the crowdsourcer may have access to the original items themselves. That access constitutes side information in the JSCC problem. While side information solely at the encoder may not help, side information at the decoder (or at both encoder and decoder) can be highly beneficial. The model would be more convincing and realistic if it explicitly incorporated side information and analyzed its impact.

3) Interpretation of lower bounds in Section 4.2 and Figure 2. The paper states that the lower bounds for k-ary incidence coding “improve” as k and q grow. Presumably this means the lower bound on error decreases with larger k and q, which suggests better code performance. However, this does not imply that the lower bound itself “improves” in a definitive sense without a corresponding upper bound for comparison. The text should be clarified to avoid overinterpreting the lower bound.

Edited following authors’ response: Modeling crowdsourcing via JSCC is interesting, but, as another reviewer noted, a nonasymptotic characterization of the fundamental limits would be more useful in practice. The authors should consider recent finite-blocklength results and converses for JSCC, e.g., V. Kostina and S. Verdú, “Lossy Joint Source-Channel Coding in the Finite Blocklength Regime,” IEEE Transactions on Information Theory, vol. 59, no. 5, pp. 2545–2575, May 2013.
