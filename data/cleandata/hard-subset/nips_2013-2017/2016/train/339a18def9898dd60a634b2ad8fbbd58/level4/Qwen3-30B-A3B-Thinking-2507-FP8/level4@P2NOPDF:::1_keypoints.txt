- Models crowdsourcing workers as noisy communication channels (symmetric memoryless and spammer/hammer), reframing labeling as a channel coding problem.  
- Maps labeling budget to code rate to analyze distortion–budget tradeoffs.  
- Proposes k-incidence coding: label tasks in subsets of size k to aggregate responses while accounting for worker reliability.  
- Channel-based perspective is novel and well-motivated; theoretical tradeoff analysis is sound.  
- k-incidence coding is a practical, interpretable first coding scheme.  
- Experimental results support theory but are limited in scope and lack real-world crowdsourcing datasets.  
- Needs clearer justification of how channel parameters (e.g., error probabilities) correspond to real worker behaviors.  
- Should compare k-incidence coding to existing aggregation methods (majority voting, Dawid–Skene) in theory and practice.  
- Budget–rate mapping is ambiguous—clarify whether “rate” means code rate and exactly how it constrains tasks or assignments.  
- Paper structure could better highlight the gap relative to Bayesian/ML approaches and include a stronger related-work comparison early.  
- Formal definitions (distortion, budget) should be stated rigorously up front to avoid ambiguity.  
- Lacks concrete implementation details and discussion of scalability to large-scale, multi-label, or hierarchical tasks.  
- Summary: solid theoretical contribution that needs stronger practical validation and clearer links to real-world crowdsourcing.
