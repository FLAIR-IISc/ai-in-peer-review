- Paper presents a novel information-theoretic framework modeling crowdsourcing as human-in-the-loop computation to analyze budget-fidelity trade-offs.  
- Well-structured and clearly written with thorough related work, but should cite alternate research on economic incentives for workers.  
- Provides a unified framework and derives fundamental information-theoretic bounds on minimum queries per item for target fidelity, for known and unknown worker skills.  
- Introduces k-ary incidence coding, a novel query scheme that empirically and theoretically reduces queries for given fidelity.  
- Numerical results and bounds demonstrate the practical potential of the proposed scheme as a benchmark for algorithm efficiency.  
- Some information-theoretic background, notation, and theorem proofs are hard to follow for non-experts; recommend restating results in crowdsourcing terms.  
- Paper would benefit from a more detailed conclusion summarizing key takeaways and implications for crowdsourcing.  
- Minor editorial issues: line 84 grammar, clarify crowdsourcer vs. taskmaster (106), correct "type in" at 122, rephrase lines 275–276, and add a more detailed caption for Figure 2.  
- Overall assessment: valuable contribution with potential for high impact after minor clarity and related-work improvements.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel unified framework, theoretical bounds, new k-ary coding scheme, and supporting numerical results.  
- Arguments against acceptance: accessibility issues for non-information-theory experts, need for expanded related work on incentives, and a more explicit concluding summary.
