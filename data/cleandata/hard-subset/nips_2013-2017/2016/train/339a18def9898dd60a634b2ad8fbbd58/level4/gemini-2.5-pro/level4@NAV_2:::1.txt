This paper presents an information-theoretic analysis of the label crowdsourcing problem, aiming to establish fundamental limits on the trade-off between budget and fidelity. The authors model the crowdsourcing process as a communication system: the true labels are the source, the query scheme is a source-channel encoder, the crowd of workers are parallel noisy channels, and the inference mechanism is the decoder. The budget is mapped to the code rate (queries per item), and fidelity is measured by distortion (error probability). This framework is used to derive lower bounds on the required budget to achieve a target fidelity under two worker models (M-ary Symmetric Channel and Spammer-Hammer Channel) and two scenarios regarding knowledge of worker skill levels. This approach contrasts with much of the existing literature (e.g., Dawid & Skene, 1979; Karger et al., 2011; Raykar et al., 2010), which typically focuses on designing and analyzing specific aggregation algorithms or worker models. By using rate-distortion theory, this work seeks to establish ultimate performance bounds that are independent of any specific query or inference algorithm, thus providing a valuable theoretical benchmark for the field. The paper also introduces and analyzes a practical query scheme, k-ary incidence coding (kIC), as a concrete example within this framework.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of this paper is its novel framing of the crowdsourcing problem through the lens of joint source-channel coding. This provides a principled and powerful way to reason about the fundamental limits of what is achievable. Establishing theoretical bounds on the budget-fidelity trade-off is a significant contribution, as it offers a baseline against which any practical crowdsourcing algorithm can be measured.
*   **Technical Quality:** The theoretical development appears sound. The application of rate-distortion theory and the source-channel separation theorem to this domain is appropriate and leads to insightful results (Theorems 2 and 3). The distinction between the "skill level unknown" (SL-UK) and "skill level known to crowdsourcer" (SL-CS) cases is clear and the resulting bounds intuitively show the value of estimating worker expertise.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written. It systematically introduces the communication system analogy, defines the components of the model (source, channels, coding), states the main theoretical results, and then provides a concrete analysis of the kIC scheme. This logical progression makes the core ideas accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Practical Implications of Asymptotic Results:** The information-theoretic bounds are derived under the assumption of large block lengths (n → ∞), which is standard in the field. However, the paper could be strengthened by a more detailed discussion of what these asymptotic results imply for practical crowdsourcing scenarios, which often involve finite datasets and budgets.
*   **Simplification of "Budget":** The paper uses the code rate (queries per item) as a direct proxy for budget. While this is a reasonable starting point, it overlooks the fact that different query types have different costs in terms of worker time, cognitive load, and monetary payment. For example, a k-ary incidence coding query (k > 2) is significantly more complex than a simple binary query. A more nuanced discussion connecting query complexity to the budget would make the analysis more robust and practically relevant.
*   **Completeness of kIC Analysis:** The analysis for kIC provides a lower bound on performance with an oracle decoder. While useful, this is an optimistic scenario. It would be beneficial to discuss or analyze the performance with a more practical decoder to bridge the gap between this lower bound and what might be achievable in practice.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a novel and significant theoretical framework for analyzing crowdsourcing, a problem of high interest to the NIPS community.
*   It derives fundamental performance limits, providing a valuable benchmark for future research on crowdsourcing algorithms.
*   The work is technically solid and clearly presented.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The practical implications of the asymptotic bounds are not fully explored, potentially limiting the paper's immediate impact on practitioners.
*   The model's mapping of "rate" to "budget" is a simplification that ignores the varying complexity and cost of different query types.
