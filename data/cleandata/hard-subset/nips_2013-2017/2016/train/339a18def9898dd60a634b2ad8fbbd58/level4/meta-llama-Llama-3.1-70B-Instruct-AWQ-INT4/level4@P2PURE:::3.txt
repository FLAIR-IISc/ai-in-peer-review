The authors address the problem of aggregating worker output in crowdsourcing using the language and tools of information theory, providing fundamental limits on the budget-fidelity tradeoff. Although I found the paper challenging to fully comprehend, I appreciated the interesting and potentially useful insights and model presented, offering a unique perspective on designing crowdsourcing aggregation techniques. To enhance the paper's impact, I suggest highlighting the high-level intuition and insights derived from these results more prominently, such as those at the end of Section 3, and including a conclusion that summarizes the key takeaways.

While I appreciate the authors' efforts to make the paper accessible to non-experts in information theory, I still found it difficult to follow. To improve clarity, I recommend rephrasing many of the results in terms of the crowdsourcing application itself, rather than relying on the language of channels, codes, and distortion. This would facilitate a more intuitive understanding of the concepts and their relevance to crowdsourcing.

In the related work section, it would be beneficial to mention the alternative line of research on robust economic incentives for workers, often referred to as "crowdsourcing mechanism" or "mechanism design for crowdsourcing." This body of work also explores varying worker abilities and the combination of incentives with sophisticated aggregation methods.
