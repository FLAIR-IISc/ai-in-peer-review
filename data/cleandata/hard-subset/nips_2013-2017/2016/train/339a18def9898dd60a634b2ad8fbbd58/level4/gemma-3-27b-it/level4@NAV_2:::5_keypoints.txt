- Paper studies fundamental limits of digital crowdsourcing using an information-theoretic framework to find minimum queries per item for a target accuracy.
- Workers modeled as parallel, independent noisy channels, analyzed for symmetric discrete memoryless channels (MSC) and spammer-hammer channel (SHC).
- Considers both known and unknown worker skill scenarios.
- Analyzes k-ary incidence coding and provides lower bounds on its rate requirements.
- Novel application of information theory to crowdsourcing, addressing a gap in prior work.
- Rigorous mathematical framework with well-structured derivations of lower bounds (Theorems 2–4).
- Intuitive and clear modeling of workers as noisy channels.
- Results have practical relevance for system design and pricing; includes pricing discussion for k-ary coding.
- Lacks empirical validation or simulations to demonstrate tightness and practical relevance of bounds.
- Some sections (Section 2.4 and Section 3.1 background) are overly long and could be streamlined or moved to the appendix.
- Main text omits proof details; providing proof sketches would improve accessibility.
- Assumption of non-adaptive coding may be limiting; adaptive query schemes should be explored.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, rigorous, and potentially impactful theoretical contribution.
- Arguments against acceptance: missing empirical validation and need for more concise presentation and proof sketches.
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions to address presentation, proof accessibility, discussion of assumptions, and future work on adaptive schemes.
