The authors address the problem of aggregating worker output in crowdsourcing using the language and tools of information theory, providing insights into the fundamental limits of the budget-fidelity tradeoff. Although I did not fully grasp the paper, I found the insights and model itself to be a very interesting and potentially useful take on the design of crowdsourcing aggregation techniques.

I would have liked to see the high-level intuition and insights stemming from these results, such as those presented at the end of Section 3, highlighted more prominently. Additionally, a conclusion summarizing the key takeaways would be beneficial. I appreciate the authors' efforts to make the paper accessible to those not familiar with information theory, like myself. However, I still found it challenging to follow. To improve clarity, I recommend rephrasing many of the results in terms of the crowdsourcing application itself, rather than retaining the language of channels, codes, and distortion.

In the related work section, it would be beneficial to mention the alternate line of research on robust economic incentives for workers, often referred to as "crowdsourcing mechanism" or "mechanism design for crowdsourcing." This body of work also discusses varying worker abilities and, more recently, how to combine these incentives with more sophisticated aggregation methods.

On a minor note, I suggest correcting "is reviewed in" to "are reviewed in" on line 84. I was initially confused about the distinction between the crowdsourcer and taskmaster, possibly due to the term "crowdsourcing" being a portmanteau of "crowd" and "outsourcing," which one would expect to occur before workers are involved. Additionally, there is a typo in line 122, which should read "A A(X1,X2)". The sentence on lines 275-276 was difficult to parse, and a rephrasing might improve clarity.

Lastly, I found Figure 2 to be unclear, and a more detailed caption might help to better understand the figure.
