The paper "Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing" presents a novel approach to analyzing crowdsourcing systems using information theoretic tools. The authors model the crowdsourcing problem as a joint source channel coding problem, where the taskmaster is the transmitter and the crowdsourcer is the receiver/decoder. The unreliable links from the workers to the crowdsourcer constitute a communication channel. This mapping allows the authors to derive a lower bound on the budget (number of queries) for a desirable fidelity level, which is independent of the schemes used by the taskmaster to design the queries and the algorithm used at the crowdsourcer to process the information collected from the workers.

The reviewer finds the parallel drawn between the rate-distortion framework and the crowdsourcing system with unreliable workers quite interesting. This work may invite other researchers to study the crowdsourcing framework using information theoretic tools. However, the reviewer is not sure if the results presented in this paper model real crowdsourcing systems in a very realistic way. One reason behind this could be that the paper wants the crowdsourcing problem to exactly map to the point-to-point communication problem so that the existing results from the information theory literature can be directly applied with minimal efforts.

The paper assumes that the transmitter (taskmaster) allocates the same number of queries to all the workers irrespective of their skill level. This may not be true in real systems where workers may carry some rating with them which represents their skill level. The channel models used to take the unreliability of the workers into account may not be realistic, as they do not consider workers who can have some knowledge of the task at hand and try to degrade the fidelity in an adversarial manner.

The explanation of numerical results is not very clear, and the labeling of various curves in Fig. 2 can be improved. The $k=1$ case corresponds to the scenario where the worker directly gives the correct label of the presented query, as one cannot present a pair and ask if they share the same label in this case.

Additionally, the paper assumes knowledge of $P(B(X))$, which should have been stated earlier in the paper. There are also some minor errors in the text, such as incorrect figure references.

The authors repeatedly use 'Theorem', 'Lemma', and 'Corollary' even when these terms are not accompanied by a specific theorem or lemma number. It would be more appropriate to use 'theorem', 'lemma', and 'corollary' in such cases.

Overall, the paper presents an interesting approach to analyzing crowdsourcing systems, but it may not model real-world systems accurately. The authors should consider addressing the issues mentioned above to improve the paper's clarity and realism.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, and the authors have provided a clear and concise explanation of their approach. However, the assumptions made in the paper may not be realistic, which affects the quality of the results.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written, but there are some minor errors and unclear explanations. The authors should improve the labeling of figures and provide a clearer explanation of numerical results.

**Originality:** The paper presents a novel approach to analyzing crowdsourcing systems using information theoretic tools. However, the authors could have explored more realistic channel models and worker behavior.
