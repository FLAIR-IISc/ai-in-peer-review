This paper proposes a novel framework for modeling and analyzing the problem of labeling data from crowdsourcing. By representing each worker as a noisy channel, the authors transform the problem into a coding problem, allowing them to study the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing. Two types of channels are considered: a symmetric memoryless channel without feedback and a spammer/hammer channel model. The budget used is mapped onto the rate of the code, enabling the analysis of distortion versus budget tradeoffs.

The paper presents a thorough investigation of the crowdsourcing problem, including a detailed system model, a discussion of query schemes and inference, and an analysis of the distortion and design problem. The authors also introduce a basic coding scheme, termed k-ary incidence coding, which corresponds to requiring the crowdsourced tasks to be that of labeling a subset of k items at a time. This coding scheme is analyzed in detail, and its performance is compared to information-theoretic lower bounds.

The paper's use of information theory to model and analyze the crowdsourcing problem is a significant strength.
