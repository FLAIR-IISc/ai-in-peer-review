- Establishes an information-theoretic bound for the budget–fidelity tradeoff in crowdsourcing using rate-distortion theory and channel coding; derivation appears sound.  
- Could be novel if the first application of these tools to crowdsourcing, but novelty and practical significance are questionable because the derivation is relatively straightforward and the bound may be loose.  
- Major concern: design freedom for the input sequence u^n in crowdsourcing is far more limited than in standard channel coding (no rich codebook design).  
- Equation (3) implicitly assumes many mappings from B(X)^n to U^(∑m_i) are implementable; if not, the channel-capacity approximation could be poor.  
- Repetition coding (majority voting) is achievable in crowdsourcing, but extending beyond repetition codes is challenging.  
- Key difference: you cannot repeatedly query the same worker on the same task, so i.i.d. assumptions underlying (1) and (2) are violated.  
- More generally, crowdsourcing imposes constraints absent in standard channel coding, likely restricting feasible encoders and making the bound loose.  
- Empirical comparison of the derived bound to state-of-the-art crowdsourcing methods is needed to establish practical relevance.
