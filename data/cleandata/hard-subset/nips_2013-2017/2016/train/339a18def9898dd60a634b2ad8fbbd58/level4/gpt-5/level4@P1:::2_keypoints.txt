- Paper frames crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding (JSCC) problem with random channel states and derives asymptotic query-rate limits for target labeling accuracy.  
- Labels are modeled as an i.i.d. source, workers as noisy channel uses with random skill states, the taskmaster as encoder, and the crowdsourcer as decoder.  
- Main theoretical results apply source–channel separation to obtain fundamental query-rate vs. fidelity bounds for two scenarios: skill unknown to both parties (SL-UK) and skill known at decoder (SL-CS).  
- The paper analyzes k-ary incidence coding (kIC) under a spammer–hammer model and provides a lower bound on its error probability.  
- The work is positioned as complementary to algorithmic label-aggregation literature by focusing on information-theoretic performance limits rather than new estimators.  
- Strength: Clear, principled JSCC modeling connects crowdsourcing to classical information theory.  
- Strength: Clean bounds illustrating the benefit of decoder-side knowledge of worker skills.  
- Strength: Concrete kIC analysis yields interpretable insights on how k and hammer fraction affect performance.  
- Strength: Potential to inform design and pricing of query schemes by benchmarking achievable performance.  
- Concern: The paper relies on source–channel separation despite channel having random states; it must justify separation under precise assumptions (memorylessness, i.i.d. states) or cite/prove the applicable theorem.  
- Concern: The model omits realistic side information at encoder/decoder (e.g., original items); incorporating or discussing Wyner–Ziv–style side information effects is important.  
- Concern: Language around kIC “improving” with k and q is misleading—smaller lower bounds don’t imply tighter bounds or achievability; wording should be clarified.  
- Concern: Results are asymptotic; practical finite-blocklength (nonasymptotic) characterizations (e.g., Kostina & Verdú 2013) would increase applicability.  
- Quality: Technical development is sound under standard separation results but needs explicit handling of state-dependent channels and side information.  
- Clarity: Generally well written with clear modeling and worker channel descriptions; clarify the interpretation of “improvement” for lower bounds.  
- Originality: Novel framing of crowdsourcing as JSCC in this community and complements prior inference-centric work.  
- Significance: Provides useful benchmarks that could guide query scheme design; justifying state assumptions and adding nonasymptotic results would raise impact.  
- Pros for acceptance: conceptually clean/foundational perspective, clear SL-UK vs SL-CS limits, concrete kIC analysis.  
- Cons against acceptance: Unjustified reliance on separation for state-dependent channels, exclusion of decoder side information, and asymptotic-only focus.  
- Overall: A solid first step toward an information-theoretic foundation for crowdsourcing that would be strengthened by addressing separation/state assumptions, side information, and finite-blocklength analysis.
