

The authors present an information-theoretic framework modeling crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding (JSCC) problem with random channel states representing worker skill levels. They establish fundamental limits on the asymptotic query rate per item under two scenarios: (1) when worker skill levels are unknown to the taskmaster but may be known to the crowdsourcer (SL-CS), and (2) when skill levels are unknown to both (SL-UK). The work extends prior NIPS contributions on crowdsourcing aggregation (e.g., Dawid-Skene [5], Zhang et al. [11]) by introducing a rate-distortion perspective, deriving closed-form bounds (Theorems 2–3), and analyzing k-ary incidence coding (kIC) under spammer-hammer channel models (SHC). The paper is well-structured, technically rigorous, and offers a promising first step toward unifying crowdsourcing design under information-theoretic principles, with clear relevance to practical platforms like Amazon Mechanical Turk.

**Strengths:**  
- **Originality:** First application of JSCC to crowdsourcing with random channel states, distinct from prior aggregation-focused works (e.g., [5, 8, 11]). The kIC scheme and its error analysis (Lemma 1, Theorem 4) provide novel combinatorial insights.  
- **Significance:** Establishes fundamental trade-offs between query budget (rate) and fidelity (error probability), addressing a core challenge in real-world crowdsourcing systems where cost and accuracy must be balanced.  
- **Clarity:** The modeling (Section 2) and derivations (Section 3) are logically presented, with intuitive examples (e.g., image labeling, clustering).  
- **Quality:** Theorems 2–3 are theoretically sound, leveraging standard JSCC results with appropriate adaptation to the crowdsourcing setting.  

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Theoretical Gap:** The authors apply the standard source-channel separation theorem without justifying its validity for channels with random states unknown at the encoder (Section 3.2). While the theorem likely holds, a proof sketch or citation to existing results (e.g., for channels with state information at the decoder) is required for completeness.  
2. **Model Restriction:** The model assumes the taskmaster/crowdsourcer lack access to the original data (items), ignoring potential side information. This is unrealistic for many applications (e.g., image labeling where the taskmaster sees the images), which could significantly improve query efficiency. The authors should address how side information (e.g., at the decoder) might alter the bounds.  
3. **Misstatement in Results:** In Section 4.2, the claim that "the kIC bounds improve as k and q grow" is misleading; the lower bounds *decrease* (indicating better error performance), but the phrasing implies the bound itself improves without context. This requires clarification.  
4. **Limited Practical Impact:** The asymptotic analysis (e.g., Theorems 2–3) offers little guidance for finite-budget deployments. As noted by other reviewers, nonasymptotic bounds (e.g., Kostina & Verdú [14]) would better serve practitioners.  

**Pro/Con Acceptance Summary:**  
- **Pro:** Rigorous information-theoretic foundation, novel kIC analysis, clear exposition of fundamental limits.  
- **Con:** Unaddressed theoretical gap (state-dependent channel separation), unrealistic model simplification (no side information), asymptotic focus limits practical utility.  

The paper makes a valuable conceptual contribution but requires stronger theoretical grounding and discussion of practical constraints to meet NIPS standards for significance and quality.
