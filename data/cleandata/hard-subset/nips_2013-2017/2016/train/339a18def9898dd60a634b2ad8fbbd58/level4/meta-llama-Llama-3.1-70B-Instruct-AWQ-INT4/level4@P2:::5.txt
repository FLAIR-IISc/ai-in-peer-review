The paper "Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing" models digital crowdsourcing in an information theoretic framework to provide lower bounds on the needed number of queries per task to achieve a given probability of error. The authors consider a communication system model for crowdsourcing, where the crowd is modeled by a set of parallel noisy channels, each representing a worker with imperfect skill levels. The paper analyzes two cases: when the crowdsourcer knows the skill level of all workers and when it does not.

The authors derive information theoretic bounds on the minimum number of queries per item required to achieve a certain fidelity in crowdsourcing. They consider two worker pool models: the M-ary symmetric channel model (MSC) and the spammer-hammer channel model (SHC). The results are established by a joint source channel (de)coding scheme, which represents the query scheme and inference over parallel noisy channels.

The paper also presents and analyzes a query scheme dubbed k-ary incidence coding and studies optimized query pricing in this setting. The authors show that k-ary incidence coding can provide an error correction capability, which improves the overall crowdsourcing performance.

The paper is well-organized and well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their framework and results. However, there are a few areas that could be improved. Section 2.4 could be shortened, and Section 3.1 could be shortened or removed, as it provides background information that is not essential to the paper's main contributions. Instead, proof sketches to the theorems would be more valuable to the reader.

The paper's contributions are novel and useful, as they provide designers of crowdsourcing projects with a framework to determine the minimum rate needed to accomplish a task with a given probability of error. This can help in pricing such projects and optimizing their performance. However, the paper would benefit from including experiments or simulations to show how far current used schemes are from the limits, for example in Amazon Mechanical Turk (AMT).

Overall, the paper is a valuable contribution to the field of crowdsourcing and information theory, and it has the potential to impact the design and optimization of crowdsourcing systems.
