This paper develops an information-theoretic framework for crowdsourcing by modeling it as joint source–channel coding (JSCC) with random channel state. Items’ labels form an i.i.d. source; the taskmaster is the encoder; multiple worker responses constitute uses of a channel; worker skills are modeled as random channel states; and the crowdsourcer is the decoder. Under this model, the paper leverages established JSCC results to characterize asymptotic fundamental limits on the number of queries per item. It also provides a lower bound on the error probability of k-ary incidence coding (kIC) under a specific worker model. The paper is clearly written and represents an interesting first step toward a principled information-theoretic treatment of crowdsourcing.

Strengths
- The mapping from crowdsourcing components to JSCC elements is clean and insightful, and it yields interpretable asymptotic limits on query complexity.
- The random-state channel model captures heterogeneous worker skills in a natural way.
- The lower bound analysis for kIC provides concrete, if model-specific, guidance.

Main concerns and suggestions
1) Use of source–channel separation with random channel state.
- The key asymptotic limits (Theorems 2 and 3) appear to follow by invoking source–channel separation. However, when the channel has a random state unknown at the encoder (and possibly known or unknown at the decoder), separation requires careful justification. While I believe separation still holds under standard models (e.g., memoryless state known to the decoder yields a capacity characterized by conditional mutual information; unknown at both ends yields a capacity averaged over state), the paper should explicitly state the precise channel model, list the assumptions (e.g., stationarity/memorylessness of state, independence across uses), and cite or prove the corresponding separation result.
- Actionable suggestion: Add a short lemma or an appendix citing a standard reference that establishes separation for JSCC over channels with random state unknown at the encoder (with the two decoder-knowledge cases treated explicitly). Without this, the logical bridge to the asymptotic query limits is incomplete.

2) Modeling access to the original items (side information).
- The current model assumes the taskmaster (encoder) only observes labels and the crowdsourcer (decoder) only sees worker responses. In practice, both often have access to the original items (e.g., images, text), which can serve as side information.
- Side information at the decoder or at both terminals can strictly improve performance (e.g., Wyner–Ziv-type gains, or more generally JSCC with correlated side information). Side information only at the encoder is not always helpful, but the other cases can substantially reduce required query rates.
- Actionable suggestion: Discuss an extension where the item features are available as correlated side information at the encoder/decoder. Even if a full treatment is deferred, clarifying the impact and providing references to JSCC with side information would make the model more realistic and strengthen the paper’s relevance.

3) Interpretation of “improving” lower bounds in Section 4.2.
- The statement that “lower bounds for kIC improve as k and q grow” is potentially misleading. If “improve” means the numerical lower bound on error probability decreases with larger k and q, please state that explicitly. A smaller lower bound suggests better potential performance, but it does not imply the bound itself is tighter unless an accompanying upper bound is provided for comparison.
- Actionable suggestion: Rephrase to “the numerical lower bound on the error probability decreases as k and q increase” and avoid claims about bound tightness unless supported by upper bounds or matching achievability.

Nonasymptotic perspective
- While the asymptotic analysis is valuable, nonasymptotic characterizations would be especially relevant for practical crowdsourcing systems with finite budgets and blocklengths. Recent finite blocklength results for JSCC provide sharp converses and achievability bounds that could translate into concrete query–error tradeoffs.
- Actionable suggestion: Discuss the applicability of finite-blocklength JSCC bounds (e.g., V. Kostina and S. Verdu, “Lossy Joint Source-Channel Coding in the Finite Blocklength Regime,” IEEE Trans. Inf. Theory, 59(5):2545–2575, 2013) to the proposed crowdsourcing model, and outline how such tools might yield nonasymptotic guarantees or guidelines for budgeted querying.

Overall assessment
- The paper offers an appealing and principled bridge between crowdsourcing and JSCC with random state, yielding clear asymptotic insights and a concrete lower bound for kIC. To solidify the contribution, the authors should (i) make the separation-based arguments rigorous for the stated channel-state knowledge regimes, and (ii) broaden the modeling to acknowledge side information at the encoder/decoder. Clarifying the interpretation of the kIC lower bounds and pointing toward finite-blocklength analyses would further enhance practical relevance.
- With these improvements, the work could serve as a compelling foundation for a deeper, practically relevant theory of crowdsourcing grounded in information theory.
