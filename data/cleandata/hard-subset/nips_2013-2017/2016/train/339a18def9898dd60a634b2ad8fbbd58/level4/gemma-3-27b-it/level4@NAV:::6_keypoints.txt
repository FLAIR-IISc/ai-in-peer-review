- Establishes an information-theoretic lower bound on the budget–fidelity tradeoff in crowdsourcing using rate-distortion theory and the channel coding theorem; derivation is mathematically sound.  
- If this is the first application of these tools to crowdsourcing, it could be a notable contribution, but practical significance and novelty are uncertain.  
- Derivation is relatively direct and the resulting bound may be overly conservative.  
- Concern about applicability of standard channel coding concepts: unclear if the same design freedom for codebooks exists for designing the input sequence u^n in eq.(3).  
- For tightness, many mappings from B(X)^n to U^(Σm_i) must correspond to viable encodings; otherwise the calculated capacity may underestimate true performance.  
- While repetition coding and majority-vote decoding are feasible, it is unclear how to extend to stronger error-correction schemes in crowdsourcing.  
- Critical distinction: channel coding leverages i.i.d. repeated transmissions, but repeated queries to the same worker produce correlated responses, violating the i.i.d. assumptions in eq.(1)/(2).  
- Crowdsourcing likely has far more constraints on allowable encoding schemes than standard channel coding, which may make the proposed bound loose.  
- Suggested improvement: empirically compare the derived information-theoretic bound to performance of state-of-the-art crowdsourcing methods.
