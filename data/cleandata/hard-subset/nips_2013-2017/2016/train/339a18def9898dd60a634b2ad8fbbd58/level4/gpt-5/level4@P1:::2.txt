This paper models crowdsourcing as a joint source–channel coding (JSCC) problem with random channel states, and uses information-theoretic tools to derive asymptotic limits on the number of queries per item needed to achieve a target labeling accuracy. Specifically, item labels are treated as an i.i.d. source; the taskmaster is the encoder; each worker corresponds to a noisy channel use; worker skill is modeled as a random channel state; and the crowdsourcer is the decoder. Within this framework, the authors invoke source–channel separation to obtain fundamental limits on the required query rate under two scenarios: worker skill unknown to both encoder and decoder (SL-UK) and worker skill perfectly known at the decoder (SL-CS). They also analyze k-ary incidence coding (kIC), providing a lower bound on its error probability under a spammer–hammer channel model, and compare these coding-theoretic lower bounds to information-theoretic limits.

Relation to prior work: This contribution is orthogonal to the large literature on label aggregation and inference (e.g., majority voting, Dawid–Skene/EM, spectral and convex methods). Rather than proposing a new estimator, it seeks fundamental performance bounds, thereby complementing prior algorithmic work commonly seen at NIPS.

Strengths
- Clear, principled modeling that connects crowdsourcing to JSCC with random channel states.
- Clean information-theoretic bounds on the query budget vs. fidelity trade-off under both SL-UK and SL-CS scenarios, highlighting the benefit of decoder-side knowledge of worker skills.
- A concrete coding scheme (kIC) is analyzed under the spammer–hammer model, yielding interpretable lower bounds and qualitative insights about how k and the fraction of “hammers” affect performance.
- The work has the potential to guide the design and pricing of query schemes by benchmarking what is fundamentally achievable.

Concerns and suggestions
1) Justification of separation with random channel state. The main limits (Theorems 2 and 3) rely on source–channel separation. Here the “channel” has a random state (worker skill) unknown at the encoder and possibly known at the decoder. The paper should justify that the separation theorem still applies in this setting, with precise assumptions (memorylessness, state distribution i.i.d. across channel uses, etc.). I expect separation to hold for discrete memoryless state-dependent channels with no encoder state information and receiver state either unknown (averaged) or known (conditioned), but the authors should supply a proof or cite an appropriate theorem.

2) Side information at encoder/decoder. The model treats the encoder as observing only labels and the decoder as observing only worker responses. In many realistic settings, both the taskmaster and the crowdsourcer have access to the original items (images, texts), which can serve as side information. Encoder-only side information may be less helpful, but decoder or common side information can significantly change the rate–distortion function and thus the fundamental trade-offs. Incorporating side information (e.g., via Wyner–Ziv or related settings) would make the model more compelling and realistic, or at least the implications of excluding it should be discussed.

3) Interpretation of kIC lower bounds. In Section 4.2, the paper states that the lower bounds for kIC “improve” as k and q increase. I believe the intended meaning is that the lower bound on the required rate decreases with larger k and q, indicating that better performance may be achievable. However, a lower bound becoming smaller does not itself “improve” the bound in the sense of tightness, as there is no accompanying upper bound; please clarify the language here.

4) Finite-blocklength (nonasymptotic) characterization. The current limits are asymptotic in blocklength. For practical crowdsourcing systems, nonasymptotic bounds would be more actionable. The authors should consider recent finite-blocklength converses for lossy JSCC, e.g., V. Kostina and S. Verdú, “Lossy Joint Source-Channel Coding in the Finite Blocklength Regime,” IEEE Trans. IT, 59(5):2545–2575, 2013, which may yield sharper guidance for moderate dataset sizes and realistic query budgets.

Quality: The technical development is sound given standard separation results, but the paper should explicitly handle the state-dependent channel and the role of side information to solidify the claims.

Clarity: Generally well written and easy to follow. The modeling assumptions and worker channel models (MSC, spammer–hammer) are clearly described. A brief discussion clarifying the “improvement” of lower bounds (point 3) would help.

Originality: Framing crowdsourcing in a JSCC-and-bounds perspective appears novel in this community and complements prior inference-centric work.

Significance: The limits provide a useful benchmark and could inform the design of query schemes and budgets. Incorporating state-information justifications and nonasymptotic results would increase impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually clean and potentially foundational perspective on crowdsourcing.
- Clear limits for two important scenarios (SL-UK vs. SL-CS).
- Concrete analysis of kIC under a standard worker model.

Cons against acceptance
- Reliance on separation without a clear argument for the state-dependent channel setting used here.
- Modeling excludes potentially crucial side information at the decoder.
- Asymptotic focus; practical finite-blocklength guidance is missing.

Overall, I view this as a well-executed first step toward an information-theoretic foundation for crowdsourcing. Addressing the separation/side-information issues and adding nonasymptotic insights would strengthen the contribution considerably.
