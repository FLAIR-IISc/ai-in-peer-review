- Frames crowdsourcing as a human-in-the-loop, information-theoretic problem, modeling workers as noisy channels (MSC and SHC).  
- Derives lower bounds relating query budget (cost) to labeling fidelity (accuracy).  
- Analyzes k-ary incidence coding (k-IC) as a concrete query scheme and discusses query pricing.  
- Connects crowdsourcing to coding theory and rate-distortion theory, building on prior aggregation and worker-modeling work.  
- Novel framing enabling application of powerful analytical tools from information theory.  
- Provides fundamental limits that give theoretical insight into inherent crowdsourcing constraints.  
- Presents rigorous mathematical analysis with clear definitions and treatment of different worker models.  
- Offers practical implications via k-IC analysis and Appendix on pricing.  
- Lacks empirical validation, so practical tightness of bounds is unclear.  
- Relies on simplifying assumptions (memoryless channels, perfect skill estimation) without thorough robustness analysis.  
- k-IC error-correction capabilities and links to implementation are not explained in sufficient detail.  
- Focuses on non-adaptive coding; lacks exploration of feedback/adaptive schemes that might improve performance.  
- Overall: valuable theoretical contribution; recommends acceptance conditional on addressing clarity and limitation concerns.
