Summary
The paper studies crowdsourced labeling through the lens of channel coding. Each worker is modeled as a noisy channel—specifically, either a symmetric memoryless channel without feedback or a spammer/hammer model. Under this abstraction, the task allocation and aggregation process becomes a coding problem, with the annotation budget mapping naturally to the code rate. The authors analyze the trade-off between budget and distortion (labeling error) and introduce a coding scheme termed k-incidence coding, in which tasks ask a worker to jointly label subsets of k items. This reframing provides a principled way to reason about reliability, redundancy, and cost in crowdsourcing.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: Casting crowdsourcing as channel coding is elegant and allows the use of powerful tools from information theory for performance analysis.
- Modeling choices: The two worker models (symmetric memoryless and spammer/hammer) capture common stylized behaviors and bound a range of practical scenarios.
- Budget–distortion analysis: Mapping budget to code rate provides an interpretable axis to study fundamental trade-offs between cost and accuracy.
- Methodological innovation: k-incidence coding is a simple, concrete scheme that departs from standard per-item labeling and may exploit cross-item structure to improve reliability.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Scope of realism: The symmetric memoryless and spammer/hammer models, while analytically convenient, may be restrictive. Real workers are often heterogeneous, class-dependent, and time-varying. It would help to discuss robustness to model mismatch and how results might extend to more general confusion matrices.
- Practicality of k-incidence tasks: Asking workers to label k items jointly can increase cognitive load and completion time in practice. The paper should discuss how k affects worker effort, adherence, and effective per-task cost, and whether common platforms support such composite tasks.
- Adaptivity and feedback: The analysis assumes no feedback and (apparently) non-adaptive task assignment. Many practical systems use adaptive allocation or quality control. Clarifying whether the bounds extend (or do not extend) to adaptive strategies would strengthen the contribution.
- Relation to prior art: There is a substantial literature on coding-theoretic and probabilistic approaches to crowdsourcing (e.g., channel-based worker models, message passing, spectral and EM estimators). A more explicit comparison—both conceptually and in terms of guarantees—would help situate the novelty.
- Empirical validation: The paper appears primarily theoretical. Even limited experiments (synthetic and real datasets) demonstrating the predicted budget–distortion behavior and the practical benefits or trade-offs of k-incidence coding would improve the paper’s impact.
- Multiclass and class imbalance: It is unclear whether the results extend beyond binary labels and balanced priors. Many crowdsourcing tasks are multiclass and imbalanced; clarifying the scope would be useful.

Questions for the authors
- What distortion metric is used throughout (e.g., Hamming error), and how sensitive are the results to the choice of metric?
- How is budget defined precisely (number of worker-item interactions, time, or monetary cost)? If worker effort per task grows with k, how does that adjust the effective “rate” in k-incidence coding?
- Do your bounds include both converses and achievable schemes that are order-wise tight, or are there gaps? If gaps exist, can you characterize where they matter most?
- How does k-incidence decoding work algorithmically, and what is the computational complexity? Are there efficient decoders for large-scale settings?
- How should one choose k under a fixed budget? Is there a principled rule or phase transition revealed by the analysis?
- How robust are the results when workers have heterogeneous and unknown reliabilities? Can your framework incorporate priors or estimated reliabilities?

Suggestions for improvement
- Expand the discussion of modeling assumptions and robustness. Even a short section on worker heterogeneity, class-dependent errors, and potential extensions would broaden applicability.
- Add a related-work comparison, highlighting how your budget–rate mapping and k-incidence coding differ from, complement, or improve upon prior coding-theoretic and probabilistic approaches.
- Provide empirical illustrations: (a) synthetic experiments validating the theoretical curves and sensitivity to model mismatch; (b) small-scale real-data experiments demonstrating feasibility and the practical overhead of k-incidence tasks.
- Discuss platform and human factors for k-incidence: task design, UI considerations, potential increases in completion time, and effects on worker quality.
- Clarify whether adaptivity or partial feedback could improve the rate–distortion trade-off and where your analysis would need modification.
- If possible, generalize statements to multiclass settings or clearly delineate binary-only assumptions.

Clarity and presentation
The paper is generally clear and well motivated. To improve readability:
- Make the budget–rate mapping explicit early, with a running example that carries through the analysis.
- Clearly separate results for the two channel models and summarize the key takeaways in a figure or table.
- Introduce k-incidence coding with an intuitive example and a short pseudocode description of the decoder.

Overall evaluation
This is an interesting and potentially impactful perspective on crowdsourced labeling that leverages established information-theoretic tools to reason about fundamental trade-offs. While I am not a specialist in this area, the conceptual framing and the k-incidence scheme appear promising. The main opportunities are to better connect the theory to practical constraints, compare with prior art, and, if space permits, include empirical evidence. With these enhancements, the paper would be substantially strengthened.
