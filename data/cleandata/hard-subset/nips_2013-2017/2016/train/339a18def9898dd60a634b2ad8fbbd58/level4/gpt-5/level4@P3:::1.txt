Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies crowdsourced labeling through an information-theoretic lens. Workers are modeled as noisy channels—either an M-ary symmetric channel (MSC) with error parameter, or a spammer–hammer channel (SHC) with hammer probability q. A crowdsourcing campaign is viewed as joint source–channel coding: the “source” is the true labeling B(X) of a large dataset; the “encoder” poses queries (potentially over multiple items); the “channels” are workers’ responses; and the “decoder” infers labels. Budget is mapped to coding rate (queries per item), and fidelity to distortion (e.g., Hamming error).

The paper derives rate–distortion style lower bounds on the minimum number of queries per item needed to achieve a target average error, with two scenarios: (i) unknown worker skill levels to both taskmaster and crowdsourcer (SL-UK), and (ii) worker skills known to the crowdsourcer (SL-CS). In both cases, the bounds follow from Shannon’s source–channel separation: the necessary condition is that channel capacity exceeds the source rate at the target distortion. The authors also analyze a concrete query scheme, k-ary incidence coding (kIC), which asks workers to cluster k items at once. For SHC workers, they provide a converse bound for kIC using an oracle decoder and derive a simple pricing rule of thumb comparing kIC variants.

This framing complements prior work that focuses on aggregation and worker modeling/selection (e.g., majority vote, Dawid–Skene EM, Karger–Oh–Shah, spectral/EM hybrids, bandit survey formulations). To my knowledge, using explicit rate–distortion and separation arguments to characterize budget–fidelity limits in crowdsourcing is relatively novel within the NIPS literature.

Strengths
- Clear information-theoretic framework that separates the effects of source uncertainty (label distribution and target distortion) and channel noise (worker reliability).
- General lower bounds for MSC worker pools under SL-UK and SL-CS that illuminate how knowledge of skills shifts the effective capacity.
- Analysis of a concrete, practically relevant query scheme (kIC) that naturally embeds redundancy by disallowing inconsistent answers and exploits combinatorial structure across items.
- A converse bound for kIC under SHC and a simple pricing heuristic relating costs across different k.
- Potential to guide system design: how many queries per item are fundamentally required for a desired error, and whether complex queries (higher k) are worth their cost.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Assumptions and scope. The main bounds rely on large-blocklength separation and iid item labels. Real datasets often have class imbalance, correlations between items, and heterogeneous item difficulty. The authors briefly mention extensions (e.g., feedforward to capture difficulty) but do not develop them. It would be valuable to discuss how sensitive the results are to non-iid labels and to adaptive querying.
- Worker models. MSC and SHC are analytically convenient yet stylized. Many crowdsourcing works use instance-dependent accuracies and confusion matrices per worker (beyond symmetric noise). A discussion bridging the MSC to these models (e.g., via average symmetric capacity or bounds under asymmetric noise) would strengthen applicability.
- kIC analysis. Theorem 4 provides a necessary condition using an oracle decoder that knows spammer/hammer identities. While this yields a clean converse, it can be quite loose relative to what is achievable by practical decoders that must infer skills. Could the authors add achievability results or simulations to illustrate the gap between the converse and a concrete kIC decoder?
- Empirical validation. The paper would benefit from experiments on synthetic and real crowdsourcing data to (a) validate the tightness of the rate bounds, and (b) compare kIC against standard single-item queries under a fixed budget (including realistic costs for higher-k tasks). Even small-scale studies would ground the theory.
- Clarity and notation. Several notational conventions and definitions are hard to follow or inconsistent (e.g., overloaded M vs. N; definitions of HN(·) and HM(·); missing details in some equations; typesetting issues). A careful editing pass to unify notation, restate the key bounds in plain language, and add a small worked example (e.g., binary labels with class imbalance) would significantly improve readability.
- Related work coverage. The connection to budget-optimal task allocation (e.g., Karger et al.) and to algorithms that simultaneously learn worker reliabilities and labels is acknowledged, but a more explicit comparison of guarantees (what those works optimize vs. what the present bounds capture) would help position the contribution.

Quality
The technical approach is sound and the use of separation yields interpretable single-letter lower bounds. However, the practical impact is limited by strong assumptions (iid labels, non-adaptive querying, simplified worker models) and the absence of empirical corroboration. The kIC converse under SHC is mathematically clear, though its tightness is not assessed.

Clarity
The paper is generally well structured, but notation and typesetting need substantial cleanup. Providing intuition alongside theorems (e.g., “with known skills, capacity is the average of per-worker capacities; with unknown skills, you effectively see the capacity of an average worker”) would aid comprehension. References are missing years and have inconsistent formatting.

Originality
Framing crowdsourcing budget–fidelity trade-offs via rate–distortion and channel capacity in this explicit way is original within the NIPS crowdsourcing literature. The kIC angle is also interesting, though related multi-item query ideas exist in clustering and graph-based labeling.

Significance
If refined and empirically validated, the results could inform designers about fundamental budgets and motivate richer query designs. As written, the contribution is primarily conceptual/theoretical; its immediate practical impact is modest.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually clean, principled framework for budget–fidelity trade-offs.
- General lower bounds illuminating the role of worker-skill knowledge.
- Interesting analysis of multi-item queries (kIC) with a practical pricing heuristic.

Cons for acceptance
- Strong modeling assumptions; limited discussion of robustness and adaptivity.
- No empirical results; tightness of bounds and kIC benefits not demonstrated.
- Clarity and notation issues; references incomplete.

Actionable feedback
- Unify and simplify notation; add a running binary example to illustrate the main bounds.
- Include simulations comparing observed error vs. theoretical lower bounds under MSC/SHC.
- Provide at least one practical kIC decoder and show performance vs. single-item queries at equal monetary/time budgets.
- Discuss extensions: adaptive querying, instance difficulty, asymmetric worker confusions, and non-iid labels.
- Improve and complete citations (years, venues) and relate more explicitly to budget-optimal task allocation and inference-with-unknown-skills guarantees.

Overall recommendation
Borderline. The paper offers an appealing theoretical perspective and potentially useful insights, but it needs clearer presentation and empirical support to meet NIPS standards for significance.
