- Paper frames crowdsourced labeling as a joint source–channel coding problem, using rate–distortion and capacity to study budget–fidelity trade-offs.  
- Labels are treated as a source B(X); workers are noisy channels with two models: M-ary symmetric channel (MSC) and spammer–hammer channel (SHC).  
- Two skill-information scenarios analyzed: skills unknown to both (SL-UK) and skills known to crowdsourcer (SL-CS).  
- Authors derive lower bounds on queries per item for a target error via a separation-style argument.  
- Introduce k-ary incidence coding (kIC) as a structured query design that leverages combinatorial redundancy and invalid-response detection.  
- Provide an SHC analysis for kIC assuming an oracle decoder and offer a simple pricing heuristic.  
- Novelty: framing crowdsourcing limits using rate–distortion/capacity appears uncommon and could be a new contribution.  
- Strength: clear information-theoretic formulation separating source uncertainty from worker noise.  
- Strength: closed-form Rmin expressions for MSC that highlight the SL-UK vs SL-CS gap.  
- Strength: kIC is a concrete design that gives intuition about error-correction and pricing.  
- Strength: framework clarifies ultimate performance ceilings independent of specific inference algorithms.  
- Concern: bounds may be loose because realistic crowdsourcing severely restricts admissible encodings (human comprehensibility, per-worker limits, adaptivity).  
- Concern: limited freedom to design codebooks in crowdsourcing (no iid reuse, worker memory/strategy, meaningful queries), undermining operational relevance of capacity-style bounds.  
- Concern: kIC analysis relies on an oracle that perfectly identifies spammers/hammers and assumes non-adaptive, uniform assignment; SHC is stylized.  
- Concern: no empirical validation or comparison to state-of-the-art inference/assignment methods to calibrate bound tightness.  
- Quality: derivations seem technically sound under assumptions but need explicit justification for using HM(E(ε)) from worker heterogeneity (cite separation with channel state).  
- Quality: Theorem 4 requires careful sign handling (ln(1−q) < 0) and clarification of ε̄S vs ε̂.  
- Clarity: paper is generally organized but needs clearer, centralized statements of definitions and assumptions (feedback, independence, sampling).  
- Clarity: proof sketches—especially Theorem 4—should be tightened and made fully explicit; fix notation/confusable symbols (M vs N).  
- Originality: potentially novel application of information theory to crowdsourcing; kIC links to prior clustering-by-queries work but adds an information-theoretic perspective.  
- Significance: informative for idealized limits, but practical impact depends on tightness under realistic encoding constraints and relation to modern algorithms.  
- Pros for acceptance: clean theory, closed-form bounds, insight on known vs unknown skills, concrete kIC analysis and pricing heuristic.  
- Cons for acceptance: potentially unrealistic encoding assumptions and oracle elements, lack of empirical calibration, and some technical/clarity issues.  
- Suggestions: explicitly constrain admissible encodings (non-adaptive, per-worker distinct queries, bounded arity) and derive converses/achievability for them.  
- Suggestions: empirically compare limits to majority vote, Dawid–Skene, Karger–Oh–Shah, spectral–EM, convex clustering on synthetic MSC/SHC data and real tasks.  
- Suggestions: clarify M vs N and justify HM(E(ε)) with citations; expand Theorem 4 proof, define ε̄S, and resolve the ln(1−q) sign issue.  
- Suggestions: discuss adaptivity/feedback and whether separation-style results extend or yield gains.  
- Minor: check equation (8) minimization constraints, Theorem 4 inequality direction, explanation for HM(E(ε)) in (17), and correct typographical/reference errors.
