The paper presents a novel information-theoretic framework for modeling digital crowdsourcing, providing lower bounds on the number of queries per task required to achieve a given probability of error. The authors model workers as parallel independent channels, employing symmetric discrete memoryless channels (SDMC) to capture errors made by workers and the spammer-hammer channel model SHC(q) to account for the scenario where a worker is a spammer with probability 1-q. The analysis assumes nonadaptive coding and considers two cases: one where the crowdsourcer, responsible for determining the value of each task after collecting worker responses, has knowledge of all workers' skill levels, and another where this information is unknown.

In both scenarios, the taskmaster, who assigns tasks to workers, is assumed to be unaware of individual worker skills, resulting in a uniform task distribution across workers. The paper also derives a necessary condition for the minimum required rate, expressed as the number of queries per task, for k-ary incidence coding with an SHC(q) worker pool. The paper is well-organized and well-written, making it easy to follow.

However, the absence of experimental or simulation results to illustrate the gap between current schemes and the proposed limits is notable. For instance, comparing the performance of Amazon Mechanical Turk (AMT) to the theoretical bounds would have strengthened the paper. Sections 2.4 and 3.1 could be shortened or even omitted, as they do not contribute significantly to the overall understanding of the paper. Instead, including proof sketches for the theorems would have been more beneficial.

The paper's novelty lies in its information-theoretic analysis of the crowdsourcing problem, which has not been explored before. This framework is particularly useful for designers of crowdsourcing projects, as it provides a means to determine the minimum rate required to accomplish a task with a given probability of error, ultimately informing pricing decisions. Unfortunately, this review did not include an examination of the appendix, which contains the proofs, so no comments can be made regarding their validity.
