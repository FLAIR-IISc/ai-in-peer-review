The authors address the problem of aggregating worker output in crowdsourcing using the framework of information theory, providing fundamental limits on the budget-fidelity tradeoff. Although I did not fully grasp the paper, I found the insights and model to be an interesting and potentially useful approach to designing crowdsourcing aggregation techniques.

One potential improvement would be to more prominently highlight the high-level intuition and insights stemming from these results, such as those presented at the end of Section 3. Additionally, a concise conclusion summarizing the key takeaways would be beneficial. While the authors made efforts to make the paper accessible to non-experts in information theory, I still found it challenging to follow. I recommend rephrasing many of the results in terms of the crowdsourcing application itself, rather than relying on the language of channels, codes, and distortion.

In the related work section, it would be helpful to mention the alternative line of research on robust economic incentives for workers, often referred to as "crowdsourcing mechanisms" or "mechanism design for crowdsourcing." This body of work also explores varying worker abilities and the combination of incentives with sophisticated aggregation methods.

Minor corrections and suggestions include: changing "is reviewed in" to "are reviewed in" on line 84, clarifying the distinction between the crowdsourcer and taskmaster, correcting the typo in "A A(X1,X2)" on line 122, and rephrasing the sentence on lines 275-276 for improved clarity. Furthermore, a more detailed caption for Figure 2 would enhance its interpretability.
