This paper addresses the problem of data labeling from crowdsourcing, framing it as a channel coding problem. The authors model individual workers as noisy channels – specifically, both symmetric memoryless channels without feedback and a spammer/hammer channel model are considered. This allows them to analyze the problem through the lens of coding theory, mapping the crowdsourcing budget to the rate of the employed code. The central focus is on exploring the trade-off between distortion and budget, and the authors investigate a basic coding scheme, termed k-incidence coding, which effectively requires workers to label subsets of *k* items simultaneously.

I found the paper’s approach to be conceptually interesting and novel. The connection drawn between crowdsourcing quality control and channel coding is a clever and potentially fruitful one. By leveraging tools from information theory, the authors provide a formal framework for analyzing the inherent limitations and potential benefits of different crowdsourcing strategies. The consideration of two distinct worker models – the more general symmetric channel and the more targeted spammer/hammer model – demonstrates a thoughtful approach to capturing real-world complexities. 

However, my expertise lies outside the specific intersection of information theory and crowdsourcing. Consequently, I am unable to provide a detailed technical critique of the proofs, algorithmic analyses, or the specific coding scheme presented. While the abstract and introduction clearly articulate the problem and proposed solution, a deeper assessment of the technical contributions requires specialized knowledge I do not possess. 

Specifically, I would be interested to know (for reviewers with more relevant expertise): 

*   **Practicality of the k-incidence coding scheme:** How does the performance of k-incidence coding compare to existing, more common crowdsourcing strategies (e.g., majority voting, Dawid-Skene)? What are the practical constraints on choosing an appropriate value for *k*?
*   **Assumptions of the channel models:** How sensitive are the results to deviations from the assumed channel models? Are the models sufficiently representative of real-world worker behavior, or are more sophisticated models needed?
*   **Scalability:**  How do the proposed methods scale with the number of items to be labeled and the number of workers?
*   **Empirical Validation:** While theoretical analysis is valuable, empirical evaluation on real or synthetic crowdsourcing datasets would significantly strengthen the paper. Is there any experimental support for the theoretical findings?

Despite my inability to offer a deep technical assessment, the core idea presented is compelling. The paper offers a potentially powerful theoretical framework for understanding and optimizing crowdsourcing systems. I believe that, with rigorous scrutiny from reviewers possessing relevant expertise, this work could represent a valuable contribution to the field.
