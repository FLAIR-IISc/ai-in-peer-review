This work investigates the fundamental budget-fidelity tradeoff in label crowdsourcing using tools from information theory, specifically rate-distortion theory and channel coding. The paper presents a well-derived information-theoretic bound on this tradeoff, and if this is indeed the first application of these techniques to crowdsourcing, it represents a potentially valuable contribution. However, the novelty and practical relevance of the result remain questionable. While the derivation is sound, it appears relatively straightforward, and there is a significant risk that the bound will be loose in realistic settings.

The core concern stems from the applicability of channel coding concepts to the crowdsourcing problem. In classical channel coding, a large degree of freedom exists in designing codebooks and codewords – repetition codes, Hamming codes, convolutional codes, and LDPC codes are just a few examples. This flexibility allows for approaching channel capacity. However, in crowdsourcing, the analogous design space for the input sequence *u<sup>n</sup>* (as defined in equation (3)) is severely constrained. The authors implicitly assume that many mappings from B(X)<sup>n</sup> to U<sup>(∑mi)</sup> are valid codebooks; if this assumption is incorrect, the derived bound will be overly optimistic. 

Consider, for instance, the possibility of employing techniques akin to repetition coding. While majority voting can be seen as a natural decoding method analogous to repetition decoding, extending this concept beyond simple repetition is non-trivial. A key difference between channel coding and crowdsourcing lies in the independence assumption. Channel coding often relies on the ability to transmit the same message multiple times through the channel, leveraging the independence of each transmission. However, in crowdsourcing, the i.i.d. assumption underlying equations (1) and (2) is violated if the same query is presented to the same worker repeatedly, as this worker is likely to provide the same answer. This fundamentally limits the ability to enhance error correction through redundancy. 

In general, crowdsourcing problems are likely to possess substantial constraints not present in traditional channel coding scenarios, where mapping {0,1}<sup>nR</sup> to {0,1}<sup>n</sup> has relatively few restrictions. These constraints on valid encoding schemes, specifically in equation (3), likely render the proposed bound loose. Further justification for the validity of applying channel coding principles in this constrained environment is needed.

To strengthen the paper, a crucial next step is to compare the derived information-theoretic bound with the performance of state-of-the-art crowdsourcing algorithms. Demonstrating the tightness – or lack thereof – of the bound in practice would significantly clarify its practical value. Currently, without such a comparison, the contribution remains largely theoretical.
