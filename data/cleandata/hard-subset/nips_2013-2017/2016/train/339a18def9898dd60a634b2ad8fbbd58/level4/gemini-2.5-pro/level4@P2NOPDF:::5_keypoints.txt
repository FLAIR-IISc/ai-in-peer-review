- Proposes a novel information-theoretic framework that derives lower bounds on queries per task (rate) to achieve a target error probability.  
- Models workers as parallel, independent channels, considering symmetric DMCs for random errors and a spammer-hammer channel SHC(q) for adversarial behavior.  
- Assumes a non-adaptive query scheme.  
- Analyzes two aggregator cases: one with full knowledge of worker skill and one with no knowledge.  
- Assumes the taskmaster is unaware of worker skills, leading to uniform task assignment across workers.  
- Derives a necessary condition on the minimum rate for k-ary incidence coding under the spammer-hammer model.  
- Strength: first rigorous information-theoretic analysis of crowdsourcing limits, providing theoretical benchmarks relevant for system design and pricing.  
- Major weakness: no experiments or simulations to contextualize or validate the theoretical bounds against practical algorithms/platforms.  
- Suggestion: include empirical validation and comparisons (e.g., using Mechanical Turk data) to quantify the gap between theory and practice.  
- Presentation suggestion: shorten Section 2.4, tighten Section 3.1, and add proof sketches in the main text for accessibility.  
- Conclusion: novel and potentially impactful work, but limited by lack of empirical validation; reviewer did not verify appendix proofs.
