This paper studies the fundamental trade-off between budget (number of worker queries) and fidelity (expected accuracy) in crowdsourcing by casting the problem as joint source–channel coding. The taskmaster (encoder) partitions an inference problem into queries; workers (channels) return noisy responses; and the crowdsourcer (decoder) aggregates responses to estimate the desired labels or clustering. Using a rate–distortion framework, the authors derive lower bounds on the number of queries per item needed to achieve a target error rate under two scenarios: (i) unknown worker skill levels (SL-UK), and (ii) skill levels known to the decoder (SL-CS). They model workers primarily as M-ary symmetric channels and as spammer–hammer channels (SHC), and show that when skills are known at the decoder, the effective capacity corresponds to the average of worker capacities, whereas without skill knowledge it corresponds to the capacity at the average worker quality. They also analyze k-ary incidence coding (kIC), where each query presents k items and asks for within-query clustering, showing that restricting workers to valid responses gives error-correction benefits. For SHC workers, they derive a necessary condition on the rate for kIC to achieve a given error target, and compare these bounds to information-theoretic limits, with some discussion of query pricing.

Relation to prior work: The paper connects to extensive literature on aggregation and worker modeling (e.g., Dawid–Skene EM; Karger–Oh–Shah budget-optimal task allocation; spectral/EM approaches such as Zhang et al.; and graph-based clustering/convex methods), but takes a different angle by seeking Shannon-style limits independent of specific inference algorithms. This perspective is fresh and could influence the design and evaluation of crowdsourcing protocols.

Strengths
- Clear, principled use of rate–distortion and separation theorems to derive budget–fidelity lower bounds that are agnostic to the specific inference algorithm.
- Clean comparison between SL-UK and SL-CS cases elucidates the value of estimating worker skills.
- kIC is a concrete, interpretable querying scheme, and the analysis under the SHC model yields intuitive necessary-rate conditions and a pricing rule of thumb.
- The framework may inspire new coding-like query designs and sharpen thinking about redundancy versus efficiency in crowdsourcing.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Modeling realism: Point-to-point channel assumptions (iid items, memoryless worker errors, no adaptivity) abstract away important aspects of real systems: instance-dependent difficulty, intra-worker correlations, learning effects, and strategic behavior.
- Worker heterogeneity and allocation: The analysis assumes uniform rate allocation across workers. In practice, platforms often have worker ratings and support targeted or adaptive assignment. It would be useful to discuss how separation-based limits change under adaptive allocation or bandit-style policies.
- Channel models: MSC and SHC are stylized. The framework does not cover adversarial workers or coalitions trying to degrade fidelity; robustness to such behavior is important in practice.
- Assumptions on priors: The paper assumes knowledge of P(B(X)); this should be stated early and the implications discussed. Robust bounds when P(B(X)) is unknown or misspecified would strengthen the contribution.
- Empirical connection: Numerical results are limited and not compared to practical baselines (e.g., majority vote, DS, spectral-EM). Even synthetic experiments illustrating gaps between kIC and the information-theoretic lower bound, and benefits from skill estimation, would improve significance.
- Clarity issues in the exposition and figures (see detailed suggestions below).

Quality
- The theoretical development appears sound and leverages standard information-theoretic tools appropriately. The SL-UK vs SL-CS comparison is insightful. However, proofs rely on asymptotics, and guidance on finite-sample effects and constants would help practitioners interpret rates.

Clarity
- Overall organization is reasonable, but several assumptions (e.g., knowledge of P(B(X)), non-adaptivity, iid data) should be declared up front in the modeling section.
- Figure 2 needs clearer labeling. Explicitly identify what each curve represents (k values, SHC parameters q, and the information-theoretic limit).
- Please clarify the k=1 case: under kIC, k=1 reduces to directly asking for the label of a single item (no incidence), not a pairwise query.
- Editorial corrections:
  - Page 4, line 135: “Figure 1.a” → “Figure 1.b”; “Figure 1.b” → “Figure 1.c.”
  - The manuscript sometimes uses “Theorem,” “Lemma,” and “Corollary” without numbers. Use lowercase when not referring to numbered results.
  - Page 6, line 246: the assumption that P(B(X)) is known should be stated earlier.
  - In Appendix E (pricing), the ratio formatting is unclear; if intended, please write π(k1)/π(k2) ≈ k1/k2.

Originality
- Applying rate–distortion and separation to derive budget–fidelity limits for crowdsourcing is a novel and useful perspective relative to prior NIPS work focused on algorithms (DS, spectral/EM, bandits, convex methods). The kIC analysis, while conceptually simple, is a nice instantiation of coding ideas in query design.

Significance
- As a theoretical contribution, the paper offers a unifying lens and lower bounds that can benchmark algorithmic advances. The practical impact will grow with (i) extensions to adaptive/instance-dependent models and adversarial settings, and (ii) empirical demonstrations that the bounds are informative for realistic workloads.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- How would the bounds change if the taskmaster can adaptively allocate queries based on inferred worker skill (e.g., via bandits) or item difficulty?
- Can you incorporate instance-dependent difficulty (stateful channels) or worker learning into the channel model, perhaps via feedforward or state-dependent capacity?
- Do you have robustness results when P(B(X)) is unknown, e.g., worst-case over priors or minimax bounds?
- For SL-CS, what procedures and sample complexity are needed to estimate skills to the accuracy assumed by the bounds?
- Can you analyze adversarial workers (e.g., Byzantine models) and provide robust budget–fidelity bounds?
- For N>2 labels, can you quantify kIC gains and the number of invalid response patterns more explicitly?
- Please expand the numerical section: compare kIC to baseline aggregation (majority, DS, spectral-EM) under matched and mismatched worker models, and show finite-sample effects.

Pros
- Conceptually clean information-theoretic framework yielding algorithm-agnostic lower bounds.
- Insightful comparison between known/unknown worker skills.
- Concrete analysis of kIC and useful pricing guidance.

Cons
- Strong modeling assumptions limit realism (non-adaptive, iid, simple channels).
- Limited empirical validation and unclear figure labeling.
- Some assumptions and editorial details need earlier, clearer presentation.
