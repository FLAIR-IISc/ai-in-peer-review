This paper models the problem of label crowdsourcing within an information-theoretic framework to establish the fundamental limits of the trade-off between budget and fidelity. The core idea is to represent the items to be labeled as an information source, the crowd workers as a set of parallel noisy channels, and the query/inference process as a joint source-channel coding scheme. Using this model, the authors derive theoretical bounds on the minimum number of queries per item (rate) required to achieve a target level of accuracy (distortion). This is analyzed for two key scenarios: when worker skill levels are unknown (SL-UK) and when they are known to the aggregator (SL-CS). The paper also introduces and analyzes a specific query scheme, k-ary Incidence Coding (kIC). This work connects the practical problem of crowdsourcing design to the foundational principles of information theory, in a similar spirit to how information theory has been used to understand limits in other areas of machine learning.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality:** The primary strength of the paper is its novel and principled approach. Framing crowdsourcing as a joint source-channel coding problem is a very interesting perspective that provides a new language and a powerful set of tools for analyzing the problem. It moves beyond algorithm-specific analysis to establish ultimate performance bounds for *any* possible query and inference scheme.
*   **Significance:** The theoretical results, particularly Theorems 2 and 3, represent a significant contribution by characterizing the fundamental limits of the budget-fidelity trade-off. The insight derived from comparing these two theorems—that knowing worker skills allows the system to perform based on the *average of worker qualities* rather than the quality of an *average worker*—is an important and intuitive takeaway that is rigorously grounded by the theory. This framework has the potential to influence the design of future crowdsourcing systems.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   **Clarity:** While the authors have made an effort to introduce information-theoretic concepts, the paper remains challenging for readers not deeply familiar with the field. The heavy reliance on terms like "channels," "codes," and "distortion" can obscure the practical crowdsourcing insights.
    *   **Suggestion:** The paper's impact could be significantly broadened by consistently translating the theoretical results back into the concrete language of crowdsourcing. For instance, after presenting a theorem, explain its implications directly in terms of queries, worker reliability, aggregation, and labeling budgets. This would help a wider audience appreciate the significance of the findings.
*   **Highlighting Insights:** The paper contains valuable insights, but they are not always highlighted prominently. The discussion at the end of Section 3 is a key result, but it is presented briefly.
    *   **Suggestion:** Consider adding a dedicated conclusion section to summarize the main theoretical results and, more importantly, to spell out the high-level takeaways for researchers and practitioners in crowdsourcing. What should one learn from this work about designing better labeling tasks or allocating budgets?
*   **Related Work:** The literature review is focused on aggregation algorithms but omits a relevant and growing body of work on economic incentives and mechanism design in crowdsourcing. This research also addresses worker heterogeneity and is increasingly being integrated with inference methods.
    *   **Suggestion:** The related work section would be strengthened by acknowledging and briefly discussing this line of research to provide a more complete context for the paper's contributions.

**Minor Points:**
*   The distinction between the "taskmaster" and the "crowdsourcer" is not immediately clear. A more explicit definition of these roles in Section 2 would be helpful.
*   The sentence on lines 275-276 ("In case...") is difficult to parse. Please consider rephrasing it for clarity.
*   Figure 2 is hard to interpret without more context. A more detailed caption explaining what the different curves represent and what conclusions should be drawn from them would be beneficial.
*   There appears to be a typo on line 122 ("A A(X1,X2)").
*   Line 84: "is reviewed in" should likely be "are reviewed in."

In summary, this paper presents a highly original and potentially impactful theoretical framework for understanding the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing. Its main limitation is in its accessibility to the broader NIPS audience. By making the connections to the practical crowdsourcing domain more explicit, the authors could greatly increase the reach and influence of this excellent work.
