The problem of aggregating workers' output in crowdsourcing is solved using the language and tools of information theory, and the fundamental limitations between budget adherence are highlighted. I didn't fully understand the paper (see below), but I felt that the insights and models themselves were a very interesting and potentially useful perspective on the design of crowdsourcing aggregation techniques. I would have liked to see the high-level insights/intuitions (for example, the end of Section 3) arising from these results highlighted more prominently, as well as the conclusion of what one should take away from it (I know there's not enough space...). Although I certainly appreciated the authors' efforts to make the paper accessible to those who are not very familiar with information theory, such as myself, it was still quite difficult to understand in the end. I would recommend retelling most/many of the results in the context of crowdsourcing applications, rather than maintaining the language of channels, code, and distortion. Please mention the alternative (and slowly merging) line of research on strong economic incentives for workers in the relevant work (usually by the words "crowdsourcing mechanism" or "mechanism design for crowdsourcing"). Some of this work also discusses the different capabilities of workers, and how to combine these incentives with more sophisticated aggregation methods.
