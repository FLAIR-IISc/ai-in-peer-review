This work investigates the fundamental budget-fidelity tradeoff in label crowdsourcing, leveraging tools from information theory – specifically rate-distortion theory and the channel coding theorem – to derive a theoretical lower bound on achievable performance. The derivation of this bound appears sound. If this represents the first application of these information-theoretic concepts to crowdsourcing, it could represent a valuable contribution to the field. However, the practical significance and novelty of the work are questionable.

The core issue lies in the potential looseness of the derived bound. While the mathematical derivation is relatively straightforward, the applicability of standard channel coding principles to crowdsourcing is not fully justified. In traditional channel coding, significant flexibility exists in designing codebooks and codewords (e.g., repetition codes, Hamming codes, LDPC codes).  However, in the context of crowdsourcing, it is unclear whether comparable design freedom exists when constructing the sequence of channel inputs, *u<sup>n</sup>*, as defined in equation (3).  For the channel capacity to serve as a tight bound, nearly all mappings from *B(X)<sup>n</sup>* to *U<sup>(∑ mi)</sup>* should correspond to implementable codebooks; otherwise, the bound will likely be overly conservative. 

A simple example illustrates this point: repetition coding—where the same query is presented to multiple workers, with majority voting used for decoding—is feasible. Beyond this, however, it's unclear how to strategically design *u<sup>n</sup>* to improve error correction. This contrasts sharply with channel coding where encoding schemes like mappings from {0,1}<sup>*nR*</sup> -> {0,1}<sup>*n*</sup> face minimal constraints. 

A fundamental difference between the crowdsourcing problem and the classical channel coding problem is the inability to repeatedly present the same query to the same worker. Doing so violates the independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) assumption in equations (1) and (2), as the worker is highly likely to provide the same response to the identical query, breaking the i.i.d. assumption. Standard communication channels, however, *can* accommodate repeated transmissions of the same signal without violating underlying assumptions. This is just one instance of the stricter constraints inherent in the crowdsourcing setting.  It’s probable that numerous fundamental constraints exist on the allowable encoding schemes in equation (3) that are absent in conventional channel coding, and these constraints likely will render the derived information-theoretic bound quite loose.

To strengthen the contribution, it would be beneficial to compare the performance of existing state-of-the-art crowdsourcing methods against the derived information-theoretic bound. This comparison would provide a concrete understanding of the gap between theory and practice and highlight the potential utility of this theoretical framework.
