- Paper derives an information-theoretic bound on budget–fidelity in crowdsourcing via rate-distortion and channel coding; derivation appears sound and could be significant if novel.  
- Novelty and practical value are questionable: derivation is straightforward and the bound may be too loose to matter in practice.  
- Central concern: the direct analogy to channel coding is tenuous because channel-capacity tightness requires designing many powerful codebooks, which may not be possible in crowdsourcing.  
- For the bound to be tight, almost all mappings from B(X)^n to U^(∑ m_i) would need to be implementable as valid "codebooks," which is unclear.  
- A key limitation: you generally cannot assign the same query to the same worker multiple times (violates i.i.d. assumptions), unlike repeated symbol transmission in channels.  
- This constraint on allowable encoding schemes suggests the proposed information-theoretic bound is likely unachievable and therefore very loose.  
- Recommendation: empirically compare the theoretical bound to state-of-the-art crowdsourcing algorithms to quantify the gap between theory and practice.  
- Minor comment: in (8), explicitly state the minimization is over C and C' subject to E[d(Z, \hat{Z})] ≤ D^t.  
- Minor comment: the proof of Theorem 4 needs further elaboration.  
- Minor comment: in Theorem 4, clarify the relationship between ε_S and \hat{ε} given ln(1−q) < 0—is ε_S necessarily larger than \hat{ε}?  
- Minor comment: in (17), justify the use of H_M(E(ε)) with a brief explanation or citation.
