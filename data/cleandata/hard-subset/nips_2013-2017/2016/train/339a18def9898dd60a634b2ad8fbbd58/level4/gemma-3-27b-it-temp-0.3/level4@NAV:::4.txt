This paper investigates a crowdsourcing system where a taskmaster decomposes a complex inference problem into smaller tasks for distribution to a crowd of workers. The system accounts for worker heterogeneity in skill and potential unreliability in reported information, aiming to achieve a desired level of fidelity in the final inference despite these challenges. The core contribution lies in establishing a fundamental trade-off between the system’s budget – the total number of queries issued to workers – and the resulting fidelity of the crowdsourced inference. 

This trade-off is elegantly framed by mapping the crowdsourcing process to a joint source-channel coding problem from information theory. The taskmaster is modeled as a transmitter, the crowdsourcer as a receiver, and the communication links between workers and the crowdsourcer as a noisy channel. Leveraging established information-theoretic bounds, the authors derive a lower bound on the required budget to achieve a given fidelity level, independent of specific task design or information processing algorithms. They then analyze the performance of a specific task design scheme, k-ary incidence coding, comparing it to the derived theoretical lower bound under a defined channel model.

The connection drawn between rate-distortion theory and this crowdsourcing framework is a particularly insightful aspect of the work, and it has the potential to stimulate further research applying information-theoretic tools to crowdsourcing. However, the reviewer questions the degree to which the model captures the complexities of real-world crowdsourcing systems. The strict mapping to a point-to-point communication problem, while enabling the direct application of existing results, may oversimplify certain aspects.

Specifically, the authors would benefit from addressing the following points: 1) The current model conflates worker reliability and skill level into a single channel representation, and assumes uniform query allocation regardless of worker proficiency. Real-world systems often incorporate worker ratings or skill assessments to inform task assignment. 2) The realism of the chosen channel models in representing worker unreliability warrants further discussion, particularly concerning the potential for adversarial behavior where workers intentionally degrade fidelity. 

Furthermore, the presentation of numerical results could be improved. The labeling of curves in Fig.~2 is unclear, and the meaning of the $k=1$ case requires clarification – does this correspond to workers directly providing correct labels, negating the need for paired queries? The assumption of knowledge of $P(B(X))$ should be stated earlier in the paper (currently mentioned on page 6, line 246). Minor corrections are also needed on page 4, lines 135 (Figure 1.a should be Figure 1.b, and Figure 1.b should be Figure 1.c). Finally, the authors should exercise more caution in their use of “Theorem,” “Lemma,” and “Corollary,” reserving these terms for formally numbered results and using “theorem,” “lemma,” and “corollary” in other instances.
