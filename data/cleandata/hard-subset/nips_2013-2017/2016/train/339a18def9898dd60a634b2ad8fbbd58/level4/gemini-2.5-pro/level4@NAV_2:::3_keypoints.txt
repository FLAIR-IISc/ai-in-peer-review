- Models crowdsourcing as an information-theoretic joint source-channel coding problem to establish budget–fidelity limits.  
- Represents items as an information source, workers as parallel noisy channels, and queries/inference as coding.  
- Derives theoretical bounds on the minimum number of queries per item (rate) to achieve a target accuracy (distortion).  
- Analyzes two scenarios: worker skills unknown (SL-UK) and worker skills known to the aggregator (SL-CS).  
- Introduces and analyzes a specific query scheme, k-ary Incidence Coding (kIC).  
- Originality: novel, principled framing that moves beyond algorithm-specific analysis to fundamental limits.  
- Significance: Theorems 2 and 3 characterize fundamental budget–fidelity trade-offs; knowing worker skills yields performance based on the average of worker qualities rather than the quality of an average worker.  
- Clarity concern: heavy information-theory terminology makes the paper hard to follow for non-experts.  
- Suggestion: after each theorem, translate results into concrete crowdsourcing terms (queries, reliability, aggregation, budgets).  
- Highlighting insights: key results (e.g., end of Sec. 3) are underemphasized; add a dedicated conclusion summarizing high-level takeaways for practitioners.  
- Related work gap: omits literature on economic incentives and mechanism design related to worker heterogeneity.  
- Minor: clarify the distinction between "taskmaster" and "crowdsourcer" in Section 2.  
- Minor: rephrase the sentence on lines 275–276, which is difficult to parse.  
- Minor: Figure 2 needs a more detailed caption explaining curves and conclusions.  
- Minor: typo on line 122 ("A A(X1,X2)").  
- Minor: grammatical error on line 84 ("is reviewed in" → "are reviewed in").  
- Overall: highly original and potentially impactful work whose reach would be increased by clearer practical translations and broader contextualization.
