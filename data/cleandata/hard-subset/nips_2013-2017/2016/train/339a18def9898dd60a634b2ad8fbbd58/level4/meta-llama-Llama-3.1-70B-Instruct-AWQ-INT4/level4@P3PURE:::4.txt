The paper explores a crowdsourcing setup where a taskmaster divides a large task (e.g., an inference problem) into smaller tasks. These tasks are then presented to a crowd of workers, who provide information to a crowdsourcer after completing their assigned tasks. The crowdsourcer processes this information to conclude the inference task. This setup accommodates workers with varying skill levels and allows for unreliable reporting of information to the crowdsourcer. The primary objective is to ensure a certain level of fidelity or 'goodness' of the final outcome at the crowdsourcer, even in the presence of incorrect information from participating workers. This requires the taskmaster to carefully design small tasks (or queries) for allocation to workers.

The paper defines the total number of queries presented to workers as the budget of the crowdsourcing system. It investigates the fundamental trade-off between budget (number of queries) and fidelity (quality of the inference task performed at the crowdsourcer). This trade-off is achieved by mapping the crowdsourcing system to the problem of joint source-channel coding in information theory literature. The taskmaster is treated as a transmitter, and the crowdsourcer is mapped to a receiver/decoder. The unreliable links from workers to the crowdsourcer constitute a communication channel. Given this mapping, a lower bound on the budget (number of queries) for a desirable fidelity level follows from standard results in information theory literature. This bound is independent of the schemes used by the taskmaster to design queries and the algorithm used at the crowdsourcer to process collected information.

The authors then consider a specific scheme at the taskmaster, called k-ary incidence coding, and analyze its performance. They compare the performance of this encoding scheme with the obtained information-theoretic lower bound for a specific channel model. The reviewer finds the parallel drawn between the rate-distortion framework and the crowdsourcing system with unreliable workers quite interesting. This work may invite other researchers to study the crowdsourcing framework using information-theoretic tools.

However, the reviewer is not sure if the results presented in this paper model real crowdsourcing systems in a very realistic way. One reason behind this could be that the paper wants the crowdsourcing problem to exactly map to the point-to-point communication problem so that existing results from information theory literature can be directly applied with minimal effort. It would be great if the authors could comment on the following issues:

1. It seems that the reliability and skill level of workers are merged and modeled as a single channel. The paper assumes that the transmitter (taskmaster) allocates the same number of queries to all workers, irrespective of their skill level. This may not be true in real systems, where workers may carry some rating representing their skill level.

2. How realistic are the channel models in taking the unreliability of workers into account? What about workers who can have some knowledge of the task at hand and try to degrade the fidelity in an adversarial manner?

The explanation of numerical results is not very clear. The labeling of various curves in Fig.~2 can be improved. What does the $k=1$ case correspond to? Does the worker directly give the correct label of the presented query, as it is not possible to present a pair and ask if they share the same label in this case?

In page 6, line 246, the authors mention that they assume knowledge of $P(B(X))$. The reviewer believes that this should have been stated much earlier in the paper.

In page 4, line 135, the correct references should be: Figure 1.a --> Figure 1.b and Figure 1.b --> Figure 1.c.

The authors repeatedly use 'Theorem', 'Lemma', and 'Corollary' even when these terms are not accompanied by a specific theorem or lemma number. The reviewer believes that 'theorem', 'lemma', and 'corollary' would be more appropriate in such cases.
