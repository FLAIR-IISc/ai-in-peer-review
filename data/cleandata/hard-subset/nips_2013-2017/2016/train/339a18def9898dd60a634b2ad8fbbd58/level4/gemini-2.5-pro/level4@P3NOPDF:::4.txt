This paper addresses the problem of performing a large-scale inference task using a crowdsourcing system. In this setup, a taskmaster decomposes the main task into smaller micro-tasks or queries, which are distributed among a group of workers. A crowdsourcer then aggregates the responses from these workers to arrive at a final inference. The framework explicitly accounts for the fact that workers may have varying skill levels and can be unreliable, potentially providing incorrect information.

The central objective is to design the queries in a way that guarantees a desired level of fidelity (i.e., quality) in the final outcome, given a limited budget. The paper defines the budget as the total number of queries posed to the workers. The main contribution is an information-theoretic analysis of the fundamental trade-off between this budget and the achievable fidelity.

To achieve this, the authors establish an elegant mapping between the crowdsourcing system and the classic problem of joint source-channel coding. The taskmaster is modeled as a transmitter/encoder, the crowdsourcer as a receiver/decoder, and the collective unreliability of the workers is modeled as a communication channel. This novel perspective allows the authors to leverage standard results from information theory to derive a scheme-independent lower bound on the budget required to achieve a specific fidelity level. This bound holds universally, irrespective of the specific query design or aggregation algorithm used. The paper then proposes a concrete query design scheme, "k-ary incidence coding," analyzes its performance, and compares it against the derived information-theoretic bound for a specific channel model.

**Strengths:**

The primary strength of this paper is the novel and insightful connection it establishes between crowdsourcing with unreliable participants and the rate-distortion framework. This is a compelling analogy that provides a principled way to analyze the fundamental limits of such systems. This work has the potential to inspire other researchers to apply powerful tools from information theory to better understand and design crowdsourcing systems.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

While the theoretical mapping is elegant, its direct application raises concerns about how realistically the model captures the complexities of real-world crowdsourcing platforms. The simplification required to fit the problem into a point-to-point communication framework may overlook several important aspects. The authors are encouraged to comment on the following points:

1.  **Worker and Channel Modeling:** The current model appears to merge the distinct concepts of worker skill level and reliability into a single, uniform channel model. Furthermore, the framework assumes that the taskmaster allocates the same number of queries to all workers. This is often not the case in practice, where platforms utilize reputation scores or skill ratings to assign tasks adaptively, potentially giving more or different types of queries to more trusted workers. How might the framework be extended to accommodate heterogeneous workers and adaptive task assignment?

2.  **Realism of Unreliability Models:** The paper should provide more justification for the realism of the channel models used to represent worker unreliability. A particularly important scenario not addressed is that of adversarial workers, who may possess knowledge of the task and strategically provide incorrect answers to deliberately degrade the fidelity of the final outcome. How would the proposed framework and the derived bounds be affected by such strategic, rather than random, noise?

3.  **Clarity of Results and Presentation:** The presentation of the numerical results could be significantly improved. The explanation for Figure 2 is not sufficiently clear, making it difficult to interpret the performance comparison. The labeling of the various curves in the figure should also be more descriptive.

4.  **Clarification on the k=1 Case:** Could the authors please clarify the practical meaning of the `k=1` incidence coding scheme? In this case, since a comparison between items is not possible, does this correspond to a baseline scenario where a worker is directly asked for the correct label of a presented item?

**Minor Issues:**

*   **Placement of Key Assumption:** On page 6, line 246, the authors state the assumption that the prior distribution `P(B(X))` is known. This is a critical assumption for the entire framework and should be stated much earlier, preferably in the main problem formulation section.
*   **Figure References:** There appear to be typos in the figure references on page 4, line 135. "Figure 1.a" should likely refer to "Figure 1.b," and "Figure 1.b" to "Figure 1.c."
*   **Formatting:** The terms "Theorem," "Lemma," and "Corollary" are consistently capitalized throughout the text, even when not referring to a specific, numbered statement (e.g., "follows from the Corollary"). The lowercase versions ("theorem," "lemma," "corollary") would be more appropriate in these contexts.
