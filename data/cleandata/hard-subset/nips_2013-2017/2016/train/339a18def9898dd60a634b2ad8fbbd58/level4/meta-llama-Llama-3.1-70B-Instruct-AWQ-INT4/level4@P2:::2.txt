The paper "Fundamental Limits of Budget-Fidelity Trade-off in Label Crowdsourcing" presents an information-theoretic treatment of the crowdsourcing problem, modeling it as a joint source channel coding (JSCC) problem with random channel state. The authors use this model to establish fundamental limits on the number of queries per item in crowdsourcing, and provide a lower bound on the error probability of a coding scheme called "k-ary incidence coding" under a specific crowd-sourcing model.

The paper is well-written, and the authors' use of existing results on the coding rate in the JSCC problem to establish the fundamental limits of the asymptotic number of queries per item in crowdsourcing is a significant contribution. The model presented in the paper, where the labels of the items are modeled as an i.i.d. source, the taskmaster is modeled as an encoder, the crowd workers are modeled as multiple uses of a channel, and the workers' skill levels are modeled as random states of the channel, is a reasonable and intuitive way to approach the crowdsourcing problem.

However, there are a few technical comments that can be made about the paper. Firstly, the authors use the standard source-channel separation theorem in information theory to obtain their results (Theorem 2 and 3), but they do not argue that this theorem still holds when the channel has a random state, where the state is unknown at the encoder, but may or may not be known at the receiver. While it is likely that the separation theorem still holds in these situations, the authors should provide a proof or reference to an existing proof to support their claims.

Secondly, the model presented in the paper may be too restrictive, as it ignores the fact that both the taskmaster and the crowdsourcer may have access to the original data (the items). This knowledge of the original data can provide side information for the encoder and decoder in the JSCC problem, which may be helpful in certain situations. The authors should consider side information in their model to make it more convincing and realistic.

Finally, when commenting on the curves in Fig 2, the authors say that the lower bounds for kIC improve as k and q grow. However, this statement is slightly misleading, as the lower bound itself does not improve, but rather becomes smaller as k and q grow, indicating that the performance of the code may get better.

Overall, the paper is a good start towards understanding the fundamental limits of crowdsourcing using information-theoretic tools. However, the authors should consider addressing the technical comments mentioned above to strengthen their results and make their model more realistic.
