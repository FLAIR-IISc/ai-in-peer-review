- Models crowdsourcing as a communication system: true labels = source, workers = noisy channels, queries = code, aggregation = decoder; budget maps to code rate.
- Derives fundamental budget–accuracy (rate–distortion) trade-offs using joint source–channel coding; gives minimum budget to achieve target error.
- Analyzes two scenarios: unknown worker skills (SL-UK) vs known skills (SL-CS), showing known skills improve performance based on average worker qualities.
- Proposes k-ary Incidence Coding (kIC) as a practical query scheme and analyzes its performance under the spammer-hammer worker model.
- Compares kIC performance against the derived information-theoretic bounds to illustrate practical gaps.
- Strength — originality: novel application of information theory to establish fundamental crowdsourcing limits.
- Strength — technical quality: sound theoretical translation of channel capacity and rate-distortion concepts to crowdsourcing.
- Strength — clarity: well-written, clear analogy and logical structure from theory to practical coding scheme.
- Strength — theory-practice bridge: kIC grounds the framework in an implementable query strategy and design insights.
- Weakness — model assumptions: relies on i.i.d. items and memoryless workers, ignoring item difficulty, fatigue, or learning effects.
- Weakness — asymptotic bounds: results are asymptotic (L → ∞) with limited discussion of tightness for finite dataset sizes.
- Weakness — kIC practicality: higher k increases worker cognitive load and potential errors not captured by simple channel models.
- For acceptance: introduces a powerful theoretical framework, provides essential benchmarks, and connects theory to a practical query design.
- Against acceptance: relies on idealized assumptions that may limit applicability and underexplores practical cognitive costs of kIC.
- Overall recommendation: accept — strengths and significance outweigh the noted limitations.
