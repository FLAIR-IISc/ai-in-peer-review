This paper introduces an information-theoretic framework to analyze the fundamental limits of the budget-fidelity trade-off in label crowdsourcing. The authors model the crowdsourcing process as a communication system, where the true labels of a dataset represent the "source," the crowd workers are modeled as parallel "noisy channels," the query design is an "encoder," and the label inference algorithm is a "decoder." Using rate-distortion theory, the paper derives theoretical bounds on the minimum budget (queries per item) required to achieve a certain fidelity (label accuracy). The analysis considers two main scenarios: one where worker skill levels are unknown (SL-UK) and another where they are perfectly known to the inference algorithm (SL-CS). Additionally, the paper proposes and analyzes a specific query scheme called k-ary Incidence Coding (kIC) as a practical example within this framework.

**Strengths**

*   **Novel Framework:** The primary strength of this work is its novel application of a formal information-theoretic framework to the problem of crowdsourcing. Framing the budget-fidelity trade-off as a joint source-channel coding problem is an elegant and principled approach that offers a new perspective on the fundamental limitations of such systems.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The derivation of the information-theoretic bounds in Theorems 2 and 3 appears technically sound and is a correct application of established results from rate-distortion and channel coding theory.
*   **Interesting Insights:** The comparison between the SL-UK and SL-CS scenarios provides a clear theoretical insight: when worker skills are unknown, the system's performance is limited by the capacity of an "average worker," whereas if skills are known, performance is determined by the "average of the workers' capacities." This formalizes an intuitive concept.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

The main concern with this paper lies in the direct and largely unexamined analogy between communication channel coding and the design of crowdsourcing queries. This raises questions about the practical significance and tightness of the derived "fundamental limits."

1.  **Constraints on Query Design ("Coding"):** In classical information theory, the channel coding theorem relies on the existence of codebooks with specific properties (e.g., random codes) that can approach the channel capacity. There is immense freedom in designing the structure of these codes (e.g., repetition, Hamming, LDPC codes). In contrast, the design of queries for human workers is subject to significant practical and cognitive constraints that are not modeled in this framework.

    *   For example, the paper assumes a memoryless channel, which may be violated in practice. Asking a worker the same question multiple times would likely yield a highly correlated or identical answer, not an independent sample from a noisy process. This limits the straightforward application of simple error-correcting schemes like repetition codes.
    *   More complex query schemes, like the proposed kIC for large *k*, may impose a high cognitive load on workers, potentially altering their error characteristics in ways not captured by the simple channel models (MSC, SHC).
    *   Therefore, the set of valid and effective encoding schemes (mappings in Eq. 3) for crowdsourcing is likely far more constrained than for a typical communication channel. The paper does not discuss these constraints or how they might create a large, unbridgeable gap between the derived theoretical bounds and what is practically achievable. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a dedicated discussion on this topic.

2.  **Lack of Empirical Context:** Without a comparison to existing methods, it is difficult to assess the significance of the derived bounds. Are state-of-the-art algorithms operating far from this theoretical limit, suggesting a large potential for improvement? Or are they already close, suggesting the bounds are reasonably tight? The authors should compare their theoretical limits to the empirical performance of well-established crowdsourcing algorithms (e.g., Dawid & Skene's EM, spectral methods) on a benchmark dataset. This would ground the theoretical contributions in a practical context and provide a much clearer picture of their relevance.

3.  **Practicality of kIC:** While kIC is an interesting example of a more complex query code, its practical benefits are not fully established. The analysis provides a lower bound on its performance, but a more thorough evaluation, perhaps including a user study or simulation that models worker behavior with complex queries, would be more convincing. The pricing model proposed in Appendix E also seems heuristic and would benefit from stronger justification.

**Minor Comments**

*   In the definition of the rate-distortion function (Eq. 8), the minimization is typically over the conditional distribution P(Ẑ|Z) that satisfies the distortion constraint, i.e., `min_{P(Ẑ|Z) : E[d(Z,Ẑ)] ≤ Dt} I(Z; Ẑ)`. Please clarify the notation.
*   The proof for Theorem 4 could be elaborated further to improve clarity for the reader.
*   In Theorem 4, the condition for the rate is `R < (1/k) * [ln(1-q) / ln(ε̄_S)]`. Since `1-q < 1` and `ε̄_S < 1`, both logarithms are negative, making the ratio positive. However, the relationship between `ε̂` and `ε̄_S` should be clarified to understand when this bound is non-trivial.
*   In the proof of Theorem 2 (Appendix A), the capacity for the SL-UK case is shown to involve `H_M(E(ε))`. This result follows from the convexity of the entropy function and Jensen's inequality. It would be helpful to briefly state this reasoning or provide a citation to support this step, especially as it contrasts with the `E(H_M(ε))` term in the SL-CS case.
