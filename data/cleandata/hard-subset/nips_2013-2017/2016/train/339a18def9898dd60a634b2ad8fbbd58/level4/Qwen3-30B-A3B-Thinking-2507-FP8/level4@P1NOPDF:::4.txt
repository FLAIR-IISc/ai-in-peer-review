

The paper investigates a crowdsourcing framework where a taskmaster decomposes a large inference task into smaller subtasks assigned to workers. Workers provide responses to their assigned tasks, which the crowdsourcer aggregates to produce the final inference result. The system accommodates workers with varying skill levels and unreliability in reporting, aiming to guarantee a specified fidelity (quality) of the final outcome despite potential errors. The taskmaster's design of subtasks (queries) critically impacts this trade-off between the budget (total number of queries) and fidelity. The authors establish this trade-off by mapping the crowdsourcing system to a joint source-channel coding problem in information theory: the taskmaster acts as the transmitter, the crowdsourcer as the receiver, and the unreliable worker-to-crowdsourcer communication links constitute the channel. Consequently, an information-theoretic lower bound on the budget for a given fidelity level follows directly, independent of specific query design or aggregation algorithms. The authors then analyze a concrete scheme—k-ary incidence coding—and compare its performance against this lower bound under a specific channel model.

The parallel drawn between rate-distortion theory and crowdsourcing with unreliable workers is intellectually compelling and could inspire further research applying information-theoretic tools to crowdsourcing. However, the reviewer questions whether the model adequately reflects real-world crowdsourcing dynamics. Specifically:

1.  **Worker Heterogeneity:** The model conflates worker reliability and skill into a single channel parameter, assuming the taskmaster allocates identical query volumes to all workers regardless of skill. In practice, workers often possess skill ratings, suggesting adaptive query allocation based on worker profiles would be more realistic.

2.  **Channel Realism & Adversarial Behavior:** The channel model for worker unreliability appears overly simplistic. The paper does not address scenarios where workers possess task knowledge and intentionally degrade fidelity (e.g., maliciously). The realism of the assumed channel model warrants deeper justification.

3.  **Clarity of Results:** The explanation of numerical results (Section 5) lacks clarity. The labeling in Figure 2 is ambiguous: What does the $k=1$ curve represent? For $k=1$, the formulation does not permit presenting pairs of items (e.g., "Do these two share the same label?"), implying the worker directly provides the correct label for the single query. This critical detail should be explicitly clarified.

4.  **Technical Presentation:** 
    *   The assumption of knowledge of $P(B(X))$ (page 6, line 246) should be stated earlier, as it is fundamental to the analysis.
    *   Figure references contain errors: "Figure 1.a" (page 4, line 135) should be "Figure 1.b", and "Figure 1.b" should be "Figure 1.c".
    *   The authors consistently label results as "Theorem," "Lemma," or "Corollary" without providing corresponding numbering, which is inconsistent with standard mathematical writing. These terms should be omitted or accompanied by explicit numbering.

The core information-theoretic bound and its comparison to the k-ary scheme are technically sound. However, the model's simplifications regarding worker heterogeneity and channel realism, coupled with the presentation issues noted above, significantly impact the paper's practical relevance and clarity. Addressing these points would substantially strengthen the contribution and make the theoretical insights more applicable to real-world crowdsourcing systems. The reviewer recommends the authors explicitly discuss the limitations of their model in relation to practical worker behavior and provide clearer explanations for the numerical results.
