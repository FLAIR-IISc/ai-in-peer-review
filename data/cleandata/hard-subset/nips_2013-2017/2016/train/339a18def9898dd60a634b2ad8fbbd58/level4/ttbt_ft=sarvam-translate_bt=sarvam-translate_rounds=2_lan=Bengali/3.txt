The problem of integrating labor in crowdsourcing has been solved using the language and tools of information theory, providing fundamental limits to budget-friendly compromises. I didn't fully understand the article (see below), but I felt that the insights and the model itself were a very interesting and potentially useful concept on the design of crowdsourcing integration strategies. I would like to highlight the high-level insights/feelings (such as the end of section 3) that arose from these results more clearly, as well as a conclusion on what should be taken from them (I know space is limited...). Although I certainly appreciate the authors' effort to make the article accessible to those not very familiar with information theory, such as myself, it was quite difficult to follow until the end. I would suggest reconsidering most/many of the results in terms of crowdsourcing implementation rather than keeping the language of channels, codes, and distortions. See the line of research on strong economic incentives for employees in related work (and gradually integrating) (usually through the terms "crowdsourcing mechanism" or "mechanism design for crowdsourcing"). Some parts of this work also discuss the different capabilities of employees and how these incentives can be combined with more refined integration methods.
