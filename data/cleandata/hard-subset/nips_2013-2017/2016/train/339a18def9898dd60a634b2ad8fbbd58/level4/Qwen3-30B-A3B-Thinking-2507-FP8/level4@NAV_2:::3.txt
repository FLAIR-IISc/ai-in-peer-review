

The paper addresses the problem of aggregating worker outputs in crowdsourcing using information theory, establishing fundamental limits for the budget-fidelity tradeoff. The authors model crowdsourcing as a communication system with workers represented as noisy channels, and derive information-theoretic bounds on the minimum number of queries required to achieve a target fidelity. While I found the paper's theoretical approach to be interesting and potentially valuable for crowdsourcing system design, I had difficulty fully grasping several technical aspects of the model and results. The authors make a commendable effort to make the paper accessible to those unfamiliar with information theory, but the presentation remains challenging to follow in places. I would recommend strengthening the high-level intuition and insights emerging from the results, particularly at the end of Section 3, and providing a clearer conclusion summarizing the key takeaways for practitioners. The results would be more impactful if presented primarily in crowdsourcing terms rather than maintaining the language of channels, codes, and distortion throughout the paper.

The review should also mention the related work on robust economic incentives for workers (often referred to as "crowdsourcing mechanism" or "mechanism design for crowdsourcing"), which discusses varying worker abilities and how to integrate these incentives with sophisticated aggregation methods. This line of research is increasingly relevant and has begun to merge with the approaches discussed in the paper.
