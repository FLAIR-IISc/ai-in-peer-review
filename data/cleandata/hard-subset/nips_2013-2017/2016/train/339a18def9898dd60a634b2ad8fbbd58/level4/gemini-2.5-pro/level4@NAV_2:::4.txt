This paper frames the problem of label crowdsourcing within an information-theoretic rate-distortion framework to study the fundamental trade-off between the budget (total number of queries) and the fidelity (inference quality). The authors model the crowdsourcing system as a communication problem: the taskmaster acts as a source-channel encoder designing queries, the workers are modeled as parallel noisy channels, and the crowdsourcer functions as a decoder performing inference. Using this analogy, the paper derives information-theoretic lower bounds on the budget required to achieve a desired level of fidelity. These bounds are general and independent of the specific query or inference scheme. The paper then introduces a specific query scheme, k-ary incidence coding (kIC), analyzes its performance under a spammer-hammer worker model, and compares it to the derived fundamental limits.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The primary contribution of this work is the novel and elegant mapping of the crowdsourcing problem to the well-established joint source-channel coding framework. This provides a principled and powerful new lens through which to analyze the ultimate performance limits of such systems. This work could inspire further application of information-theoretic tools to problems in human-in-the-loop computation.
*   **Quality:** The theoretical analysis establishing the fundamental bounds appears to be technically sound. Deriving these ultimate performance limits is valuable as they provide a clear benchmark against which the performance of any practical crowdsourcing algorithm can be compared.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Clarification:**
*   **Model Realism:** A key concern is that in the effort to map the crowdsourcing problem neatly onto a classical point-to-point communication model, several simplifying assumptions are made which may limit the model's applicability to real-world systems.
    *   The model merges worker reliability and skill level into a single channel parameter and assumes the taskmaster allocates the same number of queries to all workers. Real systems often have worker ratings or histories that could inform an adaptive and non-uniform query allocation strategy.
    *   The chosen channel models (MSC and SHC) are standard but may not capture more complex worker behaviors. For instance, the model does not consider adversarial workers who might have partial knowledge of the task and strategically provide incorrect information to degrade performance. A discussion on the robustness of the framework to such scenarios would be valuable.

*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The manuscript could be improved with some clarifications and revisions.
    *   The assumption that the source distribution P(B(X)) is known is a strong one and is critical for the information-theoretic analysis, yet it is not mentioned until late in the paper. This should be stated prominently in the problem formulation section.
    *   The numerical results section is difficult to fully interpret. The labels for the various curves in Figure 2 could be made more descriptive.
    *   The concept of k-ary incidence coding for k=1 is confusing. As kIC is defined by presenting a set of k items, what does the k=1 case represent? Does it correspond to a baseline scheme of directly asking for the label of a single item?
    *   There are minor typographical errors that hinder readability. On page 4, line 135, the references to "Figure 1a" and "Figure 1b" seem to correspond to Figures 1b and 1c, respectively. Additionally, the terms 'Theorem', 'Lemma', and 'Corollary' are consistently capitalized when not referring to a specific numbered item; using lowercase would be more standard.
